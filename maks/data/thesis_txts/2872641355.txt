1
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Offshore wind power has become a cornerstone of Europe’s climate-neutrality plan. The European
Union now sets a headline ambition of at least 300 GW of offshore capacity by 2050 [1], with
Denmark, the United Kingdom, Germany and the Netherlands each planning multi-gigawatt build-
outs. Yet the macro-economic environment in which the first cost-competitive projects that once
allowed Danish and Dutch projects to offer zero-subsidy or even negative bids [2] has deteriorated
sharply. Steel and copper spot prices more than doubled between 2020 and 2024 [3], [4], and rare-
earth magnets, essential for direct-drive generators, registered price swings of similar magnitude
[5]. At the same time, successive interest-rate hikes by the European Central Bank have raised
the weighted-average cost of capital (WACC) for utilities by several hundred basis points [6], and
supply-chain bottlenecks in monopile fabrication and installation vessels have introduced schedule
risk that translated directly into higher uncertainties [7].
These pressures have coincided with an unprecedented surge in wholesale-price volatility brought
by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent re-pricing of European gas. The cumulative
effect is that several high-profile projects – among them Ørsted’s Ocean Wind 1 in the United States
– have searched for contract renegotiation, delaying final investment decisions or, in extreme cases,
cancel altogether [8].
Denmark’s 3 GW North Sea offshore wind tendering procedure launched back in December 2024,
crystallized these headwinds. Contrary to expectation, no bids were received [9], despite Denmark’s
world-leading wind resource and mature industrial base. The tender design required developers to
pay for seabed lease, to finance and build the grid connection, and to shoulder full market risk with
no revenue-stabilization mechanism, such as a Contract for Difference (CfD), which made it less
attractive for developers compared to similar offshore wind auctions in other European countries. In
an environment of rising material costs and financing rates, the uncapped negative-bidding design
back-fired, with developers preferring the option value of waiting instead of securing the site. The
failed tender is more than an isolated policy misstep, it signals a structural gap in the analytical
tools used by both governments and investors. Traditional deterministic spreadsheets models are
not equipped to evaluate project whose viability depends on the joint probability distribution of
wind resource, wholesale prices, policy interventions and commodity-cost inflation.
An important takeaway from Denmark’s abortive auction is the pivotal role that revenue-stabilization
contracts play in unlocking capital for offshore wind. In markets where projects sell power at the
spot price, lenders typically demand wide debt-service-coverage margins and offer only short-term
due dates, forcing sponsors to rely heavily on expensive equity. By contrast, a two-sided Contract
for Difference (CfD), the mechanism used in the United Kingdom’s Allocation Rounds, converts
a volatile merchant revenue steam into a bond-like annuity anchored to a strike price. That pre-
dictability raises permissible leverage and lowers the weighted average cost of capital. One-sided
CfD, such as those adopted in the Netherlands, sit between the two extremes, cushioning downside
risk while still allowing some market upside. Denmark’s decision to couple uncapped negative
bidding with full merchant exposure therefore removed the contractual pillar that has supported
most European projects in the last decade. The result, zero bids, was not a reflection of weak
resources or industrial capacity but of a financing environment that could no longer absorb the
compounded risks without a establishing policy barrier.
Commodity-price volatility now challenges project feasibility as much as wholesale-price risk.
BloombergNEF’s turbine-price index shows supply contracts about 20% more expensive in 2024
than in 2021 [10], driven by hot-rolled-coil steel, copper and rare-earth elements price increase.
OEMs have absorbed the shock first, with Siemens Gamesa booking €1.6 billion of contract losses
in 2023 [11] and GE Vernova pausing bids on its 18 MW platform until cost stabilization [12].
Developers now hedge steel on LME futures, accept allow surcharges and lift contingency allow-
ance toward 15% of total CAPEX. Such uncertainty cannot be captured by a single budget line,
therefore a probabilistic CAPEX model can quantify the costs that determine covenant compliance
and equity returns.

The Department of Wind and Energy Systems at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) has
long advanced an integrated, uncertainty-aware view of offshore wind economics. On the physical
side lies PyWake, DTU’s open-source Python library of engineering wake models whose vectorized
architecture makes large-scale Monte-Carlo sampling computationally tractable [13]. Existing tools
already simulate joint distributions of annual energy production and O&M costs, yielding unlevered
cash-flow forecasts rather than single-point estimates.
However, there are notable gaps in the
current research:
• A capital expenditure module covering the product acquisition (with geometric calculation
for some of the components) and a subsequent stochastic modeling that links future curves
for commodities to the project’s bill of materials (BoM)
• A project finance cascade that converts available cash flow into project valuation metrics
• A flexible policy section capable of valuing the model under different schemes
1.2
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to quantify how variations in support-scheme architecture and com-
modity price volatility jointly affect the probability that an offshore wind project meets bankability
thresholds and secure a positive NPV.
• Measure joint risk: evaluate how revenue-stabilization design and commodity price influ-
ence NPV and DSCR distributions.
• Extend CAPEX modeling: link commodity forward curves to the project’s bill of mater-
ials to create stochastic capital-cost scenarios.
• Implement project-finance cascade: convert probabilistic operating cash flows into valu-
ation metrics such as NPV, DSCR and subsidy cost.
• Compare support schemes: test two-sided CfD, one-sided CfD and merchant exposure
within the same Monte-Carlo engine.
• Provide decision metrics: make tail-risk transparent for developers, lenders and policy-
makers, showing when a project progresses from bid to build.
1.3
Main contributions
The main contribution of this research is to advance offshore wind techno-economic analysis by
embedding uncertainty modeling directly into the financial calculus. A single Monte-Carlo engine
couples commodity-linked CAPEX curves, mean-reverting jump-diffusion electricity price paths,
and probabilistically sampled financing terms, delivering probability distributions, rather than
point estimates, for net present value (NPV), debt-service-coverage ratio (DSCR) and subsidy
cost. This makes tail-risk transparent for developers, lenders and policymakers and shows when a
project truly progresses from bid to build.

2
Literature Review
This section presents the status of previous research across the different relevant topics for this
project. The European and Danish offshore wind support schemes have been studied to identify
the current challenges in the industry. Commodity price forecasting and project finance research
has also been carried out to develop the project and make the necessary assumptions.
2.1
Introduction to support schemes for renewable energy
The deployment of renewable energy sources (RES) across global electricity systems, and partic-
ularly in Europe, has been strongly shaped by public support schemes, designed to achieve their
renewable deployment and penetration objectives, attracting investors and lenders with less risky
conditions[14]. State support can be in form of Investment aids (e.g grants, loans discounts or
rebates), or through operational aids.
The academic and policy literature broadly classifies these operational schemes into two major
categories: price-based mechanisms, such as Feed-in Tariffs (FiTs), Feed-in Premiums (FiPs), and
Contracts for Difference (CfDs), and quantity-based mechanisms, including renewable portfolio
standards and tradable green certificates[15]. These policy instruments are not only subsidies, also
a price risk mitigator, and market signals that influence how RES interact with electricity markets,
investment decisions, and long-term renewable penetration[16].
Feed-in Tariffs (FiTs), were the most common form of support for renewable energy project before
the widespread adoption of auctions [15]. These guarantee a fixed, administratively-set payment
for every megawatt-hour (MWh) of realised production over a set period, were instrumental in
early renewable deployment across Germany, Spain, and other EU countries [17]. However, the
literature highlights significant drawbacks: FiTs decouple revenue from market prices, potentially
leading to overcompensation, high system costs, and distorted dispatch signals [18].
During the last 20 years, an acceleration in competitive bidding processes for selecting the most
competitive projects has occurred in the EU member countries, specially since regulation also
supports this. (e.g European Commission Guidelines on State Aid for Environmental Protection
and Energy, 2014)[19]. In Europe, successful bidders typically receive support in form of access to a
fixed remuneration or premium essentially an add-on to the wholesale electricity price, that exposes
them to different levels of price risk, depending on the individual designs. IRENA also published
a study and established a framework for energy auctions [20], in which the different remuneration
models are presented, and the European Commission Project: Auctions for Renewable Energy
Support (AURES) I and II, which aimed at supporting policymakers in EU Member States in
improving the effectiveness and cost-efficiency of their auction-based support schemes [21].
Feed-in Premiums, in which a fixed premium (€/MWh) is added on top of the market price,
emerged as a transitional instrument, exposing RES producers to some market volatility by provid-
ing a premium over the wholesale electricity price. Sliding premium variants attempted to better
track price movements but still left producers exposed to risks that are difficult to hedge, particu-
larly in markets with low liquidity or frequent price spikes[22].
Recent literature converges on two-sided Contracts for Difference (CfDs) as the preferred
support instrument, especially for capital-intensive technologies like offshore wind[23]. The EU
also stipulated in the Agreement between European Parliament and the Council on December
2023 [24], that two-sided CfDs, or equivalent remuneration methods should be used in competitive
auctions for new investments, lifetime extension or repowering projects.
In a standard two-sided CfD, the RES generator receives the difference between an agreed (or
awarded) strike price and a reference wholesale price (averaged over a certain period). When the
market price falls below the strike price, the generator receives a top-up. On the contrary, when the
market price exceeds the strike, the generator pays back the difference. This mechanism ensures
revenue stability while preserving market-based dispatch incentives. Kitzing et.al. publication
in March 2024 [16], presents the application and impact areas of Contracts-for-Difference in a
European context and a discussion on each design risk factors.
3
==PAGE:9==
Risk mitigation is a central reason why CfDs are favored in academic and financial analyses. CfDs
lower the cost of capital by reducing exposure to wholesale price volatility, which disproportionately
affects technologies with high upfront costs and long payback periods. In the recent years, and
according to [25], the Cost of Capital (CoC) for these projects has decreased, specially for large
investors, due to lenders decrease in debt margins and a decrease in investors hurdle rates.
Numerous studies examine comparative national implementations of CfDs [15], [26]. The UK’s
auction-based CfD regime is widely credited for driving rapid cost reductions in offshore wind.
Denmark’s hybrid CfD model combines a guaranteed price with developer obligations. Germany,
in contrast, relies more heavily on sliding market premiums but is considering CfDs to reduce
revenue volatility. A summary table with the original sources is presented in Section 2.2.2.
However, CfDs are not without challenges. The literature points to multiple design sensitivities that
can affect their effectiveness. These include: the choice of reference price (e.g. hourly, baseload,
profile-weighted, technology-weighted), the settlement frequency (monthly or yearly vs. hourly),
indexation to inflation, exposure to imbalance costs, curtailment, or negative prices. Poorly de-
signed CfDs can lead to inefficiencies or unintended consequences [16].
Denmark has only implemented CfDs for offshore wind, as only one technology-neutral auction was
launched in 2021, which received no bids. Seven round have been completed for several offshore
wind sites, but recent auctions have faced challenges, in April 2024, Denmark launched a 6 GW
offshore wind tender divided in 7 sites [26].
This tender, without subsidies, based on 30-year
concession payments and 20% state co-ownership, was canceled as no bids were received for the
first 3GW [27].
After a market dialogue published in February 2025 [28], which reflected the
industry challenges due to high costs, uncertain revenues and slow hydrogen demand, the Danish
Energy Agency is planning to launch a CfD remunerated tender in Autumn 2025 . Pausing payout
during hours with negative market prices was introduced with the Anholt offshore wind farm in
2010 [29].
In summary, the literature supports a transition from subsidies determined through administrative
processes to mechanisms aligned with the market that continue to mitigate investors risk. Two-
sided CfDs have emerged as the dominant model due to their balance between investment certainty,
market integration, and fiscal control. However, their success depends heavily on careful design,
and the Danish case is a challenging case were Contracts-for-Difference can help the country in
reaching its installed capacity objectives for 2030.

2.2
Review of renewable energy support schemes
The economic viability of renewable energy projects, specially capital-intensive offshore wind farms,
depends heavily on the structure of revenue support mechanisms. Electricity markets expose gener-
ators to wholesale price volatility, which can lead to financing challenges for developers promoting
new capacity additions. To mitigate these risks, governments have implemented a variety of support
schemes, including Feed-in Tariffs (FiTs), Feed-in Premiums (FiPs), and Contracts for Difference
(CfDs). Each scheme design offers a different balance between market exposure and revenue cer-
tainty, with CfDs increasingly becoming the dominant tool across Europe. This chapter explains
these support schemes in detail, with a focus on CfDs, their design elements, and their specific
application to offshore wind energy.
2.2.1
Electricity markets exposure
In liberalized electricity markets, revenues from renewable energy projects are tied to volatile
wholesale prices. This merchant exposure poses a significant challenge for new project financing,
as average wholesale market prices tend to decrease with the increasing renewable penetration. Off-
shore wind farms have distinct operational and cost profiles, with high upfront capital expenditure
(CAPEX), long development lead times, and relatively stable operational expenditure (OPEX),
which make them particularly sensitive to long-term price risk. The variability of electricity mar-
ket prices introduces financing uncertainty, which can raise the weighted average cost of capital
(WACC) and discourage investment unless mitigated by effective support mechanisms [30], [31].
The dynamics of the electricity markets influence how and when the producers can pay back
their initial investment. The volatility of day-ahead and intraday market prices, which depend on
factors like demand, renewable penetration, fuel costs, and congestion, leads to revenue uncertainty.
Moreover, offshore wind generation often peaks during hours with low market prices due to the
merit-order effect and high wind correlation across large regions [32]. This reduces captured prices
compared to the average market price. These challenges expose the need for support schemes
that lowers the merchant exposure help countries reach their renewable capacity and generation
objectives.
2.2.2
Contracts for Difference (CfDs)
Contracts for Difference (CfDs) are a cornerstone of renewable energy support in Europe, particu-
larly for offshore wind. As a market-based mechanism, a CfD stabilizes project revenues by setting
a long-term strike price. The generator continues to sell its electricity on the wholesale market but
receives or pays the difference between the strike price and a predefined market reference price [33].
Contracts for Difference (CfDs) are not a singular, fixed policy instrument but rather a flexible sup-
port scheme with multiple design dimensions, a wide range of design choices are available. These
can significantly affect the economic efficiency, bankability, and market integration of renewable
energy projects. This chapter structures the CfD design space according to key design dimensions
and explains their implications, with a particular focus on offshore wind.
Design of CfD Options and Implications
Contracts for Difference (CfDs) are designed to hedge long-term price risk faced by renewable
generators. By guaranteeing a fixed remuneration per MWh — typically through a strike price—
CfDs can shift price volatility from the producer to a public entity or counterparty. However,
the design of CfD depends on how the risk will be tackled. While often presented as a uniform
mechanism, the CfD framework can vary substantially across implementations depending on how
volume, price, timing, and asymmetries are specified.
This section unpacks these dimensions and formulates them mathematically. We also distinguish
between generation-based and generation-independent CfDs, and explore how combinations of

choices affect market integration, dispatch incentives, and investment risk.
Reference Volume: Generation vs Potential vs Capacity
The CfD payout is always tied to a reference volume, Vt, which defines how much energy is
financially settled. There are three primary definitions:
• Generation-based: Vt = Gt, the actual generation in MWh. Payouts scale with output.
• Capability-based: Vt = AAPt, the available active power calculated from wind speed,
turbine characteristics, and system constraints.
The general CfD formula becomes:
CfD Incomet = (S −Pt) · Vt
This formulation assumes a linear hedge: if market price Pt is below the strike S, the difference
is paid on the applicable volume. When Vt = Gt, the volume may vary significantly with weather
and maintenance. When using Vt = AAPt, it may hedge weather/curtailment risk but introduces
basis volume risk.

Two-sided CfDs cap upside and downside and are budget-neutral for the state. One-sided CfDs
expose the public budget to more risk but preserve investor upside, often lowering required strike
prices in auctions [34].
Strike Price Indexation and Risk Allocation
Strike prices S can be indexed to inflation or construction input indices:
St = S0 · (1 + πt)
where πt is the inflation adjustment. Indexation preserves the real value of support. CfDs without
indexation expose producers to cost inflation and reduce their hedge value over time.
Safeguards and Advanced Features
Other design options include:
Negative price safeguards: Suspend payments below Pt = 0 €/MWh to prevent overproduc-
tion.
Cap-and-floor systems: Apply a corridor [Sfloor, Scap] around the strike price. No clawback
occurs within the range.
Dynamic clawbacks: Reduce clawbacks in hours with low prices to preserve curtailment signals.
These modifications aim to reduce distortions in dispatch, encourage system-friendly operation,
and align incentives across market segments.

administrative complexity. Table 1 summarises key characteristics of CfD implementations in selec-

contract terms—each of which can significantly influence project risk profiles, dispatch incentives,
and public cost exposure.

in particular, adjusted premiums based on market price deviations, thereby combining price
security with some market exposure [33].

2.3
Electricity and commodity price forecasting
2.3.1
Electricity Price Forecasting
Electricity price forecasting has moved to the center stage in offshore wind finance because merchant-
revenue risk now outweighs even construction overruns in industry deliberations. Some Nordic-
Baltic bidding zones in the European electricity market hit 4,000 €/MWh price cap twice during
2022 [50]. This happened due to a combination of factors, including the Russian war in Ukraine,
which significantly impacted energy markets, creating extreme price volatility and uncertainty in
the energy supply. This happening outlined the importance of having a stochastic revenue treat-
ment to be able to cope with these scenarios. DNV’s 2024 advisory brief [51] summarizes the trend
for new lenders to consider that price variance is a binding constraint on project gearing. Although
CAPEX is also a very important, what DNV was trying to remark is a proper design for electricity
prices.
The forecasting literature spans traditional econometrics, data-driven machine-learning, and in-
creasingly, jump-diffusion processes that applies mathematic pricing procedures to electricity series.
Each of these models brings transparency, data hunger and the ability to reproduce sharp price
spikes.
Most early power-market papers reflect on the metal studies in structure. They combine an AR-
9
==PAGE:15==
IMA model to assess the conditional mean, while applying a GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive
Conditional Heteroscedasticity) specification for volatility. A 2023 MDPI review ranks ARIMA-
GARCH among the best performers for electricity price modeling [52]. These models align without
interruption with macro-scenario narrative (e.g. GDP, weather) that lenders are already monit-
oring. By contrast, fundamental dispatch models such as Balmorel [53], [54] take stock of recent
developments in the electricity market and generate forward-looking price paths—using the shadow
price ELECPRICE [53]—and now underpin scenario-driven outlooks like Green Power Denmark’s
Elpris Outlook 2023 [55] and Ea Energy Analyses’ long-term Nordic forecast for Skellefte˚a Kraft
[56].
As volatility worsened, researchers have been studying and including two extensions. First, mul-
tivariate and correlation-aware frameworks that have the ability to identify or recognize the cor-
relation between electricity zones under possible shocks. Second, mean-reverting jump-diffusion
models add a Poisson jump term so Monte-Carlo can reproduce large electricity peaks without
distorting the main body of the distribution. NREL´s 2023 ”Stochastic Price Generation” guide
[57] popularized this technique, while current commercial risk toolkits now ship calibrated jump-
diffusion examples for expeditious deployment.
Longer-term pathways are imported as a smooth deterministic line, but academic cashflow tools can
let the analysts enter a normal distribution for prices in the following year. Lately, cloud machine
learning services like ENFOR’s PriceForTM predominate [58], which publishes three probabilistic
updates every trading day. Although both feeds are useful and rich, the correlations are manually
enforced rather than modeled in the spreadsheets, and only one ”base”, ”high” or ”low” scenario
survives.
Industry practitioners already possess live prices, professional forecasts and even jump-diffusion
recipes. However, a module that converts these inputs into a fully stochastic forecast of electricity
prices suitable for Monte-Carlo revenue analysis still lacks. Delivering that missing link remains
an open opportunity, just as correlation between metal pricing is also an important matter on the
CAPEX side.
2.3.2
Metals price forecasting
Steel, aluminum and copper form the ”metal triad” of offshore-wind : steel supplies monopiles,
towers and jackets; aluminum dominates high-voltage export cables and nacelle components; and
copper threads every generator, transformer and array cables [59]. Because these three metals
together represent well over half of raw-material CAPEX, their prices has a direct impact on bid
strategy and financing margins. Yet spot and future markets have grown more turbulent as hot-
rolled coil steel prices have doubled and halved twice since 2020 [60], while copper and aluminum
each set record highs in 2024 [61] [62].
To cope with these volatile factors, analysts now attach price-forecast sub-modules to CAPEX
models. The metal-forecasting literature offers a spectrum of techniques - traditional econometrics,
machine-learning and hybrid ensembles [63] - each showing transparency, data needs and ability
to handle brusque movements across metals.
Most early studies rely on ARIMA for levels of prices as it captures trends and patterns in the
data, and GARCH-family (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity) models to
predict volatility to analyze how risk affect the model. They remain competitive for short-run point
forecasts and, crucially, align with macro-scenario inputs such as exchange rates or Purchasing
Manager Index (PMI, economic indicator derived from monthly surveys of supply chain managers
in the manufacturing sector). A 2023 World Bank working paper [64], for example, an ARIMA
variant continues to be the highest ranked model for predicting aluminum prices one quarter ahead,
especially when data availability is limited.
Because metal prices move under shared demand and policy shocks, researchers have extended
uni-variate models to dynamic-correlation GARCH [65]. Studies of Bloomberg metal sub-indices
show that up to a third of copper’s conditional volatility flows into aluminum during stress periods.
These models give costs analysts an off-the-shelf way to sample joint price paths for Monte-Carlo
10
==PAGE:16==
CAPEX. Academic Monte-Carlo tools such as the probabilistic O&M optimizer by Farhan et al.
[66] allow the user to input a normal distribution for steel price, but copper and aluminum remain
deterministic. In short, the offshore wind sector has recognized metal price risk but has not yet
adopted the more advanced, correlation-aware forecasting methods that the commodity literature
has developed.
In industry practices, developers do track metals daily - but mainly through subscription feeds
from data providers such as S&P Global Commodity Insights (Platts). Procurement teams pull-
hot-rolled-coil, LME copper, and aluminum benchmarks straight from the Platts API and drop
them into cost-tracking spreadsheets [67]. S&P also publishes quarterly and multi-year forecasts -
for example, its Metals Market Forecast series projects hot-rolled coil, LME copper and primary
aluminum out to 2035 with baseline, high-case and low-case tracks [68]. These curves are widely
used in financing models to set escalation factor or to stress-test debt covenants.
Yet even with access to forward-looking trajectories, the information usually enters engineering cost
tools as a single deterministic path per scenario. Correlations among steel, aluminum and copper
are imposed only explicitly and the day-to-day volatility evident in historical data is merged into
a smooth annual line. In other words, the industry now possess both live prices and professional
forecasts, but it still lacks a forecasting module that turn those inputs into stochastic, correlated
price paths suitable for Monte-Carlo CAPEX analysis. Delivering such a module remains an open
opportunity for offshore wind cost modeling.
2.4
Introduction to Offshore wind farms cost modeling
Global offshore wind capacity reached 78.5 GW in early 2025 after 11 GW of new projects were
commissioned in 2024, sprinting toward the 380 GW target that governments and the Global
Offshore Wind Alliance have set for 2030 [69]. Those growth targets collide with a reality of zero-
subsidy auctions and thin profit margins, so both developers and financiers demand cost models
that tracks where every euro of capital expenditure (CAPEX) is incurred and where it can be
reduced.
First-generation “top-down” spreadsheets — most notably NREL’s Cost & Scaling Model — re-
gressed tower, nacelle, and foundation prices on rotor diameter or rated power. This tools were
quick and useful when turbines were 2–3 MW in shallow water, but are increasingly blind to
today’s 15-MW machines, deeper sites, and floating concepts [70]. Over the past years the field
has pivoted to “bottom-up” frameworks that start with explicit geometry or a bill-of-materials
(BoM), convert those inputs into mass and logistics needs, and only then attach costs. NREL’s
open-source ORBIT installation model and the 2024 Annual Technology Baseline (ATB) cost lib-
rary represents that shift, offering Python functions that ingest YAML or JSON inputs and return
detailed, scenario-ready CAPEX breakdowns [71], [72].
The academic and research records shows a clear progression from heuristic estimates to physics and
data-rich engines. During the 2000s the NREL Cost & Scaling spreadsheet and similar parametric
regressions dominated the research. This tool treated cost as a smooth function of turbine size
and depth, useful for global scenarios but insensitive to site-specific geology or commodity shocks
[70]. By the mid-2010s researchers were already probing those limits by folding the same cost-
breakdown structure into multivariate regressions and neural networks, signaling an appetite for
component-level fidelity [73].
That appetite crystallized in process-based, bottom-up platforms. ORBIT converts user-supplied
geometry — for turbines, monopiles, jackets, cables, or installation vessels — into masses, weather-
constrained schedules, and fully itemized CAPEX components that can be looped through Monte-
Carlo simulations [71]. Because every dimension is explicit, swapping a monopile for a jacket or a
feeder-barge strategy is as simple as pointing the model to a new YAML file.
Geometry has proved particularly powerful for steel-heavy support structures. Parametric finite-
element studies show that shell thickness, pile embedment, and transition-piece height explain more
than 80 % of monopile mass variance. Contemporary sizing routines embed those relations so that
mass — and therefore cost — emerges automatically from various geometric tokens [74]. For float-
11
==PAGE:17==
ing foundations, techno-economic comparisons of spar, barge, and semi-sub concepts demonstrate
how hydrostatic dimensions cascade into tonne-metre budgets and fabrication cost, making floating
offshore wind an expensive alternative [75].
Still, geometry alone cannot capture supply-chain volatility, so material-resolved accounting has
become the second pillar. NREL’s 2023 inventory quantifies 24 materials in kilograms per megawatt
and confirms that steel supplies count for four-fifths of an offshore plant’s mass, while copper and
rare-earth magnets dominate price risk [76].
Beyond that, analysts draw on benchmark balance-of-plant (BoP) and soft-cost tables.
BVG
Associates’ Guide to an Offshore Wind Farm remains the most-cited open dataset (e.g. £220 k
/ MW for cable installation), though its authors note that post-2022 commodity swings require
adjustments, since it dates back to 2019 [77]. NREL’s Cost of Wind Energy Review 2024 updates
those figures with bottom-up BoP tools and shows that project management and construction
finance now rival turbine hardware in fixed-bottom projects [78].
Almost every modern study overlays these deterministic engines with probabilistic wrappers [66]
that propagate distributions for steel price, weather downtime, and inspection intervals through
a Monte-Carlo optimization of support-structure maintenance, trimming expected life-cycle cost
by roughly 15 %. The 2024 ATB uses a similar philosophy: geometry flows through ORBIT for
CAPEX, spatial modifiers come from NRWAL, OPEX estimates from WOMBAT, and learning-curve
trajectories convert it into full CAPEX percentiles under alternative subsidy regimes [71], [72].
The cumulative result is a fully Python-native tool-chain that is transparent, modular, and readily
embedded in risk analytics. It enables researchers and practitioners to pivot effortlessly among
design alternatives, commodity scenarios, and policy incentives without rewriting code, and has
become the common benchmark against which new CAPEX studies are judged.
In short, offshore wind CAPEX modeling has traveled from spreadsheet heuristics to geometry
and material aware engines with uncertainty embedded in the model. Geometry turns out to be
the master key for steel-intensive structures and material inventories reveal exposure to critical
minerals. Therefore, probabilistic wrappers translate both into the risk metrics that financiers and
policymakers now demand.
Three challenges remain ripe for research:
a) Automated geometry–material tagging, so each BoM line carries density, unit costs and other
useful materials;
b) Tighter coupling between CAPEX distributions and support-scheme cash-flows (CfD strike
prices, two-sided auctions, feed-in premiums), ensuring that cost uncertainty feeds directly
into bid strategy;
c) Real-time commodity feeds for steel, copper, aluminum, and rare-earth magnets, allowing
stochastic inputs to refresh with market data rather than annual reports.
By resolving (a) and (b), the thesis advances bottom-up CAPEX modelling and its integration
with financial valuation, while addressing (c) is out of our scope but is expected to yield additional
benefits as industry data infrastructures mature.

2.5
Review of offshore wind farm cost modeling
The economics of offshore wind energy are shaped by complex, interdependent cost structures that
span the entire life-cycle of a project. As the sector matures and the project scales in capacity,
distance from shore, and technological sophistication, accurate cost modeling becomes increasingly
important for financial viability, policy evaluation, and long-term energy system planning.
Cost modeling in offshore wind is typically categorized into four main expenditure categories: cap-
ital expenditure (CAPEX), operational expenditure (OPEX), development expenditure (DEVEX),
and abandonment expenditure (ABEX). Each of these categories corresponds to a distinct phase in
the project timeline and is influenced by numerous factors, including component geometry, material
selection, commodity prices, supply chain maturity, and site-specific constraints.
2.5.1
Offshore wind farms components and materials
In offshore wind farm cost modeling, the role of geometry and material composition is fundamental
to estimating capital, operational, and decommissioning costs with high accuracy. As turbine sizes
continue to grow and site conditions become more challenging (deeper waters and farther offshore),
the need for detailed, component-level modeling has become increasingly evident in both academic
and industry research.
Historically, top-down estimates dominated the offshore wind industry, relying on aggregated cost-
per-MW figures derived from market averages or benchmarks. Although these approaches were
useful for early-stage planning, these models struggle to reflect the nuances of technological innov-
ation, component scaling, and site-specific conditions. Recent literature increasingly emphasizes
bottom-up modeling frameworks, where turbine component are geometrically defined and costs are
calculated based on mass, volume, and material composition. These models allow for greater trans-
parency and are better suited to assessing the impact of new technologies (e.g. floating offshore
wind). Open-source tools like windIO or NREL’s System Advisor Model (SAM) now integrate geo-
metric parameterizations to estimate structural mass, fabrication costs, and installation logistics
at the component level.
There are a few materials that are commonly used in the offshore wind industry:
• Steel: Used in monopiles, towers, nacelle frames and substations. Steel prices are volatile
due to geopolitical factors and global supply chain constraints (e.g. COVID-19, 2022 global
energy crisis triggered by the war in Ukraine)
• Composites (Carbon/Glass Fiber): Very dominant in blades due to high stiffness-to-
weight ratios. Carbon fiber is costlier and very hard to recycle, prompting design trade-offs.
• Copper and Aluminum: Found in cables, transformers, generators, and switchgear. Off-
shore cables, in particular, are high-cost items whose price is strongly affected by copper
markets.
• Rare Earth (Neodymium, Dysprosium): Used in permanent magnet generators, in-
creasingly common in direct-drive turbines. While these magnets improve turbine efficiency
and reduce maintenance, supply risks from concentrated markets (mainly China) make them
a geopolitical vulnerability [79]
The materials involved in offshore wind are not only cost drivers but also strategic levers affecting
feasibility, resilience and environmental impact across the project life-cycle. As offshore wind farms
scale in size and global competition for resources intensifies, the role of materials in these projects
is becoming more pronounced. Additionally, most of these materials are influenced by geopolitical,
economic, and regulatory factors, often turning them into critical bottlenecks within the supply
chains of offshore wind developers.

2.5.2
Offshore wind turbines geometry
The geometry of offshore wind turbine components - particularly blades, towers and foundations
- is one of the most direct determinants of cost, not only affecting material requirements but also
logistical complexity (transport, installation) and operation and maintenance (due to load-induced
fatigue and accessibility difficulties).
- Blades: Blades are typically modeled using a combination of Blade Element Momentum (BEM)
theory and Finite Elements Methods (FEM), where geometry defines thickness distributions, chord
profiles, and internal structural layouts. Research shows that blade cost scales roughly with the
cube of length due to increasing structural demands and material volume. Advanced materials like
carbon fiber reduce weight but increase cost.
- Towers: Tower cost is strongly tied to height and base diameter, which influence steel thickness
and thus structural mass. Models often define towers as tapered hollow cylinders and calculate
mass using shell theory and steel densities.
- Foundations: For fixed-bottom foundations, the geometry is driven by water depth, seabed
conditions, and turbine load. Monopile and jacket structures are most common, and are typically
modeled using sectional diameters and wall thickness. Floating structures require 3D volumetric
models and hydrodynamic stability assessments.
In component-level cost models, material costs are typically derived from component mass and
unit prices. Mass is computed from geometric models (e.g. towers and monopiles wall thickness,
blade layers), while unit prices are obtained from historical procurement data or price simulated
curves. These costs may be:
• Fixed prices: Developers often secure long-term supply contracts for bulk materials like
steel to stabilize prices
• Indexed prices: Materials linked to global commodity prices. Copper, aluminum, and rare
earth elements are frequently indexed to commodities exchanges (e.g. LME)
• Stochastic prices: Forward-looking models (e.g. SARIMAX, jump-diffusion) are used in
research and advanced industry tools
This bottom-up calculation is crucial for parametric studies of cost drivers in large-scale wind farms.
For example, in the Dogger Bank project (UK), with 13 MW turbines and deep foundations,
structural steel mass per turbines exceeds 1,000 tonnes, contributing a significant share of the
CAPEX [80].
2.5.3
Offshore wind projects costs
Offshore wind costs are typically categorized into four primary expenditure types, each associated
with a specific phase of the project and driven by a set of variables.
CAPEX
Capital Expenditure covers all the costs associated with the procurement, manufacturing, transport
and installation of wind farm infrastructure. It is generally the largest single category of offshore
wind costs, often accounting for 70-80% of the total investment at the time of the financial close.
These payments include:
• Turbines: Blades, nacelles and towers
• Foundations: Monopiles or jackets and transition piece
14
==PAGE:20==
• Electrical infrastructure: Offshore & Onshore substation, array cables and export cables
• Installation and commissioning: Jack-up vessels, port logistics and cranes
CAPEX is typically incurred upfront, with most of the payments concentrated during the first
years construction window.
The payments are fixed once contracts are signed, but may still
sensitive to turbine size and configuration, distance to shore and water depth, seabed conditions,
and commodity prices.
OPEX
Operational Expenditure encompasses the recurring annual costs of operating and maintaining the
wind farm over its lifespan. This includes:
• Preventive and corrective maintenance: scheduled inspections, lubrication and firm-
ware updates, and unplanned gearbox, generator or blade repairs
• Vessel charters and offshore logistics: crew-transfer vessels (CTV) and service-operation
vessels (SOV), helicopter hoists, port fees and bunkering
• Remote diagnostics and control systems: SCADA-based condition monitoring and
digital twins platforms
• Insurance, leasing, and site access: all-risk and business-interruption cover, seabed lease
payments and harbour access charges
• Spare parts and component replacement: major components exchange contracts and
associated logistics
While some OPEX elements are fixed, many are performance and environment dependent (e.g.
turbine reliability, wind conditions, wave height, etc. [81]). Projects farther offshore or installed
in harsher environments typically exhibit higher OPEX due to weather downtime and limited
accessibility.
Recent OPEX optimizations have been endured through digitization, including predictive main-
tenance using SCADA data, drones, and digital twin modeling [82].
DEVEX
Development Expenditure covers all pre-construction and permitting costs, typically incurred the
previous years before installation. These payments include:
• Environmental and seabed surveys: geophysical mapping, benthic sampling and bird
monitoring
• Metocean measurements: LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) wind campaigns, wave-
rider buoys and current profilers
• Permitting, grid connection studies: Environmental Impact Assessments (EIA), trans-
mission system impact studies and tariff calculations
• Legal, financial and regulatory services: PPAs structuring, tax modeling and stake-
holder consultation
DEVEX is relatively small in scale, usually 5% of the total investment, but carries high risk, as
this payments are made prior to project certainty. The magnitude of the DEVEX depends on
jurisdictional complexity, site characteristics, and availability of pre-consented zones.
15
==PAGE:21==
ABEX
Abandonment Expenditure (ABEX) refers to the costs associated with decommissioning the off-
shore wind farm at the end of its operational life. These typically include:
• Turbine and foundation dismantling: blade segmentation, tower lowering and monopile
or jacket cutting using heavy-lift vessels
• Cable retrieval or burial: grapnel-hook pulls, trenching or controlled-flow excavation for
array export circuits
• Seabed restoration and environmental compliance: reef removal, back-filling of scour
protection and post-decommissioning habitat surveys
Due to the limited availability of real-world decommissioning data, ABEX is typically modeled as
a fixed proportion of the initial capital expenditure (CAPEX), often assumed to range between
2-5%. More advanced models may apply component-specific scaling factors to reflect differences
in dismantling complexity, disposal costs, or circularity considerations.
In recent years, regulators have increasingly required developers to secure dedicated decommis-
sioning funds during the early stages of project development, such as through deposits or financial
guarantees. This makes ABEX financially relevant already at the feasibility stage [83].
2.6
Renewable energy projects stochastic financial modeling
Renewable energy project valuation increasingly relies on stochastic financial modeling to capture
the fundamental uncertainties in electricity prices, policy environments, resource variability, and
technology performance. Traditional deterministic cashflow models are insufficient to reflect the
nonlinear, path-dependent risks faced by capital-intensive renewable energy projects.
The literature identifies two broad categories of uncertainty modeling: (1) Stochastic process mod-
els, which simulate variables like electricity prices and wind output using random processes (e.g.,
geometric Brownian motion, mean-reverting models, jump diffusion); and (2) Stochastic optimiza-
tion frameworks, where investment or operational decisions are optimized under uncertainty using
Monte Carlo simulation, multistage programming, or risk-adjusted decision rules.
A core application of stochastic modeling is in the simulation of electricity prices, which are known
to exhibit mean-reversion, seasonality, price spikes, and fat tails. Several studies adopt mean-
reverting processes with jump components to capture these dynamics. This approach allows the
modeling of hundreds of possible price paths, capturing realistic market dynamics. The price paths
are then used to compute cashflows, project-level metrics (e.g., NPV, IRR), and policy impacts
under different support schemes like CfDs.
Stochastic models also facilitate the use of risk-adjusted metrics: Value at Risk (VaR), Conditional
Value at Risk (CVaR), and Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) distributions. These enable the
analysis of downside risk, debt sizing, and exposure to unfavorable market conditions. Monte Carlo
simulation is especially useful for modeling path-dependent outcomes like tax deductions, payment
delays, and inflation effects.
Multistage stochastic optimization also appears to be useful for capacity expansion and bidding
strategy problems.
Although computationally intensive, such methods provide robust decision
rules under uncertainty. Compared to closed-form analytical models, simulation-based approaches
offer more flexibility and higher realism, particularly when modeling complex financial structures
and market conditions.
In conclusion, stochastic modeling is essential in modern renewable energy project finance. Whether
through jump-diffusion for price forecasting or Monte Carlo-based NPV estimation, these methods
provide a robust framework for integrating uncertainty into investment decision-making.

3.1
Uncertainty Modeling
To capture part of the financial risk profile of renewable energy investments, a comprehensive
stochastic modeling framework is adopted. Key uncertainties in market prices, capital costs, fin-
ancing, and wind conditions are treated as random variables or stochastic processes. These are
propagated through a Monte Carlo simulation pipeline to evaluate the probabilistic distribution
of financial metrics such as Net Present Value (NPV) and Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR).

3.1.2
CAPEX
The cost of major components in an offshore wind farm - including monopiles, tower and blades -
is sensitive to material prices. While the geometric design determines mass, CAPEX is ultimately
influenced by material price uncertainty.
To model this uncertainty, we use correlated stochastic simulations of future material prices, driven
by historical monthly data. This approach reflects interdependence between markets, while cap-
turing seasonal and trend dynamics.
Step 1: Material Contribution Analysis
To identify which materials significantly influence the capital expenditures (CAPEX) of the offshore
wind farm, a breakdown of material contributions was performed. This was visualized using a pie
chart representing the relative cost impact of each material across all components.
Figure 6: Percentage Material Usage
As shown in Figure 6, the majority of the CAPEX is driven by four main components: steel, triax,
copper and aluminum. Triaxial fiber (triax), a composite material used extensively in the blades,
also represents a significant portion of total material usage. However, due to the lack of publicly
available historical price data and the complexity of its fabrication process triax was excluded from
the forecasting study. Steel, copper and aluminum are primarily used in the structural components
(e.g. tower, monopiles), electrical system (e.g. generators) and supporting infrastructure. It is

Step 2: Mathematical Modeling
We model the historical monthly price time series of each material using a Seasonal AutoRegressive
Integrated Moving Average with eXogenous variables (SARIMAX) model. This choice is grounded
in empirical evidence showing that SARIMAX excels at capturing seasonal dynamics, trends, and
external shocks in time series. For example, a rolling SARIMAX model was successfully applied
to jointly forecast gold and crude oil prices, capturing their co-movement and market interplay
using correlated innovations [88]. Similarly, SARIMAX outperformed LSTM and ARIMA models
in medium to long term energy price forecasting — successfully modeling seasonality and volatility
through Monte Carlo-style residual shocks [89]. Finally, building on the approach of Panigrahi and
Mohapatra (2024), who applied SARIMAX to univariate gold prices [90], our study extends this
by injecting correlated stochastic shocks — sampled from the empirical multivariate distribution
of metal log-returns—into multiple SARIMAX models to simulate joint uncertainty across steel,
aluminum, and copper.
The historical dataset from 2012 to 2024 is sourced from the World
Bank Commodity Price Database [91]. Each price series Pt is first transformed using the natural
logarithm:
Xt = log(Pt)

• Non-seasonal part (1,1,0)

the next. This captures short-term dependencies.

monthly level.

– 1 (Seasonal Moving Average term): It accounts for unexpected shocks that happened
during the last year.
– 12: Indicating the model that seasonality repeats every 12 months.
Therefore, the non-seasonal part captures short-term behavior and general trends, while the sea-

3.2
Cost model
Offshore wind farm cost modeling is a multifaceted process involving detailed estimation of costs
across the full life-cycle of a project, from early-stage development and engineering to long-term
operations and eventual decommissioning. These costs are typically categorized into four different
types of expenditure: capital expenditure (CAPEX), operational expenditure (OPEX), develop-
ment expenditure (DEVEX), and abandonment expenditure (ABEX). This section presents a de-
tailed modeling framework that integrates both bottom-up (component-based, geometry-driven)
calculations and top-down (empirical, macroeconomic) estimations.
The approach is designed
around the 10 MW DTU reference wind turbine and is implemented using windIO’s modular
structure [102], enriched by updated methodologies and updated cost models from DTU’s offshore
wind turbine economic research [103].
A key objective of this work is to develop a robust, transparent, and data-driven framework for
estimating the capital expenditures (CAPEX) associated with offshore wind farms. Traditional
CAPEX model often rely on fixed cost assumptions and linear estimations. While this may provide
a rapid estimate, it lacks traceability and fails to account for dynamic market forces such as raw
material volatility, engineering-driven mass variation and supply chain complexity.
To address these limitations, this thesis introduces a modular and simulation-ready CAPEX model,
designed to explicitly track material costs, geometry-dependent component masses, and real-world
uncertainties in commodity prices. The framework is implemented across different modules, each
handling a distinct aspect of the modeling process, from physical modeling of components to
economic simulations under stochastic conditions. This structure not only ensures accuracy in
27
==PAGE:33==
estimating costs but also provides flexibility in adapting the model to different turbine sizes, site
conditions, or material assumptions.
3.2.1
Introduction to Cost Modeling Philosophy
The economic assessment of offshore wind farms requires a cost modeling approach that is not
only detailed and component-resolved, but also flexible enough to adapt to varying project config-
urations, site conditions, and uncertainty in market conditions. For this reason, the present study
adopts a modular, hierarchical, and component-based cost modeling framework, implemented as
part of a broader CAPEX simulation engine.
This methodology ensures a transparent and scalable approach to cost estimation, based on geo-
metrical design and material usage, rather than relying on empirical heursitics or scaling models.
Modularity and Hierarchical Structure
The model architecture of our framework is organized around a hierarchical cost tree consisting of:
• Categories (e.g. Turbine, Foundation, Electrical)
• Subcategories (e.g. Rotor, Tower, Monopile)
• Subcomponents (e.g. blades, hub, transition piece)
• Material instances with material properties, cost parameters, and scaling coefficients.
This modular decomposition allows:
• Cost tracking at every level
• Independent substitution of components without affecting the rest of the model
Scalability and Reusability
By abstracting component-level definitions into reusable YAML files (e.g. blade structure, tower
geometry, material properties), the framework allows the same modeling logic to be applied across:
• Different turbine designs (e.g. 10 MW, 15 MW)
• Varying farm sizes (e.g. 20 vs. 80 turbines)
• Different geographic conditions
This reusability is essential for performing comparative design studies, layout optimization or
scalability.
Integration with Sensitivity and Scenario Analysis
A key advantage of the components-based model is its compatibility with:
• Time series-based material price forecasting (e.g. steel, aluminum)
• Monte Carlo simulations for uncertainty propagation
• Parametric studies involving geometry, location, or turbine configurations
In summary, this modeling philosophy provides a robust and extensible foundation for economic
assessment in offshore wind development. By coupling geometric analysis with modular structure,
our framework supports not only accurate cost estimation, but also informed engineering decisions
and flexible scenario testing.

• Local airfoil relative thickness

• Chord length

3.2.5
Full CAPEX Computation with Forecast Integration
This section details the methodology used to compute the capital expenditures (CAPEX) of an
offshore wind farm at high resolution, integrating geometric definitions, material databases, cost
hierarchies, and forecasted material prices. The framework is designed to allow for modular ex-
tension if needed. The structure is implemented in Python but is well computed both engineering
and economic speaking, making it suitable for use in other contexts.
The model’s goal is to provide a realistic, component-level financial representation of wind farm
capital investment costs, based in actual design data and incorporating uncertainty in future ma-
terial prices.
Structural Mass Estimation for Steel-based Components
Before any economic assessment can be made, the physical properties of major components must
be quantified - particurlary mass, which forms the basis for material cost estimation.
Two of the most material-intensive components in offshore wind turbine infrastructure are the
tower and the monopile (foundation). Their mass is not estimated via generic empirical formulas
but through explicit geometric integration over their 3D structures, based on actual cross-sectional
and longitudinal design data extracted from YAML input files from windIO [111].
Geometry Definition

The CAPEX model reads structured data from the YAML configuration files, defining the entire

• ”Categories” (e.g. Turbine, Foundations)

• Nested subcomponents, each with their own costs and materials
Material Costing Logic
For each material instance used in any component:
• The mass is scaled by its complexity factor (set to 1 in our case) and multiplied by its current
unit cost (€/kg).
• If the material is steel, copper, or aluminum, its price may be overriden by simulated forecasts
(see Section 5.1).
• All material instances are aggregated globally to allow tracking of total material usage and
cost composition across the entire farm.
For components such as the blades, where costs are precomputed externally (from a separate blade
cost model), the value is simply multiplied by the number of blades (3 per turbine) and added as
a lump sum.
The mass-based material costs are accumulated up the hierarchy, from ”susbsubcategories” all the
way to the category level, and then summed across all categories to obtain the overall cost.
The category costs are then multiplied by 40 turbines (size of our wind farm) to obtain the total
turbine-engineering CAPEX.
3.2.6
Economic Evaluation Module: Site-Level Cost Modeling
In addition to the detailed, bottom-up cost modeling of turbine components (blades, towers,
monopiples, etc.), a complete capital expenditure (CAPEX) model for offshore wind must ac-
count for the economic part of it. These include development costs, operation and maintenance,
and infrastructural investments such as electrical substation and cable networks. These costs can-
not be derived from physical component geometry (due to the scarcity of these geometric designs in
YAML format) or materials alone. Instead, they are modeled using empirical relationships based
on wind farm parameters.
The methodology implemented in this study is adapted from the DTU Offshore Turbine Cost
Model [112], originally developed as part of the TopFarm2 framework. Specifically, it follows the
structure of DTU’s economic evaluation module, authored by Witold Skrzypinski and based on
work by Søren Ø. Lind. This model provides a deterministic, scalable approach to estimating
large-scale offshore wind project costs, and has been tailored here for integration into a broader
CAPEX assessment. The numerical coefficients used in this model are derived from DTU’s cost
model [103] developed through industry benchmarking, empirical data from real offshore projects.

To address this, a dedicated economic evaluation module is implemented as part of the CAPEX
engine. This module provides deterministic estimations of three key expenditure categories:
• DEVEX (Development Expenditures)
• CAPEX (BOP) (Balance of Plant-related Capital Expenditures)
• OPEX (Operational Expenditures)
A fourth category, ABEX (Abandonment Expenditures), is derived subsequently as a fixed per-
centage of total CAPEX.
Together, these estimates provide a complete picture of the full life-cycle financial cost of the wind
farm, beyond the direct material and component assembly costs.
Overview and Inputs
The economic module is initialized with the following high-level parameters:
• Distance from shore (km): Affects cable costs, met mast deployment, and service logistics.
• Rated power (MW): Power capacity per turbine; determines scale of infrastructure and
O&M needs.
• Rotor diameter (m): Influences cable lengths and spatial layout.
• Hub height (m): Indirectly used as a proxy for turbine size and complexity.
• Water depth (m): Not used directly in this module, but can influence foundation decisions.
These values are defined for each turbine, enabling a site-specific, turbine-level (see Table 9). The

3.3
Valuation Model
This section describes the methodology used to assess the financial performance and bankability
of the offshore wind project under different support schemes. The approach consists of simulating
the project’s cashflow for its entire lifetime, using stochastic electricity prices, CAPEX, ABEX
and WACC values and computing metrics such as Net Present Value (NPV) and Debt Service
Coverage Ratio (DSCR), to later compare its profile across the different support scheme designs.
3.3.1
Energy Remuneration under Support Schemes
The financial viability of a renewable energy project such as an offshore wind farm is strongly linked
to the revenues it receives from the electricity it generates or can generate. In this analysis, we
simulate project-level energy remuneration under several support scheme designs, including Con-
tracts for Difference (CfDs), Feed-in Tariffs (FiT), Feed-in Premiums (FiP), and the unsubsidised
Day-Ahead Market case. Because support scheme payments can include both inflows (top-ups)
and outflows (clawbacks), we refer to the resulting term as remuneration instead of revenue.

Let Pt denote the market electricity price at time t, Vt the wind farm’s power output in MWh,

Day-Ahead Market-based remuneration:
In the full market exposure case, the project sells
all its production at the market price. Remuneration is calculated and settled on a daily basis to
reflect real-world Day-Ahead electricity market operations, Nordpool in this case [114]. The total
revenue at time t is:
Rt = Pt · Vt
This scheme provides full exposure to spot price volatility and serves as a baseline for comparing
support mechanisms.
Fixed Feed-in Tariff (FiT):
The Fixed Feed-in Tariff mechanism ensures that the producer
receives a fixed price per megawatt-hour of electricity generated, regardless of fluctuations in market
prices. PFiT is this fixed tariff, the total remuneration at time t is given by:
Rt = PFiT · Vt
Under this scheme, all market price risk is assumed by the government, offering revenue certainty
to the project developer.
Feed-in Premium (FiP):
Under FiP, the generator receives a fixed premium π on top of the
market price. If Pt is negative or zero, remuneration is curtailed assuming the government will not
pay during hours with negative prices. Remuneration can be expressed as:
Rt = max(Pt + π, 0) · Vt
FiP still presents partial market exposure while offering a generation dependent revenue uplift.

3.3.3
Monte Carlo Simulation Framework
To evaluate the financial performance of an offshore wind project under uncertainty, a Monte
Carlo simulation framework is implemented. This approach enables the joint modeling of multiple
stochastic input drivers and their impact on project cashflows, profitability, and risk.
Sources of Uncertainty.
The simulation accounts for three primary sources of uncertainty:
• Electricity Prices: Hourly prices for the 2026–2050 period are simulated using a mean-
reverting jump diffusion (MRJD) model calibrated to historical data. Each run generates a
distinct 25-year hourly price path.
• Investment Costs: Capital expenditures (CAPEX) and abandonment expenditures (ABEX)
are sampled from pre-generated distributions. The same CAPEX sample is used to derive
corresponding ABEX values (Section 5.2.7)
• Cost of Capital: A per-path weighted average cost of capital (WACC) is sampled using a
PERT distribution calibrated to literature and inflation-adjusted assumptions.
Simulation Structure.
For each support scheme under analysis, the model evaluates a large
number of financial scenarios by randomly combining uncertain input parameters. Each simulation
path is defined by a randomly selected input combination:
(price pathi, capexj, wacck)
where i, j, and k are independently drawn indices for the price path, CAPEX sample, and WACC
sample, respectively.
This avoids the computational burden of a full Cartesian product while
preserving input diversity.
For each of the Nsim combinations, a monthly revenue series is generated using the logic of the se-
lected support scheme (e.g., Contract-for-Difference, Feed-in Tariff, Feed-in Premium, or merchant
exposure). These revenues are transformed into project-level cashflows by incorporating:
• Operating expenditures (OPEX), adjusted for inflation;
• Corporate taxation based on EBIT and a declining-balance depreciation schedule;
• Discounted capital outflows: CAPEX, DEVEX, and ABEX.

Wind Farm Assumptions
The offshore wind farm is assumed to have a total installed capacity of 400 MW, comprising 40

The valuation module can simulate a wide range of support schemes, including:
• Market-based: Energy is sold at Day-Ahead market wholesale prices, exposing the gener-

• Feed-in Tariff (FiT): A fixed strike price is paid per MWh generated, independent of
market price and without settlement delays.
• Feed-in Premium (FiP): A fixed premium (€/MWh) is added to the market price. No
payback during negative prices is considered
• Contracts for Difference (CfD): The project receives or pays the difference between a

Where St is the strike price at time t, Pt is the market reference price at time t, and Vt is

The following CfD options are modeled:
– One-sided or two-sided: Determines if clawbacks apply when Pt > St.
– Generation- or capability-based: Defines whether Vt reflects actual generation or theor-
etical available power.
– Indexation: Strike price St may escalate annually with inflation.
– Timing: Reference and settlement periods can be monthly, quarterly, biannual, or an-
nual.

full project economics. This top-down sequence guarantees that every euro and every kilogram is
counted once, and that each headline number can be traced unambiguously back to the component
subtotal that generated it.

CAPEX is affected by this uncertainty. Therefore, we run 1,000 simulations for these materials
prices with a 24-month horizon, generating a 1,000 possible CAPEX outcomes for every month.

Insights on Scheme Design
• One-sided CfDs, particularly in their capability-based form, consistently deliver the highest
NPVs across all strike levels. At 110 €/MWh, the capability variant achieves a mean NPV
of M€439, while the generation-based version reaches M€220. Their standard deviations
are also the highest (up to M€36), not due to volatility but to their retained market upside.
These schemes cap downside risk through the strike while allowing full participation in high-
price events.
• Two-sided CfDs produce narrower and more compressed NPV distributions (standard
deviations typically around 23 and 34M€), but this comes with constrained earnings. The
generation-based two-sided CfD remains negative up to 90 €/MWh, highlighting the impact
of curtailment during negative price periods. While two-sided CfDs reduce uncertainty, they
require high strike levels (e.g., 110 €/MWh) to achieve acceptable returns.
• FiTs and two-sided capability CfDs are esentially the same under the modeled assump-
tions, as no reference or settlement frequency differences have been set for them. Both are
availability-based, whihc removes curtailment risk, and deliver stable NPVs once strike levels
exceed around 90 €/MWh.
Their standard deviations remain moderate (around 30 and
M€35), still reflecting low exposure to electricity prices volatility.
• These results confirm that variance should not be viewed purely as risk. One-sided CfDs and
FiPs exhibit wider distributions, but this reflects optionality and potential for higher returns
under favorable market conditions. Scheme designs that combine downside protection with
exposure to upside seem to require lower strike prices, this is assessed in more detail in Section
6.6.
In conclusion, scheme structure determines not just average profitability, but also how projects
respond to uncertainty. Market-based designs offer growth potential but expose investors to losses.
One-sided contracts and premiums minimize losses while maintaining market upside gains, which
may be particularly attractive in volatile or low-subsidy environments. It also becomes evident
that if capability-based auctions are paused during negative-price hours and the bidding zone is
expected to have negative clearing prices, the expected bids in capability-based auctions may be
lower than those those in comparable generation-based auctions.
This demonstrates that increasing the strike price alone is not sufficient to ensure project viability,
scheme design plays a critical role in shaping both the mean and the spread of the outcomes.

The biggest contrast between the two strike levels is observed for the one-sided generation and
capability CfDs. With a moderate strike (Figure 19) these contracts are “activated” in roughly
half of the hours, leaving the remaining half fully exposed to the market price volatility. When

the spread between the 5th and the 95th percentile narrows dramatically, even as the median
revenue almost doubles. This indicates that raising the strike simultaneously boosts returns and
compresses variance as it removes an ever larger slice of merchant exposure. This floor-and-cap
asymmetry is characteristic of the one-sided design: the generator keeps all upside when prices
exceed the strike but is protected against downside when prices fall below it.
By contrast, two-sided capability CfDs already deliver near-flat cash flows at the lower strike,
since symmetrical difference payments neutralise both negative and positive price shocks. Their
inter-annual coefficient of variation (CV) remains below 0.5 % in both figures, leaving little room
for further smoothing once the strike is increased.
Merchant and Feed-in-Premium (FiP) sales, situated at the opposite end of the risk spec-
trum, display the widest revenue bands in both panels. Because these schemes leave hourly prices
largely unhedged, the inter-quartile range remains broad regardless of the strike assumed for the
CfDs shown alongside. A modest decline in their relative CV over time is visible, though, as the
common inflation indexation gradually lifts absolute revenue levels while spot- price variance grows
at a slower pace.
Key findings: Figures 19 and 20
1. Strike amplification of revenue. Increasing the strike from 60 to 120 /MWh raises median
revenues by roughly 90 % across all CfD types (compare the vertical position of the blue,
orange and green boxes between the two figures).
2. Variance compression for one-sided CfDs. The same strike hike shrinks the inter-quartile
width of one-sided capability revenues by about two-thirds (Figure 20 vs. Figure 19), because

3. Persistence of merchant risk. Market and FiP revenues keep a coefficient of variation an order

the residual need for additional hedging in these cases.

6.3
High CAPEX Scenario
As seen in Section 6 CAPEX has a huge impact on the project NPV, as cost overruns can amplify
debt service requirements and lower equity returns, irrespective of market conditions. While the
high price analysis checks upside optionality, a complementary high-CAPEX analysis isolates the
worst-case cost paths to quantify how different contract structures share, or concentrate, construc-
tion cost and abatement costs risk. From a theoretical point of view:
• Debt-service sensitivity: Increased CAPEX raises the loan amount and thus the annuity
factor for debt repayments, reducing free cash flow in every operating period, which will
increase the project payback time and ultimately reduce the NPV.
• Contractual insulation: Price-linked contracts (one, or two-sided CfDs, FiP) impact the
revenue distributions but cannot directly offset higher financing costs. Availability-based
schemes (capability CfDs, FiT) fix revenue flows irrespective of the market price but still
carry full capex-driven debt burden.
• Outcome dispersion: By selecting the top 5 % most expensive CAPEX realizations from
the Monte Carlo sample, we trigger a cost stress scenario that removes low-capex variance
and thus tightens, but uniformly shifts downward the NPV distribution.

(ii) Liquidity drop and DSCR breaches: Whenever settlement time is shorter than the
reference window (∆pay < ∆ref) the project receives no support during the initial months

settlement the gap lasts eleven months, pushing Pr[DSCRmin < 1.2] to (or near) 100 % in
every CfD scheme. Perfect alignment (∆pay = ∆ref) reduces that probability to single digits.
(iii) Reference-period smoothing: In two-sided, generation-based CfDs, a longer reference

(iv) Contract symmetry.
At a 110 €/MWh strike, market prices are below the strike in
roughly 80% of hours, so negative difference payments are rare. Consequently one and two-

This has been tested and contrasted with lower strike price levels, which
resulted in higher differences between the two types of designs, as there are more hours with
higher price than the strike.
(v) Fixed-tariff neutrality. Under the FiT regime, cash-flow variance is zero by construction.
The loss in NPV follows the discount-timing penalty alone.
(vi) Unbankable partial-merchant exposure. With a 30 €/MWh premium the FiP case
already fails the DSCR test in 87–100% of draws having at least a minimum DSCR below 1.2.
A fully merchant profile is worse, showing negative mean NPV in every timing configuration.
No combination of reference or settlement can compensate for it, at least at 75% leverage.
First, timing controls the present value of cash purely influenced by higher discounting and the
sequencing of cash relative to debt service (a liquidity effect), which dominates DSCR outcomes.
Second, reference-price smoothing redistributes option value between the counter-parties. For a
strike price above the mean spot price the generator benefits from longer windows, whereas the
off-taker would prefer shorter ones.
Some implications for the Policymakers would be:
• Aligning ∆pay with ∆ref, or extending the grace period, is a ”cost-free” way to eliminate
early DSCR breaches and lower the debt-service reserve requirement.
• Policymakers can safely mandate two-sided settlement provided the reference window is long
enough. The incremental burden is then negligible compared with the liquidity benefits of
timing alignment.
• For merchant or premium-only exposures timing optimization is insufficient; either the premium
must rise markedly or leverage level must fall to maintain the project bankability.
6.5
Required strike price for NPV break-even and bankability
Setting the right conditions and design is crucial for the state or auction caller, and an appropriate
strike price level is an important choice for bidders to make in renewable energy auctions. On the
one hand, if the strike is too low the project produces a negative net present value (NPV) or does not
generate enough earnings to pay back debt and the initial investment. On the other hand, if it is too
high the bid will have a low chance of being awarded, specially in very competitive environments.
With an excessive strike price, the scheme transfers unnecessary cost to the state and inflates costs
to consumers or the public budget. From the lender’s perspective a second constraint applies: the
project must generate a cash–flow buffer large enough to service debt during the tightest months
of the repayment schedule. This buffer is measured by the Debt-Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR),
and senior lenders in offshore wind projects have been assumed to require a minimum DSCR of
1.20 [137].

(i) Economic break-even: the after-tax, unlevered project cash flow discounted at the project
WACC (Section 3.1.3) is non-negative, i.e. NPV ≥0;
(ii) Financial resilience: the tightest point of the repayment profile satisfies min

By decoupling payments from

• Equity de-risking is fully achieved once strikes hit the NPV P90 threshold; any further support

• Revenue smoothing via capacity linkage delivers bankability at far lower subsidy levels than

• Budget certainty under FiP may be crucial when fiscal planning demands minimal volatility,

Conclusion
By synthesizing engineering detail, market stochasticity and finance structure within a single optim-
ization project, the thesis advances offshore wind valuation from deterministic sensitivity analysis
to a more probabilistic design model. The framework could be used by governments, lenders and
developers for allocating risk, an essential prerequisite for scaling the North Sea offshore wind
capacity without sacrificing fiscal discipline or financial robustness.
This thesis analytical core is a Python framework that combine together (i) a geometry-based
and material-resolved CAPEX module, sensitive to contemporaneous movements in steel, copper
and aluminum, with (ii) stochastic generators for hourly electricity, steel, copper and aluminum
prices, and (iii) a project finance cashflow cascade able to track every euro from seabed lease
through debt service. A PERT-based sampler draws WACCs from current danish offshore data,
so each Monte-Carlo path embodies a self-consistent set of market conditions, supply-chain costs
and financing terms.
A strike price search routine then adjusts the different support schemes
parameters in every path until predetermined bankability constraints are met.
Repeating this
procedure thousand of times transforms what is normally a single and deterministic strike price
into a set of different solutions, obtaining a curve of results, defining where equity certainty, lender
security and expected fiscal cost can be traded.
Interrogating that optimization yields three conceptual insights.
First, the architecture of the
revenue stabilization contract dominates conventional levers such as strike level, capital cost or
measured commodity inflation. Alterations to reference volume and payoff symmetry reshape the
variance. Second, supply chain turbulence and contract design interact in a nontrivial way. While
generation-based contracts expose lenders to concurrent drops in power prices and increases in
capital expenditures, capability-based compensation systems mitigate the spread of metal-price
shocks into debt-service pressure. Third, making the optimization multidimensional reveals that
risk can be reallocated without proportionate increases in public expenditure. Carefully calibrated
one-sided capacity-based contracts secure lender covenants while containing the public subsidy tail.
These findings carry direct implications for both policymakers and capital providers. Governments
seeking to maximize installed capacity per euro should treat CfD form as the primary policy
variable. Contracts that weaken curtailment incentives also benefit system operators because they
lower balancing costs, a system-level effect that the model can take into account.
The five main objectives have been developed and quantified in the Results section, not only
with the implementation of the different modules but also testing and stressing the model with a
relevant case study. Besides that, the point of view of the state has also been investigated, but it
is considered to be worth to investigate further, providing a more tailored tool for policymakers.
Potentially integrating a competitive auction module, in which the state could predict in more
detail the bids to be received for a specific scheme design.
Several other avenues invite for further investigation. Calibrating the stochastic modules with
confidential engineering, procurement and construction terms sheets and high-frequency imbalance
price data would sharpen tail estimates and enable lender specific covenant mapping. Interacting
live commodity feeds would convert the engine from an ex-post evaluator into a real-time decision
aid for auction designers. Physically, extending the cost model to other technologies would test
whether the observed contract-risk interactions persist. The tool could become a strategic cockpit
for multi-project portfolios if further developed to optimize bidding behavior and contract selection
under changing auction rules.
Lastly, a welfare-oriented evaluation of how different support schemes impact grid congestion,
curtailment, and eventually consumer surplus would be possible by linking the project engine to
transmission-system models. While this research aims to provide a comprehensive framework for
probabilistic project valuation and financial-risk management in offshore wind, there are expected
limitations. Results remain sensitive to input accuracy, as confidential EPC contract and intra-day
imbalance prices were unavailable and therefore not included. Future work should calibrate the
model with real-term sheets and extend it to new and promising technologies.
