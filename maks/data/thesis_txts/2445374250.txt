Abstract
Introduction: Neuroimaging with Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a valuable
asset for drug development. Using PET, it is possible to determine whether a new drug
can diffuse across the blood-brain-barrier and bind to the target of interest. The target
occupancy (‚ąÜ) is given as the fraction by which a drug occupies the target of interest.
Studies can be conducted to estimate the target occupancy. These studies are conducted
with several PET scans where one scan is taken at baseline when no drug has been ad-
ministered and the other scans taken after a drug dosage has been administered (block
scans). The common approach, called the Lassen plot (Cunningham et al., 2010), to an-
alyze these data from the several PET scans has been shown to produce biased outcomes
and be sensitive to noise. A recently developed method, called the Likelihood Estima-
tion of Occupancy (LEO) (Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018), has shown major
improvements compared to the Lassen plot in all relevant aspects. LEO estimates the
values of the radioligand non-displaceable distribution volume (VND) and ‚ąÜthat max-
imize the likelihood of the radioligand total distribution volume (VT) values observed
in multiple brain regions (ROIs), using the same assumptions as those implicit in the
Lassen plot. However, LEO can only handle the situation where a single dose has been
administered to each research subject. When developing a new drug, in order to deter-
mine the relationship between an administered drug dose and the resulting occupancy,
it is common that each research subject is scanned several times with multiple doses of
the drug. The aim of this project was to generalize LEO into Likelihood Estimation of
Multiple Occupancies (LEMO) so that it can accommodate multiple measurements at
varying doses for each research subject scanned. The project will consist of an analytical
derivation of a high-dimensional log-likelihood function and data simulation.

between an administered drug dose and the resulting occupancy when several block
scans are used. LEMO has been shown to have superior VND estimates compared to
LEO and the Lassen plot regardless of noise and occupancy.
In particular, for low

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Studies of the brain as a research field is under the broad and multidisciplinary field of neuro-
biology. The neurobiology field includes all research fields of the nervous system. One of these
research fields is the neurology field which deals with diseases such as Alzheimer‚Äôs Disease
(AD). The cause of AD is poorly understood and unknown, but it is believed genetic factors
could be of influence. Treatment of AD focuses on improving symptoms and life quality of the
patient as there is no cure for AD. (Lane et al., 2018) Like AD there are many other neurologic
diseases. Many aspects of these neurologic diseases can be studied. These aspects are ranging
from diagnosing the diseases to drugs for the treatment of the diseases. One tool which can
both be used to diagnosing of diseases and supporting drug development is Positron Emission
Tomography (PET).
PET is a technique used to observe the metabolic processes in the body. The use of PET
is broad and ranging from diagnosing of diseases to research areas such as supporting drug
development. In either case, the overall use of PET is based on the same concept. PET consists
of compounds which are labelled with radioactive material, a camera typically containing
scintillation crystals and a computer. PET via the camera counts the number of positron
emissions from the beta decay of the radiolabelled compound. However, PET provides very
limited structural information of the body which is one of the reasons for coregistering PET
images with other imaging modalities such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI). (Zhang
and Fox, 2012)
The compounds used in PET are biochemical substances which are radiolabelled. These bio-
chemical substances that are radiolabelled are called radioligands as they consist of radioactive
materials. There are many ways to combine biochemical substances with radioactive materials
and this is a research area itself. (R. N. Gunn and Rabiner, 2017; Matthews et al., 2012) A
property of the radioactive materials is that they can lead to ionization. Another property
of the radioactive materials is their half-life. Depending on the objective of the human brain
study the half-life of the radioactive material can span from a few seconds to hours as most of
the radioactive decay is desired to happen inside the body when the patient is being scanned.
The radioligand is administered to the patient via injection into the bloodstream where the ra-
dioligand then flows with the bloodstream and diffuses across the Blood-Brain-Barrier (BBB)
into the brain.

1 Introduction
Depending on what is wanted to be studied in the brain specific radioligands can then bind to
specific targets such as a serotonin neuroreceptor which is implicated in a variety of diseases
such as AD. With only PET data one would be able to use the spatial information from the PET
image to see where in the brain the uptake of the radioligand is. The spatial information from
the PET image could also be used to see the location of the specific targets in the brain. With
the application of mathematical models called kinetic modelling and the acquired PET data
one can be able to quantify the kinetics of the radioligand such as quantifying the rate of influx
of a radioligand into brain tissue or other relevant parameter estimates. The kinetic models
are typically non-linear and hence parameter estimations are done by non-linear regression.
This type of parameter estimation is specific for the model chosen. (Mark Slifstein and Marc
Laruelle, 2001)
PET can also be used as a tool for drug development. With PET one can estimate how much
a certain drug is occupying the target for which the drug is intended to work on. The kind of
studies where occupancy is estimated is called target occupancy studies. The target for which
a drug is intended could be a neuroreceptor hence a receptor occupancy would be estimated.
The occupancy estimated is a fractional occupancy (‚ąÜ) and can be estimated by having at
least two PET scans. One scan before any drug is administered (baseline scan) and one scan
after the drug has been administered (block scan). The administered drug and radioligand
binds to the same neuroreceptors and will therefore compete for the same binding sites. Hence
the difference between a baseline scan and a block scan can then be used to estimate the target
occupancy of the drug.
Another method to estimate ‚ąÜis by the Lassen plot (N A Lassen et al., 1995) which was later
revised and simply called the revised Lassen plot (Cunningham et al., 2010). Both the original
Lassen plot and revised Lassen plot are graphical approaches which by linear regression seeks
to estimate the occupancy. Both plots though cannot be used if parameters estimated from
kinetic modelling is not at hand.
Even though the Lassen plot, or the revised Lassen plot, is simple to use and implement it
violates the assumptions of linear regression. First, the baseline scan is both a variable in the
dependent and independent variables and secondly the baseline scan is noisy which makes the
noise correlated with the dependent variable (Cunningham et al., 2010; Naganawa, Gallezot,
et al., 2017; Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018; Slifstein and Laruelle, 2000). Hence
alternative methods are sought.
Recently one such new alternative was proposed. The new method is maximum Likelihood
Estimation of Occupancy (LEO). (Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018) LEO is based
upon the same assumptions as the Lassen plot which are that ‚ąÜand volumes of distribution
in each region estimated are the same and that only ‚ąÜdiffers between baseline and block
scan. Thereby a joint probability density function can be constructed and the likelihood which
maximises the estimates of ‚ąÜand volume of distribution can be found. The LEO method
has shown major improvements in all relevant aspects compared to the graphical approaches
(Lassen plot/revised Lassen plot). The LEO method is though limited to only handle the
situation where a single dose is administered to each research subject, i.e the case where each
subject has underwent one baseline and one block scan. To determine the relationship between
administered dose and the resulting occupancy, it is common in drug development applications,

This project aims to extend the LEO method so that it can accommodate multiple block

2.1
Positron Emission Tomography
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a functional imaging technique used in nuclear
medicine that enables the study of metabolic processes in the body.
The technique relies
on radiotracers to emit radiation resulting from ő≤+ decay to trace the biochemical processes
happening inside the body.
The ő≤+ decay occurs when the nucleus has a low neutron-to-
proton ratio and can attain stability by emitting a positron. (Cember and Johnson, 2008) If
the positron interacts with an electron they are annihilated. The annihilation of a positron
with an electron will lead to two photons separated by 180¬į. The annihilation process is an
essential part of the PET imaging technique.

Radioactive compounds
There are over hundreds of radionuclides but only a few out of these are suitable for PET ap-
plications. The prerequisites that has to be fullfilled to use radionuclides for PET applications
can be many depending on what is wanted to be studied. (Ametamey et al., 2008; Matthews
et al., 2012; Takano et al., 2016; Varn√§s et al., 2013) Furthermore, the biochemical processes
behind these prerequisites can be complex.
Therefore the prerequisites presented here are
simplifications.
Radionuclides are by some complex biochemical process combined with ligand (i.e.
ion or
molecule) to make what is called a radioligand. Some of the prerequisites for these radioligands
used for brain PET imaging are the following:
‚ÄĘ Selectivity/affinity for the target: Affinity is the inverse of KD (radioligand equilib-
rium dissociation constant). KD is a term describing the dissociation/separation from
bound complex to unbound complex. The radioligand has to have some selectivity/affin-
ity for the target site. The binding to the target site should ideally have the highest
affinity for the target site which is wanted to be studied.
‚ÄĘ Metabolism: When the radioligand in any way is administered into the blood stream
it will to a degree metabolize i.e degraded into some other smaller compound which is
referred to as metabolites. Depending on how the radioligand is metabolized and what
the radioligand is metabolized into then the metabolites can either be bound with the

radionuclude or not. The PET signal can only measure compounds which emit radiation
hence the metabolites which do not have any radionuclide would disappear from the
signal measured by PET and are therefore not considered. If the radioligand metabolizes
to some metabolite which has a radinuclide bound then this would be measured by the
PET signal. The metabolite bound with a radionuclide is referred to as the radiolabelled

A physical property of radionuclides is their half-life (T1/2). The half-life is a quantity that
describes the time it takes for half the radionuclides to decay. Depending on the radionuclide
the T1/2 can span from few ns to billions of years.
Though for the radionuclides used in
human subjects T1/2 usually spans from a few seconds to a few hours (Cember and Johnson,
2008; Prince and Links, 2015). The cause for using radionuclides with relative short T1/2 is
to minimize the radiation dose. The radiation dose is not directly related to the radionuclides
T1/2, but the lower the half-life, the faster the radionuclide will decay and thereby less risk of
depositing energy to the surrounding tissue. How much energy which is deposited into tissue
depends on the particles energy, radiation type and tissue (Matthews et al., 2012; Takano
et al., 2016). Moreover, the relative short half-life makes the PET images towards the end of
the studies more noisy as there will be less emissions to detect.

2.2
Kinetic modelling
The PET images obtained are high-quality 3 dimensional images. These PET images provide
spatial information and with TACs also quantitative information. Further quantitative analysis
of the TACs can be done. One method often used to describe the TACs are kinetic modelling
which is a mathematical toolbox. Knowledge of the radioligand are used together with the
measured data to develop a mathematical model. The separate pools of radioligand substance
are defined as compartments. (K.L. Leenders, 2007) Thereby these mathematical models are
also known as Compartmental Models (CM). A CM describes the uptake and clearance of a
radioligand in tissue.
Before diving further into the CMs some general assumptions about the PET radioligand must
be made. These are general assumptions, 1) the radioligand does not affect the physiological
and molecular interactions. An extension to 1) assumption is 2) that the interactions are in
steady-state and lastly 3) concentrations in the compartments are homogeneous. Assumption
3) is an essential assumption to be able to use the mathematical models concerning the com-
partments. If this does not hold then distributive models could be used (K.L. Leenders, 2007)
but this is not the scope of this thesis.

2.3.1
Competitive binding in target occupancy studies
If looking back at Figure 2.6b then some time after the drug has been administered a block
scan will be conducted. If one where to look at the regional VT values one would see that
the regional VT values measured for the block scan would have changed from the regional VT
values measured from the baseline scan. This change between the regional VT value for the
baseline and block scan would be due to competitive binding between the administered drug
and radioligand. The competitive binding between administered drug and radioligand is based
on the assumption that both drug and radioligand bind to the same specific target thereby
competing for the same binding site of the target. Hence competitive binding is an underlying
principle for target occupancy studies as the competitive binding for the same target translates
to changes which can be measured and thereby calculation of ‚ąÜ. (Marc Laruelle, 2000; Takano
et al., 2016)
If one where to further look at the change seen in regional VT values between baseline and block
scan for one subject and for one region. The change in one regional VT could be described by
expanding on Figure 2.6b with a 2TCM (a 3TCM would also be viable) as seen in Figure 2.7.
As stated earlier it is assumed that the drug and radioligand compete for the same target
binding site and furthermore that the administered drug does not in any way affect the ra-
dioligand uptake into tissue, i.e changes in cF , nor does the drug compete for non-specific
binding sites with the radioligand, i.e. there are no changes in cNS. Hence it is assumed that
cND = cF + cNS is constant across both baseline and block scans. Furthermore if no drug was

3.1
Covariance matrix of LEO and LEMO
If we go back to the covariance matrices ő£ in Section 2.3.3 and Chapter 3 they can be described
as the within-subject covariance matrix of the regional VT values across ROIs for one subject,
i.e the covariance between VT values for each ROI hence a k x k matrix.
If one subject
has undergone at least two PET scans under same conditions then the differences seen in
the regional VT values across ROIs can be attributable to measurement errors since biological
variability should have no influence as it is the same subject being scanned repeatedly. Thereby
ő£ can be interpreted as the covariance across regional VT values for one subject that has been
scanned repeatedly. The diagonal elements of ő£ then describes the variances for each ROI
while off-diagonal elements of ő£ describes the covariance between ROIs.
The real true covariance matrix ő£ is usually unknown (for both Section 2.3.3 and Chapter 3 it
was treated as known) but can be estimated from a TRT experiment (see Figure 2.6a) where
a subject has been scanned twice under same conditions with the same radioligand which also
has or is going to be used for a BB experiment (either Figure 2.6b or Figure 2.6c).
It has been shown in Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018 that ő£ can be estimated as

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
6.1
Likelihood Estimation of Multiple
Occupancies
Even though the Lassen plot is not strictly valid (see Section 2.3.2). The Lassen plot is a
common approach to estimate occupancy for radioligands where no suitable reference region
exist, due to its simplicity in implementation and interpretation. (Cunningham et al., 2010;
K√•gedal et al., 2013; Paul et al., 2014; Salinas et al., 2013)
LEO was developed as a method to avoid some of the problems (e.g correlated errors) with
the Lassen plot while LEMO was developed to expand the LEO method to multiple occupancy
estimation as a one-step-procedure. In order not to introduce new assumptions in the derivation
of LEMO. LEMO was based on the standard assumptions in compartmental modelling, that
when the radioligand has diffused across the BBB then each molecule of the radioligand is either
specifically (VS) bound to a target or non-displaceable (VND), at any given time point (Innis
et al., 2007). Further LEMO was based on the assumption in likelihood theory that the error
terms are normally distributed.
Another assumption in LEMO comes from the assumptions of PET target occupancy studies.
The assumption is that in eq. (3.2) (shown with error terms) that neither VND or the occupancy
is changing between the included ROIs for one scan and that VND is constant between scans.
If these assumptions are violated then it could lead to bias. To test if the assumption of VND
uniformity between ROIs is valid it could be done in the way of conducting a baseline-block
scan where a drug dosage is given that leads to ‚ąÜ= 100%. Though a drug dosage leading
to ‚ąÜ= 100% is often not possible in human studies, due to pharmacological effects of the
drug dosage. (Naganawa, Gallezot, et al., 2017) On the other hand testing for the assumption
of uniform ‚ąÜbetween ROIs is not obvious (Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018) and
furthermore the assumption of uniform ‚ąÜcan easily be violated if there is some endogenous
ligand in tissue which also competes for the same specific binding sites as drug and radioligand.
As described in Section 2.3.3 and Chapter 3 all measurements contain some form of error. The
errors in estimation of regional VT values (Slifstein and Laruelle, 2000) could come from either
the PET data or noise in the input function (cP ). Depending on the noise source these errors
==PAGE:72==
56
6 Discussion
can either be independent or dependent across ROIs for one particular subject with one PET
scan. Local image noise, e.g due to a low count in a region, would only influence that region and
hence this type of noise would be independent between ROIs. If on the other hand the noise is
due to e.g calibration errors (either in PET scanner or measurement equipment for the blood
samples) or ROI delineation errors in the coregistered image between PET and MRI. These
two examples of error sources could influence different ROIs similarly and hence errors from
these two examples would be dependent across ROIs. (Naganawa, Gallezot, et al., 2017; Mark
Slifstein and Marc Laruelle, 2001) Hence the assumption of noise being independent across
scans but not between ROIs in the derivation of LEMO. Furthermore to assess dependency
across ROIs the covariance was considered in LEMO.
In the derivation of LEMO it was assumed that the true covariance matrix ő£ describes the
measurement errors (some of the above described). Furthermore in the derivation of LEMO it
was assumed that the measurements errors in a target occupancy study can be estimated from
a test-retest dataset with eq. (4.2). In the derivation of eq. (4.2) (Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd
Ogden, 2018) biological variation between test and retest was assumed to be negligible but
under certain circumstances it can not be assumed neglible, e.g. a disease (Naganawa, Zheng,
et al., 2015) or diurnal variation (Matheson et al., 2015).
The covariance estimated with eq. (4.2) was considered the ‚Äôtrue‚Äô covariance matrix in this the-
sis and furthermore this ‚Äôtrue‚Äô covariance matrix was used to generate noise matrices (see Sec-
tion 4.2). If the regional VT estimates (e.g, in the TRT [11C]Cimbi-36 dataset) for any reason
was erroneously estimated then this could lead to erroneously generated noise matrices and
thereby possibly lead to erroneously generated instances (eq. (4.4)).
Furthermore the use of eq. (4.2) in real applications of LEMO would limit the number of ROIs
which could be included (i.e invertebility problems, see Section 3.1). The use of the non-linear
shrinkage method is not limited by the number of ROIs which can be included as the test-retest
sample size is not constrained in any way (see Section 3.1 and Ledoit and Wolf, 2015)). Lastly
obtaining a TRT dataset providing a perfect description of the measurement errors is unlikely
in practice.
6.2
Effect of increasing number of test-retest
subjects
The newly proposed LEO method has shown in Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018 that
the LEO method executed with the Shrink covariance matrix resulted in higher accuracy and
precision in estimating occupancy and VND, compared to the Lassen method. In the paper
by ibid. a simulation study was conducted and furthermore the LEO method was applied to
real data. The simulations were based on a dataset with regional VT estimates (Ogden et al.,
2007; Parsey et al., 2006) and data was collected by using the serotonin transporter radioligand
[11C]DASB. In this thesis the same dataset used in Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018
was acquired to both replicate the findings with LEO and to apply the newly extended version
of LEO.

6.2 Effect of increasing number of test-retest subjects
57
The results obtained in this thesis for LEO executed with Shrink is in accordance with what
was found in ibid. with the [11C]DASB dataset; First of all, that a higher number of test-retest
sample size leads to better estimates (in the sense that the value of summed squared differences
becomes smaller) of occupancy. (see Figures 5.1 and 5.3) Secondly, that a higher number of
test-retest sample size also leads to LEO executed with Shrink asymptotically approaching
LEO executed with Oracle. The same is also seen applicable for LEMO executed with MD-
Shrink aprroaching LEMO executed with MD-Oracle. Except for the 75% occupancy in the
[11C]Cimbi-36 dataset (Figure 5.3) where, in a few cases, it is seen that LEMO executed
with MD-Shrink surpasses LEMO executed with MD-Oracle. It should not be possible for
LEMO executed with MD-Shrink to surpass LEMO executed with MD-Oracle, as LEMO
executed with MD-Oracle is the ‚Äôgold‚Äô standard and hence should be the upper bound of
LEMOs performance. In real applications it is very unlikely to reach the upper bound. There
can be a number of reasons for the estimation with MD-Shrink surpassing the estimation with
MD-Oracle. First of all, it could be differences due the regional VT estimates in test-retest
scans used from the [11C]Cimbi-36 dataset. Secondly, it could be due to bias (see Section 6.3
for further discussion) and lastly it could be due to that the number of instances used (I=1000
instances) is not enough to describe the simulated [11C]Cimbi-36 data.
In Figures 5.1 and 5.3 is observed for 25% occupancies that LEMO executed with MD-Shrink
has lower values of summed squared distances compared to LEO but becomes less evident
for higher occupancies. This observed difference between LEMO and LEO could be due to
procedure differences in how they estimate occupancies between the two methods, while LEMO
uses information/data available from four scans, LEO only uses information/data from two
scans at a time. The procedure difference between LEMO and LEO also explains the low
values seen for LEMO estimates of VND in Figures 5.2 and 5.4 compared to LEO and the
Lassen plot. The difference seen between the different methods becomes less evident when
comparing the VND estimates from the 75% occupancy scan (3rd block scan).
From Slifstein and Laruelle, 2000 one would expect a lower summed squared difference value
with higher occupancy levels. In Figures 5.2 and 5.4 it is seen that for all methods the values are
getting lower with higher occupancy levels and as such is as expected. Though in Figures 5.1
and 5.3 the values for the summed squared differences are seen to be fluctuating and not
necessarily becoming smaller with higher occupancy levels.
This is especially seen for the
Lassen plot. The reasoning for this observation is due to a combination of the failure rates
(see Appendix B) being higher, the spread in the estimated occupancies and the constraints
set in Section 4.3.1.
In eq. (4.5), the normalization is based on how many successes there are out of the total
number of simulated instances. A ‚Äôfail‚Äô, in this thesis, is defined as a VND estimate that is
higher than the regional maximal VT value for that particular subject, which, from a theoretical
standpoint, is not correct. In theory, a ‚Äôfail‚Äô should be defined as a VND estimate higher than
the regional minimal VT value for that particular subject, but this would lead to a too strict
definition. Because in the case of defining a ‚Äôfail‚Äô based on the theoretical way, then imprecise
VND estimates around the correct value could also be counted as a fail. However, an imprecise
VND estimate around the correct value is not an outlier or ‚ÄĚfail‚ÄĚ but a consequence of the
estimation procedure. On the other hand, an estimate higher than the maximal regional VT
value is considered a ‚ÄĚbad‚ÄĚ estimate but also an outlier. For the LEMO method there is only

Effect of noise level
From the simulations in Section 5.1 is seen that a higher noise level leads to higher values of

It is also seen that LEMO executed with either MD-Oracle or MD-Shrink looks to have the
lowest spread for high occupancy cases in Figures 5.5 and 5.7. In the low occupancy cases
(‚ąÜ= 15%) it is seen that a higher noise level can lead to that the estimates are distributed
around 0 and not the true occupancy (see Figure 5.5). LEMO still though seems to have a
more narrow distribution around the true occupancy compared to LEO and Lassen.
When looking at Figures 5.5 and 5.7 for the occupancy at 15% a cap at 0 is seen. This is due
to the constraints set in Section 4.3.1. For LEMO in Figures 5.5 and 5.7 for high occupancies
(i.e., 75% and 85% occupancy) a cap is seen at 1 (i.e, 100% occupancy) and a few dots at 0
(i.e, 0% occupancy). The cap seen at 1 is also most likely due to the constraints set and the
dot could be due to local minima. As the LEMO uses information from all scans it could be
that noise from one scan effects the estimates leading to estimates that are capped at 1 or dots
at 0 due to constraints.
In ibid. an overestimation was reported for VND estimated at 25% occupancy with both the

and retest scans then these overestimated measurement errors could maybe be reflected in the
simulated instances. Further the bias observed for the Lassen plot and LEO has disappeared
for LEMO executed with either MD-Oracle or MD-Shrink, most likely due to LEMO having
information/data available from all simulated scans.
In Figure 5.8 an underestimation is seen for VND estimate for the simulated 15% occupancy
scan for LEO. This underestimation is most likely due to the radioligand [11C]Cimbi-36 leading
to underestimated measurement errors in the regional VT estimates for the test and retest scans
and hence reflected in the simulated instances. The Lassen plot is distributed around 0 which

6.4
Effect of varied occupancies
From the results in Section 5.2 it was seen that at low occupancies when increasing the noise
level could lead to higher spread and in a few cases could lead to estimates being distributed
around 0.
Further it was seen for high occupancy cases that a higher noise level leads to
relatively higher spread but the distribution is still around the true value. For LEMO these
observations were only seen for the occupancy estimates and not for the VND estimates which
seems to be unaffected by the increased noise level. As LEMO is a one-step procedure, and
thereby uses information available from all simulated scans, a visual investigation of how
different sets of defined true occupancies was investigated in Section 5.3.
It is seen in Figure 5.11 for the LLH case at 70% occupancy that there is a cap at 1 for
LEMO executed with MD-Shrink and for the LLM case when the true defined occupancy is
changed from 70% to 50% no cap is seen. With the cap gone then for 10% occupancy there
is seen a more dense (more yellow colour) distribution around the true value in the LLM case
compared to the LLH case. Hence when estimates from a high or medium occupancy scan is
estimated with low spread and bias it will lead to better estimates for low occupancy scans
when using LEMO. Further it is seen in the LMM case that LEMO executed with MD-Oracle
seems to be the only method which seems to have a distribution around the true value for the
5% occupancy scan.
The aforementioned observations are not observed in Figure 5.9 for the 10% occupancy in LLM
compared to LLH. Neither is it observed that the LEMO executed with MD-Oracle seems to
have a distribution around the true value for the 5% occupancy scan in the LMM case. This
observed difference between the [11C]Cimbi-36 dataset and [11C]DASB dataset can be due to
that the TRT [11C]DASB dataset does not describe the measurement errors well in low occu-
pancy scans. For LEMO to work satisfactory a good description of the measurements errors
are needed and likewise for LEO (Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018). Furthermore
from Tables 5.1 and 5.2 is seen a relatively high difference in the # of zeros in VND estimates
for the Lassen plot between the two datasets which is most likely due to the bias observed.
6.5
General observations
In Section 6.1 there is a discussion about the assumptions behind LEMO. In addition, the
results in Chapter 5 and the discussions from Sections 6.2 to 6.4 show that there are some
general observations. It can be seen that LEMO, regardless of occupancy and noise level, has
a superior VND estimate, and that LEMO has a smaller spread and bias at low occupancies.
Below are some of the reasons for LEMO‚Äôs superiority in VND estimation, as well as a discussion
on why there is the difference seen in the two datasets at low occupancies.
Due to procedure differences in LEMO compared to LEO and Lassen one VND estimate is
obtained from LEMO and N number of VND estimates are obtained from LEO and Lassen, even
==PAGE:76==
60
6 Discussion
though it is assumed in the Lassen and LEO method that VND estimates are constant across
scans (eqs. (2.13) and (2.15)). Strictly speaking then obtaining N number of VND estimates for
LEO and Lassen violates the assumption of VND being constant across scans as also described
in Radua et al., 2011 (described for the Lassen method). Furthermore this violation of VND
being constant across scans could lead to a loss of efficiency in the occupancy estimates or
unpredictable bias. In Cunningham et al., 2010 it was suggested for the Lassen method to
constrain the VND estimates to one common x-axis. This could be done by constraining the
x-axis to the best estimate of VND (i.e, the one with lowest spread and bias). The best VND
estimate for the Lassen method was obtained in the high occupancy cases Sections 6.2 to 6.4
The same could be applied to LEO by using the best VND estimate. Though in real applications
of Lassen and LEO it is not apparent how to assess if a VND estimate is the best one obtained.
In addition due to Lassen and LEO violating the assumption of constant VND across scans
and hence obtaining N number of VND estimates. It is not viable to compare the superior
VND estimates (due to the low spread and bias) obtained from the LEMO method with those
obtained from Lassen and LEO.
LEMO was seen to provide the best estimates for low occupancies compared to LEO and Lassen.
Low occupancy scans tend to have more noise compared to high occupancy scans as described
in Slifstein and Laruelle, 2000 and can be indirectly seen from Sections 5.2 and 5.3 due to the
distributions not being distributed around the true value. Hence it is thought that LEMO uses
information from the high occupancy scans to estimate the low occupancies in low occupancy
scans but simultaneously makes estimates of high occupancy more noisy (the dots and caps
seen). The noisy high occupancies would then be caused by the low occupancy scans also being
used to estimate the high occupancies from high occupancy scans. Though it could also be
that the best estimates for low occupancies obtained with LEMO is due to the superior LEMO
VND estimates. Furthermore the dots could also occur due to local minima. The problem of
local minima could possible be solved by taking the gradients into account and thereby use of
another optimization procedure (i.e., instead of using the ‚Äôfminsearch‚Äô in MATLAB). Another
way of possible solving the problems with local minima and simultaneously solving the problem
of noise from different scans effecting each other in LEMO is to use LEO combined with LEMO.
E.g by using LEMO to obtain a VND estimate and then constrain LEO to use this VND.
There are some general differences observed between the estimated values from the [11C]DASB
dataset and [11C]Cimbi-36 dataset. It seems that the occupancies estimated from the [11C]DASB
dataset has a relatively higher spread than the occupancies estimated from the [11C]Cimbi-36
dataset. Furthermore there is an overestimation in the VND estimates for low occupancy scans
with the [11C]DASB dataset compared to the underestimation seen with the [11C]Cimbi-36
dataset. As previously stated this difference could be due to how the regional VT estimates
were obtained for the respective TRT datasets. One difference is that for the TRT [11C]DASB
dataset the regional VT values were estimated with a 1TCM while for the TRT [11C]Cimbi-36
dataset the regional VT values were estimated with a 2TCM. Using the 1TCM to estimate
regional VT values for the TRT [11C]Cimbi-36 dataset did not describe the regional TACs well
and hence was not used. Furthermore as we did not have access to the TACs from the TRT
[11C]DASB dataset it was not possible to estimate the regional VT estimates with a 2TCM.
Therefore it was not possible to assess if choosing of the CM to estimate regional VT estimates
would make a difference in what was observed. Another difference for the two datasets was in
their targets of interest (see Section 4.1.1). It could be that the different targets of interest
==PAGE:77==

61
leads to unpredictable measurement errors in the regional VT estimates for the respective TRT
datasets. It could also be that there is a violation of negligible biological variability due to the
targets of interest being different. To assess this would require further studies.
Lastly as a final remark all the simulations from Section 4.3 and results obtained from these
simulations in Chapter 5 were based on having obtained a TRT dataset so as to estimate ňÜő£.
In practice, it is not always possible to obtain a TRT dataset. If no TRT dataset is available
then it would beg the question of how LEMO (or LEO) could estimate occupancies and VND.
It has been shown in Schain, Zanderigo, and Todd Ogden, 2018 that an identity matrix as the
covariance matrix could be used (i.e, the diagonal elements consists of ones). Though it was
seen that using an identity matrix as the covariance matrix for the LEO method is similar to
using the Lassen plot. The same would be expected for LEMO using an identity matrix as
the covariance matrix and it could possibly lead to worse estimates than the Lassen plot due
to noise from all scans being present in the one-step-procedure. Another method could be to
estimate the noise characteristics of the PET data as suggested in Naganawa, Gallezot, et al.,
2017. To assess the scenario where no TRT dataset is present would require further studies.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The extension of the LEO method to accommodate multiple block scans for a subject when
varying doses are administered was carried out by analytically deriving a high-dimensional log-
likelihood function. The extension resulted in the LEMO method. The LEMO method can
accommodate an arbitrary number of block scans for a subject and was tested on simulated
data.
The simulation of the effect of increasing the number of TRT subjects included in a TRT
study showed that LEMO is resulting in lower normalized summed squared differences when
increasing the number of TRT subjects included in a TRT study. When increasing the noise
level the normalized summed squared differences obtained with LEMO also increased.
The simulation of increasing the noise level was visually investigated with violin plots. It was
shown for the estimated occupancies with LEMO that an increased noise level leads to higher
spread and bias, but this higher spread and bias was the lowest compared with the LEO and
Lassen method. The higher spread and bias was most evident for the estimated low occupancies
for the low occupancy scans. The VND estimates obtained by increasing the noise level showed
superior VND estimates with the LEMO method regardless of increased noise level.
The simulation of defining varying sets of true occupancies was visually investigated with violin
plots. It was shown that depending on which set of true defined occupancies was used that
LEMO could obtain low occupancy estimates even in the noisy low occupancy scans. Further
it was shown that regardless of the defined true set of occupancies that the LEMO method
could obtain superior VND estimates.

Future perspective
This project leads to interesting observations and to LEMO as an important tool that could
be used in target occupancy studies and as such a conference paper has been written up
(see Appendix C). Furthermore these interesting observations needs to be explored before a
journal article can be written.
It was seen that LEMO could obtain accurate estimates of low occupancies and these low

Furthermore it could be of interest to extend LEMO by including gradients to test if this
would have an influence on the estimates. Another interesting extension of LEMO could be a

Objective 
Neuroimaging with PET can be used to measure in vivo the occupancy (őĒ) by a drug. For radioligands 
for which no brain reference region exists, the only established method for estimation of őĒ is the 
Lassen plot1, which is based on linear regression. Simple linear regression is, however, not ideal for 
analyzing this type of data for several reasons, including regression attenuation and correlated 
errors2.  
We have recently proposed to use Likelihood Estimation of Occupancy (LEO)2 instead. LEO estimates 
the values of VND and őĒ that maximize the likelihood of VT values observed in multiple ROIs. In the 
scenario in which each occupancy experiment consists of one baseline and one block measurement 
(i.e., taken after administration of a competitor), it was shown that LEO provides improved 
estimation relative to the Lassen plot.  
The objective of the current study was to generalize LEO into Likelihood Estimation of Multiple 
Occupancies (LEMO), to accommodate an arbitrary number of blocking scans, where a different 
value of őĒ is to be estimated based on each block measurement, while VND is assumed to be the 
same across all scans.

Conclusion

increased accuracy and precision for both [11C]Cimbi-36 and [11C]DASB datasets. In particular, the 
estimates of VND and occupancy are superior using LEMO for the lowest simulated occupancy case.
