III 
 
Abstract 
This study investigates the use of electrostatic precipitators (ESPs) as sustainable alternatives 
to traditional air ϐilters (F7 and F9) in HVAC systems focusing on indoor air quality 
enhancement, energy efϐiciency, and environmental impact.  
Given the rising awareness about indoor air quality in dwellings and commercial buildings, ESPs 
present potential to capture ϐine particulates while reducing energy consumption associated 
with mechanical ϐiltration. This research combines computational ϐluid dynamics (CFD) 
simulations with full-scale laboratory testing to investigate ESP ϐiltration efϐiciency, pressure 
loss, and energy requirements.  
The study shows that ESPs achieve similar particle capture rates to high-efϐiciency ϐilters at 99% 
efϐiciency for variable particulate sizes with considerably lower pressure loss and signiϐicant 
energy savings depending on the use case. Moreover, environmental assessment has also 
considered ESPs to have a lower carbon footprint throughout their life cycle due to their 
washable and reusable nature and reduced energy demand.  
Results indicate the potential application of ESP as a ϐiltration method for modern HVAC 
systems concerning sustainability goals without compromising indoor air quality. This study 
provides useful insights into the adoption of ESP technology in the HVAC industry advancing 
both the energy-efϐicient building practices and healthier indoor environments.

1 Introduction 
 
1 
Introduction 
1.1 
Context 
In the past decades, indoor air quality (IAQ) has been one of the major concerns on account of 
its impact on the health and productivity of people. It is now more common that IAQ is 
considered as an integral part of building design and operation in contemporary constructions. 
This has even been stressed since newer regulations require more airtight building envelopes, 
which impacts the air inϐiltration and thereby directly the IAQ. Studies show that an average 
human being spends about 87% of their time inside, thus the quality of inhaled air greatly 
affects their well-being, comfort, and everyday wellness [1]. 
Regarding occupants of buildings, whether they are residential, commercial, or institutional, it 
is inevitable that they will breathe a variety of airborne chemicals. These can be particles, 
gaseous organic compounds, and living organisms. As a result, the provision of acceptable IAQ 
has been given the utmost importance by architects, engineers, as well as building management 
personnel. The challenge therefore lies in effective ϐiltration of these contaminants, especially 
focusing on ϐine particles, which pose a health risk [2]. 
 
Electrostatic precipitators (ESPs) have been a signiϐicant technology for industrial air pollution 
control since their invention. The ESP invention dates to the early 20th century, as developed 
by Dr. Frederick Gardner Cottrell in 1907. Dr. Cottrell's principle was based on the use of the 
potential difference between the ground and high-voltage wires to electrostatically separate 
particulates from industrial exhaust streams and remove such particulates from the gases, 
which serves as the basic principle of ESP design up to the current time [3].  
After Cottrell's work, ESPs underwent signiϐicant improvements, in the mid-20th century. The 
1930s and 1940s marked a critical period for the signiϐicant improvement in the concepts 
applied and efϐiciency of the equipment, thus making it more effective and reliable. The high 
rate of industrialization during the period resulted in increased pollution and, thus, demanded 
more stringent measures in controlling the spread of pollution. 
In the second half of the 20th century, this device became widely applied in different industries, 
such as the power generation sector, cement factories, and steel reϐineries. It was especially

This research investigates the mechanisms of adequate air ϐiltration, speciϐically through the

buildings. 
This is achieved through analysis and laboratory experiment of airϐlow, ϐiltration efϐiciency, and 
pressure loss. This study seeks to contribute nuanced insights to the ongoing discourse in 
architectural engineering. 
 
The goal is to offer a practical way forward for a seamless transfer of ESPs as components in

2 Background 
 
2 
Background 
2.1 
Particles 
Each human being breathes approximately 22,000 times a day, for the duration of which a 
predicted 40,000 to 70,000 dust particles are ingested with every inhalation. Particle size is 
generally measured in microns (μm), with variations relying on their source. A micrometer 
(μm) is a length measurement equal to one-millionth of a meter. Comparatively, the width of a 
human hair strand serves as a useful reference for distinguishing between larger and smaller 
particles. This comparison can be seen on Table 2.1. 
 
Table 2.1 Particle size comparison 
 Size [μm] 
Human hair strand 50-150 
Pollen 10-110 
Cigarette smoke 0.01-1 
Bacteria and virus 0.001-10 
 
The size of atmospheric particulates ranges from 0.001 μm to 100 μm, with many settlings 
caused by gravitational forces. Particles larger than 10 μm can be eliminated from the body by 
blowing the nose and coughing. The body can, to a substantial extent, eliminate particles larger 
than 5 μm quickly. Exposure to particles smaller than that will be a health risk for the human 
body [7].  
Particulate matter (PM) smaller than 2.5 μm, known as PM2.5, can settle deep inside the lungs, 
wearing extra irritations such as bacteria and endotoxins. PM2.5 particles are categorized as 
"ϐine" particles and are related to increased death count and higher incidences of respiration 
and cardiovascular illnesses in comparison to larger particles [8].

2.1.1 
Sources of particles 
Particles can be categorized as natural and human caused sources. Each one contributes to the 
creation and behaviors of such airborne particles. Natural sources may involve volcanic 
eruptions, wildϐires, wind-borne dust, and sea sprays. Biogenic emissions refer to the natural 
sources of particulate matter, which include pollen and fungal spores. By contrast, human 
caused sources are a key focus in health effects, given that they affect air quality in cities much 
more profoundly and directly than biogenic emissions do. 
 
Much of the human caused particulate matter comes from activities associated with combustion 
processes, industrial operations, agriculture practices, and construction activities. Activities can 
be categorized as outdoor and indoor pollution. Outdoor combustion processes result in the 
formation of ϐine particles (PM2.5) and ultraϐine particles (UFPs) from vehicle emissions, power 
generation, and residential heating. Indoor sources for UFPs include activities such as cooking 
and burning candles [10].  
Industrial processes for the preparation of cement, steel, and chemicals produce particulate 
matter often loaded with toxic materials and heavy metals. Similarly, in the case of agricultural 
activities, particulate matter is generated in the form of soil preparation and dust evolving from 
plowing, harvesting, and spreading of fertilizers and pesticides.

Construction and demolition activities are very dust intensive, and therefore contribute 
towards the atmospheric particulate burden, particularly in urban environments. Construction 
dust may contain hazardous substances such as silica, hence endangering the health of workers 
and people living in its area.  
There is an important role for outdoor-to-indoor air quality dynamics in this context. 
Particulate matter released from outdoor sources readily inϐiltrates an indoor setting, 
particularly a building with rather poor ventilation or with inadequate ϐiltration systems. 
Modern building practices emphasize airtight construction as this reduces energy consumption 
yet limits the exchange of indoor and outdoor air which leads to accumulation of indoor 
particulate matter, caused by indoor sources. 
Secondary particles are chemically formed with precursor gases such as sulfur dioxide (SO₂), 
nitrogen oxide (NOx), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are major contributors to the 
formation of particulate matter levels in this case which are harmful to human health. Thus, 
meteorological parameters related to temperature, humidity, and sunlight contribute hugely to 
the development of secondary particles. These are formed in an indoor environment by the 
chemical transformation of air pollutants with the outside air that penetrates indoors. [11]  
2.1.2 
Behavior 
The behavior of particulate matter in the atmosphere represents a combination of many 
interacting physical and chemical variables and environmental factors. These factors determine 
particle transport, deposition, and ultimate removal from the atmosphere together with their 
effects on humans. 
The transportation of particulate matter is inϐluenced by size, shape, and mass of particles. The 
larger ones, especially the PM10 fraction, settle under gravity and normally deposit near the 
source. Both ϐine particles and UFPs may thus remain suspended for long periods when 
supported by wind and atmospheric transportation, even reaching human respiratory exposure 
sites far away from the emission source, this is also called airborne transmissions. Such 
pollutants can be transported over vast distances, sending particulate matter some hundreds, 
and in a few cases thousands, of kilometers from a source. Inϐiltration of indoor environments 
is therefore more common for urban areas in which outside pollution levels are high. [12]

2 Background 
 
2.2 
Air ﬁlters 
Air ϐilters are designed to remove impurities from the air, thereby improving IAQ. Filters remove 
particulate matter such as dust, pollen, mold, and bacteria that would otherwise contaminate 
indoor air. This forms one of the very common and essential components of HVAC systems used 
to assure cleaner provisions of air both in residential and non-residential setups. The air ϐilter 
effectiveness is determined by the ability to capture particles in varying sizes. IAQ has 
undergone enhancements followed by the evolution of air ϐilters. This has led to better overall 
comfort in buildings.  
Filters adhere to strict classiϐications set forth by European and global requirements, making 
sure that they meet unique performance standards. These standards function as benchmarks 
for assessing the effectiveness and reliability of ϐiltration systems in numerous environments. 
Despite the advancements in mechanical air ϐiltration, certain boundaries persist, prompting 
the exploration of alternative opportunities. These limitations might also include restrictions 
related to ϐilter efϐiciency, maintenance requirements, and adaptability to diverse 
environmental conditions and current ventilation systems. However, it is within these 
demanding situations that new opportunities emerge, using innovation and prompting the 
development of innovative ϐiltration technologies. 
Therefore, the quest for effective air ϐiltration extends beyond traditional mechanical methods, 
including a variety of innovative solutions. From advanced ϐiltration media to complicated 
ϐiltration techniques, the pursuit of superior air quality maintains to evolve, propelled via a 
dedication to sustainability, performance, and improved IAQ [13]. 
2.2.1 
Modern air ﬁltration techniques 
Air ϐiltration techniques include a numerous range of methods aimed toward removing 
airborne contaminants. These strategies range from traditional ϐiltration strategies to 
innovative approaches that leverage advanced technology.  
The following air ϐiltration techniques represent a few of the diverse range of procedures aimed 
toward developing healthier indoor environments. By means of understanding the ideas and 
applications of every technique, stakeholders can pick the most appropriate ϐiltration method 
based totally at the speciϐic requirements in their indoor climate.

Filters are typically graded in a variety of speciϐications. These grades are based on the 
applicable standard that the ϐilter is graded after. The class of the ϐilter tells how good a ϐilter is 
at capturing particles, thereby its efϐiciency. 
Bag ﬁltration 
Filtration by bag ϐilters is a conventional method of retaining particles in ventilation systems. 
Bag ϐilters are usually made from synthetic materials of polyester or polypropylene to catch 
impurities in the air while passing through it. These types are helpful due to their cheap price, 
large face, and designed collection, as they give a good result of collection with less clogging. 
They are helpful in many wide-ranging applications for air quality in commercial and residential 
use. 
Bag ϐilters are often used as pre ϐilters for coarser particles. This allows for a lower grade, yet 
still a persistent class. Bag ϐilters are often classiϐied in grades from F5 to F9, with a efϐiciency 
range of 40-95%. [14] 
Compact ﬁltration 
Compact ϐilters are used in ventilation systems where space is limited but larger ϐilter surface 
is required. The ϐilter media design features a mini-pleated surface allowing for optimal area 
usage. Compact ϐilters are particularly effective for ϐine particles and dust and can be applicable 
for both comfort and process ventilation. Low energy consumption and long lifespan makes the 
ϐilters versatile for different environments with high airϐlow or turbulence. 
Compact ϐilters are also graded on the same terms as bag ϐilters, though starting from F7 to F9, 
with an efϐiciency at 80-95%. [14] [15] 
High-eﬃciency particulate air ﬁltration 
High-efϐiciency particulate Air (HEPA) is an improved mechanical ϐiltration system known for 
its exceptional performance in particle removal. HEPA ϐilters are generally required to 
demonstrate high efϐiciency in the ϐiltration of PM0.3 or smaller.  
Hence, they are highly effective in the ϐiltration of a wide range of dirt, pollen, and 
microorganisms. HEPA ϐiltration is widely applied in systems where high air ϐiltering efϐiciency 
is essential and needed, such as clean rooms. Efϐiciencies for HEPA ϐilters are therefore higher 
and classiϐied as H13 and H14 with a rate of >95% and >99.997% respectively. [14]

Carbon ﬁltration 
Activated carbon is a substance used in carbon ϐiltration methods to adsorb pollutants present

2.3 
Electrostatic precipitators 
ESPs utilizes the force of static electricity to efϐiciently cleanse the air of particulate matter. This 
modernized ϐiltration method exploits the natural tendency of charged particles to draw or 
repel every unique particle, resulting in the removal of airborne contaminants. 
The process starts as air loaded with particles enters the ϐilter. Inside the ϐilters structure, 
specialized components generate electrical charges, starting reactions designed to purify the 
air. The electrical system contains a unit for changing alternating voltage to direct voltage. This 
conversion is essential for producing the electrical ϐield to ionize the particles as they circulate 
through the ESP. Direct voltage is supplied to the discharge electrodes, starting a negative 
electric ϐield round them. This negative ϐield induces a negative charge which creates a pulling 
effect on the particles, drawing them towards the positively charged collector plates [21] [22]. 
This process can be seen on Figure 2.3, where the particle loaded air inϐiltrates the ϐilter from 
left to right. The result of the ionizer and the collector is thereby clean air. 
 
Figure 2.3 Operational principle of ESP [23] 
 
Besides this, ESPs are also multi-phased or layered, which increases their ϐiltration 
performance. The above process makes such ESPs have a process that ensures total puriϐication.

When the voltage difference between the wire and plate electrodes gets bigger, the air close to 
the cord undergoes an electrical breakdown, inϐlicting air molecules to be excited. During this 
excitation, electrons in the molecules shift to higher energy levels. When the molecules go back 
to their initial state, energy is released, some of which manifests as light, creating a visible 
corona discharge, typically seen as a bluish glow near the wire.  
The area between the wire and plate is divided into two regions: an active and passive zone. In 
the active area, in which the corona discharge takes place, electrons leave the wire electrode 
and collide with air molecules, ionizing them. This device, called electron avalanche, continues 
till the electric ϐield weakens to the factor that the electrons do not have enough energy to cause 
ionization.  
A corona may be both positive and negative, depending on the charge of the discharge electrode. 
In a negative corona, positive ions circulate in the direction of the wire electrode, at the same 
time as electrons travel towards the plate. As electrons move through the passive zone, they 
ionize electronegative gases, forming slower moving negative ions that attach to aerosol 
particles. In a positive corona, the positive charge carriers maintain their form across to the 
plate. Negative coronas generate much more ozone compared to positive coronas, that have 
fewer free electrons. For this motive, positive coronas are desired in air-cleaning systems to 
minimize ozone production. [23] 
 
Besides the efϐiciency of particle collection, advantages usually increase because of ESPs being 
more efϐicient than the standard approach to ϐiltration. Usability is often guaranteed and 
considering that the ϐilters are designed in a way that they can be cleaned, therefore related 
needs toward its replacement and eventually waste reduction. Moreover, the strong design 
concept and long-lasting product make them sustainable as well as an economical solution for 
ventilation systems.  
In this way, ESPs provide a relatively new and renewable method in this regard to ensure indoor 
air comfort and health. Given their high collection efϐiciency for a wide range of airborne 
contaminants, they are an excellent option for application in most residences, ofϐices, and 
hospitals. In a healthcare setting, ESPs can play an important role in the control of sterile 
environments by removing bacteria, viruses, infectious particles, and other contaminants from 
the air. For instance, two Danish hospitals, Rigshospitalet and Herlev Hospital, installed ESPs to 
help protect patients and staff as well as to improve the IAQ.

2.3.1 
Filtration eﬃciency 
Filtration efϐiciency of an ESP depends on various parameters such as electrode design, applied 
voltage, size of the particle etc. Generally, ESPs provide as high as 99% ϐiltration efϐiciency for 
varied sizes of particulates due to strong electrostatic attraction forces that attract the particles 
and collect them. For example, a developed ESP conϐiguration with foam-covered collecting 
electrodes reduced the particle re-entrainment and achieved 99% ϐiltration efϐiciency [24]. A 
recent study reported a hybrid ϐiltration system of ESP and ϐibrous ϐilter (HESP), where it was 
observed that the particles which bypassed the ESP carried a charge and resulted in improved 
ϐiltration efϐiciency of the downstream ϐilter [25].  
ESPs also tend to improve with higher operating voltages and with electrode designs that are 
optimized for this application. For instance, an experimental study of a positively polarized ESP 
with carbon ϐiber ionizers reported collection efϐiciencies between 50% and 95% depending on 
the voltage and ϐlow rate applied [26].  
2.3.2 
Pressure loss 
One of the major beneϐits of ESP is their low pressure loss which promotes airϐlow and energy 
savings. Unlike traditional bag ϐilters, the metallized ϐibers are not packed together very tightly. 
This allows air to pass through the ϐilter with little driving pressure, making ESPs efϐicient and 
energy saving [27]. 
Furthermore, ESPs can maintain their efϐiciency at very high rates for extended periods of time 
with practically no pressure loss. A very recent study on a high-ϐlow rate ESP for the collection 
of PM2.5 reported efϐiciencies above 80% while keeping ozone emissions remarkably low [28]. 
2.3.3 
Ozone generation 
Based on the described operational principles of the ESP and the tendencies to generate corona, 
the possibility of ozone generation occurs. This is a matter that is subject to regulations based 
on health and IAQ. Ozone (O3), is a strong oxidant that can affect the human respiratory system, 
particularly in vulnerable groups like children, elderly, and people with asthma or other lung 
conditions [29]. High ozone concentrations can cause symptoms like throat irritations, cough, 
chest pain, and breathing difϐiculty. Long-term exposures have shown to be linked with reduced 
lung capacity and increased risk of respiratory illness [30].

2.3.4 
Long term performance 
ESP’s long term performance depends on various parameters like dust loading, electrode 
degradation and maintenance. The performance of ESPs tends to decrease particulate loading 
on the collecting plates and electrodes. This particulate loading creates a shielding effect on the 
electrostatic charge and decreases the efϐiciency of the ϐiltration process. Hence maintenance 
and frequent cleaning are highly recommended to ensure continuous reliable performance. 
Periodic washing or mechanical cleaning of the electrodes and plates restores the efϐiciency of 
the ESP and prevents dust and other particulate build up. 
The performance of ESPs can be maintained constantly or even slightly increased after long 
periods of operation if good maintenance practices are applied. As an example, an experimental 
study conducted on an electrostatically assisted air ϐilter (EAA) demonstrated that the overall 
ϐilter efϐiciency did not decrease during a 458 hour test and slightly increased at the end [32]. 
This seems to indicate that, when well designed and maintained, ESPs can be a consistent and 
reliable technology for extended periods of operation. 
Moreover, ozone-free ESPs have been developed using polymer-arrayed collectors. Results 
showed that the ϐilters achieved extremely high collection efϐiciency up to 89.5%, without 
producing any harmful ozone and were therefore considered suitable for continuous operation 
in indoor environments [33].

ESP environmental and economic implications 
The ϐinancial advantages of ESPs are considerable. Although initial investment for the 
installation of ESPs is high capital expenditure, the ongoing savings made from its efϐicient

and have longer service lives, which means decreased downtime and increased overall

goals of environmental protection and the reduction of greenhouse gases as well as the 
economic prosperity of industries and the people they employ can go hand in hand. The 
experience made with ESPs in numerous industries proves that exhaust gas puriϐication from 
dust and gaseous pollutants as well as the recovery of valuable products can be a win-win 
business for society, industry and nature [34].

High ﬁltration eﬃciency 
The main beneϐit of ESP is the ease of achieving a high degree of ϐiltration efϐiciency. The air

Reusable and washable 
ESPs are often reusable and washable, which makes them an environmentally friendly option

The design of efϐicient ESPs relies on the detailed analysis of the ϐlow patterns and the

performance of ESPs and thereby give detailed suggestions on design improvements.

efϐiciency of the ESP. As an example, in one study, CFD simulations of the ESP revealed non-

2.4.4 
Aerosols 
An aerosol is a combination of solid particles or liquid droplets in a gas or ϐluid. This can both 
be natural, like fog and dust, or it can be anthropogenic, like air pollutants and smoke. Aerosols 
come in various sizes from nanometers to micrometers. 
To perform a test, in accordance with ISO 16890, two types of aerosols are used. Aerosols are 
used to simulate and generate particles in a controlled environment for desired size and 
exposure. These two aerosols are categorized as liquid and solid phase aerosols. Regarding the 
liquid phase, DEHS (Di-Ethyl-Hexyl-Sebacat) is used and is a synthetic oil. This oil can generate 
aerosol particles with the desired size range. Furthermore, as solid phase KCI (Potassium 
Chloride) is used for simulation of dust particles found in real environments. Both types of 
phase aerosols are used to provide evaluation of a ϐilter’s performance though used accordingly. 
Filtration performance can be measured in two phases. Phase one is measured performance 
from 0.3 µm to 1.0 µm and shall use the liquid phase aerosol. The second measuring phase is in 
the range of 1.0 µm to 10 µm and shall use the solid phase aerosol. [40]

Methodology 
The main objective of the research is to evaluate the performance of ESP in terms of several 
factors including pressure loss and particle capture efϐiciency compared to conventional bag 
ϐilters (F7) and pleated ϐilters (F9). This research combines experimental work and CFD 
simulations to provide a comprehensive analysis. The ϐilter efϐiciency rating is based on 
determining the effectiveness of the ESP in removing particles of different sizes according to 
ISO 16890, under controlled conditions. CFD simulations were developed to complement the 
full-scale testing and provided a simulation based airϐlow for comparison. Simulations have also 
been used to visualize and analyze airϐlow behavior in the ϐilter.  
This brings forward some key elements to be tested. 
Velocity proﬁle 
• Measure velocities to establish correct airϐlow and air uniformity. 
Filter eﬃciency assessment 
• Determine the effectiveness of ESP in removing PM of different sizes. 
• Measure the ϐiltration efϐiciency of ESP in collecting particles in comparison to bag ϐilters 
and pleated ϐilters. 
Pressure loss analysis 
• Determine the pressure loss throughout ESP at different airϐlow rates to assess their 
resistance to airϐlow. 
• Compare the pressure loss characteristics of ESPs with the traditional bag ϐilters and 
pleated ϐilters to compare their effect on performance and energy consumption. 
• Analyze the relationship between airϐlow rate and pressure loss to optimize ϐilter 
selection and operational parameters for IAQ improvement. 
 
By addressing these points, this study aims to provide valuable insights and comparisons into 
the performance and suitability of ESPs for indoor air ϐiltration uses.

4.3 
Performance analysis 
The ESP showed better ϐiltration efϐiciency in comparison to conventional mechanical ϐilters F7 
bag ϐilter and F9 pleated ϐilter, whereby capture efϐiciency was emphasized with regards to the 
capture of small particles with low pressure loss.  
 
Filtration efϐiciencies for the ESP were high, with the consistency of performance in removing 
ϐine PM. For particles in the PM0.3-PM2.5 range, ϐiltration efϐiciencies for the ESP were above 99%, 
making this device highly effective to capture both ϐine and ultraϐine particles. The F7 and F9 
ϐilters performed relatively well but did not reach the same high level of efϐiciency, especially 
for ϐiner particles. It showed less efϐiciency for ϐine particles but still delivered solid 
performance on larger-sized particles. Since the F9 pleated ϐilter is of denser ϐilter media, it's 
expected to have a higher ϐiltration efϐiciency over most of the particle size range when 
compared to the F7 ϐilter. 
 
The other most important aspect of ϐiltration performance is related to the pressure loss

5.1 
Performance evaluation of ﬁltration methods 
The results from experimental testing underlined how the advantages and trade-offs of ESP, bag 
ϐilters, and pleated ϐilters are related to ϐiltration efϐiciency and pressure loss.  
Among the key aspects was that the ESP ϐiltration efϐiciency remained high throughout various 
particle sizes, the efϐiciencies surpassing 99% for both PM1 and PM2.5 concentrations, which are 
much higher than the results from the bag and pleated ϐilters. The F7 bag ϐilter showed good 
performance in a moderate way for larger particles but dropped to less efϐicient performance 
for ϐiner particles. This is not surprising since this was some of the expected outcomes, however, 
both tested F7 ϐilters failed in the small sub-micron particle range. 
The unexpected increase in particle counts downstream in the 0.3 to 1 µm size range of F7 bag 
and panel ϐilters may be accounted for by the discussed shedding behavior in ISO 16890-1:2016 
Annex A. ISO 16890 recognizes that some ϐilters, especially lower-efϐiciency ones such as F7 
may shed particles when they are put into practical use. This generally occurs when particles, 
previously captured by the ϐilter, are released into the airstream due to mechanical or 
aerodynamic stresses within the ϐilter itself. 
Another mechanism for releasing previously captured particles is if the incoming particle 
collides and dislodges it, returning it to the airstream. Also, since the particles occupy space 
within the ϐilter, as more particles are captured, the airϐlow velocity in the channels through the 
medium increases. Increased resistance to airϐlow can also compress the ϐilter medium, further 
raising the velocity in these channels. The increased ϐluid drag on deposited particles can result 
in some of the particles becoming re-entrained into the air ϐlow. 
These statements can also be derived since the testing procedure according to ISO requires two 
types of aerosols for the ranges 0.3 to 1 µm and from 1 to 10 µm. The measurements done used 
the aerosol NaCl rather than KCl. KCl is the prescribed aerosol to be tested in the 1-10 µm range, 
therefore results below 1 µm may not be accurate. Furthermore, replacing KCl with NaCl has 
been a deviation, yet a chloride substitution suitable for generating particles for ϐilter testing. 
The substitution in injection aerosol also showed a limited range of particle sizes which did not 
fulϐill the criteria of 1-10 µm, but only 1-4 µm.

If a larger AHU is required to accommodate the more powerful fan, it may not ϐit within the 
designated space, forcing engineers to either reconϐigure the technical room layout or consider 
structural modiϐications to create additional room. These changes can lead to increased 
installation costs and project complexity, including additional ductwork, support structures, 
and even delays due to design revisions. The worst case, space limitations could decrease the 
ability to install a properly sized AHU altogether, compromising system performance. On the 
other hand, the lower pressure loss of ESP ϐilters means that smaller fans and more compact 
AHUs can be used, making them a more suitable option for projects where space constraints are 
a key concern. This spatial efϐiciency also translates into lower installation costs and fewer 
complications during system design and implementation.

Conclusion 
This study represents an analysis of ESP performance compared to conventional F7 bag ϐilters 
and F9 pleated ϐilters within the scope of HVAC ventilation systems. The ϐindings show that ESP 
technology has higher initial cost, bringing long-term beneϐits connected with energy efϐiciency, 
cost savings, and high ϐiltration performance. 
 
This research underlines that the ESPs achieved better performance in ϐiltration, especially in 
the removal of ϐine particles, which play a very important role in maintaining IAQ. The 
mechanical ϐilters, though effective within larger particle ranges, show a remarkable efϐiciency 
drop with smaller particulates. The ESP, on the other hand, proving the specialized advantages 
in environments where air quality and ϐine particle removal are crucial, like hospitals, ofϐices. 
Though cannot be used for clean rooms, with its classiϐication of E10-E11, do not match the 
requirement of ϐilter classes as H14 according to EN 1822.  
 
The ESPs go further in terms of pressure loss, opposed to the F7 and F9 ϐilters; this directly 
correlates to lower energy consumption depending on the airϐlow rate. The study showed that 
higher airϐlow rates resulted in bigger economical savings. In the long term, this reduced energy 
need not only decreases operating costs but also elevates sustainability towards lower carbon 
footprints in energy-consuming establishments. 
 
The cost-beneϐit analysis showed that though ESPs have high initial investments, their low 
maintenance and energy costs return the investment much faster, and payback periods are 
shorter than those from mechanical ϐilters. The ESP showed the capability of a payback period 
withing 2 years in comparison to both tested ϐilters. In fact, building operators may consider 
ESP technology as a feasible alternative when the goal is to balance the initial capital cost with 
the long-term operational savings. 
 
However, some issues and limitations were found: the exclusion of preϐilters in CFD simulations 
and scaled-down experimental conditions imply a need for more robust real testing and 
simulation reϐinements. Further research will be necessary to develop greater accuracy in CFD
