ABSTRACT 
Inflammation is an essential biological response required for tissue homeostasis after injury or infection. 
Chronic inflammation however is destructive, leads to tissue damage, and is a hallmark of many diseases 
such atherosclerosis and, ultimately, the first cause of death worldwide, cardiovascular diseases. For this 
reason, new potential therapeutics able to dampen inflammation and enhance its resolution in a 
controlled manner with minimal host collateral damage are of great interest. 
The current project presents the development and in vitro efficacy studies of controlled-release polymeric 
nanoparticles incorporating interleukin-4 (IL-4) for the treatment of atherosclerosis. IL-4 reported 
capacity of triggering anti-inflammatory responses on macrophages is herein exploited to tailor the 
atherosclerotic plaque microenvironment in order to help in tempering inflammation. Nanoparticles were 
nanoengineered via self-assembly of biodegradable polymers by bulk nanoprecipitation and designed 
to achieve long circulation half-life to facilitate accumulation, small size to enhance permeation and 
retention within the lesional site, and preservation of IL-4 activity after release. A systematic combinatorial 
strategy including bioassays such as ELISA, RT-qPCR, Western Blot and FACS, was used to screen the 
biophysical properties of the generated nanocarriers and their anti-inflammatory capability.  
Results showed a clear role of IL-4 as a driver of inflammation resolution, and a promising efficacy profile 
after release from the produced nanoparticles. The outcome of these findings supports the concept that 
defective inflammation resolution plays a role in these chronic conditions and means a step forward on 
the way towards the implementation of this innovative therapeutic approaches for the treatment of 
inflammatory diseases.

INTRODUCTION 
In the 19th century, Rudolf Virchow already described atherosclerosis as entzündun (inflammation) [1]. 
However, the relevance of this words wasn´t really appreciated and grasped by the medical community 
until the 80s [1]. Lately, inflammation and its failure to resolve have been firmly established as central to 
the progression of several chronic inflammatory pathologies, such as atherosclerosis, arthritis and chronic 
neurodegenerative diseases [2]. Atherosclerosis constitutes the foremost underlying pathological feature 
associated with cardiovascular diseases [2][3], which occupy the first position in the list of the top 10 
causes of death worldwide reported by the World Health Organization (WHO) [4]. For this reason, new 
potential therapeutic targets in addressing atherosclerosis and its fundamental mechanisms are essential.  
Recent discoveries describe an unbalanced macrophage polarization situation favored by the presence 
of inflammatory cells to be a key factor in the defective inflammation resolution process observed in 
atherosclerosis [5]. Therefore, therapeutics able to dampen inflammation, enhance resolution, and lead 
the situation back to tissue homeostasis are in the spotlight of the biomedical field. Natural endogenous 
mediators of inflammation resolution have emerged as good candidates for the intended application. 
Their peptidic nature, though, hampers their intravenous injection by rapid circulation clearance and 
untargeted delivery, leading to unwanted side effects [6].  
The advent of nanotechnology and its exponentially increasing applications within the medical field have 
set new opportunities to overcome these setbacks. The encapsulation of these protein mediators into a 
nanoparticle has shed light in the delivery of such therapeutics, by making it safer and more effective [7]. 
Built up on biodegradable and biocompatible materials, polymeric nanoparticles allow for a targeted, 
protected, stable and controlled-release delivery of biologics [8]. 
The current project presents the development and in vitro characterization of anti-inflammatory 
controlled-release polymeric nanomedicines incorporating interleukin-4 (IL-4) for the treatment of 
atherosclerosis using the body´s innate immune system pathways. By tailoring the local plaque 
environment with a pro-resolving mediator such as IL-4, the purpose is to trigger a macrophage 
phenotype shift towards an anti-inflammatory signature and thus help the natural resolution process to 
happen. An extensive platform of bioassays, such as ELISA, RT-qPCR, Western Blot and FACS, conform 
the strategy employed to carry such evaluation and will be exposed along the project.

BACKGROUND 
1.1. The disease: atherosclerosis 
1.1.1. 
Overview 
Atherosclerosis, also called hardening of the arteries, stands for the pathological process by which fatty 
substances, low density lipoprotein (LDL or ´bad cholesterol´), infiltrated immunocytes, calcium and cell 
debris accumulate within the arterial wall leading to the buildup of a plaque [9]. Along with disease 
progression, the plaque grows, resulting in the narrowing of the arteries (stenosis), which limits the flow 
of oxygen-rich blood to organs and body tissues [9]. Eventually, plaque rupture can expose 
thrombogenic material to the circulation, leading to platelet aggregation, humoral coagulation and the 
formation of a blood clot (thrombus). This may either in situ block the lumen immediately, causing partial 
or even complete blood flow obstruction, or detach and move into the circulation, ultimately blocking 
blood flow distal to its point of origin, causing embolisms [10]. 
Despite old theories, it is now known that atherosclerosis is a chronic progressive inflammatory process 
and not just a disorder due to abnormalities in lipid metabolism, which had been the main explanation 
for years [11][12]. As such, constant invasion, accumulation and death of monocytes triggered by LDL 
infiltration within the intima of healthy vessels is the main driver of the disease (Figure 1) [13]. A more 
detailed explanation of the disease development process is further discussed in the following section. 
 
Figure 1 Atherosclerotic plaque formation process is mainly driven by an LDL-triggered inflammatory condition [13]. Infiltration of 
cholesterol within the intima of the vessel (stage 1) elicits a process by which inflammatory cells are attracted to the area to fight 
the originated pathologic state (stage 2). Macrophage derived foam cells (macrophages full of lipids) start to die and build up a 
necrotic core (stage 3). This process continues until plaque ruptures, leading to thrombosis (stage 4 and 5). 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Atherosclerosis can affect any arteries in the body, including those in the heart, brain, arms, legs, pelvis, 
and kidneys [9]. As a result, different diseases may develop. Coronary heart disease (when affecting the 
coronary arteries of the heart), cerebrovascular disease (carotid arteries of the brain) and peripheral artery 
disease (associated with legs, arms and lower body) constitute the main clinical manifestations of the 
disease [9]. Atherosclerosis alone, though, is rarely fatal. It is thrombosis originated from a ruptured 
atherosclerotic plaque that precipitates the life-threatening clinical events such as acute myocardial 
infarction and sudden cardiac death, stroke and acute limb ischemia, respectively [14][15].  
The stated pathological conditions form part of a well-known group of heart- and blood vessel-
associated disorders called cardiovascular diseases (CVDs). WHO evaluation from 2016 reported CVDs 
as the leading cause of death worldwide. An estimated 17.5 million people died from CVDs in 2012, 
representing 31% of all global deaths. Of these, an estimated 7.4 million were due to coronary heart 
disease and 6.7 million, due to stroke [15]. Statistics make a clear call for further study of the disease, its 
underlying mechanisms and the need for seeking after new therapeutic targets.  
 
1.1.2. 
Disease development process  
Atherosclerotic plaque formation is a silent stepwise process (Figure 2) triggered by an increased blood 
concentration of apolipoprotein B100-containing lipoproteins, of which LDL is usually the most prevalent 
form [16]. LDL is the principal transporter of cholesterol and fat in human blood. Circulating LDL 
guarantees a constant supply of cholesterol for tissues and cells, where cholesterol is required for 
membrane synthesis, modulation of membrane fluidity and the regulation of cell signaling pathways [16]. 
At its normal levels, LDL is needed for the body´s usual function. However, raised circulating levels of LDL 
lead to its deposition on the surface of the endothelial cells within the vessel wall and its infiltration within 
the intima (the sub-endothelial space, Figure 2). This, ultimately causes damage and breakage of the 
endothelium [17]. Dysfunctional endothelium generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) and 
metalloproteases (MMPs) that oxidize infiltrated LDL (oxLDL), leading to endothelial cell activation. This 
means endothelial cells start expressing adhesion molecules to trap circulating immune cells in an attempt 
to clear LDL from the area, which translates into a signal for the immune system to recruit monocytes 
and lymphocytes within the damaged site [17]. Once in the intima, monocytes differentiate into 
macrophages, which start scavenging oxLDL, eventually becoming foam cells (lipid-laden macrophages) 
[17]. These macrophage-derived foam cells: (1) secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines that stimulate further 
monocyte retention and sustain the settled inflammatory condition, (2) promote proliferation and 
migration of smooth cells residing in the layer below the intima, and (3) die, releasing their lipid content 
and driving the growth of the plaque. These dying cells surrounded by the large amount of lipids released 
constitute the basis for the formation of the lipid-rich necrotic core (Figure 2) [17]. Besides that, smooth 
muscle cells set off their migration from the smooth muscle layer into the fatty plaque. There, they secrete 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
collagen and elastin that serve for the buildup of a protein fibrous cap matrix located over the lipid core 
and intended to cover the plaque and shield it from the bloodstream [17].  
Eventually, uncontrolled inflammatory cell infiltration, cell death through apoptosis, accumulation of 
lipids, cell debris and matrix degradation through proteolysis creates a vulnerable plaque with a thin 
fibrous cap and a lipid-rich necrotic core prone to rupture and cause to thrombosis [18][19][20]. 
 
Figure 2 Molecular mechanism of plaque formation in the artery. The accumulation of LDL leads to endothelial dysfunction, 
generating species able to oxidize LDL. oXLDL activates endothelial cells, which start expressing adhesion molecules able to trap 
circulating monocytes. Recruited monocytes differentiate into macrophages and take up the surrounding oxLDL, becoming foam 
cells. These ones help in the settling of an inflammatory condition by secreting inflammatory cytokines and constituting the basis 
of the lipid-rich necrotic core, formed by dead foam cells and its released lipidic content. Besides that, they also promote migration 
of smooth cells, which secrete the required materials, elastin and collagen, for the creation of a fibrous cap covering the plaque. 
All this process results in an unstable plaque with a high likelihood of generating a thrombus within the artery [19].  
In a normal inflammatory situation, lipid molecules like resolvins and protein mediators such as 
Interleukin-10 (IL-10), Transforming Growth Factor-β (TGFβ) or annexin-1 (Appendix 1), take the lead in 
the inflammation resolution phase and the way back to tissue homeostasis. Named for their function pro-
resolving mediators, they cease pro-inflammatory signals, hinder inflammatory cell recruitment and 
potentiate the clearance of dead cells (named efferocytosis), thereby driving the situation towards tissue 
repair and tempering of inflammation [21]. 
However, in a chronic inflammatory disease such as atherosclerosis, the pathologic stimuli doesn´t 
diminish (LDL keeps accumulating), inflammation keeps up, and so the resolution phase is not properly 
conducted. This leads to tissue damage and exacerbation of the underlying situation [22]. Therefore, 
defective resolution of inflammation constitutes the key pathogenic clinical feature of the disease 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
progression and the real hallmark of atherosclerosis [22]. The constant generation of pro-inflammatory 
cytokines arising from increasing subendothelial levels of oxLDL results in constant immune cell infiltration 
and impaired egress [22]. This, together with insufficient efferocytosis to engulf the increasing amount of 
lipid-releasing dead cells leads to the common pathological manifestations of the disorder, including 
growth of the necrotic core, fibrous cap thinning (due to constant production and release of matrix-
degrading proteases from activated macrophages), high oxidative stress and tissue damage [21]. 
1.1.3. 
Current treatments and future perspectives 
Interestingly, atherosclerosis accounts for a long-lasting progressive developmental process that goes 
unnoticed before a life-threatening situation occurs, remaining asymptomatic for decades. Certain traits 
or habits have been identified as risk factors, able to raise the likelihood of developing the disease. These 
include tobacco and alcohol use, unhealthy diet and obesity and physical inactivity [9]. Factors such as 
age and family history of disease, which lay beyond what a person´s control, are also part of the list. 
Therefore, lifestyle changes and physical activity can help in preventing atherosclerosis [9]. However, 
sometimes this is not enough and severe cases require from specific medical procedures or surgeries [9]: 
(1) Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or coronary angioplasty stands for the nonsurgical 
practice by which blocked or narrowed coronary arteries are opened by insertion of a catheter 
tube (cardiac catheterization), aiming at an improvement in the heart blood flow. Sometimes a 
small mesh tube, a stent, is placed in the artery to keep it opened after the procedure [9].   
(2) Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) is a type of surgery in which arteries or veins from other 
areas in your body are used to circumvent the impaired coronary and leg arteries [9]. 
(3) Carotid endarterectomy is a type of intervention intended to remove plaque buildup from the 
carotid arteries in order to restore blood flow to the brain, and thus help preventing stroke [9]. 
Fortunately, some medications are available in the market that can reduce systemic cholesterol and avoid 
reaching surgery. The common mechanism of many of these medicines is that their end-point is the 
reduction of circulating LDL and lipid levels, the trigger of the disease. They include small molecules such 
as statins, fibrates or niacin, and, more recently, biologicals in the shape of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) 
against the pro-protein convertase subtilisin/kexin type 9 (PCSK9) enzyme [21]: 
(1) Statins are the mainstay treatment currently used. These drugs hold the ability to competitively 
inhibit the first enzyme involved in the LDL synthesis pathway, the 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-
coenzymeA (HMG-CoA) reductase [23]. 
(2) Fibrates are a class of amphipathic carboxylic acids intended to reduce triglyceride levels, fats 
that, in parallel to LDL, also contribute to the disease development [23]. 
(3) Niacin, which is a vitamin that reduces LDL and triglyceride levels and increases High-Density 
Lipoprotein (HDL or ´good cholesterol´) [23]. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
(4) Alirocumab and evolocumab belong to a new class of drugs consisting of mAbs targeting PCSK9 
[23]. PCSK9 binds to LDL receptors (LDLr) and drives their internalization and lysosomal 
degradation. This results in few receptors recycled to the cell surface and, thus, being available 
to trap and metabolize circulating LDL (Figure 3) [24]. These new drugs bind to PCSK9 adjacent 
to the region that is required for interaction with LDLr, thereby preventing it from binding to 
LDLr. After endocytosis, LDLr is recycled back to the cell surface, with few receptors degraded 
and more ready to capture and internalize LDL, thus removing it from the circulation [24].   
 
Figure 3 Mode of action of anti-PCSK9 antibodies. a)  Extracellular PCSK9 binds LDLR, driving internalization and lysosomal 
degradation of the receptor. LDL cholesterol keeps high in circulation. b)  PCSK9-targeted monoclonal antibodies block PCSK9-
LDLR interaction, with LDLR, as a result, remaining available to remove LDL from the bloodstream and incorporate it into cells [24] 
The stated therapies, though, only delay the progression of atherosclerotic disease, not being fully 
effective in curing it. The key role reported for the innate immune system and inflammation in the 
progression of the disease, set the next generation of therapeutic strategies under current progress [25]. 
This novel approach shifts the focus from the trigger to the natural fighter of the disease and is directed 
to help the innate immune system to defeat the pathological condition by enhancing the effective natural 
resolution of inflammation within the local plaque environment. The endogenous biological mediators of 
inflammation (pro-resolving lipids and cytokines) hold, as mentioned, a great potential for dampening 
inflammation and promoting tissue repair, importantly, without comprising host defence. Thus, their use 
as a means to enhance inflammation resolution has recently gained interest in pharmacology and is 
referred to as pro-resolving therapy [21]. The nature of these pro-resolving mediators, mainly peptides 
and proteins, comes along, though, with associated drawbacks: (1) when intravenously injected, these 
molecules display very short half-lives due to rapid clearance from the body, preventing them from 
exerting their beneficial effect, and (2) since they are not specific to the site of action, its untargeted 
delivery can lead to collateral adverse effects by unwanted interactions elsewhere in the body [6]. Here 
is where nanotechnology steps in as the way to overcome the stated hurdles by allowing for an 
encapsulated, targeted/specific and controlled delivery using nanoparticles (NPs). These upcoming 
treatments, which will be addressed further later in the text, are expected to be safer and more effective.

1.1.4. 
Macrophage subpopulations in atherosclerosis 
To pick the best therapeutic targets for the aforementioned medical strategies, a closer approach to the 
immunity of the disease is required. 
While atherosclerosis was thought to be a type 1 T helper cell-driven inflammatory response, the concept 
has changed lately [18]. The huge heterogeneity of macrophages observed within the plaque (Figure 4) 
and the fact that macrophages have been reported to be key modulator and effector cells in the immune 
response has caused the emergence of the idea that macrophages constitute the central cells in 
atherosclerosis. This is not only because they are the most abundant ones, but also because of the impact 
of their roles on the evolution of the disease [18]. Mainly derived from recruited monocyte differentiation, 
they have been reported to be highly pleiotropic (able to display highly diverse functions) and to possess 
the capacity to rapidly shift polarization profile due to subtle changes in their microenvironment. In 
response to local stimuli such as modified lipids or cytokines, their phenotype in plaques has been 
reported to change along the different disease stages and highly influence disease evolution [18][26][27].  
Based on their expression markers, production of specific compounds and biological roles, not less than 
six clearly differentiated macrophage subtypes have been described to coexist within atherosclerotic 
lesions: M1, M(Hb), Mhem, M2, Mox and M4 (Figure 4) [18][28].

factor (erythroid-derived 2)-like 2; NF-kB, nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells; TLR, toll-like receptor; 
TNF, tumor necrosis factor [18].

Briefly, M1 macrophages respond to a pro-inflammatory phenotype stimulated by lipidic molecules 
(mainly oxLDL and cholesterol crystals accumulated during early stages of disease development) and 
pro-inflammatory cytokines present within the plaque [18]. M(Hb), Mhem, and M2 macrophages are 
resistant to lipid accumulation and foam cell formation and show anti-inflammatory effects. Besides that, 
oxidized phospholipids can also drive monocyte differentiation towards the Mox phenotype, which 
displays an antioxidant gene expression profile and reduced phagocytic and chemotactic capacities, but 
retain pro-inflammatory cytokine production abilities [28]. However, the Mox type has only been 
observed in mice and its presence in humans remains to be determined [18][28]. Last, CXCL4-triggered 
M4 macrophages express inflammatory genes, but lack the phagocytic capacity [18][28]. 
So far, M1 and M2 macrophages, the phenotypic extremes, are the ones seen to have major roles in the 
disease development [18][5]. In general terms, M1 macrophages participate in the removal of pathogens 
during infection and the subsequent generation of ROS. Their chronic activation can, therefore, mediate 
tissue damage and impair homeostasis [18]. In turn, M2 macrophages counteract the pro-inflammatory 
M1 response by promoting tissue repair and suppressing inflammation [18].  
In the atherosclerotic context (Figure 5), primarily governed 
by the presence of M1 macrophages, (1) their pro-
inflammatory cytokine profile sustains the immune system 
activation leading to tissue damage and (2) their apoptosis 
supports the necrotic core formation, which increases 
plaque vulnerability [18]. Alternatively, M2 macrophages (1) 
release anti-inflammatory cytokines able to dampen and 
slowly turn down the immune system activation and (2) 
phagocytose debris and M1 apoptotic cells via efferocytosis 
[18]. Thus, it is believed that M1:M2 balance is a key 
determinant on the disease evolution [18][5].  
 
 
Bearing in mind what was stated in the previous section, where the hallmark of atherosclerosis was 
defined to be a defective resolution phase of inflammation due to sustained stimuli and insufficient 
clearance of apoptotic macrophages (insufficient efferocytosis), M2 macrophages turn out to be pro-
resolving and, thus, crucial in plaque regression.  
Classically-activated M1 macrophages, typically induced by Th1 cytokines, lipopolysaccharide (LPS) or 
oxidized lipids recognition (Figure 4), produce high levels of IL-12, low levels of IL-10, and secrete the 
pro-inflammatory cytokines TNFα, IL-6, and IL-1β [18]. On the other hand, alternatively activated M2

macrophages are characterized by low production of IL-12 and high production of both IL-10 and TGF-

Figure 6 depicts a simplified scheme of how the differentiation process works once monocytes have 
extravasated the endothelium and become macrophages prone to polarize one way or another based 
on the microenvironment they come across. Stimuli are many, and so are their origins. In addition, the 
molecular pathways involved are also various and cross-talk among each other, establishing a reciprocal

1.1.5. 
Pro-resolving cytokines in atherosclerosis 
So far, it has been clearly stated that (1) macrophages are key in the development of atherosclerotic 
lesions since they take part in all stages of plaque formation and progression, (2) during their lifetime, 
they are exposed to a plethora of environmental signals that influence their transcriptional program and 
functional phenotype, (3) plaque evolution is, to a large extent, a matter of macrophage phenotype. 
Aligned with that, it has been suggested that local accumulation of macrophage subpopulations highly 
competent in clearing apoptotic cells (M2) can sustain efferocytosis, thereby contributing to the resolution 
of inflammation and prevention of necrotic core formation within plaques, leading to plaque stability.  
As mentioned in the previous section, the M1:M2 balance boils down to the microenvironmental 
molecules present. Among the factors or mediators that can shift the balance in favor of M2 macrophage 
differentiation are an increase in Th2 cell-secreted molecules, such as IL-4 or IL-10 [22]. Pro-resolving IL-
4 and IL-10 have been seen to have the ability to attenuate inflammatory cytokine production and 
promoting monocyte polarization into efferocytic M2 macrophages (Figure 9) [22]. 
 
 
IL-10, secreted by Th2 cells and by efferocytes (M2) during apoptotic cell clearance [22], has been reported 
to (1) block inflammatory responses in M1 macrophages by inducing suppression of macrophage-
mediated activation of inflammatory T cells and decreased production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, (2) 
stimulate the monocyte differentiation into M2c macrophages (Figure 8) and (3) enhance efferocytosis 
itself both in vitro and in vivo (Figure 9) [22]. Fat-fed low-density lipoprotein receptor knockout (Ldlr)-/- 
atherosclerotic mice transplanted with IL-10-transgenic bone marrow cells showed a 47% decrease in the 
atherosclerotic lesion size and an 80% decrease in the necrotic core area compared with controls [22][35].  
Briefly, IL-4, which will be discussed further for its relevance in the project, has been reported to (1) 
support monocyte differentiation into M2a macrophages, thus increasing efferocytosis and IL-10 
production and (2) drop pro-inflammatory cytokine production by induction of PPARγ transcription 
factor, an inflammation suppressor (Figure 9) [22].

1.1.5.1. 
Role of IL-4 in atherosclerosis 
IL-4, a multifunctional pleiotropic cytokine, is produced mainly by activated T cells 
but also by mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils [36][37]. A typical cytokine 
structurally, with molecular weight varying between 12 and 20 kDa as a result of 
variable natural glycosylation, IL-4 has a compact, globular conformation 
stabilized by three disulphide bonds and formed by four anti-parallel α-helix and 
two stranded β-sheets (Figure 10) [37].  
Functionally, it is best known for defining the so-called Th2 phenotype of lymphocytes, controlling IgE 
antibody production in B cells (both involved in allergic responses and in extracellular bacteria and 
parasite immunity) and for regulating cell proliferation, apoptosis, and expression of numerous genes in 
various cell types, including lymphocytes, macrophages, and fibroblasts, as well as epithelial and 
endothelial cells [36]. Upon interaction with its specific surface receptor, IL-4R, composed of the IL-4R 
chain paired with the common C one, a series of phosphorylation events mediated by receptor-
associated kinases  take place, which ultimately regulate the expression of several critical inflammatory 
genes [36][38]. Importantly, in monocytes and macrophages, IL-4 activates Jak1-Stat6 pathway driving its 
alternative M2a activation [18][39], which is depicted in Figure 8.  
At this stage, it is meaningful to highlight, though, that some controversy exists around the role of IL-4 
in atherosclerosis. Despite the fact that Th2-biased responses were proposed to antagonize pro-
atherogenic Th1 effects and thereby confer atheroprotection, the role of the Th2 pathway in the 
development of atherosclerosis remains controversial depending on the stage and site of the lesion, as 
well as on the experimental model [40]. A growing body of evidence indicates that IL-4 may play a role 
in atherosclerosis through induction of inflammatory responses, such as upregulation of VCAM-1 [38], 
which prompts the immune cell arrest. Consistent with this hypothesis, transplantation of bone marrow 
stem cells from IL-4 -/- mice into LDLr -/- mice decreased atherosclerotic lesion formation in a site-specific 
manner [41]. Similarly, IL-4-/-apoE-/- mice (apoE mediates LDL metabolism) showed reduced 
atherosclerosis in the aortic arch, but not in the aortic sinus, compared with apoE-/- mice [42]. In contrast, 
king et. al. reported no effect of genetic IL-4-depletion in models of cholesterol-induced atherosclerosis 
[43] and George et. al. showed that IL-4 deficiency in C57BL/6 mice fed with an atherogenic diet did not 
affect the development of early lesions [44]. However, the same group found that lipid core formation in 
IL-4-/- mice was significantly lower compared with lesions in wild type C57BL/6 [45]. 
Besides studies tackling the role of IL-4 in atherosclerosis by the cytokine gene knock out, IL-4 
administration effect on apoE deficient mice subjected to high-fat diet was evaluated by investigating 
isolated peripherial mononuclear blood cells (PMBCs) from the treated mice. In this case, IL-4 
administration led to increased expression of M2 markers and attenuated atherosclerotic lesions [46], 
which supports IL-4 potential benefit.

Aligned with that, Soo-Jin Park et.al reported that IL-4 secretion and its signaling were key mediators of

1.2. Nanotechnology strategies for drug delivery  
Nanotechnology is defined, by the National Nanotechnology Initiative, as the science embracing the 
manipulation of matter on an atomic, molecular and supramolecular scale with at least one dimension 
sized from 1 to 100 nanometers. Properties of matter at submicron scale are unique at all levels (optical, 
mechanical, electrical, physical, and chemical) and their rational tuning can translate into systems with a 
wider range of capabilities than their macroscale counterparts [7]. The application of this science to the 
medical field is referred to as nanomedicine and comprises the use of nanotechnological knowledge and 
tools with the goal of developing new devices for research, diagnosis and treatment of disease. The study 
and employment of the distinctive features held by nanomaterials is foreseen to provide exciting 
opportunities with large impact on the pharmaceutical and biotechnological industry´s landscape. 
With the advent of nanomedicine, drug properties such as solubility, metabolism, plasma binding or 
biodistribution have experienced a step back in the list of constraints associated with the chemistry of the 
drug when dealing with its delivery. A fast pace of innovation and emerging success of nanoparticle (NP) 
based drug delivery systems has allowed to circumvent hurdles related to drug navigation and 
distribution throughout the body. NPs are typically biodegradable and biocompatible structures generally 
between 10 and 150 nm in size that display multiple potential therapeutic advantages such as: (1) 
improvement of the drug pharmacodynamics (PD) and pharmacokinetic (PK) properties (improved half-
life) without a strong need to change its chemistry, (2) improved safety, efficacy and biodistribution 
profiles due to (3) targeted delivery to specific tissues, cells and cell locations (passive and active) and (4) 
spatiotemporal, triggered and controlled release of the drug over extended periods [7][8].  
From liposomes, dendrimers and micelles to gold, silica or polymeric nanoparticles, NP behavior relies 
strongly on their inherent biophysicochemical properties, which can be rationally tailored at a larger or 
lower extent depending on the specific NP type, to get the desired delivery. Composition, size, shape, 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
surface charge and chemistry (including targeting ligands), hydrophobicity, roughness and elasticity 
heavily influence their performance [7], as exemplified below. 
Passive targeting, which stands for the preferential accumulation of NPs (carrying no affinity ligands) at 
active sites, directly relies on size, shape, charge and flexibility of the NP, among other properties. Adding 
a further degree of specificity, active targeting defines the performance of NPs surface modified to 
incorporate affinity ligands (such as antibodies, antibody fragments, peptides, proteins, etc.) with 
specificity to disease tissues and cells [8]. Targeted delivery of NPs allows for their differential spatial 
localization within the body, minimizing the drug payload´s off-target adverse effects. 
Interestingly, evolution of earliest NPs (liposomes and polymer-drug conjugates) experienced a tour de 
force when NP surface modification using polyethylene glycol (PEG) polymers showed to prevent at a 
higher extent non-specific binding of NP surfaces to blood components, thereby reducing their rapid 
uptake and clearance in vivo by the cells of the mononuclear phagocytic system (MPS), monocytes and 
macrophages. This led to the achievement of prolonged blood circulation half-lives [21][22].  
An additional advantage that this new medicines can potentially provide, mainly polymeric-based NPs, 
is the possibility to, at certain degree, regulate the release, turning drug kinetics into a spatiotemporally 
controlled parameter. The chance to have a say in where, when and how the body exposure to the drug 
occurs means a huge step forward in raising efficacy and safety of the treatment, since drug 
concentrations are under control in terms of location, time and rate of release. Controlled-release systems 
also allow for lowering the dosage, resulting in lower toxicity and more patient-friendly systems. Thus, 
although a vast array of materials have been used to formulate NPs (lipids, metal oxides, etc.), polymers 
are gaining relevance due to their distinguished ability to form controlled-release systems [8][48]. For this 
and other reasons stated further, polymeric NPs will be the ones used employed in the current project. 
 
1.2.1. 
Polymeric NPs 
Besides all the advantages coming along with the inherent fact of being nanomedicines (explained in 
section 1.2), polymeric materials have emerged as a major class of controlled-release systems [8]. A 
summary of their key properties is shown in Figure 11 [21].  
The most important advantage of polymeric NPs over the first generation of clinically approved NP drug 
delivery systems (liposomes, micelles, etc.) is the broad range of possibilities to tailor their properties, 
which allows the development of self-assembled and customizable, controlled release therapeutic NPs. 
Polymers are versatile building blocks for NP development that can be custom-synthesized with unique 
biocompatibility and degradation properties facilitating improvements in drug therapeutic index by 
localized/targeted and sustained/controlled-release delivery [49]. In other words, by careful selection of 
the composition of polymeric NPs resulting in optimal PK/BD, the total amount of drug and the duration 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
of drug exposure in target tissue can be altered and improved substantially [8]. This is ideal for potent 
therapeutic proteins, such as pro-resolving mediators. 
 
In addition, noticeable signs that this is becoming a pretty well-established technology is the fact that 
most of the polymers and agents used for their generation are already FDA (Food and Drug 
Administration) approved. 
 
1.2.1.1. 
Nanoparticle design 
Biodegradable polymers have been studied extensively over the past few decades for the fabrication of 
drug delivery systems and have been recognized for their potential in such applications since the 1960´s. 
To date, the most commonly used polymers for controlled drug release applications include poly(D,L-
lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA), poly(lactic acid) (PLA), poly(glutamic acid) (PGA), poly(caprolactone) (PCL), 
N-(2-hydroxypropyl)-methacrylate copolymers (HPMA), and poly(amino acids) (Figure 12) [48]. In 
particular, PLGA, PGA and PLA have been widely exploited, particularly due to their favorable 
biocompatibility and biodegradability properties, requiring from simple clearance of the polymer matrix 
by the body´s metabolic pathways. This means that over time they break down inside the body producing 
nontoxic natural byproducts such as water and carbon dioxide, which can be easily eliminated. An 
overview covering all the commonly used polymers is shown in Figure 12 [8][48].

As already mentioned, the most extensively investigated degradable polymers are PGA, PLA and PLGA. 
The poor solubility and fast degradation makes PGA the worst choice among them. PLA is hydrophobic 
due to the presence of the methyl side group and its properties can be adjusted by tuning the D- and L-
isomer racemization, leading to PLLA and PDLA. PLLA degradation rate is lower than PGA´s one. Thus, 
copolymers resulting from the combination of different ratios of the respective monomers (glucolide (G) 
and lactide (L)) were developed, giving birth to PLGA, which is the most commonly used polymer 
nowadays. By easily tuning the G:L ratio, PLGA properties can be rationally devised [48].  
The use of block copolymers of PLGA and polyethyleneglicol, PEG, (PLGA-PEG) (Figure 13) meant a 
significant progress in NP circulation half-life, minimizing the adhesion of plasma components, such as 
opsonins, and subsequent immunological responses. The incorporation of the inert and lipophilic 
polyether PEG on top of the NP leads to the formation of a hydrophilic shell that provides immune 
shielding, thus increasing circulation time and facilitating passive targeting, overall allowing dosage 
lowering. PEG is an FDA approved polymer and defined as GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) [48].

All the stated features gathered by the NP components directly influence the mechanism of drug release 
of the system. The main mechanisms of drug release include: (1) drug diffusion through water filled pores, 
which stands for the random movement of drug molecules driven by a chemical potential gradient, (2) 
drug diffusion through the polymer matrix, whereby drug molecules simply diffuse out of the polymer 
matrix, (3) osmotic pumping, a convection driven drug transport through water-filled pores, and (4) 
surface and bulk erosion of the NP, coming along with polymeric matrix degradation (Figure 14) [48].

The classical drug release from polymeric drug delivery systems follows a triphasic pattern comprising: 
(1) a burst release phase, which involves the rapid release of surface-bound drug molecules, (2) a steady 
controlled-release phase related to slow drug diffusion through the polymer matrix or existing pores, and 
(3) a last phase of faster release due to bulk erosion. However, the release performance of polymeric NPs 
relies on a wide range of physicochemical properties and should be addressed case-by-case [48].

1.2.1.2. 
Nanoparticle properties 
Already stated in section 2.2, NP physicochemical properties can have a strong impact on NPs behavior 
and performance and may be optimized through the effective design of NPs. 
NP size is a key parameter influencing the cellular uptake rate of NPs, their internalization mechanism, 
and, importantly, their in vivo circulation half-life and passive accumulation in specific tissues [8]. In 
general, 10–100 nm is a generally accepted size range for the development of NPs for in vivo applications, 
based on their in vivo clearance and biodistribution patterns [8]. These upper and lower bounds are 
basically defined by kidney filtration cut-offs, interactions with the immune system and, based on that, 
the possibility to capitalize on the EPR effect to reach the desired fate [51]. Smaller NPs are rapidly cleared 
by kidney glomerular filtration, while larger ones are exposed to increased interactions with opsonins and 
faster clearance rates in vivo by the MPS system [8].  
NP surface charge has a major impact on the nonspecific binding of NPs to cells and plasma proteins. 
Positively charged NPs favor cellular binding by interaction with the negatively charged head of the 
phospholipids on the cellular membrane, thus promoting its rapid removal from circulation by cells of 
the MPS [8]. Negatively charged NPs also display high cellular uptake. Therefore, charged NPs will 
inevitably have short half-life in vivo and high non-specific cellular uptakes due to interaction with blood 
proteins and immune complement activation [8]. Surface charge of NPs, though, is somehow a highly 
controllable property since it can be easily tuned by modifying the functional PEG end group [8].  
In addition, hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity plays an important role in NP targeting to cells and also 
influence cellular uptake and blood circulation. While NPs surface hydrophobicity favors cell engulfment, 
hydrophilicity (obtained i.e. by PEGylation) ensures reduced surface protein adsorption, so prolonged 
circulation due to reduced phagocytosis and clearance [8].  
Other properties exist that influence NPs´ behavior but the ones presented are the most relevant and will 
be key in the design, preparation, characterization and performance of the NPs used in the project.  
 
1.2.1.3. 
Nanoparticle preparation 
A wide range of methods are available for the preparation of polymeric NPs using biodegradable 
polymers. Most of these involve NP´s self-assembly of block copolymers driven by the difference in 
solubility between the two different polymer chains. The choice of NP formulation method usually 
depends on the drug physicochemical properties along with the loading requirements and particle size. 
Nanoprecipitation, oil-in-water (O/W), and water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) emulsifications, are three of 
the most commonly used [8][48]. Nanoprecipitation will be the technique employed herein. 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
The nanoprecipitation technique encompasses the instantaneous formation of NPs driven by interfacial 
interactions between two different liquid phases, an organic solvent and water. The polymer or polymers 
of interest are dissolved in the organic solvent and the resulting solution is added dropwise to the 
aqueous one under stirring. Once in contact with water, polymers self-assemble into core-shell like 
spherical particles locating the more hydrophobic structures inside and the more hydrophilic ones facing 
the outside, in order to reduce the system´s free energy. In general, smaller NPs are obtained by this 
method when compared to others. Nanoprecipitation is a simple method, amenable to scale-up and 
requiring only mild stirring under minimal shear stress. Some downsides involve poor entrapment of 
hydrophilic drugs (due to an hydrophobic core environment) [8]. 
All emulsification techniques (single emulsions water-in oil (W/O) or oil-in water (O/W) and double 
emulsions (W/O/W)) consist of the formation of emulsion, followed by the homogenization of the formed 
mixture [8]. The single emulsion technique (O/W) is suitable for entrapping hydrophobic drugs and 
generally results in higher drug loading and encapsulation efficiencies (EE) than nanoprecipitation. 
However, it needs an additional input of energy such as homogenization and the obtained NPs are 
usually larger [8]. Double emulsion (W/O/W) is generally used for encapsulation of hydrophilic drugs and 
normally yields NPs with the largest size and moderate drug loading and EE [8]. 
In addition, drug loading into NPs can be done by (1) covalently attaching the drug to the polymer 
backbone, (2) adsorbing the drug to the polymer surface, and (3) entrapping the drug in the polymeric 
matrix during NP preparation. For its simplicity, the latter is the most commonly used method. However, 
it yields low drug entrapment and EE. Alternatively, conjugation of the drug to the polymer backbone by 
chemical modification of the polymer or the drug raises loading and EE exponentially and allows for 
tuning of the release kinetics by varying the number of drug molecules attached to the polymer [8]. 
Incorporation of targeting ligands (bioconjugation) can be done both by (1) the conventional approach 
involving the conjugation of bioactive molecules to the surface of pre-formed NPs via coupling 
chemistries or (2) modular NP self-assembly using pre-functionalized polymeric materials. The second 
technique allows for control and tuning of the ligand density and prevents batch-to-batch variability.

2.1. Hypothesis 
The integration of all the previously introduced concepts brings as to formulate this project hypothesis, 
which encompasses two complementary ideas (Figure 17). 
First, we believe that IL-4 delivery to atherosclerotic plaques will cause the macrophages present within 
the lesion, primarily pro-inflammatory M1, to phenotypically switch towards an M2a anti-inflammatory 
profile. The resulting situation, favored by the presence of M2a macrophages, is thought to drive the 
pathological state towards inflammation resolution and subsequently translate into a reduced 
inflammatory environment, increased efferocytosis and cap size, reduced necrotic core formation and 
oxidative stress and, as a result, a gain in plaque stability. 
Free IL-4 delivery in vivo, though, is a hurdle due to its rapid clearance from circulation and uncontrollable 
fate when administered intravenously. Therefore, we think that the loading of this cytokine into a 
polymeric nanocarrier will allow IL-4 delivery to be targeted, safe and controlled-release.

2.2. Overall aim 
To prove the defined hypothesis, the aim of the project was defined as the development of IL-4-loaded 
polymeric NPs for anti-inflammatory macrophage polarization towards an M2a phenotype meant to 
contribute to inflammation resolution (Figure 18). 
 
Figure 18 Schematic representation of the overall aim of the project. Figures adapted from Kamaly et.al [21]. 
The current project was based on kamaly et.al. setup for the development of targeted Interleukin-10 
nanotherapeutics for the resolution of inflammation in advanced atherosclerosis [21], which provided 
promising results for the delivery of IL-10 as anti-inflammatory therapeutic agent.  
IL-4-loaded NPs design aims to fulfill the same requirements the IL-10 NPs were built up on: long 
circulation half-life to facilitate accumulation, small size to enhance permeation of the endothelium, 
molecular targeting to ease retention within plaques, sufficient loading and entrapment of the cytokine 
and retention of IL-4 activity after ideally a controlled-release. By delivering them to the atherosclerotic 
plaque, the goal is to tailor the plaque microenvironment to prompt a phenotypical shift on M1 
macrophages towards M2a. This should lead to a dampening effect on the inflammatory environment, 
increased efferocytosis causing a reduction on the necrotic core, altogether driving the situation towards 
inflammation resolution and tissue homeostasis. 
Importantly, the main objective of this specific thesis project only entails the nanosystem design, 
formulation and characterization in vitro, with a special focus the macrophage phenotypical shift. The 
range of ex vivo and in vivo analysis, as well as the inclusion of a targeting ligand, will be set aside for 
further future research and will not be part of the scope of this project.

To pursue the stated aim, the methodology established in this thesis (section 2.3) tries to address the

(1) What is the IL-4 effect? What are the biological outcomes of IL-4 administration? (positive control)

2.3. Experimental strategy 
To solve the aforementioned question marks, the experimental plan was structured in 3 main sections: 
(1) Proof of concept of IL-4 efficacy: involving a panel of assays addressing questions 1 and 2. 
(2) Nanoplatform formulation: encompassing the rational design of the NPs (third question). 
(3) Nanoplatform characterization: to solve fourth and fifth questions. 
An overview of the investigational strategy is shown in this section but will be further discussed along the

2.3.2. 
Nanoplatform formulation 
As previously mentioned, due to the short systemic half-life of proteins for effective therapeutic purposes, 
encapsulation and therefore protection of IL-4 in a nanocarrier system with controlled release properties 
was thought to be the optimal approach. Hence, biodegradable controlled-release polyesters in the form 
of PLA and PLGA polymers were chosen to design the final NP system for IL-4 delivery. The polymer 
election was made in compliance with the following: both polymers are (1) FDA approved and “Generally 
Recognized as Safe” (GRAS), (2) biodegradable and biocompatible and (3) controlled-release [48]. 
Bearing this in mind, the formulation of the NPs was conducted by means of the blending of two core 
polymers: NH2-PLGA-NH2 and the PEG diblock polymer PDLA-PEG-COOH (Figure 21). The former was 
intended to provide charged terminal moieties for more effective electrostatic interactions with the 
cytokine, and the later, a PEG diblock polymer for stability, immune shielding and enhanced circulation 
[21]. The inclusion of a PEG diblock polymer bioconjugated to a targeting ligand to provide the NP with 
the targeting capacity will be part of further studies and is out of the scope of the present project. 
NP formulation, though, was still devised to translate into an in vivo targeting strategy relying both on 
the passive delivery of the NPs to the disease region thanks to dysfunctional endothelia and permeable 
vessels (EPR effect) and the active targeting by the incorporation of a ligand functionalized on the PEG 
diblock polymer of the NP [21]. Possible targeting ligands are shown in Table 3. 
Nanoparticles were produced via self-assembly of the stated biodegradable polyester polymers by bulk 
nanoprecipitation. The mixing of an organic solvent (dimethylformamide, DMF) containing the polymers 
==PAGE:38==

at a 1:1 ratio and water containing IL-4 led to the formation of core-shell like structures holding an 
hydrophobic inside harboring IL-4 and an hydrophilic outer layer [21].

3.1.7. 
Conclusive remarks from IL-4 efficacy´s proof of concept 
As a summary of the results obtained from the proof of concept panel of assays, increased anti-
inflammatory cytokine production (IL-10 and IL-4), reduced pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-6, 
TNFα, MCP-5, sICAM-1, and MIP-1α, among others), approached by ELISA, cytokine array and ELISA, 
presence of pSTAT6 activity, downregulation of the CD80 surface marker and upregulation of CD206 
proved to reflect an ongoing phenotypical shift towards M2a under IL-4 administration. Thus, the 
designed setup and employed markers were concluded to reflect quite accurately the underlying 
processes of IL-4 treatment and collect the required amount of information to capture and evaluate the 
hypothesized macrophage phenotypical switch.  
The lowest IL-4 dose showed to be enough to observe significant changes for most of the markers after 
its administration. However, for IL-10 secretion approached by ELISA, for instance, 10 ng/mL of IL-4 was 
insufficient to trigger a significant effect. Therefore, 20 ng/mL of IL-4, already used as minimal dose for 
western blot and FACS, was the chosen dose to go with for the nanocomponent of the project, addressed 
in the following section, in order to ensure a clear visualization of IL-4 effects. 
 
3.2. Nanoplatform formulation and characterization 
As explained in detail in chapter 2, IL-4-loaded NPs were fabricated via nanoprecipitation by efficiently 
mixing the polymer-containing DMF solvent phase with the non-solvent IL-4-containing water phase. 
NPs self-assembled into a hydrophobic core containing the therapeutics surrounded by a hydrophilic 
PEG shell. Following synthesis, the NPs were purified by repeated centrifugation and washing steps and 
collected for characterization, both at a biophysical and biological level. Empty NPs were included as a 
control for comparison with loaded NPs. 
3.2.1. 
Biophysical properties 
The specific read-out parameters that were addressed were hydrodynamic NP size and NP charge, the 
percentage of IL-4 loading and encapsulation, and the in vitro release kinetics profile (Figure 22). 
3.2.1.1. 
Size, PDI and charge by DLS  
Size and charge, key parameters influencing NP circulation time, clearance and passive targeting capacity, 
among others, were assessed by dynamic light scattering (DLS). The basic principle of the technique relies 
on the detection of the fluctuations on the scattered light emitted by an illumination sample. The way

Charge read-outs showed a pretty high variability, resulting in average values of 2.22 ± 5.78 and -2.54

delivery and retention strategy to the lesional site. 
 
3.2.1.2. 
Encapsulation efficiency and loading capacity  
Encapsulation efficiency (EE) and loading capacity were addressed in order to know how much of the 
protein added into the formulation had actually been entrapped within the NPs. This would constitute 
the real dose expected, if released, to be able to exert its effect. The experimental strategy to calculate 
such amount was to measure the non-loaded IL-4 present in the NP filtrates by ELISA [21]. 
EE, described as the % fraction of initial protein entrapped in the NPs, was found to be 99.92% (Table 6).

3.2.1.3. 
Release kinetics 
Last, release kinetics analysis was performed in order to collect information regarding the speed and the 
pattern followed by the drug when getting out of the nanosystem. By incubating the NP solution (0,5 
mL) at body temperature (37°C) for 96 hours and analyzing the amount of IL-4 concentration in the 
solution (released from the NPs) at different time points by ELISA, a curve tracing the release profile was 
produced [21]. 
As can be seen in Figure 34, the IL-4 NPs release showed to follow a biphasic profile starting off with an 
initial burst release, attributed to the rapid release of surface-bound protein molecules (0-20h), and 
followed by a second phase characterized by a slow-release governed by slow protein diffusion through 
the polymer matrix (20-100h).

In general terms, an additional factor that could be influencing the obtained data was the presence of 
nanoparticle internalization by the macrophages as soon as administered. Macrophages have an inherent 
phagocytic capacity that leads them to engulf basically everything that does not have an endogenous 
signature. Thus it should be expected at least some engulfment of the injected therapeutic NPs by the 
cells. The IL-4 receptor whose activation is responsible for the alternative activation of the macrophages 
is a surface receptor, so NP internalization would impede IL-4 from reaching its target and exerting its 
effect. It exists a soluble form of the receptor but this is secreted and exerts a different function compared 
to the membrane-bound [85]. However, IL-4 NPs were designed to possess a closed to neutral surface, 
also coated with PEG, this minimizing the possibility of this phagocytic process to happen [86]. Therefore, 
some NP internalization should be expected to be happening and influencing the results by lowering the 
expected IL-4 to be delivered but not in a massive manner. To further study this, polymer building blocks 
could be fluorophore-conjugated and the NP location tracked by microscopy but, ultimately, only in vivo 
testing would provide an answer to this concern.  
Overall, and regardless of each specific marker and treatment, it could be said that: (1) IL-4 was actually 
properly loaded into the NPs because we can see significant differences when comparing samples treated 
with empty and IL-4-loaded NPs, (2) IL-4 keeps its bioactivity after release, because an actual effect is 
seen to be elicited compared to non-IL-4 treated controls and (3) the incubation time matters probably 
due to the slow-release exerted by the polymeric NPs, that seems to enhance IL-4 effects.

CONCLUSIONS and FUTURE RESEARCH 
In recent years, unresolved inflammation has gained recognition as an underlying contributor to virtually 
every chronic disease. In atherosclerosis, an unbalanced macrophage polarization within the lesional site 
has been defined to play a key role in delaying or even impeding the completion of the inflammation 
resolution and the way back to tissue homeostasis. Therefore, the biomedical field has placed the focus 
on the development of therapeutics in the shape of endogenous biological mediators of inflammation 
aiming at assisting the immune system to complete the natural resolution process [5]. The intravenous 
injection of these therapeutics is hindered by rapid circulation clearance and untargeted delivery, leading 
to unwanted side effects [6]. By means of their incorporation into a nanosystem, some of these hurdles 
can be bypassed and a targeted, protected and controlled-release delivery be achieved. 
Here we present the development and in vitro characterization of a PLGA-PLA polymeric-based NP 
delivery system loaded with the pro-resolving mediator IL-4. We demonstrate that its administration 
successfully triggers the macrophage phenotypical shift from a settled inflammatory profile towards an 
anti-inflammatory M2a one, and potentiates the free cytokine actions by a slow and controlled-mediated 
release. This will hopefully translate in vivo to increased efferocytosis, reduced necrotic core area, an 
improvement in cap thickness, and, their combination, in a gain of plaque stability and a contribution to 
inflammation resolution, as observed with delivery of IL-10 NPs [21]. 
The produced IL-4 loaded particles gathered the appropriate characteristics, in terms of size and charge, 
to optimally make use of the EPR effect as the passive targeting strategy and guarantee a sufficient 
circulation time to be effective. However, the NP formulation should be further optimized by trying other 
polymer concentrations and combinations, and further characterized, to ensure optimal conditions and 
complete drug release, which could not be achieved herein. The incorporation of a targeting ligand to 
the atherosclerotic plaque should also be matter of future studies, thereby adding an extra degree of 
specificity to the nanosystem and providing the NP with higher chances of reaching and getting retained 
within the atherosclerotic plaque. 
Besides the NP formulation, the cell differentiation and treatment strategy can be further optimized by 
using additional or different combinations of M1 and M2a stimuli, as well as increased incubation times 
to evaluate long-term effects. The mixture of IFNγ and LPS has been reported as a more powerful M1 
differentiation stimuli. At the same time, LPS is not an endogenous trigger found in the evaluated 
condition in vivo and the real trigger of the disease is oxidized LDL. The use of 7-ketocholesterol, a 
molecule closely resembling cholesterol, has also been reported for similar studies and could be a more 
accurate stimuli to trigger the desired inflammatory condition aiming at mimicking the real atherosclerotic 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
plaque [87]. In turn, for M2a polarization, IL-10 and IL-13 also constitute strong stimuli, thus any 
combination with IL-4 could be a matter of investigation to see if observed IL-4 effect is further enhanced. 
Also regarding the treatment setup, within the current project we pursued what could be called a 
therapeutic scenario, in which the pathological condition was settled by LPS stimulation of the cells and 
the therapeutical treatment with IL-4 came later, to act on top of it. We also conducted the simultaneous 
incubation of both M1 and M2a stimuli (data not shown) but discarded considering not to accurately 
resemble the real condition. Nevertheless, a third scenario could be considered, and this is the preventive 
one, by which IL-4 treatment could be the first delivered stimuli, followed by the pro-inflammatory LPS 
trigger. In reality, this would capture a situation in which IL-4 would be given as a prophylactic strategy 
used prior to disease development. 
An additional thing to bear in mind is that the whole characterization evaluation was done in vitro, which 
leads us to be making two different assumptions. First, we assume that the phenotypical shift observed 
in vitro and in cells from mice is going to be resembled in vivo and in humans. In regards to that, it should 
be outlined that the plethora of factors that regulate macrophage activation in vivo is far away from the 
simplified strategy employed herein [73], which could highly influence the outcomes. Furthermore, and 
as mentioned previously, the heterogeneity of the macrophage population within the plaques is high and 
many more phenotypes besides the studied M1 and M2a are present, together with the fact that there 
are other cells in the plaque, such as T cells, smooth muscle cells or endothelial cells, that can also be 
affected by the treatment or interfere somehow in the ongoing situation. Therefore, it is hard to ensure 
translatability. Second, since the project was narrowed down to the evaluation of the macrophage switch, 
the assumed translation of a successful M2a favored situation into an in vivo increased efferocytosis and 
reduction of the necrotic core, among others, could not be assessed, so it is not guaranteed. 
Regardless of all the limitations and the need for further research on the topic, this work is an encouraging 
step towards the clinical translation and implementation of targeted pro-resolving nanotherapeutics for 
inflammation resolution in atherosclerotic patients.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
6.1. Cell culture 
The RAW 264.7 mouse macrophage cell line was obtained from the American Type Culture Collection 
(ATCC). Cells were cultured in Dulbecco´s Modified Eagle´s Medium (DMEM) – high glucose (Sigma-
Aldrich, D5671), supplemented with 10% inactivated Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS), 1% penicillin-streptomycin, 
as the antibiotic source to avoid contamination, and 1 mM sodium pyruvate (Sigma-Aldrich, TMS-005-
C), as additional carbon source. Macrophages were grown in 75 cm2 cell culture flasks and maintained in 
a cell culture incubator at 37°C and 5% CO2. Medium was changed every second day and cell sub-culture 
performed by scrapping when cells were 70-80% confluent.  
6.2. Formulation and characterization of IL-4 loaded NPs 
All required polymers were commercially purchased. For each NP, both NH2-PLGA-NH2 and PDLA-PEG-
COOH polymers (50 and 49,7 wt/wt % of total polymer mass) were pre-dissolved in 500 μL the organic 
solvent N,N-Dimethylformamide (DMF), vortexed, and sonicated for optimal mixing, and filtered through 
0.45 μm syringe filters (Millipore, SLHP033NB) with total polymer concentration of 6 mg/mL. Recombinant 
mouse IL-4 (R&D Systems, 404 ML) (0, 33 wt/wt % of total polymer mass) was dissolved in 100 µL of 
deionized water) and mixed by gentle pipetting together with the DMF solvent phase (containing the 
polymer blends). Nanoparticles were generated by adding the resulting solution drop by drop to 5 mL 
of the non-solvent water phase whilst stirring at room temperature. After nanoprecipitation, the NPs were 
collected and concentrated by centrifugation at 3000 g and 4°C for 20 min using Amicon Ultra-15 
centrifugal filter units (MWCO 50 kDa, Sigma-Aldrich, Z648000-8EA), washed with 5 mL PBS (3 x) re-
suspended in 1 mL of PBS and stored at −80 °C until use.  
Following synthesis and purification, IL-4 loaded NPs were characterized for their physicochemical 
properties. The specific read-out parameters addressed were hydrodynamic NP size, PDI and NP size, 
measured by means of dynamic-light scattering (DLS) using a ZetaPALS potential analyzer (Brookhaven 
Instruments Corporation, Holtsville, New York). Other parameters measured were NP surface charge 
(Zeta Potential, ZP) and the total percentage of IL-4 encapsulation and loading into the NP. 
In order to calculate IL-4 encapsulation efficiency (% fraction of initial protein entrapped by the NPs) and 
the loading capacity (% mass fraction of total protein in the polymeric NPs, wt/wt % of total polymer 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
mass) of the produced NPs, the multiple flow-through from initial filtration and all subsequent washes 
were collected together, lyophilized, and further IL-4 quantitated using the mouse DuoSet IL-4 ELISA kit 
and protocol (R&D Systems, cat no: DY404). IL-4 was used at a 0.33% nominal loading of total polymer 
weight, as aforementioned. 
For size and charge measurements, 200 µL of the resulting NP solution were then diluted in PBS to a 
final volume of 1.7 mL (8.5-fold dilution), and nanoparticle size and surface charge measured using DLS.  
To analyze the release profile of the IL-4 NPs, 0.5 mL of the protein-loaded NPs solution were incubated 
at 37°C. At each desired time point (0, 4, 8, 24, 48, 72 and 96h), the protein released from NPs was 
isolated by filtering the solution using Amicon Ultra-0.5 centrifugal filters (MWCO 50 kDa) at 12,000 rpm 
for 10 min at 4 °C. The IL-4 NPs were re-suspended in PBS and incubated in the same manner until the 
last defined time point. Filtrates were kept at -20°C until analysis and the amount of released protein at 
each time point was measured at 450 nm using the mouse DuoSet IL-10 ELISA kit and protocol (R&D 
Systems, DY417).  
 
6.3. Cell differentiation treatment  
RAW 264.7 cells were seeded into smaller containers (6-well plates when conducting ELISA, cytokine 
array, RT-qPCR or Western Blot as evaluation methods, and T25 cm2 flasks when performing FACS) at a 
final concentration of 5x105 cells per well/flask in 3/4 mL of medium and allowed to grow to 70-80% 
confluence (around 2 days). Next, cells were pretreated with 100 ng/mL LPS (Sigma-Aldrich, L2654) for 4 
hours at 37°C. The LPS medium was then removed and replaced with medium containing recombinant 
mouse IL-4 (R&D Systems, 404 ML) at different doses (10, 20, 50 or 100 ng/mL) or IL-4 loaded NPs, 
depending on the specific setup of the experiment, followed by additional 24h incubation. Untreated 
cells, cells treated with no LPS and with LPS but without IL-4 or IL-4 loaded NPs were included as controls 
and were treated with just medium instead in the corresponding step. Following treatment, culture plates 
were finally centrifuged at 300G for 10 min at 4°C to separate supernatant from cells.  
 
6.4. Quantification of secreted IL-10, IL-4 and IL-6 by ELISA 
Murine IL-10, IL-4 and IL-6 secretion in culture supernatants was assayed using regular sandwich ELISA 
by means of three commercially available ELISA kits obtained from R&D Systems (DY417, DY404 and 
==PAGE:75==

DY206, respectively) and following the manufacturer’s instructions. Supernatants were preferably used 
straight after separation, or kept at -20°C until analysis instead.  
Plates were coated with 50µl of capture antibody diluted at the defined working dilution, sealed and 
incubated overnight at 4°C. The following day, plates were washed 3 times in Wash Buffer (PBS 0.05% 
Tween20) by inverting and blotting against clean paper towels, and blocked by incubating with 150 µl of 
Reagent diluent (PBS 1% BSA) for 2 hours at RT. The wash step was repeated followed by standard and 
sample addition to the respective wells. The plates were covered with an adhesive strip and incubated 
O/N at 4°C. Next, the plates were washed again as described and incubated for 2 hours with 50 µl of 
detection antibody diluted to the predefined working concentration. Plates were washed once more, 
followed by addition of 50 µl of the working dilution of Streptavidin-HRP (horseradish-peroxidase) and 
20 minutes incubation protected from light. The last wash step was conducted previous to the enzyme 
Substrate Solution addition. 50 µl of peroxide-TMB was added to each well and left reacting until color 
development (~10 minutes). The reaction was stopped by addition of 50 µl of Stop Solution (2N H2SO4). 
The optical density of each well was determined using the microplate reader Victor3 set to 450 nm and 
corrected for optical imperfections by subtraction of the readings at 570 nm. The standard curve 
developed following the given protocol allowed for the conversion of the experimental absorbance 
values into cytokine concentration in the tested samples. Each sample was measured in triplicates and 
both duplicates for each sample averaged to get the final value. 
6.5. Cytokine array

detection antibody cocktail to achieve binding of the complexes to its specific capture antibody. Following 
3 washes, membranes were incubated shaking with Streptavidin-HRP for 30 minutes at RT, washed again 
and mixed with the enzymatic substrates in order to produce a chemiluminescence signal proportional 
to the amount of analyte bound.

(3×3mL) and harvested by scraping in 1 mL of cold PBS and transferring into Eppendorf tubes, followed 
by centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 5 min at 4 °C. For protein extraction, supernatant was removed and 
the cell pellet was resuspended and lysed in 180 µL of protein extraction reagent (Thermo Fisher, 78501) 
supplemented with 10 µL of protease inhibitor cocktail and 10 µL of phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Sigma-
Aldrich, P8349 and P5726, respectively) to a final volume of 200 µL, and then centrifuged at 12,000 rpm 
for 10 min at 4°C. Protein extracts were kept at the -80ºC until analysis. 30 µL of the protein solution was 
mixed with 10 µL of 4× Laemmli sample buﬀer (from Bio-Rad, and supplemented with mercaptoethanol 
in a 1:10 ratio), and the mixture was boiled for 7 min at 95°C using the PCR thermocycler. The protein 
solutions, together with the protein ladder (Bio Rad, 1610375), were loaded and separated by SDS-PAGE 
using Novex 4−12% Tris-Glycine mini gels (Thermo Fisher, XV04125PK20) in cold 1x Tris-Glis-SDS (TGS) 
buffer (Bio Rad, 1610732) by running the gel 30 minutes at 60V, followed by 90 minutes at 115V. Separated 
proteins were subsequently electro-transferred onto an Immuno-Blot Low Fluorescence Polyvinylidene 
diﬂuoride (PVDF) membrane (Bio-Rad, 1620260), pre-treated with methanol. The transfer was run in 20% 
methanol Tris-Glycine buffer (Bio-Rad, 1610734) for 90 minutes at 30V. Next, the membranes were 
washed with filtered tri-sec-buﬀered saline 0.1% Tween 20 (TBST, Bio-Rad, 1706435) and blocked with 1% 
BSA TBST for 2 h at 4°C on a shaker. The primary antibodies, β-actin, as a reference protein, and pSTAT6 
(Cell Signaling, 8457 and 9361, respectively) were both used at the recommended dilution (1:1000) in 1% 
BSA TBST and incubated with the membranes overnight at 4 °C on a shaker. After that, the membranes 
were washed with TBST solution for 10 minutes on a shaker and then incubated with secondary antibody 
anti-rabbit IgG HRP-linked (Cell Signaling, 7074) at the recommended dilution 1:2000 in TBST with 1% 
BSA for 1 h at room temperature on a shaker. Finally, the membranes were washed with TBST solution 
three times for 5 minutes and visualized using the SuperSignal West Femto Maximum Sensitivity Substrate 
(Thermo Fisher, 34095) and chemiluminescence detection in Li-Cor Odyssey Fc. pSTAT6 levels were 
normalized to β-actin for each specific sample.

6.8. FACS 
Following the cell differentiation treatment (section 6.3) in T25 flasks, cells were washed in 4 mL PBS (2x), 
harvested in 5 mL of media by scrapping and transferred to 15 mL falcon tubes. Next, cells were spun 
down at 1500 rpm and 4 ºC for 5 minutes, supernatant discarded and pellet resuspended in FACS buffer 
(PBS with 10% FBS and 0.1% sodium azide). The obtained cell suspension for the different samples was 
transferred to a round bottom 96-well plate aiming a concentration of 1x106 cells/well in as many wells 
as required for the different intended staining treatments. Plates were spun down at 400G rpm and 4 ºC 
for 5 minutes, supernatant removed and cells resuspended and incubated in 50 µL of Purified Rat Anti-
Mouse CD16/CD32 (Mouse BD Fc BlockTM) (BD PharmigenTM, 553141) in FACS buffer at a 1:40 ratio on ice 
for 10 min. Without removal of the Fc blocker, cells were then stained by addition of 50 µL of the 
respective primary antibody solution (in FACS buffer) and incubation for 30 minutes at 4 ºC in the dark. 
The experimental setup included the following staining treatments for each of the investigated samples: 
unstained, single stains (only the specific antibody) and FMO (all but the specific antibody) controls for 
each of the primary antibodies and fully stained (all the antibodies). The investigated antibody markers 
included: anti-mouse CD80-PE antibody (0.2 µg/106 cells, NordicBioSite, LS-C5587-100) and anti-mouse 
CD206-Alexa Fluor 647 antibody (1:200, NordicBioSite, 141712), with the conjugated fluorochromes 
selected to exhibit low spectral overlap between them. All of them were used at the recommended 
manufacturer’s dilution. Another antibody was included in the panel and investigated by FACS (anti-
mouse CD163- Alexa Fluor 488). Its expression outcomes, though, are not shown in the results section 
and its use is not even considered in the experimental strategy, due to findings late in the project 
describing this marker as a non-optimal one for evaluation of our case study. However, the use of a panel 
of three antibodies is what explains the use of single stains and FMOs for the proper definition of positive 
and negative signals. Next, plates were spun down at 400G and 4 ºC for 5 minutes and pellets washed 3 
times in 100 µL of PBS by centrifuging and pouring supernatant. After the last wash step centrifugation, 
cells were reconstituted in  100 µL of FACS buffer and transferred to FACS tubes already filled with 200 
µL of FACS buffer, to obtain a final volume of 300 µL, the minimal required by the FACS analytical 
equipment. Sample FACS analysis was performed in a Gallios flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter). 
Flow cytometry data was analyzed in the FlowJo software version 10.2. Acquired data was gated according 
to FMO, single stain, and unstained controls, those providing the required signal thresholds to define 
positive and negative events accurately. In line with that, and by plotting Side Scatter-Area (SSC-A, 
providing information about the internal cell complexity, i.e. granularity) vs. Forward Scatter-Area (FSC-

To get a better understanding of how the RT-qPCR data analysis was conducted, a brief conceptual 
introduction to RT-qPCR principle is herein presented. qPCR technique enables, contrary to standard PCR 
(which gives information about the final product), reliable detection and measurement of amplified 
product as the reaction progresses, with product quantification after each cycle. This is achieved by the 
inclusion of a fluorescent reporter molecule that yields increased fluorescence with an increasing amount 
of amplified product, allowing for detection and monitoring of the ongoing process. The selected 
detection method herein is SYBR Green. This dye only binds and fluoresces when bound to double 
stranded DNA (dsDNA), thereby emitting a signal proportional to the amount of amplified product. What 
is interesting about this technique is that, by following the process, you can get information from the 
initial amount of product in the sample. 
 When the starting material is RNA, an extra step previous to DNA amplification is included, the reverse 
transcription of RNA into DNA that DNA polymerase (enzyme in charge of DNA replication for 
amplification) can use as a template. This method is called RT-qPCR. Step-wise, the isolated RNA 
population is converted to cDNA (DNA copy of RNA) by reverse transcription (RT), and then the cDNA is 
amplified by the standard polymerase chain reaction (PCR) (Figure 48). This can be done in a one-step 
reaction (including all the required enzymes in a single reaction) or in a double step reaction, in which 
RNA reverse transcription constitutes the first step, followed by the amplification one. The kit employed 
in this thesis is a one-step reaction kit.

RT-qPCR data analysis was performed with the software Opticon Monitor 3.1, which provides as output 
the detected amount of fluorescence along with reaction cycles for each sample. Below, the procedure 
followed to perform the analysis is explained with data from one of the experimental samples included 
in the report.  
All PCR reactions include an initial exponential phase, in which, if efficiency is high, product is doubled in 
each cycle. A linear phase follows in which reaction slows down reaching a last phase, called plateau 
phase, in which no more product is accumulated and the amount remains constant. The stated 
exponential phase is the region of the graph representing fluorescence vs cycles where data can be 
assessed with reliability. When this fluorescence signal is converted to the log scale, the exponential phase 
becomes a straight line, optimal for analysis (step 1, Figure 49). Falling within the straight region of the 
graph, a threshold is manually set, by which Ct for each specific sample is defined (step 2). Ct (green) 
corresponds to the amount of cycles necessary to reach the amount of fluorescence set as the threshold.  
 
Figure 49 Plot of log fluorescence vs Number of cycles. The dashed line represents the threshold, and circled, the different Ct (in 
green) for the different RNA dilutions 
The Ct of a reaction is determined mainly by the amount of template present at the start of the 
amplification. So, large Ct values imply few amplification cycles are required to accumulate a specific 
fluorescence signal (set as the threshold), meaning a high initial amount of product is present. 
This way, by preparing a series dilution for each RNA sample, a relationship between Ct and [RNA] can 
be traced for each sample by plotting Ct vs log [RNA] (step 3, Figure 50). From this, real efficiency (blue) 
of the reaction can be calculated and Ct values provided by the software can be adjusted (giving Ctadj 
values) by the real efficiency value, using the formulas shown below (step 4).

Finally, a melting curve analysis, by plotting fluorescence vs temperature (step 6, Figure 51), allows to 
assess whether the qPCR assay has produced a single specific product (translated into a unique peak), or 
the presence of any contamination or primer dimer (other peaks). The temperature at which the peak

double stranded DNA. Since Tm is specific for each product, this analysis allows for identification.
