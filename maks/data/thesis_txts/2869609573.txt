Executive Summary 1
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a growing global health concern, particularly in countries
with widespread antibiotic use in livestock, human medicine, and environmental areas. This
study investigates the prevalence and patterns of AMR in Bangladesh by analyzing resistance in
Escherichia coli (E. coli) isolated from animals, hospitalized humans, food, and the environment.
The main objective is to understand how resistance varies between sources and to compare these
findings with data from Denmark, where antibiotic regulations are strictly maintained.
By
identifying key resistance trends, this study aims to provide insights that can inform policies to
mitigate AMR risks in Bangladesh.
To achieve this, data were collected from previously published studies in Bangladesh, covering
antimicrobial resistance in E. coli bacterial isolates. We focused on resistance proportions for key
antibiotics, including amoxicillin, tetracycline, sulphamethoxazole, nitrofurantoin, ciprofloxacin,
colistin, levofloxacin, erythromycin, and ampicillin.
Binomial tests and Wilcoxon rank-sum
tests were applied to compare resistance levels between different sources. Principal Component
Analysis (PCA) was conducted to visualize clustering patterns among antibiotic resistance
profiles, while correlation analyses identified potential co-resistance relationships.
The results indicate that resistance levels are significantly higher in Bangladesh compared to
Denmark, particularly in broiler chicken samples, where resistance to tetracycline reached 86% in
Bangladesh versus only 11% in Denmark. Bangladesh hospitals and poultry farms emerged as key
AMR hotspots, with high resistance to beta-lactams. The PCA results showed distinct clustering
patterns, suggesting that certain antibiotics, such as beta-lactams and tetracycline, frequently co-
occur in resistance profiles. The correlation analysis highlighted strong links between amoxicillin
and tetracyclines resistance, supporting the hypothesis that resistance genes are often carried
together.
The discussion further explored these findings, linking high resistance levels to antibiotic usage
practices.
The comparison between Bangladesh and Denmark shows the impact of banning
non-therapeutic antibiotic use in livestock, have led to significantly lower resistance rates in
Page 1 of 56
==PAGE:6==
Denmark. This emphasizing the role of supervising of antibiotic use in Bangladesh to reduce
AMR resistance.
Additionally, source-specific differences highlighted the interrelationship of
AMR across human, animal, and the environmental sectors in Bangladesh.
The study also
acknowledged limitations, including reliance on secondary data, variability in sample sizes, and
the lack of direct genetic analysis of resistance mechanisms.
This research suggests the urgent need for strict antibiotic regulations in Bangladesh to fight
rising resistance levels.
The findings support the adoption of policies that promote strong
limitation of antibiotic use in agriculture and human medicine while improving surveillance
and monitoring systems. Future research should focus on a more comprehensive and targeted
approach by incorporating diverse sample sources beyond just poultry because there is a huge
lack of data of other livestock, like-cattle and fish, which also greatly impact on AMR sectors
in Bangladesh. Addressing AMR in Bangladesh requires a collaborative effort across multiple
sectors. It is important to maintain strict prescription regulations to prevent overuse and misuse
of antibiotics when purchasing antibiotics from pharmacies. Additionally, there should be regular
monitoring of antibiotic use in agriculture and livestock sectors.
Overall, increasing public
awareness about waste disposal management and adverse effects of high AMR resistance is
essential for improving the AMR situation in Bangladesh.

Introduction 2
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has appeared as one of the most concerning global health
challenges, particularly in countries like Bangladesh, where the excessive and often unchecked use
of antimicrobials in livestock production exacerbates the issue (Lucas et al., 2019). Antimicrobial
use (AMU) in the livestock sector including cattle, poultry, and fish plays a critical role in the
development of resistant bacterial strains, posing a significant threat not only to animal health
but also to human and environmental health (Chowdhury et al., 2021). The frequent use of
antibiotics, such as fluoroquinolones, sulfonamides, and tetracyclines in poultry (Islam et al.,
2023), and oxytetracycline and ciprofloxacin in aquaculture (Chowdhury et al., 2022), accelerates
the increase occurrence of AMR (Chowdhury et al., 2022) (Chowdhury et al., 2021).
The core problem is that continued excessive AMU in livestock’s driving the rapid spread of AMR,
particularly through the overuse and misuse of antibiotics in livestock and aquaculture, which
threatens not only animal productivity but also public health and food availability and safety
(Chowdhury et al., 2021) (Hosain et al., 2022). Antimicrobial resistance threatens public health
by making infections caused by resistant bacteria harder to treat, which can lead to untreatable
infections in humans. Resistant bacteria from livestock can spread to humans through the food
chain, direct contact, or the environment. This makes it difficult to treat common infections and
leads to longer hospital stays, higher medical costs, and increased mortality rates (Almansour
et al., 2023; de Mesquita Souza Saraiva et al., 2022). Stakeholders including industry leaders,
society, and government bodies are beginning to recognize the severity of this issue. However,
the current efforts to curb AMU and control AMR have proven insufficient (Chowdhury et al.,
2021).
Through a comprehensive analysis of existing data, this research will explore both past and
current solutions, reviewing the effectiveness of practical and scientific methods to date.
Specifically, the research will involve a systematic review of quantitative data related to
antimicrobial use in livestock and poultry, as well as the occurrence of antimicrobial resistance
in animals, humans, food and the environment. This review will form the basis for formulating
Page 3 of 56
==PAGE:8==
hypotheses on AMU reduction in treating animals and humans and its impact on AMR in
Bangladesh. Additionally, the research will offer a comparative analysis between Bangladesh
and Denmark to understand the differences in AMR trends in a cross-country perspective on
resistance management. The findings will hopefully also provide better insight into how AMR is
distributed among animals, humans, food and the environment in Bangladesh.

3.1
Study Design
Figure 3.1. The figure illustrates the link between antimicrobial use (AMU) in livestock, poultry, and
fish and the spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) across animals, humans, food, and the environment.
AMU in food-producing animals contributes to the development and transmission of AMR, which can
spread through food consumption, direct contact, and environmental contamination.
This study is designed to investigate the pathways among antimicrobial use (AMU) in livestock
(poultry), contributing to antimicrobial resistance (AMR) spread across animals, humans, and
the environment in Bangladesh. The main goal is to understand how antibiotic resistance differs
between animals, humans, food, and the environment in Bangladesh. The study also compares
these findings with Denmark, where antibiotics are more strictly controlled, to see how different
rules affect resistance in each country.
In Bangladesh, antibiotics are frequently used in livestock for growth promotion and disease

prevention, particularly in poultry and cattle (Hosain et al., 2021). When antimicrobials are
overused or misused, bacteria can adapt, survive and develop resistance to commonly used
antibiotics (Murray et al., 2022). The transmission of AMR occurs through several interconnected
pathway-like resistant bacteria from animals can reach humans through direct contact with
animals or indirectly through the food chain when people consume animal products contaminated
with resistant strains (Graham et al., 2019).
The study will systematically track the occurrence of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in E. coli
across animals, humans, food, and the environment by collecting and analyzing quantitative

This data includes sample types,

patterns, we aim to identify trends and hotspots of AMR. Statistical methods will be used
to estimate confidence intervals and compare AMR prevalence across different sources and

Additionally, this structured approach allows for a comparison between Bangladesh

The findings will contribute to a better understanding of AMR distribution and inform future
research and surveillance efforts.
3.2
Literature Search
The literature review for this study was conducted using Google, Google Scholar, and the
Bangladesh Journal of Medicine, to ensure a comprehensive and relevant data collection on
antimicrobial use (AMU) and antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in Bangladesh.
To capture a
broad range of perspectives, a systematic approach was used with tailored search strings to
target specific sectors: livestock, poultry, fish, human health, food safety, and environmental
sources(sewage). The searches were confined to the period from 2014 to 2024 to capture recent
developments and insights.
The first search focused on AMR in livestock, poultry, and fish with the search string,
“(antimicrobial resistance or AMR) and (poultry or chicken or livestock or fish or cattle) and
Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli) not older than 2014,” yielding 31 results on Google
and 421 results on Google Scholar. For AMR in humans, we used “(antimicrobial resistance
or AMR) and (human) and Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli) not older than 2014,”
resulting in 57 hits on Google and 2,820 on Google Scholar. AMR in food was explored using

“(antimicrobial resistance or AMR) and (food) and Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli)
not older than 2014,” with 49 results on Google and 2,820 on Google Scholar. Additionally, we
examined AMR in sewage and environmental sources with “(antimicrobial resistance or AMR)
and (sewage or environment) and Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli) not older than
2014,” producing 47 results on Google and 1,770 on Google Scholar.
The review also included AMU data, starting with “(antimicrobial usage or AMU) and (animal
feed or poultry or livestock or food) and Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli) not older
than 2014” to gather information on AMU in livestock, poultry, and fish, which yielded 16 results

usage or AMU) and (human) and Bangladesh and (E. coli or Escherichia coli) not older than
2014” returned 26 results on Google and 686 on Google Scholar. In addition to these sources, the
Bangladesh Journal of Medicine provided six relevant studies under the search term “antibiotic
resistance.”
Each search was systematically conducted between 25 August 2024 and 24 October 2024, in
different dates: 4th September 2024, 10th September 2024, 13th September 2024, 18th September
2024, and 25th September 2024, 15 October 2024, and 24th October 2024, with a careful review
of the top results to ensure that the data aligned with the study’s objectives. By focusing on
studies that discussed AMR impacts across livestock, human health, food, and environmental
reservoirs in Bangladesh, this structured approach allowed us to compile a transparent and
relevant data set. The precise methodology applied in our literature review ensures that the
data collected addresses AMU and AMR’s most significant aspects across animal, food, human,
and environmental dimensions, aligning comprehensively with the goals of this study.
3.3
Literature Review and Selection
After collecting papers from the initial search, we began by sorting them into two main
categories: antimicrobial resistance (AMR) and antimicrobial use (AMU). Within each category,
we identified whether the studies were qualitative or quantitative. For AMR-focused papers, we
then organized them into specific groups based on the study’s focus, AMR in animals, AMR in
humans, AMR in food sources, and AMR in the environment. To streamline this process, we set
up an Excel table with columns for sample type, source of sample, sample number, test, result,
research type, and references. This approach allowed us to quickly review each paper’s relevance
by noting details like the type and source of samples tested, test methods, results, and research

Once the papers were organized and screened, we refined our selection further, focusing
exclusively on quantitative studies, as these align with our primary objective to conduct a
quantitative analysis. By narrowing our selection to studies with measurable data, we ensured
that our review would support a robust analysis of AMR and AMU patterns. This multi-tiered
sorting process allowed us to efficiently filter through the literature, ultimately identifying those
studies that are best suited to contribute to a comprehensive quantitative assessment of AMR
and AMU across animal, food, human, and environmental sectors in Bangladesh.
3.4
Data Extraction
The data extraction process aimed to gather consistent information on antimicrobial resistance
(AMR) in Bangladesh, focusing specifically on E. coli.
After selecting relevant studies, we
organized the data in an Excel sheet to keep track of key details. Each study was categorized
by its source, animals, food, human and environment and further specified into subcategories-
broiler and layer chickens, beef, milk, water, commercial poultry farms, dairy farms, wet markets,
supermarkets, hospitals, diagnostic labs, We also noted the sample types- fecal, intestinal
samples, meat, muscle samples, liver samples, eggs, milk, vegetables, sewage, river water, coastal
water, urine, blood, floor, soil, and recorded the year the data was collected to observe changes
over time.
For each study, we documented the total number of samples tested and how many of those tested
positive for E. coli. We then focused on recording resistance levels to specific antibiotics, including
amoxicillin, tetracycline, sulfamethoxazole, nitrofurantoin, ciprofloxacin, colistin, levofloxacin,
erythromycin, and ampicillin. Additionally, we noted instances of multidrug resistance (MDR),
where E. coli showed resistance to multiple antibiotics, highlighting the more complex resistance
cases. Finally, references were included to cite each study accurately and enable easy cross-
referencing and verification during the analysis.
During data extraction, priority was given to studies with quantitative results, as our research
relies on measurable data to analyze trends in AMR across animals, food, human and
environment sectors in Bangladesh. In cases where studies provided a mix of qualitative and
quantitative data, only the quantitative findings were extracted to maintain consistency with
the study’s objectives. This approach allowed us to build a comprehensive dataset focused on

3.5
Data Management
The quantitative data collected from the literature review was prepared and analyzed in R
Studio to explore patterns in antimicrobial resistance (AMR) across animal, food, human, and
environmental sources in Bangladesh. The process started by importing the extracted data into
R Studio, where it was cleaned and structured for analysis. Data cleaning included standardizing
variable names, handling missing values, and converting data types to ensure consistency.
Once the data was organized, we applied statistical methods to examine resistance levels for
specific antibiotics and identify trends across different sample sources. This analysis involved
calculating the proportion of positive samples to quantify AMR levels in each sector. Additional
statistical techniques were used to assess the significance of resistance patterns and to explore
correlations between antimicrobial use (AMU) in livestock and anti-microbial resistance (AMR)
in other sources.
This structured approach allowed us to systematically manage, visualize, and analyze the
quantitative data, laying the groundwork for a deeper understanding of AMR transmission
pathways in different sources, which will be further detailed in the Data Analysis and Results
sections.
3.6
Overview of Data Preparation
The first step in the analysis involved cleaning and organizing the data to ensure consistency and
reliability. Depending on the relevance and context of antibiotic resistance levels were addressed
by either imputing data or removing incomplete entries to manage the missing values in critical
variables. Resistance proportions were calculated by dividing the number of resistant samples
for each antibiotic by the total number of samples tested. These calculations provided metrics
for individual antibiotics- resistance proportions for amoxicillin, tetracycline, ciprofloxacin,
ampicillin, erythromycin, levofloxacin, colistin, sulfamethoxazole and nitrofurantoin, which were

Descriptive Analysis

3.8
Statistic Analysis
Inferential analysis was conducted to identify statistically significant patterns and relationships
in the data. Non-parametric Wilcoxon tests were used to compare resistance proportions between
different pairs of sources, including animals, food, humans, and the environment. These tests
were selected because they do not require the assumption of normality, making them well-suited
to the dataset.
The analysis focused on resistance to key antibiotics, including amoxicillin and erythromycin, by

in animal samples differed significantly from those in human samples. A summary table of p-

Logistic regression models were applied to measure the relationships between resistance

Weighted regression was implemented to account for differences in

Discussions 5
This discussion examines the study’s key findings, including antibiotic resistance patterns across
animals, humans, food and environment in Bangladesh, high-risk sites, and differences between
sources and countries. The implications for addressing antimicrobial resistance are also explored:
• Correlations between resistance to antibiotics, like amoxicillin and tetracyclines.
• High resistance levels in broiler chickens and hospitals.
• Patterns from PCA, including source-specific clustering and unique levofloxacin resistance.
• Differences in resistance between Denmark and Bangladesh.
• Source-specific variations in resistance among animals, humans, food, and the environment.
This study highlights significant patterns in AMR across various sources. A key observation is the
strong correlation between resistance to certain antibiotics, such as amoxicillin and tetracyclines.
This can be attributed to two possible factors: (1) these antibiotics are often co-administered,
leading to bacteria developing resistance to both, or (2) resistance genes for these antibiotics are
frequently located on the same mobile genetic elements, such as plasmids. Referring to ASuT
(Ampicillin, Sulphamethoxazole, and Tetracycline) is relevant here, as this marker is commonly
used to identify multidrug-resistant E. coli strains.
In this study, the high resistance levels
in broiler chickens to ampicillin (81%), sulphamethoxazole (77%), and tetracycline (86%) in
Bangladesh suggest the presence of ASuT profiles, highlighting the co-selection of resistance
genes.
These findings align with previous research indicating that co-resistance to amoxicillin and
tetracycline can serve as an efficient indicator of multidrug resistance in E. coli isolates from
animals. Given the widespread use of penicillin and tetracyclines in animal health, monitoring
this co-resistance can be a practical approach to approximating multidrug resistance levels
(Bourély et al., 2019).
Broiler chickens and hospitals were identified as critical hotspots for AMR in Bangladesh. In
broiler chickens, resistance proportions for tetracycline and ciprofloxacin were alarmingly high
in Bangladesh. This reflects the widespread, unregulated use of antibiotics in poultry farming,
Page 40 of 56
==PAGE:45==
often as growth promoters or for disease prevention. On the other hand, hospitals exhibited
consistently high antibiotic resistance levels to beta-lactams, possibly due to the intense use of
antibiotics for treating infections.
These results are consistent with studies from Bangladesh, where a high prevalence of
ciprofloxacin-resistant E. coli has been detected in broiler chickens, farm environments, and
hospitalized human patients. The prevalence of ciprofloxacin-resistant E. coli in broiler chickens
was reported at 77.6%, indicating significant antimicrobial use in poultry farming (Das et al.,
2023).
The PCA results revealed clear patterns of clustering for antibiotics like erythromycin,
tetracycline, beta-lactam, and sulphamethoxazole, which frequently appeared together in
resistance profiles.
However, levofloxacin resistance exhibited distinct behavior, indicating it
may be influenced by other factors, such as its unique mechanism of action or differing patterns
of use.
The clustering of resistance profiles suggests common resistance mechanisms. In contrast, the
distinct behavior of levofloxacin may be explained by its specific resistance mechanisms, such as
mutations in DNA gyrase rather than plasmid-mediated resistance (Poirel et al., 2018).
Comparing resistance between Bangladesh and Denmark revealed stark differences. In broiler
chicken samples, Bangladesh showed high resistance across all tested antibiotics, while Denmark
displayed much lower levels, such as tetracycline resistance at 11% compared to 86% in
Bangladesh. These differences highlight the impact of stricter regulatory frameworks and better
antibiotic stewardship in Denmark (Sofia et al., 2023).
For human samples, resistance to
amoxicillin and ciprofloxacin was significantly higher in Bangladesh, whereas Denmark showed
slightly higher resistance to ampicillin. These findings underscore the importance of regulation
and monitoring in reducing resistance levels across sectors in Bangladesh.
The sharp contrast between Denmark and Bangladesh supports previous research showing that
strict antibiotic policies can reduce AMR in food production. In contrast, Bangladesh lacks
strong enforcement of antibiotic regulations, contributing to higher resistance levels (Imam et
al., 2020).
Finally, there were notable differences in resistance patterns among the sources.
Similar
resistance profiles were observed between animal and human samples, suggesting possible
transmission pathways for resistant bacteria. However, food and environmental samples showed
Page 41 of 56
==PAGE:46==
greater variability in resistance proportions, reflecting differing antibiotic exposure levels.
Environmental E. coli samples, in particular, exhibited distinct resistance patterns.
Limitations:
While this study provides valuable insights into antimicrobial resistance (AMR) across animals,
humans, food, and the environment in Bangladesh, certain limitations should be acknowledged.
First, the data used in the analysis were sourced from previously published studies, which may
have variations in methodology and data quality. This reliance on secondary data could introduce
inconsistencies, especially in the reported resistance proportions across different sources.
Second, the sample sizes for certain sources, such as environmental and food samples, were
smaller compared to animal and human samples and there is a lack of other livestock data such
as cattle and fish. This imbalance may limit the generalizability of the findings, particularly for
sources with lower data representation. Additionally, the geographic distribution of the data was
not uniform, with some regions of Bangladesh underrepresented in the data, potentially missing
localized resistance patterns.
Lastly, the comparative analysis with Denmark highlights the regulatory and environmental
differences but does not account for all contextual factors, such as socioeconomic or cultural
influences on antibiotic use. These factors, while critical, are not part of this study.

Conclusion 6
This study provides an image of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in animals, humans, food,
and the environment in Bangladesh, while also comparing resistance levels with Denmark. The
findings confirm that resistance to multiple antibiotics is widespread, with particularly high levels
in broiler chickens and hospitalized people.
The study highlights the close connection between resistance patterns in animals and humans.
The similar resistance proportions in these samples suggest that bacteria may be moving between
these sources, emphasizing the need for a coordinated approach to AMR control.
However,
food and environmental samples showed more variation, indicating that other factors, such as
contamination sources and antibiotic exposure levels, contribute to resistance patterns in these
areas.
A key observation from this study is the strong link between resistance to amoxicillin,
tetracyclines, and sulphamethoxazole.
This could be due to their frequent combined use or
because the resistance genes for these antibiotics are often found together on mobile genetic
elements. The high resistance levels seen in Bangladesh’s broiler chickens further suggest the
presence of ASuT resistance profiles, emphasizing the role of co-selection in spreading resistance.
Comparing Bangladesh with Denmark showed clear differences in resistance levels, particularly
in livestock. Resistance to multiple antibiotics was much lower in Denmark, reflecting the impact
of stricter regulations and better monitoring of antibiotic use. In contrast, the unregulated and
excessive use of antibiotics in Bangladesh, especially in poultry farming, appears to be a major
driver of resistance. While regulatory policies play a crucial role, other factors, such as differences
in healthcare systems and farming practices, likely contribute to these variations as well.
In conclusion, this study highlights the need for stronger antibiotic regulations, better monitoring,
and improved awareness to reduce the spread of resistance. Strengthening veterinary oversight,
promoting responsible antibiotic use in hospitals and farms, and expanding surveillance efforts
can help control AMR in Bangladesh. Learning from countries like Denmark, where banning
non-therapeutic use of antibiotics has successfully reduced resistance, could provide valuable

guidance for improving antibiotic use in Bangladesh. Since AMR is a global concern, tackling
it will require a coordinated effort across animals, humans, food and environment sectors to
prevent the further spread of resistant bacteria and protect public health in Bangladesh.
6.1
Policy Recommendations
To address the growing challenge of AMR in Bangladesh, this study highlights several areas
for action and further research. First, stricter regulations on antibiotic use in agriculture and
healthcare are urgently needed. Policies should focus on limiting the use of critical antibiotics,
such as tetracyclines and beta-lactams, and enforcing guidelines on antibiotic administration in
livestock and poultry farming.
Improved monitoring and surveillance systems are essential to track AMR trends across all
sectors. Integrating genetic analyses, such as sequencing for ASuT markers or mobile genetic
elements, can provide a clearer understanding of the mechanisms driving resistance and co-
resistance.
Cross-sectoral collaboration is vital to tackling AMR effectively.
Initiatives should involve
stakeholders from agriculture, healthcare, environmental management, and public health to
implement holistic strategies. Educational campaigns targeting farmers, healthcare providers,
and the general public can raise awareness about the dangers of antibiotic misuse and promote
responsible practices.
By implementing these recommendations, Bangladesh can make significant progress in controlling
AMR, protecting public health, and contributing to global efforts in combating this critical issue.

Future Research 7
Future research should adopt a more comprehensive and targeted approach by incorporating
diverse sample sources beyond just poultry. While previous studies in Bangladesh have primarily
focused on poultry, there is a significant lack of information regarding cattle and fish because
these animals also have a significant impact on AMR situation in Bangladesh. To gain a clearer
understanding of antibiotic resistance patterns in Bangladesh and their interconnections among
humans, animals, food, and the environment, future studies should collect data from a broader
range of food items, human subjects, and environmental sources.
This will ensure a more
holistic and precise analysis. Moreover, the quality of research can be significantly improved by
involving highly skilled researchers and utilizing modern laboratory equipment because advanced
technology and expertise will enhance accuracy, making the findings more reliable and impactful.
Securing increased funding is also essential to support large-scale data collection from various
sources, including farms, hospitals, industries, and domestic environments by involving more
manpower. Additionally, funding also helps to publish findings in reputable international journals
and to inform government officials and policymakers to protect human health while promoting
the sustainable use of antibiotics.
