Abstract 
This project investigates the frost resistance of concrete with ordinary Portland cement (CEM I). The frost 
resistance is evaluated using an accelerated freeze-thaw test, where concrete is exposed to a 3% NaCl 
solution during repeated freezing and thawing. Furthermore, changes in the amount of freezable water in 
mortar specimens exposed to a 3% NaCl solution during freezing and thawing cycles are investigated using 
low temperature calorimetry.  
The frost resistance is evaluated for concrete with a w/c ratio of 0.40 and 0.45 and three air content levels: 
Low, medium, and high. Additionally, the concrete is exposed to two different temperature cycles of 
freezing and thawing. The air temperature curves for these cycles are called LT and HT. LT heats to a 
maximum temperature of +5°C, and HT heats to a maximum temperature of +20°C. Both cycles cool to a 
minimum temperature of -20°C.  
Low temperature calorimetry is performed for mortar specimens with a w/c ratio of 0.40 and two levels of 
air content: Low and high. After low temperature calorimetry measurements, an IC analysis of the saline 
solution used as a freezing medium on the mortar specimens is performed.  
Results show that the frost resistance for concrete with a w/c ratio of 0.40 is better than for concrete with a 
w/c ratio of 0.45. Increasing air content also proved beneficial in the mitigation of frost damage. 
Results from specimens exposed to the LT curve show a tendency for increased scaling compared to 
specimens exposed to the HT curve. This is mainly attributed to the time below 0°C being longer for 
specimens exposed to the LT curve. However, other factors are also discussed.  
Results from low temperature calorimetry do not indicate changes in the amount of freezable water. 
However, it has been shown that fluctuations and measuring uncertainties are the same magnitudes as the 
possible changes in the amount of freezable water. IC analysis of the freezing medium showed that the 
chloride concentration decreases during cyclic freezing and thawing. However, the change in the amount of 
freezable water corresponding to the decrease in chloride concentration was small.

Resumé 
Dette projekt undersøger frostbestandigheden af beton med Portland cement (CEM I). 
Frostbestandigheden undersøges ved en accelereret fryse/tø-test, hvor beton udsættes for en 3% NaCl-
opløsning under gentagende frysning og optøning. Endvidere undersøges ændringer i mængden af frysbart 
vand i mørtelprøver udsat for en 3% NaCl-opløsning under gentagende frysning og optøning ved brug af 
lavtemperaturkalorimetri.  
Frostbestandigheden undersøges for beton med et v/c-forhold på 0,40 og 0,45 samt tre 
luftindholdsniveauer; lavt, middel og højt. Derudover udsættes betonen for to forskellige 
temperaturcyklusser. Lufttemperaturkurverne for disse kaldes LT og HT. LT varmer til en maksimal 
temperatur på +5°C, og HT varmer til en maksimal temperatur på +20°C. Begge kurver køler til en 
minimumstemperatur på -20°C.  
Lavtemperaturkalorimetri udføres for mørtelprøver med et v/c-forhold på 0,40 og to luftindholdsniveauer; 
lavt og højt. Efter lavtemperaturkalorimetrimålinger udføres en IC-analyse af den saltopløsning, der 
anvendes som frysemedie for mørtelprøver.  
Resultaterne viser, at frostbestandigheden af beton med et v/c-forhold på 0,40 er bedre end for beton med 
et v/c-forhold på 0,45. Øget luftindhold viste sig også at være fordelagtigt for at reducere frostskader.  
Resultater fra prøver udsat for LT-kurven viser en tendens til øget afskalning sammenlignet med prøver 
udsat for HT-kurven. Dette tilskrives hovedsageligt at tiden under 0°C er forlænget for prøver udsat for LT-
kurven. Dog diskuteres andre faktorer også.  
Resultater fra lavtemperaturkalorimetri indikerer ikke nogen ændring i mængden af frysbart vand. Dog 
vises det, at variationer og måleusikkerheder er af samme størrelsesorden som de mulige ændringer i 
mængden af frysbart vand. IC-analyser af frysemediet viser at kloridkoncentrationen falder under cyklisk 
frost-/tøpåvirkning. Dog er ændringen i mængden af frysbart vand, der svarer til faldet i 
kloridkoncentrationen, lille.

1 Introduction 
Production of concrete involves large CO2 emission, and therefore there is a need to develop new and 
greener types of concrete for future use. Using supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs) is one way to 
reduce the CO2 emission related to concrete production. SCMs are used in most concrete produced today, 
but in concrete with Portland composite cement, larger amounts of SCMs may be used as cement clinker 
substitution.  This allows for further reduction of CO2 emission.  
The amounts of conventional SCMs are limited, and therefore new materials are studied. In a new type of 
Portland composite cement, the combination of calcined clay and limestone is used as cement clinker 
substitution. This combination has proven excellent for higher levels of cement clinker substitution.  
As these cement types are new on the market, there is no or very limited experience with especially long-
term durability issues such as frost resistance. Furthermore, the frost deterioration mechanisms for 
conventional concrete with Portland cement are still not fully understood. Therefore, in this project, the 
frost resistance of both conventional concrete with Portland cement and concrete with Portland composite 
cement is studied. Furthermore, there is a focus on measuring changes in the content of freezable water to 
study if this can improve the understanding of frost deterioration mechanisms for different cement types.

2 Theory 
2.1 Portland cement 
Portland cement is a hydraulic cement that reacts with water and forms a binder used in concrete and 
mortar. Portland cement is produced by heating mainly limestone and clay minerals in a rotary kiln at 1400-
1500°C to form clinkers. The clinkers are pulverised with small amounts of gypsum to create Portland 
cement [1]. During heating of the raw materials, a calcination process occurs, where CO2 is released from 
the limestone. 
The clinkers consist mainly of oxides of calcium (CaO), silicon (SiO2), aluminium (Al2O3), and iron (Fe2O3). 
Additionally, it consists of smaller amounts of oxides of magnesium (MgO), sulphur (SO3), and alkali metals 
(K2O and Na2O). These oxides form the four main clinker minerals: Tricalcium silicate, dicalcium silicate, 
tricalcium aluminate, and tetracalcium alumino-ferrite [2]. The chemical formula and the cement chemical 
notation of these minerals and gypsum are shown in Table 1 [1] .

2.2 Chemical composition and hydration of cement 
The chemical reactions of cement and water are referred to as the hydration of cement. As described in 
section 2.1, Portland cement consists of several minerals that will react simultaneously when mixed with 
water [1]. Therefore, the hydration process of Portland cement is a complex system of chemical reactions. 
However, as an approximation, the process can be described as individual hydration processes of each of 
the four main clinker minerals.  
C3S and C2S make up the largest part of Portland cement. The hydration of C3S and C2S forms a wide range 
of products of calcium silicate hydrates that vary in calcium/silicate ratio but are structurally similar. This 
family of products, referred to as C-S-H, is poorly crystalline and forms a gel-like solid. The chemical 
composition of C-S-H depends on the water to cement ratio (w/c ratio), the degree of hydration, and the 
temperature. However, the product at complete hydration may be approximated as C3S2H3. The chemical 
reactions of C3S and C2S can therefore be described by equations (1) and (2). 
 
2 C3S + 6 H →C3S2H3 + 3 CH 
(1) 
 
 
2 C2S + 4 H →C3S2H3 + CH 
(2) 
Both the hydration of C3S and C2S contributes to the final strength of the concrete. However, the reaction 
of C3S is faster than C2S, and C3S contributes to the early age strength and the final setting time.  
Calcium hydroxide (CH) is also a product of the hydration of C3S and C2S. Calcium hydroxide does not 
contribute to the strength of the concrete, but it causes an alkaline environment in the concrete, which 
protects reinforcement from corrosion.

The hydration of C3A with water happens immediately, and reaction products such as C3AH6, C4AH19 and 
C2AH8 forms if gypsum is not present. This results in so-called flash setting of the concrete, where the 
workability of the concrete is lost very fast. Therefore, gypsum is added to the cement. The hydration of 
C3A in the presence of gypsum can be described by the reactions given in equations (3) and (4). The 
reaction process is described in two steps. In step 1, C3A reacts with gypsum and water to form ettringite 
(C6AS̅3H32, also called AFt). In step 2, ettringite reacts with C3A and water to form monosulfate (C4AS̅H12, 
also called AFm, which is short for a family of hydrated calcium aluminate hydrate phases).

The hydration of C4AF does not contribute much to early or later strength development.  
2.3 Pozzolans 
Pozzolans are a group of silicious or silicious and aluminous supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs). 
SCMs have little or no cementing properties in itself. However, they contain varying amounts of amorphous 
silicon oxide, SiO2 and aluminium oxide, Al2O3 that will react with calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)2 formed during 
hydration of the cement clinker to form calcium silicate hydrates, C-S-H. As pozzolans vary in SiO2 and Al2O3 
content and as the pozzolanic reactions and cement clinker hydration happen simultaneously, the 
hydration products formed in the pozzolanic process vary in calcium/silicate ratio, and some products will 
also contain aluminium which is sometimes referred to as C-A-S-H [3]. However, the pozzolan reaction 
between calcium hydroxide and amorphous silicon oxide (CH and S using cement chemical notation) can be 
described as: 
 
2 S + 3 CH →C3S2H3 
(7) 
The pozzolan reaction is slow compared to hydration of the clinker minerals, and therefore concrete 
containing pozzolans will often have a lower early age strength compared to concrete without SCMs.  
Several materials have shown pozzolanic properties, and calcined clay is a group of materials used as 
pozzolan in concrete. Calcined clay is produced by heating clay minerals to 700-850°C, where hydroxyl 
groups are released to form a highly reactive pozzolan [4]. Calcined kaolinite or metakaolin has proven 
excellent pozzolan properties in recent studies. However other clay types, such as illite and smectite, are 
also calcined and used as SCMs [5]. Clay minerals are composed of silica and alumina layers. The chemical 
composition depends on the clay type; therefore, the chemical composition of C-A-S-H formed in the 
pozzolan reactions also varies.  
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
 
Pozzolans are widely used to save cement or improve concrete properties. Cement is the most expensive 
constituent in concrete. Furthermore, the CO2 emission from cement manufacturing is large. By replacing 
some of the cement clinkers with SCMs such as pozzolans, the CO2 emission related to concrete 
manufacturing can be significantly reduced, as the CO2 emission from the manufacturing of pozzolans is 
generally lower.  
2.4 Portland composite cement 
Portland composite cement is a group of blended cement where one or more blending materials replace 
part of the cement clinker. DS/EN 197-1:2012 uses Portland composite cement as a term for a large group 
of cement types referred to as CEM II. These cements include Portland-slag cement, Portland-silica fume 
cement, Portland-pozzolana cement, Portland-fly ash cement, Portland-burnt shale cement, Portland 
limestone cement, and Portland-composite cement (CEM II M cement).  
CEM II M cement is cement where up to 35 % of the cement clinker can be replaced by other constituents 
like pozzolans and limestone that can be combined. A combination of calcined clay and limestone 
substitutes part of the clinker in a new type of CEM II M cement. The notation of this cement type is CEM 
II/B-M (Q-L) or CEM II/B-M (Q-LL), depending on the purity of the limestone.  
Using calcined clay together with limestone has made it possible to substitute up to 30-50% of the clinker 
minerals without reducing compressive strength at 28 days [4]. This would not be possible just using one of 
these SCMs. As described in section 2.2, monosulfate (C4AS̅H12) is formed by hydration of C3A and C4AF. 
When adding limestone to ordinary Portland cement, calcium monocarboaluminate (C4AcH11) and 
hemicarboaluminate (C4Ac0.5H12) are formed instead of monosulfate as the AFm-phases. This leaves more 
sulphate for ettringite formation. Then adding more aluminate phases from calcined clays, calcite from the 
limestone can further react to form more monocarboaluminate and hemicarboaluminate, thereby 
increasing the amount of cementing hydration products, which will refine the porosity and increase the 
strength [4]. This is referred to as the synergetic effect of limestone and calcined clays. 
2.5 Chemical admixtures 
Admixtures or chemical admixtures are a wide range of products used to modify the concrete in both fresh 
and hardened states. One or more admixtures are used in most concrete produced today [1]. In this 
project, a superplasticiser is used to reach better workability of the fresh concrete, and an air entraining 
agent is used to increase the air content of the concrete.  
2.5.1 Superplasticiser  
Superplasticisers are a group of surface-active chemical admixtures used to increase workability. These are 
also called high-range water-reducing admixtures. These admixtures reduce the surface tension of the 
mixing water. Furthermore, it helps disperse the water and cement particles. Cement particles tend to 
cluster in agglomerates because of attracting forces between positively and negatively charged parts of the 
cement grains. The polar superplasticiser is a long-chain anionic organic molecule with many polar groups. 
These adsorb on the cement particle surfaces, whereby the surface becomes negatively charged. This will 
lead to electrostatic repulsion, which will disperse the cement particles and increase workability [1].  
Furthermore, the dominating dispersion mechanism for newer superplasticisers is the inhibition of reactive 
sites. These superplasticisers with long side chains also create steric repulsion where physical barriers 
separate the cement particles as one side of the polymer chain will be adsorbed on the cement surface. 
These mechanisms are illustrated in Figure 1 [1].

2.5.2 Air entraining agent 
Air entraining agents (AEA) are also surface-active chemical admixtures that consist of long-chain organic 
molecules that contain a nonpolar hydrocarbon chain and an anionic polar group. This is illustrated in 
Figure 2. Figure 2 shows that the anionic polar group is oriented towards the water phase at the water-air 
interface. This will result in lower surface tension, promoting and stabilizing air bubble formation. At the 
cement-water interface, the anionic polar group is oriented toward the cement surface, which will make 
the cement surface hydrophobic and stabilize air bubbles at the surface [1].  
 
Figure 2: Mechanism of air entraining agents. Figure 8-1 (b) from [1]. 
Most AEAs also have a plasticising effect, which will result in increased workability. Furthermore, the risk of 
bleeding and segregation can be reduced when using AEAs. 
AEAs are most often used to increase the frost resistance of concrete. This will be further described in 
section 2.8.  
2.6 Compressive strength of concrete 
The compressive strength of concrete is the most valued property in the design of concrete structures. The 
compressive strength depends on several parameters such as porosity, degree of hydration (temperature, 
age and curing conditions), cement type, and admixtures. Furthermore, the measured compressive 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17 
 
strength depends on test parameters such as specimen parameters (dimension, geometry and moisture 
state) and load parameters (loading rate and stress type). 
For concrete with conventional low porosity, high strength aggregates, the strength of concrete is governed 
by the cement paste and the strength of the interfacial transition zone, where the interfacial transition 
zone is the zone in between the large aggregate particles and the hydrated cement paste. At an early age, 
the volume and size of voids are larger in the interfacial transition zone. This result in lower strength of the 
interfacial transition zone compared to the remaining mortar phase of the concrete. However, the strength 
of the interfacial transition zone increases with increasing age and may become equal to or larger than the 
matrix [1]. 
In general, solid material’s strength and porosity are inversely related. The relationship between w/c ratio 
and porosity is the most important factor, as it affects the porosity of both the cement mortar matrix and 
the interfacial transition zone between the matrix and large aggregates.  
Furthermore, air entrainment increases the porosity of the concrete, and thereby air entrainment also 
result in decreasing strength. Bolomey’s modified equation describes the relationship between strength, air 
content, and the w/c ratio. Bolomey’s modified equation is expressed as [6]

The cement type is also an important factor for strength development. The degree of hydration depends on 
the cement type and directly affects the porosity of the concrete. The speed of the hydration process is 
different for different cement types. Cement containing pozzolans typically has a slower strength 
development, as the pozzolan reaction generally is slower than hydration of the clinker minerals.  
Generally, the 28-day uniaxial compressive strength is accepted as the concrete strength.  
2.7 Chloride ingress  
Chloride ions may penetrate concrete from external saline solutions in marine environments or from de-
icer salts. The rate of chloride transport in concrete is influenced both by the pore structure and the 
concrete’s ability to bind chlorides. Chloride can exist as free chlorides in the pore solution and as bound 
chlorides as either chemically bound or adsorbed chlorides on the surface of hydration products. Only free 
chlorides in the pore solution, increase the risk of corrosion, and the binding of chlorides is, therefore, a 
potential mechanism to retard the diffusion process of chlorides in cement pastes. Though if bound 
chlorides are released, these will also constitute a corrosion risk. The binding capacity of cement paste is 
especially related to C-A-S-H and the AFm phases.  
Generally, it is known that using pozzolans will increase the resistance of chloride transport in concrete 
because of pore refinement. Furthermore, the phase composition of hydration products is altered when 
pozzolans are used, which typically increases the chloride binding capacity [7].  
The chemical binding of chlorides in concrete is especially related to the AFm phases. The chemical binding 
of chlorides with AFm phases is well documented. Thermodynamically it is predicted that Kuzel’s salt 
3CaO·Al2O3·1/2CaCl2·11H2O is formed in Portland cement systems at low chloride concentrations, and for 
higher chloride concentrations, Friedel’s salt 3(CaO)·Al2O3·CaCl2·10H2O is formed. In composite cement, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18 
 
where limestone and aluminate (from calcined clay or cement) are present, hemi- and 
monocarboaluminate AFm phases form. These phases can partially react when chloride ions are present 
and form Friedel’s salt [7].  
AFm phases have a crystalline layer structure, where anions are present in between layers containing 
cations [8]. For example, these anions can be SO42-, OH-, Cl- and CO32-. Historically, AFm phases are defined 
as containing SO42- and OH- as anions in commercial cement, but in composite cement, CO32- is present in 
hemi- and monocarboaluminate AFm phases. Chloride binding is a complex mechanism as several 
processes act simultaneously, but it involves an exchange of these anions with chloride ions.   
The physical binding of chlorides is related to the C-A-S-H, as chlorides adsorb on the surfaces of these 
hydrates. The binding capacity of C-A-S-H depends on its compositions. In former studies, it has been 
claimed that a lower calcium/silicate ratio of C-A-S-H results in a decreased binding capacity in cement with 
SCMs [7]. As earlier described, concrete with calcined clay has a lower calcium/silicate ratio due to 
increased uptake of aluminate in the C-A-S-H structure [7]. Furthermore, the pore solution’s pH value and 
the source of the chloride ions (NaCl or CaCl2) influence the binding capacity of the C-A-S-H. 
2.8 Frost resistance of concrete 
Frost deterioration of concrete is an important durability issue in moderate and cold climates. Several 
deterioration theories have been proposed, but the actual mechanism is still not completely understood. 
Generally, there are two types of deterioration: Internal cracking (or frost deterioration without salts) and 
salt frost scaling. Air entrainment has proven to be an efficient method to improve the frost resistance of 
concrete; therefore, this is described further in section 2.8.3.  
2.8.1 Internal cracking 
Internal cracking is a frost deterioration mechanism where cracks form inside the concrete structure when 
exposed to cyclic freezing and thawing. The simplest mechanism describing this phenomenon is the “closed 
container mechanism”, where the cracking is explained by the expansion of water that freezes to form ice. 
The specific volume of ice is 9% larger than that of water. Therefore, ice formation will cause the pressure 
to increase if there is too little space for expansion. The pressure will develop if, initially, more than 91.7% 
of the air voids (container volume) are filled with water before freezing. The pressure rise due to ice 
formation may be calculated from the Clapeyron equation [9].  
Another mechanism is the “Hydraulic pressure hypothesis” proposed by Powers in 1945. Powers had noted 
that even though the concrete was not fully saturated, frost damage still occurred; thus, it was not the ice 
pressure itself that would cause deterioration. Therefore, Powers proposed that the hydraulic pressures 
that arise during ice formation when water is expelled from the pore in which freezing takes place would 
cause expansion of the cement paste and is thereby the main cause of frost damage [9]. The magnitude of 
this pressure depends on the distance to the nearest air void, the permeability of the cement paste, and 
the rate of the ice formation [1]. In 1949 Powers presented further experimental results and showed that 
measured air void characteristics of frost-resistant concrete corresponded well with calculations of a 
maximum spacing factor. Powers used a simplified model, assuming that air voids are all of the same sizes 
and are cubically arranged. The spacing factor is then the maximum distance from anywhere in the paste to 
an air void [9]. Figure 3 illustrates this. Powers’ spacing factor is calculated as:

There are several objections to the hydraulic pressure hypothesis. One is that if the hydraulic pressure 
causes the expansion during the cooling of the concrete, this expansion will stop if the cooling stops. 
However, experimental data have shown that this is not the case [9]. Therefore, this hypothesis does not 
fully explain the frost damage mechanism. 
Powers and Helmuth also observed this phenomenon and came up with a new hypothesis referred to as 
micro ice lens growth. According to this theory, ice formation begins in larger pores, and due to differences 
in the level of free energy, thermodynamically, water then diffuses towards pores where ice has formed. 
This will create osmotic pressure. This hypothesis uses the same principle previously described by Taber, 
who described macro ice lens growth in soil [9].  
Which of these hypotheses is the most important has not yet been determined, but all of these hypotheses 
agree that air entrainment increases the frost resistance of concrete. Both field exposures and laboratory 
tests have shown that internal cracking is not a problem in properly air entrained concrete [11].  
2.8.2 Salt frost scaling theories  
Salt frost scaling of concrete is a progressive deterioration mechanism, where the surface scales in thin 
layers when concrete is exposed to cyclic freezing and thawing. It usually only happens in the presence of 
de-icer salts such as sodium chloride, which are often used in cold climates on roads or walkways. Valenza 
and Scherer have performed reviews of both laboratory and field studies of salt frost scaling and compiled 
the following list of characteristics [12]: 
1. Salt scaling consists of the progressive removal of small flakes or chips of the binder. 
2. A pessimum exists at a solute concentration of ~3%, independent of the solute used. 
3. No scaling occurs when the pool of solution is missing from the concrete surface. 
4. No damage occurs when the minimum temperature is held above −10°C; the amount of damage 
increases as the minimum temperature decreases below −10°C and with longer time at the 
minimum temperature. 
5. Air entrainment improves salt scaling resistance. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
20 
 
6. The salt concentration of the pool on the surface is more important than the salt concentration in 
the pore solution. 
7. Susceptibility to salt scaling is not correlated with susceptibility to internal frost action. 
8. The strength of the surface governs the ability of a cementitious body to resist salt scaling. 
The salt frost scaling mechanism is not fully understood, but several theories have been proposed. It is a 
more complex problem than internal cracking, mainly because it is related to the microstructure of the 
concrete at the surface [11].  
Powers proposed that the salt frost scaling mechanism was a combination of hydraulic pressure and 
osmotic pressure. When salt and ice coexist in pores, the salt concentration of the solution increase as ice 
formation occurs. The highly concentrated solution will attract more water, thereby creating an osmotic 
pressure. This would increase the ice formation and thereby increase the hydraulic pressure as water is 
expelled from the pore. This hypothesis is though doubted and, today, not considered the primary 
mechanism [9]. 
Another mechanism is the glue spall theory, proposed by Valenza and Scherer. Glue spalling is a technique 
used to decorate a glass surface. The procedure consists of applying a layer of epoxy on a sandblasted glass 
surface at high temperatures and then lowering the temperature. When the temperature decreases, the 
epoxy contracts more than the glass material. This will induce tension in the epoxy, and eventually, it will 
crack into smaller pieces. Along the perimeter of the epoxy, tension is introduced in the glass surface, 
which will make the glass scale in small flakes. Figure 4 illustrates this procedure. It is this mechanism that 
is applied to concrete when a solution freezes, on the concrete surface. When a saline solution freezes it 
tends to contract 5 times as much as the concrete surface therefore, the solution plays the role of the 
epoxy layer [12]. The behaviour of the ice layer depends on the concentration of the solution. If it is pure 
water, it is not expected to crack, but solutions of moderate concentrations will crack. Solutions of high 
concentrations do not gain strength for the cracks to penetrate the concrete in the temperature range of 
interest [12]. This explains that a pessimum exists at a solute concentration of around 3%.

temperature, T0. (c) The temperature T of the composite decrease below the initial temperature T0, and the glass scales and leaves a 
scalloped glass surface. (d) Epoxy/glass/epoxy sandwich seal, dimensions, and orientation. (e) Illustration of the stresses. σg is the

A third mechanism is referred to as the cryogenic suction theory. This is not a new theory but a 
combination of the theory of micro ice lens growth and frost heaving of soil. Lindmark first proposed this 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21 
 
and was later extended by Lui and Hansen. This theory combines the theory of micro ice lens growth and 
frost heaving of soil. Thus, according to this theory, ice formation also begins in larger pores, and the 
differences in the level of free energy make water diffuse towards pores where ice has formed. The new 
approach is then that the external saline solution works as a moisture reservoir. Therefore, the ice formed 
near the surface does not only attract water from small pores but also from the external solution, which 
will cause further ice growth and scaling of the surface, according to Lindmark [13]. According to Lui and 
Hansen, scaling happens due to stresses caused by the expansion of the saturated surface layer and the 
shrinkage of the bulk concrete during freezing. According to Lindmark, the pessimum concentration of 3% 
of the saline solution exist as de-icer salt is needed to ensure that the solution is liquid. However, a high 
concentration would reduce ice formation and thereby cause less scaling.  
Both the glue spall theory and the cryogenic suction theory are the only theories that can describe all 
observed characteristics of salt frost scaling, but the theories are conflicting [13].  
Different laboratory test procedures have been suggested. Those mainly used are the American Standard 
ASTM C 672 [14], the Swedish Standard SS 137244 [15], and the European specification DS/CEN/TS 12390-
9:2016 [16].  
2.8.3 Mitigation of frost damage 
Generally, all theories on both internal cracking and salt frost scaling agree on the importance of air voids 
and the distance between the air voids, where water can freeze without creating disruptive pressures in 
mitigation of frost damage. Air voids are entrained in the cement paste by the mixing action, but to achieve 
an adequate air void structure AEAs (described further in section 2.5.2) are needed.  
From laboratory studies, it has been shown that a critical value of the spacing between air voids exists and 
that this holds for most types of concrete [11]. The spacing factor is calculated as described in equation (9). 
Below the critical value of the spacing factor, deterioration from internal cracking is low, and beyond the 
critical value, it is very high. This proves that the air void structure is the most important parameter in 
predicting the frost resistance of concrete. Some studies show that a critical value for the spacing factor 
also can be established for surface scaling, and other studies show that this is not possible.   
Even though the spacing factor that is an important measure to obtain frost-resistant concrete, it is more 
practical to specify a minimum total air content. Thus, assuming that the spacing factor is inversely 
proportional to the total air content. According to DS/EN 206 DK NA:2020 [17] the minimum required air 
content in the fresh concrete is 4.5% for exposure class XF1 to XF4. The description of the exposure classes 
XF1 to XF4 is shown in Table 2 according to DS/EN 206:2013 [18].

2.8.4 Other factors influencing the frost resistance of concrete 
Several other factors influence the frost resistance of concrete. Some of these are described here.  
The pore structure of the cement paste is an important parameter for frost resistance. The pore structure 
mainly depends on the w/c ratio and the degree of hydration (where the degree of hydration is the mass 
ratio of cement that has reacted to the total mass of cement before reaction). Generally, the higher the w/c 
ratio and the lower the degree of hydration, the higher the volume of large pores in the cement paste. As it 
is the water in large pores that freezes rapidly, it can be assumed that the amount of freezable water is 
larger at a given temperature for a higher w/c ratio and lower degree of hydration [1].

w/c ratio is prescribed for each exposure class. For exposure class XF1 to XF4, the maximum allowable w/c

2.9 Problem statement 
The purpose of this project is to study, the frost resistance of concrete with Portland composite cement, 
CEM II/B-M (Q-LL), with a combination of calcined clay and limestone. This is compared to concrete with 
ordinary Portland cement (CEM I) when exposed to a saline solution. Previous studies have shown that 
concrete with Portland composite cement (CEM II/B-M (Q-LL)) has a larger tendency to frost scaling and 
thereby is less frost resistant than concrete with ordinary Portland cement (CEM I) [20]. 
The frost resistance is measured and evaluated by accelerated freeze-thaw tests where the specimens are 
exposed to freeze-thaw cycles while being exposed to a NaCl solution at the surface. The damage 
development is evaluated as the amount of surface scaling at time intervals of 7 to 14 days and up to 56 
days (56 cycles of freezing and thawing). In addition, the frost resistance is evaluated for two different 
temperature ranges to study the effect of the maximum temperature and to study a potential pumping 
effect. The hypothesis is that a pumping effect would result in increased chloride binding and thereby 
increase the amount of freezable water, which will reduce the frost resistance.  
Furthermore, low temperature calorimetry is used to measure the amount of freezable water during 
freeze-thaw cycles. This is done to study if the frost resistance is related to the different cement types’ 
ability to bind chloride from an external solution, thereby increasing the amount of freezable water in the 
system.  
Thus, the questions that are studied are:  
- 
Is the frost resistance of concrete with Portland-composite cement (CEM II/B-M (Q-LL)) comparable 
to the frost resistance of concrete with ordinary Portland cement (CEM I) for comparable strength, 
air void structure and w/c ratio? 
- 
Are there indications that the contraction and expansion of air in voids create a pumping effect due 
to temperature changes and that this result in increased frost scaling? 
- 
Does the amount of freezable water change with cyclic freezing and thawing in a system containing 
a mortar sample and an external NaCl solution?

3.9 Low temperature calorimetry 
Calorimetry is a method for measuring absolute or differential heat flow. It can be operated either 
isothermally or in a constant temperature gradient, also called scanning mode. In this project, low 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
 
34 
 
temperature differential scanning calorimetry was used. Thus, the calorimeter was operated in a constant 
temperature gradient, measuring the difference in the amount of heat required to increase/decrease the 
temperature of a sample and a reference as a function of temperature. This was used to study the phase 
transition of water.  
The ratio of heat input to the temperature increase of a system is referred to as the heat capacity of the 
system. Therefore, the calorimeter measures the apparent heat capacity of the sample.  
When a sample undergoes a phase transition or a chemical change, more or less heat is required to keep 
the temperature of the sample at the same temperature as the reference. If it requires more heat to flow 
to the sample compared to the reference, the reaction is endothermic, and if it requires less heat to flow to 
the sample, the reaction is exothermic. The phase transition of water from ice to liquid is endothermic, as 
heat is absorbed when ice is melted, and the phase transition of liquid water to ice is exothermic. 
Differential scanning calorimeters can be used to measure the heat absorbed or released during such phase 
transitions by measuring the difference in heat flow between the sample and the reference cell.

3.9.1 Test procedure for low temperature calorimetry measurements 
Before deciding the test procedure for low temperature calorimetry measurements, three trial experiments 
were conducted. These are described in appendix D.  
Two specimens from each mix design were used for calorimetric measurements. One specimen from each 
mix was used to study ice formation without the influence of an external NaCl solution during one freezing 
and thawing cycle. The other specimen was used to study the ice formation with the influence of an 
external NaCl solution during several freezing and thawing cycles. The calorimetric measurements were 
started at an age of 27 days for the specimen that was not exposed to a NaCl solution, and the 
measurements were started at day 28 for the specimen that was exposed to a NaCl solution.  
The saline solution used is a 3% NaCl solution. This is prepared by mixing 15 g of NaCl with 485 g of 
deionised water. After mixing, the solution was stored in a closed container. The container was shaken 
before the solution was used to ensure an even concentration of the solution.

concentration of the remaining solution will increase, as the frozen water will not contain any ions. The 
phase diagram for the NaCl and water system is shown in Figure 14 [13]. For a 3% NaCl solution, freezing 
will start at -1.76°C. When the temperature decreases below -1.76°C, it is seen that ice will form, and the

3.9.4 Freezing medium analysis 
IC analyses were performed for the freezing medium after completing the calorimetric measurements. This 
was done to study the interaction between the freezing medium and the mortar specimens.  
The freezing medium was collected after completing the calorimetric measurements. The freezing medium 
was weighed with an accuracy of 0.01 mg. Then it was diluted about 10 times with deionised water. The 
weight of the deionised water was also recorded. Then the solution was filtered through a syringe filter 
with a pore size of 0.45 µm. Afterwards, the solution was stored in small plastic containers with lids at 5°C,

The p-value, 𝑝(𝑇≤𝑡), is the probability of obtaining test results as extreme as the results actually 
observed under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. A small p-value means the outcome is 
unlikely if the null hypothesis is true. The significance level, 𝛼, of the p-value is commonly chosen to be 
0.05, meaning that the p-value where the null hypothesis is rejected is below 0.05. If doing so here, none of 
the p-values shown in Table 14 will lead to rejecting the null hypothesis. Sometimes the p-value is chosen 
to be 0.10, but this would not reject the null hypothesis either. Thus, this test shows no statistically 
significant difference between the accumulated scaling after 56 cycles of specimens exposed to the LT and 
HT temperature curve, and going above a significance level of 0.10 is usually not recommended, as this 
means that there is little or no evidence against the null hypothesis [32].  
The Student’s t-test assumes equal population variance. To test this, an F-test is performed. The F-test is 
also a hypothesis test that can be used to test that two populations have equal population variance. The 
significance level of the F-test is chosen to be 0.05. Thus, if the p-value of the F-test, 𝑝(𝐹≤𝑓), is below 
0.05, the hypothesis that the population variance of the groups is equal cannot be rejected. Table 14 shows 
that the p-value of the F-test is above 0.05 for all specimens. Therefore, the hypothesis is not rejected. This 
means there is no statistical significance between the variance of specimens exposed to the different 
temperature curves.  
Not being able to reject the null hypothesis from the Student’s t-test does not mean it is true. It might be 
that the statistical power of the test is too small for the test to be able to detect meaningful differences. 
The statistical power is the probability that the test will detect some effect when there actually is one. The 
statistical power depends on the effect size, i.e. how large an effect the test should be able to detect. The 
smaller the effect size, the larger is the risk of not finding the effect. The effect size (Cohen’s d) is the 
difference between two groups’ means divided by the pooled standard deviation. Thus, a size effect of 0.5 
means a difference in population means of 0.5 standard deviations. The effect size, 𝑑, of the tests is seen in 
Table 14.  
Knowing the effect size, a post hoc power analysis can be performed to calculate the power of the t-test. 
Table 14 shows the calculated post hoc power of the t-test (the calculations have been performed using the 
G*Power software [33]) using a significance level of 0.10 for the t-test. Table 14 shows that the power of 
the t-test is in the range of 0.10 to 0.47. Often a power of more than 0.80 is accepted. The low values of the 
power, therefore, indicate that the t-tests cannot detect if there is a difference. Therefore, a priori analysis 
has been performed to find the minimum number of samples needed to get a power of at least 0.80, again 
using a significance level of 0.10 and the effect size shown in Table 14. Table 14 presents the minimum 
number of samples needed, 𝑛𝑚𝑖𝑛. Therefore, the recommendation for future work is that the number of 
samples should be increased depending on which level of the effect size the test should be able to detect.

As described earlier, scaling increases more for specimens with low air content than those with high air 
content when comparing specimens exposed to the LT and HT curve. Therefore, it seems that there might 
an effect that counteracts the prolonged freezing for air entrained specimens.  
This effect could be a pumping effect, where the external solution is sucked into the specimens during 
freezing and pumped out during heating due to the thermal expansion of the air in the concrete. Suppose 
such a pumping effect exists. Then the volume change of the air would be higher for specimens exposed to 
the HT curve than the LT curve, as the difference in maximum and minimum temperature is larger. This 
effect would be higher for specimens with high air content than for those with low air content. If the 
external solution is sucked into the specimen, chloride ions may react with the cement paste as described 
in section 2.7 and thereby increase the amount of freezable water and cause more scaling. This effect 
would therefore cause more scaling for specimens exposed to the HT curve and would be more significant 
for specimens with high air content.  
If the temperature curves shown in Figure 28 were more alike until reaching 0°C in the period from around 
18 h to 21 h, it would be clearer whether the thermal expansion of air results in a pumping effect. 
Therefore, this could be considered for further analysis.  
However, chloride binding might not only lead to increased amounts of freezable water. The chemical 
binding of chlorides might also change permeability and transport properties. To the knowledge of the 
writer studies of chloride binding’s effect on the permeability and transport properties of concrete are 
limited. However, the effect of carbonation on permeability and transport properties has been studied. 
These studies show that carbonation reduces the total pore volume for concrete with ordinary Portland 
cement, CEM I. On the contrary, coarsening of the pore structure has been reported for concrete with 
SCMs [34]. Whether the chemical binding of chloride also leads to changes in the pore volume and 
permeability or not, cannot be concluded from this study. However, it would affect the results, as reduced 
permeability would result in less freezing medium being transported into the concrete, thereby reduce 
frost damages due to reduced water content. Chloride transport is temperature-dependent and increases 
with increasing temperature. Therefore, it would be expected that chloride transport is faster for 
specimens exposed to HT than LT. If the chemical binding of chloride leads to changes in the permeability, it 
would therefore be expected that the effect is more significant for HT than LT. As scaling is generally higher 
for LT than for HT, as shown in Table 15, these measurements indicate that transport might be reduced. 
However, it is difficult to predict whether this only happens due to prolonged freezing for specimens 
exposed to LT or if changed transport properties also play a role. Therefore, the effect of chloride binding 
on transport properties might be a topic for further research.

4.7.3 Mechanisms causing changes in the amount of freezable water 
The results presented in Figure 33 indicate that the amount of freezable water is constant for RH during the 
freezing and thawing cycles. Furthermore, the amount of freezable water first increases and thereafter 
decreases for RL.  
For this experimental setup, the calculated amount of freezable water is the sum of the water within the 
mortar and in the external solution. However, only the amount of freezable water in the mortar can cause 
frost damage. In the following, mechanisms that potentially could cause changes in the total amount of 
freezable water are discussed.  
If chloride-ions bind to the mortar, the concentration of the external solution decreases. This would 
increase the amount of freezable water. However, the effect depends on how chloride is bound. If chloride 
chemically reacts with AFm phases in the cement paste, this is an ion exchange as described in section 2.7, 
resulting in other ions being dissolved in the solution. These ions will also cause freezing point depression. 
However, freezing point depression depends on the number of dissolved ions. If chloride is exchanged with 
sulphate-ions or carbonate-ions, which are divalent ions, the number of ions in the solution is reduced, as 
two chloride ions are exchanged with one sulphate- or carbonate-ion. This increases the freezing point. 
However, if chloride is exchanged with hydroxide-ions, which are monovalent, the freezing point stays 
constant. As described in section 2.7, chloride can take the place of all of these three ions to form Friedel’s 
salt, resulting in increased amount of freezable water.  
If chloride physically binds by adsorption, this directly results in a decrease in the total amount of ions 
dissolved in the solution, whereby the amount of freezable water increases. In a 3% NaCl solution, the 
theoretical amount of unfreezable water can be calculated as 
 
𝑚𝑛𝑓,𝑠𝑜𝑙= ( 3%

amount of freezable water is equal to 𝑚𝑛𝑓,𝑠𝑜𝑙, which would happen if all chloride-ions in the solution were

A mechanism that could potentially reduce the total amount of freezable water in the system is if water is 
bound either chemically or by physical adsorption to the mortar during the experiment. The amount of

reason to think that the surface area of these phases increases or that the volume of solids increases during

water is bound chemically, as the hydration process is slow when the experiment is conducted. As the 
results presented in Figure 33 do not indicate decreasing amounts of freezable water, it would be unlikely 
that this effect would be equal and opposite to the effect of chloride binding; the effect of water-binding is 
considered unlikely.

amount of unfreezable water would decrease by 32.7% and 31.4% for RL and RH, respectively. This 
corresponds to an increase in the amount of freezable water of around 0.023 g, as shown to the right in 
Table 23. This is a relatively small increase compared to the fluctuations of the graphs in Figure 33 and the 
mass loss presented in Table 19. Thus, measuring uncertainties might be why this is not observed from the 
low temperature calorimetry measurements.

5 Conclusion 
The frost resistance of concrete with ordinary Portland cement, RAPID® AALBORG CEMENT, was 
investigated using the accelerated freeze-thaw test. Furthermore, low temperature calorimetry 
measurements were performed to study changes in the amount of freezable water for mortar specimens.  
In the accelerated freeze-thaw test, concrete with a w/c ratio of 0.40 and 0.45 was studied. Results showed 
that the frost resistance of concrete with a w/c ratio of 0.40 was better than for a w/c ratio of 0.45. 
Furthermore, three levels of the air content of the concrete were investigated. This proved the beneficial 
effect of high air content in the mitigation of frost damage.  
Additionally, the concrete was exposed to two different air temperature curves. It was not possible to 
prove a statistically significant difference in the scaling of specimens exposed to the different temperature 
curves using a Student’s t-test. However, this might have been possible if more specimens had been tested. 
Even though a statistically significant difference was not proven, there was a tendency that the scaling of 
specimens exposed to the LT curve (where the maximum air temperature was +5°C) was higher than for 
specimens exposed to the HT curve (where the maximum air temperature was +20°C). It is concluded that 
this difference mainly happens due to the prolonged freezing of specimens exposed to the LT curve. 
However, increased chloride binding at the HT curve may also play a role if chloride binding reduces the 
permeability. Furthermore, it was observed that scaling increased less for air entrained concrete when 
exposed to the LT curve. This may happen if a pumping effect exists, resulting in increased chloride binding 
and thereby increased amounts of freezable water.  
The calorimetric measurements of mortar specimens with a w/c ratio of 0.40 and two levels of air content 
did not indicate any significant changes in the amount of freezable water. However, it was also shown that 
fluctuations and measuring uncertainties were of the same magnitude as the possible changes in the 
amount of freezable water. Measurements of the amount of evaporable water indicate that the water 
content of mortar specimens increases. Hereby the amount of freezable water in the mortar might also 
have increased even though the amount of freezable water in the entire system of the mortar and the 
external NaCl solution did not change.  
IC analysis of the NaCl solution used as a freezing medium for mortar specimens during the calorimetric 
measurements showed that the chloride concentration had decreased during the cyclic freezing and 
thawing. However, the possible increase in the amount of freezable water resulting from this decrease in 
chloride concentration was small compared to the mass loss and fluctuations in the amount of freezable 
water during the calorimetric measurements. It is therefore concluded that this might be why changes in 
the amount of freezable water were not observed from the calorimetric measurements. Furthermore, the 
sulphate concentration of the freezing medium was found. Assuming that this stems from Friedel’s salt 
formation, only around 1% of the decrease in chloride ions stems from this reaction. Thus, indicating that 
the contribution of Friedel’s salt formation to chloride binding is small.

6 Further research  
Based on the studies and results obtained in this project, there are several potential areas for further work. 
Some of these are mentioned below.  
The effect of the temperature cycle on frost damage is a possible topic for further research. From the 
accelerated freeze-thaw test, it was not possible to prove a statistically significant difference in the scaling 
of specimens exposed to the different temperature curves using a Student’s t-test. However, this might be 
possible by increasing the number of specimens.  
Furthermore, if the time below 0°C is the same for specimens exposed to different temperature cycles, it 
might be possible to investigate other mechanisms affecting the scaling of concrete. Therefore, this could 
also be a topic for further research.  
The effect of chloride binding on permeability and transport properties might also be investigated further 
to understand salt frost scaling better.  
During low temperature calorimetry measurements, a mass loss was observed. The reason for this and 
potential ways of mitigating this might also be investigated.  
The calorimetric measurements showed that the air content seemed to affect the shape of the melting 
curves. This might also be a topic for further research. Additionally, the shape and position of the freezing 
curves might also be useful in understanding of frost deterioration mechanisms.  
Furthermore, in the experimental setup used here, it was only possible to measure changes in the total 
amount of freezable water, thus both in the mortar and in the external solution. However, as it is changes 
in the amount of freezable water in the pore system of the mortar that causes frost damage, the changes in 
freezable water in the pore system during freezing and thawing might be further investigated.
