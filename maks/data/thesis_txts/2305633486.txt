Abstract
Inspired by the performance enhancing tubercle structures on the humpback whales ﬂipper, this project
investigated the aerodynamic potential of two blade sections with span-wise wavy surface patterns through
numerical ﬂow simulations. The designed blade sections had a rectangular planform and was based on the
wind turbine applicable S809 airfoil. The wavy surface shape was modelled as a sinusoidal wave pattern with
amplitude A = 0.015c and wavelength λ = 0.125c, and the two addressed blade sections were characterized
by a leading edge conﬁned wave pattern and a wave pattern extended towards the trailing edge, respectively.
A numerical scheme employing the RANS equations and the k-! turbulence model was established, and
the γ-Re✓transition model was included after initial studies showed improved agreement with experimental
results compared to fully turbulent ﬂow under the given conditions. The aerodynamic coeﬃcients obtained
for Re = 106 ﬂow over the wavy surface geometries were compared to corresponding results for a smooth
baseline foil, revealing very limited or no improved performance for the wavy surface geometries under the
given conditions. Early stall for both wavy blade sections resulted in reduced lift and increased drag, all
properties in direct contrast to the prospects of the wavy surface design concept. Comparison to existing
results suggests that positive e↵ects might be limited to lower Reynolds numbers or particular airfoils and
planforms.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In societal dealing with the present challenges of climate change and limitation of fossil resources, transition
to renewable energy sources for power production has become an essential element. The harvesting of energy
from the wind has turned into an important ﬁeld within the developed sustainable energy solutions. The
amount of electrical energy produced by wind turbines in the EU has increased rapidly within the last 15
years and accounted for 10% of the renewable energy production in EU by 2012 [1]. In some countries a
major part of even the total electrical energy production comes from wind energy, like in Denmark where
it was 39% in 2014 and is still expected to increase [2]. The challenge of wind energy is, however, still to
become competitive to conventional energy production in terms of cost per produced energy unit, especially
for o↵shore wind energy. Part of meeting this challenge is to keep optimizing aerodynamic design and develop
new methods and technologies that can bring down the cost of produced energy. Progress can be achieved
either by improving ability to harvest energy from the wind or by improving design to reduce the weight and
hence cost of the wind turbine itself. In the light of the increasing size of developed wind turbines, weight
reduction is important and especially bringing down the weight of the blades, since they a↵ect most other
components and hence the total weight and cost of the turbine. A lot of e↵ort is therefore still being put
into improving the aerodynamic eﬃciency of wind turbine blades, hopefully allowing the building of more
slender and lighter blades in order to reduce the cost of energy.
1.1
Motivation
The aerodynamic performance of a wing can be improved by modifying it in a way that facilitates increased
lift without causing the drag to increase accordingly, i.e. increasing the lift-to-drag ratio. Further, delay of
wing stall can improve performance since the lift of an airfoil decreases while form drag increases when it goes
into stall. Several passive ﬂow control techniques like leading edge slots and vortex generators have already
been addressed for the purpose of lift augmentation and stall delay, but these are associated with a penalty
in drag [3]. Alternatively, an interesting candidate to performance enhancement by airfoil modiﬁcation has
developed within resent years, initially inspired by the shape of the well-performing ﬂippers of the humpback
whale. The shape of the humpback whale ﬂipper is unique because of the presence of large tubercles or
protuberances along its leading edge, illustated in Figure 1.1. It has been suggested that these structures
might act similarly to passive ﬂow control devices and a model version was investigated by Miklosovic et al.
[5] showing positive e↵ect on both lift, drag and stall properties compared to a smooth ﬂipper version. Thus,
studying aspects of this novel concept relevant for application in a wind turbine context is interesting both
in terms of investigating and understanding the mechanisms at play and possibly discovering new ways to
improve wing performance.
This project therefore sets out to investigate the ﬂow over two blade sections with wavy surface geome-
tries inspired by the humpback whale, in order to determine the ﬂow characteristics and the aerodynamic

potential of the design. Before going into further detail however, an account on the current state of the art
within this area is given in the next section.
1.2
State of the art
The fundamental idea of the conducted investigations in the current literature is to mimic the tubercles on
the leading edge of the humpback whale ﬂipper by modelling the structure as a sinusoidal wave pattern and
then evaluate the performance by comparison to a straight leading edge model. Initially, the model planforms
were mostly formed after typical humpback whale ﬂippers, but a transition was also made to rectangular
planforms where performance is not dependent on the ﬂipper speciﬁc shape. A so-called swept-leading-edge
planform was also addressed, and the three mentioned planforms are sketched in Figure 1.2 for clariﬁcation.
Figure 1.2: Illustrative sketch of the fundamental planforms addressed in wavy surface designs
Due to the novelty of the technology, there are various terms being applied for the same phenomenon in the
literature. It is therefore noted that the geometric elements of the sinusoidal leading edge pointing upstream
in the ﬂow are referred to as tubercles, protuberances, undulations or even just bumps. When describing the
9
==PAGE:13==
wave pattern geometry, wave maxima are called peaks while the minima areas are called troughs. In the case
of the wavy surface extending chord-wise to the trailing edge, the extended elements corresponding to peak
and trough are referred to as hump and groove, respectively. In the following, the terminology will adapt in
accordance with the addressed article or account, but in the remaining study the wavy surface pattern will
be referred to as tubercles or just as sinusoidal leading edge.
Among the ﬁrst to elaborate on the hydrodynamic signiﬁcance of the leading edge tubercles on the humpback
whale ﬂipper were Fish and Battle [6], who undertook a detailed study to better understand the morphol-
ogy of the ﬂipper. The humpback whale is unique in terms of the leading edge tubercles, but also due to
its exceptional ability to undertake acrobatic, underwater manoeuvres. The manoeuvrability is associated
with the feeding behaviour of the humpback whale, since it utilizes the extremely mobile ﬂippers in order to
catch its prey. The investigation of an acquired ﬂipper showed it had a wing-like, high aspect ratio planform
and that the cross-sections were symmetric with a blunt rounded leading edge and a tapered trailing edge.
The size of the present tubercles and the distance between them were both observed to generally decrease
distally. Based on the position and number of tubercles on a ﬂipper, it was hypothesized that the tubercles
may generate vortices in a manner similar to vortex generators, acting to prevent stall by enhancing mo-
mentum exchange in the boundary layer to keep the ﬂow attached. In accordance, it was suggested that the
leading edge tubercles might function as passive ﬂow control devices maintaining lift at high angles of attack.
At an early stage, Watt and Fish [7] simulated inviscid ﬂow over a rectangular planform geometry based
on the NACA-634-021 airfoil, with and without leading edge tubercles, using a panel method. The airfoil
and leading edge tubercles were inspired by the humpback whale and tubercles were modelled as a sinusoidal
wave pattern. Results were only reported for angle of attack ↵= 10◦where a 4.8% increase in lift and
10.9% reduction in induced drag was observed for the modiﬁed geometry compared to baseline airfoil. An
included study of characteristic viscous forces, however, indicated an increase in form drag comparable to
the reduction in induced drag. Unfortunately, no Reynolds number or geometry characteristics were reported.
The e↵ects of the leading edge tubercles of humpback whale ﬂippers on performance was later investi-
gated experimentally by Miklosovic et al. [5]. A ﬂipper model capturing the morphology common to the
species was formed by creating a sinusoidal leading edge pattern where intertubercle spacing and tubercles
amplitude decreased span-wise towards the ﬂipper tip. The models with and without tubercles were con-
structed based on the symmetric NACA 0020 airfoil, which is similar to the ﬂipper cross section proﬁle. A
wind tunnel experiment was conducted at Reynolds numbers in the range 505.000 to 520.000 and sensitivity
of results to Reynolds number was assumed negligible above Re = 400.000. The aerodynamic coeﬃcients
were obtained in the angle of attack range ↵= [−2◦, 20◦] for both the smooth and the scalloped ﬂipper and
the results were compared. It was shown that the presence of tubercles delayed stall to higher ↵, increased
maximum lift, Clmax, and to some extend decreased drag. The stall angle was increased by as much as 40%
and the maximum lift, Clmax, was increased by 6% for the scalloped ﬂipper compared to the smooth. The
lift-to-drag-ratio of the scalloped ﬂipper is generally equal to or higher than the value of the smooth ﬂipper
except in the small interval ↵= [10◦, 12◦], where the smooth ﬂipper performs better until it stalls [5].
The ﬂipper models investigated experimentally by Miklosovic et al. [5] were put through a numerical in-
vestigation by Pedro and Kobayashi [8] using the DES (Detached Eddy Simulation) formulation. The
numerical domain was set to imitate the wind tunnel, and parameters were scaled to reach a similar Reynolds
number of 5 · 105. The obtained aerodynamic coeﬃcients were in good agreement with the experimental
results, thereby supporting the observation of improved aerodynamic performance by the ﬂipper with lead-
ing edge tubercles, compared to a corresponding smooth ﬂipper. Further, the DES approach allowed an
investigation of the vorticity ﬁeld, showing that the wavy ﬂipper displayed stream-wise vortices aligned with
the tubercles in the ﬂipper midsection, a pattern not shared by smooth ﬂipper. As a consequence, it was
shown that the ﬂow over the wavy surface ﬂipper possessed more momentum in that area and hence could
delay separation. An illustration of shear stress streak-lines on the ﬂipper surface additionally pointed to,

that the vortices associated with the tubercles helped conﬁne leading edge separation to the tip region.
Transferring the concept of a wavy leading edge to rectangular planforms, a series of experimental in-
vestigations were conducted by Johari et al. [9], Zverkov et al. [10] and Hansen et al. [11] in 2007-2008.
Johari et al. [9] investigated the aerodynamic loading resulting from the presence of leading edge pro-
tuberances with characteristic parameters similar to those of the humpback whale ﬂippers. Seven airfoil
sections based on the NACA 634-021 proﬁle were manufactured; one baseline with smooth leading edge and
six with sinusoidal leading edge geometries deﬁned by wavelengths, λ, and amplitudes, A, of λ = {0.25, 0.50}c
and A = {0.025, 0.05, 0.12}c. In a water tunnel experiment conducted at Re = 183.000, the forces and mo-
ments on the seven foils were measured as function of angles of attack in a broad range of ↵= [−6◦, 30◦].
The foils with sinusoidal leading edge showed di↵erent stall characteristics than the baseline. The wavy
leading edges caused reduction in lift in the prestall regime of the baseline, while in its poststall regime,
wavy foil lift was up to 50% higher than the baseline lift. In terms of drag, the foils with sinusoidal leading
edge produced higher drag in the prestall regime of the baseline airfoil, while drag was almost independent
of leading edge shape in the poststall regime. Lift-to-drag-ratio could hence be increased substantially in
the poststall regime by a sinusoidal leading edge. In the linear regime, foils with wavy leading edge (except
one) had lower Cl slopes, dCl
d↵, than baseline, and dCl
d↵is observed to decrease with protuberance amplitude.
Comparison in terms of wavelength showed little di↵erence between results, and the e↵ect of this parameter
was considered minor [9]. It is noted however, that the shorter wavelength is associated with slightly higher
Clmax and lower drag. Additionally, a set of three wider foils were employed for ﬂow visualization using
tufts at selected angles of ↵= {12◦, 18◦, 24◦} at the same Reynolds number. For the foils with leading edge
protuberances, separation was observed to initiate from the though while the ﬂow remained attached to the
peak regions. Even in baseline poststall where the entire ﬂow over the baseline was separated, ﬂow over the
protuberance structures remained partly attached, allowing the higher lift observed for the wavy geometries
in this regime [9].
Though also employing a rectangular planform, the wavy wing geometry addressed by Zverkov et al.
[10] is distinct due to the design of wave humps extending along the entire chord of the wing, as opposed
to the previously leading-edge-conﬁned conﬁgurations. The investigation was concerned with the growth of
disturbances in the boundary layer on a classical and extended-wavy surface wing. A series of complement-
ing experiments including oil-ﬁlm visualization and measurements of pressure distributions and ﬂow velocity
was conducted at Re = 170.000 based on the airfoil TsAGIR-3a-12. The ﬂow characteristics of the classical
and wavy design were obtained and compared. The oil-ﬁlm visualization showed a separation bubble at
↵= 15◦and stall at increased angles for the classical wing, while separation bubbles were observed only
in the grooves of the wavy wing and stall occurred at ↵= 28◦. The ﬂow structure was observed to be
similar at ↵= 0◦and ↵= 15◦for both wings, and pressure and velocity measurements were carried out at
↵= 0◦. The measurements showed some similarities between the ﬂow of the classical wing and in the groove
of the wavy wing, while there was a signiﬁcant di↵erence to the ﬂow on the hump. Oil-ﬁlm visualization and
pressure distributions showed that laminar separation occurs on the classical wing and in the grooves of the
wavy wing, but not on the humps where the ﬂow remains attached. Hot-wire measurements indicated, that
the separation bubbles of the classical wing and wavy wing groove were of similar lengths, but separation,
subsequent transition and reattachment happened earlier for the groove. On the hump, transition was not
completed at 0.74c where measurements stopped. The complex wavy wing boundary layer should better
withstand high positive pressure gradients without turbulent boundary layer separation or bubble busting,
and it was therefore assumed that maximum lift and stall angle could be increased for ﬂow of Re < 200.000
by giving the wing a wavy span-wise surface, [10]. The achievement of signiﬁcant stall delay by the wavy
wing was noted by Zverkov et al, but no aerodynamic coeﬃcients, such as Cl or Cd, for performance evalu-
ation were measured, since focus was on investigating the boundary layer in detail.
In Hansen et al. [11] the airfoil proﬁles NACA 0021 and NACA 65-021 with various, sinusoidal leading edge

the baseline airfoil. On the other hand, this came at the expense of decreased lift performance in the prestall

Wings and wing sections with sinusoidal leading edges were later addressed numerically by Zhang and
Wu [12], Asli et al. [13] and Skillen et al. [14]. While Zhang and Wu employ an entire rotating wing, Asli
et al. and Skillen et al. consider rectangular wing sections.
Zhang and Wu [12] simulated a rotating NREL phase-VI blade, [15], with and without a sinusoidal leading
edge at various wind speeds, employing a range of leading edge wavelengths and amplitudes. At design con-
ditions, the wavy leading edge shape was shown to be unfavourable due to early separation resulting in shaft
torque reduction, but at high wind speeds the shaft torque was signiﬁcantly improved by the wavy leading
edge wing compared to the baseline. The enhanced performance was ascribed to greater peaks in suction
pressure brought about by vortex shedding from leading edge tubercles. On the contrary to other investi-
gations, beneﬁts were reported to increase with increasing amplitude and wavelength. Further, robustness
of power output was improved for the wavy blade from 10 to 20m/s when treated as a stall regulated turbine.
Asli et al. [13] numerically investigated 3D ﬂow over the wind turbine applicable s809 airfoil with and
without a sinusoidal leading edge using a SST k-! DES method at Reynolds number Re = 106. The prop-
erties of the sinusoidal leading edge were selected within the range typical for the humpback whale with
A = 0.025 and λ = 0.25. A comparison of the wavy wing against the baseline foil in terms of Cl and Cd
showed no improvement in lift and a slight increase in drag at low angles of attack. The stall trend of the
modiﬁed wing was seen to be smoother that baseline stall, but it can also be observed that this comes at the
expense of earlier stall and decreased maximum lift, Clmax. In the baseline poststall regime, the ﬂow over
the wavy wing remained attached due to the presence of vortices brought about by the leading edge bumps
acting as vortex generators, [13].
A numerical LES simulation of the ﬂow over a NACA 0021 wing with leading-edge undulations at ↵= 20◦
were performed by Skillen et al. [14] at a Reynolds number of Re = 120.000 equal to that of [11]. The
sinusoidal leading edge undulations had amplitude A = 0.015c and wavelength λ = 0.11c and the wing had
a rectangular planform. The angle of attack, ↵= 20◦, was selected to set a poststall condition and at this
angle a 58% increase in Cl and a 59% decrease in Cd was reported in comparison with a baseline wing.
The performance was improved due to a reduced area of ﬂow separation for the wavy wing compared to the

layer, were investigated and identiﬁed. First, the wavy leading edge geometry gives rise to a span-wise pres-

The study showed the e↵ect of a sinusoidal leading edge depended strongly on the planform geometry. For
the rectangular planform, large-amplitude protuberances showed softer stall characteristics, while medium
and small-amplitude protuberances revealed a more abrupt stall. On the other hand, only the small and
medium-amplitude with λ = 0.25c exhibited higher Clmax than the baseline model in prestall. In poststall
all models had Cl equal or less that the baseline value. The drag was always greater for the wavy wing
models, resulting in lift-to-drag-ratios of the wavy models being everywhere comparable to or lower than the
baseline value. Overall, leading edge protuberances did not enhance performance of the ﬁnite span airfoils
appreciably under the given conditions. The ﬂipper model revealed a 4◦range of reduced drag, yielding
increased lift-to-drag-ratio compared to the baseline. Lift of the wavy ﬂipper was comparable to the smooth
ﬂipper. The swept-leading-edge geometry was most a↵ected be the presence of a sinusoidal leading edge.
Drag for the wavy version was considerably higher at all angles and lift was reduced in the prestall regime.
In baseline poststall however, the swept-leading-edge wing did not stall and showed continues increase in lift.
In several articles the e↵ect of leading edge tubercles are considered to be similar to the e↵ect of vortex
generator, [5] [8] [11]. Another explanation is o↵ered by Nierop et al. [17], suggesting an aerodynamic
model where stall control e↵ects of leading edge tubercles are connected to alterations of the pressure dis-
tribution. The variation in chord for sections of similar thickness results in more adverse pressure gradients
in the short-chorded thoughs and causes separation to initiate there. At the peaks separation is delayed
due to a nonuniform downwash component, reducing the e↵ective angle of attack. This gives a spanwise
variation in local stall angle which leads to the decired gradual, global stall. The model successfully predicts
delayed stall and a ﬂattening of the lift curve with increasing tubercle amplitude in comparison with the
experimental results of [9].

1.3
This project and its objective
Inspired by the performance enhancing tubercle structures on the humpback whales ﬂipper and motivated
by the continuous need to improve aerodynamic eﬃciency of wind turbine blades, this project sets out to
investigate the aerodynamic potential of two blade sections with a span-wise wavy surface. The focus is
on application of the novel concept of wave pattern modiﬁcation of wings to conditions relevant in a wind
turbine context. This is done by selecting a rectangular planform based on an airfoil designed for wind
turbine purposes instead of directly mimicking the whale ﬂipper. Further, the ﬂow Reynolds number will
be set to a level of one million in order to imitate typical conditions for a large scale turbine as opposed to
adopting the considerably lower values of Re 5 · 105 addressed in most of the literature.
The study will be concerned with two wing sections with sinusoidal surface patterns as well as one smooth
baseline wing section. One wavy wing design will follow the concept adopted by [9], [11], [16], among others,
and the sinusoidal pattern will be shaped according to parameters considered most promising in the light
of the existing literature. The other wavy wing will be shaped by the same sinusoidal parameters, but the
wave pattern will be extended to continue towards the trailing edge in a fashion similar to that of Zverkov
et al. [10]. In [10] a signiﬁcant delay in stall angle is noted for this modiﬁcation, but its potential in terms
of lift and drag has not yet been investigated. These two particular designs are therefore considered in
the prospect that the modiﬁcations could improve their aerodynamic performance in comparison with the
smooth baseline model, and this is hence the primary object of investigation of this project.
The ﬂow investigations will be carried out through numerical simulations and the project will consist of
two phases, where the ﬁrst serves as necessary preparations for the latter. In the ﬁrst part, the numerical
scheme is established and tested is order to evaluate the results and validate the set up for use in the main
study. The second part is where the two wavy surface wings are designed and investigated by ﬂow simula-
tions. Presented in a little more detail, the main parts and goals of the two phases are:
1) To implement smooth wing sections, establish meshes and numerical schemes in the applied CFD
software in order to conduct a series of initial 2D validation cases. The test cases are carried out and
the results are compared to existing results for evaluation of the established scheme, especially in terms
of transition modelling. This is reported in Chapter 4.
2) To design one smooth baseline and two sinusoidal surface, 3D geometries in a CAD software, implement
them in accordance with the established numerical scheme and carry out numerical ﬂow simulations,
in order to conduct a comparative study of the wavy wing performances against the performance of
the smooth baseline wing. The details and results are reported in Chapter 5.
Before getting to these parts, however, a few fundamental, theoretical aspects of relevance for the study
are considered in Charter 2 while the implementation of the numerical scheme and the applied methods are
outlined in Chapter 3. Discussion and recommendations follow later in Chapter 6 and 7.

2.2
Boundary layer transition, pressure gradients and separation
In an external ﬂow as the one around an airfoil, the boundary layer develops freely and might be laminar
or turbulent depending on the ﬂow Reynolds number, Re = ⇢Uc
µ , where ⇢is density, U is the free stream
velocity, c is the chord and µ is dynamic viscosity. Above a critical Reynolds number, Recr, which depends

of changing from laminar to turbulent ﬂow is known as transition, and the ﬂow is said to transition from
laminar to turbulent ﬂow. The critical Reynolds indicating the occurrence of transition varies greatly with
conditions, but as a pointer the value for a smooth, ﬂat plate boundary layer is around

laminar boundary layer, because turbulent ﬂuctuations increase the transport of wall-normal momentum
compared to the transport caused alone by steady viscous di↵usion in laminar boundary layer ﬂow. Another

2.3
Brief on passive ﬂow control devices
When an airfoil goes into stall, the lift decreases and form drag increases, and delaying stall will therefore
improve airfoil performance. Several passive ﬂow control techniques like leading edge slots and vortex gener-
ators have been addressed previously for this purpose. Passive ﬂow control mechanisms consist of modifying
a wing shape or adding a device to it, in order to manipulate the ﬂow in a preferable manner. By ’passive’
ﬂow control is meant that no auxiliary power is required, as opposed to active ﬂow control techniques, [3].
The mentioned vortex generator is an additional vane attached to the lifting surface in order to create a
streamwise vortex and enhance mixing within the boundary layer [20]. The leading edge slot is a gap behind
the leading edge of the wing, allowing air from below to accelerate through the slot to the upper side and
mix with the existing boundary layer there. In both cases, the idea is to enhance lift and delay stall to higher
angles of attack, by keeping the ﬂow attached to the surface at points where it would normally separate.
By mixing high momentum particles from elsewhere in the ﬂow with the decelerated near-wall ﬂow, the
shape of the velocity proﬁle is modiﬁed to be more ‘full’ and have an increased velocity gradient at the wall.
This modiﬁcation helps to overcome the retarding e↵ect of existing adverse pressure gradients and hence to
prevent separation and stall.
The drawback of the near-wall momentum addition methods is however, that the mixing techniques in-
clude a penalty in drag and the tripping of transition from laminar to turbulent ﬂow upstream of the
potential, laminar separation point. The experienced drag penalty is mainly due to the device itself and
to the increase in skin friction drag for a turbulent boundary layer compared to a laminar boundary layer,
[3]. Even though this increase in drag might be preferable when compared to the increase in form drag and
loss of lift associated with separation and stall, avoiding it or minimizing it would improve the aerodynamic
eﬃciency of the airfoil. This conclusion motivates the idea of improving airfoil performance by creating an
alternative modiﬁcation of the airfoil, which would provide the mechanisms necessary to increase lift and
delay stall without or with a minimum of associated drag penalty.

Models and implementation
The investigations conducted for this project will be numerical ﬂow simulations performed in the CFD
software STAR CCM+ developed by CD-adapco. The STAR CCM+ program is based on the ﬁnite volume method
and uses unstructured meshes and solvers in order to solve the governing equations of ﬂuid ﬂow. It allows for
import of geometries created in suitable CAD (Computer Aided Design) tools such as CREO Parametric 3.0

As mentioned, the numerical grid, or mesh, is a discrete representation of the computational domain on
which the given problem will be solved. The STAR CCM+ program uses mainly unstructured grids and sup-
plies a range of mesh types for volume meshing. The mesh type applied to the domains constructed for this
project is of the type Trimmer mesh, which generates a volume mesh by cutting or ’trimming’ a hexahe-
dral template mesh using the input geometry surface. It should provide a robust and eﬃcient method for
producing a high-quality grid and is one of the recommended mesh settings for incompressible, external aero-
dynamic ﬂows with small variation in ﬂow direction, [22]. It allows for grid reﬁnement based upon surface
mesh size and user deﬁned reﬁnement controls, options which will be exploited during the meshing processes
of this study. In general, the procedure of setting the grid conditions starts with selecting a base size, which
this grid will adapt to in regions where no other reﬁnement is speciﬁed. Then surface and volume reﬁnement
controls can be added at choice. The rate at which the cells change size from the condition speciﬁed in one
area to another, the volume growth rate, is set to Very Slow in order to best capture the ﬁeld properties.
Since a proper resolution of the boundary layer near the wall surface is critical, a so-called prism layer
with orthogonal, prismatic cells next to the wall surfaces is added to the core mesh. The prism layer is set in
terms of its thickness, the number of cell layers and the size distribution in form of a stretch factor. These
parameters are selected in a manner to ensure wall y+ values of y+ 1, which allows the assumption that
the viscous sublayer is resolved. The wall treatment can hence be set to ’low’, since no wall functions are
needed, [22].

external ﬂow where the airfoil geometries have been subtracted. The given airfoil geometry is ﬁrst con-

coupled manner by linking the momentum and continuity equations with a predictor-corrector approach. It
is suitable for constant density ﬂow and has the advantage of requiring less memory and hence computational
resources.[22]
3.2
Turbulence and transition modelling
At the mentioned Reynolds numbers of order Re ⇠106, the encountered ﬂows are expected to be turbulent or
experience transition to turbulence. It is therefore necessary to consider turbulence and transition modelling
for the ﬂow simulations and how these can be incorporated with the applied CFD tool, STAR CCM+.
3.2.1
Turbulence modelling and the SST k-! model
A turbulent ﬂow instantaneously satisﬁes the Navier-Stokes equations, but resolving the enormous range
of length and time scales present in a turbulent ﬂow is almost virtually impossible. It is done in Direct
Numerical Simulation (DNS), the most accurate approach to turbulence, but for most purposes this is by far
too expensive in terms of computational resources and therefore other methods have to be considered. There
are approximate methods such as Large Eddy Simulation (LES), where the large ﬂow scales are solved while
the small are modelled, and the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS) where the equations are
averaged under the assumption of being statistically stable, [21]. For the study at hand, the RANS approach
is adopted since the main interest is with the pressure distributions and forces on the inserted geometry and
not so much with the unsteady features of the wake, which would require an LES approach or similar.
The RANS equations are obtained by decomposing the instantaneous ﬁeld quantities into the sum of two
components; one representing the mean value and one representing the ﬂuctuation about the mean value.
This is then substituted into the Navier-Stokes equations and these are averaged to yield the Reynolds-
averaged Navier-Stokes equations

three other eddy viscosity models, the Spalart and Allmaras model, the k-✏model and RNG model. Evalu-

gave the best agreement with experimental data for these ﬂow types, [27]. This further indicates this model
is a reasonable choice for the ﬂow type at hand, and this will be the turbulence model applied during this
study.
3.2.2
Transition modelling with the γ-Re✓model
The application of the SST k-! model will allow for modelling of turbulent ﬂow, but at the considered
Reynolds numbers of order ⇠106 it is expected that ﬂow could be initially laminar and then transition to
turbulent ﬂow. This process if often ignored, but can be important for the aerodynamic performance and it
is therefore desirable to utilize a transition model to account for this e↵ect, [28]. Models requiring evaluation
of non-local quantities can not be used with STAR CCM+ since the non-local quantities can not be evaluated
on unstructured grids. This is due to the lack of a good infrastructure for global boundary layer intergration
in unstructured grids where grid lines normal to the surface are not easily identiﬁed. Instead STAR CCM+
o↵ers a correlation-based transition model, the γ-Re✓transition model, strictly based on local variables,
making it compatible with unstructured grid, [22].
As indicated by the name, the γ-Re✓model formulation is based on two transport equations: one is an
intermittency equation for triggering the transition onset and the other is constructed to avoid non-local
operations and is formulated in terms of the transition onset Reynolds number, Re✓t. The idea of the model
is to relate the local momentum thickness Reynolds number Re✓to a critical value Re✓c and then switch
on the intermittency production, when Re✓exceeds the local critial value. The transport equations do not
attempt to model the physics of the transition process, as opposed to the turbulence models, but form a
framework for implementation of correlations-based models into CFD methods. The physics of the transi-
tion phenomenon is instead hoped to be captured in the experimental correlations provided as a part of the
model, [29].
The intermittency function, γ, is set to one in the turbulent free stream and zero in fully laminar ﬂow.
The production term of the intermittency transport equation is triggered by a function formulated in terms
of the already local vorticity Reynolds number, Rev and the critical momentum thickness Reynolds number,
Re✓c. Switching on the production term means an increase of the intermittency in the boundary layer, which
occurs upstream of the transition Reynolds number, Re✓t. The critical Reynolds number, Re✓c, along with
another virtual parameter, however, depends on the local transition onset momentum thickness Reynolds
number g
Re✓t. In general, the Reynolds number of transition onset, Re✓t, is experimentally correlated to free
stream quantities such as turbulence intensity and evaluating it for the intermittency in the boundary layer

imum lift of Cl,max ' 1.5. The Mapflow and HMB2 codes both predict a later stall and a higher maximum
Cl value of Cl,max ' 1.7. The measurement results on the other hand, show a small decrease in Cl already
from 10◦to 12◦, followed by an increase to the same level as before from 12◦to 15◦, before decreasing again
at 20◦. It is not evident whether this behaviour is due to an experimental issue or it reﬂects the nature of
the ﬂow. In either case, there is reasonable agreement of CCM+ results with the experimental values, and this
along with the consistency with the EllipSys2D serves as a validation of the Cl results. It can also be noted
that the CCM+ results for fully turbulent and transitional ﬂow in the linear Cl region agree for the lowest
angles, but the slope of Cl obtained with transition is steeper than for fully turbulent ﬂow.
Figure 4.2b shows Cl as function of Cd revealing the so called drag bucket. The results of the EllipSys2D
code seems to best capture the bucket shape of the experimental results, while the Mapflow and CCM+ tran-
sitional results show a more rounded, parabolic behaviour. For the transitional CCM+ results this results
in a slight underestimation of drag at the smallest angles and a over-prediction at higher angles, but the
results are still in acceptable compliance with the experimental results and the results of the EllipSys2D
code. The Mapflow generally slightly overestimates the drag, while HMB2 with transition notably overpre-
dicts. Especially important to notice here however, is the signiﬁcant di↵erence i drag prediction between
fully turbulent and transitional ﬂow for the CCM+ code. The prediction of the CCM+ fully turbulent ﬂow has a
nearly parabolic shape and the drag level is clearly overestimated. This is expected, since it treats the whole
ﬂow as fully turbulent and the wall shear stress associated with turbulent ﬂow is larger than for laminar
ﬂow. The considerably better agreement of the CCM+ transitional ﬂow with experimental and Ellipsys2D
results points to the relevance of considering the inclusion of a transition model.

4.4
Summary
The test case investigations served as a validation of the numerical scheme in CCM+ and especially the γ-Re✓
transition model by comparison to established results. Here, a short wrap up and conclusion on the three
cases is done
1) In the ﬁrst case, the ﬂow over the airfoil DU212 was simulated at Re = 3 · 106 and compared to results
of other codes as well as an experimental result. In terms of lift prediction, a good agreement with
all parts was observed in the linear range while in the stall area results continuously coincided with
EllipSys results but predicted higher stall angle and maximum lift than was observed experimentally.
For the drag predictions is was clear that inclusion of the γ-Re✓transition model improved agreement
with experimental results signiﬁcantly compared to fully turbulent calculations. A similar, but less
critical, tendency was observed for the pressure distribution, Cp, where the transitional ﬂow better
captured the details of the distribution. All in all, the turbulent and transitional ﬂows simulated in
CCM+ were validated against experimental results in terms of key aerodynamic coeﬃcients and it was
clear that inclusion of a transition was necessary in order to correctly predict the drag coeﬃcient and
capture the details of the pressure distribution.
2) In the second case, fully turbulent and transitional ﬂow over the DU240 arifoil was calculated at
Re = 20 · 106 and compared to corresponding EllipSys results.
A good agreement between the
results of the CCM+ and EllipSys codes was observed for fully turbulent ﬂow, despite a slightly higher
prediction of the drag by CCM+. For transitional ﬂow the agreement is not as consistent, since the drag
predicted by CCM+ is notably higher than that of EllipSys. This is ascribed to the tendency of the
γ-Re✓model to predict too early transition points and hence considerably overestimate the drag at
very high Reynolds numbers. Since this seems to be a high Reynolds number issue, however, it will
not be of concern for the later cases.
3) In the third case, the ﬂow over the NACA-64-415 airfoil is simulated for fully turbulent and transitional
ﬂow at Reynolds number Re = 1 · 106 and the results are compared to corresponding XFOIL results.
A good agreement was observed in terms of lift results for both turbulent and transitional ﬂow, while
a di↵erence in drag predictions was seen at positive angles of attack in both cases where CCM+ predict
higher values than XFOIL. Despite this there is still a reasonable agreement in terms of shape and
values for the drag predictions. Further, a comparison of transition points, showed coinciding point
locations in most of the cases. All together, these elements combined serves as validation of the CCM+
transition model, γ-Re✓, at the given Reynolds number of one million, which will be adopted in the
main investigation.
In general, small di↵erences and deviations between CCM+ and established results are not of great concern and
will not be investigated further, since the ﬁnal study is of comparative nature, where all models and conditions

Chapter 5
Investigation of wavy blade sections
In this chapter the e↵ects of adding a sinusoidal surface to a S809 airfoil blade section are investigated
through numerical ﬂow simulations. Two di↵erent wave geometries will be addressed, and the obtained
results will be compared to the results of a classical, unmodiﬁed blade section, referred to as the baseline
proﬁle. The ﬁrst wave geometry is characterized by primarily having a leading-edge-conﬁned wave pattern
in accordance with the concept addressed in [9], [11] and [14] among other, while the second geometry has a
wave shape extenting towards the trailing edge similar to the design of [10]. For convenience, the two wavy
wing shapes will occasionally by referred to as ’wavy front’ (WF) and ’wavy back’ (WB), respectively. For
illustration an early glance can be cast at Figure 5.1 and 5.2. The planform of all three blade sections is
rectangular to avoid any dependency of results on a speciﬁc planform shape, such as the ﬂipper model by
[5]. The Reynolds number for all ﬂow simulations is set to Re = 106 to imitate conditions for large scale
wind turbines, as opposed to the lower values of Re 5 · 105 adopted in literature due to other purposes or
experimental capacity limitations.
First, the airfoil model and the wavy wing designs will be introduced, then a short validation against
existing data is reported and ultimately the results in terms of aerodynamic coeﬃcients and other relevant
parameters are presented and compared.
5.1
The airfoil model
The airfoil chosen as the base for the blade section designs is the S809 airfoil, a part of the S-series airfoils
created for application in horizontal axis wind turbines by NREL in cooperation with Airfoils Incoperated.
It has thickness 21%, design Reynolds number, Re = 2 · 106, and it was designed for the primary part of
a wind turbine blade [41]. The S809 shape is cambered as opposed to most of the airfoils addressed in the
literature, which were symmetric NACA airfoils chosen for their resemblance with the cross-section of the
humpback whale ﬂipper. Fish and Battle [6] noted a strong similarity of the cross-section to the symmetric
NACA 634-021 airfoil, later employed by Watt et al. [7], Johari et al. [9] and Custodio et al. [16]. Symmetric
NACA airfoils were also chosen by Miklosovic et al. [5] with NACA 0020 as well as Hansen et al. [11] and
Skillen et al. [14] with NACA 0021, due to their matching properties with the whale ﬂipper cross-section.
Hansen et al. [11] also addressed the symmetric NACA 65-021 airfoil for comparison due to its maximum
thickness being more aft on the chord. Even though the NACA airfoils nicely resemble the ﬂipper cross
section, they were designed for applications in airplane technology rather than wind turbine technology
[42]. Recently however, the wind turbine applicable S809 proﬁle was addressed by Zhang and Wu [12] and
Asli et al [13], which both indicated that improved performance could be achieved with sinusoidal leading
edge above design conditions, i.e. at high wind speeds or angles of attack. Further, it is noted, that the
maximum thickness of 21% located at 0.4c for the S809 proﬁle matches the corresponding properties of the
cross-section-resembling NACA 634-021 proﬁle employed in several studies. The S809 airfoil is therefore also
41
==PAGE:45==
selected for the current study, especially due to its relevance in wind turbine applications.
5.2
Design of wavy surface geometries
The inspirational tubercles of the humpback whale ﬂipper are actually distinct structures, but the pattern
is generally modelled as a continues sinusoidal wave modiﬁcation in the existing literature. This approach is
convenient in relation to research since it favours systematization, comparability and reproducibility, aspects
that, along with simplicity, are desirable in a engineering or manufacturing context as well. Therefore, the
wavy surface geometry will also be modelled as a sinusoidal wave pattern in this study, where the perspective
is wind turbine applications. The wave shape is characterised by an amplitude, A, and a wavelength, λ, and
the applied values of A and λ were selected based on the following observations from the literature presented
in section 1.2.
The e↵ects of the amplitude, A, and wavelength, λ, of the sinusoidal wave structure on foil performance were
investigated during the experiments of Johari et al. [9], Hansen et al. [11], and Custodio et al. [16]. The topic
was ﬁrst addressed by Johari et al. [9] who observed that the maximum lift, Clmax, and the lift slope, dCl
d↵, in
the linear regime increased with decreasing tubercle amplitude, while the e↵ect of wavelength variation was
found to be minor, since shorter wavelength was associated with only slightly higher Clmax and lower drag.
Considering the e↵ect of the amplitude, Hansen et al. [11] similarly concluded that reducing tubercle ampli-
tude led to higher maximum lift and larger stall angle, but concerning the wavelength found improved lift
properties with decreased wavelength, until a certain point for the given airfoil. Further, Custodio et al. [16]
essentially investigated the same foils as [9] at a higher Reynolds number and evaluated the e↵ect of amplitude
and wavelength in relation to span-eﬃciency. They showed, that larger amplitudes resulted in more signiﬁcant
reduction in span-eﬃciency, while the e↵ect of the wavelength was found to be minor. These accounts gener-
ally point to smaller amplitude values being more beneﬁcial, while wavelength is suggested small or considered
insigniﬁcant. It should be noted however, that the trend is not unambiguous since large amplitude geometries
might o↵er other qualities such as smoother drag or increased performance in extreme poststall. Despite
these considerations, following the observed trend leads to the values of A and λ listed in Table 5.1 as the most
favourable conﬁguration parameters. The values employed and reported by Skillen et al. [14] and Zverkov
et al. [10], who both showed improved performance for the investigated wavy geometries, are also listed.

larger or small chord, but it was shown by Johari et al. [9] that the di↵erence in performance between this
and adding it onto the baseline was negligible.

5.3
Set up and validation of baseline blade section
The three constructed blade sections; baseline, WF and WB, were implemented in CCM+ in accordance with
the numerical scheme and models described in Chapter 3 and tested in Chapter 4. The physical condi-
tions were again set corresponding to a temperature of 15◦C with air density ⇢= 1.225kg/m3 and dynamic
viscosity µ = 1.785 · 10−5kg/(m·s). Computational domains were constructed such that the distance from
the given foil was 6c to the inlets and 15c to the outlet, and the inlet velocity magnitude was then set to
U = 14.57m/s in accordance with the Reynolds number of Re = 106. In order to establish a suitable mesh
providing grid independent results, an extensive grid study was conducted using the baseline blade section.
Unfortunately, since the S809 airfoil has a sharp trailing edge, the prism layer is prevented from extending
around the trailing edge, as it was also the case for the the NACA 64-415 airfoil in section 4.3. Therefore
the approach of introducing a very reﬁned trailing edge reﬁnement region was again adopted in the grid
44
==PAGE:48==
study process. After selecting an appropriate grid it was checked that the grid settings could be applied to
the wavy surface geometries and still provide grid independent results. The entire grid study for the three
dimensional S809 baseline blade section is available in Appendix B. The established grid consists of a total of
9216687 cells with a typical wall face size of 4mm and 143105 surface cell faces, corresponding to more than
250 cell faces in both the x and the z-direction on the pressure and suction side of the airfoil, respectively.
Normal to the wall the prism layer is about 10mm thick and the smallest cell height in the prism layer is
of order 2.3 · 10−5m ensuring y+ values of y+ < 1 within the prism layer area. Since the prism layer ends
abruptly before the trailing edge, the last row of cells before the edge are of order 1mm, unavoidably yielding
higher y+ values of up to y+ ' 25. The boundary layer can hence be assumed to be resolved in the prism
layer area, but not in the last cell row which, however, has a negligible size compared to the whole geometry.

5.4
The resulting aerodynamic coeﬃcients
The ﬂow over each of the three blade sections were simulated for both fully turbulent and transitional ﬂow
in CCM+ for an angle of attack range of ↵= [−2◦, 24◦]. The aerodynamic coeﬃcients obtained from the ﬂow
over each foil can then be compared in order to evalutate their performances. Simulations were conducted
as steady until stall of the given foil, where a coinciding overlap with unsteady results was ensured before
continuing with unsteady simulations. All simulations were run until convergence on a speciﬁc or periodi-
cally oscillating value, which required about 5000 iterations at small angles of attack and up to 25.000 at
the high angles of attack. Unsteady simulations were conducted with a time step of 0.001s generally provid-
ing Courant numbers1 between zero and one except especially in the problematic area at the trailing edge,
missing a prism layer. The run time of a steady simulation was of order 10 hour wall-clock time2 on average,
while it was 30 hours for unsteady simulations. At high angles of attack, small ﬂuctuations and periodic
oscillations started appearing in drag and especially lift results. Since the simulations were unsteady at this
point, the behaviour is assumed to be of physical nature, and lift and drag coeﬃcients were found as the
average of the values in the stabilized regime. At some point in ↵, however, the oscillations became radical
and it was no longer reasonable to express the result as one distinct lift value. In order to set a speciﬁc,
limiting criteria the standard deviations was calculated along with the mean values, and lift force results
displaying a standard deviation in lift corresponding to more than 5% of general force values were omitted.
This happened earlier for the baseline blade section at ↵= 19◦than for the wavy WF and WB sections, but
at ↵= 24◦all foils had reached an unstable behaviour.
Since the transitional CCM+ ﬂow simulations repeatedly have shown better agreement with existing (in-
cluding experimental) results than the simulations of fully turbulent ﬂow, especially at moderate Reynolds
numbers, the focus will be on transitional ﬂow results. Last in this section however, a comparison is made
between the results of fully turbulent and transitional ﬂow results. The aerodynamic coeﬃcients obtained
from the transitional ﬂow simulations over the three investigated blade sections are now presented in Figure

5.5
Pressure, skin friction and intermittency distributions
To gain more insight in the ﬂow characteristics and hopefully cast some light on the occasionally very di↵er-
ent performance of the three investigated blade sections, distributions of pressure, Cp, skin friction, Cf, and
intermittency γ, on the foil surfaces are considered. For this purpose three angles of attack, ↵, were selected,
at which considerable di↵erences in foil performances were observed. The chosen angles are ↵= {0◦, 5◦, 10◦}.
At the two smaller angles ↵= {0◦, 5◦} the performance of the baseline and WF blade sections are similar,
but at ↵= 0◦the WB foil shows better results than the other foils while it performs considerably worse
than the other blade sections at ↵= 5◦. Finally, at angle of attack ↵= 10◦the WF foil is in stall and
its performance is worse than baseline, but still better than the performance of the WB foil. All results
presented in this section were obtained from simulations of transitional ﬂow.
The distribution plots were obtained by extracting pressure, shear stress and intermittency on the sur-
face of each of the three blade sections at the given angles of attack, and in the case of pressure and shear
normalizing them to get the pressure coeﬃcient Cp and skin friction coeﬃcient, Cf. Since the mesh, and

hence the data, is unstructured the distribution results had to by plotted as 3D scatter plots showing the
parameter value at each point using a color indicator, but due to the extensive amount of data points, the
resulting plots are practically surface color plots. As an example of the constructed 3D plots, the pressure
distribution, Cp, for the WB blade section at ↵= 0◦is shown in Figure 5.8. The stagnation line is clearly seen
on the front were Cp = 1 and then Cp gradually decreases until around x = 0.5c where is starts increasing
towards the trailing edge. Plots of this type give a nice overview, but provide limited insight in the details

Distributions at angle of attack ↵= 0◦
First, the distributions obtained from ﬂow simulations at ↵= 0◦are presented in Figure 5.9 showing the
pressure and skin friction distributions and in Figure 5.10 showing intermittency and constrained stream-

consulted in Appendix C.
Considering the top views of the pressure distributions in the left column of Figure 5.9, a stagnation line
can generally be observed on the foil fronts (left side) followed by a shared behaviour among all three foils
of decreasing pressure until just above midway on the chord where pressure start increasing. The pressure
distributions of the baseline and WF foils are very similar, mainly di↵ering at the point of reaching in-
creasing pressure, where the baseline pressure front consists of a straight line while the WF pressure front
displays a more blurry, wave-a↵ected front line. Despite sharing the aforementioned, main features, the
behaviour of the pressure on the WB foil di↵ers substantially from the baseline and WF pressures in the
range x = [0.2, 0.5]c. In this region, a considerably lower pressure is established in the grooves of the WB foil,
while the pressure on the humps is comparable to baseline and WF pressure. Looking into the skin friction

tendencies of decreasing skin friction until the mid-chord area where very low values are observed before
increased values are seen again in the adverse pressure gradient region. A largely opposite behaviour is seen
for the WB foil skin friction where values are high on the ﬁrst half chord and then very low on the second half.
Further insight in the ﬂow can be gained from the intermittency distributions and constrained streamlines
in the left and right columns of Figure 5.10, respectively. For the baseline and WF foil a distinct increase in

the jump in WF foil intermittency, it is noted that the increase happens ﬁrst in locations behind the troughs

might be due to the presence of the humps simply providing more wall surface and hence more drag, but the
values on the second half are relatively low even compared to the laminar regions of the baseline and WF foils.
The lower pressure level obtained at the upper side of the WB foil compared to the baseline and WF
foils along with the fully attached ﬂow on the WB humps, could be indicators to why the WB foil experi-
ences a higher lift at the present angle of attack, ↵= 0◦. Generally, for the two wavy foils, the areas behind
the trough are associated with less drag and earlier occurrence of transition, as opposed to the areas behind
peaks where transition occurs later or not at all.

5.6
Summary
In order to summarize the considerations and results presented in this chapter, ﬁve points presenting each
of the subsections are listed with key points to note from the respective parts.
1) The s809 airfoil proﬁle was selected as the base for the investigated blade sections due to its similarities
with tested, ﬂipper-resembling proﬁles and especially due to its relevance in a wind turbine context.
2) Three blade section were designed using 3D CAD, including one baseline and to wavy surface blade
sections. The ﬁrst wavy surface design consisted of a sinusoidal, leading-edge-conﬁned wave pattern
(WF) while the second employed an extension of the pattern towards the trailing edge (WB). The
adopted amplitude A = 0.025 and wavelength λ = 0. were selected in accordance with observations of
beneﬁcial values and trends in the existing literature.
3) Comparison of fully turbulent and transitional CCM+ ﬂow results of this project against the experimental
results of Somers [42] and Ramsay [43] as well as corresponding Ellipsys results, showed agreement
in the overall lift behaviour of results, despite the higher lift level calculated by both ﬂows of this
project in the range ↵= [7◦, 13◦]. The transitional ﬂow results were seen to coincide with existing
results for drag and linear lift, while the fully turbulent results displayed lower lift slope and over-
estimation of drag. This hence served as validation of the transitional ﬂow results in accordance with
earlier observations of more reliable performance compared to fully turbulent ﬂow, and focus will thus
be on results of the transitional ﬂow simulations while the fully turbulent will only be consulted for
comparison.
54
==PAGE:58==
4) The aerodynamic coeﬃcients for the transitional ﬂow over each of the three investigated blade sections
were extracted and compared to evaluate the aerodynamic performance of the respective blade sections.
The WF blade section was observed to be comparable to baseline in terms of both lift and drag until
↵= 9◦where the WF foil went into stall and lift started decreasing to levels 30-40% below baseline
while drag increased and at the worst exceeded baseline by 100%. Resultantly, the lift-to-drag ratio
of the WF foil never showed improved values compared to baseline. The WB blade section displayed
a higher level of lift compared to baseline and the WF foil in a short, small-angle range ↵= [−2◦, 3◦]
with up to 50% in the positive range, but then went into an early, temporary stall before lift increased
again from ↵= 4◦at a much lower rate. Drag of the WB blade section was everywhere higher than
corresponding baseline and WF values and reached extreme di↵erences around ↵= 7◦with 400%
higher drag than baseline. Improved lift-to-drag ratios were therefore only seen for the WB foil up
until ↵= 1◦with comparable value at ↵= 2◦and lower, decreasing values in the remaining range.
A comparison of the transitional ﬂow results of the WF and WB blade sections to corresponding
turbulent results showed that lower lift and higher drag was experienced by both foils in turbulent ﬂow
compared to transitional ﬂow, in accordance with baseline observations and general expectations due
to earlier observations. It was noted that the improved linear ↵range for the WB foil was not present
for turbulent ﬂow.
5) The consideration of distributions of pressure, skin friction and intermittency as well as constrained
streamlines on the baseline, WF and WB foils at ↵= {0◦, 5◦, 10◦} revealed similarities and di↵erences
in the nature of the respective ﬂows. At ↵= 0◦the baseline and WF ﬂows were observed to be largely
similar, but the di↵erence in ﬂow behaviour was seen to increase with increasing angles of attack. The
ﬂow over the WB foil deviated from the other ﬂows at all angles of attack, especially with the formation
of span-wise vortex-like structure at ↵= 5◦. A similar vortex appearance was also observed for the
WF foil at ↵= 10◦where it had entered stall conditions. The superior aerodynamic performance of
the WB foil at ↵= 0◦was associated with numerically higher suction side pressure and fully attached
groove streamlines. On the other hand, the appearance of the span-wise vortices were associated with
reduction in aerodynamic eﬃciency of the WB foil at ↵= {5◦, 10◦} as well as the WF foil at ↵= 10◦.

Chapter 6
Discussion of results
In this chapter the obtained results and observations are discussed in the light of the expectations prior to
this project, as well as in the light of the existing results in the presented literature.
A fundamental matter addressed during the establishment of the numerical scheme for the investigations
of this project, was the application of a transitional model which for the employed CFD program, CCM+,
was the correlation-based γ-Re✓transition model. Comparison to established numerical and experimental
results in the ﬁrst 2D test case in section 4.1 as well as in the 3D validation of the baseline blade section
in section 5.3, showed that ﬂow simulations utilizing the transitions model better captured the aerodynamic
coeﬃcients and ﬂow characteristics compared to the fully turbulent ﬂow simulations, especially in terms of
drag. This is in accordance with expectations, since application of a transition model allows formation of
initially laminar ﬂow, resulting in lower drag than for corresponding turbulent ﬂow. An investigation of the
performance of the γ-Re✓transition model by comparison with Ellipsys results at high Reynolds number in
section 4.2 however, showed limited ability of the the γ-Re✓transition model to correctly predict transition
locations and drag. This was in agreement with the observations of [40], that the γ-Re✓model predicts early
transition points and too high drag at high Reynolds numbers. The main investigation of this project was
conducted at a more moderate Reynolds number of Re = 106, at which reasonably good agreement between
transitional results of CCM+ and correspoding XFOIl results was found in section 4.3. This ﬁts well with the
observations of [28] and [40] that good agreement of γ-Re✓model results with measurements can be obtained
at moderate Reynolds number. The γ-Re✓transition model was therefore employed in the ﬁnal study and
the minor short comings of the model at the given ﬂow conditions were not of major concern, since the study
was of comparative nature where addressed blade sections were implemented in the same numerical scheme.
The aerodynamic coeﬃcients resulting from the main investigations of the ﬂow over the three designed
blade sections: the baseline, the leading-edge-conﬁned wavy surface foil (WF) and the extended wavy sur-
face foil (WB), generally showed lower performance of the wavy surface foils compared to the baseline. The
leading-edge-conﬁned wave pattern (WF) was at the best comparable to baseline both in terms of lift, drag
and lift-to-drag ratio. The extended-wave foil (WB) displayed a limited, small-angle range of increased lift
compared to the baseline, resulting in increased lift-to-drag ratio despite increased drag for this region. In
the remaining range however, the WB foil showed signiﬁcantly lower performance than the baseline for both
lift and drag, with the lift-to-drag ratio decreasing throughout the remaining range.
Both wavy foils were associated with comparable or higher drag in the entire angle of attack range,
and especially the WB foil displayed very high drag values. Some increase in drag was expected due to
the presence of the tubercles which unavoidably provide more no-slip wall area, but the increases in drag
observed were quite signiﬁcant, in particular for the WB foil. The considerably increased drag is obviously
a drawback for these particular foils, but it also challenges the fundamental idea of the wavy surface design
concept to increase or maintain lift with low penalty in terms of drag. Increased drag was also observed by
[9] and [11] in the non-linear part of the prestall regime, and by [16] and [13] in the entire non-linear pre-
62
==PAGE:66==
and poststall regime. Only [16] showed lift-to-drag ratio results, but in that case it was clearly seen that
even a considerable improve in lift is easily neutralized by increased drag when it comes to the lift-to-drag
ratio performance. Poststall drag decrease was reported by [5] and [11], but it noted that [5] employed a
ﬂipper-planform model and [11] operated at a very low Reynolds number of 120.000.
The hope that the wavy surface foils would delay stall and maintain or even increase maximum lift as
it was reported by [5] was not observed for the wavy blade sections designed during this project. On the
contrary, the modiﬁed, wavy foils showed considerably worse stall properties than the baseline foil. The
WF stalled slightly early to the baseline and much more abruptly, while the WB foil stalled temporarily
at a very early stage before employing a slow lift increase behaviour. Apart from [5] employing a ﬂipper
planform, only [16] reported a few cases of increased maximum lift, so this could hardly be expected in the
current study. In terms of stall behaviour, [9], [11] and [13] reported several cases of smoothened stall and
signiﬁcantly improved poststall lift, at the expense of decreased maximum lift value. Neither smoothened
stall or improved poststall performance was however observed for the wavy foils during the current study,
but it should also be noted at this point that the employed S809 airfoil proﬁle already had a very docile stall
behaviour.
Having discussed the lift and drag performance of the wavy surface foils in relation to literature and expec-
tations, the two foil sections are considered individually. Taking the extended-wave (WB) foil ﬁrst, it was
inspired by the design employed by [10] who reported a signiﬁcant increase in stall angle and favourable
boundary layer properties for the wavy wing at zero angle of attack. In the present study, a provisional
stall was observed for the WB foil at 3-4◦, but after this point lift increased in the remaining range without
providing a ﬁnal stall angle in the traditional sense of the word. This seems to be in reasonable agreement
with the result of [10] where ↵stall was increased from 14◦to 28◦. The improved stall angle found by [10] is
above the range where lift and drag properties could be determined during this study. Further, it is noted
that improved performance of the WB foil was observed at the numerically small angles of attack, while [10]
found the boundary layer behaviour of the wavy foil to be favourable compared to the baseline at zero angle
of attack. It should be mentioned however that even though the employed planform, wave amplitude, A,
and wavelength, λ are comparable between this study and [10], the Reynolds number of the latter is only
170.000 and the investigations are therefore not directly comparable. Since [10] did not measure the lift and
drag forces, the aerodynamic coeﬃcients obtained in this study stand alone, pointing to WB design being
beneﬁcial in a very limited small-angle range, while being highly unfavourable in the remaining range.
Next, considering the leading-edge-conﬁned wavy blade section (WF), it was created in accordance with
the design concept primarily addressed in the literature; [9], [11], [14] among others.
The aerodynamic
performance of the WF foil investigated in this study turned out to be comparable to or less favourable
than the baseline foil in the entire range, while other studies have reported di↵erent levels of improvement
of sinusoidal leading edge foils compared to baseline. As mentioned in section 1.2.1 there are, however,
several parameters possibily a↵ecting performance which di↵er between reported studies. Two of these are
the amplitude and wavelength of the sinusoidal wave pattern, which were shown by [9] and [11] among others
to inﬂuence foil performance. The values employed in this study were selected in accordance with trends
and observations of beneﬁcial values in the literature as discussed in section 5.2, but it can not directly be
excluded that other values of amplitude and wavelength would improve performance to some extend.
Another important, fundamental parameter is the planform which to this point can be rectangular, swept
or a ﬂipper model. Though the rectangular planform is the most addressed in the collected literature, it
is noted that the most unambiguously positive results are those of [5] where a ﬂipper-imitating model was
compared to a similar, smoothened model. The results of [16] also showed that the ﬂipper-model beneﬁted
from a sinusoidal leading in a certain angle of attack range, while the rectangular planform models at the
same Reynolds number showed reduced performance or at the best, insigniﬁcant improvement. This could
point to that favourable 3D and/or tip e↵ects as those reported by [8] being present in ﬂipper model ﬂow
are lost when a ﬁnite rectangular planform is employed, as also discussed by Fish et al. [44] where wings
without tip formation are expected to showed delayed stall, but not increased lift or reduced drag.
A
63
==PAGE:67==
noted fundamental di↵erence between the rectangular and ﬂipper-model planform is also that the tubercles
were inherited by the ﬂipper model making the smooth ﬂipper model the modiﬁed case, while the smooth
rectangular planform foil presents proﬁles already optimized for use to which a wave pattern is added on
top.
The fourth parameter possibly determinative for the beneﬁt of a blade section having a sinusoidal leading
edge is the Reynolds number. If considering only the rectangular planform results, it is observed that [9]
and [11] showed a smoother stall and improved poststall performance at the very low Reynolds numbers of
183.000 and 120.000 for wavy foils compared to baseline, and the observation of [11] was later supported
by [14]. The aforementioned improvements were not observed by [16] at Re = 450.000 and only to a small
extend by [13] at Re = 106. These di↵erences could also be due to the last considered parameter, which is
the employed airfoil proﬁles, but it is noted that [9] and [16] employed the same proﬁle with positive results
only a the lower Reynolds number, indicating that Reynolds number is an inﬂuential factor. In either case, it
is seen that the baseline lift curves of the foils investigated by [9] and [11] experience more abrupt stall than
those of [16], [13] and this study. Judging from these results, at more smooth stall and improved poststall
performance can be achieved if the baseline lift curve has an abrupt stall behaviour, while improvements are
sparse in cases where baseline stall behaviour is already docile. This could point to the positive e↵ects of
sinusoidal leading edge patterns being a phenomenon restricted to low Reynolds numbers, below the range
of large scale wind turbines.
Additonally, the swept-planform design addressed to some extent by [12] and [16] showed mixed results in
terms of performance with reduction at design conditions and improvement at extreme conditions, but it
was considered the most practical planform geometry for the implementation of leading edge tubercles by
[16] and could be employed in further investigations.
The observation that lift, drag and stall properties were generally less favourable for the wavy surface
foils compared to the baseline under the conditions of this project, shows that for the smoothly stalling S809
airfoil at Re = 106, imitating a large wind turbine setting, no performance augmentation is gained. This
indicates a limitation of the area, in which positive e↵ects are present, to lower Reynolds numbers or airfoils
displaying more abrupt stall properties.
Further, since existing improvements observed are generally only partial, it is questionable whether the
gain is worth the additional complication and cost in manufacturing of a wavy surface blade.
Further
investigations should be conducted to determine the e↵ect of the di↵erent parameters on performance in
order to obtain optimized designs and perhaps achieve more consistent improvements worth the e↵ort of
practical applications.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and recommendations
This section consists of a ﬁnal conclusion on the present study and some considerations and recommendations
for potential future work on the addressed subject.
7.1
Conclusion
This project set out to investigate the ﬂow over two bio-inspired, wavy surface blade sections through
numerical RANS simulations in CCM+, in order to evaluate their aerodynamic performance compared to a
smooth baseline blade section, and consider them for wind turbine applications.
During the establishment of the numerical scheme utilized for the ﬂow simulations, the ﬂow was assumed
to be incompressible ﬂow in accordance with Ma < 0.3 and the SST k-! turbulence model appropriate for
incompressible aerodynamic ﬂows was applied. Further, the correlation-based γ-Re✓transition model was
considered and found to provide better agreement with experimental results than corresponding fully turbu-
lent ﬂow results. Comparison with existing, transitional ﬂow results from EllipSys and XFOIL, employing
the established en transition model, showed limited prediction ability of the γ-Re✓model at high Reynolds
number, but reasonable agreement in results at the Reynolds number of this study, Re = 106. The γ-Re✓
model was therefore applied during the wavy blade section investigations and fully turbulent simulations
were considered only for comparison.
The baseline and the two wavy surface blade sections under investigation were designed with a rectangular
planform, based on the S809 airfoil proﬁle. The rectangular planform was chosen to avoid any dependency
of results on a particular planform and the S809 proﬁle was selected since it has features comparable to the
proﬁles resembling the idea-sparking humpback whale ﬂipper, but was created for application in horizontal
axis wind turbines. The wavy surface shape was modelled as a sinusoidal wave pattern with amplitude
A = 0.015c and wavelength λ = 0.125c, which were selected in accordance with trends in the literature
pointing to low amplitude and wavelengths being more beneﬁcial. The wavy surface of the ﬁrst blade section
was conﬁned primarily to the leading edge (WF) following the concept mainly addressed in the literature
while the wavy surface of the second blade section was extended towards the trailing edge to form humps
and grooves on the foil suction side (WB). The two wavy surface geometries along with the smooth baseline
were designed using 3D CAD program CREO Parametric.
Initiating the investigation, a comparison of baseline ﬂow results against existing experimental and EllipSys
results served as a validation of the transitional ﬂow simulation, despite deviations in lift level in the stall and
poststall regime. Since the investigated blade sections are all implemented in the same numerical scheme,
they can be compared even if not all details of the baseline show are in agreement with existing results.
Transitional ﬂow simulations were hence carried out for the baseline and the two wavy surface foils and
the resulting aerodynamic parameters were compared in order to evaluate their performance. The perfor-
mance of the sinusoidal leading edge foil, WF, was observed to be comparable to baseline in the linear lift
65
==PAGE:69==
region, both in terms of lift and drag, but it stalled prior to the baseline foil resulting in slightly reduced
maximum lift, increased drag of up to 100% and a broad range of 30-40% lower lift compared to baseline.
Resultantly, the lift-to-drag ratio of the WF foil was at the best comparable to the baseline values, and its
performance was thus never favourable compared to the baseline. The early, more abrupt stall, loss of lift
and considerable increase in drag were all on the contrary to the expected e↵ects of the sinusoidal leading
edge modiﬁcation. The behaviour of the ﬂow results of the extended-wave design foil WB was seen to di↵er
signiﬁcantly from those of the baseline and WF ﬂow results. In the range of very small angles of attack, the
WB foil showed a higher level of lift and lift-to-drag ratio than the baseline and WF foil with an increase
in lift of 50% compared to baseline at zero angle of attack, but the WB foil then experienced a temporary
stall already at 4◦. After this point lift continuously increased at a slower pace, but increases in drag were
severe, up to 400% higher than baseline, causing the lift-to-drag ratio to decrease in the entire range above
4◦. The small-angle of attack region of improved performance by the WB foil compared to baseline and
WF is hence accompanied by a major reduction in performance for the remaining range, making the beneﬁt
very limited and questionable is terms of applications. Additionally, a comparison to turbulent ﬂow results
showed that the biggest di↵erence between transitional and turbulent results was seen for the WB foil where
the small-angle, high-lift region was not observed in the turbulent results.
In order to gain more insight in the ﬂow characteristics associated with the di↵erent behaviours displayed
by three investigated foils, the surface distributions of pressure, skin friction and intermittency along with
constrained streamlines were extracted for three representative angles of attack, ↵= {0◦, 5◦, 10◦}. At zero
angle of attack the distributions obtained for the similar-performing baseline and WF foil were observed to
be largely comparable, while the WB foil results deviated from the baseline and WF results at all angles. The
superior aerodynamic performance of the WB foil at ↵= 0◦was associated with numerically higher suction
side pressure and fully attached groove streamlines, compared to the baseline and WF foils where separation,
transition and reattachment was observed at a near mid-chord location. The di↵erence in ﬂow behaviour
was seen to increase with increased angles of attack and formations of span-wise vortex-like structures were
observed at for the wavy blade sections under their stall conditions, indicating the span-wise vortices were
associated with reduction in aerodynamic eﬃciency.
Overall it can be concluded that the wavy surface blade sections, the WB and the WF foils, designed
during this project provided very limited or no improved performance, respectively, under the conditions
given in this study. The physical mechanisms responsible for the positive e↵ects observed by other studies
under di↵erent conditions are still an object of discussion in the literature and it is therefore also not clear
at this point why the e↵ects are not present under the present conditions. It is noted however, that positive
e↵ects are seen primarily for ﬂipper-planform or very low Reynolds number results, and including the results
of this study in the observations, it is suggested that the beneﬁt of the wavy design might be limited to some
areas of application.
7.2
Recommendations for future work
In order to obtain a more favourable wavy surface design, it is recommended to further investigate the e↵ects
of the ﬁve parameters observed to inﬂuence ﬂow performance.
• Airfoil proﬁle
• Reynolds number, Re
• Planform shape
• Wavelength, λ
• Amplitude, A
Determining the dependency on these parameters could facilitate identiﬁcation of areas where wavy surface
designs are beneﬁcial, as well as the limitations of the concept. The e↵ect of amplitude and wavelength
66
==PAGE:70==
has already been investigated to some extent, and it is therefore recommended to focus on the remaining
parameters, i.e. airfoil proﬁle, Reynolds number and planform.
• Keeping the planform constant and only considering studies of rectangular planform foils, positive
e↵ects of wavy surface modiﬁcations were observed at low Reynolds numbers by [9] and [11], among
others, while only minor improvements were seen by [16], [13] and this study at higher Reynolds
number.
An investigation of dependency of performance on Reynolds number would therefore be
interesting in order to demarcate a range of Reynolds numbers where wavy surface design is beneﬁcial.
This would most likely further depend on the selected airfoil proﬁle, at least to some extent, and hence
this parameter would have to be considered as well.
• From the literature study it was noted that the only (almost) exclusively positive results observed
for a wavy surface model was that of [5] where a ﬂipper planform model was employed. Further, at
a similar Reynolds number employing a di↵erent airfoil, [16] showed partially improved performance
of a wavy ﬂipper compared to a smooth model, while no signiﬁcant improvement were seen for wavy
surface, rectangular foils under the same conditions. Thus, investigations of whether utilizing a three
dimensional planform including a tip, as for instance the swept-leading-edge planform by [16] or the
whole turbine blade by [12] would allow positive 3D and/or tip e↵ects, are suggested.
At the same time it is also recommended to conduct investigations providing more insight in the details
of the ﬂow and the physical mechanism at play during ﬂow over a wavy surface foil. This could be done
with a combination of numerical and experimental investigations. If a LES or DES approach was adopted
for the numerical scheme, a more realistic representation of the ﬂow could be gained than by only applying
RANS simulations, and the experiments would further ensure independence of results on numerical scheme
or utilized models.
Additionally, on a practical level, it is suggested to consider a simulation program facilitating C-type meshes
which would be beneﬁcial in terms of establishing good grids and boundary layer resolution. If a struc-
tured grid was utilized, it would also be possible to extract cross sections plots of, for instance, velocity
distributions in wavy humps and grooves for inspection.

A.3
Grid study for the NACA-64-415 airfoil domain
The grid generation for the NACA-64-415 airfoil domain in CCM+ was complicated by the very sharp trailing
edge which made it impossible for the prism layer to reach around it. Instead of going around the edge, the
prism layer decreases rapidly right before the edge and comes to an abrupt end. In order to solve this issue,
three solution concept were approached
• Grid t - Intense volume reﬁnement of the trailing edge area to compensate for the missing prism layer
• Grid r - Rounding of the edge to help the prism layer get around it
• Grid b - Adding an interface extension of the airfoil trailing edge, allowing the prism layer to develop
along this interface and dissolve in a less critical area.
The resulting meshes obtained from the three approaches are shown in Figure A.5.

(f) Grid b - Prism layer extends behind edge

The grid of the ﬁrst approach, grid t, is shown in Figure A.5a and A.5b where it is seen how intensive

allows the prism layer to extend to a point 0.03mm from the edge and the cells behind the edge have sides of
order 0.25mm. This however is not enough to resolve the boundary layer in the last cells and it will therefore
have to be accepted that relatively high y+ values can occur here. The second approach, grid r, resulted in
the grid seen in Figure A.5c and A.5d which show how the rounded trailing edge allows the prism layer to

Appendix B
Grid dependency study for 3D foils
In order to choose a suitable grid for the investigation of the s809 airfoil proﬁle with and without sinusoidal
surfaces, a grid dependency study using the baseline proﬁle was made and is reported here. At ﬁrst the
e↵ect of three types of reﬁnement is examined at constant base size to decide on a setup, and then the base
size is varied for the chosen setup to reveal the grid dependency of the solution.
As for the 2D cases, the grid was created in CCM+ using the mesh type Trimmer with a prism layer at
the airfoil wall surface. The domain was chosen such that the distance from the airfoil was 6c to the inlets
and 15c to the outlet, having a width of 1c. The sharp trailing edge of the s809 airfoil prevented the prism
layer from extending around the trailing edge, as it was also seen for the validation case of the NACA 64-415
airfoil, section 4.3. Therefore, as in the mentioned case, the approach of introducing a very reﬁned trailing
edge reﬁnement region was adopted. The prism layer was set to a size of 4% of the base size, containing 40
cell layers with a stretch factor of 1.1 in order to ensure values of wall y+ < 1. This was achieved within
the prism layer region, while in the trailing edge reﬁnement region y+ unavoidably went up to higher values
of y+ ' 25. This is not a completely satisfying solution, but since the size of the region of concern is of
the order 1mm constituting only a thousandth of the chord length of 1m, it is accepted for the further studies.
Apart from inclusion of the trailing edge reﬁnement region which was mandatory for this approach, two
other elements were addressed for grid reﬁnement. First, the size condition set on the surface of the airfoil
was considered. Secondly, volumetric reﬁnement blocks were added for the near airfoil and wake areas with
di↵erent size conditions. The reﬁnement regions are listed in Table B.1 and as a ﬁrst step, these will be
considered while the base size is kept constant at 0.25m. Examples of the domain reﬁnement regions are
shown in Figure B.1.
