ABSTRACT
Abstract
The aim of this master thesis is to find complications in order to evaluate stability in high-risk
hospitalised patients early to alert the clinical staff that can then act as needed to reduce the
severity of the complication. The thesis is based on the article Vital-Sign Data Fusion Models
for Post-operative Patients by Marco A. F. Pimentel et al.[1]. This paper is replicated resulting
in the ReferenceStability algorithm. Afterwards, two novelty algorithm are proposed; Stabili-
tyEstimationI and StabilityEstimationII. The algorithms are conducting stability estimation by
using kernel density estimation. They take vital signs as an input, finds the probability den-
sity function through kernel density estimation and uses this to estimate novelty scores. The
StabilityEstimationI algorithm uses hyper parameter optimisation to improve the results of the
ReferenceStability algorithm. The StabilityEstimationII algorithm uses the optimal parameters
from the StabilityEstimationI algorithm, but investigate time of early warning by looking at
segments before the severe adverse event in order to alarm the clinical staff.
The algorithms are applied on WARD data from post-surgery patients. The stability definition
applied in this thesis is defining stable patients as those without any adverse or severe adverse
event during hospitalisation, while the unstable patients are those with a severe adverse event.
The ReferenceStability algorithm obtained an accuracy, sensitivity and specificity for the 4-
dimensional model only build on vital signs on 63.55%, 55.56%, and 67.61%, respectively, while
the 6-dimensional model also incorporating variability indices obtained an accuracy, sensitivity
and specificity of 62.62%, 8.33%, and 90.14%, respectively.
The StabilityEstimationI algorithm obtained an accuracy, sensitivity and specificity of 79.17%,
80.00%, and 78.26%, respectively.
The results of the StabilityEstimationII algorithm can be read in many ways. The most optimal
model is estimating the stability of the patient each hour based on 60 minutes of data provid-
ing a sensitivity of 70% and a specificity of 86.3%. With this algorithm, the alarm is provided
approximately 12.8 hours before the complication and a false alarm occur every 7.29 hours.
From the performance of the algorithms, it is clear that a more precise definition of stability
needs to be investigated before using it in a clinical support system. It is important to be clear
on what to alert and it is crucial to an algorithm that the stability estimation only investigates
complications actually presenting in the vital signs. Otherwise, it will cause too many false
alarms and thereby a system that does not function properly. Furthermore, some improvements
to the algorithm could be made as for instance including the circadian rhythm into the algorithm
in order to take variation of vital signs during the day into account.

RESUME
Resume
Målet med dette speciale er at finde komplikationer med henblik på at evaluere stabiliteten
af hospitaliserede højrisiko patienter tidligt for at alarmere det kliniske personale til at agere
efter behov for at reducere alvoren af komplikationen.
Dette speciale er baseret på artiklen
Vital-Sign Data Fusion Models for Post-operative Patients af Marco A. F. Pimentel et al.[1].
Denne artikel replikeres resulterende i ReferenceStability algoritmen. Efterfølgende præsenteres
to nye algoritmer; StabilityEstimationI og StabilityEstimationII. Algoritmerne udfører stabilitets
estimering ved brug af kernel density estimation. De tager vitale værdier som input, finder prob-
ability density function gennem kernel density estimation og bruger denne til at estimere novelty
scores. StabilityEstimationI algoritmen benytter hyper parameter optimering til at forbedre re-
sultaterne fra ReferenceStability algoritmen. StabilityEstimationII algoritmen benytter de opti-
male parametre fra StabilityEstimationI algoritmen, men undersøger muligheden for at alermere
det kliniske personale før komplikationen forekommer ved at undersøge segmenter før komplika-
tionen.
Algoritmerne anvendes på WARD data fra post-kirurgiske patienter.
Stabilitets definitionen
anvendt i dette speciale definerer stabile patienter som dem uden et adverse eller severe adverse
event, hvorimod de ustabile patienter er dem med et severe adverse event.
ReferenceStability algoritmen opnår en accuracy, sensitivitet og specificitet for den 4-dimensionelle
model, der kun bygger på vitale værdier, på henholdsvis 63.55%, 55.56% og 67.61%, hvorimod
den 6-dimensionelle model, der også benytter variabilitets index opnår en accuracy, sensitivitet
og specificitet på henholdsvis 62.62%, 8.33% og 90.14%.
StabilityEstimationI algoritmen opnår en accuracy, sensitivitet og specificitet på henholdsvis
79.17%, 80.00% og 78.26%.
Resultaterne for StabilityEstimationII algoritmen kan fortolkes på mange måder. Den mest op-
timale model estimaterer stabiliteteten af en patient hver time basereret på 60 minutters data,
hvilket giver en sensitivitet på 70% og en specificitet på 86.3%. Med denne algoritme alarmeres
personalet 12.8 timer før komplikationen og en falsk alarm forekommer hver 7.29 time.
Ud fra resultaterne fra algoritmerne står det klart, at der er behov for at undersøge en mere
præcis definition af stabilitet før en sådan algoritme tages i brug i et alarm system. Det er
vigtigt at være klar om, hvad der skal alarmeres omkring, og det er afgørende for en algoritme,
at stabilitets estimeringen kun undersøger komplikationer, der rent faktisk kan ses i de vitale
værdier. Hvis dette ikke er tilfældet, vil der opstå for mange falske alarmer og dermed et system,
der ikke fungerer optimalt. Ydermere kan der foretages nogle forbedringer på algoritmerne såsom
at inkludere døgnrytme i algoritmen med henblik på at tage højde for variationer i vitale værdier
gennem dagen.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Chapter 1
Introduction
1
Clinical Background
Around 230 millions major surgeries are performed across the world every year.[2] Undergo-
ing surgery is associated with a risk of complications, which occur in 15-45% of all surgical
patients.[2][3] These complications can be fatal and may result from delayed detection of the
complications.[2] These postoperative complications are the third leading cause of dead causing
it to be an important area to investigate. 30% of patients undergoing abdominal cancer surgery
experience severe adverse events such an pneumonia, sepsis, myocardial infarction, and cardiac
arrest after the surgery.[4]
In Denmark, a systematic monitoring of hospitalised patients occur at least 2 times a day through
the Early Warning Score (EWS) system. It is a manual monitoring system monitoring respiratory
frequency, saturation, oxygen supply, pulse, blood pressure, consciousness, and temperature.[2]
[3][5][6] The purpose of the system is to be able to direct the staff’s attention towards those
patients being in risk of deterioration.[2] Thereby, the aim of the system is to prevent the
postoperative complications by detecting micro-events. Though, a disadvantage of the system is
that the complications can occur between the manual measurements, which can cause them not to
be discovered. Not all complications are immediately symptomatic, so if they are not discovered
early, it can result in a worse condition.[3][7] This manual monitoring is thereby too infrequent to
be able to detect clinical deterioration in patients. For instance, only 5% of the serious cases of
hypoxia is caught by the EWS system. Furthermore, the data is manually interpreted, which does
not allows capturing of trends and combinations of the measured physiological parameters.[4]
Some of this would most likely be caught with the use of continuous monitoring which would
hopefully allow preventive interventions to be initiated before the complication turns into a fatal
condition.[2]

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
2
Problem Statement
The aim of this master thesis is to find complications in order to evaluate stability in high-risk
hospitalised patients early to alert the clinical staff that can then act as needed to reduce the
severity of the complication. This is done through development of an algorithm that is capable
of evaluating the stability of these patients based on vital signs. At hospitals worldwide, the
EWS system is used to monitor hospitalised patients. The EWS score is an aggregated score
calculated from vital sign measurements. The purpose of the EWS system is to secure a timely
and equal assessment of a developing deterioration in the patients. Furthermore, it is the purpose
that clinical deterioration is caught early in order to act appropriately based on the condition
of the patient. The measurements are taken with an interval between every 12 hours and every
30 minutes depending on the condition of the patient. The vital signs measured for the EWS
score is body temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, level of consciousness, and
systolic and diastolic blood pressure. The amount of oxygen supplement is also noted.[5]
It is widely known that for instance stressful situations can cause a temporarily rise in blood
pressure[8]. This will cause attention towards the patient, but this is not always necessary, since
the blood pressure can rise without it being dangerous. One of the problems with the EWS score
is that the modalities are not measured continuous, which can cause important information to
be left out between the measurements.
Other problems concern the manual protocol of the
system which makes the system prone to human errors in many ways. Another problem is the
pre-defined ranges, which can cause problems, since vital signs can vary for different patient
cohorts. Therefore, a stability index based on data fusion of continuous measurement of vital
signs is relevant, since it will evaluate the stability based on an overall estimation of the patient
instead of an evaluation of one modality alone. Stability can be evaluated in many ways, but is
in this thesis defined as follows; stable patients are those without any adverse or severe adverse
event during hospitalisation, while the unstable patients are those with a severe adverse event.
2.1
Project Goals
In order to achieve the aim described above, a set of project goals are defined:
• Make a literature study concerning the use of stability index in high-risk patients

1
Introduction
Wireless Assessment of Respiratory and Circulatory Distress (WARD) is a research project
developing an innovative clinical support system. It is made in collaboration between the Tech-
nological University of Denmark (DTU), Rigshospitalet, and Bispebjerg Hospital. The project
uses intelligent algorithms to process input from patient monitoring devices into real-time alarms.
The aim of the project is to use the continuous monitoring of vital signs to reduce complica-
tions and death. Two main groups of patients are included in the project; hospitalised patients
due to either chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) exacerbation or cancer patients
whom underwent major abdominal surgery. The system is furthermore intended for use in those
patient settings where it is beneficial to have continuous monitoring. The system alerts the
medical personnel in order to initiate the treatment at the earliest stage possible. The system
allows the medical staff to view the current as well as previous measurements of the vital signs.
The system works by having a number of small wireless sensors placed on the patient’s body.
The data from these sensors are then send to a computer analysing and interpreting through
intelligent algorithm thereby alerting the medical staff if necessary.[7][9]
2
This Master Thesis’ Contribution to the Project
The contribution of this master thesis to the WARD project regards stability estimation of
the hospitalised patients. This thesis investigates if the stability of these patients can be esti-
mated in order to catch some complications before they occur. The thesis is based on vital sign
measurements.

Data Description
The data described above is preprocessed by Søren Møller Rasmussen. The preprocessed data is
the data used in this report. The preprocessed data is made on some assumptions.[10] The heart
rate (HR) is calculated as an average from the valid R-R intervals within the given minute. An
R-R interval is deemed valid when being within 20 and 4000 ms. The HR is deemed valid if:
1. There is a 10 seconds electrocardiogram (ECG) segment within the same minute that is
also deemed valid
2. There is not measured an ECG signal within the same minute that can disprove good
quality
In the case of missing data either due to errors caused by bad connections or a bad ECG segment,
the last value is extrapolated in up to 60 minutes.[10]
The respiratory rate (RR) is a measured value from Isanys providing one value each minute.
The RR is deemed valid if:
1. There is a 10 seconds ECG segment within the same minute that is also deemed valid
2. There is not measured an ECG signal within the same minute that can disprove good
quality
In case of missing data caused by bad connections or a bad ECG segment, the last value is
extrapolated in up to 60 minutes.[10]
The oxygen saturation (SPO2) is calculated as an average from the available second measure-
ments from Nonin. There need to be at least one value within the minute to provide an average
minute value. Values where the drop from the previous value is larger than 4%-points per second
is removed. In case of missing data due to either bad connection or a bad PPG segment, the
last value is extrapolated in op to 60 minutes.[10]
There is no preprocessing of the blood pressure (BP) besides extrapolation of values in up to 60
minutes.[10]

This chapter is concerning the background knowledge regarding vital signs and the current system

1
Physiology
1.1
Vital Signs
The WARD project deals with the following vital sign modalities; respiratory rate (RR), oxygen
saturation (SPO2), systolic and diastolic blood pressure (BP), and heart rate (HR). The vital
signs indicate some of the most important functions of the human body. This master thesis
make use of all these vital signs.
RR defines the number of breaths taken every minute. This parameter can be manually obtained
by counting the number of breaths in one minute at rest. This can be obtained by counting
the number of times the chest rises. This parameter is important since it can increase with
conditions such as fever or illness.[11] In Denmark, the normal range defined in the EWS scheme
is 12-20 breaths per minute.[12]
SPO2 is indicating the amount of oxygen in a persons body. The oxygen travels with the red
blood cells through the body, and it is this amount that is measured. The level is dependent of
different factors such as how much oxygen a person breathes in, how well the haemoglobin of the
body attracts the oxygen, and how much haemoglobin that is concentrated in the red blood cells.
Normally, the haemoglobin of the body is able to contain the amount of oxygen needed in order
for the body to function properly. Unfortunately, some diseases affects the ability of the body to
bind oxygen such as respiratory infections, COPD, and asthma.[13] A normal saturation level is
set to be above 95%.[12] It is often tested through pulse oximetry that is a non-invasive test using
a sensor to read the reflected wavelengths from the blood. It is mostly done through a probe at
the finger, but it can also be performed on the earlobe and other places on the body. A decline in
oxygen saturation level is called hypoxemia and is often caused by less oxygen in the air that is
breathed in, different conditions and diseases, or breathing of another substance such as carbon
monoxide. If the oxygen saturation is low, it affects the concentration of oxygen in the body’s
tissue, which is called hypoxia. If the lack of oxygen is sufficiently low, the cells of the body is able
to adapt, but if the lack is large, it can cause cell damage leading to cell death. Hypoxia is most
often a complication from hypoxemia, but it can also occur due to other causes; an insufficient
amount of red blood cells that can carry the oxygen to the tissue, bleeding, inadequate blood
flow, or severe infections. Therefore, oxygen saturation is an important parameter to monitor,
since the treatment is quick to initiate through oxygen therapy. It is important that action is
provided as soon as possible, since brain cells can often die within five minutes, and if the lack
Page 18 of 144
==PAGE:20==

sustain longer, either coma, seizures, or even brain death is possible to occur.[13]
BP is defining the force of the blood that is pushing against the artery walls during both con-
traction and relaxation of the heart. Every time the heart is beating, the blood is pumped
into the arteries. The highest BP is found when the heart contracts, whereas the lowest BP is
found, when the heart relaxes. When measuring the BP, two numbers arise; the systolic and the
diastolic BP. The highest number indicates the systolic pressure illustrating the pressure inside
the artery in a contraction phase, where blood is pumped through the body. The lower number
indicating the diastolic pressure refers to the pressure inside the artery at rest, where the heart
is filled with blood. The BP is measured in millimetres of mercury (mmHg). High BP is called
hypertension and increase the risk of different diseases such as heart attack, heart failure, and
stroke. This is due to the fact that a high pressure increase the resistance against the blood
flow, which causes the heart to pump even harder in order to circulate the blood properly. The
normal BP is defined to be around 120/80 mmHg, where the elevated systolic BP is defined to
be above 120.[11] Though, in the EWS scheme, a normal score of the systolic BP is ranging from
111-219 mmHg.[12]
The last parameter often measured is the HR. It defines the number of times a heart beats
every minute. The heart rate indicates more than just the number of time a heart is beating;
it also indicates something about the rhythm of the heart and the pulse strength. The normal
heart rate ranges from around 60 to 100 beats per minute (bpm) and varies due to factors such
as illness, injury, and emotions. Furthermore, the HR of women are often higher than the one
for males, whereas athletes often have a low HR. These factors should therefore be taken into
account, when investigating HR.[11] The EWS system defines a normal range zero score as a
heart rate of 51-90 .[12]
1.2
The Early Warning Score System
The EWS system is a systematic observation and risk evaluation of hospitalised patients. The
system also includes an algorithm for action. The system is used at hospitals worldwide to mon-
itor these patients. It is based on an aggregated score that is calculated based on the vital signs.
The purpose of the system is to make sure that there is a timely and equal assessment of clinical
deterioration in the patients. Another aim with the system is that the clinical deterioration gets
caught as early as possible making it possible to acts as appropriate as needed depending on
the condition of the patient. The system is used in several wards of the hospital. The system is
applied on hospitalised patients above 18 years - both acute and elective. Some patient groups
will still need more often and broader monitoring than stated by the system. The algorithm is
based on the following vital signs; body temperature, pulse, RR, SPO2, level of consciousness,
and systolic and diastolic BP. The amount of oxygen supplement is also noted. The measured
values are then entered in the EWS scheme. After entering all data for the same time stamp
into the scheme, the score is aggregated automatically and an electronic score is documented.
The measurements are taken with an interval between every 12 hours and every 30 minutes
depending on the condition of the patient and the score.[5] The EWS observation scheme is seen

Pancreatic Resection
Pancreatic resection, also known as pancreatectomy, describes the removal of part of the pancreas
through surgery. The surgery is most commonly used to treat pancreatic cancer. The pancreas
is placed just to the right of the midline and runs to the upper abdomen on the left side. The
pancreas hooks around large blood vessels on the right side that supplies the liver and common
bile duct. It is furthermore wrapped around the side by the duodenum. The pancreas touches
the spleen on the left side. This describes that there is a difference in the structures surrounding
the pancreas depending on the side thereby leading to different surgeries depending on if it is
a left side or right sided surgery. The pancreas and surrounding structures are illustrated in
Figure 3.1.

A left sided pancreatic resection is called distal pancreatectomy. The surgery removes the left
part, also called the tail, of the pancreas. The surgery is either performed as an open procedure
or as a laparoscopic procedure. Whether the spleen is removed too depend on the diagnosis
and location of the tumour.
The surgery isolates the tail of the pancreas while controlling
its blood supply after which the wanted part of the pancreas is removed.
One of the most
important complications is the risk of leakage of pancreatic juices after cutting off the tail of the
pancreas.[17]
The right sided pancreatic resection is called a Whipple or Pancreaticoduodenectomy.
The
surgery removes the right part, also called the head, of the pancreas. The surgery is mostly
performed as an open procedure. As described, the head of the pancreas is surrounded by many
important structures, which causes this surgery to be more complex. Besides removing the head
of the pancreas, the duodenum, a part of the stomach and bile duct, and the gallbladder is also
removed. The removal of these structures is the first part of the surgery after which the second
part focuses on reconnecting the structures again in order for the patient to be able do eat and
digest. The most serious complication in this surgery is leakage from any of the three sites.[17]
Depending on the severity of the cancer and tumour, it is also possible to perform a total
pancreatectomy although this procedure is not performed as commonly as the two described
above. A total pancreatectomy is a surgery combining a distal pancreatectomy and a Whipple.
This therefore involves removal of the entire pancreas causing some consequences for the patient;
there will be no insulin secretion which will lead to diabetes and there will be no pancreatic
enzymes secretion essential for digestion of food, which therefore leads to enzyme supplements
with meals.[17]
There are risks at any surgery and due to the fact that these surgeries are major surgeries, the
risks are significant. Some of the surgical risks are:[17]
• Pancreatic leak: Pancreatic leak occurs in more than 1 out of 10 patients. It is considered
more serious in a Whipple, since the pancreatic juice leaked can be in activated form due
to activation by substances in the bile, which can cause digestion as well as damage to the
surrounding structures.
• Bleeding: Major bleeding can occur due to the pancreas being surrounded by major blood
vessels.
• Pancreatic insufficiency: Since these surgeries involve removal of part of the pancreas, a
consequence can be that the pancreas afterwards does not produce enough insulin and
digestive enzyme to function properly, which can lead to diabetes and malabsorption.
• Death: All major surgeries include a risk of death either during or after the surgery. The
risk of dead is less than 1 out of 20 in a Whipple and 1 out of 100 in distal pancreatectomy.
• Other: Some general complications after undergoing these major surgeries is wound or
deep infections, hernias, adhesions, clots in the legs of the lungs as well as urinary or chest
infections.
Bowel Resection
Bowel resection involves removing a part of the bowel including the small intestine, large intes-
tine, or rectum. Bowel resection is used as treatment for various diseases as for instance cancer.
The amount of bowel removed due to cancer depend on both the location and the size of the

out the needed portion of the intestine. With laparoscopic resection, two to four incisions are
made on the belly. A thin tube with a camera is inserted into one of the incisions. The needed
surgical tools are then inserted into the other incisions in order to remove the needed part of

Esophageal Resection
Esophageal Resection, also called esophagectomy, is the surgical treatment for esophageal cancer.
The surgery is performed either to remove the tumour and cancer or to relieve symptoms. The
surgery removes either some or all of the esophagus. The esophagus is the swallowing tube there
is between the mouth and stomach. During the procedure, either a part or the entire esophagus
is removed by an incision in either the neck, chest, or abdomen. When removing the esophagus,

in the chest and abdomen, or as a transhiatal esophagectomy, where the procedure is performed
by incisions in the abdomen and neck. The minimally invasive procedure can be performed
laparoscopically meaning through small incisions in the abdomen or thoracoscopically meaning

Method
The data set used consist of data from 200 post-operative patient at
the Oxford Cancer centre. The data consist of measurements of vi-
tal signs recorded periodically by nurses as well as demographic data
and outcomes. It comprises measurements of heart rate (HR), respi-
ratory rate (RR), arterial blood oxygen saturation (SPO2), systolic
blood pressure (SysBP) and temperature. A variability index is used
defined as the difference between the maximum and minimum values
in a 24-hour period for each of the physiological variables wanted.
Two different types of models were investigated; one that takes the
five vital signs as input and one that furthermore takes the variabil-
ity indexes of RR and SysBP as input. The algorithm contains the
following steps; normalisation, probability density function (PDF),
novelty score estimation, and classification. It is assumed that all
input are of equally importance. Therefore, the inputs needs to be
normalised in order to have the same dynamic range. The variabil-
ity indices of RR and SysBP is normalised through the zero-mean
unit-variance transformation for each day instead.
The PDF was
estimated through kernel density estimation. The variance used is found through the nearest-
neighbour method. Hereafter, the likelihood is calculated for both groups. In order to be able
to estimate the abnormality of a data point, x, the departure from normality is found using
a novelty score defined as z(x) = −log(p(x|θ)), where z(x) is the novelty score, θ = {xi, σ},
and p(x|θ) the likelihood. Normal data have higher likelihood and thereby generate low nov-
elty scores, whereas abnormal data have lower likelihoods and higher novelty scores. The block
diagram illustrating the method is seen in Figure 4.1.
Results
The KDE with five vital signs obtained 71%, 84%, and 70% for area under the receiver operating
characteristic (AUROC), sensitivity, and specificity, respectively, whereas these number for the
KDE with the five vital signs as well as the two variability indices obtain 73%, 87%, and 70%.
Conclusion
The algorithm performs better than the EWS system. Future work will among other be looking
at continuous data from patients wearing body sensors instead of manually measured data.
Comments
It is a small data set causing the conclusion to be limited. They define a patient as abnormal
if just one of the observations are abnormal throughout the hospital stay causing more false
positives. The method seems promising and provides a novelty score being relevant for this
project, and the variability indices provides an approach for incorporating a time perspective.

method is that it is sensitive in a multivariate setting. It is possible to model several modalities
in a normality model and thereby investigate several modalities rather than a single one.

Chapter 5
Implementation of
ReferenceStability Algorithm
This chapter contains the description of the implementation of the reference method based on
the article Vital-Sign Data Fusion Models for Post-operative Patients by Marco A. F. Pimentel
et al.[1]. It contains description of the method, results, and discussion for this reference method.
1
Method of ReferenceStability Algorithm
The ReferenceStability algorithm is developed based on the article Vital-Sign Data Fusion Models
for Post-operative Patients by Marco A. F. Pimentel et al.[1]. This section describes the steps
and methods used in the algorithm developed as in this article.
The block diagram for the
ReferenceStability algorithm is seen in Figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Block diagram illustrating the method used in the reference algorithm.
The algorithm
consist of two different models; a model with only vital signs as input and a model with vital signs
as well as variability indices as inputs. The input is the only difference between the two models. The
algorithm then consist of the following steps: Normalisation, kernel density estimation, novelty scoring,
and classification into either a stable or an unstable patient.

1.1
Stability Definition and Data Amount
As the reference article describes, the stable data is an average of the last 24 hours before
discharge. When investigating the post-surgery cohort, the database consist of 498 patients.
The discharge information on the patients is only available for 491 patients. 298 patient have
either an AE or an SAE, which provides 193 stable patients. Though, in order to be able to use
these patients for the stable data amount, it is needed to have data up to the discharge time.
The WARD database only contains maximum four days of data. This is only the case for 106
patients out of the entire cohort. Correlating the 193 patients without any event with the 106
patients with data up to discharge, the stable data amount is then down to 96 patients. As
mentioned, the stability definition needs the last 24 hours of data for the stable patients. 4 out
of the 96 do not have 24 hours of recording, and thereby the stable patient group ends up with
92 patients. The model is also in need of data for all four vital signs in the last 24 hours of the
recording. 21 of the 92 patients are missing data for one or more of the vital signs in the last 24
hours, which therefore narrows the stable patient database down to 71 patients.
The unstable patients are considered those that have an SAE. There are 194 patients with an
SAE in the post-surgery patient cohort. Though, only 69 of these have an SAE within the days
of monitoring. The reference method needs 24 hours of data defined as the 24 hours before the
onset of the SAE. This 24 hours of data is available for 58 of these patients. As stated earlier,
the reference method needs data for all four vital sign parameters, and this is only the case for
36 patients. Thereby, the unstable patient cohort consist of data from 36 patients.

of the physiological variables has equal importance in the model. This is done by normalisation
in order to make sure that each variable are scaled to have the same dynamic range.

normalisation of the vital sign measurements is performed through the zero-mean unit-variance
transformation defined in Equation 1.1.

1.3
Kernel Density Estimation
The reference article used KDE to estimate the PDF. Two different models are used defined
by their inputs. The first models take only vital signs as input, whereas the second model also
incorporates variability indices. The article uses five vital signs; HR, RR, SPO2, systolic BP
and temperature, which makes the first model a five dimensional model. The second model also
uses variability indices calculated as the difference between the maximum and minimum value
for each vital sign parameter in a 24-hour period. The article uses this measure for RR and
systolic BP making it a 7-dimensional model.[1]

1.4
Classification
A model is defined based on a set of stable patients. Afterwards, the wanted data can be evalu-
ated by calculating the likelihood from Equation 1.3 and afterwards estimating the abnormality
of this data through the departure from normality through the novelty score in Equation 1.6.[1]
z(x) = −log(p(x|θ)),

where z(x) is the novelty score and θ = {xi, σ}. The patient is thereby classified as either stable
or unstable based on a threshold. Stable data generate low novelty scores, whereas unstable
data generate high novelty scores.[1]
1.5
Threshold Selection
The threshold selection is performed for both models, since the threshold varies for the two
different dimensions of the model.
The novelty scores for all patients are estimated.
All of
these novelty scores are then used to find the most optimal threshold. A range of thresholds are
defined. For the 4-dimensional model, a range from 0 to 32 with steps of 0.01 is defined, whereas
this range for the 6-dimensional model is ranging from 0 to 35 with steps of 0.01. The maximum
of this range is defined to be the maximum of the the novelty scores plus 5 to make sure the
entire range is covered. The sensitivity and specificity based on the entire range of thresholds
are then calculated in order to make a receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve. The most
optimal threshold is then found by calculating the distance to (0,1) in the ROC curve for each
of the points on this curve based on Equation 1.7[49].
distance to (0, 1) =
p
((1 −sp)2 + (1 −se)2),
(1.7)
where sp is the specificity and se is the sensitivity. The point having the smallest distance to
(0,1) provides the index for the most optimal threshold.

Discussion of ReferenceStability Algorithm
When investigating the plots of the hourly mean value for the 71 stable patients in Figure 5.2, it
is seen that the HR values as well as the RR values are within the defined normal ranges. When
investigating the HR, it is seen that it starts around 80-85 bpm in the first hours, after which
is drops a bit towards hour 16, and then it rises around 15-20 bpm the last 8 hours of the plot.
Even though it rises, it still stays within the normal range. The RR is more uniform and stays
within a deviation of a few breathes per minute in the entire 24 hours. When looking at the
SPO2, it is seen that it should be above 95% to be in the defined normal range. Unfortunately,
this is not the case for the stable patients. All values are below this lower limit. It is seen
that the SPO2 is ranging around 92-94% in the entire 24 hour range meaning that it does not
deviate a lot. The corresponding plot for the unstable patients is seen in Figure 5.3. The HR
is seen to be a bit more consistent than for the stable patients ranging around 85 bpm. The
values of the HR is still seen to be within the normal ranges. The RR is ranging around 16
breathes per minute and is consistent throughout the entire day still being within the normal
ranges. When investigating the SPO2 plot, it is seen that it is also below the lower normal limit
for the unstable patients. It still ranges around 92-94%. The systolic BP seems to be lower
for the unstable patients than for the stable patients, but it still seems to be higher than the
normal value. The mean values for the systolic BP deviates more than for the stable patients.
Comparing these plots for the stable and unstable patients, it is important to bear in mind that
it is an average taken over all the stable and unstable patients, respectively. Though, an average
is good at giving an indication of how the vital signs for the two groups is developing over time.
Another aspect when comparing these two patient groups by these plots is that is does not seem
like there is a large difference between them. When the values for the two groups are this much
alike, it is difficult for an algorithm to separate these and thereby classify them correctly. One
problem is that 24 hours is a large time range to define the two classes. A lot happens in a day.
The values will naturally develop over time due to for instance night time where the values will
more typically be lower than during day when the patient is awake. Since there is a difference
in time and the patients do not get discharged the same time of day or get an SAE at the exact
same time, this difference in values over time in a day can not be seen in these plots. These
plots illustrates the last 24 hours for each patients, but the start and end time of these 24 hours
is not the same for all patients. This will also affect the average values. The 24 hours is a large
problem for especially the unstable patients. It is not always the case that it is possible to see a
difference in vital sign values 24 hours before an SAE occur. Furthermore, the unstable patient
group is based on many different types of SAEs. The unstable patients are based on all outcomes
described in the method section. Not all of these SAEs will necessarily affect the vital signs,
which will therefore cause the unstable patient parameters to look like a stable patient, but is
defined as unstable.

patient cohorts. This table describes the mean and standard deviation for the entire 24 hours
and not hourly as the previous described plots. It is seen that the mean value for the HR is

being slightly higher for the unstable patient group, but still within the normal range. It is seen
that the standard deviation is 16.44 bpm for the stable patients, whereas it is 18.42 bpm for the
unstable patients. This indicates that the deviation is slightly higher for the unstable patients.

16.49 breathes/min, and the standard deviation 3.48 breathes/min and 4.15 breathes/min for
the stable and unstable patient group, respectively. Another pattern is seen in the systolic BP,
where the mean value is highest for the stable patient group. The mean values are 135.38 mmHg
and 125.92 mmHg, respectively. The standard deviations are 18.97 mmHg and 21.10 mmHg
for the two patient groups, respectively, thereby having a higher deviation for the unstable
patient group.
The mean values for the SPO2 are almost alike for the two patient groups

a higher novelty score having a variability of 0 mmHg than 50 mmHg for the systolic BP. This
is unnatural, since an unstable patient will most likely vary more than a stable patient. The
same pattern is seen in the variability index of the RR, where it is deemed more normal to vary

article are 70% and 70%, respectively. This indicates that the ReferenceStability algorithm is
better at catching the stable patients in the 6-dimensional model and a bit worse for the 4-
dimensional model. Though, for the 6-dimensional model, this comes with the expense of not
classifying many of the unstable patients correctly. When comparing the performance of the
ReferenceStability algorithm with the reference model, several aspects are important to bear
in mind. First of all, the definition of unstable patients are not the same. In the reference
article, unstable patients are defined as patients transferred to the ICU or being death, while
the unstable patients in the ReferenceStability algorithm is defined as patients having an SAE.
When being transferred to the ICU, the condition of the patient is really bad. When having an
SAE, the patient is not in the same bad standing as those being transferred to the ICU. The
unstable patient definition for the reference article is thereby based on patients whose condition
are much worse than the ones used in this report. This makes it easier for a model to classify the
unstable patients, since the condition will probably affect the vital signs more in the case with
ICU or death compared to SAEs. Second of all, the foundation of the data is not the same. The
reference article uses data obtained manually a couple of times a day, while the data in this report
is continuous data throughout all 24 hours. Lastly, the reference article uses a 5-dimensional and
a 7-dimensional model. They have an extra vital sign to use for the classification since they also
use the temperature of the patient. This data is not available in the WARD data. Therefore,
the model of this report has one vital sign less to classify with making it more difficult for the
model to distinguish the groups.

Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation
The thesis make use of Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV) when evaluating the algo-
rithms. This is due to the size of the data set after removing those patients not containing data
up to discharge and those not having an SAE. Besides this, if divided into a training, test, and
validation set, it would never cause the same amount of data in the sets respectively all the time
anyway since the amount of patients will differ due to different time windows. Patients without
data in the given time window is removed, which would therefore make different sets for each
window length. LOOCV is a type of k-fold cross-validation which is a procedure that estimates
model performance. This cross-validation is defined by the hyper parameter k controlling how
many subsets a given data set should be split into. When the data set is split, each of these
subsets is used as a test set while using all of the other subsets together to form the training data
set. Thereby, k-fold cross-validation fits and evaluates k different models.[55] This thesis uses
LOOCV for evaluating the novelty score of each patient against the subset of stable patients.
First, each of the stable patients are evaluated against all the other stable patients as illustrated
in Figure 6.1 providing a novelty score for each of these patients. Afterwards, all of the unstable
patients are evaluated against all of the stable patients thereby providing a novelty score for
each of the unstable patients too as seen in Figure 6.2.
LOOCV is appropriate if the data set is small. This is due to the fact that the model elsewise
can be overfitted during the training of the model, which lead to a biased estimation of the
performance of the model. Another benefit of using LOOCV is that it provides many fits leading
to that the evaluated models will be a more robust estimate of the performance of the model
since each patient thereby represent the entirety of the test set. Furthermore, as estimation
procedure like LOOCV is deterministic avoiding randomness.[55]

Window Length Before the SAE
Window length before the SAE is referring to the amount of time used of the data before the

be able to most accurately classify the patients. The window length is defined in hours and
afterwards applied in number of samples. The feature optimisation is investigating the following

optimisation is investigating the following values of hours before the SAE: 0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 12,

two classes. This window is taken just before the discharge.
Number of Nearest Neighbours
Number of nearest neighbours refers to the number of nearest neighbours used in the calculation
of the standard deviation used in the model. The feature optimisation investigates either 5, 10,
or 15 nearest neighbours.
Number of Inputs to use
Number of inputs to use is referring to the dimension of the model. The model is investigating
if it is most optimal to use only the five vital signs; HR, RR, SPO2, systolic BP, and diastolic
BP or if it is more optimal to use the five vital signs as well as the variability indices for these
five vital signs. The feature optimisation is thereby investigating if the dimension of the model
should be 5 or 10.
Statistical Parameter to use
Statistical parameter to use is referring to the statistical parameter used when calculating the
input to the model. The model needs one value for each input. The feature optimisation therefore
investigates whether it is most optimal to use the mean value or the median value for the inputs.

Another new aspect in the StabilityEstimationII algorithm is the estimation of TEW. This is

categories depending on the prediction of the data or patient. True Positives (TP) are the ob-

is in fact unstable. False Positives (FP) are the patients classified as unstable, who are in fact
stable. False Negatives (FN) are the patients classified as stable, but who are actually unsta-
ble. True Negatives (TN) are the patients who are classified an stable and actually are stable.[56]

types of validation features are chosen. First, the accuracy is calculated. This feature describes
how often the classifier is able to classify the patients correctly.[56] Two other features are the

describes the proportion of patients classified as unstable actually being unstable. This means

CHAPTER 8. DISCUSSION
Chapter 8
Discussion
The purpose of this chapter is to discuss the results obtained in the work of the novelty algo-
rithms developed in this thesis. The comparison of the results with previous work on the topic
is discussed as well as the performance of the StabilityEstimationI and StabilityEstimationII al-
gorithms. The use of such algorithms in a clinical alarm systems is analysed. Furthermore, the
future work is proposed and discussed.
1
StabilityEstimationI Algorithm
The performance of the StabilityEstimationI algorithm is seen in Table 7.2 and in the confusion
matrix illustrated in Figure 7.1. Based on these results, it is seen that the performance of the
classifier can be improved. It classifies many patients correctly, but it also classifies some patients
incorrectly. Some of the correctly and incorrectly classified patients are seen in Figure 7.2, 7.3,
7.4, and 7.5. Figure 7.2 illustrates a patient correctly classified as unstable. It illustrates patient
388 suffering from a surgical site infection. It is seen that the HR is ranging just below and
above the upper normal limit thereby indicating a HR in the high end. The RR varies a lot

Novelty Methods for Stability Index Estimation
An aspect of the algorithms is that they investigate stability through several parameters and not
just one parameter. It is well known that some specific situations can cause a rise in for instance
the BP. Though, that does not mean that the patient is unstable. Therefore, it is important to
investigate several measures and combine these to estimate stability.
One of the things being difficult in this master thesis is the stability definition. It is difficult to
accurately classify and distinguish several classes of patients on a definition that is not precisely
defined. All results obtained in this thesis is based on an assumption about stability definition.
It also makes it difficult to compare with other algorithms doing the same, since the stability
definition needs to be exactly the same in order to compare. Stability is not precisely defined as
for instance ST elevation for ischaemia. When dealing with stability algorithms, many assump-
tions needs to be made due to the fact that stability is not precisely defined. This thesis defines
instability based on 36 different complications and SAEs. Unfortunately, it is not possible to
define that the vital signs are affected every time by these SAEs. The clinical assumption is

that these will affect the vital signs often, but it is not possible to say that it is the case always.
This fact makes it difficult to evaluate the algorithm with certainty. At least, it needs to be
considered when the results are interpreted. The algorithm is based on the assumption that the
vital signs are affected by all the SAEs every time they occur, but since this is not clinically
possible to say that it is the case always, it is important to remember when interpreting the
results. It is also important to bear in mind that whenever stable and unstable patients are

stable and unstable in this thesis is the same in other papers.
When using a definition of unstable patients based on 36 different outcomes, it will also affect

the outcome. This also means that not all outcomes necessarily affect vital signs, and even if
they do, it will not be at the same time. Some outcomes will affect the vital signs maybe up to 24
hours before occurring, while others will affect the vital signs after the outcome occurred. There
are many ways to be sick. Sometimes values gets affected, some times they do not. Thereby,
it is also a possibility that there is a lack of information about a patient’s illness. This could
lead to an inclusion of a patient with a disease into the stable group, which would affect the
performance of the algorithm.
When dealing with vital sign measurements, another issue arises. Vital sign measurements are
affected by numerous different factors. It can vary due to age, time of day, gender, activity,
medication, emotions, and the environment. When dealing with activity, the BP, RR, and HR
will rise, while the SPO2 will be lowered. This affect the performance of the algorithm since the
patients do not lie in bed all day. The vital signs will be affected when they leave the bed to
walk around or go to the bathroom or if they are nervous. This type of information is unknown
to the algorithm, which therefore will be affected by this.[59][60][61][62][63][64]
Another issue with defining stability depending on complications is that the time stamp for the
complication is the time where the the complication is caught by the medical staff. This time
is not necessarily the time where the complication occur. This creates some uncertainty since
it is unknown when the complication actually arises. Therefore, the alarm system is based on
predicting before it is diagnosed since the information regarding when the complication occur
is unknown. It also needs to be taken into account when evaluating all results including the
analysis of the TEW. Furthermore, when dealing with TEW, it is also important to remember
that it is found as an average between all complications. It is therefore not possible to say that
it is always possible to alert the clinical staff X hours before.
When interpreting the results of the StabilityEstimationII algorithm, it is also noticeable to
remember that if indeed some of the false alarms provided is a real alarm, it changes the per-
formance of the algorithm. The algorithm investigate each segment of the 24 hours. If a patient
labelled as stable is in fact unstable, as is indicated by some of the examples illustrated, this
exact patient causes a false alarm each segment it will be deemed as unstable. The algorithm
is not designed to stop monitoring the patient, if an alarm is given. So for the stable patients,
all segments are evaluated. This would therefore cause many false alarms for just one patient,
if the patient has an undiscovered diasease, but is included in the stable patient group. So for
each segment evaluated, this type of patient will cause a false alarm which could in fact be a
true alarm.

Future Work
In order for the algorithm to be used in a clinical alarm system, some improvements needs to
be made.
First of all, the model can be tuned with more inputs such as laboratory values,
demographic information, underlying diseases, medication and so on, and more grid search can

An aspect affecting the performance of each of the novelty algorithms developed in this thesis
is the lack of knowledge about underlying diseases or problems by the patients. If the patient
suffers from another disease which affects the vital signs, it is unknown for this model. When
developing these models, the only available information is the SAEs, which are used to define an
unstable patient. A stable patient is a patient without either an AE or an SAE in the monitoring
period. Thereby, a patient labelled as stable can have a condition illustrated in the vital signs,
which therefore affects the model. In order to make sure that the model performs most optimal,
it is needed to take these underlying conditions into account so the model is based on patients
actually being stable. It is furthermore not known if the patients take any kind of medicine.

If a patient for instance take medication to lower their BP, it affects the inputs used to this

the circadian rhythm. It is well known that the vital signs change along with the circadian
rhythm. This is not taken into account in the current version of the algorithms. Though, it is
an important aspect to investigate. The models are based on stable patients in a specified time
period. If for instance one hour of data is used, it will often be within the same time of day since
the stable patients are based on discharge and discharge of patients is done around twice a day.
Therefore, when using the algorithm, the time of day will affect the classifier.

be set to provide an alarm with a lower likelihood of instability than the one already defined,
since the clinical staff already have an eye on this patient.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
The overall goal of this master thesis is to find complications in order to evaluate stability in
high-risk hospitalised patients early to alert the clinical staff that can then act as needed to
reduce the severity of the complication. The algorithm performing this is developed for the
clinical support system in the WARD project. The goal thereby is to monitor these high-risk
hospitalised patients continuously 24/7 in order to catch deterioration as early as possible to
prevent severe complications.
First, a literature study was conducted to review the state of the art methods concerning sta-
bility estimation. The literature study showed that stability estimation can be performed in
several ways, but the method of using kernel density estimation seemed promising. Therefore,
the algorithms in this study is based on kernel density estimation.
The thesis build upon three different algorithms; ReferenceStability algorithm, StabilityEsti-
mationI algorithm, and StabilityEstimationII algorithm. The ReferenceStability algorithm is
building on the reference method based on the article Vital-Sign Data Fusion Models for Post-
operative Patients by Marco A. F. Pimentel et al.[1]. The StabilityEstimationI algorithm is an
improvement of the ReferenceStability algorithm performing hyper parameter optimisation. The
StabilityEstimationII algorithm investigates the use of stability estimation in an alarm system
by looking at time of early warning. All algorithms include preprocessing consisting of zero-mean
unit-variance normalisation of the inputs. These normalised inputs are then used to estimate
the probability density function through kernel density estimation after which the novelty score
for each patient is found. The novelty score is used to evaluate whether the patient is stable or
unstable.
The algorithms are used on WARD data from post-surgery patients.
Stability is defined as
follows; stable patients are those without any adverse or severe adverse event, while the unsta-
ble patients are those with a severe adverse event. The ReferenceStability algorithm obtained
an accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity for the 4-dimensional model only build on vital signs
on 63.55%, 55.56%, and 67.61%, respectively, while the 6-dimensional model also incorporat-
ing variability indices obtained an accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity of 62.62%, 8.33%, and
90.14%, respectively.
The StabilityEstimationI algorithm obtained an accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity of 79.17%,
80.00%, and 78.26%, respectively.
The results of the StabilityEstimationII algorithm can be read in many ways. The most optimal
model is estimating the stability of the patient each hour based on 60 minutes of data provid-
ing a sensitivity of 70% and a specificity of 86.3%. With this algorithm, the alarm is provided
approximately 12.8 hours before the complication and a false alarm occur every 7.29 hours.
The future work on the algorithms consist of improvement of the issues. It is possible to build the
algorithm on more inputs such as laboratory values, demographic values, underlying diseases,
medication and so on. Furthermore, the stability definition needs to be reviewed. In order for
