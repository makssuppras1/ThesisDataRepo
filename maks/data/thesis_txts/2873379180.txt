Benchmarking IBD Detection Tools with Simulated Genomic Data 
Abstract 
Identity-by-descent (IBD) segments are continuous stretches of DNA inherited from a shared 
ancestor without recombination. They are widely used to study recent relatedness, population 
history, and demographic events in human genetics. However, the accuracy of IBD detection 
tools varies depending on both algorithmic design and the characteristics of the underlying 
data, including recombination rate, population structure, and admixture. 
This thesis evaluates seven widely used IBD detection tools: Beagle (FastIBD), RefinedIBD, 
GERMLINE, Hap-IBD, IBDseq, RaPID-2, and Truffle, across a series of simulated 
demographic scenarios. All data were generated using msprime, a coalescent-based simulator 
that allows full control over recombination, mutation, and ancestry. Four simulation groups 
were tested: (1) varying effective population size (Ne), (2) different times since population split, 
(3) varying admixture proportions, and (4) varying times since admixture. Ground-truth IBD 
segments were extracted directly from simulation tree sequences, allowing direct comparison 
of tool outputs to the expected signal. 
Each tool was evaluated on total IBD detection, segment length distribution, and pairwise 
sharing. The results showed that most tools deviated from the simulated IBD segments, some 
overestimating dramatically, others underestimating or ignoring demographic structure 
altogether. Admixture proved especially difficult: no method accurately recovered the expected 
decay in IBD signal. Hap-IBD and Truffle were the only tools that consistently mirrored 
biological expectations across all non-admixed scenarios. Beagle and RefinedIBD often 
produced inverted trends and inflated sharing, while RaPID overestimated IBD even though it 
captured the correct direction of change. GERMLINE was highly sensitive to parameter 
settings, with inconsistent behaviour. IBDseq was conservative but missed much of the signal. 
These findings highlight the importance of benchmarking IBD tools under realistic and varied 
demographic conditions. No tool was universally reliable, and their performance depended 
heavily on both algorithmic design and demographic context. This work provides a 
reproducible framework for evaluating IBD detection methods and emphasizes the need for 
careful tool selection and parameter tuning when studying relatedness in genomic datasets.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Genetic Relatedness and Inheritance  
A core concept in genetics is that individuals, who are closely related, share more DNA with 
each other because they inherited DNA segments from the same recent ancestors [1]. The 
more recent the common ancestor is, the more DNA is typically shared. As the relationship 
between the individuals becomes more distant, the shared DNA decreases, typically in shorter 
and fewer segments [2]. This is because of recombination, a process during reproduction 
where chromosomes are shuffled, breaking long ancestral segments into smaller pieces each 
generation. Over time, this reshuffling dilutes the original shared segments [3]. 
These shared segments between individuals, known as genetic relatedness, can be used in 
different aspects of genetic research. Across all species, genetic relatedness helps 
researchers trace lineage, estimate evolutionary distances and study inheritance patterns [4]. 
In humans, the scope of genetic relatedness expands even further. It helps map the inheritance 
of complex traits, identify genetic risk factors for disease, and understand population history 
[1] [3]. So needless to say, DNA holds extremely important information.   
As commonly known, the human genome is made up of DNA inherited from our parents. But 
what makes each person genetically unique is the way 
this DNA differs at specific positions [5].  
A basic unit of such variation is an allele, which is a 
version of a gene or sequence found at a specific 
position on the genome. Most people carry two alleles at 
each position: one from each parent [6].  
When alleles are inherited together along a stretch of 
DNA, they form a haplotype: a combination of variants 
that tends to be passed on as a block.  
One of the most common types of variation is the single 
nucleotide polymorphism, or SNP. These are positions 
in the genome where a single base (A, T, C, or G) can 
differ between individuals, shown in Figure 1 [7].  
 
1.2 Identity-by-Descent (IBD) 
Within all of this structure and variation, one aspect of the 
genome stands out and is particularly informative for 
studying genetic relatedness: contiguous segments 
inherited from a common ancestor without recombination. 
These segments are known as identity-by-descent (IBD), 
illustrated in Figure 2 [3]. Unlike most regions of the 
genome that are broken up and shuffled each generation 
by recombination, IBD segments are known to be passed 
down intact [10].   
This intactness is part of their informative power. Because 
they preserve a continuous stretch of ancestral DNA, the length of an IBD segment can act as 
a ‘natural clock’: long segments suggest recent relatedness (e.g. cousins), while short 
segments reflect older connections (e.g. shared ancestry 10-20 generations ago) [10].

In this way, IBD segments offer a direct and measurable signal of shared ancestry. Their size, 
number and genomic distribution carry information not only about individual relationships, but 
also about how entire populations have changed over time [11]. This makes them a powerful 
foundation for broader insights into genetic history, especially for population genetics [2].   
 
1.3 Population Genetics 
1.3.1 Effective Population Size and Coalescence  
Population genetics is the study of how genetic variation changes due to evolutionary 
processes like mutation, migration and natural selection. It provides the foundation for 
understanding patterns in diversity within and between populations [12]. One key concept in 
population genetics is the effective population size (Ne). This isn’t the actual number of 
individuals in the population, but rather a measure of how many individuals are effectively 
passing on their genes to the next generation. A smaller Ne means that genes are inherited 
from a narrower pool of ancestors, which makes random changes more noticeable over time 
[13].  
This ties directly into the concept of coalescence, which refers to tracing DNA back through 
generations to find a common ancestor. In small populations, this happens more quickly, so 
people tend to share more and longer IBD segments. In large populations, it takes longer to 
find a shared ancestor, so shared segments are shorter and rarer [14].  
 
1.3.2 Population Splits and Admixture 
Another major concept in population genetics is the population split. A split occurs when an 
ancestral population divides into two or more groups. This can happen, for example, when one 
group migrates to a new geographic region and becomes isolated from the rest. Over time, 
each group will develop its own unique mutations and genetic drift, creating new distinct 
genetic profiles [15].  
Even after a split, individuals from different populations may still share IBD segments. These 
shared segments can provide clues about how recently the populations separated, and 
whether there has been genetic exchange since 
then. This back-and-forth movement of genetic 
material after a split is known as admixture [16].  
Admixture occurs when previously isolated 
populations start interbreeding, blending their 
genetic backgrounds. The result is a genome 
composed of ancestry blocks from multiple 
sources as shown in Figure 3. These mixed 
genomes 
are 
highly 
informative 
and 
by 
identifying where each segment comes from, it 
can be estimated when admixture occurred and 
how much each ancestral population contributed

1.3.3 Evolutionary Forces  
Besides population splits and admixture, several other processes play important roles in 
shaping genetic variation. Migration introduces new genetic variants into a population as 
individuals move between groups [19]. Selection drives changes in allele frequencies by 
favouring traits that increase survival or reproduction [20]. Genetic drift, the random fluctuation 
of allele frequencies over time, is especially influential in small populations [21]. One extreme 
case of drift is a population bottleneck, where a population temporarily shrinks to a small size 
which will cause a sharp, random reduction in genetic diversity [22]. Each of these processes 
leaves distinct signatures in the genome.   
 
1.3.4 IBD in Population Genetics  
When population genetics and IBD research are combined, they offer a powerful look into 
human history. By quantifying how often and how many genomes share IBD segments, 
researchers can estimate recent effective population sizes, detect historical bottlenecks, and 
trace rare gene variants [10] [23].  
Applied across multiple individuals or entire populations, IBD sharing enables even broader 
insights. It can help infer divergence times between subpopulations and uncover historical 
gene exchange [11] [24]. In admixed populations, the length and origin of IBD segments can 
reveal not only whether admixture occurred, but also when and between which groups. Even 
in large populations without strong structure, IBD can detect subtle patterns like hidden 
relatedness or gradual genetic shifts across geography [25] [26].  
But to extract any of this information, IBD segments must first be reliably identified across the 
genome. That’s where IBD detection comes in. 
 
1.4 IBD Detection 
Detecting IBD segments is the computational process of identifying long stretches of DNA that 
two individuals inherited from a shared ancestor without interruption from recombination [27]. 
Because these segments reflect shared ancestry, and their length relates to how recent that 
ancestor was, reliable detection is key to translating genomic data into demographic insights 
[28].  
In practice, this means scanning genomes to find haplotypes that are identical and unlikely to 
match by chance, while being long enough to suggest descent from a common ancestor. The 
challenge is distinguishing true IBD segments from random similarity or technical noise [29]. 
To do this, different tools use a variety of algorithmic strategies which generally fall into three 
categories. 
 
1.4.1 Algorithmic Strategies for IBD Detection 
First, probabilistic approaches, often built on Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), treat the IBD 
state as a hidden variable and estimate the likelihood that a genomic region is truly IBD, given 
the observed genotypes. Some tools, use models that compare how well a haplotype can be 
"copied" from others in the dataset, which allows for more accurate detection in structured 
populations. These models incorporate factors like recombination rate, allele frequency, and 
genotyping error, which makes them more accurate but are computationally intensive [30] 
[31].

Second, seed-and-extend or hashing-based methods begin by identifying short, identical 
stretches between individuals (seeds), often using exact or near-exact matching. Once a seed 
is found, the algorithm tries to extend the segment in both directions while tolerating some 
mismatches. These methods are computationally efficient and scale well, but they’re more 
vulnerable to data quality [26].  
Third, graph-based or clustering approaches build networks where nodes represent individuals 
or haplotypes, and edges indicate shared similarity across the genome. By clustering these 
graphs, tools can detect regions of likely shared ancestry. These approaches can capture 
complex population structure, but they’re less precise for pinpointing exact IBD segments and 
often require additional filtering to get clean, pairwise results [32].  
 
1.4.2 Data Requirements and Preprocessing 
Despite their algorithmic differences, most IBD detection tools rely on similar inputs, 
assumptions, and preprocessing steps. A key requirement is phased data, where each allele 
is assigned to its original chromosome copy. While a few tools can handle unphased 
genotypes, most perform better with phased haplotypes because IBD segments are defined 
as continuous matches on the same chromosome. Without phasing, even perfectly shared 
segments can be missed [33].  
Another essential input is the genetic map, which translates physical base-pair positions along 
the genome into genetic distances, measured in centimorgans (cM). A centimorgan reflects 
recombination probability rather than physical length: 1 cM roughly corresponds to a 1% 
chance of recombination between two positions per generation [34]. This allows segment 
lengths to be interpreted in terms of generational time rather than raw DNA distance. This is 
crucial because IBD segment age is estimated based on recombination, not physical distance, 
so interpreting length in cM provides a more 
meaningful measure of generational time. On 
average, 1 cM spans about 1 million base pairs 
(1 Mb) in the human genome, visualized in 
Figure 4 [36]. Recombination is not uniform; 
some regions, like hotspots, recombine more 
frequently than others. In humans, population-
specific 
maps 
exist 
that 
reflect 
known 
recombination hotspots, but in cases where no 
population-specific map is available, uniform or 
simulated maps may be used [37].  
Most current IBD detection tools expect 
genotype data in Variant Call Format (VCF), a 
widely used standard that stores variant 
positions, genotype calls, and sometimes 
phasing or quality metrics. VCFs are flexible and 
transparent, but can be large and slow to parse 
in some contexts. Alternatively, some tools use 
PLINK file formats (e.g., .ped, .map, .bed), which are more compact and computationally 
efficient but sometimes lack detailed metadata [38]. In practice, datasets often need to be 
converted between formats depending on the requirements of the chosen method [39].  Finally, 
the data must represent diploid genotypes since most tools are optimized for human genomes 
[40]. This presents a limitation when applying IBD detection methods to organisms with 
different ploidy levels or to ancient DNA samples where coverage is low or alleles are 
uncertain.

1.5 Challenges in IBD Detection 
While many tools exist to detect IBD segments, their accuracy depends on both the quality of 
the input data and the underlying assumptions of the algorithms. Several challenges limit how 
precisely and reliably these segments can be identified:  
1. Recombination erodes shared ancestry over time  
By definition, IBD segments are regions inherited from a shared ancestor without 
recombination. But recombination occurs in every generation, breaking up shared haplotypes 
into smaller fragments. Over time, these fragments become shorter, harder to detect, and more 
likely to fall below the detection threshold. As a result, older segments are frequently missed, 
which means there’s a clear bias in IBD detection towards recent relatedness [3] [41].   
2. Identity-by-state (IBS) can mimic IBD  
Not all long allele matches between individuals indicate shared descent. In regions of low 
diversity, unrelated individuals can appear to share long segments purely by chance, which is 
known as identity-by-state. If detection algorithms don’t account for this, false positives can 
inflate estimates of relatedness and obscure true demographic signals [42] [43].  
3. SNP density limits resolution  
Detecting short IBD segments, which is important for identifying older shared ancestry, requires 
dense genetic data. Whole-genome sequencing provides this, but many studies, especially 
older or large-scale population studies, rely on SNP arrays. These arrays measure only a 
subset of variable positions in the genome, so the number and spacing of SNPs directly affect 
how precisely shared segments can be detected. If SNPs are too sparse, short segments are 
often missed, which reduces the accuracy of distant ancestry detection [41].   
4. Admixture complicates detection and interpretation  
Admixture poses a major challenge for IBD-based analyses. In admixed individuals, 
haplotypes from different ancestral sources are intertwined throughout the genome. This 
pattern can throw off expectations about how long IBD segments should be, especially for tools 
that assume individuals come from a single, uniform population. Some tools may misinterpret 
ancestry switches as recombination breakpoints, or miss segments that span multiple 
ancestries altogether. If these issues aren’t accounted for, estimates of when admixture 
happened, which groups were involved, or how much each group contributed can end up being 
inaccurate [44][45].   
5. Other sources of noise: mutation, phasing, and genotyping errors  
Even when a segment is truly IBD, technical artifacts can obscure its detection. Mutation, 
although rare at the scale of these segments, introduces mismatches that may break apparent 
sequence identity, especially in older segments. Phasing and genotyping errors can both 
distort IBD detection, either by breaking up true segments or creating false ones. At the same 
time, genuine IBD segments, especially older or shorter ones, may be missed altogether, 
contributing to false negatives. This skews demographic inference by underestimating shared 
ancestry [28][46].

1.6 Gaps in Current Benchmarking of IBD Detection Methods  
Despite the number of tools now available for detecting IBD, there are still improvements to be 
made in benchmarking methods. Without reliable benchmarking, researchers risk drawing 
misleading conclusions about ancestry, population history, or disease risk from IBD-based 
analyses, especially in diverse populations where tool assumptions may not hold. 
Most evaluations use real human datasets where the true IBD segments aren’t known, so 
accuracy can’t be measured directly. Instead, studies rely on indirect comparisons, how well 
they recover known data, or how useful their output is for related tasks like estimating kinship 
or inferring demographic history [47][48].  
Simulations offer a valuable way to benchmark IBD detection tools, since they provide full 
control over population parameters and known segment boundaries. But in practice, many 
published benchmarks have used different datasets, inconsistent performance metrics, or 
simulations with poorly documented settings. Details like recombination maps, genotyping 
error models, or segment definitions are often missing, which makes it hard to reproduce 
results or understand tool performance across studies. Even when simulations are used, 
they’re not always described clearly enough to allow direct comparison or replication [49].   
Another issue is that tools are usually tested under the same conditions they were designed 
for. For example, a method made for phased, high-density genotype data might be tested only 
on exactly that, even though many users apply it to datasets with lower coverage, older 
samples, or complex ancestry [49].  
Even when tools are compared, the output is often filtered first using thresholds on segment 
length, SNP density, or scoring metrics. But these filters vary widely across studies, which 
makes it hard to compare results in any consistent way [49].  
And finally, most benchmarks don’t actually test how tools behave when you change key 
demographic parameters like effective population size, bottlenecks, split times, or migration. 
These factors are known to shape how much IBD we expect and what it looks like. But most 
studies don’t test tool performance across these conditions [49].  
These gaps highlight the need for systematic benchmarking on simulated data with known IBD 
segments and explicitly defined demographic histories. Only then is it possible to evaluate 
whether tools recover the segments that truly reflect shared ancestry, especially under 
conditions that mimic real evolutionary processes.

1.7 Aim of the Project 
This project set out to systematically evaluate the performance of seven IBD detection tools 
using simulated genotype data where the true IBD segments are known. By running all tools 
on the same controlled datasets, the goal was to assess how each method performs under 
different demographic scenarios and to identify their strengths and limitations.  
The simulations were generated with msprime [50], a coalescent-based simulator that allows 
precise control over demographic parameters and tracks exact segment inheritance. Msprime 
was selected because it allows complete control over the coalescent history, efficient 
simulation of large populations, and direct output of tree sequences from which true IBD 
segments can be extracted which makes it particularly well suited for benchmarking identity-
by-descent detection methods.  
Four types of population scenarios were simulated:  
• 
Varying effective population size (Ne): Randomly mating populations with no splits or 
admixture, but different Ne values. These simulations tested how IBD abundance 
changes with population size.  
• 
Varying population splits: Pairs of populations that diverged at different timepoints, 
without further gene flow. These scenarios assessed how tools perform under ancient 
divergence and reduced shared ancestry.  
• 
Varying admixture proportion: Populations formed by mixing two ancestral sources in 
different ratios (e.g., 10/90, 30/70). This tested whether tools are biased when one 
ancestry is underrepresented.  
• 
Varying admixture timing: Populations with fixed admixture ratios but different times (in 
generations) since the admixture event. These tested whether tools can still detect 
older, more fragmented segments of shared ancestry.  
1.8 Hypothesis 
Each simulation scenario was chosen not only to test tool compatibility across conditions, but 
also to examine specific hypotheses about how demographic complexity influences IBD 
detection.  
• 
Varying effective population size (Ne):  Smaller Ne leads to more recent common 
ancestors, increasing the frequency and length of IBD segments. In contrast, large Ne 
results in deeper genealogies and fewer detectable segments [3][51].  
o Expectation: Total IBD should decrease as Ne increases.  
• 
Varying population splits:  Older population splits reduce the amount of shared ancestry 
between populations. Over time, recombination fragments any remaining shared 
segments [3][51].  
o Expectation: As the time since divergence increases, both the number and 
length of IBD segments between populations should decline.  
• 
Varying admixture proportion: In admixed individuals, chromosomes are mixes of 
different ancestries. When one ancestry is much less represented, its segments are 
shorter and less likely to be detected [3][51].   
o Expectation: Tools may detect fewer segments from the minor ancestry, 
especially in highly imbalanced admixture (e.g. 10/90 splits), skewing IBD 
sharing across pairs  
• 
Varying admixture timing: As more generations pass since an admixture event, 
recombination breaks long ancestry tracts into smaller pieces [3][51].   
o Expectation: Older admixture should result in shorter, harder-to-detect IBD 
segments. Total detected IBD is expected to decline with time since admixture.

2. Materials & Methods 
This chapter outlines the full pipeline used to evaluate identity-by-descent (IBD) detection tools 
across a range of simulated demographic scenarios. A visual overview of the process is shown 
in Figure 5.  
All python scripts, files and tool execution bash scripts that were used in the project are 
available in the GitHub repository: https://github.com/MI00s/IBD-tool-comparison.git   
File paths in scripts are modular and can be adjusted to replicate the entire pipeline from 
simulation to plotting. Outputs are version-controlled, and results shown in this thesis can be 
regenerated by following the README instructions. 
 
Figure 5: Overview of the IBD detection benchmarking pipeline. The pipeline is divided into several main stages: simulation of 
populations, maps creations, running the tools, analysis and plotting the results.  
 
2.1 Simulate populations  
The first step was to generate simulated genotype data for each of the four population groups 
using msprime v 1.3.3 [50] and tskit [52]. Both packages were installed and run within a conda 
environment.   
To benchmark IBD detection tools under controlled and transparent conditions, all simulations 
were based on human-like settings. Each simulation modelled a diploid genome of 250 
megabases (Mb), chosen to roughly match the size of human chromosome 1. A uniform 
recombination rate of 1×10⁻⁸ and a mutation rate of 1.25×10⁻⁸ per base per generation were 
used, following standard estimates for the human genome [53][54]. At these rates, a 
recombination event is expected every 100 million bases per generation, and a new mutation 
every 80 million bases. These parameters control how much genetic variation is introduced 
and how quickly shared segments are broken up.  
All simulations generated 100 diploid individuals (200 haplotypes). Genotypes were saved in 
VCF format, and the complete genealogical information was stored as .trees files. IBD 
segments were extracted directly from the tree sequences using a minimum span of 1 cM, 
which is a commonly used lower threshold for reliable IBD in human populations [41][55]. 
Segments shorter than this are more likely to reflect background linkage rather than recent 
shared ancestry.  
==PAGE:14==

Simulations were run using the Discrete Time Wright-Fisher (DTWF) model, which msprime 
applies automatically when standard coalescent assumptions no longer hold, e.g. when 
simulating recent generations [56]. This model produces more accurate patterns of recent 
shared ancestry and is better suited for benchmarking IBD detection. 
 
2.2 Baseline Population Simulations  
To assess tool performance in a simple, structure-free context, populations with different 
effective population sizes (Ne) were simulated with no populations splits or admixture. The Ne 
values: 150, 500, 1.000, 2.500, 5.000, and 10.000, were selected to span a wide spectrum, 
from extreme bottlenecks to realistic values observed in present-day human populations. The 
smallest values (e.g., 150) were included to generate longer and more easily detectable IBD 
segments, while higher values (5.000–10.000) reflect realistic demographic settings [57][58].  
This simulation set was generated using the script simulate_msprime_ne.py, available in the 
GitHub repository.   
 
2.3 Population Split Simulations  
To test how tools perform when shared ancestry becomes older and more degraded, 
simulations were created where a single ancestral population split into two separate groups at 
different timepoints: 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1.000, and 2.000 generations ago. This range 
was chosen to capture a gradual loss of IBD over time, spanning both recent and distant splits, 
without linking them to any specific historical event. The idea was to observe how quickly IBD 
segments decay as shared ancestry becomes more ancient.  
For each split time, two equally sized populations were simulated, and 50 diploid individuals 
were sampled from each. Simulations were repeated for three different effective population 
sizes: 250, 500, and 1.000. While these values are lower than current estimates for modern 
human populations (typically between 5.000 and 10.000) they were intentionally chosen to 
make it easier to detect IBD [57][58]. In small populations, genetic drift is stronger and common 
ancestors are found more recently, which increases both the number and length of IBD 
segments [3][51]. Using smaller Ne values allowed for clearer comparisons across split times, 
especially when segments become shorter and harder to detect.  
All simulations for this scenario were generated using the script simulate_msprime_split.py, 
available in the GitHub repository  
 
2.4 Admixture Simulations  
To evaluate tool performance in the presence of mixed ancestry, simulated populations were 
created with recent admixture events. These scenarios were designed to test how well IBD 
segments can be recovered when ancestral tracts come from genetically distinct populations 
and vary in size or age.  
All simulations were based on a modified version of the Out-of-Africa (OOA) demographic 
model from Gutenkunst et al. (2009), implemented using a Demes YAML file 
(gutenkunst_admixture.yaml), which can be found in the GitHub repository [59]. An initial 
attempt to create admixture directly in msprime was abandoned because the simulations ran 
extremely slowly, even with simple setups. Using a Demes file offered a faster and more 
transparent solution. This graph-based model was chosen because it offers clear control over 
population structure, timing of events, and admixture proportions, while building on a well-
==PAGE:15==
15 
Benchmarking IBD Detection Tools with Simulated Genomic Data 
established evolutionary framework. The base model was adapted to include an additional 
population, ADMIX, which receives ancestry from three source populations: Yoruba (YRI), 
Central Europeans (CEU), and Karitiana (KAR). 
 
2.4.1 Varying Admixture Proportions  
To test how ancestry imbalance affects IBD detection, nine simulations were created with 
different proportions of YRI ancestry ranging from 10% to 90%. The remaining proportion (90% 
to 10%) was equally split between CEU and KAR. When a particular ancestry is 
underrepresented, the segments inherited from that ancestry tend to be fewer and shorter, 
making them harder to detect. These simulations were intended to assess whether tools are 
sensitive to this kind of bias.  
This simulation set was generated using the script simulate_msprime_admix_percentage.py, 
available in the GitHub repository.  
 
2.4.2 Varying Admixture Times  
To examine how segment degradation over time affects detection, additional simulations were 
run where the admixture event occurred either 10, 50, 100, or 500 generations ago. The 
admixture proportions were fixed (YRI 15%, CEU 42.5%, KAR 42.5%) across all runs. As 
recombination continues to break up haplotypes over generations, older admixture leads to 
shorter and more fragmented IBD segments. This set of simulations was meant to test how 
well each method performs under such conditions.  
This simulation set was generated using the script simulate_msprime_admix_time.py, 
available in the GitHub repository.  
 
2.5 Tool Execution  
To compare IBD detection tools under controlled conditions, different tools were chosen based 
on:  
• 
Compatibility with synthetic data: All tools had to run on simulated genotypes, without 
needing real-world reference maps.  
• 
Support for simple genetic maps: Tools needed to accept user-supplied or uniform 
maps, since empirical human maps weren’t applicable to simulation-based 
benchmarking.  
• 
Acceptable input formats: VCF was the primary format used; tools had to accept VCF 
directly or allow conversion to PLINK.  
These conditions were set to ensure that all tools could be tested using the same simulation 
framework, without relying on population-specific inputs or reference datasets.  
After reviewing available methods, the following seven tools were selected: Beagle (FastIBD) 
[31], Germline [42], Hap-IBD [26], RefinedIBD [31], IBDseq [60], RaPID-2 [61], and Truffle [62]. 
Each tool was tested across all simulation types (baseline Ne, population splits, admixture 
proportion, and admixture time) using two modes: default and sensitive parameter settings.

assigning 0 cM to the start and 250 cM to the end. This ensured consistent interpretation of 
genetic distances across tools and simulations.  
RaPID-2 and Germline required more specific maps and preprocessing:  
 
2.5.1.1 RaPID map  
RaPID-2 expects a genetic map in a two-column format: marker index and genetic position (in 
cM). To generate this, the make_rapid_map.py script was used, which extracted marker 
positions from each VCF file using bcftools query and converted them to genetic distances 
based on a uniform recombination rate of 1 cM per Mb. The conversion assumes a linear 
relationship, where position x base pairs maps to x × (250 / 250,000,000) cM. A separate map 
file was created for each VCF, matching its marker density and position ordering.

2.6 Post-processing and Evaluation  
After all tools were run, their outputs were parsed and summarized using the script 
summarize_output.py, available in the GitHub repository. The same evaluation pipeline was 
applied consistently across all simulation scenarios (baseline Ne, population splits, and 
admixture) with only the input file paths and sample labels adjusted per group. For comparison, 
IBD segments from msprime were also added into the pipeline and included as an additional 
baseline in all output files.  
2.6.1 Summary Metrics  
The following metrics were calculated from each tool’s output:  
• 
Total IBD length (cM): The sum of all detected IBD segment lengths per tool and 
simulation, calculated by converting reported lengths (either directly in cM or from base 
pairs using the uniform map).  
• 
Number of segments: The total count of IBD segments detected across all pairs. In 
tools reporting base pair positions (e.g., GERMLINE, RaPID), overlapping or adjacent 
intervals were first merged to avoid inflated counts.  
==PAGE:18==
18 
Benchmarking IBD Detection Tools with Simulated Genomic Data 
• 
Distinct pairs: The number of unique sample pairs with at least one detected segment, 
tracked by maintaining a pairwise dictionary for each tool and simulation.  
• 
Fraction IBD: Total IBD length divided by the theoretical genome-wide sharing 
possible (genome length × number of distinct pairs). This normalized the IBD across 
simulations and tools.  
• 
Relative fraction IBD: Each tool’s Fraction IBD divided by that of msprime within the 
same simulation. This allowed direct comparison to the baseline.  
All final summaries were saved in a single .csv file per simulation group and formed the basis 
for the IBD sharing plots shown in the results section.  
 
2.6.2 Extended Summaries for NE and Split Simulations  
For simulation groups focusing on Ne and population split time, the summary script was 
extended to also output:  
• 
Segment length distributions per tool and mode: All individual segment lengths were 
saved to separate files, allowing analysis of how each tool reports segment size and 
resolution.  
• 
Total IBD per pair: The total IBD length was also recorded for each individual pair, 
enabling comparison of how IBD is distributed across the population.  
 
2.6.3 Relative IBD Calculations  
Based on the main summary CSVs and specifically the Relative Fraction IBD, an additional 
script, avg_rel_ibd.py, was used to compute average values across conditions. It grouped 
values by tool and mode, averaged the Relative Fraction IBD across simulations, and saved 
the output in long format for bar plotting.  
 
2.6.4 Visualization  
Visualizations were generated using matplotlib and pandas. For each simulation group (Ne, 
split time, admixture proportion, admixture time), line plots were created to show Fraction IBD 
(%) across simulation conditions. These plots were generated using the script 
plot_ibd_fraction.py.  
In addition, the average Relative Fraction IBD values across tools and modes were plotted

This section presents the benchmarking results for the seven IBD detection tools that were 
tested across simulated datasets. Tools were evaluated on their ability to recover population-
level IBD patterns, detect variation between individual pairs, and estimate biologically realistic 
segment lengths. Each result is contextualized against the expected patterns from coalescent 
simulations and the IBD output from msprime.

supported a single parameter setting was IBDseq, and is only included in the default plots. 
Across all figures, the msprime segments are shown as a dashed black line to distinguish them

IBDseq, which does not support a sensitive mode, remains relatively flat. It ranges from 2.4%

mode, is one of the few tools that consistently recovers the expected shape of IBD decline 
without extreme overestimation. 
Taken together, these results suggest that only a subset of tools; Hap-IBD and Truffle (sensitive 
mode) closely follow the expected biological signal, remaining just under the msprime line 
across the full Ne range. Other tools either overestimate IBD (e.g., RaPID), show the wrong 
trend entirely (Beagle and RefinedIBD), or lack responsiveness (IBDseq). The msprime 
segments provide a clear baseline that helps disentangle overcalling from genuine signal, 
especially in scenarios involving sparse or ancient shared ancestry.

4. Discussion 
This section reviews the performance of seven IBD detection tools across simulated 
demographic scenarios, including changes in population size, divergence time, and admixture. 
Rather than summarize overall trends, the following discussion focuses on each tool 
individually, highlighting where methods aligned with biological expectations, where they failed, 
and how specific design choices contributed to their behaviour.  
 
4.1 Hap-IBD  
Hap-IBD was the most consistent and biologically plausible tool across all simulation 
scenarios. It closely tracked expected demographic trends: reporting lower total IBD with 
increasing effective population size (Ne) and with older population splits, in line with coalescent 
theory and prior work [11][63]. Its total IBD levels were neither inflated nor overly conservative, 
staying within a realistic range compared to the msprime outputs.  
This robustness stems from Hap-IBD’s algorithmic design. It uses a seed-and-extend strategy 
that allows mismatches but avoids overextending segments through overlapping seed 
placement and careful filtering [26]. Like Beagle and RefinedIBD, it relies on a genetic map to 
define segment boundaries. All three were run using the same uniform 1 cM/Mb map, but Hap-
IBD made more effective use of this information. While other tools using the same map 
produced exaggerated segment lengths, Hap-IBD returned distributions that were narrower, 
with medians closer to the simulated expectations. Segment lengths were still somewhat 
inflated but the overall shape and range were more realistic.   
In terms of individual-level sharing, Hap-IBD captured the full variation in IBD across pairs. Its 
per-pair distributions were wide and asymmetric, echoing the diversity of recent genealogical 
connections expected under coalescent models [11]. This ability to recover both highly sharing 
and weakly sharing pairs suggests that Hap-IBD is sensitive to a broad spectrum of segment 
strengths, rather than just the most obvious matches.  
Switching to relaxed mode slightly increased total IBD and allowed detection of shorter 
segments, but didn’t significantly compromise performance. Unlike tools that became erratic 
or overly permissive in sensitive mode, Hap-IBD’s outputs remained directionally accurate and 
biologically interpretable.  
Overall, Hap-IBD consistently aligned with demographic expectations, preserved segment and 
pairwise structure, and maintained performance under both default and relaxed settings. Prior 
benchmarks have also highlighted its accuracy in large datasets, and this evaluation confirms 
its reliability across a broader range of demographic contexts [26].

4.2 Truffle  
Truffle was one of the few tools that consistently mirrored expected demographic patterns 
across both effective population size and population split simulations. It reported lower IBD 
levels in larger and more diverged populations (the correct biological direction) and kept total 
IBD within plausible ranges compared to msprime outputs. Its total IBD values were slightly 
higher than Hap-IBD and segment lengths were more inflated. Despite this, Truffle remained 
stable across scenarios without strong overcalling or collapse. This aligns with its underlying 
method: a global haplotype-graph approach that represents shared ancestry via topological 
paths rather than local matching, helping preserve demographic structure while reducing false 
positives [62]. 
Unlike Hap-IBD, Truffle operates purely in physical (base-pair) space and does not incorporate 
a genetic map. This map-free design plausibly explains the inflated segment lengths observed 
across simulations: Truffle consistently returned some of the longest IBD tracts of any tool. 
While the spread of lengths was narrower than RaPID’s extreme outliers, medians remained 
far above msprime’s, even in sensitive mode. This inflation appears to result from Truffle’s 
tendency to smooth over local recombination signals and merge nearby matches into a single 
continuous tract—an artifact not widely reported in previous evaluations, which have focused 
more on runtime or kinship classification than segment-level resolution. 
Still, in terms of pairwise IBD distributions, Truffle performed well. Its output captured a broad 
range of sharing levels between individual pairs, with asymmetric, skewed distributions that 
reflected the genealogical variation expected under coalescent models [11]. This structure held 
across both default and sensitive modes. When the minimum segment threshold was lowered, 
total IBD increased substantially and median sharing shifted upward, but the tool retained its 
demographic signal and didn’t collapse into uniform overcalling. Compared to other tools, 
Truffle balanced sensitivity and structure especially well: sensitive mode made it more 
permissive without sacrificing directional accuracy or distorting individual-level variation. 
Overall, Truffle stands out for its demographic sensitivity, stable pairwise patterns, and 
moderate total IBD estimates. Its main weaknesses, segment inflation and limited 
configurability, stem from its map-free architecture. But even with those trade-offs, it remained 
one of the few tools that preserved biologically coherent patterns under diverse demographic 
settings. These findings build on recent evaluations showing that Truffle is particularly well-
suited to large-scale population datasets, especially where ancestral relationships span long 
genomic intervals and run-time efficiency is essential [62].

4.3 IBD-seq  
IBDseq was one of the most conservative tools in the entire benchmarking. It consistently 
produced the lowest total IBD across simulations and showed minimal sensitivity to 
demographic differences. Whether effective population size increased, populations split, or 
admixture occurred, IBDseq’s output remained largely flat. This conservatism stems from its 
strict scoring approach, which calculates likelihood ratios at each SNP and only reports IBD 
segments once a cumulative score exceeds a defined threshold. While this reduces false 
positives, it limits the ability to detect shorter, older, or noisier segments in scenarios with higher 
recombination rates, lower SNP density, or complex ancestry [60].  
Like Truffle, IBDseq operates entirely in physical space and does not use a genetic map. This 
simplifies usage but prevents any adjustment for regional variation in recombination rate, which 
can impact segment boundary accuracy. One practical advantage of IBDseq is that it does not 
require phased genotypes, making it useful in studies where high-quality phasing is 
unavailable or where phasing artifacts might distort IBD calls. Despite this, IBDseq produced 
some of the shortest segment lengths of all tools which is a reflection of its internal thresholds 
and conservative state transitions. Median segment lengths were still inflated compared to 
msprime, but much less so than in tools like RaPID or Truffle. This restraint was also evident 
in the distributions: IBDseq avoided extreme outliers and reported few long segments, 
suggesting tighter control over overextension.  
However, this conservativeness came at a cost. IBDseq's pairwise IBD distributions were 
narrow and compressed, missing most of the expected variation between sample pairs. In both 
small and large populations, and in both simple and structured models, the tool failed to capture 
the genealogical diversity predicted by coalescent simulations [11]. This limits its usefulness in 
studies aiming to infer fine-scale demography, population splits, or variable degrees of recent 
relatedness.  
Importantly, IBDseq has no adjustable sensitivity modes for segment size or detection 
stringency, and as a result, it produced identical results across all tested conditions. This rigidity 
might be beneficial for reproducibility but limits the tool’s adaptability to different analytical 
scenarios. Results from this benchmarking indicate that IBDseq performs best at detecting 
clear, recent IBD segments, but struggles in scenarios requiring sensitivity to shorter 
segments, older ancestry signals, or subtle demographic changes.  
In this evaluation, IBDseq served as a low false-positive, low-recall outlier. It preserved clean 
output and resisted segment inflation, but missed substantial portions of biologically relevant 
IBD. This behaviour is not inherently a flaw as it may be well-suited for clinical studies, forensic 
applications, or strict relationship inference where false positives carry a high cost. But for 
population-scale exploration of ancestry or structure, its narrow detection window is a major 
limitation.

4.4 Germline  
GERMLINE was one of the most parameter-sensitive tools in this evaluation, showing large 
discrepancies between its default and sensitive modes. By default, it dramatically under 
detected IBD across all simulation types, often reporting levels close to zero. The reason is 
mechanical: GERMLINE’s hash-based seed-and-extend strategy insists on finding long, 
perfectly matching seeds before any extension, so short or error-ridden tracts are filtered out 
at the hashing stage [42].  
In sensitive mode, however, GERMLINE's behaviour reversed sharply. The number of detected 
segments increased substantially, particularly in structured populations, but the total IBD 
reported often overshot plausible ranges and no longer aligned with the demographic 
expectations of the simulation. For example, IBD sharing sometimes increased in larger or 
more divergent populations, contrary to theoretical expectations [3][51].  This instability reflects 
how much the tool relies on exact matching rules and pre-phased input: small changes in 
marker spacing, phasing quality, or SNP density can cause dramatic differences in output [42].  
Despite this, segment lengths remained relatively short. Unlike tools that inflate IBD by 
overextending matches, GERMLINE often erred on the side of fragmentation. Median segment 
lengths were among the shortest observed, especially in default mode, suggesting that the 
tool’s filtering rules favour conservative endpoint definitions. This aligns with earlier studies 
such as Huff et al. (2011), which found that GERMLINE tends to miss IBD in distant 
relationships but maintains low segment inflation when signal is detected [2]. 
Like RaPID, GERMLINE required custom genetic maps to define SNP distances. However, its 
interpretation of these maps was more rigid: map distances were used mainly as filtering 
thresholds rather than as part of a scoring or extension model. This may partly explain why it 
produced shorter and stricter segments compared to RaPID, even when using structurally 
identical maps (1 cM/Mb).  
When examining pairwise IBD distributions, GERMLINE again showed limitations. The output 
was narrow and compressed, failing to capture the range of variation in ancestry sharing 
across pairs that’s expected under realistic genealogical scenarios [11]. This held even in 
sensitive mode, though total IBD increased, the distribution remained top-heavy or skewed. 
The result is a tool that cannot meaningfully distinguish distant cousins from recent co-ancestry 
in many simulation contexts.  
Taken together, GERMLINE’s strengths lie in its speed, simplicity, and relatively clean segment 
boundaries. These properties make it attractive for very large-scale datasets, especially when 
segment inflation is a concern or when downstream analyses can tolerate conservative 
detection. 
But its reliance on exact matches, high sensitivity to parameters, and poor resolution of 
demographic gradients make it ill-suited for population-scale inference unless extensively 
calibrated. Without careful tuning, the tool risks either missing most of the IBD signal or 
overcalling in unpredictable ways, depending entirely on the input characteristics and 
parameter choices.

4.5 RaPID-2  
RaPID tracked demographic trends surprisingly well, reporting less IBD in larger populations 
and under deeper splits, in line with coalescent expectations [3][51]. However, its absolute 
values were substantially inflated. Across virtually all scenarios, RaPID returned the highest 
total IBD among all tools, frequently nearing the maximum possible sharing.   
The underlying cause is rooted in the tool’s algorithm. RaPID finds IBD by repeatedly sampling 
a few SNPs in small windows and looking for exact matches; if two haplotypes agree in several 
of these sampled passes, the whole stretch is called shared. Because the same long region 
can be hit more than once, neighbouring hits get stitched together and the segment ends up a 
little longer than it really is, so total shared length is slightly inflated, even though the number 
of detected segments stays about the same [61]. Even in non-admixed, simple population 
scenarios, RaPID frequently reported segment lengths that approached or equalled the full 
chromosome length (250 cM).   
RaPID also required a simplified two-column genetic map to define segment boundaries in 
centimorgan units. Unlike tools that integrate the map into scoring or model-based extensions 
(e.g., Hap-IBD or Beagle), RaPID used the map solely for thresholding. This permissive 
interpretation further amplified the overextension of segments, especially in relaxed mode, 
where internal filters were loosened. Relaxed settings led to even shorter minimum segment 
thresholds, and consequently more IBD, but with little improvement in fidelity. Rather than 
increasing resolution, the added sensitivity mostly expanded the number of inflated or 
overlapping matches.  
This inflation wasn't limited to segment lengths. RaPID’s pairwise IBD distributions were 
unusually narrow and top-heavy, with most pairs receiving similarly high total sharing. This 
compressed the natural variation in genealogical relationships and undermined the tool’s ability 
to distinguish close relatives from unrelated individuals. In simulated datasets where true 
pairwise sharing was asymmetric and skewed, as expected from coalescent variation, RaPID 
produced flattened profiles that masked this structure entirely [11].  
Despite these issues, RaPID stood out as one of the only tools to show any response to 
admixture gradients. As the proportion of admixture increased, total IBD declined modestly, 
albeit inconsistently. While this trend aligned with biological expectations, it was difficult to 
interpret due to the overall level of inflation and variability across replicates. In the time-based 
admixture simulations, no clear pattern emerged, suggesting the tool may not reliably 
distinguish between recombination-driven erosion of ancestry and noise introduced by 
segment merging.  
In summary, RaPID excelled at preserving broad demographic direction but failed to deliver 
biologically plausible values. Its strength lies in its speed and scalability, especially for massive 
cohorts. However, this comes at a steep cost to resolution, interpretability, and individual-level 
accuracy. Without post-filtering or correction for inflation, RaPID’s outputs may be misleading, 
particularly in downstream analyses that rely on precise segment boundaries or realistic total 
sharing estimates.

4.6 Beagle (FastIBD) & RefinedIBD  
Beagle and RefinedIBD, both based on the Li and Stephens haplotype copying model, showed 
the most consistent pattern of overestimation and some of the most counter-intuitive 
results [28] Across nearly all models they yielded the largest total IBD fractions (apart from 
RaPID) and often increased inferred sharing precisely where theory predicts less, namely in 
populations with larger effective size, deeper splits, or recent admixture. A plausible 
mechanistic explanation is that the copying model can interpret dense background haplotype 
similarity, which is common in structured or recently-admixed samples, as genuine IBD. 
Consequently, large stretches of what appear to be shared ancestry may reflect population 
structure rather than true recent co-ancestry.  
While both tools rely on the same underlying haplotype copying framework, RefinedIBD adds 
an additional refinement step: it first grabs GERMLINE-style exact haplotype matches and then 
re-scores each candidate with a Li-and-Stephens’s likelihood model, trimming or splitting low-
evidence tails and only rarely extending a segment when neighbouring windows raise SNPs 
[31]. This second-pass filter was designed to reduce false positives and sharpen segment 
boundaries. However, because the model still equates high haplotype similarity with recent co-
ancestry, the refinement step cannot fully separate genuine IBD from background sharing in 
structured or admixed samples. In this benchmark, the filter sometimes rescued borderline 
seeds that should have been discarded, leading to the largest over-estimation of any tool; up 
to 80× the msprime segments in the admixture scenario. In contrast, Beagle (FastIBD) skips 
the refinement and directly calls segments based on posterior probabilities from the initial HMM 
scan, leading to broadly similar trends but often slightly less exaggerated totals. 
Beagle and RefinedIBD were also two of the three tools (along with Hap-IBD) that required a 
genetic map, and all were run on the same uniform map (1 cM/Mb) to standardize comparison. 
However, unlike Hap-IBD, which showed well-bounded segment lengths and stable behaviour, 
Beagle and RefinedIBD still returned inflated values. This suggests that while the map 
determines nominal distances, it’s the internal scoring model and particularly how segments 
are extended, that shapes the final outcome. In these tools, map use alone wasn’t enough to 
prevent biologically implausible output.  
Relaxed detection settings had little impact on either tool. Both Beagle and RefinedIBD 
remained relatively insensitive to changes in thresholds, suggesting that their segment calling 
is driven more by internal scoring than user-defined cutoffs. Some studies suggest downstream 
filtering based on likelihoods can partially mitigate overcalling, though this was not explored 
here [31]. 
In summary, Beagle and RefinedIBD did not respond reliably to the demographic structure in 
the simulations. Their overcalling, poor sensitivity to thresholds, and reversed trends in key 
scenarios raise concerns about their interpretability in complex or diverse samples. While 
RefinedIBD offers additional refinement logic, this alone was not sufficient to control inflation 
or distinguish genuine recent co-ancestry from background similarity. Users applying these 
tools may need to rely on downstream filtering or segment calibration strategies to make 
outputs suitable for population-level inference.

4.7 Admixture simulations  
The admixture simulations posed a different kind of challenge. As recombination breaks up 
ancestral haplotypes over generations, true IBD segments become fragmented and sparse, 
especially when ancestry is drawn from genetically divergent populations [3][51]. This was 
reflected in the msprime outputs, which showed extremely low levels of sharing even under 
idealized, noise-free conditions. Simulating this kind of complexity was not trivial; an initial 
attempt to implement admixture directly in msprime proved unrealistically slow, so a Demes 
YAML file was used instead to represent the population graph and simulate mixed ancestry 
more efficiently. 
Given how little IBD signal remained after admixture, it’s not surprising that tools struggled. In 
some scenarios, like increasing YRI admixture proportion, a few tools did show weak 
directional trends, but the absolute values were inconsistent and difficult to interpret. In time-
based simulations, even that structure broke down. This wasn’t a failure of the tools so much 
as a reflection of the problem itself: admixture doesn’t just reduce IBD; it scatters it across 
ancestral backgrounds, fragments segments, and introduces similarity that’s hard to 
distinguish from recent co-ancestry [44][45]. 
Most current IBD detection methods aren’t designed to model this explicitly. And in these 
simulations, where the ground truth was clean and parameters were controlled, it still wasn’t 
possible to recover a clear biological pattern. This suggests that in real datasets, where 
phasing errors, uneven coverage, and additional structure are present, IBD-based inference 
in admixed populations is likely to remain extremely difficult without ancestry-aware 
preprocessing or new tool designs tailored to this context. 
 
4.8 Practical recommendations 
This benchmarking study provides a clear takeaway: no single IBD detection tool performs 
optimally across all conditions. Tool choice should be guided by the properties of the dataset 
and the goals of the study. 
If your dataset includes admixed individuals, this benchmark showed that it becomes difficult 
to evaluate tool performance meaningfully as ground-truth segments are sparse, and outputs 
are often too variable or inflated to interpret with confidence. In such cases, IBD detection 
should be approached with caution. Results may still provide useful signals, but they often 
require additional processing, such as masking by local ancestry inference or integrating 
ancestry-aware methods. Until tools explicitly account for the mosaic structure of admixed 
genomes, interpretation will remain limited. 
When analysing relatively homogeneous populations or groups with recent shared ancestry, 
Hap-IBD emerges as the most reliable choice. It consistently captured the direction and shape 
of demographic trends, making it suitable for studies investigating recent population structure 
or relatedness patterns. 
If segment boundary accuracy and realistic segment length distributions are important (e.g., 
when estimating time to most recent common ancestor or segment age), Hap-IBD again 
outperformed tools like RaPID, which tended to overextend or inflate segments. 
Truffle, despite slightly inflated segment lengths, also preserved demographic trends well and 
is especially appealing for very large datasets due to its scalability and minimal preprocessing 
requirements.

For exploratory work in datasets with suspected deep relatedness but low SNP density or 
unphased data, IBDseq may be a conservative starting point even though its insensitivity to 
population history limits its broader utility. 
Although runtime and scalability were not formally benchmarked in this project, practical 
differences were observed. Most tools, including Hap-IBD, IBDseq, RaPID, RefinedIBD, and 
Truffle, ran successfully on a personal laptop. However, Beagle and GERMLINE required 
significantly more memory and had to be executed on the DTU HealthTech computing cluster. 
While computational performance was not a central focus of this study, these differences are 
important to keep in mind when planning analyses on large or complex datasets, especially for 
researchers working with limited computational resources. 
On choosing between default and sensitive modes: 
• 
Use default mode when working with datasets where high specificity is critical (e.g., 
disease variant tracing, close relationship detection), or when input data quality is 
limited. 
• 
Use sensitive mode when analysing populations where short or ancient segments are 
expected and detecting weak signals matters more than reducing false positives. In this 
study, sensitive settings often brought total IBD estimates closer to the msprime results, 
but in some tools (e.g., RaPID, Germline) also amplified noise. 
In general, the recommendation would be running both modes and comparing results if the 
goal is exploratory. For downstream analyses requiring quantitative accuracy, stick to default 
mode unless tool documentation or past benchmarks justify otherwise. 
 
4.9 Tool Limitations 
A major challenge in evaluating and using IBD detection tools lies in how opaque some of their 
internal mechanisms are. Tools like Beagle and RefinedIBD, for instance, use Hidden Markov 
Models (HMMs) and haplotype copying likelihoods that are difficult to fully interpret from the 
outside [31]. This means it’s often unclear why a particular segment was called, extended, or 
missed. It also complicates debugging and makes it harder to predict how these tools will 
behave under non-ideal conditions. This lack of transparency also makes it difficult to identify 
and fix failure modes when segment counts are unexpectedly high or low. The lack of 
interpretability also underscores the need for standard validation datasets, simulated or 
synthetic, that tools could include as built-in testing modules. Such datasets could help users 
verify correct installation and behaviour and detect silent failures or misconfigurations. 
Moreover, most tools do not provide any internal confidence scores or statistical support for 
the segments they detect. Unlike variant callers or imputation tools that often include 
likelihoods or posterior probabilities, IBD tools tend to give binary calls: a segment is either 
shared or it isn’t. This limits the user’s ability to filter based on confidence or calibrate 
thresholds empirically. 
Another underappreciated limitation is that most tools do not report whether segments overlap, 
either between different individual pairs or within the same genome. Overlapping or nested 
segments are common in dense genomic data, and without explicit tracking, these overlaps 
can complicate downstream tasks like clustering, relatedness estimation, or population 
structure analysis [41]. Biases introduced during detection, whether through overextension, 
segment merging, or inflated sharing, can propagate through the analysis pipeline and distort 
interpretations of genetic relationships. Without clearer reporting of segment context, it’s 
difficult to assess whether observed patterns reflect true biological signals or artefacts of the 
detection method.

Another general limitation is that tool performance is highly dependent on assumptions that 
are rarely made explicit. For example, some tools implicitly assume constant recombination 
rates, panmictic populations, or no selection. When these assumptions are violated, as they 
often are in real human datasets, the results can become difficult to interpret or trust. 
Finally, this benchmark did not include every IBD detection tool available. Notably, iLASH [64]: 
a scalable method for ultra-large biobank-scale datasets, and IBIS [65]: a tool designed to 
detect distant relationships in low-coverage or ancient genomes, were excluded due to 
incompatibility with the simulation design or lack of support for custom maps. Their inclusion 
in future benchmarks would help extend this framework and test scalability and error 
robustness in more realistic settings. 
 
4.10 Strengths, Limitations, and Future Perspectives 
This study had several key strengths compared to earlier IBD benchmarking efforts. First, by 
simulating data with msprime, it was possible to control population size, divergence time, and 
admixture transparently. This allowed for clear expectations of how IBD patterns should 
behave under different demographic scenarios. Second, all tools were run on identical 
genotype data, using consistent formats, parameter settings, and genetic maps. By 
standardizing the input, the analysis isolated tool behaviour from confounding differences in 
input structure. Third, the outputs were directly evaluated against ground-truth IBD segments 
extracted from the underlying tree sequence using tskit. This allowed not just visual 
interpretation, but quantitative comparisons of total IBD, segment lengths, and pairwise 
distributions which is something most prior studies did not attempt, often relying instead on 
indirect proxy metrics or inter-tool consistency [47][48]. 
To ensure clean and interpretable comparisons, the simulations in this study were 
deliberately simplified. Only a single 250 Mb region was modelled, with uniform mutation and 
recombination rates, and without structural complexity such as recombination hotspots, gene 
conversion, indels, or sex-specific recombination. Genotypes were fully phased, evenly 
spaced, and free of genotyping error or missing data. These choices maximized consistency 
and made it possible to isolate differences in algorithmic behaviour without confounding 
artifacts. However, they also reduce ecological realism. 
Real genetic datasets introduce additional complexity such as, uneven recombination, 
sequencing that introduces noise and missingness, and phasing errors that are common [1]. 
Population structure, low-coverage regions, and batch effects all distort the signal that IBD 
tools try to recover. Prior studies have shown that these factors inflate IBD segment counts 
or cause false positives [29][47]. Given that tools like RaPID already overestimate sharing 
even under ideal conditions, such distortions are likely to be amplified in empirical settings. 
On the other hand, methods like Hap-IBD and Truffle, which behaved more conservatively 
and predictably in this study, may offer more robust performance in applied analyses though 
even these showed sensitivity to admixture and noise. 
For population geneticists modelling demographic history, these deviations can lead to mis-
estimated effective sizes or incorrect inferences about migration. For epidemiologists or 
genealogists, inflated segments can distort relationship estimates or disease mapping efforts. 
Future benchmarks should bridge this realism gap. Simulations ought to span full genomes, 
include multiple chromosomes, and model a broader range of biological processes such as 
recombination hotspots, selection, mutation clusters, and structural variants. Controlled 
introduction of phasing errors, missingness, and sequencing noise would better reflect the 
challenges tools face in practice. Evaluations should also go beyond total IBD counts, 
incorporating segment-level precision, recall, F1 score, and overlap with known segments. 
These metrics would clarify how tools balance sensitivity and false positives.

While runtime and memory use were not systematically evaluated in this benchmark, 
differences were clear during tool execution, as mentioned earlier. Future benchmarking 
efforts could include systematic timing and memory profiling to better assess tool suitability 
for biobank-scale data. 
In this benchmark some tools also had to be excluded from specific evaluations due to 
problematic outputs. For example, RefinedIBD and Beagle produced non-biologically 
possible IBD values and were omitted from certain comparisons. Meanwhile, IBDseq lacked 
adjustable parameters and could not be tuned for different detection thresholds. While every 
effort was made to apply tools fairly, these issues naturally limit direct, one-to-one ranking 
across all dimensions. Future benchmarks could address this by applying flexible, tool-
specific evaluation metrics rather than relying on strict one-size-fits-all comparisons. For 
instance, scoring tools on both accuracy and adaptability may allow fairer evaluation even 
when tools differ in configurability. Moreover, tools with fixed parameters could be 
benchmarked across a wider variety of simulated conditions to identify where they perform 
well versus where they fail, rather than excluding them entirely. 
The admixture simulations highlighted a key limitation of this benchmarking framework: 
under complex ancestry, even ground-truth segments from msprime were sparse and difficult 
to interpret. As discussed earlier, admixture fragments shared haplotypes and introduces 
background similarity, reducing the IBD signal to levels that make meaningful evaluation 
difficult. This limits not just tool performance, but the benchmark’s ability to assess it. 
To improve future evaluations, simulations must be designed with more realistic ancestry 
mosaics and clearer expectations for IBD patterns in admixed genomes, ideally in ways that 
remain computationally feasible. Likewise, new detection methods should aim to integrate 
local ancestry information directly, rather than relying on external masking or preprocessing. 
Without these changes, benchmarking in admixed contexts will remain constrained in both 
scope and interpretability. 
Finally, the downstream effects of biased IBD inference deserve closer scrutiny. As 
mentioned earlier, biases introduced during detection, whether through overextension, 
segment merging, or inflated sharing, can propagate through the analysis pipeline and distort 
interpretations of genetic relationships. Future evaluations should therefore test end-to-end 
pipelines to understand how errors in IBD detection propagate through downstream 
analyses. 
In summary, while no IBD detection method performs flawlessly across all conditions, this 
study establishes a transparent and reproducible framework for future evaluations. With 
careful simulation design, consistent tool inputs, and ground-truth comparison, it is possible 
to systematically explore tool behaviour and push IBD inference closer to the complexity of 
real genetic data.

5. Conclusion  
This benchmarking study compared seven widely used IBD detection tools across controlled 
simulations generated with msprime, covering a range of demographic scenarios. While most 
tools recovered broad IBD patterns, all showed systematic deviations from the simulated 
ground truth by either overestimating, underestimating, or misrepresenting key trends. Tools 
like RaPID, Beagle, and RefinedIBD frequently inflated IBD totals or produced biologically 
implausible trends, especially under high Ne or deep population splits. 
Admixture posed a distinct challenge. Even in clean, controlled simulations, true IBD was 
sparse and fragmented due to recombination between divergent ancestries. The signal was 
simply too weak to produce clear demographic patterns, highlighting the difficulty of IBD-based 
inference in admixed populations and the need for ancestry-aware methods.  
Only Hap-IBD and Truffle most often aligned with biological expectations across metrics like 
total IBD, segment lengths, and pairwise sharing. Even so, they required careful parameter 
tuning and still showed some inflation. IBDseq, GERMLINE, and RaPID each had strengths in 
specific contexts but lacked robustness across conditions. 
No tool was universally reliable. This work underscores the importance of simulation-based 
benchmarking to uncover hidden biases, test tool assumptions, and guide method selection. 
As datasets grow larger and more complex, reproducible evaluations like this should become 
a standard step in tool development and usage, ideally supported by shared benchmarks, open 
code, and transparent testing frameworks.
