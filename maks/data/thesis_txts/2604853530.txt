Abstract
Microbial life abounds in such diversity and number its individuals must often occupy the same habitats simultaneously.
Some of the important elicitors of subsequent intra- and interspeciﬁc interactions include secondary metabolites
(SMs) and these have been subjected to great study due their various and interesting bioactivity, ranging from highly
beneﬁcial compounds, e.g. pharmaceuticals, to the detrimental like potent toxins. While important and worth great
study the ’human-aﬀect’ bias has dominated the investigation of SMs often at the expense of the understanding of
the physiological and ecological role of these SMs.
In this project a simple system setup is devised and developed allowing unobstructed photographic documentation of
the progression of and the interaction between the fungi B. cinerea, M. fructigena, P. expansum and T.roseum when
grown on apples.
The apple system is compared to standard laboratory conditions utilizing growth media through visual evaluation
and both targeted and untargeted chemical analysis of the secondary metabolites of mono and co-cultures of the
aforementioned fungi. Especially the P. expansum metabolite proﬁle changes in the apple mono and co-cultures
with little to no production of the most abundant mono culture metabolites in the co-cultures, including several
Communesins, Aurantioclavin, Expansolide A and B, and a unknown compound. Patulin production seems to
increase in the apple co-culutures. The in vitro growth media conditions seems to preferable for the production of
Roquefortines, and expansolides in apples.
The results of this project also indicates a functional PEG mediated protoplastation transformation protocol for M.
fructigena by showing viable growth of both fresh and 9 days -80 °C stored protoplasts following the temperature
steps and maximal osmotic stress of the proposed transformation protocol.

Introduction
Life abounds in such form, diversity and number it can
come as no surprise that its individuals must often occupy
the same habitat, be they similar or vastly diﬀerent, and
microorganisms are great examples of this (L. Frank and
Gram, 2017; Kramer and Abraham, 2012). Being quite
ubiquitous everywhere on earth naming places on earth
where microorganisms are absent is diﬃcult, yet simul-
taneously much easier than listing the places where they
are present; and although small, microorganisms can have
profound impact on their environments, each other, and
others both beneﬁcially, detrimentally or somewhere in
between (L. Frank and Gram, 2017).
Scientists have tried to describe and form deﬁnitions for
the types of impact organisms exert between diﬀerent
organisms and/or their environments as types of interac-
tion. This is essential for communicating observations,
but formulating these deﬁnitions in a clear, concise, and
fully encompassing manner can prove challenging as na-
ture and life is inherently dynamic and always changing.
Setting boundaries on microbial interactions, that leads
to a typiﬁed behavior agnostic of the involved organisms
and underlying mechanisms is no exception, despite an
overall general consensus (Selim et al., 2017; Dattagupta
and Zielinski, 2011; Klepzig et al., 2001).
The following section will brieﬂy describe typical interac-
tion types, deﬁnitions and potential controversies.
1.1
General Microbial Interaction Types
Microbial interaction types are broadly divided into ei-
ther positive, neutral or negative interactions, erro-
neously insinuating preferable interaction types, but are
referring to mainly the growth of both axenic or com-
munal microbial populations, described as intraspeciﬁc
or interspeciﬁc interactions respectively for each type
of population (Dattagupta and Zielinski, 2011; Klepzig
et al., 2001; Selim et al., 2017).
The most common interspeciﬁc interaction types and
their trends on population growth in co-culture compared
to axenic culture are illustrated in ﬁgure 1. Some dis-
agreement exists on some of the terms, mainly the ones
associated with positive interactions like mutualism, pro-
tocooperation and especially symbiosis (Selim et al., 2017;
Dattagupta and Zielinski, 2011).
In positive interactions mutualism describes a mutually
beneﬁcial and highly specialized coexistence, even to the
point where separation is mutually fatal, called obligate
mutualism, or one of the organisms can survive alone
under certain conditions, called facultative mutualism.
This is contrasted with protocooperation, also called
synergism, where all involved parties can function on
their own when separated or even be replaced in the
relationship by another organism performing a similar
or even diﬀerent but equally beneﬁcial tasks. This can
include tandem metabolizing of precursor, waste, or toxic
compounds from one population and resources for another
(Selim et al., 2017; Balestrini et al., 2018).
The main disagreement here is whether all described as
mutualism should be categorized as obligate mutualism
and protocooperation as facultative mutualism.
Commensalism diﬀers from the other two by only one
population’s growth beneﬁting from the association while
the other is neither beneﬁcially or harmfully impacted
(Selim et al., 2017).

Symbiosis might be one of the most known and con-
tested terms. Originally it included all positive interac-
tions types and parasitism, later to include all positive
interactions and neutralism, and then being increasingly
restrictive in its meaning until it was used synonymously
with mutualism as described earlier, thus rendering the
term ambiguous if not explicitly speciﬁed each time (Selim
et al., 2017; Dattagupta and Zielinski, 2011).
In neutralism, cohabiting microorganisms do aﬀect each
other, just not via growth, and this interaction is thought
to be rather rare or short-lived in nature as the environ-
mental conditions that allows it might be likely to change,
e.g. cohabiting microbes with initially dissimilar nutrient
requirements switching to similar nutrients upon local en-
vironmental depletion of the aforementioned (Selim et al.,
2017).
Negative interactions are those where at least one is
harmed, while the another can be harmed, beneﬁted, or
unaﬀected. Common to parasitism, predation and amen-
salism, is that one population’s growth rate increases or
at the very least is maintained at the expense of another,
but they all vary slightly from each other.
Parasitism is typically a longer-lived relation where the
parasite can live in (endoparasitism) or on (ectoparasitism)
its host while predation tends to be a short-lived relation
with the predator attacking the prey and subsequently
feeding on it by absorbing its tissues.
Ammensalism, however, diﬀers by not consuming the
other, but rather increases its own growth by inhibiting
the other either by lytic enzymes or via antibiosis where
the aggressor produces growth inhibiting small molecules,
peptides, or non-lytic proteins.
Competition occurs when two or more populations com-
pete over the same limiting resource(s) or factor(s) and
can be detrimental for all partners involved. It typically
manifests as an indirect approach of reducing the avail-
ability of the limiting resource, e.g. some iron binding
siderophores require specialized uptake mechanisms as
reviewed by Haas, 2003, or as a direct approach of dis-
turbing or corrupting the habitat reducing its ability to
sustain the other population, e.g. acidiﬁcation (Selim
et al., 2017). Some contention on the terms deﬁnitions
exist, as most view ammensalism as a form of competition,
referred to as antagonism, rather than its own interaction
type (Selim et al., 2017; Klepzig et al., 2001)
Some observable behaviors have been categorized in a
standardized manner but as mentioned earlier life is in-
herently dynamic, always changing and so does microbial
interaction. Interaction types might shift or even oscillate
in response to changes in resource availability, environmen-
tal conditions, e.g. temperature, water activity, external
signal transduction triggers, organism development cycles,
or many other conditions. This will almost certainly hap-
pen if microbial community interactions are viewed over a
long time, whereas short time examinations can lead to a
singular interactive relationship, easily labeled by one well
deﬁned interaction type. This can however, if not con-
sidered carefully, give rise to much confusion, where the
same partners and even conditions can be characterized
as seemingly opposing interaction types (Klepzig et al.,
2001; Shearer, 1995; Nai and Meyer, 2018; Deveau et al.,
2018).
1.2
Interactions and Metabolites
One important follow-up question of interaction observa-
tions could be: what are the important elicitors of these
interactions? One of many important answers is natural
products (NPs). (Brakhage and Schroeckh, 2011; Deveau
et al., 2018; Netzker et al., 2015; Demain and Adrio, 2008;
Fajardo and Mart´ınez, 2008). NPs includes all compounds
and substances produced by living organisms and also
includes metabolites, primary and secondary (PMs and
SMs respectively) both of these two being organic com-
pounds of low molecular mass.
The main diﬀerence between PMs and SMs, is that PMs
are directly involved in the growth, development, and
reproduction of the producing organism, while SMs are
not (Netzker et al., 2015; Marinelli and Marcone, 2019;
Brakhage and Schroeckh, 2011). SMs are usually pro-
duced after the main growth period and are not found
in every cell (Demain and Adrio, 2008; Marinelli and
Marcone, 2019).
Despite these diﬀerences the demarcation is not clear cut
as organisms can utilize the same compound diﬀerently,
being a PM for one and a SM for another. There is also
signiﬁcant interplay between the two discrete metabolisms,
maybe most obvious in the many precursors for secondary

metabolism is provided by the primary metabolism, or
that both metabolisms share numerous cellular compo-
nents, e.g. Co-factors, for their respective biosynthesis
(Sheridan et al., 2015).
1.2.1
The Interest of Secondary Metabolites
SMs are compounds that have often attracted great at-
tention due the frequent occurrence of both interesting or
problematic bioactivity. This includes their pharmacologi-
cal potential e.g. as antibiotics and anticancer agents, and
consumer production like food additives, -enhancers, and
pigments (Newman and Cragg, 2016; Netzker et al., 2015;
Brakhage and Schroeckh, 2011; Davies et al., 2006) or the
impact of mycotoxins and allergens on human and animal
health or consumer and industrial production(Andersen
et al., 2004; Davies et al., 2006; Nunes, 2012; Demain
and Adrio, 2008; Stein and Bulboac˘a, 2017; Pal et al.,
2017; JH, 2015; Br¨ase et al., 2012). This could include
about 25 % of all crops worldwide that are aﬀected by
fungal spoiling carrying serious economic ramiﬁcations
in addition to disease causation, e.g. consumer risk via
accumulated mycotoxins, most famously patulin in apples
(Andersen et al., 2004; Stein and Bulboac˘a, 2017).
And while all of these eﬀects are important and worth
great study, they also represent a solely human bias which
has dominated the SM investigative science leaving our
understanding of why microbes, would spend energy and
resources on keeping and maintaining that which have no
direct involvement in growth, development, and reproduc-
tion, not non-existent but comparatively grossly lacking.
One measure taken to expand on this was the formation of
The Center of Microbial Secondary Metabolites (CeMiSt)
a fundamental research center with the explicit goal:
”.. to unravel the biological role of microbial secondary
metabolites in natural microbiological communities.” -
Quote from http://www.cemist.dtu.dk/about.
as it is generally accepted that SMs must confer a sub-
stantial beneﬁt to the producer, be it to defend its habitat
(Netzker et al., 2015; L. Frank and Gram, 2017) or acqui-
sition of traits that provide advantageous ability or allows
it to respond better to ecological pressures (Wisecaver
and Rokas, 2015; L. Frank and Gram, 2017). Be it all,
either, or for entirely novel reasons than those stated, it is
worth understanding the physiological or ecological role
of SMs in the perspective of the microbes.
1.2.2
Organization of ﬁlamentous fungal secondary
metabolites
Fungal SMs belong to a highly diverse and complex group
of chemical compounds that, despite this, are often biosyn-
thesized by one or a few typically large core enzymes
forming the compound backbone, which often undergoes
multiple chemical modiﬁcations by tailoring enzymes re-
sulting in the ﬁnal SM or SMs. The genes encoding these
biosynthetic enzymes are, in many microbes, including ﬁl-
amentous fungi, generally clustered together and referred
to as biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) containing the
genes encoding the biosynthetic pathway and often genes
relevant for cluster expression regulation, product trans-
portation and potentially resistance to the end product
(Marinelli and Marcone, 2019).
This is again unlike PMs whose BGC-equivalent genes
are typically scattered in the fungal genome (N. P. Keller,
2019).
With the development of sequencing technologies the com-
monallity of this BGC formation became evident but it
also revealed that almost all microorganisms (>99 %) had
not been cultivated (Kaeberlein et al., 2002; Begani et al.,
2018), and also that most cultivable species contained
many more BGCs than had been traced via chemical anal-
ysis under laboratory conditions (Brakhage and Schroeckh,
2011; P. Trautman and M. Crawford, 2015; Begani et al.,
2018; Netzker et al., 2015). These became referred to
as silent gene clusters and it was quickly found that the
expression of many of theses could be triggered by alter-
native growth conditions and/or through co-cultivations
(X. Zhang et al., 2019; Netzker et al., 2015; Brakhage
and Schroeckh, 2011; Begani et al., 2018). Meaning that
despite a long interest of SMs to which many have already
been characterized many more remain (Netzker et al.,
2015; Begani et al., 2018).

1.2.3.1
Polyketides
are compounds resultant of re-
peated condensations of typically acetyl-co enzyme A
(CoA) or malonyl-CoA giving it a characteristic structure
of alternating carbonyl groups or a leftover pattern of
reductions or other modiﬁcations thereof.
PKs are biosynthesised by polyketide synthases (PKSs),
which are typically divided into type I, II, and III whose
categorizing is determined by the synthases’ structure
and domains. Fungal PKSs are multidomain and iterative
enzymes (iPKS), belonging to either type I or type III
(Rohr and Hertweck, 2010; Morita et al., 2010; Katsuyama
and Ohnishi, 2012; Keatinge-Clay, 2012; R. J. Cox et al.,
2018).
Fungal type I PKSs
are characterized as a continu-
ous protein containing one copy of diﬀerent domains in a
ordered fashion allowing multiple catalytic iterations to
form the PK backbone, see ﬁgure 2.
The Acyl transferase (AT), β-ketoacyl synthase (KS) and
Acyl carrier protein (ACP) domains are the main domains
responsible for the PK chain extension, while the Starter
unit acyl transferase (SAT) domain only appear in non-
reducing iPKSs.
Other domains e.g. Dehydratase (DH), C-methyl trans-
ferase (C-MeT), keto reductase (KR), and enoyl reduc-
tase (ER) are processing domains, while domaines like
thioesterase (TE) and Claisen cyclase (CLC) are release
domains. The latter two domain types might not con-
tribute to the length of the PK chain, but are essential
for the chemical diversity and structure of the PK. The
most common fungal type I iPKS is the highly reducing
iPKS, carrying the N- to C-terminal domain order of:
KS - AT - DH - C-Met - KRs - ER(*) - KRc - ACP -
(TE)
Where the ER domain, may or may not be functional, thus
sometimes a tailoring enzyme performing this function
can be found elsewhere in the BGC or genome (Keatinge-
Clay, 2012; R. J. Cox et al., 2018). All known PKS-NRPS
hybrids follow the non-functional ER highly reducing
iPKS form (Boettger and Hertweck, 2013; Cacho et al.,
2014).
Fungal type III PKSs
are homodimers of single β-
ketoacyl synthase (KS) domains, synthesising their prod-
ucts in an ping-pong sort of iterative manner passing the
growing product between the dimer itself and CoA or an
acyl-acyl carrier protein (Katsuyama and Ohnishi, 2012).
Thus, categorising fungal PKSs are quite simple in this
genome-sequenced and bioinformativ era, but reliably
predicting the PK product is currently beyond the capa-
bility of the scientiﬁc community as little is understood
about the enzymes ”programming”, or what determines
e.g. the number of iterations or the consistent activation
of the processing domains at speciﬁc times and not others
(Keatinge-Clay, 2012; R. J. Cox et al., 2018).
1.2.3.2
Nonribosomal peptides
(NRPs) are pep-
tides or small polypeptides not synthesised via ribosomes

but by nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPSs), multi-
modular megaenzymes that can be either one continuous
protein, as is often the case in fungi, or a colinear ag-
gregate of several proteins, though iterative exceptions
do exist (Winn et al., 2016; N. Keller et al., 2017; Ca-

is an ordered collection of a few domains solely devoted
to the elongation and modiﬁcation of one amino acid in
the NRPs chain. The modules appear in a linear fashion,
with an initiation module followed by a varying number of
elongation modules. The basic module structure consists
of the condensation (C), adenylation (A) and thiolation

does not include a C domain (Winn et al., 2016). The A
domain selects and activates the amino acid, determining
the polypetide sequence, and can utilize both proteino-

2014), though it does this with varying ﬁdelity of some
A domains (Winn et al., 2016). The ﬂexibility of the T

1.2.3.5
Tailoring enzymes
Like mentioned before,
these large synthase encoding genes are often ﬂanked by
additional genes encoding other proteins, like the enzymes
responsible for the chemical modiﬁcation of the SMs de-
scribed, which are responsible for much of the chemical
diversity observed in nature (Breitling and Takano, 2016;
Cacho et al., 2014; Matsuda and Abe, 2016).
Examples of tailoring enzymes include: methyltransferases,
acyltransferases, gylycosyltranferases, and oxidoreduc-
tases. Presence of genes encoding tailoring enzymes be-
longing the last mentioned class of enzymes, suggests the
biosynthetic pathways end product undergoes oxidative
transformation and include non-heme iron oxygenases,
ﬂavoenzymes, monooxygenase P450s (Cacho et al., 2014;
Marinelli and Marcone, 2019; Walsh et al., 2001) whose
role in the biosynthesis must often be determined individ-
ually as they can catalyze diverse redox reactions (Cacho
et al., 2014). This applies to most genes in SM BGCs as
they exhibit redundancy, often containing genes for more
or less enzymes necessary for the end product biosynthesis,
regulation, and transportation (Cummings et al., 2014).

1.3.2
Botrytis cinerea Pers. Fr.
Botrytis cinerea is a polyphageous necrotrophic pathogen
and facultative parasite with a worldwide distribution that
can infect more than 235 diﬀerent plant species. This
included important commercial crops where it acts either
as a important part of the production, like for some wines,
or a spoiling agent causing great economical loss (Robert
A. Samson et al., 2010; Choquer, Fournier, et al., 2007;
Collado et al., 2007; Romanazzi and Feliziani, 2014). B.
cinerea infections occur through secretion of nonspeciﬁc
phytotoxins, allowing colonization and penetration of the
dead plant tissues, or via reactive oxygen species (ROS)
in early infection of plant lesions and induces an oxida-
tive burst in the plant again allowing penetration into
the tissues (Choquer, Fournier, et al., 2007; Naets et al.,
2018). The main phytotoxin produced by B. cinerea is the
sesquiterpene botrydial, beloning to a family of botryanes
(Moraga, Dalmais, et al., 2016; Collado et al., 2007; Cho-
quer, Fournier, et al., 2007), and botrydial is known to
reproduce the symptoms of B. cinerea infection, except
the mycelial growths; but another important phytotoxin
family is the polyketide botrylactones (Moraga, Pinedo,
et al., 2011; Moraga, Pinedo, et al., 2011; Collado et al.,
2007).
It can be recognized by its gray-ish powdery rot, often at
the apple calyx area, ranging from a more white-gray to
a more brown-gray coloring due to its branched hyaline
hyphae and brown conidiophore with pale brown conidia -
the asexual reproductive structures and spores - while scle-
rotia are black to dark brown and varies in size (Collado
et al., 2007; Robert A. Samson et al., 2010; Romanazzi
and Feliziani, 2014). In nature the spreading of the fungus
happens by discharged ascospores - sexual spores - from
fruiting bodies, apothecia - a sexual structure - sprouting
form the overwintering sclerotia or via conidia from over-
wintering mycelium or unfertilized sclerotia both typically
found on infected crop debris in the topsoil or mummiﬁed
fruits left in the tree canopies (Rodenburg et al., 2018;
Romanazzi and Feliziani, 2014).
1.3.3
Monilinia fructigena (Aderh. & Ruhland)
Honey x Whetzel
The Monilinia Junctoriae section contains species causing
brown rot on many types of fruit, primarily stone or pome
fruit. While the genus has a worldwide distribution some
of the individual species exhibit a more limited geographic
distribution, though this is less and less prominent, among
other this things, due to global fruit exports. In Europe
the most dominant brown rot species are M. laxa and
M. fructigena both capable of infecting pome and stone
fruits, but with M. laxa predominantly infecting stone
fruits and M. fructigena is the main brown rot pathogen
on pome fruits like apples and pears (Francesco and Mari,
2018; Hrusti´c et al., 2012; Miljan Vasi´c et al., 2018; Abate
et al., 2018).
M fructigena is likewise a necrotrophic fungi and can
infects apples by phytotoxins and/or ROS, presumably as-
sisting germinating spores to directly penetrate the apple

1.3.4
Penicillium expansum Link
P. expansum is a ubiquitous soil and common airborne
fungus with a worldwide distribution and can grow on
a wide range of organic material, including pome fruits,
especially apples, stone fruits, nuts, legumes, dried meat,
cellulose containing building materials, and wood (An-
dersen et al., 2004; Tannous, N. P. Keller, et al., 2018;
R. A. Samson and Hadlok, 1976; Robert A. Samson et al.,
2010; S´anchez-Torres et al., 2018; Frisvad and Robert A.
Samson, 2004; Errampalli, 2014).
Unable to penetrate the apple peel on it own, it is a
wound pathogen whose infection relies on injuries caused
by unfavorable weather, insect or bird inﬂected injuries,
or rough handling during harvest, transport, or storage
(Errampalli, 2014; Tannous, Atoui, et al., 2016). The
infection can be seen as the formation of soft, light brown,
and watery lesions with quite sharp margins and as it ages
small tufts of conidiophores with green to blueish-green
conidia form. No sexual form of P. expansum is known,
meaning the fungus spreads by the conidia typically via
the air, rain, debris in fruit bins at harvest, or diﬀerent
watering systems (Errampalli, 2014).
P. expansum produces several famous and important my-
cotoxins - toxic SM produced by fungi - like the potentially
carcinogenic PK patulin (Pal et al., 2017) and nephro-
toxic citrinin (JH, 2015), the PK ochratoxin A, the indole
alkaloid NRP roqufortine C and many more (Andersen
et al., 2004).
1.3.5
Trichothecium roseum (Pers.:Fr.) Link
T. roseum is a common soil fungus with a worldwide dis-
tribution (Robert A. Samson et al., 2010; Snowdon, 1990)
that can infect many commercial grops including pome
fruits, stone fruits, melons, tomatoes and some grains (Z.
Zhang et al., 2020; ˇZabka et al., 2006; R. A. Samson and
Hadlok, 1976; Snowdon, 1990). It is also a mycoparasite
capable of parasitizing the mycelium of other fungi, and is
therefore often tested or even used as a biocontrol agent
against phytopathogenic fungi (Xian et al., 2012; Sharma
et al., 2014), including B. cinerea (G. Q. Li et al., 2004)
and Monilinia spp. (May-De Mio et al., 2014; Sharma
et al., 2014). T. roseum is a oppotunistic pathogen on
fruits often being a secondary infection, utilizing the le-
sions greated by other phytopathogens to enter and infect
fruits (Sharma et al., 2014; Snowdon, 1990) though it is
also known to infect, especially apple blossoms and core
by the calyx end through natural openings of the apple
(Snowdon, 1990; Gong et al., 2019). The visual symptoms
of infection are rather discrete being being the formation
small black spots if infectiong young apples, the formation
of pink spores at the infection site, and a bitter taste of
infected apple ﬂesh (Snowdon, 1990; X. Li et al., 2019).
No sexual form is known and the infections spread via

The Project Aims
The aim of this project is to develop methods that helps study the metabolic interaction between co-cultures of the
ﬁlamentous fungi Botrytis cinerea, Monilinia fructigena, Penicillium expansum, and Trichothecium roseum in vivo on
apples and in vitro under laboratory conditions and secondary to develop or propose genetic engineering strategy to
possibly aide the understanding of the metabolites physiological role in the respective organisms and/or interaction.
To cover the diﬀerent aspects of this broad aim, the following is investigated:
1. The interaction between fungi will manifest both visually, through altered growth or growth rate, and in the
metabolic proﬁles, with compounds appearing or disappearing when comparing the mono and co-culture, when
grown both in vivo and in vitro.
2. In extension of the above: The metabolite proﬁles between equivalent samples in vivo and in vitro are dissimilar,
due to the diﬀerence of the apples and the laboratory growth medium.
3. Some of the already known metabolites are involved in the diﬀerences observed between both mono and co-cultures
as well as apples and laboratory growth medium. And likewise for unknown or not recognized metabolites in this
project.
4. All of the above can be investigated via simple experimental setups and relevant methods.
5. Which molecular engineering tools for transformation with exogenous DNA is available in literature for the four
fungi?
6. Which of these tools might be possible to develop for the potential fungi which have not been genetically edited
before, with the aim of later measuring the interaction between fungi in vivo?

3.1
Metabolite analysis via harvest, ex-
traction, HPLC, and MS/MS
Due to the extreme diversity in metabolite polarity, mass,
concentration, location and stability, great care should be
given to the method of analyzing. As no known method is
capable of detecting and analyzing all and every metabo-
lite originating from any organism or tissue, the intent of
the study or the speciﬁc submetabolome sought investi-
gated leads to diﬀerent sampling, extraction and analyzing
methods with intrinsic advantages and limitations.
Investigation of unknown compound are done by untar-
geted methods where the aim is to detect as much as
possible of the metabolome or submetabolome of interest
while targeted methods tend to be either: 1. optimized
for the compound(s) of interest excluding most possible,
or 2. of high resolution of the compounds of interest allow-
ing for detailed data processing (Jones and H¨ugel, 2013).
Both of these approaches can, however, be divided into
sample harvest, metabolite extraction, and instrument
analysis, with diﬀerent sample preparations bridging the
transitions between each division (De Souza, 2013).
Little of the diversity and pattern of this will be exem-
pliﬁed by brieﬂy describing the utilized methods in C.
Garcia-Benitez et al., 2017, Kim et al., 2018, and Ander-
sen et al., 2004.
C. Garcia-Benitez et al., 2017 did a untargeted investiga-
tion of the occurrence of secreted phytotoxins by Monilinia
sp. on infected nectarines via several liquid-liquid ex-
tractions of increasing polarity (n-hexane to butanol) of
nectarine juice giving several fractions and narrowing
down the polarity range for later high performance liquid
chromatography and mass spectrometry (HPLC-MS) de-
tection.
Here the sample harvesting and -preparation was the juic-
ing of infected nectarines, exclusion of the pulp made it
comparable with the liquid-liquid metabolite extraction
where immiscible solvent systems, typically biphasic, al-
lows for metabolite separation by polarity driven solute
partitioning (Jones and H¨ugel, 2013). The sample prepara-
tion for and speciﬁcs of the tandem HPLC-MS instrument
analysis will be covered in greater detail in section 3.1.1
as this is the relevant analysis for this project.
Kim et al., 2018 did a untargeted investigation of changes
to the volatile submetabolome of apples by placing disease
free and fungal infected (including P. expansum) apples
in airtight jars 1 hour before a 20 minutes solid-phase
micro-extraction (SPME) ﬁber exposure to the internal
atmosphere which were then analyzed via gas chromatog-
raphy (GC)-MS.
Here the sample harvesting was the 1 hour saturation
of the jars internal atmosphere, the metabolite extrac-
tion was the 20 minute SPME ﬁber adsorption and the
instrument analysis the GC-MS.
Andersen et al., 2004 did a targeted analysis of known
P. expansum mycotoxins. One method included wetting
mycelium covered agar plugs with a little solvent and
placing them mycelium down on thin layer chromatog-
raphy (TLC) plates. In another method large mycelium
agar plugs were covered in solvent, sonicated 1 hour, the
supernatant transferred, evaporated, redissolved by ultra-
sonicatin in a new solvent, and analyzed via HPLC-MS.
Here the sample harvest was the cutting of agar plugs for
both analyses. In the ﬁrst analysis the solvent wetting was
both the metabolic extraction and sample preparation for
the TLC instrument analysis. In the second the utilized
solvents and sonication was the metabolite extraction and
the rest sample preparation for HPLC-MS instrument
analysis. Here the targeting was mainly performed during
the data processing of the instrument analyzes.

Chromatography
Chromatography is generally a method of compound sep-
aration utilizing a porous stationary phase capable of
retaining the metabolites and yet let them elute gradu-
ally with a mobile phase. The mobile phase can either
change in polarity over time, thus being a gradient or
remain unchanging, being syncratic. If the stationary
phase depends on non-polar interactions to retain the
sample compounds it is referred to as Reverse phase (RP)
and the mobile phase is typically of a more polar nature;
eluting initially more polar compounds and increasingly
11
==PAGE:19==
non-polar compounds while the opposite is true for nor-
mal phase chromatography.
In RP ultrahigh-performance liquid chromatography (UH-
PLC) a continuous mobile phase, e.g. a water/acetonitrile
gradient, is carried through a stationary phase, e.g. phenyl
and hexyl covered spherical silica particles 1.9 µm diame-
ter, by 600-1200 bars of pressure to increase compound
separation of even complex samples (Callahan and Elliott,
2013; Brochure, n.d.).
HPLC to MS sample preparation
Sample preparation from the UHPLC to the MS is nec-
essary since the MS can only process and detect gaseous
ions.
This can be done via a electrospray ionization (ESI) where
the UHPLC eluent is nebulized under strong shear forces,
creating a super ﬁne mist, into a chamber containing a
strong electrostatic ﬁeld and heated drying gas. Analyte
ions can be formed in solution before nebulization de-
pending on the chemistry of the sample analytes, buﬀers
and utilized solvents and these ions, either positively or
negatively charged, will migrate to the mists droplet sur-
face due to the strong electrostatic ﬁeld. As the solvent
evaporates due to the heated drying gas, the charged sur-
face analytes must get closer together which can induce
ionization of other analytes.
When the droplets have shrunk suﬃciently in size the
charges repulsion, called Coulomb repulsion, causes the
droplets to explode into smaller droplets and eventually
bare ions now in a gaseous phase.
Diﬀerent analyte ions can form from the same compound,
which are called adduct ions, and while they will diﬀer
in mass, they are characterized by co-elution with other
adduct ions.
The ions are then attracted into the oppositely charged
MS sampling oriﬁce (Manual, 2015)

3.2
PEG mediated transformation of pro-
toplasts - a common method for facil-
itating DNA uptake
Genetic transformation always requires transfer of DNA
into a recipient organism and following isolation or selec-
tion of those recipients that contains the transferred DNA.
In ﬁlamentous fungi several obstacles must be overcome
in order to achieve this (Poyedinok and Blume, 2018).
One major obstacle to DNA transfer, or DNA uptake, in
fungi is the fungal cell wall (Poyedinok and Blume, 2018;
D. Li et al., 2017). Typically consisting of several layers
of diﬀerent covalently interconnected polysaccharides and
proteins, the cell wall is both made to be malleable, al-
lowing growth at the hyphal tips, and the toughest cell
wall found in nature, build to resist the internal pressure
of the cell, caused by the high osmotic uptake of water
due to the high solute and salt concentration in the cells.
This pressure, called turgor, is often 2-20 times that of
the normal atmospheric pressure (Gow, 2017), and this is
important to remember as cell wall disruption is a prereq-
uisite for the vast majority, if not all ﬁlamentous fungal
transformation techniques.
Great obstacles to isolating or selecting for transformants
is the multinucleate nature and interconnection via septal
pores of many mycelial cell. This creates the potential
for cells containing nuclei with both the intended mod-
iﬁcation(s) and unmodiﬁed nuclei, called heterokaryon,
or even cells without any of the intended modiﬁcation if
the selection marker can diﬀuse through the septal pores
(Poyedinok and Blume, 2018; D. Li et al., 2017).
One of the most common methods of transforming ﬁl-
amentous fungi include the polyethylene glycol (PEG)
mediated transformation of protoplasts of which a generic
overview of the method is illustrated in ﬁgure 8.
The method includes a enzymatic digestion of the cell wall
of germinating spores or mycelium via enzyme cocktails.
Several commercial enzyme cocktails for fungal cell wall
digestion are available, of which the optimal cocktails
or cocktail mix will be species dependent (D. Li et al.,
2017) as the speciﬁc cell wall construction diﬀers between
species, though usually involving the polysaccharide chitin
and glucan (Gow, 2017). As the cell wall digests the tur-
gor causes the cell membranes to bulge out forming large
spherical vesicles, called protoplasts, of which viable ones
13
==PAGE:21==
will contain all the cellular organelles necessary for cellular
function. To prevent the protoplasts from bursting, again
due to the turgor, the cell wall digestion is performed
under osmotically stable conditions typically achieved
by high molar concentrations, 0.8-1.2 M, of sugars, e.g.
sucrose or sugar alcohols, e.g. sorbitol in combination
with other stabilizing solutes, often including chloride and
calcium ions.
The calcium ions and PEG is added during the transfor-
mation to assist the protoplast uptake of exogenous DNA,
increasing their competency. Calcium is thought acheive
this by being involved in the formation or opening of
channels that can facilitate the uptake of exogenous DNA,
and PEG likewise facilitates uptake, though by altering
the permeability cytomembrane by interacting with its
phospholipids.
The protoplasts are then incubated, now in/on osmoti-
cally stabilized medium, with immediate or latter addition
of the selection pressure, to regenerate and (hopefully)
resulting in transformants which should then be tested
for heterokayosis and addressed if present (Poyedinok and
Blume, 2018; D. Li et al., 2017; Bashi et al., 2010).
3.2.1
DNA repair systems of ﬁlamentous fungi
Transformations can rely on incorporation of independent
vectors like circular plasmids (Aleksenko and Clutterbuck,
1996), or by chromosomal integration by linear transfor-
mation vectors.
Circular plasmids can usually be ideal for testing transient
traits, whereas chromosomal integration typically is of
a more permanent nature (Poyedinok and Blume, 2018)
and utilizes the natural DNA repair systems of the fungus
(Krappmann, 2007; San Filippo et al., 2008).
The some of the relevant natural DNA repair systems in
regard to chromosomal integratetion of exogenous DNA,
are those that deal with DNA double stranded breaks
(DSB)s. DSBs can be cytotoxic to the organism if left
undealt with or ﬁxed incorrectly. Despite this all organ-
isms often have to deal with DSBs as these form either
expectantly, e.g. cell division, or randomly - often due
to exogenous agents. The two major and common re-
pair systems that ensure genome integrity in fungi are
the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) and homologous
recombination (HR) pathways.
The NHEJ pathway utilizes the KU70, KU80, or analo-
gous proteins, to non-speciﬁcally bind DNA DSBs and
later recruit the rest of the pathways protein complexes
resulting in mitigation of the DNA lesion requiring no or
little homologous sequence (Krappmann, 2007; Heﬀerin
and Tomkinson, 2005).
HR likewise involves a host of proteins, with Rad52, or
homologs, being important for commitment to the HR
pathway (Krappmann, 2007; San Filippo et al., 2008).
When committed, the HR pathway acts by a 5’ trim-
ming, or resection at the DSB site leaving a 3’ single
stranded DNA overhangs that are organised by binding

USER Cloning
The Uracil-Speciﬁc Excision Reagent (USER) cloning
(New England Biolabs) is a technique used to direction-
ally introduce one or multiple PCR amplicon into USER
friendly transformation vectors, Illustrated on ﬁgure 10.
These transformation vectors contains a cut- and nicking
site that when treated opens up the vector and results
in 9 nucleotide (nt) 3’ overhangs, complementary to the
utilized restriction enzymes. Typically PacI is utilized
ensuring that the overhangs 3’ end nt is an adenosine
and the nicking enzyme Nt.BbvCI is used to create the
overhang size, with the ﬁrst nt in the overhang likewise
being an adenosine.
PCR amplicons can have these overhangs matched in the
preferable direction by adding a complementing 9 nt tail
to the 5’ of the PCR amplicon primer design containing
a 2-deoxyuridine near the end.
By creating a primer
set with a deoxyuridine at the 9th position from the 5’
the PCR amplicon overhangs are created by treatment
with a uracil DNA glycosylase and DNA glycosylase-lyase
Endo VII enzyme mix that cuts out the deoxyuridine.
Likewise principles governs the seamless connection of
multiple amplicon, each with an unique over hang to
ensure fragment direction, as the overhangs are stable
enough to be transformed into E. coli whose DNA repair
system will ligate the fragments. (Frandsen et al., 2008;
Rendsvig et al., 2019). This technique requires a special-
ized high-ﬁdelity DNA polymerase that can tolerate the
deoxyuridine (Nour-Eldin et al., 2010; Nørholm, 2010),
of which several are commercially available (Nour-Eldin
et al., 2010)

Apple and PDA growth experiment
All work with sterile equipment and under sterile condi-

remove hypochlorite and sat to dry before being placed
in sterile beakers with new petri dish lids.
The apple surface was breached using an metal rod with
a 3 mm diameter (Ø). Control apples were ﬁnished, but
other apples were inoculated with cultures form the IBT

fructigena IBT42670 2 and Penicillium expansum IBT34679

42718, Monilinia fructigena IBT42603, Penicillium ex-

apples with 5 replicates. PDA plates were done in trip-
licates, except the control plate where there was only
one.

ples and PDA plates.

5 mm Ø plugs with the relevant fungi into the breaches
with all inoculations done in the order of M. fructigena, P.
expansum, B. cinerea and T. roseum with full equipment

toothpick spore inoculations for the rest.

No methods in any of the literature I have read indicated a
better contamination control, though (Sauer, 1986) found
that a 30-85 seconds >95 % ethanol pretreatment espe-

Instead of a more disinfecting method, the studies men-
tioned seemed to rather rely on high replication numbers,
typically somewhere between 16 (Z. Zhang et al., 2020)
or 20 (S´anchez-Torres et al., 2018) for each experiment
type, comparable to each of this projects mono and co-
cultures. This practice is also reﬂected in other fruits, e.g.
in peaches where May-De Mio et al., 2014 used 10 to 15
fruits for each replicate of 6 replicates for each unique
experiment.
If this project is to be repeated with more replications for
each mono and co-culture, the overall time of the steril-
ization and inoculation process has to be reduced.
Therefore, with or without the pretreatment, I would rec-
ommend a 1 min <1.5 % hypochloride dip, rinsed with
sterile distilated water or wiped with 70 % ethanol paper
towels and letting the apples air dry in petri dish bottoms
left over from the utilized lids.
Changes to the sodium hypochloride concentration is in-
spired by the majority of the articles typically using 1-2
% with a exposure time of often 1-2 minute followed by
either an ethanol wipe, 1 to 3 water rinsings and air
drying (Naets et al., 2018; Rodr´ıguez-Pires et al., 2019;
Grantina-Ievina, 2016; M. Vasi´c et al., 2016), but also the
ﬁndings by Naets et al., 2018 showing hypochlorite results
in cracks of the apple peel which might oﬀer pathogens
- or here contaminants - pathways of invasion. The last
part with the petri dish is what was done in the ﬁrst
apple experiment but not the second - in an attempt to
save material - but is recommended again to avoid the
smears that otherwise might appear on the lid, obscuring
the photographic documentation. These smears are most
prominently seen on ﬁgures 21B and 19G and not visible
on any of the pictures in ﬁgures 59 - 66.
Additionally the control apples should include uninocu-
lated PDA plugs to further mimick the inoculated apples

Mari, 2018; Miljan Vasi´c et al., 2018).

5.4
A Look Into the Genetic Tractability
of the Fungi
Future experiments investigating interesting metabolites
and their roles in interspeciﬁc interactions involving these
fungi will most likely require genetic engineering, e.g.
to increase or even force production of down regulated
metabolites; knockout, delete or down regulate metabo-
lites and much more in order ”... to unravel biological
role of microbial secondary metabolites in natural micro-
biological communities”. It is therefore important to ﬁnd
out whether or not the fungi is genetically tractable.
I could to the best of my abilities not ﬁnd any report of
genetic transformation or engineering of M. fructigena,
making the protoplastation and indications of functional
PEG mediated transformation protocols reported in the
following paragraphs exciting.
A diﬀerent project focused on similar investigation of P.
expansum and was therefore not covered in this project.
B. cinerea has several established protocols for genetic
engineering (Espino et al., 2014; Ish-Shalom et al., 2011;
Choquer, Robin, et al., 2008); and so does T. roseum
(Gnanam, 2013; Balasubramanian et al., 2003).

Luria Broth (LB) and LB agar 10 g/L Bacto™tryptone, 5 g/L Bacto™yeast extract, 10 g/L NaCl, demineralised
water added to 9/10 of total volume, pH = 7.25 ±2.25, 20 g/L agar (optional), and demineralised water added to
total volume. Autoclaved at 121 °C for 15 minutes.
Potato dextrose agar (PDA) 39 g/L Difco™Potato Dextrose Agar, ﬁnal pH =5.6 ±0.2. Autoclaved at 121 °C
for 15 minutes.
Potato dextrose broth (PDB 24 g/L VWR Chemicals BDH ®, ﬁnal pH =5.6 ±0.2. Autoclaved at 121 °C for 15
minutes.
Homemade PDA 200 g/L potatoes is boiled for 30 minutes water, afterwards it is cooled and ﬁltered/sifted to
remove all potato pieces. The liquid is added 20 g/L dextrose, 15 g/L agar and demineralised water to the total
volume. Autoclaved at 121 °C for 15 minutes.
Trace element solution - stock 0.4 g/L CuSO4 · 5 H2O, 0.04 g/L Na2Ba4O7 · 10 H2O, 0.8 g/L FeSO4 · 7 H2O, 0.8
g/L MnSO4 · 7 H2O, 0.8 g/L Na2MoO4 · 2 H2O, 8.0 g/L ZnSO4 · 7 H2O with Milli Q water to ﬁnal volume.
20x Nitrate salts solution - stock 120 g/L NaNO3, 10.4 g/L KCl, 10.4 g/L MgSO4 · 7 H2O, 30.4 g/L KH2PO4
with Milli Q to ﬁnal volume.
20 % (w/V) D-glucose solution 200 g/L D-glucose, Milli Q water to desired volume and autoclaved at 121 °C
for 15 minutes.
Minimal Media (MM) 50 ml/L 20x Nitrate salts solution, 1 mL/L trace elements solution, 1 mL/L 1% Thiamine
solution. 21 g/L agar is optional. The medium is added Milli Q water 19/20 of ﬁnal volume and autoclaved at 121
°C for 15 minutes. Before potential solidiﬁcation or prior to use of the medium, 1/20 ﬁnale volume of a 20% w/V
D-glucose solution is added sterile.
Transformations medium (TM) 342.3 g/L sucrose, 50 ml/L 20x Nitrate salts solution, 1 mL/L trace elements
solution, 1 mL/L 1% Thiamine solution. 21 g/L agar is optional. The medium is added Milli Q water to its ﬁnal
volume and autoclaved at 121 °C for 15 minutes.
Yeast extract Peptone Dextrose (YPD) 10 g/L yeast extract, 20 g/L peptone, 20 g/L agar (optional), and
demineralised water to 9/10 of the ﬁnal volume, and autoclaved at 121 °C for 15 minutes. Before potential solidiﬁcation
or prior to use of the medium, 1/20 ﬁnale volume of a 20% w/V D-glucose solution is added sterile.
Medium supplementation of uracil and uridine If medium broth or agar is to be supplemented with uracil
and uridine, 5-10 % ﬁnal volume of demineralised water is withheld from the bulk medium recipie. This water is
heated and added 2.44 g/L uridine and 1.12 g/L uracil, measured to ﬁt the ﬁnal volume of the ﬁnished medium,
being a 10 mM ﬁnal concentration. The solution is ﬁlter sterilized into the medium prior solidiﬁcation or use.
10 mM Na-phosphate buﬀer utilizing Na2HPO4, NaH2PO4, Milli Q water to ﬁnal volume, pH 5.8 adjusted with
1 M Na2HPO4 or 1 M NaH2PO4.
