Abstract
Through a circular economy lens, this thesis explores the transition from disposable to reusable
medical instruments in Danish hospitals, within Region Midtjylland. The research is motivated by
the substantial environmental and economic impacts of disposable instruments and aims to de-
velop a framework supporting the transition to reusable alternatives. By employing a multi-faceted
methodological approach, including a comprehensive analysis of the use context and environ-
mental and economic aspects, the study critically examines the use of disposable and reusable
instruments in hospital settings. The ﬁndings reveal key challenges and opportunities associated
with this transition. The research culminates in conceptualizing and developing a practical work-
book, designed to guide hospital departments in Region Midtjylland through the complexities of
adopting reusable instruments. This workbook serves as a tool for environmental and economic
evaluation, encouraging a sustainable shift in healthcare practices. The thesis thus extends its im-
pact beyond academic discourse, providing a tangible resource for real-world application in the
pursuit of sustainability within healthcare.

Learning objectives
Through the execution of this project, we will able to:
1. Plan, structure and execute a long-term project in collaboration with multiple stakeholders.
2. Collect and structure information and data from a problem context, using contextual inter-
views and other appropriate methods, for analysis.
3. Conduct a methodically planned literature review.
4. Perform a comprehensive actor-network analysis of relevant actors and products.
5. Assess the feasibility and impacts of implementing reusable products to replace single-use
products, using life cycle screening principles.
6. Demonstrate the ability to critically select and apply relevant circular strategies to generate
alternate solution concepts.
7. Demonstrate the ability to develop a chosen concept to a level of societal readiness.
8. Demonstrate the ability to identify, describe, and address risks and failures by performing a
risk analysis.
9. Demonstrate the ability to design an implementation plan based on ﬁndings from the anal-
ysis.
10. Demonstrate the ability to design a framework that supports the transition from single-use to
reusable product solutions, considering environmental, economic, and social aspects.

Methodology
The structure of this thesis is built around the
Design Research Methodology (DRM) (Blessing
and Chakrabarti 2009). The overall objective of
the DRM is to provide a systematic framework
for understanding and addressing complex de-
sign problems, identifying knowledge gaps,
and fostering collaboration among stakehold-
ers. This provides a systematic and structured
way to improve design and, by that, improve
the chances of producing a successful prod-
uct.
The DRM consists of 4 stages in total:
Research Clariﬁcation (RC), Descriptive Study
(DS), Prescriptive Study (PS), and Descriptive
Study II (DSII). The process through each stage
is illustrated in ﬁgure 1.
An important aspect
of any design process is to iterate between
phases, a principle that was also employed in
this project. Here, the process will be described
linearly to establish the overall ﬂow of the pro-
cess:

Research Clariﬁcation
The main activity in RC
is to understand and identify the core of the
problem related to transitioning from dispos-
able to reusable metal instruments, as per-
ceived by relevant stakeholders and the client
CfBH. Based on thorough desktop research,
client interviews, and the site visit to Aarhus Uni-
versitets Hospital, the research questions and
project goal for this project were established.
Descriptive Study
Upon setting a direction
for the project, the purpose of the DS is to in-
vestigate the problem context. This was done
through a thorough system analysis regarding
the use of both disposable and reusable metal
instruments. The analysis built upon multiple site
visits to hospitals in Region Midtjylland accom-
panied by stakeholder interviews and the re-
view of background literature.
Additionally, a literature review on the environ-
mental and economic impacts of transitioning
was used to develop the foundation for model-
ing the consequences of transitioning from dis-
posable to reusable instruments, necessary to
assess the viability of a reusable solution.
The output of the DS was an overview of de-
sign challenges and implications for the devel-
opment of support in the shape of a require-
ments speciﬁcation, a design speciﬁcation for
a workbook, and the foundation for modeling
the impacts of using reusable and disposable
metal instruments.
Prescriptive Study
Based on the groundwork
of the DS, PS focuses on developing a solution
to address the identiﬁed design challenges.
Through iterations, a support in the shape of
a workbook was developed, utilizing a design
sprint, failure mode and eﬀect analysis, co-
creation, and life cycle modeling.

1.1
Setting the Scene
The overall goal of this thesis project is to de-
velop a framework for the implementation of
circular economy principles within Danish hos-
pitals, with a speciﬁc focus on transitioning from
disposable to reusable medical instruments.
This framework should address environmental
and economic aspects of sustainability, oﬀer-
ing practical insights, guidelines, and recom-
mendations for healthcare management and
practitioners.
Additionally, the project aims
to contribute to the ongoing eﬀorts of the
Center for Bæredygtige Hospitaler by building
on their existing knowledge and ﬁndings, ulti-
mately contributing to a more environmentally
responsible healthcare system in Denmark.
This section serves to deﬁne the nature of the
project and its relation with Region Midtjylland-
jylland as a collaboration partner.
Literature
on circular economy, and sustainability within
healthcare along with background literature
from Region Midtjylland was reviewed accom-
panied by preliminary interviews and site vis-
its at Aarhus Universitets Hospital (AUH) and
Randers Regionshospital (RHR) and desktop re-
search in instrument use. The outcomes of this
section are:
1. An initial understanding of the current
systems revolving around the use of dis-
posable metal instruments and reusable
metal instruments and the expected im-
pacts and changes caused to these sys-
tems by an intervention.
2. An introduction to the stakeholder land-
scape revolving around the transition
from disposable to reusable metal instru-
ments.
3. An overall research goal and a series of
research questions to further guide the
research project.
1.1.1
Sustainability in the Health-
care Sector
Due to its size and its operational activities,
the global healthcare sector is responsible for
a major environmental footprint. It has been
reported, that emissions from health care ac-
count for nearly ﬁve percent of the global total
(Karliner et al. 2019; Macneill et al. 2020), and in
Denmark, health care accounts for more than
6% of total national carbon emissions (Karliner
et al. 2019). Along with a growing population,
aging demographics, and improved access to
healthcare services in developing nations, the
footprint associated with healthcare can only
be expected to increase (Benedettini 2022).
This is further escalated due to the widespread
adoption of advanced medical technologies,
equipment, and procedures, many of which,
are energy- and resource-intensive (Karliner et
al. 2019).
Since the 1980s, modern healthcare has be-
come heavily reliant on the use of cheap
mass-produced disposable single-use prod-
ucts
(Moultrie,
Sutcliﬀe,
and
Maier
2015;
Benedettini 2022). The use of these products
has a tremendous impact on both the car-
bon footprint and waste generation of hospi-
tals (Bhutta 2021; Benedettini 2022), account-
ing for upwards of one quarter of total emis-
sions and waste generation (Bhutta 2021; Re-
gion Midtjylland 2022). Historically, sustainabil-
ity initiatives in the healthcare industry have
lagged behind, compared to other industries,

1.1.2
Sustainability
in
Region
Midtjylland
These ﬁndings are well reﬂected when ac-
counting for the footprint of hospitals in Re-
gion Midtjyllandjylland. According to their cli-
mate accounting, 83% of emissions stem from
the healthcare sector, of which 86% stem from
the procurement of medical products and ser-
vices, of which 30% are directly from the pur-
chase of medical equipment and instrumen-
tation (Region Midtjylland 2022).
Much of the equipment purchased consists
of disposable items, that are only intended
for single-use or single-patient-use, where the
items are disposed of. The use of disposable
medical instruments is problematic in terms of
sustainability, yet, it is diﬃcult to combat due to
the many beneﬁts associated with disposables.
Disposable instruments eliminate the need for
washing and sterilization between uses as they
are disposed of directly after use, which also
eliminates the risks of cross-contamination and
infections.
This also saves hospitals time and
eﬀort, compared to reusable that do require
cleaning, sterilization, and maintenance be-
tween uses, and eliminates the need for elab-
orate sterilization and transport infrastructure.
This makes disposable instrumentation a cost-
eﬀective solution in conjunction with technical
advancements within manufacturing and out-
sourcing to low-cost manufacturing countries.
Additionally, hospitals avoid signiﬁcant upfront
costs associated with purchasing and main-
taining reusable instruments, which are signif-
icantly more expensive in procurement.
Dis-
posable instruments also contribute to a more
streamlined workﬂow, as there is no waiting
time for sterilization between uses thereby se-
curing supply, as long as vendors can supply
required instrumentation.
However,
recent sustainability concerns by
healthcare
personnel
and
the
Center
for
Bæredygtige Hospitaler in Region Midtjylland,
have led to a project that questions the use
of disposable instruments in Region Midtjylland
and aims to explore how reusable instruments
can replace disposable instruments while re-
ducing carbon emissions and keeping it aﬀord-
able, using circular economy strategies.
1.1.3
The Client
Center for Bæredygtighe Hospitaler, from here
on referred to as (CfBH) was established in 2021
to gather, develop, and share new, practical
knowledge about sustainable transformation
within hospitals in Region Midtjylland. They aim
to help achieve Region Midtjylland’s 2030 sus-
tainability targets by reducing consumption by
30%, reusing 30% of all products, and recycling
70% of all materials, compared to 2018. CfBH

1.1.5
Disposable and Reusable
Metal
Instruments
within
Scope
The notion of metal instruments (both dispos-
able and reusable) in the context of this thesis
refers to basic ’invasive’, or ’critical’ metal in-
struments that are widely used across all hos-
pitals in Region Midtjylland. Critical instruments
refer to instruments that during procedures are
in contact with bodily tissues or the blood-
stream, and are therefore required to be sterile.
They must therefore undergo sterilization before
they can be used again on another patient
(Statens Serum Institut 2019).
This is opposed
to semi-critical instruments and non-critical in-
struments that only need disinfection post-use
(see ﬁg. 1.3). Basic instruments include surgical
scissors, forceps, needle holders, clamps, and
hemostatic forceps amongst others, and are
often found in disposable instrument sets (see
ﬁg. 1.2). Within the scope of this thesis are crit-
ical basic instruments that are entirely made
of steel and can endure reprocessing. In the
following, reusable metal instruments will be re-
ferred to as RMI and disposables as DMI.

1.1.7
Circular Economy
Circular Economy (CE) is an economic sys-
tem that emphasizes sustainability to minimize
waste and make the most of resources. In a
circular economy, products, materials, and re-
sources are kept in use as long as possible, and,
at the end of their service life, they are recov-
ered, regenerated, or recycled to create new
products or materials. The value of products
and materials is thereby contained within the
system for as long as possible.
Bocken et al
(Bocken et al. 2016) summarized CE practices
into three fundamental strategies:
1. Slowing:
Products
are
designed
for
longevity and to have their product life
extended through repair and remanu-
facturing, thus slowing the time before
end-of-life.
2. Closing: Products are recycled post-use,
and become a resource for the produc-

==PAGE:20==

tion of new products, thereby creating a
circular ﬂow of materials.
3. Narrowing: Products are designed with
resource eﬃciency in mind, thereby nar-
rowing the resource ﬂows, resulting in
fewer resources used per product.
In relation to this thesis project, these circular
strategies can serve to support the develop-
ment of a framework for more sustainable in-
strument use in Region Midtjylland. As the aim
of this thesis is to promote the reuse of instru-
ments, the strategies for slowing and closing
are the most prominent. More speciﬁcally, de-
sign for product-life extension as RMIs are in-
tended to be used over and over and are de-
signed for the technological cycle, as these
instruments require elaborate reprocessing sys-
tems to support their repeated use cycle.
Reusing has the potential to save the resources
and values placed into the extraction and pro-
duction of new products.
This includes en-
ergy, labor time, and materials and reduces
the waste stream, and saves time, resources,
and energy used to repair, remanufacture, or
recycle old products (CIRCit Nord 2020).
1.1.8
Circular
Economy
within
Region Midtjylland
The concept of a circular economy is not a
novel practice within healthcare. Until recent
advances, starting in the 1980’s reusable instru-
ments were predominant. The majority of med-
ical instruments were designed for reusability
and as such a closed-loop practice. At that
time, disinfection processes were not as ef-
fective, leading to instruments often being a
source of cross-contamination. However, sig-
niﬁcant advancements have occurred since
then. Modern reprocessing techniques ensure
that instruments can be treated to a standard
that eliminates the risk of cross-contamination,
marking a substantial improvement in health-
care hygiene.
As such, reprocessing of in-
struments enables the reuse of a wide array
of medical instruments and products, which is
much preferred in terms of sustainability.
In 2021 CfBH, in collaboration with internal
and external partners (Viegand Maagøe) con-
ducted an in-depth analysis focusing on the
usage of disposable single-use and RMIs. The
analysis was speciﬁcally conﬁned to "suture
sets," used each time a patient requires sutures.
The calculations from this analysis demon-
strated CO2-eq savings of over 90% per use
when utilizing multi-use instruments. Additional
calculations were carried out on sterilization
packaging, revealing an 80% reduction in CO2-
eq emissions when opting for multi-use metal
containers instead of single-use plastic wraps.
Based on this eﬀort the stage has been set for
an exploration of the potential for implement-
ing not only reusable suturing sets but all RMIs
and packaging across the region.

2.1.5
Instrument Life Cycle
The life cycle depicted on ﬁg. 2.3 illustrates
the product life of both DMIs and RMI in ﬁve
phases. Both products follow the same take-
make-use-dispose linear ﬂow. The instruments
are produced from surgical steel, where raw
materials are transformed into ﬁnished instru-
ments. They are then sterilized, packaged, and
transported to Denmark, where they are dis-
tributed to the hospital, where they are used by
healthcare professionals. After the use phase,
both types of instruments become, what in CE
terms can be thought of as a type of func-
tional obsolescence, "hygienic obsolescence",
where the product is rendered obsolete and
no longer meets the required hygienic stan-
dards for use (Kane, Bakker, and Balkenende
2018).
However, this is where the inherent
diﬀerence between the two types of instru-
ments lies.
Following use, disposable instru-
ments are required to be incinerated following
national standards for handling clinical waste
(Miljøstyrelsen 1998), which allows for no ma-
terial recovery.
Reusable instruments, on the
other hand, enter a re-manufacturing loop,
where the instruments are recovered through
reprocessing. This allows for multiple uses of the
same instrument.

Immediately after
use reusable instruments must be pre-cleaned,
sometimes even during surgery. Gross debris,
blood, and other visible contaminants are re-
moved, using either a damp cloth or water to
ﬂush. The purpose of this prompt cleaning is to
prevent organic materials from adhering to the
instruments and salts from corroding metal sur-
faces, which could complicate reprocessing.
Eﬀective pre-cleaning is important, as inade-
quate or delayed pre-cleaning can lead to
corrosion, signiﬁcantly reducing the lifespan of
instruments.
After pre-cleaning, instruments
need to undergo washing, typically utilizing an
instrument washer, to eliminate any residual
debris. Ideally, this washing process should oc-
cur within 6 hours of use, setting a timeframe for
the instruments’ return to the CSU.
To address this time constraint, the hospital
department may choose to wash instruments
before returning them.
Once washed, these
instruments can be stored indeﬁnitely before
eventually being returned to the CSU, where
they are, however, required to undergo wash-
ing again. As such, introducing washing at the
hospital department adds an additional task
for the staﬀthere, and introduces an extra wash
cycle, which negatively impacts the potential
environmental beneﬁts of reusable instruments.
The additional task of pre-cleaning and wash-
ing instruments is a mutual concern amongst
healthcare personnel at all hospitals.

Reprocessing of medical instru-
ments, also known as sterilization is a core ac-
tivity within Danish Hospitals. The goal of this
process is to eliminate the risk of transmitting
infections by cleaning, disinfecting, and steril-
izing medical instruments before use. In Dan-
ish hospitals, reprocessing is done according to
the National Infection Control Guidelines (Na-
tionale Infektionshygiejniske Retningslinjer) on
reusable medical equipment (Statens Serum
Institut 2019) and is covered in six steps (see
ﬁg. 2.8).
Reprocessing is elaborate and requires many
manual processes.
Upon arrival in the CSU,
the case carts are disinfected using a cart
washer, and the instruments are transferred to
pre-treatment tables on the unclean side of the
CSU. At the pre-treatment table, a sterilization
technician inspects the individual instruments
and ensures that instrument sets are kept to-
gether, and all content accounted for. Instru-
ments are then scanned, either individually or
on a set basis before they are cleaned and
transferred to washing trays designed for the in-
strument washer. The washer, equipped with
openings to both the unclean and clean sides
of the CSU, facilitates the transition of instru-
ments.
After washing, the instruments reach
the packing tables on the clean side of the
CSU. Here, a ﬁnal inspection takes place be-
fore the packaging step. Instrument sets are
packaged together using metal trays or steril-
ization wrapping, while individual instruments,
known as "løstpak," are packaged using dispos-
able peel bags.
The packaged instruments then undergo high-

2.1.7
Socio-technical
Chal-
lenges
As depicted in this chapter, the transition to
reusable instruments involves multiple stake-
holders, and the challenges encountered span
various individual departments and are often
interconnected.
A future solution concept
should be able to address these identiﬁed is-
sues, while also recognizing that not all chal-
lenges necessarily are present in the same hos-
pital or department. The challenge therefore
lies in identifying and addressing relevant issues
on an individual basis. This necessitates a holis-
tic overview of the transition to implement a so-
lution utilizing reusable instruments.
The main barriers to the transition are estab-
lishing service agreements between depart-
ments, and determining a price, that is true to
the actual cost of reprocessing and economi-
cally viable for the hospital departments. Loss
of instruments is a risk to the viability of the tran-
sition because losing RMIs can quickly become
very expensive and it threatens and security of
supply when the instrument stock is reduced.
Additionally, the initial investment required to
initiate the transition poses a risk of projects not
being initiated, because hospital departments
may not have the funds readily available, and
there is uncertainty regarding the payback pe-
riod.

2.2
Environmental and
Economic
Assess-
ment
The economic and environmental viability of a
solution utilizing RMI depends on two factors.
Firstly, the use of RMI has to be a more sus-
tainable solution. In this context, more sustain-
able means that the overall solution has a lower
carbon footprint throughout its life cycle, com-
pared to the use of disposables. Secondly, the
solution has to be comparable to the price of
utilizing DMI to remain economically viable.
On behalf of CfBH, Viegand Maagøe has de-
veloped a comprehensive CO2 /TCO analysis
tool called "Klimaberegneren" (Andersen and
Maagøe 2021) to assess the impacts of tran-
sitioning to a reusable solution. The develop-
ment of Klimaberegneren revolved around su-
turing kits, which are the most common instru-
ment kits found within the hospitals in Region
Midtjylland.
Based on these calculations from Klimabereg-
neren, CfBH has concluded that transitioning
to reusable suturing kits holds the potential
CO2-eq reductions of upwards of 90%, and at
a price comparable to the cost of using DMI.
Recognizing the signiﬁcance of this conclu-
sion, the objective of this section is to investi-
gate whether the tool developed by Viegand
Maagøe is representative of not only suturing
kits but also the transition to RMIs in general.
This was done through a literature review of
studies comparing RMIs and DMIs. The objec-
tive of this review was to mitigate potential bi-
ases inherent in the internal study by Viegand
Maagøe, while also highlighting key concerns
from other studies. The purpose was also to illus-
trate the methodology behind conducting Life
Cycle Assessments LCA and Life Cycle Costing
LCC.
Building upon the insights from the evaluation
of Klimaberegneren and the conducted liter-
ature review, an LCA model was developed.
The purpose of this model was to evaluate
Klimaberegneren and implement ﬁndings from
our study. To guide the research of this section,
the following research questions were used:
1. In which cases is the use of RMIs more sus-
tainable than disposable ones, and how
can this be assessed?
2. In which cases is the use of RMIs a more
cost-eﬃcient solution compared to dis-
posable ones, and how can this be as-
sessed?

2.2.2.2
Life Cycle Inventory
According to (Hauschild, Rosenbaum, and
Olsen 2018) the life cycle inventory analysis is
the collection and compilation of all data, that
is used to generate the life cycle impact as-
sessment. The environmental data are gener-
ated mainly through ecoinvents database and
"bæredygtig bundlinje", a project facilitated by
Gate21 (Gate 21 2022).
The economic assessment is seen from the per-
spective of the hospital. All expenses concern-
ing procurement, sterilization, handling, and
disposal are inserted by the user in the model
and not generated from an LCC database. Ex-
penses related to the depreciation of equip-
ment in the hospital departments and the ster-
ilization facility are also not included. Potential
social- and environmental impacts in monetary
terms are not included in the assessment.

ﬁlled out by CfBH in close cooperation with the

ﬁrst-hand data. The case is suturing kits.

Method

2.2.3.2
Life Cycle Inventory Analysis
The system boundaries of Klimaberegneren
can be seen on (ﬁg. 2.9). This assessment em-
ploys a cradle-to-grave perspective, encom-
passing the entire life cycle. However, as high-
lighted by Viegand Maagøe, the primary fo-
cus lies within the use phase. The literature re-
view shed light on how other practitioners have
approached their LCAs concerning both DMI
and RMI. Utilizing this knowledge, an analysis
was conducted to inspect the inventory data
to identify potential gaps within it.
The gaps
are categorized into 3 areas: 1) Inventory data
gaps, which are gaps within the life cycle in-
ventory. 2) computation errors, which cover er-
rors in Excel resulting in wrong results, 3) Data
input errors, which cover inputs from the user,
that are faulty.
Inventory Data Gaps
The use of electricity
and other resources used to run the washing
machines and autoclaves in the sterilization
process is included. However, the carbon foot-
print related to the production, shipment, and
waste handling, of the equipment is not in-
cluded in the LCA.
The washing process constitutes 6.1% of the
total carbon footprint of the reusable instru-
ment life cycle,
according to Klimabereg-
neren, which is substantial.
A Master thesis
from the University of Halmstad (David, Anan-
thakrishnan, and Madhusoodhanan n.d.) con-
ducted a full LCA on a professional Steam Au-
toclave, similar to the one used in the ster-
ile centrals in Region Midtjylland.
The Study
ﬁnds that the use phase is the dominant phase
constituting 84% of the total carbon footprint.
Adapting this assumption to washing and ster-
ilization equipment, the absence corresponds
to 1% of the total carbon footprint for RMI, that
is not unaccounted for. This is assessed to be
insigniﬁcant.
The life cycle assessment does include the pro-
duction of steel and the manufacturing of the
instruments, but in terms of packaging and in-
strument accessories, only the material pro-
duction is included - not the manufacturing.
The carbon footprint of manufacturing the in-
struments constitutes 1.3% of the total carbon
footprint associated with instruments. Adapt-
ing this assumption to packaging and ac-
cessories manufacturing, the absence corre-
sponds to 0.04% of the total carbon footprint for
RMI, and is therefore assessed bo insigniﬁcant.
The protective equipment in the sterile central
refers to all the clothing, protective aprons, pro-
tective gloves and hairnet that is used in both
the contaminated and sterile area. This is not
included in Klimaberegneren. The biggest con-

Computation Errors
There are 3 types of pack-
aging used when shipping instruments.
The
weight and material can be typed by the
user in Klimaberegneren. However, the model
calculates the carbon footprint by multiply-
ing the wrong values in the model, both for
the RMI and DMI. By correcting this mistake
the master pack and transportation packag-
ing for DMI increases by 587.5% and increases
the overall carbon footprint by 1.67%, and for
RMI the carbon footprint for the master pack
and transportation packaging is reduced by
86.3% which decreases the overall carbon foot-
print for RMI with 2.2%.
Peel bags is a type of packaging, that is used to
wrap instruments for sterilization. They both ap-
pear as transportation packaging, as all instru-
ments are transported in peel bags, and they
are also used to wrap instruments before steril-
ization in every use cycle.
In this case on suturing kits, the number of trans-
portation peel bags is derived from the num-
ber of use cycles and not the number of instru-
ments. As the instrument is used 50 times, the
category "packaging (Sterilization packaging/
peel bag)" in ﬁg. 2.12 is wrong by a factor of 50.
The peel bag that is used per use cycle, is mod-
eled under the category "Repackaging". Cor-
recting this mistake the total carbon footprint
for reusable instruments is reduced by 17.4%.
Data Input Errors
The expected lifespan of a
suturing kit is modeled to be 50 use cycles.
This is the lifespan the instrument manufacturer
can vouch for.
However, according to Karin
Nedergaard Jacobsen, the number of use cy-
cles before disposal is at least 200 times, and
probably more. When changing the lifespan
from 50 use cycles to 200 use cycles the over-
all carbon footprint of reusable suturing kits de-
creases by 14%. It seems that the actual life-
time has not been communicated properly by
the CSU, or maybe the conservative lifetime
has been chosen on purpose.

Klimaberegneren
The purpose of the economic aspect of
Klimaberegneren is to calculate and compare
the economic consequences of transitioning
from disposable to reusable metal instruments.
This is achieved by calculating the TCO for both
types of instrument and is based on data in-
serted by the user.
Here, the economic calculations are evalu-
ated, based on the insights found in the litera-
ture review and other insights gained through-
out this project.
2.2.4.1
Economic Gaps
In Klimaberegneren, expenses related to the
reprocessing of instruments are included. This
includes the cost of energy, water and de-
tergent and is equivalent to 2.07% of the to-
tal cost. Additionally, data for handling instru-
ments during the reprocessing process are also
included as time and salary data, inserted by
the user. However, the decontamination of the
case carts has been excluded, which is a po-
tential source of error in the economic evalua-
tion.
Furthermore, Klimaberegneren does not ac-
count
for
the
depreciation
of
sterilization
equipment, which in a study on reusable scis-
sors was found to account for 18% of total costs
(Ibbotson et al. 2013), and therefore should also
be included. The depreciation of other equip-
ment necessary to transport instruments or fa-
cilities in the departments using the instruments
is included by Klimaberegneren.

2.2.4.2
Cost of Handling Instruments
Through the diﬀerent phases of the use cy-
cle, the reusable instruments are handled by
hospital personnel.
Time spent on tasks such
as purchasing instruments, sorting instruments
for collection, cleaning, washing, sterilization,
packaging, and waste handling are handled
in Klimaberegneren by prompting the user to
insert the time spent on the diﬀerent tasks, and
the hourly wage by the person doing the task.
According to Klimaberegneren, the salary con-
stitutes 76. 2% of the total expenses related to
reusable suturing kits and thereby constitutes
the biggest entry.
Because salary is such a large post, the results
of Klimaberegneren are very sensitive to the
user data inserted regarding this entry, and in-
accuracy in measuring the exact time spent on
tasks can mean the diﬀerence between a so-
lution being viable or not.
For example, the time allocated to sorting and
collecting instruments for the suturing kit case
is 0.75 min per instrument. If the time allocated
were to increase by 50%, equivalent to 30 sec-
onds - less time that you typically spend waiting
for an elevator - the TCO will of reusable instru-
ments increase by 6%.
A conﬁdential report made on behalf of Re-
gion Hovedstaden concluded that reusable in-
struments are not an economically viable solu-
tion due to the expenses related to handling in-
struments. This highlights the importance of ac-
curate time tracking in assessing the economic
viability of reusable instruments.
2.2.5
Environmental
and
Eco-
nomic Implications
As indicated by studying literature, by assess-
ing Klimaberegneren, and through the devel-
opment of our environmental model and de-
velopment of Klimaberegneren 2.0, the con-
clusion is, that transitioning to using reusable
metal instruments results in a lower carbon foot-
print, and can potentially save cost. In the fol-
lowing, the most important aspects to consider
in in further development of a support is pre-
sented.
2.2.5.1
Environmental Aspects
The use phase of reusable instruments is the
highest contributor to the environmental foot-
print. This phase includes the electricity con-
sumed during reprocessing of instruments and
decontamination case carts and is therefore
dependent on the utilization of sterilization ma-
chinery and electricity mix.
Also signiﬁcant to the footprint of the use
phase, is the use of disposable peel bags, con-
sumed for each use cycle.
Lastly, the lifespan of reusable instruments also
plays a role.
Even though the production of
reusable instruments is insigniﬁcant compared
to the use phase, this can change roles, if the
reusable instruments are lost or wear out before
reaching the expected number of use cycles.
2.2.5.2
Economic Aspects
Just as in the environmental analysis, the use
phase is a signiﬁcant factor in the TCO for
reusable instruments. The majority of expenses
stem from activities such as handling, washing,

2.3.1.1
Where to Make Changes
The use cycle used to analyze the challenges
in section 2.1, resulted in an overview of the
challenges that spans from hospital depart-
ments to speciﬁc processes, all related to the
transition to reusable instruments in a hospital.
Many challenges are interrelated and should
be taken into account when developing a fu-
ture solution concept. It is essential to acknowl-
edge, that not all challenges simultaneously
exist in each hospital or department. The anal-
ysis underscores that while these challenges
can be categorized under the archetype hos-
pital, distinct problems related to each hospi-
tal and department signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the
feasibility and desirability of potential solutions.
Therefore, the context is important to consider
for each instance.
Additionally, the analysis revealed that not all
challenges exist within the loop of the use cy-
cle of reusable instruments. Before implemen-
tation, challenges were identiﬁed as a lack of
structure and overview of the transition and dif-
ﬁculties in coordinating eﬀorts between hospi-
tal units. The support should therefore be able
to provide structured guidance on implement-
ing the solution.
In section 2.2, the literature review, the eval-
uation
of
Klimaberegneren,
and
the
de-
velopment of Klimaberegneren 2.0 revealed
hotspots concerning the use of DMIs and RMIs.
It was concluded that transitioning from dispos-
able metal instruments to a reusable alterna-
tive in hospitals in Region Midtjylland has a sig-
niﬁcant environmental potential. However, the
magnitude of the potential carbon savings de-
pends on the individual context, and it is there-
fore necessary to make an individual environ-
mental assessment to get individual results. The
results are important, because they motivate
healthcare personnel to engage with sustain-
ability initiatives.
Furthermore, the economic impact of switch-
ing to a reusable solution was assessed, and
hotspots were identiﬁed.
Again, the insights
were context-dependent but indicated that a
reusable solution has the potential to compete
with disposable instruments in the long run in
terms of cost.

ered above, a detailed requirements speciﬁ-

ﬁg. C.3.
The requirements speciﬁcation con-

needs and speciﬁc criteria for a potential solu-

vide clarity on what is expected of the imple-
mented solution concept.

2.3.2
Solution Space
In the above, the requirements for a future im-
plemented solution are outlined. Now, the ob-
jective is to strategically position the support
mechanism where it will generate the most
immediate and sustained value for the client,
CfBH. CfBH who is also working on the transi-
tion, has a much longer project timeline than
the span of this thesis project. The goal is there-
fore to provide foundational work, that can in-
spire and be built upon in future work by CfBH.
Because CfBH is working parallel with this
project on the development of an implemen-
tation plan for the transition, there is an inher-
ent risk that our support will be absorbed or
overshadowed, by the future work of CfBH. It
is therefore essential, that the development of
the support is coordinated and in collaboration
with CfBH, to ensure its relevance.
Through discussions with CfBH’s project man-
ager, Mathias Sehested Hansen, it was ac-
knowledged that a valuable form of support
would be a tool to structure and facilitate the
transition, drawing insights from this project,
which was also later conﬁrmed at a steering
group meeting. This led to the idea of a work-
book aimed at facilitating the transition from
the perspective of hospital departments, nar-
rowing down the solution space to focus on
their needs and interactions with other units
relevant to support the use of reusable instru-
ments within the hospital.
As such, the solution would revolve around the
hospital department, but have a broader area
of eﬀect, as the transition process involves a
multitude of actors, as can be seen in ﬁg. 2.16.
Focusing the solution space around the hospi-
tal department aligns well, with the ﬁndings of
the analysis. As the primary decision-makers re-
garding instrument selection and use, the hos-
pital departments are central to the transition.
Additionally, they possess the user perspective
and insights into the day-to-day utilization of in-
struments and are thereby the most informed
about the challenges and requirements of the
transition. The hospital departments also carry
the ﬁnancial burden in terms of initial invest-
ment and operation costs. At the same time,
they are dependent on the selected solution
for instrumentation to continue daily opera-
tions, and as such the inherent risk associated
with changing from one system to another lies
with the hospital department.
2.3.3
A Workbook to Facilitate
the Transition
Creating a workbook is not about creating a
speciﬁc solution for using RMIs, but rather a way
of sharing the insights gathered through this
project, to enable hospitals in Region Midtjyl-
land to build upon our knowledge when devel-
oping solutions on their own. The task is there-
fore to incorporate the insights from our analy-
sis, in a format that hospital departments can
use. The workbook should enable the hospi-
tal departments to assess the economic, envi-
ronmental, and work-practice-related aspects,
before implementation as well as develop a so-
lution concept of their own, to accommodate
their own speciﬁc needs.
To do this, the workbook should provide a struc-
tured approach for the hospital departments
guiding through the transition process from us-
ing DMIs to using RMIs by oﬀering a step-by-

A critical element in the revised workbook was

The problem space is explored through a prob-
lem brainstorm. By introducing the use context
through the instrument use cycle presented in
section 2.1, it is the purpose to both inspire and
constrain the problem generation process.
Discuss Most Important Problems
The second
activity relates to narrowing down the problem

to reduce the carbon footprint by 30% across
the whole region in 2030 compared to 2018.
This target equates to a total reduction of ap-
proximately 173.400 tons of CO2-eq. The sav-
ings from utilizing the workbook to the fullest will
bring Region Midtjylland 0.028% closer to their
2030 emission goal. The direct potential car-
bon footprint savings by utilizing this workbook
in all departments in Region Midtjylland is mod-
est, but that comes down to the potential sav-
ings of this transition that makes the scope of
this project.
However, the notion of developing a frame-
work to implement circular economy initia-
tives holds a potential for the development
of other workbooks which holds the potential
for broader, systemic changes that extend be-
yond the immediate scope of this project.

4.2. Conclusion
Chapter 4. Descriptive Study II
4.2
Conclusion
This thesis took its starting point in analyzing
the challenges and assessing the environmen-
tal and economic impacts of transitioning from
disposable to reusable metal instruments in Re-
gion Midtjylland with the intention of develop-
ing a framework to implement circular econ-
omy principles in Danish Hospitals.
To assess the challenges associated with tran-
sitioning, the use of instruments was compre-
hensively analyzed at three diﬀerent hospitals.
The analysis revealed, that challenges spun
across individual departments and were of-
ten interconnected and context-speciﬁc. No-
table issues were the extra tasks attributed
to personnel within hospital departments, re-
garding cleaning and handling reusable instru-
ments and diﬃculties in establishing function-
ing collaborations between hospital depart-
ments and the central sterilization unit.
The environmental analysis indicated,
that
transitioning
to
reusable
metal
instruments
holds great potential for reducing the carbon
footprint associated with instrument use by up-
wards of 80%. Because reductions in carbon
emissions were found to be almost assured in
Danish hospitals, the economic aspects be-
come the determining factor of whether the
transition is viable or not.
The economic im-
pacts of the transition can sway in favor of both
disposable and reusable and are very sensitive
to the labor involved when using reusable in-
struments.
The core challenge emerged as the need for a
comprehensive overview of the transition, en-
compassing workforce implications, and en-
vironmental and economic aspects from the
perspective of hospital departments.
To address this issue, a workbook was de-
veloped.
The workbook suggests a process
and methods enabling hospital departments
to map their consumption of disposable in-
struments and develop an alternative solu-
tion based on reusable instruments through
problem-solving. It oﬀers a way of quantifying
the environmental and economic impacts of
the reusable solution concept, while also sug-
gesting heuristics on implementation and best
environmental and economic practices.
In conclusion, this thesis aims to bring circu-
lar practices into Danish Hospitals, by replac-
ing disposable instruments with reusable alter-
natives with regard to work practice, and envi-
ronmental and economic aspects. In doing so,
the work in the thesis project paves the way for
more environmentally sustainable healthcare
practices in Region Midtjylland.

4.3.1
A Workbook Catalogue for
other Products
Within Danish hospitals, many other disposable

4.3.2
Development of a Design
Framework
As a suggestion for further work, after the com-
pletion of this thesis, we recommend expand-
ing the scope of the workbook to encompass
a broader range of product categories be-
yond metal instruments. The current workbook
focuses speciﬁcally on the transition from dis-
posable to reusable metal instruments. While
the relative carbon savings related to replacing
metal instruments are signiﬁcant, they are lim-
ited in a broader context. However, the project
could be extended to develop a more generic
framework applicable to diﬀerent product cat-
egories.
This expanded framework would in-
volve identifying relevant disposable products,
proposing appropriate courses of action, and
establishing necessary structures to support the
transition. Such a framework could be useful
not only within the healthcare sector but also in
other industries, including manufacturing com-
panies dealing with packaging and other en-
vironments reliant on disposable products.

Baggrund og motivation
I Danmark står sundhedssektoren for over 6% af nationale CO2 -udledninger. 
Størstedelen af sundhedssektorens udledninger (86% i RegionMidt) stammer 
fra indkøb af medicinsk udstyr og services, herunder engangs metalinstrumen-
ter. Disse instrumenter er fabrikeret i rustfrit stål, og bliver ofte kun brugt en 
enkelt gang, inden de kasseres som klinisk risikoaffald. Brugen af éngangs 
metalinstrumenter har høje miljømæssige omkostninger, hvorfor det er 
relevant at undersøge, hvordan de kan blive erstattet med mere miljøvenlige 
flergangs metalinstrumenter.
Denne Drejebog – Fra éngangs- til flergangsinstrumenter på danske hospitaler er 
resultatet af et specialeprojekt, udført som afgangsprojekt på DTU i Design og 
Innovation i samarbejde med Center for Bæredygtige Hospitaler (CfBH) og er 
et skridt på vejen mod et mere bæredygtigt sundhedsvæsen. 
Formål
Formålet med denne drejebog er at give hospitalsafdelinger et værktøj til at 
lave en helhedsorienteret vurdering af, hvordan et skift fra éngangs- til 
flergangs metalinstrumenter vil påvirke arbejdsgange, afdelingsøkonomien 
samt mulige miljømæssige konsekvenser ved skift til flergangs.
 
Tak til
Tak til især Mathias Sehested Hansen og Karin Nedergaard Jacobsen, og de 
mange andre fra medarbejder fra hospitaler i Region Midtjylland som har 
hjulpet til med fremskaffelse af data, gode råd og vejledning. Uden jeres hjælp 
og vejledning var denne drejebog aldrig blevet til noget.

Der findes ingen definitiv metode til 
at identificere alle éngangs metalin-
strumenter. Derfor bør kortlægningen 
foregå som en iterativ process, hvor 
man tager udgangspunkt i potentiale-

bottom-up og top-down metoden.
2. Bottom-up metode: Undersøg 
forbruget af instrumenter på afdelin-
gen
Bottom-up, går ud på at finde frem til instrumenter 
der anvendes på afdelingen ved at se på, hvilke 
instrumenter findes på lokale lagre, hvilke 
engangsinstrumenter oplever personale at de ofte 
smider ud, og hvad ender med at blive smidt ud 
som klinisk risikoaffald eller metalskrot. Formålet er 
her, at kunne identificere varenumre som herefter 
kan efterprøves i indkøbsdata.
Fremgangsmåde
Der er flere måder at gribe denne opgave an på, 
men fællestrækket er, at det handler om at komme 
ud i felten og se hvad man kan finde for derefter at 
efterprøve ens fund ved at søge i indkøbsdata. 
BHK kan bistå med udførelse af denne opgave.

3. Top-down metode: Søg i afdelingens 
indkøbsdata
Top-down metoden, går ud på at finde frem til 
engangs metalinstrumenter gennem indkøbsdata 
på engangs metalinstrumenter. Udfordringen her 
er, at det ikke nødvendigvis fremgår om et instru-
ment er engangs eller flergangs og om det er lavet

det eksakte indhold af alle instrumentpakker og 
dermed er det også svært at se om et instrument-
sæt indeholder metalinstrumenter, uden at have 
indgående kendskab dertil.

Systemdesign
03
Levering
Første fase omhandler levering af instrumenter på afdelingen.
Overvej: hvornår er der behov for levering på afdelingen. Hvor skal instrumenterne leveres 
til? Afdelingens behov for levering af flergangsinstrumenter påvirker logistik og forsynings 
opgaver, hvor timing og afstande er vigtige elementer.    
Opbevaring
Når instrumenterne er leveret til afdelingen skal instrumenterne opbevares indtil de anven-
des. 
Overvej: hvor stor er den nødvendige lagerbeholdning af instrumenter? Hvordan opbeva-
res instrumenterne på afdelingen -  eksisterer der lagre eller skal de oprettes? Kan instru-
menterne opbevares i instrumentvognen hvori de er blevet leveret?
Brug
Efter opbevaring anvendes flergangsinstrumenterne af sundhedspersonale.
Overvej: Hvordan kommer instrumenter frem til anvendelsesstedet og klargøres? Hvordan 
sikres det at brugeren ikke forveksler flergangsinstrumentet med éngangs? Hvad sker der 
med instrumentet efter brug? Skal der også hentes tilbehør der anvendes i forbindelse med 
brug?
Soignering og vask 
Efter brug skal flergangsinstrumenter straks soigneres for at undgå skade på instrumenter 
ikke at komplikere genbehandling. Det anbefales at instrumenter vaskes højst 6 timer efter 
soignering. Vask kan foregå lokalt på afdelingen eller i sterilcentralen. Vaskes instrumenter, 
kan returnering til sterilcentralen udskydes og dermed kan transportopgaver effektiviseres. 
Vask på afdelingen kommer dog med miljømæssige konsekvenser, idet instrumenter altid 
vaskes når de ankommer til sterilcentralen. Soigneres instrumenterne kun, kræves det at 
instrumenterne bringes tilbage til sterilcentralen hurtigst muligt til vask.
Overvej: skal flergangsinstrumenter blot soigneres eller også bør vaskes på afdelingen 
samt, hvem kan varetage denne opgave. Eksisterer de nødvendige faciliteter til soignering 
og vask på afdelingen eller skal disse etableres?
Returnering 
Efter soignering (og vask) skal instrumenter returneres til sterilcentralen. Denne opgave 
varetages typisk af logistik og forsyning.
Overvej: Hvor mange kørsler er der brug for, for at overholde krav om vask? Er det muligt 
at samle returopgaver med andre logistikopgaver? Hvem forbereder instrumenter til retur 
og hvor skal de samles op?
Genbehandling 
Når efter transport til sterilcentralen, genbehandles instrumenter. Dette trin involverer at 
instrumenterne først forvaskes og inspiceres, maskinvaskes, indpakkes og steriliseres, 
hvorefter de opbevares. 
Overvej: Hvor mange instrumenter skal der i gennemsnit genbehandles om dagen, og hvor 
komplekse er instrumenterne at genbehandle? Skal instrumenterne pakkes i sæt eller som 
løstpak? Hvor opbevares instrumenterne efter genbehandling, inden de leveres til afdelin-
gen?

Design af Flergangsløsning
Givet at systemdesign workshoppen og forhandlinger 
med sterilcentral og logistik og forsyning har været 
udbytterige, er næste skridt at implementere 
flergangsløsningen. For at sikre, at implementering 
også bliver en succes, er der en række forhold som 
bør overvejes således at flergangsløsningen forbliver 
en økonomisk og miljømæssig succeshistorie. Derfor 
har vi afslutningsvis formuleret en række principper 
der bør følges ved implementering. 
Økonomi- og Miljøprincipper
 1. Tag hånd om svind i tide. 
Instrumenter der mistes eller bliver væk, kan være 
den største årsag til at en flergangsløsning bliver 
urentabel og har desuden også store miljømæssige 
konsekvenser.
 2. Vask så effektivt som muligt. 
Vask og sterilisering af instrumenter er langt den 
største miljøpost for flergangsinstrumenter. Sørg for 
at fylde instrumentvasker op, hver gang og undgør 
unødig vask, hvis instrumenter sendes tilbage til 
sterilcentralen samme dag som de bruges. 
 3. Pilot projekt eller fuld implementering 
Bør flergangsløsningenførst teste som et pilotprojekt 
på afdelingen eller skal den implementeres fuldt ud 
med det samme? Et pilotprojekt kan give gode indsig-
ter i, præcis hvordan arbejdsgange bliver påvirket og 
giver samtidig mulighed for at kunne sætte tid på 
ekstra håndterings- og soigneringsopgaver, som 
dermed kan være med til at give et mere præcist 
billede af den fuldt implementerede løsning. Vær dog 
opmærksom på, at delvis implementering som et 
pilotprojekt også kan være med til at skabe forvirring 
på afdelingen, og dermed ikke giver det sande billede 
af, hvordan en fuldbyrdes flergangsløsning vil se ud.
 4. Løbende forbedring
Evaluer løbende flergangsløsningen. Efter implemente-
ring er det en god ide, at høre personale omkring, 
hvordan løsningen har påvirket arbejdsgange, og 
hvordan de oplever den nye flergangsløsning. Dette 
kan lede til ændringer og forbedringer af den originale 
løsning, dermed kan man også undgå problemer på 
sigt.
 5. Hav en kommunikationsstrategi
Overvej, hvordan flergangsløsningen skal kommunike-
res på afdelingen. Der kan være flere grunde til dette.  
Det kan være for at komme i kontakt med det persona-
le der påvirkes af løsningen, som skal informeres om 
nye arbejdsgange. Det kan også være for at vise 
personale, hvordan afdelingen tager hånd om 
miljøhensyn, hvorfor det kan give mening at kommuni-
kerer hvor meget CO2 der spares per år ved den nye 
løsning. Dette kan være med til skabe motivation 
blandt personale for at implementere den nye løsning.
