Abstract
Renewable energy sources are a vital part of reducing the consumption of fossil fuels
and decreasing harmful emissions. Whenever the sky is grey or the weather is cold,
the demand for heat increases. This is why thermal energy storage could increase the
availability of renewable energy, by storing the heat when production exceeds demand,
and release it whenever there is a deficit. By developing a numerical simulation model,
of a gravel/water thermal energy storage system, the process of properly dimensioning
these systems could be facilitated. The model was initially developed as a water model,
and simulations were compared with operational data from a Danish solar district heating
storage, before adapting the model to simulate a storage volume consisting of a mixture
of gravel and water. The model was used to simulate heat storage for a greenhouse in
Thessaloniki, Greece, and the two different storage media was compared with respect to
solar fraction and reduced gas expenses. The same greenhouse was used to simulate
different combinations of solar collector area and storage volume, and a comparative
economic analysis was performed in order to investigate an optimum.
It was concluded that the simulation model was able to simulate the operation for the
greenhouse and that the solar fraction could be increased by implementing thermal storôÙ
age. It was found that the economic estimates were high compared to values found in the
literature and that the optimal configuration of storage volume and solar collector area,
was found to be a large storage for water and a smaller storage for gravel. When looking
at the simple payback time, it was found that a small storage and large collector area
provided the lowest value for either media. In the end, different proposals for improving
the reliability of the simulations, as well as further development of the model were given.

Introduction
In the transition from a fossilôÙbased energy supply to a more sustainable, and increasôÙ
ingly carbonôÙneutral, energy generation, the use of renewable energy sources such as
wind and solar are imperative to decrease the environmental burden of burning fossil fuôÙ
els. One obstacle in substituting fossil fuels with wind and solar is the natural volatility of
availability. To decrease the impact of intermittent energy sources, storage systems need
to be designed and implemented to facilitate the dispatch of clean energy at the time of
consumption as described by Pedersen et al. in 2019 [1].
Many developed countries are focusing on reducing the energy intensity of polluting secôÙ
tors. An example of this is the Netherlands which is the secondôÙlargest exporter of agriôÙ
cultural produce which accounts for ã¥ã˜65 bil. according to the Dutch Government [2].
Because the agricultural and horticultural sector is such an important part of the Dutch
economy, several initiatives reducing energy consumption are promoted by the governôÙ
ment. These initiatives include the e.g. use of solar energy which combined with storage
technology can contribute to a significant decrease in fossil fuel consumption. By 2020
all new greenhouses in the Netherlands must be climateôÙneutral, meaning the use of natôÙ
ural gas should be displaced by clean energy sources [2]. The ambition of carbonôÙneutral
horticultural sectors can be extended to other countries as well.
This study contributes to the transition by investigating the design, modeling, and appliôÙ
cation of solar heat storage to help the horticultural industry consume responsibly and
reduce the carbon footprint of the sector. The type of storage under investigation is the
soôÙcalled gravel/water pit storage which could provide structural benefits as described by
Novo et al. in 2010 [3], and economical benefits compared to other types of large heat
storage technologies, such as concrete tanks as described by Pfeil and Koch in 2000 [4].
An example sketch of the gravel water storage is seen in fig. 1.1.

The storage in fig. 1.1 is shown with three indirect heat exchangers in three different
levels, however, it could also be constructed with direct heat exchange. By filling the pit

1.1.1
Existing Gravel/Water Storage Systems
The aboveôÙmentioned studies have mainly been focused on water pit storage, however,
since the technology closely resembles the GWTES these studies are still relevant. The
following section aims to present some of the realized gravel/water storage systems and
their purpose.
Stuttgart
In 1985 a gravelôÙfilled storage was constructed at the ãInstitut fû¥r Thermodynamik und
Warmetechnikã in Stuttgart. The storage was intended as a pilot project for seasonal storôÙ
age. After 15 years of operation, E. Hahne looked at the operation in 2000 [13]. The plant
was in many ways first of its kind, and the idea was to create fullôÙscale seasonal storôÙ

develop monitoring programs, and analyze the storage water chemically and biologically.
The storage was constructed as a truncated cone filled with coarse pebbles at the top and
bottom, and finer gravel in the middle to provide a flow resistance for evenly distributed
flows. The storage had three different heat exchange possibilities. Two direct heat exôÙ
change, i.e. ring or star configuration, and one heat exchanger solution. After 15 years
of operation, no major problems were reported except for a heat pump that needed to be
changed. The pilot plant served as inspiration for later large storage facilities in Germany.
Chemnitz
One of the storage units that was based on the pilot plant in Stuttgart was the 8000 m3
gravel storage in Chemnitz, Germany [14] commissioned in 1999. Originally the storage
was planned to be a truncated pyramid, however ended up as a rectangular storage supôÙ

soil which needed to be exchanged and the hole was used for the storage volume. Using
direct heat exchange, an additional inôÙ/outlet was arranged in the middle of the storage as
a supplement to the ones at the top and bottom. This ensured better use of the storage
volume in case the top half was already hot. It was reported that the use of traditional
construction processes for a new storage concept proved a success, thus facilitating conôÙ
struction by contractors inexperienced in the construction of seasonal storage. Because
of the structural strength of the gravel storage, the area above could be used for other
infrastructure, and a road/parking space was constructed above the storage.

1.2
Perspectives
The subject of this thesis is the simulation and evaluation of dualôÙmedia storage systems.
In the essence, GWTES is very similar to the water pit storage however with gravel/pebôÙ
bles in the storage volume. This results in a relatively larger storage volume in order to
store the same amount of energy as described by Schmidt et al. in 2003 [16], however,
because of the structural stability, the area occupied by the storage can be utilized by
other infrastructure.
The horticultural sector is energyôÙintensive and the food is often produced in enormous
greenhouses. In order to increase the utilization of solar energy, huge storage systems
need to be implemented and the main benefit of using a GWTES for this is that the greenôÙ
house could benefit from the structural stability and e.g. construct production area above
the storage. This way, crucial space is not taken up by an unusable storage area, while
fossil energy consumption is reduced. This project has been proposed by the Dutch comôÙ
pany SOLHO, whose team has more than 10 years of experience in the power industry.
The need for simulation models facilitates the design and sizing of such systems which
are heavily affected by location and available area for solar collectors. By creating and
using the simulation model to simulate the storage operation with a specific load profile,
it can be possible to see potential benefits e.g. with respect to operational expenditure.

thesis. The simulation model developed does not include a discretized ground model

heat losses from the wall, are assumed to be oneôÙdimensional horizontally. The oneôÙ

each segment and that the conductive heat transfer in the storage medium only transfers
vertically. This assumption was sustained by the fact that monitoring is recommended in
the center of a storage since the temperature is the same everywhere at the same height,
as described by Schmidt et al. in the design aspects of large storage systems [5].

2.1.1
General Objective and System Description
Figure 2.1 shows a sketch of a generic storage system charged by solar collectors and
discharged by a load on the heat exchanger. The purpose of thermal storage is to retain
thermal energy during times of overproduction, and release it when there is a deficit. In
gravel water thermal energy storage (GWTES) the heat is stored in a mixture of gravel
and water buried in a pit below ground level. The heat can be transferred to/from the
storage in different ways. It is possible to exchange heat from the storage both indirectly
as seen on the left side of fig. 2.1 as well as directly as seen on the right side.
Figure 2.1: Generic sketch of a storage system with indirect (left) and direct (right) heat
exchange.
It is possible to construct storage systems with both direct and indirect heat transfer as was
the case with the storage in Stuttgart [13]. During charging of the storage, heat transfer
fluid (HTF) is drawn from the bottom of the storage and heated in the heat exchanger
connected to the solar collector field. The solar collectors absorb energy from the sun and

dissipate it to the HTF. The warmer HTF can then either directly serve the load or charge
the storage by letting the HTF enter the top of the storage. If the absorbed heat is used
directly, or the storage is discharged, the HTF will dissipate heat in the heat exchanger
seen to the right in fig. 2.1. This heat exchanger can be connected to a loop with an
auxiliary heat source that serves the process that needs energy. When the HTF has

2.1.2
Energy Flows and State Points
The main objective of the system was to store energy absorbed from the sun when it is not
possible to dissipate it to the load. External interactions occur through energy absorption
in the solar collectors as well as the energy dissipation in the heat exchanger to the load.
Furthermore, auxiliary energy input was necessary when the storage was completely disôÙ
charged or in other ways were unable to satisfy the complete demand. In order to move
the HTF, a couple of electric pumps were needed. To facilitate the construction of a calôÙ
culation model, the system was divided into state points with a given number as seen in
fig. 2.2 (Larger diagram is found in appendix A.1). The overall operation of the system can
be divided into three subôÙprocesses or operation modes, which are: Charging, storage,
and discharging.
Figure 2.2: State points.
The first state point was identified in the water loop at the rightmost of fig. 2.2, where the
temperature was defined as 70 ô¯C. The energy was delivered to the consumer by a fan
coil unit, control volume 1 (CV1), with a defined temperature drop of 20 K adjusted by
the fan speed. The energy demand was an input to the model, which together with the

the HTF entered the ãdischarge heat exchangerã (CV2) and was heated by the HTF from
the storage. The mass flow rate was controlled using the pump in CV3. If the resulting
temperature in state point 3 was not sufficient, it was heated further by a gas burner in
CV4. The discharge of the storage occurred when the pump CV5 drew HTF from the top
of the storage through state point 5. It was then cooled in CV2, before entering the bottom
of the storage. The charge process was driven by the heat collected in the Solar Field

2.1.3
Capacity and Construction
The capacity of thermal storage systems is highly scalable depending on the purpose.
Very small capacities are known from domestic hot water preparation where small tanks
are installed to quickly deliver hot water when needed. Larger capacities exist in solar disôÙ
trict heating systems where water pits have far greater volumes (>200 000 m3) [5] and are
able to store heat seasonally for district heating purposes. NonôÙpressurized water storage
systems are limited by the boiling point, however, highôÙtemperature storage is achievable
with other technologies such as phaseôÙchange materials, metals, rockôÙbed. The construcôÙ
tion of GWTES and relevant components are described in the following sections.
Storage
The GWTES is often constructed as a partly or fully buried tank or pit which can be shaped
like a truncated pyramid, conical or rectangular. The excavated soil can serve as banks
for the partly buried storage. A schematic of the underground solution can be seen in
fig. 2.3, with the storage in Chemnitz as an example of such a storage.
Figure 2.3: A sketch of an underground storage.
The sides of the storage in Chemnitz were constructed with sheet pile curtains covered
with a protective fleece. A drainage lattice was then installed before thermal insulation
and was then covered with a plastic liner. The pit was filled with ã¥13 600 tonnes of gravel
simultaneously with water. After smoothing the surface, the top layer was constructed
similarly to the wall composition [14].

Many different types of solar collectors exist however these can be divided into three main

Heat exchangers
Heat exchangers exist in a variety of different designs and sizes. The main objective is
to transfer heat from one fluid to another. Heat exchangers can either transfer sensible
heat or serve as evaporators/condensers where eat at least one of the media changes
phase. A common type of heat exchanger is the plate type. The capacity of a plateôÙtype
heat exchanger is highly scalable and easy to disassemble for maintenance. A plateôÙtype
heat exchanger can typically operate in either counterflow or parallel flow. The typical
construction of a plate heat exchanger is seen in fig. 2.6, where the two fluids flow alterôÙ

not soldered together, which could be the case for highôÙpressure applications, they are
easily disassembled for cleaning.

Pumps
The pumps in the system ensure the HTF is transported between the components at
the correct mass flow rate. In the system at hand, two pumps were used to supply the
solar collectors and charge the storage, as well as two pumps that served the load and
discharged the storage. It was assumed that these pumps were equipped with variable
frequency drives that allowed them to adjust the speed as needed. This allowed for more
accurate control of the temperatures after the solar collectors and the heat exchangers.
A common pump design is a centrifugal pump which has a wide variety of applications in
terms of pressure head and mass flow rate. A simple sketch of a typical centrifugal pump
is seen in fig. 2.7. The water enters in the middle of the rotating impeller and thereby

2.2
Physical Phenomena of the System
Some of the physical phenomena that occur in the operation of the storage system are
described in this section. As mentioned, the physical properties change when mixing
gravel and water. Some of these properties are straightforward using simple mixing laws,
however, the thermal conductivity is different. The water and gravel have different thermal
conductivity, and these are mixed using more complex correlations accounting for e.g. the
physical shape of the gravel. These correlations are presented in section 2.3.5. In a body
of water with a temperature gradient, natural convection, also known as BuoyancyôÙdriven
flows, can occur. This phenomenon happens when an upper layer of water is being cooled
by e.g. heat loss to the surroundings and the density increases as a consequence. If the
density is increased enough for the density to be higher than the water below, the water
will start to flow downwards. This is also seen in stratified steel tanks where heat from
the upper layers can conduct downwards through the tank wall to the lower layers. In the
simulation model, this BuoyancyôÙdriven flow was considered using an enhanced thermal
conductivity as described by Dahash et al. in [17]. When the storage volume consists of
a mixture of water and gravel, the gravel provides an extra flow resistance and limits the
natural convection. By restricting the BuoyancyôÙdriven flows, the conditions for thermal
stratification could be improved as described by Bauer et al. [21].
The conditions around the heat exchangers were defined from the pinch temperature
which is the minimum temperature difference between the two fluids. If two pure liquids
exchange heat without phase change, the pinch point temperature would occur in one of
12
Modelling and TechnoôÙEconomic Analysis of DualôÙMedia Storage Units
==PAGE:23==
the ends of the heat exchanger. The lower the pinch point temperature the more effective
utilization of the heat exchange area.
The capacity of a pump has physical limitations. As mentioned in section 2.1.4, the capacôÙ
ity of the pump driving the flow in the solar collector field was limited between 25 % and
200 %, however, the other pumps in the system were assumed to be able to deliver calcuôÙ
lated loads. Besides pumping a mass flow at a given rate, pumps can be used to ensure
adequate pressure in the system. This could prove essential in e.g. large solar collecôÙ
tor fields, where pressure loss should be considered for practical reasons. The pressure
loss in the simulation model was neglected, as the storage was nonôÙpressurized, and the
change in physical properties of the water (and even less the gravel) is negligible.

The numerical performance of the model was evaluated in terms of the number of iteraôÙ

of the time steps were solved within 3 iterations and the resulting maximum residual was

Discussion
The developed model was used for simulations of operational data for the storage in DronôÙ
ninglund. Optimally, longer time periods should have been used, however, due to time
limitations only a few weeks were chosen. The resulting simulations of the model validaôÙ
tion seemed to overall resemble the operational data, however, some obvious differences
were observed. When using the data from week 6, it was seen that the temperatures in
fig. 3.2b both reached significantly higher temperatures, but also that T13 decreased to
levels lower than the below temperatures, resulting in a negative gradient with respect
to height. Gradients like this where the top temperatures decrease to a lower temperaôÙ
ture than the segments below, and vice versa, are due to the storage dynamics and the
corresponding time steps. Ideally, very small time steps could reduce these fluctuations,
however, this would not be feasible in terms of computation time. The choice of the length
of the time step was, as mentioned, based on a tradeôÙoff where the simulations performed
overall as expected, within acceptable computation times.
In order to improve the validation of the simulation model, data for a gravel storage could
be used as a reference, however, this has not been obtained. If comparing the simulations
with the operation of a gravel storage, better validation for assumed parameters such
as convective heat transfer coefficients, conduction, and a mixture of properties could
be obtained. Furthermore, in order to provide a more clear comparison, temperatures
representing the same depth should be used. The simulation model was built to have
equal volumes in each segment, and because of the conical shape, this led to a variable
segment height. The data used for comparison, was equidistant, which could be confusing
to compare.
The economic analysis based on the simulations, resulted in extremely high CAPEX esôÙ
timates, with a factor of ã¥3ôÙ4 compared to the investment costs reported by Kallesû¡e
and VangkildeôÙPedersen in 2019 [8]. In the simulation model, chosen contributions were
estimated by Ochs et al. in 2020 [24], however these may not have been appropriate
estimates for this project. The analysis was performed as a comparison between the
configurations, and the different storage media. In order to achieve a better comparison,
the effect of different storage media should be taken into account when estimating the
CAPEX. It was observed that the economic optimum and the optimum was not the same
configuration for the water storage. For the gravel storage, one would have expected that
the smallest storage volume would have the highest OPEX, as the solar fraction would be
assumed to be lower, however, this actually resulted in the lowest. These discrepancies
could be due to the control of the mass flow rate through the solar collector field, and
thereby the charging of the storage. This mass flow rate was related to the size of the
solar collector area, where it could operate in the range of 25 %ôÙ200 % of the nominal
mass flow rate. Furthermore, if the bottom temperature of the storage was less than 10 K
from the highest permissible temperature the mass flow rate would also be stopped. This
could mean that a relatively large storage was not charged properly, because the pump
capacity only was related to the solar collector area, and not the storage volume. It could
therefore be essential to test other control strategies. The upper limit of the pump capacôÙ
ity was held constant for all scenarios, but it could be relevant to adjust it with e.g. larger
storage volumes. To limit the mass flow rate of a 90 000 m3 storage to the same limit as
a 30 000 m3 storage, might not be a very effective strategy, however, this was done for
the simplicity of simulations given the limited time frame for this thesis. As mentioned, the

number of storage segments was chosen as Nseg = 32 regardless of the configuration
simulated. This produced 127 equations, which converted in 0.8 s ôÙ 1.6 s depending on
the computer. In order to obtain better simulation results, the number of segments should
ideally be increased with the storage volume. Since the model was created with segments
of equal volume, the relative height of each segment increased with the vertical position
of the segment. In the largest volumes, the bottom segments stretched for 6 m, whereas
for the smallest volumes this was only 0.45 m. This means that the few bottom meters
of the larger storage simulations were not properly discretized, which could be why these
were not found to be cheaper than the small storage volumes in terms the gravel storage.
The computational performance was mainly challenged by the tradeôÙoff between accuracy
and computation time. As mentioned in section 3.3, each time step was solved with few
iterations and within few seconds. Depending on the ratio between solar collector area
and volume, it was observed that smaller time steps could be needed in order to avoid
large fluctuations, significantly increasing the total computation time. The iteration proôÙ
cess could be improved by using smarter stop criteria instead of the maximum absolute
residual. Since the guess for the following time step was defined as the previous solution,
one would expect that only a few, if more than one, iterations would be needed to solve
the next time step. It was however observed, that all time steps needed three iterations
except for the first time step, which needed four iterations. Another common criterion is
the relative residual where the relative change in variables value is below a defined threshôÙ
old. The absolute convergence criteria could be a better choice when the root is relatively
large, whereas the relative convergence criteria could perform well when the root is small
[30]. A way of ensuring more accurate solutions could be to combine the convergence
criteria so more than one criteria should be met before continuing. Since the only criteria
used in the simulation model was the max absolute residual below a threshold, this could
end the iterations prematurely, before obtaining the best solution.
Since it has been observed that the simulations were sensitive to the length of the time
steps, a more effective way of solving the change in storage temperature could be imôÙ
plemented. The forward Euler is a 1stôÙorder method, which assumes a constant gradient
within the time step. If then the length of the time step was chosen inappropriately, this
method tends to approach the solution slower, or more easily overshoot the change in
temperature in the storage. In order to circumvent this problem, other methods exist, as
described by Butcher and Wanner [31]. These include higherôÙorder methods such as the
KuttaôÙmethod or Heunãs method, where derivatives are used both in the beginning and
the end of the time step, and giving these a certain weight when calculating the next evalôÙ
uation point. The 4thôÙorder RungeôÙKutta estimates the derivatives at interpolated time
steps and is a widely used method [32] for solving differential equations in engineering
problems. By using another solving scheme for the determination of change in energy,
the length of the time steps could be improved, and thereby the total computational time.
Other methods for improving the computation time, could be to look at the modules used
for data management in the Python script. The script uses the widely known Pandas modôÙ
ule to manage data frames containing the information about variables and the obtained
solutions [33]. An alternative to Pandas, could be the Vaex module [34]. This module
also provides data frames, reading and writing .csvôÙfiles, but at an increased speed as deôÙ
scribed by A. Anis in [35]. The reason for the better performance by Vaex is due to the fact
that Pandas works singleôÙthreaded on a core, whereas Vaex is able to multiôÙthread and use
less memory. The reason Vaex was not implemented in the simulation model, was that
the module was unknown to the author at the time of modeling, and that Pandas is very
common. Therefore, it could be advantageous to try other data management modules.

Conclusion
After developing a simulation model and performing simulations of both a water and a
gravel/water thermal energy storage, it is concluded that it is possible to implement a solarôÙ
powered gravel/water storage to use in the horticultural industry. The water model was
compared to operational data of a water storage and overall showed similar tendencies,
however, some differences were observed that could indicate numerical errors. Two anôÙ
nual simulations with similar collector areas (35 000 m2) and storage geometry (59 287 m3)
were performed, using both water and gravel/water as storage media. These system obôÙ
tained solar fractions of 37.74 % and 27.75 %, respectively compared to 16.07 % without
storage. It was found that compared to covering the demand solely with a gas burner, the
water storage reduced the costs of gas by 77.66 kã˜ and the gravel storage by 57.08 kã˜
in the simulated year. The thermodynamic mechanisms included in the numerical simôÙ
ulation model included conduction of heat between the discretized layers, heat losses
through the storage envelope, and in the water model, an enhanced conductivity to acôÙ
count for BuoyancyôÙdriven flows. In order to simulate the storage dynamics, during the
charge and discharge of the storage, the simulation model included differential equations,
which were solved by a forwardôÙEuler method, in order to estimate the temperature from
an initial value. The magnitude of the time step proved an important parameter in order
to converge but was chosen as a tradeôÙoff between accuracy and computation time.
A comparative economic analysis was performed using different combinations of solar colôÙ
lector area and storage volumes, and it was found that the estimate of CAPEX resulted in
values 3ôÙ4 times larger than reported in the literature, however, comparisons between the
configurations were performed. The analysis of the OPEX yielded two different configuôÙ
rations as the minimum OPEX for water and gravel respectively. For the water storage,
the configuration 90 000 m3 ôÙ 35 000 m2 was observed the lowest, while unexpectedly, the
30 000 m3 ôÙ 35 000 m2 was for the gravel. It is expected that these results could be imôÙ
proved if the number of storage segments was determined as a function of the storage
volume. The simple payback time was estimated based on the CAPEX, and the configôÙ
urations with the shortest payback time were with a storage volume of 30 000 m3 and a
solar collector area of 35 000 m2. In general, the water storage proved cheaper compared
to a gravel/storage of equal size, indicating that the gravel storage might not be chosen
for economical reasons, however other benefits such as the structural properties could
be involved in the design considerations.

Further Work
For further development of the simulation model, the focus should be on increased robustôÙ
ness, flexibility, and speed. As mentioned in chapter 4, higherôÙorder methods of solving
the temperatures in the storage could be implemented. By implementing a higherôÙorder
method, the solution could be more robust as it does not rely on a single gradient. Even
though a higherôÙorder method would increase the computation time itself, it could provide
robustness as well as potentially contribute to a faster computation time because it will
approach the solution faster than a lower order method with the same size time step [22],
and possibly provide a better guessôÙvalue for the following time step reducing the number
of iterations before convergence.
In terms of flexibility, it could be beneficial to implement more diffusers in the storage
volume, e.g. and in/outlet in the middle of the storage. Besides being more effective in
maintaining the stratification, this could potentially also improve the numerical procedure,
as half the storage is ãnot operationalã when the middle diffuser is in use. In terms of
computational accuracy, a discretized ground model could be a potential extension of the
simulation model. The constructed model uses a lumped approach regarding heat losses
to the environment, however, models like the one used by Dahash et al. [17], have a
coupled discretized ground model in order to evaluate temperature distributions and get
a better representation of the heat transfer to the ground. This could most likely increase
computation time, as several more equations would be solved. Another important point
for further development of the simulation model is better validation and calibration of the
resulting simulations. Since the objective was to simulate a dualôÙmedia storage, a valiôÙ
dation with such a storage could be beneficial. This could provide better information on
assumed parameters such as the convective heat transfer coefficients, the mixture of
properties, number of storage segments, etc.
In order to further investigate the influence of the design parameters, simulations with
varying aspect ratios, wall slopes, insulation thickness, and control strategies should be
performed. These were assumed to be similar in all simulated scenarios. As mentioned in
chapter 4, it could also be essential to test other control strategies, especially with respect
to how the charge of the storage should be limited. As mentioned in chapter 4, the number
of storage segments could beneficially be determined from the storage volume, in order
to provide sufficient discretization of the bottom layers.
One of the main challenges with the model has been to obtain reasonable results within
reasonable computation times. In terms of speed improvement, a few examples of posôÙ
sible solutions were presented in chapter 4. A potential promising approach could be to
replace the Pandas module with the Vaex which allowed for multicore calculations, howôÙ
ever, in order to get the best performance optimization, a more detailed code profiling
identifying bottlenecks should be performed.
