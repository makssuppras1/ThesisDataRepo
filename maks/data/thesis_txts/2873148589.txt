Abstract
A well­designed public transit system should balance accessibility with efficiency, which
can be achieved through optimizing the number of stations on a track. A 29­station Light
Rail track (Letbane) is set to open in Copenhagen in the fall of 2025, begging the ques­
tion: does having all 29 stations strike this balance? This thesis centers the social welfare
changes of removing stations from the Light Rail in order to determine optimal number of
stations on the track. Fewer stops result in improved travel times at the cost of accessibil­
ity. The welfare analysis integrates demand modeling using a multinomial mode choice
model calibrated on Danish National Travel Survey data. This simulates travel behavior
response to alternative station arrangements. The social welfare functions developed in
this study emphasize the health related externalities from changes in walking and biking
behaviors, as well as time saving from fewer stops. The results suggest that removing cer­
tain stations from the Copenhagen Light Rail will create societal benefit. Furthermore, the
methodology developed for the study can act as a framework for future transit projects in
Denmark seeking to maximize social welfare through finding the balance between access
and efficiency.

1
Introduction
Public transportation networks are fundamental to an urban transport system. However,
the trade­off between access and travel time has significant implications for both passen­
gers and broader social welfare. These implications can be assessed through a study of
the network’s granularity. If the network is too coarse, it fails to provide enough acces­
sibility, resulting in low demand. Conversely, if it is too fine­grained, it leads to an ex­
pensive system with longer travel times, which can also result in low demand. In central
Copenhagen, the Metro system allows for quick transportation between the city’s neigh­
borhoods. Copenhagen’s suburbs are connected to the city by S­trains and buses. The
city’s bordering suburbs are only directly and frequently connected to one another by bus.
The Copenhagen Light Rail (Hovedstadens Letbane) is a major infrastructure project cur­
rently under construction. From this point forward the Light Rail will be referred to as LR
for brevity. Designed to improve suburban connectivity, the project includes 29 planned
stations and nearly 28 kilometers of track. Importantly, six out of 29 stations are S­train
stations, meaning the S­train lines A, B, C, and H all intersect the LR track. The average
spacing between stations is less than one kilometer. This raises the question of whether
all stations are necessary and if benefits can be generated from reducing travel time and
increasing demand for active modes by removing selected stations. The research aims
to quantify the social welfare changes generated by station removal, offering insights for
policymakers on how to balance health benefits, accessibility, and operational efficiency.

Background
2.1
Public transportation planning principles
An important consideration in public transport planning is the trade­off between accessi­
bility and efficiency. In this context, accessibility is made up of two components: access,
being the ease of use for the commuter in terms of access and egress distance; and ge­
ographical coverage, which is reach of service within a given time [1]. There is usually an
inverse relationship between these two factors, as increased access means adding more
stops, which reduces geographical coverage because of slower travel speed.
Striking a balance between accessibility and efficiency is a key component in the design
of public transportation systems. This balance may be discussed in terms of granularity.
In the context of this thesis, the term refers to the spacing between stations. If stations
are too far apart, few people will use the system as it becomes an unattractive alternative
compared to other modes. If the stations are too close to each other, the system becomes
inefficient because of longer travel times and higher operational expenses. Furthermore,
short distances between stations might cause active mode trips to be substituted for public
transportation trips, which results in a societal health loss.
2.1.1
Urban Light Rail system
For over three decades, there has been a discussion about the need for a more sophis­
ticated public transport line connecting the Copenhagen suburbs directly to one another
without having to enter the city. The public transportation infrastructure that comes clos­
est to this is the ”Ringbanen”, but with urban expansion it is located fairly close to the city
center in comparison to the greater Copenhagen area. A solution to this decade­long dis­
cussion was agreed upon in 2011 when the municipalities, the City of Copenhagen, and
the Ministry of Transport reached a cooperation agreement regarding the development of
the Greater Copenhagen Light Rail (Hovedstadens Letbane) [2].
A LR system has advantages over other conventional rail solutions in Denmark, such
as Metro and S­trains. They do not require tunnels or elevated tracks. Furthermore,
the ability to drive in mixed traffic and follow curving road sections makes it an attractive
option [3]. The LR option allows for connection of multiple municipalities without major
space disruption within them.
Although technology and infrastructure between public transit systems differ, the same
principles must be followed regarding balancing accessibility and efficiency. The direct
relevance of the thesis to the Copenhagen LR, which is currently under construction,
is limited. But this is far from the last large­scale public transit infrastructure project in
Denmark, and discussion about expansions of the LR system has already begun.
2.2
Mode choice modeling
In transportation system analysis, when the aim is to forecast demand, mode choice mod­
els are widely used. The role of mode choice models is to determine the split between
transport modes, which is important for quantifying the effects of potential levels of service
changes, such as travel access, cost, or time [4, p. 28].
Discrete choice models are a common approach to this; they are micro models, meaning
that demand is calculated for the individual based on their probability of choosing a par­
ticular mode from a set of alternatives. For this thesis, a Multinomial Logit (MNL) mode

choice model was used. MNL models belong to the family of discrete choice models.
Each mode has an associated utility expressed in a utility function that takes in different
variables that are used to calculate the perceived utility for alternatives. The individual is
assumed to pick the mode which results in the highest utility. An example of variables in­
clude access, cost, time, and specific information about the individual. The utility functions
also include components that are unobserved in the data being used. Those components
are then referred to and generated as synthetic logit data using Gumbel distribution [4,
p. 91]. The MNL mode choice framework then allows for estimation of demand changes
in response to level of service changes.
The thesis uses an MNL mode choice model to evaluate demand responses to level of
service changes, such as travel time and access distance to the Copenhagen LR sys­
tem. For the model, observed components are derived from TU data, and unobserved
components are estimated using Biogeme.
2.3
Welfare analysis
Welfare analysis is a fundamental tool for assessing the societal benefits and costs of
policy interventions and/or infrastructure changes. In this thesis, welfare changes are
estimated using the rule of half, which is a widely applied approximation for calculating
consumer surplus variations when demand shifts. The rule of half is used when general­
ized cost changes cause demand to change. The main idea of the method is that existing
users receive the full externality of the price change while added users only receive half of
it. This study develops a welfare function using a rule of half approximation, with emphasis
on focusing on mode by mode. That is, separate welfare functions are developed for the
different modes [5], [6]. The welfare functions are developed with a focus on the value
of time (VoT) and health benefits from positive externalities linked to increased activity
levels.
2.3.1
Health benefits
In Rich 2024 [5], a review of a wide range of studies on walking and biking concludes that
these active travel modes yield substantial health gains. For instance, Kelly et al.’s 2014
meta­analysis [7] links greater levels of walking and biking with lower all cause mortality,
though effect estimates vary by study. In the WHO’s Health Economic Assessment Tool
(HEAT), most benefits are captured as averted premature deaths valued via a standard
value of a statistical life [8], [9]. Denmark’s forthcoming transport appraisal framework will
incorporate the Ministry of Transport’s updated health links between physical activity and
reduced risks of cancer, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease [10] [11].
The health benefits used in this work are in accordance with the aforementioned. The
value of walking and biking, from hereafter referred to as VoW and VoB, is derived from
TERESA (the Ministry of Transport’s spreadsheet model for socioeconomic analysis from
2024) [12]. The values for the year 2025 are picked, which are VoW of 9.29 DKK/km
and VoB of 7.11 DKK/km after adjustment of 2.18 DKK/km in negative externality from
the increased accident rate from biking. Note that more than one value for VOW can be
found in the spreadsheet, and the one used for this work is the lowest mentioned.
2.4
Research objective and structure of thesis
The study aims to determine whether the frequency of stations on the Copenhagen Light
Rail is too high by incrementally removing the stations and analyzing the resulting societal
welfare change. A welfare analysis with a focus on health benefits from activity level and
travel time achieves this. The goal is to create a replicable methodology for determining

3.1.2
The Light Rail track
In order to analyze the LR and the effects of removing stations, the system needed to be
well defined. This enables distance calculations between stations along the track. The
distance would then be used to calculate the driving speed for different segments of the
track.
The LR track Shapefile contained information not necessary for the analysis such as
switching mechanisms and service stations.
This presented challenges as additional
linestrings within the Shapefile can cause the algorithm to err.
The Shapefile consists of 48,138 coordinate points that make up 8,270 linestrings. The
structure, when converted from a Shapefile, is a data frame with three columns; ID, latitude
and longitude. Table showing the data frame can be observed in table A.2, the first column
in the table is the index.

Consecutive linestrings
After manually cleaning the data set it was attempted to find a consecutive linestrings
from station 0 to 28. This failed but after some trial and error of removing defects a

one from station 28 to 22. Those consecutive linestrings failed to measure the distance
between stations, but they did contain points that were useful to trim down the data set,
since the two stretches of consecutive linestrings were made up of only 3,291 coordinates
points compared to 48,138 in the original Shapefile. The coordinates points were stored

Calculating distances
The distances between all stations within both segments were calculated using a greedy
nearest neighbor algorithm in listings A.2 to A.3. The algorithm was initialized at the co­
ordinate point with the lowest longitude in line segments B, and always traveled to the
nearest point until all points had been visited. The last point being the dark red support
point shown in fig. 3.7. Then the nearest point in segment A from the support point was
located and the algorithm initialized at that point and all distances for segment A calcu­
lated.
After running the algorithm for both track segments, the results were combined in one data
frame using listing A.4. Since the algorithm was re­initialized for segment A the distance
at the last point in segment B had to be added to all distances in segment A to get the
cumulative distance between all points. The combined data frame then contained 3.291
coordinates points, 29 station coordinates and one support coordinate point. To find the
cumulative distance at each station the rows that matched the station coordinates were

The Green Mobility Model (GMM) is a traffic model developed by the Danish road directory.

useful to filter out relevant trips for the LR simulation. The TU data does not come with any
location information about the GMM zones therefore a Shapefile from the road directory
was made use of.
The Shapefile contained much more than just GMM zones within Denmark, to trim it down
all zones with a zoneid ≤200, 000 were extracted and can be observed in figs. A.3 to A.4,
these include all the GMM zones in the greater Copenhagen area. However, to further trim
it down to extract the relevant zones and therefore the relevant trips some geographical
constraints have to be set to limit the area that the zones can lie within.

4.5
Impact of Light Rail station removal
Benefit of removing a station
When a station is removed it will likely produce benefits for passengers that did not plan
to enter/exit at said station. Removing a station will shorten the time spent on the LR
and therefore decreases the overall travel time. This happens due to the fact that for
each station that is removed a time saving equal to the dwell time at that station is pro­

Station removal simulation
The baseline scenario as shown fig. 5.1 is used as the baseline in the station removal
process. The removal process will affect the level of service. It will increase the ac­
cess/egress walking time for those that are using the removed station in the baseline
scenario and decrease the LR driving time for those that travel with the LR through the
removed station. These changes along with the change in probabilities throughout the
removal process is what is used to calculate the affects on social welfare.
6.1
Social welfare function development
When developing the social welfare functions it must be kept in mind which changes affect
the social welfare at each removal. Social welfare function must be developed for the
public transportation mode using the rule of half, since the level of service changes which
affect both the travel time and access/egress distance. For the active modes (walking,
biking and e­biking) only the changes in total travel distance due to changes in probabilities
are taken into the welfare function. This is because no level of service changes are made
for these modes. Welfare functions are not developed for car and car passenger modes
since only travel time and health benefits derived from level of service changes to the LR
are being analyzed.

6.2.2
Iterative removal algorithm
The process begins with the 22 removable stations shown in table 6.1. The algorithm splits
into an inner loop and an outer loop visualized in fig. 6.2 in blue and red respectively. The
function of the inner loop is to make temporary removals of one station at a time to identify
the one whose individual removal results in the greatest social welfare increase. The
station identified is permanently removed from the LR system by the function of the outer
loop which moves the stations from ”available for removal” to column ”station removal
order” in table 6.1. The inner loop runs again with one less station than before until the
22 stations deemed possible to remove have all been removed by the outer loop and only
7 fixed stations remain in the system. Thus the outer loop runs total of 22 times while
the inner loop calculates social welfare change 253 times total. Given that when a station
is permanently removed it is no longer part of inner loop calculations, when one station
is removed from the LR system the inner loop calculates the new level of service (LoS).
The new level of service is put into the choice model which calculates new mode choice
probabilities. The new probabilities and the new LoS are plugged into the social welfare
functions and compared against the baseline scenario.

Discussion of results
In this chapter, all results are scaled up to the population of 303,000 within a weekday un­
less otherwise stated; this also applies to the demand discussion. The first, second and
third removal of stations will be evaluated, as well as removal of more stations. These
evaluations lend themselves to discussions on optimum station spacing and policy rele­
vance. The discussion of results is concluded by presenting the project limitations.
8.1
Interpretation of results
The results in fig. 7.2 suggest that stations 12 and 2 should be removed as shown in
table 7.1. Netting a societal gain of 44,646 DKK, giving a per capita social welfare gain
of 0.1147 DKK/person. The majority of the welfare gain comes from travel time savings,
while 4,684 DKK are attributed to combined health benefits.
First removal
In the first outer loop, in fig. 7.3, removal of station 12 yields in the highest welfare gain. It is
just one of two stations that can produce welfare gains by their sole removal. Station 12’s
location near the track’s center means a higher potential of commuters passing through
the stop on their way, thus its removal will benefit all those passengers through time saved.
The distance from station 12 to 13 is 961 meters and from 12 to 11 is 1,004 meters, as
can be observed in table 4.3. The average spacing from station 12 to its neighbors is
961m+1004m
2
≈983meters, which is around the average for the LR system.
Despite this similarity meaning station removal could lead to significantly increased access
distance, the LR does not get substituted for car modes; on the contrary, LR trips get
substituted for active mode trips while the car modes lose share to the LR. Furthermore,
in table A.11 the majority of the substituted LR trips go to the walking mode. This indicates
that the LR was too attractive for some shorter distance trips.
Therefore station 12 is favorable for removal, both because of its location, which allows
for many commuters to receive travel time benefits, and the spacing being that narrow.
The removal of station 12 is in this way able to eliminate redundancy in the system without
shifting demand toward car modes.
Second removal
Regarding the removal of stations 12 and 2, in fig. 7.13 the demand for the LR slightly
decreases; however, fig. 7.14 shows that the combined demand for the LR and active
modes slightly increases compared to the baseline scenario. In table A.11, demand shifts
by mode are listed. The active modes gain 49.09 people, while the LR only loses 41.57
passengers, meaning that 7.52 people substitute car trips for LR trips. This further sug­
gests that the spacing between stations was too tight. Since the time savings produced
steal share from car modes, while active modes substitute LR trips.
It may be inferred that the LR system with 29 stations was too attractive for some trips
within biking and walking distance, while it was unable to compete with car modes for
some trips due to the time lost from having to stop at stations 2 and 12.
In fig. 7.4, the removal options from the second run of the outer loop can be observed after
the removal of station 12. Removal of stations 1, 2, 3, or 26 all result in a welfare gain.

that this might be a weak link in the track. These stations are also located near the north
end of the track, meaning that the potential of passengers receiving travel time benefits

Throughout the removal process
Until this point, only the station removals resulting in a positive welfare gain have been
discussed. It is, however, important to look at the negatives as well. After the third station
removal, each station removed creates negative social welfare as shown in fig. 7.1. The
convex decline is explained by value of time loss, impacting the over all social welfare.
After the fifth station removal the magnitude of the social welfare loss multiplies greatly,
resulting in increasingly significant social welfare decline.
8.3
Activity level
The material presented in this section is not scaled up, thus is discussed in terms of the
1450 trips. The average distances traveled for active modes are presented in fig. 7.17.
The average distance for walking, bikes, and e­bikes does not significantly increase through­
out the removal process. The exception is for e­bikes when the 21st station is removed
from the system. However, as shown in figs. 7.10 to 7.12, the demand for all active modes
increased throughout the removal process. The LR trips substituted by active modes have
equivalent average distances to the baseline scenario, suggesting that the LR was replac­
ing generally active trips. This allows for the conclusion that increase in average spacing
positively impacts activity level. What may also be noted is that the access mode applied
in this model was only walking, meaning bikes and e­bikes were not considered. This will
be further discussed in section 8.6.
8.4
Optimum station spacing
Results in table 7.2, which is the removal of stations from the current configuration, sug­
gest that 2 stations should be removed, resulting in 44,646 DKK positive welfare change
per weekday for the population of 303,000 people. The average spacing in the system
after the removal of the two stations is 1,061 meters, which is 76 meters more than in
the baseline scenario. Findings suggest that the average spacing should therefore be
increased to maximize social welfare. By increasing average spacing, redundancy in the
LR system can be eliminated while raising activity levels resulting in societal time savings
and health benefits.
8.5
Policy relevance
The Copenhagen Light Rail is currently under construction and set to start operations this
fall. Therefore, the findings in this thesis have limited impact on the project. However, the
project has great relevance for future public transit infrastructure projects in Denmark. The
replicable methodology developed for the study can act as a foundational framework that
can be improved and built on for future projects. The study provides valuable insight for
transit project planning in Denmark. When aiming to balance accessibility with efficiency,
it is important to consider health and time loss from systems being too fine­grained.
8.6
Limitations and room for improvement
This study has its limitations, the most impactful of which will be covered in this section
with suggestions for improvements in future research presented.

Trips included
One limitation in the research is the trips included in the analysis. Only trips within the
affected zones defined in section 3.3.1 were used in the study. This neglects all trips
coming into or leaving the affected zones. The LR is planned as an important connection
infrastructure to the S­train whose lines are not confined to the affected zones. Future
research should aim to include these trips, as they can greatly increase the trips included
in the removal simulation, providing valuable insight into the balance of accessibility and
efficiency.
Light Rail connection modes
In this research, the LR was made the sole public transportation mode, and walking was
the only connection mode to the LR. Future research should consider including other pub­
lic transportation alternatives as connection modes, but also bicycles. The LR is equipped
and encourages passengers to bring bikes onto the vehicle. Therefore, bikes could be­
come an important access mode to increase the geographical coverage of the LR without
affecting efficiency.
Walking factor
For travel time modeling in the thesis, a general walking factor of 1.3 was applied to the
aerial access distance to calculate a realistic walking access distance. As concluded in
section 7.8 this method was not looked at as a concern for this thesis. However, it was
shown that the walking factor can significantly alter the baseline probabilities. Therefore,
a suggested improvement in future work would be to apply a route choice model to more
accurately calculate access and egress distances.
Investment and operational cost
In this study station CapEx and OpEx were slightly touched up on. The cost analysis was
largely built on assumptions. This could be improved with a more detailed cost structure.
There is another cost analysis that should be explored in future research. It relates to the
spacing increase effect on LR vehicles and service cost. With improved system efficiency,
there might exist a scenario where the LR could be operated with the desired headway of
five and ten minutes, utilizing fewer vehicles. Resulting in reduced CapEx and OpEx.

Greedy nearest neighbor preparation and algorithm
In listing A.2, the code loads two sets of linestring segments (”NTT” and ”TTÞT”), the
light rail station coordinates, and the track geometry from CSV files. It then splits and
recombines each line at latitude 55.6625, which is Glostrup station, to handle the U­turn.
Then the station locations are appended to the segment that they belong to, along with
a “support” point that is later used to bridge between the two segments. Finally runs a
greedy nearest­neighbor tour, starting from the northernmost point on the blue line (from
station 0 to 21) and the westernmost point on the red line (from station 28 to 21). This
is done to assign a sequential Rank to each point by always moving to the closest un­
ranked neighbor. The result is the track split up into two segments at Glostrup station and
stored in separate data frames ”nulltilTT”, storing the segment from stations 0 to 21, and
”TTtilþrjátíu”, storing the segment from stations 21 to 28.
