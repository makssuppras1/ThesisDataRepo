Abstract

Abstract  
In recent years, Augmented Reality technology (AR) increasingly experiences intensive 
growth records on the market. It is seen as one of the essential emerging technologies and is 
identified as one of the building blocks in the field of industry 4.0. AR enlarges human 
awareness by providing additional information to the real environment in the form of an 
interactive and virtual surface projected on the surroundings. Thereby, the surroundings are not 
constituted virtually. Instead, the environment is augmented with virtual objects and 
information. Experts predict AR as an essential technology of industrial companies in the 
future, if AR continues to develop as fast as in the previous years. 
 
This study investigates the potential of AR in an industrial context to assess whether the 
technology can create value for companies or if it is a temporary hype. The outcome of the 
thesis primarily addresses to companies considering the implementation of AR as well as for 
other interested parties that want to gain knowledge of the technology and its usage. Within the 
thesis the benefits the implementation of AR provides, as well as the existing barriers are 
identified. Moreover, it is investigated in which areas of the value chain of an industrial 
company AR has the potential to be embedded. These factors are analysed by also considering 
the influence the companies’ characteristics have on the potential of AR in their organisation.  
 
The knowledge in this thesis is created based on an extensive literature review enlarged by an 
empirical study carried out in the form of expert interviews of previously defined interest 
groups related to the technology. The interviews were conducted with researchers in the field, 
companies developing AR solutions, companies that have implemented AR, and management 
consultants advising within AR.   
 
The outcome of the research discovered that the technology is still not entirely mature, but AR 
provides the potential for companies under specific circumstances. These circumstances are 
affected by three factors: the application area of implementation, the specific use case and the 
suitability of the company for the implementation of AR. The factors are stressed by the 
maturity of the technology as well as the maturity of the market. AR is an emerging technology 
which has not reached its maximal performance in all areas yet. However, this study concludes 
that the technology will become more and more relevant in the future.

1 Introduction 
 Industrial Revolutions 
The world is said to have gone through three industrial revolutions, and now the fourth is taking 
place with the name Industry 4.0. The first three industrial revolutions were ushering a new 
epoch and lead to a strategic and technologic transformation. The first industrial revolution, 
which took place in the 18th century, implemented water- and steam machinery within their 
mechanical production. The main characteristic of the second revolution in the 19th century 
was driven by line- and mass productions using electricity and the division of labour. This new 
development made it possible to mass produce and decrease the cost of production. In the 
1970s, the digital revolution improved the factories with new IT systems, computerization and 
further automatizations and replaced workers with machines. (Brettel et al., 2014) 
The central technology of the fourth industrial revolution, also called “Digital Revolution”, is 
the internet and thereby the digital linkage of systems. (Brettel et al., 2014) The World 
Economic Forum expresses in its article the fourth industrial revolution as “[...] essential to 
breaking out of productivity stagnation.” (Leurent & De Boer, 2019:24) 
 
The figure below summarizes each of the four revolutions, their underlying concept, their 
temporal classification and their degree of complexity. (Brettel et al., 2014) 
 
 
Figure 1: The four industrial revolutions (cf. Fonseca, 2018) 
The focus of the revolution is increasing digitalization of earlier developed analogue systems 
and techniques which is realized by the following basic concepts. The idea of a self-organized 
production is realized by the direct communication and interaction of people, machines,

facilities, logistics and products in real time by their linkage to the internet-of-things (IoT). The 
IoT can be understood as the digital linkage of objects over the internet to realize 
communication and interrelation between them to reach a common goal. Another relevant term 
in the industry 4.0 context is the Internet of Everything. IoE is enlarging the IoT, which only 
focuses on the machine-to-machine communication, to enable the interaction between the 
devices connected to the IoT with data processes and people. (Hermann, Otto and Pentek, 
2016)  
Data is consistently gathered within the whole supply chain and includes all phases of the 
product lifecycle from the idea of the product, its development, production, use, maintenance 
and recycling. This allows decentralized control and ad-hoc process design. (Fonseca, 
2018) Though, the data needs to be used efficiently and be capable of discovering errors 
patterns using algorithms. This makes the data become smart data. Self-learning systems are 
systems which can recognize patterns and connections and can, therefore, draw connections to 
the causes. (Hermann, Otto and Pentek, 2016)  
To be able to allow this communication and interaction, objects inside the value chain need to 
have the ability to generate unique data consistently and to further transfer it in real-time to the 
data infrastructure such as IoT. These intelligent products are called cyber-physical systems 
(CPS). These are systems in which information and software technology (cyber-computational 
space) is interrelating with mechanical and electrical components on the concrete factory floor. 
CPS are smart products, machines production facilities or storage systems which have a unique 
identity due to numerous sensors, actuators as well as a connected network. (Lee, Bagheri and 
Kao, 2015) 
 
 AR in an Industry 4.0 Environment 
Industry 4.0 is an international concept which has many different expressions and forms around 
the world. Besides the terms “Industry 4.0”, the name “Smart Manufacturing” and “Smart 
Factory” are also standard wordings to express the new age of technology in Asia, the US and 
partly Europe. Even though the terminology may vary, the approach with its technologies and 
main ideas remains mostly the same. (Mittal et al., 2018) In this thesis, the term “Industry 4.0” 
is used. 
 
The concept was developed in 2011 by a group of German business, political and academic 
representatives to improve and save the competitiveness of German manufacturers. Before the

concept was introduced, companies were primarily adopting the Lean Management Principles 
into their companies' businesses to deal with complexity and increasing the effectiveness of 
operations. Since the complexity of operations is consistently increasing, the lean approach 
itself cannot meet all its challenges. Today, several promising digital technologies were 
developed to address these challenges and to improve companies’ processes, operations and 
business growth. (Küpper, 2017) 
 
The Boston Consulting Group, one of the leading global consultancies, is defining nine 
technologies as the "[...]Building Blocks of Industry 4.0” (Scalabre, O., 2018) which are 
transforming the business and processes of manufacturing companies. Industry 4.0 is able “[...] 
to gather and analyze data across machines, enabling faster, more flexible, and more efficient 
processes to produce higher-quality goods at reduced costs.” (Scalabre, O., 2018) These nine 
technologies are among other big data analytics, autonomous robots, additive manufacturing, 
the Internet of Things (IoT) and Augmented Reality (AR). The technology AR is associated 
with “Industry 4.0” where it is widely used. Companies are termed as industry 4.0 
manufactories when they are utilizing data networks and information as well as communication 
technologies for their operations, as explained earlier. (Mittal et al., 2019) The innovation 
which AR entails is the computer-aided extension of the perception of reality. It displays digital 
images, including the necessary data into the real world while the user is interacting with it at 
the same time. (Deac, C. et al., 2017)  
 
The development of AR solutions grew rapidly in the past ten years, and steadily new 
application areas were explored. When AR was developed in the 1990s, it was a high-end 
technology which was only available for specific purposes. (Hsu PH., Huang SY., Lin BS., 
2013) Today, AR is realized by using AR glasses or devices such as smartphones and tablets. 
Indeed, the wide distribution and use of smartphones and their increasing graphical processing 
power makes the technology easily accessible and makes it become a commercial technology. 
AR received its widespread attention with the app-based game “Pokémon Go” in 2016 and the 
face filters which can be used in the app “Snapchat”. (Aouf, 2017) But also people in several 
surroundings can be supported by the technology: whether it is the production worker receiving 
instructions over AR glasses while working (Apple, 2019), the visitor in the museum who, by 
the use of AR get the opportunity to see lost or partly destroyed sculptures (Manuri, F. and 
Sanna, A., 2016) or the customer that is virtually arranging a new furniture into his apartment 
to take his purchase decision. (Chaykowski, 2018)

Even well-known US American companies such as Facebook, Apple, Microsoft and Google 
see an enormous potential in the future of AR technology. The Facebook founder Mark 
Zuckerberg values AR as “[...] such an important trend [because] AR is going to help us mix 
the physical and digital in all new ways, and that is going to make our physical lives better.” 
(Aouf, 2017) He invests into a team of 100 engineers to develop an AR solution for the 
Facebook App (Chaykowski, 2018). The technology company Apple invested in the 
development of an AR Software called ARKit, which can lead to AR experiences in 
combination with iOS operating systems and Apple’s high-performance hardware (Apple, 
2019). Microsoft replied to this trend by bringing the hardware HoloLens compatible with 
Microsoft edge on the market (Microsoft, 2019). 
 
The global spending on immersive technology, which includes AR and VR, is predicted to be 
20.4 Billion US$ in 2019 (Torchia and Shirer, 2018) and will grow significantly up to 160 
Billion US$ in 2023 (Torchia and Shirer, 2018). Whereby the commercial sector initiates 
around 65% in 2019 with a tendency to 80% in 2022. The Manufacturing industry represents 
only 1.54 Billion US$ in 2019. (Torchia and Shirer, 2018) Even if the specialists foresee an 
increase of user cases in manufacturing specifically within the performing tasks like assembly, 
maintenance and repair for the upcoming years, industrial companies are still behind in seeing 
the investment in immersive technologies. (Torchia and Shirer, 2018)   
Implementing the technology is still a complicated process and companies do not have the 
knowledge on how their investment in AR can be worth it (Rabah et al. 2018). At the time, 
according to Gartner, an US-American company focusing on market analysis in the field of IT 
technologies, the technology is not fully mature and still requires five to ten years until it is 
fully developed. Therefore, companies hesitate to commit to the technology. (Pettey, 2019) 
 
 Problem Statement 
The global market, with its subtle changes, requires continuous improvement of organizations’ 
operations to stay competitive. Customers demand high-quality and innovative products for 
less money and with an increased speed of delivery. Additionally, new technologies enable a 
short time to market for new products. All these circumstances create new challenges for the 
companies’ businesses. Thus, decision makers are facing an immense sense of urgency.

Technologies as AR promise opportunities to strengthen the strategic positioning of a company 
and to improve operations, increase productivity and efficiency as well as higher flexibility and 
agility inside the company. A more detailed examination shows how vague the knowledge of 
AR is for many companies and that organizations are still hesitating to implement it into their 
business. At the same time, experts forecast a consistent growth in the market of AR 
technologies within the next years. Hardware manufacturers reply to this market trend and 
develop several AR devices such as the HoloLens 2, which was launched this year. 
 
The term “Hype” often stands in relation to innovations and is also used in the context of AR 
because of all its attention on the market. The Cambridge Dictionary defines the wording as 
“Information that makes something seem very important or exciting." (Cambridge Dictionary, 
2019). So, is AR a hyped technology that will pass by within the next decade or is it a 
technology companies can profit from in the long run?  
Based on the above motivation, the research aims for answering the following research 
question: 
 
RQ1: “What is the potential of the hyped industry 4.0 technology Augmented Reality for 
industrial companies?”  
RQ1.1: What can companies benefit from the implementation of AR in comparison to the barriers?  
RQ1.2: Where and how can companies gain from the implementation? 
RQ1.3: Which companies are most suited to implement AR? 
 
Having the success of AR on the commercial market in mind, this thesis focuses on the current 
and future state of the technology concerning internal company operations as well as external. 
The thesis is built on knowledge from the literature and an empirical approach which is based 
on expert interviews from different interest groups, including developers (D), companies (C), 
researchers (R) and management consultants (MC).

 Delimitations of Research 
The following section elaborates on the delimitations of the research, which are influencing the 
knowledge created within this thesis. 
 
Firstly, the thesis is delimited by the population targeted in this research. The authors are based 
in Denmark and Germany, so the interviews took place in Denmark and Germany because of 
time and cost restriction. Furthermore, the study focuses on the observation of four specific 
interest groups and excludes the perspectives of other potential groups. The interest groups 
were selected according to their influence and knowledge on the development of AR and were 
considered as representative by the researchers of this thesis to provide interdisciplinary 
insights of AR. From these interest groups, a limited number of people have participated 
because of the time limitation of the study.  
 
Secondly, the delimitation of this study is related to the scope of the thesis itself. This thesis 
will not explain the AR technology into depth and its problems in a technical context. The 
focus is more set towards exploring the usage of AR in an industrial context and the motivation 
of companies to implement the technology. Hence, the literature review covers the main fields 
related to AR without discussing specific technical solutions into detail. The literature aims to 
provide the reader with the necessary knowledge about AR to better understand the later parts 
of the thesis. The focus is in an industrial context therefore, other fields of applications such as 
the retail sector, service sector and the gaming industry are excluded.

2  Methodology 
The purpose of this section is to present and formulate the choices made in terms of the research 
path. This implies the overall research design, the data collection and analysis methods. The 
type of methodological approach is selected based on the research question and the focus of 
this study. 
 
 Research Design 
To answer the research question “What is the potential of the hyped industry 4.0 technology 
augmented reality for industrial companies?”, an inductive approach is chosen, resulting in 
qualitative research design. The inductive procedure claims “[…] theory is following data [...]” 
(Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009), meaning that data is collected, analyzed and based on 
the outcome of the observation a theoretical concept is developed. 
  
The decision for an inductive research approach is primarily due to the aim of this study. This 
study focuses on observing the current state of AR in an industrial focus and its potential for 
the future. Thereby, the problem is addressed why companies still hesitate to apply that 
technology, whose answer cannot be derived from existing literature. In addition, AR is an 
innovative technology which attention has risen significantly in recent years leading to the fact 
that the number of available scientific researches in the field of the AR development in the 
industrial context are modest so far. Also, the further development of AR happens quickly, 
resulting in quickly outdated literature depending on how the technology evolves. (Saunders, 
Lewis and Thornhill, 2009) 
  
The research of this Master Thesis was conducted as an exploratory study, exploring the 
innovative technology AR in an industrial perspective and draw conclusions to the existing 
theories. The comprehensive understanding is build based on the observation of the opinions 
inside the different interest groups. The insights are gathered conducting a qualitative research 
in form of in-depth interviews. (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009)

 Literature Review 
The literature research was done in a structured manner, following the process in figure 2 
The following five topics are considered as relevant for the further empirical investigation and

Figure 2: Methodology of literature review 
Firstly, the literature was searched concerning the overall topic of AR in the industry 4.0 
context to receive an overview of where to allocate the new technology. As a second step, the 
industrial context of the technology was examined regarding its application areas and the 
advantages that are achieved with its implementation. The aim was to receive an understanding 
of what drives companies to use AR and in which areas they can achieve benefits. Thirdly, 
researches were conducted about the digital technology itself to provide the reader with a basic 
understanding with the technical components of an AR solution. Thereby, the awareness led to 
the maturity stage of the technology and further weaknesses of AR investigated. The last step 
traced back to the industry 4.0 subject from a company perspective. Literature was gathered in 
terms of influencing factors and pre-requirements for a successful implementation of AR 
solutions.   
The aim of the literature review was to receive a deep understanding of the current state of the 
art of the technology and its influencing factors. During the literature review it was found that 
it is lacking on profound data relating to the topic which had an influence on the research design 
of this master thesis.

 Expert Interviews  
The chosen data collection technique for this study was the qualitative research method. The 
main research goal was to receive in-depth insights and understanding of the technology and 
its benefits for the four previously defined interest groups. Thereby, semi-structured interviews 
were conducted with experts from different groups that represent their perceptions of the 
current state of AR within an industrial context. 
 
AR is still a relatively new technology that received much attention in the past years. To be 
able to evaluate its real value for the future, the opinion of experts in that field is needed. At 
the same time, the technology evolves faster than books are published. Hence, literature written 
last year can already be partly outdated which makes it difficult to receive access to the 
knowledge of the actual development stage of the technology. Moreover, it was particularly 
difficult to receive access to possible success stories since the number of companies using AR 
is limited. Furthermore, the implementation of new technologies is considered as a sensitive 
strategic topic companies are not willing to share. These factors limit the number of potential 
participants for the study which is estimated as too low to conduct a quantitative research

 Data Analysis Approach 
In this subsection the analysis technique for the evaluation of the interviews is discussed. The 
inductive approach, which is used in this thesis to answer the research question, plays a major 
role when it comes to choosing the analysis technique. The outcome of the research is the 
definition of a theory depending on the gathered data. Hence, the Grounded Theory approach, 
developed by Glaser and Straus in 1960, is applied. This method is chosen because it is 
especially supportive when the formulation of a universal theory based on the qualitative data 
is targeted. Thus, this technique ensures a holistic data analysis for this research project. 
(Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009) 
 
The collected interviews are transcribed and are the basis for the systematic analysis. In contrast 
to the deductive approach, the categories are not predefined based on literature review concepts 
but derived from the information gathered during interviews. These categories are later the 
basic building blocks for the formulated theory. (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009) 
 
The approach consists of a coding process that contains three different steps that are followed 
in this thesis. The goal is to narrow down the text elements to receive transparency of the 
material. First, the content-bearing quotes are filtered out of the transcriptions. Then, 
statements with interrelated or contextual aspects are grouped together and are represented 
under a new statement. This step is called “Open Coding”. The “Axial Coding” entails the 
clustering of statements, emerged from the previous step, with similar meanings to one 
category. The analysis of these first two steps of coding are done in an iterative way. The 
modeled relationships within the categories are continuously challenged and compared with 
the knowledge gained from new data collections. This characteristic is essential to develop 
==PAGE:21==

valid theories. (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009) Some of the categories were already 
defined during the design of the questionnaires. They functioned as a first idea on how to 
structure the categorization. However, categories are continuously adapted, changed or 
replaced. The last step of the coding process is the “Selective Coding” which is done after all 
interviews were executed and categories were built. This step is defined by Strauss and Corbin 
as “This is intended to identify one of these principal categories, which becomes known as the 
central or core category, in order to relate the other categories to this with the intention of 
integrating the research and developing a grounded theory”. (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill,

 Definition of the Technology AR 
As humans, we have the ability to hear, touch, smell and see the world but constrained by rules 
of physics. These rules do not exist in a virtual world which is seen in computer games. 
Virtually, almost, anything is possible. The figure below shows the different degrees of

degree of virtual elements is higher than the real elements, it is considered to be Augmented

  AR Software  
An AR solution consists of a hardware and a software component. In this chapter, the focus on 
an explanation of the functionality of the software component required for the AR solution. 
The subsequent chapter then gathers information regarding the hardware component. The 
hardware component is built on a computer, a display device, a tracking device, an input device, 
and network infrastructure and is used as a media for the software. (Esengün and İnce, 2018) 
Besides the hardware part, the software part consists of an app which is running locally on the 
device, a web service and a content server where the main content is located. (Kipper and 
Rambolla, 2013) 
 
The computer is used to control the different components as well as to model the augmentation. 
The computer receives information from the tracking device, to know the environment, where 
the user is located in the environment and where the virtual objects are placed. (Esengün and 
İnce, 2018) The crucial factor is the Extent of World Knowledge (EWK), which means how 
much knowledge the system has about the environment from the device. The system can have 
a full modelled world where it knows which objects are placed where in the world and if 
changes happen from interaction with the user. For the development of a VR system, the system 
has to have a complete modelled world. The opposite is a world where the system does not 
know where objects are and do not distinguish between the objects. For an AR solution, the 
system does not necessarily have to have a full modelled world. (Milgram et al., 1994) 
 
The input device creates a natural user interface (NUI) by making it possible for the user to 
interact with the environment. The input device tracks gestures or voice and sends the 
information to the computer. Input devices can be a camera, microphones, wireless devices, 
haptic technology devices. (Dini and Mura, 2015)  
A final part needed in an AR device is a network infrastructure which makes it possible to be 
connected. The four tasks an AR device needs to solve are scene capture, scene identification, 
scene processing and then scene visualization. Within the process of scene capturing physical

Typical usage of head-up displays is in airplanes where the pilot receives information on the

Holographic displays place the 3D-model into the real environment by using light diffraction. 
However, the focus of this thesis is on the most suitable devices for the industrial context which

HMDs were the first devices to be used for the projection of the virtual world into the real 
environment. The main distinction between HHDs and HMDs is the way how they are utilized 
by the user as well as “[...] the conceptions users may develop in their mind.” (Hsu PH., Huang 
SY., Lin BS., 2013:540) Using HHDs, the user is consistently aware of having an AR 
experience on the screen and cannot mentally immerse completely into the virtual world. In 
contrast, wearing HMDs, the screen-viewer relationship is closer and allows the user a more 
realistic experience. (Hsu PH., Huang SY., Lin BS., 2013) 
 
Headsets and smart glasses are either developed as an optical see-through (OST) or video see-
through (VST) device. Optical see-through glasses use the graphical overlay to project objects 
into the real environment. These are equipped with optical elements (i.e. half-silvered mirrors) 
which are half-transmissive and half reflective. This makes it possible for the user to see the 
environment directly through the mirror as well as the virtual elements which are projected on 
the mirror through a display component. The environment is inspected in real-time to be able 
to place digital objects prospectively correct into the real world. Examples for this technology

 Factors Influencing the AR Experience 
AR experiences aim to allow the user a real virtual experience. Hence, several human factors 
play a significant role when it comes to the design of AR devices. The most important ones are 
presented shortly to give the reader an idea of how intricate the design and development of the 
devices are, and which influences these factors to have on the user experience.  
 
The field of view is defined as “[...] the range of observable world at a given point in time, 
which is measured in degrees.” (Yadav, 2019). Typically, humans have a field of view 
minimum of 120 degrees with a fixed gaze and up to 270 degrees, including eye motion. The 
current technology has a range of 40-100 degrees, which does not fit the human field of view 
and limits the user’s experience. Users need to step back or move their head to enlarge the field 
of view and get the full virtual picture. (Yadav, 2019) 
 
 
Figure 11: Comparison of the field of view between different Headsets (cf.Fineman,2019; c.f.Heaney,2019) 
 
The human focus is a further factor which needs to be realized by HMDs. The human eye 
allows a limited depth of field, which means that a specific area of the image is sharp for the 
eye, called, ”Focal Point”, and the surroundings are faded out and blurred. The human eye can 
focus on different distances far-field and near field by changing the focal point of the eye. The 
integrated flat screens in HMDs need to represent this depth of field. The problem is that the 
physical surface of the screen is not the same as the focal point of the simulated environment 
the user is looking at, which is way further away. That means that the human eye automatically 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Literature Review 
 
34 
realizes that the image plane is not aligned with the object’s depth and starts to refocus the 
focal point automatically. This causes visual fatigue and limits the time of usage of the glasses. 
(Yadav, 2019) 
Optical see-through headsets face the challenge of contrast and brightness, especially outdoors. 
The difficulty lies in highlighting virtual objects and text in the real environment, which is a 
wealth of different colours and brightness. (Yadav, 2019) With VST devices, the contrast and 
the brightness of the real environment and the virtual elements can be adopted before the user 
receive the image and allows a smooth experience. (Martins, 2019) 
 
The resolution is vital to visualize the virtual picture, which is perceived by the human eye. 
The resolution rate of VST depends on the camera capturing the image, whereas the picture 
quality of OST devices is influenced by the display presenting the picture. Commonly, the 
resolution rate of HMDs is measured in pixels per degree. The human retina records it is 
surrounding with 60 pixels per degree. Hardware solutions under this amount of pixel the 
virtual experience seems less realistic for the proband. (Yadav, 2019) 
 
The refresh rate is another component measuring the motion of the environment and influences 
the human experience. This value quantifies how often per second a new image is produced 
and shown to the user. It is crucial to present non-stationary text in the picture to the user. 
(Yadav, 2019) 
 
Latency describes the waiting time for the user to receive the new picture when the user moves 
the head or body. N. Yadav sees the causes of motion sickness in misalignments between the 
motion recognized by the headset and the actual movements of the user. The headset 
development needs to focus the waiting time between action and reaction. (Yadav, 2019) VST 
devices can track movements of head and body more accurately. (Martins, 2019)

which have the significant advantage that the expert trainer does not need to be on-site to give 
real-time instructions. The instructions are done via a microphone and visual signs. (Esengün 
and İnce, 2018)

3.7.1 Hype Cycle for Emerging Technologies 
Gartner observes new emerging technologies and classifies them on the Hype Cycle for 
Emerging Technologies as it can be seen in the figure below. The y-axis consists of the 
expectations to the technology and the x-axis show time. This cycle describes the development 
of new technologies by assigning five different stages.  
 
• Innovation Trigger which is a stage where people start talking about the technology, 
but there are not yet useable products on the market.  
• Peak of Inflated Expectations is a stage where stories of the use of the technology start 
arising, some with success and some as a failure. Few companies start to react. 
• Through Disillusionment is the stage where the technology must prove itself and either 
fail or starts growing. If the producers start satisfying the early adopters, then the 
technology will survive.  
• In Slope of Enlightenment, the technology becomes better understood and success 
stories arise. Implementations of the technology, and the new generation of the products 
appear. The conservative companies will still not implement the technology.  
• Plateau of Productivity is when the mainstream starts to use the technology, and the 
technology investments clearly pay off.

3.8.4 Technical Maturity of Companies 
The literature mentions the technical maturity of companies as a characteristic of companies to 
be suitable for Industry 4.0 technologies. A certain stage of digital maturity is required to profit 
from digital solutions. (Schumacher, Erol and Sihn, 2016) However, there is no specific 
concept in the literature addressing the required digital maturity of a company to implement 
AR. For a company considering the implementation of new technologies, it can be challenging 
to evaluate if the company is technical mature for these new concepts. Therefore, the literature 
suggests that maturity models assess the companies’ readiness.  
 
For organizations, it is difficult to understand how new technologies will impact their business 
model and process as well as if they have the required capabilities for it. (Schumacher, Erol 
and Sihn, 2016) Schumacher, Erol and Sihn (2016) have developed a maturity model for 
manufacturing companies to measure their readiness. It starts with the evaluation of how a 
company performs related to industry 4.0 in nine areas, which are essential for the maturity 
state of a company. These are defined as strategy, leadership, customers, products, operations, 
culture, people, governance and technology. Each of these criteria needs to be evaluated from 
1, which is not supporting the factor in an industry 4.0 direction, to 5, which is state of the art 
within industry 4.0 focus. (Schumacher, Erol and Sihn, 2016)  
This study will not go into the depth of the model but points out that looking at the nine factors 
a company needs to have an industry 4.0 focus throughout the company and not only parts of 
it. Companies which have a high focus on industry 4.0 within these nine areas are more mature 
than those that are not focusing on these areas.   
 
3.8.5 Organizational Culture and Structure 
Human Capital Experts see high importance in the organizational culture and structure within 
the digital transformation of the company. Employees need to have appropriate skills and 
especially the openness towards digitalization. The implementation of flat or decentralized 
hierarchies increases the multidisciplinary communication and close cooperation inside the 
company outside of corporate functions. This structure supports the integration and alignment 
with the digitalization. (Dr. Proff and Dr. Sandau, 2016)  
 
A study conducted by PwC in 2018 underlined this dependency by saying that besides 
organizational competencies, organizational culture is an influencing factor when it comes to

the implementation of digitalization. The surveyed companies that were clustered as “Digital 
Champions” indicated to have flat hierarchies within their organization. (Geissbauer et al., 
2018) 
 
One of the reasons, why companies are not willing to invest in or able to successfully 
implement new technologies and modern technological strategies is among other things caused 
by the strictly adhere on old patterns and strategies as Gordon Müller-Seitz and  Werner Weiß  
argue in their book "Strategien im Umgang mit der Digitalen Transformation". Dynamic 
capabilities are defined as the willingness and ability of a company to respond quickly and 
flexible on market and environmental changes. These capabilities are strengthened with an 
open organization and communication culture. This culture is especially important when it 
comes to the digital transformation of companies. To be further established on the market, a 
common strategy across functional boundaries is vital to avoid rivalling opinions. Direct 
communication ways inside the company are, therefore, from a high significance to come up 
with new innovative ideas and to adopt outdated strategies. (Müller-Seitz and Weiss, 2019)  
G. Müller-Seitz and W. Weiß give an example of the multinational German concern Bosch that 
was facing problems inside the organization when they decided to invest into digitalization to 
become to an “Internet-of-Things Company” and offer digital products and services. The 
earnings from traditional lucrative product segments were invested into the new digital 
segments, which lead to misalignments between the departments. An open organizational 
culture is essential when it comes to changes in the existing strategy. (Müller-Seitz and Weiss,

AR is a way of digitalizing something that could be done physically instead. This means that a

"But you have to have a degree on a maturity of technology inside your company. As maturity

4.6.4 Quality 
Increasing transparency of processes and products is seen as one of the AR benefits in the

error detection. The gains are apparent in low costs for error detection and improvement of 
quality control. (D2; MC1; MC2; R3; C3) The interviewees verify their statements by instance 
examples of companies. 
 
One company is implementing AR into their production line to enhance the worker's accuracy

 Category 7: Company Barriers 
The reason why the interviewees are asked about the challenges of AR is to receive an

In this chapter, there will not be mentioned the specific challenges related to the technology

4.7.1 Unknown Technology 
Although AR has existed for many years, it is still new in the market and far from a standard 
technology used by all companies. Thus, a developer mentions their most significant challenge 
is to get people to understand AR and its opportunities.   
 “It is a new technology, so it is difficult for people to imagine what it could be; client is not 
experienced and needs more expert service [...] difficult technology not easy to 
understand. So, a lot of collaboration with the client is needed to convince him form the 
success” (D1) 
 
The technology is unknown and incremental innovation, very different from what we as a 
human usually experience. It brings a part of something unnatural which challenges peoples 
and makes it difficult to understand. When a person does not understand technology, it is not 
natural to invest in it. Currently, there is a knowledge gap about the technology and how it can 
be applied. This process of convincing companies from the benefit is the first challenge for 
developers but also the fact that the companies are not experienced in this field. Very few 
companies have a specialist and therefore require guidance from experts as developing studios. 
This barrier is named by developers as well as by companies.

“We do not know enough of the technology and how to use it. It is difficult to pull out enough

(C2)  
As the quote above confirms, the lack of knowledge is an issue even for a large company which

A researcher sees the barrier from a different angle by saying that the technology is mature, but

Another aspect identified, briefly mentioned under category 1, is the bad experiences

4.7.4 Level of Digitalization in Companies 
Many companies go through digitalisation these years to be able to compete. (Hall, 2019) 
According to one of the interviewed companies, the level of digitalisation influences the 
outcome of the AR solution.  
“The AR will be implemented for the new products a company creates since the old products

His argumentation is that AR solutions need access to the relevant data to be able to portray it.

5.1.3 Comparison of Maturity of Technology 
The maturity of the AR technology is evaluated by Gartner to be in the Through of 
Disillusionment stage where the expectations are low to the technology, and the technology has 
to demonstrate itself after showing some success stories but also some unsuccessful stories. 
Gartner expects the technology to be mature in 5 to 10 years in the Slope of Enlightenment. 
(Panetta, 2018) In overall, this is a similar belief the interviewees have since they are all placed 
in the Growing part of the Technology Life Cycle Curve, which is the part where the 
technology starts to prove itself. 
 
All interviewees agree on the technology being in the Growing stage, but developers have a 
more positive view on maturity. Even though the developers have detailed knowledge about 
the technology and its possible use cases, they are interested in selling their projects, which 
makes their answer less reliable. However, the researchers with in-depth knowledge in that 
field and a more credible point of view, place the technology in the upper part of the improving 
stage and support the opinion of the developers.  
The opinions of the companies show significant differences. They are split in two, at each end 
of the Growing part. This is based on the experiences they made with the technology. The ones 
having a good experience and are satisfied with the performance of the technology tend to 
evaluate it higher than the ones with the bad experience. This is aligned with the statement of 
Garner that the technology is in this stage where it needs to prove itself.  
 
Based on the statements of the interviews and the literature, it can be stated that the technology 
is at a point where it is ready to be implemented into companies at least in certain situations. 
Simultaneously, it still needs to prove itself and meet the high expectations of the users. Until 
now, the experiences with the technology vary and inhibit the achievement of the next stage. 
The literature, as well as the interviewees, have high expectations in the future development of 
AR but are confident in its improvements.

5.1.4 Conclusion of Benefits and Barriers  
The outcome of the study provides an overview based on the collected data about the gains to 
be obtained and the barriers to be anticipated with the existing technology. How companies 
can profit from the implementation in specific depends on how the benefits can outbalance the 
barriers to support the decision of companies to implement AR. If a company cannot 
compromise on the facing barriers for its specific application, the company will tend to search 
for another solution. There is no general answer to if the benefits make up for the barriers, and 
each company needs to decide based on their specific circumstances if the faced barriers get 
aligned with the specific solution. For instance, the barrier, that the employee gets a headache 
from long term use of HMDs, makes it impossible to use them for a longer duration at its 
current stage whereas in cases where the worker needs to wear it for a limited time duration 
the company might consider the implementation.  
 
It is shown that representatives in the literature, as well as the interviewees in the conducted 
study, believe in the future of the technology and its advantages. However, also, barriers could 
be gathered. These are concerning the maturity of the technology as well as the maturity of the

the technology since the providers as well as the developers to have access to problems users 
are facing and receive specific areas of improvements for the technology they can work on. On 
the opposite, with a higher maturity and certainty of AR, more companies will e AR, and with

5.2.1 Evaluation of Findings in Product Development 
The conducted study disproves the potential to reach success in product development at the 
current maturity stage of AR. Despite, several authors mention product development where AR 
theoretically can create benefits for organizations (Chatzimichali, Gijselaers and Segers, 2011; 
Esengün and İnce, 2018; Grace, 2018; Porter and Heppelmann, 2017; Purdy, 2014; Shen, Ong 
and Nee, 2010) the study did not support their arguments. 
 
During the interviews, product development was not often discussed and received, on average, 
the lowest valuation by the interviewees when they evaluated the value it could create. It just 
played a role for the interest group of management consultants where both consultants see 
benefits of AR in product development and agree with the literature. The other interest groups 
do not contradict in terms of opposing the general use of AR in the development context. 
However, at the current stage, they do not see the usability of it. The statements of the 
management consultants indicate two principal vulnerabilities and are therefore not seen as 
representative. Firstly, the opinion of management consultants, in that case, point to an 
inevitable scarcity of neutrality. Their opinion might tend to a more idealistic imagination of 
the usability of AR at its current maturity stage and might be based on the literature. They have 
their strengths in facing strategic issues, seeing the need to solve problems with AR solutions 
without considering the feasibility of implementation whereas the interest groups of companies 
and developers represent a more realistic idea of what is currently possible with AR. 
Companies and developers put these generated ideas into practice and develop the specific AR 
solution. Therefore, these interviewees are closer to direct implementation and its occurring 
problems. Secondly, the examples named by the consultants to substantiate their opinion are

increase the quality of the communication between designer and engineer. This issue addresses

5.2.2 Evaluation of Findings in Operations 
The empirical investigation in this thesis acknowledges that applying AR within Operations 
allows sufficient information support, collaboration and guidance, which results in decreasing 
the processing time and achieves higher throughput and better quality within the process.

reached with the usage of AR in Operations, expressing that the number of productive working 
hours of worker will increase since repetitive movements and quality errors are reduced. 
 
Further, the conducted study indicates several situations when an AR step-by-step guidance 
within operations is suitable. These solutions are appropriate in cases when there are many 
different variants manufactured on one production line and produced in a random sequence.

stage HMDs face maturity problems. Therefore, AR solution might be affected by the 
restriction of these headsets. However, those are assessed as minor since the time period of 
training sessions are manageable and assumed to be in the time frame HMD can be used 
unhesitating. This makes AR useable in training already now and not necessarily until the 
technology is more mature and with the increasing complexity of products and production 
processes it has a high potential for the future.

5.2.8 Conclusion of Application Areas 
As the above discussion verifies, that the potential benefits AR can lead to, differ between the 
stated application areas and their specific circumstances. The application areas were assessed 
by their potential use and rough use cases were developed. 
 
Besides the suitability to the company’s capabilities, the usage of HMDs plays currently a 
major role and is a big limitation for application areas. This means that areas such as 
Maintenance, Warehouse and Operations are highly affected by the maturity of HMDs whereas 
areas such as Sales and Marketing have the alternative to switch to HHDs without being 
restricted in their activities. This means that Sales & Marketing are further in the development 
than others. Training counts to the areas where AR is used the most due to its promising benefits 
and the time limit the HMDs need to be used. In some cases, currently existing methods are 
better than AR for instance in Warehouse which makes this area less ready for AR. For Product 
Development there is not a gain currently and no clear benefits in the future. How AR can be 
implemented is not clear since it is a new technology needing success stories and the lack of 
knowledge makes the implementation difficult. The implementation of AR is easier with HHDs 
compared to HMDs and for this reason the implementation of AR is different depending on the 
application area.

 Which companies are most suited to implement AR?  
The third research objective investigated the characteristics supporting the suitability of

5.3.1 Evaluation of Findings in Companies’ Characteristics 
The empirical investigation demonstrates that characteristics and capabilities can be gathered, 
which indicate a higher suitability and resulting motivation of some companies to implement 
AR compared to others. As such are defined company size, - ownership, the industry the 
company operates in as well as the digital maturity inside the company. Similarities and 
extensions of existing literature could be identified. 
 
Firstly, the suitability of organizations to implement AR is influenced by the company’s size. 
As a result of the study, it is found that mainly MNEs are willing to implement AR whereas 
SMEs hesitate in making these investments. The main reason is the lack of resources which 
prevents SMEs implementing AR. Human, as well as financial resources, are needed to develop 
the digitalization of companies further. Notably, in terms of new unknown innovation which 
did not establish themselves on the market constitute a high risk for an organization with 
limited resources. It can also be derived based on the statements of the interviewees that bigger 
companies face problems in agreeing on a common strategy when it comes to the 
implementation of new technologies. This results in a longer time duration until the solution is 
in use.  
In the literature, it is documented that smaller companies profit from their short communication 
channels and are more flexible to adapt changes in their processes. This aspect encourages a 
successful implementation of AR. (G. Müller-Seitz and W. Weiß, 2019)

Also, the literature grades access to resources as crucial for the digital transformation of 
organizations. G. Müller-Seitz and W. Weiß (2019) see the availability of internal resources as 
a competitive advantage for companies, which means at the same time that companies with 
lacking human and financial resources have a harder time to implement digital strategies and 
will lead to lower motivation and a lengthy period of hesitation. The fact that organizational 
wise it is easier to implement in smaller companies contradicts with the previous outcome that 
it is the bigger companies implementing the technology since they have the money to invest. 
Thus, it is deduced from these results that at the current maturity stage, MNEs have an 
advantage in embedding AR in their organization. Still, with decreasing cost for the 
development in the future, AR is also possible to be implemented into smaller organization. 
There is a clear overlap between the literature and the study of this master thesis which indicates 
that these estimates for a digital transformation in general also count for AR solutions. 
 
Secondly, the industry the company operates is identified as a characteristic influencing the 
suitability. A pattern, based on the experiences the interviewees made, can be seen. Specific 
industries are more interested in implementing AR, although there is no correlation between 
the industry and the desired success of AR solutions. However, there are tendencies of 
industries which are more willing to implement AR than others, proven by the number of user 
cases. Organizational culture, profit margin and type of products are underlying causes that 
differ between industries and influence their willingness to take risks. Companies in more 
conservative industries are more cautious in making investments in the field of unknown and 
innovative technologies. Those companies implement AR to change their long-established 
processes but wait until success stories are already known. Whereas, companies with an open 
organizational structure are more modern and interested in trying out new technologies and 
being the first implementing. Besides, open organizations have a better communication flow 
inside their company and ideas can be forwarded quicker to the responsible person.  
Moreover, the empirical investigation provides evidence that the profit margin makes a further 
distinction between industries. Industries with higher profit margin tend to invest more in AR 
than those with a lower profit margin. This aspect is related to the previously mentioned aspect 
of available resources needed for the implementation. Financial resources are, among others, 
influenced by the size of the company as well as the industry the company operates.  
It is also detected that the product portfolio of industries influences the motivation to apply 
AR. Industries with large, expensive and customized products acknowledge AR as a possible

6 Conclusion  
To answer the main research question “What is the potential of the hyped industry 4.0 
technology Augmented Reality for industrial companies?” the authors of this thesis have 
observed areas influencing the potential of AR for companies based on several interest groups. 
Thereby, the overall benefits and barriers for companies were investigated. As a next step, the 
different application areas were analyzed with the focus on their possible benefit and readiness 
for AR implementation. As the last step, the sub-question was answered regarding the influence 
of organizational characteristics and how they support the application of AR. 
 
This master thesis derived that the literature as well as the practitioners believe in the 
technology being beneficial for several application areas. However, due to the maturity of the 
technology, which is also influenced by market maturity, the performance of AR is restricted. 
This is shown specifically when it comes to the evaluation of application areas where AR can 
potentially support the company’s processes. AR is useful in several application areas, but it is 
not equally mature in each of them. Sales and Marketing, as well as Training count to the more 
mature application areas whereas Operations, Maintenance and Product Development, are less 
mature. For processes within Warehousing, AR faces competition with alternative solutions 
because of the lack of maturity and the price.  
As presented in the figure below, the decision of companies to implement AR does not only 
depend on the availability of a suitable use case within the company or their conviction of the 
benefits offered by an AR solution. It is also impacted by the company’s characteristic, which 
influences the company indirectly in making the decision. It was found within the study that 
companies' differences in terms of size, ownership and the industry the company operates in as 
well as the digital maturity inside the company which were developed as factors impacting the 
suitability of an AR solution in their company. Implementing AR depends on the company’s 
willingness and suitability if they tend to invest in the less mature and more uncertain areas, 
more mature and certain areas or if they do not invest at all.  
The study also confirms that limitations of the technology are given by its maturity as well as 
the maturity of the market and influences the potential of the technology.

8 Limitations 
This chapter will depict the most pronounced limitations of the study. It needs to be mentioned 
that these factors are considered being outside of the researchers’ control. Although they are 
uncontrollable, they are still important to mention as these are regarded to have influenced the 
knowledge created throughout this thesis. 
 
Firstly, the selection of interviewees needs to be considered as a limitation of the study. The 
selection of interviewees was done in a purposive non-probability method of sampling which 
means that they were chosen based on the authors’ subjective judgements in terms of their 
experience, their relevance for the study and their availability. Thus, the sample of each interest 
group does not account for the entire population. (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2009) The 
fact that the companies were selected applying the snowball sampling method, might take the 
consequence that the interviewees tend to propose existing clients which are already convinced 
by AR. Hence, there is a risk of receiving similar opinions for the study. During the interviews, 
it turned out that the experiences made in relation to AR differ between each other due to the 
high amount of possibilities where and how to apply the technology. Therefore, the risk is 
evaluated as relatively low. It also needs to be considered that each of the interviewees brings 
a bias which in this case is caused by several factors in the persons environment, education, 
experiences, job and beliefs. However, experts from different interest groups with different 
backgrounds were selected to guarantee some representativeness of the results and decrease 
the bias of the respondents. 
 
Secondly, the maturity of the technology has created implications since it is quickly developing 
further and is still not mature. Therefore, when understanding the reliability of the data gathered 
and asking the question “Will the results be the same on other occasions?” (Easterby-Smith, 
M., Thorpe, R. Jackson, P. and Lowe, A. (2008), the answer in this case would be no because 
the outcome could differ in half a year. Several developers mentioned that solutions looked 
very different in the type of projects now compared to a year ago. (D1; D5) The maturity of 
the technology also constrained the available literature in the field. It has been difficult to create 
a knowledge base in certain areas of the study. Therefore, it has been even more important 
gathering data from specialists and practitioners. This is a risk which is present in every 
research but more important in studies of emerging technologies. The current study provides 
profound knowledge of the current stage of the maturity and uncovers possible improvements 
==PAGE:127==

Lastly, the study is based on opinions of the authors which can create an observers’ bias and

background of the authors are partly different complement each other. Working together on the

Future Research

9 Future Research 
The exploratory procedure of the investigation combines with a qualitative approach including 
expert interviews gives the thesis a preparatory status. Thus, the results presented cannot offer 
significance in the sense of a statistical evaluation induced by the fact that AR is an innovative 
technology still in the early maturity stages. The thesis presents the answers to occurring 
questions in terms of the current development stage of the technology in an empirical way. 
However, it raises a range of new questions which serve a starting point for further research. 
 
The two different field of research are identified during the investigation. Firstly, the study 
gives incentives for the discussion of the development of a unique implementation procedure, 
based on a neutral opinion, that companies can follow to feel more secure within their adaption 
of the AR solution. Secondly, the need of a concrete cost-benefit analysis and the resulting cost 
drivers for specific use cases. These research approaches could not be solved within the scope 
of this study. Nevertheless, both issues are frequently addressed by the interviewees and cannot 
be answered with the available literature. A study in that area could give interested companies 
in the technology a detailed perception and a better idea about what they get involved with. 
This would decrease the estimated risk in terms of the implementation of a new technology and 
motivate companies to apply AR. 
 
Unequivocally, the research area still requires considerable refinement. The outcome offers 
essential insights in terms of factors influencing the potential of AR for industrial companies, 
but so far, they lack any quantitative prove and precision. A closer observation was not the 
intention of the work due to the broad field of investigation and the limited access to valid data. 
Therefore, further investigations on how to quantify the different areas to provide companies 
evaluation criteria to assess if AR offers potential in their specific case. Additionally, a 
catalogue of measures on how a company can improve its capabilities to be suitable for AR or 
how a company can expressly limit the barriers arising with an AR implementation shows a 
gap for further researches in the field.
