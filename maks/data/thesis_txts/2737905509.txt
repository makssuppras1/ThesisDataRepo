Abstract
This study focuses on the evaluation the capability of MidôÙInfrared (MIR) spectroscopy
for realôÙtime prediction of substrate and metabolite concentrations in fermentation proôÙ
cesses. The objective is to develop and validate calibration models that accurately preôÙ
dict concentrations of key metabolites such as acetate, ethanol, glycerol, and glucose in
a fedôÙbatch Saccharomyces cerevisiae fermentation process. Four Partial Least Squares
(PLS) models were tested to evaluate their potential for realôÙtime concentration predictions
and monitoring. The models were developed using datasets comprising pure synthetic
samples, mixed synthetic samples, and inôÙline fermentation data. The most accurate preôÙ
dictions were achieved using inôÙline spectral data from actual fermentations, highlighting
the importance of representative training data.
The study underscores the significance of using highôÙquality, representative training data
that captures the true variability of industrial fermentation processes, along with robust
modeling techniques. The findings are particularly relevant for the application of chemoôÙ
metrics in Process Analytical Technology (PAT), illustrating the potential of PLS models
to enhance process efficiency and product consistency through realôÙtime monitoring and
control. Future research directions include integrating advanced machine learning algoôÙ
rithms with PLS models to handle complex, nonlinear relationships, expanding training
datasets to include a broader range of fermentation conditions, and ensuring transferôÙ
ability of the successful prediction models across various MIR spectroscopic instruments.
The integration of sophisticated realôÙtime data acquisition and processing technologies,
will be crucial in advancing bioprocess monitoring and optimization.
Overall, this study highlights the significant potential of MIR spectroscopy combined with
advanced PLS modeling for realôÙtime monitoring and control of fermentation processes.
The research provides a solid foundation for future advancements in bioprocess monitorôÙ
ing, contributing to the objectives of Bioprocess 4.0.

Industry 4.0 builds upon the ongoing modernization of industrial processes, succeeding

WildôÙtype yeast fermentation, a commonly used model system, involves complex dynamôÙ

growth curve of wildôÙtype yeast and the production of key metabolites such as ethanol,

WildôÙtype yeast growth typically follows a characteristic growth curve comprising lag, log,
deceleration, and stationary phases (Figure 1.1) [7], [8], [9]. Wild yeast fermentation inôÙ

acetate. Each of these metabolites is produced through distinct biochemical pathways,
which are influenced by the phase of yeast growth and the availability of nutrients. During
the exponential phase, cells rapidly consume available substrates, leading to significant
metabolic activity and the production of primary metabolites. Ethanol is a primary product

Ethanol production is closely tied to the yeastãs metabolic state and the availability of gluôÙ

decreases as the cells enter the stationary phase. In the stationary phase, the need for
glycerol decreases as the rate of glucose metabolism slows down and osmotic pressure

OffôÙline measurements occur away from the process, while atôÙline measurements involve

OnôÙline measurements are performed continuously, with data being processed and inteôÙ

provide realôÙtime data by monitoring directly within the process stream without interruptôÙ
ing operations. OnôÙline measurements, on the other hand, are conducted by recirculating
the liquid from the reactor through an external loop, allowing continuous monitoring with
a potential delay for data processing before reintegration into the system. Each of these
measurement modes offers distinct advantages and tradeôÙoffs concerning measurement
accuracy, speed, and the required level of process integration [11], [12].

1.3
Spectroscopic Measurement Methods
Spectroscopy involves the interaction between electromagnetic waves and analytes as a
function of wavelength [11], [6]. It typically involves using a spectrometer, a specialized
instrument that records the wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation. By analyzing these
wavelengths, we can gather information about the structure and properties of the sample.
Different materials will emit or absorb different wavelengths of light based on their unique
properties and structures. These recorded wavelengths are then compared to known
standards to determine the materialãs composition. It is broadly classified into atomic and
molecular spectroscopy (Figure 1.5) [11].

The key difference between atomic spectroscopy and molecular spectroscopy is that
atomic spectroscopy refers to the study of the electromagnetic radiation absorbed and
emitted by atoms whereas molecular spectroscopy refers to the study of the electromagôÙ
netic radiation absorbed and emitted by molecules.
When matter absorbs electromagnetic radiation, it gains energy. This energy can be used
to excite electrons in the atom or molecule to a higher energy level. When the excited
electrons return to their original energy state, they emit electromagnetic radiation. The
frequency of the emitted radiation corresponds to the energy difference between the two
energy levels.
The primary wavelength range for spectroscopic sensors in bioprocesses spans from UV
to MIR, including techniques like Raman and fluorescence spectroscopy, as shown in

comprehensive tools for analyzing the composition and properties of samples in biotechôÙ

1.3.1
Vibrational Spectroscopy
Vibrational spectroscopy was the first widely adopted structural spectroscopic technique
among organic chemists. Its key advantage is the ability to analyze virtually any sample
in any state. This includes liquids, solutions, pastes, powders, films, fibers, gases, and
surfaces, given the appropriate sampling technique. The development of advanced inôÙ
strumentation has enabled the examination of previously challenging samples with new,
sensitive techniques. Compounds with covalent bonds, whether organic or inorganic, abôÙ
sorb various frequencies of electromagnetic radiation in the infrared region. Vibrational
spectroscopy encompasses several techniques, with the most significant being MIR, NIR,
and Raman spectroscopy.

IR spectroscopy is useful for quantifying known compounds or elucidating the structures
of unknown molecules. In this technique, an IR source emits light towards a sample conôÙ
taining chemical compounds. These compounds absorb IR radiation as motion energy
when the absorbed energy meets the resonance condition of a tone or related overtones.

compounds without a dipole are IR inactive. However, for IR active compounds, funcôÙ

Raman spectroscopy is a technique based on the inelastic scattering of photons, known

Rayleigh scattering, and then dispersed onto a detector. Spontaneous Raman scattering
is inherently weak, making it challenging to distinguish from the intense Rayleigh scattered
light. Modern Raman spectrometers use notch or edge filters for laser rejection and CCD

1.3.2.3
Mass Spectroscopy (MS)
Due to its sensitivity and speed, mass spectroscopy (MS) has become an essential tool
in various bioprocesses. MS significantly aids in the identification of proteins present at
low concentrations within complex mixtures. Additionally, it facilitates highôÙthroughput
analysis and quality assessment of various biological samples. MS can automate proôÙ
cesses, further increasing throughput and efficiency. MS is also crucial for identifying and
monitoring biomarkers in physiological fluids, contributing to the evaluation of biochemical
processes, efficacy, and safety. Its potential extends to rapid screening of biomolecular
interactions, including both the strength and sites of interaction [44].
MS measures the massôÙtoôÙcharge (m/z) ratio, not the mass itself. The resulting mass
spectrum is a plot of ion abundance versus m/z. The molecular weight reflects the molar
mass, while the mass spectrometer measures individual ions in units of kilograms per
Coulomb. Isotopes, such as those of chlorine in chlorobenzene, also affect MS readings,
showing ions at different m/z values based on isotopic composition [44].
A mass spectrometer consists of an ionization source, a mass analyzer, and a detecôÙ
tor. While the primary data from MS are m/z ratios and component abundances, it also
provides structural information about molecules. This combination of structural analysis,
sensitivity, and speed highlights the power and versatility of MS in bioprocesses [44].
In summary, each method excites different molecule types. UV/Vis, NIR, and MIR specôÙ
troscopy mainly measure light absorption and scattering by molecules while fluorescence
spectroscopy utilizes light emitted from excited fluorophores, and Raman spectroscopy
detects rare inelastic scattering effects [11]. By leveraging the entire spectrum, specôÙ
troscopy makes nearly all important physical, biological, and chemical variables in a bioôÙ
process accessible. Spectroscopic sensors provide realôÙtime information almost without
any delay, enabling process automation. Additionally, they offer several advantages: no
sampling is required (except for calibration), the sensors do not interact with the bioproôÙ
cess stream, and multiple process variables can be monitored simultaneously [11], [45].

Multivariate Data Analysis
Traditional sensors in fermentation processes provide single data points, whereas specôÙ
troscopic methods generate complex arrays of multivariate data. For instance, a UVôÙVis
spectrometer measures absorbance at multiple wavelengths simultaneously, necessitatôÙ
ing multivariate data analysis to derive meaningful insights. Techniques such as multiple
linear regression (MLR), principal component analysis (PCA), and partial least squares
(PLS) exploit variable relationships, traditionally seen as linear models. PCA and PLS
are especially useful for their ability to manage nonlinear relationships, crucial in scenarôÙ

els is challenging due to media interference and variable correlations. Effective training
typically involves extensive datasets with diverse spiking profiles. At the same time, apôÙ
plying a design of experiments approach can reduce calibration set size. Preprocessing
methods, including scatter correction and baseline corrections, are essential to address
undesired phenomena, enhancing model performance.
1.4.1
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis is a statistical technique used for dimensionality reduction,
transforming highôÙdimensional data into a set of principal components, which are linearly
uncorrelated variables. This transformation simplifies complex datasets, making them
easier to visualize and interpret. Regarding MIR spectroscopy studies of fermentation
processes, PCA is particularly valuable as it helps in handling intricate spectral data and
improving process monitoring and control.
PCA works by identifying the directions (principal components) in which the data varies the
most. The first principal component captures the greatest variance, and each subsequent
component captures the highest variance possible under the constraint that it is orthogonal
to the preceding components. By projecting the data onto these principal components,
PCA reduces the number of dimensions without significant loss of information, as depicted
in Figure 1.9 [46].

2.6.2
Model Development and Selection
In our study, the development of PLS models was integral to predicting critical concenôÙ
trations from spectral data. The PLS algorithm establishes a calibration model between
analyte concentrations and IR spectra, making it highly suitable for handling numerous,
correlated variables. This relationship is modeled using latent variables, which are linear
combinations of the original spectral values. These latent variables maximize the covariôÙ
ance between the predictors (X) and responses (Y) using the least squares approach.
For each dataset used to train the model, the right reference concentrations were mapped
34
Evaluating the Capability of MidôÙInfrared Spectroscopy for RealôÙTime Prediction of Substrate and
Metabolite Concentrations in Fermentation Processes
==PAGE:49==
to the spectral data files. This was crucial for supervised learning tasks, ensuring that
each spectrum was paired with the correct concentration value. Accurate concentration
mapping was fundamental for building reliable predictive models. The process involved
initializing storage for spectral data (X) and concentrations (Y), processing pure comôÙ
pounds, mixed samples, and fermentation data, and finally converting the lists to arrays
for modeling.
During the development and evaluation of the PLS regression models, crossôÙvalidation
was employed to determine the optimal number of components, as described in detail
in Subsection 2.6.4. The best PLS models were selected regarding a high coefficient of
determination, a low predictive error, and a low number of components, facilitating enough
data coverage.
The code used for PLS regression included steps for crossôÙvalidation, model selection,
and training the PLS model. The chosen number of components was then used to train
the final PLS model on the complete dataset. PLS regression was implemented using
the PLSRegression class from scikit-learn, facilitating the analysis of relationships
between variables. Specifically, Standard PLS was employed to optimize the covariance
between predictor and response variables, as described in detail, in the code listing in
Appendix B. This approach enhances the linear relationship between the variables.
Mathematically, the PLS model involves the decomposition of the predictor matrix X and
the response matrix Y as follows:
X = TPãÊ+ E
Y = UQãÊ+ F
where:
ãÂ T and U are matrices of the latent scores,
ãÂ P and Q are matrices of the loadings,
ãÂ E and F are the residual matrices.
The relationship between T and U is assumed to be linear:
U = TB + H
where B is a diagonal matrix of regression coefficients and H is a residual matrix.
The PLS regression aims to maximize the covariance between T and U, ensuring that the
extracted latent variables explain as much as possible of the covariance between X and
Y.
In practice, PLS is performed using an iterative algorithm that extracts one latent variable
at a time and deflates the matrices X and Y.
2.6.3
Wavenumber Selection
The selection of specific wavenumbers in PLS modeling offers several advantages. Firstly,
it enhances model accuracy by focusing on relevant spectral regions, reducing the inôÙ
fluence of noise and irrelevant areas [84]. Secondly, it reduces model complexity by

Metabolite Concentrations in Fermentation Processes
35
==PAGE:50==
decreasing the dimensionality of the data, making the model more interpretable and comôÙ
putationally efficient, particularly for large datasets [85]. Additionally, selecting specific
wavenumbers improves the signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio by reducing noise and emphasizing sigôÙ
nificant spectral features [86]. Finally, it facilitates model transferability across different
instruments and experimental conditions, as models constructed with chosen wavenumôÙ
bers rely on distinctive chemical characteristics rather than specific instrument settings
[87]. Continuous updating of wavenumber selection based on model performance is also
recommended to maintain the modelãs effectiveness [84].
Our approach involved several steps. First, we conducted offôÙline measurements of pure
analytes. Next, we created mixtures of the analytes with varying concentrations. To further
refine our models, we incorporated both offôÙline and onôÙline data. In each of these steps,
careful visualization of the raw and preprocessed spectra led to the choice of the most
suitable wavenumber range, which could facilitate the model predictions and accuracy.
This process began by examining the calibration curves of pure compounds to identify
their distinctive spectral peaks. By narrowing the wavenumber range using the trial and
error method, and observing the model performance changes, we aimed to capture the
unique spectral patterns of each component. The selection of wavenumbers is crucial
because the absorbance bands in spectroscopy are often broad and overlapping. This
can lead to noise and irrelevant information affecting the modelãs performance.

2.6.4
Cross ôÙ Validation and Selection of Principal Components
Spectroscopic data often contains complex and correlated information, making PLS reôÙ
gression a valuable tool for building predictive models. The process of finding the optiôÙ
mal number of components begins by plotting crossôÙvalidation error against the number
of components. Through crossôÙvalidation, the error is assessed for different component
numbers, and the number of components that minimize the error is selected. Next, PCA is
applied to the spectral data using the chosen number of components, reducing dimensionôÙ
ality while retaining significant variance. PLS regression then utilizes these principal comôÙ
ponents as inputs, effectively addressing the high dimensionality and residual collinearity

==PAGE:51==
in the data. Indicative coding details for each of these steps mentioned, can be found in

to follow a robust method of component choice. Initially, a figure depicting the CrossôÙ
Validation Error against the Number of Components was created, followed by another
displaying the RôÙsquared against the Number of Components.
The number of components in a PLS model should be chosen based on the point where
the crossôÙvalidation error is minimized without significantly increasing, and where RôÙsquared
values are high but not indicative of overfitting. This balance ensures that the model capôÙ
tures the essential variance in the data while avoiding noise, thereby maintaining generôÙ

2.6.5
Validating the PLS Model: Unseen Data & Statistical Tools
Validation is crucial for ensuring the reliability and precision of PLS regression models. A
reliable method we employed for validating our PLS models was using separate test sets,
specifically leveraging data from Fermentation 2 (F2). Generally, this approach entails diôÙ
viding the available data into two distinct sets: a training set utilized for model construction
and an independent test set reserved solely for validation purposes. By employing this
method, we were able to impartially assess the predictive performance of our models on
new data. Utilizing an independent test set comprised of unseen data enabled us to gain
insights into the modelãs performance in scenarios not encountered during the training
phase [90].
The below statistical tools were extensively used during the model validation process, to
evaluate the performance of the developed models. These methods offer a comprehenôÙ
sive evaluation of the modelãs generalizability to novel, untested data.
ãÂ Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)
RMSE is a frequently used statistic to compute the differences between expected
and observed values. It presents the modelãs prediction error as a single number,
where smaller values indicate better model performance. RMSE is highly useful in
PLS modeling since it gives a clear indication of the modelãs prediction accuracy on
the validation set. [86].
For PLS models predicting metabolite concentrations in fermentation samples, good
RMSE values typically range from 0.5 to 2.0. Values below 1.0 generally indicate
excellent model performance, whereas values above 2.0 suggest a need for model
improvement. For instance, in a study on modeling ethanol concentrations, RMSE
values were reported as low as 0.86, indicating strong model performance [91]. AnôÙ
other study reported RMSE values of 3.89 for ethanol prediction, suggesting modôÙ
erate performance [92].
ãÂ Coefficient of Determination (RôÙsquared)
RôÙsquared is a significant statistic that indicates the proportion of the variance of
the dependent variable that can be anticipated using the independent variables. An
RôÙsquared value close to 1 indicates that the model is able to explain a substantial
portion of the variance in the data, which is desirable. When paired with RMSE,
RôÙsquared provides additional insights into the modelãs explanatory power and faôÙ
cilitates assessment of its overall quality [85].
In the context of PLS models for fermentation monitoring, RôÙsquared values typiôÙ
cally range from 0.70 to 0.95, with values above 0.85 indicating strong explanatory
power. For example, an RôÙsquared value of 0.84 was reported for a PLS model preôÙ
dicting metabolite concentrations, reflecting a high level of explained variance and
indicating a robust model [91]. On the other hand, lower RôÙsquared values, such as
those around 0.70, indicate that the model explains a lesser degree of variance and
may require further refinement [74].
To create dependable and accurate prediction models, independent test sets and statistiôÙ
cal metrics like RMSE and RôÙsquared must be used for PLS model validation. By offering
a thorough assessment of the modelãs functionality, these techniques guarantee that it
performs effectively when applied to new data.

3.2
Univariate Mixtures: PLS 1 ôÙ Predictive Model trained by
Pure Component Synthetic Samples
The aim of this Subchapter is to calibrate the model based on MIR spectral data for accuôÙ
rate detection and quantification of key analytes such as acetate, ethanol, glucose, and
glycerol. For the calibration of the model, pure synthetic samples were constructed as
described in detail in the Subchapter 2.2.1. The acquired data include MIR spectra from
these samples, selected to represent different concentrations of the metabolites of interest
in the water background, providing a comprehensive dataset for model calibration. This
approach focuses on pure component samples to establish baseline spectral features for
each analyte without interference from other compounds.
Initially, a detailed examination of the MIR spectra from these pure synthetic samples was
conducted to identify and compare the spectral features corresponding to their respective
concentrations. These values were also compared to different literature sources, to idenôÙ
tify visually the correct peaks, and to familiarize ourselves with the noise of the instrument
and the water background, which is very strong in MIR analysis. The ultimate goal was to
obtain the unique absorption bands for ethanol, glucose, acetate and glycerol, allowing for
their identification and correct model calibration. Understanding spectral covariance is an
important matter in the beginning of the calibration attempts, ensuring that the developed
models will be highly tailored to each componentãs spectral features.
After the model calibration, the results were presented, showcasing how the presence and
absence of each compound influence the spectral features and the model performance.
Despite appearing wellôÙcalibrated due to their perfect fit, the models performed poorly
when predicting real fermentation samples. This indicates that the training data lacked
the necessary variability to effectively handle real fermentation data.
In summary, the calibration process described in this Subchapter was crucial for underôÙ
standing the spectral characteristics provided by the compounds of interest against the
water background and for initially calibrating the model. Analyzing spectral data from pure
synthetic samples laid the foundation for advancing model development, offering the opôÙ
portunity to get familiar with the compound spectra and the model requirements.

3.2.2
PLS 1 Discussion
The PLS 1 model, trained exclusively on synthetic data, exhibits excellent performance
on synthetic training data when calibrated, achieving almost perfect RôÙsquared values
and explaining many variations with a low component number. This indicates that the
model has successfully captured the patterns inherent in the synthetic samples. However,
when applied to realôÙworld fermentation data, significant discrepancies between expected
and measured ethanol concentrations emerge. These inadequacies align with research
showing that models trained solely on synthetic data often fail in realôÙworld scenarios due
to their inability to generalize the unseen data effectively [93]. Synthetic samples may
not fully represent the complex dynamics of fermentation processes, including factors like
temperature, background media, pH, and byôÙproduct accumulation, leading to overfitting
and poor predictive accuracy in real scenarios.
To address these issues, it is crucial to expand the training dataset with more represenôÙ
tative samples encompassing the variability found in actual fermentation processes. AdôÙ
ditional validation and calibration with real fermentation data are necessary to enhance
the model robustness. Incorporating real media in training samples can significantly imôÙ
prove the model ability to handle the complexity and diversity of realôÙworld applications,
as supported by studies emphasizing the importance of representative training sets for
PLS modeling [94], [95], [96].

applied, providing a satisfying tool for fermentation monitoring.

3.3.2
PLS 2 Discussion
The PLS 2 model shows significant improvements in predictive performance compared to
PLS 1. By training on synthetic samples of various compound combinations, mixed with
real media, the model better captures the complex interactions and variability present in
realôÙworld data. This approach leads to highly improved predictive accuracy on unseen
fermentation data, demonstrated by closer alignment with reference values and trends,
and reduced prediction errors. Diluting the mixed samples in a media matrix allows the
model to generalize more effectively, addressing most of the shortcomings of the PLS
1 model. The absence of the cells is noticeable though, because despite the improved
background composition, the real fermentation samples contain the cells which is a maôÙ
jor source of inaccuracies. The presence of cells in the fermentation broth is taken into
account in the next two models.
Studies support this approach, showing that diverse and representative training sets enôÙ
hance PLS model performance by accounting for matrix effects and variabilities [85], [86],
[97], [98]. The PLS 2 success highlights the importance of using training data that reflects
actual application conditions.

3.4.2
PLS 3 Discussion
The PLS 3 model, utilizing onôÙline spectral data from a real labôÙscale fermentation process,
demonstrates further improvements in predictive performance. By training exclusively on
realôÙtime spectral data, the model avoids the limitations of synthetic samples and captures
the full range of variability and noise present in fermentation processes. This results in the
most accurate predictions among the models, maintaining high prediction accuracy when
applied to unseen data from a different similar Fermentation (F2), even for acetate and
glycerol whose spectra are harder to capture. The low RMSE values reflect the modelãs
robustness against the intrinsic unpredictability and dynamic changes in the fermentation
environment.
The superior performance of the PLS 3 model can be attributed to several key factors:
ãÂ RealôÙTime Monitoring: Captures complex interactions and variability present in
realôÙworld scenarios, allowing the model to adapt to dynamic changes.
ãÂ Absence of Synthetic Samples: Avoids the limitations associated with synthetic
samples, ensuring more accurate representation of the fermentation environment.
ãÂ Presence of Cells: PLS 3 is the first model trained directly from onôÙline MIR spectra,
where the cells have not been centrifuged and removed. This supports the trainôÙ
ing process of the model, as the fermentation broth resembles exactly the spectra
collected from any fermentation process, as the F2, where they were later applied.

3.5
Multiple Datasets: PLS 4 ôÙ Predictive Model trained by the
Spectral Data from Pure & Mixed Synthetic Samples and
OnôÙline Data from Fermentation 1
The aim of this Subchapter is to calibrate and evaluate the predictive model based on MIR
spectral data for accurate detection and quantification of ethanol, glucose, acetate and
glycerol. The calibration of the PLS 4 model utilizes a comprehensive dataset comprising
pure component samples, mixed synthetic samples, and onôÙline spectral data from F1.
This thorough calibration process aims to enhance the model robustness and generalizôÙ
ability across different sample types, backgrounds and fermentation conditions.
The data used for the calibration includes the MIR spectra, mentioned in the previous
Subchapter 3.1, from pure synthetic samples, mixed samples with various concentrations
of the target metabolites, and realôÙtime spectral data collected during F1. By integrating
these diverse datasets, the PLS 4 model was expected to capture the spectral variations
and interactions among the studied components more effectively. The goal was to develop
a predictive model that can reliably monitor these metabolites in complex fermentation
environments.
Following the calibration, the PLS 4 model was applied to unseen data from Fermentation
2 to assess its predictive performance. The results were presented to demonstrate the
modelãs capability to track the different concentration profiles over time in a real fermenôÙ
tation process.
In summary, this Subchapter details the calibration and application of the PLS 4 model
using a combined dataset from pure, mixed, and F1 samples. Despite the thorough caliôÙ
bration process and comprehensive data used, the PLS 4 model did not manage to outôÙ
perform the previous models, PLS 2 and PLS 3, highlighting the advanced modeling reôÙ
quirements needed, when dealing with more complex datasets, during the training as well
as the validation process.

3.7.1
Impact of Data Quality
The quality of data, whether from offôÙline synthetic samples or onôÙline fermentation samôÙ
ples, can substantially impact the results of PLS models. Synthetic samples, while conôÙ
trolled and replicable, often fail to capture the full complexity and variability of real fermenôÙ
tation processes. Overfitting to the controlled settings, might result in models that perform
well on training data but suffer in realôÙworld applications.
Conversely, onôÙline fermentation samples provide realôÙtime data that more accurately reôÙ
flects the dynamic nature of fermentation processes. However, these samples can be
noisy and contain fluctuations that complicate model training and prediction. Despite rigôÙ
orous preprocessing efforts, some noise and outliers remain, which can affect the model
performance. The significance of utilizing relevant training data that encompasses the
entire spectrum of conditions seen in real fermentations is shown by the disparity in data
quality between synthetic and real samples.
3.7.2
Challenges in Data Acquisition
The data acquisition process, particularly for onôÙline MIR spectroscopy, faces several
limitations that can significantly impact the performance of PLS models in fermentation
monitoring and prediction. These limitations are multifaceted, affecting various aspects
of data quality and model reliability.
ãÂ Noise and Variability in Spectral Data
One of the primary challenges in data acquisition for PLS modeling is the presôÙ
ence of noise and variability in the spectral data. MIR spectra are inherently comôÙ
plex, with high variance and nonôÙlinear interactions that can complicate the modôÙ
eling process. Even with sophisticated preprocessing methods like SavitzkyôÙGolay
smoothing, residual outliers may still cause inaccuracy to some extent. PLS models
frequently find it difficult to fully capture the range of spectral variability due to the
intricacy of MIR spectra, especially at the extremes.
In the context of fermentation, the variability might include differences in batch condiôÙ
tions, fluctuations in measurement instruments, or inherent biological variations. On
the one hand, high variability complicates the modeling process because the model
must learn to generalize across diverse conditions, as observed with PLS 4. On
the other hand, training on a dataset with high variability can lead to a more robust
model that is better equipped to handle the unpredictability of realôÙworld scenarios.
From the PLS 4 results, we can conclude that it is imperative, that the modeling
techniques followed should be elevated when dealing with high variable datasets.
ãÂ Temporal and Spatial Variability
OnôÙline data acquisition introduces additional layers of complexity due to temporal
and spatial variability in the fermentation process. While spatial variability relates
to variations within the fermentation tank itself, temporal variability describes how
the fermentation environment evolves over time. The spectral data may become
inconsistent as a result of these circumstances, which makes it difficult for PLS
models to remain accurate at various times and locations across the fermentation
system.
ãÂ Instrumental Drift and Calibration

ãÂ Sampling Frequency and Resolution

ãÂ Data Integration and Synchronization

3.8.1
Tail Inaccuracies
PLS regression is wellôÙknown for its ability to handle noisy and collinear data. The inacôÙ
curacy seen at the prediction rangeãs tails, as seen, for instance, in Figures 3.18b, 3.21b
and 3.30, is a persistent problem with PLS models. The training data frequently underôÙ
represents these tails, which stand for the very high and very low ends of the response
variable and predictor distributions. Because of this underrepresentation, the model has
fewer data points to train from at these extremes, which causes underfitting at the tails and
overfitting in the core area where data is more plentiful [103]. PLS model performance
often deteriorates at these extremes, as crossôÙvalidation often demonstrates [104].
3.8.2
Continuous Offset Values
When using ATR ôÙ MIR spectroscopy for analytical measurements, one critical issue that
can arise is the presence of an offset between the predicted values from the instrument
and the reference values, as depicted for example in Figure 3.18a. This offset can often
be attributed to the physical setup of the MIR instrument and the layout of its sensitive
cables.
The ATRôÙMIR instrument relies on capturing light that interacts with the sample, and the
cable used in this setup is highly sensitive to positional changes. The placement and
movement of the instrument and its cables can introduce variations and baseline shifts
in the recorded spectra, leading to discrepancies in the predicted values [105]. This is
because the ATRôÙMIR setup involves a highly sensitive optical path where even minor
adjustments can significantly affect the light capture and, consequently, the measurement
accuracy. The observed spectra may be offset by even the smallest movement of the
wires or the device, which can alter the light path. Therefore, inaccurate predictions may
result from any movement of the cable without reôÙmeasuring the background spectrum
[106]. Because of its sensitivity, careful setup and handling techniques are required to
guarantee accurate and repeatable results. Thus, there is a direct correlation between
the offsets and baseline shifts and the setupãs physical dynamics. Even little adjustments
to the sensitive cablesã placement can vary the light they capture, which has a big effect on

3.8.3
Overfitting
Overfitting is a critical issue in predictive modeling that occurs when a model becomes too
complex and starts to capture the noise and random fluctuations in the training data inôÙ
stead of the underlying patterns. This phenomenon happens when a model is excessively
tailored to the specific characteristics of the training dataset, leading to high accuracy on
the training data but poor performance on new, unseen data.
In the context of PLS models, overfitting can occur for several reasons:
ãÂ High Model Complexity: When the number of components or latent variables in a
PLS model is too high, the model can start to fit the noise in the training data. PLS
models aim to find a linear regression model by projecting the predictors and the
response into a lowerôÙdimensional space. However, if too many components are
used, the model may capture irrelevant patterns and noise. This is the reason, that
the PLS models of this thesis have been designed using approximately 3 compoôÙ
nents, and not more than 6. By carefully examining the effect of more components
in each case, we managed to design each model version, which could capture the
best pattern and predictions, without adding too much noise.
ãÂ Insufficient or NonôÙrepresentative Training Data: If the training data does not
adequately represent the variability of realôÙworld conditions, the model may perform
well on the training data but fail to generalize. For example, training on synthetic

enrich the spectral signals.

3.8.5
Training on Low Analyte Concentrations
Models trained on very low concentrations of analytes, such as glucose, acetate, and
glycerol, are inherently expected to perform poorly due to several factors. In the conôÙ

back to the low signalôÙtoôÙnoise ratio associated with low analyte concentrations. When
the concentration of an analyte is very low, the resulting spectral signals are weak and
often buried in noise, making it challenging for the model to discern meaningful patterns
and relationships.
Qualitative insights from the spectroscopic data represent a strong argument in any chemoôÙ
metric context. For example, understanding the spectral features that correspond to low

els trained on such data. In this study, the qualitative examination of the spectral data

guishable from the background noise, leading to high RMSE values and poor predictive
performance. Moreover, these qualitative insights underscore the importance of developôÙ
ing models with robust preprocessing and data enhancement techniques. For instance,
methods that enhance signal strength or reduce noise can significantly improve the model
ability to accurately predict lowôÙconcentration analytes. In conclusion, the medium perforôÙ

ãÂ Preprocessing Spectral Data
Preprocessing the spectral data involved normalization, smoothing using the SavitzkyôÙ
Golay filter, computing the first derivative, and mean centering. Each technique adôÙ

2. SavitzkyôÙGolay Filter: Smoothed the spectral data, reducing noise while preôÙ

3. First Derivative Calculation: Enhanced the resolution of overlapping peaks and
eliminated baseline shifts.
4. Mean Centering: Centered the data around zero by subtracting the mean of
the first derivative values. This standard preprocessing step facilitated better
model performance by removing any constant offsetô .
