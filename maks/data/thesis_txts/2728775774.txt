Abstract
Preeclampsia is a hypertensive disorder of pregnancy that a↵ects 2-5% of pregnancies and is one of the
leading causes of maternal, fetal, and neonatal morbidity and mortality globally. The clinical course varies
widely among patients, but the disorder can progress rapidly. Preeclampsia patients are therefore followed
closely with weekly checkups and scans which is a burden for both the health care system and the patients.
Being able to predict the risk of adverse outcomes for individual patients and adapt their care accordingly
would therefore be very beneﬁcial.
This project focused on developing random forest models for the prediction of adverse outcomes for
patients with suspected preeclampsia using only routinely collected clinical and laboratory data. The models
were developed based on the Danish PEACH cohort which consists of data on 691 women with suspected
preeclampsia.
During the model development process, di↵erent imputation methods were tested, hyperparameters of
the random forest classiﬁer were tuned, and two di↵erent methods for feature selection were tried. One
where features were included in the model based on their individual AUC when used for predicting the
outcomes, and one where features were added randomly to the model through a Markov Chain Monte
Carlo process. At the same time, the importance of all included features was investigated, and individual
conditional expectation plots were created for the most important features.
The project resulted in random forest models with an AUC of 0.765 and 0.708 for the prediction of adverse
o↵spring and maternal outcomes, respectively. The most important features for predicting the o↵spring
outcomes were systemic vascular resistance, maternal height, level of urate in plasma, maternal weight,
gestational age at the time of suspected preeclampsia, and fetal abdominal circumference at gestational
week 20. While the most important features for the prediction of the maternal outcomes were mean systolic
blood pressure at the time of suspected preeclampsia, mean diastolic blood pressure at gestational weeks
27-30, age at delivery, and the level of alanine aminotransferase in plasma. The relation between predictions
and feature values was investigated and can possibly help cast light on new connections related to adverse
outcomes for suspected preeclampsia patients.

Introduction
Preeclampsia is a hypertensive disorder of pregnancy (HDP) that a↵ects 2-5% of pregnancies and results in
70,000 maternal and 500,000 fetal and neonatal deaths every year globally. It is one of the leading causes
of maternal, fetal, and neonatal morbidity and mortality [1]. Preeclampsia is characterised by new-onset
hypertension and organ dysfunction at any point after week 20 of gestation. Even though hypertension is a
central common symptom of the disorder, the clinical course varies widely among patients. Time of onset,
symptoms, progression speed, and outcome can vary greatly, and the disorder ranges from asymptomatic to
severe life-threatening disease [1, 2, 3].
Despite the severity of preeclampsia, treatment options for the disorder are limited [4]. Delivery is the
only cure and treatment during pregnancy focuses on the management of hypertension and any clinical
symptoms [5]. Close monitoring is crucial because of the unpredictable and potentially rapidly evolving and
deadly course of the disorder. Thus, patients are followed at least twice weekly with checkups and fetal
assessments [1]. This of course results in high costs for the health care system and is very time-consuming
for the patients [2, 6].
Being able to identify patients at increased risk of adverse outcomes and adapt their care accordingly
would thus be very beneﬁcial [3]. At the same time, outcome predictions can help identify patients at lower
risk and avoid unnecessary hospitalisations and costs.
Assessment of the risk of adverse outcomes for individual preeclampsia patients has historically been based
on expert opinion-based guidelines and groups of clinical characteristics. These have however been shown to
perform unsatisfactorily when predicting adverse outcomes for the patients [7, 8, 9, 10]. One of the things
making it hard to predict outcomes of preeclampsia is the still unknown aetiology of the disorder. At the
same time, many researchers believe that it is actually a group of similar disorders with potentially di↵erent
aetiology rather than one disorder [11]. This has led to the development of di↵erent models for better risk
assessment independent of the proposed aetiology.
Currently, the most-used model for maternal outcome prediction is the regression model, fullPIERS
[3, 7]. This model was developed and validated for women diagnosed with preeclampsia with the aim of
identifying adverse maternal outcomes within 48 hours of hospital admission. For these women, the model
has an area under the curve (AUC) of 0.88. Predictors used in the model are gestational age, chest pain
or dyspnea, oxygen saturation (SpO2), platelet count, creatinine, and aspartate transaminase (AST) level
[7]. This model is thus built on features that are easy to obtain in a clinical environment making it highly
usable. It is however only usable for already admitted patients and can thus not be used to assess the care
level for outpatients which is crucial as described above. At the same time, the adverse o↵spring outcomes
are not included in the model.
Also currently in use are the Prediction models for Risks of complications in Early-onset Pre-eclampsia
(PREP). PREP-L is a logistic regression model developed with the aim of assessing the overall risk of adverse
maternal outcomes for patients with early-onset preeclampsia from the point of diagnosis until postnatal
discharge [12]. During the development of the model, Thangaratinam et al. found that the most important
features for predicting the adverse outcomes were maternal age, gestational age, systolic blood pressure,
protein-to-creatinine ratio (urine), platelet count, serum urea concentration, SpO2, as well as treatment with
antihypertensive drugs and magnesium sulphate [12]. Again, this model does not take adverse outcomes for
the o↵spring into account, and it can only be used for women diagnosed with preeclampsia before 34 weeks
gestation.
Schmidt et al. have investigated the use of a gradient-boosted tree as well as a random forest (RF)
classiﬁer when predicting adverse outcomes of preeclampsia for both mother and o↵spring from the initial
visit until 14 days post-delivery [2]. Their gradient-boosted tree achieves an AUC of 0.82, while the RF
classiﬁer achieves an AUC of 0.77.
Using Shapley values, they ﬁnd that the features with the highest
impact on predictions are gestational age, maternal height, a soluble Fms-Like tyrosine kinase-1 (sFlt-1) to
placental growth factor (PlGF) ratio outside of the 95th percentile, sFlt-1-to-PlGF ratio multiples of the
median (MoM), sFlt-1 MoM, and PlGF deviations from the median [2]. A limitation of these models are the
use of both sFlt-1 and PlGF as these are not standard laboratory tests and require a resource-rich clinical
environment. They are thus not possible to collect for most hospitals in the world making these models
usable only in limited geographical regions.

Zheng et al. tested six di↵erent machine learning algorithms and concluded that a RF classiﬁer performed
best across all performance measures yielding an AUC of 0.967 for adverse neonatal outcomes and 0.963 for
adverse maternal outcomes [13]. The women included were already diagnosed with preeclampsia. At the
same time, the adverse neonatal outcomes were only predicted after birth excluding several intrauterine out-
comes and limiting the possibility of adapting the care level based on the risk for the o↵spring before delivery.
For the included adverse neonatal outcomes, the best predictors showed to be birth weight, triglyceride level,
urinary casts, early-onset type, and 24-h urinary protein. For the adverse maternal outcomes, 24-h urinary
protein, serum phosphorus, urinary casts, and cholesterol and triglyceride levels were the best predictors [13].
Even though several of these studies show promising results of using machine learning in outcome prediction
for preeclampsia, more research into the topic is needed to develop clinically useful and well-validated models
at all stages of the disorder as well as for all outcomes [2].
In this project, several random forest models for the prediction of adverse maternal and o↵spring out-
comes for suspected preeclampsia patients have been developed. This was done using data from the Danish
Preeclampsia, Angiogenesis, Cardiac dysfunction, Hypertension (PEACH) cohort. The aim of the project
was to investigate whether it is possible to predict adverse outcomes based on routinely collected clinical
and laboratory data in a Danish population - and which features are most important when doing so. The
outcomes were predicted at the point where preeclampsia was ﬁrst suspected in order to adapt the level of
care as early as possible. At the same time, patients under suspicion of preeclampsia have a higher risk of
developing adverse outcomes regardless of whether they ultimately receive the diagnosis or not, making it
relevant to predict the risk for all patients in this group and not just the ones who end up with the diagnosis
[14].

Theory
Before going into detail with the data and modelling process, I will present some background on preeclampsia,
adverse outcomes, random forest models, and the evaluation metric, AUC.
2.1
Preeclampsia
There is a lot of ongoing discussion and inconsistency surrounding the deﬁnition of preeclampsia within the
research community. Disagreements are found both in the classiﬁcation and management of preeclampsia
which has led to delays in research into both pathophysiology, e↵ective screening, and treatment of the
disorder [15]. This all leads to poorer pregnancy outcomes according to The International Society for the
Study of Hypertension in Pregnancy (ISSHP) [1]. Based on the most current evidence, ISSHP has therefore
created a new set of guidelines covering all HDPs. Their intent is to update the guidelines regularly so they
can act as an up-to-date and easy-to-use guideline for all health personnel working with these disorders [1].
The deﬁnition of preeclampsia in the ISSHP guidelines is:
”Preeclampsia is gestational hypertension accompanied by one or more of the following new-onset conditions
at or after 20 weeks gestation:
1. Proteinuria
2. Other maternal organ dysfunction, including:
(a) Acute kidney injury (creatinine ≥90µmol/L; 1 mg/dL)
(b) Liver involvement (elevated transaminases e.g. ALAT or AST > 40 IU/L) with or without right
upper quadrant or epigastric abdominal pain)
(c) Neurological complications (examples include eclampsia, altered mental status, blindness, stroke,
clonus, severe headaches, persistent visual scotomata)
(d) Haematological complications (thrombocytopenia – platelet count below 150, 000/µL, DIC, hemol-
ysis)
3. Uteroplacental dysfunction (such as fetal growth restriction, abnormal umbilical artery Doppler wave-
form analysis, or stillbirth)”
Where proteinuria is deﬁned as a 24h urinary protein ≥300 mg or a spot urine protein/creatinine ratio
≥30 mg per mmol [1].
Thus, preeclampsia is a sign- and symptom-based diagnosis that is not based on aetiology. As can be seen
from the ISSHP deﬁnition, clinical manifestations of preeclampsia are very diverse and include proteinuria,
headaches, visual disturbances, dyspnea, eclampsia, stroke, pulmonary oedema, and renal failure [16, 17].
2.1.0.1
Early- and Late-Onset Preeclampsia
Many researchers distinguish between early-onset and
late-onset preeclampsia. But as for the rest of the preeclampsia classiﬁcations, the timely distinction between
the two varies a lot in di↵erent papers. According to ISSHP, early-onset preeclampsia should be deﬁned as
preeclampsia before 34 completed weeks of pregnancy, i.e. 34 weeks + zero days [18]. With this deﬁnition,
early-onset preeclampsia constitutes 10 −15% of all preeclampsia cases [17].
The distinction between early- and late-onset preeclampsia is important because early-onset preeclampsia
is more likely to be associated with severe maternal and fetal complications. Late-onset on the other hand
is more likely to have milder symptoms and better outcomes [17, 19]. The distinction is however not always
clear-cut, and there is overlap between the presentation and outcomes of the two subtypes [18].
2.1.1
Aetiology
Several risk factors for preeclampsia are well-established. These include high maternal weight, advanced ma-
ternal age, previous preeclampsia, chronic hypertension, gestational diabetes, and pregnancy with multiples
[20, 21].
The aetiology behind preeclampsia is still incompletely understood [17]. Many researchers agree that
preeclampsia probably consists of several di↵erent subtypes with di↵erent aetiologies making it hard to

develop predictive tests and interventions to prevent disease [11]. The huge clinical heterogeneity in the
disease course of preeclampsia in regard to both onset, symptoms, a↵ected organs, and progression supports
this standpoint [17].
Currently, many researchers believe that the placenta plays a crucial role in the
development of preeclampsia [11, 17, 20, 22]. However, another popular theory is that preeclampsia arises
from poor cardiovascular adaption to pregnancy [17, 23, 24].
Both theories claim that preeclampsia is
triggered by hypoperfusion of the fetoplacental unit which results in the release of anti-angiogenic factors such
as sFlt-1 and PlGF as well as other vasoactive substances [20, 24]. These factors have been shown to cause
hypertension and proteinuria in humans and pregnant mice [25, 26]. At the same time, reducing placental
perfusion in non-human primates has been shown to induce an angiogenic imbalance and preeclampsia [27].
The two theories however propose di↵erent causes of this hypoperfusion.
According to the Two-Stage Placental Model ﬁrst introduced by Redman et al. in 1991 [28], the pla-
cental hypoperfusion is the ﬁrst stage of preeclampsia and is caused by placental abnormalities. These are
either due to poor placentation with incomplete spiral artery remodelling early in pregnancy (early-onset
preeclampsia) or to intraplacental malperfusion later in pregnancy (late-onset preeclampsia). In both cases,
the abnormalities are thought to lead to hypoperfusion and the release of anti-angiogenic factors described
above which triggers the second and symptomatic stage of preeclampsia [20, 28].
In the other well-established theory of the aetiology of preeclampsia, preeclampsia is considered a failed
cardiac stress test where the patient’s cardiovascular system cannot meet the demands of pregnancy [24]. The
model is based on research that has documented both functional and structural cardiac changes in patients
with preeclampsia. The excessive pregnancy demand combined with poor cardiac reserve are thought to
result in preeclampsia [17]. Due to this, fetal demands are thought to exceed the supply causing the release
of anti-angiogenic factors and other vasoactive substances described earlier [24].
There are however weaknesses and unexplained interrelations for both theories. Thus, more research is
deﬁnitely needed before any model behind the aetiology of preeclampsia can be ruled either in or out.
2.1.2

stable over weeks with only slow progression and end in a natural delivery at term. But it may also require
delivery within a few hours or days due to sudden progression to a point where it is life-threatening for
both mother and child [4, 29]. But overall, the probability of having an adverse outcome of the pregnancy
is higher in preeclampsia pregnancies [4].
As with the general deﬁnition of preeclampsia, the discussion is still ongoing in regard to the exact
outcomes of the disorder, and the reported outcomes vary greatly between studies [30].

2.1.3
Management
As described in the introduction, treatment options for preeclampsia are limited [4]. No matter the gesta-
tional age, delivery might be necessary in order to save the pregnant woman’s life.
The ISSHP guidelines for the management of preeclampsia include blood pressure monitoring, repeated
checks for proteinuria, clinical assessments, blood tests (haemoglobin and thrombocytes), as well as tests of
liver and renal function at least twice a week. In addition to this, the fetus should be monitored with serial
ultrasound surveillance to detect possible fetal growth restriction (FGR). Hypertension should be treated
according to the severity. Induction of labour is recommended from 37 weeks gestation or if patients develop
severe symptoms such as pulmonary oedema or progressive thrombocytopenia [1].
After delivery, patients should have their clinical condition and blood pressure monitored at least every
four hours (while awake) for the ﬁrst three days because of a high risk of preeclamptic complications [1].
All in all, the management of preeclampsia is very demanding and currently requires at least two check-
ups per week for all preeclampsia patients.
2.2
Decision Trees
A decision tree is a supervised machine learning algorithm that aims to predict the outcome class or value
of an observation. It does so based on a set of rules that is deﬁned based on a training data set [38]. The
resulting tree consists of a root node as well as several decision and leaf nodes. The root node represents the
entire sample which is split into smaller samples based on the rules at both the root and decision nodes. Each
leaf node is associated with an outcome. When making a prediction for a new observation, the observation
starts at the root node and travels through the tree based on the rules in the individual nodes along the
way until it ends up in a leaf node. The leaf node deﬁnes the predicted outcome for the observation. An
example of a simple decision tree can be seen in Figure 1.

Decision trees have the advantage of being intuitive and easy to interpret for the user. However, they are
prone to overﬁtting which makes them less generalisable [38, 39].
2.2.1
Building a Decision Tree
In order to build a decision tree, the algorithm needs to deﬁne the variable that should be used to split the
data at each node as well as the cut-o↵value for the decision.
The variable that should be used to split each node is the one that maximises information gain at this
point in the decision tree. Information gain is a measure of uncertainty, also called entropy. This means
that the algorithm chooses the variable that results in the highest reduction of uncertainty after a split.
Uncertainty - or entropy - for one node is measured by the below formula when considering two classes [38]:
E = −p ⇤log2(p) −q ⇤log2(q)
where p is the probability of an observation in the training data belonging to one class, and q is the probability
of it belonging to the other class - at this point in the tree. The information gain is the di↵erence in entropy
before and after splitting a node:
Information Gain = Eparent node −Echild nodes
where Echild nodes is the average entropy in the child nodes resulting from the split. The entropy of each
child node should be weighted in the average based on the number of observations belonging to this node
[38].
The Gini impurity is generally used for determining the cut-o↵values of variables when building decision
trees for classiﬁcation [40]. Gini impurity is a measure of the extent of inequality within a node. The most
pure node is one where all observations belong to one class while the most impure node is the one where
the observations are equally divided between the classes. Gini impurity in one node when considering two
classes is deﬁned as:
Gini impurity = 1 −(p2 + q2)

where p and q are the probabilities associated with the two classes in this node [38]. When choosing the
cut-o↵value, the overall impurity of a split is used, meaning the total impurity in the two child nodes that
this split would result in:
Overall impurity = Impurityleft ⇤#observationsleft + Impurityright ⇤#observationsright
Total #observations
The value which has the least overall weighted impurity is chosen for the split [38].
If the impurity in a node is > 0, it can be split again. However, splitting until all nodes are pure can
lead to overﬁtting which reduces the generalisibility of the decision tree. The more (pure) terminal nodes,
the higher accuracy for the training data, but at some point, more (pure) terminal nodes will lead to lower
accuracy on the test data. In order to avoid overﬁtting, the splitting process should stop when the accuracy
of the validation data does not improve anymore [38].
2.3
Random Forest
Random forest (RF) is a machine learning method used for classiﬁcation and regression problems. The RF
algorithm is based on ensemble learning and thus gives its prediction by combining the output of multiple
decision trees.
Each decision tree is built from a random sample of data, and the decision trees all have a vote with
regard to the prediction for a new observation. Together, this approach is called bagging which is short for
bootstrap aggregating. Bootstrap refers to selecting a random sample from data, and aggregating refers to
taking the average of the predictions made by the individual decision trees. The resulting algorithm can be
seen below [39].
1. For each decision tree:
(a) Sample x observations from the original data set (with replacement)
(b) Sample y features from the original data set
(c) Build a decision tree based on the subset of x observations and y features
(d) Make a prediction based on the validation or test set
2. Find the ﬁnal prediction of the RF as an average of the predictions made by the individual decision
trees
In regression problems, the prediction of the RF will be the mean of all the target values predicted by the
individual decision trees. In classiﬁcation problems, the prediction can either be the probability for each
class based on the proportion of the decision trees that have predicted the class - or it can consist of the
so-called majority vote. This means that the ﬁnal prediction will be the class that most of the decision trees
determined [40].
By combining multiple trees, the RF overcomes the general problem of overﬁtting when using single
decision trees [39].
2.3.1
Hyperparameters
The hyperparameters of the RF algorithm include the number of trees, the number of features sampled for
each tree, and the fraction of training data sampled for each tree. Decision tree parameters are also required
e.g., the maximum depth of each tree, the minimum number of observations required to split a node, and
the maximum number of leaves in the tree [40].

of classiﬁcation models. The receiver operating characteristics (ROC) curve is a probability curve created
by plotting the True Positive Rate (TPR) of a model against the False Positive Rate (FPR) at di↵erent
threshold values. These are deﬁned as [41]:
TPR =
True Positives
True Positives + False Negatives

predicting the reversed classes than the real classes, predicting 1s as 0s and 0s as 1s.

Data: The PEACH Study
The data used in this project came from the PreEclampsia, Angiogenesis, Cardiac dysfunction, Hypertension
(PEACH) Study conceived in 2016-2018 and led by the Department of Epidemiology Research at Statens
Serum Institut, Copenhagen, Denmark [14]. PEACH is a prospective longitudinal cohort study established
with the aim of studying HDPs and their consequences for the mother and their o↵spring both short-term
and long-term. The goal of PEACH is to combine molecular, genetic, clinical, and cardiac proﬁles of women
with and without preeclampsia and hereby hopefully identify new preeclampsia subtypes. This could e.g.,
help physicians in their assessment of women presenting with suspected preeclampsia allowing for more
adequate follow-up and treatment.
The full cohort consists of 1101 cis-women, of whom 691 were enrolled with suspected preeclampsia
and 410 were enrolled without suspicion of any HDPs. All women were followed by the Departments of
Obstetrics at either Copenhagen University Hospitals Rigshospitalet or Hvidovre Hospital (Denmark). The
women without HDPs were enrolled after their routine 20-week anomaly scan. The women with suspected
preeclampsia were enrolled after referral to one of the obstetrics departments for clinical evaluation due to
suspected preeclampsia. This referral could happen at any time between 20 weeks of gestation and delivery.
However, the mean gestational age at enrollment for these women was 36.7 weeks. The referrals were based
on signs and symptoms of preeclampsia such as elevated blood pressure, headache, and visual disturbances.
Women were however only enrolled in the suspected preeclampsia group if a physician upon referral chose to
order a package of preeclampsia-related blood work, emphasising the suspicion of preeclampsia. This means
that pregnant women who were referred due to signs and symptoms but where the evaluating physician
ruled out preeclampsia without ordering blood tests were not included in the study [14]. Before enrollment,
women (in both groups) were also screened for eligibility according to the following criteria:
1. Age between 18 and 45 years
2. Ability to understand spoken and written Danish
3. Address in Greater Copenhagen
4. Singleton pregnancy of at least 20 weeks duration
5. No pre-existing chronic hypertension or elevated blood pressure noted before week 12 (in the absence
of documented white coat hypertension [42])
6. No use of anti-coagulant medications, including low-dose aspirin
7. No diagnosis of preeclampsia more than two weeks before recruitment [14]
Women in both groups were followed with regular blood samples and cardiac function measurements as
well as placenta samples and postpartum follow-up measurements. In addition to this, clinical and personal
information such as comorbidities, parity, and medications were obtained from the women’s medical records.
This also included medical examinations and samples taken in other contexts during the pregnancy (e.g.,
due to suspicion of other diseases than preeclampsia). The variables of the full data set and the schedule
for obtainment of samples for the two groups respectively, are further described in [14].
Of the 691 women with suspected preeclampsia at enrollment, 310 were diagnosed with preeclampsia,
and 172 were diagnosed with gestational hypertension. Among the 410 women with healthy pregnancies at
enrollment, 37 were diagnosed with a HDP [14].

Methods
In this section, I will present and explain the methods used throughout the project. First, I have included
some general comments, and afterwards, I will go into detail about the individual methods.
All data preparations and plotting were done in R while modelling was done in Python. All RF classiﬁca-
tion models were trained using the RandomForestClassiﬁer from the scikit-learn (Sklearn) package [43] using
Gini impurity to measure the quality of the splits. The outcomes were predicted using the predict proba
function which predicts the probability that the observation belongs to each class based on the number of
decision trees in the forest that vote for each.
All models in this thesis will be evaluated based on their AUC under the ROC-curve. This will just be
referred to as the AUC of the models. Any AUCs < 0.5 have been transformed to AUC = 1 −AUC.
I will often refer to the results ”of a test set”. This refers to results obtained based on both the training
set, validation set, and test set of a given test set. Meaning that the results are not necessarily obtained
based on the test set itself - but on the split of data that the test set represents. The splits and CV procedure
leading to the results will be further described in Section 4.2.
4.1
Outcome Categories
In order to investigate the use of machine learning models to predict outcomes of preeclampsia, outcomes
need to be deﬁned for every woman in the data set. As mentioned, the outcomes used in this project were
inspired by the outcomes deﬁned in a consensus study by Du↵y et al. (Table 1) [31]. It was however not
possible to include all the o↵spring core outcomes on the PEACH data due to missing variables. In addition
to the outcomes from Du↵y et al., several both maternal and o↵spring outcomes that were deemed relevant
in a Danish context were added. The deﬁnition of each outcome (e.g., threshold values) is based on Du↵y
et al.’s deﬁnitions in [32] as well as deﬁnitions by The Danish Paediatric Society and The Danish Society of
Obstetrics and Gynaecology. The number of patients experiencing each included outcome was counted and
can be seen in Appendix, Table 24. As many of the individual outcomes were very rare among the women
participating in the PEACH study, it was decided to make outcome categories instead of trying to predict the
individual outcomes. The 46 individual outcomes were divided into soft, moderate, and hard categories for
mother and o↵spring, respectively. This division was based on the reality in the Danish obstetrics divisions
and can also be seen in Table 24.
Each woman in the data set can be in none, one, two, or all of the categories based on her own and her
child’s outcomes. If for example, she has su↵ered from two di↵erent maternal outcomes where one belongs
in the soft category and one in the hard category, she will be labelled as having su↵ered both a soft and a
hard maternal outcome and therefore be present in two categories.
The focus throughout this project will be on predicting the hard o↵spring outcomes and the combined
hard and moderate maternal outcomes. All outcomes have been modelled, but since showing the results for
all outcome categories would be too much to include in this project, the focus will be on the two categories
that yielded the most valuable results. At the same time, being able to predict these two outcome categories
would be of great clinical value - e.g., greater than predicting the soft outcomes.
4.2
Five-Fold Cross-Validation
Five-fold cross-validation (CV) was used to assess the performance of di↵erent models throughout most of
the following analyses. Therefore, I will describe the general CV procedure used before going into detail
with the individual analyses.
First, an outcome category to predict was chosen and saved as the y-vector of the data set. All other
outcome category columns were removed from the data set. The data set and the y-vector were then split
into ﬁve equal parts (four parts consisting of 138 observations and one part consisting of 139 observations).
The split was done randomly, but the same splits were maintained throughout all analyses.
When performing the CV, each part took turns being held out as the test set. For each test set, the
remaining four parts took turns being the validation set while the rest (3/5 parts of data) was used as the
training set. The missing values of both the training set, validation set, and test set were imputed based on
this training set. This means that e.g., for the Majority Method, the most represented value of a feature
across the training set in one round was used to impute missing values of this feature in both the training

4.3
Imputation Method
Four di↵erent methods were tested and compared in order to decide how to impute the missing values of
the data set:
• Zeros Method: All missing values in data were imputed with zeros.
• Majority Method: All missing values were imputed with the most represented value of the column
they belong to.
• Zeros and Majority Method: All missing values for binary features were imputed with zeros. All
missing values for continuous features were imputed with the most represented value of the column
they belong to.
• Majority and Median Method: All missing values for binary features were imputed with the
most represented value of the column they belong to. All missing values for continuous features were
imputed with the median of the column they belong to.
Five-fold CV as described above was used to assess the four methods. The whole CV procedure was carried
out for all four imputation methods. The method with the highest validation AUC for each test set was
recorded.
The whole CV process was run 100 times for each imputation method resulting in the recording of the
best method for 500 test sets (because the best method for each of the ﬁve test sets was counted in each of
the 100 runs). This was done in order to determine which imputation method yielded the highest validation
AUC most often - and thereby, which imputation method should be used. The resulting counts for the hard
o↵spring outcomes and the combined hard and moderate maternal outcomes can be seen in Results Section

4.5
Clustering Based on Correlations
The PEACH data contains many correlated features which a↵ect the results of the feature importance and
feature selection methods below. To be able to analyse these results properly, I chose to create clusters
based on the correlations between features and use these in the analyses.
First, the full data set without outcome columns was imputed. I created one version of clusters based
on imputation with the Zeros and Majority Method and another version based on imputation with the
Majority Method in order to accommodate the results for both the hard o↵spring and hard and moderate
maternal outcomes where these two imputation methods are used for all other analyses.
A correlation matrix for each method based on Spearman correlations was created, and KMeans clus-
tering was run on these using Sklearn’s KMeans function [44]. The number of clusters that the function
should ﬁnd was varied from 1 to 152. Default parameters were used including the Lloyd algorithm. After-
wards, the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS) of each cluster combination was plotted in elbow plots.
The optimal number of clusters for each method was decided based on the elbow plots and the usability of
clusters, meaning not too many to keep an overview of all the clusters and not too few to keep an overview
of the features within each cluster. The ﬁnal clusters were found using the KMeans function with the chosen
number of clusters as input.

With the purpose of exploring the data set and investigating the impact of each feature on modelling, I found
the individual AUC, built-in random forest feature importance (RF feature importance), and permutation
feature importance of each feature for each of the ﬁve test sets used throughout the analyses. The methods
for computing each of these are described below.
Afterwards, I compared the top 30 features of each method for each test set with each other in order to
see whether the methods found the same features important. This was done via Venn-diagrams that can be

[45].
Individual AUCs and feature importances can be used to reduce the feature space of a model. In order

validation and test AUCs for each test set as well as one overall mean test AUC for the combined test set

4.6.1
AUCs for Individual Features
One way to investigate the impact of each feature on the RF classiﬁer model is to look at the feature’s
individual AUC when used as a prediction for the outcomes. I found these AUCs for each of the ﬁve test
sets. For each test set, I extracted the raw feature from the full training set (the remaining 4/5 parts of
data not used as the test set) and imputed the missing values. The extracted feature column was used as
the y-prediction. The AUC of the feature was then found by comparing this prediction to the real y-vector
of the training set.
This resulted in one AUC for each feature for each of the ﬁve test sets. The top 30 for two representative
test sets can be seen in Results Section 5.4.
4.6.2
Random Forest Feature Importance
The RF classiﬁer from Sklearn has a built-in feature importance estimator [46]. This works by ﬁnding the
mean decrease in impurity caused by each feature while building the RF. For every split where a speciﬁc
feature is chosen to split on, the drop in impurity is saved. After building the complete forest, the mean
drop in impurity across all splits in the forest is found for each feature. These mean drops in impurity for
all features are subsequently weighted so the feature importances for all features sum to 1.
For each of the ﬁve test sets, I imputed the missing values of and trained a model on the 4/5 parts
of data not used as the test set and extracted these RF feature importances for all features. The top 30
features for two representative test sets can be seen in Results Section 5.4.
4.6.3
Permutation Feature Importance
The RF feature importance has some disadvantages which will be discussed in Discussion Section 6.2.3.
Therefore, I decided to also compute the permutation feature importances of the model in order to see if
these overlapped with the RF feature importances (and the individual features’ AUCs) [47].
The permutation feature importances were computed using ﬁve-fold CV. A baseline AUC was found
for each validation set by training a model on the corresponding training set with all features and default
parameters. This model was used to make a y-prediction based on the validation set that was compared
to the real y-vector of the validation set. Both training set and validation sets were imputed based on the
training set. Afterwards, each feature of the validation set was shu✏ed 20 times (by random rearrangement
of the values of the chosen feature) and a y-prediction was made for each of these new validation sets. The
AUCs of these predictions were also found by comparing them to the real y-vector of the validation set.
Then, the di↵erence between each of these AUCs and the baseline AUC was found. This resulted in 20
AUC-di↵erences per validation set and therefore 80 AUC-di↵erences per test set per feature. These are the
permutation importances of that feature. All the permutation importances for each feature for each test
set were plotted in a boxplot and sorted based on the mean permutation feature importance. The top 30
permutation feature importances for representative test sets can be seen in Results Section 5.4.

4.8
Feature Selection
I tried two di↵erent methods for choosing the feature subset of the models. These methods are described
below.
4.8.1
Based on Individual Feature AUC
At ﬁrst, I investigated the performance of feature selection based on the individual features’ AUCs in ﬁve-
fold CV as described in Section 4.2. This type of feature selection was done to investigate the number of
features needed to obtain a good performance of the RF classiﬁer.
The AUC of using each individual feature as a prediction for the outcome variable was found for each
test set based on the 4/5 parts of data not in the test set. This was done to obtain comparable feature
subsets across the validation sets of each test set. Features were added to the applied feature subset one
by one, starting with the feature with the highest individual AUC. However, features with a correlation
> |0.7| with any already added feature were skipped in order to avoid increasing complexity without adding
additional information to the model. For each feature combination, a model was trained for each validation
set (based on the 3/5 parts of data used as the training set). This model was used to make predictions for
both the validation set and the test set as described in Section 4.2, yielding one validation AUC and one
test AUC per test set per feature combination. These AUCs were plotted as a function of the number of
features included. The plots for up to 30 features can be seen in Results Section 5.6.
Afterwards, the overlapping features of all ﬁve test sets were found and plotted using the Python package
VennDiagram [45].
4.8.2
Markov Chain Monte Carlo
In order to investigate whether the combination of features was more important than the individual feature’s
predictive value (AUC), I also used a Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulation for feature selection.
I ran 1000 steps for each test set using the same feature subset for all validation sets in each step. In the
ﬁrst step for each test set, I drew one random feature. Training, validation, and test set were imputed and
reduced to only contain this feature. A model was trained for each of the four reduced training sets and used
to make a prediction for the corresponding validation set as well as the test set. As described in Section 4.2,
a validation AUC based on the concatenated validation set predictions and a test AUC based on the mean
prediction for the test set were found. In each following step, one out of three actions was performed with
equal probability: A random feature was added, a random feature was removed, or a random feature was
swapped with a new random feature. The only requirement for the added features was that they did not
have a correlation > |0.7| with any of the features already in the feature subset. Models were run in each
step (and thus for each feature combination), and the validation and test AUCs were found as described
in the ﬁrst step. Whether or not to accept the new feature subset and continue with this was determined
based on the di↵erence in validation AUC between the last accepted step (AUC0) and the proposed step
(AUC1). When doing this comparison, all AUCs were reduced with λ = 0.00001 per feature in the applied
feature subset. This served as a penalty for adding features with the goal of ﬁnding the best and most
simple feature combination for each test set.
If AUC1 > AUC0, the step was always accepted. If AUC1 AUC0, the step was only accepted with a

4.10
Partial Dependence and Individual Conditional Expectation
To investigate the e↵ect of the most important features in the data set with regard to predicting the outcomes,
I found the individual conditional expectations of each of these features. This was done by making several
predictions for each observation where the chosen feature was varied for each prediction while all other
features were kept constant [48]. This was again done using ﬁve-fold CV and the predictions of the four
validation sets for each test set were plotted together in Individual Conditional Expectation (ICE) plots.
In practice, a model was trained for each training set consisting of 3/5 parts of data as described before.
The feature subset of the best model for each outcome was used. Sklearn’s partial dependence function
was then applied to the corresponding validation set to make the predictions [49]. This function ﬁnds all
possible feature values of the chosen feature in the validation set and then makes a prediction for each
observation with each of the possible feature values - resulting in as many predictions per observation as
there are di↵erent feature values in the validation set.
For the continuous features, I centred the predictions for each observation to obtain a more readable plot.
This was done by ﬁnding the prediction of the lowest feature value for each observation and subtracting this
from all other predictions for the same observation. These centred predictions were afterwards plotted as a
function of the feature values used, resulting in one line per observation, all starting at y = 0.
Afterwards, I found the rolling average of the centred predictions for each feature value based on all
observations in the four validation sets of each test set. A rolling average was used since not all feature
values were present in all validation sets causing spikes in the average line (due to internal di↵erences in
predicted probabilities for the four validation sets). The rolling average was plotted on top of the individual
observations’ lines in order to visualise the average development of predictions as a function of feature value.
All resulting plots can be seen in Results Section 5.9 and interpreted with regard to the outcomes in
Discussions Section 6.4.

5.6
Feature Selection: Based on Individual Feature AUC
In order to investigate the number of features needed to obtain a good performance of the RF classiﬁer, I
tested two di↵erent methods of feature selection (in addition to selecting features based on feature importance
as described above). The results of the second method will be shown in Section 5.7.
In the ﬁrst method, I sampled features based on their individual AUCs. For each test set, the individual
AUCs of all features of the remaining 4/5 parts of data were found. For each validation set, one feature
was added at a time beginning with the feature with the highest individual AUC. Features were however
skipped if they had a correlation > |0.7| with any of the already added features. A model was trained on
the training set and evaluated on both validation set and test set for each feature combination. The method
is more thoroughly described in the Method Section 4.8.

5.7
Feature Selection: Markov Chain Monte Carlo
To investigate whether the combination of features is as or even more important than each feature’s individ-
ual AUC, I did a feature selection based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo. The chains were run independently
for each test set, but dependently on the validation sets of each test set. Meaning that a random action (add
feature, remove feature, or swap feature) and the involved feature(s) were drawn and models were trained
and run with this feature set for all training set/validation set combinations of a test set. The features were
drawn completely random, but steps, where the feature to add was highly correlated (correlation > |0.7|)
with features already in the feature subset, were skipped. 1000 steps were run for each test set. Whether
to accept or reject the action of a step was determined based on the validation AUC di↵erence from the
last accepted step to the proposed step. A penalty of 0.00001 per feature was subtracted from the AUCs
in order to favour feature subsets with fewer features. The full method and how the steps were evaluated
more concretely are described in Methods Section 4.8.2.

Partial Dependence and Individual Conditional Expectation
In this section, I will show the ICE-plots of the most important features when predicting the hard o↵spring
and combined hard and moderate maternal outcomes of preeclampsia.
The plots are done using ﬁve-fold CV. For each validation set of each test set, a model was trained on
the corresponding training set (3/5 parts of data). The overlapping features of the top 30 features from
the feature selection based on individual AUC were used as these resulted in the best model performance
in Section 5.8. This model was used to make a prediction for every individual observation in the validation
set with every possible value of the chosen feature. The possible values correspond to all present values
of the feature in the validation set. This means that the values are not necessarily completely the same
for the di↵erent validation sets. All other features were kept constant. This allows for investigation of the
development in the prediction for the individual observation conditioned on the chosen feature which can
give valuable clinical insights. E.g., the ICE-plots can possibly aid in establishing cut-o↵s for the di↵erent
features that can be used in clinical decision-making.
The development for the individual observations of all four validation sets for each test set are plotted
together. The predictions are centred around zero, meaning that the prediction of the lowest feature value
for an observation has been subtracted from all other predictions for that observation. This is done in order
to see the development in predictions instead of the actual size of each prediction which would make it
harder to catch general trends.
In addition to these individual lines, a rolling average of the centred predictions is plotted on top (the
red line in the plots). The rolling average was applied in order to avoid big ﬂuctuations in the average due
to di↵erent feature values being present in di↵erent validation sets.
Small ticks are added at the bottom of each plot showing the 2.5%, 25%, 50%, 75%, and 97.5% percentiles
of the feature values present in the combined validation sets of the shown test set. These are included in
order to show when the predictions are performed for feature values that the model has rarely been trained
on.

6
Discussion
In this section, I will discuss the methods and results of the project as well as evaluate the model performance
and discuss the clinical explanations for the di↵erent important features.
6.1
Data Limitations
I will start out by discussing the utilised data and how it a↵ects the model development and usability.
The designers of the PEACH study state several limitations of the data set which will of course a↵ect
the results of my project.
1. Women with early-onset preeclampsia and other severe forms of preeclampsia are underrepresented in
the cohort. Many of these were excluded due to chronic hypertension and aspirin therapy, and others
were never enrolled because they were admitted urgently.
2. Many women (57%) were enrolled in the study at term where induction of labour is recommended as
soon as a HDP diagnosis is conﬁrmed [1]. This and other factors leading to di↵erent numbers and kinds
of samples obtained from each women (e.g., due to women not showing up for their PEACH appoint-
ments and/or having di↵erent kinds of examinations done in other contexts during their pregnancies)
result in a lot of missing values for some features in the data set.
3. Participants in PEACH di↵ered from non-participants with respect to socioeconomic factors: Partic-
ipants had longer educations and higher household incomes. At the same time, understanding both
spoken and written Danish was a prerequisite for participation in the study. This combination may
limit the generalisability of the PEACH data and studies based on this [14].
Points number one and three both a↵ect the generalisability of my ﬁndings with regard to who the model
can be used for as well as the clinical interpretation of important features.
It is possible that the important and chosen features of this study are not the most important or predictive
features for women with more severe forms of preeclampsia. However, women in this group who are e.g.,
admitted urgently, are probably easier to assess in regard to the risk of an adverse outcome as they are
already very sick at the time of arrival. At the same time, the women with severe preeclampsia are probably
already at the hospital due to present symptoms, and an assessment of whether or not to admit them or do
thorough follow-ups is not needed. This would of course have to be investigated thoroughly, but one could
argue that a model is more necessary as an assessment tool for the less sick women that are not already
admitted or undergoing treatment with e.g., aspirin. At the same time, the models currently in use focus
on women with more severe forms of preeclampsia who are already admitted to the hospital [7] or who get
the diagnosis before 34 weeks gestation [12]. The development of a model that predicts the outcomes for a
broader group of women with milder presentations and later-onset disease is therefore very relevant.
In regard to point number three, the women included in PEACH are probably the women who can easier
manoeuvre in the Danish health care system which could have an e↵ect on their care level and therefore
their risk level - and thus probably on the probability of experiencing an adverse outcome as well as on
the important features indicating adverse outcomes in the model. The impact of this on the model results
would be interesting to investigate further, and the fact that it is currently unknown should be taken into
account when evaluating the model.
Point number two is one of the reasons the feature space is greatly reduced when removing features
with more than 20% missing values as seen in Section 3.2. This of course a↵ects the model as potentially
important and informative features are removed from the data set before modelling. However, including the
features and imputing the missing values could result in misleading results, and it would be hard to assess
the actual importance of features with many missing values. Thus, the most important features found in
this study can of course only be interpreted as such in regard to the rest of the available feature subset and
not necessarily to all possible preeclampsia- or pregnancy-related features.
Another point with regard to the generalisability of the results is that all women in the data set were
enrolled on suspicion of preeclampsia, but only 45% ultimately received the diagnosis while 25% received a
diagnosis of gestational hypertension. At the same time, the included blood samples and cardiac measure-
ments are from enrollment ±7 days. This of course a↵ects the subset of women that the model can be used

6.2
Machine Learning Methods
The RF classiﬁer model was chosen as this is generally e↵ective when solving classiﬁcation tasks [39]. At the
same time, it is somewhat interpretable in regard to important features which was deemed relevant in this
context where it is clinically interesting to ﬁnd out more about the aetiology behind preeclampsia and the
adverse outcomes of the disorder. In addition to this, two of the previous studies performed using machine
learning to predict adverse outcomes of preeclampsia showed good performance for the RF classiﬁer [2, 13].
The best AUC of any of the trained RF classiﬁers is 0.765. It could be interesting to see whether other
machine learning methods could provide better predictions on the PEACH data. E.g., it could be interesting
to try a gradient-boosted tree as this showed slightly better performance than the RF classiﬁer in [2], and
a support vector machine as this showed slightly better performance on the maternal adverse outcomes in
[13].
6.2.1
Hyperparameter Tuning
In Section 5.5, the results of trying di↵erent values for four di↵erent hyperparameters of the RF classiﬁer
were presented. Except for a small rise in performance from using 10 to 100 trees in the forest, they all
show that the hyperparameters did not make a di↵erence. Thus, I chose to run all models with default
parameters (100 trees is the default).
There are several other parameters in the Sklearn RF classiﬁer that could potentially be tuned (e.g., the
minimum number of samples per leaf and maximum number of leaves). However, since the ﬁrst four tested
hyperparameters did not make a di↵erence, it seemed as if the signal in the data was not a↵ected by the
hyperparameters of the classiﬁer. Thus, I chose not to spend time looking into the rest of the parameters
or combinations of parameters.
6.2.2
Imputation Method
Based on the results in Section 5.1, I chose to use the Zeros and Majority Method when imputing missing
values before training models to predict the hard o↵spring outcomes, and the Majority Method when
predicting the hard and moderate maternal outcomes.
It is interesting that the Zeros and Majority Method is better than the Majority method for the hard
o↵spring outcomes - and almost as good for the hard and moderate maternal outcomes. However, as shown
in Section 5.1, the majority value of the vast majority of binary features was 0. At the same time, one could
imagine that many of the missing values should be zero as the probability of the health care personnel e.g.,
not recording the presence or absence of a symptom would be higher if the symptom is absent (and thus
the feature would be 0). Very few of the continuous features have a majority or median value close to zero
which is probably why the Zeros Method is outperformed by the other methods.

6.2.3
Feature Importance
In general, I found some overlap between important features when comparing the three methods of investi-
gating feature importance (the individual feature AUCs, the RF feature importance, and the permutation
feature importance). However, 1/4 −1/3 of the features found by each method was not present for the
other methods. This proportion decreased substantially when looking at the overlap between clusters of
correlated features instead of between the individual features.
The RF feature importances are easy to compute and interpret. However, they are sometimes criticised
for being inaccurate for models that overﬁt on the training set (because they are found based on the
training set) and for favouring continuous and high-cardinality features. Thus, some argue that the RF
feature importances are not appropriate when dealing with predictors that vary in scale and cardinality
[50]. The PEACH data set contains features that are very di↵erent in scale and cardinality which led me
to investigate the e↵ect of these on the feature importances by also computing the permutation feature
importances. The latter can however be very a↵ected by correlations as it can mask the importance of a
shu✏ed feature if the information of this feature is also present in another feature. This seems to happen
for my data as the top 30 features when calculating the permutation feature importances come from many
di↵erent clusters, which indicates that only one feature out of a group of correlated features present as
important. On the other hand, the RF feature importances represent fewer clusters, indicating that these
are not as a↵ected by correlations.
I tried to overcome these individual cons of the methods by looking into the overlap between the two
methods and the individual AUCs. It seems that the feature subsets found here consist of actual important
features as the models based on them have almost as good a performance as the baseline model.
6.2.4
Feature Selection
I tested two methods of feature selection in order to investigate whether the individual features’ AUCs or
speciﬁc combinations of features were most important for the model performance. The feature selection
process based on Monte Carlo resulted in higher validation AUCs than the feature selection based on
individual feature AUC. Indicating that the combination of features can possibly out-compete the features
with the highest individual AUCs. However, the test AUCs did not follow and thus the selected feature
subsets did not seem to generalise well indicating that the models overﬁtted the validation sets in this
method. On the other hand, the feature subsets of the feature selection based on individual feature AUC
seemed to generalise well as the test AUCs matched the validation AUCs. Even though this method resulted
in lower validation AUCs, it, therefore, seems like the most appropriate method of feature selection for this
data set. At the same time, there were no overlapping features between the feature subsets chosen for each
test set with the Monte Carlo method. This again indicates that the chosen features based on the validation
sets do not generalise well to other parts of the data.
A way of optimising the Monte Carlo algorithm could be to limit the feature space from the beginning.
As it is implemented here, the feature space consists of 152 features which is probably part of the reason why
there are no overlapping features between the ﬁve test sets. A way of limiting the feature space could be to
only include the feature with the highest individual feature AUC from each of the clusters found based on
feature correlations. This could probably create a bigger overlap between chosen features and thus maybe
take both the most predictive features and their combination into account.
The feature selection based on individual feature AUC showed that 15-20 features were suﬃcient to achieve
an optimal model performance for both outcomes.
The overlaps between features chosen for the ﬁve test sets were considerable, but as for the feature
importance, the overlaps were bigger for the clusters than for the individual features. When running the
feature selection, features that were highly correlated with already added features were skipped. This could
cause di↵erences in the chosen features due to di↵erences in the order of the feature AUCs. Thus, if e.g.,

6.3
Model Evaluation and Interpretation
The models with the overlapping features of the top 30 features with the highest AUC for each test set
showed the best performances. These yielded mean AUCs for the concatenated test sets of 0.765 and 0.708
for the hard o↵spring and combined hard and maternal outcomes, respectively. Using the overlapping 16
and 19 features, respectively, for all test sets performed better than using the di↵erent top 16 and 18 features
for the individual test sets. This shows that the overlapping features probably generalise better to the test
sets than the features with the highest validation AUC for each training set do. It is however worth noting
that there were 19 overlapping features for the maternal outcomes and I only used 18 individual features
for each test set since the feature selection process showed that 18 should be adequate. The use of one
more feature could of course be part of the reason why the overlapping features perform better than the
individual ones.
For the maternal outcomes, the model with only the 9 overlapping features from the top 18 features for
each test set however showed better performance than the individual top 18 features. This suggests that the
overlapping features do in fact generalise better - and therefore that the di↵erence in the number of features
described above is not the reason for the better-performing model.
For both outcomes, the best models found through the feature selection process ended up being slightly
better than the baseline models with all 152 features, even though default hyperparameters were used for
both. This at least shows that it is possible to reduce the feature space of the data set substantially while
maintaining performance.
The achieved AUCs of 0.765 and 0.708 indicate that the data set contains some information with regard
to predicting the outcomes, but AUCs < 0.8 is generally not deemed acceptable for diagnostic predictions
[51, 52]. Thus, the developed models are not good enough to decide the level of care and follow-up for
patients with preeclampsia on.
The AUCs almost match the AUC achieved by Schmidt et al. in their RF model predicting combined
maternal and o↵spring adverse outcomes of preeclampsia (AUC 0.77) [2]. Zheng et al. however achieved a
much higher performance of their RF models with AUCs of 0.967 and 0.963 for the neonatal and maternal
outcomes, respectively [13]. The reason for the higher AUCs found in the two other studies could be that
their models are built on slightly bigger data sets (1647 and 733 observations, respectively). It is however
probably more likely that the di↵erences arise from the included features. My data set did not include any
of the ﬁve most important features for the adverse maternal outcomes found by Zheng et al. Out of the ﬁve
most important features for adverse neonatal outcomes, my data set only contained a version of their binary
variable early-onset type in the form of gestational age in weeks. One of the ﬁve features is however birth
weight because Zheng et al. collected the neonatal variables after birth. At the same time, the women in the
study were enrolled after being diagnosed with preeclampsia making the time and situation of prediction
di↵erent from the one used in this project. Out of the six features highlighted by Schmidt et al. based
on Shapley values, my data set only contained gestational age and maternal height. This could indicate
that the PEACH data is missing (enough values for) some important features with regard to predicting the
adverse outcomes of patients. Out of the features that would be possible to collect at the time of suspected
preeclampsia from the two studies, these features are 24-hour protein, serum phosphorous, urinary casts,
cholesterol, triglycerides, sFlt-1, and PlGF.
It is however positive for the evaluation of the PEACH data set and the constructed models in this
project that the actually included features that were deemed important by the two studies (gestational age
and maternal height) were also deemed important by my feature importance and selection methods.

6.4
Impact and Clinical Relevance of Important Features
In this section, I will discuss the clinical relevance of the most important features across the feature impor-
tance methods and the feature selection.
For the hard o↵spring outcomes, the SVR stood out as an important feature across all analyses. According
to the ICE-plots presented in the Results Section 5.9, the predicted probability of su↵ering an adverse
outcome rises with higher SVR. Higher SVR will most often cause a higher blood pressure as MAP is given
as: MAP = CO ⇤SV R where CO is cardiac output [53]. Thus, SVR is very related to the preeclampsia
diagnosis. High blood pressure in the mother can cause FGR due to underperfusion of the placenta [54]. At
the same time, high maternal blood pressure might result in the need for preterm delivery which also poses
a risk for the baby.
Maternal height is also deemed important through the above analyses and by other studies [2, 13]. The
predicted probability of su↵ering a hard o↵spring outcome goes down with rising maternal height until
approximately 1.65 m.
It might seem odd that height in itself should have an inﬂuence on the risk of
adverse outcomes, but as height is correlated with both CO and SV, the importance shown by the feature
might be an indication of the importance of heart function rather than actual height. Taller people have a
higher SV and a higher CO which could thus seem favourable in regard to outcomes of preeclampsia [55].
If this is the case for height, it is however interesting that the heart function measurements themselves
are not more present among the important features for the hard o↵spring models. But they could be missing
due to their correlation with height.
Maternal weight is correlated to CO and SV in the same way as maternal height [55]. The predicted
probability of a hard o↵spring outcome is however unchanged from 75+ kg. This could indicate that only
having a low weight while pregnant poses a risk for the o↵spring. It has been shown that underweight
women who gain less than 12.7 kg during pregnancy have a higher risk of small-for-gestational-age (SGA)
infants [56]. This might be the explanation for the relationship between maternal weight and hard o↵spring
outcomes rather than the connection between weight and cardiac function. It is however hard to conclude
anything based on this feature as it has been obtained at di↵erent gestational ages for the di↵erent women
in PEACH.
The fetal size exempliﬁed in the ICE-plots by abdominal circumference at the 20 week scan also impacts
the predicted probability of su↵ering a hard o↵spring outcome. The bigger the abdominal circumference,
the lower the predicted probability of su↵ering an outcome. This might be directly related to SGA being
one of the outcomes included in the hard o↵spring outcomes as second trimester abdominal circumference
lag has been shown to increase the risk of SGA and FGR [57].
Along the same lines, the predicted probability of a hard o↵spring outcome seems to decrease with
gestational age at enrollment. This is probably connected to the bigger risk of having an early delivery
due to preeclampsia for women suspected of having preeclampsia early in the pregnancy. The predicted
probability seems to even out at term (gestational age of 37 weeks).
The last important feature considered for the hard o↵spring outcomes is the level of urate in plasma. In

For the hard and moderate maternal outcomes, I have chosen to focus on the important features mean SBP
at the clinical visit, maternal age at delivery, level of ALAT in plasma, and mean DBP in weeks 27-30. As
mentioned, SBP and MAP at the cardiac appointment stood out as important for the feature importance
methods but were not included in the feature selection based on AUC - probably due to correlations with
the other blood pressure measurements.
Here, blood pressure stands out as important through both mean SBP at the clinical visit and mean
DBP at weeks 27-30. Especially, the mean SBP at the clinical visit has a bigger impact on the predicted
probabilities than any other feature included in the ICE-plots. The higher the SBP, the higher the predicted
probability of su↵ering a hard or moderate maternal outcome. One of the reasons could be that one of the
hard maternal outcomes is the ordination of three types of antihypertensive medicines as one must expect
the probability of giving antihypertensive drugs to rise with rising blood pressure. It is however interesting
that the predicted probability of adverse outcomes begins to rise at SBPs < 120 mmHg and DBP < 80
mmHg which are the cut-o↵values for normal blood pressure [59]. This indicates that the normal range of
blood pressure - and thereby the cut-o↵for when healthcare personnel should be alert - for pregnant women
is possibly lower than for non-pregnant women.
Higher maternal age also seems to increase the predicted probability of su↵ering a hard or moderate
maternal outcome. There is a general consensus that advanced maternal age increases the risk of adverse
pregnancy outcomes, including maternal outcomes such as postpartum haemorrhage and cesarean section
[60]. This general trend might be the underlying reason for the observed rise in predicted probabilities at
higher maternal age rather than a connection between age and adverse outcomes for preeclampsia patients
speciﬁcally.
The last feature that stands out as important through several analyses when predicting the hard and
moderate maternal outcomes is the level of ALAT in plasma.
This is linked to liver damage and the
relationship between predicted probability and ALAT level could be a direct consequence of one of the
moderate maternal outcomes being raised liver enzymes. As for the urate level in plasma, the normal range
for ALAT changes through pregnancy from < 30 U/L until week 12 up to 5 −42 U/L in weeks 35-41
making it harder to interpret the results as the samples are not obtained at the same gestational age for all
women [61]. It is however interesting that the predicted probabilities begin to rise around 25 U/L when the
diagnostic criteria for preeclampsia is ALAT > 40 U/L.
Gestational age, height, high SBP, and advanced maternal age have been pointed out as risk factors for
adverse outcomes in other studies [2, 7, 12, 33, 34]. This increases the credibility of the model and results.
6.4.1
Di↵erences Between Models for O↵spring and Maternal Outcomes
All in all, predicting the hard o↵spring outcomes resulted in a higher AUC than predicting the combined
hard and moderate maternal outcomes. There were also generally more agreement between the important
features found by the three feature importance methods and the features chosen in the feature selection
process for the hard o↵spring outcomes. 85 of the women in PEACH su↵ered a hard o↵spring outcome, and
391 su↵ered a hard and/or moderate maternal outcome. This could indicate a more diverse group for the
hard and moderate maternal outcomes than for the hard o↵spring outcomes possibly making it harder for
the RF classiﬁer to ﬁnd patterns. The percentage of missing values in the important features for the two
models are very similar so this does not seem to be the reason. Another reason could be that the included
features have higher predictive value for the o↵spring outcomes than for the maternal outcomes. E.g., several
studies showed the importance of sFlt-1 and PlGF [2, 36, 37] which is not included in the PEACH data. If
these features are more important for the maternal outcomes than for the o↵spring outcomes, it could be
part of the reason why the maternal outcomes are harder to predict based on this data set.

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate whether developing random forest models for the prediction of
adverse outcomes for suspected preeclampsia patients was possible using only routinely collected clinical
and laboratory data.
The models of this project were trained on a data set from the PEACH cohort consisting of 691 women who
were referred to the hospital due to suspicion of preeclampsia. Through investigation of feature importance
and two di↵erent methods for feature selection, I found that selecting features based on their individual
predictive power (AUC) resulted in the best-performing models.
Models were developed for the prediction of both maternal and o↵spring outcomes. The 46 individual
outcomes were grouped into categories based on their severeness in a Danish context. Predicting the hard-
est o↵spring outcomes resulted in an AUC of 0.765 for the best model using a reduced feature subset of
16 features. The most important features found through analyses of both built-in random forest feature
importance, permutation feature importance, and individual feature AUCs were systemic vascular resis-
tance, maternal height, level of urate in plasma, maternal weight, gestational age at the time of suspected
preeclampsia, and fetal abdominal circumference at gestational week 20.
Predicting the combined hard and moderate maternal outcomes resulted in an AUC of 0.708 for the
best model using 19 features. The most important features for these outcomes were mean systolic blood
pressure at the time of suspected preeclampsia, mean diastolic blood pressure at gestational weeks 27-30,
age at delivery, and the level of alanine aminotransferase in plasma.
All features included in the two models are routinely-collected and easy to obtain in a Danish context
making the models available for healthcare personnel. Most of the important features found in this project
have also been deemed important by other studies. The current performance of the models is however not
good enough to base clinical decisions upon. Other studies have used additional easily obtainable features
not included in PEACH and achieved a higher AUC when predicting outcomes for diagnosed and admitted
preeclampsia patients. This could indicate that the performance of the models developed in this project
could be improved by the inclusion of extra features in the training data, e.g., 24-hour protein in the urine
and triglyceride levels.
In conclusion, the project showed that it is possible to develop random forest models for the prediction
of adverse outcomes for suspected preeclampsia patients. However, more relevant features might need to be
included in the training data for the models to obtain clinically relevant performances.
