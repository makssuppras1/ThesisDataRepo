Abstract 
This project aimed to optimize the volumetric 3D printing of conductive zwitterion based hydrogels which are 
able to sense hydrogen peroxide in skin wound exudates. The photopolymer resin should be optimized with a 
sufficient amount of photoinitiator and photoinhibitor to be solidified during the volumetric 3D printing 
process and have high sensitivity towards H2O2 with good mechanical properties. Moreover, the printed object 
should have a complex geometry with smooth surface finish.  
In this project, I conducted (ⅰ) Calibration of the tomographic volumetric additive manufacturing (TVAM) 
printer, (ⅱ) Optimization of the material, (ⅲ) Electrochemical sensing, (ⅳ) Swelling test and (ⅴ) Mechanical 
test. Calibration of the TVAM printer and material optimization was carried out to achieve the best resolution 
of the printed result. The resolution showed promising results to print out much more complex structures in 
the future. The material was optimized to have a higher sensitivity towards hydrogen peroxide, and this was 
checked with electrochemical sensing method resulting limit of detection (LOD) value of 38.92mM, limit of 
quantitation (LOQ) value of 129.77mM and sensitivity of 0.000764µAmM-1mm-2. The swelling behavior was 
SBMA concentration dependent, showing the zwitterion’s higher water containing and slower water uptake 
properties. The printing results showed batch to batch consistency of mechanical properties compared to the 
casting ones, proving that the printing method is much more suitable and reliable for fabricating object with 
our resin. 
The fabrication of smart wound bandages lead to the development of the new generation of hydrogel based 
wound sensor that can detect and respond to bacterial infection in real-time and enables more effective wound 
healing and reduces the risk of secondary infections.

1. Introduction 
Human skin is the largest organ in the body which is around 15% of our body weight [1]. Skin defends our 
body against pathogens, prevents water loss, and protects from the trauma such as thermal, chemical, and 
ultraviolet radiation [2]. Thus, managing skin wounds are essential for preventing trauma and 
pathophysiological disorders.  
Several molecular processes, including hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and extracellular matrix 
remodeling, are involved in the proper healing of wounds. Large trauma wounds brought on by burns, type Ⅱ 
diabetes and obesity, or accidents cause substantial skin loss that cannot be healed properly. Skin itself has a 
strong ability for regeneration than other human tissues via scarring. Numerous chronic wounds place a heavy 
strain on patients and medical providers as well as raising the cost of national healthcare systems on a yearly 
basis [1].  
Traditionally, the skin wound dressings such as bandages, body netting and composite dressings were used for 
shielding the skin from contamination. The main disadvantage of these conventional ones are that they adhere 
to freshly formed granulations and cause discomfort when removed and even cause the secondary damage. 
Nowadays, the wound dressings offer not only physical protection but also keep the wound microenvironment 
moisture and hasten the healing process by enhancing wound healing rates [3], [4]. Diverse material choice 
for the modern wound dressing can characterize the biodegradability, biocompatibility, and hydrophilicity [4]. 
Additionally, recent studies show that the combining 3D printing technologies with hydrogels to fabricate skin 
wound dressing can offer personalized smart skin bandage. Since the 3D printing utilizes CAD, the shape, size, 
flexibility, and the structure of the wound dressing can be adjusted for each patient since the wound tends to 
occur in diverse shapes, sizes, and places of the body [5]. Moreover, while printing, hydrogels can be loaded 
with antibacterial nanoparticles, drugs, and antibiotics, opening the new era of patient specific dressing [3]. 
Here we provide the volumetric 3D printed smart skin wound bandage based on zwitterion hydrogel that can 
sense hydrogen peroxide in wound exudates. In this study, the photopolymer resin was optimized for its 
sensing ability, crosslinking degree, and printability. We demonstrated the sensing ability of the conductive 
zwitterion hydrogel towards the hydrogen peroxide generated from the skin wound to detect whether the skin 
is healing properly or causing other infections. Moreover, the volumetric 3D printing technology was 
investigated to open the possibility for fabricating personalized wound dressing using the photopolymer 
zwitterion hydrogel.

2. Theoretical Background and Materials 
2.1. Additive Manufacturing 
Additive manufacturing (AM), also called rapid prototyping in the beginning or 3D printing in other terms, is 
a technique using a three-dimensional Computer-Aided Design (CAD) to create a physical object. The 
principle of this process is to stack materials in layers which are created by slicing the CAD data into a thin 
cross-section. Usually, the thinner each layer is, the closer the printed outcome will be to the original [6], [7]. 
The traditional manufacturing processes such as milling, grinding, and machining require process planning in 
order to fabricate the desired structure by removing the materials from its original stock or sheet which does 
not able to meet the highest resolution or complex outcomes. To use other traditional techniques such as 
injection modeling and compression modeling, molds should be premade to produce the parts of the products 
[8]. However, the AM doesn’t need those processes, it only requires a simple dimension information and a 
rudimentary knowledge of how AM machine works and the materials that are used for printing. Furthermore, 
by 3D printing technology, highly complex structures are generated by layer-by-layer process with minimum 
waste and without fabricating extra molds for manufacturing parts, which result saving time and less costs [7], 
[8]. Figure 1 shows the conceptual difference between traditional and additive manufacturing techniques.  
AM processes can be defined into seven categories based on ISO/ASTM52900, which is described in Table 1 
[9]. This includes the materials that can be used for printing, the various processes used to generate and bind 
the layers, and the potential applications. These variations will influence factors like the precision of final parts 
as well as their mechanical and material qualities. Additionally, the time it takes to build the parts, the amount 
of post-processing required, the size of the AM machine utilized, and the total cost of the machine and 
processes will all be determined by these variables [6].  
Figure 1 │ Conceptual difference between (a) Traditional and (b) Additive manufacturing techniques. Figure (a) 
shows one of the traditional methods called subtractive manufacturing, which removes materials little by little to
make a desired outcome. Figure (b) shows layer by layer process of addivitve manufaturing. Additive 
manufacturing has less waste after fabrication.

Selecting materials for 3D printing plays a significant role in 3D printer choice. The available product on 
market is listed in Table 2 [10]. Materials which are feedstocks for the AM process need to be shaped into a 
condition that works with the corresponding process. They are typically formed into the powder, sheet, and 
liquid state. For instance, the feedstock for vat polymerization and photopolymer-based material jetting must 
be a liquid thermoset plastic monomer which crosslinks under the proper electromagnetic radiation exposure 
[11]. 
Originally, AM technology was merely used to construct visualization models of products while they were 
still in the development stage. As time goes on, the materials, precision and the output quality have all enhanced 
while following this basic objective of simple model manufacturing. Generated models were rapidly used to 
provide information of the “3FS”, form, fit and function. The earliest models were the means for understanding 
the shape and overall purpose of the design, which correspond to the ‘Form’ of 3FS. Due to the enhanced 
accuracy of the process, the components were produced within the tolerances vital for assembly purposes, 
which stands for ‘Fit’. With improved material properties, the parts could be appropriately controlled resulting 
in the proper evaluation for how they will ultimately ‘Function’[6]. 
Currently, it would be misleading to say AM technology is solely useful for creating models. AM may 
dramatically reduce the time and expense of the product development when combined with other techniques 
to create process chains. Some of the AM technologies nowadays have advanced to the point that the outcome 
is suited for end application, meaning that they are ready to use right after printed. This explains why in the 
early stage we called this technique as rapid prototyping and now changed its terminology as additive 
manufacturing [6]. Furthermore, AM is incorporating with the machine learning (ML) algorithms in order to 
automatically adjust the printing parameter for the novel material which allows the real-time controlling system 
and able to rearrange quickly under the unexpected situations for example by using a machine-vision system 
[12].

The AM consist of four main steps: (ⅰ) designing, (ⅱ) printing, (ⅲ) post-processing, and (ⅳ) application. 
Designing phase uses CAD software such as SOLIDWORKS, AutoCAD and Fusion 360 to create wantable 
three-dimensional (3D) structure. This is then transformed into the Standard Tessellation Language (STL) file 
format and then sliced into 3D images or layers using slicing software. The STL file contains some general 
adjustment to make it into the ideal size, position, and orientation for layer constructions. The AM machine 
receives this STL file containing detailed information of the components and ready for printing [6], [19]. 
Before printing starts, the printing parameters such as exposure time, infill density, travel speed, or temperature 
should be adjusted which mainly relies on the 3D printing method that you chose to use [19], [20]. Ongoingly, 
3D printers employ G-codes created by the software of the printer which are compatible with various 3D file 
formats. These G-codes include all the information needed for printing such as sliced images, exposure time, 
printer movements, and temperature that the traditional STL format does not. While printing, 2D slices are 
printed one layer after another resulting in 3D printed outcome. After the desired 3D object is fabricated, the 
parts are removed from the machine or scaffold and ready for post-processing. Post-processing step is 
necessary to remove extra raw material or to apply the final finishing. This step is highly dependent on the 
specific AM method, material used to print, and the intended use of the generated parts. The application is now 
ready for usage after completing all these procedures [6], [19].

Vat photopolymerization  
Among those seven categories, vat photopolymerization (VPP) is the most conventional and the oldest 
commercially available one, which is used in various fields such as dentistry, biomedical engineering, and 
metamaterial [6], [21]. It also provides one of the widest available material options, as well [22]. VPP solidifies 
the liquid photopolymer resin made of (macro) monomers and photoinitiator in a vat by irradiating ultraviolet 
(UV) light [23], [24]. These monomers are activated by the photolysis of the photoinitiator into primary 
radicals causing polymerization reaction and transform the photocurable resin to a solid object. Photoinitiator 
is an essential material for photopolymerization since it generates reactive species such as free radicals, cations, 
or anions, right after exposed to UV or visible light [24]. VPP provides high resolution and good surface quality 
of the printed outcome, which allows to fabricate complex micrometer scaled applications [23], [24]. For 
example, patient tailored bioresorbable vascular stent was printed by using microCLIP technique with 
minimum width of 97µM, proving that sub-micron scaled elaborate structure is fabricable through VPP [25].   
VPP includes several light-induced printing methods such as Stereolithography (SLA), Digital Light 
Processing (DLP), Two-Photon Polymerization (TPP), Continuous Liquid Interface Production (CLIP), and 
Volumetric Additive Manufacturing (VAM) (Figure 2). These lithography-based 3D printing techniques allow 
to produce parts with resolution around 100µm to 100nm [18]. The most widely used VPP techniques for skin 
wound bandages are SLA and DLP since they are capable of printing biocompatible polymeric materials such 
as hydrogels, which may replicate the extracellular matrix (ECM) of the human skin (Figure 2 (a-b)). The main 
difference between the SLA and DLP methods is how the light source is generated and cures the resin. The 
SLA printers adopt a UV-laser beam as the light source, and the DLP printers use a UV-projector as an energy 
source [26]. The SLA employs a point-by-point method to crosslink the resin and on the other hand, DLP 
polymerizes the entire resin layer at once with plane-by-plane method [27]. Due to these differences, SLA 
provides better spatial resolution compared to DLP by having a small spot size of the laser. On the contrary, 
DLP allows faster printing speed than SLA since it is using a digital micro-mirror device (DMD) which 
irradiates a cross-sectional layer image on the vat containing resin, making all of the targeted spots solidified 
at once [26], [27]. Thus, SLA allows us to print larger size objects, but it has lower resolution than other 
photocuring techniques because the resolution is dependent on the size of the laser beam. For DLP, the size of 
the printable model is smaller than SLA but allows the higher precision and the resolution is etendue limited 
[28].  
CLIP is the advanced version of DLP 3D printing method [29]. Unlike SLA and DLP, CLIP uses light-emitting 
diode (LED) digital projection and an oxygen-permeable window (Figure 2 (d)). Oxygen-permeable window  
is a liquid interface of uncured resin, also called a “dead layer” or a “dead zone”, between the printed part and 
the window. Since the building platform is designed to be continuously lifted and the dead layer provides 
oxygen inhibition mechanism by sufficiently supplying oxygen while print, the printing speed is around 
1000mm per hour which is about two orders of magnitude compared to other bottom-up stereolithography 
techniques [21], [29]. However, this fast-printing speed does not allow the heat generated during the 
polymerization process to dissipate in time, making the CLIP only suitable for printing the object with 
somewhat small contact area [21].

absorption (TPA), 3D printing technique emerged [21], [30] (Figure 2(c)). This direct laser-writing (DLW)

Even though these AM technologies mentioned above have several advantages, there are still some drawbacks 
that need to be improved. First, most of the AM technologies, such as SLA, require supporting structure in

manually removed implying that it’s not ready-to-use and requires extra steps after the print [35], [36]. 
Moreover, the layer-by-layer stacking methods provide poor surface quality, have a negative impact on 
throughput, limit the geometric capabilities and may result in mechanical performance anisotropy [37]. On top 
of those, the major problem of this layer-by-layer and other building methods (such as TPP and CLIP)is that 
they provide relatively slow building speed [21], [23].  
There are several goals that the AM technologies should aim to achieve. The initial goal is the resolution of 
the printed outcome. But apart from that, the first and foremost is the speed of 3D printing. It will decide 
whether the 3D printed object has better potential than the traditional manufacturing for mass production [21].  
To solve these above-mentioned problems and meet the aims, especially the printing speed, volumetric additive 
manufacturing (VAM) emerged. This technology provides extremely fast building speed by abandoning 
traditional layer-by-layer method and on top of that without any need for supporting structure while printing 
[38].

2.1.1. Volumetric Additive Manufacturing 
Volumetric 3D printing, VAM, is a novel vat polymerization technique which abandoned layer-by-layer 
construction method and chose to irradiate the light from diverse angles resulting the huge improvement of the 
printing time [21], [23], [24]. Photosensitive resin is used for 3D print and this only crosslinks at the localized 
regions where the single light exposures accumulate enough to surpass the crosslinking threshold [23]. There 
are different approaches for VAM technology which are xolography, holography, and tomography [23], [24], 
[39], [40]. In our case, a topographic reconstruction method was selected.

2.2. Photopolymerization 
The main printing principle of VAM is based on photopolymerization. Photopolymerization employs a liquid 
precursor resin which solidifies through the light source in order to generate 3D printed structure [57]. The 
small molecules in the resin, such as monomers, transform themselves into the larger molecules of highly 
crosslinked polymer network [18], [58]. There are a variety of radiation types to initiate this reaction, such as 
gamma rays, X-rays, electron beams, ultraviolet (UV), and occasionally visible light. Mainly, UV and visible 
light are used for photopolymerization printing techniques [19].  
The VAM requires transparent photopolymer or prepolymer resin which is sensitive to the certain wavelength 
of light that carries the adequate amount of photon energy to initiate a photopolymerization reaction [18]. The 
reason why highly transparent resin is needed is to make sure that the pattern of the light passes through the 
entire depth of the rotation vat’s building volume [39]. Moreover, highly viscous resins or prepolymer mixtures 
rather than monomer solutions are desirable for this printing method. They inhibit gravitational sedimentation, 
restrain inhibitor or free-radical diffusion [43], [59] . The elements that make up VAM photopolymer resins 
include: (ⅰ) monomers or polymers, (ⅱ) photoinitiators, (ⅲ) photoinhibitors or absorbers, and (ⅳ) diluents [6], 
[18], [19], [39]. Briefly, monomers and polymers are the building units of 3D printed objects. These building 
units start to grow and form polymer networks due to the photoinitiators [6]. The photoinitiators are reactants 
which create reactive species such as free radicals, cations, or anions due to its sensitivity towards light 
exposure [24], [42]. Photoinhibitors, however, control the maximum resolution that can be achieved while 
printing as well as inhibiting premature crosslinking [30], [42]. These materials require diluents to become a 
homogeneous solution [39]. Theoretically, the higher intensity of the light, the higher amount of initiator, the 
lower content of inhibitor, the faster the photoinitiation procedure begins [30].  
The mechanical properties of printed results, the viscosity of the polymeric resin, the speed of printing process 
and the post-processing requirements are mostly determined by not only the combination of monomers and 
prepolymers, but also the photoinitiators, photoinhibitors and diluents [30], [43]. Thus, understanding the basic 
working principles of these materials are important for achieving desired resin.  
 
Photoinitiator (PI)  
Among these components, the initiator plays an important role in starting the polymerization reaction. When 
the UV light strikes a photopolymer resin, the chemical transformation of the photoinitiator takes place and 
becomes reactive to the liquid state monomers [60], [61]. This is due to the electromagnetic irradiation, the 
photoinitiator molecules absorb photons and become excited states [6].  These excited states create reactive 
species which initiates polymerization process [60], [61] . The monomer molecule in the resin interacts with 
an active state photoinitiator and begins to create polymer chains. These polymer chains then subsequently 
crosslink to form strong covalent bonds between themselves [6]. There are various types of crosslinking 
mechanisms, free radical, cationic, and anionic photopolymerization. In this paper free radical one is used, 
which is described in section 2.2.1.

Photoinhibitor  
For the volumetric 3D printing, the resin shows the nonlinear photopolymerization behavior due to the 
photoinhibitors and this behavior is called ‘threshold dependent response’ (figure 8) [43]. The photoinhibitor 
curbs premature crosslinking ensuing improvement of printing contrast by terminating photoreactions [39], 
[42]. During the free-radical polymerization process, which is described in section 2.2.1, the reactive radicals 
react with the monomers in order to induce the chain growth of the polymer. The inhibitors react with reactive 
radicals faster than the monomers, generating inactive species and retarding the polymerization process. The 
oxygen, for example, acts as an inhibitor. Almost all the radicals in the beginning of the light irradiation react 
with the O2 molecules since the rate constant of reaction with O2 is greater than that for the polymer chain 
propagation. From the point when the resin starts to run out of O2, polymerization begins. Thus, the solidifying 
process of the resin only takes place if the intensity of the light is strong enough to produce enough amount of 
radicals to completely eliminate the O2 within the printing time [42].

2.2.1. Free-Radical Photopolymerization 
The polymerization process was classified as ‘chain growth polymerization’ and ‘step growth polymerization’ 
by Paul Flory in 1953. The chain growth polymerization first creates the active radicals, such as free radical 
or ion, and monomers propagate together with these generated reactive radicals to create a larger molecular 
weight polymer. Meanwhile, in the step growth polymerization, the monomer first forms the dimer, then 
trimers, tetramers, and so on, until it becomes the higher molecular weight polymer [66]. 
Currently, the photopolymer resins for VPP adopted free-radical mediated chain growth polymerization 
mechanisms. This is due to the fact that the chain growth polymerizations may produce longer chain polymers 
during the solidification of the resin compared to the step growth one with a comparably low percent of 
monomer conversion. Also, the step growth photopolymerization has an issue with stability and kinetic control 
as well as the need of the significant number of reactive monomers to achieve the resin crosslinking [22]. On 
account of these, this project used the resin based on the free-radical chain growth photopolymerization. This 
provided our resin to create the backbone of the chain rapidly at the early stage of the process and assured that 
there is no further polymer chain growth after the termination step which is shown in figure 9.

2.2.3. Composition of Photopolymer resin 
The photopolymer resin composition plays a critical role in volumetric 3D printing process. Ideally, the 
printable resin should provide reasonable optical, surface, and mechanical properties, as well as an acceptable 
resolution even for the complicated designed object [43].  
The resin used for the project is hydrogel. Hydrogels have hydrophilic structures that are capable of holding 
large amounts of water in their polymeric network. The reason why this is possible is due to their polymeric 
backbones which contain hydrophilic functional groups [73]. High water content can provide sufficient 
flexibility almost similar to the natural human tissue, having both viscoelastic and solid-like properties [74], 
[75]. Even though they keep a lot of water or aqueous media inside their network structures, they have an 
ability of maintaining their shape [75]. Moreover, they show high permeability for small molecules, water-
soluble components, and others due to their three-dimensional network. These outstanding properties allow 
them to be the perfect tools for encapsulating drugs and biomolecules and fabricating contact lenses, biosensors, 
tissue scaffolds and skin wound bandages [74]–[76].  
Hydrogels can be either natural or synthetic. These days, synthetic hydrogels are gathering more attention due 
to their longer service life, higher water absorption capacity, and higher strength of the gel. Also, their 
structures can be modified to have better degradability and functionality [73].  
As shown in figure 15, the materials used for this research are SBMA, AAm, PEI, LAP, and TEMPOOL, 
which were dissolved in Milli-Q water for use.  
 
2.2.3.1. Zwitterion hydrogel  
Zwitterionic polymer is the main and the most important component of this project. As shown in figure 14, it 
has both cationic and anionic groups with a zero net charge of the entire molecule [77].  
As compared to the ionic liquids in which cations and anions move independently under the influence of 
electrostatic forces as well as the interactions from other ions such as the hydrogen bonding or dispersion 
forces, zwitterions keep their covalently bonded ion pairs still. Due to the fact that the independent mobility of 
anions and cations are prohibited, they cannot have carrier ions. This results in low ionic conductivity (<10-6 
S cm-1) of zwitterion although having high ion density [78]. However, compared to the nonionic hydrogel, 
zwitterion certainly shows much higher conductivity.

2.3. Biosensor 
Hydrogel is porous, soft, flexible, and highly water-absorbable material which has a three-dimensional network 
structure after crosslinked [75], [100]. It can be tuned to have different biochemical, mechanical, rheological 
properties as well as the stimulus responsiveness to soft tissue [101]. Thus, it is widely used in flexible and 
wearable electronics, batteries, and biosensors [100].  
Biosensors play an important role in monitoring the physiological status and clinical diagnosis of the patients, 
personalized medicine, and pharmaceutical research [75], [77]. Hydrogels with conductive property are 
excellent choice for a biosensor. To fabricate a biosensor using conductive hydrogels, which are part of the 
smart hydrogel, requires not only the good electrical conductivity, stability, and mechanical properties, but 
also the selectivity to detect a specific analyte, reproductivity for several time use, sensibility, and linearity 
[100], [102].  
Figure 18 describes the components of the biosensor: (ⅰ) Analyte, (ⅱ) Sensor, (ⅲ) Transducer, (ⅳ) Electronics, 
and (ⅴ) Display. The analyte is a substance which is ready for detection [100], [102]. For example, in our paper, 
H2O2 is an analyte. Sensor detects the analyte and generates the signal. Then, this signal transmits to the 
transducer and converts one form of signal into another form. Based on this transducer, the type of biosensor 
is determined [102]. For example, if the transducer produces an optical signal, it would be an optical biosensor, 
and if it produces electrochemical signal, it would be an electrochemical biosensor [103]. Electronics amplify 
the signals that come from the transducer and transform it from analogue into the digital form. Then, this is 
displayed on the screen. The displayed signal can be numeric, graphic, or an image, depending on the software 
that you choose [102].  
The role of the transducer is to change the biologically recognized signal into a measurable one. Thus, 
depending on the selection of the transducer, the type of biosensors is decided [102]. They can be optical, 
electronic, acoustic, thermal, gravimetric, and electrochemical biosensor [103].  
Electrochemical biosensors can be divided into several classes which are amperometric, potentiometric, 
conductometric, and voltametric. In this project, electrochemical voltammetry biosensor was selected.  
Electrochemical voltammetry biosensor employs cyclic voltammetry (CV) to carry out the detection. 
Voltammetric transducers are the most commonly used for biosensing analysis. It measures the current-
potential relationship based on the chemical species’ redox property [104]. The appearance of the redox peak 
depends on the specific species that are examined, and the size of the current peak is proportional to the species 
[90], [105].

2.3.1. Hydrogen Peroxide detection 
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is a reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced from the injured tissue [106]. It is 
generated by the neutrophils, which is one of the white blood cells, during the first line defense in human body 
against toxins, bacteria, and viruses. The high concentration of H2O2 in blood plasma, however, can be the 
pathogenic element that damages the vascular organs [104]. Also, the specific concentration of H2O2 in body 
fluids and breath can be the signal of metabolic disorders such as diabetes, pulmonary diseases, Alzheimer’s, 
neurodegeneration, or other health conditions [107], [108].  
On contrary, in the early stages of the injury the H2O2 rapidly produced in the wounded site. This action 
minimizes the risk of infection, activates the signaling pathways for epidermal regenerations, and recruits the 
white blood cells, which help the healing process of the wound [106]. 
Thus, having a particular concentration of H2O2 in the wounded area or plasma can be either harmful or helpful 
to the body. This shows the importance of fabricating biosensors, which are accurate, sensible and have great 
limit of detection (LOD) value.   
 
Detection mechanism  
The mechanism of detecting H2O2 depends on the behavior of the conductive material, as the change in 
conductivity is a result from interaction between the conductive material and the hydrogen peroxide [107]. The 
cyclic voltammetry method is used for the detection of redox reactions that happens from the material and 
H2O2. The sensor measures the faradaic current from the surface of the working electrode (WE). The potential 
difference at the interface of the electrode is measured and adjusted in comparison to a reference electrode 
(RE) which drives the redox reactions. Also, the counter electrode (CE) acts as an electron source or sink to 
allow the current to flow [109]. Then, we can get the quantitative value of both oxidation and reduction.  
Hydrogen peroxide contains an unstable highly active peroxide bond. Typically, two hydroxyl ions (OH-) and 
radicals are formed during the H2O2 degradation. During the process, they produce compounds that are either 
charged or partially charged. The electrochemical sensing mechanism, such as cyclic voltammetry, use these 
breakdown process of H2O2 for detection [107].

3.2. Volumetric 3D Printer 
There were four main things to consider operating a tomographic volumetric 3D printer (TVAM): (ⅰ) Printer 
design, (ⅱ) Printer control, (ⅲ) Printing process, and (ⅳ) Post-processing process. Printer design includes the 
composition and the characteristics of the printer. Since the TVAM printer used in our project utilizes various 
instruments, which are CAL software based on MATLAB, DLP Light Crafter4500 software and the control 
panel based on G-code, understanding the systems before controlling was important. After being used to these 
gadgets, the printing and post-processing process were conducted.

3.2.1. Printer design 
As shown in figure 22 (a-b), the printer consists of a DLP projector (red dotted line box) and rotation stage 
(blue dotted line box). The DLP projector (DLP® LightCrafter™ 4500, Texas Instruments) contains the DLP 
0.45 WXGA chipset (DLP® 0.45 WXGA DMD, Texas Instruments) and the UV diode mounted in the blue 
LED slot. The DLP projector projects the patterned light to the vat in order to crosslink desired object. The 
generated light has a 385nm wavelength, which is in the range of the LAP absorption spectrum shown in figure 
16. Unfortunately, the printer does not contain the lens which can collimate the light; thus, the path of the light 
will diverge. Moreover, the beam is a gaussian beam and not a flat-top beams due to the lack of the beam 
sharper. Thus, the beam always contains superfluous energy and has non-consistent intensity distribution. 
The rotation stage has two containers: (ⅰ) test tube (yellow cylinder) and (ⅱ) polymer vat (gray cylinder). The 
inner test tube contains the photocurable resin that is ready to be solidified right after the irradiation of the light 
with certain wavelength. In our case, the printer has dominated light at 385nm, and the LAP photoinitiator has 
peak absorption at 365nm with a long-volumed tail until the visible light range. The outer test tube is filled 
with the refractive index matching fluid. A similar refractive index between the refractive index matching fluid 
and the photopolymer resin will cause less deformation of the light path.  
Figure 22 (c) shows the characteristics of TVAM. Due to the distortion, the printable focal height is maximum 
30mm and the printable focal depth is maximum 10mm. Due to this maximum focal height and depth, there is 
a limitation in the printable size. If the size is bigger than this given criteria, the structure will be printed with 
lower resolution. The focal distance between the projector and the focal plane is around 92mm. The focal 
distance should be checked before printing since this decides whether the image we are printing is on-focus or 
not.

3.2.2. Printer control 
Controlling the printer has three main steps to follow: (ⅰ) Controlling the CAL software based on MATLAB, 
(ⅱ) Controlling the LightCrafter 4500 software provided from Texas Instruments, and (ⅲ) Controlling the 
tomographic volumetric 3d printer and its G-code control panel. In this section, only the method to control the 
systems is written. How we downloaded and installed the given software is written in appendix A.  
CAL software Prepare the STL file before running the CAL software. CAL script utilizing MATLAB was 
used to convert the STL file into the intensity-modulated projection video format. The intensity modulated 
video is based on the back-projection algorithm of computed tomographic (CT) reconstruction. First, the right 
value was put in CAL script, such as rotation velocity (degree/sec), cone angle, and size scale factor. Then the 
STL file was uploaded, and the code was run. The file was first voxelized by the algorithm and generated the 
voxelized geometry shown in figure 23 (a).  Afterwards, the geometry was optimized (figure 23 (b-c)), and 
the three-dimensional energy dose (figure 23 (d)) was calculated. The energy dose is an important factor for 
photopolymer to be crosslinked in a desired shape. The intensity modulation, shown in figure 23 (e), is based 
on 180 cross-sectional images with different intensity profiles. Each image corresponds to 1degree. Thus, the 
video first played the images from 0O to 180O, and then for 181O to 360O, it played the images from 0O to 180O 
in a reverse manner.  
Subsequently, the intensity-modulated projection video was generated with the same image changing speed of 
the rotational speed of the rotation stage. This projection, shown in figure 24, is a grayscale image having 0 to 
255 values. The different grayscale values determine the solidification of the photopolymer resin while printing. 
This generated video can be directly used for printing via Gstreamer, or it can be embedded in a PowerPoint 
slide with a black background.  
Lightcrafter 4500 Software This software from Texas instruments allowed to control the light intensity of the 
projector by changing the LED value of the blue light from 0 to 255 (Appendix C. figure 65). In this experiment, 
100 value was mostly used.

3.2.3. Printing process 
In order to print the desired 3D object, there are two parts that need to be considered: (ⅰ) Laptop or computer, 
and (ⅱ) TVAM printer.  
 
Setting up the projector Turn on the printer and connect the laptop with the printer. There are two cables that 
should be connected, which are HDMI and connector cable. Go to the LightCrafter software, and check if it 
displays the green light in the connect section. If so, it means that the printer is connected to laptop and ready 
to use. Modify the intensity values (from 0 to 255) of blue LED from the LightCrafter software. This will 
provide a different intensity of 385nm wavelength light.  
Setting up the vat Fill the photopolymer vat with the refractive index match fluid. For the resin used for this 
project, mineral oil was used as a RIM fluid. Then, pour the photopolymer resin into the 30mm outer dimeter 
test tube (DURAN™ Test Tube, with beaded rim, # 261303805, DWK Life Sciences). Make sure both liquid 
solutions do not contain any bubbles (or even microbubbles) to prevent any unwanted diffraction of light. 
Printing Play the video from the laptop. Since the printer is connected to the laptop with HDMI cable, the 
intensity modulated projection video will be automatically projected onto the focal plane of the test tube. At 
the same time, run the G-code for printing. The G-code will make sure the patterned light is on focus and the 
rotation stage rotates with a specific speed for a certain duration. The corresponding G-code is in appendix B. 
While printing, check if the resin is solidified into the desired shape.  
After printing After printing is done, gently take the printed sample out from the test tube using a spatula. 
Also, transfer the refractive index matching fluid from polymer vat to a bottle using a peristaltic pump system. 
 
3.2.4. Post-processing 
Post-processing was done in order to get a better resolution and remove the non-solidified resin from the 
crosslinked object. For our material, both ethanol and PBS were used.  
PP1 Soak the printed object inside the ethanol first to remove the non-crosslinked part of the resin. After, rinse 
the sample with PBS (Dulbecco’s Phosphate Buffered Saline, DPBS, #D8537, Sigma-Aldrich) to smooth out 
the surface and remove the non-crosslinked part closer to the outer part of the sample. Then, immerse the 
sample into the ethanol. UV cure the sample for 1min 30sec under 365nm and 405nm mixed simultaneous 
LED wavelength with UV Curing box (CUREbox UV Post-Cure Chamber, WICKED ENGINEERING, USA) 
to completely crosslink the material.  
PP2 Fill up the test tube with PBS (Dulbecco’s Phosphate Buffered Saline, DPBS, #D8537, Sigma-Aldrich) 
and gently pull out the sample. Immerse the sample into PBS and rinse it in order to remove the non-crosslinked 
resin. When most of the non-solidified photopolymer was removed, rinse the sample with ethanol to decrease 
its sticky property and define the shape. UV cure the sample under 365nm and 405nm mixed simultaneous 
LED wavelength with UV curing box (CUREbox UV Post-Cure Chamber, WICKED ENGINEERING, USA) 
to completely crosslink the material and remove the remaining stickiness.

4. Results and Discussion 
4.1. Basic concept 
Personalized smart wound bandage was fabricated with photopolymer resin and tomographic volumetric 
additive manufacturing technology. TVAM exhibits fast printing time with less heat generation. Moreover, it 
utilizes 385nm UV light in order to solidify the resin, which makes it suitable for biological and personalized 
applications due to its wavelength closer to the visible wavelength area (380nm -700nm).  
In order to synthesize a photopolymer resin for a smart wound bandage, AAm, PEI and zwitterion were used 
as polymeric materials. These three materials crosslink together using various chemical interactions which are 
schematically described in figure 27. The backbone of the polymer is based on acrylamide. This main polymer 
chain decides the properties of the polymer such as glass transition temperature and mechanical strength [112]. 
Both zwitterion and PEI are the side chains. The side chain highly affects the mechanical properties, water 
content of the hydrogel, and polymeric network [113]. For instance, PEI was used as a crosslinker in order to 
strengthen the mechanical property of the resin. The zwitterion has a dipole-dipole interaction between its 
sulfonate anion and ammonium cation. Negative charge of sulfonate anion from one zwitterion is attracted to 
the other zwitterion with positive charge of ammonium cation. On the other hand, PEI shows the hydrogen 
bond, which is a special type of dipole-dipole interactions and the covalent bond. For the H-bond, the NH2 
from the PEI and the oxygen from the AAm can either work as the hydrogen bond accepter or doner. If NH2 
decides to be a doner, than the oxygen becomes acceptor and vice versa. Additionally, PEI and AAm share 
their electrons and form electron pairs between atoms in order to formulate covalent bonds.

means that the beam has a beam waist, which converges and diverges during the light propagation [116]. Thus, 
we can estimate that the ability to print out sharp and angled structures using this printer is limited.

Test tube design  
Before talking about the printed result, the test tube used to print xLR resin was modified in a particular way 
to save the amount of material. Figure 31 (e) is the schematic image of the test tube depicting the length of the 
printable area and non-printable area and how it was modified. As shown in figure 31 (a), the test tube which 
was not used for printing was filled with BPCE and fully crosslinked under UV light (F-BPCE) (figure 31 (e) 
orange area). To make sure that this area was not used for printing, the light was propagated through the test 
tube. In figure 31 (f), the light was not reaching the area where the BPCE resin was crosslinked, and it was 
only passing through the part above the fully crosslinked BPCE.  
To use this test tube, the xLR resin was filled on top of this fully crosslinked BPCE resin (figure 31 (b), figure 
31 (e) blue area) and used for printing (figure 31 (g)). The light was not reflected from the surface of the F-
BPCE and only irradiated the xLR resin. Thus, it was concluded that this part of the test tube wouldn’t cause 
any problems related to the resolution or the printing process.  
 
0.2LR Trial  
Firstly, 0.2LR photopolymer resin was chosen for printing. Figure 31 (c) is the image of the printed resin. It 
was exposed under UV light of the printer for 6mins with the rotation speed of 900degree/min and the intensity 
of 255. There wasn’t any collectible printed structure and the whole resin was completely crosslinked together 
and could not be removed from the test tube due to its high degree of solidification even the part irradiated the 
light without pattern which has theoretically zero intensity. Moreover, in figure 31 (d), the resin was used for 
printing with the rotational speed of 900degree/min for 3mins. The resin itself was crosslinked all together 
without any visible pattern or structure. This was maybe due to the lack of photoinitiator amount. The 
concentration of the photoinitiator was too low to generate enough free radicals to initiate the 
photopolymerization process. With less free radicals, since some of them must be consumed by the reactive 
oxygen and quenched, there are much less reactive initiators to initiate the free radical chain growth 
photopolymerization. Thus, the sample remained undercured and did not generate any recognizable structure. 
So, there were two things to be modified as a conclusion of this experiment: (ⅰ) Check if the resin is solidified 
all together in the given volume regardless of the area that the high or low intensity of light reaches, and (ⅱ) 
Increase the photoinitiator sufficient to fully solidify the resin where the patterned light irradiates.

is around 1.33 to 1.47 based on the temperature and homogeneity, PEI is 1.61 and SBMA is unknown [119]–
[121]. These values do not exactly match either with the refractive index matching fluid or the borosilicate 
glasses. All these materials have a different refractive index, as depicted in figure 48. Moreover, due to Snell’s 
law, the path and the speed of the light will be changed during the transmission of the light. The change of 
these criteria can cause the deformation of the printed outcome in the end and result in poor resolution and

4.3. Electrochemistry 
Electrochemical characterization of our resins was done using CV technique. Two methods were considered, 
ES-01 and ES-02. Both techniques used 10mL PBS to dissolve hydrogen peroxide in order to make a similar 
condition of blood serum/plasma in the wounded area of human skin.  
For each vial, 10mL of PBS were used. This volume of PBS consists of (ⅰ) Sodium chloride (NaCl) 0.08mg 
(137mM), (ⅱ) Potassium phosphate (KH2PO4) 0.002g (1.8mM), (ⅲ) Sodium phosphate (Na2HPO4) 0.0115g 
(10mM), and (ⅳ) Potassium chloride (KCl) 0.002g (2.7mM). Human blood plasma is made up of 90% water, 
and 10% solids. The solids contain plasma proteins, electrolytes, immunoglobulins, and coagulants [122]. The 
main electrolytes in plasma are Sodium (Na+), Potassium (K+), Chloride (Cl-), Ionized calcium (Ca2+) and 
Phosphate (PO43-). The values of each component were: (ⅰ) Sodium (Na+) 136-156 mM, (ⅱ) Potassium (K+) 
3.50-5.00mM, (ⅲ) Phosphate (PO43-) 0.81-1.45 mM, (ⅳ) Chloride (Cl-) 98-107mM, and (ⅴ) Calcium (Ca2+) 
2.15-2.55mM [123]–[125].

100mM H2O2 solution for 10mins. The sensing was done at the scan rate of 0.05 V/s with a step size of 0.01 
V.  
For GC electrode immersed in PBS, no current peak was observed. The H-GC immersed in 100mM H2O2 
solution showed the cationic peak at -0.3V and anodic peak at 0.8V, but the H-GC immersed in PBS did not 
exhibit any redox response. This shows that the both H-GC and GC do not show any andic(Epa) and 
cathodic(Epc) peak potentials in the absence of H2O2. However, with a sufficient concentration of H2O2, the 
oxygen reduction reaction of decomposing the unstable hydrogen peroxide into water happens at –0.3V (Epc) 
[129]. Furthermore, the oxygen oxidation reaction of the hydrogen peroxide also occurs at 0.8V (Epa).  
In figure 60 (b), GC and H-GC electrodes were immersed in 100mM H2O2 solution for 10mins. The GC 
electrode has a cationic peak at -0.6V and anodic peak at 0.9V. On the other hand, H-GC electrode has a 
cationic peak at -0.3V and anodic peak at 0.8V. This shows the H-GC electrode has a low cathodic to anodic 
peak potential separation values which indicates good electron transfer ability [130]. Moreover, the H-GC 
electrode shows cationic shift both in reduction and oxidation reactions. This peak potential shift towards 0V 
shows that the H-GC has the higher electrocatalytic activity and better conductivity of the electrode [108]. 
The cyclic voltammetry of H-GC was detected in N2 purged PBS solution after immersing in diverse 
concentration of H2O2 solution (figure 60 (c)). The concentrations used for cyclic voltammograms were 0µM 
(PBS), 500µM, 1mM, 5mM, 10mM, 50mM and 100mM. When the hydrogen peroxide is absent, the redox 
reaction does not happen. From the concentration of 5mM, the respective reduction and oxidation peak started 
to be visible at the potential of -0.3V and 0.8V. This means that the concentration below 5mM are not either 
detectable with our hydrogel sensor or the absorbed amount of H2O2 is not enough for 10min duration, thus it 
needs more immersion time.  
The oxidation and reduction peak current plotted against the various concentrations of H2O2 (figure 60 (d)).

one are higher than 1. This denotes that the redox process is not thoroughly reversible.

4.4. Swelling 
Hydrogel based skin-integrated wearable biosensor provides flexibility, stretchability, and human tissue like 
soft nature due to its higher water content [141]. However, its water absorbing property makes hydrogel swell 
and inflate when the water content is too much. As a result, the mechanical property of the hydrogel 
substantially decreases, which is harmful to human tissues when it is injected and further damages the 
surrounding tissues due to its expansion. Furthermore, when the over-swollen hydrogel is used as a biosensing 
application, the sensing ability decreases and even changes the results [142], [143]. Thus, it is important to 
develop anti-swelling hydrogel for its usage.  
In all experiments, the amount of AAm, PEI, LAP, TEMPOOL and Milli-Q water were fixed. However, 
different amounts of SBMA were used for each series of sample, which are S0A1 (SBMA 0mg), S0.2A1 
(SBMA 0.4mg), S0.6A1 (SBMA 1.2mg), and S1A1 (SBMA 2mg). The component of S1A1 is the same as 
1.0LR which is used for TVAM printing and electrochemical sensing.  
Figure 61 shows the result of the swelling ratios of the SxA1 hydrogels. The samples were immersed in PBS 
solution under room temperature. The comparison of hydrogel and blood plasma properties is described in 
section 4.3. The swelling ratio of SxA1 showed similar values until 20 mins. The remarkable thing is that the 
sample without any SBMA started to degrade after 20 mins and totally degraded in 60 mins. Followed by the 
sample with the second smallest amount of SBMA, which is S0.2A1, showed a decrease in mass after 30 
minutes, began to dissociate in 90 minutes, and was completely degraded after 120 minutes. Furthermore, 
S0.6A1 showed an increase in mass until 90 minutes and started to degrade (Appendix E). The image of this 
swelling and degradation behavior of each series is depicted in figure 62. This demonstrates that the hydrogel 
with higher SBMA concentration swell less and have a longer duration time. This might be due to the water 
absorption property of SBMA that the higher the SBMA concentration, the more compact structure due to its 
linear shape. Moreover, the ionic interactions between cation and anion decreases the water uptake. The 
ammonium and sulfonate moieties acts as a dynamic crosslinker and reduces the water volume fraction [144].

4.5. Mechanical Test 
A compression test was conducted to get a stress-strain curve to investigate the compressive properties of cast 
and printed cylinders using 1.0LR resin. Figure 64 (a) is a representative plot of one of the seven attempts 
depicting non-linear elastic behavior of the material and (b) is the one with average and standard deviation of 
all seven trials. In figure 64 (b), the casting sample showed the mean stress 129.69kPa and the printing one 
showed 196.12kPa at strain 0.7. This shows that to compress the sample having 30% of its original height, the 
casting sample requires less stress to be loaded, meaning it is brittle than the printing one.  
Young’s modulus (E) of the samples were calculated as the slope of the linear part of the stress-strain graph. 
Casting has an average young’s modulus of 43.62 kPa and printing has 99.56kPa. Since the young’s modulus 
stands for the material deformation under applied compressive force before the plastic deformation starts, the 
printing one is much stiffer than the casting one. Thus casting shows ductile, and printing shows brittle property. 
The young’s modulus of the human skin is around 100kPa for dermis and muscle and thyroid are approximately 
50kPa [145]. Since our printed sample has an E value of 99.56kPa, this shows a perfect application for skin. 
Due to the similar E value between printing sample and human skin, our wound bandage can provide great 
conformability while use.  
The area under curve (AUC) of stress-strain compression test graph is the energy to the maximum stress. This 
energy is used to deform the sample and is called toughness. The larger the AUC, the tougher the sample, 
which means that it requires more energy for a sample to break or deform. By MATLAB (appendix I), AUC 
was calculated. The printing sample has an AUC of 45.171kPa and the casting one has 22.716kPa. This shows 
that the printing sample is tougher than the casting one, requiring more energy for its shape to be deformed.  
As shown in figure 64 (b), casting samples show higher standard deviation compared to the printing samples. 
This means that in relation to the mean, the data are more spread out and having less consistency even though 
they are generated under same condition. On contrary, printing samples demonstrated that the stress and stress 
values collected from each trial are clustered more around the mean, suggesting the samples fabricated in same 
conditions have less variance with the uniform outcome. Hence, printing is preferred to casting for our material.

5. Conclusion and Future works 
Conclusion In this study, I have successfully demonstrated the capability of tomographic volumetric 3d 
printing to fabricate zwitterion hydrogel based smart skin wound bandage with simple structures designed by 
CAD program and optimized by CAL software. The TVAM auspiciously printed the bear, cone, cube, and 
hollow structure with a better mechanical property compared to casting method. This showed the possibility 
to print out much more complex structures with a higher resolution when the proper optical characterization 
of the printer and the optimization of the material is done. Additionally, this study proved that the 1.0LR 
hydrogel detects the H2O2 having the LOD value of 38.92mM, LOQ value of 129.77mM and tends to swell 
less than the other series of hydrogel containing less zwitterions. The combination of user-oriented, high-speed 
TVAM technology and biocompatible, characterizable, human tissue like hydrogel are promising for 
application as patient specific, mass production, and highly accessible smart wound bandage.  
 
Future works To begin with, in our studies, the hydrogen peroxide detection was conducted in PBS. Even 
though PBS somehow possess similar chemical agents, this is not an ‘ideal’ human blood plasma. This suggests 
that in future, the detection should be conducted in human blood serum or plasma for better accuracy and 
feasibility for in vivo use.  
It was impossible to know the exact ability of resolution for the TVAM printer since we did not know the value 
of the projector’s magnification, numerical aperture, side length of the voxel and so on. If I knew these criteria 
and calculated the etendue-limited resolution, it might be much helpful for setting the resolution goal of my 
project and finding out the best characterization of resin. This also goes same for the photopolymer. I did not 
conduct the experiment to figure out the molar absorption coefficient of the photoinitiator and the intensity of 
the light transmitted. When I knew these values, I could have checked the minimal amount of photoinitiator 
needed for our resin, thus it might provide me with better photopolymer resin optimization and higher 
resolution.  
Due to the flexibility of designing the architecture, diversity of material choice to print, there are wide range 
of applications fabricable for the future studies. First, the micro-needle structure can be designed with CAD 
program and printed with VAM. Micro-needle provides minimal-invasive transdermal drug delivery. 
Hydrogel-based micro needle can provide a cell-friendly environment to enhance the tissue generation. 
Moreover, the interlocking of tissue with needles will improve the tissue adhesion and support the closure of 
the wound. The piercing pain won’t be a problem for micro-needles compared to the common syringe injection 
since the micro-needle has a miniaturized structures which does not reach the nerve endings by overcoming 
the stratum corneum barrier [146], [147].  
Second, by using the SMP material, 4D printing can be done instead of limiting its ability to 3D. SMPs have 
a deformable molecular chain. They are rigid at room temperature, but they become rubbery state above a glass 
transition temperature and return to their rigid state after they cool down [148]. By using this property, I can 
fabricate a biodegradable stent for treating narrowed coronary arteries or a soft, biocompatible, biodegradable 
gripper that can be used during the surgery. Moreover, if SMP is combined with biodegradable battery, it will 
allow us to fabricate portable-implantable biodevice.

Third, the cell containing hydrogel wound dressing can be printed directly from the TVAM. The extensive 
burn injuries need a surgical excision of the injured skin and reconstruction with skin substitutes. In order to 
fabricate the substitutional skin, bioink can be used. During the experiment, volumetric printing does not put 
any stress on the ink compared to the layer-by-layer printing process and provides fast printing speed which 
are a huge benefit for increasing the cell viability [3]. By scanning the wound using computed topography and 
magnetic resonance imaging, generating a CAD file for personalized design, and selecting adequate 
biomaterials and cells, the bioink based volumetric 3D printing technology can fabricate the wound dressing 
with excellent skin repair and regeneration properties.  
Fourth, the drug loaded printed tablets can be printed with this technique. Since the volumetric 3D printer can 
print axisymmetric structure and by diverting SBMA amount the degradation rate can be adjusted, the drug 
containing hydrogel can be printed as a tablet. In the swelling test, when the amount of SBMA decreases, it 
showed a tendency to degrade faster. This shows the possibility of our material can be used as a drug 
encapsulation. By adjusting the resin’s SBMA amount and its pH, it can disintegrate in our body and release 
the drug particles. Furthermore, since it has the ability to be part of the liquid solution, it can be easily dissolved 
in stomach and intestines and be absorbed. Additionally, the printed structure will be layerless compared to 
other AM techniques. This means there are no delamination problems, hence, there are less possibility of drug 
to be released on their path before reaching the reactive site. Moreover, since the crosslinking time of the 
printer is fast without any stress generated compared to the extrusion-based printer, the nanoparticle-based 
drug have less possibility to get deformed while printing.      
Moreover, by embedding electronics to our skin wound bandage, this will allow our biosensor to detect other 
sensible criteria. For example, by UV lithography technique, which is used for semiconductor industry, I can 
fabricate the silver nanowires (AgNWs) electrode on top of our skin wound bandage. The AgNWs has 
antibacterial properties, low toxicity, biocompatibility, good sensitivity with high chemical stability [149]. 
Thus, by embedding this on top of our biosensor, we can add additional applications apart from sensing H2O2, 
such as organic photovoltaics, microelectronics, and biological sensors for sensing glucose and cholesterol and 
others.
