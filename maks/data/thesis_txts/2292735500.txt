Abstract
As for the necessity of decreasing the CO2 emissions and therefore
avoid global warming many alternative ways of producing energy are
being developed nowadays. Solar energy plays an important role as
it is a clean and endless energy source. The development of existing
solar technologies together with the creation of new and more eﬃcient
solar devices will yield on a higher employment of solar energy around
the world.
A new micro solar collector which purpose is to deliver thermal
heat up to 400oC and electricity from building roofs will be tested. To
reach that high temperature multi wall carbon nanotubes diluyed in
water and in Therminol 55 nanoﬂuids will be tested. Due to external
factors the micro solar device was not able to be used anymore thus
a new device was selected, in this case a glass capillary tube.
A µPIV experiment was carried out to determine the ﬂuid behavior
in a rectangular capillary tube concluding on a laminar ﬂow with a
Reynolds number Re=3.46. Also the velocity proﬁle was obtained and
compared with the theoretical one.
Moreover several thermal experiments with a ﬂow rate of 10 and
20ml/h and DI nanoﬂuid were done and the optical eﬃciency of the
collector was calculated and compared yielding on a η = 52.6% and
η = 87% respectively. Furthermore DI and TH nanoﬂuid were tested
and once again the eﬃciency was calculated yielding on ηDI = 87%
and ηTH = 37.35%. By using DI nanoﬂuid a stagnation temperature
of 35oC was reached.
Last experiment where a concentrating lens with a concentration ratio
of 16 was done and an optical eﬃciency of ηconc = 17% was obtained.
A stagnation temperature of 44.5oC was reached.
The results of the experiments with and without concentration
were compared concluding that a better eﬃciency is obtained if no
concentration is applied due to the poor optical performance of the
lens but a lower temperature is achieved. The capillary tube will not
be able to reach the required temperature and therefore will not be
useful for high temperature processes.

Introduction
Renewable energies are nowadays playing an important role in the energy
sector since one of the main aims for the next years is achieving a 0%
CO2 emission thus reducing considerably global warming.To be able to reach
that milestone energy sources such as solar energy, wind power, biomass or
geothermal energy will take a big part from now on to the detriment of pol-
luting energy sources as coal, oil or natural gas. Australia has the highest
average solar radiation per square meter in the world although solar energy in
Australia provides only a 0.1% of Australia’s total primary energy consump-
tion with thermal water heating as the most common [1]. The development
of existing solar technologies will yield in more eﬃcient solar panels and solar
collectors and therefore a higher use of solar energy in the country will occur.
Many other countries such as Germany, China or Italy have made a great
step forward in the use of solar energy.
By 2010 Germany produced 9.8
GJ just from PV panels while China and Italy produced 3.05 and 1.2 GJ
respectively. In 2014 the electricity production for the same countries were
increased up to 35.5 GJ for Germany, 18.3 GJ for China and 17.6 GJ for Italy
being the Top three countries in solar electricity production in the world [2].
Denmark has already reached its goal for solar energy generation capacity in
2020 eight years in advance. It has been possible to a large extent on the
implementation of net metering, meaning that the electricity surplus can be
stored in the public grid making solar energy more attractive [3].
Regarding solar thermal energy the total installed capacity in operation
worldwide by the end of 2013 was 374.7 GWth
corresponding to a surface of 535 million square meters of collector area
with China as the main user of thermal energy with a total capacity opera-
tion installed of 262.3 GWth followed by Europe with 4.1 GWth. The total
amount shared by China and Europe yields on the 82 % of the total installed
capacity in the world. The remaining is shared by USA and Canada with
an installed capacity of 17,7 GWth, Asia minus China with 10 GWth, Latin
America with 8.7 GWth, the MENA2 countries with 6.1 GWth, Australia and
New Zealand with 5.9 GWth and the Sub-Sahara Countries with a total in-
stalled capacity of 1.2 GWth.
All this thermal energy is collected by means of diﬀerent devices. 75.5% is
collected by evacuated tube collectors, 22.4% by glazed ﬂat plate collectors,

1.1
Solar Thermal Collectors
A solar collector can be considered as a kind of heat exchanger that converts
solar radiation into heat that would lately be transferred to a medium (water,
air, solar ﬂuid). The collector is divided in diﬀerent parts such as outer and
inner cover, heating ﬂuid, absorber, insulation, heat transfer medium and the
collector box. There are two diﬀerent kinds of solar collectors, concentrating

1.1.1
Flat Plate Collectors
Flat plate collectors consists on an absorber, a cover, a heat-transport ﬂuid
and insulation. The absorber is a dark surface which transfers the absorbed
energy from the sun to a ﬂuid, the transparent cover reduces convection and
radiation losses to the atmosphere. The heating ﬂuid removes heat from the
absorber and the insulation avoid thermal losses through the backside of the
collector. Air heaters are considered ﬂat plate collectors and follow the same
principle as the water or solar ﬂuid ones but in this case the ﬂuid tubes are
replaced by ducts.
Location and time of the year are also important factors to consider when
mounting ﬂat-plate collectors. The amount of solar radiation varies widely
and can reach values up to 1100W/m2 and the wavelength range goes from
0.3 to 3µm [6].

1.1.3
Overall Heat Loss Coeﬃcient of the Collector
An overall loss coeﬃcient for a solar collector will be developed in order to
simplify the equations since non-linear and diﬀerential equations will apply.
Figure 2 shows a thermal network for a two-cover system. The tempera-
ture at some point of the plate will be Tp, an amount S of solar energy will be
absorbed by the plate where S is equal to the incident solar radiation reduced
by the optical losses. This energy S previously absorbed is now distributed
to thermal losses through the bottom and top and also to useful energy gain.
Notice that the energy loss through the top is due to radiation and convec-
tion between parallel plates. The energy transfer between the plate at Tp
and the ﬁrst cover at Tc1 is the same that between any other two neighboring
covers. Also notice that the energy lost to the surroundings coming from the
top cover will match the previous assertions.

1.1.7
Flow Distribution in Collectors
Performance calculations are based on assumptions of uniform ﬂow distribu-
tion in all the risers in all collector units. If ﬂow is not uniform the parts
with ﬂow through risers will have lower FR. Therefore the design of headers
and risers to obtain a good performance is very important. It is even more
important in forced-circulation systems (Dunkel and Davey 1970). Flow is
turbulent in headers and laminar in risers.
The implication of pressure distributions are as follow: pressure drops are
the same all along the absorber meaning that high ﬂows will occur in end
risers and low ﬂows in center risers [6].

1.6.2
Operating principle of a rotary vane vacuum
A rotary vane vacuum pump consist of a housing, an eccentrically installed
rotor, vanes that move radially under centrifugal and resilient forces, the inlet
and outlet, the vacuum chamber and the outlet valve.
Figure 8: Operating principle rotary vane vacuum pump
The working chamber is located inside the housing and is restricted by
the stator, rotor and the vanes. The working chamber is divided into two
separate compartments with variable volumes by the eccentrically rotor and
vanes. As the rotor turns, gas ﬂows into the enlarging suction chamber until
it is sealed oﬀby the second vane. The enclosed gas is then compressed
until the outlet valve opens against atmospheric pressure. The outlet valve
is oil-sealed. When the valve is open, a small amount of oil enters the suction
chamber and not only lubricates it but also seals the vanes against the housing
(stator) [29].

Conclusion
The eﬃciency of a micro solar collector was meant to be studied in this the-
sis, nevertheless due to external factors the eﬃciency of a capillary tube was
ﬁnally studied and analyzed.
A µPIV analysis was carried out to determine the behavior of the ﬂuid in-
side a capillary tube, resulting in a laminar ﬂow. Moreover the vectors and
therefore the velocity proﬁle was obtained and compared with the theoretical
one.
It is worth to say that an improvement on the results could have been done
by better understanding the functioning of the software.
Furthermore several thermal experiments where the capillary tube per-
formed as a solar receiver were carried out. Two diﬀerent ﬂow rates of 10
ml/h and 20 ml/h with the same nanoﬂuid as main thermal liquid, a solar
intensity of 1000 W/m2 and a measured ambient temperature Tamb = 23oC
77
==PAGE:79==
4
CONCLUSION
were accomplished. The results showed a collector eﬃciency of 53% with a
collector mean temperature Tmean = 28.4oC and 87% with a collector mean
temperature Tmean = 28.3oC respectively, setting 20ml/h as the desired ﬂow
rate.
Moreover two diﬀerent nanoﬂuids were tested under the same conditions in
order to compare its performance and select the best. In this case the solar
collector obtained an eﬃciency of 87% when DI nanoﬂuid was used against
a 37.4% for TH nanoﬂuid.
These high diﬀerence between both liquids is
mainly due to their diﬀerent speciﬁc heat properties and, to a less extent, to
their diﬀerent concentration of nanoparticles.
Once the nanoﬂuid and ﬂow rates were determined diﬀerent set ups of
the solar collector were tested. First the collector was tried out under the
solar simulator and its eﬃciency curve was determined by varying the inlet
temperature. Second a concentrating rod lens with a concentration ratio of
16 was implemented. Additionally the eﬃciency curve was determined again
by modifying the inlet temperature of the device and compared.
The optical eﬃciency obtained in the solar collector without applying con-
centration was η = 87% versus ηconc = 17% and a diﬀerence between mean
collector and ambient temperature of 12oC and 21.6oC respectively.
The
stagnation temperatures were therefore calculated resulting on Tstag = 35oC
and Tstag conc = 44.5oC.
In conclusion a ﬂow rate of 20ml/h and DI nanoﬂuid yielded on best re-
sults for a constant solar intensity of 1000 W/m2 and an ambient temperature
of the room Tamb = 23oC. A better performance was also obtained without
applying concentration due to the poor optical quality of the rod lens and
the impossibility of fulﬁlling the new focal distance with the available set up.
Nevertheless higher temperatures were obtained when the concentrating lens
was implemented. Moreover generally low temperatures were achieved in all
the experiments mostly due to heat losses in the capillary tube since no in-
sulation was applied together with a low concentration of carbon nanotubes
in the nanoﬂuid, not yielding on a 100% absorptance ﬂuid.
