Abstract
Forecasting of future events is a relevant subject in a lot of contexts. A fore-
caster often has to make decisions on which forecasting model is most well-suited
for his or her purpose. These decisions are based on so-called scoring rules, that
are designed to ﬁnd the superior model by rewarding accurate and penalizing
erroneous predictions. This thesis aims to examine the ﬁeld on evaluation of
distributional forecasts, on which the knowledge is limited compared to the
simpler and more traditional point forecasts. We assess this by applying the
logarithmic (log) score, the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) and
the interval score to a selection of models in two case studies. The ﬁrst case
study concerns forecasted normalized wind power and the second concerns ex-
change rates. Both of them contains multivariate forecasts, encouraging us to
apply the multivariate analogs of the log score and CRPS too. We hope to be
able to make some useful conclusions with regard to how one should evaluate
univariate as well as multivariate forecasts, especially the latter, as the research
on this topic is quite sparse.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
Accurate prediction of future events is a highly desired and beneﬁcial skill in
numerous contexts. For instance, a provider of wind energy would have an eco-
nomic interest in being able to forecast changes to wind speed ahead of time,
since wind power production depends heavily on wind speed. Another example
of a ﬁeld with a heavy demand for reliable forecasting is the ﬁnancial sector
that wishes to minimize risk and potential loss from its investments.
Even though real variables like wind speed and share prices are in fact deter-
ministic, their realizations will appear to have a stochastic component, either
because measurement with inﬁnite precision is impossible, or because the un-
derlying generating process is too complex to fully account for when modeling
the process. Hence, forecasts of such variables are probabilistic, why a thorough
forecast should ideally predict the full density of the variable in question or at
least include some measure of the variability, e.g. in the form of quantiles.
1.2
Goal and overview of this thesis
This paper aims to dive into the subject of how competing models for forecast-
ing of continuous variables can be evaluated in a way where they are directly
compared to one another. First, a brief literature review in chapter 2 will pro-
vide an overview of the research that has been done in this ﬁeld and which
questions remain to be answered. This will motivate the introduction of scor-
ing rules which are deﬁned and explained in chapter 3. Chapter 4 presents the
other mathematical methods necessary for analysis. Chapter 5 and 6 contains
two case studies on real data, where the scoring rules in question are applied to
competing models and evaluated. The ﬁrst case study deals with normalized
wind power while the second concerns ﬁnancial data. Both cases motivate the
7
==PAGE:9==
exploration of univariate as well as multivariate models and scoring rules. The
results are discussed in chapter 7 and the main conclusions are summarized in
chapter 8.
In short, the goal of this thesis can be outlined as the desire to discuss and
contribute to the following questions:
• How are predictive models evaluated in practical situations today?
• Which issues are associated with the current practice, and how can we
assess them, i.e. how do we make sure to score models reliably?
• How well do the proper scores, the logarithmic score, the continuous
ranked probaility score (CRPS) and the interval score apply in real prob-
lems, and how do they compare? (cf. section 3)
• How can we evaluate models for multivariate forecasts?
• Do we gain anything from the eﬀort of evaluating a multivariate forecast
with multivariate scores compared to evaluation of its univariate compo-
nents individually with univariate scores?
• What are the advantages and disadvantages of application of diﬀerent
scoring rules in terms of stability and eﬃciency?
The ﬁrst two questions are covered by the literature review, while the four last
questions requires further analysis and will be in focus throughout the report
including the two case studies.

Chapter 2
Background
Clearly, any forecast that is going to aﬀect decisions by some user should be
evaluated in terms of accuracy. Since the true model behind an observed process
is never known, the selection of possible models is virtually inﬁnitely large,
which invokes the need to be able to compare diﬀerent models in a reliable way.
Both of these goals can be met by applying of a suitable so called scoring rule
to the forecast (Gneiting 2007)[1]. What constitutes a suitable scoring rule will
be covered in section 3.1. For now, we shall just use the fact that a scoring rule
is a forecasting evaluation method.
Motivation
Gneiting (2011)[2] made a comprehensive eﬀort to reveal how forecasters are
evaluating their forecasts, and what guidelines they follow, by the investigating
the evaluation approaches in more than 100 forecasting articles from 2008. It
concluded that scoring rules like the squared error (SE), the absolute error (AE)
and the absolute percentage error (APE) were very popular in the forecasting
ﬁeld, despite lack of a careful reasoning behind selecting and trust in those
scoring rules. Gneiting demonstrated that this is a problematic practice, by
conducting a simple simulation study. He simulated from the stochastic process,

Overview
Generally, we can divide forecasting models into three main categories: point,
density, and cumulative distribution forecasts (cf. section 3.1). For all of them,
diﬀerent scoring rules have been proposed and applied, with great diﬀerence in
extent (Gneiting 2007)[1].
The research on how one should evaluate and compare competing forecasts
goes back decades. Traditionally, point and interval forecasts have been most
widely used and generally regarded as ﬁne (Diebold et al. 1997)[3], but they
lack versatility and it has proven infeasible to establish a universal scoring rule
for point forecast evaluation.
Broad studies on various kinds of time series
were conducted by Makridakis (1992)[4] and Armstrong (1992)[5], where scor-
ing rules such as the mean squared error (MSE) and mean average percentage
error (MAPE) among others were used for comparison of models. It was found
that diﬀerent scoring rules favored diﬀerent forecasting models w.r.t. diﬀerent
10
==PAGE:12==
data series, suggesting that it was necessary to select speciﬁc scoring rules to
evaluate models for diﬀerent types of data[4]. An example of this was provided
by Armstrong (1992)[5], as he concluded that the median relative absolute error
(MdRAE) was recommendable for comparing forecasting models on short times
series, while for longer series, the median absolute percentage error (MdAPE)
was better[5].
Over time, density forecasts have gained increased attention from researchers,
because of the need to capture the additional information they contain, espe-
cially in quanitative ﬁnance (Diebold et al. 1997). There is a challenge associ-
ated with density forecast evaluations in the sense that densities do not always
behave appropriately, as severe errors in estimation of the tail-areas (intervals
on a p.d.f. with small probability mass) can be hard to avoid and deal with
(Gneiting 2007)[1].
As opposed to the p.d.f., the CDF generally behaves more neatly due to its
double boundedness.
For this type of forecast, the very versatile (Gneiting
2007)[1] continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) was proposed by Mathe-
son (1976)[6].
Evaluation of multivariate forecasts
Considering multivariate time series, multivariate forecasting models that are
able to explain correlation are likely to capture more information, and thus yield
better predictions. There are limited published research on how to optimally
select the best multivariate model. Diebold (1999) [7] proposed an evaluation
method for multivariate densities, which was added to by Ko (et al. 2013)[8].
For the CDF, a multivariate generalization of the CRPS is stated in the lit-
erature (Gneiting 2007)[1], but apart from a paper by A. Møller (2013)[9] no
further work on this scoring rule is to be found.
Møller applied a general-
ized version of the multivariate CRPS to weather forecasts for the purpose of
post-processing them to yield better forecasts.
Concluding remarks
Regarding what has been achieved and what remains to be investigated in
this ﬁeld, Gneiting (2007)[1] among others (Bremnes 2004)[10] has called for
more attention on this, and despite this paper being 10 years in the past, there
has not been published a general summarizing recommendation guide on how
one should approach any forecasting evaluation problem.
Especially for the
multivariate CRPS there is a gap with respect to assessing whether and when
it should be recommended, and how well it performs compared to multivariate
density-focused scoring rules.

3.1
General characteristics of scoring rules
In this chapter, we shall limit our scope to univariate densities and continuous
forecasts, but generalize to multivariate forecasts in the case studies. Section
3.1 was much inspired by Gneiting (2007)[1].

sity or a cumulative distribution, and let x be the corresponding observation.
The task of a scoring rule, S(f, x) is to compare the forecast to the observed
value. If a sample size of n observations to be forecasted is available, we regard
the average score ¯S(f, x),

An intuitive way of scoring a forecast is to measure the loss associated with
the forecast with respect to the observation. For a point estimate, f = ˆx, an

better and therefore we consider scoring rules to be negatively oriented, i.e. if

From now on, we shall just denote average scores as well as individual scores
as S(f, x).
3.1.1

We now move on to consider a very important characteristic of scoring rules
called propriety. In continuation of the idea of a loss function, we note that in
the case of a 100% accurate prediction, the loss attains the value 0, while in

3.2
Important scoring rules
In this section, f is considered a forecasted p.d.f., F is the corresponding CDF
and x is the observed event.
We shall consider four diﬀerent scoring rules,
all aimed at issuing forecasts that includes an estimate of the uncertainty, by
predicting:
• the density (log score)
• the cumulative distribution (CRPS)
• quantiles (quantile score)
• prediction intervals (interval score)
All the scoring rules presented are proper (Gneiting 2007)[1].
3.2.1
Logarithmic score
The logarithmic score (Good 1952)[11], just referred to as the log score or LogS,
is deﬁned as
LogS(f, x) = −log(f(x))
(3.4)
Hence, under this scoring rule, the f with the largest mode will be selected, and
the mode will be the point prediction, ˆx. Note that the log score is identical to
the log-likelihood and the point prediction is the maximum likelihood estimate.

Quantile score

(Gneiting 2007 and 2008)[1][12].

4.1
Stochastic diﬀerential equations
The stochastic diﬀerential equation (SDE) is the basis for several models ana-
lyzed in the case studies. Consider a real time-dependent dynamical process,
xt. The SDE describing the behavior of xt can then be stated with the following
representation:
dxt = θ(xt, t)dt + σ(xt, t)dwt
(4.1)
Where θ is called the drift coeﬃcient, reﬂecting the deterministic part of the
process, while σ reﬂects the stochastic part and is refered to as the diﬀusion
coeﬃcient[13]. Simulating from eq. (4.1) will thus result in a distribution for
each t, and those are the kind of distributions that will be subject to evaluation
by scoring rules.

Case Study 1 - Normalized
Wind Power Production
For the ﬁrst case study our attention shall be directed towards wind power pro-
duction, especially the predictive distribution of the latter, which is a relevant
subject for several reasons. First, wind power has the desired property of being
asymmetrically distributed, and secondly, predictions with adjacent horizons
are hypothesized to be positively correlated, motivating evaluation analysis in
a multivariate framework as well. Furthermore, wind power is a non-negative
quantity, so the need to be able to deal with boundary conditions is brought
into question too. Finally, research in wind power is currently a hot subject
due to its economically and environmentally relevant applications[10][17].

The raw data to be predicted are wind power data from the danish Klim wind

Chapter 7
Discussion
Through the case studies on forecasting of normalized wind power and exchange
rates, we have sought to assess the problems deﬁned in 1.2.
Logartihmic score vs. CRPS
The case study on wind power has provided insight in the behavior and appli-
cability of especially the log score and the CRPS. With regard to this, there
are diﬀerence aspects to consider. Firstly, there is stability, which refers to how
well the scoring rule performs in terms of scoring similar models in a meaning-
ful way. We have observed that the CRPS is able to recognize similarity, by
assigning very comparable scores to them. The log score generally fails to do
this properly, as great diﬀerences in log scores are frequently observed in cases
where a forecaster likely wouldn’t consider the issued models to be diﬀerent. It
is noted that the interval score exhibited a stable performance, similarly to the
CRPS.
The superiority of CRPS and IvS on this matter comes down to how outly-
ing observations w.r.t. the evaluated model are treated. Because CRPS and
IvS are based on the CDF and two quantiles, respectively, outlying observations
are treated very similarly, since the former quantities are fairly stable. Given a
decent number of simulations, CDF and quantiles of a distribution can be well
approximated, as change in distribution of probability mass in the tails have
negligible impact on those. The log score, on the other hand, is based on the
p.d.f., where even small changes in the tails can result in many-fold change to
probabilities of unlikely events, and hence, to the log score.
Another grave problem arise at the appearance of observations predicted to
have a probability of 0 by the forecasting model. Such observations evaluate to
LogS = ∞. Clearly, it is not possible to average over a set of log scores contain-
46
==PAGE:48==
ing inﬁnite values, creating the need to assign small, nonzero probabilities to
those observations. But this is problematic, because the choice of a small prob-
ability still has huge impact on the resulting log score, and one cannot blindly
choose some low number. Therefore, at the very least some standardized ap-
proach for this problem is necessary to successfully make use of the log score in
these cases. Exactly this conclusion was also reached by Selten (1998)[21].
When not forced to deal with 0-probability observations, the log score might
still be useful, if the forecaster for some reason ﬁnds great importance in being
able to detect small diﬀerences in the tails of the predictive distributions. A very
recent study on evaluation of Markov Chain Monte Carlo forecasts (Gneiting
2017)[?] also applied and compared LogS and CRPS and reached conclusions
similar to ours w.r.t. stability. The diﬀerence in performance of log score contra
CRPS can be seen by comparing the results in ﬁg. 5.4 and 5.5. We did not
ﬁnd any signs of IvS diﬀering from CRPS in terms of performance, hence, how
it compares with the other scores is not discussed.
Thoughts on multivariate forecast evaluation
One of our goals were to investigate the advantages of putting an eﬀort into eval-
uating multivariate forecasts by multivariate scores rather than by univariate
scores on the marginal forecasts. In the case study on exchange rates this eﬀort
resulted in a great reduction in uncertainty of scores compared to the univariate
scoring rules. It is therefore indicated that for multivariate forecasts, multivari-
ate scoring rules should be preferred over univariate ones, because they appear
to distinguish between models more easily. Whether they do so in a meaningful
way will require further study, in which we have more prior knowledge on the
underlying true model.
All successful work on multivariate forecast evaluation in this study was based
on non-parametric models, limiting the options of stability of the multivariate
scores. Simulation studies on the log score, however, indicated that estimation
of this score by kde[20] is actually very accurate as long as it is not applied to
too observations with very large true density function values (cf. 5.14). Hence,
we can recommend this scoring method, but strongly suggest that its limita-
tions on accuracy are evaluated before used for decision-making.
A future simulation study analogous to the one just discussed, but concern-
ing CRPS instead of LogS should be interesting for assessing stability w.r.t.
probability - i.e. does the multivariate CRPS have limitations similar to the
log score, or is it everywhere stable?
Furthermore, for multivariate forecasts, execution time of numerical compu-
47
==PAGE:49==
tations become increasingly relevant with more dimensions. In both case stud-
ies this consequence was experienced for calculation of log scores, which were
rather slow for whole data sets. For instance, calculating the 3D log score for
all 2593 forecasting times in the wind power study took 1-2 hours, while CRPS
applied to the same data took 32 seconds on average (the latter being the only
simulation carefully tested for execution time). For the smaller data set in the
exchange rate study the log scores took around 20 minutes to compute, and
CRPS took a few seconds.
The reason for the diﬀerence lies in estimation of the p.d.f. vs. the CDF. The
CDF can be estimated fast and eﬃciently by the empirical CDF (eq. (5.9)),
while the p.d.f. estimation by kde is much slower. The execution time of the
latter is very dependent on grid resolution and the number of simulations, but
so are the goodness of the estimate. Surely, kde followed by interpolation is
likely a suboptimal approach, but it is still noteworthy that CRPS is a fast
method for multivariate problems. For future work in this regard, we imagine
extensive exploration of the possibilities and limitations of CRPS in terms of
accuracy and execution times for various problems.
Overall this study has been very exciting to perform, the biggest conclusion
being that much more work is left to be done on this subject.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
We have compared the logarithmic score, the CRPS and the interval score for
univariate forecasts, and found that all are easily applicable.
W.r.t.
small
changes in the predictive distribution, the CRPS and interval scores are stable
scoring rules, while the log score is very sensitive, and this sensitivity should be
taken into account, if a forecaster wishes to apply it. Furthermore, we have ap-
plied the log score and CRPS for multivariate forecasts, where CRPS was found
to be advantageous in terms of computation time, while the log score was slower,
although heavily inﬂuenced by our way of estimating the p.d.f. The multivari-
ate log score estimates were seen to be very accurate above some threshold,
given suﬃciently large grid size and number of simulations. A similar conclu-
sion regarding CRPS is yet to be made, but its stability in one dimension might
indicate that this also holds for higher dimensions. Finally, we conclude that
there is a gain associated with applying multivariate scoring rules on multivari-
ate forecasts, in terms of precision, compared to their univariate counterparts
on the marginal forecasts. Based on the whole analysis, we hypothesize CRPS
to be a very eﬃcient, versatile and stable scoring rule for multivariate forecasts,
and should make a good candidate for being subject to future studies.

Trilinear interpolation
Tilinear interpolation of a pointwise estimate V (x) of a function f(x) is summa-
rized below, as copied directly from wikipedia[22]. First, estimate (xd, yd, zd):
xd = (x −x0)/(x1 −x0)
yd = (y −y0)/(y1 −y0)
zd = (z −z0)/(z1 −z0)
(A.3)
Then estimate (c00, c01, c10, c11):
c00 = V [x0, y0, z0](1 −xd) + V [x1, y0, z0]xd
c01 = V [x0, y0, z1](1 −xd) + V [x1, y0, z1]xd
c10 = V [x0, y1, z0](1 −xd) + V [x1, y1, z0]xd
c11 = V [x0, y1, z1](1 −xd) + V [x1, y1, z1]xd
(A.4)
Then estimate (c0, c1):
c0 = c00(1 −yd) + c10yd
c1 = c01(1 −yd) + c11yd
(A.5)
And ﬁnally, compute the approximate function value f(x) ≈c:
c = c0(1 −zd) + c1zd
(A.6)
