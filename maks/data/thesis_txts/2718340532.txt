Abstract 
The End-of-Life (EoL) treatments of wind turbine blades have been compared 
according to their Life-cycle Assessment, Absolute Environmental Sustainability 
Assessment, and cost analysis performances. The focus of this study was the 
recycling of the blades; hence, processes such as Mechanical Grinding, High Voltage 
Pulse Fragmentation, Pyrolysis (microwave), Fluidized Bed, and Solvolysis have 
been compared. Additionally, co-combustion (i.e., Cement Kiln Route) has been 
assessed as it is one of the mostly discussed solutions in the literature. Moreover, 
carbon fiber- and glass fiber-reinforced composites have been compared based on 
their environmental and economic performances. Wind turbine blades manufactured 
from Magnesium Alloy, Aluminum Alloy, Steel (Stainless), and Steel (Low-carbon) 
have been researched and evaluated similarly and are compared to fiber-reinforced 
composites. At present, only Aluminum Alloy wind turbine blades are available, the 
other metals are hypothetical. It became evident, that the reuse scenario of the 
recovered materials plays a crucial role in the ranking of the EoL processes. Reuse of 
the recovered matrix mix powder as PLA for 3D printing in Mechanical grinding is 
the most beneficial approach for glass and carbon fibers. From an environmental 
perspective carbon fiber performs better than glass fiber, whereas, from an economic 
perspective, glass fiber performs better. Alloys perform notably superior, in economic 
and environmental performance than composites from an EoL perspective –excluding 
mechanical grinding of composites. Additionally, the EoL treatment of metals is ripe 
and easier to apply. As a result, alloys could be a sustainable alternative to composites 
for wind turbine blade manufacturing.

Introduction 
Humans have been exploiting wind energy to propel boats along the Nile River as early as 
5,000 BC [1]. With the invention of electricity, another way of utilizing wind energy has 
been invented during the research to replace fossil fuels with renewable energies leading to 
the wind turbine's birth in July 1887 in Glassgow, Scotland [2]. In 1970, the oil shortages 
gave cause for governments to research and develop large-scale implementations of 
alternative energy sources, such as wind energy, to generate electricity [1]. Thus, only after 
the 1970s did the Wind Turbine's popularity rose [3]. The number of decommissioned wind 
turbines is estimated to increase in the near future, as prognosed in Figure 1.  
 
Figure 1 Prognosis of installed and decommissioned wind turbine plants in Denmark [4] 
The pattern of waste stemming from the Wind turbines is represented in Table 2. We observe 
that the wind turbine blade materials have the lowest recovery rate. Metals generate more 
waste than wind turbine blade materials but have significantly higher recovery rates. The 
expected lifespan of wind turbine blades is 20 years [4]. Therefore, in the future, the 
percentage of wind turbine blade material ending in landfills is expected to further increase. 
Unlike steel, iron, aluminum, and copper, the composition of wind turbines is seen as rather 
too complex to be efficiently recycled.

As represented in Table 3, the significant component of a Wind turbine blade is Carbon 
fiber-reinforced composite (CFRC) or Glass fiber-reinforced composite (GFRC), which are 
both fiber-reinforced composite (FRC) materials. While recycling methods for FRC have 
been identified in the literature, current applications are not widespread as the technology 
behind has not yet matured. Thus, most blades are currently sent to landfills [5] [6]. Material 
and energy usage of 1.5 MW wind turbine blades are represented in Table 3, where Carbon 
and Glass fiber (CF & GF) cover 60% of the overall weight. 
Table 3 General composition of Wind turbine blades [5] 
 
As more and more turbines reach their End-of-Life (EoL) stage, the necessity to analyze the 
best EoL option has emerged. While wind turbine blades are composed mainly of recyclable 
materials, the technical difficulty of recycling Glass or Carbon fiber-reinforced thermoset 
composites has made the media represent them as impossible to recycle or reclaim. Current 
research focuses on developing technical processes that can either transform the composites 
into shredded materials or recover the glass/carbon fibers by separating the composite 
constituents [7].  
Presently, the landfill of wind turbine blades is not banned, contributing to their pile-up in 
Landfills [8]. Increasing awareness of the topic has led the wind industry to call for an EU-
wide ban on landfilling wind turbine blades [9].

In Figure 2, the cross-section of a generic wind turbine blade has been represented. As seen

EoL Management Strategies of wind turbine blades 
Prior to identifying available EoL processes, it is vital to identify a valid waste hierarchy to 
categorize and prioritize the presently available EoL management strategies. From most to 
least preferred options, the stages of the waste hierarchy are Prevent, Reduce, Reuse, 
Recycle, Recover, and Dispose. The EU waste hierarchy has a similar order but does not 
differentiate between prevention and reduction [13]. 
 
Figure 3 Waste Management hierarchy of Wind turbine blades [14] 
Prevention and Reduction 
Waste prevention and reduction are used interchangeably in industry and literature sources 
and refer to waste minimization/reduction at the source. This practice works best if the EoL 
of a particular product bears high costs and the industry seeks improved manufacturing 
techniques to reduce waste production [15]. WindEurope suggests the following [11]: 
Mass reduction 
By reducing the mass of the blade, the waste that will stem from the blade will also 
be reduced. CFRC materials are known to be 20% stronger, 400% stiffer, and weigh 
15% less than GFRC materials [16]. Therefore, CFRC materials can be utilized to 
prevent and reduce waste production. 
Improved design standards 
Standards and certifications that promote a blade with a lower failure rate and a 
longer lifespan by design should be introduced. 
Introduction of international blade standards 
International blade standards will ensure that all blades can be serviced even if the 
manufacturer is no longer in the sector.

Reuse and Repurposing 
The wind turbine blades can either be refurbished to be reused in other wind farms or 
repurposed and used as a playground, furniture, or built structures (e.g., bridges, walkways, 
use as electrical transmission poles, and architectonic reuse). While both applications sound 
promising, the latter is unlikely to be an industrial-level solution to the issue [11].  
The lifespan of Wind turbine blades can be extended by 20 years if adequately refurbished 
[17]. For refurbishment, a 'remaining useful lifetime assessment' must be conducted [11]. 
The refurbishment process involves visual inspection using Scanning electron microscopy 
(SEM), ultrasonic inspection, and natural frequency measurements of the blades and may be 
followed up with weighing, rebalancing, reinforcing, and repainting. This method is only 
suitable for Wind turbine blades that are shorter than 50m (10kW – 1Mw) as the blades have 
to be transported first to the inspection facility and then to the new wind farm, which is rather 
challenging for larger blades [18]. 
Recycle 
In theory, almost all materials from refuse can be recycled; nevertheless, recycling still 
remains a technologically challenging and costly process. Segregation and separation of the 
materials remain challenging [15]. The challenging part of recycling Wind turbine blades is 
that the materials' separation can only occur after the FRC has been treated. Recycling 
processes applicable for CFRC and GFRC are represented in Figure 4. 
 
Figure 4 Adopted recycling processes for GF and CF [19] 
There are three types of recycling: upcycling, recycling, and downcycling. Upcycling is the 
process in which the waste is converted into a material with greater value or functionality 
than its original value. In recycling, the value of the reclaimed material is equal to the source 
material. Downcycling refers to a process in which there is a loss in value or functionality 
compared to the original product [20]. To identify if a process is up-, re-, down- cycling the

quality of recycled Carbon or Glass fiber (rCF & rGF) is highly relevant. The reclaimed rCF 
and rGF properties must be compared to virgin Carbon or Glass fiber (vCF & vGF). If the 
properties are better, equal, or worse, the processes can be classified as up-, re-, down- 
cycling, respectively. Before recycling, the blade has to be cut into slabs that allow easier 
transport and are suitable for the respective application. 
Mechanical Recycling 
Mechanical grinding: Mechanical grinding can be applied as a downcycling solution. 
Shredding results in a granulate-like mixture that can be sorted or classified using sieves and 
air-based techniques. Balsa wood and PVC may be present in the shredded composite if used 
as core materials. The shredded materials can be used as PLA (polylactic acid/polylactide) 
filaments for 3D printing, to prepare mortars and concrete (sand or aggregate replacement), 
to manufacture sound isolation panels, and as core material for hollow decking boards or 
multilayer solid boards. Furthermore, they can be used to manufacture new polymer 
composites for railroad ties, subway ties, yard rails, jersey barriers, bollards, and utility poles 
[7]. 
Electro-mechanical Recycling 
High voltage pulse fragmentation: High voltage pulse fragmentation can be used to recycle 
composite materials such as Wind turbine blades. Compared to mechanical grinding, the 
fibers recovered have higher quality, are longer, and are cleaner. Compared to alternative 
processes, other than mechanical grinding, the recovered fibers are relatively short. The 
quality of the recovered fibers is directly proportional to the energy used. Currently, there 
are only laboratory- and pilot-scale operations available [11]. 
Thermal Recycling 
Pyrolysis: The quality of the reclaimed CF in inert atmospheres has been analyzed using 
thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). Pyrolysis and post-pyrolysis can be used to recycle CF 
without harming fiber properties in controlled TGA conditions [6]. We could not identify 
commercial applications of this technology. 
Combustion 
Combustion technologies have been studied in comparison to pyrolysis, where GF showed 
a loss of 50% in tensile strength rendering the rCF useless for similar applications. rCF using 
combustion can be used for producing insulation wool. If well-optimized, pyrolysis (e.g., 
batch pyrolysis) can be used for both CF and GF. More optimum results can be achieved 
using post-pyrolysis (i.e., oxidation) or chemical modifications on fiber surfaces [6]. 
The market value of rGF is too low to allow efficient reuse of the recovered fibers. As it is 
not feasible, it is generally not applied. On the other hand, rCF only loses a 5–10% in tensile 
strength compared to combustion, leading to a loss of 18% of its initial tensile strength. rCF 
has a high market value and can compete with vGF and vCF [6]. 
Fluidized bed process: An alternative process to pyrolysis is the fluidized bed process. The 
recovered fibers lose most of their properties and have low value.

Steel and copper can be separated, by breaking the matrix or mechanical grinding, depending 
on the process configuration. The separated metals will be scrapped and then sent for 
smelting. Purification is generally unnecessary as the metals are not mixed with municipal 
waste. 
Chemical Recycling 
Solvolysis: Solvolysis-based processes can be conducted at sub- or supercritical conditions 
and can avoid char formation on the fiber. The temperature and pressure to be applied depend 
on the solvent used. Fibers are separated from the matrix by breaking the matrix polymer 
bonding [21]. The resins and adhesives are recovered as a liquid. 
Research conducted on polyamide 6 (PA6), polyurethane resin (PUR), and epoxy resin (EP) 
matrix under subcritical conditions at a laboratory scale succeeded in the complete removal 
of the polymer matrix through multi-stage solvent extraction without reducing both the 
length and tensile strength of the recovered CF. Moreover, the dissolution properties of the 
solvents were not compromised. The applied solvent was CreaSolv® [21]. 
On the contrary, other studies suggest that rGF are brittle and have low market value, 
rendering the process unfeasible. Furthermore, the recovered resins and adhesives cannot 
always be reused as material matrices since not all solvents can be separated from the solvate. 
Another problem with this process is that the applied chemicals generally have high costs 
and associated toxic risks as they are not always reusable [7]. 
Recover 
In this process, the wastes are transformed into refuse-derived fuel (RDF) or thermal energy 
after segregating materials that can be recycled (e.g., metals). 
RDF from blades is derived by mechanically grinding, separating metals, and mixing the 
shredded composite with wastepaper and water to bind fine dust. The ashes retrieved after 
combustion can be used as a source of silica in the clinker matrix. This process is referred to 
as cement co-processing (cement kiln route). Nevertheless, this process is not available 
worldwide as it requires modifications to cement facilities (e.g., cement factories) [7].

At this stage, the Wind turbine blades are landfilled or directly incinerated without energy 
recovery [11]. Currently, this is the most widely applied solution.

Methodology 
In this section, we will introduce the methodologies behind the LCA, Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment (AESA), and cost analysis. 
LCA 
LCA, which is one of several environmental management techniques (e.g., risk assessment, 
environmental performance evaluation, environmental auditing, and environmental impact 
assessment), uses an internationally standardized methodological framework to analyze and 
depict the environmental impacts of the respective life-cycle phases (e.g., material 
extraction, manufacturing, packaging, transportation, use, and End-of-Life) of a product or 
system [26] [25].  
For the comparative assessment of the identified EoL processes, this study will utilize a gate-
to-gate approach, starting after the wind turbine blades decommission to the end of the 
recycling process, and will be based on ISO 14040/4 standards. Sensitivity analysis will be 
performed to assess the uncertainties. 
AESA 
The aim of absolute environmental sustainability assessments (AESAs) is to assess the 
environmental sustainability in an absolute sense of a product or system by comparing the 
associated environmental pressure to its allocated environmental safe operating space (SOS) 
[27]. Global carrying capacities are scaled according to identified sharing principles and then 
will be upscaled industrial share within Denmark. 
Alternative materials Analysis 
This section is dedicated to comparing the EoL performances of various materials that could 
be used as alternative materials to construct wind turbine blades. The ranking will be based 
on raw materials cost, and their associated EoL processes Life-cycle Impact Assessment 
(LCIA) performance. 
Cost analysis 
The cost analysis will be based on Capital (CapEx) and Operational Expenditures (OpEx) 
data aggregated from Literature and online markets to compare the costs of the analyzed 
EoL processes. The sum of CapEx and OpEx per kg of materials will be reported as the total 
net cost. The recovered materials costs will be reported as added value. The price of the raw 
materials will be reported as the acquisition cost. The sum of these costs will be demonstrated 
as the balance of the process.

CFF 
The EU Product Environmental Footprint Category Rules Guidance (PEFCR) demands that 
the Circular Footprint Formula" (CFF), originally the End-of-Life (EoL) formula, is used to 
deal with multi-functionality in recycling, re-use, and energy recovery situations to model 
product waste [28]. Thus, it will be applied to prevent double accounting of the recycling, 
i.e., to prevent the recycling is substituted in multiple steps through out the Life-cycle of a 
product. 
Current market trends, demand, and quality of the recovered materials are considered by the 
CFF, so a more realistic crediting for recycling can be achieved in theory.

Discussion 
This section will synthesize the finding and discuss outcomes. 
LCA, AESA, and Cost Aspects 
In all assessments conducted, we observe that mechanical grinding was always one of the 
top-performing EoL treatment solution. The environmental and economic costs of PLA, 
which is the substitution of the recovered powder, are the reason why it performed this way. 
Therefore, it is vital to comprehensively research the substitution option of the recovered 
matrix powder. Major stakeholders have been invited to share their approaches to the EoL 
treatment of wind turbine blades, and it seems that the industry is already on track as the 
majority reported that they are working on mechanical grinding and new reuse applications 
for the recovered materials. 
The hotspot of the EoL treatments was electricity consumption for most of the systems. 
Variance in electricity consumption has distinct effects on the impact scores. As electricity 
is one of the hotspots in most EoL systems it should be analyzed in more detail. The Cement 
Kiln Reactor is not sensitive to this parameter. If a sustainable electricity market can be 
achieved, it can be argued that EoL processes such as Pyrolysis and Solvolysis will be even 
more competitive in terms of environmental performance. The major benefit of these two 
processes is that they can recover the carbon- and glass-fiber in its original length and 
physicochemical property. So, a closed material flow loop can be achieved with these 
processes in the future. The wind energy sector could also right now sustainably implement 
pyrolysis and solvolysis by cooperating with MRF. Ironically, to do so, they would need to 
ensure that the electricity used by the EoL processes is coming from wind turbines. 
Only mechanical grinding and its alternative, high voltage pulse fragmentation for GFRC 
are sustainable in absolute terms in water consumption and climate change. The full Cement 
Kiln Reactor has not been assessed (i.e., only the effect of co-combustions has been 
analyzed); therefore, it is not possible to conclude if it is sustainable in absolute terms. The 
AESA results are only valid for Denmark. Some of the EoL processes are not performing 
well in environmental terms and are stressing analyzed planetary boundaries carrying 
capacities considerably. We believe that the processes need further optimization and must 
perform significantly more efficiently to become environmentally sustainable. Therefore, 
further research and development to increase the efficiency of the EoL processes are 
necessary, if the fibers are to be reused for the production of wind turbine blades. Mechanical 
grinding can be used until alternative recycling processes ripen and become competitive in 
absolute environmental terms. 
Currently, most wind turbine blade manufacturers are using GF for their blades as it is 
cheaper compared to CF. In our assessment, we observed that GF indeed offers an economic 
benefit over CF. Nevertheless, the EoL treatment of CF is more conservative in the impact 
categories. So, as long as one of the best performing three EoL system is not to be used as 
the EoL treatment, we believe that the utilization of CFRC is the more viable option.

We believe that currently, the EoL processes of FRC have not become mainstream enough. 
Therefore, alternative materials could be used to manufacture wind turbines. The best 
alternatives would be lightweight alloys that can compete with FRC in terms of physical 
properties. In our assessment, we observed that Magnesium alloy is currently the most 
suitable alternative to FRC. Magnesium Alloys demonstrate highly competitive 
environmental and economic performance compared to FRC. Magnesium is listed in The 
2020 EU Critical Raw Materials List, in which the economic importance and supply risk are 
the two main parameters used to determine criticality for the EU [29]. Metals have the 
advantage that their EoL processes are better studied than FRCs. 
When we compare Figure 5 and Figure 16, we see that our results align with the literature 
except for the Fluidized bed system. This is because the CapEx of the fluidized bed system 
is significantly higher than other EoL systems. 
Inconsistencies in Impact Score Calculation 
The Life-cycle inventories were built on a merged database of OpenLCA 2.0 and EF 
secondary data. Therefore, the OpenLCA Impact assessment methods used (e.g., ILCD 
Midpoint+, EF 3.0) could not properly credit the recycling of carbon fiber for Pyrolysis, 
High Voltage Pulse Fragmentation, and Solvolysis. Furthermore, the matrix recovery in the 
Solvolysis processes could also not properly be calculated. 
LCA 
The rankings of the EoL solutions of FRC have considerably changed, especially for 
chemical recycling processes. The impact calculation across different inventories is not 
working properly. Therefore, this approach was used. The change in the impact scores also 
changed the rankings of the systems in other impact categories. Moreover, we observe that 
CFRC performs significantly better than GFRC in the EoL treatment processes. In the 
previous impact scores, CF was not performing this well, as its contribution to the impact 
cores was not modeled properly. 
The problem has been solved by creating an inventory based on literature for Carbon Fiber 
manufacturing replacing Carbon fibre production, production mix, at plant,technology mix, 
100% active substance - GLO. The previous flow of Epoxy resin, epoxy resin production, 
production mix, at plant, technology mix, 100% active substance – RER,  has been replaced 
with market for epoxy resin, liquid | epoxy resin, liquid | Cutoff, U – RER. 
EU PEFCR Circular Footprint Formula 
The EU PEFCR has been used to re-evaluate the EoL systems' performances in terms of

processes are Pyrolysis (CF), High Voltage Pulse Fragmentation (CF), Pyrolysis (GF) and

The cradle of the wind turbine blades is the extraction of the raw material. This stakeholder

Alternative Materials 
 
Figure 18 Life-cycle of wind turbine blades, manufactured from composite metals 
When metals are used for the manufacturing of wind turbine blades, then Life-cycle changes 
considerably. A closed loop can be achieved using metal, where the scrapped blades can be 
recycled by the initial material supplier. The blades will be scrapped in situ before sending 
them back to the manufacturer, so there would be no need for MRFs. This is only an 
assumption, and the behavior of the coating and paint are not regarded. Further, treatment 
may be required before recycling, or the ovens could be enough. 
We hypothesize that wind turbine blades produced from metals can perform as well as or 
even better than FRC. According to our research, the most ideal material is Magnesium 
Alloy. Magnesium alloys are explosive hazards and might cause risk in case of lightning 
strikes. Their ignition temperatures are between 470 to 550°C. However, magnesium alloys 
with high strength and high ignition temperature that passed the FAA flammability test 
without burning at all have been developed without compromising the strength of the 
material [30]. Moreover, initial acquisition costs are the cheapest amongst all materials 
tested. Its weight is slightly more than CFRC, yet significantly less than GFRC for blades 
with identical shape, and net volume. 
We neglected the hollowing of blades. This could mean that wind turbine blades with 
appropriate design, manufactured from Aluminum alloys or Steel could become competitive 
in weight. Their initial acquisition costs may be higher than GFRC, but in a long term, as 
they can be recycled infinitely, they would be a significant win. The EoL processes of metals 
are also well established and do not require as much research and development as FRCs. All 
of the alternative materials have significantly lower costs than the EoL treatment methods 
of FRC (excluding Mechanical grinding). Therefore, metals could be a viable replacement 
for FRC.

Three Pillars of Sustainability 
In this section, the findings will be assessed on the basis of three pillars of sustainability, 
namely Economy, Social, and Environment. 
From a social perspective, the implementation of the EoL processes will provide 
employment opportunities for people and is definitely advantageous. Therefore, as all EoL 
processes have similar social impacts, we assume that all processes are performing equally. 
In the cost analysis, we observed that mechanical grinding is the most profitable EoL process 
for composites and is followed by the EoL processes of metals. Metals are performing 
significantly better than composite materials in environmental terms. When these three are 
synthesized with equal weighting, we believe that metals are the better option from an EoL 
performance view.  
In our stakeholder assessment, we found that the majority of stakeholders have dedicated 
sustainability divisions, which are investigating sustainable EoL practices for FRC. They 
also stated that the environmental impacts throughout the life cycle play a role while 
selecting the type of wind turbine blade to be used. Thus, a cradle-to-cradle approach for 
metals should also be investigated and compared to composite materials to be able to identify 
the most sustainable option. 
To prevent wind turbine blades from piling up in landfills, the following solutions could be 
introduced: 
✓ Deposit-refund system would ensure that the turbine blades do not end up in landfills 
✓ Economic incentives should be used to make recycling more lucrative 
✓ Wind Park owners should also pay a portion of the EoL gate fee 
The deposit-refund system would mean that wind park owners pay an extra deposit for each 
wind turbine blade, which would only be refunded to them if the blades are sent to recycling 
facilities. So, the sustainable energy providers would have to pay extra attention to the EoL 
treatment of the wind turbine blades they acquire. This system works well for plastic and 
metal containers and could also be applied to wind turbine blades. 
Economic incentives could be introduced to make recycling more lucrative. The EoL gate 
fees could be covered by the governments. As renewable energy is to replace fossil fuels it 
will be used by everyone. Hence, it makes a lot of sense to spend some of the tax collected 
to ensure that the energy sector can develop sustainable EoL practices for not only wind 
turbine blades, but for the whole renewable energy market. 
An alternative solution would be to have Wind Park owners pay a portion of the EoL gate 
fee. According to our anonymous sources, currently, the biggest problem is that none of the 
stakeholders want to have to pay the EoL gate fee and is trying to pass it to the other party. 
A solution could be that all relevant stakeholders pay a reasonable share of the EoL gate fee. 
This portion should not be determined by academics but should be negotiated among the 
relevant stakeholders. Therefore, we would like to invite the stakeholders to be responsible.

Conclusions 
The economic and environmental performances of the wind turbine blades' EoL processes 
have been assessed using LCA, LCC, AESA, and cost analysis based on CapEx and OpEx. 
In the LCA, we observe that, based on the top three impact categories, from best to least in 
terms of overall environmental performance, are Mechanical grinding, High Voltage Pulse 
Fragmentation, co-combustion, Solvolysis, Pyrolysis, and Fluidized Bed. For GFRC the best 
EoL process is Mechanical grinding, and the worst process is Fluidized Bed. For CFRC the 
best EoL process is High Voltage Pulse Fragmentation, and the worst processes are Fluidized 
Bed, Pyrolysis, and Fluidized Bed. The Fluidized Bed performs more or less like Pyrolysis 
and Solvolysis for CFRC. If the fiber recovery rate could be increased, it could be on par 
with the other chemical recycling process. However, for GFRC, Fluidized Bed generally 
performs significantly worse. 
In the AESA, in terms of absolute environmental performance, only Mechanical grinding, 
High Voltage Pulse fragmentation, and co-combustion are sustainable for the impact 
categories of Climate change and water use. Other EoL processes are performing 
considerably worse in water consumption are not sustainable in absolute terms except for 
Solvolysis of CFRC. For GFRC, the best EoL process is Mechanical grinding and for CFRC 
the best EoL process is High voltage pulse fragmentation. 
In the alternative materials analysis, we observed that Magnesium Alloy, Aluminum Alloy, 
and Steel (both low-carbon and stainless) are performing well in terms of price and 
environmental performance of their EoL processes. Hence, it is worth investigating them as 
alternative blade materials and investing in them. 
The cost analysis shows that profit can be made if Mechanical grinding is used as EoL 
treatment of the wind turbine blades, assuming that the recovered materials are used as PLA 
for 3D printing. From most to least expensive, the processes can be ranked as Mechanical 
Grinding, Steel, Magnesium Alloy, Aluminum Alloy, High Voltage Pulse fragmentation, 
Pyrolysis, Solvolysis, and Fluidized Bed. For both CFRC and GFRC, the best EoL process 
is Mechanical grinding. The EoL treatment methods of metals are ripe, whereas FRC has a 
lot of uncertainties. 
In light of the collected data and the assessments, we concluded that, as currently, most of 
the wind turbine blades are manufactured from GFRC, the most suitable EoL treatment in 
terms of cost and Environmental performance is the Mechanical grinding process. 
Magnesium Alloys are competing well with GFRC and could be used as an alternative 
material for wind turbine blade production.

Appendix#1: Life-cycle Assessment of Wind turbine blade recycling 
Life-cycle Assessment (LCA), which is one of several environmental management 
techniques (e.g., risk assessment, environmental performance evaluation, environmental 
auditing, and environmental impact assessment), uses an internationally standardized 
methodological framework to analyze and depict the environmental impacts of the respective 
life-cycle phases (e.g., material extraction, manufacturing, packaging, transportation, use, 
and End-of-Life) of a product or system [26] [25].  
For the comparative assessment of the identified EoL processes, this study will utilize a gate-
to-gate approach, starting after the wind turbine blades decommission to the end of the 
recycling process. 
The goal of the LCA 
Intended application. This research is commenced to compare the environmental 
performances of the recycling processes of wind turbine blades. Evaluating improvement 
potentials from changes in product designs based on the EoL processes and identifying the 
most environmentally friendly EoL process by materials. 
Limitations due to methodological choices. This analysis cannot compare raw material 
extraction through production, use, and final disposal of the wind turbine blade life-cycle 
stages as it follows a gate-to-gate approach. Suggested product design changes are limited 
to their EoL performance. Reactors, vessels, and surrounding facilities are not part of the 
study because the focus is to study the use stage of EoL processes. Therefore, the results 
cannot be used to compare the environmental impacts associated with the technical 
equipment. 
MRFs did not want to share the information required (e.g., energy consumption) to model 
the recycling processes due to patent concerns. Data has been collected from literature and 
is not specific to the modeled process. Most data are from other composite materials EoL 
applications. Furthermore, the composition of the wind turbines has also been drawn from 
the literature. The composition can change the results drastically, and the Life-cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) results are specific to the presumed composition. Outflows of processes 
such as Pyrolysis and solvolysis were drawn from Aspen PLUS models or experiments 
available in the literature. 
Decision context and reason for carrying out the study. This study aims to support the wind 
energy sector in finding the most appropriate EoL processes (in terms of environmental 
performance) as an alternative to landfilling the wind turbine blades. This research ideally 
fits into decision context B (i.e., Meso/macro-level decision support) as it could lead to a 
global scale of change in the EoL stage of wind turbine blades. However, low stakeholder 
interest indicates that it will be in context A (i.e., Micro-level decision support). 
Target audience. The target audience is the thesis defense committee, Energy Utility 
companies, Wind turbine manufacturers, owners of wind farms, and Material Recovery

Use context 
Prior to defining the functional unit, a generalized application will be formulated. The typical 
use case will standardize the reference flow and help deal with fluctuating parameters (e.g., 
temperature). This study assumes that the environmental parameters (e.g., pressure) will be 
equal at the application locations and that the wind turbine blades consist of the materials 
defined in the Table 3. Two types of blades (carbon fiber and glass fiber fabric based) will 
be compared. The composition of the blades will be 60.4% CF or GF, 32.3% Resin and 
Adhesives (Epoxy Resin), 1.1% Steel, 0.3% Copper, 0.01% Aluminum, 2.3% Balsa, 1.7% 
PVC, 0.9% Paint, 0.7% Putty, 0.01% Spray Adhesive by weight. The total weight of each

on literature, and readily available Life-cycle Inventories (LCI) such as openLCA database 
2, Environmental Footprints, and Exiobase. The data has been modified where required to 
optimize the representativeness of the model. Unfortunately, the databases could not be 
harmonized, so the study had to be limited to the openLCA 2.0 database. 
Technological representativeness 
The data is from up-to-date Literature resources and is Technological representative. The 
background systems (e.g., energy sources) technologies were chosen that were as close as 
possible to the current electric market of the specified location. The modeled EoL processes 
are based on pilot-scale research projects, as no full-scale applications were found in the 
literature. In short, the modeled systems are hypothetical processes and references for how 
full-scale implementation would behave.

Preparation of the basis for the impact assessment. Applied LCIA methods meet 
international standards and are scientifically and technically valid. For this assessment, 
midpoint level Impact categories will be compared, which consist of Climate change, 
(Stratospheric) Ozone depletion, Human toxicity, Respiratory inorganics, Ionizing radiation, 
(Ground level) Photochemical ozone formation, Acidification (land and water), 
Eutrophication (land and water), Ecotoxicity (freshwater, marine, terrestrial), Land use, 
Resource depletion (of minerals, fossil, and renewable energy resources, water) [31]. ILCD 
2011 Midpoint+ and ReCiPe 2016 (E), midpoint level impact assessment methods, will be 
used and compared. An Egalitarian approach is used, which is long-term based on 
precautionary principle thinking [34], to ensure that the impacts are not underestimated and 
the decision support does not mislead the industry. As midpoint results were considered to 
provide a sufficient level of aggregation for the comparison, endpoint level impact categories 
were not assessed. 
Normalization and weighting have been applied using World (2010) E and EU27 2010, equal 
weighting for ReCiPe and ILCD, respectively. The midpoint impacts will be compared to 
identify the major impact contributors and analyze how much Environmental problem 
shifting will be caused by each system. 
Special requirements for system comparisons. The scope was prepared according to ISO 
14044. The system's scope was appropriately defined, allowing the systems to be compared. 
Moreover, the systems will be compared using the same functional unit equivalent 
methodological considerations, such as but not limited to performance, system boundaries, 
data quality, allocation procedures, decision rules on evaluating inputs and outputs, and 
impact assessment. Differences between these parameters of the systems will be identified 
and transparently reported [35].

High voltage pulse fragmentation. High voltage pulse fragmentation or high voltage 
fragmentation is an alternative to mechanical grinding and utilizes repetitive pulse electrical 
discharges within a dielectric liquid environment, usually water, to disintegrate solid 
material. Research on GFRC shows that the lowest resin residue was approximately 32% on 
the fibers and was achieved at 2,000 electrical pulses with an energy consumption of 
89.1MJ/kg. The best energy demand and efficiency performance was observed at 500 pulses 
with an energy consumption of 6.7MJ/kg and a resin residue of approximately 35% [22]. 
Investigation of CFRC suggests that the highest performance was observed at a throughput 
of 5.65kg/h using 4.3MJ/kg utilizing Electrodynamic fragmentation and a recycling quote 
of 60%. The water consumption of the setup was 2m³/h in a closed circuit with a loss of 5% 
[42]. For this study, it was assumed that the water loss is negligible and that the performance

fibers were recovered as no loss ratio was found in the literature. The recovered matrix

Life-cycle interpretation 
When weighted impact scores in Figure 26 are compared, it is evident that the most 
significant impact scores for the analyzed EoL processes are Water resource depletion, 
Human toxicity (cancer effects), and Freshwater ecotoxicity. The ranking is based on the 
highest positive weighted impact scores. Thus, this study will focus mainly on these impact 
categories. Based on the weighted and normalized scores, the EoL processes from most to 
least environmentally conservative are as follows High voltage pulse fragmentation (CF), 
Mechanical Grinding (CF), Mechanical Grinding (GF), High voltage pulse fragmentation 
(GF), Solvolysis (CF), Cement Kiln (CF), Cement Kiln (GF), Pyrolysis (CF), Fluidized Bed 
(CF), Solvolysis (GF), Pyrolysis (GF), Fluidized Bed (GF), respectively. The ranking is 
based on the Water resource depletion category because it has the biggest variance in the 
impact scores, and ecotoxicity data is mainly based on assumptions and bears high 
uncertainties. Unless a difference higher than a factor of over 1,000 and 100 is present for 
human toxicity and freshwater ecotoxicity scores, respectively, it is difficult to call an option 
preferable [44]. When analyzing the normalized impact scores, we observe that if other 
impact categories are prioritized, that the rankings can change significantly. ReCiPe and 
ILCD midpoint impacts scores are mostly in harmony. The small variances are caused by 
the characterization factors of the impact assessment methodologies. 
From this point on, this studies focal point will be the three most environmentally friendly 
EoL processes: Mechanical Grinding, High voltage pulse fragmentation, and Cement Kiln 
Reactor the most significant three impact categories: Water consumption, Human 
carcinogenic toxicity, and Freshwater ecotoxicity. ReCiPe is more recent than ILCD, so it

Uncertainty Analysis. This statistical procedure is used to quantify the uncertainties 
associated with the impact scores. The main aim of uncertainty analysis is to be precise on 
an absolute indicator, where the purpose is to quantify the relevant parameters. Systematic 
errors affecting all compared EoL systems will shift all results up or down systematically 
and, therefore, will not affect the result in absolute terms but in relative terms. To conduct 
an uncertainty analysis, statistical and corresponding statistical parameter values must be 
collected for quantitative parameters in the foreground system. Nevertheless, the analyzed 
processes are mainly lab- or pilot-scale applications, and as they are based on literature, it is 
impossible to collect primary data. Therefore, such an uncertainty study cannot be performed 
for this research [31]. The sources of uncertainty and variability for each type of uncertainty 
per LCA phase are given in Appendix#5: Sources of Uncertainty. A semi-quantitative 
assessment can be found in Appendix#7: Uncertainty Quantification and Propagation: The 
semi-quantitative Pedigree Matrix Approach.

further improvements will significantly affect the impact scores. The sensitivities were

Limitations of the LCA 
This study is entirely based on theoretical and lab-scale BATs, which have been upscaled, 
assuming that the respective inputs can be upscaled linearly with respect to the weight of the 
FRC scarp being processed. Data on surrounding facilities and reactor components could not 
be collected due to a lack of full-scale implementations. Nevertheless, this data is assumed 
to have a negligible impact on the absolute results. 
The gate-to-gate approach excludes processing of the blades into transportable scraps, 
transportation of the FRC scrap to the MRF, and transportation from the MRF to the new 
application site (landfill or reuse). 
The reuse applications are based on literature and are hypothetical. 
An uncertainty analysis could not be performed since first-hand data on the foreground 
systems (the unit processes) could not be gathered. 
Future scenarios are not considered (i.e., electricity production in 2035). 
Endpoint impact scores are not assessed. 
Life-cycle cost (LCC) was not performed due to a lack of data. 
The inventories are based on readily available databases and have not been adjusted to 
represent the state of 2022. 
A detailed inventory for Carbon fiber production is not present. 
Conclusions and Recommendations 
LCA has been conducted on recycling wind turbine blades in six EoL processes. The best 
performing options were identified as Mechanical grinding, High Voltage Pulse 
Fragmentation, and Cement Kiln Reactor from best to less environmentally friendly. The 
identified hotspots are reuse cases of recycled materials, Electricity consumption, and LHV 
of the FRC scrap (i.e., coal replacement). The hotpots are most important for the LCA and 
can drastically change the results. 
From the uncertainty analysis methods, we concluded that the type of substitution (i.e., reuse 
application of retrieved materials), electricity consumption, and coal are of major importance 
to this study and need to be assessed in more detail as they significantly impact the results. 
While the supplier of the electricity (i.e., country) did not significantly affect the results, the 
amount of energy consumed does matter. The LHV (i.e., coal replacement) was also 
sensitive.

Appendix#2: Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment of 
Wind turbine blade recycling 
The aim of absolute environmental sustainability assessments (AESAs) is to assess the 
environmentally sustainable in an absolute sense of a product or system by comparing the 
associated environmental pressure to its allocated environmental safe operating space (SOS) 
[27]. 
For this study, the impact assessment methods EF 3.0 Method (adapted) and EPD (2018) 
have been used as they are more recent compared to ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ and ReCiPe 
2016 used in the LCA section. Even though EF 3.1 was published, it was not used in this 
assessment as it was not present in the OpenLCA impact method database. The purpose of 
using more recently published impact assessment methods is that characterization and 
weighting factors may change over the years. 
The most important three impact categories according to EF 3.0 are Water use, Climate 
change, and Resource use (fossils), from most to least significant, respectively. In 
comparison to ILCDs weighted impacts, we observe that while Water use remains the most 
significant impact category, Human toxicity (cancerogenic) and freshwater ecotoxicity are 
no longer in the top three impact categories. The change may be linked to the high 
uncertainty in toxicity prediction methods used for the impact assessment.

Global Boundaries and Safe Operating Space 
Global Warming 
The Planetary boundary of climate change in literature is proposed as 350ppmv and 1W/m² 
for Atmospheric CO2 concentration and energy imbalance, respectively [45]. These 
boundaries are equal to 1.30E+13 and 2.83E+12CO2-eq kg/yr, respectively. The pre-
industrial levels are 280 ppmv and 0 W/m² [46], and the current state is 412.5ppmv (in 2020)  
[47] +2.83W/m² (in 2011) [48]. Another well know boundary set in the Paris Agreement is 
to limit global warming to below 2, preferably to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels [49].  
The first sharing principle we will use is an equal per capita distribution based on value to 
human beings (i.e., egalitarian) principles.  
Egalitarianism and luck egalitarianism holds respectively that it is unjust for 
individuals to be worse off than others, and that it is unjust for individuals to be worse 
off than others due to no responsibility of their own. In luck egalitarianism, personal 
responsibility works as a constraint on an otherwise equal distribution [50]. 
Nykvist suggests an equal per capita allocation to all inhabitants of the planet Earth as he 
describes climate change as a genuinely global boundary and suggests a boundary of 2tons 
per capita of CO2 annually [51]. This value is calculated by dividing the CO2 budget by the 
global population. We will calculate our budget based on the literature in the following 
sections.

The remaining global CO2 budget to not exceed +1.5°C (Paris Agreement preferable limit) 
of global warming is 400GtCO2, and the remaining time is 15–20 years [52]. Since currently, 
the annual global CO2 emission is 40GtCO2, the budget is expected to be used before the 
targeted time frame [53]. The remaining budget in 2022 is estimated to be 240GtCO2. The 
global population in 2020 is estimated as 7,753,000,000 [54]. Assuming that the population 
did not vary drastically from 2020 to 2022, for the following 11–16 years, the CO2 budget 
is 30.96 metric tons per capita. Being precautious and assuming that this budget is for the 
following 16 years, we conclude that the annual CO2 budget is 1.93 metric tons per capita 
of CO2. 
The second approach that will be discussed is the acquired rights approach based on 
Grandfathering (i.e., Inegalitarian) principle, where each stakeholder is entitled to earn a 
similarly large share in the future as it already owns in the present.  
In acquired rights principles, the sharing of the safe operating space among activities 
is based on the activities' current or historical acquired rights or property, such as 
occupied land area or contribution to environmental pressures [50]. 
We will look at Denmark's annual average CO2 emission for this approach. Based on this, 
we will assign Denmark a share of the CO2 budget that is available. Denmark's and the global 
average CO2 emissions are reported (in 2018) to be 5.76 and 4.48 metric tons per capita of 
CO2 annually [55]. Denmark's average emissions are 28.57% higher than the average. Thus, 
Denmark will receive a higher share of the available budget and reaches 2.48 metric tons per 
capita of CO2 annually for the following 16 years (i.e., 28.57% more than calculated in the 
egalitarian approach). 
Next, we will approximate how much of the total GHG emissions are coming from the waste 
sector and how much of it belongs to the specific EoL process to find the final boundary for 
each theory. In Denmark, the waste sector contributed 2.8% in 2019 of the total emission in 
CO2 equivalents [56]. No data on the share of specific EoL processes have been found in the 
literature. 
Hence global waste generation data was analyzed. Denmark's annual waste generation per 
capita is estimated to be 810 to 840kg [57]. Total waste in Denmark, including industries, is 
estimated at 18,318,599ton per year [58]. The EU's annual decommissioning of wind turbine 
blades will be between 40,000 to 60,000 tons annually by 2023 [11]. As Denmark's share is 
unknown, following the precautionary principle, we will assume it to be 20,000 tons of EUs 
complete Wind turbine blade waste as Denmark is one of the leading countries in the wind 
sector. Thus, assuming that the waste share by weight can be used to account for CO2 
emission share, The share of wind turbine blades is 0.33% of the carrying capacity of the 
waste sector.

Discussion 
The annual emissions related to the EoL processes have been divided by the SOS to retrieve 
a sustainability ratio in absolute terms where results above 1 are unsustainable. According 
to statistical data aggregated for Denmark, the global warming boundary was upscaled for 
only Wind turbine blade share. However, due to a lack of information, water use has been 
upscaled to Denmark's whole share of industrial activities. Even though water consumption 
was not upscaled to cover only the share of wind turbine blades, it is already highly 
unsustainable. In other words, the treatment of wind turbine blades, assuming that the lab-
scale processes are upscaled on a 1:1 performance, would consume more water than 
Denmark's current share of the whole industrial activities – excluding the negative results). 
Therefore, further data has not been collected, as it would not change the results in absolute 
terms. 
Both sharing principles show that the EoL systems with positive scores would emit more 
CO2 and consume more water than the share they would be assigned from the global carrying 
capacities. 
Allocation methods using welfare- or value-based methods where everyone gets the equal 
share of SOS could significantly change the results. Nevertheless, the lack of specific data 
on retrieved values did not allow the utilization of these methods. 
Altering the impact assessment methods did not affect the results in absolute terms. This was 
expected as characterization factors for water consumption and global warming is well 
studied and based on scientific consensus models. 
Conclusion 
The AESA shows that for CFRC in terms of Climate change all processes are sustainable in 
absolute terms and for GFRC chemical EoL processes (excluding co-combustion) are 
unsustainable. For water consumption, except for Fluidized Bed, Pyrolysis, and Solvolysis 
(for GFRC) all EoL treatment are sustainable in absolute terms.

Appendix#3: Materials assessment 
This section is dedicated to comparing the EoL performances of various materials that could 
be used as alternative materials to construct wind turbine blades. The ranking will be based 
on raw materials cost, and their associated EoL processes LCIA performance. 
To compare the effect of the weight of wind turbine blades, primarily drag force was 
intended to be used. However, no direct correlation between drag force and the mass of the 
wind turbine blade could be observed. This is because even though blades built with different 
materials with the same weight could have different volumes, the hallowed structure of wind 
turbines could also lead to an approximately equal volume. On the other hand, blades with 
the same volume/shape/size can have different weights depending on the material used. 
Thus, drag force cannot be used to compare different material turbine blades in terms of 
electricity generation efficiency. 
Alternatively, the rotational moment of inertia was correlated with the mass of the wind 
turbine blade. The moment of inertia measures how much force must be exerted on an object 
to reach a specific acceleration. We assume that a change in the required force to rotate the 
blades will alter the blades' electricity generation efficiency. The mass is directly 
proportional to the moment of inertia, which we assume to be directly proportional to the 
electricity generation efficiency. 
The hollowed structure of the blades plays a crucial role in this assessment. Nevertheless, it 
was neglected due to the lack of understanding of how much the blades could be hollowed 
without compromising the quality and strength of the blade. In other words, even though the 
bulk volumes are equal, the net volumes (when the hallowed volumes are subtracted from 
the bulk volume) could vary depending on the materials' properties as we could not estimate 
how much the blades could be hallowed; to prevent misinterpretation, we decided that it is 
best to neglect it for this study. So, we assumed that the blades are hollowed equally. We 
suggest that for future studies this should be included to get a more accurate estimation of 
the environmental performance of the materials.

Life-cycle Impact Assessment 
The impact assessment method used for this study is EF 3.0 Method (adapted) with the EF 
3.0 normalization and weighting set.  
Water Use: The best-performing three systems, from best to least performing, are Steel 
(Stainless), Aluminum Alloy, and Magnesium Alloy.  
Particulate Matter: The best-performing three systems, from best to least performing, are 
Magnesium Alloy, Steel (Stainless), and Steel (Low-carbon). 
Climate Change: The three best-performing systems, from best to least performing, are 
Magnesium Alloy, Steel (Stainless), and Steel (Low-carbon). 
Discussion 
Water use remains the primary impact category for recycling wind turbine blades, regardless 
of which material there are built from. We observe that Climate change remains a vital 
impact category even for alternative materials. Particulate matter has become the second 
most relevant impact category with the introduction of alternative materials. 
Based on the performance in LCA and the raw material cost, we observe that Magnesium 
Alloy is the best performing alternative material for the production of wind turbine blades. 
It is the cheapest option among all researched materials and performs significantly better in 
almost all Impact categories compared to GFRC and CFRC. Magnesium alloy wind turbine 
blades weigh less (theoretically) than GFRC but still more than CFRC. In other words, it is 
a perfect lightweight material that can compete with composite materials in weight (i.e., 
electricity production efficiency), price, and environmental performance. Its EoL is well 
studied and relatively easier to apply than composite materials. 
Aluminum alloy costs more than GFRC composites but is still cheaper than CRFC. Its 
environmental costs are also on par with GFRC and CFRC. As the recycling of metals is 
well studied and can be implemented relatively easier than composite materials, assuming 
that the blades are recycled, it becomes a competitive alternative to composite materials. As 
the blades are recycled, the aluminum must be purchased only once, while composite 
materials must be re-purchased, assuming that mechanical grinding is used for its EoL. 
Steel (both low-carbon and stainless) could weigh less if hollowing could be applied and 
might thus become a viable alternative to composite materials. Its environmental 
performance is significantly better than composite materials, and its EoL is one of the best-
studied processes. 
Conclusion 
This study shows that from best to least viable alternatives, Magnesium Alloy, Aluminum 
Alloy, and Steel (both low-carbon and stainless) are performing well in terms of price and 
environmental performance of their EoL processes. Hence, it is worth investigating them as 
alternative blade materials and investing in them.

Discussion and Conclusions 
The LCC results were not used as they are misleading for the wind energy sector. The 
revenues that raw material producers are making is not relevant for the wind turbine blade 
manufactures. Thus, the assessment is based on CapEx and OpEx. This will help decision 
makers in the wind energy sector to maximize their profits. 
We observe that the cost associated with the EoL treatments of wind turbine blades ranges 
widely depending on the process. Profit can be made only if the blade is substituted as PLA. 
Note that the costs of processing the matrix powder into printable powder have not been 
included. This could significantly lower the profit and even make the balance worse than 
metals. The substitution is hypothetical and may not reach the expected economic value. 
Hence, we conclude that metal wind turbine blades are more competitive overall than wind 
turbine blades made of composites from an economic perspective. For composite-based 
blades, we see that after mechanical grinding, High Voltage Pulse Fragmentation is the 
cheapest option and solvolysis and pyrolysis are the second and third most economical 
systems. 
As the cost of the cement kiln reactor could not be estimated, it has been excluded from this 
assessment.

Calculation and Results 
Adjustments to the CFF have been done. Firstly, instead of calculating the production 
burdens of the whole wind turbine blade, it has been calculated depending on the recycling 
case. In other words, if the blade contains recycled steel only, the steel has been separately 
calculated and added to the score. The used impact scores have been calculated using ILCD 
Midpoint+ excluding carbon fiber and epoxy resin, which have been calculated using the 
Environmental Footprint (Mid-point indicator) impact assessment method. The emissions 
related to recycling fibers and the matrix have been assumed to equal the impact scores

Burdens and benefits related to secondary material inputs for the metals that are recycled are 
calculated separately and are summed to the total score. Fiber recovery has been treated as 
open loop recycling as currently the recycled fibers are not used for wind turbine 
manufacturing. The burdens and benefits of the secondary material outputs have been 
calculated by assuming R2 values of 0.3 for benchmarking purposes. 
Another adjustment has been made for Energy recovery emissions where the emission 
arising from the recovery (i.e., EER) have been estimated based on their equivalent hard-coal 
substitution. The emission that would originate from combusting hard coal has been used to 
estimate the specific emissions and resources consumed (per functional unit) that would have 
arisen from the specific substituted energy source, heat, and electricity respectively (ESE,heat 
and ESE,elec) [28].  In layman's terms, we credited the mining process of hard coal for the 
recovery process.

Discussion 
The rankings have considerably changed after using the CFF. According to the CFF results, 
for CFRC and GFRC the best two processes are Pyrolysis and High Voltage Pulse 
Fragmentation, Pyrolysis and Cement Kiln, respectively. Note that CFF includes the 
manufacturing burdens of the wind turbine blades as well. Thus, GFRC performs better than 
CFRC. 
This significant change is caused by the R2 factor, which has been assumed as 0.3 for fiber 
and matrix recycling and PLA and sand replacement as no data was available in the annex 
of the PEFCR guide. This assumption is an educated guess and is not based on any market 
data and serves for benchmarking purposes. As these systems are theoretical, company data 
could also not be included. Furthermore, the complex structure of the wind turbine blade, 
made it challenging to apply the CFF formula in general. A lack of instruction on how to 
handle R3 when data cannot be retrieved from Annex C led to assuming them as 1.
