Summary
One obstacle for the implementation of quantum key distribution protocols is the
need for transmitting a strong local oscillator (LO), together with the relatively weak
signal. This imposes limitations on the achievable key rate, complicates the setup
and causes security loopholes. To address these issues protocols have recently been
proposed where the LO is not transmitted, but instead generated at the receiver. The
phase relationship between Alice and Bob’s lasers is then established by measuring
regularly transmitted reference pulses.
We investigate how the relative frequency drift between the Alice and Bob’s lasers
influences the protocol and identify a possible security hole which sets a limit for the
relative frequency between the signal and LO. We measure the relative frequency
drift of the Koheras BASIK E15 and X15 lasers an conclude that the X15 is suitable.
Finally we perform a simple CVQKD experiment using a single quadrature CVQKD
protocol. The experiment is not successful, which we attribute to a high loss and
poor control of the measurement basis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cryptography, the science of codes, have been used since the dawn of civilisation to
communicate secret information between two or more trusted parties. Today it is
widely used on the internet as a way of encrypting data and ensuring the security
of everything from online banking to e-mail, and it plays a vital role in keeping gov-
ernmental and military secrets. Cryptography requires the use of keys, for example
random strings of bits, which should only be known by the sender and intended recip-
ient of the information. The keys are usually shared using public-key cryptography,
which relies on the fact that certain mathematical functions are hard for a classical
computer to calculate. However, this is not the case for a quantum computer, which
in theory will be able to break public-key encryption.
Continuous variable quantum key distribution (CV-QKD) is an alternative to
public-key distribution, where the key is distributed via the quantum mechanical
atributes of light. The security is based on the fact that interacting with a quantum
system changes the state of the system, which can be detected by the users of the
QKD protocol. Standard QKD protocols requires the weak quantum signal to be
accompanied by a bright reference pulse called a local oscillator (LO), which is used to
measure the quantum signal and to establish a common frame of reference between the
communicating parties, who by convention goes by the names Alice (the sender) and
Bob (the recepient). The LO and signal therefore have to originate from the same laser
source. Transmitting a strong LO with a weak signal is a major technical obstacle for
the further development of CV-QKD. Multiplexing is required to avoid leakage from
the LO to the signal during transmission, which complicates the setup and introduces
noise[Huang205]. Furthermore, security weaknesses has been identified where the
LO is manipulated to get access to the otherwise secret communication[Ma+13a].
To address these issues, protocols where the LO is not transmitted with the signal
have been proposed[Qi+15][Soh+15]. In these ”local” LO (LLO) protocols Bob has
his own laser which he uses to generate the LO. To establish a common frame of
reference Alice regularly has to send out reference pulse, which she has prepared in
a state known to Bob. By measuring the phase of these states Bob can rotate his
measurements in phase space to agree with Alice’s. Since the reference pulses are
not used in detection they can be weaker than the usual LO pulses, which limits the
leakage into the quantum signal. Additionally, the security loopholes related to the
manipulation of the LO are closed.
Current developments have focused on the noise associated with imprecise mea-

surement of the reference pulses[Soh+15] or the phase noise of the lasers[Qi+15].
The first of these can be limited by increasing the amplitude of the reference and the
second by using a narrow linewidth laser.
In this thesis we will instead investigate how the frequency difference of the two
lasers can influence the protocol. It is generally thought that the frequency should be
matched, but no sources state by how much. We also study the long term frequency
stability of two commercial lasers, the X15 and E15 from the Koheras BASIK series

an LLO in a single quadrature QKD protocol.
The structure of the rest of the thesis is as follows:
• Chapter 2 describes cryptography in general and the requirement of secure key
distribution.
It then describes a typical quantum key distribution protocol,

• Chapter 3 contains a calculation of the secure key rate of the protocol described
in Chapter 2.
• Chapter 4 describes the LLO protocol in detail, including variations of pulse
configuration, noise contributions from imprecise phase compensation and a
summary of the state-of-the-art. We follow this by looking at how the frequency
difference between the lasers influence the protocol and discuss a possible secu-
rity loophole related to the LLO.
• Chapter 5 describes the experiment used to measure the frequency difference
between the lasers.
• Chapter 6 covers the theory behind single quadrature CVQKD. This is followed
by an QKD experiment where signal and LO originate from different sources.
• Chapter 7 is a summary of the results of the thesis.

In this chapter we will explain the basics of quantum key distribution. We start with a
short description of general cryptography and the problem of key distribution. We will
then look at the currently used key distribution protocols and discuss their security.
Next, we will describe a standard quantum key distribution protocols. Finally we
discuss the security weaknesses and technical limitations due to the local oscillator.
2.1
Classical Cryptography
The steps taken to encrypt, transmit and decrypt information is called a security
protocol and it is by convention described with the sender Alice, receiver Bob and
eavesdropper Eve (Figure 2.1). In our first example Alice would like to invite Bob to
a surprise birthday party for their mutual friend Eve. She does this by mail, but she
knows that the nosy Eve, who works at the post office, will most likely read the letter.
She therefore decides to encrypt it. To encode a message Alice needs an encryption
protocol and a key. The encryption protocol describes how the key should be applied
to the message to create the cipher text. Alice then sends the cipher text to Bob and
calls him on the phone to describe the protocol and give him the key. Since Eve does

In practice it would be nice for Alice and Bob if they could use a standard encryp-

By combining the non-random sequence of message-bits with a the completely

original bit values were because each were encoded independently and the probability
that they were either a 0 or 1 originally is 1/2 for each of them. As long as Alice can
find a secure way to share her key with Bob, the security of the protocol is ensured.
The Vernam cipher is impractical since it requires the key to be just as large as
the message, and more sophisticated protocols can be used where the key length is
reduced. Nonetheless, the Vernam cipher illustrates the fact that we can reduce the
problem of sharing secret information to the problem of sharing random keys. This
might at first glance not seem like a huge reduction, but the randomness of the key
is what makes quantum key distribution possible.
2.2
Classical Key Distribution
It turns out that the symmetric key protocols are impractical, as Alice and Bob has
no way of exchanging their keys without meeting up or sending them via an insecure
channel. To distribute the key then therefore use public-key cryptography instead.
This form of key distribution is based on one-way functions, mathematical operations
that are computationally easy one way, but where the inverse is computationally hard.
The most well known example of such a function is the factorisation of a product of
two primes, which is the basis of the so-called RSA protocol. It is relatively easy to
calculate that

but the inverse problem, finding the two prime factors of 1057799, will take you a
while, even with a super computer. To test the limits of their protocol, the RSA

However there is a looming threat to the public-key protocols. In 1994 it was
shown that a quantum computer would be able to factorise large numbers in polyno-

also be able to solve the discrete log problem and elliptical curves. The realisation
of a quantum computer capable of running Shor’s algorithm therefore poses a risk to
the current security protocols. The situation is even more critical when you consider
that in some cases the applied encryption has to hold for many years. It is not hard
to imagine that hackers and spies are currently recording encrypted transmissions,
which they hope they can decrypt once the technology, such as a quantum computer,
allows it.
The need for quantum secure protocols has created an entire field of post-quantum
cryptography, which seeks to create even more complicated classical key distribution
protocols that are secure, even when attacked using a quantum computer. Another
approach to the problem is to fight fire with fire by using (QKD).
2.3
Quantum Key Distribution
Whereas the security of classical key distribution protocols is based on the limited
computing power of Eve, the security of quantum key distribution is rooted in the
laws of physics, more precisely, from the no-cloning theorem. The no-cloning theorem
states that it is impossible to create a copy of an unknown quantum state[GG02]. This
means that if Alice and Bob uses quantum states to communicate, Eve will not be
able to just copy the transmitted states. Instead, she will have to interact with the
states meant for Bob. These interactions will change the quantum states, creating
excess noise, which Alice and Bob can then detect.
The earliest QKD protocols can be categorised as Discrete Variable QKD (DV-
QKD) because the information is transmitted using discrete variables of quantum
states, usually using single photons or very weak coherent states. The most well-
==PAGE:16==
6
2 Quantum Key Distribution
known example is the very first QKD protocol, BB84, proposed by Bennett and
Brassard in 1984[Ben84], where the information is encoded in the polarization of single
photons. However the implementation of DV-QKD is tricky because measuring and
manipulating the weak states requires specialized equipment such as single photon
sources and avalanche photodiodes.
Alternatives to the DV-QKD protocols began to emerge in 1999 [Ral99]. These
became known as Continuous Variable QKD (CV-QKD) because the information is
encoded in the quadratures of the states, which can take on a continuum of values. At
first these protocols used ”non-classical” states of light, such as squeezed or entangled
states. In 2002 it was shown that even the ”quasi-classical” coherent states are suitable
for QKD[GG02]. This gives CV-QKD an advantage over DV-QKD because coherent
states are easy to generate using a laser source and measuring the quadratures can
be done using standard photodiodes, both standard telecom components.
The family of CV-QKD protocols have since grown quite a bit.
They can be
classified depending on their type of quantum state (including coherent, squeezed or
thermal states), the modulation used to encode the information in the states, which
method Bob uses for measurements (homodyne or heterodyne detection) and finally
the post-processing methods that Alice and Bob use to agree on a shared key.

The protocol we will describe here is sketched in Figure 2.2 and is based on Weed-
brook et al. [Wee+04]. The protocol follows the following seven steps:
Initialisation
Before Alice and Bob can communicate they need to establish two channels: A quan-
tum channel and a classical channel. The quantum channel is the optical fiber used
to transmit the quantum states as well as a the bright local oscillator used in the
measurement at Bob’s side. Eve is free to manipulate with this channel as long as
==PAGE:17==
2.3 Quantum Key Distribution
7
she obeys the laws of physics. The classical channel is for all other communication
between Alice and Bob. Eve can listen to their conversations freely, but it is assumed
that the channel is authenticated, meaning that Eve cannot masquerade as either
Alice or Bob. This authentication can be done using a previously exchanged key.
State Preparation
Alice splits her laser beam into a weak signal beam and a strong LO beam. She then
samples two independent numbers (xA, pA) from a Gaussian distribution with zero
mean and variance VA (in SNU), known to Bob, and prepares the signal in the coher-
ent state |xA + ipA⟩using a phase modulator (PM) and amplitude modulator (AM).
She then sends it to Bob via the quantum channel together with the local oscillator.
Measurement
Bob performs a heterodyne detection on the signal, using the local oscillator supplied
by Alice and gets a pair of numbers (xB, pB). Bob’s measurements should be more
or less correlated with Alice’s (xA, pA), depending on the quality of the quantum
channel and the actions of Eve.
Parameter estimation
To estimate the security of the transmission Alice and Bob compare a random subset
of their numbers via the classical channel. They then use this information to esti-
mate the transmission and excess noise of the quantum channel. They have no way
of knowing if the excess noise is due to the quantum channel or the actions of Eve.
They therefore choose to be pessimistic and assume that all noise is due to Eve. By
doing this they can at least set an upper bound on the information that she might
have acquired. In some cases the error rate can be too high to create a secure key and
Alice and Bob will have to try again. If the problem persists they will have to use
another quantum channel or track Eve down and tell her to stop. The measurements
used for parameter estimation are of course discarded, since the classical channel is
open to Eve.
Binning
Alice and Bob convert their measurements into a string of bits, called a raw key, by
mapping their numbers to some previously agreed upon bins.
Error reconciliation
In this step Alice and Bob will correct the errors and agree on a shared list of num-
bers. This can be done with a series of parity checks over the classical channel. Earlier
protocols used direct reconciliation where Bob would correct his data to agree with
Alice’s, but this method is limited by the line loss, which needs to be below 3 dB
to ensure security. Instead they can use reverse reconciliation, where Alice corrects
her data to agree with Bob’s. Reverse reconciliation does not have a loss limit, so it
performs better at longer distances. For short distances direct reconciliation is still
prefered.

2.5
LO issues
Transmitting the LO together with the signal has proved to be a problematic part of
the conventional QKD protocol. First of all it limits the allowable LO power, since
a strong LO would cause interference with the weak signal. This then becomes a
problem at long distances where a strong LO due to transmission loss. It also limits
the detection rate, since high speed detectors require a stronger LO to achieve a good
clearance between shot noise and electronic dark noise.[Hua+15]
To minimise interference between the weak signal and strong LO, different kinds
of multiplexing are commonly used.
Examples include time division multiplexing
(TDM), where the pulses are transmitted at different times and polarisation-division
multiplexing (PDM) where the polarization of one pulse is rotated 90◦relative to
the other This complicates the setup and is especially an obstacle when it comes to
developing an integrated photonics version of QKD[Soh+15].
The multiplexing also adds noise. For example the time division demultiplexing is
performed by seperating the signal from the LO and then delay one of them by using
a longer fiber. Since LO and signal no longer travel together they become sensitive
to phase noise[Soh+15].
The transmitted local oscillator also provides Eve with a couple of opportunities
for attack. First of all she can perform a denial-of-service attack by just blocking the
LO, which would leave Bob unable to measure the quantum states. This is equivalent
to just blocking the signal, so it is unavoidable in any QKD protocol.
Another way of attack is the LO intensity attack. This attack is based on the
assumption that Bob scales his measurements to SNU with a constant instead of

taking the power fuctuations of the LO power into account. By attenuating the LO,
Eve can make Bob and Alice overestimate the shot noise, and thereby underestimate
the excess noise of the channel, even make it appear noiseless. They thereby also
underestimate how much information she has acquired and will falsely believe that
their communication is secure. The solution to this form of attack is to measure the
instantaneous LO power, which further complicates the setup. [Ma+13b]
But even then the protocol is not secure. In a LO calibration attack, Eve can
exploit that the LO pulses are used as triggers for the homodyne detection. By atten-
uating the first part of the LO pulses she can delay the trigger for the measurement,
which will decrease the variance of the homodyne detection. If Bob uses a predeter-
mined relation between the LO power and shot noise, he will again overrestimate the
shot noise and underestimate the excess noise, which again conceales Eve’s attack.
To avoid this form of attack Bob needs to perform real time measurements of the
shot noise. This would also hinder the LO intensity attack. [JKD13]
The issues related to the transmission of the LO are regarded as some of the most
critical for CV-QKD protocols and these are the problems that the local LO protocol
tries to address.

In this chapter we calculate the secure key rate of the CVQKD protocol described in
the previous chapter with reverse reconciliation and ideal preparation and detection.
The proof is based on the method used in [Fos+09] and [Lod+07].
The protocol we are using is a so-called prepare-and-measure (PM) protocol, where
Alice prepares a state and Bob measures it, as described in Section 2.3. The proof
can however be simplified by instead considering a corresponding entanglement-based
(EB) protocol, picture in Figure 3.1. In the EB protocol Alice instead generates a
two-mode squeezed vacuum state, also called an EPR state.
She then sends one
mode to Bob and measures the other mode using heterodyne detection. This mea-

her measurement results. Her measurement then also acts as the random number
generator.

The key rate is limited by which kind of attack Eve uses to eavesdrop on the
communication. She has several options which are usually categorized, in the order
of least to most sofisticated, as individual attacks, collective attacks and coherent
attacks[Fos+09].
In individual attacks, Eve interacts with each pulse individually by performing
measurements on them as they pass her in the quantum channel. In that case her
information is limited by the Shannon bound, IBE, which is simply the mutual infor-
mation between Bob’s state and Eve’s state.
In collective attacks, Eve again interacts with each pulse with an ancilla state
which she then stores in a quantum memory. She then waits for Alice and Bob to
finish the reconciliation before she performs an optimal measurement. In this case
the information she acquires is limited by the Holevo bound, χBE.
Finally, in coherent attacks Eve is free to do anything she wants and is only limited
by quantum mechanics. The proofs for these kinds of attack can be quite cumbersome
to deduce, so we will limit ourself to collective attacks in this thesis. However, the key
rates for coherent attacks and collective attacks have been shown to be the same in
the limit of infinite key sizes[DL15], so proving the security under collective attacks
should be sufficient for infinitely large keys.

4.1
State-of-the-art
As of this writing we are aware of only three articles related to the experimental
realisation of the LLO protocol.
Qi et al. has demonstrated the phase correction over a 25 km distance with a
repetition rate of 50 MHz. They used two 1550 nm lasers with a central frequency
difference that stays within 10 MHz[Qi+15].
Soh et al. has performed a series of experiments that demonstrates the signal
retrieval under strong phase noise using a 1550 nm laser with 100 kHz linewidth over
a 5 m channel. They also performed a QKD experiment using a homodyne detector
which yielded a key rate of 0.509 bit/use under collective attacks, but at a short dis-
tance. However, they used the same laser source as both signal and LO. They note
that this is not a problem since the different paths of the signal and LO caused the
phase difference to be random and fluctuating [Soh+15].
Finally, Huang et al. conducted a full scale QKD experiment over a 25 km distance
with a repetition rate of 100 MHz. They used 1550 nm lasers with a 1.5 kHz linewidth
and short-term stability of 4 MHz per hour for the signal and a 1550 nm, 1.9 kHz
linewidth laser for the LO which they tuned to within 20 MHz of the signal. They
achieved a key rate of about 100 kbit/s. They state that the high repetition rate was
made possible by the LLO protocol, which allowed them to increase the LO power
for their 1 GHz detector [Hua+15].

the frequency at the peak’s maximum was recorded. The measurement was started

The measurement was repeated with the X15 lasers when they became available
to us the weekend after. Since one of the lasers was also being used in a different
experiment at a wavelength of 1550.300 nm, we set the other laser to 1550.306 nm,
equivalent to a 749 MHz frequency difference.
Finally a measurement was taken with the X15 lasers at a wavelength closer to the
standard wavelength of the lasers. One laser was tuned to 1550.120 nm and the other
to 1550.126 nm, equivalent to a 749 MHz frequency difference. This measurement
was not performed during the weekend, but rather from Tuesday to Thursday.

long time to reach stability. At 22:00 and again at 4:00 we see a sudden shift in the
behaviour, which we assume has to do with temperature control of the lab or corridor,

CHAPTER 6
QKD Experiment
Based on the apparent stability of the X15 we built a working QKD setup to test
the use of a local LO. The setup would only perform the exchange of quantum states
from which we would be able to compute the covariance matrix and the secret key
rate.
The setup was for a single quadruature CVQKD (SQ-CVQKD) protocol in which
the coherent states are only modulated in one quadrature. This protocol has been pro-
posed recently using either amplitude modulation[UG15] or phase modulation[GJA15].
This allowed us to simplify the setup by only using a PM and a homodyne detector.
Unfortunately we lacked a phase modulator for the LO, which we could have used con-
trolling the measurement basis and to implement the twin-reference setup described
in the previous chapter. Instead we had to rely on the systems own phase drift an
the DC output of the homodyne detector to show us which quadrature was being
measured and then do post-selection of the data.
6.1
Single quadrature QKD
The SQ-CVQKD protocol is similar to the standard protocol with Gaussian modu-
lated coherent states, but this time Alice only modulates her states in one of the
quadratures. She thereby sends out states |x + ip⟩, where either x or p follows a
Gaussian distribution with variance VA and the other one is vacuum.
Bob measures the signal using homodyne detection. If Alice encodes the signal in
the phase quadrature he will measure that for the majority of the time, but once in a
while he will switch to the amplitude quadrature for parameter estimation purposes.
Since Alice only needs one kind of modulator it greatly simplifies the setup. In
the case of amplitude modulation it also makes it possible to use a laser diode whose
intensity can be directly modulated via the pump current in which case no modulator
is needed. In this experiment, however, we used phase modulation.

calculating the covariance at each step. The correct delay would then be where the ab-

the AC measurements with the standard deviation of the shot noise measurement to

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
We have shown that frequency difference does not influence the phase correction, as
it can be compensated for by using more than one reference pulse. We have however,
shown that the detector can be manipulated with by changing the signal frequency,
which can be used to hide the excess noise associated with an attack. We used this
to set a benchmark for what frequency difference, fd would be allowed in relation to
the integration time of the homodyne detector, ∆ti 2πfd∆ti < 0.1.
We measured the long term stability of the Koheras BASIK E15 and X15 laser
and concluded that the X15 had both a lower dependency on ambient temperature
and a smaller relative frequency drift, measured to be 1.5 MHz over an hour in the
most stable period. A python script for controling the lasers was also produced which
can be used in future projects.
Finally we performed a CV-QKD experiment using modulation in only one quadra-
ture both with and without LLO. We only achieved a secure key rate of 0.06 bits/use
without the LLO, which can be attributed primarily to a high loss and low control
over the quadrature measurements.
Future investigation could look into the improvement of the LLO version of the
SQ-CVQKD setup. A phase modulator is needed to accurately control the LO and
to implement the twin-reference pulses. The intensity noise of the X15 should be
investigated to ensure that it does not impact the performance.
Finally, a more
in-depth look at the possible security loop-hole could be interesting.
