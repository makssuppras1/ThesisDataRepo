Abstract
This thesis investigate the performance of the wave energy converter(WEC) Tordenskiold
constructed by the company Crestwing www.crestwing.dk. The exploration is desirable
as the wave energy sector is expected to have a larger impact in the energy produced by
renewable sources. The potential of the wave energy sector is exploited in the report and
shows great potential for future energy production for an efficient WEC.
The results analysed throughout the report is estimated by CFD simulation of a simplified
model of Tordenskiold using the program Star CCM+.
An earlier model test has showed that an implementation of skirts on the side of the pontoons
of the WEC would increase the performance of the concept. A full scale test was initiated for
Tordenskiold by CFD simulation. The results verified that the addition of skirts definitely
has a positive contribution to the power generation of the WEC.
To test Tordenskiold for its potential, CFD simulations of different wave states is carried
out and analysed within the report. The results showed that Tordenskiold has a natural
frequency regarding efficiency at wave lengths corresponding to its total length. Furthermore
the results showed that shorter waves would affect the relative movement of the pontoons
significantly more than longer waves when fitted with equal PTO settings. These tests also
exploited the influence of wave height in relation to the generated power. Larger wave heights
did show to generate more power but showed to decrease the efficiency of the WEC. The
analysis of wave heights showed that the optimum PTO setting was affected significantly by
increasing wave heights.
In the report a results from full scale CFD simulation are compared to a case assuming
symmetry to ease computational time. This assumption showed to be valid and was used
for the main part of the CFD simulations. To verify the solution from the simulation, a
convergence test was carried out. This tested the convergence in regard of amount of cells
pr wave height, cells pr wave length time steps pr wave period.

1
Introduction
In the energy production market, oil and gas has been the cheapest solution for many years.
So far it is still the most used solution on the market. But as these resources are finite, a
transition to other energy sources will be necessary in the future.
The current energy crisis does not seem to pass by quickly, and prices for oil and gas are
increasing, making the prices of electricity provided by oil and gas increase as well. The fact
that the environmental crisis is a major topic within politics, with the Paris agreement in
mind, the energy provided by oil and gas might soon be outdated. For many years renewable
energy has been preferred solution, but has not yet been able to overtake the market. This
is even though the environmental impact of these solutions seems far more ideal, for the
green transition towards a carbon neutral energy production. The future environmental
restriction could provide an assisting hand for the breakthrough of wave energy converters,
as a way to reach the goal of carbon neutral energy. As a start, the energy from waves will
most likely be seen as an contribution to energy provided by wind and solar.
Wave energy is a sustainable and endless energy source, and could easily contribute to the
renewable energy mix. Increasing the diversity in the renewable energy sector, as it increases
availability and reduces the need of fossil fuels. Furthermore the electricity from wave energy
would make certain countries more self sufficient. Especially in the current market, where
oil and gas are imported from politically unstable counties.
As the world population is still increasing, the energy consumption will keep increasing. As
fossil fuel reserves are depleting, this would make the production of renewable energy even
more important. The price for electricity will increase due to the depletion of fossil fuels.
As this happens, it will only be a matter of time before the energy produced by wave energy
converters will be cost effective.
A contribution to the wave energy sector is the company Crestwing. Crestwing is a Danish
company situated in Frederikshavn who strive to have the first commercial park consisting
of wave energy converters of 2.5 MW 2025. This report will be testing the concept of their
wave energy converter Tordenskiold through CFD simulations to help reach this goal.

2
Wave Energy Sector
The current wave energy sector has far from been exploited to its full potential. The wave
energy sector could help overcome some of the problems occurring in the overall electricity
production today. The key issues of the current electricity production being the environ-
mental problems, the depletion of fossil fuels and a security in supply and job creation
[25].
Environmental problems being effects such as local pollution and emission of CO2 from fossil
fuels [25]. In regard of fossil fuels, the depletion is a big problem. These seem to deplete
completely within the next couple of years [25]. In the coming years, this could give rise to
conflicts due to the uneven distribution of resources, getting even worse as the depletion get
more severe.
The wave energy resources are generally not cost competitive yet and relies on political
support. This is likely to change in the near future, due to the increasing cost of fossil
fuels. Another factor being the reduction in cost of technology regarding renewable energy
resources as the market expands [25].
In present time, the realistic goal is to be 100%
independent of fossil fuels by 2050, while keeping the cost for the society insignificant. To

3
Wave Energy Converters
Wave energy converters (WEC) has been around since 1799, where the first wave energy
converter was patented [10].
However it was not until after 1973, that wave power was
exploited due to massive increase in oil prices [10]. In this period Denmark, United Kingdom
and a few others started to introduce support measures and relatable programs to investigate
how wave energy could be utilized [10].
Even with the oil crisis raging back then, the wave energy converters still did not become
the preferred option for renewable energy supported by figure 1 and stated in [35]. This
would be even though electricity generated by ocean waves can be produced offshore and
would not inflict any visual or noise impact to the communities.
To compare the cost of wind turbines and wave energy converters, a wind turbine of 3.6MW
would have a CapEx of 7.2mil euros while a wave energy converter producing 3.6MW is
expected to have a CapEx of around 6 mil euros [25]. CapEx defining the capital expenses
such as the construction, maintenance, equipment and improvements. Smaller WECs of
750kW has a cost of 2 mil euros [25]. These estimates highlights the importance of the
possibility of scaling the concept of a WEC.
These estimates are based on calculations for the annual energy production. The calculation
shown below.
AEP = Pwave · wabsorber · ηw2w · availability · hannual
(1)
The factors of the equation being, Annual Energy Production (AEP), mean wave power level
Pwave, width of absorber wabsorber, wave to wire efficiency ηw2w and lastly the availability
and yearly production hours hannual.
This indicates the importance of location and scale of the WEC. Another factor that makes
the size important, is the lowered expenses of maintenance work. The reason being that
maintenance could be done on site for large WEC, where smaller WEC has to be towed
to shore for inspection. One of the most important factors for a WEC would probably be
the wave-to-wire efficiency. This is the efficiency from wave to grid and thereby the overall
efficiency. This is often seen as a weighted average over all conditions. The wave-to-wire
efficiency is influenced by wave condition, availability and maximum power rating.
Other important factors for WECs is stated below [25]:
• Survivability: A sufficient WEC requires a reliable mooring system, and might as
well require a passive safety system.
• Reliability and maintainability: Easy access to inspection of essential parts. Prefer-
ably at location.
7
==PAGE:22==
• Overall power performance: Must consist of an efficient wave energy absorbing
technology and PTO. PTO is the Power Take-off system, the system converting wave
energy into electric power. It must produce a sufficient smooth electrical power with
high capacity factor. Otherwise huge losses could occur.
• Scale-ability: In full scale, the WEC needs to be producing multiple-MW to be
economically viable. To be able to level the cost of energy, the construction needs to
be scaleable. Most WECs reach the optimal dimensions at a small scale, restricting it
in the amount of Watt’s that it can possibly produce. But a good scale-ability could
make the concept sufficient in the occurring sea states.
• Environmental benefit: The WECs are expected to deliver a sustainable energy
output and having a great environmental benefit with the slightest environmental
footprint [25].
These factors are used to estimate the efficiencies and to rate the WECs. ∼cf[25] it would
take approximately 13 years to repay the base of CapEx cost for a small WEC. A large WEC
could repay the CapEx in approximately 4 years, supporting the importance of scale-ability.

3.1
Overtopping wave energy device
One way to utilize wave energy, is the over topping wave energy device.
The working
principle can be seen in figure 5. The illustration shows a floating version of a overtopping
wave energy device. The principle also works for fixed structures either near shore or fixed
to the seabed. The working principle being that the incoming wave goes over the ramp
and into a reservoir, filling up the reservoir. When the water is in the reservoir it runs
through the turbine outlet, which is connected to a PTO system in form of a turbine and a
generator. This effect generates energy as the water runs through the turbine. It only works
as long as the construction is at a certain level above the mean water level. At this level the
potential energy from the water in the reservoir can be utilized. This concept is also used
for hydro-power energy conversion [25].

3.2
Oscillating water columns
Another concept is the oscillating water column. Just as the over topping device it can be
floating or fixed to shore or seabed. An illustration of the concept is shown in figure 6 of
a fixed structure connected to the shore. The working principle being that the incoming
wave makes the water in the chamber push the air(the yellow part) out and then suck the
air into the chamber, when the water level falls. For the concept to work, this chamber has
to be partly submerged. The fluctuation in air pressure activates the turbine connected to
the outlet of the chamber. Hence the wave energy is converted to air pressure and kinetic
energy to be harvested [33].

Wave activated bodies
A third concept is the wave activated bodies, sometimes mentioned as point absorbers.
These can be of various types of concepts, but they all get their energy by mechanical
movement of parts. These can consist of multiple barges or be an oscillating bouy etc. Just
as the other concepts it can be floating, but unlike the others, it can be fully submerged
below the sea level. The illustration shown in figure 7 is a floating concept, that works by
a rotational concept around a hinge. The interaction between the two barges shown in the
illustration, is what activates the PTO that generates power from the movement. Here the
best conditions will be when the waves are around the same length as the structure [25].

Wave Theory
Waves can contain enormous amounts of energy. This will inflict a lot of forces to the struc-
tures interacting with the waves. In many industries the wave energy for design purposes
regarding barges or coastal constructions are evaluated. But as the wave energy converters
has to withstand the wave forces and still be able to convert it to energy, a different ap-
proach need to be taken for WECs. An evaluation has to be made for the waves as well as
the response of the interacting structures, before the wave converter should be put to sea.
5.1
Sources of Ocean Waves
Ocean waves is an energy disturbance in the water surface of the ocean. The waves are
generated by different disturbing or restoring forces acting on the ocean surface.
These
different waves can be categorized by their origin.
Looking at the wave period and the
relative energy these categories is defined within figure 16 [25].
Figure 16 shows different categories of waves, even showing tsunamis and the tides. The
waves exploited by WECs is mostly gravity waves. These waves are commonly generated
by blowing winds across the ocean surface. This process is illustrated in figure 17 ∼cf[25].
Figure 17 illustrated how waves start like ripples within the category of capillary waves. As
the wind continues to influence the ripples, these will turn into fully developed waves until
they reach a certain limit. This limit is caused by energy losses such as white-capping as
the wave breaks if it becomes steep. An important characteristic is that the waves continue
even when the wind stops blowing. These waves are able to travel long distances without
any energy loss. For these wave states the waves are usually called swell waves. These are
also illustrated in figure 17. The gravity waves is often divided into wind waves, created
by local winds, and swell waves, when the wind has no contribution to the waves anymore.
But even thought they are categorized differently there is no fundamental different in the
hydrodynamics of these waves [25].

Estimation of power approach
To calculate the power generated by the PTO, the force absorbed by the PTO system is
recorded along with the elongation of the spring. The elongation would correspond to the
movement of the push-rod in the real world case. Then by multiplying the absorbed force
with the relative velocity of the spring. The velocity of the spring is estimated by eq. 73, as
inputs the spring elongation is used to estimate the velocity. This is done for different PTO
configurations in the following way:
First the data is logged from the CFD simulation. Here the data is for the force applied to
the spring and the derivative of the spring elongation. In figure 35 the logged data for a full
simulation can be seen:
In the CFD simulations a force is applied to the spring and is only active when the velocity
is below zero, corresponding to when the spring is in compression.
The figure shows that it takes a few seconds for the results to get to a state where the velocity
and force can be seen as continuous periods. Figure 35 shows that it takes a few second for
the WEC to adapts to the incoming waves. The solution in figure 35 seems periodic after 7
seconds. To have a proper estimate of the power, a specified period is chosen for more than
one peak. Such a period is chosen in figure 36:

Conclusion
It can be concluded that the WECs definitely has a future contribution to the energy sector if
utilized correctly. The fact the at WECs has plenty of potential for power production in the
renewable energy sector definitely gives Crestwing an advantage in the future. And with the
depletion of fossil fuels and the carbon emission of these resources taken into consideration,
it will create a window of opportunities for the wave energy sector in the future.
To ease computational time the symmetry of the structure showed to be exploitable. It was
confirmed that the Symmetry assumption was valid. It was possible to scale the results if
the incoming wave was moving along the surge direction. This spared a lot of computational
time as the results could be done in half the time of the full scale model.
Regarding the Crestwing energy converter, it can be concluded that it has great potential
for energy production. The wave parameters seem to influence the energy production sig-
nificantly. Meaning that the location is a big concern in regard of the produced power. The
analysis showed that for waves of high steepness, the efficiency will decrease. Though the
magnitudes of the efficiencies for this report should be investigated as the efficiencies seemed
to be overestimated due to the magnitude of the average generated power.
In regard of the contribution of adding skirts to the construction. The addition of the skirts
showed to be beneficial in regard of the produced power. The maximum power production
comparing the cases showed that the case including the skirts has the potential to generated
8.92% more power than by exclusion of the skirts.
The optimum PTO settings was found for various configurations of wave heights.
The
analysis regarding optimum damping coefficient showed that by increasing the wave height
the optimum damping coefficient would decrease. This was only tested at one wave length,
witch by earlier analysis was approximated to be the optimum wave length as the device
showed indications of a natural frequency at this wave length.
The effect of wave height showed to be significant in regard of generated power. By increasing
the wave height the generated power would increase as well. The analysis also showed that
the efficiency of the system would decrease as a function of the increased wave height.
A relation regarding the influence of wave length to relative motion of the two barges showed
a linear relation between the wave length and relative velocity ratio of the PTO system. It
showed that shorter waves were more affected then longer waves regarding the velocity of
the push-rod in the PTO system.
These results could show to be a great contribution for further investigation of the Crestwing
project. Helping them reach their goal for deploying large energy converters of more that
2.5MW in 2025 by up scaling the device.

Future work
Future work for the Crestwing project could be to compare the results in this report with
results from the actual Tordenskiold. Comparing the movement or the energy produced
in specific wave conditions during operation. This was not possible in this report as wave
measurements and measurement from the WEC was not corresponding to the same time
interval due to malfunctions in equipment. Results from Tordenskiold is present, but as the
wave state was not measured for these results, the comparison was not possible. Testing
Tordenskiold in a wave tank would give the desired results to verify the movement. Though
as these test are expensive a real world test could also be an option. This could give an
explanation regarding if the produced power was overestimated or why the CFD simulation
could be inaccurate. This could help to verify the CFD solutions by comparing it to a real
world wave state or wave tank tests.
Further work could be to test how the mooring stiffness affect the movement of the pontoons.
If the pontoons are affected by the mooring system the relative movement between the
pontoons would be affected as well. Thereby the potential for production of energy would
be affected. To exploit an optimum mooring stiffness could show to be beneficial.
Another thing to look into, is how different skirts length affect the power output. As the
skirts showed beneficial in the analysis in section 10.1, prolonging or shortening the skirts
to find a optimum length would be interesting. An analysis of the restoring moment in yaw
could be initiated. By changing the angle for the incoming waves and see how the rotation is
affected when the waves hits the WEC from different wave directions. An evaluation of the
forces on the WEC or the time to restore optimum position could be done. The symmetry
assumption would not be valid for this analysis as the wave would have to come at the WEC
in its optimum position. Optimum position meaning head waves coming straight at the
front of the WEC. A test of the power output when the WEC is not at an optimum position
could also be interesting.
In relation to the meshing a more narrow domain could be beneficial, when looking at head
waves. The width is probably to wide and a more narrow domain could mean decreasing the
computational time. Another thing to change in the mesh could be to enlarging the oversets.
In the common state it only spans 0.5m beyond the front and rear of the pontoons and the
same for underneath the WEC. Prolonging and widening these oversets would probably give
more accurate results, but would also increase the computational time. By doing this the
time step would need to be decreased as the oversets should not move to much between the
time steps.
A last comment could be to scale the WEC in different length and widths. This would
investigate how the scaling affects the generation of power. To test new optimum conditions,
or to test how the energy potential scales when the device is twice the length and width
67
==PAGE:82==
or even further than that. It could definitely be interesting, as many locations has waves
length beyond 30m.
Nonetheless this report could be used in future work to test some of the tendencies shown
throughout the results. Either for real world testing or CFD simulations.
