Summary
In this thesis we set up mathematical foundations needed to explore and control
the properties the photonic nanojet problem. We express those properties in terms of
different parameters in a Dirichlet boundary problem for the Helmholtz equation. We
prove boundedness of the operator that maps Dirichlet boundary conditions to the
solution of Helmholtz equation on a sphere in theorem 12. We prove both boundedness
and compactness of the operator that maps the incident field to the scattered field
in theorems 13 and 14. A sufficient condition for inverting the operator that maps
trace of the incident field to the trace of the total field using Neumann series is given
in theorem 17. We also set up and explore the problem in the sense of microlocal
analysis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Optical microscopy is an important imaging technique. One of the biggest drawbacks
of this method is that its spatial resolution is fundamentally limited by the wavelength
of light used to illuminate the object being looked at. To be resolved under an optical
microscope, an object or a pattern must be illuminated by a comparable sized spot
of light. In most modern microscopes the size of attainable diameter of a light spot is
larger than 0.625λ where λ is the wavelength of light being used [M B97]. In practice
λ = 445nm is used for instance if using blue light, or other λs of similar size are
used. This limits the size of particles that can be imaged to well above many objects
of interest, such as viruses or some proteins which can be in order of magnitude
of 10nm [bio] [al13b].
In order to tackle this problem we can use a phenomenon
known as photonic nanojet. A photonic nanojet, or PNJ for short, is a very narrowly
focused beam of light, allowing the creation of light spots as small as 0.27λ [JH00]
[CG97] [CT04]. The main problem with PNJs is in obtaining the nanojet effect with
desired shape, length, width and direction. Given a set of such properties, finding an
appropriate lens and illumination is still an open problem.
In this thesis we mathematically state the problem of designing a PNJ as an inverse
problem. A PNJ focused at a certain point is modelled by the Helmholtz equation,
which is used to model electromagnetic fields, with singularities set in appropriate
places in the inhomogeneous term. We explore the operator mapping the illumination,
or incident field, to the scattered field and prove that that mapping is bounded as well
as compact in theorems 13 and 14. We also attempt to apply methods of microlocal
analysis to design the shape of lenses needed to produce PNJs. In the end we attempt
to determine parameter values for which it is possible to use Neumann series to invert
the incident field to total field mapping, which would give a way to determine the
needed illumination to create a PNJ.

1.2
Microlocal analysis
This section is largely based on chapter 7 [Tay11b] and [al14].
The idea of using microlocal analysis comes from the fact that a lens is modelled
as a compactly supported piecewise constant function. So the only singularities that
this function can have are those coming from discontinuities along the boundaries of
the constant regions. Since these regions are of physical relevance, and are in fact
what we want to reconstruct, it is natural to try and state the problem in the sense of
microlocal analysis and use its tools to determine singularities, or even the wavefront
set, of the function modelling the lens.

1.3
Fredholm operators
In this section V and W will be understood to be Banach spaces. In theorem 14 we
prove that the mapping from incident field to scattered field is compact. This means
that mapping from incident field to total field, which is the one that is physically
observed, is given as a compact perturbaton of the identity operator. This is because
utot = uinc + usca. The idea behind exploring the theory of Fredholm operators is to
use it to more easily prove, or disprove, that this mapping is a bijection.
Definition 17. An operator T ∈L(V, W) is said to be a Fredholm operator if
• Ker(T) is finite dimensional
• T(V ) is closed in W
• W/T(V ) is finite dimensional
We will denote the space of all Fredholm operators from V to W by Fred(V, W).
Definition 18. Index of an operator T is defined as

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
The compactness and boundedness of F from theorems 13 and 14 are important and
strong results that justify applying methods such as SVD analysis to the incident
field to scattered field mapping. Also, since the spaces F is defined on are infinite
dimensional compactness from 14 implies that the inverse of F is not bounded, so the
inverse problem is ill posed in the sense of Hadamard. Furthermore it also allows for
a new line of investigation, in that I + F is a compact perturbation of the identity
operator, and so it is a Fredholm operator. This could potentially be used to prove
that I + F is a bijection, proving the existence of an isomorphism between incident
and total fields. For practical purposes of inversion 17 provides a sufficient condition
to use Neumann series to efficiently solve the inverse problem.
The approach using microlocal analysis, though unsuccesful in this thesis, is
promising as well.
Further investigation could lead to efficient algorithms recon-
structing the needed shape of a lens to generate a target PNJ.
