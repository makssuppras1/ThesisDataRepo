Abstract
To address the great challenges of a rising electricity demand and a more variable energy
production, more end-user participation is required. A potential arises through local en-
ergy communities addressed by the EU. These are entities which through investments into
generation or storage technologies may be able to provide the desired ﬂexibility. However,
such communities have not until recently been addressed in Denmark, and participation
may in fact provide economical losses.
This thesis investigates how regulative frame-
work conditions may be identiﬁed to encourage engagement in energy communities. It
establishes a mathematical model to reﬂect a scenario with and without said energy com-
munities. The consumption data is based on a local district in Denmark, and includes two
blocks of apartments. Optimizing the models provides the input to enable comparison of
their outcomes. The results suggest that the energy communities perform marginally bet-
ter than the base scenario in all cost categories, and that distributed energy investments are
similar. Energy communities are identiﬁed to show great potential in peak management,
where they are able to trade electricity and exploit storage technologies. The potential
of the energy communities would be further underlined when considering the ﬂexibility
services they could provide. However, for them to have the optimal opportunities it is
suggested that they are allowed a tax and tariﬀrelief in the internal trade.

Introduction
As a result of the Paris Agreement, the European Union has adopted the Green Deal; a
set of initiatives to enhance the actions towards improving the climate, while at the same
time maintaining a sustainable economic business model[1]. This has pushed forward a
green transition, forcing sectors to rethink their practices to pursue climate neutrality.
Being responsible for the highest amount of greenhouse gas emissions, the energy sector
is the most aﬀected one – an issue that has been addressed since the late 20th century
when large-scale renewable energy sources ﬁrst were exploited and included in the energy
systems. However, as the energy systems become more reliant on renewable energy sources
– and considering the high degree of variability related to their energy production – the
energy systems require an increasing amount of ﬂexibility. Currently, energy systems are
dependent on reliable energy sources like thermal power plants, which are not as sustain-
able, in order to provide the necessary ﬂexibility. With the newly adopted plans, the goal
is to phase out the emitting energy sources, meaning that this ﬂexibility has to be provided
elsewhere. Furthermore, the electriﬁcation of both the transport and heat sector increases
the electricity demand drastically, which underlines the need for action.
To accommodate these challenges, literature suggests that a higher degree of end-user
participation is necessary. In fact, demand response is said to be essential to align con-
sumers and the energy system in the best way possible during the transition. This will
be presented in the 2nd section of this thesis. Opportunities to do so arise locally, where
emerging renewable energy communities using renewable DER, may be able to fast forward
the green transition and provide the desired ﬂexibility for the energy system. As a result,
the need for investments in the collective electricity grid may be postponed or canceled.
They are based on households, housing associations, companies, or even municipalities,
who unify in creating energy communities.
In 2019, these energy communities are mentioned in the RED and IMED, after which
the Danish government attempted to implement it into their policies in 2020. However,
not much has happened, as they state that participation is already available in Danish
legislation. In fact, they fear it may end up hurting the collective Danish energy grid
if support schemes are implemented. However, it is argued that participation in ECs is
not yet able to provide the anticipated beneﬁts and that participants may even encounter
economical losses. To incentify the engagement in ECs, regulatory framework conditions
have to be implemented. They must ensure that participation in energy communities does
not grant economic losses and thereby increase the motivation for local energy commu-
nities to invest in renewable DER and in that engage in the market as ﬂexibility providers.
A benchmark analysis will identify the trends in EC deﬁnitions and policies in diﬀer-
ent countries, making it possible to recognize how the ECs are run, and how regulatory
conditions may be implemented to impact them. Using the benchmark, a base scenario
and an EC scenario will be built through a mathematical model, in order to analyze the
impact of introducing ECs in the system. To make an assessment of this, it is required to
1
==PAGE:10==
optimize the local energy systems, which also gives an overview of how each scenario may
aﬀect the energy systems while meeting demand, ensuring minimal carbon emissions, and
keeping a low cost at all times. Each optimization will provide an investment strategy and
will through the rise and fall in e.g. imported and exported energy grant an overview of
how the money would ﬂow from taxes and tariﬀpayments into investments in DER.
A sensitivity analysis of changes to certain parameters will then be conducted to show
how sensitive the system is to changes in certain parameters. This will provide the neces-
sary output for further analysis, which will be critically discussed in the latter part of the
thesis. In the end, a recommendation for legislators will be made.
The diﬀerent ﬁndings from this thesis will make it possible to answer the research question:
How can regulatory framework conditions be developed, to encourage engagement in local
energy communities and thereby enhance their role in the green transition?

2
General Context
First, this section will grant a brief insight into how the energy system is developing based
on the electriﬁcation of vehicles and heat. Second, a review of demand response – a tool
to mitigate the experienced challenges – will be provided.
2.1
Electriﬁcation
The energy systems around the world currently face increasing electriﬁcation with the
objective of decreasing CO2 emissions. In Denmark, the goal is to have carbon-neutral
consumption by 2050[2].
Especially the transport and the heating sector are aﬀected
by this. As a result, they are becoming more reliant on electricity as a fuel. Thereby,
the heat sector seeks to decrease carbon emissions by introducing more heat generated
from electricity. For the district heating system, this requires investments into large-scale
heat pumps, electric boilers, and heat storage[3]. This is also attempted on a residential
basis, where the same technologies are expected to rise in installed capacities.[4] However,
[5] states that the heat demand may be expected to fall due to warmer temperatures
and renovation of houses, granting some uncertainty to the amount of heat which will
be needed.
The electriﬁcation of privately owned cars has been addressed by the EU
commission, proposing that after 2035 no diesel or gasoline cars may be sold[6].
Figure 1 shows the expected fraction of the total electricity consumption, which the EVs
will be responsible for, showing that the fraction may rise to 10% for the EU and almost
15% for Denmark.
As a result of the above-described electriﬁcation, the overall electricity demand is expected
to rise drastically.[4]

2.2
Demand Response
Given the nature of the renewable energy technologies, which produce a variable amount
of energy as well as the electriﬁcation described above, the energy system experiences
pressure with varying prices depending on the amount of produced electricity and demand.
Literature suggests that end users may be a tool in providing the necessary ﬂexibility to
the energy system by expanding heat pumps, charging stations, or storage systems[7].
[8] introduces the ability of end-users to provide ﬂexibility by local engagement in the
energy market, by providing services that can lower the demand in peak hours or increase
their demand when the renewable production exceeds it. This is known as DR, which
seeks to decrease the challenges related to peak load hours by including end-users. [9]
describes how DR can decrease the peak load signiﬁcantly by 2050, and that it can decrease
investments into both peak generation and energy storage technologies for the collective
energy system. [10] conﬁrms that end-user participation through DR or energy storage
may be a source of a more ﬂexible energy system, but states that political challenges are
identiﬁed. It is described how regulations should be improved and targeted the end-users
by supporting the incentives to provide the necessary ﬂexibility.
A case study from Ireland analyses the impact of including DR in combination with a heat
pump, water tank, PV array, and EV, showing that the DR, in this case, may reduce the
carbon emissions and generation costs signiﬁcantly[11]. An overview of DR is provided by
[12], who states that the recent development in DR measures relates to the involvement
of energy communities through a smart grid. [13] underlines the fact that multi-energy
communities can become an important source of DR ﬂexibility, specially equipped with
storage technologies.

3
Energy Communities
As previously described, local energy communities may be able to provide the energy grid
with ﬂexibility by providing DR and investing in energy generating technologies or storage.
This section grants an overview of how the energy communities are deﬁned and how they
are included into policies. In order to do so, a benchmark analysis have been conducted,
presenting literature and legislation from three countries and EU, and ﬁnally sums up the
areas which may be of further interest. The countries are chosen due to their activities
and available literature on ECs.
3.1
Benchmarking by Country
3.1.1
EU-Directive
Energy Communities
By including energy communities in directives on Renewable Energy of 2018, as well as
on the directive on the internal market for electricity of 2019, EU seeks to amplify their
opportunities. [14] [15].
The communities have the role of advancing energy eﬃciency on a household level, while
they should also help ﬁght energy poverty [14]. They are deﬁned as legal entities, which
are based on voluntary and open participation [15], providing environmental, economic, or
social beneﬁts to its members, shareholders, or local areas, rather than ﬁnancial proﬁts as
is the case for large-scale energy providers [15]. Finally, the directive on common rules for
the IEMD deﬁnes that the ECs may engage in numerous services, including generation,
distribution, supply, consumption, aggregation, energy storage, energy eﬃciency services,
or charging services for EV [15].
RED deﬁnes RECs similarly to the IMED, however specifying that the energy services
must be based on renewable energy sources [14]. [16] states that the ways the two direc-
tives restrict the eﬀective control of the ECs diﬀer, as the RED directive does it based on
criteria on locality and geographic proximity, whereas IEMD limits the control by the size
of the shareholder and their commercial activity.
Legal Situation
The directives seek to provide guidelines for the member states of the EU, in which they
can create frameworks enabling ECs [14] [15]. It is clearly stated in the IMED that the
implementation of the provisions of the directive in policies and regulations is supposed
to happen in each member state separately, provided that the implemented policies are
non-discriminatory and lawful, thereby acknowledging the competencies of each member
state [15]. One of the key points besides conceptualizing the energy communities is the fact
that they should be allowed to participate in available support schemes like larger energy
providers[14] and thereby have the potential to become a player in the energy system.
In articles 16 and 22 respectively the IMED and the RED each bring a section on which
regulatory framework conditions shall or may be included in the frameworks designed by
5
==PAGE:14==
the member states to ensure future opportunities for the ECs [14] [15]. The proposed
conditions are similar in the two directives, however, IMED is one year newer and thereby
more comprehensive in terms of the requirements made for the frameworks. The guidelines
for the framework conditions distinguish between may and shall, dependent on whether it
is required in the frameworks or not.
Only four conditions are present in both directives as a requirement for the enabling frame-
works; the ECs accessibility towards all electricity markets either directly or through ag-
gregation, the fact that member states are entitled to arrange sharing of electricity that is
produced by the units owned by the community, ECs are subject to non-discriminatory,
fair proportionate and transparent procedures and charges and the fact that DSOs has
to cooperate with ECs to facilitate electricity transfers within the community [14] [15].
Among other notable aspects is the fact that the member states are required to include
an implementation strategy of the main points of these framework conditions in their
integrated national energy and climate plans (NECP)[14].
3.1.2
Austria
Energy Communities
Currently, RECs are receiving increased attention in Austria to promote a decentralized
energy supply[17]. The decentralization causes an increased amount of regionalization of
the energy supply, which is expected to increase the supply security and robustness of the
system through smart microgrids[18]. Based on Austrian legislation, [17] states that a
REC can produce energy from renewables as well as consume, store or sell the produced
energy.
From the NECP of Austria, it is described how they expect the RECs to enable bilateral
supply contracts to be set up, as well as co-operative-type structures beyond property
boundaries, in terms of generation, storage, and supply of renewable electricity[18]. This
further has the intention of increasing the possibilities in operating and setting up lo-
cal microgrids cost-eﬀectively[18]. According to The Renewable Energy Expansion Act
(REEA) made by the Austrian legislators, the purpose of the RECs must not be ﬁnancial
proﬁts, but rather to provide environmental, economic, or social beneﬁts to its members
or the area[19]. This purpose is further outlined in the NECP, stating that the focus of a
REC should be on the common use of renewable energy[18].
Legal Situation
The grid costs that are supposed to be paid, are stated in the NECP as a pro-rata prefer-
ential grid tariﬀfor shared use of the public grid, however not stating in-depth how this is
done[18]. Furthermore, a reduced distribution grid tariﬀis introduced for the electricity
exchanged within the REC, as well as other ﬁnancial advantages to improve the incentive
for REC participants[17]. It is however not clariﬁed to what extent the grid tariﬀs are
reduced.
Tax relief on electricity generated from renewable energy sources and self-consumed up to
6
==PAGE:15==
an amount of 25000 KWh, which they adopted in 2014 is mentioned in the NECP[18] as
something usable for the ECs. In that regard, they further state that investment support
for PVs was extended to include storage[18]. The PV investment support is presented in
the Green Electricity Act of 2017, in which they state that 30% of construction costs on
small-scale and private PVs are supported by the government[20].
From the REEA the opportunity of leasing renewable generation facilities has become
available for the energy communities[21], under the requirement that the energy commu-
nities are still in eﬀective control over the generation facilities.
A lot of general ideas on how to enable the Renewable energy communities are proposed,
but only with very little actual numbers on the amount of support that is in fact available
to the ECs. Austria did react to the two directives on ECs, however, most of the available
initiatives were already available, indicating that some possibilities for ECs were already
present, but still new initiatives were needed for the possibilities of the ECs to align with
the EU requirements.
3.1.3
Netherlands
Energy Communities
Several examples of energy communities are already present in the Netherlands and de-
scribed in literature[22][23]. [23] further states that the number of energy communities
operating in the Netherlands in 2018 was around 500, involving 70000 citizens and a total
capacity PV of 74.5 MWp and wind capacity of 159 MW. The Dutch NECP voices that
energy communities can contribute towards increased energy eﬃciency. Policies related to
ECs are described to have the aim of promoting support for the energy transition, being
the most vital goal[24].
[25] states that the role of ECs in the energy system will be to provide and trade ﬂexibility
on the energy grid, and argues they should do so by using a community based virtual power
plant – a solution for grid issues and challenges related to the integration of renewable
energy sources in the energy mix. [23] describes that Energy communities, Microgrids,
and virtual power plants, in general, are very similar and that most policies on the area
would apply to all of them.
Energy Cooperative Loenen(ECL), a Dutch EC consisting of private households, social
housing, industry, SMEs, and schools, has the motivation of increasing the local renewable
energy generation and a future-proof electricity system, while also increasing self-reliance
and improving the local economy by increasing local revenues from generation[25].
It
does seem that the most important motivation for ECL is that of maximizing the self-
consumption of renewable energy within the community[22].
Legal Situation
At this point, the Dutch legislators have not committed to a plan on how to improve the
conditions for ECs, but some legislations can apply to the potential of ECs in the Nether-
lands. The NECP of the Netherlands states that PV owners are reimbursed the VAT on
7
==PAGE:16==
their purchase of the solar panels and that they further have the possibility of using the
“net-metering scheme” to be compensated for their excess electricity production (this has
however been decided to be phased out from 2023 to 2030) [24]. It is further described
how they are not supposed to pay taxes for the sharing of jointly generated renewable
energy[24].
In the Netherlands, the consumer pays the network operator for their connection and the
transport of energy, based on a yearly average consumption of 3500 KWh for each house-
hold, thereby splitting the costs between all customers[23].
In Heeten, a Dutch EC of 47 households, the energy transport costs are decoupled from
the connection costs and are paid at a variable level dependent on the amount that is
demanded from the EC on a transformer level, and thereby also dependent on the energy
production on an average every 15 minutes[23]. They then use batteries to reduce the cost
of energy transport when the demand is at its highest.
Yet, the directives of the EU have not directly been transferred into Dutch law, but they
do, as stated previously, already have a quite high amount of energy communities running,
implying that opportunities are present. The fact that the net-metering scheme is going
to be phased out, will be a barrier for the emergence of renewable energy communities as
it is for any future owner of PVs.
3.1.4
Denmark
Energy Communities
In Denmark, there is currently an ongoing discussion on how ECs as proposed by the EU
directives, will be allowed to become a player in the green transition. The Danish Energy
Agency states that the ECs are expected to have the role of involving the consumers in
promoting ﬂexibility in the energy system[26]. The legislation on ECs in Denmark deﬁnes
them as proposed by the EU directives, in terms of all requirements, and in general, states
that ECs should be treated without any kind of discrimination neither positive nor nega-
tive[27].
Few ECs are currently in function in Denmark, however, Energy Community Avedøre is
an EC that brings together several actors of Avedøre Stationsby in the municipality of
Hvidovre[28]. Based on quite high investments, they have successfully installed PV panels
on quite a lot of the municipal buildings in the area and are currently installing charging
facilities on the local high school. This has the purpose of smoothing out the peaks, as well
as ensuring that the variable renewable technologies will be exploited to the maximum of
their capacities[29]. Furthermore, the expansion of the EC may lead to fewer investments
in the energy grid being required in that area, as production and demand will be dealt
with much more locally[28]. Still, the major ambition of the EC is, that they expect to
become climate neutral by 2050.
Legal Situation
Dansk Energi implies that advantageous legislation for ECs may, in fact, lead to increased
costs for the remaining users of the system[30]. That is further underlined by the Minister
8
==PAGE:17==
of climate, energy, and supply of Denmark, Dan Jørgensen, who states that currently, no
particular economic initiatives will be launched towards ECs[31].
The ﬁrst Danish legislation on ECs was adopted in December 2020 and asserted that ECs
are supposed to be treated like any other participant in the energy market, and are in that
regard forced to pay for any imbalances they may cause to the energy system[27]. In the
case that energy is shared within the EC, they are aﬀected by the applicable net tariﬀs
and fees[27]. It is however declared that in the case of the ECs providing savings towards
the collective distributors, they should be compensated to match the level of beneﬁts as
assessed by the distributors themselves. Denmark currently has some support schemes
trying to enhance the green transition, which could also be of use to participants in ECs.
To lower the amount of gas and oil-powered heating systems in private households, citizens
are oﬀered 20000 DKK in support to exchange it for a district heating system[32]. The
support on PV systems has been withdrawn as it was too favorable, causing harm to the
collective energy grid, thus now lowering the incitement to invest in these resources[29].
3.2
Beyond State of the Art
Denmark has a goal of becoming independent of fossil fuels by 2050, essentially meaning
that they must be able to produce enough renewable energy, however, given the variable
nature of these resources, a ﬂexible grid is a requirement. According to Danish legislation,
the ECs have all possibilities needed to be functioning well, like in other countries, and
the guidelines proposed by the EU directives are already implemented. It does however
become apparent, that only a very few ECs are currently operating, despite the great po-
tential of providing ﬂexibility, while at the same time lowering the number of investments
required towards the renewable energy grid.
The ECs may as proposed by the Austrian government be able to increase the possibilities
in operating and setting up local microgrids cost-eﬀectively. Similarly, a well-functioning
virtual power plant could be usable in trading the ﬂexibility services, as seen in the Nether-
lands, and thereby hopefully not cause the feared amount of disturbance to the existing
collective grid.
Large investments are further required for the ECs to obtain the required renewable en-
ergy resources, as seen in the case of Avedøre EC, and perhaps smaller entities may not
have the same opportunities available to them. Thereby, investment potential must be
identiﬁed, whether it is that an EC makes one large investment for everyone, or that the
participants do it individually. An alternative could be the possibility of leasing renewable
energy resources as made possible in Austria.
It does seem that the greatest potential of ECs in Denmark is related to the ﬂexibility
that they can provide, as the share of RE is already at quite high levels. Thus, research
and support should potentially emphasize the possibilities related to energy storage and
charging of EVs. Another point would be to amplify the opportunities for municipal and
private entities to participate in the same system, to exploit the maximum potential of
the diﬀerent energy resources, as it is seen in the Netherlands and wished for in Avedøre.
The collective Danish energy grid seems to be in a very protected state, where former

4
Mathematical Modelling
The research that was conducted on energy communities above allows for an analysis to
be made by mathematically modeling the energy systems with and without ECs. The
mathematical model is then optimized to enable comparison between diﬀerent aspects of
the system numerically.
4.1
Modeling of Energy Systems and Communities
To mathematically investigate the opportunities and eﬀects of introducing regulatory
framework conditions on energy communities, mathematical models are built and later
optimized upon. The topic of energy modeling has been extensively researched in litera-
ture, and in most recent research, energy communities are modeled as well.
[17] provides an overview of how energy communities may be modeled, based on a case
study conducted in Austria.
It is modeled using a Mixed-integer-linear programming
(MILP) model which brings together nine diﬀerent entities with diﬀerent energy proper-
ties, using three scenarios that are optimized to minimize both the emissions as well as the
total energy system costs for a local district. Despite the research covering a broad variety
of areas on both strategy/policy recommendation as well as modeling, heating could be
included as an energy source to get an even better understanding of the energy community
system and investments in it, as well as how electricity consumption may be optimized.
They suggest that the trade within the energy community should be based on ﬁxed tariﬀs,
which are paid by the participants importing from other participants in the EC.
Internal power trading has further been optimized upon by [33] on a microgrid level to
which both feed-in tariﬀs and retail prices have been included. However there may be
distinguished between an energy community and a microgrid, and to an extent, energy
communities may be preferred. Still, despite some diﬀerences, microgrids may be under-
stood as an energy community, and some modeling can be reused[34].
Energy communities may also be a provider of ﬂexibility services as proposed by [35],
which investigate and optimize upon the potential of contracting ﬂexibility from an en-
ergy community with 30 households in the UK that have heat pumps. Introducing the
ﬂexibility of charging EVs and building heat, is investigated by [36] who set up a MILP
based on an energy community in Norway, and seek to investigate the inﬂuence on the
European market.
Beyond Literature Modeling
When modeling energy communities, it is sought to introduce a model which considers
both heating and electricity demand, as well as the possible sector coupling based on the
two energy carriers. Diﬀerent scenarios will be included to outline the economical chal-
lenges of setting up an energy community and make a comparison between situations with
and without the possibility for ECs.

4.2
Model Objective
The objective of the model developed for this thesis is as recently described to mathe-
matically model two diﬀerent scenarios, each representing a case with and without energy
communities.
The model minimizes the total costs for the system – the diﬀerent cost components include
investment costs into technologies, O&M costs as well as costs related to electricity import
to the grid or earnings from exporting to the grid. The model ensures that diﬀerent de-
mand components are satisﬁed by including balance constraints. Furthermore, constraints
will ensure that the technological assumptions described in section 5 will also be satisﬁed.
Finally, constraints will mathematically connect certain technologies to each other, allow-
ing e.g. electricity generated from PV to be used for the heat pumps or for the load. The
outcome of the optimization will be a proposal of how the investments in technologies
optimally should be. It will further grant insight into how the analyzed energy system
should be managed at every hour in the time series to keep the costs minimized. The total
system costs will as said, be the objective, which can then be compared to see the overall
diﬀerences between the scenarios. The diﬀerent technologies included in the model, which
the optimization will provide investment recommendations for are listed below:
• Photovoltaics
• Electric vehicles
• Li-ion batteries
• Heat pumps
• Electric boilers
• Water tanks
Each of these technologies are based on electricity, or produce it in the case of PVs, and
their relations are included in the model. The technologies have been chosen due to their
potential in a further electriﬁed energy system.
The ﬁrst scenario will represent the base case in which the diﬀerent apartment blocks un-
der investigation will not be able to transfer electricity between each other and are thereby
only connected to the grid. This should reﬂect the reality as it is today. An illustration
of how the system works is presented in ﬁgure2. With E representing the export costs
relying on the spot price, and I representing the import costs which relies on the spot price,
taxes (energy and carbon) and tariﬀs. The grid limit represents the maximum amount of
electricity which can be ﬂowed at any hour.

Sensitivity Analysis
This section will analyze in-depth how changes to certain parameters may inﬂuence the
outcome of the model in terms of investments and system costs for both scenarios. These
potential changes are then compared to what was identiﬁed in the results section. Thus,
the changes described in this section will be those compared to the initial results for both
scenarios, and will always be presented as a relative change. When comparing with the
already presented results, the previous edition of the model will be referred to as the ref-
erence case.
The ﬁrst performed sensitivity analysis is that of limiting the grid connection, as there
are some uncertainties related to the capacity of the grid connection. Secondly, an assess-
ment of the heat demand is analyzed. As described earlier, heat eﬃciency may have the
ability to decrease the heat demand signiﬁcantly. The ﬁnal sensitivity regards an EV-free
system. The assumption that EVs are owned by every household without the requirement
of investments, is representative of a future situation. To identify the potential related to
having EVs in ECs the sensitivity is conducted.
7.1
Grid Limitation Sensitivity
7.1.1
Variation From Reference Case
This sensitivity analysis will identify the impact of limiting the capacity of the connection
to the grid. As described in the data section, the consumers of electricity should ideally
be allowed unlimited electricity supply. However, this is of course not representative of
reality, as there are in fact limitations on the capacities of the connections. In the reference
case, an upper limit was set at 50 kWh. In order to measure the sensitivity towards a
lower capacity, the model has been run using an upper limit of 36 – the lowest possible
limit for the model to still be feasible – and representing a connection of 4 kWh for each
apartment. This should be suﬃcient in satisfying the demand for most households.
7.1.2
Sensitivity Outcome
The optimal investment mix for the limited grid connection capacity is shown on ﬁgure 9.
Since there is now a stricter limit on the possible amount of imported electricity, more in-
vestments have to be made. Consequently, the capacity of installed batteries experiences a
remarkable increase for both apartment blocks in the base scenario; 1497% for apartment
block 1 and 1372% for apartment block 2. Regarding the EC scenario, the total capacity
of installed batteries increases as well, however, in this case, all investments are made by
apartment block 2. This is the opposite of the reference case in which no investments were
made for batteries in block 2 and all in block 1. The optimal heat pump investments are
almost the same as in the reference case. Apartment block 1 reaches the limit in both
scenarios, while apartment block 2 experiences rises of 0.01% and 0.14% for the base and
EC scenarios. Considering investments into the electric boilers, an increase of 68.55% is
observed for apartment block 1 in the base scenario, which allows assisting in charging up

8
Discussion and Limitations
To best enable the results to be used for political recommendations, this section will pro-
vide a discussion on how regulatory framework conditions may be identiﬁed or developed
upon, based on the results and learnings from the benchmark analysis. To analyze in-depth
the speciﬁc areas where regulatory framework conditions may have the biggest impact, the
outcome of the sensitivity analysis will be used alongside the results.
Furthermore, a lot of assumptions have been used to make mathematical modeling possi-
ble. The key assumptions and limitations will be critically discussed to grant an overview
of how the results may be assessed.
8.1
Energy Community Design
The most crucial part of this analysis is the design of the energy community. The way
it is included in this thesis allows for the participants to trade freely within the commu-
nity. Also, the costs are expected to be shared collectively, granting everyone the same
opportunities. While this assumption of free trade, in general, seems to be the most ap-
propriate way of managing the communities, it may be argued that in some cases this is
not possible. Either because the participants do not want to engage in a system where all
costs are shared, assuming that some of the participants have already installed renewable
technologies, or simply because it is not allowed from the legislation.
Currently, the legislation in Denmark does not grant any beneﬁts to internal trade be-
tween participants of a community, in fact, the taxes and tariﬀs apply in the same way
when electricity is shared. However, this is a point of interest, as energy communities
are not generally revenue-based, as they only seek to lower the costs and emissions of the
system. The ideal solution for an EC then regards the case where all participants have
the minimum cost – in this case, the lowest average cost. The costs for the system would
then end up being just taxes and tariﬀs, as it would be expected that the import and
export costs would equal each other out. However, the way the energy market is built, the
spot price is dependent on the most expensive producers’ marginal costs. In the case of
the energy community, it would be expected that the traded energy would be either from
an overproduction of renewable electricity or as a discharge from storage. The produced
renewable electricity would be considered to have a marginal cost of 0 or at least consider
only the O&M costs, therefore being relatively close to 0.
Regarding stored electricity, the ECs may have stored it in case of a spot price valley, or
an overproduction as described before, and the cost to trade between each other would
be unknown. As the ECs cannot be associated with revenue the trading costs are very
complex.
Finally, in spite of taxes and tariﬀs not being adjusted, it is safe to assume that if these
costs were present on the import of electricity from another participant, the losses from
the EC scenario would be great, as the trade costs would be limited to just these, and the
trade would thereby solely provide costs for the energy community.
Regarding the system which is assumed to be solely dependent on electricity to satisfy
37
==PAGE:46==
heat and electricity demand, as well as the necessary transport for each household, it is
expected that more heat will be generated through electricity-driven heat production tech-
nologies in the future. Also, the goal is to have carbon-neutral consumption in Denmark
in 2050. Likewise, as previously described, district heating systems also seek to develop
in that direction by investing in large-scale heat pumps, electric boilers, and heat storage.
The fact that the system considered in this thesis does not include district heating, drives
up the investments into these technologies for the two apartment blocks of investigation.
Furthermore, the electricity demand as a result rises to very high levels, which pressures
the connection capacity. The eﬀect of this as compared to the costs of importing from the
district heating grid is not analyzed, and would potentially cause the investments to be
diﬀerent.
It is apparent that especially both small- and large-scale heat pumps are expected to be
a vital part of the transition towards green heat production. Currently, a support scheme
operates from which households who transition from gas or oil-driven boilers to heat pumps
will be supported in doing by receiving 20000 DKK. To encourage more households to in-
stall heat pumps a similar support scheme could be implemented for households connected
to the district heating grid, thus lowering the necessary investments in the district heating
system.
Considering the electric vehicles, the assumption that all households own such a car is
deﬁnitely still some years away. However, as stated earlier, the diesel- and gasoline-fuelled
cars will be phased out and are proposed to no longer be available for purchase after 2035.
The results implied that the EC was superior in exploiting all the storage and demand,
which the EVs were responsible for. This was underlined in depth by the sensitivity anal-
ysis in which they were not included, and where the diﬀerences between the two scenarios
were negligible. Thereby, ECs are very much suitable for a future in which the amount of
electric vehicles increases. However, it may not grant the same beneﬁts at this point in
time.
8.2
Potential in Peak Management
Throughout the result section, one thing has become apparent: The EC performs better
than the regular system in terms of peak management. This was witnessed based on the
upper limit of the heat pump capacity. In these situations, the EC was able to exploit the
available electricity storage provided by the EVs in particular. The situation currently
experienced in the world sees a massive peak in the electricity price. If peaks like this
will occur more frequently in the future, ECs may be able to manage it better and the
participants may experience greater beneﬁts. To that extent, one of the main objectives
for the ECs is to be able to provide ﬂexibility to the system, and may in the future be able
to sell ﬂexibility services. These potential earnings are not included in the analysis since
this is based solely on a comparison between the base and EC scenarios. By including
this, the overall beneﬁts related to ECs could drastically improve.
Although the scope of this thesis has not been of addressing ﬂexibility services and demand
response, it may be argued, that the decrease of imported electricity in the peak hours can

indirectly be considered that. It was witnessed that the EVs were of signiﬁcant importance
to this aspect, and regulatory framework conditions addressing support of the interaction
between EVs and ECs could provide beneﬁts.
The concern for the Danish legislators seems to be that when granting beneﬁcial regulatory
framework conditions for the ECs it would end up hurting the collective electricity system
and make it more expensive for the average consumer. However, if the ECs are in fact
able to provide ﬂexibility services, it could be argued, that the overall impact on the
collective electricity system would be positive.
Especially since the ﬂexibility services
should emphasize to even out the spot price curve, thus ensuring fewer peaks and valleys.
In this regard, diﬀerent parts of the grid are already under pressure and the implementation
of certain ﬂexibility services may allow investments in the grid to be kept at a lower level.
However, it must be taken into consideration that the ECs still are consumers in the end,
and must have their demand satisﬁed. Therefore, they may not be considered completely
reliant. On the other hand, the two apartment blocks share a very similar demand pattern.
If commercial buildings with completely diﬀerent demand patterns were included, it is
possible that the total beneﬁts of ECs would increase drastically and that they – as a
result of the internal trading – would become much more reliable.
Regarding the taxes, tariﬀs, and carbon costs, it was initially expected that the ECs would
be able to save costs in this area which they could then reinvest into DER. However, the
results proposed that only in very rare cases were the optimal investment costs for the EC
higher than the base case. This may be a result of the limitations that each technology,
perhaps mostly related to the PV, included.
8.3
Further Limitations
Some technologies which have not already been described are associated with uncertain-
ties or limitations in general. First, regarding the storage technologies, neither of them
have been modeled with linear losses. This may cause very low investments into batteries
throughout almost every model run. As batteries, in general, have lower linear losses than
heat storage, the results are deﬁnitely skewered towards investments in these more than
they should be.
Second, the carbon impacts of installing the diﬀerent technologies have not been included
in the analysis.
Thereby, the carbon emissions which are presented may in fact be a
lot higher. The carbon costs which were identiﬁed throughout the diﬀerent results were
shown to be of quite small values. This is of course a result of the system solely relying on
electricity, and the emissions which could have been experienced from including e.g. gas
boilers would potentially provide more costs to analyze.
Third, no inﬂation has been applied to the monetary values, meaning that they may diﬀer
in the years from which they were derived. However, the monetary values regarding the
technologies are all based on data from the Danish Energy Agency and do reﬂect the same
year’s values.
Lastly, it was previously described that the Danish legislators fear that beneﬁcial condi-

are very complex to analyze and are beyond the scope of this thesis. They have thereby

Conclusion and Future Work
9.1
Conclusion
This thesis has sought to identify the potential related to ECs and how it is possible
politically to amplify the engagement in such communities. Research conducted on ECs
made it possible to build a mathematical model reﬂecting the diﬀerences between a base
scenario and an EC scenario. Based on an optimization of the mathematical, model many
results were obtained.
The results implied that investments into DER were preferred for both scenarios.
In
fact, in every optimization of the model, the PV were always reaching the limit for both
of the apartment blocks that were analyzed. Heat pumps were preferred over electric boil-
ers as the source for heat generation, however, investments were very similar for the two
scenarios. The possibility for internal trade between participants did nonetheless decrease
the total investment costs for the EC scenario as the base case had to invest more into
storage technologies. The total electricity consumption was decreased by 0.7% from the
EC scenario. In total, the EC scenario performed better in any cost category, and would
be preferable in spite of the only marginal diﬀerence.
The costs of taxes and tariﬀs were deﬁnitely of signiﬁcance to the system, but the dif-
ference between what the base and EC scenario ended up paying was minimal. It was
expected that the EC would decrease the amount of imported electricity signiﬁcantly, and
thereby be able to reinvest into technologies. However, the base scenario did in all cases
end up having the highest investment costs.
It was identiﬁed that ECs enabled better peak management and in general performed
better when the connection was under pressure, and as identiﬁed in the sensitivity analy-
sis, the storage of the EVs was eﬀectively exploited in this regard. Given the assumption
that the electricity demand is expected to rise in the coming years, peak management is
considered essential.
In conjunction, one of the main potentials for ECs were identiﬁed as their ability to pro-
vide ﬂexibility to the energy system. Although is was not analyzed, it is expected that
moree beneﬁts can be discovered in this regard
Regarding the design of the EC in this thesis, it was discussed that free trade would be
ideal due to the complexity of price-setting. If taxes and tariﬀs were to be applied to the
internal trade, the system would experience losses. This is quite apparent based on the
small diﬀerences between the scenarios.
As a ﬁnal conclusion, the thesis has granted an insight in how regulatory framework
conditions may be identiﬁed, and which ones may be of importance to encourage engage-
ment in ECs.

When considering the current state of ECs in Denmark, in which they would have taxes
and tariﬀs applied to them, an EC would potentially encounter losses. Therefore, the main
recommendation is to politically implement the regulatory framework condition allowing
for free trade within the EC at a tax and tariﬀrelief to maximize the potential beneﬁts.
Furthermore, ﬂexibility services must be addressed to optimize the beneﬁts of the ECs. To
amplify the potential of the ﬂexibility services, support schemes could be built centered
on heat pumps or energy storage.
9.2
Future Work
To further investigate the potential related to the ECs, it would be very interesting to
include data from an entity which does not share the consumption pattern, as it is in the
case of this thesis. Furthermore, the heating system could be developed to use district
heating or gas to identify how large the impact of the electricity generated heat is.
