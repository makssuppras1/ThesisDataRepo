Summary
This thesis aims at constructing a Fully Non-linear Potential Flow solver to accurately
simulate non-linear wave-body interactions. The basic principles of potential flow are
explained along with a method for estimating hydrodynamic pressure and force, namely
the Acceleration Potential Method. Regarding the numerical method, a Spectral Element
Method is employed detailing the theory and setup of such method. Numerical results
are produced for a variety of cases starting with the simplest linear waves to end up
with a free-floating body simulator. Convergence is tested to the extent possible and the
results show high potential.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Goals and motivation
Green and sustainable energy is globally one of the most important topics of the mod-
ern world to ensure a healthy planet for future generations to come. The population
keeps growing, increasing the demand for electricity and necessities which in turn raises
the energy consumption of the industry. As engineers we should strive to develop new
methods and technologies to help equip our Earth to withstand the great strain we are
putting on it.
One way to reduce the load on the climate is to utilise green energy sources over non-
renewable ones like coal, oil or nuclear power. Seeing how Earth’s surface is covered by
approximately 70% water it is no mystery that hydropower is one of the oldest ways
used to obtain energy. Watermills and dams are well established ways of converting hy-
dropower into either electrical or mechanical energy. These techniques require elevated
water masses for building pressure or creating flow. A new class of technology called
Wave Energy Converters (WECs) is designed to extract energy from waves making the
ocean an unlimited resource of power. Many different types of WECs exist but the ones
closest related to this project are the type called point absorbers some of which are shown
in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Point absorber WEC. Picture from [3]
Development of new technologies requires extensive testing and experimentation is
usually expensive. Given the computational resources available today constructing meth-
ods for simulating physical models allows for fast and efficient ways of verifying structural
design. Many Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) based tools exist with different de-
grees of accuracy and computational cost, mostly very expensive. Since many methods
and formulations exist studies have been conducted to determine what types are best

suited for WEC design, see [5] and [3]
Understanding and describing the behaviour of water in motion are the first steps of
creating value towards real applications. In the present thesis Potential Flow theory will
be applied to describe hydrodynamic properties and set up the necessary equations. Ac-
curate approximation of pressure and forces is extremely important for creating durable
constructions, and Tanizawa [30] presents a method for this, the Acceleration Potential
method. This approach has been used in several studies some of which are summarised in
[13] in combination with potential flow solvers. Introducing a Spectral Element Method
(SEM) as the numerical tool for constructing a non-linear potential flow solver to sim-
ulate wave-body interactions. [9], [22], [1], [23], [10] and [11] are just some examples
of work where SEM based models have been used to accurately and efficiently simulate
wave-body interactions.
The goal is to implement a Fully Non-linear Potential Flow solver for the accurate mod-
elling of wave-body interactions by using the Acceleration Potential method, and to
investigate cases ranging from linear flow with no structure to fully non-linear flow in
interaction with a freely-heaving body.
1.2
Outline
The thesis is structured as follows. Chapter 2 introduces the fundamental hydrodynamic
concepts for potential flow and free surface waves. It also introduces the idea of using
the acceleration potential for efficient and accurate computations of pressure and force.
In Chapter 3 the Spectral Element Method is presented, introducing the concept of
polynomial expansion and polynomial basis functions. Spatial discretisation leading to
quadrature rules and numerical integration is also presented here as well as the Galerkin
Method for presenting the problem in its weak form. Chapter 4 briefly explains the theory
of linear waves and presents results for a linear standing wave. Nonlinear stream function
waves and solitary gravity waves are described in Chapter 5. An analytic formula for
constructing non-linear stream function waves is also presented in this chapter, it will be
used for conducting convergence analysis. Benchmarks in terms of attachment analysis
and instantaneous wall force are also assessed in this chapter for the reflection of solitary
waves against a vertical wall. Chapter 6 explains the three cases of wave-body interaction;
fixed, forced motion and free floating, and benchmarks for these cases will be used when
available. This chapter also introduces the essence of this project, namely the implicit
body boundary condition from the acceleration potential needed for updating position
and velocity in the free-floating case. Chapter 7 concludes on the findings of this projects
and reflects on the procedure and what future work could be relevant.

CHAPTER 3
The Spectral Element
Method SEM
This chapter will present the different parts needed to formulate a Spectral Element
Method for solving the Laplace problem.
The theory is much like that of Finite Element Methods but instead of linear basis
functions, the solution is here approximated by orthogonal polynomials. This chapter
will also briefly cover the mesh generation since the node distribution is quite different
from that in FEM but follows directly from the chosen basis functions. The procedure
deduced here is based on the theory described in [8], [16] and [19].

5.2.2
Reflection of Solitary Gravity Wave against wall
In this section the case of solitary waves propagating against a fixed vertical wall is
studied. This case has been studied experimentally in [22] and numerically in [31], [1]
and [2] using respectively a Boundary Integral Method, Nodal DG-FEM and a Boussinesq
type method all of which have obtained excellent results. The setup is to propagate a
solitary wave towards a vertical wall where it will collide and begin to run up. When the
maximum surface elevation occurs at the wall the wave is said to have attached and that
time is denoted the attachment time ta with corresponding surface elevation ηa. After
the wave has attached it will continue to shoot up the wall until it reaches a maximum
run up η0 at time t0. Running down again the detachment time td denotes the last time
that the maximum surface elevation occurs at the wall with corresponding elevation ηd.
The experiments here are carried out using a computational domain x ∈[−15, 15] and
z ∈[0, −1] discretised using Nx ∈[60, 100] depending on the examined height, Nz = 2
and polynomial order P = 6. A solitary wave is initialised due to [7] with its peak at
x = −1, characterised by its amplitude a/h.
Initially, the incident wave has the profile of a pure solitary wave while after collision
some energy is lost to a dispersive wave train resulting in a reflected solitary wave with
smaller amplitude and thus lower speed. Figure 5.6a shows the different incident profiles
at t = 0 and Figure 5.6b shows their corresponding final profiles at t = 10. It is clear
that the dispersive wave train becomes more prominent as the wave height increases. For
a/h = 0.2 it is almost non-existent whereas for a/h = 0.6 a clear train of smaller waves
can be seen behind.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Future
Work
In this thesis a method for solving Fully Non-linear Free Surface Potential Flow has
been presented. Using the Acceleration Potential Method in combination with a Spec-
tral Element Method an efficient way to compute pressure and forces has been shown.
The efficiency lies in the reusable set of operators based solely on domain dicretisation.
Simulations of non-linear wave-body interactions for fixed structures, structures under
forced vertical motion and free-floating heaving structures has been shown and tested as
much as time has allowed.
Although great potential has been shown by the presented model, a more structured
verification would be needed before it can qualify as useful for real life applications. Ex-
tensive tests should be performed in all cases to ensure that physical aspects are modeled
correctly, e.g. ensuring conservation of mass, energy and momentum. Implementation of
the missing term for the free-floating case is also a main concern for future work. Another
way is to search for and obtain more data suited for the current setups but this might
not be possible since such data is not always available. A future aspect of great value
is to implement the handling of more complex structures allowing for a wider range of
benchmarking opportunities and for a wide range of real life applications. Once verified,
a natural next step would be to extend the model from 2D to 3D. Extending to 3D
and allowing for complex structures and domain geometries opens up the possibility of
creating a model that can compete with CFD tools used in the industry in both accuracy
and speed.
Another important aspect related to creating a model suitable for the industry is to
enhance computational performance e.g. by parallelisation of the code. Incorporation of
iterative methods for solving the big systems of linear equations is another possible way
of enhancing performance. Code efficiency and good management of computational re-
sources are, along with a robust model, also key points in keeping the energy consumption
as low as possible and this is indeed an important goal.
