Summary
Wind resource assessment is an essential aspect of the planning and design of wind farms. The wind is
affected by terrain features, such as elevation and surface roughness. Linearized flow models are com-
monly used to quantify the effects of terrain variations in speed up and relative wind directions. For
complex terrains, such as mountainous or hilly areas, nonlinear flow models based on Reynolds-Averaged
Navier Stokes (RANS) solvers have become increasingly popular in the wind industry. However, RANS
does not always predict better results than linearized flow models, partly because the more sophisticated
RANS consequently relies on a larger number of input parameters. These can deteriorate the solution
if set to inappropriate values, and establishing ‘standard’ values can contribute to more consistency in
the industrial use of RANS for resource assessment. The extent to which various parameters affect the
predicted winds has not been systematically characterized; thus, it is essential to perform sensitivity
analysis and numerical verification of relevant RANS parameters. These include grid resolution, domain
depth, grid radius, inner domain area, and parameters in the turbulence model.
This thesis dealt with the parametric study of different input parameters and their effect on speed
up by perturbing input parameters in the model setup. The simulation tool used in the thesis was
EllipSys3D, where the flow was modeled explicitly with Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes and two-
equation k-ε turbulence model assuming a neutral atmospheric boundary layer. The terrain cases used
for analyzing different input parameters were Akservein Hill and one complex site located in Norway.
The first part of this thesis dealt with the effect on speed up by tuning different input parameters for a
single transect. In the second part, statistical methods were implemented to understand the variation of
speed up for multiple points over terrain. Further, estimated speed up at different input parameters was
compared to the reference case values to understand bias when dealing with different input parameters.
In the sensitivity analyses part, grid resolution and domain depth were considered for both the terrain
sites. Also, one extra parameter Cµ was considered for complex terrain study. Finally, a systematic
sensitivity analysis was performed to understand how much the speed ups were varying to change in
input parameters.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Investments in renewable energy sources are growing substantially across the world, and wind energy
is one of the main contributors towards the growth of electrical power capacity [6]. With a further
expected growth of the wind energy industry and more wind turbine installations in the future, de-
termining the optimal sites for these installations becomes a major area of concern. With the best
locations already occupied by the existing turbines ; thus wind farm developers have to look for new
and still available sites which may not be ideal, such as complex terrain landscapes [6].
In the wind energy sector, the optimal locations are usually onshore or near the coastline, but such
places are generally found in densely populated areas. The development of wind energy in these areas
often creates acceptance issues by residents living nearby. There are many sites located across the
world where there are strong winds, and the turbulence is low. However, these sites are mostly situated
in off-shore regions, which increases the costs of installing wind turbines. Therefore, an alternative
is to erect wind turbines in complex terrain. These sites have different topographies and roughness,
influencing the wind in the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL). The interaction of the complicated
landforms with the turbulent ABL flow may significantly affect both the character of the large-scale
flow over wind farms and the local flow features such as flow acceleration, separation, and recircula-
tion [18]. Hence, a detailed wind resource analysis is required for a particular wind site so that wind
engineers can accurately predict the power output from the wind power plants. Additionally, in the
wind industry, hills have always been used for different studies, primarily because of the speed up
effects. On the other hand, complex sites have also been used for siting wind turbines, but there has
always been a trade-off between increased production and increased loads. Apart from analyzing speed
up effects, there is also an interest in studying the flow behavior in complex terrain such as flow attach-
ment cum detachment recirculation zones, which can severely impact wind farms power production [32].
Different models are used in wind energy for the simulation of wind fields, such as mesoscale models,
climate prediction models, and global circulation model [12], where mesoscale models generally use a
grid spacing between 1 km and 10 km. These different models are used for predicting the velocity and
temperature fields for flow over topography. However, the use of mesoscale atmospheric simulation
codes is limited to complex terrains where near-surface characteristics are important [21].

Most commercial computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solvers used in the wind industry are based
on a finite volume method with unstructured grids that are capable of handling geometrical features
of arbitrary complexity [29]. For complex sites such as mountainous or hilly areas, nonlinear models
based on Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) solvers have become increasingly popular in the
wind industry. These solvers are attractive compared to mesoscale model codes because CFD solvers
can quickly evaluate the details near the surface with finer resolution.
The commercial codes are not usually open-source and are sold as closed packages with restricted
access to internal routines and algorithms [29].
Therefore, the CFD codes must be evaluated, and
their modeling capacity should be checked when analyzing ABL simulations. However, RANS models
are sensitive to the input parameters and require a detailed analysis when considering a complex terrain.
When dealing with heterogeneous terrain, different numerical models can be used to gain insight
into flow properties. These models can range from simple to complex types for analyzing other flow
properties. The more the model gets complex, there is also an increase in the computational demand for
running the models. Therefore, it’s always better to have a trade-off between accuracy and CPU time
when dealing with complex flow analyses. The models listed below are ranked in order of complexity,
with the most simple in the beginning and the most complex at the end [19].
1. Simple models gives some insight into a given problem by solving different analytical equations
and is thus relatively easier and can be calculated quickly [19].
2. Linearized models are based on the linearization of the RANS equation. The mean wind speed
solution is obtained in a given coordinate system and includes both hills and roughness change.
The models have several limitations when predicting the flow phenomena at complex sites such
as steep hills in complex terrain. This model cannot capture complex flow phenomena such as
flow around steep hills in complex terrain. An example of this model is LinCom model used in
WEng [19].
3. Reynold’s average Navier-Stokes (RANS) model is based on a set of Navier-Stokes equations,
which are averaged to give RANS equations as shown in Equation 3.6. This averaging results in
additional terms, called Reynold’s stresses, characteristic of turbulent flows. To solve a full set
of RANS equations in a computational grid, a turbulence model is employed that assumes a
relationship between Reynold’s stresses and the mean characteristics of the turbulent flow. The
eddy viscosity is modeled; often by k-ε model in which two equations, one for kinetic energy and
the other for dissipation rate, are added to the Equation 3.6 [19].
4. Large Eddy Simulation (LES) uses spatial averaging of Navier-Stokes equations. The turbu-
lence is resolved in LES simulations down to scales in the order of grid size. This model is capable
of producing turbulence spectra used for load calculations.
LES is a significant step towards
proper physical modeling of ABL. However, the computational costs are high, and to get good
turbulence spectra, a good representation of the turbulence must be present in the model [19].
5. Direct Numerical Simulations (DNS) solves turbulence on all scales from the scale of forcing
to the Kolmogorov scale, where dissipation kicks in.
The Equation 3.6 is often translated in
spectral space with Fourier transforms. Due to very high computational costs, applications in
ABL is not achievable at the moment and is of very little interest [19].
CFD has gained popularity for wind energy applications in recent years, including WRA (Wind
Resource Assessment) in complex sites. The use of CFD methods has been used in the planning phase
of the Wind Energy Industry. It is essential to understand the results sensibility to variations of the
setup parameters to build standard analysis procedures. Many industrial tools such as linear and non-
linear models today rely on different parameters and do not always produce accurate results. Linear
models can effectively predict the flow at the upstream side of a hill but cannot account for complex
phenomena, such as flow separation, and are only limited to hills with low slopes [6]. Therefore, many

investigations and research have been devoted to considering the flow phenomena at complex sites
using nonlinear models. However, in a complex terrain, the wind is affected significantly by terrain
features such as elevation and surface roughness. Therefore, proper predictions of flow at a complex
site are of great importance in the wind industry. To minimize the number of field observations, RANS
(Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes) model has been widely used to simulate the topographic effects of
complex terrain [8, 11]. However, the increased computational capacity combined with the need for more
accurate wind flow predictions over complex terrain has made CFD models both practical and necessary.
In the past, researchers have devoted their time to understand the flow behavior at a complex site
using different numerical tools and experimental tests. Various atmospheric boundary layer flow tests
have been conducted in the past few years with a prime focus on hills with gentle slopes upto 17◦[47].
In the early 1980s, Taylor and Teunissen [42, 43] conducted experimental results at Askervein Hill
and analyzed the boundary layer flow. In this study, they performed field experiments to understand
mean wind and turbulence characteristics induced by the hill for different wind directions. Jackson
and Hunt [20] analyzed the effect of shape and size of the hill, including roughness changes that can
influence wind speed and shear stress. Ramechecandane and Gravdahl [32] compared two-equation
turbulence models for flow over three different types of topographies (Cosine Hill, Bolund Hill, and
Askervein Hill). They compared the velocity profile and turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) using other
numerical models with the experimental dataset. Moreira et al. [29] evaluated the RANS equations
using ANSYS CFX 12.1 for simulating the neutral atmospheric boundary layer and attempted to get
the right numerical simulation procedure; these simulations were compared to the experimental data
of Askervein Hill. Uchida et al. [45] studied the airflow over and in the vicinity of a 3-D isolated
hill having a steep slope. In this study, three different CFD packages (WindSim, Meteodyn, WT, and
RIAM-COMPACT) were used, and the results were compared with each other. Furthermore, differences
in the airflow patterns were observed around the isolated hill (downhill) between RANS and LES results.
Castro et al. [14] performed simulations for a neutrally stratified flow over Askervein Hill, using a
terrain following coordinate system and k −ε turbulence model. Different numerical grids were assessed
in this study, considering the turbulence model’s limits and the significance of spatial discretization.
In another study, Kim and Patel [17] simulated different RANS turbulence models and found out
that RNG k −ε turbulence model for steady and unsteady simulations analyzed by Castro et al. [14]
performed well in predicting the mean flow at different grid resolutions. Undheim et al. [46] studied
the mean flow field based on steady k −ε turbulence closure with Coriolis effects taken into account.
Furthermore, they investigated the flow sensitivity for different wind directions, grid resolutions, and
vertical resolution of topographical data.
Silva Lopes et al. [37] performed LES simulations for
Askervein Hill along 210◦wind direction and obtained better results using RANS k-ε model simulated
by Castro et al. They also encountered some limitations computing grid convergence using the LES
approach at the lee side of the hill.
Shen et al. [36] studied the streamline and vertical wind field on a 3-D model hill as well as performed
wind tunnel tests. Furthermore, he compared different parameters with the numerical code. Few studies
have also dealt with steep slopes, such as the Bolund Hill Case. This study was subdivided into two
stages. In the first stage of the experiment Berg et al. [10] performed a measurement campaign at the
Bolund Hill, and different flow features were measured. In the second stage, a blind experiment was
performed by Bechmann et al. [9] where different microscale models, including linear and nonlinear,
were compared with the measurements.
So far, the aforementioned work only dealt with understanding the flow over different hills. Some
research studies have also focused on the parameters that dealt with wind turbine interaction in complex
and flat terrain. Forsting et al. [16] studied the upstream flow field in complex and flat terrain. In
this study, the site modeled was Perdigão (Portugal), and various simulations, including different wind
directions, were performed with a turbine located on one of the two parallel running ridges. Castel-
lani et al. [13] investigated the effects of wakes and terrain-driven flow on wind turbines over complex
terrain located in southern Italy. Sessarego et al. [35] computed simulation of flows in a wind farm
at a complex site located in Shaanxi, China using a RANS solver and LES, and the results were com-

pared with the field measurements. Alfredsson and Segalini [6] discussed the general issues related
to complex terrains, mainly focusing on aerodynamic, meteorological, and noise propagation points of
view. Han and Michael [18] described the process for performing micro-scale RANS simulations over
complex terrain. Furthermore, they studied different parameters influencing the mean wind speeds and

Many different microscale models have been considered for analyzing flow over hills during the past
few years. However, there is a lot of uncertainty present when assessing different parameters. In gen-
eral, several models are being implemented in the wind energy industry, and different models have
various challenges when predicting the flow over complex terrain. A detailed study of various input
parameters is necessary for any given model to analyze the results effectively. Currently, wind energy
relies on different microscale models, and there are no such guidelines on what input parameters the
model should use for predicting the flow over complex sites. On the other hand, different models are
applicable, such as LES, to understand the atmospheric boundary layer. Still, one should also consider
the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy when dealing with terrain flows. As discussed
earlier, many studies have been dealing with simple hills, sinusoidal ridges. Still, only a few studies exist,
focusing on a standardized methodology for assessing complex terrain resources. Most of the studies in
the past have dealt with the validation of microscale models with experimental results. Therefore, this
thesis aims to characterize the different input parameters used in the CFD RANS model effectively,
potentially helping wind engineers make more robust models in the future.
This thesis investigates the systematic study of different input parameters in the CFD RANS model
for predicting flow over a wide range of hills. Furthermore, it helps to understand how sensitive is speed
up with respect to change in input parameters.

1.1
Objectives and Research Questions
This thesis aims to numerically verify the important RANS parameters to contribute to more RANS
industrial use for wind resource assessment (WRA). Moreover, as the airflow over hills and other pa-
rameters such as wind speed and wind direction varies, a careful study of different input parameters
would help the wind industry to understand the effect on speed up at various terrain sites.
In this thesis, several CFD simulations are performed for two different terrain sites to investigate
the behavior of speed up with varying different input parameters such as domain depth, grid resolution,
inner domain depth, grid radius, etc. The outcome of these simulations is to understand the sensitivity
to change of speed up with respect to change in input parameters; thus contributing to more consistency
in predicting future results.
This thesis aims to provide an understanding of the objectives mentioned above by answering the
following questions in two parts :
1.
• What is the effect on speed up when varying grid resolution for a chosen terrain site.
• What is the effect on speed up when varying domain depth for a chosen terrain site.
• What is the effect on speed up when varying inner domain area for a chosen terrain site.
• What is the effect on speed up when varying grid radius for a chosen terrain site.
• What is the effect on speed up when choosing different turbulence models for a chosen terrain
site.
2.
• How sensitive is speed up to varying domain depth and grid resolution for multiple points
considered over different terrain sites?
• How sensitive is speed up to varying eddy viscosity coeﬀicient (Cµ) for complex terrain site?
To answer these questions, the work is presented in several different chapters.
In Chapter 2, a
theory about some of the essential meteorological concepts are discussed. The CFD simulation setup is
described in Chapter 3, understanding the domain specification, turbulence model, RANS solver, etc.
Chapter 4 explains the variation of speed up due to change in different input parameters along a transect
for two different terrain sites. Research Question 1 is answered in section 4.1 and section 4.2. The
findings to answer RQ1 are used to identify important input parameters for statistical and sensitivity
analyses study in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6. In Chapter 5, statistical analyses of a simple hill case
(Askervein hill) and a complex site located in Norway are studied for multiple points over terrain by
considering two input parameters (grid resolution and domain depth). Additionally, a Cµ parameter
is also considered for complex terrain analysis. Finally, Chapter 6 presents sensitivity analyses for the
input parameters considered in Chapter 5.

On the other hand, a suitable site plays a vital role in estimating energy production apart from the wind
farm project’s economic feasibility. In the following sections, few terminologies are discussed, explaining

terrain is generally flat and higher where the terrain is not homogeneous. The change in wind speed
and direction is due to the effect called wind shear. Moreover, the mean wind profile can be represented

2.2
Atmospheric Boundary Layer (ABL)
ABL is the lowest most of the atmosphere, where the wind is directly affected by the Earth’s surface.
Friction is significant in the boundary layer as it is responsible for the magnitude and direction of the
wind in the near-surface, thus impacting wind resources. This frictional force is called a drag force. On
a warm sunny day, the ABL might reach 1 km or more from the ground and is filled with turbulent
structures. On the contrary, ABL might be as shallow as 100-200 m deep during clear nights over land,
resulting in no turbulent structures. This characteristic of increasing and decreasing of ABL during day
and night is due to heat fluxes from the Earth’s surface (land or water) and due to the interaction with
air above the ABL [19].
In ABL, turbulence is generated by wind shear (mechanical forcing) and conventional (thermal forcing).
The first mechanism is because the wind speed should decrease as it approaches the surface. The second
mechanism is buoyancy (thermal effect); In situations like a warm sunny day (unstable conditions), a
relatively warm surface heats the cooler air next to it. The turbulence is enhanced and generated by
the conversion of thermal energy to kinetic energy. The same phenomenon is observed during the night,
but this time the strong cooling of the surface occurs (stable conditions), where the stratification of the
ABL prohibits the development of turbulence [19].
ASL is defined as the bottom part of the ABL, where the vertical fluxes of momentum and heat are
almost constant. In this layer, the wind structure is determined primarily by surface friction and the
surface heat flux (vertical temperature gradient). This layer is usually between 1 m to 200 m and is of
great interest to wind engineers.
In the cases when temperature differences are considered negligible compared to wind shear, the flow is
said to be ’neutral.’ This is the case for high wind speeds, i.e., the mechanical production of turbulence
dominates. The neutral conditions are still the basis for modeling turbulence, and near-surface flow in
wind energy [19].

outside boundary layer [19].

2.3
Topography
As discussed earlier, the wind is strongly influenced by the Earth’s surface when it gets closer to the
ground. Therefore, in this section, some terminologies associated with topography are discussed.
2.3.1
Surface roughness
The wind is affected by varying terrain near the ground; this phenomenon is expressed as the terrain’s
roughness. The roughness of terrain is characterized by the roughness length z0. In general, whenever
the wind flow is encountered, a change in surface roughness, a new wind profile in equilibrium with
the new surface characteristics downstream of the transition is building up. This new transition is
called the internal boundary layer (IBL). This layer is seen when the flow transitions from a rough to
a smooth surface and vice versa, although with different strengths [19]. Figure 2.3 depicts the change
in roughness from the rough surface to the smooth surface. Here, z01 and z02 refers to the upstream
roughness length and downstream roughness length. Also, u∗1 and u∗2 refers to surface friction at the
upstream and downstream sides [19].

2.3.2
Orography
Orography is defined as the difference in terrain elevation [1]. It can be described by height contour
lines. In general the terrain can be classified as flat, hilly, or mountainous.
1. In flat terrain the orographic effects are insignificant and only roughness changes affects the wind
flow [1].
2. In hilly terrain the slopes are less steep than about 0.3 [15]. In this type of terrain, the flow is
mostly attached. A smooth and less steep hill causes acceleration of the wind and accounts for
an increase in wind speeds at the Hilltop [1].
3. In mountainous terrain, the slopes are steeper compared to hilly terrain, and it results in flow
separation. This type of terrain is usually referred to as complex terrain, and usually, the wind
flow is hard to predict and model by linear models [1].
2.3.3
Terrain features
There are different terrain features, that one should know when locating a point on the ground or
navigating from one location to another on a terrain map. Some of the terrain features are discussed
here to clearly understand the various terms used in identifying different features on a map.
• A hill is a point or an area located at a high ground. The ground slopes down when one is located
at the Hilltop [2].
• A ridge is a line of high ground with variation in heights along the crest. In general, all ridge
crest points are higher than the ground on both sides of the ridge [2].
• A Valley is a level ground bordered on the sides of the higher ground. This may or may not
contain a stream course [2].

2.3.4
Speed up
In hilly areas, one may experience that the wind speeds tend to be higher than the surrounding area.
This effect is seen when the wind approaches the hill’s windy side and becomes compressed, known as
the speed up effect. Once the air reaches the ridge, it expands and soars higher down to the low-pressure
area, called the leeward side of a hill. As seen from Figure 2.5, the inflow over a flat uniform surface

due to varying topography and roughness at a site. The wind speeds up is defined as the wind speed
ratio above the hillcrest divided by the wind speed reference at the same local height above the ground
at some distance upstream where the flow is unaffected.

3.1
RANS solver
The physical phenomenon of fluid fluxes and the wind blowing through hills is translated in the Naiver-
Stokes equation’s mathematical form. The equation is used to calculate velocities, pressure, etc., which
appear as a function of space and time. In general, there are different lengths and time scales in a
turbulent flow. In practice, an enormous length scale depends upon the geometry, and the smallest
length scale relies on the size of the dissipative eddies. The shortest time scale of the flow is equal to the
turbulent fluctuation time, and the largest time scales are of the size of the slowly changing mean varia-
tion of the flow [38]. While considering these time scales, the computational mesh has to be suﬀicient to
model the dissipative eddies and, at the same time, cover the entire domain requiring a large number of
cells. On the other hand, to resolve the flow’s time scale, small-time steps are needed to capture the fast
==PAGE:19==
3.2 Governing Equations
12
turbulent fluctuations, and large time steps are required to capture large-scale time variation of the flow.
To ease the process, CFD computer code using the RANS approach has been implemented. RANS
equation solution gives information about the mean wind and turbulent kinetic energy at a particular
interest site. The code is put together in general curvilinear coordinates, which accurately describes
the terrain and uses non staggered grids with values stored at the cell center. In this approach, all
flux unsteadiness is averaged out, and all unsteadiness is regarded as turbulence. On averaging the
Navier-Stokes equations, nonlinearity gives rise to terms that must be modeled as the problem is not
entirely defined. For this reason, CFD code models these terms using the k-ε turbulence model, which
is one of the turbulence model used for terrain flow analysis in this thesis. This turbulence model will
be described in section 3.3 in detail.
For this thesis, the in-house DTU flow solver for incompressible fluids, EllipSys3D, is used to perform
RANS simulations. EllipSys3D is developed in the works of Michelsen [25, 26] and Sørensen [38] and
has been implemented for various wind energy applications such as ABL flows, flow over complex
terrain, and wind turbine rotor computations at DTU Wind Energy. In this work, the Navier-Stokes
equations are solved using the SIMPLE1 algorithm. This algorithm solves the momentum equation
to estimate the velocity field, updates the pressure distribution, and then corrects the velocity field in
an iterative procedure until convergence is reached. The partial differential equations are discretized
using a finite volume method called QUICK2 scheme for the convective terms and a central differencing
scheme. EllipSys3D can be used with Python due to the framework of PyEllipSys and the front end
Python-interface PyWakeRANS.

3.4
Pre processing of terrain map
In PyWake EllipSys, terrain simulations are carried out by providing terrain elevation and roughness,
which can be provided in two ways:
• A single contour file following the .map WAsP format
• A list of elevation and roughness maps using the .grd format
In this thesis, .map files are considered for the terrain sites.
The contour file of elevation and
roughness in the .map is provided using “grid_terrain_map” = < my_map > .map. This contour
map is converted to two gridded maps of elevation and roughness using the .grd format [5]. In the next
step, one of the elevation and roughness maps is assigned to the inner domain and the other one to
the outer domain. The maps for the inner domain are smaller and finer, and for the outer domain, the
maps are larger and coarser. Also, the size of inner maps and the outer maps can be modified by the
user specifying the domain size and horizontal spacing. The default value for the inner map is set to
“grid_terrain_map_Lxy_inner” = 8500 m and “grid_terrain_map_dxy_inner” = 5 m and the outer
map is set to “grid_terrain_map_Lxy_outer” = 42500 m and “grid_terrain_map_dxy_outer” = 25 m.
For complex terrain simulations, the value for the outer map is set to “grid_terrain_map_Lxy_outer”
= 200000 m and “grid_terrain_map_dxy_outer” = 100 m, but these values have to be adjusted
accordingly for the chosen grid radius.
It should be noted that the values of the outer map are different from the WAsP CFD default setup.
In PyWake EllipSys the values of the outer map are set higher because larger grids are required for
wind farm simulations in complex terrain. Moreover, setting higher values means some of the farfield
terrain information is lost in Pywake Ellipsys compared to the WAsP setup [5].

To answer RQ-1 and understand how speed up is affected by varying input parameters, this section is
subdivided into two sections. The first section deals with understanding the effect on speed up when
different input parameters are varied for a simple hill case (Askervein Hill). The second section deals
with the same study but with a complex terrain site located in Norway.
4.1
Simple Terrain
To identify how the RANS model predicts speed up to changing different input parameters, Askervein
Hill is chosen as a starting point. Many researchers have validated the results using this terrain case,
and various numerical models have been compared with the experimental data. Many papers have
also dealt with the atmospheric models of the Askervein Hill test case considering the wind direction
210◦which is aligned along the minor axis shown in Figure 4.1. Two field campaigns were conducted
by Taylor and Teunissen [40, 41] during 1892-1985, which marked the first experimental study
to investigate mean flow profile and turbulence over Askervein Hill. All analyses were considered in
the near-neutral atmosphere, and relevant information was documented in the summarized report of
Akservein Hill Project of 1987 [43]. Some studies dealt with CFD models for understanding different
parameters influencing the flow over an isolated Askervein Hill. Raithby et al. [31] studied the flow
over Askervein Hill with a mesh of 20×20×19 = 7600 cells. In his study, different parameters such as the
effect of topography, domain size, grid resolution, turbulence closure, and terrain boundary conditions
were analyzed. In the upcoming sections, the effect on speed up is analyzed along a transect by varying
different input parameters.
4.1.1
Askervein Hill
Askervein Hill is located near the west coast of South Uist, on the southern end of the Outer Hebrides
island chain. The Hill is elliptical with a 1 km minor axis and 2 km major axis. The major axis is aligned
in the NW-SE direction, and the two minor axis are aligned in the SW-NE direction. In this study,
one of the minor axis passing through the Hilltop (HT) is considered. For this study, wind direction
of 210◦is considered for understanding the speed up. The Hilltop height (H) considered for this case
is 120 m, and the distances from the Hilltop are normalized using H. For the Askervein Hill case, all
simulations presented in the following sections are studied at an elevation height of 10 m above the
terrain surface except for the grid resolution study, where a height of 100 m above the terrain surface
is also considered. As shown in Figure 4.1, the red star represents the Hilltop, and the black line is
the transect considered for this study. The Hill is a bit isolated from the downstream Hill, and for this
study, a roughness value of 0.0002 m and 0.02 m is specified in the terrain map taking into account
the water to land roughness change. The inlet velocity profile is defined by the log law, and the mean
reference wind speed considered is 10 m/s at a reference height of 10 m. The inflow roughness value
considered for this case is zo=0.0002 m, and the rest of the model constants used for this study is given

4.1.2
Grid Resolution
While performing numerical simulations, it is always good to have a sanity check on numerical con-
vergence errors. In this study, the grid convergence error (discretization error) is computed first in
order to see the computational mesh adequacy. To see which grid resolution would be the best to
analyze different input parameters, a mixed-order analysis is performed for three different grid levels
to understand the numerical errors better [30, 34]. The main objective to perform this analysis is to
compare the speed up for different grid resolutions and quantify the discretization error, as mentioned
in Appendix A. Three different grid resolutions are considered for this study,
• 40 m (coarsest)
• 20 m (medium)
• 10 m (finest)
For three different resolutions, the speed up is calculated several distances upstream and downstream
from the Hilltop. The grid convergence study is performed at the height of z = 10 m and z = 100 m
above the terrain surface. For this analysis, distance ranging from −12.5H to 12.5H is considered, and
discretization error is plotted versus the Hilltop distance. Note that the the x-axis is normalized by
the Hilltop height (H=120) as seen from Figure 4.2 and Figure 4.3. Figure 4.2 illustrates that for the
finest resolution, the numerical discretization error is approximately in the range of ±3%. In contrast,
for the medium and the coarsest mesh, the error exceeds ±3% at few locations. At the leeward side of
the Askervein Hill, the discretization error shoots up to 8% for the coarsest mesh and approximately to
3% for the medium mesh. At distances greater than 5H, the error drops approximately to −8% for the
coarsest mesh and −5% for the medium mesh.
In conclusion, the discretization error reduces with an increase in grid resolution. Thus, signifying
that the finest mesh results in low discretization error. Although finest mesh shows a low discretization
error, one should also consider a trade-off between the computational time and accuracy when choosing
a grid resolution.
Similarly, a grid resolution study is also performed at the height of z = 100 m above the terrain
surface. As shown in Figure 4.3 the discretization error for the finest mesh is approximately within
±0.1%, but for 40 m and 20 m grid resolution, the error is in the range of ±0.4% and ±0.2%.

4.1.3
Inner domain area
In this study, the effect on speed up for different inner domain areas is considered. As a starting point,
the inner domain area of 16 km2 is chosen as a reference case for this study. As illustrated in Figure 4.6
three different areas are considered i) 4 km2 ii) 16 km2 and iii) 64 km2 to analyze the variation of speed
up. For three different domain areas, the speed up has a slight variation at different locations from the
Hilltop. Additionally, the actual change in speed up is plotted to understand the variation of speed up
from the reference case.
Figure 4.7 illustrates the actual change in speed up observed for different inner domain areas. The
actual change in speed up between 4 km2 and 16 km2 inner domain area is positive for most locations
at uphill and downhill. This signifies that the speed up is overpredicted for 4 km2 domain area and
the difference in speed up observed is in the range ±1%. On the other hand, the actual difference in
speed up between 64 km2 and 16 km2 is negative at various locations. This signifies that the speed up
is underpredicted for 64 km2 domain area.
Note that addressing the inner domain area issue is quite problematic and is not well understood.
The transition from crude to finer terrain being resolved, and a transition zone within the inner domain
is diﬀicult to analyze. As the cruder (upwind) turbulence becomes erased by the more-resolved flow; the
width and height of the transition zone are diﬀicult to model and characterize and depends on the site.
To understand this parameter, one should consider the distance of the point relative to the inner/outer
domain border and the transition zone’s height. Therefore, for the rest of the study, an inner domain
of 16 km2 is chosen considering a trade-off between accuracy and computational time.

4.1.4
Domain depth
In the model setup, parameters denoted as “_D” are normalized by rotor diameter, as discussed pre-
viously in section 3.7. One such parameter normalized in the model setup is the domain depth. The
domain depth parameter is the target domain height above the terrain surface, set by “grid_zlen_D”.
In general, a suitable domain depth should be considered so that there is no domain blockage,
which would overestimate the results. The domain blockage refers to obstruction of flow, which leads
to an overestimation in results.
For this purpose, different domain depths are analyzed to see the
effect on speed up. Seven different domain depths (0.5 km, 1 km, 2.5 km, 5 km, 10 km, 15 km, and
20 km) are considered, and as a starting reference, a domain depth of 20 km is chosen as a reference case.
The speed up at z = 10 m above the terrain is calculated for different domain depths.
For a
domain depth of 0.5 km, the speed up is overpredicted, and as the domain depth increases, the speed
up converges with slight variations. The overprediction in speed up for lower domain depths is due to
the domain blockage resulting in unphysical acceleration. Therefore a suitable domain depth should be
considered when analyzing flow over hill.

In this section, systematic sensitivity analyses of the input parameters are considered which were dis-

an alternative way to represent speed up as seen in Equation 2.9. The reference speed up here signifies
the speed up at the reference input parameter. In the upcoming sections, the normalized derivatives
of speed up vs speed up reference are plotted, and plots without normalization are also considered for
comparison. Moreover, the approximate derivative using finite differences is carried out to calculate the
non-normalized derivative and normalized derivative. In the following sections, the y axis in the nor-

7.1
Conclusion
In this thesis, different parametric studies were performed for a simple terrain (Askervein Hill) and a
complex terrain case in Norway, considering the flow and turbulence modeled with the RANS and k-ε
equations. In the first part of the study, different input parameters were studied for a single transect
over terrain. For the Askervein Hill case, the optimal input parameters are listed in Table 7.1. The
grid resolution study for simple terrain case showed that a grid resolution of 20 m or 10 m can be
chosen as the discretization error between these two resolutions were in the range ±0.1% at the height
of 100 m. In the inner domain area study, the speed up differences was negligible due to varying inner
domain area. Note that addressing the inner domain area parameter was out of this thesis’s scope, and
therefore 16 km2 was chosen for the simple terrain case study. The domain depth study showed that
no significant difference in speed up was observed for a domain depth greater than 2.5 km. The grid
radius study showed that the speed up was affected if a smaller grid radius was considered. Therefore,
a 50 km grid radius was chosen as a default value in the model setup. Finally, for the turbulence model
study, significant differences in speed up were seen between k-ε and k-ε-fP turbulence model at the
leeward side of the hill. The difference between these two models was approximately 15%.

7.2
Future research
Based on the results seen in this thesis, some suggestions are provided to verify the trends observed for
different input parameters
Consideration of another complex site
To verify the Norway case trends, it is recommended to consider different complex sites and carry out
the same parametric study. Additionally, one should also consider a set of input parameters with equal
intervals to understand the sensitivity of speed up in a better way.
Considering different roughness contours for a terrain site
In the present study, constant roughness was considered for the complex terrain site. One could add
different roughness contours for a chosen site and carry out the same procedure to understand the
effects on speed up in complex terrain.
Implementing different turbulence models and extending the outer domain area
In this thesis, the outer domain margin was set to zero as no study of the wake was considered. One
could implement different turbulence models and extend the outer domain margins to understand the
behavior of speed up at the downhill.
Parametric study of local TI
The effect on local TI by varying different input parameters can be studied using the same approach
considered for analyzing the speed up in this thesis.
