Abstract
The monitoring of offshore wind turbines in terms of structural health is an important
aspect of the maintenance and design veriﬁcation process. Today the structural health indi-
cators are measured using a series of different strategies, although all with great economic
implications. The REMORA project from the Technical University of Denmark proposes
the use of autonomous underwater vehicles for underwater tasks, which could include
monitoring of offshore turbines.
Based on previous work on the subject of using underwater sensor nodes for monitoring
structural health indicators by utilizing AUVs as deployment actor, this project offers a
novel literature search on the subject of underwater sensor nodes, and the design challenges
connected to said sensor nodes, where it is found that a general architecture from terrestrial
sensor nodes can be used although with a focus on improvement in some aspects of the
design process, involving communications, memory and energy aspects.
A sensor node design focused on the main processor, housing, memory, power control
and the use of two sensors; strain and vibration is proposed, and the design speciﬁcations
are accounted for. Based on the said design, a prototype system is produced and is tested
for feasibility in a ﬁnal system. Based on these tests, it can be concluded that the main
design of the system could in fact be implemented into a ﬁnal system, although some
system elements must be completed prior to an application, which include improved power
control, improved strain sensing and the entire communications system.

Resume
Overvågningen af havvindmøller med hensyn til struktur "sundhed" er et vigtigt aspekt i
vedligehold og deisgn veriﬁkationsprocessen. Idag måles struktur sundheds faktorer via en
række strategier, dog alle med stor økonomisk risiko. REMORA projektet fra Danmarks
Tekniske Universitet foreslår brugen af autonome undervandsrobotter til undervandsop-
gaver hvilket også kunne inkludere overvågningen af havvindmøller.
Baseret på tidligere arbejde på emnet omkring brugen af undervand sensor noder til
overvågning af struktur sundheds faktorer ved brugen af undervandsrobotter som fartøj til
opsætninging, giver dette projekt et litteratur studie på emnet omkring undervands sensor
noder hvor der er fundet frem til at den generelle struktur for landbaserede sensorer kan
bruges, dog med en række ændringer i design processen, heri inkluderet kommunikation,
hukommelse og energi aspektet.
Et sensor node design fokuseret på hoved processoren, indkapsling, hukommelse, strøm
kontrol og brugen af to sensorer; stræk og vibrationer bliver foreslået, og design speci-
ﬁkationer bliver foreslået. Baseret på det foreslåede design bliver et prototype system
produceret og testet i sammenligning med et færdigt system. Baseret på disse tests kan
det bliver konkluderet at det overordnede design kan bruges til implementering i et en-
deligt system, dog skal nogle system elementer færdiggøres før system kan endeligt
implementeres, hvilket inkluderer strøm kontrol, forbedret stræk målinger samt hele
kommunikationssystemet.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Within the offshore wind turbine industry structural health monitoring is a key aspect
of design veriﬁcation and maintenance, in which key parameters called structural health
indicators are monitored which can give information on how "healthy" the structure is.
In this project the tower of monopile offshore wind turbines is chosen as case for further
work on a design for a sensor node from the project named "Enabling offshore structural
integrity monitoring by utilizing autonomous underwater vehicles"[29]. The proposed
sensor node design is a part of an overall project called REMORA, in which a swarm of
small autonomous underwater vehicles (AUV) could be used to deploy, service and collect
sensor nodes placed on an oil platform. In this project the proposed design is analysed in
the light of use for structural health monitoring (SHM) of offshore wind turbine (OWT)
towers, and a design is proposed which utilizes relevant sensors and architecture of an
underwater sensor node.
In this project a design is proposed which utilizes vibration and strain measurements for
the monitoring of dangerous resonance frequency movement and accumulated fatigue.
From the proposed design a prototype is made which consists of a mechanical housing
holding electronics which have onboard vibration sensing as well as a concept for strain
sensing, and also including onboard memory, processing unit and power control.
The system has been implemented in a prototype format, and has shown great potential as
a possible application for the REMORA project, although communications and some low
power changes are required before the sensor node could be implemented as a ﬁnal product.
This report is split into a theory section where theory on the subject is given, a deﬁned
project goal section, an analysis and design section where the previous design is analysed
and speciﬁcations are deﬁned, a test and results section where the design is tested by ﬁrst
producing it and secondly testing the produced designs followed by a sectional and ﬁnal
conclusion on the project.

CHAPTER 2
Problem formulation and
delimitation
In order to specify and guide the work a series of problem formulation questions and a
delimitation has been made.
In the offshore wind turbine industry structural health monitoring is an inherent part of
the design qualiﬁcation and maintenance planning, but today a large part of offshore wind
turbines are not monitored mainly due to ﬁnancial reasons.
A previous work on the subject of structural health monitoring suggests that sensor nodes
placed using underwater autonomous vehicles could be used to monitor structural health /
integrity indicators, although further development is needed in order to verify feasibility of
a such system.
For this reason a series of questions could be asked:
• Within the ﬁeld of offshore wind turbines a need for monitoring of speciﬁc health
indicators exist, but which indicators are monitored today and which indicators are
valuable and could be sensed by a sensor node?
• Sensor nodes are known technology and widely implemented in a long range of
applications, but if sensor nodes are to be implemented underwater, what are the
speciﬁc challenges faced? And what are suggested architectures used?
• Based on the previously proposed system design and the found valuable structural
health indicators, what are the challenges in the proposed design, and how could a

4.1
Structural health monitoring
Structural health monitoring (SHM) can be deﬁned as "... the process of in-service damage
detection for aerospace, civil and mechanical engineering object and is a key element of
strategies for condition based maintenance and damage prognosis" [22], which in this
project context is about the monitoring of structural health indicators for the tower structure
of offshore wind turbines. Furthermore gathered data from structures is used to verify
designs in terms of expected stresses and other key features of the speciﬁc design [32]
Due to the fact that accessing and monitoring OWTs is more difﬁcult hence more expen-
sive, the operation and maintenance can cost up to 25% to 30% of the total cost over the
service life of an offshore wind turbine [30], which also shows a great need for cheaper
offshore monitoring systems [22] [30].
Although being a key part of the maintenance and veriﬁcation process, it is not common
for OWTs to be ﬁtted with monitoring equipment [22], which also makes structural health
monitoring a key concern for the scientiﬁc community [30], although newer turbines in
greater numbers are ﬁtted with monitoring equipment.
Besides from directly being an indicator of structural health and / or verifying the design
process, the structural health indicators are used as a managerial tool in order to plan
further investigation of possible failures and maintenance. Although a wast and interesting
subject, the concept of SHM as a managerial tool will not be discussed further in this
report.

4.3.1
Changes in natural frequency of structure
"For an optimal energy output, their components are aligned in such a way that almost per-
manently large static and dynamic stresses arise. In operation, the structure is permanently
excited by dynamic loads, which are mainly caused by the rotation of the blades." [32].
This constant dynamic load also poses one of the largest threads to the structure, as the
structure is then constantly excited by this dynamic loading which might coincide with the
natural frequency of the tower, which could leads to excessive fatigue [15].
The natural frequency of a wind turbine tower can, to a certain degree, be described by the
formula for the cantilever beam [9] (in a single degree of freedom):

Where k is the beam stiffness and m the tower mass, hence if stiffness or mass changes

Reasons for changes in natural frequency
A series of different factors can inﬂuence the natural frequency of the turbine tower as a
whole:
Design
Great focus is paid to the subject of designing the towers correctly in terms of natural
frequency, especially of the ﬁrst natural frequency (described in greater detail later on),
but changes from design to reality has been showed. In a case from the Walney site [10]
the actual ﬁrst order natural frequency was found to be 6-7% higher than the designed.
Marine growth
During submersion into seawater marine growth, including mussels and corals, is almost
inevitable [21]. This affects the natural frequency as per the equation above by increasing
the total mass without increasing the stiffness of the structure.
Aside from the direct impact on the natural frequency, increased marine growth also
impacts the wave loading by adding diameter to the structure, as well as in increasing
surface roughness which then decreases the ﬂow stability, hence increases wave loading
by unpredictable behaviour [21].
Scour
Scour, which is the process of sediment and soil being removed from the base of the
structure by high velocity water, poses a great threat to the structure integrity, and is known
to lower the natural frequency of the tower [3].
Current measures are normally in place for protecting against scour, but keeps on being a
subject of study.

Rotational frequencies
The dynamic load containing the largest risk of excitation is the rotational frequency of
the blades, known as 1P (or f1p), and the blade shadowing effect, known as 3P (or f3p),
both due to their close proximity to the natural frequency in most monopile structures[32].
The 1P frequency is a spectrum on multispeed turbines, which is the case for the large part
of off-shore turbines and lies in the area of 0.115 Hz to 0.2 Hz [15].
The 3P spectrum is three times the spectrum of the 1P spectrum on a three bladed turbine,
which the large part of turbines are [32], and is caused by the blades of the turbine passing
in front of the turbine tower, causes a temporary loss of wind on the tower. Due to this
mechanical connection, the 3P spectrum is simply the frequency band limits for 1P multi-
plied by three (on a 3 bladed turbine).

In the design of the structure, one might think that it would be wisest to place the natural

the fact that the avoidance of resonant frequency will probably maintain to be a matter of
interest in the future of structural health monitoring.

The different system parts can be identiﬁed as:
Processing unit / controller
Processing unit responsible for scheduling tasks for the system as well as interfacing and
controlling other system components.
Furthermore the controller is responsible for reading out data from sensor, saving to
memory and managing power modes. In order to save energy, the controller often operates
in low power modes in order to save power, as well as operating low power modes for
sensors and memory[8].
For reading the sensory data, the controller, in this conceptual context, also holds the
possibility for sampling using internal ADCs, as well as interfaces for digital sensors.
Lastly the controller can also hold real time clock (RTC) abilities in order to schedule and
tag measured data.
Memory
Sensor node memory used for storing data and data results between transfer of data. Most
common technologies are FLASH, EEPROM and RAM. [36].
Power supply / energy provider
For the sensor node to operate, either batteries or energy providing units are used to provide
energy for the sensor node to operate. As also mentioned later, energy is a key aspect in
underwater sensor nodes, and thus the both supply and management of power is of key
interest to the design of underwater sensor nodes.

4.4.2
Challenges in underwater sensor system vs. on-ground sensor
systems
Literature generally lists a series of design challenges with underwater sensor nodes com-
pared to on-ground / terrestrial sensor nodes.
Communication
Wireless communication for sensor nodes without the possibility of tethering is a key
aspect of the design process, as many of the commonly used wireless technologies are not
feasible in standard form underwater.
Most commonly employed is acoustic communication which employes the use of acoustic
waves to transfer information. A challenge with this form of communication is that
it experiences heavy attenuation, including path losses, geometric spreading and multi
pathing [8] [13]. As this is not part of the focus for this project, this will not be discussed
although being a waste and very important subject for the design of underwater sensor
nodes.
Power
Mainly originating in higher needs for power for communication, power is a challenge to
underwater sensor nodes. Some projects have looked at the opportunity to produce energy
==PAGE:36==
18
CHAPTER 4. THEORY
in sensor nodes (so called energy harvesting), although this does not seem to be widely
used nor implemented [26].
Memory
Also tightly connected to the communication challenges, the memory capabilities of
underwater sensor nodes can sometimes be more strict [8], as the periods between data
dumps can be longer in duration.
Deployment
Being a key interest to the REMORA project, the deployment is often a larger challenge
than terrestrial sensor node deployment, as the underwater environment is harsh [8] [36].
The deployment in most cases consists of boy-like deployment, where the sensor nodes
are deployed from boat or alike, and they either sink to the bottom where they are held by
weights, or stay at the top of the water level and ﬂoat like a boy.
As shown later, this project uses a slightly different approach, where the sensor nodes have
to be deployed and collected, this further increases the challenges of deployment of the
sensor nodes.
Housing
As mentioned earlier, the environment of the sea is harsh due to high salinity which causes
increased corrosion. This together with marine growth and possibly high pressure at
depths, makes the design of underwater sensor nodes housings a key challenge to the
design process.

CHAPTER 5
Project goal
Based on the described motivation for an application for the REMORA project, as well
as the need for SHM of offshore wind turbines, the goal of this project is to analyse the
possible architecture and components of an underwater sensor node based on the previous
work presented. Further more the goal is to propose a design for a speciﬁc implementation
based on the said analysis, test these designs through an implementation into a real world
prototype which can verify the feasibility of a such system.
As mentioned, not all parts of a such system is of interest to this project due to time and
manpower restrictions, and as so the following parts will be in the scope of this project:
• Design, implement and test of an electronic platform for an underwater sensor node
system, including power input, processing unit, memory and sensors. Thus not
including communications, interfacing to AUVs and low power design..
• Analysis, design and test of ﬁrmware for a such system, which can interface to
sensors, memory and power system.
• Identifying the "lowest hanging fruits" in terms of sensors which could be use-
ful for the analysed case and be implemented into a such system, as well as the
implementation and testing of said sensory systems.
• Propose mechanical design based on previous work which can house said electronic
platform and sensory system.
On an overall level, it is the goal to move further with the developed concepts from previous
work on the work with underwater sensor nodes in REMORA [29], and in that context to
implement and test key components of a such system in order to determine the feasibility
in moving forward with the speciﬁc project.

6.2.1
Communications strategy
Although the communication aspect is not part of the scope of this project, a key design
aspect is how and why the sensor node should communicate with the AUVs, which also
inﬂuences a series of other design decisions. Therefore an overall communication strategy
is chosen and described.
A key aspect to the communication strategy revolves around the limitation of underwa-
ter communications. The communicaiton could potentially be either wired or wireless,
but due to high loads on wired solutions [22], this is not practical. Therefore only a
wireless solution resides, where the main limiting factor is the highly limited range, due
to the attenuation of electro magnetic waves normally used in wireless communication [22].
Basically two different communication strategies could be implemented based on the
above limitations; poll-based and event-based.
The poll-based strategy relies on a scheduled read-out of data from the sensor node to the
AUV. This scheme would allow for the sensor node, if no other requirements are present,
to only implement near-range communication technology. The limitation of such a scheme
is that if a danger scenario occurs between read-outs, no action can be taken, and slow
==PAGE:50==
32
CHAPTER 6. ANALYSIS AND DESIGN
response time is imposed.
An event-based strategy on the other hand constitutes a long range communication for
alerting in the case of danger scenarios. In such a case an AUV could approach the sensor
node and read out the data in a near vicinity. The disadvantage of this strategy is that a
more complex communication system is needed. The advantage on the other hand is that a
possible danger scenario could be reacted to relatively fast.
The two scenarios are depicted in appendix C.
For the current implementation the event-based strategy is chosen, as it offers a more
dynamic use of the sensor nodes, not only in the case of SHM on off-shore wind turbines.
This architechture is also mentioned in [24] as a potential network methodology, where a
so called "super node" acts as a relay to an on-shore data central, just as described above.
Furthermore there is an interesting case in the area of underwater localization using ﬁxed
point acoustic beacons, which could potentially be interesting for the REMORA project.
6.2.2
Local danger scenario detection
As described above, the system relies on being able to calculate and detect possible danger
scenarios if the event-based communication strategy is used. This means that the sensor
node must be able to reliably calculate and determine when a certain sensed value or a
derivative thereof is outside a certain limit.
The actual required sensor resolution for this to be true is mentioned in the following
sections on sensor requirements.
6.2.3
Data sample rate
The rate at which data is gathered on an overall level is dependent on the rate at which the
indicators of SHM change, as well as the desired time-resolution.
In described measurement campaigns [32], measurements are taken with an estimated
resolution of 0.1 day, which roughly translates to a full sensor scan and calculation every
5000 seconds, which will form the basis for analysis for this project.

6.3.1
Mechanics
From the description of the former proposed system, a series of points in the former design
have been identiﬁed as being problematic, and thus would have to be addressed in order to
fulﬁl an implementation in a prototype.
1. Water prooﬁng / sealing
The connection of the upper and lower parts of the sensor node are made up of screw
connections in the inner part of the sensor node. This type of connection is difﬁcult
to waterproof, because a seal would need to be mounted on the inner side of these
screws, which is not possible in the design.
Further more, deﬂection in the outer ring, where a connection groove is made, has
no connection points or screws, which poses a threat to the possibility of making the
overall structure stable and waterproof.
2. Place for electronics
In the current design there are no place for electronics inside the upper part of the
==PAGE:58==
40

sensor node. The EPM part ((9) on ﬁgure 4.15) holds no electronics, and thus there
is a need for physical space to place the electronics needed.
3. Complex sensor interface to structure
As described, the sensor core holding the actual sensor is a separate unit residing
inside the collar / adapter which makes changing the sensor for a speciﬁc purpose a
complex operation. Further more this design relies on transferring energy and data
wireless using electromagnet waves between the sensor node and the sensor core,
which further increases the complexity of the system and introduces transmission
losses mainly in the power transfer system.
4. Complex sensor node design
The design of the sensor node has a series of curvatures and complex surfaces which
increases production complexity mainly in terms of produceability. This could easily
be solved by utilizing a more "edged" design, with less curved surfaces.

quality of the structure as it introduces new possible weak points [29]. The restrictions
include all forms of intrusive measures; weldings, bolting, holes or other measures that
might change the structure. This was also one of the major points in previous works on the
REMORA project, which is deemed not applicable as shall be described later.
Current methods
A series of different methods are applied today for the case of strain sensing of surfaces.
The most applied method for measuring strain is to use strain gauges mounted directly
to the structure [33]. This unfortunately requires a clean smooth surface, which can not
be expected in the current case, and thus would not directly be applicable to the current case.
Another well established surface strain sensing method which can be applied to the course
surface of a specimen, is to apply a secondary strain bar where the actual fragile strain
gauge or FBG sensor is then mounted. An example can be seen below:

Several such systems exist [6] [7], which all have the disadvantage in the current setting
that they are required to have either welding, gluing or bolting to the surface. This causes
some challenges to the proposed system because this is not possible in the case of an
underwater setting and OWTs.
For the actual sensor, a series of different sensor types exist. One of them is a light based
Fiber Bragg Grating sensors, which uses light to detect changes to light ﬁber. This method
has been applied in SHM on OWTs [30] but as the method is still quite new, and the signal
condition very complex [2], it is not suitable for implementation in a small system like the
proposed.
Other implementations are vibrating wire, where the vibration frequency of a wire excited
by a magnetic ﬁeld, and is dependent of the tension it experiences, an application where
the instrumentation is a little more complex than a traditional foil gauge strain gauge.
The last option is the mentioned foil gauge strain gauge, which is a common strain sensing
method, based on the elongation and compression of a metal foil. From the options the last
two mentioned are deemed the ones most suitable for this application, and for a prototype
the metal foil strain gauge is chosen for implementation, due to the good availability and
ease of implementation.

has been chosen. Due to the mechanical limitations, only a quarter-bridge implementation
can be utilized, due to the fact that the sensor can only be stressed in one direction, and
not both. As the case allows it, a quarter-bridge strain gauge circuit with temperature
compensation is then used.

The next parts of the sensor chain, is the ampliﬁcation and anti-aliasing ﬁltering. For

a dynamic solution, where the ampliﬁcation can be easily set in the ﬁnal solution and
allowing for the microController to dynamically compensate for mechanical changes
which might offset the sensor, thus utilizing the entire dynamic range of the internal ADC.

which means that the system states terminates in a sleep process where the main con-

draw, and is "woken up" on the basis of an external or internal interrupt. The interrupt

cycle, where, based on a time interval, the sampling of sensors starts. In the proposed
system, the samplings / triggers are then be performed by a trigger running on an other
clock than the main controller, thus enable the microController to sleep during most of
sensor the measurement cycle. Once a sensor channel has been sampled, the data is loaded
to a local dataspace, in this case mainly the FFT dataspace, and hence analysed (in this
schema, FFT is taken as primary). The derived data is saved to external memory, and a
household analysis is made, consisting of checking sensor integrity, battery status and
communications in a future version. If necessary during the sensor data analysis, an alert

Lastly, a data dump could be performed, in which the local memory is read out in some,
yet unknown, form, after which the local memory can be free for use to other purposes.

from the datasheet, and consists of a 3 order of magnitude system of ceramic capacitors
starting from 1µF to 0.01µF placed close to the 4 power connections on the controller.
As can be seen, the design uses two external crystal oscillator circuits, one with a nominal
frequency of 32.768 kHz and an other with 7.3725 MHz. This double oscillator design
has been chosen to allow for the main processor to perform sleep with the main oscillator
(7.3725 MHz) turned off, but with the low power slow oscillator still running to facilitate
RTC functionality, which is used as a wakeup interrupt source for controlling when
measurement cycles are running.
Lastly a programming / debugging connection is made as a line header array, onto which
the programmer can be attached, as well as a uart interface for debugging and readout of
information.
Memory
The memory chip (M45PE16) is connected to the SPI communication bus including a
digital chip select (CS)line, as well as write enable digital line and a reset digital line.
The chip is decoupled according to design recommendations in datasheet with 3 ceramic
capacitors; 1 1µF and 2 18pF. The memory chip functions as well directly from the
onboard 3.3V power line.
Accelerometer
The chosen accelerometer chip (ADXL362) also functions directly from the on board 3.3V
power line, and is decoupled close to power terminals with 3 ceramic capacitors as per
recommendations in data sheet. Besides also being connecting to the SPI bus with standard
pins (DI, DO and CS), the chip also has a 2 way interrupt functionality, one (INT1) for
being able to sample and convert vibration data on an external signal, which is needed
for the chip to perform at precise and speciﬁc intervals, and an other (INT2) to perform
interrupts to the main controller to indicate when the internal memory has reached a certain
level and thus is ready to be emptied.
Strain sensing
The chosen AD8555 programmable ampliﬁcation and zero drift chip is connected both
directly to the quarter bridge strain sensing circuit and a analog capability input on the
main controller, as well as a series of external power / programming headers.
The quarter bridge sensing circuit is powered directly from the on board 3.3V power line,
which the main controller analog reference is as well. In order to minimize switching noise
from the digital circuitry other places on the board, the ground plane for this analog part
==PAGE:80==
62
CHAPTER 7. TEST AND TEST RESULTS
has been separated up to the power connection, where it is heavily decoupled, which can
be seen in the bottom copper pour of the board layout.
An internal anti-aliasing ﬁlter capability is used, which is utilized by an external capacitor.
The external capacitor acts as part of a simple low pass ﬁlter, although without a speciﬁc
transfer function the diagram shows a 20dB attenuation per decade, which leads to the
conclusion that it is a ﬁrst order low pass ﬁlter. With a 100nF capacitor, this leads to a
cutoff frequency of 1 kHz, which is chosen relatively high as little noise is expected in the
low frequency area hence the risk of aliasing is deemed low.
The chip is made such that the gain can be digitally programmed and irreversibly burned
into the chip with a 5V power connection. For this purpose an external programming and
power connection can be connected by setting a series of jumpers and using a series of
headers. The concept is the system is assembled, after which the gain is adjusted to meet
the speciﬁc needs for dynamic range and resolution, after which the setting is burned to
the chip after which the chip is used as a normal component in the system.
The output of the chip i purely analog, and is connected directly to a main controller ADC
input pin.
As seen, there are two separate strain sensing networks, which is because the system design
is supposed to service two axis of strain sensing. Both strain connections are made as line
pin array headers for external connection in the sensor node casing.
Permanent electromagnets control
For controlling the engaging and disengaging of the sensor node from the collar, control of
the permanent electromagnets is needed. The magnets are controlled using a 12V supply,
acting as a second power supply on the board, which is controlled using a n-channel
MOSFET (TSM2314CX). The setup is based on a digital output from the main controller
turning the MOSFET on and off through an in-rush current controlling resistor. The
MOSFETs source is connected to ground and the drain to the electro magnet. When a
voltage above the threshold level is applied to the gate on the MOSFET, the electro magnet
is conducting and thus working. Further more a ﬂywheel diode is used across the electro
magnet output to protect the MOSFET from the stored energy in the inductor in the electric
magnet.

at least to a minimal level and it is estimated to hold true to larger depths of up to 10 m.
Further more, the tested design has shown to function at an overall attachment level in
the sense that it can connect to the collar and be held in place during operation, although
problems regarding rotational ﬁxing and water escape must be optimized.
With regards to the design of the strain sensor, two major faults were found in the design;
deﬂection of the sensor membrane due to misalignment of the magnets and the need for a
lowering of the detachment force when the sensor node needs to be removed, which could
be implemented using electro permanent magnets.

In a general sense the ﬁrmware functions by waking from sleep on an interrupt, ﬁnding out
why sleep was interrupted and then acting accordingly. For this reason a key part of the
ﬁrmware is the check start reason function in which it is determined the cause of interrupt.
This function uses a series of interrupt ﬂags set by interrupt service routines to determine

7.3.2
FFT
The used chip dsPIC30F6014A is specially designed for implementation of a Radix 2 FFT
algorithm in which summation of arrays is optimized using MAC (multiply-accumulate)
functionality and dual data fetch operations. The actual implementation in software
requires that a special part of program memory known as x-data and y-data is used to store
the signal vector which will also hold the DFT samples after the algorithm has run.
The implementation of the algorithm and use of functions is poorly documented, and hence
a lot of work went into getting the functions etc. to work.
The complex array in which the signal data is stored is deﬁned as typedef with two
fractional arrays; one real and one imaginary part for each index. From the h ﬁle the
following can be seen:
Figure 7.10: Complex signal typedef.
As seen the typedef ﬁelds are deﬁned as fractional, which is deﬁned as a 1.15 fractional
number between -1 and +1. During operation it is needed for the input signal to be within
-0.5 and +0.5, which is performed by a scaling to ±1 and then shifting data one step to
the right. It is important that the data be scaled to the ±1 range before calculations as the
calculated frequency samples otherwise suffer from rounding errors.
The initialization of the data space is performed with a macro shown below:
Figure 7.11: Initialization of signal complex vector in y-space.
which is needed in order to implement complex vectors and to implement large arrays in
y-space.

When the actual FFT is performed the "FFTComplexIP" function is used on the complex
vector along with an equal size twiddle-factor vector containing the pre-calculated twiddle-
factors for each frequency point deﬁned in x-space.
After this call the frequency samples are placed in the same array, and the samples can be
read after a power function is used on the samples in order to go from magnitude to power
of the samples.
As shown in the section on danger scenario detection, the FFT calculations suffer a bit from
limited precision calculations, but the overall relative calculations of frequency sample
correspond to calculations performed in Matlab, and thus the FFT algorithm seems to be
working according expectations.
Further more the implementation of the FFT algorithm only allowed for a sample length
of 512, although a sample length of 1024 samples should be possible in the 2048 x and
y dataspace. The reason for this is still unknown, but it was not possible to ﬁx the error
leading to a small sample length and hence the FFT resolution suffered slightly.
7.3.3
Sleep and wake
Two major modes are used during program ﬂow; running and deepsleep. Either the pro-
cessor runs and performs commands on the main crystal oscillator, or the processor is in
deepsleep in which a timer (Timer3) is running off an external low-power 32 kHz crystal
oscillator to provide RTC functionality. This timer runs at all times, and thus provides an
RTC functionality independent of the main processor running.
On the speciﬁc microController all interrupts which are enabled during deepsleep causes
the processor to wake up and continue operation from the place it went into deepsleep
mode.
When tested, the system does initialize and functions. Deepsleep also work according
to speciﬁcations, which means that the processor goes into deepsleep mode and wakes
up every 1 second to update the RTC and at speciﬁed time increments perform sensor
readings and data analysis.
7.3.4
Sensors
Strain sensor
The strain sensor is interfaced through the internal ADC which is a 16 channel 12 bit ADC
with up to 200 ksps. In the implementation used for testing, the ADC is set to sample at
speciﬁc interval using a timer interrupt which "manually" ﬁres a sample and hold and start
conversion command to the ADC module. When 16 conversions have completed an other
==PAGE:92==
74
CHAPTER 7. TEST AND TEST RESULTS
interrupt is ﬁred which loads data from the ADC FIFO butter into program memory for
signal analysis.
Accelerometer
The accelerometer is interfaced using an SPI bus and two interrupts; data ready and external
trigger. The speciﬁc accelerometer did not offer precise timing in the sampling sequence,
and hence for time critical operation like the FFT, a timer interrupt is implemented which
triggers the accelerometer externally to ﬁre a sample.
Up to 256 samples can be stored internally in the accelerometer FIFO memory, and a
future implementation could use the connected interrupt signal to initialize the dump of
data from the accelerometer in order to save time on the overhead of data transfer.
In the tested version, data is read every 800 ms and loaded into local memory over the SPI
bus.
7.3.5
Memory
The ﬂash memory used is connected to the SPI bus, the same as the accelerometer and is
controlled through SPI commands and two logic lines; write and reset.
Use of the reset and write logic lines can protect from accidental write or erasing of data
to the memory, and hence these are set according to wanted action.
Code has been written which can interface to the memory, and it has been tested that
memory is stored and can be retrieved on the memory chip, also across memory pages.
Due to time restrictions, it was not possible to implement an actual memory mapping with
actual data being saved and retrieved over the course of several measurement cycles.
7.3.6
Conclusion
Main functionality of the sensor node ﬁrmware has been proven to work. The overall ﬂow
of the program is running and the program runs through the different states without issues.
Each of the different parts on the board; memory and sensors are working and data can be
read and stored in memory.
With the overall function of the program ready, the project could easily be taken further by
a future project which could implement the missing parts or even implement functions for
other sensor or danger scenario detecting algorithms.

Conclusion
In this project and the previous work presented a clear need for automated structural health
monitoring is presented. Already implemented today, monitoring of structural health
indicators is an important part of the design veriﬁcation and maintenance planning process
but problems with high cost means that a low number of offshore wind turbines today are
ﬁtted with monitoring equipment.
It has been shown that the most important / implementable factors to monitor are vibration
of the tower structure in terms of vibration frequency spectrums, which can be used to
determine structural integrity changes, including scour, marine growth and fatigue in the
tower structure. Another important factor is strain measurement, in which historical data
on size and frequency of strain can be used as well.
Based on the overall structure of previous work on this project, a sensor node platform has
been developed which could potentially be deployed using AUVs at target locations, which
could lower the overall monitoring cost as well as providing semi real time monitoring
of offshore wind turbines. Key components, including power input, controller, sensory
system, memory and housing, of a sensor node platform have been designed, produced
and tested.
Based on the tests which were performed on the different system parts it can be concluded
that the vibration sensor system on a real life wind turbine has the required sensitivity to
determine the frequency spectrum reliably, and also the danger scenario detection shows
great potential in terms of determining frequencies of interest. The strain sensing concept
has shown potential for implementation, although work is required for a full implementa-
tion.
The overall platform including electronics with processor, power, memory and housing
has shown great potential and has passed tests for functionality and feasibility.

Future work
This project has proven the feasibility of some core components of a sensor node design,
although it is clear that further work is needed before a sensor node like this could be
implemented into a real world scenario.
Electronics system
The electronics have been designed with the possibility of adding an extension pcb to the
extension port located in the revision B of the hardware. This opens for the opportunity of
adding more sensors or other functionality such as communications.
In order for the electronics to be implemented into a production ready product, the elec-

with product standards (CE, FFC or alike).
Firmware
Also mentioned as a key part of a sensor node design is communications, and hence an
implementation of a communications technology and strategy is needed for ﬁnal sensor
node design.
Furthermore, a memory mapping is to be implemented before the system can operate
deployed.
Mechanical components
For the sensor node to be serviceable by the AUVs in the REMORA project, it would be
needed to develop a mechanical interface which could be used to deploy sensor nodes.
Further work is needed in the docking procedure / mechanics, as the current setup only
involves a simple working concept. Further design work on the mechanical locking and
holding mechanism is needed before the sensor node is ﬁrmly mounted in the collar.
Further testing is needed to fully verify the design of the sensor node housing on lower
water depths to verify the waterprooﬁng and general strength of the design.

Appendix H - Different offshore turbine structures
Monopiles
Figure .10: Depiction of monopile structure from [12]
==PAGE:125==
.8. APPENDIX H - DIFFERENT OFFSHORE TURBINE STRUCTURES
107
The monopile structure as depicted above consists of a single pile, hence the name
monopile. Shown below in greater detail, it can be seen that the structure consists of a
sub-ground structure embedded into the seabed, onto which a transition piece is mounted
around sealevel onto which the actual turbine tower is mounted.
Figure .11: More detailed view of typical monopile structure, from [28]
As also seen on ﬁgure .10 and .11 a large part of the structure is also the scour protection
which is also an inherent part of all the structure types.
The main part of the monopile structures are made of steel (around 80%) [19] whilst
around 20% are made of enforced concrete.
Placement methods for monopiles include driving down the monopile by pile-driving
hammer, but other methods are used (which will not be discussed further in this report).
==PAGE:126==
108
CHAPTER 9. FUTURE WORK
Gravity based structures
Figure .12: Depiction of gravity based structure, from [12]
As the name implies, the gravity based structure type is based on a dead load placed on the
seabed. This structure type has the advantage of leasening the uplift forces on the structure
[12].
The structures are normally produced in building yards and transported to location, where
they are ﬁlled with material to weigh down the structure [12].
These structures are often used when enviromental loading are smaller, and also have the
ability to be more suitabel on higher water depths than the monopile structures, as also
mentioned later [19].
Space frame structures
Figure .13: Depiction of space frame structures, from [12]
==PAGE:127==
.8. APPENDIX H - DIFFERENT OFFSHORE TURBINE STRUCTURES
109
A series of different space frame structures exist mainly for deeper locations. These types
are mainly used at high water depths, and the concepts tries to improve the mass to stifness
ratio by utilizing several smaller structures with a distance from the main structure [12].
Floating structures
Figure .14: Depiction of a series of different ﬂoating structures, from [12]
Not yet as proven and implemented as the other structure types are a series of ﬂoating
structures.
Specially design for very high water depths, these structures are build to ﬂoat, manly while
ankered to the seabed. Whilst having the beneﬁt of lower cost in regards of installation
and decommission, the structure type implies a more complex design and electrical
infrastructure [12].
