Abstract
This master’s thesis, titled ”AGENT-BASED MODELING OF THE NORWEGIAN
OFFSHORE WIND SUPPLY CHAIN,” investigates Norway’s critical energy transition
towards floating offshore wind (FOW) in light of ambitious national targets. The nation
faces a strategic challenge: whether to leverage the established, capital-rich European
supply chain or to foster a dedicated local Norwegian one to enhance resilience and create
domestic jobs.
The primary objective of this research is to investigate the economic, logistical, and
strategic implications of these two supply chain approaches. To accomplish this, an agent-
based model (ABM) was developed. ABM was chosen as the optimal methodology due to
its ability to capture complex, emergent behaviors from decentralized interactions, model
agent heterogeneity, and incorporate dynamic stochastic elements such as weather, which
are critical for offshore operations.
The simulation tool is built upon the open-source MESA framework in Python, aug-
mented by specialized libraries for geospatial data and environmental modeling.
The
model architecture includes a central offshore wind market class and five key mesa.Agent
classes: ComponentAgent, SupplierAgent, VesselAgent, MarshalingPort, and WindFar-
mAgent. Agents are parameterized using publicly available data and expert consultations,
covering supplier capacities, production rates, and logistical constraints. Critical environ-
mental data from the Copernicus Data Center is dynamically integrated to assess suitable
weather windows for offshore Transport & Installation operations.
The study simulates two distinct supply chain configurations—an Existing European
Supply Chain (components fabricated across Europe, consolidated in Norway) and an
assumed local Norwegian Supply Chain for both small-scale (six 14MW turbines) and
gigawatt-scale (sixty 14MW turbines) wind farms. The Utsira Nord offshore wind farm
serves as the reference site.

Introduction
Norway, a nation historically dependent on hydropower and oil and gas production, is now
strategically navigating a complex energy transition, driven by both environmental impera-
tives and economic diversification goals. This transition involves a multifaceted approach that
incorporates ambitious emission reduction targets, electrification of various sectors, and sig-
nificant investments in renewable energy sources [1]. Offshore wind energy has emerged as a
key component of this transition, which holds substantial promise to meet Norway’s growing
electricity demand and strengthen its position as a leader in renewable energy technologies [2].
The strategic development of marine energy clusters, promoted through robust governmental
support and policies, exemplifies an ideal model to expand industrial competitiveness within
the marine sector [3]. Norway’s energy goals are ambitious, aiming to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions significantly by 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, aligning with interna-
tional climate agreements and commitments. These goals require a fundamental change in the
national energy mix, moving away from fossil fuels and towards sustainable alternatives. This
transition requires a significant change in its energy landscape, with an ambitious target of gen-
erating 390 TWh of renewable energy by 2050, primarily through the large-scale deployment
of offshore wind [4]. However, realizing this vision is contingent upon overcoming substantial
grid expansion challenges, particularly given the projected waning of Norway’s power surplus
by 2026 and existing local power deficits [5].
Currently, Norway’s energy mix is dominated by hydropower, which represents a substantial
portion of its electricity generation. The nation also has significant oil and gas reserves, making
it a major exporter of these resources, but this reliance on fossil fuels poses a challenge to its
climate goals [6].
The energy transition is further complicated by the country’s extensive
coastline and access to vast offshore areas, presenting both opportunities and challenges for
developing offshore wind capacity. To facilitate the energy transition, Norway has implemented
a variety of policies and support mechanisms, including tax incentives, subsidies, and regulatory
frameworks designed to promote the development of renewable energy. Governmental support
is particularly strong for offshore wind, recognizing its potential to contribute significantly
to the nation’s energy supply and create new industrial opportunities. The nation’s energy
policies are also influenced by its membership in the European Economic Area, which requires
alignment with the EU energy directives and targets[7].
Offshore wind energy is poised to play a transformative role in Norway’s energy transition by
providing a clean, reliable, and scalable source of electricity. The waters surrounding Norway
offer a major potential source of electricity supply from offshore wind energy [8]. Norway’s
extensive coastline and favorable wind conditions make it ideally suited for offshore wind devel-
opment. Floating wind technology, in particular, holds immense promise for Norway, allowing
for the exploitation of deeper waters and stronger wind resources further offshore. The de-
velopment of offshore wind farms can also create new jobs and stimulate economic growth in
coastal communities. Offshore wind sites exhibit substantially higher mean wind speeds and
reduced levels of turbulence, implying higher power production and lower fatigue loads [9]. The
deployment of offshore wind farms requires significant investments in infrastructure, including
wind turbines, foundations, subsea cables, and grid connections. The average capacity rating
of installed offshore wind turbines is around 10-12 MW [10] and continuously increasing. In
addition, eﬀicient and reliable electrical infrastructure is essential for transmitting power from
offshore wind farms to the mainland grid. Investments in offshore wind farms have increased
substantially due to their potential as a reliable source of renewable energy, particularly in
locations with consistent wind resources [11].
Norway embarked on its journey into offshore wind energy around 2005, driven by a national
commitment to renewable energy sources and the imperative to reduce carbon emissions. A
7
==PAGE:10==
significant early step was the development and deployment of the first MW-class pilot project,
Hywind Demo (later renamed Unitech Zefyros), by Statoil (now Equinor) in September 2009
[12]. This pioneering spar-type substructure, anchored in 210 meters of water off the coast of
Karmøy in the North Sea, supported a 2.3 MW Siemens SWT-2.3-82 wind turbine, marking
Norway’s initial step in harnessing offshore wind resources.
Subsequently, the Norwegian government has actively begun to shape a clearer regulatory
landscape to foster investment and stimulate research within the offshore wind sector [13].
The energy authorities issued guidelines and policies designed to encourage and facilitate off-
shore wind projects. Recognizing its established maritime and oil and gas expertise, Norway
strategically increased investments in research and development, particularly focusing on the
advancement of floating wind technology, which holds significant potential for its deep coastal
waters.
The successful realization of these ambitious offshore wind projects, particularly complex
floating installations, depends critically on the development of a robust and eﬀicient supply
chain [14]. Unlike traditional onshore or fixed-bottom offshore wind, floating offshore wind in-
troduces unique logistical and technical challenges, from the fabrication and assembly of massive
floating platforms to the specialized maritime operations required for towing and installation
in deep waters. A well-functioning supply chain, encompassing everything from raw material
procurement, component manufacturing, port infrastructure, specialized vessels, and skilled
labor, is paramount to ensuring project viability, cost-effectiveness, and timely delivery. Given
Norway’s established maritime and oil and gas industries, there exists a significant opportunity
to leverage existing expertise and infrastructure, adapting it to the specific demands of floating
offshore wind. However, this also necessitates strategic investments in new capabilities and
fostering collaboration across diverse industries to build a globally competitive supply chain
capable of supporting the large-scale deployment required for Norway’s energy transition goals.
1.1
Background and motivation
Norway’s Offshore Wind Vision 2050 [15] articulates the nation’s strategic ambition to become
a prominent player in the global offshore wind market, emphasizing sustainable growth aligned
with national energy and climate objectives. This transition to offshore wind is intended to
strengthen Norway’s renewable energy portfolio and contribute meaningfully to international
climate change mitigation efforts.
Norway aims to substantially increase its offshore wind capacity, with a target of reaching
at least 30 GW by 2040, which aligns with the broader ambitions of Europe for offshore wind
development. As Norway has considerable expertise in floating wind technology due to its mar-
itime industry, the vision emphasizes the development and scaling up of floating offshore wind
farms. This technology allows wind turbine installations in deeper waters, where traditional
fixed-bottom structures are not feasible.
The vision promotes the synergistic development of offshore wind alongside other renewable
energy sources, such as hydropower and solar energy, to create a balanced and resilient energy
system. Norway emphasizes environmentally sustainable development practices that safeguard
marine biodiversity [16] and other uses of the ocean. This includes conducting extensive envi-
ronmental impact assessments and engaging with local communities and stakeholders.
To achieve Norway’s ambitious vision for floating offshore wind, significant investments
are needed across research and development, critical infrastructure, and technological advance-
ments. These investments are essential to cultivate a competitive industry capable of attracting
both domestic and international capital. The expansion of offshore wind energy is also expected
to create new employment opportunities and stimulate economic growth in various sectors, in-
cluding marine engineering, manufacturing, and specialized services. Crucially, this growth
8
==PAGE:11==
will be amplified by the robust development and utilization of a local supply chain, ensuring
that economic benefits are retained within Norway and that specialized expertise can flourish
domestically.
An effective and supportive regulatory framework is essential to accelerate offshore wind
projects. This includes establishing clear policies and guidelines to streamline project approval
and implementation. This framework not only provides certainty for investors but also fosters
an environment in which the local supply chain can confidently invest in new capabilities and
capacity. By ensuring predictable processes and fostering collaboration between regulators and
local businesses, Norway can maximize the economic and industrial benefits derived from its
floating offshore wind projects.
1.2
Problem statement - Offshore Wind Supply Chain Role
The established European offshore wind supply chain boasts the main players with extensive
experience in the entire life cycle of offshore wind farms, from development to operation [17].
Engaging with these companies offers Norway access to significant capital investments, special-
ized technological expertise, and proven project management skills, all crucial to the successful
realization of its offshore wind projects. Furthermore, collaboration with European suppliers
and technology developers can introduce innovative solutions and advanced technologies to the
Norwegian market, particularly in areas such as floating wind technology, cutting-edge turbine
designs, and eﬀicient installation methodologies, potentially accelerating project timelines.
The mature European market also features well-established financing models [18] and in-
vestment mechanisms capable of supporting large-scale offshore wind developments. Norwegian
offshore wind initiatives can potentially leverage these existing frameworks to attract the nec-
essary funding, thus mitigating the financial risks for local developers. Furthermore, given that
offshore wind projects frequently require transnational cooperation for electricity interconnec-
tion, the European supply chain plays a pivotal role in facilitating the development of the
crucial infrastructure required to seamlessly integrate offshore wind energy from Norway with
the broader European grid. Beyond technology and finance, the European offshore wind sector
has cultivated standardized best practices that include safety protocols, rigorous environmental
assessments, and operational eﬀiciency. Adopting these established practices could significantly
improve the regulatory framework of Norway and streamline project execution processes[19].
However, in addition to the benefits of leveraging the European supply chain, the develop-
ment of a robust local Norwegian supply chain presents potential advantages. A strong domestic
supply chain could significantly improve resilience against global supply disruptions, ensuring
more secure and stable sourcing of essential project components and services within Norway.
This localization could also foster domestic job creation, stimulate regional economic growth,
and potentially tailor solutions more specifically to the unique challenges and opportunities of
the Norwegian offshore environment.
Currently, the established European offshore wind supply chain plays a vital role in sup-
porting Norway’s offshore wind ambitions. However, as Norway aims to significantly increase
its offshore wind capacity, a critical question emerges regarding the optimal approach to sup-
ply chain development. This analysis will involve a comparative study that contrasting the
sourcing of offshore wind farm development in Norway through the existing European supply
chain with the scenario of establishing a dedicated local Norwegian offshore wind supply chain.
To effectively analyze the complex economic, logistical, and strategic implications of these two
distinct approaches, there is a clear need for a specialized analytical instrument. This master’s
thesis addresses this need by developing an agent-based model (ABM) tool that can be applied
to study the economic, logistical, and strategic implications of these two distinct supply chain
approaches for Norway’s offshore wind development goals.
9
==PAGE:12==
In conclusion, while the European offshore wind supply chain offers substantial benefits
in terms of experience, technology, and established infrastructure, the strategic development
of a complementary local supply chain in Norway deserves careful consideration. This thesis
aims to analyze the trade-offs between these two approaches. Understanding the respective
roles, potential synergies, and inherent challenges of both the European and a prospective
local Norwegian supply chain is crucial to effectively achieving Norway’s ambitious offshore
wind goals beyond 2025. The optimal pathway likely involves a strategic blend of leveraging
European expertise and fostering domestic capabilities.
1.3
Objective and Research Question
The primary objective of this master thesis is to investigate the potential advantages and
challenges associated with developing a local offshore wind supply chain in Norway, compared
to relying on the existing European offshore wind supply chain for the development of offshore
wind farms within Norwegian waters. Using an agent-based model, this research aims to provide
insight into the economic, logistical, and strategic implications of these two distinct supply chain
approaches to achieve Norway’s ambitious offshore wind energy goals.
To achieve this overarching objective, the following specific research questions will be ad-
dressed:
1. What are the key stakeholders in the floating offshore wind farm supply chain and their
unique characteristics and activities ?
2. How stakeholders interact with each other in the supply chain?
3. What is the required capacity of the supply chain for the execution of a 1GW floating
offshore wind farm?
4. What is the expected project execution period given the capacity of the supply chain?
5. How can environmental conditions and weather windows affect the cost and duration of
the wind farm execution?
These research questions will be explored through the development and simulation of an
agent-based model, which allows the analysis of complex interactions and dynamics within
different supply chain configurations and their impact on the development of offshore wind
farms in Norway. The findings of this research are intended to provide valuable information for
policymakers, industry stakeholders, and researchers involved in shaping the future of Norway’s
offshore wind industry.

Literature Review
The supply chain plays an important role in reducing costs in the floating offshore wind energy
sector, which is a young industry working to lower its energy cost to compete with other sources
[20]. The UK and the US are two countries that represent different stages of the development
of the floating offshore wind farm supply chain ahead of Norway. Practical experiences and
research conducted on the development of the local supply chain in these two countries will
provide a valuable guide for establishing resilience and sustainable floating offshore wind sup-
ply chain in Norway. The UK government viewed offshore wind as a significant contributor to
meeting its 2020 renewable energy and CO2 emissions [21]. By 2014, the installed operational
offshore wind capacity of the UK reached 3.6 GW, with an additional 1.4 GW in development,
and projections aimed at 10 GW. To achieve the target of £100/MWh by 2020, cost reductions
through learning effects were crucial. A strong supply chain and reduced operating and main-
tenance costs were necessary to meet the government’s targets for the sector [22]. From then,
several challenges and opportunities were identified and mitigated within the UK’s offshore
wind supply chain:
• Supply Chain Readiness
• Cost Reduction and Optimization
• Complexity Management
• Local Content and Policy
• Floating Offshore Wind
Floating offshore wind projects are complex and require heavy components such as floating
substructure, turbines, mooring systems, cables, and electrical substations, [23]. Meeting the
demand for heavy raw materials such as cement and steel should ideally be handled by the local
supply chain, especially given local commitments in seabed leasing and authorization processes.
There is a risk that the local supply chain may not make the necessary timely investments to
meet demand, which would negatively impact both the local economy and developers. The UK

Methodology
3.1
Research Design
This section outlines the research design, detailing the overarching strategy and methodolog-
ical choices used to answer the research questions posed in Chapter 1. The complexity and
dynamic nature of the execution of the floating offshore wind farm (FOWF) requires a specific
methodological approach capable of capturing emergent behaviors from decentralized interac-
tions. Given the inherent complexities and dynamic interdependencies of stakeholders within
the execution process of the floating offshore wind farm, an agent-based modeling approach
(ABM) was adopted as the primary research methodology.
This thesis employs a computational simulation approach in which a digital model is con-
structed to mimic the behavior of a real-world system. The model will be quantitative (for
example, the lead time of the row material, the production rate, the annual capacities, the
duration of the project, the capacities of the vessels and the speed and the environmental con-
ditions) allowing measurable insights and comparisons between scenarios. While ABM can be
used inductively for answering specific research questions about ”what-if” scenarios, it leans
more towards deductive reasoning where you design a system and observe its behavior under
controlled conditions to test hypotheses. The research aims to generate empirical data through
simulation experiments, enabling a deductive analysis to test hypotheses related to project
performance under varying conditions.
Floating offshore wind farm execution is a complex adaptive system. The overall project
duration and cost (macro-level outcomes) are not simply sums of individual parts, but emer-
gent properties arising from non-linear interactions between various components and actors
(micro-level agents). ABM’s bottom-up approach is ideal for capturing this. Traditional linear
project management tools often struggle to capture the full complexity and emergent behaviors
characteristic of large-scale infrastructure projects such as FOWF development. ABM, with
its bottom-up approach, is uniquely positioned to model how macro-level phenomena, such as
overall project duration and cost overruns, emerge from the localized interactions and decisions
of individual components and entities.
The productions of various floating offshore wind farm components (substructures, blades,
mooring, and dynamic cables) are not identical. Their productions have different characteris-
tics, capacities, lead times, production rates, and behaviors. ABM allows for explicit modeling
of this diversity. The FOWF supply chain is characterized by a high degree of heterogeneity
between its constituent elements. Different component suppliers possess unique production
capacities and lead times, while vessels and ports have varying operational constraints. ABM
facilitates the explicit representation of these diverse attributes, which is challenging for aggre-
gate models.
Different ”agents” (e.g., component suppliers, transport vessels, assembly ports) make de-
cisions relatively independently, based on their local information and rules. ABM naturally
accommodates this decentralized control. Decision-making within the FOWF execution net-
work is often decentralized. Suppliers determine their production schedules, vessels optimize
their routes and utilization, and port operations manage their resources autonomously. ABM
provides a framework for modeling these independent decision-making processes and observing
their collective impact.
Real-world parameters such as raw material lead times, production rates, and weather con-
ditions are uncertain and variable. ABM can easily incorporate stochastic elements to simulate
realistic fluctuations and their impact. Real-world project execution is subject to significant
stochasticity, particularly regarding raw material lead times and production rates, which can
introduce considerable uncertainty. ABM allows for the incorporation of probabilistic distribu-
tions and dynamic variations, offering a more realistic assessment of risk and resilience. ABM
14
==PAGE:17==
is excellent for testing hypothetical scenarios and interventions without real-world risk or cost.
A significant advantage of ABM is its utility for ’what-if’ scenario analysis. This enables
the evaluation of different operational strategies, resource allocation schemes, or mitigation
measures (e.g., increased port capacity, alternative vessel types) and their effects on project
outcomes, providing actionable insights for decision-makers.
3.2
Alternative methods for supply chain modeling
There are two primary alternatives to Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) for simulating a floating
offshore wind farm’s supply chain: Discrete Event Simulation (DES) [40] and System Dynamics
(SD) [41].
These methods offer different perspectives and are suited for different types of
analysis.
Discrete Event Simulation (DES)
DES models a system as a series of events that occur at specific points in time. It’s a ”bottom-
up” approach, like ABM, but focuses on the chronological flow of processes and resource allo-
cation rather than the autonomous behavior of individual entities.
Main Pros:
• Excellent for operational detail and bottlenecks: DES is ideal for understanding
the sequence of operations, resource utilization (e.g., vessels, ports, cranes), and identify-
ing bottlenecks in a complex process. It can easily track queue times for vessels waiting
at a port or the idle time of an installation crew.
• Handles stochasticity well: It’s great for modeling random variables, such as weather
delays, equipment breakdowns, or variations in transit times, by using statistical distri-
butions.
• More computationally eﬀicient than ABM: DES models are generally less complex
and require less computational power, especially for large-scale systems, because they
don’t simulate the complex, continuous decision-making of individual agents.
• Familiar to engineers and managers: The event-based logic is often intuitive for
those with an engineering or operational background, making models easier to build and
validate.
Main Cons Compared to ABM:
• Lacks autonomous, adaptive behavior: DES assumes pre-defined rules and event
sequences. It can’t capture the complex, adaptive decision-making of different companies
(e.g., a logistics company choosing a new port due to congestion) or their competitive
interactions.
• Struggles with emergent phenomena: Because the model’s logic is top-down and
pre-programmed, it cannot reveal new, unexpected system behaviors that emerge from
the interactions of individual agents.
• Less flexible for social/economic aspects: If your simulation needs to model market
behavior, contract negotiations, or how different companies react to changing conditions,
DES is limited.
—
15
==PAGE:18==
System Dynamics (SD)
SD is a ”top-down” approach that focuses on the aggregate behavior of a system over time,
using feedback loops, stocks, and flows. It’s used for understanding the long-term, high-level
dynamics of a system, not the minute-by-minute details.
Main Pros:
• Ideal for strategic, long-term analysis: SD is best for policy analysis and under-
standing the long-term behavior of a supply chain, such as how changes in component
production rates or port capacity affect the overall project timeline and cost over several
years.
• Focuses on feedback loops: It can highlight how interconnected variables create com-
plex behavior. For example, it can show how a lack of installation vessels (a stock)
creates a backlog of floating platforms (another stock), which in turn delays revenue and
limits future investment.
• Computationally inexpensive: Since it models aggregated variables rather than indi-
vidual entities, it can simulate large-scale systems over long time horizons very quickly.
Main Cons Compared to ABM:
• Homogeneity assumption: SD models typically assume that all components within a
”stock” are identical and behave in the same way. It cannot capture the heterogeneity
of different types of vessels, varying supplier capabilities, or individual company decisions.
• Lacks micro-level detail: It cannot answer specific operational questions like ”What
is the average wait time for a vessel at Port A?” or ”How does one specific component’s
late delivery affect the final installation date?”
• Cannot model spatial interactions: SD does not have a concept of agents interacting
in a physical space, which is critical for a floating wind farm supply chain that involves
distinct locations such as manufacturing sites, ports and the installation site itself.
3.3
Agent-Based Modeling Framework
Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) is a computational modeling approach that is used to simulate
interactions of agents with each other within a given environment to assess their effects on
the system as a whole.
It is a powerful method for studying complex dynamic systems in
which individual components follow simple rules but collectively exhibit intricate behaviors.
[42]. Agent-based models are especially useful when investigating group behavior or evolution-
ary processes and can uniquely reveal nonlinear dynamics and emergence [43]. Agent-based
modeling dates back to the 1930s and has resulted in key scientific breakthroughs [44].
The key features of agent-based modeling include the following.
Agents: The primary components of ABM are agents, which are individual autonomous
entities with different characteristics and behaviors. Agents can represent various entities such
as people, animals, or even companies depending on the model’s focus.
Rules and Behaviors: Each agent operates on the basis of a set of rules that define how it
interacts with other agents and the environment. These rules can be fixed or adaptive, allowing
agents to learn and change their behavior over time.
Environment: Agents interact within a defined environment that can influence their ac-
tions and decisions. The environment can be static or dynamic and is integral to shaping the
interactions and evolution of the system.
16
==PAGE:19==
Interactions: Agents within the model interact with each other and with the environment.
These interactions can be direct (for example, communication between agents) or indirect (e.g.,
altering the environment, which in turn affects other agents).
Emergence: One of the most compelling features of ABM is emergent behavior, complex
global patterns arising from simple local interactions. These patterns cannot necessarily be
predicted by analyzing the actions of individual agents in isolation.
3.4
The MESA Framework as a Modeling Tool for Floating Offshore
Wind Farm Execution
MESA (Modeling and Evaluation of System Architectures) is a framework designed to facilitate
the creation of agent-based simulations using Python [45, 46]. Agent-based modeling provides
a means to represent systems through the interactions and behaviors of individual agents [47].
Agent-based modeling is a third way of doing science in addition to deductive and inductive
reasoning [48]. Agent-based models have emerged as a valuable tool for simulating complex
systems across various domains [49]. The core idea behind agent-based modeling is to define
the microscopic details of the basic elements within a system and then observe how the system
evolves through agent-to-agent and agent-to-environment interactions. Agent-based modeling
can capture the emergent phenomena that arise from these interactions. Agent-based modeling
has gained prominence as a simulation technique, finding applications to address real-world
business challenges [50].
Agent-based modeling tools have expanded greatly in number and capabilities over the past
decade. MESA differentiates itself through its emphasis on simplicity and ease of use, making
it accessible to researchers and practitioners with varying levels of programming expertise [42].
MESA is written in Python, a high-level and versatile language known for its readability and the
extensive ecosystem of scientific computing libraries. The rule-based approach of agent-based
modeling, in contrast to equation-based methods, offers simplicity, flexibility, and intuitive
appeal, making it well suited to capture the inherent heterogeneity of decision-making agents
[51]. It enables explicit modeling of the complexity that arises from individual actions and
interactions within the real world. The Wolf-Sheep Predation Model, as shown in Figure1 is a
classic example often used to illustrate ecological dynamics, using MESA as a popular Python
framework to build agent-based models.[52]

3.6
Modeling and Simulation of Floating Wind Farm Execution
The modeling and simulation of a floating wind farm execution with MESA involves a series of
steps, beginning with the fundamental task of defining the agents that make up the system [56].
The agents within a floating offshore wind farm execution simulation could represent individual
wind farm components: substructure, tower sections, blades, nacelle, mooring, cables, anchors,
in addition to suppliers, T&I fleet. Figure3 gives an overview of the main sets of agents which are
considered in this thesis. The process involves creating software agents that mimic the behavior,
preferences, and dynamics of the elements on the wind farm. This involves assigning each agent
a set of attributes and methods that define its state, such as the state of the wind turbine
component (designed, ordered, in production, or installed), and location. Next, it is important
to establish the interactions between agents and the environment. Next, we define how these
agents interact with each other and with the environment. These interaction rules govern how
agents perceive and respond to changes in their environment, how they communicate with one
another, and how they make decisions. The connections between the agents and between the
agents and environment is accommodated with a set of global variables and dedicated methods
for each agent. Each agent possesses a method named a step that the model will call at each time
step. The behavior of agents can be defined through various mechanisms, such as rule-based
systems, state machines, or more complex decision-making algorithms. The next step involves
specifying the simulation environment, which could be a spatial grid mainly representing the
ocean surface and the onshore area where the suppliers are located, and a continuous space
that accounts for environmental factors. Once the agents, interactions, and environment are
defined, the simulation can be initialized. The simulation is then executed step by step, each
step representing a discrete time interval, and during each step, the scheduler activates the
agents’ step method according to a predefined order. As the simulation progresses, data is
collected from the agents and the environment.

Agent Definition and Parametrization
This section describes the definition and parameterization of the agents, and input parameters
used for agent-based modeling of floating offshore wind farm execution. The foundation of
the model is built on the MESA framework, which is employed as the primary tool in the
development of the simulation.
The data collected encompasses various real-world parameters relevant to offshore wind farm
deployment, including environmental conditions, suppliers’ annual capacities and production
rates, vessel specifications, construction and installation durations, and logistical constraints.
These input data are critical for accurately representing the complex dynamics of floating
offshore wind farm fabrication, transport, and installation.
The input parameters are derived from both public data sources and expert consultations
and are structured to reflect the operational, environmental, and technical aspects that influence
the execution of the project.
The application of the MESA framework allows for the integration of these data and pa-
rameters into a comprehensive simulation environment. Its suitability for modeling complex
project execution scenarios enables the assessment of different strategies and their potential
impacts on project performance. Adaptations made to the framework specifically address the
challenges associated with the deployment of floating offshore wind farms, such as variability
in weather conditions, logistic complexities, and the coordination of multiple agents involved
in the process. In the subsequent sections, each of these agent parameters will be explained in
detail, providing a comprehensive overview of their definition and application, and how they
were incorporated into the simulation framework.

As illustrated in Figure12 the evolution of the component during its lifecycle is divided

simulate the evolution between each state. Figure12 shows the operation carried out on the

only handle the parent component and the state of the child component and its life cycle will

Marshaling and Assembly Port Agents
This agent is used to simulate the substructure construction and the WTG and anchor and
mooring assembly prior to offshore wind farm installation. In this study, it is assumed that
the substructure is constructed in the dry dock of the marshaling port. All WTG components
will be shipped to the assembly port to assemble on the substructure.
A fully assembled
substructure will be wet stored near the port before being towed to the wind farm.
This
marshaling port will simulate the assembly, floatout, and wet storage of the integrated wind
turbine and substructure. The main attributes of the Marshaling port include.
• Port GIS position [Long, Lati]
• Port name
• Port state
• Wet storage capacity
• Dry dock capacity
• Float out duration
The GIS position and Port name are used to uniquely address the assembly port. the Port
state used to define the different operating state of the port from the time components arrived at
the port to the fully assembled floating wind turbine wet stored in the sheltered area. The Dry
dock and Wet storage capacities determine the number of substructures that can be constructed
and assembled simultaneously in the dry dock and number of wind turbines that can be moored
next to the queue, respectively.

The wind farm makes a wind farm

4
The Offshore Wind Supply Chain: A Tiered Perspec-
tive
The successful and sustainable development of offshore wind projects is highly dependent on
the eﬀiciency, robustness, and localization of its intricate supply chain. This chapter delineates
the multi-tiered structure of the offshore wind supply chain, classifying suppliers based on their
directness of engagement with project developers and the complexity of their contributions.
Understanding these tiers is important for analyzing bottlenecks, identifying opportunities for
local content development, and formulating effective industrial policies.
4.1
Introduction to Supply Chain Tier
The concept of a supply chain tier [58] as shown in Figure 13, widely adopted in complex manu-
facturing industries such as automotive and aerospace, provides a clear hierarchical structure to
understand the flow of goods, services, and information from raw materials to finished products.
In the context of offshore wind, this tiered approach helps to disaggregate the vast ecosystem
of suppliers, enabling a more granular analysis of their roles, dependencies, and potential for
innovation and localization.
Figure 13: Simplified offshore wind supply chain tier concept.
4.1.1
Tier 1: Finished Components and Prime Contractors
Tier 1 suppliers represent the uppermost layer of the offshore wind supply chain, directly
communicating with project developers and often serving as original equipment manufacturers
(OEM) or prime contractors responsible for delivering the major integrated systems or services
(T&I). Their direct contractual relationship with developers places them at the forefront of
project execution and overall system integration.
• Wind Turbine Manufacturers: These are arguably the most prominent Tier 1 players.
They design, manufacture and often install the complete wind turbine units, including
the nacelle, rotor, blades (though sometimes sourced from Tier 2), and control systems.
31
==PAGE:34==
Their role extends beyond manufacturing to include complex engineering, logistics, and
often long-term service and maintenance agreements. Examples include Vestas, Siemens
Gamesa Renewable Energy, GE Renewable Energy, and MingYang Smart Energy.
• Substructure Manufacturers: This category includes companies specializing in the
design and fabrication of large-scale foundations, such as monopiles, jacket foundations,
gravity-based foundations and, critically for Norway, floating substructures. These com-
ponents are massive, require specialized fabrication facilities. Their contracts are typically
direct with the project developer due to the scale and complexity involved.
• Offshore Substation Contractors: This group includes the companies responsible for
the complete engineering, procurement, construction, and installation (EPCI) of offshore
substations. These substations collect power from individual turbines, transform it to
higher voltages, and then export it to the onshore grid.
Their role involves complex
structural design, electrical integration, and offshore installation, making them a critical
Tier 1 entity.
• High Voltage (HV) Cable Suppliers: These specialized companies design, manufac-
ture, and often install the crucial subsea export cables (transmitting power from offshore
substations to shore) and inter-array cables (connecting individual turbines to the offshore
substation). Given the extreme lengths, high voltages, and rigorous installation require-
ments for these critical infrastructure components, HV cable suppliers such as NEXANs
and NKT operate as direct Tier 1 contractors.
• Mooring System Providers (Chains & Anchors): Particularly vital for floating
offshore wind, this group comprises companies that design, manufacture, and supply
the complete mooring systems, including chains, synthetic ropes, and various types of
anchors (e.g., drag embedment anchors, suction anchors, driven piles, gravity anchors).
These systems are crucial for station-keeping and ensuring the stability of floating plat-
forms against extreme environmental loads. Given the bespoke design and high-strength
material requirements for each project, and their direct impact on the integrity of the
floating structure, these providers operate at the Tier 1 level.
• Transport and Installation (T&I) Vessel Fleet Operators: This group includes the
owners and operators of highly specialized vessels essential for the logistics and installation
of major offshore wind components. This encompasses:
– Wind Turbine Installation Vessels (WTIVs): Jack-up vessels or heavy-lift ves-
sels designed to transport and install turbine components (towers, nacelles, blades)
and sometimes foundations primarily for bottom fixed wind turbine.
– Heavy Lift Vessels (HLVs) / Crane Barges: Used for transporting and in-
stalling large components like foundations and offshore substations, wind turbine
components or fully assembled tower, nacelle and rotor on a floating platform.
– Cable Laying Vessels (CLVs):
Specialized vessels for laying and burying subsea
array and export cables and dynamic inter-array cable including cable accessories
such as buoyancy element, tether and clamp and bend stiffener.
– Service Operation Vessels (SOVs) and Crew Transfer Vessels (CTVs):
While primarily used during the O&M phase, their deployment planning and initial
contracts often fall under the project’s installation phase considerations, making
their provision a critical Tier 1 service for the operational lifetime.
32
==PAGE:35==
– Anchor Handling Tugs (AHTs) and Towage Vessel Operators: These highly
specialized vessels play an indispensable Tier 1 role, particularly for floating offshore
wind projects. AHTs are specifically designed and equipped with powerful winches
and stern rollers for the complex tasks of deploying, recovering, and handling heavy
anchors and mooring lines. They are also crucial for the tow-out and precise position-
ing of large floating structures (such as semi-submersibles or spars) from fabrication
sites or marshaling ports to the offshore wind farm location. General towage vessels
also support the movement of barges carrying large components, ensuring safe and
eﬀicient transport of critical project elements. Their operations require exceptional
maneuvering capabilities, high bollard pull, and often dynamic positioning (DP) sys-
tems, making them highly specialized and directly contracted by project developers
or main installation contractors.
The availability, capability and cost of this specialized fleet are significant determinants of
project timelines and overall capital expenditure, solidifying their Tier 1 status due to their
direct involvement and critical dependence for project execution.
Tier 1 companies are characterized by their significant capital intensity, advanced tech-
nological capabilities, and often global operational footprint. They drive innovation in core
technologies and dictate many of the specifications for components sourced from lower tiers.
4.1.2
Tier 2: Subassembly and Major Components
Tier 2 suppliers provide major components, sub-assemblies, and specialized systems directly to
Tier 1 manufacturers (e.g., turbine OEMs, substation contractors) or directly to project devel-
opers for specific, large components. Their products are critical enablers for the final assembly
of Tier 1 offerings. While they do not typically contract directly with project developers for
system integration, their performance and reliability are paramount to the success of the Tier
1 products.
• Blade Manufacturers: While some wind turbine OEMs produce their own blades,
many rely on specialized Tier 2 suppliers. Blade manufacturing is a highly specialized
process requiring advanced composites engineering and large-scale manufacturing facili-
ties. Companies like LM Wind Power (a GE subsidiary) are prominent in this area.
• Gearbox Suppliers: For geared turbines, the gearbox is a complex and vital component
responsible for converting the slow rotation of the rotor into the high-speed rotation
required by the generator. Specialized engineering firms focus solely on the design and
manufacturing of these high-precision components.
• Tower Section Manufacturers: Wind turbine towers are typically manufactured in
sections due to logistical constraints. Tier 2 suppliers produce these massive steel or
concrete sections, which are then assembled on site or at port facilities.
• High Voltage (HV) Equipment OEMs: These are the original equipment manufac-
turers that design and produce high voltage electrical components that are integrated into
the offshore substation by Tier 1 substation contractors. This includes critical equipment
such as transformers, switchgear, protection and control systems, and reactive power
compensation equipment. Their specialized engineering and manufacturing capabilities
make them essential suppliers of the overall electrical infrastructure.
• Nacelle Component Suppliers:
This broad category includes manufacturers of gen-
erators, pitch and yaw systems, cooling systems, and other internal components that
make up the nacelle.
33
==PAGE:36==
The relationship between Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers is often characterized by long-term
contracts, collaborative design, and strict quality control measures, reflecting the critical nature
of the components provided.
4.1.3
Tier 3: Subcomponents and Specialized Manufacturing
Tier 3 suppliers provide raw materials, highly specialized sub-components, or manufacturing
services to Tier 2 companies.
This tier is typically characterized by a greater diversity of
suppliers, often smaller and more specialized, forming the foundational layer for more complex
assemblies.
• Steel Mills and Aluminum Producers:
These companies supply the raw steel and
aluminum, which are fundamental materials for towers, foundations, nacelle structures,
and various other components. The quality and type of steel (e.g., high-strength, low-
alloy) are critical for structural integrity in marine environments.
• Fiberglass and Resin Producers: Essential for blade manufacturing and other com-
posite structures, these suppliers provide the raw materials for advanced composite pro-
duction.
• Manufacturers of Smaller Electrical Components: This includes a vast array of
companies producing switches, sensors, circuit breakers, control units, and other electronic
components that are integrated into larger electrical systems by Tier 2 suppliers.
• Bearing Manufacturers:
Specialized bearings are crucial for various rotating parts
within the turbine, including the main bearing, gearbox bearings, and yaw/pitch bear-
ings. These are precision-engineered components with high demands for durability and
reliability.
• Hydraulics and Lubrication System Suppliers: Companies providing the intricate
hydraulic systems for blade pitch control, braking systems, and lubrication systems for
gearboxes and generators fall into this category.
Tier 3 often represents the broadest base of the supply chain pyramid.
While their direct
impact on the final product might seem less immediate, any disruption or quality issue at this
level can cascade upwards, affecting the entire supply chain.
4.1.4
Tier 4 (and Beyond): Raw Material Extraction and Highly Specialized Parts
Tier 4 and subsequent tiers represent the foundational level of the supply chain, encompassing
raw material extraction, fundamental processing, and the manufacturing of highly specialized,
often generic, parts that are widely used across industries. While seemingly distant from the
final offshore wind farm, these suppliers are indispensable.
• Raw Material Extractors: This includes mining companies for iron ore, bauxite, cop-
per, and rare earth elements (critical for permanent magnet generators). These are the
absolute starting point of many manufacturing processes.
• Fastener Manufacturers: Companies producing bolts, nuts, washers, and specialized
fasteners are crucial. Given the high stresses and corrosive environment of offshore wind,
these components require specific materials, coatings, and manufacturing precision.
34
==PAGE:37==
• Specialized Electrical Wiring Suppliers: Beyond the main cables, this includes
manufacturers of internal wiring harnesses, connectors, and specialized cabling for control
systems and instrumentation within the turbine and substation.
• Chemical Suppliers: Providers of paints, coatings (especially anti-corrosion and anti-
fouling), adhesives, and sealants vital for protecting components in the harsh marine
environment.
• Logistics and Transportation Providers (Raw Material Phase): Although not
direct ”material” suppliers, the companies involved in the transport of raw materials
from extraction sites to processing plants and then to manufacturers form a critical part
of these lower levels.
The distinction between Tier 3 and Tier 4 can sometimes be fluid, with some companies po-
tentially operating in both. The key characteristic of these lower tiers is their foundational
role, often providing components or materials that are not unique to offshore wind but are
nevertheless essential.
4.2
Interdependencies, Challenges, and Opportunities within the
Tiered Supply Chain
The supply chain encompasses a broad spectrum of activities, ranging from the extraction and
processing of raw materials to the fabrication of turbine components, the transportation and
installation of these components at sea, and the long-term operation and maintenance of the
wind farms [59]. The effective management of this intricate network presents both considerable
challenges and significant opportunities for innovation, economic growth, and the creation of a
resilient and sustainable energy future [60].
The development of a robust offshore wind supply chain is a multifaceted endeavor that
demands strategic coordination among a diverse array of stakeholders, including governmental
bodies, original equipment manufacturers, component suppliers, community representatives in
host locations, project developers, port and vessel operators, educational institutions providing
workforce training, labor organizations, and the research and development community.
A
resilient and sustainable supply chain is essential to achieve ambitious deployment targets for
offshore wind energy, to ensure the long-term success of the sector, and to allow the domestic
market to effectively compete on the global stage [59].
Understanding the tiered structure highlights several critical aspects of the offshore wind
supply chain.
• Inter-dependencies and Risk Propagation: A disruption at any tier, whether due to
material shortages, quality issues, or geopolitical events, can propagate upwards, impact-
ing higher tiers and ultimately project delivery. This necessitates robust supply chain
risk management strategies.
• Global vs. Local Sourcing: While Tier 1 companies are often global players, there is
increasing pressure and opportunity to localize aspects of Tier 2, Tier 3, and even some
Tier 4 manufacturing. This supports job creation, economic development, and reduces
logistical complexities and carbon footprint.
• Innovation Flow: Innovation can originate at any tier. Breakthroughs in raw materials
(Tier 4), manufacturing processes (Tier 3), or component design (Tier 2) can significantly
impact the performance and cost-effectiveness of Tier 1 products.
35
==PAGE:38==
• Strategic Importance of Local Content: For countries such as Norway that want to
build a strong domestic offshore wind industry, it is crucial to identify which levels offer
the greatest potential for the development of local content. This often involves supporting
the growth of specialized Tier 2 and Tier 3 manufacturers, leveraging existing industrial
strengths (e.g., maritime, oil and gas experience for floating foundations).
• Logistical Complexity: The sheer size and weight of offshore wind components at
every tier necessitate highly specialized logistics, from raw material transport to the final
installation of massive components offshore.
• Quality Assurance and Certification: Maintaining consistent quality across all tiers
is paramount due to the high capital costs, long operational lifespan, and harsh envi-
ronment of offshore wind farms. Robust quality management systems and certification
processes are critical throughout the supply chain.
The tiered framework of the offshore wind supply chain provides an indispensable analytical
tool to understand its structure, identify critical inter-dependencies, and pinpoint strategic
areas for development. As the offshore wind industry matures, particularly with the advent
of floating technologies, the dynamics within and between these tiers will continue to evolve.
For nations like Norway, a nuanced understanding of these tiers is fundamental to fostering a
competitive local supply chain, attracting investment, and ensuring the long-term success of
their offshore wind ambitions.

Model Development
5.1
Model Structure
This section will delve into the procedure for creating a floating offshore wind farm execution
simulation within the MESA framework, delineating the key elements of the simulation, and
providing detailed definitions for each component to ensure a comprehensive understanding
of the modeling process.
Agent-based modeling, particularly using the MESA framework,
provides a powerful approach to simulate the intricate interactions and dynamics involved in
floating offshore wind farm construction and installation. To create a floating offshore wind farm
execution simulation in the MESA framework, one must first define the scope and objectives
of the simulation, identifying the key performance indicators to be measured and the specific
questions to be addressed. [63]
5.2
The Model Architecture
The model architecture refers to the mathematical and conceptual representation of the real-
world system or phenomenon which is simulated. It’s about what is modeled and the rules
governing its behavior.
Key aspects of model architecture include:
• Mathematical Equations: The core of a numerical model.
This involves selecting
and formulating the relevant equations that describe the physics, economics, or other
principles that govern the system.
• Domain Discretizations: How the continuous real-world domain (space and/or time)
is broken down into discrete elements or steps that a computer can handle.
• Governing Rules: How the system and its components behave under various conditions.
• Boundary Conditions and Initial Conditions: Defining the external influences on
the system and its state at the beginning of the simulation.
• Assumptions and Simplifications: The approximations and idealizations made to
make the model tractable and computationally feasible. A good model is a judicious
trade-off between realism and simplicity.
• System Components and Interactions: How different parts of the system interact
with each other.
5.3
MESA Model Architecture
In the MESA framework for Agent-Based Modeling (ABM), the ”model architecture” is defined
by a clear, object-oriented structure. It is built around two core components known as Model
Class and Agent Class, each with a specific role and applications, as stated below.
5.3.1
Model Class
Role: The mesa.Model class serves as the global container and orchestrator for the entire
simulation. It represents the ”offshore wind market” in this thesis, in which the agents live and
interact. Its primary role is to manage the overall simulation logic, including:
• Setting up the simulation: Initializing global parameters, creating agents, defining
the environment (space), and setting up the scheduler.
48
==PAGE:51==
• Controlling simulation flow: Advancing the simulation by one time step, typically by
activating all agents via the scheduler step method .step().
• Maintaining global state: Storing data or variables that belong to the entire system
rather than individual agents (e.g.
total resources, met-ocean data, simulation time
increment).
• Providing a reproducible random number generator: Make sure that the simula-
tions can be replicated by setting a seed.
• Facilitating data collection: Coordinating data collection at the model and agent
levels throughout the simulation.
Applications: The Model class is applied in various ways to define the simulation’s overall
structure and behavior as listed below:
• Initialization of agents: In its __init__ , method it defines how many agents of each
type are created and their initial properties and initializes the global attributes to be
used by all agents.
• Defining the environment and space: To instantiate and manage the simulation’s space (
mesa.space.MultiGrid, mesa.space.ContinuousSpace, mesa.space.NetworkGrid) within
the model, dictating how agents interact spatially or through networks. From MESA
Rev.3 the model spatial structure is arbitrary.
• Implementing global rules: Any processes that affect the entire system, not just individual
agents, are handled here.
• Managing time and scheduling: To set up a mesa time scheduler mesa.time.Scheduler
to control the order in which agents execute their actions at each step.
• Data collection: The DataCollector is instantiated and configured in the Model class to
collect metrics about the overall system and individual agents.
• Ending conditions: It defines conditions within the Model’s step method or a separate
function that determine when the simulation should stop running (e.g., after a certain
number of steps, when a specific global state is reached).
5.3.2
Agent Class
Role: The mesa.Agent class (which is used to create specific agents types) represents the indi-
vidual autonomous entities within the simulation. Each instance of the custom Agent subclass
is a distinct actor with its own state, behaviors, and ability to interact with its environment
and other agents. The main role of an agent in the model constitutes
• Encapsulate individual state: Hold unique attributes that define the agent’s proper-
ties (e.g., position, length, weight, type, model, state, lifecycle).
• Define individual behaviors: Implement the rules and decision-making logic that dic-
tate how an agent acts in response to its internal state, its environment, and interactions
with other agents. This is typically done in the step() method.
• Interact with the environment: Allow agents to perceive and modify their immediate
environment or change their behavior in respond to environment (e.g., move on a grid,
consume resources from an agent, change vessel speed as a function of wind and wave).
49
==PAGE:52==
• Interact with other agents: Allow agents to communicate, trade, compete, or coop-
erate with other agents. (e.g., a vessel lifts or installs a component).
Applications: The Agent class is applied to model the heterogeneity and dynamic behavior
of individual actors:
• Representing diverse entities To create multiple Agent subclasses to represent differ-
ent types of individuals in the simulation.
• Autonomous decision-making: The step() method of an agent is where its au-
tonomous logic resides. This allows for emergent behaviors to arise from the bottom-up
interactions of many simple (or complex) rules.
• Spatial movement and interaction: Agents often have a position in a Space and
methods to move or interact with elements in neighboring cells or continuous space.
• Learning and adaptation: Agents can be designed to change their internal state and
behavior over time based on experiences or external stimuli.
• Agent state management: Agents can have methods and attributes to control and
manage the state and life cycle of other agents.
5.3.3
Methods and Attributes
The concept of agents having attributes (data) and methods (actions) is central to object-
oriented programming, and it is how one defines their behavior and interactions within a sim-
ulation.
Attributes: What an agent’s attributes are: Attributes are essentially the data or state
that an individual agent possesses. They represent the characteristics, properties, or internal
conditions that distinguish one agent from another and influence its behavior. Key Character-
istics of Attributes in MESA Agents:
• Agent state: Attributes define the agent’s different states. For example, for a wind farm
component: Designed, Produced, Storage, Transport, Assembled, Onboard, AtWind-
Warm, Installed, Operational.
• Variable over time: The attributes can change over the course of the simulation. An
agent’s state can change from ”Designed” to ”Produced” at the supplier factory and then
to ”Transport” by vessel transport agent.
• Unique to each Agent: While agents of the same class share the same type of at-
tributes, each individual agent instance will have its own specific value for that attribute.
• Accessible: Attributes are accessed like regular Python object properties (e.g., agent.weight,
agent.pos) by other agents and models and can be used to simulate the interactions of
the agents with other agents and environments.
• Initialized in _init_: Typically, attributes are defined and given initial values in the
agent’s __init__ method when the agent is created.
Examples of attributes for a component agent are: position, component name, model, length,
weight, life cycle, state, child, and parent agents name.
Methods: Methods are the actions or behaviors that an agent can perform.
They are
functions defined within the agent’s class that operate on the agent’s attributes or interact
with the environment and other agents.
The key characteristics of the methods in MESA
agents are:
50
==PAGE:53==
• Behavioral logic: Methods encapsulate the rules and logic that dictate how an agent
acts.
• State manipulation: Methods often modify an agent’s own attributes. For example, a
move() method might update the agent’s pos attribute.
• Interaction with environment: Methods allow agents to interact with their simulated
environment (e.g., adjust the speed of the vessel as a function of the height of the wave
and the speed of the wind, consume fuel as a function of the mileage). This is often done
by calling methods of the model.grid or model.space object or can be modeled by a global
data frame accessible to all agents.
• Interaction with other Agents: Methods facilitate communication and interaction
between agents (e.g., vessel can load and transport a component when the component
life cycle reached a desired state). This often involves: Finding other agents, Accessing
or modifying their attributes, Calling their methods.
• step() Method: The most crucial method in a MESA agent is the step() method. This
method defines the agent’s behavior for a single simulation step. The scheduler in the
MESA Model class calls each agent’s step() method in a defined order (e.g., random,
simultaneous).
• Encapsulation: Methods promote encapsulation, keeping the agent’s behavior logically
grouped with its data.
Examples of methods for supplier agents are:
• Estimate_Order_Leadtime()
• Check_Order_Complition()
• Produce_Component()
Attributes and methods determine the behavior and interactions of the agent in several
ways:
• Individual behavior: An agent’s behavior at any given time is a function of its current
attributes and the logic encoded in its methods.
• Interaction with the environment: Agents interact with the environment by Reading
environmental attributes such as wave height and wind speed, Modifying environmental
attributes, Moving within the environment.
• Interaction with other agents: This is where the dynamic complexity of ABMs comes
alive. Sensing other agents using agents methods, Accessing other agents’ attributes,
Calling other agents’ methods
•
Shared resources/common space: Agents interact indirectly by competing for or sharing
resources in the environment, or by occupying the same spatial locations. The environ-
mental attributes act as mediators for these interactions.

5.5.4
Wind Farm Agents:
The wind farm agent is designed to establish the layout and design parameters of the wind
farm, which it then uses to plan and execute the development of the entire offshore wind farm.
This process involves a series of subsequent actions, including:
• establish bill of material based on wind farm design parameters
• send purchase orders to supplier and use the estimated delivery date to make wind farm
execution plan
• use the wind farm execution plan to send the transport orders to vessels
• establish wind turbine assembly sequence
• establish T&I plan and send orders to vessels
• track wind farm development and update wind farm component life cycle
To simplify the definition of a wind farm, a standard parametric layout is used. This layout
is square-shaped with uniform turbine spacing based on the rotor’s diameter. The inter-array
cable configuration uses either a radial layout or a branched string topology. For floating wind
turbines and substations, a tether-wave cable configuration serves as the base case for dynamic
cable setups. Each inter-array cable string originates from a dynamic cable at the substation
hang-off point. This dynamic cable terminates at a factory-made, wet-mate connector on the
seabed. From this point, the static, buried portion of the inter-array cable extends to the end
of each string. Along this static section, wet-mate connectors are used to divide each string
into multiple branches, with each branch connecting to a single wind turbine. The cable within
each branch is divided into two segments joined by a factory-made connector. The dynamic
portion of the cable begins at the floating turbine’s substructure hang-off point, passing through
a tether point before terminating at the seabed with a factory-made connector that joins it to
the static cable segment.
The reference floating wind turbine is a triangular semi-submersible model with a single-line
mooring system at each corner. These input data will be used to establish a wind farm layout
as shown in Figure5 .

This subsection outlines the key parameters and component specifications that define simulated

as follows:
• Wind Farm Location: The simulations are based on a representative offshore site
on the Norwegian continental shelf, chosen to reflect the logistical and environmental
conditions typical of a future Norwegian offshore wind development.
• Wind Turbine Reference: A generic 14 MW capacity wind turbine model is utilized
as the reference technology for all cases. The dimensions and performance characteristics
of this model are based on publicly available specifications for next-generation offshore
wind turbines.
• Substructure: The chosen substructure is a floating semi-submersible platform, selected
to represent a viable solution for the deep waters of the Norwegian Sea.
• Mooring and Dynamic Cable Systems: The model includes a catenary mooring
system designed to anchor the floating substructure, as well as an associated dynamic
cable system for power transmission.
• Vessel Fleet: The simulation incorporates a comprehensive vessel fleet, which includes
heavy-lift vessels, supply vessels, tugs, and cable-lay vessels. The operational capabilities
and transit speeds of each type of vessel are defined on the basis of industry standard
data.

6.2.2
Floating Offshore Wind Farm Reference
The Utsira Nord offshore wind farm as shown in Figure 19, located in the North Sea,
west of the island of Utsira, Rogaland, Norway is selected as a reference floating offshore
wind farm. The area spans approximately 1,010 km2 with water depths ranging from 185
meters to 280 meters. This significant depth makes it unsuitable for traditional bottom-
fixed turbines and ideal for floating offshore wind technology. The nearest turbines will
be situated approximately 6.5 to 7 kilometers from the coast of Utsira. The geographical
position of the site is selected at this GIS position:
– (Lati)58.9340° N, (Long)5.3868° E
The Norwegian government, through the Ministry of Energy (MoE) and the Nor-
wegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate (NVE), has done an initial
assessment in 2013 and identified 15 zones as suitable for offshore wind development. In
2020 the government oﬀicially opened the first two areas for offshore wind production:
Sørlige Nordsjø II (primarily for bottom-fixed installations) and Utsira Nord (for floating
installations). In April 2023, [65] the NVE presented a report that identified 20 new ar-
eas suitable for offshore wind development. This report also proposed expansions of the
already opened Utsira Nord and Sørlige Nordsjø II areas. This was a significant public
announcement that laid the groundwork for future licensing rounds.
The Utsira Nord location is selected since it is an immense strategic importance for
Norway’s energy future, and its position in the global floating offshore wind market.
– Pioneering Floating Technology: With water depths exceeding 60 meters, the
vast majority of the world’s offshore wind resources are in deep waters. Utsira Nord,
66
==PAGE:69==
Figure 19: The location of the Utsira North in NorthSea and intended supply chain region
due to its depth, serves as a crucial testing ground and a launchpad for commercial-
scale floating offshore wind. This builds upon Norway’s existing expertise from
the Hywind Tampen project, the world’s first floating offshore wind farm.
– Industrial Development and Export: The project is a deliberate effort to create
a new, high-tech industry in Norway. By leveraging its extensive expertise from the
oil and gas sector, Norway aims to become a world leader in floating offshore wind.
The development of Utsira Nord is expected to drive cost reductions, foster a new
supply chain, and create thousands of jobs, with a significant potential for exporting
technology and services globally.
– Domestic Power Supply: Utsira Nord will contribute to Norway’s ambitious goal
of allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2040. The project will
provide a significant boost to the domestic power grid, particularly for a region with
high electricity demand, and help to balance the country’s hydropower-dominated
energy system.

7
Conclusion
7.1
Summary of Findings
This master’s thesis, titled ”AGENT-BASED MODELING OF THE NORWEGIAN
OFFSHORE WIND SUPPLY CHAIN,” addressed Norway’s strategic challenge in transi-
tioning to floating offshore wind (FOW) by comparing an existing European supply chain
with a prospective local Norwegian supply chain. The research utilized an Agent-Based
Model (ABM), developed using the open-source MESA framework in Python, which
was chosen for its capability to capture complex, emergent behaviors from decentralized
interactions, model agent heterogeneity, and integrate dynamic stochastic elements like
weather, all critical for offshore operations. The study simulated two distinct supply chain
configurations: 1. Existing European Supply Chain: Components were assumed to be
fabricated across Europe and then consolidated in Norway. 2. Assumed Local Norwegian
Supply Chain: Key components were hypothesized to be manufactured and assembled
locally within Norway. These configurations were applied to simulate both small-scale
(six 14MW turbines) and gigawatt-scale (sixty 14MW turbines) floating offshore wind
farms, using the Utsira Nord offshore wind farm as a reference site due to its strategic
importance for Norway’s energy future and its role as a launchpad for commercial-scale
floating offshore wind technology. The primary finding from the simulations highlighted
a significant difference in project execution times:
– A floating offshore wind farm project relying on the current European supply chain
could take over five years to complete.
– However, by substituting with an assumed local Norwegian supply chain, the project
duration could be shortened by almost seven months. This substantial time saving
is attributed directly to the significantly shorter vessel transport routes inherent in
a domestic supply chain.
7.2
Contribution to the Field
This master’s thesis makes several key contributions to the field of offshore wind energy
development and supply chain management:
1. Development of a Novel Agent-Based Model for FOWF Supply Chains
in Norway: The research addresses a noted gap in the literature by developing an
ABM specifically tailored for the simulation of floating offshore wind farm (FOWF)
supply chains in Norway. While ABM is widely recognized for its utility in complex
systems, its application to the specific context of Norwegian FOWF supply chains
is less extensive. This model, built on the MESA framework and augmented with
specialized geospatial and environmental libraries, provides a sophisticated tool for
understanding the intricate dynamics of this nascent but critical sector.
2. Quantitative Insight into Supply Chain Alternatives for Norway’s Energy
Transition: The thesis offers a quantitative assessment of the economic, logistical,
and strategic implications of developing a local Norwegian offshore wind supply chain
versus relying on the existing European supply chain. By simulating the flagship
Utsira Nord project, the research provides concrete, data-driven evidence that a
local supply chain could reduce project execution time by almost seven months. This
insight is invaluable for policymakers and industry stakeholders in Norway, guiding
74
==PAGE:77==
strategic decisions on investment in domestic capabilities and fostering resilience
against global supply disruptions.
3. Enhanced Realism in Offshore Wind Project Simulation: The ABM design,
incorporating detailed agent heterogeneity (e.g., varying supplier capacities, produc-
tion rates, vessel specifications), dynamic stochastic elements (like weather windows
affecting offshore T&I), and geospatial data for routes and locations, significantly
enhances the realism and predictive power of FOWF supply chain simulations. This
level of detail allows for the observation of emergent behaviors and complex interac-
tions that simpler models might miss, providing a more comprehensive understand-
ing of project performance under varying conditions.
4. Framework for Strategic Decision-Making and Risk Management: By pro-
viding a robust simulation tool, the thesis offers a framework for ’what-if’ scenario
analysis. This enables the evaluation of different operational strategies, resource
allocation schemes, and mitigation measures (e.g., increased port capacity, alterna-
tive vessel types) without real-world risk or cost, providing actionable insights for
decision-makers in the evolving FOWF industry. This is particularly relevant for a
nation like Norway aiming for ambitious offshore wind capacity targets and seeking
to leverage its maritime and oil and gas expertise to build a globally competitive
supply chain.
7.3
Recommendation for Future Research and Industry Prac-
tices
Future research will build upon the foundational model by investigating a range of critical
factors to enhance the realism and practical utility of the findings. A key objective is
to integrate a comprehensive cost model for transport and installation vessels, which
will allow a thorough economic analysis and optimization of the entire project lifecycle.
This will enable a more holistic understanding of project costs beyond just operational
expenses.
Furthermore, we will introduce a stochastic failure and repair model for vessels. This
model will account for the probabilistic nature of equipment failures and the associated
repair times, allowing us to study the consequences of vessel unavailability on the project’s
execution schedule and overall costs. This analysis is important for developing robust
project plans that can mitigate risks associated with unexpected vessel downtime.
In addition to these enhancements, future work will explore the influence of various
parameters, including different production capacities, vessel specifications, and alternative
construction methodologies. These investigations will also examine different assembly
sequences and work-shift schedules to identify optimal strategies to maximize eﬀiciency
and minimize project duration. These topics will be explored in a separate upcoming
publication, contributing valuable insights to the evolving field of offshore wind farm
development.
