Abstract
Motion blur is a persistent challenge in face recognition systems (FRS), as it can signifi­
cantly degrade image quality and reduce recognition accuracy. This thesis evaluates the
performance of state­of­the­art Face Image Quality Assessment (FIQA) algorithms in de­
tecting motion blur by comparing quality scores by algorithms to human expert opinions.
The primary dataset used for this evaluation is the Essen Darmstadt Motion Blur (EDAMB)
dataset, which consists of 1341 face images labeled by five human experts. To address the
lack of demographic diversity in EDAMB, the Monk Skin Tone Examples (MST­E) dataset
was introduced, enabling a fairness evaluation across varying skin tones. Five state­of­the­
art algorithms from the Norwegian Biometrics Laboratory and Hochschule Darmstadt were
evaluated: CNN­R, Laplace, Densenet161, Densenet169, and FourierTransform. The as­
sessment employed multiple metrics, including Error versus Discard Characteristic (EDC)
curves, Kullback­Leibler divergence, and ANOVA analysis. The CNN­R algorithm demon­
strated superior performance on the EDAMB dataset with the lowest Partial Area Under the
Curve (pAUC) value. In contrast, algorithm fusion experiments revealed slight improvement
when combining CNN­R with DenseNet161 using Z­score as a weighting method. Testing
on the MST­E dataset revealed minimal performance variations between algorithms when
assessing synthetic motion blur, with pAUC values differing by only 0.00025. Fairness eval­
uation across skin tone groups showed promising results, with most algorithms achieving
high fairness scores across multiple metrics. The FourierTransform algorithm achieved the
highest fairness scores, while CNN­R demonstrated a strong balance between fairness and
detection accuracy. Among these, CNN­R demonstrated the most consistent performance
and accuracy when tested on both datasets.

Introduction
Face Recognition Systems (FRS) have become present everywhere in our daily lives, finding
applications in diverse scenarios such as smartphone authentication, surveillance systems,
passport control, and more [1, 2, 3]. While FRS have significantly improved in recent years,
they still face challenges that affect performance [4]. One such challenge is motion blur,
often caused by the subject’s movement or camera motion, which can degrade the quality
of captured face images and lead to lower recognition rates [5].
Given that motion blur is inevitable in many real­world scenarios, there is growing interest
in improving the accuracy and performance of motion blur detection in FRSs [6]. Effective
motion blur detection can lead to more robust systems with reduced false match rates (FMR)
and false non­match rates (FNMR), ultimately enhancing overall system performance [7].
To address this challenge, a set of state­of­the­art face image quality assessment (FIQA)
algorithms and models used to assess the quality of face images based on the intensity of
motion blur, and the quality score it outputs is used to compare against the human expert
opinion. The human expert opinion is individuals who have looked at and manually labeled
the Essen Darmstadt Motion Blur (EDAMB) dataset, consisting of 1341 images rated on a
scale from 10 to 90, where 0 indicates a fully blurred image, and 100 indicates no blur.
The EDAMB dataset provides valuable insight into motion blur since five people manually
labeled the images based on the intensity of motion blur. However, the dataset lacks de­
mographic diversity, which limits its ability to assess the fairness of the quality scores of the
algorithms it outputs across different demographic groups.
To address this limitation, the Monk Skin Tone Examples (MST­E) dataset was used for addi­
tional evaluations. This dataset includes images from diverse demographic groups, allowing
for a fairness evaluation of the algorithms to determine potential performance inconsistency
across demographic groups where skin tone is one of the relevant demographic variables
of interest.
Five human experts’ labels from the EDAMB dataset will be fused while carefully considering
outliers. This approach ensures the collective human expert opinion on motion blur intensity
is captured while minimizing the influence of potential individual biases or labeling errors.
The fused labels will be the Human Expert opinion ground truth for comparing algorithms’
quality score output.
The Norwegian Biometrics Laboratory (NBL) at the Norwegian University of Science and
Technology (NTNU) and Hochschule Darmstadt (HDA) will implement state­of­the­art mod­
els and algorithms for FIQA. NBL and HDA labs have recently developed has five algorithms
that will be evaluated against the ground truth derived from the EDAMB dataset. The best­
performing algorithms will then be fused and benchmarked to determine whether the fusion
improves the accuracy of motion blur detection.
By benchmarking these algorithms against the human expert opinions and evaluating their
performance on the EDAMB and MST­E datasets, the strengths and limitations of current
motion blur detection techniques will be identified, with the knowledge of the performance of
these algorithms on different demographic groups.

1.1
Research Questions
This thesis aims to evaluate and identify the most accurate FIQA algorithms based on sev­
eral benchmark metrics. The benchmarking process will involve assessing the algorithms’
performance on the EDAMB dataset and extending the evaluation to the MST­E dataset
to analyze fairness across demographics. The following research questions will guide this
evaluation:
• What is the most effective method for fusing several human expert opinions to establish
a reliable ground truth that minimizes potential outliers for motion blur detection in face
images?
• Which motion blur detection algorithm demonstrates statistically significant improve­
ments in accuracy when benchmarked against state­of­the­art methods on the EDAMB
dataset?
• How do the fused outputs of the top­performing algorithms compare to the human
expert ground truth in terms of accuracy?
• Can algorithm fusion techniques improve the quality scores for motion blur in face
images compared to individual algorithm performance?
• How do the evaluated algorithms perform across different demographic groups when
tested on the MST­E dataset, and are there significant performance differences based
on demographic attributes?

Background
2.1
Face Recognition System
An FRS is a widely used biometric technology that ranges from personal devices to large­
scale surveillance systems. These systems typically operate through a series of steps[2, 8,
9].
1. Face Detection: Isolating face regions within an image
2. Pre­Processing: Facial images are enhanced and normalized to handle variations
such as lighting, pose, and alignment
3. Feature extraction: Identifying and extracting distinctive facial features
4. Feature Comparison: Comparing extracted features against a database of known
faces.
5. Feature Classification: Determining or verifying the claimed identity based on the ver­
ification results.
Face recognition accuracy is highly dependent on the quality of the input face images. Low­
quality face images can degrade the performance of FRS since important facial details may
be lost [10]. Several factors contribute to determining the quality of face images. For ex­
ample, a face photo can be affected by pose (viewing angle), facial expression, illumination
conditions, occlusions (objects or shadows obscuring the face), and image clarity or blur [4,
11, 10]. Researchers highlight that assessing face image quality in terms of its “biometric
utility” (i.e., how valuable the image is for recognition) is crucial, as it allows the detection
of low­quality samples and informed decisions on how to handle them [10]. For instance, if
an image is predicted to be of poor quality, the system might reject it or prompt the user to
capture a new photo, thereby maintaining overall recognition reliability [10].
2.1.1
Controlled vs Unconstrained Capture Scenarios
FRS operates in various environments, from highly controlled studio­like settings to unpre­
dictable, ”in the wild” conditions. In controlled scenarios ­ such as passport enrollments or
ID card photos ­ the subject is cooperative, and environmental factors (lighting, background,
camera position) are managed to optimize image quality [10]. Here, an automated FIQA
module can be used during image capture to ensure a certain level of quality by providing
immediate feedback to the user or operator [10]. However, in unconstrained scenarios (e.g.
surveillance footage or images captured without user cooperation), the system has little to
no control over how the face appears since the subject is in motion. FIQA is useful after
capture in such cases: the system can automatically filter out or discard images that fall
below a quality threshold before attempting recognition [10].
2.2
Face Image Quality Assessment
To ensure that FRS is introduced to images without motion blur, FIQA can be used to assess
different quality components of an image, such as lighting, motion blur, sharpness, or, for
example, the subject’s pose. FIQA aims to improve the utility of face images for FRSs by
providing quality estimations, ultimately enhancing face recognition performance [10].
Recent advancements in FIQA highlight the increasing use of deep learning­based ap­
proaches, improving the accuracy of face image quality estimation [10]. The methods for

2.2.1
Face Image Quality Assessment Toolkit
The Face Image Quality Assessment Toolkit (fiqat)2 is a framework developed by Schlett et
al. [14]. The framework is provided for research to conduct face recognition and quality as­
sessment experiments. This toolkit combines several key features into one package. It can
detect faces in images, figure out which face is the main one when there are multiple people,
process face images by cropping and aligning them, extract traits for face recognition, and
generate EDC (Error versus Discard Characteristic) curves for analysis.
2.3
Motion Blur
Motion Blur occurs when the subject/person moves or the capture device is in motion as
shown in Figure 2.1. An object’s motion is then projected onto the camera, leading to a
blurred effect that traces the object’s path. In low light conditions with long exposure times,
the path of the object is extended in the image, leading to an increased blurring [15].
Figure 2.1: Examples of motion blur from the EDAMB dataset. These images were captured
in the city of Essen, Germany.
The de­focus of an image is due to incorrect camera focus adjustment, thereby decreasing
the sharpness of an image. In the image formation process, light from a point (specific
location on the image) that is in focus is projected as a sharp point on the camera. However,
light from points not in the plane of focus spreads over a small area on the camera, called the
circle of confusion[16]. De­focus blur becomes noticeable when the circle becomes large
enough for the human eye to detect [17]. Fine details are lost when an image is out of focus,

2.3.1
Types of Motion Blur
Motion blur in digital images can appear in various forms, each presenting unique charac­
teristics and challenges for detection and analysis. According to Hao et al.[20] and Zhang et
al.[21], Linear motion blur, caused by relative movement between the camera and the sub­
ject during exposure, can be mathematically represented as the convolution of the original
image with a linear motion kernel.
g(x,y) = f(x,y) * h(x,y)
Where g(x,y) is the blurred image, f(x,y) is the original image, and h(x,y) is the point spread
function (PSF) representing the motion blur[20, 21].
Rotational motion blur occurs when there is angular movement during image capture, result­
ing in a curved blur pattern[22]. The Point Spread Function (PSF) for rotational blur follows
an arc pattern, with the blur intensity varying based on the distance from the rotation cen­
ter[22]. This type of blur is particularly challenging in face recognition as it can distort facial
features non­uniformly. As discussed by Dai and Wu[23], complex motion patterns emerge
from combinations of linear and rotational movements or irregular camera/subject motion.
These patterns create spatially varying blur kernels that cannot be represented by simple
mathematical models, requiring more sophisticated analysis approaches[23].
2.3.2
Blur Formation Process
The formation of motion blur is linked to camera exposure mechanics. During image cap­
ture, the camera’s sensor accumulates light overexposure. Any relative motion between
the camera and subject during this time causes light from the subject to be spread across
multiple sensor pixels, causing the motion blur effect.
The exposure duration is crucial in how blur forms. Longer exposure times increase the
likelihood and severity of motion blur, following the relationship [24]:
Blur Length = Relative Motion Speed × Exposure Time
Environmental factors significantly influence blur formation. Low­light conditions often ne­
cessitate longer exposure times, increasing blur probability. Additionally, lighting conditions
affect the camera’s automatic exposure settings, potentially leading to unintended motion
blur effects [25].
2.3.3
Impact on Image Properties
In facial images, motion blur particularly impacts image properties in the representation of
the facial traits. Fine details such as skin texture patterns, essential for advanced recog­
nition systems, become lost or degraded. Critical facial landmarks, including eye corners,
lip contours, and nose edges, lose definition, reducing their reliability for recognition algo­
rithms. A Study by Knezevic et al.[26] has shown that blur and motion blur adversely af­
fect face recognition performance, with enhanced images exhibiting the worst performance.
While the overall face structure may still be recognizable, the fine details crucial for accurate
identification are blurred under motion. Research indicates that non­uniform motion blur,
resulting from relative motion between the camera and subject, poses significant challenges
for face recognition systems [27].

2.5.1
Convolutional neural network (CNN) and Laplace
The CNN­R is a regression­based convolutional neural network model, which evaluates blur
by processing patches of images and translating their blur intensity into a numeric value [32].
This method facilitates refined, automatic blur detection by learning from labeled examples
where image sharpness varies.
Another approach is a handcrafted Laplacian method, which utilizes a deterministic ap­
proach by calculating the variance of the Laplacian operator across image patches [32].
This method is based on the principle that areas of an image with a higher variation in pixel
intensity are likely to be sharper and, therefore, less blurred. Unlike the adaptive learning
approach of CNN­R, the Laplacian method applies a fixed mathematical formula to directly
asses image sharpness, making it computationally efficient and straightforward in implemen­
tation.
The selection of pixel patch size is helpful for both CNN­R and the handcrafted approach
(Laplace). This size has been determined to optimally balance capturing of details and com­
putational efficiency. It is sufficiently large to include crucial facial traits that are significant
for motion blur analysis yet compact enough to limit the inclusion of irrelevant details that
might have a negative impact on the blur assessment. The patch size also facilitates a
manageable computational load, particularly beneficial for real­time processing applications
requiring quick blur evaluation.
2.5.2
Advanced Deep Learning Techniques
In addition to the conventional CNN­R and handcrafted Laplacian methods, recent state­
of­the­art deep learning architectures were exploited, specifically Dense Convolutional Net­
works (Densenet), to enhance the detection and quantification of motion blur in biometric
face images [33, 34]. Two configurations of Densenet, namely Densenet169 and Densenet161,
have been integrated and extensively fine­tuned to suit the specific requirements of motion
blur assessment in FRS, which is given a quality score between 0 and 1 [33]. Densenet169
offers an effective framework due to its efficient connectivity pattern, where each layer con­
nects to every other layer in a feed­forward fashion. This design facilitates feature reuse [35].
The model’s depth allows it to capture fine details and subtle variations in image sharpness,
which are crucial for assessing motion blur. A study by Nandy et al. [36] has demonstrated
the effectiveness of the Densenet architecture in handling variations such as lighting and
occlusion, which are also relevant in motion blur scenarios. Densenet161 and Densenet169
differ primarily in their width (number of feature maps) rather than depth. While both archi­
tectures are capable of extracting fine­grained features, Densenet­169 has a deeper struc­
ture, which may enhance its ability to analyze subtle variations in motion blur [35]. This
adaptability makes it suitable for FIQA, which is necessary for reliable face recognition. The
ability of Densenet to process images of varying quality, including those affected by motion
blur, has been highlighted in a study by Angeline and Nithya [37]. Furthermore, Dong et
al. [38] explored the integration of Densenet with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
for facial expression recognition, demonstrating its capability to handle variations in image
quality, including potential blurriness. While their study does not explicitly address motion
blur detection, it reinforces Densenet’s effectiveness in challenging face analysis tasks.

Fourier Transform
In contrast to the deep learning techniques like CNN and Densenet which was proposed,
the handcrafted method employed relies on Fourier Transform­based techniques to detect
and quantify motion blur without the need for training data [33, 39]. This method utilizes
the Fourier Transform to analyze images’ frequency domain, identifying patterns indicating
motion blur. According to Al­Azzawe and Zaidan [40], Fourier Transform methods can effec­

The primary technique involves converting the images from the spatial domain (an image

Error versus Discard Characteristic (EDC)
The Error versus Discard Characteristic (EDC) curve is a key metric in evaluating the predic­

errors evolve as progressively lower­quality images are excluded. For a good predictive
FIQA algorithm a decrease of the FNMR is expected. The stronger the decrease the better
the algorithm. This characteristic is crucial in FIQA, where excluding low­quality face images
can significantly enhance recognition accuracy.

3.4
Analysis of Variance
Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a versatile statistical method that tests differences between
independent groups’ means. While it is commonly applied to multiple groups, it can also
analyze differences between two groups by determining if the variation between groups is
more significant than within groups, providing a structured approach for hypothesis testing
[51]. ANOVA’s key advantage is distinguishing between differences caused by experimen­
tal conditions and natural random variation. It determines whether the observed differences
between groups are meaningful rather than due to chance [52]. In analyzing algorithm perfor­
mance, ANOVA helps identify whether the differences reflect actual performance variations
or random chance, thereby validating experimental results and ensuring confident identifi­

settings, such as comparing the effectiveness of different computer training programs. It can
identify which educational approaches produce better learning outcomes, making it valuable
across various research fields [52]. In face recognition research, ANOVA has been a valu­
able tool for comparing performance differences between groups, such as super­recognizers
and typical observers, in detecting manipulated face images. For instance, Davis et al. [53]
demonstrated how ANOVA can assess whether one group outperforms another in specific
tasks. Their study examined the abilities of 787 participants, showing significant differences
in how well super­recognizers detected digitally manipulated faces compared to regular ob­
servers. The statistical analysis using ANOVA highlighted that super­recognizers performed
much better at identifying manipulated passport photos, providing robust evidence of their
superior detection skills. This example underscores ANOVA’s effectiveness in validating
performance data in security­related research.

4.2.3
Algorithm Quality Score Fusion
Building upon the fusion strategies discussed by Schlett et al. [43], this study implements
score fusion to combine the outputs of the five algorithms (CNN­R, Laplace, Densenet161,

a standard scale using Min­max normalization as described in Section 4.2.1, the fusion is
performed using a weighted averaging approach.
Ablation Study
To better understand the contribution of each algorithm to the overall image quality assess­
ment, an ablation study is conducted following the methodology introduced by Zeiler and
Fergus [67]. While their work focused on understanding convolutional networks by system­
atically occluding different parts of the network, this study adapts the concept to analyze the
impact of varying algorithm combinations in quality score fusion.
The ablation study begins with a baseline evaluation using all five algorithms combined
through score fusion. Subsequently, algorithms are systematically removed one at a time to
create different combinations of four algorithms. This process continues with combinations
of three and two algorithms to comprehensively understand each algorithm’s contribution to
the overall performance.
Using each combination’s previously established evaluation metrics, the fused scores are
compared against the human expert opinion (Ground Truth) and the synthetic motion blur
(ground truth) for each respective dataset more so each fused combination of the algorithm
will also be shown in an EDC curve and consider the pUAC value. This systematic removal
of algorithms helps identify the following:
• The optimal combination of algorithms for score fusion
• The relative importance of each algorithm to the overall assessment
• Potential redundancies in the combined system
• Whether fusion consistently outperforms individual algorithms
The performance metrics for each combination are evaluated against the baseline to quantify
the impact of removing specific algorithms from the fusion process. This analysis provides
insights into which algorithms are most important for accurate image quality assessment
compared to expert evaluations. Each algorithm’s weighting method is the simple mean,
Z­score, and pAUC value.

Quality Assessment Algorithms
The five algorithms provided by NBL and HDA have been developed to assign a quality score

5.2.1
Laplace (Handcrafted Approach) by HDA
The ’Laplace’ algorithm includes several methods for evaluating image quality. It uses three
different techniques:
• Laplacian Variance: This method measures the variance from the image’s Laplacian
to indicate image sharpness. Generally, a higher variance means the image is clearer
and more detailed [68, 32].
• Sobel Operator: This method uses the Sobel operator to calculate the variance of the
image gradient in both horizontal and vertical directions. It helps identify edge strength
and for the overall image clarity [69, 32].
• Fourier Transform Analysis: This method uses a Fourier Transform to analyze the
image’s frequency components. It then adjusts the frequency domain to highlight cen­
tral frequencies and computes an inverse transform. The resulting magnitude spec­
trum helps assess the image’s blurriness [70, 32].
Each method outputs an overall score that quantifies the quality of each image based on its
sharpness, edge visibility, and blurriness.
5.2.2
CNN­R by HDA
The ”CNN­R” algorithm utilizes a deep learning approach to assess the quality of images.
This algorithm, based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs), is designed to process
preprocessed images divided into patches.
• Image Preparation: Each image is first divided into smaller patches, a technique that
effectively captures localized features and variations in image quality [71]. The size of
the patches varies depending on the dimensions specified for the analysis. This step
ensures that the CNN can analyze localized features of the images, which is important
for assessing quality across different areas of an image.
• Model Loading and Prediction: The model is loaded from a predefined path that
corresponds to specific face and patch sizes (50x50 as recommended) [32]. Once
the image patches are prepared, they are input into the CNN model to predict quality
scores, a method validated in previous studies for practical image quality assessment
[19]. These scores represent the model’s image quality evaluation based on learned
features from the training data.
• Output Generation: The predictions for each image patch are compiled, and a quality
score is then outputted for each image in a dataset.
This algorithm is particularly valuable for using deep learning to automatically discern and
quantify aspects of image quality that are important for applications requiring high levels of
image clarity and detail.
5.2.3

several traditional image processing techniques enhanced by modern computational meth­

5.4.4
Fairness Score Measure
To evaluate potential demographic biases in motion blur quality assessment, this thesis im­
plements the fairness measurement framework, which was recently developed by Dörsch et
al.[59]. Their paper introduces three approaches for quantifying fairness in biometric qual­
ity assessment: the Gini Coefficient­based Sample Quality Fairness Rate (SQFR), Low­
Weighted­Mean (LWM) Scores, and Mean­Discard­Gap (MDG).
The SQFR uses the ”Gini coefficient (GC)” statistical measure to produce a normalized fair­
ness score between 0 and 1, where 1 indicates perfect fairness across demographic groups.
The authors also mention an enhanced version of this measure, ”the Cubed Sample Quality
Fairness Rate (CSQFR),” which applies a cubic transformation to the result to better capture
bias in the assessment.
The LWM approach performs a linear weighting of quality scores in a given demographic
distribution, with lower quality scores receiving higher weights. This method accounts for
the fact that samples with lower scores would be rejected by more potential thresholds,
which could disadvantage certain demographic groups.
The MDG measure calculates the proportion of biometric samples below certain quality
thresholds, focusing on the most significant possible fairness difference per threshold be­
tween demographic groups. This approach ignores groups with intermediate scores and
considers only the extreme differences in quality assessment outcomes.
Following the author’s recommendation, they will consider using mean quality scores rather
than median values to produce more accurate results. The median score can mask subtle
demographic biases when distributions have similar central tendencies but different shapes.
These fairness measures will be applied to evaluate whether the NBL algorithms perform
consistently across all demographic groups in the MST­E dataset. The code to calculate the
fairness measures for all three methods is available in this GitHub repository3.

Preprocessing the MST­E dataset
Before running the images, the MST­E dataset should have synthetic motion blur applied,
which was done for the 409 images selected for analysis. ImageMagick allows customiza­
tion of the intensity of motion blur. ImageMagick’s ‘­virtual­pixel‘ setting ensured that edge
artifacts were minimized, providing a more accurate assessment of how motion blur affects
facial recognition in real­world scenarios. The intensities ranged from 0 (maximum blur) to
100 (no blur), with increments of 10, which is the same range as the EDAMB dataset. The
filename of each processed image was systematically updated to include the blur intensity
level, allowing for straightforward identification and correlation with its processing param­
eters. For instance, an original file named ‘sample.jpg‘ processed with a 40% blur level
would be renamed to ‘sample_blur40.jpg‘. The blur level will have a ”Synthetic Motion Blur
Ground Truth,” which will also be used to compare the results from the algorithm and the
Synthetic Motion Blur Ground Truth. The preprocessed images underwent face detection
using the SCRFD algorithm via the FIQAT library, with added padding (50 pixels vertical,
100 pixels horizontal) around detected faces. All images were standardized to 500x500 pix­
els, corresponding to EDAMB dataset specifications for consistent analysis conditions. The
processed dataset was categorized into three MST groups: light tones (MST 1­3) with 133
images, medium tones (MST 4­6) with 150 images, and dark tones (MST 7­10) with 125
images. The distribution across these groups demonstrated balanced representation for
comprehensive evaluation. The distribution of these images across the subjects and their
respective groups are detailed in the appendix table A.1, which provides a clear breakdown
of the number of images per subject within each MST grouping.

Discussion
7.1
Human Expert Opinion Ground Truths
Expert evaluations provide valuable insights into human perception of motion blur, which
was used to establish a human expert opinion ground truth that was used as one of the
benchmark metrics for algorithm comparison based on the ability to assess motion blur just
like humans. The scoring ranges used by the experts, varying from 10­90 to 0­100, re­
quired normalization to ensure all scores were comparable on a unified 0­100 scale. This
normalization was crucial as it enabled comprehensive analysis across varying expert opin­
ions, facilitating a standardized benchmark for evaluating motion blur detection algorithms
developed by NBL and HDA. However, as shown in Table 6.1, the original scoring scales
were adjusted to a uniform 0­100 range. It is important to note that experts using the 10­90
scale provided only nine quality scores, while those using the 0­100 scale had eleven distinct
scores. Even after normalization, this difference in the number of scores could influence the
final ground truth, as seen in the table 6.1. These variations in scoring depth might introduce
inconsistencies in the combined expert evaluations, which could affect the accuracy of the
ground truth for motion blur assessment.
7.1.1
Implications of Expert Scoring Variations
Statistical analyses, such as ANOVA and Z­scores, were essential in understanding each
expert’s scoring behavior. The ANOVA results, shown in Table 6.3, highlighted significant
differences in the scoring patterns of Expert74 and Expert94 compared to the other experts,
indicating unique interpretations of motion blur within the EDAMB dataset. These findings
are significant because they draw attention to potential biases in expert opinions, which
could skew algorithm evaluations if left unaddressed. This observation aligns with previous
findings by Schlett et al.[43], who noted similar expert variations in face image quality as­
sessment. Such systematic differences underscore the importance of using multiple experts
and robust fusion methods.
Furthermore, Z­score analysis confirmed that no individual scores were extreme outliers,
reinforcing the reliability of using expert assessments in forming the ground truth. Examining
the KL divergence for human expert opinions seen in table 6.4, the low values observed for
Expert94—closely aligned with the established ground truth—underscore the accuracy of
this expert’s perception in detecting motion blur. The EDC curve analysis for the five experts
in figure 6.2 also showed that Expert94 displayed a uniquely lower pAUC value, suggesting
superior performance in identifying motion blur.
The KL divergence analysis in table 4.1.6 revealed that, despite the statistical differences
in Expert94’s scoring pattern compared to other experts, it achieved the most substantial
alignment with the ground truth. This apparent contradiction between statistical distinctness
and ground truth alignment raises important questions about evaluating consensus in expert
scoring systems. While KL divergence primarily measures how closely an expert’s scoring
distribution aligns with the ground truth, it does not inherently reflect whether an image re­
mains usable despite a low­quality score. In contrast, the EDC curve evaluates whether
an image retains utility for recognition, even when deemed low quality by an expert. The
histogram analysis of expert scores in appendix A.2reveals that Expert94 assigned higher
quality scores than the other experts. Notably, Expert94 also had the lowest pAUC value,
suggesting that their assessment allowed the system to tolerate a higher degree of motion

==PAGE:55==
blur than the other four experts. This implies that the algorithms that face recognition sys­
tems can still recognize the images that have been given a low­quality score.
7.1.2
Limitation of the established Ground Truth
Both ground truth versions created through expert score fusion demonstrated lower pAUC
values than individual experts (0.00756 and 0.00763). Of these versions, Version 1, derived
using Z­score­based weighting, offers distinct advantages for broader applications since it
does not rely on dataset­specific metrics. This approach provides a scalable and general­
izable method for integrating additional expert opinions or applying the methodology to new
datasets. This indicates that the fusion process successfully reduced individual biases, re­
sulting in a more reliable reference standard. However, this finding should be interpreted
while considering certain limitations. The ground truth is based on the opinions of only five
experts, which raises questions about its representativeness and robustness. Expanding the
expert panel could introduce more diverse perspectives and potentially uncover additional
patterns in how motion blur is perceived—patterns that may not be evident with a smaller
group.
This limitation raises some critical methodological questions regarding the creation of ground
truth for face image quality assessment. How many experts are needed to achieve a gen­
uinely reliable consensus? If the panel was expanded, would the improved pAUC values
observed with fusion remain consistent, or would a broader range of expert opinions cre­
ate new challenges for reaching a consensus? These questions are particularly significant
when considering how this approach might scale to other datasets or applications in face
recognition.
It is important to emphasize that the established ground truth primarily serves as a reference
point for comparative analysis rather than an absolute measure of algorithm performance.
For example, CNN­R achieved an even lower pAUC value than the ground truth. In this con­
text, the ground truth acts as a standardized benchmark to evaluate the relative performance
of algorithms, helping to assess how well different approaches align with human perception
of motion blur. It does not, however, determine which algorithm performs best in absolute
terms. This nuanced role of the ground truth underscores the complexity of evaluating face
image quality assessment algorithms and highlights the need to prioritize the pAUC values
for the algorithms.
7.2
Algorithm Performance Analysis on EDAMB Dataset
The performance analysis of individual algorithms on the EDAMB dataset revealed different
patterns and capabilities across different architectural approaches. The Densenet variants
(161 and 169) aligned consistently with the Human Expert opinion Ground Truth, achieving

7.2.1
Significance of Algorithm Fusion Results
The fusion analysis revealed that combining multiple algorithms can improve performance
compared to individual approaches. The most successful combination, CNN­R with Densenet161,
achieved a pAUC value of 0.00615 using Z­score weighting, representing a 1.3% improve­
ment over the best individual algorithm performance. This improvement suggests that differ­

effectiveness of the Z­score weighting method in fusion indicates the importance of adaptive
weighting schemes that can account for algorithm­specific strengths and weaknesses.
Limitations and Potential Improvements
Despite the promising results achieved through algorithm fusion, fusing the algorithms has
limitations. The effort to run multiple algorithms in fusion scenarios may present practical
challenges, especially in real­time applications. Moreover, while the CNN­R and CNN­R
with Densenet161 models showed outstanding results with the EDAMB Dataset, they did not
perform superior to other algorithms on the MST­E dataset. When the same fusion approach
used with the EDAMB dataset was applied to the MST­E dataset, it did not perform better
than the individual algorithms, as illustrated by the EDC curve in Appendix A.19.
Potential improvements could include the development of hybrid architectures that inherently
combine the strengths of different approaches, such as integrating frequency analysis within
deep learning models. Further research into adaptive fusion methods that dynamically adjust
algorithm weights based on image characteristics could also enhance system performance.
7.3
Effectiveness of Different Evaluation Metrics on MST­E
Our analysis of quality assessment algorithms using two distinct evaluation approaches ­
KL divergence and EDC curves ­ reveals an intriguing contradiction that merits careful con­
sideration. These different evaluation methods lead to notably different conclusions about
algorithm performance, highlighting the complexity of quality assessment evaluation.
The KL divergence analysis suggests a clear hierarchy among the algorithms, with Densenet169
(0.0881) and Densenet161 (0.0958) showing superior performance in matching the statis­
tical distribution of ground truth quality scores. CNN­R demonstrates moderate divergence
(0.3986), while Fourier Transform (44.6034) and Laplace (2.1855) show substantial devi­
ation from the ground truth distribution. This ordering would suggest that the Densenet
architectures are our most effective algorithms based on the synthetic motion blur ground
truth.
7.3.1
Relationship Between Statistical and Practical Performance
The EDC curve analysis offers a different perspective, focusing on algorithms’ practical abil­
ity to identify and discard low­quality samples for improved recognition performance. While
Fourier Transform (pAUC: 0.01002) and Laplace (pAUC: 0.01007) exhibit slightly lower
pAUC values, bringing them closer to the ground truth baseline (pAUC: 0.00997), these
differences are minimal. Given the minimal variation, pAUC alone cannot definitively deter­
mine which algorithm performs best on the MST­E dataset.
KL divergence measures how well an algorithm’s quality scores statistically align with the
ground truth distribution, essentially evaluating the algorithm’s ability to replicate the pat­
tern of quality score assignment. In contrast, EDC curves evaluate the practical utility of
these quality scores ­ their effectiveness in identifying samples that, when discarded, lead
to improved recognition performance.

The divergence between these results suggests that closely matching the statistical distri­

The Densenet variants demonstrated strong alignment through KL Divergence metrics across

to be more problematic than they were.
7.4.1
Dataset­Specific Performance Patterns
The analysis reveals distinct performance patterns specific to each dataset’s characteristics.
In the EDAMB dataset, algorithms demonstrated broader variation in performance metrics,
possibly due to natural motion blur’s diverse and unpredictable nature. With its controlled
synthetic blur, the MST­E dataset produced more consistent performance patterns across
algorithms, with notably more minor variations in pAUC values (within 0.00025 of each other).
However, the size of the dataset might influence these results. The reduced number of test
cases could limit the dataset’s ability to expose subtle differences in algorithm performance,
contributing to the more uniform metrics observed.

Future Work
The current ground truth for the EDAMB dataset is based on a limited number of expert
evaluations. Future work should focus on expanding the experts to improve the represen­
tativeness of the ground truth. Increasing the number of experts would provide a broader
perspective on motion blur perception and reduce the impact of individual biases. Addi­
tionally, further research is needed to determine the optimal number of experts required to
establish a reliable consensus without diminishing the consistency of the fusion process.
While the MST­E dataset provided valuable insights into fairness across different skin tone
groups, future work should develop a more comprehensive dataset, including a more exten­
sive collection of real­world motion blur images across diverse skin tones, better simulating
an actual real setting. The current limitation is 409 images in MST­E compared to 1154
images in EDAMB after preprocessing. However, the EDAMB dataset lacks a broader skin
tone group. Expanding the dataset size and broader demographic groups could reveal more
nuanced performance differences between algorithms.
Different evaluation metrics indicated varying strengths among the tested algorithms by NBL
and HDA. Some of them aligned well statistically with the ground truth (Kl Divergence for
MST­E 6.7 and EDAMB 6.6). In contrast, others performed better in improving recognition
performance (EDC curve for EDAMB 6.5). Therefore, it could benefit from developing hybrid
approaches that integrate statistical alignment and the EDC curve. It is important since it
shows how a biometric system’s error rate changes as it discards low­quality images. The
success of CNN­R and Densenet161 fusion in the EDAMB dataset (seen in the result section
6.6, but not in the MST­E (seen in appendix A.19) could indicate the need for more adaptive
fusion methods that can perform consistently across different datasets.
The fairness metrics showed promising results. A good idea is to investigate ways to main­
tain fairness while improving overall performance. This could include developing training
methodologies that explicitly consider fairness metrics during model development and creat­
ing a fusion approach that considers both performance and fairness in its weighting method.

9
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the performance of state­of­the­art FIQA algorithms in detecting
motion blur in face images, comparing algorithmic performance against ground truth derived
from human expert opinions. The study utilized the EDAMB dataset for primary performance
evaluation and the MST­E dataset to assess demographic fairness.
The evaluation revealed that the CNN­R model consistently demonstrated the most reliable
performance for motion bur detection on the EDAMB dataset. It exhibited high consistency
with the human expert ground truth and achieved the best results when evaluated using the
EDC curves. While the CNN­R model did not have the lowest KL divergence, this discrep­
ancy was minor and did not significantly affect its performance. The CNN­R model’s strong
correlation with expert opinions and stable performance across varying levels of motion blur
makes it a promising candidate for real­world applications.
When assessing performance using the MST­E dataset, none of the algorithms stood out
significantly. The CNN­R model did not achieve the lowest pAUC value in this dataset, yet the
differences in pAUC values among all the algorithms were minimal. This indicates that the
CNN­R model maintains competitive performance even when applied to a demographically
diverse dataset. Additionally, fairness evaluation showed good fairness scores across skin
tone groups, suggesting that the CNN­R is not biased toward specific demographic groups.
Algorithm fusion was also explored as a potential performance enhancement technique.
For the EDAMB dataset, fusing CNN­R with Densenet resulted in a slight increase in per­
formance with its lower pAUC value. However, the MST­E dataset demonstrated a different
behavior, where fusion did not improve performance, highlighting the dataset­dependent na­
ture of algorithm fusion. Therefore, CNN­R alone remains the better choice in this context.
To create the ground truth for the EDAMB dataset, expert scores were aggregated using
two different weighting methods: one based on Z­scores and the other on pAUC values.
The Z­score method has the advantages of being not dataset dependent and minimizing
the influence of outliers, so it is considered to be a better choice for Human Expert opinion
fusion.
The findings suggest that the CNN­R model is the most effective algorithm for motion blur
detection in face images when evaluated on the EDAMB dataset. While it did not have
the lowest pAUC value in the MST­E dataset, the minimal performance differences across
all algorithms and the absence of demographic bias support its use in diverse operational
settings.

Version 1 uses the Z score as a weighting method:
1
BEGIN
2
LOAD data FROM Excel file '/path/to/excelfile/file.xlsx'
3
4
CREATE a copy of the dataset as normalized_data
5
FOR EACH column in data:
6
FIND the minimum value (X_min) in the column
7
FIND the maximum value (X_max) in the column
8
NORMALIZE column values using formula: (value - X_min) / (X_max - X_min)
* 100
9
10
CALCULATE z_scores AS (normalized_data - mean(normalized_data)) /
standard_deviation(normalized_data)
11
12
FOR EACH Z-score in z_scores:
13
IF Z-score ABSOLUTE value is greater than 3:
14
SET weight TO 0
15
ELSE:
16
CALCULATE weight AS 1 / (1 + MAX(0, ABS(Z-score) - 1))
17
18
COMPUTE weighted_sum AS SUM of (weights * normalized_data) for each row
19
COMPUTE weights_sum AS SUM of weights for each row
20
HANDLE cases where weights_sum = 0 BY SETTING it to NaN
21
COMPUTE weighted_avg AS weighted_sum / weights_sum
22
23
ADD a new column 'Fused_Score' to normalized_data with values from
weighted_avg
24
25
PRINT normalized_data
26
27
SAVE normalized_data TO Excel file 'path/to/output/folder/
fused_scores_output.xlsx' WITHOUT including row indices
28
END

Comparing Algorithms with Human Expert Opinions
This thesis was submitted to the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in partial fulfillment of the
requirements for the Master of Science in Engineering (MSc Eng.) degree. The work was conducted
between September 2024 and March 2025 under the supervision of Tyge, Christoph, And Wassim.
The research investigates motion blur detection algorithms in face recognition systems by compar­
ing algorithmic predictions with human expert evaluations. Using the EDAMB and MST­E datasets,
the study assesses algorithm performance and fairness, contributing to the advancement of more
accurate and unbiased biometric systems.
