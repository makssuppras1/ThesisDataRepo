Abstract (English)
As large scale networks become more and more prevalent in our society, tasks such as
link prediction and network visualization becomes increasingly important. Developing
efficient models for large-scale network is vital to handle this task.
A method to
handle these issues comes in the form of Latent Space Models, which can model a
given network in an Euclidean social space for easy interpretation and analysis. In
this thesis four Latent Space Models are presented and tested. The models extends
an already popular Latent Space model with the addition of specific biases and a
distance measure that accounts for network directionality. Furthermore the models
are presented as a maximum likelihood optimization problem solvable by gradient
descent. Minimizing the model with PyTorch the power of GPU’s are utilized, yielding
a faster model. The models are tested on three standard datasets. Finally the model is
tested against state of the art methods where it wins out in predictive power. Besides
providing strong link prediction results the suggested methods also provides a visual
and easy interpretable visual representation of the networks ”social relations”.

1.1
World of Graphs
When the new millennium began, so began the rise of several world-changing busi-
nesses. Businesses such as Facebook, Twitter, Google and Amazon. These internet
based tech-companies all share a common thing: The heart of their business is con-
nected data: Graphs. It is now almost two decades later and graphs are everywhere.
Facebook were built upon the idea, that even though there might be value in dis-
crete data and information about people (Such as their names, adresses and occupa-
tions). The real value lies in the relationships and ties between these people. The
founder Mark Zuckerberg built an entire empire around this notion of capturing these
relationships in a structure, a structure that later became known as the Social Graph.
Likewise it was not storing and processing discrete web documents and pages that
lead to Google’s major success. They build their business on how these documents
and pages are connected. They captured the web graph and by doing so they have
become one of the most impactful companies in the world.
In this present day, graphs are not only used by the web-giants to create value.
Many of the largest logistic companies in the world employ graphs to route physical
parcels in real time. Major airlines utilizes graph data to manage their media contents
meta-data. Under utilized just a couple of decades ago, graphs are now prevalent in
industries as diverse as media, oil & gas, retail, healthcare and many more [1].
Graphs aids with addressing a great macroscopic trend in business.
How do you
generate new insights, given a set of complex and dynamic relationships, resulting in
a competitive advantage?
Whether the aim is to comprehend and understand relationships between genes and
proteins, customers or data center networks. The ability to analyze and act upon
highly connected data stored as a graph will be key for companies in order to stay
competitive and outperform competitors in the coming years.

What is a Graph?
Formally a graph can be explained as a collection of vertices and edges. Or put in
another way: A set of nodes and the relationship than ties them together.
Graphs can be written as an ordered pair G = (V, E) where V is a set of vertices,
nodes or points and E is a set of edges, arcs or lines. Furthermore E are 2-element
subsets of V , meaning that an edge is always associated with two vertices. Here is
two important distinctions between two types of graphs.
• An Undirected Graph is a graph where edges have no orientation. i.e., an
edge (x, y) is equivalent to edge (y, x). They are not ordered pairs, but instead
unordered pairs. The maximum amount of edges an undirected graph can have
is therefore:

• A Directed Graph or digraph is a graph where the edges have a orientation
where (x, y) ̸= (y, x) is a possibility. The case where a directed graph is sym-
metric i.e., if all (x, y) pairs also are inverted, corresponds to an undirected
graph.

1.3
Use Cases
Only recently have we begun to scratch the surface of the predictive power of the
social network. In the book Connected [5] it is shown how it is possible to model and
predict a persons behaviour by analyzing the given persons social ties.
Applying graphs in social context allows organizations to leverage information be-
tween people, and not just about them.
This enables the organizations to better
predict behaviour and facilitate collaboration, thus giving them an operational ad-
vantage and a competitive one. Social networks can be applied to better understand
and identify both the direct and indirect relationships between people and groups.
Given the context which a person or a group react to, be it reviewing, rating or dis-
covering new people and the things they like. By modelling and understanding this
behaviour, who interacts with whom, who is more likely to do x or y based on aggre-
gate behaviour of the system being analyzed, valuable insights are being generated
that sheds light to the intrinsic forces that influences an actors behaviours.
Graph relations can be either explicitly or implicitly given.
In the social context
an explicit relation is a direct tie between two actors. This could for an example be a
friendship on facebook or a follow on twitter. Implicit relations are arguably the more
interesting relationships. Here we look at relationships that are derived through other
relationships. This could for an example be that person A does not know person B

They are connected through an intermediary. Such relation through intermediaries

this lies in the realm of latent variable models.
1.4
Latent Variable Models
A latent variable is a variable that are not inherently observable, meaning that the
variable can not be directly observed but are rather inferred through other directly
measured variables. This is often done by applying a mathematical model. Such a
model is often referred to as a Latent Variable Model. Their overarching goal is to
explain observed variables using their latent counterparts.
One form of latent variable models is the: Stochastic Block Model (SBM) [6],
which aims to model the interaction patterns that goes on in a given network. In
a protein-protein network the proteins exhibits various patterns that can be used to
==PAGE:17==
1.4 Latent Variable Models
5
infer their relationships with other proteins. An SBM can be used to model such
behaviour, by learning the networks latent group interaction patterns.
In a protein-protein network connection, or edges between pairs of protein is usually
an indication of a biological function they both are involved in. In more complex and
diverse biological systems, a single protein will be part of many processes and there-
fore have many many connections. The SBM offers a way to model these complex
behaviours and group the proteins interactions into k different ”groups”. This is done
by learning the posterior distribution for the membership of each protein to each of
the k groups and the probabilities for each group to interact. This provides a very
valuable tool for summarizing the relational data between the proteins.
The SBM is a close to cousin to the Latent Feature Models (LFM), where in
the SBM model the number of latent communities needs to be externaly specified,
they do not need to be in the LFM case.
In an LFM each node has a feature vector attached to it, which is unobserved, and
therefore latent. Relations between nodes are then determined by looking at the inter-
action between these latent feature vectors. They are powerful since they can model
underlying network structure, and discover relational ties between entities. LFM’s
represents each node i as a k dimensional feature vector zi ∈Rk where a function
ψ(zi, zj) measure the similarity between node i’s and node j’s latent vector. Depend-
ing on the ψ function we get different latent variable models. Based on the idea of
eigenvalue decomposition where: ψ(zi, zj) = z⊤
i Dzj, where D is a diagonal matrix
constructed by estimation of observed data, we get the Latent Eigen Model [7]. If
ψ(zi, zj) = z⊤
i Λzj we get the definition of the Matrix Factorization Approach
[8]. Finally if ψ(zi, zj) = −d(zi, zj) where d is the distance function, we get the
Latent Space Model [9].
1.4.1
Latent Space
As stated in the previous subsections, networks represent relational information amongst
actors. Capturing this relational information is highly valuable for various applica-
tions.
One way of capturing this information is suggested in [9] by inferring an
unobserved social space
We can construct a network that consists of a set of n actors, denoted by a rela-
tional tie yi,j, measured on an ordered pair of individual actors i, j = 1, ..., n. In
the simple case yi,j is just a binary variable, giving us an indication if there is a tie
between actor i and j. This tie could be some feature of interest such as collabora-
tion, transmission of information or friendship. A common way to represent this data
would be by an n×n adjacency matrix. If there is a relationship between actor i and
j, it is denoted by i →j.This is assuming an undirected graph. By the nature of these
problems they often exhibit transitive behaviour, such that (i →j, j →k ⇒i →k)
==PAGE:18==
6
1 Introduction
with high probability. This is a clear indication that if such a relationship is inferred
i and k should be close to each other in a given ”social” or ”latent” space. A subset
of actors in a given graph with close ties, could be an indication of a group or cluster,
and therefore they should be placed closer in our social space.
This aforementioned social space can be modelled by applying a Maximum Likeli-
hood Framework where Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedures are used
for making the inference of the latent positions. This latent space solution provides
an easily interpretable model that serves as a spatial representation of a networks
relationship. Furthermore when applied in low-dimensional Euclidean space it is also
easy to visualize. The model is flexible and can easily generalize to model relation-
ships with varying strengths. A major set back to the model is it’s high computational
cost at O(n2) (where n is the number of nodes) which comes due to the structure of
the likelihood function. This high computational cost makes it infeasible to apply on
large networks.
The computational cost issues of the model proposed in [9] has later been addressed
in [10]. In the paper a case-control likelihood is suggested as an unbiased estima-
tor for the full likelihood. This Case-control likelihood is then used to replace the
full likelihood in the MCMC estimation of the social/latent space. This substitution
causes the computational complexity to drop to O(n) at each MCMC update. That
is currently the state of the art method for latent space modelling.
One of the main issues with using likelihood modelling of networks is they are not
defined by the sparsity of the network but instead on the entire structure - not just
the connections but also the non-connections.
Once a network is modelled in a social/latent space, tasks that was otherwise complex
and difficult to carry out with precision becomes readily available. Such as community
detection, collaborative filtering and most important for this thesis: Link Prediction.
1.5
Link Prediction
One of the most interesting subject within network analysis and theory is link pre-
diction. Link prediction is the inference of the existence of a link between two nodes
based on the already existing links in the networks and on the nodes properties.
The solution of this problem requires the approaches and techniques to be designed
in a way where they extract implicit information, already present in the network, to
identify potential new links. Aside from detecting new links the method can also be
used to model and evaluate evolution of networks through time.
Link prediction has already had its fair share of success and been successfully utilized
in biological networks to predict otherwise unknown interactions between proteins[11].
==PAGE:19==
1.5 Link Prediction
7
Instead of going through all possible interactions blindly, scientists can now search
through the possible interaction suggested by the link prediction algorithm, which
has led to a significant decline in the cost of running empirical approaches. Link
predictions have also been applied to model highly dynamic systems, such as tele-
phone networks or email networks. The results that link prediction provides are for
most people widely present in their every day lives, from friend suggestions on their
favourite social media page [12] to product recommendations when they are online
shopping [13].
Link prediction is based upon the empirical evidence that two nodes are more likely
to have interaction if they share a similarity.
Similarity in this context must be
understood as an abstract context, since it can vary from network to network. Do-
main knowledge of a given network is therefore crucial in order to define a similarity
measure. In most observed network domains, it has been noted that nodes have a
tendency to cluster in highly connected communities. This has caused the common
definition of similarity between nodes to be formulated as the amount of relevant
direct or indirect paths between them.
1.5.1
Link prediction applications
Link prediction algorithms can be found in a wide variety of applications spanning
multiple different fields. Simply put: Any domain where entities have interactions
that can be described in a structural manner, link prediction can provide potential
benefits.
Given a system with millions of entities, it would make sense to assume that new
actions a given entity might take, would be similar to those action of entities it is
similar to. This could for an example be a collaborative filtering system. In such
a system link prediction can be utilized to provide better recommendations [14]. A
similar use of link prediction is found in social networks. The users of these networks
expects an effective and simple mechanism in order to find acquaintances among the
sea of registered users. Most social networks use link prediction for this exact task
of suggesting new acquaintances with high accuracy. Social network analysis have
also been applied with great success in unlikely places. As an example it has been
used to model and analyze the structure of terrorist and criminal networks. Through
link prediction researchers have found that the topology of a criminal network can be
modelled and then used to predict new links in the network, assisting the authorities
in anticipating criminal actions [15].
An additional application of link prediction can be found in the study of collaboration
in scientific co-authorship networks. The data for such network is easily accessible
since most journal indexing sites have their collections made public. Link prediction
is being used in this system, to help predict how certain research fields will evolve
over time. This is done by predicting which groups of authors could potentially col-

Link prediction has also been used to improve marketing studies, helping campaigns
go viral and marketers to reach a wider audience [17].
1.6
Contribution
One of the main hurdles of performing link prediction is achieving a decent balance
between the information used to perform the prediction, and the complexity of the
algorithm that is being used to collect that information. Since many real world net-
works usually consists of million or even hundreds of million nodes, the techniques
applied to perform the link prediction must be highly efficient. This efficiency is often
a trade-off, since considering only local information could cause the system to provide
poor predictions, especially if the network being considered is very sparse. An answer
to these problems is suggested in this thesis as an LSM presented as a log likelihood
function treated as an optimization problem solvable by gradient descent methods.
This enables the use of the vast computational power unleashed by high level GPU
acceleration frameworks.
In this work it will be examined if latent space models can be scaled to large scale
networks, through the utilization of GPU computing. The Latent Space Model is
extended to fit directed networks. The proposed models are then tested, to see which
is the best fit for a given network. This is done through implementing and testing
the various proposed latent space methods on three datasets of varying size, from the
SNAP project [18]. Furthermore it is tested if the method can make strong predic-
tions in low dimensional Eucdlidean space where visualization is possible. Finally the
method is measured against state of the art link prediction methods on a common
SNAP dataset.
In the Latent Space section 2.1 of the Method chapter, the state of the art in la-
tent space models will be outlined and the model worked on in this thesis will be
presented. In section 2.2 link prediction is described and explained how it is used as
a metric for the latent space models performance. In section 2.3 the PyTorch frame-
work is briefly explained and how it is used for this thesis is also outlined. In the same
section an example of how to optimize code to run faster on a CUDA GPU platform
through using a readily available PyTorch tool is also shown. In section 2.4 there is
a walk through of computing a pairwise distance matrix on GPU. In section 2.5 the
main problem of the proposed latent space model is touched upon: memory. Ways
of tackling this issue is presented. In section 2.6 the Adam optimizer is examined.
Finally in section 2.7 ROC curves with AUC scoring is examined as a measure for
model precision on the link prediction task.
In chapter 3 synthetic data for testing the model is presented and the 3 SNAP data
sets are examined.

2.1
Latent Space Models
Latent Space Models hinge on the simple assumption, that in some social networks
the probability of an edge existing between nodes increases as the characteristics
between them becomes more similar. Given a subset of nodes in a social graph, as-
suming these nodes share common properties (such as social ties denoted by edges)
we would assume they share a ”social sphere” and should thus be near each other in
a constructed latent space where distance is a metric for similarity and thus parity.
This idea was first introduced in [19] and later build upon in [9].
The main idea of the mentioned article is that that the observed relation between
nodes are determined by some unobserved latent characteristics. These characteris-
tics can be seen as the nodes unobserved latent positions in an Euclidean space. We
can model the probability that two nodes share a link by determining the distance
between their latent positions. Within the nodes the relational ties are independent.
For a network of size N such a latent space model boils down to:

Link Prediction
Networks are often dynamic entities that changes over time, new nodes are added,
new edges are formed. Networks more often than not follow some inherent topological
structure in forming these new connections. Link prediction is the task of predicting
these connections that are formed in the future. Accurate link prediction models are
highly dependant on being able to describe the topology of a network well, in order
to get good results. Therefore, if you have a well functioning link prediction model
you must also have a good description of the networks topology and structure. A
solid latent space model should be able to model both topology and structure of a
given network and should therefore be ideal for this task. This is why link prediction
is used as a measurement for model performance and accuracy.
This is done by creating a test adjacency matrix Ytest.
Ytest is created by removing 10% of the 1’s (links) in the adjacency matrix of the
graph, that is used for training the latent space model. All the connections (denoted
by 1’s) in the Y matrix is found, and 10% of them are selected randomly. The posi-
tions are then stored as indexes in a vector: OneIndexes. The 1’s are then removed
from the Y matrix. This process yields the Ytest matrix, which the model is then
trained on.
In order to get a gauge of how well the model is doing, only looking at links is
not entirely sufficient. The model should also be able to predict non-links. For this

network: Z which is of dimensions n × k, where n is the number of nodes in the

2.5.1
Sparse Matrices
Almost all real life networks are sparse. Sparse in the network context, means that
each node is not linked to every other node. Meaning that the links l does not reach
the number of possible connections n2 i.e, l < n2.
Sparse networks typically share a similar topography, namely they follow a scale-free
node degree distribution. This causes the network to have few nodes that are strongly
connected with many links, and many sparsely connected nodes with few links [24].
Knowing the networks have this inherent sparse structure, a sparse matrix can be
used as a way to store the otherwise n2 memory intensive adjacency matrix, as a
more convenient, memory efficient data type.

A sparse matrix is a matrix where most of the elements are 0. A dense matrix is
where all elements are ̸= 0. The sparsity of a given matrix is calculated as all zero
elements divided with the total number of elements. Which is:

A sparse matrix is typically stored in a 2-d array. Where each entry in the array
represents a single element of the matrix it is representing. Typically denoted as aij
where i is the row index and j is the column index. The trade of in this structure,
is that typical matrix operation becomes increasingly complex, since indexing and
similar operations become non-trivial.
Many different sparse matrix formats exists, they can be grouped in two categories:
• Supports efficient modification: Dictionary of keys (DOK), List of lists (LIL),
Coordinate List (COO).
• Supports efficient acces and matrix operations: Compressed Sparse Rows(CSR),
Compressed Sparse Column(CSC).
DOK: storing values in a dictionary with (row,cloumn) as keys, and the value of
the (row,column) pair, as the value. Format is good for incrementally constructing a
sparse matrix. It is however poor for accessing values in lexicographical since dictio-
naries are unordered structures.
LIL: One list is stored per row, where the entries contains the column index and
value. This structure is also good for constructing a sparse matrix incrementally.
COO: takes a little bit from both worlds of LIL and DOK, and stores a list of
(row, column, value) tuples. These tuples are ideally sorted by row index, and then
column index, to improve access times.
CSR and CRS: Similar to COO but the compression is done into 3 one dimen-
sional arrays. In the CSR case the 3 lists contains the values, extend of rows and
column indices. In that respective order. In the CSC case the 3 lists contains the
values, extend of columns and row indices. This format is ideal for matrix operations
such as multiplication, several efficient algorithms for matrix operations with these
two data types exists [25].
As a final note: LIL matrices are used in this projects for loading the datasets,
and handling the very large graphs (100.000) nodes or more. This is because they
are efficient for constructing the sparse matrix from the snap file. Further more, they

Batch Slicing
Looking at eq. (2.12), and noting that the graphics card running the model ”only”
have 11gb of ram. Networks over size ≈25000 are to large to be in memory if the
dimensions of the latent space is 2. This is handled by taking inspiration from the
machine learning world, the idea of mini batching and the case control idea from [10].
While the data is not structured in a way that allows a direct application of the
mini-batching concept, the fundamental key points still remains. Batching the data
means, that the entire data set set does not need to be handled and stored in RAM,
saving a vast amount of memory in the case of the proposed latent space model.
Furthermore when running gradient descent on the entire dataset averaging over po-
tentially enormous amount of information. Optimizers has a tendency to get stuck
in bad points such as local minima or saddle points. Using mini batching, noise is
introduced to the system, since it is not based on the entire dataset but only a smaller
batch. This ensures easier escape from local minima and saddle points [26].
Taking the idea of mini batching and carrying it over to the network case is not
straightforward. The data does not come in the form of single data points or vectors,
the data consists of nodes and their relations. Each node is meaningless without its
connections, most times in real life networks, few nodes tend to be heavily connected.
If all the nodes and their connections are not accounted for, essential structure and
topology of the network is not en-capsuled which will lead to a poor model. Take
the Condensed Matter Physics network Table 3.2 as an example. The largest Weakly
Connected Component consists of 92% of all nodes in the network. Looking at iso-
lated nodes would not make much sense.

Optimizers - Gradient Descent
Gradient descent methods is the corner stone of many machine learning applica-
tions. An optimization algorithm based upon a convex function, which parameters
are changed in an iterative process to achieve minimization of the function (or max-

the function is managed by a bunch of knobs and levers, that can be put in different
positions. Depending on the positions of these parameters the function will give var-
ious outputs. Gradient descent help us (in small incremental steps) find the optimal
position of all these knobs and levers.

3.1
Synthetic Data
The Synthetic data is supplied by a function based on the Barabási–Albert model.
This model was chosen because most of the networks in SNAP is scale-free networks.
Meaning that they exhibit scale free degree distributions. One of the main reasons it
is good at simulating real world networks, is that the model draws upon a powerful
assumption: Preferential attachment. Meaning that the more connection a node has,
the more likely it is to receive further links. This concept can be translated into a
social-network context by the following situation: A link between person A and B
denotes that person A and B is acquainted. If a node in the network has many links it
means that, that person has many relations. When someone from the outside enters
the community he is more likely to become friends/acquainted with the already ”pop-
ular” people/nodes in the network. This is the situation that preferential attachment
can model relatively well.
To simulate preferential attachment we simply start with an initial connected net-
work with m0 nodes. We then begin to add nodes to the network, one at a time.
Each new connection entered in the network is connected to m ≤m0 existing nodes,
with a probability that is given by how many links the existing nodes already posses.
The probability a new node is connected to node i is given by:
pi =
ki
∑

ki is the degree of the i’th node and the sum is over all nodes already in the network.
This attachment process, ensures that nodes that already have many connections re-
tains preferential attachment.
Since this type of networks can model real world data relatively well, it is assumed
that if the model can generalize to this kind of model, it should be able to generalize
on real world data as well.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion and
Conclusion
A solution to scaling Latent Space model to large scale networks was proposed and
implemented in this thesis. The model was successfully applied to a large scale net-
work with over 400.000 nodes.
Furthermore by using GPU computation through
gradient descent methods, the model is significantly faster than its counterparts. By
using the suggested batch slicing the amount of computation done at each step of
the likelihood summation was drastically reduced. Nothing should be in the way of
applying the method on even larger networks. Aside from one issue: The model was
successfully applied to the two ”smaller” networks, Wiki votes and Condensed matter
physics. Here the adjacency matrix was small enough to be in memory. With larger
networks such as the Amazon co-purchasing network, sparse matrix representation
is necessary. The sparse matrix implementation in PyTorch (and also in Tensorflow)
does not currently support indexing, so slicing in a fast efficient manner using CUDA
is currently not possible. This results in the larger model having significantly lower
GPU utilization than the smaller ones, by a factor of almost 12. Whereas the Ama-
zon network runs took between 2 and 3 days of computation time, the wiki network
was run in a matter of minutes and the condensed matter physic network runs took
around half an hour.
The method has an advantage over the existing latent space model in that it can
take account of directionality. It is shown through the various plots of the latent
space, that even large networks such as the Amazon co-purchasing network can be
visualized, even with its directionality.
The model was tested against state of the art results on the AstroPh [35] SNAP
dataset, where it beat the other contesters. It is therefore shown that not only can
the proposed model capture an efficient latent representation of the network, but also
deliver state of the art link prediction performance.
Overall the model is flexible and allows easily generalization to a network by being
able to model multiple relational ties. As proven by the tests the model deals easily
with missing data (connections), atleast when the data is missing at random. Fur-
thermore the model, transitive in nature is a great fit for especially social networks

5.1
Synthetic Data
First taking a look at the synthetic data. The synthetic data is constructed in a way
to mimic the structure of real life networks, whilst also being grouped in clusters,
so the results can be visually inspected to be a correct approximation. Both in the
3 cluster and 5 cluster case the model fits the data well as observed in figures 4.1
and 4.3. The latent space representation clearly show the underlying structure of the
synthetic networks.
5.2
Bias
Lets examine the specific row/column bias. Looking at tables: 4.2 and 4.4 and the
corresponding ROC AUC score per iteration plot, it can be seen that some serious
over fitting is happening, the model does not converge. The hypothesis for this is
that the extra wiggle room added by the specific biases means that the distances in
the model can be minimized to the extreme, causing a latent space model where all
nodes are densely squeezed together, causing the model to be bad at generalizing to
the networks overall structure. In the runs of the Condensed Matter Physics dataset
the specific bias model was stopped before it started dipping. Even when at peak
performance it is still outperformed by the single bias model. This is most likely
because the single bias model gets more iteration to find the ”perfect” model whereas
the specific bias model AUC score starts dipping before reaching this state.
5.3
Directed/Undirected
Comparing the results from table 4.1 and 4.3 it is seen that the directed model
outscores the undirected at all k’s, most impressively the directed model scores rela-
tively high on k = 1 with a score of 89%.
More importantly the directed model provides a really powerful way of visualizing
the network data as can be seen in figures: 4.7 4.8. These two visualizations shows
the latent space representation of the wiki votes network. Both are the same model
ran two different times. Since the distance measure is invariant under scaling and
rotation an almost infinite amount of shapes and scales exists that can produce the
same results. In both figures the test AUC score was converged around 92%. Each
user in the network is represented by two positions in latent space. One position for
outgoing connections, another for ingoing connections. A grey line between these two
positions connect the singular user. Since the orange dots represent users tendencies
to receive votes, proximity to orange dots means higher chance of receive votes from

5.4 dk representability
dk representability was hypothesised in [9] and the definition can be seen in eq: (2.5).
It is a way to determine if it is possible to model a network successfully at a given
dimension k.
Looking at figure 4.23 for the directed model it is observed that already at k = 2
a great fit for the network is found. At k = 6 no improvement in AUC score is found.
For the undirected model it is observed that there is no significant difference in per-
formance between k = 6 and k = 10. Both solutions in 2d and 3d (k = 2, k = 3)
space provides solid AUC scores. The same trend can be observed in the undirected
model results for the Condensed Matter Physics network seen in figure 4.32, descent
results for lower k’s but a higher increase is seen when k rises. Finally looking at 4.7
the same trend is seen. Here the increase in AUC is even more steep when k rises.
It looks like the more complex a model is, the larger k needs to be in order to make
a solid expressive latent space model. With that being said in all cases the 2 and
3 dimensional latent spaces still provides relatively solid AUC scores, which is very
nifty since they are easily visualized.

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
6.1
Batch slizing
An exploration of how batch sizes affects AUC convergence, would shed light on the
importance of choosing the correct batch size.
In this thesis the batch size has simply been chosen to be the maximum size the
GPU memory would allow, using eq: (2.12). This might not be necessary. Smaller
batch sizes results in faster run time due to less computation in each iteration. Smaller
batch sizes were tested and it did not seem to change the results much, more testing is
needed to make decisive conclusion. A smarter, more efficient way of picking batches
might also improve model accuracy and speed. Inspiration could be drawn from work
in: [10], where the sampling of nodes were done using a case-control based approach.
6.2
Sparse Matrix Slicing
As mentioned in the conclusion, the implementation of the model does currently not
support full GPU utilization of very large scale networks. This could be remedied
by implementing the slice operation to the sparse tensors in the PyTorch back-end.
PyTorch is an open source project, meaning everyone is able to contribute.
For
this thesis a function for slicing sparse matrices, that supports high parallelism, was
tested and implemented in python. It utilizes a dictionary, that once constructed
can be called by a lambda function that can distribute the work, writing the results
directly into a new sparse matrix. This code would need to be translated to c++
and integrated with the PyTorch back-end. With this class for sparse torch tensors,
it is highly likely to gain maximum GPU utilization for the very large scale models,
reducing computation time drastically.
6.3
Dynamic Networks
Fitting the model to a dynamic model would be an obvious next step. Dynamic in the
temporal sense, where a network can be represented at time t and the model is used
to predict the model at time t + 1. In such a situation link prediction has significant
value and great applications in many different areas. Using the temporal information
