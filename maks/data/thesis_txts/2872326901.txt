Abstract 
 
The textile industry is characterised by a take-make-waste model, where cheap mass 
production enables fast-changing trends at the cost of significant environmental pressures and 
human rights concerns. Garments are often of lower quality and used less frequently. With 
growing volumes of textile waste and ecological impact, the European Union is now working 
across several policy areas to reduce the environmental footprint of textile production, which 
has more than doubled in fibre volume since 2000. Under the Strategy for Sustainable and 
Circular Textiles, measures are being developed to improve circularity, transparency, and 
harmonisation of product environmental footprints, which mainly rely on physical garment 
characteristics and overlook behavioural factors controlled by consumers. 
This thesis explores how consumer behaviour influences the environmental footprint of two 
garment categories, t-shirts and pants, and identifies key behavioural parameters, and the 
relevance for the methodology under the Product Environmental Footprint for Apparel and 
Footwear (PEFCR A&F). 
A structured literature review was conducted to identify behavioural elements shaping how 
consumers purchase, use, and dispose of garments. These insights were used to construct two 
consumer profiles, a conscious consumer and a mainstream consumer, which subsequently 
acted as a structural element in the life cycle assessment (LCA) of the garments. This 
methodology was integrated as a proof of concept for integrating consumer behaviour into 
LCA, as an alternative to the current methodology focusing on technical drivers of longevity. 
The results show that purchase frequency, number of wears prior to wash, and retail 
distribution scenarios are key parameters significantly affecting LCA outcomes. The 
conscious consumer generally had lower environmental impacts for t-shirts across impact 
categories. However, trade-offs emerged in the pants scenarios, in areas such as water use due 
to a higher cotton content, despite lower consumption levels of the conscious consumer. 
The study demonstrates that incorporating consumer profiles into LCA is meaningful when 
the goal is to reflect user-driven differences in environmental pressures. However, limited 
data at the intersection of social and environmental sciences remains a key barrier to 
improving behavioural integration in LCA models.

1 Introduction 
We own more garments than ever before, we use it less, and the quality of it is secondary 
compared to how well it serves our individuality (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 2019). The 
interaction between the consumer and the garment has also changed, today people are less 
likely to repair or pass garments down through generations often ending with well-
functioning garments being disposed of pre-maturely (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2017). 
While wardrobes are growing, with an average size of approximately 200 garments per 
wardrobe, garments are consequently also being underutilised (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 
2017; Vermeyen et al., 2025). The most polluting products are those that are not used, and 
arguably should never have been produced in the first place (Klepp et al., 2023). 
With production volumes of textile fibres having more than doubled since year 2000, and the 
industry being based on a take-make-waste model, resources are exploited on behalf of the 
ecological integrity of the planet (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2017; Textile Exchange, 
2024). The textile industry employs more than 300 million people worldwide, with low-skill 
manufacturing steps often performed by women workers, representing approximately 75% of 
the workforce, typically in low-paid roles. While most garments are produced in the Global 
South, emerging markets in Turkey and Eastern Europe, also suffer human rights issues. Lack 
of living wages, gender-specific abuses, freedom of association, and bargaining rights are 
among the issues raised most often (Business & Human Rights Resource Centre, 2018). 
Fast fashion continues to thrive, driven by the constant turnover of trends and the widespread 
availability of low-cost garments. As of 2020, the textile industry ranked as the fourth largest 
contributor to climate change, and the third largest contributor to global water depletion and 
land use change. Moreover, approximately 20% of global water pollution can be attributed to 
dyeing and fabric treatment processes (European Parliament, 2020). In response to the 
environmental impacts of overconsumption, and the fact that only around 1% of textiles are 
recycled back into new garments, the European Union aims to reduce textile waste and 
extend garment lifetimes. These goals are part of the broader Circular Economy Action Plan 
and are supported by the EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (European 
Commission, 2022). 
As the European Union introduces stricter regulations and promotes standardised 
environmental assessment methods such as the Product Environmental Footprint (PEF), it is 
important to understand the full life cycle impacts of textiles. The interaction between 
garments and consumers spans from the point of purchase through wear and care, and 
ultimately to disposal (Dao & Martinez, 2024). The use phase represents an environmental 
hotspot in the garment life cycle, where practices like purchasing frequency, washing 
methods, laundry frequency, and disposal decisions greatly affect the overall impact (Daystar 
et al., 2019). Despite its significance, most research has focused on energy and water use, 
==PAGE:13==
2 
 
leaving consumer behaviour during garment use one of the least explored areas in textile life 
cycle assessments (LCA) (Dao & Martinez, 2024). 
This thesis explores the environmental implications of consumer behaviour during the use 
phase of garments through a prospective, scenario-based LCA. It serves as a proof of concept 
for how consumer behaviour can be meaningfully integrated into LCA models by developing 
and using consumer profiles. The study compares the environmental impacts of t-shirts and 
pants under varying use scenarios and provides insights into how such modelling approaches 
relate to the assumptions outlined in the Product Environmental Footprint Category Rules for 
Apparel and Footwear (PEFCR A&F). More specifically the following research question and 
sub-questions will be answered: 
Which consumer behaviour parameters are most relevant for describing garment use in life 
cycle assessment, and how can explorative, scenario-based modelling be used to assess their 
influence on the environmental footprint of t-shirts and pants in alignment with the PEFCR 
for Apparel and Footwear? 
1. How do different consumer profiles influence the environmental footprint of t-shirts 
and pants, and which parameters are most influential of the variability in life cycle 
assessment results? 
2. Which parameters are most relevant for describing consumer behaviour in LCA of 
garments, and how can they be operationalised in explorative, scenario-based 
modelling of garment use? 
3. How do behaviour-based LCA compare with the default assumptions in the PEFCR 
for Apparel and Footwear?

2 Background 
2.1 Garment life cycle in the textile supply chain 
The journey of a garment to reach the end-consumer involves a complex and fragmented 
supply chain. It encompasses a series of processes spanning from design, agriculture and 
petrochemical production to logistics, retail, consumer use, and ultimately, end-of-life 
treatment. The linear model of garment production aligns with the conventional take-make-
waste system, characterised by intensive use of water, energy, and chemicals throughout the 
life cycle, and continuous generation of waste across all stages (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 
2024; Niinimäki et al., 2020). While the polluting processing and manufacturing steps mainly 
take place in the Global South, the design phase most often takes place in the EU or USA 
(Leal Filho et al., 2022). The globalisation makes traceability of materials, and the associated 
process impacts difficult to track, which creates uncertainty in assessing the environmental 
impacts (Niinimäki et al., 2020). 
While fabric constitutes the primary material of a garment, additional elements, such as 
sewing thread, zippers, buttons, and rivets, play essential roles in providing both structural 
integrity and aesthetic value. These components, commonly referred to as trims, are 
necessary for the construction of any garment (Curto Valle et al., 2023). Furthermore, 
packaging materials including plastic bags, paper bags, cardboard, and pallets are produced to 
facilitate logistics and protect garments throughout the global fashion supply chain (Adams et 
al., 2025). 
Raw materials extraction and pre-processing: The production of textile fibres to meet the 
fashion industry's high output rates requires substantial resource inputs. Fibre types are 
categorised as cellulose (e.g., cotton), protein-based (e.g., silk), man-made cellulose (e.g., 
viscose), and man-made synthetics (e.g., polyester) (Logan et al., 2025). Each category 
involves different extraction and pre-processing methods that contribute to different 
environmental burdens. Natural fibres like cotton are agriculturally cultivated and demand 
significant volumes of water and chemical inputs, contributing to soil degradation and water 
scarcity (Munasinghe et al., 2021). Man-made cellulose fibres like viscose rely on wood pulp, 
linking their production to deforestation and land use change. Meanwhile, synthetic fibres 
such as polyester are derived from petroleum-based sources, contributing to climate change 
(Leal Filho et al., 2022). Common to the textile garment production is fibre blends, requiring 
the parallel extraction and processing of multiple fibre types. 
Manufacturing: After fibre extraction, the material is subjected to a spinning process to 
produce yarn, which is then used in fabric manufacturing, typically through either weaving or 
knitting techniques. The material waste during this production step includes cotton lint, scrap 
yarn and unfinished cones (Maeen Md. Khairul Akter et al., 2024). Following spinning and 
weaving or knitting, the fabric undergoes wet treatment processes, including bleaching, 
dyeing, printing, and finishing. These processes collectively represent one of the most water-
intensive stages in textile production (European Parliament, 2020). As many production 
regions are in water-scarce areas, the combination of extensive water consumption and weak 
enforcement of wastewater treatment regulations adversely affects local water supplies. 
==PAGE:15==
4 
 
Additionally, the chemical use associated with textile manufacturing, such as dyes, bleaches, 
water repellents, brominated flame retardants, phthalates, and antibacterial agents, raises 
serious concerns for both environmental safety and human health, particularly when chemical 
management systems are insufficient (Niinimäki et al., 2020). 
Retail: After production, garments enter a new transport phase to reach the shelfs, often 
returning to the Global North, where it was initially designed (Niinimäki et al., 2020). The 
distribution channels range from cargo ships, trucks, trains both internationally and 
intercontinental, with air freight increasingly being used to meet fast delivery requirements. 
Both in-store and online retail contribute to environmental impacts through repackaging, 
inventory handling, and unsold goods, including overstock and returned items (Adams et al., 
2025). Flexible return policies have led to increased return rates, where approximately 20% 
of all clothing bought online is returned, due to poor fit, the inability to assess the feel, or 
style dissatisfaction (European Environment Agency, 2024). Returns face long processing 
times across multiple sites, sorting centres with labour intensive steps, all limiting the 
potential to be resold in the context of rapidly changing trends. 
Use: The use phase begins once the garment is acquired by the consumer and includes 
activities such as wearing, washing, drying, ironing, and repair. These care processes involve 
the consumption of resources like water, electricity, detergents, and the use of household 
appliances (Luo et al., 2023). The environmental impact of this phase is highly variable, 
driven by heterogeneous consumer behaviours. Transport by the user, particularly when 
garments are transferred between first and second users, can significantly contribute to 
emissions and may even offset the environmental benefits of reuse (Niinimäki et al., 
2020).While repair is part of the use phase, it remains a niche practice (Connor-Crabb & 
Rigby, 2019). 
End-of-life: The overconsumption of garments and inefficiencies in production generate 
substantial waste volumes across all life cycle stages. Waste generated prior to consumer use 
is referred to as pre-consumer waste. For example, fabric cutting during garment assembly 
typically results in material loss, with waste ratios varying depending on garment design and 
fabric type. Design features such as pattern matching (e.g., stripes or prints) can further 
increase offcuts. Printing and dyeing errors also contribute to this waste category (Niinimäki 
et al., 2020). Additionally, unsold inventory and returned products constitute a notable pre-
consumer waste stream, often referred to as deadstock. It has been estimated that on average 
one third of returned garments from e-commerce get destroyed (European Environment 
Agency, 2024). Once a consumer discards a garment, it is known as post-consumer waste. In 
most cases, garments are either incinerated or landfilled, domestically or in lower-income 
countries, including through exports to regions such as Africa (Niinimäki et al., 2020). 
2.2 Feedback between user-ship and supply chain 
The textile industry currently faces substantial challenges related to overproduction, an issue 
that even highly efficient recycling systems are unable to fully resolve. In 2023, 124 million 
tonnes of fibres were produced with the largest share made of virgin polyester (57%), while 
==PAGE:16==
5 
 
pre- and -post consumer recycled waste made up less than 1% of the fibre market (Textile 
Exchange, 2024). 
From a sectoral perspective, recycling cannot off-set the negative impacts associated with 
increased production. In the Materials Market Report Textile Exhange (2024) found that the 
availability of recycled fibres decreased slightly in 2023. To effectively reduce the volume of 
garments in circulation, Levänen et al. (2021) propose that repair and reuse strategies may be 
more impactful at a systemic level.  
The use phase of a garment’s life cycle can be prolonged with strategies, such as reduction, 
reuse, repair, resale, and consumer exchange practices playing a central role. The 
effectiveness of such strategies in delaying product displacement, depends on the consumer’s 
ability to navigate the complex emotions and values associated with different forms of 
interaction with garments. Reuse and repair have been shown to result in lower climate 
impacts compared to textile-to-textile recycling and garment rental where consumer transport 
is likely to outweigh the benefits of the circular economy strategy (Levänen et al., 2021). 
While reselling and extending garment use are considered strategies for improving resource 
efficiency, the actual benefit may be lower than expected due to rebound effects. These can 
occur, for instance, when second-hand garments are used less frequently or deliver a lower 
level of functional use compared to new garments, ultimately leading to increased 
consumption (Ciechelska et al., 2024). The effect of lower pricing and insufficient 
substitutability of second-hand garments can partly explain the mechanism behind rebound 
effects in the garment economy. In a literature review by Ciechelska et al. (2024), a 
substitution ratio of 1:1.5 between new and second-hand garments was found to be more 
likely than a 1:1 replacement. Additionally, the study confirmed that moral licensing 
contributes to the rebound effect, a concept where over-consumption of second-hand 
garments is perceived as justifiable due to its association with sustainability.  
2.3 Defining and understanding garment longevity 
2.3.1 Functional lifetime  
The concept of product lifetime is used inconsistently across the literature, but generally 
refers to the duration of use, which can be influenced by strategies aimed at extending the 
service life. Jerome (2025) distinguishes between four types of lifetimes: service lifetime, 
technical lifetime, use time, and technical use time. Additionally, the terms absolute and 
relative obsolescence are relevant for describing end-of-life reasons. Absolute obsolescence 
refers to garments that are worn out or no longer function as intended, whereas relative 
obsolescence describes products that remain functional but taken out of use for other reasons, 
see Figure 1. For instance, Vermeyen et al. (2025) found that on average 24% of the 
wardrobes audited were not used over the past 12 months. 
==PAGE:17==
6 
 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of different definitions of lifetime from point of purchase to the conclusion of the 
use phase, with the state of the garment deciding type of obsolescence. Modified from (Jerome, 2025). 
Technical use time refers to the duration during which a garment is actively used and 
maintained until it no longer functions. In contrast, technical lifetime includes both periods of 
active use and intervals of idleness, ending when the garment physically fails. Conversely, 
service use time and service lifetime mark the end of the use phase while the garment is still 
functionally intact and capable of serving its intended purpose. The service lifetime is shorter 
than the technical lifetime for a product where the obsolescence is relative, meaning that the 
garment is disposed of prematurely considering its durability (Jerome, 2025). The different 
definitions are important to the discussion of appropriate lifetime extension strategies, for 
example increasing the service lifetime through reuse is only relevant in cases of relative 
obsolescence, while the technical lifetime is relevant to postponing absolute obsolescence. 
2.3.2 Technical durability 
Garments begin their life cycle during the design phase, where decisions regarding style, 
materials, and construction are made. Garment quality is typically evaluated based on 
physical parameters such as fabric strength, colourfastness, resistance to pilling, and wrinkle 
resistance, only considering the functionality at one point in time (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 
2019). Today the fast fashion industry is producing garments for the mass retail market with 
less focus on such properties, which lowers the overall technical durability of garments 
(Daystar et al., 2019).  
Intrinsic durability, as defined in the Product Environmental Footprint Category Rules for 
Apparel and Footwear (PEFCR A&F) - a sector-specific methodology developed by the 
Technical Secretariat to harmonise life cycle assessment (LCA) practices - refers to the set of 
product characteristics that determine a garment’s potential technical lifespan. This includes 
attributes such as fabric toughness, resistance to repeated laundering, and specific design 
features. The PEFCR A&F strongly recommends applying an intrinsic durability multiplier, 
derived from physical durability tests, to the default lifetime-use values, to better estimate a 
garment’s technical lifespan. However, the guidelines also highlight several limitations of this 
approach, particularly in linking the number of uses to garment characteristics like fibre 
composition and construction. While the effects of wear and tear from laundering are 
==PAGE:18==
7 
 
currently being studied, other socio-psychological influences on durability are not addressed 
(Adams et al., 2025). Research on laundering's impact underscores the intersection between 
user behaviour and technical durability, laundry habits not only contribute directly to 
environmental impacts but also influence how long a garment remains technically functional 
(Luo et al., 2023).                                                                                                         
A key challenge in assessing garment longevity is the absence of a scientifically approved 
method for measuring a garment’s actual lifespan. Klepp et al. (2023) argue that relying 
solely on technical durability as a proxy for lifespan is not supported by robust scientific 
evidence, a concern that also extends to eco-modulation mechanisms within the Extended 
Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework. While technical durability can serve as an 
important enabler of garment longevity, it is ultimately changes in consumer behaviour that 
drive reductions in the environmental impact of garment use (Fletcher, 2012). Industrial 
quality assessments that overlook consumer-perceived quality offer only a partial view, 
failing to account for the subjective preferences and experiences of individual users. 
2.3.3 Perceived quality 
While increasing the technical durability of garments is a commonly used strategy to enhance 
environmental performance, it assumes that improved material performance will translate 
into prolonged use by consumers. However, this perspective overlooks a critical dimension: 
the interaction between the consumer and the garment. To understand what drives garment 
longevity, it is essential to consider perceived quality, how consumers subjectively assess and 
respond to a garment's look, feel, and performance in daily use. Connor-Crabb & Rigby 
(2019) found that these perceptions are shaped by individual experiences of wearing a 
garment, with items perceived as high quality often acquired through unconventional means 
such as being gifted, inherited, or purchased second-hand. 
Fibre choice plays a key role not only in determining the technical performance of a fabric 
but also in shaping consumer perceptions, a factor that have the potential to influence how 
long garments are used (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 2019; Daystar et al., 2019). Additionally 
Qihui Hu (2024) found that garment texture had a strong positive influence on purchasing 
decisions, highlighting how sensory attributes contribute to perceived quality. This perception 
can increase consumers’ willingness to buy garments considered more “sustainable,” such as 
those made from preferred fibres, and is essential to overall consumer satisfaction. Garment 
price is another important factor, higher-priced garments typically are more valued by 
consumers, which can lead to greater care and extended use (Laitala & Klepp, 2020).  
The assessment of quality is ultimately subjective and rooted in the consumer’s experience, 
which likely makes up a discrepancy between industry quality assessment of technical 
lifetime and the actual service life of a garment. Perceived quality during use has been 
identified as a key driver in fostering long-term product engagement and extending garment 
lifespans (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 2019). 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
2.3.4 Emotional durability 
As the economy shifts toward being more socially oriented, consumer demand has 
increasingly moved beyond basic material needs to include emotional and psychological 
fulfilment. Emotional factors are becoming more influential in shaping consumer behaviour. 
For example Qihui Hu (2024) found that intangible qualities such as emotional durability 
were stronger drivers of garment purchase decisions than environmental awareness. 
Dressing is not merely functional, it is a form of identity construction, enabling both creative 
self-expression and adherence to social norms. Garments that support this process tend to be 
worn more frequently and kept longer, whereas those that fail to do so are more easily 
replaced, not because they are worn out, but because they no longer align with the wearer’s 
changing identity (Connor-Crabb & Rigby, 2019). 
In fact, research shows that over 75% of garments considered obsolete in wardrobes are still 
in good enough condition to be reused (Vermeyen et al., 2025). Many consumers retain the 
garments in good condition with the intention of wearing them again in the future until the 
sentimental value diminishes (Ha-Brookshire & Hodges, 2009). Additionally, emotional 
attachment accounts for 12% of unused garments that the consumers would not part with, as 
those garments were often viewed as irreplaceable due to the memories and personal 
experiences they represent (Vermeyen et al., 2025). 
2.3.5 The intention behaviour gap  
A recurring theme in literature is the concept of a weak relationship between preference and 
action, a social psychological phenomena, called the “intention-behaviour gap” (Rausch & 
Kopplin, 2021; Wiederhold & Martinez, 2018). Consumers who exhibit ethical intentions 
struggle to translate these into action, such as eco-conscious purchasing practices. Different 
constructs may impede consumers in following through on their environmentally conscious 
aspirations (Rausch & Kopplin, 2021). Wiederhold & Martinez (2018) present influential 
determinants of behaviour in two categories: external (institutional, economic, social and 
cultural) or internal (motivation, environmental knowledge, locus of control, attitudes and 
values). Depending on these dynamics, consumers can be categorized as either intenders or 
non-intenders, resulting in either action or inaction (Rausch & Kopplin, 2021). Decisional 
conflict may arise when consumers must evaluate the utility of choosing between options 
with varying degrees of sustainability. The stress involved in weighing these alternatives and 
making a final decision is often something consumers seek to avoid. This tension can be 
reduced if a clearly acceptable option is readily available; otherwise, consumers may resort to 
procrastination or shift responsibility to avoid making the choice (Ajzen, I, 1996). 
2.4 Policy and legislative context 
As part of the European Union’s overarching goal to achieve a circular economy by 2050, 
reducing the textile industry’s environmental footprint is on the agenda. To that end, the 
European Commission has presented the EU strategy for sustainable and circular textiles 
focusing on durability, repair, reuse and recycling. As part of the strategy the following items 
have been included:

2.4.1 The eco-design for sustainable Product Regulation for textiles (ESPR) 
The ESPR framework regulation is considered a cornerstone for achieving circularity within 
the EU as it harmonises national legislations, which entered into force in July 2024. While it 
applies to different product categories, textile garments and footwear have been listed as 
prioritised for the first working plan (European Commission, 2025b).  
The destruction of unsold textile products such as garments and footwear is acknowledged as 
a widespread problem, which became prohibited under the ESPR legislation. The prohibition 
to destroy unsold products applies to consumer textiles never used for its intended purpose, 
covering the concepts of recycling, recovery, and disposal. While the regulation does not 
apply before 2030, obligations for companies to disclose disposal categories and amounts 
together with preventive measures are expected (European Union, 2024). This will increase 
data availability and help better understand the hidden life cycle impacts related to textile 
garments.  
As part of the ESPR the Commission has presented the Digital Product Passport (DPP) and 
aims to align with the Textile Labelling Regulation to provide traceability and information 
requirements. This will include information available to consumers on durability, resource 
consumption and recycling potential of garments (European Commission, 2025b).  
2.4.2 Extended producer responsibility (EPR) for textile products 
The Waste Framework Directive establishes fundamental principles for waste management 
according to the waste hierarchy, prioritising prevention, preparation for reuse, recycling, 
recovery, and, as a last resort, disposal. Under the title “preparing for re-use and recycling” 
the Waste Framework Directive was amended in 2018 to mandate separate collection of 
textile waste by 1 January 2025 (European Union, 2018). Further, a provisional agreement to 
include EPR for textiles has been reached under the 2023 Amendment to Waste Framework 
Directive (European Commission, 2025a). EPR obligates economic operators to cover the 
costs of waste management for products placed on the EU market at the product’s end-of-life, 
to accelerate the development of separate collection, sorting, reuse and recycling. To support 
circular economy objectives, potential overlaps between EPR obligations and eco-design 
requirements for footwear are scheduled for assessment under the current working plan by 
2027 (European Commission, 2025b).  
Currently, the waste management system of textiles is underdeveloped and operates at an 
immature level, where lack of economic feasibility undermines the business model and is 
considered a key barrier (Ellen Macarthur Foundation, 2024). In the past year, this trend has 
become evident across the textile sector, as illustrated by the bankruptcy of the Swedish 
textile-to-textile recycling company Renewcell and the insolvency of the large European 
textile waste collector and sorter Soex Group, both driven by collapsing markets and 
increased competition from Asian markets (Innovation in Textiles, 2024; The Sustainable 
Fashion Forum, 2025). With the coming revision to the Waste Framework Directive all 
member states will be required to translate it into national legislation, however considering 
the globalised and complex structure of the textile supply chain, harmonisation across borders 
is central to enable better market conditions for textile circularity.

3 Literature review  
3.1 Identification of problem domain 
How a garment is worn, and cared for is greatly impacted by the consumer preferences and 
habits. To better capture the environmental impacts associated with consumption of textile 
garments, a literature review was conducted focused on consumer behaviour and their 
relevance for environmental impact assessment, particularly for use-phase modelling in LCA 
and PEFCR. The consumer use phase is often mentioned as a key driver of environmental 
burdens across the life cycle stages of garments, however consumers’ relationship and use 
patterns of clothes are frequently overlooked leading to an incomplete assessment of 
environmental impacts (Luo et al., 2023; Polizzi di Sorrentino et al., 2016). This next section 
presents a literature review on consumer behaviour and interaction with garments, forming 
the foundation for two consumer profiles, which will function as user stories, see section 4.2. 
These profiles illustrate distinct garment use patterns relevant to environmental impact 
assessments and will support modelling the use stage of the LCA. The key parameters 
applied in the LCA are presented in section 4.4.2. 
3.2 Literature review of consumer behaviour 
This section presents a literature review on consumer behaviour related to garment use. Key 
behavioural aspects influencing purchasing, wearing, caring for, repairing, and disposing of 
clothing are analysed to identify patterns relevant for modelling the use phase in a life cycle 
assessment. 
3.2.1 Garment Purchase Behaviour 
Garment purchasing behaviour refers to how consumers buy garments, including purchase 
frequency, product types, decision-making factors, and purchasing channels 
(Balasubramanian & Sheykhmaleki, 2024). The digital landscape further shapes consumer 
behaviour. Inputs from social media, brand communication, environmental education, and 
personal values, collectively influence how clothing is perceived and purchased  
(Balasubramanian & Sheykhmaleki, 2024; Harris et al., 2016). At the point of purchase 
decision, the consumer evaluates the garment across multiple dimensions, linking material 
properties to aesthetic and emotional values. Structural barriers such as price sensitivity, 
trends, and perceived effort can affect consumer choices (Harris et al., 2016). 
Clothing serves as a means of identity expression (Harris et al., 2016). Rapidly changing 
fashion trends often encourage consumers to prioritise quantity over quality, opting for 
convenience and low-cost garments (Harris et al., 2016). Fast fashion reinforces this by 
offering cheaper, less durable products that reduce both technical and perceived value. Many 
are willing to compromise on durability in favour of updating and expressing their personal 
style (Aakko & Niinimäki, 2021; Qihui Hu, 2024). For instance, when it comes to fast 
fashion items, consumers often consider the quality sufficient if it enables them to attain a 
desired style at a low cost (Aakko & Niinimäki, 2021). Fashion trend sensitivity has been 
linked to higher clothing acquisition, particularly among younger women. In contrast, style-
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
oriented consumers are motivated by a preference for timeless designs and cultivating a 
distinct personal style, which encourages more thoughtful consumption. Style orientation has 
been associated with reduced consumption, longer garment use, and positive attitudes toward 
second-hand shopping and clothing swaps (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024). Although second-
hand shopping is gaining popularity among certain groups, concerns about cleanliness remain 
a key reason why other consumers are against purchasing second hand items (Paço et al., 
2021). 
Perceived quality plays a key role in clothing purchase, both new and second-hand. Maguire 
& Fahy, (2023) suggest that consumers should pay attention ensuring complete comfort of 
garments at initial purchase point, together with careful review of fabric composition, as it 
could increase longevity of clothing use and frequent reuse of items. However, findings from 
Paço et al., (2021) indicate that environmental factors, such as selecting garments made from 
preferred fibres or with eco-labels, are among the least influential considerations in clothing 
choices. 
Perceived quality is mainly shaped by consumers' subjective attitude rather than the objective 
product attributes (Cheng et al., 2024). Aesthetic appeal and brand recognition often guide 
assessment of quality (Aakko & Niinimäki, 2021). Consumer data suggests that cotton 
apparel is perceived to have a longer lifetime compared to polyester and rayon (Daystar et al., 
2019). This perceived longevity may contribute to lower cradle-to-grave environmental 
impacts for cotton garments. Which material lasts the longest may not be a function of 
material physical performance alone, instead, it may be shaped by subjective factors such as 
appearance, odour retention, sentimental value, or other qualities unrelated to structural 
integrity. These non-technical attributes can significantly influence consumers' understanding 
of garment longevity and its associated environmental impacts (Daystar et al., 2019). For 
more environmentally conscious individuals, internal motivations like concerns about 
environmental harm can override the fear of not appearing or behaving according to social 
status (Stringer et al., 2020), and these consumers are more likely to prefer local and second-
hand options (Rausch & Kopplin, 2021). Conversely, Harris et al. (2016) found that even 
among environmentally conscious individuals, garment purchases were often driven more by 
factors like price and style than by environmental considerations. Second-hand clothing has 
gained popularity, particularly among younger consumers, driven by environmental concerns, 
economic constraints, and an interest in vintage fashion (Paço et al., 2021). These consumers 
are often less concerned with perceived contamination or social stigma. Still, barriers remain, 
such as concerns about cleanliness, ownership history, time investment, and aesthetic or 
functional uncertainty (Aakko & Niinimäki, 2021; Harris et al., 2016; Paço et al., 2021; 
Tymoshchuk et al., 2024). On the other hand, mainstream consumers may be less influenced 
by ethical concerns if such behaviours are not reinforced by visible social norms or aligned 
with aesthetic and trend preferences as some consumers avoid appearing overly “sustainable” 
for fear of judgment (Rausch & Kopplin, 2021). However, Observing environmental 
conscious behaviours within one’s social circle has been shown to increase an individual's 
likelihood of making environmentally conscious choices (Chi et al., 2021). This supports 
findings in research indicating that raising consumer awareness positively influences their 
==PAGE:23==
12 
 
purchasing behaviour and attitudes toward sustainability (Balasubramanian & Sheykhmaleki, 
2024).   
This section demonstrates that garment purchases are not driven by altruistic motives; rather, 
they are personal choices influenced by a complex decision-making context involving moral, 
psychological, and social considerations (Balasubramanian & Sheykhmaleki, 2024). The 
findings from Harris et al. (2016) supports this conclusion and highlights the concern for 
cheaper garment prices undermining the second hand market. People generally buy the 
garments because it either fits their style or makes them feel good. On the contrary, some 
consumer groups are valuing the longer-term relationship with garments more and are 
prepared to pay for higher quality items. Social norms have been acknowledged, throughout 
the reviewed literature, to be a key driver of purchase behaviour.  
3.2.2 Garment Use Behaviour 
Environmental impacts during the consumer use phase are primarily driven by the number of 
wears, laundering frequency, washing and drying methods, and end-of-life management, with 
research indicating that consumer behaviour varies across countries and garment types 
(Daystar et al., 2019). Extending use is central to reducing the environmental impacts of 
garment use and involves both intensifying and prolonging garment lifetimes (Joyner 
Martinez et al., 2024). Which partly depends on the acquisition of high-quality garments with 
long technical durability (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024), as well as consumers’ ability to 
assess fabric durability and maintain garments through appropriate care practices (Maguire & 
Fahy, 2023). The period between acquisition and disposal is therefore critical for resource-
efficient use practices. According to Joyner Martinez et al. (2024) use behaviour can be 
categorized into three dimensions: wear, care, and repair.   
3.2.2.1 Wear practices 
Wear involves managing and engaging with wardrobes to find new uses for less worn items 
(Joyner Martinez et al., 2024). Clothing use time is related to aesthetics, social preferences, 
and the ideology of use (Aakko & Niinimäki, 2021). According to Maguire & Fahy (2023) 
the average individual has a large wardrobe with 115 garments of which 30% of the items 
have not been worn in the previous year. Reasons for non-use of clothing include infrequent 
wardrobe checks, retention of items that no longer fit or require alterations, and storage of 
garments that are in disrepair (Maguire & Fahy, 2023). Extending a garment’s lifespan can be 
aided by fostering a stronger emotional connection to clothing through more mindful and 
active use, as it is ultimately the wearer’s choices that define how long a garment remains in 
use (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024). Previous studies have shown that consumers who closely 
follow fashion trends typically seek novelty and are less likely to wear items repeatedly, 
while style-oriented individuals prioritise timeless designs and extended use. However, 
Joyner Martinez et al. (2024) found that fashion trend sensitivity positively influenced 
sustainable use practices. Consumers highly engaged with current trends were more likely to 
responsibly care for, repair, and use their garments than previously assumed. Joyner Martinez 
et al. (2024) argue that the link between fashion interest and sustainability knowledge should 
==PAGE:24==
13 
 
be reconsidered, as practices like wardrobe organisation, mixing and matching outfits, and 
proper garment care are accessible to fashion-interested consumers. These findings suggest 
that fashion engagement and sustainability are not mutually exclusive but can align when 
consumers interact intentionally with their wardrobes. This also supports the findings of 
Maguire & Fahy (2023), who observed that positive practices, when present, were often 
unintentional.  
In current LCA models, these behavioural dynamics are rarely captured, garment lifespans 
are typically treated as fixed values. However, variations in how garments are worn, stored, 
and re-integrated into outfits has a direct influence on the service lifetime and thus the overall 
environmental impact. This underscores the relevance of integrating differentiated consumer 
profiles in the assessment, particularly to reflect the interplay between fashion engagement, 
garment rotation, and emotional durability. 
3.2.2.2 Care practices 
Care includes practices before and during laundry that preserve a garment’s function, 
cleanliness, comfort, and appearance, as well as actions that reduce, delay, or eliminate 
washing, drying and ironing (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024).  
For everyday clothing, care practices are a combination of inadvertent, routinized, habitual 
decision making rather than conscious action. Findings suggest that used or worn garments 
are routinely consigned as ‘dirty’ after they are worn, irrespective of whether they are dirty, 
partly because of normative injunctions to not wear the same clothes on consecutive days 
(Maguire & Fahy, 2023). However, Dao & Martinez (2024) found that an exception to this is 
house clothes which is a category of clothing consumers wear the most, and these are 
permitted to become dirty through multiple wears before washing them. These house clothes 
may be t-shirts that are no longer suitable to wear outside the home, but instead of detaching 
from the garment, the user continues to wear them (Dao & Martinez, 2024). 
Deploying conscious care approaches include wearing protective clothing, reducing machine 
washing, using line drying as well as repairing, which all supports longer garment life (Dao 
& Martinez, 2024). Laundering behaviours, particularly drying methods, vary across 
countries and cultures, making region specific data essential for accurately assessing the 
environmental impact of both laundering and overall apparel use (Daystar et al., 2019). It is 
not yet fully understood how different fabric types and odour accumulation affect laundering 
practices, making it challenging to assess the material specific impacts on consumer 
behaviour (Daystar et al., 2019). However, previous studies have explored how consumer 
behaviour interacts with garment care labels and according to Dao & Martinez (2024) 
differences in consumer behaviour related to garment care labels with regional and cultural 
differences, pending from rarely to always. Findings also include that consumers often tend 
to cut off clothing labels as they irritate or annoy (Maguire & Fahy, 2023). A major barrier to 
responsible care practices is social norms and the belief that clothes must be washed 
frequently and at high temperatures. While some retailers advise against this to help maintain 
colour and fit, they acknowledge that, despite the availability of technologies for effective 
==PAGE:25==
14 
 
cold water washing, convincing consumers to adopt these practices remains challenging 
(Harris et al., 2016; Joyner Martinez et al., 2024). Dao & Martinez (2024) highlights that air 
drying has been claimed as one of the most important choices a user can make when tackling 
the reduction of emissions within the use phase. Maguire and Fahy (2023) found that 
increasing household awareness by promoting the environmental benefits possible with line 
drying was not required as the overwhelming majority identified preferring to line dry their 
laundry. This was mainly motivated by saving energy for economic reasons. Although many 
studies report the utilisation of air drying methods, the use of a machine dryer is still 
prevalent, with larger households using it more frequently (Dao & Martinez, 2024). 
While some practices are rooted in conscious environmental intent, many are driven by social 
norms and convenience. Line drying and cold-water washing are recognised as 
environmentally preferable strategies, yet adherence to ingrained habits (e.g. frequent hot 
washes or tumble drying) persist, often due to perceived norms or lack of awareness. Cultural 
and personal habits, such as perception of cleanliness or irritation caused by care labels, 
shape how garments are maintained. From a life cycle perspective, care behaviours 
significantly affect the environmental impact of the use phase, yet they are often modelled 
with static or average assumptions. Findings highlight the complexity of care behaviour and 
indicate that uniform modelling of washing, drying and ironing habits risk oversimplifying 
actual patterns. Integrating consumer profiles to reflect differentiated consumer behaviour 
into LCA can improve accuracy. However, quantifying these patterns remains challenging 
due to regional variation as well as fabric- and garment-specific factors.  
3.2.2.3 Repair practices 
Repair refers to mending garments, either personally or professionally, through activities like 
replacing buttons, fixing seams, or patching holes (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024). These 
practices extend garment lifespans and support more responsible clothing use. Having basic 
repair skills not only extends the life of clothing but also strengthens consumers' ability to 
evaluate quality and make more informed purchases (Joyner Martinez et al., 2024; Laitala et 
al., 2018). 
Laitala et al. (2018) identified a strong connection between mending and reduced clothing 
purchases, suggesting that repair increases garment utilisation and reduces the need for 
replacements. Learning repair techniques also improves a user’s ability to assess garment 
quality, enabling more informed purchasing decisions. The exact implications on acquisition 
rate and garment quality have not been recorded. However, both visible and invisible repairs 
contribute to prolonging a garment’s life and help avoid disposal (Dao & Martinez, 2024). 
Harris et al. (2016) acknowledged a decline in sewing skills and, more notably, that even 
consumers with the ability to repair often considered it unnecessary. They also noted that 
while some consumers may be motivated to repair more expensive items, they are much less 
likely to extend the life of everyday clothing or trend-driven pieces (Harris et al., 2016). 
Interestingly, Laitala & Klepp (2020) found that consumers who performed minor mending 
tasks, such as sewing on buttons, fixing hems, or patching holes, often still reported that they 
never repaired their clothing, suggesting that such activities were not perceived as meaningful 
==PAGE:26==
15 
 
repair. This highlights a disconnect between actual behaviour and consumer perceptions of 
what constitutes repair. 
Evidence indicates that repair practices are associated with extended garment lifespans and 
may contribute to reduced clothing acquisition. As repair has been linked to both greater 
consumer involvement in garment care and improved ability to assess quality at the point of 
purchase. Repair is also described as a means of delaying disposal, with certain consumer 
groups engaging more actively in mending than others. Laitala et al. (2018) highlight the role 
of basic repair skills in reducing replacement frequency and increasing product utility. 
However, consumer engagement varies depending on perceived garment value and there 
appears to be a prevailing perception that repairs are unnecessary. Implying that there are 
social barriers in the way of repairing and re-wearing clothes (Harris et al., 2016). 
Additionally, minor repairs are often not recognized by consumers as repairs (Laitala et. al., 
2018), suggesting that perceptions influence both reporting and actual behaviour.  
Findings suggest that consumer profiles used for LCA modelling should integrate differences 
in repair attitudes, skill levels, and thresholds for what is considered meaningful mending. 
Capturing this variation is particularly relevant for garments with potential for extended use 
through small-scale interventions. However, lack of quantitative data linking repair frequency 
to actual lifespan extension, limits the ability to parameterise this behaviour in environmental 
assessments.  
3.2.3 Garment Disposal Behaviour 
The disposal of garments encompasses the act of discarding garments, whether through reuse, 
such as passing items on to family or friends, donation to charitable organisations, or resale 
via second-hand shops or online retail platforms (Paço et al., 2021). Societal pressures are 
recognised as a driving force behind intensified patterns of consumption and disposal, where 
notions of acceptable appearance are shaped by trends and the pursuit of renewal (Harris et 
al., 2016). Low-priced garments are often treated as nearly disposable, with consumers 
wearing them only a handful of times (7 to 8 times) before discarding them (Daystar et al., 
2019). Consumers who are more sensitive to fashion trends tend to dispose of their clothing 
more frequently, whereas those with a stronger orientation toward personal style are more 
likely to extend garment use, including through repair practices (Balasubramanian & 
Sheykhmaleki, 2024; Joyner Martinez et al., 2024; Paço et al., 2021). 
Disposability may also be understood as a mindset, whereby technically functional garments 
are prematurely discarded due to the accessibility of inexpensive alternatives, reducing the 
perceived value of garments and the attractiveness of repair (Harris et al., 2016). Perceived 
value of clothing is relevant in consumers attempts of extending garment life (Aakko & 
Niinimäki, 2021). Out of the different reasons for disposal, Laitala et al. (2015) found 
technical deficiency like holes and tears as the most common reason, and low durability of 
garments should be seen as a key driver of disposal rather than always the need for renewal. 
Additional individual drivers of disposal include limited wardrobe space, poor fit or sizing, 
and garments remaining unused (Paço et al., 2021). Preferences for garment disposal 
==PAGE:27==
16 
 
pathways are primarily influenced by factors such as habit, routine, and convenience (Daystar 
et al., 2019; Harris et al., 2016). The majority of European consumers identified clothing 
donation as their preferred disposal pathway, in comparison to alternative options such as 
reuse or resale, positioning donation as a central component in the end-of-life management of 
garments (Daystar et al., 2019; Paço et al., 2021). This may be partly explained by hedonic 
factors like alleviating guilt associated with overconsumption and deriving personal 
satisfaction from donating when compared to alternatives like discarding clothing as waste 
(Ha-Brookshire & Hodges, 2009). Among those who chose to donate to charity, social 
responsibility was found to be a relatively weak motivating factor. Instead, consumers were 
primarily driven by utilitarian motives, such as creating space in their wardrobes, thereby 
enabling new purchases. The criteria used to assess whether clothing was suitable for 
donation were largely based on the physical condition of the garments and the garments of 
low emotional value (Ha-Brookshire & Hodges, 2009). In contrast, Paço et al. (2021) found 
decisions to resell or donate were influenced by environmental concerns, and altruistic 
motives could not be entirely dismissed.  
To meaningfully integrate disposal behaviour into life cycle assessments, it is essential to 
understand consumers' typical disposal pathways for specific garment types. These 
behaviours may be partially influenced by factors such as style sensitivity and style intention; 
for example, garments with high emotional durability may be retained longer, reducing the 
likelihood of donation. Cultural and regional contexts also significantly shape disposal 
behaviour, with factors like technological infrastructure and generational differences 
influencing consumer decisions. Despite these complexities, the reviewed literature 
consistently identifies donation as the most preferred disposal route. However, studying the 
motivation for consumer disposal of garments should be considered in conjunction with 
garment types to inform garment LCAs.

4 Methods 
4.1 Methodology for literature review 
The literature review was conducted as a structured literature review, following a stepwise 
methodological approach inspired by the guidance from (Karolinska Institutet University 
Library, 2025). To identify literature available on consumer behaviour regarding textile 
garments, a few known articles were used as a way of identifying relevant terminology within 
the subject area. The reference articles led to the suggestion of the two search blocks, 
consumer behaviour and textile clothing products. Synonyms of chosen concepts were 
aligned and simplified thereafter the search blocks were merged using Boolean logic 
operators “OR” and “AND”. This gives the final search string: 
 ("clothing use" OR "consumer behavior" OR “user behaviour” OR “purchase behaviour” OR “purchase 
intention” OR “use practise”) AND ("clothing" OR "garment" OR "apparel" OR “textiles”)  
The search, conducted in March 2025, initially yielded 789 results, which were subsequently 
narrowed down to 19. The process is illustrated in Figure 2. The databases used were Web of 
Science, Scopus and Google Scholar. During the identification phase, inclusion criteria were 
applied to ensure either temporal relevance (2024 - 2025) or high scientific impact (≥7 
citations per year). Records qualifying under both criteria were removed as duplicates before 
the screening process. The remaining records underwent two subsequent screening processes 
following the initial identification phase. First, the titles and abstracts were evaluated to 
identify relevance within the scope of the study and ensure accessibility. Thereafter, the 
entire content of the articles was screened to ensure alignment with the analysis of consumer 
behaviour and clothing use in an European context.  
Records were excluded if they addressed topics unrelated to the research objective, such as 
B2C strategies, behaviour during the COVID-19 pandemic or if they focused on consumer 
behaviour outside the European context. For example, after the abstract was assessed the 
article with the title “The impact of digital marketing gamification strategies on consumer 
behaviour in the clothing retail industry” was removed from the list of articles. Records

4.2 Consumer profiles 
This section introduces two consumer profiles developed as short user stories to illustrate 
different garment use behaviours. The personas serve as a proof of concept for integrating 
behaviour-based scenarios into a life cycle assessment model. Rather than representing 
specific individuals, the profiles are generalised representations based on literature and are 
intended to capture key differences in how garments are purchased, used, cared for, and 
disposed of. The final parameters used for the LCA model can be seen in Table 3. 
==PAGE:30==
19 
 
4.2.1 The conscious consumer 
The conscious consumer is a young woman living in an urban environment, where 
sustainability is a core part of her lifestyle and social surroundings. She possesses a high level 
of environmental knowledge, which informs a well-established, positive attitude toward 
sustainable clothing practices. This attitude is further reinforced by her social circle, where 
environmental values are strongly shared and socially expected. 
Her perceived behavioural control is strong, driven by a personal sense of moral duty to 
protect the environment. Although it does not happen often, when she purchases new 
garments, she prefers the in-store shopping experience to avoid the environmental burden 
associated with shipping and returns. She prioritises mono-material garments for ease of 
future recycling, though second-hand clothing constitutes most of her wardrobe. Emotional 
attachment and careful selection guide her garment choices, reflecting a wardrobe curated 
around her personal style with minimal influence from fast-changing fashion trends. 
The conscious consumer actively manages her wardrobe to maximize the longevity of each 
item. After each wear, she critically evaluates whether laundering is necessary, minimizing 
wash frequency. When laundering is required, she exclusively uses cold water programs 
(30°C) and line drying washed garments to reduce energy consumption. 
Her strong knowledge of repair techniques enables a high frequency of garment repairs, 
where she fixes common issues such as broken zippers, loose buttons, and minor tears. After 
several repair cycles, once garments are no longer wearable or suitable for reuse, she ensures 
they are separately collected for textile recycling, aligning her behaviour with circular 
economy principles. 
4.2.2 The mainstream consumer 
The mainstream consumer is a young woman living in an urban setting, whose clothing 
behaviours are influenced by external determinants such as social approval and prevailing 
fashion trends. Her awareness of sustainable fashion is limited and superficial, and while she 
expresses a generally positive attitude toward reducing consumption, her intentions are often 
conflicted by a strong responsiveness to trends and a perceived lack of individual impact. 
Low perceived behavioural control acts as a key barrier to translating her environmental 
concern into consistent action. 
Financially comparable to the average citizen in Denmark, she could afford garments of 
higher technical durability. However, achieving trend alignment remains her primary 
motivation, leading her to frequently purchase low-cost fast fashion items through online 
platforms. Second-hand shopping is a low priority due to perceived inconveniences related to 
time investment, product cleanliness, and ease of access. 
Her garment care habits are centred around convenience and hygiene, leading to frequent 
washing at warm temperatures, under the belief that this is necessary for adequate 
cleanliness. Given the generally low structural quality of her clothing, garments often require 
==PAGE:31==
20 
 
mending. However, the low financial value of the items makes repair seem unjustified 
compared to easy and inexpensive replacements. 
At the end-of-life stage, she typically donates unwanted clothing. Donation serves both as a 
way to alleviate guilt associated with overconsumption and to make space for future 
purchases, reinforcing the cycle of trend-driven acquisition. 
4.3 Life cycle assessment  
To assess how consumer behaviour affects garment use and its associated environmental 
footprint, a LCA was conducted. In the following sections the goal and the scope of the LCA 
are presented. This is followed by an overview of the scenario generation (section 4.4), data 
collection and main modelling choices (section 4.5). Lastly, the methodological choices for 
the data quality assessment, uncertainty and sensitivity analysis are presented in section 4.6. 
4.3.1 Goal  
The aim of this LCA is to evaluate the environmental impacts associated with consumer 
behaviour in clothing consumption. It serves as a proof of concept, illustrating how these 
impacts are determined by factors such as social norms, fashion trends, and environmental 
awareness. By analysing consumer profiles and the motivations behind specific behaviours, 
these aspects are integrated into a more holistic LCA to better reflect real-world consumption 
patterns. As the use phase is heavily influenced by consumer practices, it is critical for 
accurately representing a garment's environmental footprint; ultimately, the sustainability of a 
garment is determined by how it is used.  
This LCA aims to clarify the differences in environmental impacts from a cradle-to-grave 
perspective between two defined consumer profiles - conscious and mainstream - across two 
garment sub-categories: t-shirts and pants. The study was informed by the Product 
Environmental Footprint Category Rules for Apparel and Footwear (PEFCR A&F). Certain 
methodological adaptations were made to suit the scope of this thesis. The LCA is designed 
to contribute insights that may inform future policy development and revisions of the PEFCR 
A&F specifically for modelling the use phase of garments.  
It is important to note that the classifications of “conscious” and “mainstream” consumers are 
region-specific. As such, the results of this study are not intended to be generalised to 
different regions. Differences in consumer behaviour, available technologies, and 
consumption patterns across regions may limit the applicability of the findings outside the 
defined geographical context. 
4.3.2 Scope 
The deliverables of this LCA study are a life cycle inventory (LCI) model and a life cycle 
impact assessment (LCIA). The LCI quantifies energy, material inputs, and emissions across 
the product life cycle, while the LCIA evaluates the associated environmental impacts using 
the EF v3.1 no Long-Term characterization method. This LCIA method was chosen to ensure 
==PAGE:32==
21 
 
a comprehensive assessment of environmental footprints across 15 out of the 16 different 
impact categories according to the PEFCR A&F (Adams et al., 2025), which are available in 
the characterization method. This is illustrated on Figure 3. 
The obligatory properties of the assessed garments are defined in PEFCR A&F (Adams et al., 
2025) as: 
1. T-shirts: Garment to cover the upper body to the elbow. 
2. Pants: Garment to cover the lower body, may protect from the elements. 
However, these functions can be delivered with various positioning properties considering a 
range of fibre composition, comfortability, aesthetics, distribution channels and care routines. 
Delivery of the obligatory properties can include different typical products such as, polo 
shirts, t-shirt, other short-sleeved shirts, outdoor pants, dress pants, shorts etc. To be more 
precise in the scope of styles assessed in this LCA, please see Figure 3 for typical styles 
representing the two garment subcategories considered in this LCA.  
It is thus the delivery of comparable functions of garments that must be assessed using the 
functional unit (FU) as a quantifiable reference point.

The system boundaries of the assessed product system cover all life cycle stages, including 
raw material extraction, manufacturing, distribution, use, and end-of-life. Environmental 
impacts of each process are quantified using an attributional life cycle inventory modelling 
framework. It is thus investigated what environmental impact the product system is 
responsible for by modelling average processes in the background system. To handle 
multifunctionality the cut-off criterium is applied to determine the separation between first- 
use and second-use cycles. Positive and negative impacts related to recycling, the export of 
post-consumer textile garments outside Europe, and reuse activities occurring outside 
Denmark are excluded, as they fall outside the scope of the defined economic market. The 
system boundaries include only incineration as the end-of-life option, along with transport to 
recycling facilities in Sweden, European sorting centre, and the operation of a sorting facility 
in Denmark. 
Multifunctionality is primarily addressed by applying cut-off criteria to exclude sub-
processes that provide secondary functions, such as the supply of second-hand garments to 
markets likely inaccessible to Danish consumers. Incineration processes may also yield 
secondary outputs, such as electricity and heat, depending on the regional context. While 
these outputs are not included in the foreground system, their effects are accounted for 
through system expansion in the background datasets (EcoInvent 3.10.1), by modelling the 
avoided impacts of displaced alternative production processes, following the ISO hierarchy.  
The studied product system is displayed on Figure 4. The system boundaries include the 
processes included in the study, moreover the diagram shows the overall process flows 
together with flows from and to the ecosphere and technosphere.  In a cradle-to-grave LCA, it 
is generally expected that only elementary flows cross the system boundary. However, due to 
the use of background data based on average values, it was not always possible to trace all 
technosphere flows back to their corresponding elementary flows. As a conservative 
approach, such flows were classified as technosphere flows. Additionally, not all unit 
processes required to fully represent the functional unit could be included individually; for 
instance, wet treatment stages encompass multiple sub-processes. 
The representativeness of the data used to model the product system is limited by the nature 
of this study as a proof of concept. Both the foreground and background systems were 
constructed entirely using average datasets, as no primary data were collected. Given the 
complexity of global textile supply chains, efforts were made to enhance geographical 
representativeness, particularly for parameters in the use phase such as the Danish electricity 
mix, waste management opportunities, legislation, as well as for distribution, covering 
international, intra-European, and local transport distances.  
Temporal representativeness is based on the year 2025, reflecting typical lead times in textile 
supply chains, where garments are usually produced and sold within the same year. While 
ongoing technological developments in textile recycling are acknowledged, they fall outside 
the scope of this study. However, recent changes in European legislation on product 
specifications and waste management are considered in the end-of-life modeling. For garment 
use, both temporal and spatial aspects of consumption patterns and user behavior are 
incorporated to reflect potential choices made by Danish consumer profiles and support the 
explorative scenario formation of the prospective LCA.

4.4 Scenario-based inventory modelling for prospective LCA 
Consumer behaviour cannot be predicted, but it can be explored. This is what this thesis is an 
example of; how some profiles may interact with clothes. To make a foresight of future 
assumptions, the scientifically tested framework for scenario-based inventory modelling for 
prospective LCA (SIMPL) has been followed looking at explorative scenarios (Langkau et 
al., 2023). The prospective nature of this approach is covered in this study through the 
unknown decisions a consumer would have to make, with a timeframe set within the near 
future, to ensure consistency with available data on consumer behaviour in the LCI 
modelling. The methodology is comprised of three steps ensuring that future decisions are 
systematically integrated: identifying key factors (Step A), adopting future assumptions for 
parameter values (Step B), scenario formation combining assumptions and checking 
consistency (Step C) (Langkau et al., 2023).  
4.4.1 Step A: Key factor identification 
Figure 5 shows Step A: the identification of the key factors which influence the results of the 
LCA model can be seen in yellow. These factors were identified through a PESTEL analysis 
looking at different areas from political, economic, sociocultural, technological, 
environmental and legal factors (Appendix I). The interaction of the key factors is displayed 
through the network of key factors and parameters (blue) forming the casual loop diagram 
(CLD). For instance, the CLD shows if a consumer intends to act based on increased 
environmental awareness it may influence the likelihood of repair behaviour in a positive 
direction, while it may also result in a lower laundering frequency.

factors are described. From the CLD it can be gathered that consumer behaviour regarding

and derived to fit the personas. While the Waste Framework Directive have been updated 
with mandatory separate textile waste collection, this has been considered, however, Eco-
design requirements and EPR have yet to be incorporated, and influence of potential 
consumer behaviour has not been considered in this study. Moreover, textile recycling is 
happening at low rates and with low economic feasibility and thus incorporation of recycled 
textile-to-textile fibres have not been included.

4.5 Life cycle inventory  
To assess the environmental impacts of the product system a comprehensive data collection 
process lays the basis for the life cycle inventory analysis (LCI analysis). In the life cycle 
inventory model all unit processes from the studied system has been constructed in the open-
source software Activity Browser build on brightway framework, using data from the 
EcoInvent 3.10.1 database. A summary of main assumptions is available in Table 4. The full 
documentation of the LCI model and input data for both consumer profiles are available in 
Appendix VIII. 
4.5.1 Data collection and modelling choices 
Reference products. The number of garments needed to fulfil the functional unit differs 
depending on the consumer profile. However, the number of garments assumes that the ratio 
of t-shirts and pants purchased equals that of the ratio in a wardrobe. To reflect actual 
purchasing behaviour, data from a survey of Polish university students was used (Ciechelska 
et al., 2024). The conscious consumer is assumed to fit the purchasing category “less but 
used”, purchasing an average of 1.8 ± 0.41 garments per month, of which the fraction for t-
shirt and pants is assumed to be 20.2% and 20.7% respectively (Vermeyen et al., 2025), and 
54% being second-hand garments.  
The mainstream consumer is assumed to belong to the “more but new” group, with purchases 
averaging 3.18 ± 0.39 items a month with 22.64% being second hand, the same share of t-
shirts and pants are adopted for the mainstream consumer. 
==PAGE:39==
28 
 
Textile raw materials and pre-processing. The modelling of garments considers the 
prevalence of fibre blends available on the retail market. Here, the conscious consumer is 
assumed to favour mono-materials. While the market availability for mono-material pants is 
sparse, a blend of 5% polyester, 5% elastane and 5% viscose were considered for pants. To 
ensure consistency in the cut between the two stages textile raw materials and pre-processing 
and manufacturing, yarn spinning was incorporated into the raw materials and pre-processing 
stage as seen on Figure 4. As datasets for elastane fibre production and viscose spinning do 
not exist in the EcoInvent 3.10.1 database, they were approximated by modifying the cotton 
process with viscose fibre, and by modifying the input to polyester fibre production with 
polyurethane as a proxy for elastane. No further changes were made to these processes to 
more accurately represent elastane and viscose yarn production. 
Manufacturing. The mass of fibre needed to produce one garment is based on the finished 
garment weight, and calculated by backtracking to the fibre amount using assumed values for 
losses throughout the apparel manufacturing process from (Adams et al., 2024) as illustrated 
on Figure 6. The calculation assumes that the input to process n equals the output of process 
n-1. To calculate the input Equation 1 was used.

The manufacturing steps were modelled using average datasets from Ecoinvent, not 
considering different technological requirements used for different fibre compositions and 
fabric properties. For instance, the weaving process available for cotton was applied to all 
yarn compositions. Fabric manufacturing for t-shirts was exclusively considered through 
knitting processes, while all fabrics for pants are woven. Wet treatment consists of a complex 
series of treatment steps to prepare and enhance the textile, requiring specific facility specific 
data to accurately represent the associated impacts. Pre-processing steps such as, desizing and 
scouring have not been possible to model and have thus been omitted, however material 
losses are included. As a proxy of transport of intermediate products one transport step is 
assumed between wet treatment and the cut-and-sew facility. The apparel manufacturing 
stage was modelled using LCI data for cotton t-shirt production in China, scaled according to 
the garment weight for electricity and steam consumption (Y. Zhang et al., 2015).  
Distribution. Distribution scenarios for both e-commerce and in-store retail were modelled 
based on the guidelines from (Adams et al., 2025). Additionally, a second hand in-store 
==PAGE:40==
29 
 
distribution scenario was included as a variation of the conventional in-store model, 
representing a local supply chain within Denmark. Distribution is assumed to happen from 
sorting centre to the second-hand shop, with equal energy consumption as the in-store retail 
shop. A substitution rate of 1:1.23 is accounted for in the second-hand distribution scenario as 
it is assumed that it takes 1.23 second-hand garments to replace 1 new garment. Due to the 
cut-off between first and second life cycle, this criterium is only applied to distribution stage 
of the second-hand garments. 
Impacts for the distribution stage is calculated based on utilisation ratios from Ecoinvent, 
supply chain distances, transport modes, provenances, product weight (garment and 
packaging), and a distribution loss of 1% to each transport leg from (Adams et al., 2025). 
Transport impacts are allocated on a weight basis, while for consumer transport it is allocated 
on a volume basis. Moreover, energy consumption from lighting and heating of warehouses 
have been allocated per product on a volume basis (see Appendix III). Losses related to the 
distribution and production of unsold products, such as excess inventory, customer returns, 
and unstocked returns not resold, were not included in the model. The need to produce more 
than one garment to sell one was therefore only partially addressed through the mass losses 
accounted for during manufacturing processes. Default transport distances for each transport 
leg are showed in Appendix II, provenances for share of total transport coming from local, 
intracontinental, or international supply chain locations are adopted from (Adams et al., 
2024). Transport modes include trucks (>32 t, EURO 4), container shipping, freight train, 
cargo plane, van, and passenger car. 
Packaging. Different packaging materials are used for different distribution scenarios to 
support transport from the garment assembly. Furthermore, packaging is also estimated for 
intermediary garment products during the manufacturing stage. Packaging is considered for 
three levels: primary, secondary, and tertiary. Primary refers to the plastic film and plastic bag 
or paper bag directly in contact with the garment. Secondary packaging is typically a 
cardboard box containing the primary packages. Tertiary packaging includes wooden pallets 
and shrink film used to transport grouped packages. Along the distribution chain, paper, 
plastic, and wood are partially recycled and the rest incinerated. Benefits achieved from 
recycling are considered outside the scope of the studied product systems, and only impacts 
from sorting, and incineration are accounted for. Primary packaging EOL for consumer 
transport is accounted for in the EOL stage, while secondary and tertiary packaging is 
accounted for during the distribution stage. Packaging materials used for each distribution 
scenario together with repackaging for different transport legs can be seen in Appendix II.  
Use. The use phase has been divided into the subsections wear, care, and repair for a better 
overview of the activities taking place. Wear: Wear frequency directly affects the number of 
laundry cycles per year, depending on how many times a garment is worn before washing. It 
is assumed that both conscious and mainstream consumers have the same physical demand 
for wearing pants and t-shirts. Based on (Daystar et al., 2019), a t-shirt is worn 200 days per 
year, and a pair of woven pants 300 days. The number of wears before washing is a key 
parameter for calculating laundry frequency, with specific values provided in Table 3. 
Care: The care phase is modelled using various laundry practices, including machine and 
hand washing, dry cleaning, detergent types, ironing, tumble drying, and line drying. These 
were applied as probabilistic shares based on garment type and fibre composition. While 
==PAGE:41==
30 
 
most care-related behaviours are modelled similarly for both consumer profiles, a key 
distinction is that the conscious consumer is assumed not to use tumble drying, instead opting 
for air drying, reflecting more conscious laundry habits. Laundry impacts depend on the 
selected machine program (cotton or synthetic), affecting washing and drying in terms of 
water (for washing) and energy use. Detergent use is modelled as a mix of liquid, powder, 
and no detergent, based on assumptions outlined in Appendix IV. The laundry probabilities 
are based on PEFCR guidelines by Adams et al., (2024) and Toft Ørbeck's (2024) 
interpretation of the work by Laitala & Klepp (2020), available in Appendix IV, together with 
visuals showing the scenarios and a detailed list of modelling assumptions related to laundry 
habits. 
Repair. Repair was considered only for the conscious consumer, assuming one repair per 
garment annually. For pants a repair constitutes replacement of a zipper consisting of 80% 
brass and 20% polyester. Due to zipper replacements being considered a more technical 
repair (Laitala et al., 2021), the consumer is assumed to transport the pants to a professional 
repair shop modelled with a consumer roundtrip of 5 km (each way). At the repair shop, the 
tailor is assumed to use electricity for operating the sewing machine and steam for ironing 
during the zipper replacement with values modified from Y. Zhang et al., (2015). For t-shirts, 
the repair activity involves mending a part of the seam, which is considered a less technical 
type of repair. It is assumed that 20% of the seam, made from polyester, is replaced using a 
sewing machine. For both thread and zipper repair the production of the raw materials are 
considered together with the distribution and packaging necessary as variations of the 
assumptions from Adams et al. (2025). While repairs do not directly reduce garment 
purchases in the model, the conscious consumer is assumed to acquire fewer garments overall 
compared to the mainstream consumer, for whom repair is not modelled. 
End-of-life. The number of garments disposed of is estimated based on the mass balance of 
textiles inflows and out-flows in Danish households (Watson et al., 2018), which considers 
both garments and household textiles. It assumes that garments follow the same proportional 
flow as all textiles and that the disposal ratio is consistent across garment categories. As a 
result, a disposal rate of 0.87 was applied in this study. Reflecting an increase in storage of 
textiles in households, according to Watson et al. (2018). Considering the newly introduced 
municipal textile waste fraction in Denmark, the modelled disposal pathways include 
municipal mixed waste, municipal textile waste, and separate collection for reuse. Based on 
Rossi et al. (2024), it is assumed that 47% of disposed garments are directed to separate 
collection intended for reuse, while the remaining 53% enter municipal residual and textile 
waste streams. The municipal streams assume a 100% consumer sorting efficiency based on 
the garment condition, ensuring that textiles allocated to the textile waste fraction are clean 
and dry. For the split across waste streams relevant to each scenario see Table 3.  
Waste management for the residual waste stream includes transport and incineration. For the 
textile waste fraction, transport to a sorting facility in Denmark and onward to a recycling 
facility in Sweden is considered, however, the recycling process itself is excluded from the 
system boundary. Textiles collected separately for donation are assumed to undergo 
preliminary sorting in Denmark, where 61% is exported for sorting within the EU, 1% is 
directed to recycling, 29% is allocated to second-hand use within Denmark, and the 
==PAGE:42==
31 
 
remainder is incinerated, with shares based on assumptions from Watson et al. (2018). 
Impacts associated with sorting processes at EU-based facilities are not included. The model 
accounts for transport between treatment steps as well as energy, water, and gas use related to 
operations at the Danish sorting facility, using LCI data per tonne of imported textiles from 
(Nørup et al., 2019). For list of assumptions see Appendix VI.

4.6 Global sensitivity analysis and data quality 
The robustness of the LCA result was evaluated through a global sensitivity analysis, using 
uncertainty propagation based on 50 Monte Carlo simulations. The analysis was performed 
using the built-in tools in the Activity Browser, which sensitivity analysis is based on 
simulating model response over the change in parameter for different trajectories in the 
parameter space. The sensitivity of the model was checked for the key parameters for the 
conscious consumer t-shirt, and the mainstream consumer t-shirt scenarios, for the impact 
categories: climate change and water use.  
To capture the variability of input and output values in the Monte Carlo simulations, the 
uncertainty was defined by assigning a triangular distribution to the key parameters. Each 
parameter was varied by ±10% around its baseline value, except for the number of garments 
purchased, which was the only parameter informed by available statistical data and used to 
define the shape of its triangular distribution. More information of the triangular distribution 
is available in Appendix VIII. 
The sensitivity ratios were provided for key parameters across processes, from which the 
absolute mean was taken to get the final sensitivity ratios for each key parameter across one 
life cycle. To meaningfully represent the sensitivity results they were subsequently 
normalised to the most sensitive parameter within the scenario and impact category. The 
normalised sensitivity ratios can be found in Appendix VII. 
For the uncertainty related to the parameter values the semi-quantitative pedigree matrix 
approach was applied to assess the data quality. The pedigree matrix utilises different data 
quality indicators to assess the quality namely: reliability, completeness, temporal correlation, 
geographical correlation, and technical correlation according to Weidema & Wesnæs (1996). 
Each indicator has been assigned a numerical value from 1 to 5 (five being low data quality) 
for each parameter. The assessment method and documentation of the data used for key 
parameters can be found in Appendix V.

6 Discussion 
6.1 LCIA results 
The normalised midpoint results showed variation across scenarios and impact categories, 
without a consistent trend. This can be attributed to the complexity of consumer behaviours, 
such as differences in fibre composition, garment quantity, washing practices, and disposal 
habits. Interpreting these results requires to consider each scenario in relation to the specific 
consumer's need for t-shirts or pants. Comparing consumer scenario results outside of this 
context could lead to misleading conclusions about their environmental performance. 
When comparing the t-shirt scenarios, the conscious consumer consistently showed a lower 
environmental burden across the selected impact categories. The mainstream consumer 
contributed between 2 to 2.7 times more to the total environmental impact (Table 6). In 
contrast, for pants the two consumer groups showed trends in terms of who contributed most 
to the environmental impacts across impact categories. 
Climate change. The climate change impacts observed across the scenarios highlight how 
both fibre composition and consumer purchasing behaviour strongly influence total emissions 
of greenhouse gases (GHG). The mainstream consumer’s higher frequency of garment 
purchases drives substantial impacts in manufacturing and distribution, particularly for pants 
made from polyester, a fibre with high global warming potential.  
A key contributor within the manufacturing stage is the fabric production process itself - 
weaving, which is more energy-intensive than knitting - in line with findings by Van der 
Velden et al. (2014). In contrast, although the conscious consumer’s use phase accounted for 
a relatively larger share of their footprint, their total emissions remained lower due to reduced 
consumption and fewer machine washes. The climate change impacts observed in this study, 
ranging from 70% to 84%, are consistent with findings in the literature, where manufacturing, 
particularly in Asian production regions, is reported to contribute up to 80% of total 
emissions (Niinimäki et al., 2020). 
The modelling did not allocate upstream impacts to second-hand garments, as the first-use 
and second-use product systems were treated separately using the cut-off approach. As a 
result, environmental burdens associated with second-hand garments were effectively treated 
as impact-free, only accounting for the transport from sorting centre to the second-hand shop, 
and to the consumer. This simplification represents a limitation when comparing consumer 
profiles, particularly those prioritising second-hand consumption. Future work should 
consider alternative allocation methods to better reflect the environmental trade-offs 
associated with second-hand use. 
Eutrophication (marine). The results indicate that eutrophication (marine) is a significant 
impact category for cotton-garments, especially in both cases of t-shirts and conscious 
==PAGE:51==
40 
 
consumer pants. This reflects the environmental burden associated with cotton cultivation, 
including fertilizer use and runoff contributing to nutrient loading in marine ecosystems. The 
dominance of the raw material and pre-processing stage suggests that upstream agricultural 
practices are critical to the overall footprint. These findings highlight how material choices 
can drive eutrophication impacts. However, the case of t-shirts also showed that even 
consumption of 100% cotton (conscious consumer) t-shirt is better than a cotton-blend t-shirt 
(mainstream), when consumption levels are minimised.  
Human toxicity (non-cancer). The human toxicity (non-cancer) impacts were notably higher 
for the mainstream consumer, particularly in the pants scenario, reflecting how increased 
garment consumption amplifies exposure-related emissions from wet treatment processes. In 
contrast, the conscious consumer's lower consumption results in fewer garments entering 
these high-impact processing stages. This highlights the influence of purchasing behaviour 
not only on carbon emissions but also on less visible, yet significant, health-related impact 
categories. 
Water use. The water use results reveal the trade-offs between consumer choices, material 
composition, and regional resource pressures. For t-shirts, the mainstream consumer's higher 
purchase rate of t-shirts, resulted in significantly more cotton being consumed, intensifying 
water use impacts due to the high irrigation demands of cotton farming. Conversely, the 
conscious consumer’s pants - despite fewer in number - had a higher cotton share, leading to 
a higher water footprint for that scenario. This highlights how both quantity of garments and 
fibre selection influence water-related impacts. Moreover, the inclusion of regional water 
scarcity in EF 3.1 supports the importance of fibre origin, as garments produced with cotton 
sourced from water-scarce regions like Bangladesh will have a higher impact per m3 water 
than for instance in Denmark. The findings align with literature values where cotton 
cultivation accounts for approximately 90% of the water footprint (Niinimäki et al., 2020). 
These results emphasise the role of consumer behaviour not only in how much is consumed, 
but in shaping demand for water-intensive materials sourced from vulnerable regions. 
The environmental footprint results reveal that the consumer with the highest impact varies 
by impact category in the pants scenarios. While the mainstream consumer generally 
contributes more to overall impacts due to higher garment turnover, the conscious consumer's 
focus on natural fibres, such as cotton, leads to trade-offs in certain categories. For climate 
change, the raw material and manufacturing stages were dominant, highlighting the 
importance of both fibre type and fabric construction method - with polyester blends and 
woven fabrics contributing significantly to GHG emissions. In contrast, water use and marine 
eutrophication impacts were higher for the conscious consumer's pants, largely due to the use 
of cotton, which is both water-intensive and a driver of nutrient runoff. Notably, the EOL 
stage remained negligible across all scenarios, contributing between 0–1% of total impacts. 
This reflects a limitation of the modelling approach, which only accounts for direct 
incineration emissions (~26% of the disposed garments), while other disposal routes only 
considered of transport out of the system boundary. This contributes to an underestimation of 
the impacts associated with the EOL phase. Future improvements would require expanding 
the system boundaries to include material exchanges from recycling and other downstream 
==PAGE:52==
41 
 
processes. Overall, the results underline that environmental performance is not only 
determined by how much is consumed, but also by what is consumed, what it is compared to, 
and where it is sourced - highlighting the need to support consumers in making more 
informed purchasing decisions. 
6.2 Model sensitivity to key parameters 
To assess which parameters most influenced the variability in model outcomes, a global 
sensitivity analysis was conducted. The analysis focused on two midpoint impact categories - 
climate change and water use - and was applied to the conscious and mainstream consumer t-
shirt scenarios. Although sensitivity ratios were calculated for all model parameters, the focus 
was placed on those identified as important during the scenario development phase (Table 3). 
See Appendix VII for sensitivity ratios. 
6.2.1 Key sensitivities for the conscious Consumer 
For the conscious consumer, the number of garments purchased emerged as the most 
sensitive parameter to water use. Other parameters showed similar levels of influence, such 
as the distribution share for second-hand garments, also in the water use category. This 
reflects the water saving potential associated with avoiding virgin fibre production, together 
with the importance of understanding where consumers purchase their garments from, and 
the consumption volumes of garments. These two parameters may also be affected by the 
substitution ratio between new and second-hand garments, which was only integrated for 
consumer transport, meaning that impacts were likely underestimated.  
The uses prior to wash parameter was highly sensitive within the use phase, as expected 
given its direct link to washing frequency, and the related electricity and water consumption. 
This further supports existing evidence that care habits - such as delaying washes can 
influence the water footprint. A main limitation to the validity of the sensitivity results goes 
back to how the two models: raw material to distribution and use to EOL were modelled 
separately. Consequently, the change in output compared to change in parameter was only 
calculated within the life cycle stages handled in each model, and the sensitivity indices 
therefore do not reflect the total model sensitivity. Uses prior to wash may, for that reason, 
show a higher sensitivity as it is only compared to the total impacts of the use and EOL stage, 
the same goes for the parameter disposal fraction for separate collection. 
Interestingly, t-shirt repair showed only marginal contribution to water footprint for the 
original conscious consumer t-shirt scenario of ~1%, out of the total life cycle impact, 
however it was found to be one of the most sensitive parameters. The uncertainty around this 
sensitivity result is further questioned by the narrow range of the triangular distribution 
assigned (0–2 repairs), which likely limited its variability in the Monte Carlo simulations. 
This highlights a modelling limitation that should be addressed in future work. Moreover, 
data investigating the correlation between repairs leading to fewer new garment purchases 
should be conducted, to meaningfully model this relationship. Buying power of Danish 
citizens, may undermine the potential environmental benefits of repair, if this does not 
translate to reduced consumption.  
==PAGE:53==
42 
 
For both the conscious and the mainstream sensitivity calculations, some processes had to be 
excluded altogether – true for knitting and transport from warehouse (DK) to consumer 
representing 250 km by van (e-commerce). This was due to a referencing issue in software 
Activity Browser during the dynamic modelling for uncertainty simulations, and the order of 
which calculations were called. This suggests that the dynamic modelling is not fail proof, 
although no error messages were detected when running calculations as was the case for 
knitting, which was excluded. It is important to note that these issues were not experienced 
during the deterministic LCA calculations. This highlights that dynamic modelling is not 
without limitations.  
6.2.2 Key sensitivities for the mainstream consumer 
For the mainstream consumer, particularly in the t-shirt scenario, variability in model results 
was driven largely by distribution-related parameters, such as the share of e-commerce and 
garment purchases. This suggests that consumer convenience and purchasing channel often 
shaped by marketing, accessibility, and online infrastructure - can significantly impact 
environmental outcomes. However, the model does not account for returns, unsold inventory, 
or reverse logistics, which are known to carry a considerable burden in fast fashion systems. 
The exclusion of these processes likely underestimates the full impact of distribution and 
could increase the sensitivity of the parameters related to distribution pathways.  
6.2.3 Parameters with limited influence 
The majority of the parameters were of low importance such as, tumble-drying ratio (relevant 
for the mainstream consumer), the disposal rate, and the distribution share second hand 
(mainstream consumer) due to their low sensitivity ratio. Parameters were varied ±10% in the 
model, limiting the analysis to moderate behavioural shifts. As a result, the model did not test 
more radical changes, such as a mainstream consumer switching entirely to second-hand 
garments. This constraint preserves realism but may prevent the model from capturing the 
grey zone between the conscious and mainstream consumer behaviour.  
6.3 Data quality assessment of high priority parameters 
The uncertainty of the key parameters was assessed (Figure 12) using the Pedigree matrix 
approach from Appendix V. This section will elaborate on some of the uncertainties related to 
those model parameters deemed of high priority (Figure 12). 
Garment number: This parameter directly scales upstream and distribution flows by 
determining fibre demand and the number of products (e.g. woven pants). Although its 
uncertainty was moderate, its high sensitivity highlights the need for more robust data on 
consumer purchasing behaviour. The value was based on a limited survey sample - primarily 
urban female students - which aligns reasonably well with the studied profiles but limits 
completeness. Moreover, self-reported data often used in this context introduces potential 
bias, suggesting that future studies could benefit from digital tracking of garment purchases, 
as proposed by Luo et al (2023). 
Disposal fraction separate collection (conscious consumer): The estimate for the conscious 
consumer’s separate collection rate (e.g. donation) is highly uncertain, as it was further 
==PAGE:54==
43 
 
extrapolated from estimates provided by Rossi et al. (2024) on the work from Watson et al. 
(2018) conducted in 2016. In contrast, the data quality for the mainstream consumer was 
higher, with the estimate was applied without modifications. The 47% baseline value reflects 
disposal behaviour across all textiles - not just garments. This highlights the need for more 
specific data on which consumer groups donate, which garments are donated, together with 
their motivations. While Daystar et al. (2019) report donation as the most common end-of-
life pathway among European consumers, it could be argued that donation rates among 
conscious consumers are lower. Given their tendency to repair and extend garment use, end-
of-life garments may more often be discarded due to low physical integrity and thus sorted as 
textile waste. 
Distribution shares (e-commerce, in-store, second hand): Distribution shares were among the 
most sensitive parameters across scenarios, particularly in relation to climate change impacts, 
and require further attention due to the variation in data quality. In this study, the share of 
second-hand purchases was derived from the same survey source as garment numbers 
(Ciechelska et al., 2024), increasing its reliability. However, the split between in-store and e-
commerce retail was based on assumptions rather than empirical data. 
The choice of purchasing channel is influenced by social and behavioural factors, such as the 
perceived service value of in-store shopping or the convenience and assortment visibility 
offered by e-commerce. Preferences differ across consumer groups, which can significantly 
affect environmental outcomes. For example, Vermeyen et al. (2025) found the share of pre-
owned garments to vary substantially between studies.  
Uses prior to wash: This parameter was found to be highly sensitive, as it dictates the 
washing frequency, which is a key contributor to environmental impacts during the use phase. 
Preferences for cleanliness are known to vary not only between different garment categories 
but also across geographical regions. Although the PEFCR for Apparel & Footwear adopts 
values from Daystar et al. (2019), these are not region-specific to Denmark, and there 
remains a notable gap in data representing Nordic consumers. Understanding drivers of 
increased wears between washes, could provide valuable insight into how different consumer 
profiles influence use-phase impacts. Moreover, washing parameters could further be 
improved by incorporating washing load levels from KLINT (2024) work on cleanliness 
(Sweden), suggesting washing impacts are vastly underestimated due to low load levels.  
T-shirt repairs: Although the model may overestimate the sensitivity of repairs, the 
underlying data quality remains insufficient. Baseline values were largely derived from 
literature indicating which types of repairs are typically carried out at home versus 
professionally (Laitala et al., 2021). While repairs such as replacing zippers are relatively 
well-documented, there is limited data on the frequency of repairs across garment categories, 
and on whether repairs lead to a reduction in new garment purchases. This represents a 
significant gap in the existing literature and limits the accuracy of repair modelling in LCA 
studies.

6.4 Operationalisation of consumer profiles 
6.4.1 The functional unit 
The functional unit is a central element in LCA and plays a critical role in how consumer 
behaviour is represented. As highlighted in the literature, one important factor to include in 
the functional unit for garments is the service lifetime (Jerome, 2025). Without accounting for 
this, comparisons between products can become misleading. For instance, in a comparative 
LCA of two t-shirts, the garment that is worn more frequently may appear to have a higher 
environmental impact - simply due to greater use - unless the functional unit reflects the total 
service provided. Including service lifetime allows the model to capture how increased use 
intensity can displace some of the upstream environmental burdens from production and 
distribution when comparing over a fixed time-period. 
How it was included: In this study, the service lifetime was not included directly in the 
functional unit. Instead, consumer needs and preferences for t-shirts and pants were defined 
based on the characteristics of different consumer profiles. These profiles reflected real-world 
purchasing behaviours, such as lower garment acquisition and second-hand purchasing 
among conscious consumers, and higher acquisition rate of new garments among mainstream 
consumers. Rather than allocating garments strictly according to service demand, the model 
incorporated consumer-reported purchase rates, assuming that purchases reflect both 
functional need and overconsumption. Additionally, disposal was modelled using mass flow 
data from Denmark, allowing for garments to be discarded for both technical and non-
technical reasons, and thereby enabling the possibility of wardrobe accumulation. 
Potential future improvements: Both service lifetime and purchase rate represent different 
forms of uncertainty when modelling garment use in LCA. One approach is to gather detailed 
data on average service lifetimes across garment categories; another is to model purchase 
frequencies according to consumer type. Each method has limitations, but this study argues 
that incorporating the consumer profile as a structuring element offers a valuable way to 
reflect behavioural heterogeneity. Doing so adds an important layer of granularity to how 
consumer behaviour is manifested throughout the life cycle, particularly in the use phase, 
where impacts can vary significantly. For example, a review by Luo et al. (2023) found use-
phase contributions to range from 2% to 96. Future modelling efforts could improve 
behavioural resolution further by linking service lifetimes of garments directly to consumer 
profiles, allowing for a nuanced understanding of garment longevity. 
6.4.2 Garment purchase behaviours  
The literature review explored key drivers of garment purchasing behaviour and found that 
clothing is generally not bought for altruistic or functional reasons, but primarily for aesthetic 
purposes and to support personal identity construction. Factors such as price sensitivity, trend 
responsiveness, and brand communication were identified as central influences on consumer 
decisions. 
How it was included: While these behavioural drivers were not explicitly modelled in the 
LCA, they informed the scenario generation using the SIMPL method (Figure 5). Purchasing 
behaviour was represented through the consumption volume parameter, which was assumed 
==PAGE:56==
45 
 
to correlate negatively with garment price, i.e., higher prices lead to lower consumption. 
Garment acquisition rates were not adjusted based on repair activity, as existing literature 
does not support the assumption that repaired garments substitute new purchases. In 
particular, Maldini et al. (2019) found that new garments are rarely purchased as direct 
replacements for old or discarded ones. As such, the influence of purchasing behaviour in the 
model is mainly reflected through consumption volume, distribution pathways, and fibre 
composition. 
Potential future improvements: Although the main retail channels were included, several 
emerging pathways, such as consumer-to-consumer exchanges, online second-hand 
platforms, and direct factory-to-consumer distribution, were excluded due to limited data in 
the field. In addition, future research should aim to collect more granular data on purchase 
frequency by garment category and garment alterations over time to better understand 
potential links between repair behaviour and purchasing patterns. Moreover, Patten et al. 
(2020) categorised customers by purchasing style: the conscious consumer may resemble a 
“connoisseur” type - emotionally involved, environmentally motivated, and likely to prefer 
in-store shopping with a higher disposable income. In contrast, the mainstream consumer 
could be associated with the “hedonist” type - trend-driven, more price-sensitive, and inclined 
toward e-commerce, free returns, and promotional offers. Applying such behavioural 
typology in future data collection could improve the quality of distribution data, especially if 
resolution over garment categories bought are considered. 
6.4.3 Garment use behaviour 
Garment care behaviours, particularly washing and drying methods, and the number of wears 
before washing, were identified as key drivers of environmental impacts during the use 
phase. Laundry frequency was shown to be influenced by social norms and personal 
perceptions of cleanliness. In addition, the literature highlighted that compliance with care 
label instructions varies significantly between users. In line, PEFCR recommends applying 
average laundry ratios consistently, due to consumer-related behavioural variations. 
How it was included: The number of wears prior to washing was included as the main 
parameter determining laundry frequency. Washing, drying, and ironing behaviours were 
modelled based on garment type and fibre composition, with technosphere flows (e.g., 
electricity, water) based on load factors and capacities sourced from appliance product 
manuals. Tumble drying shares were differentiated between consumer profiles, reflecting 
behaviour patterns. Repair activities were modelled for the conscious consumer scenario but 
were not assumed to displace new purchases, in line with literature suggesting that repaired 
garments do not consistently substitute new acquisitions. 
Potential future improvements: to fully operationalise garment care practices, future models 
should aim to better represent consumer-specific preferences, such as washing temperatures, 
tumble drying habits, detergent choices, preferred machine loads, and ironing frequency. A 
key limitation in this study was the lack of consumer-specific data, which meant that care 
practices were modelled primarily based on garment and fibre type rather than actual 
behavioural patterns. Addressing this data gap would allow for a more accurate reflection of 
user behaviour in the life cycle model.  
==PAGE:57==
46 
 
6.4.4 Garment disposal behaviour  
Disposal behaviour is influenced by social norms, particularly trend adherence, which 
increases garment turnover. Common reasons for disposal include poor fit, changes in style 
preferences, and technical wear or damage. Among European consumers, donation to 
charities is the preferred disposal pathway. However, disposal behaviour is highly dependent 
on cultural and geographical contexts, which complicates generalisations across regions. 
How it was included: Disposal pathways were considered a key parameter for reflecting 
consumer behaviour in LCA modelling. These pathways were aligned with relevant waste 
management infrastructure in Denmark, including separate collection, mixed municipal 
waste, and separate collection for donation. Allocation to each pathway was based on 
available literature and adapted to the consumer profiles used in this study. 
Potential future improvements: A major limitation is the lack of behavioural data on disposal 
motivations, which makes it difficult to assign disposal routes with confidence and 
meaningfully integrate differentiated disposal scenarios across consumer profiles. Current 
modelling relies on national level textile mass flow data for Denmark, which may not reflect 
individual consumer decision-making. Additionally, inconsistencies were identified between 
the intended description of the conscious consumer profile and the assigned disposal 
fractions. For example, the model assumes a higher share of garments disposed as separate 
collected (57% for conscious consumer and 47% for mainstream consumer), while the 
conscious profile considers absolute obsolescence more likely. Yet literature, does not 
confirm whether conscious consumers discard more through waste or through separate 
collection. In future iterations, these traits of consumer profiles should be refined to better 
align with behavioural expectations and improve disposal modelling.  
6.4.5 Consumer profiles and scenario development 
The development of explorative scenarios in this study was guided by defined consumer 
profiles, ensuring consistent and clearly distinguishable modelling across scenarios. These 
profiles aimed to capture psychological aspects such as attitudes toward garment use, 
garment interactions, and preferences across purchase, use, and disposal behaviour. The 
approach worked particularly well in combination with the SIMPL method, supporting a 
structured framework for scenario generation. However, the consumer profiles also present 
limitations. Most notably, they lack specificity with respect to socio-demographic, 
geographical, and cultural variation, as such data were not well presented in literature. 
Available indications on consumer behaviour were often based on small-sample studies, 
typically involving students, which represent only a narrow segment of the population. 
Furthermore, the framework would benefit from incorporating a broader range of consumer 
types to reflect the diversity of real-world behaviours. Moreover, garment-related data often 
focus predominantly on female consumers, which limits the broader applicability of the 
consumer profiles developed in this study.  
Typologies such as those proposed by Patten et al. (2020) for customers types, could enhance 
behavioural resolution across purchase, use, and disposal stages. While this study offers a 
proof of concept, expanding the set of profiles could improve realism and support the 
development of more robust, standardised approaches for incorporating consumer behaviour 
in garment LCA.

6.5 Evaluating PEFCR assumptions and behaviour-based LCA 
The PEFCR for Apparel and Footwear provides a harmonised technical framework for 
environmental footprint calculations across 13 product categories. It serves as a key reference 
throughout this study. However, this study also reveals key differences between the 
standardised assumptions in the PEFCR and the outcomes of behaviour-based scenario LCA 
modelling applied here. 
One major difference lies in the definition of the functional unit and reference flow. The 
PEFCR defines the functional unit as “one use,” with predefined assumptions about garment 
lifetime (e.g. 45 wears for a t-shirt), which leads to a reference flow of 1/45 garments per use. 
In contrast, this study adopts a functional unit based on actual consumption patterns and 
preferences over one year, resulting in a dynamic reference flow determined by the consumer 
profile. This allows for more realistic estimation of garment use-phase activities, and 
associated impacts. For instance, in the use phase where PEFCR uses a fixed number of 
wears prior to wash, while this study considers behavioural variations based on social norms 
and perceptions of cleanliness.  
While the PEFCR acknowledges variability in user needs, it does not offer standardised 
methods for modelling this variation. This is especially problematic when service life is 
determined by technical durability indicators (e.g. tear resistance, colour fastness), which do 
not account for, changing trends, or consumer values. Moreover, synthetics may score higher 
in technical durability tests, making it seem more sustainable, despite contributing more 
heavily to the fast fashion systems through cheaper material prices. 
This study shows that user behaviour, particularly related to use intensity and purchasing 
practices, can cause noticeable variances in environmental burdens. Such behavioural 
variability is not captured by the PEFCR’s default, average-based assumptions. These 
findings highlight the need for PEFCR frameworks to incorporate more nuanced modelling 
approaches that account for behavioural differentiation, particularly in product categories 
where use-phase contributions are significant such as pants and t-shirts. One possible way 
could be for brands to analyse the behaviour of their core customer groups to inform more 
behavioural representative assumptions. 
6.6 Study limitations 
Functional unit. The functional unit used in this study is based on consumer needs and 
preferences over one year. This introduces uncertainty due to the ambiguity of what 
constitutes a "need" versus a "want." While this approach allowed integration of specific 
consumer profiles, it requires careful interpretation to avoid misrepresenting the intention of 
the results.  
Model limitations. The modelling focused on fabric production and excluded other garment 
components such as lining, buttons, zippers, and thread. Packaging was included but hang-
tags were omitted from the system boundaries. Yarn spinning was grouped with raw material 
extraction under the "raw material and pre-processing" stage, although it is typically 
considered part of manufacturing. As a result, comparisons to other studies should be made 
with attention, and perhaps best done by summing the impacts from raw material and 
==PAGE:59==
48 
 
manufacturing stages. Additionally, weaving was modelled without accounting for variations 
in process type or fibre composition. Distribution impacts were also likely underestimated, as 
the model did not account for returns, unsold inventory, or excess stock, meaning the 
additional production and distribution required to sell a single garment were excluded. 
The use phase was modelled using predefined splits between machine washing, handwashing, 
and dry cleaning, based on average values from Adams et al. (2024) by garment type and 
fibre composition. These values were applied uniformly across both consumer profiles, which 
limited the model's ability to reflect behavioural differences, such as variation in washing 
temperatures, which was described in the profile texts. For the end-of-life phase, only impacts 
from incineration waste streams were fully accounted for. Other disposal routes exiting the 
system boundary, such as reuse or recycling, included only sorting in Denmark and transport 
to destination. As a result, the full environmental burden of end-of-life processes is likely 
underestimated. 
The modelling was divided between two collaborators, where one focused on raw material 
extraction, manufacturing, distribution and repair, while the other modelled the remaining use 
phase and end-of-life. This split approach posed challenges in merging the models, which 
ultimately were kept separate, and fully capturing combined uncertainty and sensitivity. 
While efforts were made to minimise overlap, most parameters were unique to each model, 
garment number influenced both, as disposal depends on the quantity purchased. 
Consequently, sensitivity to garment number was only accounted for in the first model 
segment. This underlines the need for more integrated and collaborative LCA modelling 
environments. Additionally, the dynamic structure of the model caused calculation issues 
during Monte Carlo simulations, which were not present in deterministic LCA runs. As a 
result, knitting and a specific transport process (250 km by van from a Danish warehouse) 
had to be excluded from the uncertainty analysis and global sensitivity analysis. 
Data limitations. Many of the online surveys used in the literature were based on large and 
diverse respondent groups, often with wide age ranges (e.g., 19 to 91 years) i.e. in Joyner 
Martinez et al. (2024), making it difficult to generalise the findings to specific consumer 
profiles. In contrast, studies using in-depth interviews tended to focus on smaller, more 
homogeneous age groups, which offered more targeted insights but came with greater 
uncertainty due to the limited sample size, as in Maguire & Fahy (2023). 
Data availability was the overarching limitation in translating consumer behaviour into LCA 
parameters. Key parameters, such as the number of laundry cycles, were derived from 
multiple assumptions, specifically the number of uses prior to washing and estimated annual 
wears per garment. This resulted in a chain of estimates, adding additional uncertainty. To 
improve the reliability of LCAs that reflect consumer behaviour, more primary data is 
needed, specifically data that captures garment-specific characteristics, consumer profiles, 
and the motivations behind use and care practices. 
6.7 Future research 
To enable a more systematic integration of consumer behaviour into LCA, future research 
should prioritise the collection of primary data that reflects variation across demographic 
==PAGE:60==
49 
 
factors such as region, gender, age, and lifestyle. Understanding the motivations behind 
consumer actions is essential for developing more refined consumer profiles and capturing 
the behavioural diversity that influences the environmental footprint of garments. However, 
given the complexity of consumer behaviour, it is important to define an appropriate level of 
modelling detail based on the purpose of the LCA. For instance, country-level assessments 
may benefit from population segments, while brand-specific studies should focus on 
modelling behaviours of core customer groups. 
A key starting point for future work is to obtain detailed behavioural data, including garment 
purchase frequency, distribution channels used per garment type, purchase motivations, fibre 
composition of acquired garments, and actual washing practices, such as temperature settings 
and machine load factors, to that end KLINT (2024) may be a place to start. Disposal 
pathways should also be investigated more closely, particularly to understand whether 
garments are discarded, donated, or recycled, and under what conditions. 
From a modelling perspective, there is a need to explore how different allocation methods, 
such as economic allocation, and substitution ratios, impact the environmental burdens 
between first-use and second-use garments. This is particularly relevant for second-hand 
markets that may overlook the environmental impacts of overconsumption or rebound effects. 
Given the global scale of garment consumption, developing a more behaviourally nuanced 
approach to LCA is considered beneficial. Such methods could help prevent misleading 
environmental claims, for example when garments made from technically durable materials 
like polyester are promoted as more sustainable, despite enabling low-cost production and 
supporting fast fashion dynamics. A more context-specific modelling framework can improve 
how environmental impacts are calculated and communicated in general. 
7 Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to explore how scenario-based life cycle modelling can reflect the 
environmental impacts of consumer behaviour, using t-shirts and pants as case products. The 
assessment was informed by the Product Environmental Footprint Category Rules (PEFCR) 
for Apparel and Footwear, while introducing behavioural variation through consumer 
profiles. To support this, a structured literature review was conducted to identify key 
behavioural drivers across the garment life cycle. These insights were operationalised using 
the SIMPL method to generate explorative, cradle-to-grave LCA scenarios that accounted for 
variation in purchasing, use, and disposal practices. 
The structured literature review revealed that consumer behaviour related to garments 
typically is grouped into consumer purchase behaviour, consumer use behaviour, and 
consumer disposal behaviour. Socio-psychological factors such as, trend-sensitivity, social-
norms, cleanliness, habits and perceived quality, were found to influence purchase rates, 
laundry, and disposal practices, while technical durability was identified as an enabler of 
prolonged garment use.  
==PAGE:61==
50 
 
To operationalise different use patterns, as a structural element in LCA, two consumer 
profiles, the conscious and the mainstream consumer, were developed as a proof of concept. 
These profiles were used to model variation in fibre compositions, distribution channels, 
repair scenarios, laundry practices, and disposal pathways. 
The normalised midpoint results (in person equivalents) showed the highest environmental 
burdens within the impact categories of water use, eutrophication (marine), human toxicity 
(cancer), and climate change, in descending order. The mainstream consumer pants primarily 
composed of polyester, showed the greatest contribution to climate change. In contrast the 
three remaining scenarios: mainstream consumer t-shirt, conscious consumer t-shirt, and 
conscious consumer pants, showed relatively higher impact on water use and eutrophication 
(marine) largely driven by high irrigation demands related to cotton cultivation. 
The results of this study underline that consumer behaviour has a significant influence on the 
environmental impact of garments. Between the two t-shirt scenarios, the conscious 
consumer was found to consistently show 2.08 to 2.74 times lower environmental impacts 
across impact categories. For the two pants scenarios the mainstream consumer had 2.44 
times higher climate change impacts and 1.22 times higher human toxicity (non-cancer) 
impacts, largely due to high purchase rate and the pollutant-intensive wet treatment 
processes. In contrast, the conscious consumer profile, defined by lower purchasing rates, use 
of second-hand channels, and increased number of wears, resulted in lower overall impacts 
but presented trade-offs, especially in categories sensitive to cotton use, such as water 
depletion (7.24 times higher) and eutrophication (marine) (7.24 times higher).  
The global sensitivity analysis for the t-shirt scenarios identified the most influential 
parameters as garment number, uses prior to wash, and distribution shares (second-hand, in-
store, and e-commerce). T-shirt repairs were not negligible in their contribution to life cycle 
impacts, and the fraction sent to separate collection also emerged as a high-priority parameter. 
Assessing the environmental footprint of consumer products with a significant use phase, 
such as textile garments, presents challenges for consistency and comparability in LCA. For 
example, the use phase impacts were shown to differ between 4% to 19% in contribution 
across scenarios for climate change. While the PEFCR for Apparel and Footwear provides a 
harmonised technical framework, it applies behavioural assumptions as static averages and 
bases service lifetime on technical durability indicators that are not necessarily reflective of 
actual consumer use. This study shows that such simplifications can overlook significant 
variability in user behaviour that directly affects environmental performance. 
The integration of consumer profiles offers a promising structure for reflecting behavioural 
variability, but the approach is constrained by limited data on consumer driven behaviours 
and motivations with a focus on quantified parameters, such as purchase rates, preferred fibre 
types per garment, washing temperatures, washing loads, and disposal habits.  
To improve this methodology, it will take collaboration across environmental science, 
psychology, and social science to develop deeper insights into understanding and quantifying 
behavioural motivations, as it ultimately is the user who decides the sustainability of a 
garment.

Laundry assumptions: 
Dry-cleaning: The dry-cleaning scenario is modelled for the electricity use, PERC amounts, 
and management of hazardous waste generated on a mass basis. 
Hand wash:  
==PAGE:75==
IX 
 
Consumer-dependent variables, such as detergent dosage, tap water use, and electricity to 
heat water, were modelled and scaled by garment weight to match the reference flows. It was 
also assumed that hand-washed garments are not tumble-dried, as these often represent more 
delicate items.  
Machine washing: The actual load of washing machines has been estimated based on average 
load ratios from German studies (Kruschwitz et al., 2014), together with the recommended 
load capacity for the specific washing machine AEG LV7KR865E. Modelling includes active 
and passive electricity consumption, water use, wastewater generation, and detergent use. 
Moreover, the average process for a washing machine in Ecoinvent 3.10.1 has been included. 
All flow data is based on the type of washing machine (front loader), while the EcoInvent 
database considers a top-loading machine. The machine wash programme considers two 
programmes, synthetic and coloured cotton, both of which are washed at 40 degrees Celsius.  
Energy use, water use, load (max) and remaining moisture are based on the machine manual. 
Exact calculations are available Appendix VIII. 
Tumble drying: Machine type is based on the model AEG LD8KR845E. Tumble drying is 
considered for garments that have been machine washed and considers programme options 
for varying levels of dryness (drying to closet dry or iron dry). Tumble drying has been 
modelled based on wet weight, considering the remaining moisture from washing. The same 
load factors from washing machines (Kruschwitz et al., 2014) have been directly applied to 
tumble drying due to lack of data availability. The energy use is taken from the appliance 
model manual.  
Detergents: The model accounts for different detergent types, however, packaging is not 
modelled nor is the use of fabric softener. The amounts of detergent used do not reflect a 
single laundry event but provides the overall representation of average detergent use. 
Detergent choice is based on whether the garment fibre composition is a blend of mainly 
synthetics or natural fibres. Detergent assumptions are based on Kruschwitz et al., (2014). 
However, a specific consumer may have preferences for a certain type of detergent (liquid, 
powder or no detergent) and use only this, although due to data limitations, this aspect was 
omitted from the modelling.  
Ironing: The activity of ironing is based on the modelling of an electric kettle, considering the 
similar function and sizes for small electrical appliances designed to heat water. Modelling is 
based on the time it takes to iron a garment, assuming that to iron a t-shirt it takes 2.6 minutes 
and for pants 4.3 minutes, based on values from Adams et al., (2025).
