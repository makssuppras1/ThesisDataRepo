Abstract
With the introduction of more sustainable binders e.g Alkali Activated Binders (AAB), a lot
of new research has to be conducted to test and understand these materials for proper us­
age in practice. One of these tests, and the main focus of this paper, is the determination
of setting time according to the standard DS/EN 196­3 [1]. Deviations from the standard
were made in order to determine the influence of various parameters.
Throughout this project it became clear that DS/EN 196­3 [1] may not be suitable for
testing setting time of slag and calcined clay based binders. Large fluctuations and devia­
tions were observed when using DS/EN 196­3 [1] for measuring setting time. The cause
for the fluctuations was investigated, but a definitive answer was not found.
Other parameters effect on the setting time were investigated and it was found that expo­
sure temperature had an effect on how fast the paste set. Lower temperatures caused
an extended setting period.
A lower w/b ratio also lead to faster setting time as well as a more smooth curvature
compared to a higher w/b ratio.
It was also found that double mixing the paste shortly after mixing could prolong the initial
set with multiple hours.
In general more research has to be conducted to fully understand the cause of the fluctu­
ations and the full impact of the tested parameters.

Introduction
The cement industry is responsible for 9% of man­made CO2 emission world wide [2].
Over the past years many options to cut the amount of emission have been explored and
tried out. This could be the use of materials that were seen as waste from either produc­
tion or waste from extraction of materials in the ground such as slag and fly ash. One of
these more sustainable materials is Alkali Activated Binders (AAB). Except for being more
environmentally friendly than OPC, AAB also provides more durability in certain situations.
Some researchers argue that AAB may be more durable towards acid attacks, sulphate
attacks and Alkali­Silica­Reactions (ASR) [3][4][5]. The use of new materials create a lot
of challenges that have to be addressed and tackled before they can be considered for
practical application.
One of these challenges is the determination of setting time. Setting time is of the up­
most importance for a variety of applications in on­ and off­site castings and experiments.
When having to cast concrete in layers, the setting time has to be known in order for the
fresh concrete not to be poured in on already set concrete creating weak zones. On the
other hand if it is not set enough actions like surface finish operations like troweling can
damage the surface.
When testing for setting, two parameters are of interest, the initial set and the final set.
The initial set is when concrete loses its workability and the final set is when the concrete
starts to develop strength [6].

Scope
The setting of AAB is highly dependent on a high pH environment. This leads to a problem
when measuring setting time. According to standard DS/EN 196­3 [1], testing of setting
time should be done on water submerged samples. Submerging AAB in water will lead
to leaching and lowering of the pH, which results in the top layer of the AAB­paste never
setting properly [7]. DS/EN 196­3 [1] suggest an alternative way of testing when water
submersion is not possible. The alternative way suggests an environment of 90 % RH
instead of the water submersion. The scope of this thesis is to contribute to the validation
of this alternative method on slag and calcined clay (CC) based AABs.
To validate DS/EN 196­3 [1] as a test method of slag and calcined clay based AAB, dif­
ferent parameters’ influence on setting time will be investigated. For the purpose of this
project the main activator used is Na2SiO3­based. The parameters which will be tested
are listed below.
• Temperature
– Exposure at 10°C, 20°C and 30°C.
• Precursor ratio
– Test for precursor ratios of 100/0, 90/10, 80/20, 70/30 and 60/40 between slag
and calcined clay.
• W/b ratio
– Tests conducted at 0.45, 0.40 and 0.35 w/b ratios.
• Different activators (was not conducted due to limited time)
– K2SiO3.
• Remixing
– Testing of remixing after 0, 5 and 10 min.
Throughout the project large deviations and fluctuation in the results appeared. The origi­
nal project plan was adjusted as the project progressed. New experiments were made in
order to locate the cause of the large deviations and fluctuations. Below an overview of
the new experiments:
• Substitute CC with fly ash
• Inclination of the sample
– 0◦and 2◦.
• Age of the activator solution
– 6,24,48,72 and 192 hours.
• Activator solid/precursor­ratio
• Repeatability
– Test of different batches of activator.

3.1
General
Slag and CC based AAB is the main focus of this project. Slag and CC need a high
pH to overcome the activation energy of the hydration reactions. Hydration of Ordinary
Portland Cement (OPC) produces Ca(OH)2 which increases the pH, while slag and CC
are dependent on an external source of alkalies to raise the pH levels. AAB are produced
by mixing an activator and a precursor.
• Precursor reacts with the activator to form a solid.
Precursor­ratio is used to
describe the ratio between different precursors (mostly slag/CC­ratio).
• Activator is what initiates the hardening process. The activator raises the pH levels
enough for the hydration reactions between the activator and the precursor to take
place. In this project the activator used is Na2SiO3 based. The molar ratio between
the amount of SiO2 and NaO in the activator is chosen to be one.

C3S, also known as alite, is the main component in OPC. Around 55% of the cement con­
sists of this clinker mineral. Water and alite react and produce Calcium Silicate Hydrates
(C­S­H) and calcium hydroxide. This reaction is the main component of the strength de­

OPC consists of around 20% C2S (belite). Its reaction with water also produces C­S­H
and is responsible for the slow strength development.
The reaction of tricalcium aluminate (C3A) happens very fast and if calcium sulfate (CS)
was not added the cement would set in a matter of minutes making it difficult to work with
[9]. This fast set is known as flashset. The retarding effect of CS is due to the formation
of ettringite around the grains of the C3A.

3.2.1
Influence of temperature on hydration rate of Portland cement
Casting and curing concrete in a 20◦C climate chamber is very easily controllable, but
when casting on site it is important to know how the concrete is affected by temperature.
It is well documented that curing temperature has a massive effect on the hydration rate of
cement [10]. Higher temperatures during curing can cause faster setting time. An uncon­
trolled temperature under curing can cause cracking due to the difference in temperature
in the core and the surface [10]. High temperatures during hydration can also reduce the
final strength of the concrete [10]. Samples may also dry out and stop hydration in the
surface.
The following figure shows the impact of increasing temperature on curing time of a Port­
land cement sample. The y­axis on the following two figures 3.1 and 3.2, is defined as
the amount combined water per gram of cement and combined water [%] [11]. Figure 3.1
shows a big correlation between the curing temperature and the hydration rate of Portland
cements.

3.4
Setting time test methods
The setting time of OPCs can be influenced by many things mainly the chemical compo­
sition, the fineness of the cement grains, temperature, w/c­ratio and admixtures. There
are many methods to test for setting time in both cement pastes, mortars and concrete.
The following methods are the ones most commonly used and mentioned in literature.
The ultrasonic method sends a pulse through a specimen and the velocity of the pulse
is determined via sensors and compared to the simultaneous measured temperature of
the material. The method is further described in the following article [14]. The ultrasonic
curves can then be analysed in order to determine the initial and final setting time. Another
method that is used to determine setting time is the proctor test (DS 423.17 [15]) where
the force required to penetrate a mortar with a needle is used to determine whether the
material has set. Another commonly used method, and the most important method for this
thesis, is the Vicat method. The method is based on the standard, DS/EN 196­3 [1] and is
described in section 5. The Vicat method can not be used directly for testing the amount of
time a concrete would be workable, but can rather be used to analyze the setting process.
The test method DS/EN 196­3 [1] suggests submerging the sample in water while testing.
When testing on AAB, submerging the sample in water would result in leaching, preventing
the sample from setting completely [7]. As water submerging is not suitable for testing
AAB, another method or slight alternation may be applied. The standard DS/EN 196­3 [1]
states an alternative method to determine the setting time of a cement if water submersion
is not possible or advised for the particular material. It states that the test should be carried

3.5
Time zero for AAB
In early age concrete, shrinkage plays a important part in the development of cracks and
therefore loss of strength [17]. In order to analyse the cementious materials for shrinkage
the final setting is needed in order for it to be determined when the material has developed
a solid structure that can restrain the chemical shrinkage. After the final setting time (”time
zero”) has been reached, the autogenous shrinkage starts to induce strain/ stress in the
material and risk of cracking can occur.
According to a review paper [17] many accept the use of the Vicat method as a suffi­
cient method to determine ”time zero” eg. ASTM C1698­09 [18]. This is also the case for
OPC, but for AAB the paper [17] suggests that the Vicat apparatus definition of final set
occurs way before any significant capillary stress develops and thus before ”time zero” is
reached. The paper suggest to use capillary pressure measured by the meiscus depres­
sion to determine whether ”time zero” has been reached.

4.2.1
Slag and calcined clay
Slag is a by­product of the steel production and used to be seen as a waste product. Slag
was found to be a latent hydraulic material which meant it could be used as an substi­
tute to cement. Fly ash is a by­product from power plants which show pozzolanic binder
properties. These coal combustion power plants are being replaced by more sustainable
options to generate power. Thus creating a short supply of fly ash, where as the steel
industry continues to be some what stable. Due to the change towards more sustainable
steel production, the slag composition may change in the future. This will change the
chemical composition and subsequently the binder properties of the slag. Some research
projects already aim to make artificial slag to accommodate future expected issues[19].
The slag used is a ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS). The chemical com­
position can be seen in table 4.2. It has to be noted that the values stated in table 4.2 are
averages from 3 tested batches of GGBFS. The full chemical composition can be seen
in appendix A.1. Due to improper storing conditions the slag used had developed lumps
that had to be sieved out.
Calcined clay does not require the same amount of heat to be calcined (700 ­ 850◦C
[20]) as OPC requires to be sintered (1400­1500◦C [9]). It also does not produce any
CO2 as well as consume as much energy during its production, thus making it a more
environmental friendly product than OPC [21]. Calcined clay is of great abundance thus
making it great for long term use in the construction industry [20]. The chemical composi­
tion of the calcined clay used throughout this project can be seen in table 4.2. For a more
in depth composition overview see appendix A.2.

5.2
Mixing of paste
Figure 5.1: The mixer used to mix the paste samples. The two containers for mixing can
be seen in the left of the image. Only the 1 liter container was used during this project.
In order to achieve the most homogeneous consistency of the cement paste a high shear
mixer instead of a mortar mixer, as proposed by the standard, was used. The following
steps are followed using the high shear mixer illustrated in figure 5.1. This method is not
according to standard, but is followed throughout this project to get the most consistent
results. Before mixing, the heights h1 and h2 (which are the positions of the mixer arm)
have to be defined. These heights are defined for safety reasons. H2 is the lowest height
approximately 0.5 cm from the blade to the mixing bowl. H1 is the highest the mixer should
go while still being able to rotate.
1. Add liquids to the container (water and/or activator solution). If activator is used,
shake activator container for 10 seconds before usage.
Setting of Alkali­Activated Binders
14
==PAGE:23==
2. Fasten the removable container in the holder by turning the screw.
3. Make sure to shut the clamp arms around the container holder.
4. When mixing the mixer should be at its lowest height (h2).
5. Start mixing at 100 rpm.
6. Add the cement/AAB by pouring it in from the side little by little.
7. Mix for 3 min at 750 rpm.
8. Stop the mixer and scrape the containers sides and bottom with a scraper.
9. Mix again for 1 min at 750 rpm.
5.3
Vicat method
The Vicat methods described in the following sections are based on the standard DS/EN
196­3 [1].
5.3.1
Filling the mould
If the sample is viscous enough the mould can be filled and voids can be removed by
tapping the mould against your hand or on the table.
For more dry samples the paste has to be scoped and pressed gently into the corners
of the mould. (Be careful not to compress the paste too much).
When the mould is slightly overfilled, the excess can be removed by sliding a straight­
edged implement in a sawing motion over the mould.
Between mixing and exposure of the specimens
The filled mould stays in a room with a constant RH of 50% and a temperature of 20◦C
for 10 min, where after it is being placed in the climate chamber with a RH of 90% and
20◦C. This was done for consistency reasons. Afterwards, the specimen is placed in the
automatic or manual Vicat apparatus.
5.3.2
Automatic Vicat setup
Calibrating the needle
In order for the Vicat device to take accurate measurements, the proper needle equilibrium
needs to be found. In the menu, the setting for ”needle calibration” needs to be chosen.
It will take a measurement at the bottom of the mould and one on top of the mould. Now
the needle is calibrated and the test can begin.
Starting the test
The mould is placed in a bowl and the bowl is filled with 20◦C ± 1◦C water and everything
is now centered precisely. It has to be noted that only a few samples where tested sub­
merged in water. When testing AABs, and some OPCs, the samples where kept at 90%
humidity as permitted by the alternative method stated in the standard DS/EN­196­3 [1].
Before the test can be started the correct information has to be put in the Vicat apparatus:
• Time of mixing.
• Specimen name.
• The standard (DS/EN 196­3 2016 [1]).
• Time of first penetration (around the time a first set is expected).
• Time in­between penetrations.

• If the final set is to be found choose ”yes” (Note that if final set is chosen, the samples
has to be manually turned upside down after initial set occurs).
Once the desired settings have been input the test can be started. The automatic Vicat
apparatus will run a total of 86 penetrations over the selected period. The penetrations
will be spread over the sample as seen in figure 5.2. The measurements start on the

Initial setting time
Start by making sure the instrument is calibrated. The instrument is calibrated by placing
the needle on the edge of the mould. The ruler should now be at zero or 40 mm, if not
the ruler needs to be adjusted.
Note the mixing time and every time a new measurement is made.
A measurement is made by removing the mould from the water bath (if submerged in
water) and placing it under the needle. The needle should now be dropped from the sur­
face of the sample. The penetration depth is noted as well as the time of the test.
Note that the standard DS/EN 196­3 suggest that measurements should be conducted at
conveniently spaced intervals e.g. 10 min. The minimum distance to the other measure­
ments should not be less than 5 mm and 10 mm to the previous measuring. Furthermore
the measurements should be made 8 mm from the sides of the mould.
In order to determine the initial setting time the dropped needle needs to be within 6 mm
± 3 mm to the bottom plate, that is the initial setting time rounded to the nearest minute.
Final setting time
The setup shown in figure 5.3b is used to manually test for the final setting time. The
apparatus consists of an up and down lifting needle with a ring attachment. After reaching
initial setting, the sample can be turned upside down to expose a smooth bottom. In order
to test for the final setting time the specimen is placed under the needle and the needle is
placed so that the tip of the needle touches the top of the cement paste. Now the needle
can be dropped and if the ring attachment imprint is not visible on the specimen surface,
final set is reached. The ring is designed to make an imprint if the needle sets more than
0.5 mm into the surface. To conclude that final set has been reached, the test is repeated

to the nearest quarter of an hour. An example of a final set test can be seen in figure 5.4.

Results
6.1
General information
The red broken line displayed at 0.5 mm penetration depth is the point where the speci­
men following the standard [1] has reached final set. The other red broken line is the, in
the standard [1] defined, initial set, at 34 ± 3 mm.
When using the automatic Vicat apparatus the standard, DS/EN 196­3 [1] states that
the sample must be turned upside down when initial set has been reached. This was not
done, instead the full setting curve was extracted from the top surface of the specimen.
Based on this final setting will not be noted as a value as the full setting curve is of more
interest.
Some of the results gathered cross the boundary for initial set multiple times, but for
simplicity and continuity sake, the term initial set, refers to the first time the curve crosses
the boundary.
Zero­time is measured from when all the cement/binder is mixed with the water/activa­
tor.
Sometimes there will be a few negative results those are due to a bad calibration of the
Vicat apparatus. This does not affect the results thus is ignored.
Some experiments/ results were deemed redundant through either being early iterations
of experiments or at the time being seen as important but in hindsight of non relevance to
this project. These results can be seen in appendix A.5 ­ A.7.

Analysis and Discussion
7.1
Vicat method for AAB
According to the standard DS/EN 196­3 [1] the specimen is preferably submerged into
water. This only works for OPCs and not AABs due to extreme leeching as previously
mentioned in section 3.4.
In order to verify this information an experiment was run submerging an AAB sample
in water as well as keeping one at 90% RH and 20°C for reference. These samples were
cast following the method in chapter 5 and then tested with the automatic Vicat apparatus.
In figure 7.1 it can be seen that the sample never set even after 24 hours. This complies
very well with the theory in section 3.4 deeming AABs unfit for water submerging. In figure
6.3 a comparison between the two methods can be seen. A clear difference between the
two methods can be seen, with the test at 90% RH giving a more complete curve progres­
sion.
For further verification of the method, a reference test was run with WPC. WPC is nor­
mally tested submerged in water. The specimen was tested at full submersion, 90% and
50%, all at 20°C. In figure 6.2 the results for this experiment can be seen. It is clearly seen
that the water submersion does not influence the result significantly. The difference in ini­
tial set from water submerged to 90% RH is around 30 min. As discussed in section 3.2.2
smaller w/c­ratios set faster. Therefore it would be assumed that tests at lower RH would
set faster due to evaporation. However this is not what the results in figure 6.2 show. In
figure 6.2 it seems to be the 90% RH which sets first, then the water submerged and lastly
the 50% RH. This is probably due to the uncertainty associated with this method. In the
American standard ASTM C191­21 [24] an uncertainty of 30 minutes is allowed between
tests of the same cement type. Even though the American standard is not followed in this
project, it is used as further evidence to accept the testing at 90% RH and 20°C through­
out this project.
To fully give justification to the use of non­water submersion testing, to determine ”stan­
dard initial/final set”, a lot more experiments should have been run. It should however be
noted that, as mentioned in section 3.5, the Vicat method may not be suitable for deter­
mining standard final set/time zero for AABs. Based on the gathered information through
tests and theory work, the test method at 90% RH and 20°C was deemed sufficient for
the intentions of this project.

7.2.1
Evaporation and absorption
As the activator in the AAB paste has different evaporation/absorption­properties com­

the estimate conservative compared to the evaporation/absorption during an actual Vicat
test. The petri dish was then weighed in conveniently spaced intervals e.g. 24­48 hours.

7.2.2
Temperature rise when mixing
One of the main concerns when working with the activator solution was the temperature
development when mixing it before use. As stated in section 3.2.1 the temperature may
influence setting time. The mixing process is described in section 5.1. The mixing of acti­
vator clearly showed an exothermic reaction. An experiment was conducted submerging
a thermometer into the activator right at the point of mixing between NaOH and Na2SiO3
to determine the rise in temperature. In figure 6.5 a spike to around 45°C can be seen
right after mixing, this is stabilized to 20°C after around 10 hours. The amount of activator
solution mixed for this experiment was 1.1kg, which is a fair amount higher than what was
usually mixed at once. Based on these results it was decided that the activator was al­
ways mixed 24 hours before use to ensure the temperature of the activator had stabilised
thus mitigating any uncertainties concerning temperature.
After the activator temperature had stabilized 889g of water was added to the 1.1 kg
of activator which corresponds to the amount of extra water needed for the concentration
to reach the same level as when casting AAB pastes with a w/b­ratio of 0.45. It is noticed
that when the water is added, the temperature drops to around 18°C which tells us that an
endothermic reaction happens when diluting the activator. As the liquid only drops about
2°C, an addition of 20°C precursor would equalize the temperature to the accepted limits.
Therefore the drop in temperature is not noted further.

about once every 24 hours the initiation of crystal formation could be prolonged. This ob­

The general procedure, as described in chapter 5, was used when mixing the paste sam­

7.3
Instrument variation
As discussed in section 7.2.3 a big variation was spotted between the two instruments
used to test for setting time. A thermographic analysis, using a thermographic camera,
was made to ensure that the instruments did not heat up the samples causing one side
of the sample to set faster than the other side. Pictures taken of the Vicat instruments
can be seen in appendix A.4. Based on the thermographic analysis no big temperature
changes were found.
In order to further investigate this issue an experiment was conducted comparing the
three instruments used. The same mix was cast 3 times for each instrument over 3 days
using 3 different activator batches (one for each day). The results can be seen in figure
6.7. A huge variation of more than 5 hours between initial set is seen. This is very peculiar
considering the same mix design was used for all specimens. At first glance, the results
shown in figure 6.7, do not demonstrate any consistency regarding the instruments used.
It was considered that the different batches of activator might have had an impact on the
variation of results. Sorting the results by their batches instead of by instrument, the plot
in figure 6.8 is made. The color in figure 6.8 represents the batch of activator and the line
type represent the instrument used. Now a more clear systematism is seen. It can clearly
be seen that each batch of activator has an influence on the setting time. All results pro­
duced from batch 1 have an initial setting time at around 2 hours, results from batch 2 set
from 2.75h to 3.5 h and batch 3 set from 6.5­7.6 h.
All batches of activator were made using the same procedure. The larger containers, con­
taining NaOH and Na2SiO3, were shaken before usage. It can be imagined that larger
containers required more stirring to thoroughly be mixed evenly and that the shaking was
not enough. This could have created an uneven concentration in the container, influenc­
ing the results throughout the project.
As the results in figure 6.8 are now sorted by activator batch, a systematism might stand
out more clearly. Looking at batch 2 and 3, a particular sequence in the instruments be­

7.4
The effect of precursor­ratio on setting time
Over the course of this project mainly slag and Calcined Clay (CC) have been investigated.
The CC and slag are used at different ratios based on the agreed upon ratios, discussed
with Ph.D. student Marvin Glissner, before start of the project. These ratios can be seen in
table 6.6. All ratios were tested at three different w/b ratios: 0.35, 0.40 and 0.45. Based
on the information discussed in section 7.3, a repetition test with w/b­ratio of 0.40 was
made, where all samples were mixed with the same activator batch. The results can be
seen in figure 6.9, 6.10, 6.11 and 6.12.
A variation between the two ”identical” 70/30 samples of 5 hours is observed. At figure
6.12 the initial set of 100/0 did not get recorded by the Vicat apparatus and must have
occurred before the 3 hour mark. Furthermore the 80/20 is fluctuating between a pene­
tration depth of 40 and 10 mm and is reaching initial set for the last time after 15 hours.
Common for all of the tests, except the repetition of 0.40, was large variation and drops in
the curves. Looking at the curvature can even cast some doubt on the method itself with
these massive variations. No systematism was very obvious from these results as they
may have been overshadowed by the large fluctuations
When taking only the repetition test of 0.40 w/b into account, there seems to be a ten­
dency for the higher slag content samples to set faster. 100/0 being the left­most curve
followed by 90/10 and 80/20. Lastly the two with the lowest amount of slag being the ones
which set slowest. This is however not a perfect trend as 60/40 precursor­ratio sets faster
than 70/30. The 90/10 and 80/20 follow almost the same setting curve and even cross
each other a couple of times.
To further support the statement, it was also noticed in the experiment testing at 30°C,
seen in figure 6.14, that a tendency for the high slag content samples to react faster than
the samples with larger amounts of CC. This also matches well with the theory that the
activation of slag with sodium silicate results in a fast setting [25].
When comparing the 4 plots according to their w/b­ratios figure 6.9 (0.35 w/b) presents the
smoothest curvature. This is no surprise due to the faster setting of the samples at lower
w/b­ratios as discussed in section 3.2.2. For low w/b­ratios the concentration of activator
when mixed will be higher and might also contribute to a faster setting. When comparing
the plots it can be said with certainty that the samples cast with a lower w/b­ratio generally
set faster than the ones with higher ratios.

7.5
The effect of temperature on setting time
To investigate the influence of temperature on setting time, two experiment series were
made. One at 30°C and one at 10°C. These were in addition to the experiments done at
20°C with 0.40 w/b­ratio. It has to be noted that all samples were cast at 20°C and 50%
RH like stated in chapter 5. Both the activator solution, precursor, moulds and water used
for mixing were stored at the tested temperature for at least 24 hours before use. The
mixer and mixing bowls could not be stored at other temperatures than 20°C before use.
The temperature was tested right after casting the sample and it had stabilised to around
20°C. Even though this was true, the materials were still kept at the tested temperatures
for consistency purpose.
As time was a limiting factor the 30°C experiments series were done first as these were
expected to set faster. The setting time from the 20°C and 30°C was then used to esti­
mate how long the setting time would be at 10°C by utilizing the Arrhenius equation. The
Arrhenius equation is described further in section 3.2.1. This could then help improve the
time management of the experiments. The calculated setting time of the 10°C experiment
for a 90/10 ­ 0.40 using Arrhenius was 26.7h. This estimate matches well the setting time
seen in figure 6.15.
When comparing the 3 figures 6.10, 6.14 and 6.15 it can clearly be seen that the sam­
ples at 30°C reach both initial and final set a bit quicker than at 20°C and a lot quicker
than at 10°C. This complies well with the theory stated in section 3.2.1.

In figure 6.16 three different ratios are compared: 0.2, 0.1 and 0.07. Mix designs for these

7.7
Remixing of paste influence on setting time
Since the timing of perfect repeatability in mixing procedure is very hard to master. It was
deemed of interest to research the influence of remixing the paste before use once more.
This was done after 5 and 10 min after finishing mixing. The paste was mixed according
to chapter 5. After the general mixing procedure it was remixed thoroughly with a metal
spoon whereafter it was cast into the moulds and placed in the Vicat instrument to be
tested. It was hypothesized that the remixing would prolong the setting time due to the
forming structures being destroyed. According to a paper [26], the impact of prolonged
mixing on setting time was tested and it extended the setting time. This is not exactly
what was conducted in this experiment but the mechanism in play are deemed close.
The results can be seen in figure 6.17 and a clear systematism agreeing with the hy­
pothesis. It can clearly be seen that the not remixed paste sets faster than the remixed
pastes. It is also clearly seen that the time waited in between mixing shows an effect on
the setting time with the longer wait prolonging the setting time even further. The results
alone show a big spread like in most of the results shown throughout this thesis. The ”not
remixed” curve reaching initial set with a difference of 2.5 hours compared to one of the
other samples in the same mix. This naturally takes some credit from the evidence, but
based on the clear systematism it can still be believed that remixing has an influence on
AAB’s setting time.
Some tampering was necessary during the filling/casting of the some samples. This may
be a cause for the large variations seen in other experiments as the time between mixing
and filling/casting could not always be kept 100% constant. Variations of up to 1 min are
to be expected in the time between mixing and filling/casting.

7.8.4
Effect of tilting the sample
It was hypothesised that the local setting (fluctuations) could be due to a combination
between bleeding and small inclinations in the Vicat instruments causing some kind of in­
consistency in the paste sample during testing. Each instrument was leveled thoroughly
but even the smallest deviation could have an effect. In order to test this an experiment
was conducted where a sample was kept at a known angle and measurements were taken
throughout the sample to see if the increase in angle showed a systematism in the results.
A normal paste was mixed following the instructions in chapter 5 and mould for mortar
prisms was filled as seen in figure 6.27. The dimension of the prisms were 4x4x16cm.
Four samples were cast, two kept at 0° as reference and two at a 2° slope as seen in
figure 6.29. The samples were measured at 5 different positions across the prism. This
was done using the manual Vicat instrument. If the hypothesis was correct the results
should show an early setting of the bottom part of the samples, namely point A and B as
seen in figure 6.29. This would be due to the solid particles settling at the bottom of the
sample while bleeding water being forced to the higher part of the sample, thus creating
a difference in w/b­ratio.

40 mm penetration could not be measured. All results started out as having set 4 mm.
This was compensated in the processing of the figures, but meant that final set could not
be measured precisely. Gaps in the graphs are due to the setting exceeding the ruler on
the Vicat instrument.
The results for the two sloped samples can be seen in figure 6.25 and 6.26. No sys­
tematism can be seen between the series of measurements as all of the series set evenly
and cross over each other along the setting curve. The reference sample seen in figure

the incline had any influence on the variations seen in the results throughout the project.

8
Conclusion
While researching the effect of different parameters on setting time (see chapter 2) vari­
ations and fluctuations were observed. Due to these fluctuations it was complicated to
quantify the precise impact of the parameters tested but some conclusions can still be
drawn.
When testing the impact of temperature the paste showed slower setting at lower tempera­
tures (10°C) than higher (20°C and 30°C). The magnitude on how much the temperature
impacted the setting time could not be determined with the limited amount of data and
variation.
The impact of a variety of precursor ratios indicate that a higher slag content leads to
faster setting than when mixed with a increasing amount of calcined clay. This was only
visible when looking at the curves, when comparing the time of initial set for the different
ratios it was more difficult to come to a conclusion.
An increase of w/b ratio showed slower setting times. It was also observed that mixes
with higher w/b ratios showed more substantial fluctuations and variations.
It has been established that remixing of an AAB paste has a massive effect on the setting
time and can delay it with several hours.
When comparing the results from the AAB and WPC it can not be rejected that local
setting also occurs for WPC. It has been shown in the heat maps experiments in section
7.8.2 that WPC also has some sort of local setting that did not show on the Vicat test
method due to the fast setting.
The reason for the fluctuations was not identified, but it was concluded that many fac­
tors could have an influence on the curvature of the setting time. More research and
experiments have to be conducted to fully understand this issue.
Larger fluctuations were observed on samples mixed with different batches of activator.
This indicated that the mixing of activator was not consistent, but for reasons unknown.
In general the method to determine setting time for AAB (DS/EN 196­3 [1]) is questioned,
not only by this paper but also by [17]. It should be considered to use/ create a different
method to determine setting time that is more suitable to handle AAB.

9
Proposal
For further research on the setting time of AAB, these following suggestions may be con­
sidered:
• Testing of different activators solid and in solution like K2SiO3, NaOH, Na2CO3 etc.
to see how they affect the setting time.
• More in depth research concerning the cause of the fluctuations noted during this
thesis.
– Use chemicals from a different manufacturer.
– Test of batch variations of slag and/or calcined clay.
• Further research on the repeatability.
• Collection of more data that could support some of the conclusions even further.
• Investigate further modification to the Vicat method to determine setting time for
AABs.
