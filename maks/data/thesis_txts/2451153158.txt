Abstract
The present project analyzes the consequences of making use of a model called ESM in a single wind
turbine and in the wind farm of HornsRev1. This model is based on the idea that extreme precipitation
events cause an erosion in the blade leading edge, forcing to repair the blade and to a loss in its aerody-
namic efﬁciency. The rotor tip speed has been proved to be a variable that inﬂuences the level of leading

Introduction
The present project is encompassed in the content of the current world transformation and tendency of
the energy sector into a gradually increasing renewable origin of the energy and electricity generation
and supply.
The global demand is still mainly supported by fossil fuels like petrol, natural gas or coal, which apart
from being the reason of a huge world pollution as a result of their massive emission of carbons and
greenhouse gases, they are a limited resource and generally under the control of a reduced number of
countries which as a result of it, keep the power of setting and managing the energy price for their own
beneﬁt. These facts contribute to the necessity of investing and continue in the investigation of alternative
ways of achieving an economic,constant and reliable energy supply
Increasing knowledge and population awareness of the direct relationship between the traditional energy
sources and their environmental consequences, are more and more the increasing sensibility of this topic,
has encouraged the energy transition leading it to a national and international level. Since 1979 twenty
different agreements on climate action have been developed to force countries change their approach to
the energy generation.
This has led to the creation of projects and goals partially supported by the governments which help in
the ﬁnancing to the big companies and utilities who share the energy market, which as a response start to
see proﬁtability and business in the growing sector of sustainable energy. Fees, taxes and in general the
policies to adopt are of special relevance at this point, and in countries like the United States are currently
a continuous debate.
It is therefore under all these circumstances where the role of wind energy starts to highlight and be per-
ceived as a solution and natural response to the energy transition. Some countries like Denmark already
have a strong and solid industry on the sector being able to provide around 43% of the annual electricity
consumption.
Institutions like the DTU Wind Energy of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in Risøﬁnanced
in a public way by the national danish government, contribute with their investigation to the development
and consolidation of the renewable energy sector, specially, in wind energy.
It is in this contest where different research topics appear, with the goal of developing and improving
speciﬁc problems or technologies inside the wind energy sector. One of these problems is the degradation
of the wind turbine blades because of natural factors like strong precipitation events. Thesis projects like
the one here introduced, contribute to create knowledge on this topic, and to help to ﬁnd a possible
solution to it.

2.2
Problem to solve
The principal topic in which the current project is based on is the Leading Edge Erosion (LEE), which
refers to the degradation that during the lifetime of a wind turbine, the blades suffer in its leading edge
because of any particles in the air caused by the external environment. The consequence is an erosion
of the blade coating resulting in an increase in the drag and a reduction of the time the wind turbine
can operate, having a big impact in terms of annual energy production (AEP)[1] and ﬁnal income or
economical beneﬁt for the manufacturer or particular stakeholder.
Recent studies [2] show the fact that leading edge erosion can actually become a relevant issue for the
aerodynamic performance of the blade in a time period of less than two years. It is even probable that a
ﬁrst reparation of the blade surface must be done within the ﬁrst 7 years of operation. The wind energy
industry is aware of this problem, and efforts are done to reach to a solution, from improvements in the
inspection or maintenance during the service life, to control systems to reduce the damage.
There are actually multiple reasons or threats in the environment that can imply a degradation of the
blade. Statistically, main factors are strong precipitation, dust, sand, insects debris, impact of hailstones
and also other minority ones in terms of consequences, like UV light and humidity, variability in tem-
perature, salt and chemicals [3]. However, as a response to the gradually increase of energy supply the
utilities are forced to face, the scale of an average wind turbine is signiﬁcantly growing. This means that
the above mentioned threats will actually be ampliﬁed, so the consequences of the leading edge erosion
is nowadays becoming a sensitive topic to focus on.
Blade length growth seems to lead to a direct growth in the tip speed of the blade. Although this relation
can be affected by the inner control system of the wind turbine, it can anyway be concluded that there is
a certain linear trend and dependency between the two variables. The fact that the velocity in the tip is
increased inﬂuences the relative impact velocity of any external object, which ampliﬁes its kinetic energy
and so the possible erosion or blade degradation per collision.
Figure 1: Leading edge erosion image. Source [4]
This reasoning makes that the correlation between tip speed and external environmental factors like
heavy rain and wind speed, in relation to the control system of the wind turbine, becomes an increasing
inﬂuencing point to study.
To sum up, leading edge erosion leads to have to repair the blades, which increases the cost of operation
and maintenance and reduces the energy obtained from the wind turbine. Consequently, the cost of wind
energy increases. As a result, to be able to solve this problem, can have a big and positive economic
impact in the wind energy industry. This point will be the main focus of the present thesis project.

==PAGE:10==
2.3
Related work
As it has been explained in the previous section, two are the main impacts of the leading edge erosion
that must be approached to ﬁnd a practical solution: the loss in aerodynamic efﬁciency, and the removal
of the blade coating leading to degradation and to a reduction of the lifetime of a wind turbine. Different
ways have been followed to provide an answer, focusing mainly on one hand, on the control system and
on the other hand, on the mechanical properties of the materials that constitute the coating in itself.
Regarding this second approach, known as the Leading Edge Protection, some advances have been de-
veloped in the last years [5], however, they still have to prove the success of their goal in long term
tests in real ﬁeld and environmental conditions. Blade materials used so far for the manufacturing of an
standard wind turbine blade make use of ﬁber reinforced plastic composite materials (like polyester or
epoxy ﬁber) which can actually implement an improvement in stiffness and speciﬁc strength, showing a
good behaviour against fatigue. [6]. However, it has also been proved [6] that these composite materials
present some weaknesses as they are specially sensitive to collisions, temperature oscillations, salinity
and humidity, leading to a non ideal performance against some external factors for LEE like strong rain
or UV.
As a result, to reduce the consequences of the impacts from external factors during normal operation,
a new group of layers or surface coatings are added to the blade during or after the moulding process
(through spray or paint), conforming a multilayered system generally known as "gelcoat", that increase
the lifetime of coating according to [6] by "matching delamination resistance between the coating and
the blade structure".
As an extra protection, some manufactures are making use of new developments like the ELLE shells by
Polytech [5] or the Polyurethane Protective Tape by 3M that absorb under a certain rate of success the
kinetic energy from droplets impacts, hailstones or smaller but continuous dust particles. These protec-
tions improve the damping properties and the fracture toughness [7]
In reference now to the wind turbine control system to reduce the signiﬁcance of the leading edge erosion,
studies like [7], developed by the Department of Wind Energy of the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU), suggest an approach through a reduction of the tip speed in the case of extreme or heavy precip-
itation events.
This study is based on a particular kinetic energy method that will further be explained. It makes use of
a raining test simulation through a whirling arm called (WA-RET), that can control different variables:
rain intensity, droplet size and rotational speed of the blade. Through different empirical tests, the study
concludes in some AEP and ﬁnal proﬁt results, depending on the maximum rotational speed allowed to
the WA-RET.
The consequences of applying this control system are currently under study (the present project, for in-
stance, contributes to it) and apart from the possibility of being able to provide a solution to the LEE
issue in the case the material approach fails, this method can turn out to be specially helpful to prevent
the erosion in wind turbines that are currently in operation.

2.4
Objective
The present project aims to study which are the consequences of reducing the leading edge erosion, in
terms of AEP and ﬁnal proﬁt, in order to check the beneﬁts of the application of an Erosion Model (ESM)
that will be described in the following sections. Also, the dependency between the results and the site
conditions (weather patterns), market conditios and wind turbine characteristics, will be a principle point
to study.
It must be noticed that under certain weather characteristics in some locations, it is probable [2] that there
is actually a necessity to repair the blades several times before the 25 years of a wind turbine lifetime
are concluded. Every reparation statement implies big expenses in O&M but also that the wind turbines
must be stopped and so the energy extraction from them.
So, the project will pursue to ﬁnd some optimal values of maximum allowed rotor blade tip speed (from
now on, called "lid") during special events where the rain overpasses a particular precipitation threshold
(from now on, called "threshold"). This is based on the idea that although reducing the rotational speed
under particular circumstances implies a consequent reduction of energy at that particular moment, it will
also mean that the erosion caused will be dismissed, which will turn out to be positive in long term, as
the cumulative erosion will affect less to the aerodynamic performance. This will allow to work closer to
the optimal design values, increasing the efﬁciency and so the energy outside these extreme precipitation
events.
According to this, the ﬁnal objective will be to reach an optimization system that under a wind farm
layout, it identiﬁes the optimal values of lid and threshold. Not only at every time step, but also at every
wind turbine, just in the case values differ as a consequence of the wakes due to the interferences between
the wind turbines.
It is also very relevant to remark that the current project aims to make use and contribute to the im-
provement of the optimization tools of TopFarm and PyWake, that are being developed by DTU Wind
Energy, Denmark. Therefore, every code that is created must be written in Python and must keep and
take advantage of the interconnection of data inside these two wind energy optimization tools.

3.1.1
TOPFARM
TOPFARM is a python package that works as an optimization tool for a full wind farm pursuing a ﬁ-
nal result in terms of annual energy production and economical income. The package is based on wind
energy theory that takes into account different wakes models, the wind farm layout, the site and wind
turbines characteristics, a load surrogate created with a code like HAWC2 and speciﬁc control systems
and cost models to reach to an optimized solution [8].
All of them are organized as it is shown in ﬁgure 2. An object called TopFarmProblem receives infor-
mation about design variables, constraints to apply to the design variables, and a particular optimizer or
driver that will depend on the characteristics of the problem. From Cost Componet it will receive the
information regarding the cost function to optimize, and in overall, TopFarmProblem will generate as
an output the optimal solution to that function (cost), plus the ideal combination of values of the design
variables (state). In addition, the recorder will provide information about the iterations carried out, like
the step size, for instance.
In particular, for the current project, "TopFarmProblem" has allowed to carry out optimizations in a fast
way interconnecting other objects like "EasyScipyOptimizeDriver" (to have access to a wide list of pos-
sible optimizers) or "IncomeModelComponent" (to deﬁne the principals inputs and outputs to focus on).
Regarding these optimizers, they are partially making use of OpenMDAO, using it as an optimization
engine. As the ofﬁcial guide of TOPFARM actually explains [9], the TopFarmProblem inherits properties
from OpenMDAO, considering OpenMDAO as an open-source platform that allows multidisciplinary
optimization in Python with efﬁcient numerical methods [10].

3.2
Optimization
3.2.1
Optimization problem
Before beginning with any optimization process it is recommended to be aware of the kind of optimiza-
tion problem it is being dealt with, in order to make a wise decision of the right optimizer to use, which
apart from saving computational time, it will provide the real optimal solution or global minima, instead
of just local minima or even an incongruous approximation to this global minima solution.
The ideal approach would be to have the mathematical expression of the function that pretends to be
optimized, however, due to the complexity of the current problem, and the different intermediate steps
and calculations made to reach the ﬁnal output key inside the cost component of TopFarmProblem, this
optimal way is not possible to be followed.
As a consequence, a theoretical analysis of the situation has been developed with the goal of reducing
the possible optimizers.
First of all, the goal of the problem must be clearly deﬁned. According to the description of section
2.4 (Objective), the problem must be to ﬁnd the maximum proﬁt modifying the values of the maximum
allowed tip speed (lid) and extreme precipitation (threshold). So, it can be deduced it is a 2 dimensional
problem as it works with two input keys.
Once this is clear it has been considered whether the optimization is either continuous or discrete. The
variables of lid and threshold are not limited to integers or binary numbers, initially they can actually be
included inside a range of any real or ﬂoat values. In optimization terms this leads to say it is continuous
optimization.
Regarding also the variables or input keys, in optimization, sometimes it happens that due to the math-
ematical or physical implication of providing possible values to the variables, there are some solutions
which cannot be considered. In the case to study, the limitation is physical, as values of rain (thresh-
old) and of tip speed (lid) below zero do not make sense, so each of the design variables have explicit
constraints of being >= 0 but also below some certain physical number, as is equally unfeasible to work
with a higher rotor tip speed value than the rated one imposed by the manufacturer, or is impossible to
have rains of 100 kg/(m2 ∗h) for instance. So the problem must be studied as a "bound constrained
optimization". According to [neos guide], this limits to Newton methods or gradient projection methods.
Another additional variable to analyze is the accuracy of the data is being used for the calculations. In
this sense, the data the problem is going to work with is supposed to be known with high accuracy (as it
comes from ofﬁcial data bases [12]), on the contrary of an stochastic optimization, where due to uncer-
tainty in measurements or forecast conditions applied to the variables, the data cannot be exactly known.
Actually, in the discussion section 6, as part of additional possible work, the case where the market price
database is obtained according to the last years trend, can be stated as a "forecast" so then the optimiza-
tion should be treated as stochastic.
It is also relevant to mention that, as it will be explained in subsection 5.1.3, the market data that is used
to work with the location of Hvinde Sande (one of the selected locations to work with), is computed by
a sample following an estimated probabilistic distribution with high uncertainty, so in strict mathemati-
cal terms, this could be considered as optimization under uncertainty of the stochastic type. Due to time
constraints, the study of the additional uncertainty parameters that in consequence should be included for

3.2.2
COBYLA, SLSQP, L-BFGS-B, TNC
These optimizers are studied as they satisfy the conditions previoulsy explained of being a Newton
method, a gradient method or an optimizer that allows to work with constrains.
Brieﬂy, a gradient base algorithm ﬁnds the minimum of a function [13] following an approximation
based on steps that are proportional to the negative of the mathematical concept of gradient, until the
active set at a particular solution is identiﬁed, in other words, when function to optimize has reached the
condition of f(x) = 0. In the case to study, this point can be a global or local minima, as by testing
different optimizers, it has been proved the optimization problem is not a strictly convex function. On his
behalf, Newton methods, approximates to the solution or stationary point by small steps through a Taylor
expansion [14]. In second dimension, these approximations are made by the gradient and the inverse of
the Hessian matrix.
So, in relation now to the four possible optimizers:
• COBYLA: although it is not considered neither a Newton nor gradient method, it reaches easily
local minimum under constraints by solving a linear programming problem in each iteration, until
the result cannot be improved and the step size is close to zero. It has proved to be useful to identify
precise initial guess points for a gradient or Newton optimizer
• SLSQP: belongs to the group of Sequential Quadratic Programming where the objective function
is replaced by a quadratic approximation, and the possible constraint functions or values by a linear
approximation. It works efﬁciently in boundary problems like the case to study [15]
• TNC: it makes use of a truncated Newton algorithm [[16]] as well as gradient information to obtain
the direction of a step following a line search method, always taking into account the bounds
delimited. The term "truncated" refers to that each iteration is drastically stopped as soon as an
updating termination criteria is fulﬁlled
• L-BFGS-B: it is a quasi-Newton algorithm for bound constrained optimization [17]. It is supposed
to not need a big memory space as it only requires the function in itself and its gradient to look for
approximations to the Hessian matrix [18]
Therefore, COBYLA is the only non gradient one of the four mentioned algorithms. Although it uses
linear approximaton which means that can get stuck on local minima, it can be used to approximate the
possible initial guess points for a gradient or Newton optimizer, constituting by this way a methodology
called sequential optimization.

has been developed to check the results obtained and the required computational time. Making use of

3.3
Erosion Model (ESM)
3.3.1
ESM theory
In this subsection the main code that has been widely used all along the project will be explained. The
functionality of this code directly responds to the objective described in subsection 2.4 so it aims to cal-
culate which is the total energy production and total proﬁt after running a wind turbine considering the
reduction in the tip speed in those extreme precipitation events. As it has been considered to be very
illustrative, a percentage in the proﬁt difference between applying or not the ESM model is one of the
main variables to take into account. So this code also analyzes the situation where the wind turbine is
operating in standard conditions, with no tip speed regulation.
So during a lifetime of 16 years, the code computes these proﬁts and annual energy productions when
particular values of lid and threshold are provided. Remember that lid is referred to the maximum al-
lowed rotor blade tip speed, and threshold to the maximum value in precipitation that is "allowed" before
the rotational speed is limited. Therefore, when the precipitation overpasses the threshold, and because
of the wind speed particular conditions, the rotor blade tip speed can be higher than the lid, then this tip
speed is limited to the lid value. It is said then, that the ESM is activated.
First of all, it must be commented that a very advanced version of this code was already provided from
the very beginning. During the development of the thesis though, several modiﬁcations have been imple-
mented by the student to adapt it to the speciﬁc situation to study. These group of modiﬁcations mainly
encompasses the adaptation of the code to be successfully read within TOPFARM and PyWake, changes
on the approach of some sub-parts to make the code run faster and some extra lines to adapt the ESM to
the particular case that is wanted to be analyzed or to allow it to load and read extra necessary data.
Regarding now directly to the ESM code, the ﬁrst part corresponds to the lecture and transformation to
the right units of the raw data. Some wind energy theory like the power law is here applied, calculating
the wind speed in the hub height. With it, it is possible the creation of the tip speed vectors (ﬁgure 4).
The ﬁrst tip speed vector will correspond to the original tip speed curve provided by the manufacturer
(blue line). It will depend on the type of wind turbine. The second vector (orange line) is the limited
one to the maximum value allowed. It is used exclusively when the ESM is activated, and it will vary
according to the particular value of lid. For the ﬁgures below, for example, the model has been computed
with a ﬁx lid value of 42 m/s and a maximum allowed precipitation of 4.8 kg/(m2 ∗h).

Gaussian Mixture distribution for market price
As it will be explained, the generation of an accurate time data series of market price is a relevant task

After the analysis of the most common worldwide distributions, non of them seemed to ﬁt with the ref-

Modelling (GMM) has been used for this purpose.
A GMM is considered a very ﬂexible probabilistic model which probability density function (pdf) is
expressed as weighted sum of a particular deﬁned number of Gaussian component densities [21]. So
technically it is a clustering algorithm where each generated data point can be created by any Gaussian
and the corresponding weight and probability of each one [22].
The python module "sklearn.mixture" has a class called "GaussianMixture" that allows training data
and also directly trying to ﬁt (through the method ".ﬁt") any data sample to a GMM of n components.
By a for loop, this model has been tested to check the most accurate number of Gaussian distributions
components used for the generation of the new pdf.
3.4.4
Covariance, Spearman, Pearson and Kendall’s tau coefﬁcients
The Spearman, Pearson and Tau-Kendall correlation parameters, are a measurement of the relationship
between two variables which value oscillates in the range of -1 to 1. They are used in the study case to
know the dependency between the data time series through which all computations are based on.
The main concept to describe at this point is the covariance. The covariance statistically is understood
as the second-order moment of two samples in time, separated by a small time lag [19], which in other
words expresses the joint variability of two variables [23]. Its sign gives information about the linear
relationship. The main problem it presents is that its magnitude actually depends on the magnitudes
of the variables used for its calculation, so the result is not normalized. This is why other correlation
parameters as Pearson are necessary.
Pearson correlation analyses exclusively the linear relationship between the two variables through divid-
ing the covariance by the product of the standard deviation. Its result is normalized, meaning that if it
is 1 there is full positive linear correlation, -1 full negative linear correlation and 0, no linear correlation
[24].
Spearman’s rank correlation coefﬁcient describes the dependence between the rankings of two variables
making use of a monotonic function, so it particularly assesses about the monotonic relationship between
the two variables [25]. As in the Pearson case, their values are inside the interval of -1 to +1.
Finally, in relation to the Kendall’s tau coefﬁcient, this is considered a non-parametric hypothesis test
for general statistical dependence, that provides an approximation of the rank correlation, so when it
gives back a value close to 1, it means there is a high agreement between the two analyzed rankings, -1
it means high disagreement, and 0 total independence [26]. The mentioned concept of rank correlation
is understood as a measure of the ranking or simply the relationship of any kind between two variables

calculations generates a table for all the wind turbines, wind speeds and wind directions. This table
already considers the updated erosion. From this table the effective wind speed is interpolated based on
the monthly wind speeds.

viding different values to its standard deviation to check which one is more accurate, the result is shown
in ﬁgures 37 and 38.

results section (section 5), the ﬁrst topic it deserves a discussion is the creation of a market price sample
using the Zealand market price data as a reference.
Paying attention to the ﬁgure 38 it is evident that the new obtained market price distribution is quite
similar to the experimental (Zealand) and theoretical one (GMM of 14 elements) but there are also some
small remarkable differences. However this fact is actually not a relevant factor, as actually the new
distribution does not have to be exactly equal to the one used as a reference. The reason of this is that
the location for both of them is different. As it is commented, the reference location is Zealand, and the
new market price sample will be used for Hvinde Sande.
According to the pool energy market price in the Nordic area [32], the island of Zealand is notated as
DK2, meanwhile Hvinde Sande is inside DK1, which in practical terms it means there is supposed to be
some differences in the market price between both locations. As market price is a variable inﬂuenced
by a wide variety of factors that are not taken into account in this project, there is always a margin for
variability in the market value. In consequence, the small difference in the distributions of Zealand and
the new one created for Hvinde Sande can be considered to be inside the acceptable possible range, so
this new market price sample must be treated as a reliable sample.
Next interesting result obtained is the relationship between the market price value and the percent-
age the proﬁt is increased by making use of the ESM (see section 5.2.2). It was appreciated here that
the lower is the price the higher is the gain in proﬁt. The reason that explains this is actually the variable
"reparation price" or the cost of reparation that the wind turbine’s owners have to face every time the
leading edge of the blade reaches its fatigue life due to erosion.
When the market price is low, the beneﬁt obtained in absolute values is also lower. In consequence, if
the reparation price is kept constant and a reparation is needed, its impact in that lower absolute beneﬁt
is going to be bigger. As one of the main reasons that leads to an increase in proﬁt due to applying the
ESM, is the difference in the number of reparations required, then this cost of reparations becomes in a
very relevant variable to pay attention to. It can extrapolated then, that for a ﬁx reparation price (which is
something that only depends on the wind turbine), the application of the ESM is more proﬁtable in those
areas where the average market price is low.
The reparation price for the wind turbine V52 was provided by DTU Wind Energy, however because of
a lack of information about the topic, that value was not updated for the simulations made with the V80.
As the V80 blade length is bigger, it would make sense to think that the cost to repair each blade should
be also higher. In order to avoid speculations, the same price for the V52 has been used for the V80.
Actually, increasing the reparation price (see table 5) increases the beneﬁts from the ESM, so the ﬁnal
results for the V80 should also be increased. This missing information is a limitation point that should
be considered to analyze if studies similar to this project want to be developed in the future.

6.2
General discussion on ESM results
In relation now to the location comparison results from section 5.3, ﬁrst of all it must be commented
that results from Hvinde Sande are considerably higher than for the other two locations. To provide
an explanation about this, the weather data from section 4 should be studied.
The mean values of wind speeds for Hvinde Sande is in the middle between Zealand and Horns Rev, and
its rain is actually according to the data half than any of the other two locations. As a result, if there is
less rain, there should be less erosion and then less effect of the ESM. However, there is a very important
factor to consider here, which is that the database from Hvinde Sande is taken from bins of 10 minutes
average, in contrast to 1 hour for Horns Rev and Zealand. This fact has an impact on the mean rain
values, and also it should have it for the ESM results.
The rain values from the databases show the accumulated rain in each in bin. Consequently the lower is
the bin size, the more probable is to ﬁnd more zero values in those bins to average with, which can lead
to a lower total average than if the mean is computed by accumulated rain in a period of 1 hour.
Working with 10 minutes rain values makes that the average rain is lower and so the rain rate. According
to [7] the less rain rate, the less droplet diameter and so less kinetic energy per impact. Following again
the study indicated in [7] the less kinetic energy of impact, the more impacts per area are needed to
remove the coating, so more fatigue life will have the blade. So in average values, the damage created in
10 minutes bins is lower than for 1 hour. However, it must be noticed that is the frequency of the extreme
precipitation events what it actually has a big inﬂuence on the ESM results, as the frequency of these
events will indicate the frequency of activating the limit in the rotor tip speed and so the ESM.
If 10 minutes bins are considered, there will be higher rain rate, as very strong precipitations do not usu-
ally last 1 hour, but they can perfectly last 10 minutes. In 1 hour bins these strong rains will be affected
of a short period of time will be affected by what happens in the rest of the hour, and then the rain value
for that 1hour bin will not be so extreme in comparison with the rest of values in the rest of 1 hour bins.
So in 10 minutes bins, following the same reasoning as before, there will be higher rain rates and so the
individual bin damage will be higher when these extreme precipitation events take place.
As a consequence, this is probably the reason why in Hvinde Sande the ESM has a bigger impact. It
has not been quantiﬁed however how much would be the impact of the ESM if bins of 1 hour had been
considered for Hvinde Sande. Therefore, this is a limitation in the project that should be corrected in
further studies. Anyway, looking at the precipitation time series in ﬁgure 12 it seems that Hvinde Sande
has a higher frequency of extreme events than the other two locations, so better results are expected (this
Hvinde Sande data in the ﬁgure is in units of 1 hour, but it comes from the 10 minutes bins, so actually
the extreme values should have a bit lower peaks because of the reason explained in the previous para-
graph).
Analyzing now the values of lid and threshold obtained for both ﬁx and linear approach, ﬁrst of all, it
must be said that at least, the threshold results are under the expected values as precipitation events in
the range of 4-7 kg/(m2*h) are actually considered as extreme rain values, but not so extreme so that the
ESM is very rarely activated.
In relation to the differences between both approaches it must be said that for Horns Rev and for Zealand,
the linear approach provides results slightly better, which it actually goes along with the expected results,
as in those cases the market price is really high, maybe it is convenient to keep the wind turbine working

project and its improvement should be studied as part of further studies.

6.4
Further work
The project has shown that there are some weak points that should be improved in order to continue
studying the control system of the rotor by limiting the tip speed to avoid degradation due to leading
edge erosion. In this subsection it is brieﬂy described a group of possible activities that could be carried
out to help in the investigation of this topic.
6.4.1
Market price variation
The energy market price is a variable that depends on multiple factors which are not part of the study of
this project. However,it is possible that during the next years because of the inﬂuence of any of these
factors the price starts to follow an increasing or decreasing tendency that modiﬁes the current values.
As a consequence it would be interesting to study the trend followed in the last years and with it, try to
forecast the market price data for the next years.
In addition, as each sample generated would be different, the proper way to develop this approach would
be to create a probabilistic distribution of the ﬁnal increase in proﬁt. By doing this, the variability of the
forecasted market price time series would be taken into account.
6.4.2
Different ESM parameters for each WT in a wind farm
This limitation has already been commented in section 5. During the simulations, it was assumed that
there was a common optimal value of lid and threshold for all the wind turbines in a wind farm layout.
By paying attention to the AEP grids or to the grids regarding the ﬁnal increase in proﬁt, it is clear that
not all the wind turbines are effected equally by the wind and rain.
As a consequence, a further study would be to analyze each individual wind turbine taking into account
its effective wind speed to carry out personalized optimization problems. Therefore, it is possible that
different ideal ESM parameters would be found for each wind turbine, and so the beneﬁts from the ESM
would be maximized for the full wind farm.
6.4.3
Bin size
This point has also already been commented during the discussion section (section 6). In order to verify
the results obtained in Hvinde Sande it would be necessary to transform all its data into 1 hour bins and
develop again the same study and simulations.
By this way it would be conﬁrmed several of the discussions and conclusions regarding the results and
their relationship with the weather characteristics of the studied locations and the selection of the wind
turbines.
6.4.4
time dependence
The study has been done over a total period of 16 years, as this was the length of the initial database
for Hvinde Sande. Consequently the length of the obtained databases for Zealand and Horns Rev was
adapted to it, in order to avoid different results because of a mismatch in the years the ESM was applied.
However, a wind turbine has an average of 25 years of lifetime, so as a further work, it would be to
estimate the implications of extending the operation period to these 25 years. Would the ESM be more
proﬁtable or on the contrary its effect would not be so beneﬁcial? Apparently, the more years the ESM is

be conﬁrmed.

Square approach
Finally, for the Horns Rev and Zealand location, it was found that working with the "linear approach"
leaded to an increase in the proﬁt. This means that, at least for those two locations, there is a linear
relationship between the market price value and the changing lid and threshold that must be set as limits

Therefore, other possible relationships could be looked for to maximize the results. A square relationship

7
Conclusions
The present project has tested the ESM under three different locations and two wind turbines. Results
show some conclusions that can be highlighted.
First of all it must be said that the application of the ESM leads to an improvement in the ﬁnal proﬁt
achieved, in all the analyzed cases for a single wind turbine. Although the results differ depending on the
location and the characteristics of the wind turbine, all of them show beneﬁts from its use. In a full wind
farm however, Hvinde Sande has turned into positive results for all the individual wind turbines, but in
Horns Rev location, some differences in ﬁnal proﬁts have been close to 0 or even slightly negative.
The maximum found ﬁnal gain in proﬁt for Hvinde Sande is of 5.47% for the V52 wind turbine, and of
2.63 % for the V80. Zealand and Horns Rev have similar results, around 2.40% for the V52 and 0.50%
for the V80.
As a consequence it can be concluded that the location is a factor that plays a big role in the ESM. The
higher frequency of extreme precipitation events there are, the more beneﬁcial will be to apply the ESM.
Regarding the wind turbines selections, it seems that both the rated rotor tip speed and the rotor tip speed
curve are the main variables to pay attention to in order to forecast the behaviour of the ESM. Operating
with wind turbines with high values on these variables will mean higher gains in proﬁt due to the ESM.
The tested linear approach in which the ESM parameters are linear dependant on the market price, seems
to lead to a slight improvement in the results. If the constant approach is considered instead, the optimal
maximum allowed tip speed value usually oscillates between 40-50 m/s, and the maximum precipitation
threshold before activating the tip speed limitation is between 4-6.5 kg/(m2*h). It also turns out that in
most cases, working with lids outside that optimal interval has a less impact than working with thresholds
far away from these optimal values. So threshold, is a more sensitive parameter.
The optimizer L-BFGS-B is the one than tends to ﬁnd a better solution to the optimization problem.
However, for the linear approach its step size at each iteration should be reviewed.
Finally, and as a relevant point to focus on in order to validate the results on Hvinde Sande, same ESM
simulations should be run with an original database of 1 hour bins instead of 10 minutes average bins.
This fact would verify the conclusions regarding the location selection.

A.4.5
Inherit class
To inherit a class inside or upon another existing class is a way to facilitate the interconnection between
different classes or objects in Python. Literally when in the deﬁnition of a class, another one is inherited,
it means that all its attributes and properties are actually imported and now are also part of the new class
created.The class that inherits is called "child class" and the one inherited "parent class" [31]
The beneﬁts from doing this are mainly two:
• Use methods from the parent class: there is no need to rewrite a method again. By inheriting the
parent class all their methods are available for its use in the child one.
• Interconnections with a third class: imagine that a third class calls a method of the parent class,
but because of the particular conditions, that method wants to be slightly modiﬁed but without
modifying neither the original one of the parent class nor the part of the code in the third class that
allows to call that method in the parent class. In these cases, the solution is to create in the child
class a method with the exactly same name as the desired method in the parent class, and introduce
there the required modiﬁcations.

Wind farm code explanation
The main code created to run the ESM in a full wind farm is explained in detail. Due to its complexity
and necessary understanding of PyWake, it has been included in an appendix.
The diagram of ﬁgure 82 shows the established relationships.
Three classes are created to allow this conﬁguration:
• BEWT (WindTurbines): Blade Erosion Wind Turbine, it is the single wind turbine ESM class
where all the calculations that strictly refer to the ESM are computed.
• BEWF (WIndTurbines): Blade Erosion Wind Farm. It creates a full wind farm by calling BEWT
and records the particular calculations of each wind turbine. It is from where the ﬁnal results are
exported. It inherits the class WindTurbines of PyWake (see ﬁgure 3), which means it can make
use of the methods inside it.
• TimeSeriesWeff (AEPCalculator): it inherits the class AEPCalculator and interconnects with BEWF,
BEWT, Site and WakeModel. It also reads the initial database and controls that computations are
made in periods of 1 month.
Figure 82: Block diagram of the main wind farm code
Initially the class BEWF is called. BEWF has BEWT in its __init so it can create by this way n number
objects of wind turbines. To create the site or wind farm, TimeSeriesWeff makes use of the properties
that inherits from the AEPCalculator which interconnects with Site and receives information about the
wind turbine it will be used, the wind farm layout and the particular wake model. Once these steps are
done, the ﬁrst monthly computations can already begin. The order followed in the diagram is explained
by:
• (1): from a created method inside TimeSeriesWeff called CalcAEPmonth, the WakeModel class
is called, starting to run the different steps of the N.O. Jensen wake model making use of default
values of wind speed and wind turbines. This default values will be used every time WakeModel
is called.

• (2): Inside WakeModel, when the thrust coefﬁcient needs to be created/updated, the class Wind-

this class, if a new CT equally named method is created inside BEWF, WakeModel will travel
directly to it as if it was doing it to WindTurbines.
• (3): this CT method inside BEWF needs to call a method inside BEWT where a vector of CT is
calculated. This CT vector will be dependent on the accumulated erosion suffered by the blade,
so by this way, every time the CT method is called, the CT vector is actually updated according to
the monthly erosion in BEWT.
• (4): BEWT returns a CT vector and a power vector to the CT method inside BEWF, creating so a

• (5): BEWF interpolates the input from BEWT with the default values of wind speed to have the

• (6): the WakeModel returns a matrix of 80x360x23 corresponding to the effective wind speeds of

• (7): making use of a vector of wind speed and wind direction that belongs to the whole i month, an

• (11): The i month ﬁnishes allowing the beginning of a new loop of computations for the next

• (12): the loop begins again, until the end of the initial database is reached. All necessary compu-
