Abstract
In this thesis, non-fullerene acceptors are studied with the grazing-incidence
X-ray scattering technique and UV-Vis spectroscopy. Grazing-incidence X-
ray scattering was used to analyze morphology, and UV-Vis spectroscopy
was used to investigate aggregation in solution.
For further operational-
ization of morphology, the effects of donor and ternary additives on mor-
phology are studied with grazing-incidence X-ray scattering. Attempts to
analyze film formation with a meniscus-guided slot-die coating technique
were made.

1
Introduction
In 2024, the annual global capacity for renewable energy increased by
around 25% to a record high of 700 GW. It is the 22nd consecutive year
renewable energy has set a new record for growth. Solar cells accounted for
over 75% of renewable capacity additions. Year-over-year photovoltaics rose
almost 30% reaching about 550 GW.[1] The first heterojunction solar cells
were made in 1986 and comprised a bilayer structure of copper phthalocya-
nine donor and bisbenzimidazo[2,1-a:2’,1’-a’] anthra[2,1,9-def: 6,5,10-d’e’f’]
diisoquinoline-10,21-dione small molecule acceptor, achieving a PCE of al-
most 1% with a fill factor of 0.65.[2] In 1992, Heeger and co-workers reported
the first evidence for photoinduced electron transfer from the excited state
conjugated polymer poly[2-methoxy-5-(2-ethylhexyloxy)-1,4-phenylenevinylene]
(MEH-PPV) to fullerene C60.[3] Nevertheless, the low solubility of fullerene
limited practical applications. To resolve this problem, functional groups
were attached directly to fullerene clusters, creating a soluble fullerene deriva-
tive, phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PC61BM).[4] Consequently blend
of MEH-PPV and PC61BM improved PCE to 2.9%.[5] In 2005, solvent
annealing was first used to control the active layer growth rate of P3HT
PC61BM blend, leading to PCE of 4.4%.[6] Despite improvements in PCE
of fullerene acceptors reaching up to 12% in 2016,[7] weak visible light ab-
sorption and difficulties with tuning energy levels led to development of new
type of materials fused ring electron acceptors (FREA) with ITIC in 2015[8]
and Y-series acceptors in 2019.[9].
1.1
Goal
The objective of the thesis is to provide insight into the morphology of or-
ganic solar cells. This paper aims to understand the aspect of morphology
in the structure-property relationship regarding organic solar cells and com-
pare structural differences between different highly efficient non-fullerene
acceptors.

of charges to electrodes. In conventional architecture, the hole transport
layer (HTL) is placed below the active layer. For inverted architecture, the

1.2.1
Working principles
The active layer of a solar cell absorbs light if the energy of the photon is
larger than the size of the band gap. Light absorption by the donor pro-
duces an electron transition between the highest occupied molecular orbital
(HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). This gen-
erates an exciton, a cubically bound electron-hole pair. Excitons diffuse to
the interface between the donor and acceptor, where dissociation occurs,
forming a free hole and an electron. The charge drifts through the donor
and acceptor domains. After charge separation, the anode collects holes,
and the cathode collects electrons from the active layer.[27]
Processes occurring in working organic solar cells can be described by an
energy diagram (Figure 1.6). In the process of charge generation, photo-
generated excited states S1 diffuse to the donor-acceptor interface with rate
constant Kdiff, where they dissociate into the singlet 1CT state with rate
Kd. Singlet exciton migration and dissociation compete with radiative and
non-radiative decay to the ground state. Efficient S1 splitting requires that
excitons reach the donor-acceptor interface within their lifetime (exciton dif-
fusion must be faster than exciton decay Kdiff, ex > kf, ex ) but also charge
transfer rate Kd,ex must be sufficiently large to ensure charge transfer before
the exciton moves away from the inter-face or decays to the ground state
(kd,ex > kdiff,ex and kf,ex).[28] Excitons are electrically neutral, and their
motion is driven by F¨orster and Dexter energy transfer.[29]
1.3
Energy transfer between molecules
Once a molecule is excited, energy transfer to the next molecule can occur in
two mechanisms: Dexter energy transfer[30] and F¨orster energy transfer.[31]
Energy transfer is classified based on the spatial proximity of interacting
chromophores of donor and acceptor.
The mechanism of Dexter energy
transfer involves the simultaneous transfer of electrons and holes from the
photoexcited donor to the ground state acceptor molecule. Dexter energy
transfer takes place when there is a short distance between chromophores in
a range of a few angstroms. The overlap of molecular orbitals of neighboring

1.4
Non-fullerene acceptors
Solar cells based on polymer donor/PCBM acceptor absorber layers show
PCE of around 11%.[35][36] As an alternative to fullerene, non-fullerene

1.6
Donor materials
Because of its extensive conjugated plane, strong electron-rich properties,
and exceptional hole transport properties, benzodithiophene with a two-
dimensional conjugated thiophene side chain is considered to be a particu-
larly good donor unit. The linear, rigid, and planar structure of benzodithio-
phene polymers ensures effective stacking and superior thermal and chemical
durability. Also, solubilizing chains do not affect the planarity of the polymer
backbone.[49] The addition of an electronegative atom, such as fluorine or
chlorine, lowers molecular orbital levels while widening the energy bandgap
of the polymer. Secondly, they improve the rigid and planar structure of
the polymer through intramolecular F-H and F-S interactions.[50] Enhanc-
ing intermolecular π −π interactions contributes to improved photovoltaic
performance of conjugated polymers. In conjugated polymers, electrons can
be transported intermolecularly by overlapping their π-orbitals with those
of neighboring main chains through a hopping mechanism or intramolecu-
larly along the main chain. 2D conjugated structure of benzodithiophene
enables the conjugated surface of polymers to expand, promoting stronger
π −π interactions between molecules, improving solar cell performance.[51]
Highly fused rings dithieno[3’,2’:3,4;2”,3”:5,6]benzo[1,2-c][1,2,5]thiadiazole
and dithieno[3,2-f:2“,3”-h]quinoxaline in D18 and PBQX-TF respectively
have higher planarity compared to PM6 leading to higher hole mobility.

polymer donors are shown in Figure 1.11. Among various polymer donor

The alkoxyl side chain on the quinoxaline unit ensures good solubility and
enhances absorption. Fluorine substituents lower the HOMO level and in-
crease hole mobility. PTQ10:IDIC solar cell exhibits an encouraging PCE of
12.7% with good thickness insensitivity, advantageous for large area organic
solar cells.[54]

1.8
Stability of OPV
In OPVs, achieving long-term stability remains a significant difficulty. Degra-
dation mechanisms are divided into intrinsic and extrinsic instability factors.
Alkyl side chains affect the stability of Y-series acceptors. Photochemical
and thermal stability decrease with increasing side chain length. In addition
to demonstrating greater resistance to photo-oxidation and photocatalytic
reactions when exposed to UV light, NFAs with shorter alkyl side chains
may also prevent the development of large phase-separated domains during
aging. Consequently, OSCs based on shorter alkyl chains exhibit better op-
erating stability.[61] Steric hindrance from longer inner side chains increases
energy barrier for vinyl group rotation, increasing stability of Y-series ac-
ceptors.[62]
The miscibility of donor and acceptor is an important factor considering the
stability of the device. Poor miscibility leads to morphological degradation
and poor charge generation.[63] Highly efficient donor and acceptor systems
often exhibit low miscibility. However, such systems are thermodynamically
unstable and undergo face separation. Phase separation forces morphology
into thermodynamic equilibrium composition.[64] Additionally, a large num-
ber of Y-series acceptors were found to have low Tg values, which causes fast
diffusion.[65]
Higher planarity of a molecule can improve stability. The stability of O-
IDTBR and its analogue IDFBR was tested. O-IDTBR is more photostable
due to a more planar backbone, which increases crystallinity and prevents
the film from oxygen permeation.[66] The same is true for polymer donors.
Worst photostability of polymer donors compared to NFA is credited to
light-induced dihedral twisting around the benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b’]dithiophene
(BDT-T) motif (Figure 1.13) shared by common high-performance donor
PM6 and D18. To address this, new materials should exhibit a planar and
rigid backbone without the BDT-T motif.[67]
Photodegradation, oxygen, and moisture are the main aspects of extrinsic
degradation.

2.1

2.1.1
Theory
When two electrons in atomic orbitals are brought together to form a bond,
a higher energy anti-bonding orbital is also formed. This orbital is empty
at ground state, and when an electron is excited by radiation, it moves from
the ground state filled orbital (σ,π) to the empty anti-bonding orbital (σ∗,
π∗). Not every transition between electronic states is allowed. Allowed tran-
sitions and, therefore, observables are determined by selection rules. These
rules are the spin selection rule and the Laporte selection rule. Spin selection
rule forbids transitions between states with different spin. Only transitions
between energy levels with the same total spin are allowed (∆S = 0). La-
porte selection rule applies to centrosymmetric molecules and states that
transitions between two states that have same symmetry with respect to
an inversion center are forbidden. It forbids transitions within one type of
orbital subshell (s ̸→s).
Absorption at a specific wavelength depends on concentration and path
length of the cell and is described by the Lambert-Beer law.
A = log(I0

Where A is absorbance, I0 is the intensity of light incident upon the sample
cell, I is the intensity of light leaving the cell, e is the molar extinction
coefficient, and l is the path length of the cell.
Linear relationship between concentration and absorption is accurate for
dilute solutions. At higher concentrations, individual particles are no longer
independent of each other due to increased intermolecular interactions.

phase-transition process of the acceptor can be separated into four stages
based on the evolution of the acceptor absorption peak: i) solvent evapo-
ration, ii) nucleation, iii) crystal growth, and iv) stabilization of the film
stages.[104][105] During i) stage position of acceptor peak doesn’t change,
but absorption intensity decreases. The acceptor starts the nucleation stage
as the concentration exceeds the solubility. Then it moves into the crystal
formation stage, which is aligned with the graded bathochromic shift of the
absorption peak. The film formation process is complete when the peak
intensity and location stabilize.
No significant peak shift was recorded; this is most likely due to the high
molar extinction coefficient of the material, preventing accurate measure-
ments at high concentrations. This technique, setup cannot precisely mea-
sure changes in UV-Vis spectra during the drying process for such highly
absorbing materials as Y-series acceptors. The reason for the decrease in ab-
sorbance occurring during the drying process is the Marangoni effect, which
is mass transfer between two phases as a result of a surface tension gradient
(Figure 3.6).

4
Conclusions and Outlook
Three different non-fullerene acceptors and sexithiophene were charac-
terized using grazing incidence X-ray scattering and ultraviolet-visible (UV-
vis) spectroscopy. The effect of different side chains and end groups on the
morphology and spectroscopic properties of Y-series acceptors was studied.
End group modifications have an impact on the orientation of deposited
molecules, where chlorinated Y7 exhibits more edge-on orientation com-
pared to slightly less edge-on oriented fluorinated Y6.
Additionally, the
effect of the ternary additive on the morphology of N3 was tested. Incorpo-
ration of N3 into PM6 has resulted in a change of morphology from edge-on
to face-on. Spectroscopic properties are also affected by changes in morphol-
ogy between different acceptors. Chlorination of the end group shifts the
onset of absorption towards higher wavelengths compared to the fluorinated
end group. This can be evidence of stronger intramolecular interactions and
aggregation.
Due to its low solubility, sexithiophene cannot be measured using the grazing-
incidence X-ray scattering technique. Additionally, it shows no signs of ag-
gregation when studied with UV-Vis. The small size of the molecule and
the freely rotating single bond between thiophene units sufficiently prevent
aggregation.
UV - Vis spectroscopy during slot die coating was performed on N3 non-
fullerene acceptor to analyse in situ morphology changes. Due to its high
molar extinction coefficient, changes in the spectra weren’t recorded. This
setup, particularly the UV vis camera, cannot be used to accurately measure
non-fullerene acceptors.
One important direction for additional research is probing morphology evo-
lution in real time. Analyzing materials during solution-to-film transition
has been conducted both using grazing incidence X-ray and UV Vis spec-
troscopy[107], giving new insight into aggregation and morphology of non-
fullerene acceptors.
