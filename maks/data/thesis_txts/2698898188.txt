ABSTRACT
There are presently few recognised technical guidelines available for structural design
and evaluating slab-on-grade constructions for light rail applications also referred to as
’slab track’ systems. This complicates engineering work when designing robust light-rail
constructions. In order to improve the current state of aﬀairs, this study investigates three
fundamental aspects of light-rail construction.
First, a simple FEM/analytical model is built to analyse the inﬂuence of a joint/gap
between slabs on the rail responses. Second, a realistic/complex FEM/analytical model,
including the inﬂuence of rail support and groove, is developed to analyse the fatigue life of
the concrete under service loads, including both train axles and vehicular loads. Finally, a
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is carried to evaluate the carbon footprint of the civil works
of the Ring 3 Light Rail project under a precast/cast-in-place scenario.
The main results obtained show: (i) the joint between concrete slabs has a negligible
inﬂuence on the rail responses; (ii) the maximum bending tensile stresses on the concrete
slab induced by train and vehicle loads do not reach the maximum allowable stress against
fatigue; and (iii) the global warming emissions of CO2 show slight diﬀerences between the
two manufacturing procedures investigated.
Overall, the mechanistic solutions of load transfer and concrete fatigue in slab tracks
through analytical and numerical models seem reliable and applicable to any slab design
for light rail applications.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
This research was conducted in the context of an ongoing project called Ring 3 Light
Rail, in which IDOM Consulting, Engineering, Architecture is involved in the detailed
design of the civil works contract among the design-build partnership with Per Aarslef
A/S, MOE A/S and IDOM Consulting, Engineering, Architecture; which includes the
design and calculations of the embedded slab track and the deﬁnitions of the individual
layers. The project aims to improve the understanding of slab track system design for
light rail applications.
In the ﬁrst phase of the Ring 3 Light Rail, Hovedstadens Letbane will connect the
terminus of Lundtofte Park north to Ishøj station.
The project, owned by Ringby-
Letbanesamarbejdet, will include 29 stations over 28 kilometres. The stations are being
built in partnership with the Danish State, Capital Region, and 11 municipalities behind
the light rail system for Greater Copenhagen [6]. Between 13 and 14 million passengers
are expected to travel on the double-track line each year with 4,000 individual trips per
day.
The main reason for the introduction of light rail systems is to stimulate economic
development, improve the public transport system with better accessibility for the
population, provide a comfortable transport system with low operating costs per passenger,
and reduce traﬃc congestion and pollution.
Trains will cover the distance in 55 minutes, leaving every ﬁve minutes. The line will allow
passengers to interchange with commuter trains at six stations. The new light rail trains
for Greater Copenhagen will be approximately 2.65 metres wide, 3.5 metres high, and 37
metres long, with seating for 260 passengers. The system is expected to reach a maximum
speed of 70 km/h, with power being supplied via an overhead catenary line.
The light rail system relies primarily on ballasted tracks and embedded slab tracks. One
advantage of a ballastless track is that it requires less maintenance. Another factor is
the load transfer to the base courses due to the higher stiﬀness of the superstructure
compared to the ballasted track. Two diﬀerent slab track models are distinguished, one
employs grooved slabs with embedded rails on continuous elastic support, which allows
train and vehicle loads, whereas the other model uses discrete rail seats on the slab track,
which permits only train loads.
The embedded slab track section consists of 59R2 rails, simply supported on the elastic
embedded rail system of the type ERS LR 60R2 MS on the groove of the slab track. The
slab track dimensions are the length Lslab=3,000 mm, the width Bslab=2,500 mm and the
height hslab=400 mm, deﬁned in the cross section in Figure 1.1.

1.2
Motivation
There are presently few recognized technical guidelines available for structural design
and evaluation of slab track systems. This results in the following challenges: (i) the
unavailability of studies centred on the inﬂuence of the joint located between slab tracks
in the rail responses, (ii) the nonexistence of standards or studies treating the design of the
slab track against fatigue in light rail applications, and (iii) the need to improve decision-
making regarding the slab track’s design, based on the carbon footprint’ calculation by
comparing an imported precast slab track element scenario and a slab track cast-in-place
scenario.

THEORETICAL BACKGROUND
This theoretical background explores the availability of standards for design based on
fatigue analysis. Such standards are not existing for light rail applications, a review is
conducted on slab track design for railways.
This work focuses on the inﬂuence of a joint on the rail responses, and the formulation
of the analytical solution of the semi-inﬁnite beam. Furthermore, to obtain the bending
tensile stresses on slab-on-grade, Westergaard’s approximations for supported plates are
reviewed.
2.1
Design Standards
There is a lack of design standards for slab tracks on light rail applications; however
diﬀerent design standards are available for railway slab track systems based on fatigue
analysis. For example, the Technical Committee’s CEN/TC 256 "Railway applications"
has developed the European Standard EN 16432 "Railway applications - Ballastless track
systems", which is divided into three parts: (i) general requirements, (ii) system design,
subsystems and components, and (iii) acceptance [12].
Furthermore, Deutsche Bahn Standard (DBS) provides its technical delivery conditions
for slab track made of concrete ("918 143: Gleis and Weichenschwellen aus Beton fur
Schotteroberbau und Feste Fahrbahn") and the German Railways AKFF speciﬁcation has
a requirements catalogue for the construction of slab track "Anforderungskatalog zum Bau
der Festen Fahrbahn (AKFF), 4th ed. (1 Aug 2002)".
2.2
Analytical Solutions for Rail and Slab-on-grade
This subsection describes two existing techniques relevant for slab track design. The ﬁrst
is the inﬁnite beam on a Winkler foundation which allows the obtainment of the responses
on the rail under point loads; the second is Westergaard’s approximations which enable
calculating the vertical displacements and bending tensile stresses on an inﬁnite plate
resting on a Winkler foundation.

Load transfer eﬃciency has not been deeply analysed on light rail applications. However,
a study elaborated on by Linh Vu and Yun Suk Kang [17] evaluates the displacement of
the structures as well as the load transfer eﬃciency of a precast ﬂoating slab track for

In contrast, concrete fatigue is widely explored amongst the procedures for rigid pavement
design.
This study focuses on the fatigue analysis of plain concrete pavements.
The
review is separated into diﬀerent concrete fatigue procedures: ﬁrst, the experimental
investigations utilising fatigue beam and slab results; second, the ﬁeld fatigue testing
results; third, the design procedures with fatigue analysis under traﬃc loads; and forth,
the Smith fatigue method considering traﬃc loading and temperature loading.
3.1
Transfer Eﬃciency between Slabs
The precast slab track system consists of a continuous rail, mounted on the concrete
structure with a fastening system. In a study conducted in Korea, continuous rails (60
K rail) were used as a connection between slabs instead of dowels to reduce construction
costs [17]. The study evaluated the displacement of the structures as well as the load
transfer eﬃciency of the precast ﬂoating slab track, and found that when the rails were

3.2
Concrete Fatigue
Concrete fatigue is deﬁned as a rupture under repeated loadings, producing a progressive
perpetual internal structural variation in the material [19]. The fatigue strength is deﬁned
as the highest stress that a material can withstand a repeated number of cycles, it is
usually represented by fatigue life curves. Numerous theories have been developed about
the phenomenon of fatigue and how fatigue strength develops for a given number of
cycles. The following methods proposed by Darter (1977), Aas-Jakobsen (1970), Roesler
(1998), Rollings and Witczak (1990), Jeﬀery R. Roesler (2005), the Portland Cement
Association (PCA), the American Association of State Highway and Transportation
Oﬃcials (AASHTO), and the Corps of Engineers are analysed here.
The American Concrete Institute (ACI) has established a classiﬁcation for plain pavements,
reinforced pavements, and continuously reinforced pavements (CRCP). Plain pavements
are diﬀerentiated as having a frequent joint, with no reinforcement, and with or without
dowels. Reinforced pavements are long slabs with distributed reinforcing and dowelled
joints, and continuously reinforced pavements are characterised as long slabs with more
reinforcement than a reinforced concrete pavement and with no transverse joints [20].
ACI has also highlighted the factors which inﬂuence the service life of concrete pavements,
such as the type of traﬃc; the environment creating stress gradients because of changes
in temperature; boundary conditions such as the subgrade friction, the load transfer
eﬃciency at joints, and the position of loads concerning the joints and pavement edges;
and the variation on the support conditions aﬀected by the loss of material by pumping,
densiﬁcation, displacement of the subgrade, and the soil volume variation due to moisture
development and frost [20].
The stress-life cycle (S-N) or Whöler curves show the relationship between constant stress
amplitude applied to the material and the number of cycles leading to failure. To obtain
the curve, each of the tested specimens is subjected to a cyclic load of constant amplitude
until the number of cycles leading to failure of the specimen is reached. The logarithm of
the number of cycles N to failure of each specimen at a given maximum stress level σmax
is plotted in a diagram.
High cycle fatigue is the stress limit which a material can withstand under cyclic loading
without signiﬁcant signs of fatigue. In the high cycle fatigue range, a limited number of
cycles N is speciﬁed. If the specimen fails before reaching this number, it is considered as

manufacturer-speciﬁc data complying with the requirements of the standard EN 15978.

OBJECTIVES AND METHODOLOGY
This work analyses precast slab-on-grade constructions designed for light rail applications.
The objectives are three-fold: (i) investigate the inﬂuence of a gap between adjacent slabs
on rail responses, (ii) quantify the fatigue life of the concrete under service loads (i.e.,
under train axles and vehicular tyres), and (iii) assess the carbon footprint of the Ring
3 Light Rail project, comparing the chosen scenario which involves precast concrete slabs
against an alternative scenario involving cast-in-place concrete slabs.
To investigate the inﬂuence of a gap between adjacent slabs on rail responses, three levels
of analysis are applied. The ﬁrst is analytical, based on the solution for a semi-inﬁnite
beam on a Winkler foundation. The beam represents the rail, and the spring foundation
represents the rubber ﬁlling under the rail.
A gap is approximated by superposing a
solution involving a point force and a concentrated moment. Comparison with the case of
continuous support (i.e. without an approximate gap) is conducted. The second analysis
level is based on a 2D (plane stress) ﬁnite element model, in which the rail is modelled as
a beam element, and the support is modelled as an elastic layer with a ﬁnite thickness –
ﬁxed at the bottom. The responses in the beam under a vertical loading are calculated for
when there is a gap (or no gap) in the elastic support. The third analysis level is based on
a 3D ﬁnite element model in which the rail is modelled as a solid elastic element, and the
support is also modelled as an elastic solid with a ﬁnite thickness – ﬁxed at the bottom.
To quantify the fatigue life of the concrete under service loads, a separate analysis evaluates
the application of wheel loads from train passes (via the rails) and vehicular traﬃc passes
(anywhere on the concrete surface). The analysis of maximum bending tensile stresses
occasioned by train passes are obtained from the application of the stiﬀness method to
the vertical displacements of the rail from the analytical solution of the semi-inﬁnite
beam on a Winkler foundation and the vertical spring stiﬀness of the elastic material; the
resultant vertical reactions are discretised and applied on the 3D ﬁnite element model of
the slab track as a solid elastic element supported on the multilayer foundation of the
granular subbase, subbase gravel, and intact soil as solid elastic elements. The assessment
of the vehicular traﬃc passes is realised by applying a pressure load on the slab centre, slab
corner, and slab edges on a 3D ﬁnite element model where the rails are neglected, consisting
of a slab track solid elastic element on a multilayer foundation.
A mesh convergence
study calibrates the slab model using Westergaard’s approximations for supported slabs
on a Winkler foundation. The allowable bending tensile stress on the concrete slab is
determined following diﬀerent procedures for slab fatigue.
Lastly, the carbon footprint calculation is done for a one-metre long segment of the civil
works in the Ring 3 Light Rail project; it is based on a commercial web application
called One Click LCA. This application is designed for the life cycle assessment of civil
engineering constructions based on six impact categories - one of which is called global

5.1
The Abaqus Platform
The ﬁnite element programme used for the analyses in this study is Abaqus, which is
explicity designed for advanced structural and heat transfer analyses [36]. It provides a
full geometric modelling capability with solid elements in one, two or three dimensions, as
well as shells, beams, pipes and pipe bends using ﬁrst, second or third order interpolation.
These elements can be deﬁned as linear and non-linear user-deﬁned element types
which are introduced into the model, which analysis methods can be mixed for useful
combinations.
The programme has a comprehensive set of tools for modelling contact and interface
problems for stress analysis. Contact is modelled by identifying and pairing the surfaces
of the elements which interact by adding their interaction properties.
The boundary
conditions are deﬁned at each supported node set, and symmetry boundary conditions
of the models with symmetric acting loads are created to reduce the computational costs.
The materials are settled as linear elastic elements with modulus of elasticity and Poisson’s
ratio.
Abaqus can adopt automatic time increments; this approach provides uniform accuracy
over the entire solution course instead of controlled ﬁxed time increments [37]. The time
increment is set for the time period of 1, with a maximum number of 1,000 increments,
with initial increment size 0.01, and minimum and maximum increments of 0.00005 and
0.5 respectively.
The element types are deﬁned in the standard library with linear geometric order, using
exclusively:
• Quadrilateral elements with four-node bilinear plane stress (CPS4R)
• Hex elements with eight-node linear brick (C3D8R)
• Three-dimensional inﬁnite element (CIN3D8)

There is a long embedded railway with a series of abutting concrete slabs. The rails are
supported by rubber on the underside and are loaded by passing an axle. The problem
involves studying the inﬂuence of the joints between slabs on the responses of the rail.
Figure 5.10: Representation of the embedded slab track system for light rail applications
The problem is solved by mechanical modelling under the following assumptions: (i) the
two rails do not interact and the solution can only focus on one side; (ii) the rubber is a
linear elastic solid with thickness hrubber=20 mm and elastic modulus Erubber=1.40 MPa;
(iii) the rail is a linear elastic solid with Erail=210 GPa and νrail=0.30. The cross-sectional
dimensions of the rail are based on the 59R2 proﬁle [41] with a base width of wrail=180
mm, and the moment of inertia is Irail=3213.8 cm4; (iv) the axle load is quasi-static
(i.e., dynamic eﬀects of the vehicle are neglected) with a total intensity of Paxle/2=30 kN,
where Paxle is the load intensity of an axle; and (v) the concrete slab and subgrade from
all deeper layers are assumed to be inﬁnitely rigid.
Two load cases are considered at the proximity of the concrete slab joint: (a) the ﬁrst of
the axles which form the pair of axles is located at the slab edge, as shown in Figure 5.11;
(b) the joint is between the two axles connecting the tandem, as shown in Figure 5.12.
The inﬂuence of the additional axles is neglected as their location does not fall within the
area of inﬂuence of the rail and slab. In parallel, two joint deﬁnitions are considered for
the analysis of the rail responses: (i) joint of 0 mm between slab tracks; and (ii) joint of
25 mm.

6
ANALYSIS OF TRAIN AND VEHICULAR
LOADS
The focus of this chapter is determining the bending tensile stresses on the slab track
under train and vehicular loads. First, a mesh convergence study is conducted to verify
the numerical model used for a given slab size against Westergaard’s analytical solutions.
Second, the numerical model of slab track, granular subbase, subbase gravel and intact
soil with the symmetry application is used to establish the bending tensile stresses induced
by rail loads on the slab track. Third, a series of vehicle loads are applied to calculate the
maximum bending tensile stresses on the slab track for the internal, corner and two edge
loading conditions. Finally, the concrete fatigue of the slab track is investigated using
the methods of Darter (1977), Aas-Jakobsen (1970), Roesler (1998), Rollings and Witczak
(1990), and the Smith fatigue model of the standard DS/EN 16432-2:2017.
6.1
Mesh Convergence Study
The slab tracks in the 3D ﬁnite element model are discretised with hexahedral solid meshes
which are orthogonal and rectangular across the length, width and depth of the model, with
three translational d.o.f.s per node. The mesh convergence study displayed in section A.1
converges the mesh to obtaining the exact results as the mesh is repeatedly redeﬁned with
diﬀerent mesh sizes, reducing computational costs.
The numerical model is validated by comparing the maximum bending tensile stresses for
diﬀerent mesh sizes obtained for a slab with a plot area of 10,000 x 10,000 mm and 20,000
x 20,000 mm, and thicknesses of 100 mm, 200 mm, and 400 mm, with the analytical
stress results from Westergaard’s equations. The mesh convergence study is performed by
applying lorry loads to the interior, corner and edge loading conditions.
6.2
Train-Induced Concrete Stresses
This model is based on a 3D ﬁnite element model analysis, where the elements are the
concrete slabs on an elastic foundation, with the following assumptions: (i) the concrete
slabs are represented by a longitudinally symmetric rectangular shape with geometric
properties hslab=400 mm, Bslab=1,250 mm, and Lslab=3,000 mm, element C3D8 with
mesh size 50 x 50 x 50 mm, with elastic properties Eslab=35.22 GPa and νslab=0.2; (ii)
the pair axle wheel loading is quasi-static, with a force intensity of Paxle/2=30 kN, its
distribution through the rail on the rubber and the slab follows the vertical reactions
shown in Figures 6.3a and 6.3b for load cases 1 and 2; and (iii) the support provided by
the lower multilayer system is assumed to be an elastic base with the properties given in
Figures 6.1a and 6.1b.

CONCLUSION
8.1
Summary
This study dealt with the analysis of precast slab-on-grade constructions designed for light
rail applications. Speciﬁc attention was given to the Ring 3 light rail project. The main
aim was to investigate the mechanics of the system under diﬀerent loading scenarios.
An introduction to the slab track construction of Copenhagen’s Ring 3 Light Rail project
was deﬁned for subsequent implementation (see Chapter 1).
After a review of the theoretical background and the literature review (see Chapters 2 and
3), three-fold objectives arose: (i) investigate the inﬂuence of the concrete under service
loads, (ii) quantify the fatigue life of the concrete under service loads, and (iii) asses the
carbon footprint of the Ring 3 Light Rail project by comparing a scenario with a precast
slab track and another one with a cast-in-place solution.
Following an analysis of the inﬂuence of a gap between slab tracks on the rail responses
(see Chapter 5), the analysis was elaborated with three levels of analytical and numerical
solutions counting with the rail and the elastic rubber elements: (i) analytical 2D solution
of a beam supported by springs where the beam support is represented by springs forming
a Winkler foundation, (ii) plane stress 2D ﬁnite element model of a beam supported
by an elastic medium using elastic support with the equivalent elastic properties of the
provided spring stiﬀness, and (iii) a 3D ﬁnite element model of a beam supported by an
elastic medium which uses elastic support with equivalent spring stiﬀness. A previous
test on a ﬁnite element model was elaborated to obtain the elastic modulus of the rubber
material support, which was validated by applying Hooke’s law in a homogeneous half-
space medium.
In the analysis of concrete fatigue (see Chapter 6), a 3D ﬁnite element model of the
slab track and course layers is conducted to obtain the maximum bending tensile stresses
under vehicular and train-induced loads. The slab track model is validated by comparing
the responses obtained from a mesh convergence study on the numerical model and the
approximations from Westergaard’s formulations. Moreover, the S-N curves from various
design methods elaborated from ﬁeld and laboratory tests are compared to the calculated
maximum bending tensile stresses.
Finally, the LCA of the civil works (see Chapter 7), comparing the manufacturing of the
precast slab track realised in Warszow to the cast-in-place slab track solution, was based
on a web application utilising material and method databases to model the environmental
impact of the processes. The results showed a slight diﬀerence between the two scenarios
on the totality of civil works.

==PAGE:77==
8. Conclusion
8.2
Findings
The main ﬁndings from this work are as follows: (i) Inﬂuence of a joint between slabs on
the rail responses has a negligible eﬀect. (ii) Maximum bending tensile stresses on the
concrete slab induced by vehicle and trainloads are not expected to pass the maximum
allowable stresses of the analysed S-N curves.
(iii) There is a slight inﬂuence of the
manufacturing procedure of the slab track on the CO2 emissions.
8.3
Future work
Based on the ﬁndings of this work, the following suggestions are provided for guiding
future studies (in prioritised order): (i) implement sensors that can measure the position
of the slab track monitoring the slab track responses; (ii) optimise the slab track design
for the desired life time of 50 years; (iii) study the inﬂuence of the levelling accuracy of
the supporting coarse layer on the bending tensile stresses of the slab track; (iv) improve
the slab track model, e.g., include nonlinearity and viscoelastic; and (v) improve the LCA
model including actual transportation distances, and operations during the service life of
the infrastructure.

with vertical stiﬀness of 0.1 N/mm3. Four models with diﬀerent load conditions are utilised
to evaluate the responses on the slab:
• (i) Load on the slab centre: vertical displacements and maximum tensile bending
stresses are measured at the bottom of the slab, directly beneath the load.
• (ii) Load on the slab corner: vertical displacements and maximum tensile bending
stresses are measured directly beneath the load at the top of the slab.
• (iii and iv) Load on the slab edges: vertical displacements and maximum tensile
bending stresses are measured at the bottom of the slab, directly beneath the load.
