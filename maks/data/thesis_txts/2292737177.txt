Summary
In this project the microbiome’s influence on the behaviour and cognition of animals has
been described and investigated. A broad review tries to tie the neuroscience behind
feelings and emotions with the impact of the microbial composition on these mental
states. We hear about how the field relating to ’psychobiotics’ is emerging and about
how behaviour has been directly linked to the gut microbiome. The chicken anatomy is
described along with the current knowledge of the chicken’s microbiome and probiotics’
influence on the gut microbes as well.
A series of different trials has been made to investigate the impact of probiotics on
the behaviour in chickens and chicks’ cognitive abilities. The behaviour part went well
and the results hint to a possible influence of probiotics on chicken behaviour, though
further trials are needed to conclude on this. It is harder to draw any conclusions from
the cognition part of the experiments. These were hindered by problems with the tests
evaluating the cognition in the chicks.
Finally the experiments have been evaluated and setups for possible new trials have
been suggested based upon the experiences gathered by this project.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The intestinal microbiome is a diverse, dynamic and very important ecosystem. The
bacteria present in the guts secures breakdown of hard-to-digest nutrients can ferment
undigestible food and modulate intestinal motility. In the past decades immunologists
have also started to appreciate the role of the gut microbiome on the development of the
immune system. From germ-free animal studies we know today that the microbiota of the
intestines is an active part of the immune system, protecting against pathogens, keeping
the immune system in shape and maintaining homeostasis in the body. In the last decade
scientists have begun to look at the microbes interaction with the mental health of people.
It has been noticed, that Irritable Bowl Syndrome is related to depression and stress and
that shifts in the microbiome can cause stress-like behaviour[Cryan and Dinan, 2012].
In 2004 [Sudo et al., 2004] showed that swapping the microbiome of the mice strains
caused the mice to swap behaviour also. Anxious mice became more explorative and
vice versa. Mating preference for Drosophila melanogaster has been shown to rely on
the microbiome as well [Sharon et al., 2010].
Project Description
The microbiome’s influence on mental health of men and mice is not the only interesting
consequence of these discoveries. Probiotics could potentially help stabilise inflammatory
guts, and ease symptoms of depression and stress [Cryan and Dinan, 2012]. A broad
knowledge of different diets’ impact on the microbiome could also help prevent these
mental states. It is already observed that depression are less prevailing in populations
eating a traditional Japanese diet or a traditional Mediterranean diet than in the western
high-fat high-sugar diet, [Selhub et al., 2014]. The question is whether the potential
’psychobiotic’ nature of some bacteria actually exist and it does, how much is it actually
influencing the brain. In this study chickens are fed known probiotics to investigate if a
behavioural difference in the birds can be observed. It is also investigated if the chickens’
cognition is influenced, as cognition likewise has been linked to the microbiome [Kang
et al., 2014].

2.1
Behaviour, feelings and emotions
Emotions and behaviour of an individual are the manifestations of the underlying neural
circuits in the brain. The general consensus in the psychiatric community is to define
three overall mental states: feelings, emotions and moods.
Feelings and emotions are tied together, in that feelings are conscious acknowledge-
ments of emotions. The brain can perceive and respond to emotionally relevant informa-
tion unconsciously, which actually is more prevalent than conscious response. Feelings
are merely the top of the ’emotional iceberg’. Sometimes feelings are better described as
signifiers of an emotional state since the reverse, having a feeling without an emotion,
is also true. An example is pain which can be experienced with no emotional impact
under the influence of opiates. While feelings are hard to measure in animals because
of the language barrier, emotions are sought to be detected through i diverse range of
tests. [Watson et al., 2012, Archer, 1975, Forkman et al., 2007]
Two systems exist to describe emotions; categorical systems and dimensional systems.
In the categorical system there exist some distinct basic emotions and other emotions
would merely reflect the intensity of these emotions. Depending on the specific theory
the basic emotions could be anger, disgust, fear, happiness and sadness. Other emotions
are a blend of these basic emotions or different intensities of one emotion. A bit like
mixing different colours on a palette or the difference between light pink and dark red.
This would also mean that specific emotions should map to specific parts of the brain
and indeed a causal link between hunger and the hypothalamic circuitry is described.
[Watson et al., 2012, Adolphs and Anderson, 2013, Sternson et al., 2013]
In the dimensional system the emotions are mapped in a dimensional space. This
emphasises that emotions can be hard to distinguish and argues that there are no such
states as basic emotions. Meta analysis of fMRI in humans supports the idea that no
basic emotions exist, and [Barrett and Satpute, 2013] finds that the neural systems

The Gut-Brain Axis
The Gut-Brain Axis is the term for the proposed bidirectional communication channel
between the brain and the gut. That the brain communicates with the gut has been
realised for some time and mechanisms such as the breakdown of fatty acids leading to a
response decreasing appetite are well established. But in recent years the gut-brain-axis
has received attention for the possibility of the gut to influence mood and emotional
states and even change the neurobiology of the brain. [Mayer, 2011]

The brain communicates with the guts through many channels. One of the most
important channels is the vagus nerves, a part of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)
that along with the Hypothalamic-Putuitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis, the sympatho-adrenal
axis and the monoaminergic pathways are important channels for the gut-brain axis.
The bidirectional communication serves to keep homeostasis. Apart from the neural
pathways, the endocrine and immune pathways also exists. Recent findings suggest that
stress response and overall behaviour can be impacted by the microbiome in the gut.

The Vagus Nerve
The vagus nerve is also known as Cranial Nerve X or the pneumogastic nerve. It is
actually two nerves, the left and right vagus nerve. The nerve extends from the brain,
where it reaches the medulla and runs through the torso down to the gut. Underway it
reaches many of the internal organs and contains between 80% to 90% of the afferent
nerves, which carries nerve impulses towards the central nervous system (CNS). It serves
as a direct anatomical link between the gut and the brain, demonstrated by experiments
with vasectomised rodents and is a major communication channel in the gut-brain axis
[Bravo et al., 2011]. Many studies highlight its importance especially in the vasectomised
animal models. In a vasectomy the vagus nerve is cut and in gut-brain experiments
this sometimes has a big impact on the neurobiology, the mood and the behaviour
of the animal in question. Activation of the vagus nerve has been shown to have an
anti-inflammatory effect and Bravo & colleagues showed that an induced neural effect
generated by treatment of rodents with a bacteria was dependent on an intact vagus
nerve. [Cryan and Dinan, 2012, Bravo et al., 2011]
Communication via Immune Pathways
The gut and the gut-associated lymphoid tissue contains over 70% of the total immune
system and the immune system comprises of the main channels of communication and
interaction in the gut-brain axis. The link between the immune system and the brain
has been known for a long time. Syphilis often leads to neurological deficiencies while
anxiety-like behaviour can be introduced in mice by administering Campylobacter jejuni
in subclinical doses [Moloney et al., 2014].
The immune system has a bidirectional
communication with the CNS and it is known that the microbiome has a critical role in
the development of the immune system as well as the way it works. Psychobiotics could
use these interactions as means of influencing the gut-brain axis for example indirectly
through cytokines. [Cryan and Dinan, 2012]
Endocrine pathways - The Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis
The Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis controls reaction to stress and is a part
of the endocrine system. Activation of the HPA-axis results in the release of hormones
from the hypothalamus, the pituitary gland and the adrenal glands. In mice and rats,
the corticosterone hormone is released, which is the equivalent of the human cortisol.
The axis is associated with depression and stress, and a common alteration in the HPA-
axis is elevated corticosterone level. When the HPA-axis is brought back to normal the
depressive or stressed state is eased or completely resolved. The microbiome has an
important role in the programming of the HPA-axis just after birth, and neonatal stress
leads to long-term changes in the diversity and composition of the microbiome. [Foster
and Neufeld, 2013, Dinan and Cryan, 2013]

The Influence of GABA
GABA is one of the most important neurotransmitters in the central nervous system
and dysfunctions in GABA signalling are associated with depression and anxiety. Two
species of bacteria in the intestine, Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium, can actively pro-
duce GABA and secrete it into the gut.[Dinan and Cryan, 2013] Interestingly, research
shows that some types of food can help enhance the psychobiotic production of GABA
and other bioactive chemicals. The two species of bacteria can produce GABA from
monosodium glutamate, which is found as a seasoner in many fabricated foods. [Foster
and Neufeld, 2013, Dinan and Cryan, 2013]
For the past 25 years antidepressants that target serotinin have been emerging. But
not all patients seems to respond to the treatment and some patients are strongly against
treatment with pharmaceuticals. It has been suggested that antidepressants work, apart
from their chemical effect, by generating cytokine IL-10, a potent immunoregulatory pro-
tein. IL-10 is an anti-inflammatory cytokine and by suppressing inflammation it could
also suppress the CNS changes that comes with depression. Antidepressants usually re-
verse abnormalities in the HPA-axis and to find a psychobiotic or a cocktail of psychobi-
otics that could have the same effect along with supplying needed neurotransmitters,
would be appreciated. [Dinan and Cryan, 2013]
Serotonin is an important player in regulating mood and there is a significant higher
level in conventional mice contra GF mice.
The levels of the precursor of serotinin,
tryptophan, can be raised by feeding Bifidobacterium infantis to rats, which also changes
the rats’ behaviour back to normal along with normalisation of inflammation. [Foster and
Neufeld, 2013, Nakamura et al., 2009, Dinan and Cryan, 2013] Other exciting interactions
are polyphenols, found in dark chocolate, that interact with the GABA receptors and
show a stress-reducing effect in humans. [Foster and Neufeld, 2013, Nakamura et al.,
2009]
Several bacteria strains gave been shown to be able to treat gut intestinal distress.
More interesting, these probiotics sometimes affect the level of BDNF and the expression
of GABA receptors in the CNS. There is a clear link between chronic stress, depression
and BDNF. [Cryan and O’Mahony, 2011] Sudo et. al. finds that the impaired HPA-
axis development due to lack of gut microbiota also shows reduced levels of BDNF in
the same area of the brain that had an reduced expression of the NMDA receptors, a
glutamate receptor involved in memory function. The alterations have been reproduced
in several studies afterwards. [Sudo et al., 2004, Dinan and Cryan, 2013]

al.

change in behaviour nor change in the GABA receptor expression. These results demon-

In the arcticle they also discuss how GABA could be crucial for maintaining normal

The intestine of the Chicken - Anatomy
Here follows an overview of the chicken gastrointestinal tract (GIT). An overview of
the chicken gastrointestinal anatomy can bee seen in figure 2.3. Food enters the bird
through the beak and is passed through the oesophagus to the proventriculus, the first
part of the stomach of the chicken. The oesophagus is a flexible tube, which allows the
bird to swallow their food whole. An expandable pouch on the oesophagus, the crop,
can store food and can be seen as a swelling of the breast in a chicken when eating. The
food can be stored in the crop for up to 6 hours and the crop also functions as a place for
initial fermentation by microorganisms before the food is passed to the proventriculus

[Barnes, 1979]. Here an excretion of mucosa, pepsinogen and hydrochloric acid is taking
place. The low pH allows a chemical denaturation of proteins and has been proposed by
[Sekelja et al., 2012] to function as a microbial barrier.
On a side note, pH varies throughout the chicken GIT. The crop can vary between
4.5 an 6 depended on last feeding. In the duodenum, described later, the pH is 6 but
can vary in young chicks, between 4.8 and 6.5. Later in the small intestine, the pH is

From the proventriculus the food travels through a little stretch, called the zona
intermedia gastric, before entering the gizzard. Here, strong muscles and small stones
eaten by the chick grinds the food and mixes it with the secretions from the proventricu-
lus. The chicken is an omnivore which is reflected in the gastrointestinal tract. The
gizzard is large and powerful, adapted to a hard diet, unlike carnivorous and piscivo-
rous species who are adapted to soft diets. The proventriculus is also relative small in
chickens compared to the meat eating birds. The stomach region is coupled up on the
nervous system by vagus and perivascular nerve fibres from the celiac and mesenteric
plexii. [Scanes, 2015]
After the stomach the food goes into the small intestine.
They are divided into
three parts; the duodenum, the jejunum and the ileum. The duodenum has a distinct
loop while the jejunum and ileum are separated where Meckel’s diverticulum is attached.
Right before hatching, the yolk, which has supplied the inoculating chicken with nour-
ishment, is taken into the naval cavity. The remaining sack is Meckel’s diverticulum,
which accumulates lymphoid tissue after the hatching. Because of the absorption of the
yolk into the intestines, chickens can go a few days post-hatching before they have to
eat. [Oláh et al., 1984]
The chicken small intestine contains villi to make the surface area bigger [Scanes,
2015]. Villi morphology is one of the anatomical areas where the intense breeding of
chickens for production has resulted in big differences. In broilers, ’meat-chickens’, the
villi are larger than in White Leghorns used for eggs. The intestines are changing in the
first days of a chicken’s life and the number of villi decreases noticeably the first 10 days.
From the development of villi, it is also possible to see that the absorption of nutrients
takes place primarily in the duodenum at first, but then extends to the jejunum after
some days. The intestinal mucosa also changes structurally with age, especially the first
7 days [en Yamauchi, 2007]. Interestingly, the composition of the microbiome in the
chicken also undergoes big changes in the early life of the chick before stabilising after
approximately 40 days. [Stanley et al., 2014]. The epithelium contains chief cells, goblet
cells and endocrine cells and, through the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems,
the intestines are also highly connected to the nervous system. Chickens have, like all
birds but not seen in other animals, a nerve called the intestinal nerve. It runs along the
small and large intestines. The large intestine is smaller than the small intestine and
contains the ceca and the colon, which runs from the ileum-ceca (ileocecal) junction to
the cloaca.
The ceca is an interesting place in the chicken. It has one of the highest densities
of microorganisms in the gastrointestinal tract and is very important for the digestion
of food. It is divided into three regions with different morphological features. From the

The Intestine of the Chicken - The Microbiome
The chicken microbiome is very diverse and have the same functions as the microbiome
found in mammals. It helps in the digestion of food and breakdown of nutrients and
possibly influences mental health.
The exploration of the chick gut microbiome has
been on for decades, due to the importance of the chicken as a production animal
and is probably nowhere near finished. Until very recently only species that can be
cultivated were able to be detected. In recent years the old cultivation methods have
been supplied with sequencing techniques which has illuminated larger parts of the
microbiome and its composition. [Salanitro et al., 1974] finds in their study 325 strains
of bacteria harbouring the chicken ceca and estimates it to be between 49% to 81% of the
microscopic count. In 2013 [Wei et al., 2013] found 915 species equivalent operational
taxonomic units (OUTs) using 16s rRNA gene sequencing. They estimate a 89% coverage
of the bacterial diversity.
The chicken microbiome varies over age. At hatching the digestive system is sterile,
but gets colonised immediately. Evidence suggest that the chicken microbiome is get-
ting richer and more diverse with the age of the chicken [Danziese et al., 2011]. [ven der
Wielen et al., 2002] divided the chicken GIT into 4 parts; the crop, duodenum, ileum and
ceca, and showed that every part has its own distinct microbial community and can be
seen as separate ecosystems in adult chickens. In 4-day-old chicks the crop, duodenum
and ileum is similar compared to the ceca, but branches out from this. The environment
in the different parts of the GIT is different, and this is possibly a reason for the branch-
ing. [Sekelja et al., 2012] finds that the temporal fluctuations in the fecal microbiota can
be mapped to specific regions of the chicken GIT. They propose that the difference in
the microbiota composition is caused by discharge from different GI segments. They also
show that the crop and gizzard have overlapping microbiota that cannot be distinguished
from each other. [Lu et al., 2003] also see a compartmentalisation of the GIT in chick-
ens. They report a fairly stable microbiome from day 14-28 during rapid skeletal growth.
In figure 2.3 some of the common species is shown. [Sekelja et al., 2012] found lacto-
bacilli and Clostridiaceae to be abundant in the crop and gizzard, which previously also
has been shown by culture-based studies, [Stanley et al., 2014]. The chicken ileum and
duodenum is dominated by Lactobacillus species, with different Lactobacillus strains suc-
ceeding each other throughout maturation of the chick [Gong et al., 2002]. The chicken

Chicken immune system and the microbiome
In mammals the microbiome not only play an important role in the digestion and break-
down of nutrients but, as mentioned shortly in section 2.2, the microbiome is also an
important player in the development, functioning and maintenance of the immune sys-
tem. The commensal bacteria is critical to the development of secondary lymphoid tissue
structure and controls the mucus layer and antimicrobial peptides [Molloy et al., 2012].
The chicken microbiome and immune system show the same interactions. In germ
free chickens the gross anatomy and cellular subsets hints to a palette of interactions
between the microbiome and the immune system. [Mwangi et al., 2010] discuss this and
show in their own research that changes in the TCRβ repertoires in the chicken gut are
associated with differences in the microbial complexity. When comparing gallinaceous
fowl to other production animals, the chick has a greater diversity of bacteria in the GIT.
[Yeoman and White, 2014] suggest this is a reflection of a less-active innate and humoral
immune system. Chickens harbour Salmonella and Campylobacter, pathogenic bacteria
that the chicken immune system do not eliminate. [Jeurissen et al., 1997] propose that
this is caused by the insufficient humoral response, which is partly confirmed in their
study.
How do probiotics work?
The studies into probiotics in chickens are primarily focused on probiotics as means
for better growth, Feed-Conversion-Rate (FCR) and disease prevention. From a pro-
ductivity standpoint, the microbiome is considered an nutritional burden for fast grow-
ing broiler chickens since the active microbiome have an increased energy requirement.
Many bacterial and yeast species have been used as probiotics, including Bacillus, Bifi-
dobacterium, Enterococcus, Escherichia, Lactobacillus, Lactococcus, Streptococcus and
Saccharomyces. [Yang et al., 2009]. The effect and mode of action for probiotics can
be manyfold: Growth promotion, improvement of the mucosal structure, breakdown of
indigestible foods, improvement of intestinal and general health, breakdown of cytotoxic
substances, production of nutrients such as vitamins, suppression of pathogens through
competitive exclusion and production of antimicrobial substances [Vilà et al., 2010].
[Santini et al., 2010] showed that administration of Bifidobacterium Longum reduced
the presence of Campylobacter jejuni in feces from the chicken. And back in 1973 [Nurmi
and Rantala, 1973] showed that chicks’ susceptibility to Salmonella can be reduced by
giving the chicks adult gut contents. The application of probiotics in chicken production
relies heavily on the competitive exclusion scheme. Unlike other production animals,
chickens are hatched in very clean environments and are not exposed to the bacteria of
a mother, but rather random exposure from transport boxes and other environments.
[Gaggìa et al., 2010].

2.4
The effect of social stress on the microbiome
Social stress can be caused by many things. Since chickens are production animals, most
research has gone into the impact of high-production environments. Here chickens suffer
from chronic social stress because of the density of animals in the artificial environment
[Cheng et al., 2002]. The immune system of the chicken is influenced by social stress.
A high level of social stress weakens the defence against Marek’s disease tumours and
cossidiosis, while social stress at the time of vaccination against cossidiosis can result
in higher immunity [Gross and Siegel, 1988]. Chickens form complex hierarchies, and
different levels of dopamine and corticosterone have been found in in dominant and sub-
ordinate chickens. On this basis [Cheng et al., 2002] did an experiment that backed the
notion from other research which suggested the avian neuroendocrine system’s response
to stimuli is analogous to that of mammals but also that the system is species-specific
among chicken strains. They also found their results to be consistent with the hypoth-
esis that the HPA and sympatho-adrenal axes control the birds’ behavioural patterns,
production and stress response.

Learning and cognition
Chickens are able to learn and comprehend complex tasks. They have the ability to count
and recognise elements based on its position in a series of identical elements [Rugani
and Regolin, 2007] and they can discriminate between small numerousities [Rugani and
Vallortigara, 2008]. What is more impressive is the chickens’ ability to do keep count of
small sets. When imprinted on an object, the chick, when presented with two groups
containing different numbers of said object, will choose the larger group. If the objects
are disappearing one by one behind the screens, the chick will be more likely to choose
the screen with the most objects [Rugani et al., 2011].

Measuring behaviour in birds
Tonic immobility
Tonic Immobility is also known as ’chicken paralysis’ or ’chicken hypnosis’. It functions
as a defence mechanism and is a state of paralysis where the chicken’s locomotion is
inhibited and the bird shows a reduced response to external stresses. In the wild the
strategy works since predators will leave apparently dead animals to hunt other members
of the flock. The bird will be able escape when the predator is not paying attention. It
can be artificially induced in birds by physically restraining them. This was studied by
[Kircher et al., 1680] as early as 1646. The most basic technique is to just place the bird
on the ground on its back or on one wing, sometimes getting the chick to focus on a
finger drawing a line from the eyes of the chick. [Gilman et al., 1950]. It is thought to be
positively related to fear and has been used in many experiments throughout the years.
However, in a 1997 study by [Fluck et al., 1997], the researchers do not find the duration
of TI to reflect general anxiety, but possibly a specific decrease of fear towards human
handlers. They find that handled birds has a decrease in TI but also have a decrease
in GABA release, which is not compatible with GABA binding at GABA-A receptors
being associated with less anxiety. Less GABA-A binding could be compatible with less
motor inhibition and they suggest that change in GABA could mediate this.
Open Field Test
The Open Field Test is a common test to measure fear in animals. Many types of the
Open Field exist and variations to measure animals’ responses to different stimuli has
been used. Sensitivity to both environment, experiential, pharmacological and genetic
manipulations have been measured in the Open Field, but the interpretations of the
results have caused debate [Jones and Carmichael, 1997]. The basic open field test is
described by [Archer, 1975] in 1975 and evaluates an animals response to a novel arena,
the ambulation of the animal and other factors such as defecation or latency to move.
[Forkman et al., 2007] have a critical review of the use of the open field tests used
in many production animals. They point to the fact that many production animals are
social creatures domesticated from wild flock animals. The social isolation when moved
to the open field is a stressful event for such an animal and some animals may suffer
more from the separation anxiety than from the fearful test itself.
In general the tests are only indicators of the fearfulness of an event, much like
measuring different biomarkers in the animal is.

Modified Open Field Test
The goal of the Modified Field Test was to measure how far away a chick would go from
the group to explore and search for food. The test was performed in an arena measuring
235 cm x 35 cm with 50 cm high sides. In one end, the arena had a grid with a small
enclosure behind allowing the subject to see and hear other chickens. The enclosure was
big enough for 4 chickens. The floor was covered with paper towels and a measuring
band extended from one end of the arena to the other. Grains were scattered sparsely
along the measuring band, with around 1 grain per centimeter. The observer sat on
a chair approximately 30cm from the arena, midway. 4 chicks were then placed in the
small enclosure and a fifth chick placed in the starting area just outside the enclosure. 2
hours before the test, the chickens were deprived of food in their cages. For 4 minutes,
the observer noted the farthest distance the chick had been from the starting position,
every 30 seconds. The distance noted was the part of the chick being farthest away from
the group, so a chick standing still for 1 minute, but stretching its wing after 30 seconds
would get two different measurements (differing
1-2 cm). Between every test, dirty
paper towels were replaced, and the arena completely cleaned when changing groups.
The group to start being tested alternated for every test-day.

Counting
The hypothesis was: ’Chickens can comprehend numbers, but learn to count at different
speeds. How fast is influenced by different parameters’. Thus, the goal of the experiment
was to determine how fast the test subject learned to count to four. In a arena measuring
200cm x 45cm and 50cm in height, 7 small bowls were placed along one side. Each bowl
had a small blind on the side facing the arena end with the starting position. This way
test subjects were unable to walk directly from one bowl to the next. If a bowl had
grains the grains would be placed in the side of the bowl facing the middle of the arena.
This way chickens could not see the grains, before choosing a bowl, walk to the bowl
and look or peck into the bowl. The setup is illustrated in figure 3.2
For the test, the chickens were placed at the starting position. 5 trial runs were
performed, 3 runs with all bowls filled with grains, easily seen by the chicken and 2 runs
where only the 4th bowl was filled with grains. After the trial runs the 4th bowl had a
couple of grains placed at one side of the bowl as described earlier. A chick had 50 tries
to choose the right bowl 5 times out of 6 in a row, thus allowing 1 failure in 6 tries still
counting as the chick successfully counting to four. The number af overall tries were
then recorded. For a successful try, a chick would have to go directly to the 4th bowl
without looking into other bowls. They were allowed to pass the 4th bowl, stop and
return, but if the chick reached the other end of the arena without pecking or looking
in any bowl, the try counted as failure. A typical failure would be for the chick to go to
the third bowl, look into it and then go to the fourth bowl and peck.

Discussion
Counting
The counting experiment sought to quantify the chickens cognitive capabilities. The
hope was to use their ability to learn and comprehend numbers as a measurement for
their cognition. It was modelled on the works of [Rugani et al., 2010, Rugani and Regolin,
2007]. In the Rugani experiments, they show that chickens can count and comprehend
ordinal numbers, i.e. a qualitative test. The counting experiment was met with limited
success. Some chicks froze when placed in the arena, likely because of the isolation from
fellow chickens. A mirror was placed and the chickens seemed to be more comfortable
with the companionship of their mirror image. For 4 chickens this was not enough and
they did not complete the test. b2 was not moving at all, while b4, s10 and s8 after
a some tries chose to opt-out of the experiment, refusing to move. Nevertheless, some
results were obtained. First of all, it was possible to teach 17/21 chickens to count to 4,
thereby reaching the same conclusion about number comprehension as previous counting
experiments done by Rugani and coworkers. In the 17 chickens no difference were to be
found in neither the group, gender, or breed subdivision. The p-values for t-tests on the
subdivisions are shown in table 5.1.

Modified Open Field Tests
The results from the MOFTs show first of all that as chickens grow older they are willing
to move farther away from the group to forage. This is expected as it is observed in
chickens raised with a mother hen. It has also been discussed and shown in various
research papers, [Väisänen and Jensen, 2003]. Newly-hatched chicks will stay under the
wings of the mother and gradually explore and forage farther away as the grow older.
The hypothesis was that this behaviour is influenced by different parameters such as
gender, breed and the microbiome.
Just from eyeballing the results graphs in section 4, one can observe that the chicks
perform differently based on group and breed. In table 5.2 p-values for each t-test for
the graph is shown for the last two test days. The ’group’ subdivision and the ’breed’
subdivision start to divert (figure 4.2, ??, 4.6 and ??), and at the last test day, both
subdivisions are significant different, wyandottes and sussex being different and plus-
minus groups being different. Interestingly, a two-way ANOVA, with breed and group
as independent variables, show no significant interaction.
This can be seen in table
5.3 and 5.4. But how can the divisions be explained? The difference in breed are not
necessarily a surprise. Breeds differ and it is for example normally acknowledged that
broilers bred for a high slaughter weight are less flighty and more docile than White
Leghorns, a breed widely used for biological research. The sussex breed and the bantam
wyandottes are likewise quite different. Sussex is large, bulky and an ancestor to the
modern broiler, while the wyandottes are small and much more prone to broodiness. A
genetic variance could explain the different performances in the arena.
The difference when dividing the chickens into the group subdivision are interesting.
It seems that the group fed probiotics are less willing to leave their social group to forage.
They are either more anxious to leave the group, less hungry or maybe a combination.
It is difficult to say what probiotics, or other microbiological explanations, potentially
==PAGE:41==
5 Discussion
31
could cause this. Probiotics’ influence on broiler performance (growth and weight) are
poorly understood, (Stanley et al. [2014]) and this author has not been able to find
any research directly measuring probiotics influence on chickens’ anxiety and emotional
behaviour.
The three probiotics fed to the chickens have different effects. The Bacillus subtilis
from the GalliPro feed helps establishing a more diverse microbiota, while increasing
food conversion ratio, absorption of nutrients and the amount of digestive enzymes.
The Lactobacillus rhamnosus has been shown to reduce stress-induced corticosterone
and anxiety- and depression-related behaviour in mice and influencing the GABA system
[Bravo et al., 2011], but not much research into the potential effects in chickens has been
done. Bifidobacterium longum has been shown to inhibit the growth of Campylobacter
jejuni in the chicken gut [Santini et al., 2010]. These alterations and effects in the guts
of the birds should make them perform better in the arena of the MOFT test. But
the microbiome is complex, and if the results from the MOFT are a true signal, the
probiotics made the birds perform worse. This would mean the sum of the probiotics
influence on the microbiome turns out to be negative. The dose of probiotics could have
been too high. Simply an overload which caused a more competitive environment for the
commensal gut microbiome to colonise in the newly hatched chickens. The microbiome
of the chicken reaches a stable density already after three days, but the composition
changes a lot during the next 40 days [Stanley et al., 2014]. The constant pressure from
the probiotics could potentially alter this balance - maybe in a negative way. In mammals
and avian species alike, we know that the initial colonisation is of great importance.
If the chickens fed probiotics have had an haltered development of their microbiome,
their immunological development would also be retarded, which could lead to harmful
bacteria entering the gut. An unbalanced microbiome could also lead to an inflammatory
state in the guts. While the chickens did not show signs of being sick, no precise record
of their food intake have been recorded. The chicks had access to food ad libitum, and
refills of the food containers were noted. The weight gain were recorded for all chickens
and no difference between the groups are to be found. This indicates that growth was
normal, and chickens did not get underfed. It shows that the minus group ate around
6% more feed per chicken than the plus group. The overall FCR are 0.2 lower for the
plus group (see tabel 4.9). The better FCR is what the B. subtllis where predicted to
do cause. The chickens do not get heavier than the minus group, so maybe the plus
group are just less hungry and therefore not as eager to walk away from their social
companions to gather food.
This could be tested in a runway test, as described by
[Väisänen and Jensen, 2003], in which the chick is placed in a runway at the other end
of a goal box containing social companions. Here latency to reinstate social contact is
measured. Together with the MOFT it could potentially be a good meassure for the
chicken’s level of anxiety and stress level.

CHAPTER 6
Evaluation of Experiments
6.1
Evaluation and discussion of experiments
Since this project has very much been a pilot project, it has been important throughout
the project to test of alternatives and improvements on the trials. The following are
thoughts on the successful experiments, but also a short review on failed ones.
Housing, feeding and chickens
First of all, it was not optimal that the trials used two different breeds. It was necessary
to supplement the wyandottes with sussex chickens, because the number of live chickens
from the initial hatching was much lower than expected. In a new run there should
be more chickens and preferably of the same breed. Some of the potential effects of
probiotics are small changes, and they risk drowning in the noise from individual differ-
ences. And when doing animal experiments, the chance of getting relative large outliers
are high because of the unpredictable nature of free will. The outliers’ influence on the
results could be lowered by more test animals. Many researchers use White Leghorn
hens as test animals. To better compare results to other researchers, it would be wise
to consider using this breed for new testings.
The housing and feeding was sufficiently controllable. But in a new set of trials,
it should be possible to upscale the housing facilities in order to keep the chickens for
longer. It would also be better if the cages could be further apart so that the possibility
of contamination of the minus group’s feed with probiotics is completely removed. In
this trial, this problem was solved by having the plus group’s cage on a lower level than
the minus group, thereby minimising the risk of contamination. Temperature should
be controlled better. The two groups had the same temperature all the way, but it
would vary between 10-20 degrees celsius. If this has affected the chickens, it would be
an systematic error, since chickens were in the same room. But to better compare the
experiments to other similar research, temperature should be controlled and if possible
gradually lowered from 28-30 ◦C at hatching to 20 ◦C after 30-40 days. In the Rugani
experiments, they only use day-old birds, and they have a temperature of 28 ◦C - 31 ◦C
[Rugani et al., 2013, 2014] while [Luo et al., 2013] and [Chloupek et al., 2009] keep the
chicks for a longer period and follow the mentioned temperature scheme.
Unfortunately, by rearing the chickens in groups, it is impossible to get a good FCR
for each chicken. There is not much to be done to improve on this. The feed containers
could be weighed every day to follow the feeding behaviour of the whole group closer.
==PAGE:44==
34
6 Evaluation of Experiments
In this experiment it was noted when the containers were refilled, which would happen
every two or three days.
Modified Open Field Test setup
The initial idea was to perform Open Field Tests on the chickens, but the attempts
failed since chickens, when removed from the cage, would rather freeze and show signs
of tonic immobility than exploratory behaviour or movement in the open field. This
is not atypical behaviour for animals reared in flocks. [Suarez and Gordon G. Gallup,
1983] touches on the subject, and describes how this sudden social seperation results in
inconsistent results in the open field. The experimenter also risk creating a simulated
predatory encounter which again can tricker TI. The chicks usually showed classic signs
of distress. First freezing and later calling for the group. To overcome this problem it
was decided to use the chickens urge to reinstate social contact with the lost group. This
was met with success using the setup described in section 3.3. The concept is similar to
runway tests, e.g. [Väisänen and Jensen, 2003], where chicks have to traverse a runway
with social companions in a box at the end. In a new trial the chickens should be filmed.
This way tracking software can be used to get a much higher resolution on distances,
than the current 8 measured distances. Also latency to move from start position should
be measured, as the initial immobilisation could be similar to TI. A filming would also
make sure that the presence of the observer is not affecting the chicken’s willingness to
leave the group to forage.
The test design will generate some great outliers, since chickens sometimes will make
a run to the other end of the arena. In the early test days where chicks would keep close
to the group and starting position, a 100cm run would influence a chicks performance
far greater than in the later tests, where most chicks happily would wander around at
the 100cm mark.
Counting Experiment setup
While the counting experiment worked, it could be improved upon. The blinds worked
well in forcing the chicken to make a choice of which bowl to walk to at peck in. The
trial was stopped when a chicken had got 5 out of 6 tries right. This would sometimes
lead to a discrepancy between number of tries and success percentage. For example,
chick s2, s4 and s6 have similar success percentages, but s6 has half the tries as the two
others. The question is whether it matters if a chick uses 20 failed tries before getting
the concept or has 10/30 tries correct before getting the concept.
The overall problem were the chickens’ discomfort from social isolation. This dis-
comfort was noticeable lowered by having a mirror along the whole wall opposite to the
bowls and blinds. The chickens still showed some discomfort. This could possibly be
lowered if the chicks had more time to acclimatise in the new environment, since new
objects and environments can cause anxiety or fear, to an extend that it is sometimes
used as a fear measurement [Forkman et al., 2007].
==PAGE:45==
6.1 Evaluation and discussion of experiments
35
The trial only ran for one day with the intention to see how fast the chicks would
learn something new. But seeing that not much difference between the chicks was noted,
their ability to remember a task could be measured, and maybe their ability to use this
knowledge to perform new tasks. If the chicks were to be taught to count to for example
4 they could be tested the next day. They should be quicker to perform the task at
hand. After training, testing and retesting the chicks should now be taught to count to
for example 6 using the same scheme as above. This should go faster if the chicks are
able to use their prior knowledge of counting. A difference between a probiotic group
and a control group would point towards a difference in cognitive ability.
Tonic Immobility setup
The test setup was based on different techniques described in the literature mentioned
earlier (section 3.3). Slight differences were imposed due to practical constraints, notably
time and personnel resources. As previously described, this test was done to get a feel
for the test in order to use it in a potential later study. The technique described in
chapter 3.3 worked as planned. Tonic Immobility was induced in all chickens within
the first three tries. The chicks would stay paralysed for approximately 1 minute. The
results are not shown in the results section (chapter 4) since testing conditions changed
underway and the results were clearly influenced by this. It seemed the chicks were
highly responsive to the presence of people and noises, which was not fully cautioned
for. In a new study, the results of a tonic immobility test could be interesting to see.
If alterations of the chick microbiome changes the social behaviour of chickens, their
fear response could possibly also be prone to changes. [Gilman et al., 1950] show that
TI-tests could be performed more than one time, but with the chickens getting more
accustomed to the test. Also the amount of handling throughout raising influences the
chicks and reduces the susceptibility to immobilisation. Still, a MOFT followed by a
TI-test could be interesting, with the TI-test measuring how many chicks are susceptible
to immobilisation and the duration of successful immobilisations.
Failed - Open Field Test
The classic open field test was tried, but the chickens were to afraid to move when placed
in the arena. The open field test could be replaced with a runway test as mentioned in
the MOFT setup discussion (section 6.1). Another fear or anxiety test could be a test
where a human or a predator representation is closing in on a chicken.
Failed - Quantification of chickens ability to discriminate
figures
This was an attempt on a cognition test. The chick was placed at the periphery of a
round arena and opposite of the chicken four bowls with four different pictures covering
the bowls. There were grains under the same picture every time, and the chicken had to
figure out to peck at the same picture. When the chick pecked, it was restarted in the
==PAGE:46==
36
6 Evaluation of Experiments
starting position and the bowls would be rearranged, so that the chick could not count
to where to peck. From preliminary tests on adult bantam wyandottes it was known
that it was possible to teach hens to peck at the same object every time. Unfortunately
the chickens did not respond as well to the experiment and were very slow to pick up
upon the idea. Here the problem with social isolation also occurred and some chicks
were not willing to perform.
Failed - Distress calling from social isolation
This test investigated if the distress calls of chickens could be used to measure how afraid
they were. The distress call is very recognisable in chickens [Collias and Joos, 1953], and
the test measured latency for an isolated chicken to make the first distress calls. As in
the TI test, the latency should correspond to the fear in the chick. It worked for most
chickens, but a few chicks did not seem to care about being isolated. Some of them even
fell asleep. This falsifies the hypothesis that courageous chickens would start calling for
other chicks sooner than their more fearful counterparts. A solution could be to measure
the frequency of distress calls given in a time interval. [Kaufman and Hinde, 1961] show
that chickens raised in groups of two give out distress calls when they are taken apart.
This could be used in a test where a chicken is isolated and the number of distress calls
are counted, along with latency to start calling.
6.2
Suggestion for new trials
The results in this report are not overwhelmingly negative. In fact, it seems that the
administration of probiotics are influencing the behaviour in chickens. Not much research
into anxiety and fear-like behaviour has been done and a new set of trials could help
elucidate whether there are a connection between altered gut composition and the mood
and anxiety-like behaviour in chickens. This is a suggestion to how a new set of trials
could look like, based on the experiences gained from the previous experiments.
Birds and housing
40 - 60 chickens of the White Leghorn breed should be housed in two cages. As they grow
older, it should be possible to recage the chickens so that the birds can be followed for at
least 40 days. The microbiome is stable after this period and less prone to big changes.
The birds have to be either hatched on location or obtained immediately after hatching
to gain the most control of colonisation of the gut. It would be interesting to have three
groups of chickens. A control group, a group fed probiotics and a group inoculated
with adult microbiota at hatching. Chickens inoculated with an adult microbiome are
less susceptible to campylobacter infections, and a fully developed microbiome from day
one should influence the chickens development. A temperature scheme as described in
section 6.1 should be used. The birds should be circa half and half female and male so
potential gender differences can be detected.

Experiments in new trials
Weighing
Chickens should be weighed more often. With possibly near daily weighings the first
week or 10 days, and then maybe every 3 days. Administered feed should be measured
with the same frequency as the weighing of the birds to give a good estimation of feed
conversion rate.
Anxiety test - MOFT
The MOFT should be performed after 5 or 6 days, since chickens for the first couple of
days are feeding on the yolk which is still present in intestines. The tests also showed
that the chickens are not that exploratory the first 10 days. The MOFT should be
performed around every five days. This will give 8 measurements the first 40 days. The
MOFT should be filmed and tracking software should be used to get a higher resolution
than the one data point per 30 seconds currently used. Video footage will also make
it possible to make a third party control the experiment to avoid any bias from the
experimenter or ’Clever Hans’ effect from manual data measuring.
Anxiety test - Runway test
A runway test could be used to back-up the MOFT. The runway test used by [Väisänen
and Jensen, 2003] could easily be replicated. Here 28/29-day old chicks are placed in
a runway with their social companions at the other end. The latency for the chick to
leave the starting position is measured, the time spend in different zones in the runway
are measured, and how many times the chick enters the different zones are measured.
In the V￿is￿nen experiment, they only use the runway once. And the test could prove to
be useless if used more than once. The novelty of the arena will wear off, which could
result in less exploratory behaviour and chicks just wandering down the runway to the
other chicks. But a one time use to back up the MOFT would still be a good idea.
Fear test - Tonic Immobility
The day after a chicken have performed a MOFT, a TI test should be done. A V-shape
or U-shape should be used and the test should be performed in an isolated room, so the
chick is not being distracted by sounds or sights of other chickens.
Counting
The counting experiment should be modified and run for several days. On day 1 the
chicks should all learn to count to e.g. 3 and the next day they should be tested, without
pretraining, for how fast they relearn the correct position of grains. All chickens should
have the same number of tries and wrong choices should be recorded. The latter should
be done to show that the chicks are using ordinality to find the correct bowl. Wrong

choices would be near the correct bowl. It is based on the experiment done by [Rugani
and Regolin, 2007].
Perhaps an experiment where the chickens’ urge to social reinstatement could be
developed. An example could be 10 identical buttons in a row where one button opens a
gate to the other chicks. Or buttons of different colours where the same colour reestablish
contact with the chick’s companions.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
7.1
Experiments & Results
The behaviour part of the experiments went fairly well. The modified open field test
showed promise as a way to evaluate chickens’ urge to reinstate social contact versus their
urge to forage. The results showed that chickens fed probiotics were less willing to forage,
but if this is anxiety-like behaviour or because caused by an alteration in the chickens’
appetite is hard to answer. A study where the modified open field is complemented by
a runway test or another measurement for anxiety is needed. If the results are taken for
granted, they do hint to a possible impact from probiotics on the feeding behaviour of
chickens. The question of whether better food makes braver chickens has been hard to
answer, though.
The cognitive measurements were not successful. The social isolation in these tests
is probably a key reason for their inability to perform as intended. The counting experi-
ment had some results, but they are hard to conclude on since there were great outliers.
It is suggested that a new cognitive experiment actively use the birds’ urge to reestablish
contact with the companions.
7.2
Current state of research into the microbiome
and psychobiotics
It is exciting times within microbiome research. With the advent of high-throughput
sequencing techniques and bioinformatic tools, the knowledge of the microbiome is ex-
panding rapidly.
It has become clear, that the microbiome should be treated as an
organ, and that the microbes are influencing a broad palette of health related issues. In
the recent years the impact on the mental health has begun to be realised by scientists.
And not only that the microbiome influences the brain, but the communication is bidi-
rectional. With the knowledge comes the ability to manipulate the microbiome, and the
search for psychobiotics has started.
