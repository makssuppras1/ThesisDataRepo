Abstract
Growing urbanization has increased the demand for faster and more efficient construc-
tion methods, making precast structures a preferred solution. This study investigates the
seismic performance of precast concrete structures, focusing on precast wall systems
as the primary lateral load-resisting elements. It explores the applicability and limitations
of design practices commonly adopted in very low-seismic regions, such as Denmark,
and evaluates their feasibility for implementation in low-to-moderate seismic areas like
Iceland. Through a case study and numerical modeling, the study evaluates the global
seismic response of the structure and examines how different reinforcement layouts in
walls and joints influence local structural performance, focusing on the most critical wall
elements under seismic loading.
The key findings highlight that standard Danish design practices for precast concrete
structures can be adapted for low-seismic regions with minor modifications, primarily by
increasing reinforcement in critical wall sections and vertical joints. However, their appli-
cability diminishes in regions with moderate seismicity, requiring more robust detailing to
maintain structural integrity. The study also emphasizes the necessity of topped hollow-
core slabs to ensure reliable diaphragm action and adequate seismic load transfer.

Introduction
The global population has increased rapidly over the past century, with a growing propor-
tion living in urban areas. This change has increased demand for housing, infrastructure,
and public facilities, prompting the construction industry to develop innovative methods
that deliver structures quickly and efficiently while lowering time and labor costs. Among
these methods, precast concrete systems have emerged as a leading solution, offering
faster construction, enhanced quality control, and reduced on-site labor. [1].
With growing global interest, the use of precast systems in regions with different natural
hazards has raised questions about their resilience in earthquakes, where dynamic lateral
demands require not only strength but also ductility and energy dissipation. Building prac-
tices vary between regions with different seismic intensity, with higher-seismicity areas
often adopting more heavily reinforced configurations and robust designs that resemble
cast-in-situ construction. This, in turn, reduces some of the core advantages of precast
construction, such as simplicity, reduced structural weight, and time savings. These dif-
ferences in precast building traditions and seismic demands are evident in the contrasting
practices of Denmark and Iceland.
Denmark provides an example of a very low-seismicity context, where wind is the primary
lateral load. Its well-established approach to precast construction is recognized for ef-
ficiency and simplicity, typically using lightly reinforced elements, simple joint detailing,
and untopped hollow-core slabs. These solutions have proven effective and economical
in these conditions.
In contrast, Iceland presents a different context, with seismicity ranging from low to mod-
erate, depending on the location, and often governing the design of taller buildings. In-
terest in precast solutions is growing due to the benefits of high-quality, factory-controlled
production, with cement being the only imported material. However, Icelandic precast
systems feature more robust connections with heavier reinforcement. Walls often contain
continuous reinforcement passing through the elements to simulate a cast-in-situ struc-
ture, and hollow-core slabs include in-situ concrete toppings.
These differences in design approaches raise an essential question about the adaptability
of simple precast systems, as found in Denmark, to regions with higher seismic demands.
This thesis addresses this by examining a typical Danish precast building layout within the
Icelandic seismic environment and comparing the performance of different reinforcement
configurations.
1.1
Research Questions
• How does a precast structure perform during a seismic event, and what factors
influence its performance?
• What are the limitations of precast systems in seismic design, and how do Eu-
rocode 2 and Eurocode 8 provisions create challenges for their implementation in
seismic regions?
• To what extent can typical Danish precast design and detailing be applied in low-to-
moderate seismic regions such as Iceland?

1.2
Objectives and Limitations
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the seismic performance of precast concrete
structures, identify key design challenges associated with their use in seismic regions, and
evaluate how current Eurocode provisions influence their application. The study further
aims to compare different reinforcement layouts in shear walls and joints under seismic
loading, using primarily numerical simulations, with wall dimensions determined analyti-
cally.
• Evaluate the seismic performance of precast concrete structures, focusing on shear
walls as the primary lateral load-resisting system.
• Develop numerical models to assess the global seismic response for the precast
building.
• Develop elasto-plastic numerical models of the most critical precast wall elements
to compare different reinforcement layouts in walls and joints, representative of very
low- and low-to-moderate seismic regions.
• Examine the limitations of precast systems in seismic design and assess how Eu-
rocode 2 and Eurocode 8 provisions influence and potentially challenge their imple-
mentation in seismic regions.
Limitations
• Experimental testing of joints is not included due to time and resource constraints
• The analysis is limited to a single case study, which may not capture the full range
of precast systems
• Only selected reinforcement layouts are evaluated. Other configurations may affect
performance
• Openings in precast wall elements are not considered, which could influence stress
distribution and overall behavior

2.1
Introduction to Precast Concrete Structures
Precast elements connect to form a three-dimensional framework that supports both verti-
cal and horizontal loads in a precast concrete structure. This type of building is often suit-
able for those requiring minimal internal obstruction and multifunctional spaces. Frame-
works of this nature are ideal for offices, car parks, retail units, and schools, as they often
require larger floor areas or the flexibility to adapt to various usages [2].
One of the fundamental differences between cast-in-situ and precast buildings lies in how
structural continuity is achieved. A cast-in-situ structure naturally achieves continuity dur-
ing construction, resulting in a cohesive framework. On the other hand, precast buildings
require careful design to ensure continuity, especially at the connections where elements
such as slabs and walls meet [3].
Precast concrete elements are manufactured in controlled, off-site factories, ensuring high
quality and consistency regardless of weather. This method also allows for efficient mold
reuse, improved working conditions, and high-precision finishes [2, 4]. In precast build-
ings, various structural systems can be utilized to ensure resistance and effective load
distribution. Frame systems are commonly used to withstand both lateral and vertical
forces. These systems can feature two types of resisting connections: either Fixed Con-
nection Frames, which provide moment resistance, or Hinged Connection Frames, where
beam-column connections allow for rotation. Additionally, lateral load-resisting systems
can consist of large wall-panel systems made up of precast panels arranged orthogonally.
In dual systems, shear walls and frames work together to distribute lateral forces [5].
Precast buildings often assume simple support for their load-bearing elements. Therefore,
calculations are straightforward; however, due to the small dimensions of the elements,
achieving rigidity in the joints can be challenging. Defining the load path and selecting
an appropriate model is crucial for precast structures. A detailed load path and model
selection are essential for determining internal forces in components and joints, as well
as the force flow to the foundation [3]. Force flow, or the movement of forces through pre-
cast concrete structures, must be carefully planned and managed. Floor slabs transfer
loads to beams as shear forces, which then convey those loads to vertical components
such as walls and columns, also as shear forces. From there, the loads are transmitted to
the foundation as compression from the columns or as shear or axial reactions from the
walls. This load path must remain clear and continuous to ensure the structure functions
safely and predictably under both service and extreme loading conditions. Since precast
elements are manufactured separately and then assembled on-site, the design and struc-
ture of joints are essential in enabling the transmission of force [2]. The primary purpose
of joints is to transfer compression, tension, and shear loads [6].
Precast concrete requires early and precise design and coordination, particularly regard-
ing joint function and connectivity, as on-site modifications are limited. This is especially
crucial in seismic zones, where connection performance is vital for structural resilience
[5].

2.2.1
General Seismic Behavior of Precast Systems
The seismic performance of a precast concrete structure depends on its ability to transfer
gravity, lateral, and seismic loads efficiently through the system down to the foundation.
The system requires both strength and ductility to ensure energy dissipation and minimize
brittle failure in elements or connections [5].
Seismic performance is commonly evaluated based on four performance levels: fully op-
erational, operational, life safety, and near collapse. These categories reflect the ex-
pected post-earthquake functionality of a structure, depending on its importance and the
seismic demands it is designed to resist [5]. Similarly, Eurocode 8 (EC8) adopts a re-
lated approach by classifying structures according to their importance and assigning cor-
responding performance requirements based on their intended use, such as hospitals,
residential buildings, or industrial buildings [7].
According to Kurama (2018), the stiffness, strength, and deformation capacity of the con-
nections are the primary characteristics that determine seismic behavior in precast sys-
tems. Inadequate connection design often led to poor performance in past earthquakes.
However, decades of research and real-world applications in seismic regions have led to
substantial improvements and updates in design codes [8].
Khare et al. (2011) [9] emphasize that seismic performance in precast structures de-
pends heavily on the quality of connections, diaphragm behavior, and joint detailing. Past
earthquakes have shown that weak connections, insufficient anchorage, and poor force
distribution—especially in hollow-core slab systems—are primary causes of failure. Ro-
bust connections between major components are thus essential for seismic resilience.

frames, diaphragms, and vertical elements. These systems must work together to ensure
stability and energy dissipation during seismic events [10].

The main structural systems used for seismic resistance in precast buildings include frame

systems, wall systems (large panel systems), wall-frame systems (dual systems), floor-
framing systems, double-wall systems, and precast-cell systems. Each of these systems
helps the structure resist lateral and vertical loads in its way. Frame systems are flexible
and are often used when there needs to be open space inside. Wall systems utilize large
vertical panels to support both gravity and lateral loads. Wall-frame systems combine the
best features of both frames and walls, offering a superior construction method. They work
better in earthquakes because they have more than one way of movement. Floor-framing
systems distribute loads horizontally and work in conjunction with diaphragms. Double-
wall systems are better at retaining heat and resisting internal shear, while precast-cell
systems, composed of three-dimensional precast units, function as modular pieces with
built-in load paths [5].
Among these different systems, unbraced frame systems mainly depend on moment re-
sistance within structural members to provide lateral stability. There are two main types:
first, cantilevered column systems, where the joints between beams and columns can
move freely, and stability is provided by the foundation; second, continuous frame sys-
tems, which have strong connections between beams and columns and can have either
free-moving or stable base connections. The latter provides flexural and shear continuity,
depending on the design approach and the stiffness of individual elements [3].

connections [3].

to resist lateral forces. These systems may include pinned or moment-resisting base
connections, and their beam-column joints can be either rigid or pinned depending on
the force redistribution and deformation compatibility required. Figure 2.3 illustrates how
shear walls provide stability, while Figure 2.4 shows the in-plane load transfer mechanism

In addition to braced frames, core walls, cavity walls, and spine walls, the structure’s
horizontal load resistance is also contributed to by these elements. They help transfer both
vertical and lateral forces to the foundation, enhancing the overall seismic performance.
While non-load-bearing panels are designed to resist specific stresses (e.g., wind or self-
weight), they do not contribute to primary seismic load paths [3].
The effectiveness of how each structural system works together and the strength of its
connections determine how precast concrete structures respond to earthquakes. Suc-
cessful seismic design depends on careful planning to ensure that forces are properly
transferred and that stiffness and flexibility are balanced, particularly at connections such
as diaphragms, wall-floor joints, and beam-column interfaces [3, 5].
2.2.2
Structural Components and Seismic Response
Frames, shear walls, and diaphragms are crucial components that help precast concrete
structures withstand earthquake loads. Each element has a specific purpose in the overall
lateral force-resisting system and presents distinct design challenges (e.g., [9, 11, 7, 12]).
Precast frames rely on the ductility of connections and the capacity of elements to deform
without breaking. A notable concern is beam elongation during seismic loading, which can
reduce joint performance and compromise overall stability. Insufficient joint ductility is a
typical failure mechanism that has been extensively seen and investigated in earthquake
design [9].
In precast construction, shear walls distribute vertical and lateral stresses to the founda-
tion [13]. Their performance under seismic loads is heavily reliant on joint behavior and
cannot be accurately examined using basic, standalone assessments. Lower-bound an-
alytical methods, such as the stringer method, are commonly used; however, they have
difficulties in capturing joint interactions. Numerical modeling is advised for more accurate
findings, especially in shear joints [11].
In seismic zones, precast shear walls are typically composed of discrete segments. Their
lateral force resistance relies heavily on the strength and integrity of the wall-to-wall and
wall-to-foundation connections. Proper detailing of these connections is crucial for en-
abling adequate shear transfer and maintaining structural stability [9].

Among the most used precast slabs is the hollow-core slab. During strong earthquakes,
the beam-to-slab connections experience severe cyclic loading, making this connection
and the slab floor system critical for ensuring robust performance and transferring hori-
zontal loads through diaphragm action to the lateral load-resisting system [14].
In seismic design, floors can function as horizontal diaphragms, transferring inertia forces
from the mass of the structure to vertical load-resisting elements. These elements dis-
tribute seismic loads mainly due to their in-plane stiffness. According to EC8, diaphragms
must have sufficient stiffness to justify the modeling assumptions used in them and can
be considered rigid, semi-rigid, or flexible. Accurate modeling of diaphragm behavior is
particularly critical in buildings with irregular layouts or mixed systems [7].
Eurocode defines three principal methods for achieving diaphragm shear transfer in pre-
cast floor systems: (a) concreted or grouted joints, (b) welded or bolted steel connectors,
and (c) reinforced concrete topping. For example, grouted joints can be used to generate
diaphragm action between precast slabs, with shear stresses ranging from 0.1 to 0.15
MPa, depending on the surface roughness of the slabs. The three different methods are
illustrated in Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Main types of shear transfer connections between precast slabs: (a) grouted,
(b) welded/bolted, (c) reinforced topping.
According to Annex D of EN 1168:2005+A3:2011, hollow core slab (HCS) floors can be
designed as diaphragms if certain conditions are met. Shear forces within the floor must
be resisted by longitudinal joints or specially designed shear members along perpendic-
ular joints or edges. Shear calculations should be based on deep beam theory and Eu-
rocode 2 (EC2). If the design shear force exceeds the capacity of the joints, the capacity
can be increased by considering the shear resistance of edge beams or by introducing
shear connectors. Annex D also states that HCS floors can function as diaphragms in
seismic zones, provided that the longitudinal shear stress is verified in accordance with
EC2, Clause 10.9.3(12). Additionally, one of the following conditions must be met: a 40
mm cast-in-situ topping is applied, shear at the interface is verified, all HCS units possess
appropriately shaped indented edges, or a system of properly designed horizontal ties is
installed [15].
The primary benefits of a composite structural topping are increased stiffness and easier
continuous ties in plans with irregular shapes or large openings. However, in areas prone
to earthquakes, the additional topping weight increases seismic design forces. Buildings
with high seismic design forces (SDC) and irregular plans, or those with large diaphragm
span-to-depth ratios, may require topped diaphragms. Untopped hollow-core slab di-
aphragms may be sufficient when the diaphragm force system is straightforward and in-
plane diaphragm deflections are acceptable. Local codes may limit the use of untopped

HCS in high seismicity areas [12].
Diaphragms need boundary elements to transfer lateral loads to the lateral force-resisting
system and maintain structural integrity. Tension reinforcement is placed in these ele-
ments to act as chords and provide shear friction steel. Collectors permit lateral forces a
complete load path to the foundation by transferring shear forces from diaphragm edges
to lateral force-resisting components. The boundary reinforcement is positioned at the
end of the slabs in a concrete-filled or grouted area. In the keyways between slabs, the
reinforcement is arranged to link the diaphragm to the shear wall or boundary element.
This boundary reinforcement is concentric, and the boundary element will exhibit better
performance in seismic areas [12].
The rigid diaphragm behavior is crucial in construction, as it ensures limited floor defor-
mation and a reliable distribution of seismic forces. Clause 5.11.3.5 emphasizes placing
joints between precast elements directly over supports and reinforcing them with steel
ties. EC8 suggests using a cast-in-place concrete topping, at least 40 mm thick for spans
shorter than 8 m and 50 mm for longer spans, anchored to vertical force-resisting ele-
ments. The in-plane stiffness of the floor must be significantly greater than the lateral
stiffness of vertical structural elements, and irregular plan layouts may necessitate addi-
tional verification. Suppose the lateral deformation of the floor under seismic loading is
less than 10% of the average interstorey drift. In that case, the in-plane flexibility of the
floor and its connections to the walls must be explicitly modelled in the structural analysis
[7].
The PCI Manual [12] emphasizes that actual diaphragm behavior often falls between fully
rigid and entirely flexible in seismic scenarios. A flexible diaphragm with rigid supports
behaves like a continuous beam, while a rigid diaphragm with flexible supports distributes
forces based on the relative stiffness of the supporting walls. In practice, diaphragm be-
havior typically falls somewhere between these two. Concrete diaphragms are generally
considered more rigid than lateral force-resisting elements. However, for hollow-core di-
aphragms, a non-rigid assumption may be necessary depending on the magnitude and
type of lateral forces. As noted in the manual, assuming rigid diaphragm behavior is rea-
sonable for most low- and mid-rise structures in low seismic risk areas.
Clause 4.4.2.5 requires diaphragms to have sufficient overstrength to ensure the reliable
transfer of forces to the lateral load-resisting system. Overstrength factors ωd of 1.3 are
suggested for brittle failure modes such as concrete shear, while 1.1 is used for ductile
failure. For structures with a high ductility class or significant irregularities, ultimate limit
state (ULS) checks are mandatory. Design methods such as deep beam analogies or
strut-and-tie models may be used [7].
According to the PCI Manual [12], it is often preferable for diaphragms to remain elastic
during design-level seismic events. This approach helps ensure that post-elastic behavior
occurs primarily in the lateral force-resisting elements. By keeping the diaphragm elas-
tic, concerns related to diaphragm flexibility and ductility requirements for connections
are reduced. Additionally, the horizontal distribution of forces to lateral systems remains
consistent throughout the seismic response.
Ghosh et al. (2017) [10] highlights that diaphragms in precast structures must be de-
signed to reliably transfer seismic forces to shear walls through well-detailed and ad-
equately strong or ductile connections. The diaphragm should be sufficiently stiff and
strong in-plane. At the same time, the diaphragm-to-wall connections must either remain
elastic under amplified seismic actions or be demonstrably ductile to avoid brittle failure.

2.2.3
Seismic Design and Performance of Precast Structures
The ability of a structure to dissipate energy and retain its load-carrying capacity when
subjected to cyclic inelastic displacements during an earthquake is known as ductility.
Achieving ductility in precast concrete systems requires close attention to connection de-
tailing and member hierarchy to prevent brittle failure mechanisms and ensure that energy
dissipation occurs in the designated ductile zones [5].
The capacity design method provides a structured approach for achieving ductility. This
approach ensures that undesirable brittle failure mechanisms within an element or con-
nection are avoided by developing an appropriate hierarchy of strength. The weakest link
is designed to behave in a ductile manner under severe deformation, while other structural
elements are given sufficient overstrength to support the desired ductile mechanism. This
concept is supported in principal design codes, including EC8, ACI 318 (United States of
America), and NZS 3101 (New Zealand), which provide extensive guidance on joint de-
tailing, reinforcement anchorage, and plastic hinge relocation [5].
On the other hand, some national codes have been found to lack essential provisions.
Khare [9] identified deficiencies in the Indian standard (IS 11447:1985), which was found
to be lacking in critical seismic detailing requirements, particularly for connections and

ductile manner as intended, the reduced design forces could lead to hazardous behavior
[5].

2.4
Precast Connections in Seismic Design
Connections are essential to the performance of precast structures because they often
control the system’s overall behavior and seismic resilience [23]. Their design must ac-
commodate not only structural demands but also practical considerations. Effective con-
nections must withstand all applied forces, including gravity, wind, seismic loads, and
earth pressures. They must also be able to accommodate volume changes, allow for
necessary deformations, and be durable and fire-resistant. [3].
The detailing and strength of a precast structure’s connections are inextricably linked to
its seismic behavior. Avoiding excessive deformation and possible failure requires that
gravity-load-carrying and lateral-force-resisting systems be compatible. To enhance seis-
mic performance, several techniques have been employed, including post-tensioned rock-
ing walls, grouted joints, and mechanical splices. Structural resilience can be significantly
improved by using a capacity-based design approach, ductile detailing, and adequate an-
chorage [5, 9].
Ductility, a crucial concept in connection design, refers to a connection’s ability to with-
stand large deformations before failure. It is measured from the point of first yield to
ultimate failure. In building frames, ductility depends on moment resistance, typically
provided by reinforcement or steel sections. Failure can result from bar rupture, con-
crete crushing, or connector failure. Connections are also subject to less apparent but
equally significant forces, such as volume changes due to creep, shrinkage, and temper-
ature variations, which can induce tensile stresses. Such stresses can be managed by
incorporating stress relief details or additional reinforcement. Durability is also a crucial
concern, as exposed steel elements are susceptible to corrosion, cracking, and spalling.
As a result, protective measures like concrete encasement, galvanization, or specialized
coatings are necessary. Appropriate fire-rated detailing is required to ensure that the fire
resistance matches the overall structural system’s performance [3].
Precast concrete seismic systems are divided into two main types: emulative and jointed.
Emulative connections replicate monolithic concrete behavior, ensuring similar stiffness,
lateral strength, and energy dissipation. They can be ductile or strong, with ductile con-
nections forming plastic hinges for flexural yielding. Strong connections remain elastic
under seismic loading by applying capacity-design principles. Jointed construction, also
known as non-emulative detailing, uses unbonded post tensioning steel to hold structural
elements together and reduce inelastic damage. This design focuses on self-centering
behavior from PT steel but provides lower energy dissipation. Research on precast seis-
mic structures has advanced significantly, leading to the development of code-compliant
structures. Hybrid moment frames are the most extensively used precast system in seis-
mic applications. However, practical adoption remains slow due to concerns about un-
bonded PT steel anchorage and interaction between lateral and gravity systems. Further
research and design improvements are expected to expand the use of precast systems
in seismic designs [8].
Recent studies have highlighted the impact of connections between precast structural el-
ements and non-structural elements, such as cladding panels, on seismic performance.
Rigid panel connections can act as unintended shear walls, altering system stiffness and
increasing seismic demands on both panels and structural elements, despite the com-
mon perception that cladding is non-load-bearing. Therefore, to ensure a reliable seismic
response, it is essential to detail and coordinate these connections carefully [24].

2.4.1
Precast Connections and Seismic Performance
Because they are unable to consistently transfer lateral forces or stop displacement dur-
ing ground motion, friction-based or gravity-reliant connections are insufficient for seismic
regions. Instead, critical interfaces between foundations, vertical elements, diaphragms,
and cladding require mechanical connections that can withstand seismic shear and mo-
ment forces [5].
A standard method for connecting precast shear walls is through keyed shear joints, en-
abling load transfer between adjacent wall elements. These joints typically feature U-
shaped reinforcement bars (U-bars) extending from each wall segment, placed within a
small gap between adjacent precast elements. To tie the U-bars together, a locking rebar
is positioned inside the loops to ensure proper tension transfer between horizontal U-bars.
Finally, grout or mortar is used to fill the joint and bond the reinforcement. The shear ca-
pacity of these joints is a key factor in determining the overall load-bearing capacity of the
wall [11].
To address the limitations of single-connection systems, few studies have been con-
ducted. Bi et al. (2024) [25] conducted an experimental study on the seismic performance
of precast concrete shear walls using a hybrid connection system combining grouted cor-
rugated metallic ducts (GCMD) and grouted splice sleeves (GSS). Five full-scale shear
wall specimens were tested under quasi-static cyclic loading to evaluate their failure modes,
ductility, and energy dissipation. The hybrid system placed GCMD in the middle of the
wall and GSS in the boundary elements. The results indicated that hybrid connection
walls achieved higher lateral load-bearing capacity and improved energy dissipation, with
performance increasing as the axial compression ratio rose. Hybrid specimens exhib-
ited earlier cracking, primarily concentrated at the corners, yet still provided up to 37.5%
higher cumulative energy dissipation than single-connection precast walls. However, their
overall performance in terms of ductility and crack distribution remained lower than that
of cast-in-place walls. Hybrid specimens maintained wall integrity under large displace-
ments, but failure was localized in post-cast regions, unlike the more uniform cracking in
cast-in-place walls. Overall, the findings suggest that precast shear walls with GCMD and
GSS connections can achieve seismic performance comparable to that of CIP systems
in certain aspects; however, further refinements are necessary to enhance their ductility
and reduce stiffness degradation. The study suggests further research should focus on
optimizing hybrid connections, expanding experimental testing, and evaluating long-term
durability to close the gap with cast-in-place systems.
In another study, Brunesi and Nascimbene (2017) [26] conducted an extensive experi-
mental and numerical investigation on full-scale precast shear wall panels typical of Dutch
construction. Their results highlighted the inherent vulnerability of conventional wall-to-
wall and wall-to-foundation joints under cyclic seismic loading, primarily due to inadequate
detailing, limited ductility, and insufficient energy dissipation capacity. The tests revealed
that failures were dominated by rocking mechanisms, premature shear failure of dowel-
based connectors, and localized crushing or spalling of concrete near the joints. Notably,
joints relying solely on friction or unanchored mechanical connectors demonstrated poor
load transfer and significant degradation in strength. These findings indicate that either
enhanced joint reinforcement or the implementation of displacement-tolerant connection
designs is necessary to ensure adequate seismic resilience in precast structures.
In a separate study, Ercolino et al. (2018) [16] examined the most common beam-to-
column connections, specifically pinned (dowel) connections characterized by low flexural
stiffness. This connection results in a flexible structural system that can undergo signifi-

cant horizontal displacements during seismic events. The authors emphasize that these
connections are especially susceptible to P-∆effects, which are geometric nonlineari-
ties arising when gravity loads are applied to a deformed structure during an earthquake,
resulting in additional second-order moments. Such effects can magnify lateral displace-
ments and undermine structural stability, making them a crucial consideration in seismic
design. Additionally, the presence of slender columns subjected to high axial loads fur-

Other studies concentrate on wall-to-slab interfaces. Arthi and Jaya (2020) [27] conducted
both experimental and numerical investigations into the seismic performance of precast
shear wall-to-slab connections that utilized dowel bars. They compared these precast
connections to monolithic cast-in-place connections under cyclic loading conditions. The
experimental findings revealed that precast connections achieved ultimate load capaci-
ties that were 38.9% and 52.1% higher than those of monolithic specimens in the push
and pull directions, respectively.
Additionally, finite element analysis (FEA) predicted
a strength increase of 41.6% and 58.4% for precast connections. Precast connections
also demonstrated superior post-elastic behavior, sustaining yield loads up to 7.3% drift,
whereas monolithic specimens experienced degradation beyond 2.5% drift. Although mi-
nor cracks were observed in the nib portion of the precast connections, no significant
damage occurred in the shear wall; the reinforcement in the slab primarily controlled fail-
ure. The study confirmed its findings through FEA, which indicated a deviation of less
than 20% from the experimental results. Overall, the research substantiates that precast
dowel connections improve seismic resistance and are suitable for application in seismic
regions.
Floor systems, particularly those utilizing hollow-core slabs, play a crucial role in seismic
performance. According to Vinutha et al. (2021) [14], past earthquakes have revealed
various failures, including beam elongation at plastic hinges and relative rotation at hollow-
core slab supports on precast beams, as well as positive and negative moment failures,
shear failures, and torsional failures. Common damages observed in hollow-core slabs
consist of corner cracks, longitudinal splitting, and transverse cracks. The formation of
plastic hinges in the supporting beam can adversely affect the support of hollow-core
slabs. To enhance performance, it is recommended to use a low-friction bearing strip
with a compressible backing board at the seats of hollow-core slabs on precast beams
(e.g., inverted T or L-beams). This approach helps reduce stress and allows for controlled
rotation. The seating width should be at least 75 mm or span 180 mm to prevent chipping
of the concrete. For partially precast systems, a cast-in-situ reinforced topping is advised,
while fully precast systems should incorporate steel ties and grout to ensure effective
diaphragm action. Hollow-core slabs are typically seated on mortar pads or bearing strips,
with grout and reinforcement applied where necessary.
Experimental studies from New Zealand have confirmed that poorly detailed connections
led to premature failures, while improved floor-frame connections significantly enhanced
drift capacity and seismic resilience. The study also noted that emerging technologies,
such as precast rocking walls and self-centering systems, showed promising results in
seismic performance testing [9].

4.1.2
Force Flow
In precast buildings, the load path must be clearly defined to ensure that vertical and
lateral forces are effectively transferred through the structure and into the foundation,
with seismic action being the primary horizontal load.
Vertical load
Vertical loads are transferred through a series of connected structural elements, ensuring
that the weight of the structure and its imposed loads are effectively transmitted to the
foundation. The HCS serves as the primary floor system, carrying both variable loads and
permanent non-structural loads. It forms a structural link between the shear walls and
Delta beams, effectively resisting vertical loads and distributing them to adjacent load-
bearing members. The Delta beams transfer these loads to either the columns or the
adjacent shear walls. Ultimately, all vertical loads are delivered to the foundation through
the steel columns or the shear walls, as illustrated in Figure 4.3. In precast buildings, it
is common practice to analyze structural elements as simply supported, meaning that no
Seismic analysis, design and performance of precast concrete structures

==PAGE:34==
moment transfer occurs at the connections and only shear force reactions are transmitted
between elements.
Figure 4.3: The load path of the vertical loads.
Horizontal load
Horizontal forces are generated during an earthquake when the building’s mass accel-
erates in response to ground motion. The HCS act as horizontal diaphragms, collect-
ing and transmitting in-plane seismic forces, assuming rigid diaphragm behavior. These
forces are then distributed to the precast shear walls in proportion to their relative stiff-
ness, as they are the primary elements resisting lateral forces in this structure. The walls
are strategically positioned in both principal directions of the building to provide balanced
lateral resistance and minimize torsional effects during seismic loading. From the shear
walls, the seismic loads are transferred down to the foundation. This requires effective
load transfer through multiple connections, adequate in-plane shear capacity of the HCS,
and anchoring to prevent uplift. Figure 4.4 illustrates the horizontal load path, starting
from the HCS diaphragms, through the precast shear walls, and finally to the foundation.

Load Analysis
Load combinations, vertical loads, and seismic parameters are established for both an-
alytical and numerical analyses. These form the foundation for determining the layout
of structural walls, including their thickness and length. This is achieved using methods
such as rigid floor and stabilizing walls, with horizontal actions derived from the lateral
force approach.
4.2.1
Load Combinations
The load combinations are determined according to EN 1990, which outlines the design
approach for the ULS. The design load, Ed, is calculated by combining permanent loads,
the main variable load, and accompanying variable loads based on the type of analysis.

combination is employed:
Ed = Kfi · (ωG · Gk)
(4.1)
Where:
• Ed: Design value of the action effect

– gk,str: Self-weight of structural elements
– gk,slab: Self-weight of slabs
• ωG: Partial safety factor for favorable permanent actions (typically 0.9)

modelled as equivalent static loads applied horizontally, to define wall dimensions and
evaluate capacity against tilting, sliding, and crushing.

Horizontal Loads
The analytical calculation of seismic forces uses the lateral force method according to
EC8, Clause 4.3.3.2. Two seismic intensities are examined: 0.15g and 0.5g, covering a
range from low to moderate seismic hazard levels across different regions of Iceland. All
relevant parameters detailed here will also be applied in the numerical study.
Horizontal loads can originate from either wind or seismic activity. Seismic loading, which
arises from ground motion caused by earthquakes, plays a critical role in structural design.
When a structure is subjected to seismic excitation, it experiences inertia forces due to the
acceleration of its mass. This behavior can be described, in simplified terms, by Newton’s
second law of motion, F = m · a, where the lateral force acting on each storey is propor-
tional to its mass and the induced acceleration. In structures of regular configuration with
constant storey height and mass, seismic forces tend to increase approximately linearly
with height due to higher accelerations in the upper storeys. This principle is reflected in
the lateral force method, a linear static approach that approximates seismic effects us-
ing the fundamental mode shape of the structure, which typically results in a similar load
distribution as shown in Figure 4.5.

According to EC8 [7], the reduction factor kp depends on the energy dissipation capacity
of the precast structure, which is primarily governed by the performance of the connec-
tions and whether the structure can be considered emulative of a cast-in-situ system. To
achieve the full reduction factor kp = 1.0, connections must either be placed outside the
critical regions and overdesigned, or be located within the critical regions but designed ac-
cording to capacity design and overstrength principles, ensuring that plastic hinges form
outside the connection rather than within the connections themselves. Both approaches
require that the connection is heavily reinforced compared to the adjacent regions. Al-
ternatively, the full value may also be applied if the connection is capable of dissipating
energy by satisfying local ductility criteria, allowing it to undergo stable inelastic deforma-
tion without brittle failure.

Numerical Modeling and Seismic
Analysis
The numerical approach adopted to evaluate the seismic response of the case study
structure involves developing two global finite element models in SAP2000. One model
includes a concrete topping on the HCS, while the other does not. The dynamic properties
of the structure were determined through modal analysis, and the seismic response was
primarily evaluated using RSA. Additionally, a THA was also performed for comparison
with the RSA results.
5.1
Global Finite Element Model and Assumptions
To analyse the structural response under seismic loading, a global finite element model of
the structure was developed using SAP2000 v19. The model was designed to realistically
represent the case study structure, with particular focus on force distribution and overall
dynamic behavior. Each node was assigned six degrees of freedom (DOF), consisting of
three translational (UX, UY, UZ) and three rotational (RX, RY, RZ) components, to fully
capture the three-dimensional response.
The model represents the full geometry, all relevant structural elements, boundary con-
ditions and mass distribution. Shear walls were modelled as the primary lateral load-
resisting system, while HCS were defined as rigid diaphragms. A separate static load
case was applied in the model to compare the resulting force distribution with analytical
hand calculations from the rigid floor method, serving as a verification step. Figure 5.1
illustrates the model and global coordinates.

HCS are load-bearing in only one direction, with considerably lower bending stiffness in
the transverse direction. To account for this, the transverse bending stiffness is often
reduced. However, modelling the slab as isotropic with significantly reduced transverse
stiffness affects the modal analysis, resulting in unrealistic mode shapes where the fun-
damental modes are no longer translational. While this simplification may be acceptable
for static analysis, it is unsuitable for dynamic analysis.

model, a bending thickness of 335 mm and a membrane thickness of 70 mm were used. In

HCS. These include the directional Young’s moduli (E1, E2, E3) and shear moduli (G12,

Seismic Analysis
This analysis examines the dynamic behavior of the structure to evaluate its seismic re-
sponse using two different structural models. Modal analysis is performed to determine
the dynamic properties of each model, while RSA provides the primary basis for seismic
force demand in the case study. Additionally, a THA is performed to compare results with
the response spectrum findings. All analyses are carried out according to EC 8, using the
seismic input parameters defined in Chapter 4.
5.2.1

ture by solving the eigenvalue problem. This involves determining the natural frequencies,

A comparison of seismic forces per floor for Wall X.2 highlights clear differences between
the Lateral Force Method (LFM) and RSA. As shown in Table 5.12, LFM results in a higher
total base shear but does not capture the increased force demand at the top floor, where

arises from LFM’s reliance on empirical base shear formulas and a simplified lateral force
distribution, which assumes the structure’s response is dominated by the fundamental
mode, with full seismic mass participation. Under the rigid diaphragm assumption, forces
are distributed based on floor mass and height. In contrast, RSA accounts for the ac-
tual mass and stiffness distribution, capturing higher-mode effects and resulting in a more
accurate force profile. These findings demonstrate that while LFM is a simplified and con-
servative method, it may fail to capture localized increases in seismic demand, particularly
in irregular structures where torsional effects are significant.

Time-History Analysis
In addition to the RSA, a linear modal time history analysis was conducted solely for
comparison purposes and will not be used in the subsequent chapter. The objective was
to evaluate the structural response to real seismic input and assess whether the critical
regions and overall response trends aligned with those obtained from the RSA.

which simplifies the solution by decoupling the equations of motion into independent
modal equations. The procedure is based on the standard equation of motion for a multi-

Overall, the results from RSA and THA show good agreement in identifying the critical
structural walls, with Walls X.1, X.2, and Y.2 consistently exhibiting the highest force and
displacement demands. However, the Hella and Solh earthquake records lead to greater
seismic demands than RSA, particularly on the upper floors, resulting in increased over-
turning moments and higher demands on vertical tie-down reinforcement for the precast
structure, as detailed in Table 5.14. It is also observed that RSA produces a more linear
force distribution along the building height, whereas THA shows a less uniform distribu-
tion between floors. This difference arises from the fundamental assumptions of the two
methods. RSA evaluates peak responses for each vibration mode independently and
combines them statistically, neglecting how modal responses might interact over time,
with results often dominated by the fundamental mode that mobilizes most of the struc-
tural mass. THA, on the other hand, computes the full time-history response by summing
the contributions of all modes at each time step, capturing their interaction and phase
relationships as well as how the earthquake’s frequency content excites them. As a re-
sult, RSA may underestimate or overestimate seismic demands compared to time-history
analyses, depending on the characteristics of the selected ground motions, as observed
for the Hella and Solh records in this study.

Numerical Modeling and Structural
Analysis in Optum CS
A numerical model was developed in Optum CS using the Optimization-Based Finite El-
ement Method (OBFEM) to evaluate the structural and joint performance of two different
reinforcement configurations under seismic loading. This chapter outlines the modeling
setup, material properties, loading conditions, and analysis procedures using both limit
and elasto-plastic analysis.
6.1
Analysis Approach and Assumptions
For the analysis of the joints and the precast system, two types of analyses were per-
formed, a limit analysis and elasto-plastic analysis. The limit analysis was used solely to
detect potential collapse mechanisms and load multiplier, while the elasto-plastic analysis
served as the primary method for evaluating structural behavior and joint performance.
According to EC 2, non-linear analysis is permitted for assessing joint performance and
capacity under ULS conditions.
Elasto-plastic analysis is performed to account for nonlinear material behavior, including
plasticity and post-yield response. This approach allows the structure to continue carrying
loads beyond initial yielding by redistributing internal forces through plastic deformation.
As a result, it provides a more realistic representation of structural performance [38]. The
analysis incorporates the stiffness of structural elements, assuming that concrete is fully
cracked and can only carry compressive stresses, while reinforcement is responsible for
resisting tension.
The analyses are carried out under ULS loading conditions, with partial safety factors
applied in accordance with EC2 and EC8, Clause 5.2.4. To account for the reduction in
compressive strength due to cracking and nonlinear material behavior, an effectiveness
factor, denoted by ν, is introduced. This factor modifies the design compressive strength
based on the concrete’s characteristic strength and the principal strain. It is defined in
Equation 6.1, where the largest principal strain ε1 reflects the extent of cracking in the
material, [38]. In this study, a constant value of ν = 0.5 is used. The reduced compressive
stress used in the elasto-plastic analysis is determined as fcd · ν.

In limit analysis, both upper and lower bound formulations bracket the true collapse load.
The lower bound uses a statically admissible stress field that satisfies equilibrium, bound-
ary conditions, and the yield criterion, giving a safe estimate. The upper bound uses a
kinematically admissible collapse mechanism satisfying boundary conditions and the prin-
ciple of virtual work, giving an overestimate of the true collapse load. A mixed formulation
is adopted to provide a more balanced and reliable estimate.

6.2
Model Geometry and Structural Detailing
Two identical numerical models were developed with varying reinforcement in both the
walls and joints. Model 1 reflects the Danish construction tradition, featuring a lower rein-
forcement ratio, while Model 2 incorporates a higher reinforcement ratio for comparison.
Both models are based on the same wall layout and geometry presented in previous chap-
ters. Concrete topping is included in both models, as determined from the global analysis
to ensure sufficient shear capacity.
To realistically capture the structural behavior, the entire core was modeled as a single
unit instead of analyzing walls X.1 and X.2 separately. Modeling them individually would
neglect their interaction and the contribution of the central core, potentially leading to un-
realistic results. Together, the walls form a delta-shaped core configuration that behaves
as a unified structural system. This setup allows the structure to resist seismic demand
more effectively, as the full core contributes to the lateral response and activates verti-
cal tie-down reinforcement in either Y.2 or Y.3, which contributed to preventing tilting of
the core walls. Moreover, modeling the full core results in a more even distribution of
compressive stresses in the concrete.

is divided into two panels per floor, resulting in vertical joints both between the divided
elements and at the corners where adjacent walls meet. Horizontal joints are placed at
each floor level and assigned to all walls. Vertical tie-down reinforcement is positioned
near the edges of each wall. Section views of the joints are provided in the figures below.

6.4
Results
The results are based on a comparison between Model 1 and Model 2, evaluating the
response of the precast wall as well as the performance of the joints and vertical rein-
forcement. The wall under examination is wall X.2, with figures scaled for clarity and
extreme values indicated in the captions. Normal stresses are defined such that negative
values represent compression, while positive values represent tension.
A preliminary limit analysis was conducted to determine the structural capacity to sustain
the applied load before collapse. Neither model experienced failure, and it was estimated
that the load could be increased by 14% for both models before collapse.
The findings presented in the following sections are based solely on elasto-plastic analy-
sis.
6.4.1
Precast Wall Response
The response of the precast wall is evaluated based on the overall system displacement,
the distribution of compressive stress and strain, and the resulting wall reactions.
The displacement results indicate notable differences between the two models. Model 1
reached a maximum displacement of 63.2 mm, whereas Model 2 remained lower at 41.9 mm.
In terms of capacity utilization, Model 1 reached 73% of its biaxial capacity of the walls,
while Model 2 utilized only 35%. This difference can be attributed to the increased wall
reinforcement in Model 2.
To evaluate the development and propagation of compressive stresses under combined

6.4.5
Summary
Model 2 showed improved structural performance compared to Model 1. The increased
wall reinforcement resulted in reduced deformation, smoother stress distribution, and
lower compressive strains, enhancing confinement and reducing the risk of crushing.
Vertical joints in Model 2 exhibited higher shear resistance due to the increased amount
of reinforcement and were less likely to experience joint opening, whereas Model 1 ex-
ceeded its capacity, showing signs of failure and high shear utilization. The horizontal
joints exhibited slightly improved performance in Model 1, where the wall’s deformability
enhanced contact along the joint interface. However, the variation between the models
remains limited, as the detailing of the top interface was consistent in both cases. In
both models, the vertical tie-down reinforcement remained within the elastic range, with
Model 1 developing higher stress in the reinforcement.
The structural response observed in both models suggests a combination of shear and
flexural behavior, with a greater contribution from shear, as evidenced by the presence of
diagonal compressive struts aligning with the shear stress distribution. The shear-related
response in both models indicates a lack of ductility and controlled energy dissipation,
suggesting that their seismic performance is governed by strength and stiffness rather
than by ductile flexural behavior. This is further supported by the absence of plastic hinge
formation at the wall base and the vertical tie-down reinforcement remaining within the
elastic range. These observations imply that ductile behavior cannot be assumed for
either model and must instead be achieved through targeted design strategies and careful
detailing.
Overall, Model 2 demonstrated superior performance under the given conditions, as the
increased reinforcement ratio contributed to greater stiffness and strength, improved shear
resistance, and reduced strain accumulation within the structure. These characteristics
make Model 2 more suitable for the evaluated configuration, particularly in terms of verti-
cal joint performance and overall structural integrity under seismic loading.

7
Conclusions
This thesis investigated the seismic performance of precast concrete systems through a
case study building, focusing on precast walls as the primary lateral load-resisting ele-
ments, joint performance, and the role of HCS acting as rigid diaphragms for seismic load
transfer. Two different reinforcement layouts were compared: one with a low reinforce-
ment ratio, reflecting common practice in Denmark, and another with a higher reinforce-
ment ratio, representative of standard practice in Iceland. The objective was to evaluate
whether the Danish approach is suitable for low to moderate seismic regions such as
Iceland and to identify any associated structural challenges. To support this, nonlinear
elasto-plastic analyses were conducted using Optum CS. Additionally, Eurocode-related
limitations were examined to assess the regulatory constraints in implementing precast
systems in seismic design contexts.
The numerical results revealed that reinforcement detailing significantly affected struc-
tural performance under 0.15g seismicity. Increased wall reinforcement enhanced con-
finement, reduced deformation, and lowered strain demand. Vertical joints exhibited im-
proved performance in the more heavily reinforced model, while horizontal joints showed
minimal variation. Vertical tie-down reinforcement was less stressed due to improved wall
confinement. The results further highlight that both models showed limited capacity for
controlled energy dissipation. As a result, structural performance under seismic loading
was primarily governed by strength and stiffness rather than ductility. These findings in-
dicate that ductile behavior cannot be assumed and must instead be achieved through
targeted design adjustments and careful detailing.
At a seismic demand of 0.5g, both models lacked sufficient overturning resistance. The
analysis showed that the vertical tie-down reinforcement layout used in the case study
was inadequate to prevent uplift, requiring reinforcement levels comparable to cast-in-
situ solutions. For this seismic intensity, adjustments such as increasing the number of
walls, adding vertical tie-down reinforcement, or lowering the building height would be
necessary to ensure adequate seismic performance.
These findings demonstrate that precast structures designed according to Danish princi-
ples can be suitable for low seismic regions with minor adjustments, such as increasing
the wall and vertical joint reinforcement ratios. However, their applicability diminishes
with greater seismic demand and building height. The results further emphasized the ne-
cessity of topped HCS in all cases studied, as untopped systems lacked sufficient shear
capacity to ensure effective diaphragm action.
Further observations indicated that the size and positioning of vertical tie-down reinforce-
ment had a notable influence on ductility. A single bar with a larger diameter was less
likely to yield compared to multiple smaller bars of equivalent total area, as the increased
bond perimeter of the smaller bars allowed the reinforcement to elongate and yield more
effectively. In addition, the numerical analysis revealed that increasing the compressive
capacity of the soil reduced the demand on vertical tie-down reinforcement while increas-
ing stress and strain at the wall base. This further highlights the importance of concrete
strength, as compressive demand at the wall base can be significant. Wall thickness is
also particularly important, as the minimum seat width of HCS limits the effective width for
horizontal joints and reduces their shear resistance capacity.

The study also identified the use of HCS as a key limitation to structural ductility. As
these elements form part of the load path and connect directly to the lateral load-resisting
system, their inability to meet Eurocode ductility criteria reduces the overall seismic perfor-
mance of the structure. Additional limitations were also identified in friction-based joints,
where cohesion must be halved under dynamic loading, increasing the demand on con-
nection design. Furthermore, the numerical and modal analyses demonstrated that mod-
eling HCS in precast buildings requires precision, as these elements are load-bearing
in only one direction and have limited transverse stiffness. Accurately capturing their
anisotropic behavior is essential for producing a reliable dynamic response, as simplified
assumptions can distort mode shapes and reduce the accuracy of the predicted seismic
performance.
To benchmark conventional approaches, analytical estimates such as the lateral force
method were compared to numerical models. These proved overly conservative, over-
estimating the seismic demand due to simplified empirical equations and the assumption
of full mass participation in the fundamental mode. Similar limitations were observed in
the analytical treatment of friction-based joints, underscoring the importance of advanced
numerical modeling for accurate seismic behavior prediction.
Precast structures in seismic regions face challenges related to both design and code
requirements. Although Eurocode provides some guidance for precast systems, it gen-
erally does not support their use in seismic design. Strict ductility requirements often
necessitate designing precast solutions to be designed almost like cast-in-situ structures,
and cohesion values for joints decrease under seismic loading. These provisions place
higher demands on precast designs, often resulting in very stiff systems with thick walls.
This trend was observed in this study, where the global model results showed relatively
low displacements and short fundamental periods, indicating an excessively stiff structural
response.
Future work should investigate alternative structural layouts and reinforcement configura-
tions, as well as examine the impact of wall openings, to assess their effect on structural
response and to identify solutions that enhance ductility and seismic performance.

A.1
Design Practices in Denmark
Denmark is located in a region of low to very low seismicity compared to other parts
of Europe. However, recent seismic hazard assessments show that certain areas still
require consideration in structural design. The design ground acceleration values range
from 0.1 m/s2 to 0.3 m/s2, depending on the region. These values correspond to a 10%
probability of exceedance in 50 years and are specified for ground type A, as shown in the
Danish National Annex to Eurocode 8 [28]. A seismic hazard map for Denmark is shown
in Figure 3.1, highlighting regional variations in design acceleration values [28].
According to Eurocode 0, Clause 4.1.1, seismic loads can be categorized either as vari-
able or accidental actions depending on the site’s seismicity. Clause 1.5.3.5 defines ac-
cidental actions as rare, short-duration events such as fire or impact. In low-seismicity
regions—typically where the design ground acceleration ag ≤0.08g or ag · S ≤0.1g—
seismic actions may be treated as accidental. The final classification depends on the
National Annex. In Denmark, the National Annex to Eurocode EN 1990 (DS/EN 1990 DK
NA:2021) classifies seismic actions as accidental, setting the material partial factor ωM to
1.0 for seismic design situations unless otherwise specified. This reflects the treatment
of earthquakes as rare, low-probability events in the Danish context.
Wind load is typically the governing horizontal action in Denmark, although this can vary
depending on site-specific conditions and structural configuration.
Design Practices in Iceland
Iceland is located on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, where the North American and Eurasian tec-
tonic plates meet, which makes it a seismically active country. Seismic activity in Iceland
is generally classified as low to moderate. Horizontal reference acceleration values can
reach up to 0.5 g for a 10% probability of exceedance in 50 years [34]. A hazard map of
Iceland is shown in Figure 3.6.
Given this level of seismic hazard, seismic actions in Iceland must be treated as part
of the seismic design situation. This aligns with EC8 Clause 5.2.4, which states that
unless strength degradation is explicitly accounted for, the standard material partial safety
factors used for persistent and transient design situations (e.g., ωc = 1.5, ωs = 1.15)
should also be used for seismic design. The National Annex may allow alternative values,
but Iceland does not make such exceptions—therefore, the full material factors apply in
seismic design situations.

Hollow Core Slab
In practice, the rigidity of hollow core slabs must be verified in accordance with Eurocode
8, which permits rigid diaphragm modeling only when the floor layout is regular and the
slab provides sufficient in-plane stiffness. Hollow core slabs typically have limited in-plane
shear capacity, so a cast-in-place concrete topping is often added to enhance diaphragm
action and ensure reliable force transfer. When rigid behavior cannot be ensured, the
slab may be modeled as a semi-rigid diaphragm. This approach accounts for in-plane
flexibility and can result in a more realistic distribution of lateral loads, especially when
the vertical walls are significantly stiffer than the slab, causing it to behave like a deep
beam spanning between supports.
Link Element
To control the release of forces between connected shell elements, SAP2000 provides
an edge release option, available only in the latest versions. Alternatively, shell elements
can be connected using link elements, which was the approach adopted in this analysis.
Link elements were used to connect the hollow core slab and the shear wall, simulating
a simply supported condition that prevents moment transfer. To achieve this, the slab
element was offset by 10 mm from the wall, and a link element was placed in between.
This setup is illustrated in Figure 5.2b.
The link elements were given a high translational stiffness of 1 × 1011 kN/m, since fixing
the translational degree of freedom (DOF) can lead to constraint conflicts and numeri-
cal issues. The rotational stiffness was assigned using a low translational stiffness of
0.05 kN/m, as setting it to zero or removing it entirely could result in constraint conflicts
or instability. This approach ensures that no moments are transferred, which aligns with
the intended simply supported behavior, as recommended by CSI [35] and Structural
Academy’s best practices for defining restraints [36]. Additionally, the slab was subdi-
vided into smaller panels to allow for more link elements between the shear walls and the
hollow-core slabs. This refinement improved the model’s ability to capture the interaction
between these components.

Seismic Records
The South Iceland Seismic Zone (SISZ) experienced two significant strike-slip earth-
quakes in June 2000, occurring four days apart, with moment magnitudes of Mw = 6.52
and Mw = 6.44, and focal depths of 6.3 km and 5.3 km, respectively. Another major strike-
slip event followed in May 2008, involving almost simultaneous ruptures on two nearby
faults, with a magnitude of Mw = 6.30. Each earthquake impacted around 5,000 low-rise
residential structures and various types of infrastructure [37]. Towns including Selfoss
and Hveragerði were located close to the rupture zones and experienced intense ground
shaking. Peak ground accelerations recorded by the Icelandic Strong Motion Network
reached up to 0.84g in the 2000 events and 0.88g in 2008. Although the 2008 event ex-
hibited higher PGA, the 2000 earthquakes resulted in more structural damage. In both
cases, buildings designed in accordance with more modern codes showed improved per-
formance [37]. The locations of these seismic events are displayed in Figure B.1.

3 Loads
The deﬁniƟon of surface loads, line loads and point loads appear in Load deﬁniƟons. 
Surface loads act only on decks and appear in Load plans. Decks distribute loads to
surrounding walls (or free edges if there is no wall) as line loads. Decks act as simply
supported, meaning that the line load is calculated as surface load · deck span / 2. 
Line loads, including loads from decks, act on walls either in-plane or out-of-plane/laterally
and appear in Load elevaƟons. 
Line loads acƟng in-plane are included in the ﬁnite element calculaƟons and in the code
check calculaƟons. 
Line loads and point loads acƟng out-of-plane/laterally are only included in the code check
calculaƟons and have no eﬀect on the ﬁnite element calculaƟons. Note that point loads are
only acƟng out-of-plane/laterally. 
Speciﬁc weight of walls and addiƟonal weight on walls appear in the deﬁniƟons of Wall
Blocks. 
Lateral loads acƟng on panels appear in Wall secƟons in the Building geometry secƟon. 
Horizontal loads and the distribuƟon appear in the Horizontal loads secƟons. A horizontal
load act in the horizontal joint above the considered level. 
A load is only included if it appears in the speciﬁc load combinaƟon, except for the always
included lateral loads acƟng on panels. Included loads appear in Finite Element Results
secƟon.

UDFORMNING AF T!SAMLINGER
Afprøvning med fugeudstøbninger har vist at risikoen for at en fuge er utilstrækkeligt udstøbt er størst 
hvor der benyttes T-samlinger med 3. elementer. Derfor anbefales det at T-samlinger som udgangspunkt 
laves som vist herunder.
ARMERINGSPRINCIPPER VED FUGESAMLINGER
FUGELÅSE I ELEMENTENDER:
Hvis man har søjlearmeringer eller u-bøjler liggende langs elementkanter, skal man være opmærksom på 
at hårnålene i støbesamlingen skal kunne være mellem hovedarmeringen/stringerarmeringer. 
Bemærk at hårnålene i samlingen normalt vil kunne anvendes til at forankre nettet med og dermed vil 
man kunne undlade u-bøjler i elementenden (billedet til venstre).  
 
Der anvendes som standard en 8mm hårnål med en udvendig bredde på 66 mm. Der kræves særlig 
opmærksomhed i vægge med en tykkelse under 200 mm, da der erfaringsmæssigt er udfordringer med 
at få plads til hårnålene i disse vægge. 
Ved overførsel af laster kan man bruge hårnålene til at forankre nettet, svarende til hårnålenes kapaci-tet. 
Dermed vil man normalt kunne undlade u-bøjlerne langs elementkanten. Det viste eksempel herover er 
en 180 mm væg med 25 mm dæklag og 8 mm net. Kantjernet kan i dette tilfælde maksimalt være 12 mm. 
Hvis nettet vendes med de vandrette nettråde udvendigt er der ikke plads til at ilægge kantarmeringsjern
Hvis elementerne ikke kan opdeles som vist herover kan man 
benytte samlingen som vist her til venstre. Der kræves dog ekstra 
omhyggelighed ved udstøbningen. 
Bemærk desuden at bærevnen for støbesamlingen er væsentligt 
reduceret i forhold til de andre T-samlinger.
