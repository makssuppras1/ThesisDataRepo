Abstract
Metal support interactions are a vital tool for tuning certain cat-
alytic processes. Among these interactions are ones that result in
morphological changes of nanoparticles (NPs). In the case of the
oxygen reduction reaction, this may be utilized to change Pt NPs,
yielding favorable catalytic properties.
This project has concerned itself with simulating morphologi-
cal changes TiO2 supported Pt NPs by employing state-of-the-art
equivariant graph neural networks. The overarching project goal
was to study and develop the feasibility of such simulations, from
creating a data set via density functional theory (DFT) calcula-
tions to conducting and analyzing the simulations.
In this process, the two equivariant neural networks, NequIP
and Allegro, have been compared. NequIP was far superior for
this task if the parallel implication of Allegro was not utilized.
Further, it has been found that consecutive DFT molecular dy-
namics steps are an insufficient method for generating data, as it
is both computationally expensive and yields a significant amount
of redundant data. A query-by-committee approach, which may
be deployed to gauge the simulation error regardless of system
size, has been implemented and demonstrated to be helpful in
evaluating said error. Simultaneously this approach can highlight
specific areas of the simulations associated with high error. Sim-
ulations and data treatment have been displayed from which the
nearest neighbor bond distance of constituent NP atoms can be
found. However, the results obtained from these simulations fail
to recreate experimental results.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project has been concerned with simulating catalytic plat-
inum (Pt) nanoparticles (NP) located on top of titanium dioxide
(TiO2). Section 1.1 provides a broad introduction to this topic,
the purpose and relevance of such simulations, and the simulation
method chosen in this project. Section 1.2 contains the concrete
project aims and 1.3 an outline of the remaining chapters and the
general structure of this report.
1.1
Context and Motivation
Chemical processes play a central role in modern society, with
applications ranging from ammonia synthesis [1] to the conversion
of toxic gases of vehicle exhaust [2]. Control and optimization of
such chemistry are often provided by a catalyst. A catalyst is a
substance that facilitates the reaction of interest, ideally providing
the desired reaction rate and products [3, p. x-ix].
In 2009 it was estimated that 85-90% of chemical manufac-
turing processes involved a catalyst [4, preface]. Developing new,
better, and more efficient catalysts has, therefore, long been of
great interest. This interest has only increased in recent decades
due to the need for engineering new green processes as alternatives
to older polluting and greenhouse gas-producing ones [3, p. 1-2].
Catalysis is often carried out with the catalyst being a metal in
a solid state and the reactants and products in a liquid or gaseous
phase. Employing a catalyst in a different phase than the reac-
tants and products is termed heterogeneous catalysis. Having the
catalyst in a solid phase in heterogeneous catalysis has the de-
sirable property that the products are easily separated from the
catalyst. This is a prime reason why heterogeneous catalysis is a
preferred method in industry [3, p. 3], being applied in 80-85%
of all chemical manufacturing processes involving a catalyst [4,
preface].
In heterogeneous catalysis, the chemical reactions occur at the
catalyst’s surface. This, in turn, means that having a catalyst with
a large surface area is favorable. In some cases, the catalyst mate-
rial may be made to be highly porous. In other instances, catalytic
NPs, of sizes 2-20 nm are distributed on a support material [3, p.
3]. Figure 1.1 shows a cartoonish sketch of this.

1.1.1
Metal Support Interactions
The support material may be inert, not partaking in the chemical
reaction, and hence, only serve to stabilize the NPs.
However,
for metallic NPs, so-called metal-support interactions (MSI) may
occur. As laid out in the review article by Tom van Deelen et al.
[5], these MSIs can significantly affect the properties of the NPs.
Notably, the catalytic properties of the NPs may change favorably
due to certain MSI. As discussed in the review article, these MSI
phenomena are typically grouped into the following categories:
Interfacial perimeter:
the interface of the NP-support may
yield unique chemically active sites, as the reactants can be in
contact with both materials simultaneously (e.g., see [6]). This
may additionally be aided by local rearrangement of the NP and
support due to their mutual interaction.
Particle morphology:
the adhesion energy of an NP-support
interface can affect the entire morphology and general configura-
tion of the NP. These effects include NP size, shape, which respec-
tive NP crystal facets are exposed, and in what proportions.
Chemical composition:
exchange of atomic species between
the support and NP can occur. This could, e.g., be the oxidation
of a metal NP due to an oxide support. This is often undesirable
as the oxide may occupy active sites on the NP.
Strong metal-support interaction (SMSI):
total or partial
coverage of the NP by the support material may occur. This can
be critical to the catalytic performance as active sites of the NP
are blocked. In other cases, this creates new environments with
the appropriate chemical conditions.
Charge transfer:
rearrangement of electrons in the vicinity of
the NP-support interface can take place. This is driven by the
differences in Fermi level between the two materials and is usually
limited to within a few atomic layers of each material.
An overview of the above MSIs is provided in Figure 1.2. These
MSIs may co-occur, and some may exert a more substantial influ-
ence than others.
What makes MSIs especially interesting, according to [5, p.
967], is that the NP metal often is decided on due to its selectivity1.
MSIs, after the fact, may offer the possibility for enhancing the
catalytic activity2. Control and tuning of MSI is, therefore, highly
desirable.

1.1.2
Catalysis and Nanoscale Modeling
More than a century ago, the best elemental metal catalysts for
ammonia syntheses (Fe and Ru) were discovered via large-scale
screening experiments [7, p. 555] (see also [8, 9]). Since then, the
scientific field of catalysis has come far, with theoretical predic-
tions enabling a higher understanding of the topic.
Introduction of Density-Functional Theory
A significant advance in the theoretical understanding of cataly-
sis has been the introduction of density functional theory (DFT),
which for two decades has enabled detailed studies of materials cat-
alytic capabilities [10]. Importantly, this includes computations of
activation energies and studies of different active sites (e.g., see [7,
10]).
Although DFT enables computations of material properties at
the atomic scale with relatively high accuracy, the computational
cost of the method dramatically scales with the number of atoms
included in the calculation. This broadly inhibits the use of DFT
for sizable systems,3 and therefore, for modeling a great deal of
MSI’s, as these systems are of nanoscale and easily include thou-
sands of atoms [12].4 In catalysis, transition metals are frequently
of interest. This means that the number of valance electrons is
often an order of magnitude greater than the number of atoms,
further exacerbating the problem of computational cost [12, 14].
A figurative illustration of the size gap between what systems are
within the realm of DFT contra the size of a nanoscale NP-support
system is provided in Figure 1.3.5
Wulff Construction
An approach for estimating the morphology of an NP is the Wulff
construction [12, 16, 17].
The Wulff construction, in principle,
assumes that the material in question is an anisotropic continuous
(non-atomistic) substance. The energy of this substance is then
minimized with respect to the surface energy of each of the NP’s
facets. This may yield a polyhedron, which gives an estimate of
the shape and ratios of the exposed NP facets.6

The Wulff construction has been used to describe morpholog-
ical changes of ZnO-supported Cu NPs (Cu/ZnO) due to changes
in the surrounding gaseous environment [18]. The ratio between
the Cu adhesion energy to the support material γ, relative to that
of a free Cu surface γ0, thus, changed as a function of the envi-
ronment. The result of this was in its extremes that either the
Cu NP made a large interface area with the support or that the
NP shape was virtually unaffected by the support material. The
principle of this phenomenon is sketched in Figure 1.4.
DFT has also been used to compute the surface energies of
different facets, which has been utilized in the Wulff construction
(e.g., [19]). From this, an atomistic NP Wulff shape may be con-
structed, allowing for estimation of the number and types of active
sites [7, p. 556-557]. Even more advanced atomistic methods have
been developed, where atomic energies and placement depend on
the configuration of its nearest neighbors [20].
With the Wulff construction, however, one may only take the
adhesion energy of the NP-support interface into account. Thus,
though the general shape of the NP is obtained, the Wulff con-
struction gives no information on the specific reconfiguration of
the atoms. Such reconfiguration may be introduced by strain ef-
fects, which can occur due to a mismatch between the lattices of
the NP and support materials and is often especially prominent
for small NPs [5, p. 957].

Machine Learning Approaches
In the recent decade, machine learning (ML) has been applied to
obtain the high accuracy of DFT methods at significantly lower
computational cost (e.g., see [21, p. 148]). One such application
is using neural networks (NN) to predict interatomic potential
energy surfaces. These methods are often referred to as ML or NN
potentials (NNPs).7 By predicting the potential energy, atomic
forces may be found, from which the dynamics of an atomic system
can be modeled.
The latest development in this field has been the pioneering of
so-called E(3)-equivariant NNPs [22, 23, 24]. These NNPs have
shown improved accuracy and only need a fraction of data applied
for previous ML methods [22, p. 7-8]. Equivariant NNPs have,
for instance, been used for studies of molecules and Li3PO4 solid
electrolytes [23].
This project has sought to explore the employment of these
state-of-the-art equivariant NNPs for modeling NP morphology
changes caused by strain effects.
Specifically, the two models
NequIP [22] and Allegro [23] have been tested for this purpose.
A DFT data set has been constructed on which an equivariant
NNP could be trained. The scalability of the NNP was thus used
for the simulation of much greater systems than that possible with
DFT. Pt/TiO2 was chosen as a test case for this purpose. An ex-
ample of a system simulated during this project is observed on the
right of Figure 1.3.

1.2
Aims and objectives
The overarching goal of this project has been to investigate the ap-
plication of equivariant NNPs for simulating morphology changes
of Pt/TiO2 NPs due to MSIs.
The key interest has been ex-
ploratory, with a focus on examining ideas and different ap-
proaches with the end goal of obtaining relevant and accurate
simulations. The overall points of interest are summarized below:
1. Optimize machine-learning models:
optimize, evalu-
ate, and possibly compare NequIP and/or Allegro for simu-
lating the NP-support systems.
2. Data-set generation:
explore methods for generating
diverse data sets computationally efficiently.
Investigate
if some types of data are more relevant than others and
whether the models generalize well beyond certain aspects
of the training data.
3. Identify data-set inefficiencies:
employ effective meth-
ods to improve and expand the data set to remedy situations
where the model behaves nonphysically or inaccurately.
4. Simulate NP-support:
simulate a Pt NP on a TiO2
support and evaluate the physicality of this simulation. Fur-
thermore, extend the simulations to other types of supports
and use these to compare the different effects of the support
materials.
5. Error assessment:
identify a valid approach to quantify
or evaluate the error/uncertainty when simulating systems
much larger than the ones achievable with DFT.
These points served as a guideline for this project and were not
necessarily sought to be answered in full.
The above goals are the ones stated in the original project
plan,11 which is attached at the end of this report. An evaluation
of the project plan and the course of the project is provided in
Appendix J.

2.3.2
Langevin Dynamics
In Langevin Dynamics, each particle interacts with a heat bath
at a specified temperature. Here it is introduced by following the
examples of [41, 42].
Langevin MD will converge to the thermal equilibrium of the
heat bath and thus represent a canonical ensemble with a constant
number of particles, volume, and temperature (constant N, V , T).
In practice, the temperature will fluctuate around the specified
temperature of the heat bath. However, given a large number of
particles, these fluctuations will be small or zero in the limit of
infinite particles.

3.1.1
Performance Measure and Loss Function
DFT yields the total energy and atomic forces of a system. Hence,
a decision of which of these (or which combination of these) should
be used for determining the model performance had to be made.
Because the atomic forces will determine the reconfiguration of the
NP-support system, it was decided that the model’s accuracy in
predicting the forces would be the measure used for the selection
of hyperparameters.3
Performance Measure for Selection of Hyperparameters
Although the loss function of NequIP and Allegro is defined via
the respective MSE (see Equation (2.5)), different error measures
were considered as the basis for choosing hyperparameters. Among
these were chiefly the mean absolute error (MAE), the root mean
square error (RMSE) and a weighted root mean square error
(WRMSE).
The justification of a WRMSE was founded in the fact that
there was a large variety of force magnitudes contained in the data
set. This is observed in figure 3.1. A model yielding a relatively
small error (say 1%) on a large force would still yield a significant
contribution to an RMSE measure (or an MAE). Furthermore,
a relatively large error (like 10%) on a small force would give a
small contribution to the total of an RMSE. Weighting these errors
according to the size of the true force was thus hypothesized to
yield a more relevant error measure, as such an error measure
could not be dominated by errors on large forces. Instead, this
WRMSE would to a much greater extent take into account the
relative errors and therefore the accuracy on a broader range of
force magnitudes.
The applied WRMSE was constructed based on the following
two principles: (1) errors on forces below 0.05 eV/˚A would be
weighted by unity and (2) that errors on forces above this value
would be weighted according to their relative error. This is demon-
strated in Figure 3.2. The value of 0.05 eV/˚A was chosen due to
the fact that forces of this size or smaller can generally be consid-
ered as nearly relaxed. This WRMSE would, hence, not punish a
relatively large error on a small force (≪0.05 eV/˚A) as long as
a model would get the magnitude of the force reasonably correct.
Relative errors on forces greater than 0.05 eV/˚A would, however,
contribute equally to this error measure. The WRMSE thus, like
the RMSE, still punishes outliers greatly, but does so with respect
to the relative error on the force in question. Specifics of the ap-
plied WRMSE is provided in Appendix A.
The WRMSE was used as the measure for choosing the opti-
mal hyperparameters. However, the trend of the WRMSE gener-
ally did not deviate from that of the RMSE. Likewise, the trend
of the energy RMSE also followed that of the force RMSE (and
thus also the WRMSE). The WRMSE therefore, did not yield sig-

3.3.1

A central factor in deciding that NequIP was superior for simulat-

both the standard Allegro model from the author’s GitHub and
the version optimized in this work. Hence, Allegro would have
to be greatly downsized (resulting in worsened model accuracy)

enough to be of any interest.

3.3.3
Applied Optimization Methods
The applied three-way holdout method seems to be a valid ap-
proach given that the test and validation errors are fairly similar
(Table 3.3).
Hence, the assumption that the data set is large
enough for the errors to be independent of the specific data split
seems reasonably justified.
This is a quite satisfactory result,
as a more advanced method would have been extremely time-
consuming with respect to the hyperparameter optimization.
If an even more accurate comparison of the optimized NequIP
and Allegro algorithms was wanted, Thomas Dietterich’s 5x2-Fold
Cross-Validation (5x2CV) method could have been invoked [53].
This method seeks to address the fact that some variance is in-
troduced in the trained models due to the specific data set split.
This is done via 2-fold cross-validation10 (50% training and 50%
testing) step, which is repeated five times with different train-
ing/test splits. Using such a method, the model variance could, to
a great extent, be eliminated from the generalization errors pre-
sented in table 3.3 [46, p. 42-48] (and even quantized). Although

used for the remainder of the project.

4.1
Computational Method
DFT was used for computing the energy and forces of all atomic
configurations included in the data sets. These calculations were
carried out via the application of GPAW, a DFT code based on
ASE. Specifically, version 22.8.1b1 of GPAW was used along with
the version of ASE found in Table 3.1.
4.1.1
Exchange correlation functional
The Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) XC functional [56] was se-
lected as a middle ground between computational efficiency and
accuracy. PBE has generally become regarded as a default choice
and has a reasonable accuracy on metals and molecules.
The deficiencies of PBE concerning van der Walls interactions
are not a concern for this project, as chemical bonding must occur
in the NP-support system.
Oxides, however, such as the TiO2
considered here, are not necessarily trivially treated within DFT
and with PBE. This is because these structures are among those
where the self-interaction error (SIE) may be significant [16, p.
3]. The SIE arises due to shortcomings in the XC functional to
cancel out the self-Coulomb and self-XC interactions for all one
electron orbitals (see, e.g., [57]). This is known to cause overly
narrow band gaps, incorrect dissociation energies in molecules,
and problems for systems with localized d and f electrons ([16, p.
3] and [58, p. 2057]). Some methods, such as DFT+U, strides to
address these issues. DFT+U is a semi-empirical approach with

4.1.3
Avoiding and Correcting Dipole Moments
Large dipole moments can be a cause for concern in DFT and
when having periodic boundary conditions. In the case of the NP-
support system considered in this work, the TiO2 could give rise to
such problems. As oxygen may assume a negative oxidation state
(and Ti a positive), this can give rise to a dipole moment across
the slab of TiO2 if there is an imbalance in the amount of O and
Ti near the two exposed surfaces.2 Therefore, as a general rule,
all configurations were constructed such that the stoichiometry of
TiO2 was kept intact. Otherwise, it was ensured that each of the
two exposed surfaces of the slab contained an equal amount of
oxygen (in cases where oxygen was removed or added).
Additionally, GPAWs PoissonSolver was specified to make a
correct correction to the potential. This correction removes the
gradient of the potential in the vacuum near the edge of the unit
cell (here, in the z-direction). This does not eliminate the above-
mentioned problem, but it is a cheap correction. It was applied in
all computations periodic in the x-y-directions but non-periodic in
the z-direction.3 For details on the PoissonSolvers dipole correc-
tions, see [60].
4.1.4
Density Mixing
Due to encountered convergence issues, a less aggressive density
mixing than the default used by GPAW was applied for some of
the later DFT calculations. This was done as it is a recommended
solution in the GPAW documentation [61]. Specifically, the den-
sity mixing was changed to a Pulay mixing (also used per default)
with a linear mixing coefficient of 0.05, five previous densities in-
cluded, and a weighting of 100 with respect to density changes of
long vs. short wavelengths. The GPAW documentation should be
consulted for specifics on the mixer: [62, 63].

4.2.2
Surface Composition and Chemistry
As mentioned above, the nature of these surfaces depends on the
environment in which they are situated. This environment effect
which and to what extent these surfaces are present. A further
complication is that the precise atomic composition of a surface
also depends on the environment. E.g., these surfaces may absorb
hydrogen, additional oxygen, or release oxygen as a consequence
of the environment [64]. These cases would most likely change the
observed MSIs. Therefore, detailed knowledge of the surface com-
position is most likely necessary to reproduce experimental results
such as those displayed in Table 1.1. DFT calculations of the cho-
sen surfaces, where the amount of oxygen was varied (removing
and adding from the ideal surfaces), were accordingly carried out.
The results of these calculations are found in Appendix D. How-
ever, making specific assumptions concerning the chemical envi-
ronment was deemed to be beyond the project’s scope. Therefore
this was dropped, and instead, the ideal surfaces obtained from
the bulk TiO2 unit cells were decided on for use in the simulations
(those observed in Figure 4.3).

4.3
Ensemble Method and Active Learning
During the project, it was decided to apply an ensemble method
for carrying out simulations. The reason behind this decision was
twofold. Firstly, an ensemble method improves accuracy (see, e.g.,
[21, p. 148]), although at a computational cost increase equal to
the number of NNPs in the ensemble. The second reason was that
it allowed for gauging the simulation errors.
This last point is
significant as it is a way of getting a quantitative error measure
of the simulations - where there is no other straightforward way
of obtaining it (as DFT can not be carried out on these large sys-
tems). In addition to this simulation error measure, the ensemble
approach allowed for AL to be carried out.
The ensembles employed in this work were constructed by the
same approach as [21] with a five-fold cross-validation split of the
training data (yielding five NNPs in an ensemble) and with dif-
ferent seeding of the weight’s initialization for each model. The
hyperparameters found via the optimization on the Au/TiO2 data
set were also applied for these models, apart from the rmax value,
for which an optimum value was instead found to be 5.5 ˚A (see
Appendix F).
To apply this ensemble method, a range of ASE calculators
was implemented. These were dubbed: the NequIP parallelized
active learning and simulations (PALS) suite. PALS is available at
GitHub [67] and consists of a range of calculators for simulations
and AL purposes.
Among these are a version implemented for
parallel computations on CPUs and an experimental version for
parallel GPU computations.
The following sections introduce the applied ensemble ap-
proach, AL, and some insights the ensemble error measure gave
into the problem of simulating the NP-support system.

4.6
Results
This Section seeks to showcase how NNPs performed on the vali-
dation data when trained on different training sets. The first NNP
deployed using some of the above data sets was a baseline. This
baseline was trained on GEN1 and served both to test out simula-
tions early on and as a performance reference for all future NNPs.
Based on the conclusions drawn in Chapter 3, it was decided to
train this baseline for a 1,000 epochs, as it seemed that increased
training time equaled increased performance (see Table 3.3).
Subsequently, ensembles were trained on all data sets.
The
NNPs of the GEN1 ensemble were trained for 250 epochs to reduce
the training time to a manageable level (250 epochs yielded a 16-20
hour training time for each NNP). As all other data sets contained
a different amount of configurations, the number of epochs was
adjusted accordingly for other NNPs to keep the number of weight
updates close to constant (across all data sets). This way, it was
ensured that any performance differences could be ascribed to the
training data rather than a differing training time.
Lastly, to evaluate the amount of information contained in
GEN1, it was subsampled for a number of the training sets on
which ensembles were trained.
The data in these subsets were
chosen randomly from GEN1. Ensembles trained on these subsets
will be referred to as, e.g., GEN1:20%, meaning that the ensembles
were only trained on 20% of the GEN1 data set. This was also done
for some GEN3 models, where the amount of GEN1 configurations
was reduced while still including the additions made for GEN2 and
GEN3, respectively. These GEN3 ensembles, e.g., will be referred
to as GEN3(GEN1:20%), meaning that only 20% of the GEN1
data set was applied.

Discussion
4.7.1
Problems of the GEN1 Data Set
Considering Figures 4.15 and 4.18, it is clear that much of the
GEN1 data set is redundant. An ensemble trained on 20% ran-
domly sampled from GEN1 yields results similar to one trained
on the complete data set when compared on the Large-val valida-
tion set.13 It, thus, seems that only every fourth MD contributes
knowledge of any novelty to the data set. Therefore, generating
data by running MD via DFT computations is a highly insufficient
way to construct a data set.
A different way of generating data is, of course, the demon-
strated AL method. However, some initial data is needed to train
an ensemble before an approach such as AL can be commenced.
One way could be to still compute every MD step with DFT but to

Alternatively, given several configurations, Gaussian noise could
be added to the atomic positions. As these configurations would
be uncorrelated, they would yield somewhat novel information to
the data set (if not too many were created from the same initial-
ization). However, depending on the amount of noise added, such
configurations would risk being unphysical or relatively extreme.

4.7.2
Data Set Additions and Generation
As observed from the results section, the AL additions to the data
set (GEN4-GEN6) did not improve it hugely (if at all). This may
be due to selecting the configurations with the maximum σF val-
ues rather than those with the highest mean σF , although this is
unclear.
Interestingly, however, when evaluating the Large-extended
validation data, the ensembles trained on these latest generations
of data sets all reign in the worst errors. This shows some evidence
of these ensembles being able to generalize somewhat better, al-
though it is only a few data points for which this is shown. The
SD-val set, in some respects, shows a similar pattern of improve-
ment for the latest generations of data sets. This is, however, only
if the most extreme percentiles are excluded.
The most significant improvements are seen for the GEN2 and
GEN3 additions, with the GEN3 ensembles performing best on
several validation sets. These additions mainly consisted of new
types of configurations (with a unique number of atoms). Hence,
it seems that this sort of data variety has yielded the most consid-
erable improvements. Presenting systems with a differing number
of atoms to an NNP during training forces it to adapt to situations
where the number of messages received varies. E.g., considering
a reasonably well-behaved system, a Ti atom may always receive
messages from four O atoms. During an extended simulation, a
situation may occur where one O atom drifts sufficiently far away
such that it is beyond the rmax, and messages cease to be ex-
changed between this O atom and the Ti. If an NNP has not been
trained on such a configuration, the outcome of this situation could
be random, as it has not been forced to generalize to this situa-
tion. Introducing systems with a different number of atoms could,
perhaps more easily, yield novel information than MD simulations,
where most atoms should stay near their equilibrium positions.
As mentioned, it was eventually decided to run AL from data
already existing in the training set to weed out systems prone to
convergence problems. Therefore, this implementation of AL did
54
==PAGE:69==

not allow for the addition of wholly new systems to the data set.
However, creating new initial systems for the data set could have
been combined with AL. Given some algorithm that could create
new or altered systems by removing or adding atoms, AL could
have been allowed to select the most novel of such configurations.
This could then have been combined with the used approach of
first executing DFT computations of the novel systems with the
highest disagreement. MD AL could be run after discarding the
configurations that encountered convergence problems.
Some literature suggests that multiple approaches should be
applied to construct a diverse data set. Justin S. Smith et al. [21,
p. 148] suggested running AL MD while heating the systems of in-
terest, thus, slowly creating more extreme systems to sample from.
This MD summation would be stopped once a threshold disagree-
ment had been reached. In the case of their application of molec-
ular systems, they also test new dimer configurations via AL. In
an article published this year, a new method named Uncertainty-
driven dynamics was also suggested [44]. Here a fictitious force
is introduced, pushing the MD toward the highest ensemble dis-
agreement. These methods could also be interesting to apply if
the topic of this work is investigated further.
It could also be interesting to supplement the many smaller
systems of the data sets with ones at the limit of what DFT allows
to investigate if this reduced the disagreement, e.g., on the SD-val
validation set. During this project, some DFT calculations of such
systems were carried out. Here it was found that the calculations
were limited by memory at ∼800 atoms, with 1.14 TB of RAM
available.
4.7.3
Specificity vs. Generality
Having gone over some considerations concerning obtaining data
diversity, it may also be worth considering what types of data va-
riety are desirable. As observed in Figure 4.15 and 4.16, the error
on the GEN1-val set increases each time more (and different) data
is added to the training set. Hence, some performance is sacri-
ficed on this data type to gain generality on other systems each
time different data is added to the training set. Therefore, it may
be worth considering training an ensemble that only encounters
anatase (and not rutile) to obtain greater performance for these
specific systems. Data variety could still be obtained for such a
data set by removing atoms randomly from anatase and the NP,
running MD, or similar. It seems extreme that anatase would shift
to a rutile crystal structure. Therefore it may be valid to give up
the generality of an ensemble here (if it is only used to simulate
anatase systems).
Similar arguments could be made concerning the exposed
facets, where one could construct an ensemble for specific sur-

4.7.4
Validation Sets
The fact that DFT can not be applied to large systems, which
are of interest for simulations, makes it challenging to construct
a validation set that resembles these and, thus, to make a gen-
uinely relevant validation set. The Large-val and Large-extended
validation sets are attempts to replicate these large systems. How-
ever, the difference between these validation sets and the systems
which have been simulated is significant. Also, evaluating data
set improvements based on these validation sets will always result
in selecting data that resembles and improves the accuracy on the
validation set the most rather than the massive systems of interest.
The SD-val set is an attempt at solving this problem, as it
is constructed from systems that have been simulated.
Never-
theless, a validation set like this naturally comes with the added
complexity of interpreting the ensemble disagreement rather than
the tangible error of the DFT sets. As observed in Section 4.3.1
and Appendix G, high disagreement generally coincides with a
higher error. The caveat is that some force has high disagreement
but a low error and vice versa. The way this has been sought to be
addressed in Figure 4.21 was that the percentiles were averaged
across the 300 systems, although this may very well not be the
best way of addressing this problem. Indeed, selecting the worst
case of each of the 300 systems may just mean selecting observa-
tions where disagreement is out of proportion with respect to the
error.
Furthermore, as the later generations of data sets have some
reasonably unique systems, this may naturally lead to an overall
higher disagreement as some NNPs have seen systems wildly dif-
ferent from that of the others and may therefore have picked up
on different trends (for an ensemble trained on GEN1, this should
not be a concern). This could possibly be addressed by training
the constituent NNPs on all the data. Considering the consistency
of the disagreement vs. error across the different ensembles could
also be interesting to investigate further.
The SD-val set was made by adding Gaussian noise to the ini-
tial systems used in the simulations. This may not be the ideal
system for evaluating disagreement. One critical point could be
that the distance between the NP and the support is based on a
guess. Hence, the distance between interface atoms may signifi-
cantly differ from what actually transpires in simulations. Also,
Gaussian noise must, in some cases, lead to rather extreme con-

Constructing this validation set differently or just considering the
disagreement of the simulations which are carried out may be a
better approach.
For future work, it would probably be best to have a carefully
thought-out DFT validating (more so than any presented in this
work) and a disagreement set containing systems of the type and
size which are of interest. The DFT validation set should prob-

Some evidence has been found of the importance of including a
large variety of systems with unique amounts of atoms rather than
including MD simulations which have led to colossal redundancy.
An AL method has been introduced and applied, but it is unclear
whether it leads to significantly more relevant systems to include
in the data set. Some results suggest that the additions made via
AL have led to a more general model, reigning in some of the worst
errors (see Figure 4.20).
Concerning identifying data set inefficiencies, it is clear that
systems on which the NNPs perform poorly can be detected via

5.5.2
Simulation and Ensemble Accuracy
Another significant aspect to consider is the accuracy of the uti-
lized ensemble. As observed from Figure 5.14 and 5.15, the en-
semble has relatively little trouble with the vast majority of the
atomic forces, but struggles in and on the periphery of the NP-
support interface. As Figure 5.14 shows, the 95’th percentile of
disagreement lies within the range of an σF of 0.1-0.2 eV/˚A. This
corresponds to ∼650 atoms having a disagreement larger than this
associated, of which some most likely have a fairly high error (see
Figure 4.6 and Appendix G). As a great deal of these atoms are
situated in the interface, this must yield a decreased accuracy of
the simulation results. As the investigations of the relationship
between disagreement and error have been somewhat limited in
this work, it is difficult to determine how significant these errors
are.
More studies are, therefore, needed in order to gain more
knowledge of this.
Interestingly, the disagreements of the simulations are far
smaller than those found via the SD-val validation set in Figure
4.21. This raises questions about the relevance of this validation
set, as it seems to be more extreme than the simulations. More
care should probably be exercised when constructing such a val-
idation set.
Alternatively, the disagreement on the simulations
themselves could be considered instead.

agrees may be found via AL. Regardless, it is more challenging to
find configurations that, when included in the training data, actu-
ally improve the performance of the systems of interest. Cutting
out parts of the simulated systems may be a way of circumvent-
ing this problem. Considering Figure 5.15, parts of the systems

The size of the simulated systems has been limited by the avail-

may be worth considering applying the CPU nodes of Niflheim, as
these have more than an order of magnitude more RAM available.
If larger systems were of interest to simulate, these could be em-
ployed. However, this would come with increased computational
time requirements due to switching to using CPUs instead of a
GPU. As discussed in Section 3.3.1, a parallel implementation of
Allegro could be used, but this would be a comparatively involved
solution.

Chapter 6
Overall Conclusions
Concerning the aims of the project, stated in Section 1.2, the
following conclusions can be drawn:
1. Regarding the models NequIP and Allegro, in Chapter 3,
it was found that large systems are easier simulated with
NequIP rather than Allegro. This is mainly because Allegro
demands more computational resources than NequIP. This
seems partially to be because of the local nature of Allegro
vs. NequIP’s message-passing implementation, as the latter
acts as a pooling mechanism. Therefore for this project’s
use case, Allegro’s memory demands far exceeded NequIP’s,
which is why NequIP was utilized in this project.
These
disadvantages of Allegro could, to a large extent, possibly
be offset if implemented in parallel across multiple devices.
This, however, is not a trivial task.
2. Concerning data generation, Chapter 4 found that conduct-
ing MD simulations via a computationally expensive method
like DFT is a highly inefficient approach. This is primarily
because little to no information is gained between immedi-
ate simulation steps. Based on this project, it is, therefore,
recommended only to compute some MD steps with high
accuracy and relax the computational load in between (pos-
sibly by application of an NNP). Otherwise, noise could be
added to atomic positions to achieve novel configurations.
Additionally, some results indicate that it is more important
to include data that not only varies spatially (as in the case
of MD) but varies with respect to the type of systems and
number of atoms.
3. In section 2.4 and 4.3, QBC approaches were introduced,
with which it can be determined if models behave inaccu-
rately. The combination of this QBC method, either with
MD or a search of new systems, can be used to remedy the
NNP in situations where it has insufficient knowledge (e.i.
AL). The fact that only NNPs are employed for discover-
ing novel systems results in a relatively low-cost method for
achieving high data set diversity. However, it has been found
that it is not trivial to determine if these systems help the
NNPs generalize to the large and fairly dissimilar systems
75
==PAGE:90==
of interest for the simulations. In other words, the fact that
the data set is remedied in one area does not automatically
yield information in another.
4. Chapter 5 details simulations of Pt/TiO2 systems that have
been carried out, and a method for analyzing the simulation
data has been presented. Regardless, the simulations failed
to recreate experiments where a contraction of the Pt bonds
is observed. In the simulations, a slight expansion of the NPs
takes place. This discrepancy likely has many causes. For
one thing, it was not demonstrated that the used support
systems were large enough to emulate a physical system.
Further knowledge of the precise TiO2 surfaces is also es-
sential to get closer to replication of the experiments. The
deployed ensemble presumably also has insufficient accu-
racy.
This is especially a problem as it struggles at the
NP-support interface. Lastly, applying a more sophisticated
DFT method than the PBE XC-functional might also be
necessary to account for the SIEs embedded in the data sets.
5. The QBC method may, regardless of system size, be used
to gauge the accuracy of simulations. This is done by mea-
suring the disagreement of the ensemble (the committee).
Furthermore, this can be done on a per atoms basis, allow-
ing for gauging the accuracy of each atomic force. This is
introduced in Section 4.3.1 and demonstrated in Figure 4.5
and 5.15. However, further investigations are needed to de-
termine how reliable this disagreement measure is.

Chapter 7
Suggestions for Future Work
7.1
Straightforward Extensions
Due to the project’s time constraints, some relatively easy exten-
sions were not followed through. Two of these are listed below:
1. Optimization of the NNPs concerning the Pt/TiO2 data set
could be carried out relatively easily. Furthermore, the latest
data set generation could be employed to discover if this is
superior.
As discussed in Section 4.7.5, the optimization
could probably be carried out only for the training time and
still yield an improvement over the used ensemble (see also
Appendix H).
2. Having obtained a new ensemble from the above optimiza-
tion, the code already developed for initializing the NP with
different rotations and translations relative to the surface
could be deployed and utilized in simulations. The disagree-
ment of these new simulations could then be compared with
that obtained for GEN3(GEN1:20%) in Chapter 5.
7.2
Future Research
Some of the most relevant avenues for future research are listed
below:
1. It would be highly interesting to investigate if reasonable
systems could be obtained and included in the training data
by cutting out the areas with the most significant disagree-
ment of the simulations. This seems promising for getting
relevant information on the simulated systems.
2. It would be helpful to obtain a better understanding of the
relationship between disagreement and error.
This would
allow for determining the validity of the results.
3. To determine if the application of NNPs for simulating MSIs
is truly successful, it would be relevant to replicate a specific
experimental result. To do this, highly detailed knowledge
of the surface and NPs would be necessary. Hence, receiving
this information and replication of this in simulations would
be highly relevant.

A.2
Choice of Weights
Given the WRMSE above, an appropriate choice of wi is needed.
For the use intended in this work, two things have been considered:
1. On large forces, the absolute error (or RMSE) is not neces-
sarily a relevant measure, i.e., an error of 1 eV/˚A may be
reasonably accurate on a force of size 100 eV/˚A. The weights
wi should, therefore, scale the error measure on large forces
such that it is relative to their size.
2. The relative error on small forces is irrelevant as long as the
model can safely identify these forces as small. I.e., an error
of 0.01 eV/˚A on a force of 0.01 eV/˚A may be reasonable
as forces of this magnitude will not significantly affect the
dynamics of the simulations.
The weights are, therefore, chosen to be a function of the mag-
nitude of the DFT force: wi(|Fi|). For small forces below a chosen
threshold Ft, the error measure shouldn’t be increased inversely to
the size of the DFT force (point 2 above). Hence, these weights are
set to be equal: wi(|Fi| ≤|Ft|) = 1. Now, to attenuate the relative
importance of the error on forces larger than |Ft| (point 1 above),

Introduction and Motivation
Heterogeneous catalysis (catalysis where the reactant and prod-
ucts are in a different phase than the catalyst) is a vital part of
many chemical processes and one of the preferred methods in in-
dustry [1, p. 3]. A heterogeneous catalyst is often a nanoparticle,
and a key factor influencing its reactivity is the surface geometry.
A simple example of this is illustrated in Figure 1.
The geometry of a nanoparticle may be affected by its support
(the material which it is located on), which is why the choice of
support material is an active field of research. The effect of the
support on the nanoparticle, however, is difficult to model with
accurate methods like density functional theory (DFT), due to the
huge amount of atoms which need to be included in the simulation,
as this makes the computational cost insurmountable.
The lattice distance of platinum (Pt) (111) facets is of interest
in several reactions, one of which is the oxygen reduction reac-
tion [2], [3]. Here the Pt-Pt distance is found to be important in
relation to the reactivity. Furthermore, the Pt-Pt distance may
change depending on the support hence the choice of support can
be a central part of designing an effective catalyst.
The overarching goal of this project is therefore, to model Pt
nanoparticles on supports by employing the state-of-the-art E(3)-
equivariant machine-learning inter-atomic potentials of NequIP
[4] and/or Allegro [5].
A DFT data-set of relatively small
nanoparticle-support systems will be created to train the mod-
els. The aim is to generalize these DFT computations to a large
system via the machine-learning potentials.
Objectives
The overall objectives of this project are listed below:
1. Data-set generation:
explore methods for generating
diverse data-sets in a computationally efficient manner. In-
vestigate if some types of data are more relevant than others
and whether the models generalize well beyond certain as-
pects of the training data.
2. Identify data-set inefficiencies:
employ effective meth-
ods to improve and expand the data-set to remedy situations
where the model behaves nonphysically or inaccurately.
