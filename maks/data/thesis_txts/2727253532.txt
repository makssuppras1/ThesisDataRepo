Abstract
Monitoring of water storage in cities is key in the field of climate change adaptation wherein
green infrastructure is an increasingly prevalent approach. To assess and evaluate water stor­
age from such solutions, evapotranspiration (ET) is a crucial component. Monitoring of ET
can be obtained from the use of satellite data, which among others provides land surface tem­
perature (LST) data derived from thermal infrared (TIR) sensors. However, to model ET in
heterogeneous areas such as cities high­resolution LST is required, which cannot be obtained
directly from the existing satellite missions. Thus various techniques have been applied to
downscale coarse resolution LST data.
In this thesis the Data Mining Sharpener (DMS) provided by DHI was used to obtain LST in a
20 m resolution, using spectral reflectance data from Sentinel 2 and TIR data from Sentinel 3.
The impact of different model alterations was investigated to test the potential of enhancing
downscaling within Copenhagen, using Landsat 8/9 for validation. To assess the impact of
using the downscaled LST to model ET in an urban area, downscaled LST was applied in a
Two Source Energy Balance ­ Priestley Taylor (TSEB­PT) model.
The alterations applied to the DMS model that generated the highest correlation with Landsat,
were seen when using a step­by­step downscaling method, while including additional urban
features as high­resolution input. Of the tested urban features a combination of horizon and
imperviousness data showed the best results. This caused an improvement of the correlation
within Copenhagen, from 0.46­0.67, 0.49­0.66, 0.52­0.58 for the data sets derived from April,
June and August respectively. ET results for three areas in Copenhagen, derived from the
adapted DMS model, showed a change in ET after the implementation of rainbeds. Changes
in the ET/Precipitation ratio for 2019 and 2022 also indicated enhanced water storage capacity
due to the rainbeds. Though these results show a promising method for determining the impact
of rainbeds, further validation of the results is needed before the method should be used for
monitoring the impact of rainbeds.

1
Introduction
Urban water storage is a field of increasing importance. Climate change impacts and the
growth of cities make urban areas more prone to flooding with extreme rain events becom­
ing more frequent. As a counteract, solutions to urban flooding have become an area of
high interest [1]. Urban heat island is also a known issue in urban areas, where the change
in land cover affects the exchange of energy fluxes between the surface and atmosphere.
This gives rise to an increase in temperature in urban areas compared to their surroundings,
affecting the local climate and causing intense summer rainfalls [2]. Green infrastructure
is an effective way to mitigate these problems. In order to assess the impact of different
nature based solutions (NBS)’s, knowledge of water storage capacity is paramount, of which
evapotranspiration (ET) has a key impact [1].
Different ET models have been developed for monitoring agricultural areas in order to obtain
the most beneficial use of water [3], but ET monitoring in urban areas is still a field with a
big knowledge gap. Some studies have already shown promising results in the estimation
of ET and soil moisture in urban areas using remote sensing, machine learning and fluxes,
though validation of these results is still lacking [4, 5, 6]. In Vulova et al. [6] an approach of
mapping ET in a city using remote sensing, machine learning and flux footprint modelling was
applied. It showed confident results using a random forest algorithm with ET measurements
from two flux towers and remote sensing data to spatially distributed data for an entire city
to generate high temporal and spatial resolution ET maps. However, in cities with no flux
towers available, different approaches are necessary to model ET.
A key parameter to estimate ET is the land surface temperature (LST). A cost and time­
efficient way to obtain LST is from satellite data using thermal infrared (TIR) remote sensing.
However, for satellite­derived LST to fulfil its potential in environmental models, it requires
both high temporal and spatial resolutions. With the current satellite missions there is a clear
gap in data meeting those requirements, due to the trade­off between high spatial and temporal
resolutions [7, 8].
The temporal resolution of the current available TIR data ranges from 35 days to 15 minutes,
while the spatial resolution varies from 60 to 8000 m. The highest spatial resolution is ob­
tained from Landsat 7 (60 m) which instead has a revisit time of 16 days. Opposite offers
Sentinel 3 a revisit time of only one day though a spatial resolution of 1 km [8].
Many downscaling techniques have been developed to downscale coarse resolution LST to
obtain a higher resolution, using high­resolution visible near­infrared (VIS­NIR) data from
other satellites. Such methods have shown sound results in agricultural areas, though with
certain limitations [9, 10, 11].
The most used methods for downscaling are under the Kernel­driven processes which include
classic­based, regression­based, machine­learning­based and TIR spectral unmixing­based
processes [8]. The most common procedures in the Kernel­driven processes are defined by
two steps. At first, a relation between the coarse and high­resolution data/scaling factor is
found, and then it is applied to the high­resolution VIS­NIR data, to obtain high­resolution
LST [12]. The scaling factors which are most commonly used for downscaling include spec­
tral bands, spectral indices, albedo, emissivity and digital elevation models [8].

Downscaling of LST from cities is yet more challenging due to high temperature­ and re­
flectance variations. Nevertheless, suggestions to overcome this have been proposed showing
promising results [13, 14, 4]. Zhou et al. [13] showed improvement in downscaling of LST
in cities, by including urban features as additional high­resolution input data to the model
while applying the downscaling algorithm step­wise using random forest, increasing the spa­
tial resolution step­by­step. Mitraka et al. [14] used another method for downscaling based
on a multi­step approach where fine resolution emissivity was used to determine the surface
cover. This was then used to improve the low­resolution thermal measurements.
In Jiang and Weng [4] downscaled LST from MODIS to Landsat was used in a Two Source
Energy Balance (TSEB) model to estimate ET and soil moisture in different urban area types.
It showed the importance of downscaled LST to estimate ET over heterogeneous surfaces.
In Guzinski et al. [3] downscaled LST derived from Sentinel 2 and Sentinel 3 satellite data
was used in a TSEB model to estimate evapotranspiration in agricultural areas in Tunisia and
southeastern Spain, showing promising results. The downscaling of LST was done using the
Data Mining Sharpener (DMS) model based on the method developed by Gao, Kustas, and
Anderson [11].
However, the TSEB using downscaled LST from the DMS model has not yet been used to
estimate ET within urban areas. Though this modelling approach has the potential to offer
the requested monitoring of urban water storage processes, it is challenged by the lack of
temperature downscaling accuracy within cities.
1.1
Research objective
This thesis aims to investigate the potential of improving the downscaling of LST in cities to
be applied as a parameter in the TSEB model. The model used for downscaling and which
will be subject to alterations is the DMS model developed by DHI. To assess the application
of the downscaled LST for urban hydrological purposes, high­resolution evapotranspiration
data obtained from the TSEB model with the downscaled LST as input will be assessed.
Thus the objective is achieved by answering the research question, and corresponding sub­
questions:
Is it possible to improve the downscaling performance of LST in urban areas, and to
what extent can it be used to estimate ET within urban areas?
• How will alterations of parameter settings within the DMS model impact the downscal­
ing performance?
• How will chosen adaptation approaches and additional high­resolution features impact
the downscaling performance?
• To what level is it possible to detect a change in ET after rainbeds have been imple­
mented, using the TSEB model?
• What impact does the quality of the downscaled LST have on the modelled ET?
To test the potential of improving the downscaling of LST in an urban area the DMS model
developed by DHI with Sentinel 2 and Sentinel 3 data as input is used. The urban area used
for the assessment is Copenhagen, while a broader area outside Copenhagen is included for
the training of the model, further details are provided in Section 3.1.

2.1
The DMS model
A wide range of downscaling methodologies have been developed and applied to fill the spa­
tial and temporal data gap, sharpening coarse resolution LST. Most studies are very area spe­
cific and therefore difficult to reuse on other sites, as the scaling factors used depend on the
area [8]. The DMS model defined by Gao, Kustas, and Anderson [11] builds upon utilising
the complete TIR and shortwave data from a given satellite, meaning the DMS determines
adaptively which high­resolution data is important for the particular area. This makes the
model highly applicable in many studies compared to other methods. In addition, it is easy to
include additional independent variables, since the DMS model does not predefine the type
of relationship between the input data, unlike e.g. spectral indices [11]. The DMS model
applied in this thesis is a publicly available Python tool developed by DHI using the DMS
method defined by Gao, Kustas, and Anderson [11] as a theoretical framework. The model
is available via Guzinski and Nieto [17]. This specific Python tool will be referred to as the
DMS model.
The description of the DMS is divided into four parts as listed below:
• Model setup: Gives a short description of the workflow of the model developed by
DHI along with a flow chart of all the processes in the model.
• Training and prediction algorithm: Contains a thorough description of the main steps
in the DMS model. Here the algorithms applied in the model and the training data are
elaborated.
• Residual analysis: Describes the final step of the DMS model after the training and
prediction are done.
• Setting options for the DMS: Yields an overview of the main settings of the model
regarding both sample inputs and regressor options.
2.1.1
Model setup
The DMS model is a machine learning­based algorithm which generates ensembles of regres­
sion trees to obtain a relationship between coarse­resolution LST data and high­resolution
spectral reflectance [12]. Initially, the high­resolution spectral data is aggregated to the same
resolution as the temperature data and subdivided using the regression trees before a linear
relationship between temperature and reflectance data is generated. The modelled relations
are then used for the prediction of high­resolution LST, from the high­resolution spectral re­
flectance. In the final step, a residual analysis is made, where the predicted high­resolution
LST is compared to the original coarse­resolution LST. Before aggregation, the temperature
is calculated to radiance to be more physically correct when upscaling [11]. As an output
the model yields a corrected high­resolution LST map and the applied residuals at coarse
resolution.

Descriptions of training and prediction algorithms of the DMS model are divided into four

Regression trees and bagging
The machine learning algorithm applied in the DMS model is based on the three modules from
the Scikit Learn libraries; the Decision Tree Regressor [18], The Bagging Regressor [19] and
the ridge regression [20].
Bagging refers to the method by which a defined number of random subsets are drawn from
a training data sample (bootstrapping) to be used as input for a chosen type of base regressor.
Predictions derived from the generated base regressors are then aggregated obtaining the final
prediction. The base regressor applied in the DMS model is the decision tree regressor. Thus
the model generates an ensemble of regression trees for each training model, drawing a ran­
dom sample subset with replacement. The final predictions are then aggregated by calculating
the mean of all regression tree predictions within the ensemble.
The regression trees take the spectral bands aggregated to coarse resolution as input, which is
then subdivided into multiple nodes before it ends up at a leaf node. The splitting is done such
that the pixels with similar values are grouped in the same nodes. The nodes are generated by
splits in the dataset. The splits in the regression trees are determined by the greatest decrease
in impurity.

2.2
Studies for downscaling improvement
To assess the potential of improving the downscaling performance in urban areas, alterations
to the DMS model are applied and the derived results are analysed. The alterations assessed
are based on methods within recent studies that have shown promising results. The back­
ground of the applied alterations will be presented in the following section.
2.2.1
Double residual correction
A method to potentially enhance downscaling is presented in Sánchez et al. [9]. Contrary to
the aim of this thesis which focuses on urban areas, the presented methodology was applied to
10
Downscaling of satellite­based LST measurements for ET estimation in urban areas
==PAGE:25==
agricultural areas. Nevertheless Sánchez et al. [9] provided an alteration for the downscaling
method, which was shown to positively impact the downscaling performance. They used the
work of Bisquert, Sánchez, and Caselles [22] to obtain LST maps in 10 m resolutions using
Modis and Sentinel 2 data, generating linear relations between NDVI (10 m) and LST (1000
m).
The original residual correction was found to generate a boxy­like effect on the downscaling
results. Thus Sánchez et al. [9] aimed to smoothen the derived results, by introducing an
additional procedure to perform the residual correction.
Instead of adding the residuals derived as the deviation between the downscaled and original
LST to the downscaled LST, Sánchez et al. [9] introduced an additional correction step. Here
the residuals derived from the downscaled LST were used to generate the relationship between
that and the high­resolution input data (NDVI). From this additional downscaling process a
high­resolution residual map was generated and applied as a first residual correction, before
the second aggregated residual correction is done. The performance of the downscaling was
assessed by both ground­measured LST and Landsat 7 LST. To account for discrepenacies
between MODIS and Landat LST normalization was applied. Sánchez et al. [9] reported a
20 % improvement measured on RMSD from the downscaled LST versus ground­measured
LST values, corresponding to a 0.5 ◦C decrease.
Both the downscaling model applied by Sánchez et al. [9],as well as their focus upon agri­
cultural areas, deviate from the focus of this thesis. However, since this method deals with
residual correction and not input data or alteration of the machine learning algorithms, it is as­
sumed to be applicable in the context of this thesis. The implementation of the double residual
correction in the DMS model will be further described in Section 4.3.1.
2.2.2
Step­by­step and urban features
In Zhou et al. [13] alterations of the downscaling approach in a city area were assessed, show­
ing promising results. They used random forest (with 700 n_features), with Satellite data
derived from Sentinel 2 and 3, as listed in Table 2.1, to downscale LST from 900 m to 30 m.
Zhou et al. [13] pointed towards two problems that challenge downscaling of LST in urban
areas: The span of spatial resolution and the lack of urban features in the input data.
The first mentioned challenge refers to the issue regarding the difference in resolution between
high and low­resolution inputs, which affects the accuracy of the downscaling. This is due to
the underlying assumption on which the downscaling model is based – that relations between
the temperature and high­resolution input data are constant between the two scales.
The second mentioned shortfall is the influence of urban 3D­parameters which are not being
accounted for using only satellite data. Zhou et al. [13] argue that additional types of input
data are required for the downscaling of LST in cities to be adequate, due to the heterogeneity
obtained from the morphological surface structures in cities.
To accommodate a potential solution for challenges derived from different resolution spans,
Zhou et al. [13] introduced a step­by­step method which is defined by an alteration of the
model flow. The method is constituted by applying additional downscaling runs, referred to
as steps. In each step the resolution of the downscaling data is increased. These procedures
allow the models to be trained on data with lower resolution spans, which advantages the
model to find a more accurate relationship between the LST and applied high­resolution data.
To combat the second challenge additional high­resolution data was introduced as model in­
put, containing information on urban features, e.g. building heights, sky view factor (SVF),
Downscaling of satellite­based LST measurements for ET estimation in urban areas
11
==PAGE:26==
number of floors etc. Both alterations were tested and showed promising results.
According to Zhou et al. [13] the direct downscaling underestimated temperatures in areas
with buildings while areas with lower temperatures such as vegetation and water bodies were
overestimated. These errors were diminished when using the step­by­step approach, where the
correlation between downscaled LST and Landsat LST increased by 0.21 (from 0.6 to 0.81).
The application of urban features improved the results further by increasing the correlation to
0.85.
2.3
TSEB­PT model
The modelling of ET is based on a TSEB model which is described in Guzinski et al. [7]. A
short introduction to the basics of the model will be presented in this section, including its
mathematical foundation as well as the required parameters used to derive ET.
2.3.1
Mathematical derivation of ET
Two of the major model approaches that exist within the field of remote sensing ET models:
the One­and Two Source Energy Balance models. Where a One Source Energy model treats
soil and canopy as one flux source the TSEB model distinguishes the surface energy fluxes
and radiometric temperature between that derived from canopy and soil respectively [7]. The
ET model applied in this thesis belongs to this group.
The energy used for evapotranspiration is the latent heat flux, which can be derived from the
energy balance, as expressed as Equation (2.13) where Rn is the net radiation, G is the soil
heat flux and H is the sensible heat flux, and λE is the latent heat flux.
Rn ≈G + H + λE
(2.13)
When using a TSEB model Equation (2.13) is divided into two equations, one describing the
energy balance for canopy and the other one for soil, as shown in Equations (2.14) and (2.15).
Rn,c = Hc + λEc
(2.14)
Rn,s = Hs + λEs + G
(2.15)
The net radiation is mainly influenced by incoming solar radiation and surface albedo and G
is estimated as a fraction of the net radiation from the soil at midday.
One of the main deviations between models is the derivation of the sensible heat flux. In the
TSEB–PT model, H is derived from the gradient between the soil or canopy temperature and
the air temperature, where the Priestley­ Taylor (PT) equation is used for the initial guess of
the canopy temperature (see Equation (A.1) in Appendix).
The final spatial resolution of ET is defined by the resolution of the input data, so all input
parameters need to be in the same resolution. The estimated fluxes are instantaneous and will
therefore be extrapolated such that a daily ET is found. This means that λE is in W/m2 and
found for each day at the time of the Sentinel 3 overpass, as the LST output determines this.
To get the λE for the whole day, the solar radiation is used. A constant ratio between the solar
radiation and λE is assumed, such that the solar radiation for the whole day can be used for
the extrapolation of daily values of λE. For the days where clouds or other disturbances limit
the data extent, gap filling is applied to get a daily ET of the whole area. To fill out the gaps,
reference ET for a day with data very close to the day with missing values is used to find a
12
Downscaling of satellite­based LST measurements for ET estimation in urban areas
==PAGE:27==
ratio between the reference and actual ETs. This ratio is then multiplied with the reference
ET on the day with missing data to fill out areas, resulting in a complete ET map. The final
output of ET is then given in mm/day for a grid size of 20 m x 20 m.
2.3.2
Data input for TSEB­PT
The data used to estimate ET using the TSEB­PT model can be categorised into three groups:
Meteorological data, Satellite data and ancillary data. The meteorological data is achieved
from ERA5 reanalysis dataset produced by the European Center for Medium Range Weather
Forecasts and distributed by Copernicus Climate Data Store (CDS). The satellite data comes
from Sentinel 2 and 3 and is used to achieve LST and biophysical inputs to estimate ET, while
the ancillary data comes from Copernicus Global Land Cover and Shuttle Radar Topography
Mission DEM [15].
Meteorological data
The meteorological data comes in a 30 km grid with an hourly temporal resolution. To obtain
a finer spatial resolution the DEM map is used as described in Guzinski et al. [15]. Both
instantaneous and daily parameters are derived for usage in the model. The instantaneous
parameters include air temperature, vapour pressure, wind speed, surface pressure, and clear­
sky solar irradiance. The daily include solar irradiance as well as air and dew temperatures,
wind speed, and pressure. The daily parameters are used to extrapolate and interpolate the
instantaneous estimates of ET such that the reference ET can be derived. The reference ET is
estimated using the FAO56 model [15].
Satellite data
The two Sentinel satellites are used to generate the high­resolution LST as described in Sec­
tion 2.1 above. Besides this, the Spectral bands from Sentinel 2 are also used to generate bio­
physical inputs to the model including Leaf Area Index (LAI), Fractional Vegetation Cover
(FVC), Fraction of Absorbed Photosynthetically Active Radiation (FAPAR), Canopy Chloro­
phyll Content (CCC) and Canopy Water Content (CWC) [16].
Ancillary data
The last data needed which can not be extracted from the spectral bands is found using the
Land Cover map from Copernicus. The DEM is used for atmospheric correction when extract­
ing the biophysical inputs from Sentinel 2 data and to correct the meteorological parameters
for effects due to terrain [15].

4.2.1
Parameter settings
The DMS model is developed with an agricultural focus and thus comes with initial settings
which are expected to favour more homogeneous areas. Therefore the analysis is conducted
to detect the optimal parameter settings for the area of interest.
The change in parameters is done one by one, meaning the different settings are all compared
against pre­defined default settings, referred to as ”default”. The altered parameters are:
• Definition of homogeneity threshold: As mentioned in Section 2.1.2 the homogeneity
of the data is defined by the Cv threshold, which can be altered either by setting a strict
Cv or by choosing how much percentage of the data should be used for training.
• Window size: The window size controls the number of pixels that the local models are
using for training, and therefore both a higher and lower number are tested compared
to the default to see the effect in urban areas.
• Penalty factor: The penalty refers to the rest of the non­homogeneous data, which are
either included in the model and punished further, or completely excluded (the last 20%
in the default setting).
• Leaf nodes: The leaf nodes estimate the maximum number of leaf nodes which are al­
lowed for both the local (L) and global (G) model in the regression trees.
• Bagging: Alterations to the bagging settings are referred to as either bag 1 or bag 2.
This is due to several parameters being changed simultaneously. Both the n_estimators

The last phase of the sensitivity analysis is to determine the resampling method for the Sentinel

will be chosen if the effect of the resampling is not affecting the results negatively.

4.3.1
Double residual correction
The double residual correction method derived from Sánchez et al. [9], was found to improve
the downscaling results in agricultural areas. To assess its potential within the objective of
this thesis, the method is incorporated into the DMS model. As described in Section 2.2.1,
the method is constituted by two downscaling sequences.
First, the downscaling is conducted as in the original DMS model, but without applying a
residual correction in the end. Then a second downscaling is conducted using the newly
generated residual file as low­resolution data input, so relations between the high­resolution
data and the LST residuals are obtained, producing a high­resolution residual map. This high­
resolution residual map is then used to correct the downscaled LST generated in the first run,
by adding the two high­resolution layers together. Finally, the corrected high­resolution map
is aggregated to coarse resolution and a second residual correction is applied.

Urban features
To assess the impact on downscaling performance from including high­resolution building
data in the model, four datasets each representing a different urban feature are generated and
included as bands to the high­resolution input data. The included urban features are building
heights, SVF, horizon and imperviousness.
As an estimate for building height, the number of floors is multiplied by 3 as a proxy of floor
height. The estimated heights are verified using Google Earth, assessing random heights
in different areas of the city. The estimate shows reasonable values, though a tendency to
overestimate the height in some areas. However, since the estimated heights in most areas
of the city are within an acceptable range of that of Google Earth, the estimate is considered
reasonable.
The software­tool r.null from GRASS (Geographic Resources Analysis Support System) is
used to give a value of zero to cells without buildings, in the extent of the new area. The
height data is used in the model both as an input of building height, generation of horizon (20
m resolution) and to generate the SVF (5 m resolution).
Both horizon and SVF quantify how much of the view is covered by buildings from a specific
pixel. The main difference between the two derived features is that SVF covers a 360◦view
while horizon feature only includes a 180◦view from east to west, facing south. The reasoning
behind only taking the horizon south of buildings into account is that the study area is located
in the Northern Hemisphere. The selection of specific view angles is not a setting option
within the SVF application.

experienced massive rain events, which is not the case for the weeks before the other chosen
dates. Thus precipitation could be a potential explanation for the non­clear data, see Ap­

a bit in June compared to the other three dates. Indistinct surface cover types are also found
outside Copenhagen, though the overall picture is clearer.
The downscaling with default settings shows that the correlation measure in Copenhagen is
challenged which indicates a need for improved downscaling. Before applying new data and
methods the relative impacts on performance measures derived from changes in the model
settings will be assessed in the sensitivity analysis.

Discussion
9.1
Improvement of downscaling performance
Internal model settings are found to have an impact on the results, where the correlations for
both April, June and August are shown to improve from the default settings, as presented in
Chapter 6.
Alterations of the model approach also had a high impact on the results. The double residual
correction was found to have a negative impact on the downscaling performance. Thus the
findings in this thesis do not detect the same promising results as reported by Sánchez et al.
[9].
Results obtained when using the step­by­step method improved the downscaling. It increased
the correlation and decreased both bias and RMSD compared to Sentinel 3, for all datasets
(Section 7.2 Table 7.2).
By including horizon and imperviousness as additional features to the DMS model, the corre­
lation improved even further (Section 7.4 Table 7.7), especially for June within Copenhagen.
The best results were found when combining both the step­by­step approach and including
these features, except for the correlation within June which is better when only the urban
features are adapted.
Though the performance measures improve, the detected enhancement is less apparent from
the visual results. A trait found throughout the downscaling analysis is the relation between
correlation improvement and the smoothening of derived LST results, as seen in both Sec­
tions 6.4, 7.3.1 and 7.4, with the final model showing the most smoothened results. With
the improved correlations comes a diminishing of the contrasts seen within the LST maps.
However, the temperature clusters are exactly what is seen in the Landsat data, and what was
expected to be seen if the downscaling occurred preferably.
This smoothening effect was not detected in Zhou et al. [13], thus despite the improved cor­
relation, the results presented in this thesis deviate from their findings. One explanation for
this deviation could be that Zhou et al. [13] use a different machine learning algorithm while
applying spectral indices. Even though the impact of using spectral indices has also been
tested within this thesis, it was excluded from further analysis due to poor performance and
therefore was not tested in combination with step­by­step. Furthermore, since Copenhagen
is a coastal city, the DMS model could possibly be challenged with some of the local models
being limited by a sudden coastline and therefore smaller training areas. By choosing an in­
land city, the model could maybe have been generating better results since more training data
would have been available.
The normalisation of the data was found to have an impact on the downscaling though it
was not expected due to the high­resolution data being within the same range. The highest
impact from normalisation is seen for June (Section 7.3.1 Table 7.3). This could indicate that
spectral data from June contains higher variations, which could potentially be related to the
precipitation observed within the period (mentioned in Chapter 5). As Bogrekci and Lee [36]
concludes, a higher water content in the soil affects the reflectance and could therefore be the
reason for the noisy data seen for June.

The correlation for Copenhagen in June also improves a lot when urban features are applied.
This might be explained by the area which is actually included when calculating correlation in
Copenhagen. The data from June covers a smaller area due to the masking of the satellite data.
The area not included has a higher ratio of green open areas, with a lower building density
(Chapter 5 Figure 5.1). Thus the correlation is more impacted by the urban features when
only dealing with the extent present for June. Overall the alterations applied in August do not
have as apparent effects as for both June and April. The downscaled LST for April and June
showed low correlations with the default settings but got enhanced through the different steps
taken, though not always in the same ones. The bilinear reprojection along with the addition of
urban features and step­by­step showed the greatest change for April. For June the change in
extent, normalisation and urban features showed the greatest improvement. Though overall
the changes made in the final model seemed to enhance the performance for all three days
indicating a more robust model when applied for time series. An overview of LST maps
generated from the different alterations are shown in Appendix J Figures J.1 to J.3.
9.2
Detection of ET from rainbeds
The increase in the relative difference of ET in area B and C between 2019 and 2022 seen in
Section 8.2 Table 8.1 could indicate that the ET model captures the effect of the rainbeds.
The derived ET also show deviations in the temporal pattern when assessing the ET/P ratio
change. While this ratio for area C is similar between the two periods, it is subject to high
change for area A and B. A potential explanation for this inconsistency might be found in the
difference in the precipitation pattern between the two years.
A much lower and more even distribution of rain is seen throughout the period in 2019, which
correlates with the higher ET/P ratio seen in Section 8.2 Table 8.1, while a clear pattern of
fewer but more intense rain events is seen for the summer of 2022, see Figures 9.1 and 9.2
Since no ratio change is detected in area C between 2019 and 2022, it might suggest that the
site has a better storage potential in 2022, preventing runoff and enhancing the potential of
evapotranspiration. The precipitation intensity in 10 min intervals for the period in 2019 and
2022 is shown in Figures 9.1 and 9.2.

tendencies.
9.3
Impact from LST input on ET estimation
The modelled ET is derived from various input data as described in Section 2.3, of which all
might have an impact on deviations in the generated ET. Thus the obtained deviations between
modelled ET from the two periods, are not necessarily caused by changes in the LST data.
Also, areas with missing data are as mentioned gap filled, which also causes uncertainty in the
results, but is necessary for a continuous time series. To consider the impact of LST quality
on the modelled ET, both the LST and ET results derived from the normalised base­ and final
model are compared.
The mean LST for the seven months derived from the two models are similar, as seen in
Chapter 8 in Table 8.3. Both models show the same spatial pattern, especially when comparing
the same periods as shown in Chapter 8 in Figure 8.5. The greatest deviation between the two
model runs occurs in area A for the period in 2022, where the application of the final DMS
model generates higher temperatures in area A, relative to the other areas. Thus, if area A is
actually warmer than the others, as what is assumed, it suggests that the final model is more
reliable. However, to arrive at an actual conclusion, the results should be validated e.g. with
Landsat, and further assessment of the time series LST results should be made.

patterns based on the physical properties of the assessed areas.
9.4
Limitations
Limitations due to lack of validation data
A major limitation of this study is the lack of validation data. Both to assess the downscaling
performance as well as for verifying the modelled ET.
The Landsat data used for validation is originally in a 100 m resolution which is then resam­
pled to 20 m. As a consequence, the validation data can not show LST variation within a 20
m resolution. If better validation data had been accessible, it could potentially have caused
deviations in the derived performance measures and led to other assessments. In addition, the
derived Landsat data comes with some large data gaps, especially within Copenhagen, due
to both clouds and the satellite itself, as there is some default area on the tile from Landsat 8
and 9 covering Denmark which can also be seen in Figure 5.1. These deficiencies of course
affect the validation potential of the Landsat data.
The lack of validation data for the modelled ET is also a great shortfall. Without any ground
truth, it is not possible to conclude that the downscaled LST actually works as input data for
the TSEB model in an urban area. Thus the generated results can only provide indications
towards that matter.
Limitations regarding alteration of the model
The procedure applied for the downscaling analysis gives rise to potential shortcomings in
the selections­ and deselections for tested model settings and input combinations. The differ­
ent types of alterations applied (e.g. parameter settings, reprojection method, step­by­step,
additional high­resolution input data) are initially tested against a default, while the best­
performing option is selected for further analysis. Thus many alteration combinations are not
assessed. This has been a predefined condition of the analysis, which nonetheless creates
some blind spots in the tested combinations of model alterations. Along with this, there are
also a number of parameter settings which have not been tested. As mentioned in Section 9.1,
there is already an improvement seen in the downscaling results after altering the parameter
settings (Section 6.2).
Limitations for modelled ET in Copenhagen
When it comes to the results of the ET in urban areas there are several limitations besides the
input data from the modelled LST. As the model is built for agricultural areas the uncertainty of

the urban pixels is difficult to determine. They are treated as sparse forests with tall but sparse
vegetation, which amongst others relates to the wind equations being used when modelling
the ET. Also, the turbulence of fluxes and other factors related to this assumption affects the
results and therefore the assessment of ET from the area with implemented rainbeds. Altering

found valuable when looking at ET in urban areas, instead of using fixed empirical values.
9.5
Outlook
For further validation of the downscaled LST, the study could have benefited from comparing
measurements either obtained by a unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) to get an LST validation
map at 20 m resolution for Copenhagen or air temperature obtained by measuring towers
which already provide available air temperature data. The 20 m LST map would give a direct
and precise foundation for comparing the LST, but would require special instruments.
The analysis regarding alterations of the model approach and input data could have benefited
from additional feature engineering methods for dimensionality reduction, e.g. principal com­
ponent analysis (PCA). As it was seen in Section 7.3.3, the addition of heights together with
the other urban features seemed to confuse the model. There is a chance that too many spectral
bands were used as this has not been tested for the DMS model. In addition, further testing of
more parameters could also benefit the downscaling before extra alterations to the model are
carried out. Eg. alterations of the complexity parameter applied in the ridge regression could
potentially improve the results.
For further analysis of using downscaled LST from the DMS model to obtain high­resolution
ET maps in urban areas, multiple time series should be made to address if the changes in ET
detected for the two years are consistent.
Furthermore, validation data from one or multiple flux towers could be used to validate the
modelled ET. The TSEB­PT have already been validated in earlier studies on agricultural
areas [12, 15, 16], but no validation of the generated ET in urban areas have taken place.

Conclusion
The analysis of impacts derived from tested alterations has demonstrated the potential for
improving downscaling of low­resolution LST within urban areas, using the DMS model.
The following conclusions were drawn in regards to improving the downscaling of LST:
• The model is shown to be sensitive towards some internal parameter settings. From the
adaptation of internal parameter settings, the correlation within Copenhagen increased
from 0.46­0.61, 0.49­0.57, and 0.52­0.54 for April, June and August respectively.
• Normalisation of input data was also found to improve the downscaling performance,
and should therefore be conducted when using the DMS model.
• The addition of spectral indices showed no greater change to the model and were there­
fore not examined further.
• Using a double residual correction in the DMS model caused an over­ and underesti­
mation of LST and a worsening in correlation with Landsat
• Both the step­by­step approach and the inclusion of urban features improved the corre­
lation of the downscaling. The tested urban features included building heights, imper­
viousness, horizon and SVF. With the best results found from a combination of horizon
and imperviousness.
• The overall best correlation was achieved when applying both horizon and impervi­
ousness as additional features to the input data while using the step­by­step approach,
which was therefore chosen as the final model. Here the correlation within Copenhagen
reached 0.67, 0.66 and 0.58 for April, June and August respectively.
• Though the correlations were improved, the LST map showed more vague tendencies,
with an increasingly spatial smoothening.
Even though the enhancement of the downscaled LST was not simultaneous across days since
the datasets showed different responses to the alterations. The alterations applied in the final
model improved the results from all of the analysed dates. This points towards the final model
being more robust when generating time series of LST, since the alterations have shown im­
provement within different months. For a better understanding of the difference between days,
the daily generated LST from both the normalised base and final model should be evaluated.
Findings from the ET assessment indicate the possibility of using enhanced downscaled LST
in urban areas as input to the TSEB­PT model to generate ET. This conclusion is based on the
following findings:
• There is an increase in detected ET from the period in 2019 (w/o rainbeds) and in 2022
(w/ rainbeds) in the area where rainbeds are installed relative to the similar area w/o
rainbeds from 2.2% to 15.6% and 8.15% to 12.6% for the final­ and normalised base
model respectively.
• The change in ET/P ratios for the impervious area and the area w/o rainbeds between
2019 and 2022 deviates from what is seen for the area w/ rainbeds. One explanation for
this could be the intensity of the rain events, supporting the thesis of enhanced water
storage capacity after the implementation of rainbeds.

• The mean LST derived from April­October for the area w/ rainbeds goes from being
the warmest to the coldest of the three areas after the implementation of rainbeds. This
accounts for time­series LST derived from both models.
• The downscaled LST using the final model shows warmer tendencies in the impervious
area suggesting it is more reliable than the normalised base model.
• The most convincing results for the downscaled LST and generated ET are obtained
using the final model.
Despite the potential of improving downscaling of LST to be used in ET models within cities,
further validation is required before the method can be applied to monitor the impact from
rainbeds using satellite data.

J
Discussion
J.1
Downscaling result from the different alterations
For April and August the greatest visual impacts are seen when deriving from the default
model to the base model, changing the internal parameter settings. While for June the alter­
ations included in the final model seem to impact the visual results the most. The RMSD
measures improve the most when arriving from the base to the final model, while the same
applies to the bias. The correlation for April is impacted the most when going from default to
base model, while the correlation for June and August is more affected by the final alterations.
An overview of LST maps generated from the different alterations are shown in Figures J.1
to J.3.
