Summary (English)
The danish water supply is based on groundwater, therefore its protection is of high priority for
the country. Chlorinated solvents, such as PCE are compounds that are common contaminants
in the groundwater bodies around Copenhagen. In this area, the water supply originates mainly
from limestone aquifers, which consist of a combination of fractures and matrix. Groundwater
in such aquifers ﬂows primarily in the fractures, whereas it is practically immobile in the matrix,
and contaminants move in or out of it by diﬀusion. Remediation with Pump-and-Treat Systems
often fails in such aquifers due to back diﬀusion of the contaminant from the matrix.
In the present study, the eﬀects of pumping on the dynamic distribution of PCE in a lime-
stone aquifer at Akacievej site, Denmark, are investigated. To achieve this goal, a pump test is
conducted, which involves PCE sampling in the pumping well and the monitoring wells before,
during and after the pump was turned oﬀ.
The design of the pump test includes reviewing
of previous investigations for the selection of the depth discrete sampling method to be used
on the pump test and evaluation of the remedial pumping system. The results indicated high
contamination in the are below the building, which was expressed as high concentrations caused
by diﬀusion from the matrix under the hydraulic gradient created by non-contaminated water
ﬂowing through the fractures. Five days after the pump was turned oﬀ, a signiﬁcant decrease
was remarked in some of the wells sampled due to the fact that some non-contaminated water
imported during pumping from areas outside of the site was trapped in the vicinity of those wells
due to slower groundwater ﬂow velocities than during pumping. Ten days after the pump was
turned oﬀconcentrations increased again to high levels, due to back diﬀusion and/or transport
of contamination from upgradient areas.
The results of previous investigations when the remedial system was in operation and when
the remedial pump was turned oﬀ, along with the results of the pump test demonstrated the need
for redesigning of the Pump-and-Treat system, with increase of the pumping rate and relocation
of the pumping and inﬁltration well. For this purpose, the ﬁndings of the pump and tracer test,
which was conducted at the same period on the site, should be incorporated to a numerical
model. Further investigations should also be carried out for the identiﬁcation of the full extent
of the plume, since PCE sampling downgradient of the site revealed contamination above the
groundwater quality criteria.
Keywords: fractured; limestone; transport; PCE; distribution; pump test; pump-and-treat;

Summary (Danish)
Vandforsyningen i Danmark er grundvandsbaseret, hvorfor beskyttelse af grundvandet er højt
prioriteret.
Chlorerede opløsningsmidler, herunder PCE, er forbindelser og forureninger, der
typisk foreﬁndes i grundvandsforekomster omkring København. I dette område stammer vand-
forsyningen hovedsageligt fra kalkmagasiner, der består af en kombination af sprækker og matrix.
Grundvandet i disse grundvandsmagasiner strømmer primært gennem sprækkerne, hvorimod det
stort set er ubevægeligt i matricen, så forureningerne strømmer ind eller ud af ved diﬀusion.
Rensning med Pump-and-Treat systemer fejler ofte i disse grundvandsmagasiner pga. tilbaged-
iﬀusion af det forurenende stof fra matricen.
I den foreliggende undersøgelse er påvirkningerne af at pumpe i den dynamiske fordeling
af PCE i et kalkmagasin på Akacievej sitet undersøgt. Til dette formål er en pumpe test ud-
ført, hvilket indebærer PCE prøvetagning af pumpebrønden og af de overvågede brønde både
før, under og efter pumpen blev slukket. Pumpe testens udformning omfatter gennemgang af
tidligere undersøgelser, som anvendes til at bestemme dybden af “discrete sampling” metoden,
der skal benyttes til pumpe testen og evalueringen af det afhjælpende pumpesystem. Resul-
taterne indikerede høj forurening i området under bygningen, som blev udtrykt af høje koncen-
trationsniveauer, hvilke var forårsaget af diﬀusion fra matricen under den hydrauliske gradient.
Dette var skabt af ikke-forurenet vand, der strømmede gennem sprækkerne.
Fem dage efter
pumpen var slukket, blev et betydeligt fald bemærket i nogle af de testede brønde. Dette var
et resultat af, at noget ikke-forurenet vand var blevet indført under pumpning fra områder uden
for sitet. Vandet var holdt i nærheden af de brønde pga. langsommere grundvands strømning-
shastigheder end under pumpning. Ti dage efter pumpen var blevet slukket steg koncentrationer
igen til høje niveauer på grund af tilbagediﬀusion og/eller transport af forurening fra upgradient
områder.
Resultaterne fra forrige undersøgelser har påvist, at når det afhjælpende system var i drift,
og når den afhjælpende pumpe var slukket sammen med resultaterne af pumpens test, at der
er behov for omlægning af Pump-and-Treat systemet inklusiv forøgelse af pumpens hastighed
og ﬂytning af pumpe- og inﬁltrationsbrønden. I denne forbindelse bør resultaterne af pumpen
og sporstof testen, som blev gennemført i samme periode på samme grund, indgå i en numerisk
model. Yderligere undersøgelser skal ligeledes udføres for at kortlægge den fulde udstrækning
af fanen, da PCE prøvetagning af downgradient sitet afslørede forurening over grundvandets
kvalitetskriterier.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Groundwater contamination can occur due to natural processes, human improper waste dis-
posal practices or accidental spills and leaks [3]. In Denmark, the national water supply is based
on groundwater abstraction, with no complicated treatment needed, making groundwater re-
sources protection one of the highest priorities of the country [24]. Nevertheless, the majority
of the groundwater bodies around Copenhagen experiences locally profound contamination and
chlorinated solvents are considered among the most problematic contaminants [27].
Chlorinated solvents is a family of compounds widely used for degreasing fats, oils, waxes etc.,
and common contaminants of soil and groundwater [45]. PCE, also known as tetrachloroethy-
lene, is a chlorinated solvent, which is highly volatile, has relatively low aqueous solubility and
according to USA EPA it is "likely to be carcinogenic to humans by all routes of exposure" [46].
In the capital region of Copenhagen, danian limestone aquifers are of major importance for
its water supply. Their structure consists of a combination of fractures and porous matrix [32].
In such fractured porous media, water ﬂows primarily through the fractures, while it is immobile
or slightly mobile inside the matrix, where contaminants can move in or out of it by diﬀusion
depending on the concentration gradient that is developed [6].
A commonly applied remediation strategy is the groundwater extraction and treatment sys-
tems (pump & treat), which is designed to prevent contaminant migration, by hydraulic control
of the aquifer. However, the prognosis for cleanup of fractured rock aquifers is worse than for
sand and gravel aquifers due to the increased geologic complexity. When attempts are made to
clean such aquifers by pumping water, contaminant removal is exceedingly slow because little or
no water ﬂushes through the porous but low conductivitiy rock matrix, which is likely to retain
the bulk of contaminated mass [33]. Moreover, when pumping ceases, the rebound eﬀect can be
signiﬁcant: contaminant concentration may rise to levels as high as if no pumping had been done
[37].

2.1
Transport mechanisms in fractured aquifers
Generally, as contaminant plumes spread through aquifers, they invade quickly to the high
permeable formations and diﬀuse to the less permeable strata, where they are stored and act as
long term sources of contamination. In fractured aquifers, water ﬂows mainly in the fractures,
while it is mobile or slightly immobile in the matrix. Therefore, contaminants are transported by
advection and dispersion in the fractures, whereas in the matrix, the main transport mechanism
is diﬀusion.
In advection, dissolved contaminants move along with the groundwater ﬂow with the same
speed as the average pore water velocity ([3], [37]). Advective transport can be calculated by
Darcy’s law and is directly dependent on the hydraulic conductivity of the sediments. The spatial
variability of hydraulic conductivities over small distances controls the corresponding distribution
of velocities and the contaminant transport rates.
Dispersion refers to the spreading of the contaminant parallel to the groundwater ﬂow (x axis-
longitudinal dispersion) and in the transverse direction (y and z axes). Hydrodynamic dispersion
is a combination of mechanical and chemical phenomena at the microscopic level. The mechanical
component is a result of the variation of the velocities of the water molecules that travel through
the pores of subsurface and the diﬀerent pathways of ﬂow into the accessible pores around each
grain. It is important because it induces mixing with uncontaminated water, leading to dilution
of the contaminant, and accelerates the arrival of the contaminants at a monitoring point, since
==PAGE:20==
4

some parts of the plume move faster than the average groundwater velocity [3].
The chemical component of dispersion derives from molecular diﬀusion that is caused due
to concentration gradients. Although mechanical dispersion is dominant at modest groundwater
velocities, at slow groundwater velocities contaminant spreading occurs mainly due to molecu-
lar diﬀusion [30]. Diﬀusion is expressed by Fick’s ﬁrst law, which at its simplest form in one
dimension is described by the following equation:

2.2
Pump and Treat Limitations in fractured aquifers
Groundwater extraction and treatment systems aim at either ensuring control of further
spreading of groundwater contamination, or restoring entirely a contaminated section of an
aquifer. This is due to the fact that they draw groundwater through the contaminated zone, and
thus ﬂushing out contaminants, more rapidly than it would naturally occur. In homogeneous
formations of high conductivities, aquifer clean-up goals can be met. However, when pumping
ceases in aquifers with geologies of low permeability as the fractured limestone, contaminant
concentrations often rise to levels as high as if no groundwater extraction had been undertaken.
Nevertheless, if no action is taken, contaminant plume will continue expanding downstream.
Moreover, generally the contaminant concentrations in the fractures is markedly reduced during
the operation of the system, and in case they reduce below the threshold levels, the aquifer can
still be used as long as pumping is maintained.
The after pumping rise in concentrations of the contaminants is a result of the following
transport processes:
• diﬀusion from the matrix
• desorption from sediment surfaces
• Non-aqueous phase liquid (NAPL)dissolution
• hydrodynamic isolation within wellﬁelds
2.2.1
Diﬀusion in fractured aquifers
In a fractured bedrock aquifer, contaminated water mainly ﬂows into the fractures, while it
is stored within the matrix, whose permeability can be low or negligible. This has important
implications for the contaminants behavior, since even if the permeability of the matrix is very
low, diﬀusion will cause retention of contamination into the essentially stagnant matrix pore
water of the rock. There are three phases of diﬀusive movements:
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Pump and Treat Limitations in fractured aquifers
5
• Initial contamination phase, where high concentrations in the mobile water in the fractures
diﬀuse into the uncontaminated water in the porous matrix
• Intermediate phase, which begins when contaminant source is removed, and clean water
enters the contamination zone, as contamination interior of the matrix block moves towards
the block’s midpoint, while simultaneously contaminants closer to the fractures diﬀuses
outward due to lower concentrations in the fractured water
• Final phase, when concentration at the midpoint of the block becomes maximum and
diﬀusive ﬂux occurs everywhere outward to the fracture ﬂow system.
Figure 2.1: Diﬀusion in a limestone with fractures. Source: [37].
The extent of diﬀusion and its signiﬁcance depends on the properties of the geologic medium
and the contaminant. It can be beneﬁcial, since it retards the contamination process into the
fractured rock and can reduce peak concentration. However, when the objective is to restore
an aquifer, the release of contaminants from the matrix can prolong the remediation eﬀorts
or fail the meeting of drinking-water standards, comparing with a simple porous medium with
equivalent hydraulic conductivity [37].
2.2.2
Desorption from sediment surfaces
Sorptive interactions between the solid sediments of the aquifer and a contaminant depend
on the contaminants properties and the pore water velocity and can retard the transport of the
compounds ([1], [26], [10]). Since pumping causes groundwater velocities higher than the natural,

chemical equilibrium might not be achieved. If the contact time between a contaminant and the
surface of the sediment is insuﬃcient, water is ﬂushed with contaminants concentrations lower
than if chemical equilibrium had been allowed, and more pore volumes are required in order to
remove the same amount of contamination [30].

2.2.3
Non-aqueous phase liquid dissolution
Compounds that belong to the category of NAPLs, are liquids that are not miscible with
water. When such substances are released in the unsaturated zone, a portion of them is stored
in the pores of subsurface sediments, forming a separate ("free") phase, while the rest of them
continues to migrate downwards. NAPLs lighter than water (LNAPLs) can ﬂoat on top of the
water table forming pools on top of the capillary fringe, while dense NAPLs (DNAPLs) behavior
is more complex: they continue vertical migration until they reach low permeability geological
units, where they can form pools. As with sorption, if there is not suﬃcient time for dissolution
of NAPL residuals due to higher groundwater velocities from pumping, they remain trapped in
the pores acting as continuous sources of contamination ([47], [48], [35]).

eﬀectively, whereas others not.

2.4
Pump Tests
Pumping tests are a method traditionally used to estimate the aquifer properties and param-
eters, in order to describe the aquifer’s or contaminants behavior in a model. They consist of
either injecting or removing water from a well and thus measuring the aquifer’s response in the
same well or the nearby monitoring wells [11]. Pump tests have also been combined with tracer
tests aiming to describe the nature of transport processes in fractured aquifers ([4],[7]), or have
been used to derive parameters for modeling the groundwater ﬂow in fractured bedrock [23].
Another type of pump test is the integral pump test, that is based on the principle that during
pumping, the contaminants concentration of a well ﬂuctuates due to the increase of its capture
zone and the spatial distribution of the contaminant. The concentration time series measured
in a pumping well positioned in a control plane perpendicular to the mean groundwater ﬂow
yields information on the spatial distribution of the contaminant within the capture zone. This
test has been applied to quantify mass discharge and analyze groundwater contamination of
chlorinated solvents and BTEX-PAH compounds in heterogeneous aquifers consisting of sand,
clay and gravels but not yet in fractured aquifers ([52], [28], [5]).

Diﬀusion cells
The sampling scheme of diﬀusion cells is similar to snap samplers (ﬁgure 4.2). The diﬀerence
is that as sample bottles, vials with a membrane permeable to the speciﬁc compound tested
are used. The vials are ﬁlled with demineralised water and are left in the aquifer for a period
of time, during which the compound of interest diﬀuses into the vials through the membrane
due to the concentration gradient that is created, till water in the vials is in equilibrium with
the well water. After the vials emerge, their lids are replaced with full cover lids to prevent
evaporation and sent to analysis. From the concentration measured at this period of time, the
concentration in the aquifer is calculated, taking into account the features of the membrane and
the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of the compound (see Appendix). This adds an additional limitation to
their usage, apart from those mentioned in table 4.1, since further uncertainties are inserted in
the calculation of the actual concentrations and when the concentrations measured are under
the detection limit, the actual concentrations are underestimated [44].

4.1.3
Separation Pumping
The separation pump system is rather complex, as demonstrated in ﬁgure 4.3. The borehole is
at ﬁrst separated in two parts, at the top and at the bottom of the well, generating a water divide,
depending on the transmissivity distribution and the ratio of the pumping rates. Pumping water
from the level of this water divide, creates two water divides, that form the sampling zone. A
Heat-Pulse-Flow-Meter is used to locate the water divide. The precision of achieving a speciﬁc
depth is then a combination of the pumping rates and the sensitivity of the instruments used,
as well as prior knowledge of the speciﬁc capacity of the well, transmissivity of the aquifer and
the well diameter. This is a supplementary limitation to those referenced in table 4.1. The size
of the borehole is a further limiting factor :small boreholes do not ﬁt the three pumps, while
large boreholes require large pumping rates to achieve high ﬂow velocities for the water divide
detection [38].

Selection of the sampling method for the pump test
Taking into account the results of the measurements in 2014-2015, the bladder pump was
chosen to be the most suitable method for the pump test performed in 2016.
The passive

in between would be required, which is less than the required exposure time of the devices.
Moreover, among the active sampling methods (bladder pump and separation pumping), the
bladder pump dominated, as some of the wells had small diameter for ﬁtting all the necessary
equipment and due to the simplicity of the bladder pump sampling system, comparing to the
complexity of the separation pumping technique. Furthermore, the separation pumping system
appeared to dilute the concentrations in the near source areas where the concentration gradients

5.4
Method of sampling
In the wells where depth discrete sampling was implemented, the bladder pump was used. The
bladder pump consists of a rigid tube that contains an internal ﬂexible bladder. Check valves
exist in both ends of the rigid tube and slots adjacent its upper end. There are two outlets on
top of it, one for the sample line that leads to the sampling bottle, and the other for the pressure
line that is connected to a compressor that feeds the tube with gas. Sampling with bladder pump
is achieved as following:
• the bladder pump is immersed slowly in the well in order not to disturb the water column,
until it reaches the desirable sampling depth.
==PAGE:47==

• the internal bladder ﬁlls with water due to hydrostatic pressure and then, pressure is
applied to the system through a lever that releases gas to the tube, opens the top check
valve, closes the bottom check ball in the end valve and squeezes the bladder forcing the
sample to the discharge line
• the bladder is deﬂated by releasing the gas with the lever to the open air
• the inﬂation/deﬂation cycles are performed multiple times until volume of 0.8L is purged
in order to replace the stagnant water with fresh formation groundwater.
For each depth 3 vials of 40ml were ﬁlled with water until they overﬂowed. Moreover, 3 drops
of acid H2SO4 of 4M concentration were added in each vial in order to preserve the samples.
Since PCE is a volatile compound, the lids were placed in the vials as fast as possible.

7.3
Future Perspectives
The results of the pump test indicated strong contamination of the bulk of the limestone. The
large pumping rate applied revealed higher concentrations than the last investigations, which in
turn gave the opportunity to reach valuable conclusions for the site.
Diﬀusion is the dominant process for the PCE transport, both in the crushed and the fractured
limestone. Although in the main external area of the site much of the contamination was removed
with excavation and ventilation drains, as explained in chapter 3, it is evident that the time
between the spill and the remedial actions was crucial for the fate of PCE in the site. During
approximately thirty years, PCE diﬀused into the matrix deep in the limestone (at more than
30 m.b.g.s. depth, according to the measurements of GEO18).
Nevertheless, when the remedial pump is on, the concentrations in GEO5, which is close to
the source of the contamination drop below the groundwater quality criteria, but this decline
is due to the inﬁltration of the treated water, hence it cannot be assured that this is the case
elsewhere at the site. However, in order to tackle eﬀectively trapped contamination in the matrix
and the sediments the following actions should be taken:
• Numerical modeling of the site, taking into account the new ﬁndings of the pump and the
tracer tests
• Redesign of the Pump & Treat system so that the capture zone of the pumping well would
include more eﬀectively the area under the building, where the highest concentrations were
measured.
• Investigation and remediation of the full extent of the plume
7.3.1
Numerical modeling of the site
The results of the tracer test would give more accurate parameters on the fracture system and
the geologic parameters of the site. In this way, the ﬂowlines obtained by the EPM model could
be more precise, ameliorating the ﬁndings of the pump test. Moreover, based on the results from
the pump test, the rough approximations that were implemented in the present thesis, could be
replaced by simulations of the PCE contamination of the site that are more accurate and closer
to reality.
7.3.2
Redesign of the P & T system
The Pump & Treat system had been operating on the site for approximately ten years by the
time of the present project. However, the main conclusion that this project reaches is that,
although an important part of the contamination has been removed, in sites with such complex
geology, complete remediation is signiﬁcantly challenging. The current scheme should be re-
evaluated, since the secondary source that is suspected to be found below the building is not
==PAGE:77==
7.3 Future Perspectives
61
entirely covered by the capture zone of the remedial pump in PB. Along with the change of the
remedial pumping well, the remedial pumping rate should be also re-examined, since the results
of PCE behavior after pumping showed that they are heavily dependent on the pumping rate
applied on the system. Moreover, the considerable concentration measured in B22 before and
during pumping, indicates that the inﬁltration well should also be relocated to capture more
eﬀectively the downgradient plume. Nonetheless, this is still something to be considered after
results of the implementation of a proper numerical modeling.
7.3.3
Investigation and remediation of the full extent of the plume
As demonstrated by in the Akacievej measurements, concentrations measured in the well concern
mainly the fractures, whereas the matrix of the limestone is much more contaminated, and can
act as a long term source of contamination. The measurements in the wells further in the plume
showed concentrations that are above the groundwater quality criteria, implying that concentra-
tions in the limestone matrix are even higher. Therefore, detailed investigations should be carried
out to quantify the extent of the contamination and the direction of the plume migration, but
mainly how changes in Akacievej ﬂow-contamination situation aﬀect the contamination situation
further in the plume. This would be beneﬁcial in case remediation of the plume to its full extent
proves to be infeasibly costly.

Conclusions
The present project aimed at investigating the eﬀects of pumping on the PCE distribution
in limestone aquifer. The site of investigation is located in Akacievej, and proved to be ideal
to achieve this goal: it is contaminated mainly with PCE, the hydrogeology consists of crushed
and fractured limestone aquifer, and the following remedial activities had been implemented:
excavation in the outdoor area, ventilation drains installation and a P&T system with inﬁltration
of the treated water.
In the present project, a pump test was performed, which involved sampling of PCE in the
pumping well and the nearby monitoring wells. The vertical distribution of the contaminant was
determined by depth discrete sampling. The selection of the sampling method was implemented
by comparing various sampling methods based on previous investigations by DTU Environment.
The sampling method selected was the bladder pump, a device that provided with minimal
disturbance of the water column in the well through slow rate of purging.
The results indicated that pumping has as an eﬀect import of clean water through the frac-
tures from uncontaminated area that caused diﬀusion of PCE from the inner of the matrix and
the sediments of the fractured and the crushed limestone respectively. In this way, in combination
with ﬂowlines obtained by an EPM model, the areas of high contamination under the building of
the site were identiﬁed. Sampling ﬁve days after the pump was switched oﬀindicated decrease in
concentrations in some wells. This was attributed to the fact that after pumping, imported clean
water was ﬂowing through the fractures under slower velocities than when pumping, hence not
much mixing between the contaminated edges of the matrix and the fractures was performed.
Nevertheless, ten days after the pump was switched oﬀ, PCE concentrations rose again, due to
back diﬀusion from the matrix in the vicinity of the well and/or shift of the plume caused by the
strong pumping rate of the pump test.
Simple calculations were additionally implemented to quantify the order of magnitude of the
concentration in the matrix and the contaminant mass under the building in the limestone layer.
The results showed maximum concentration in the matrix below the building of 3 mg/L and
total mass in the limestone below the building of 1.7 Kg.
==PAGE:80==
64
Conclusions
Sampling of the wells further in the plume indicated signiﬁcant concentrations. Moreover,
the results from the pump test were compared with previous investigations conducted by DTU
Environment. It was shown that when the remedial pump was on and clean water was re-injected,
PCE concentrations under the guidelines were measured in a well close to the source, whereas
when the remedial pump was oﬀ, PCE concentrations increased signiﬁcantly.
The results of the pump test, along with reviewing of past investigations demonstrated that
the current P&T system should be redesigned to address the current contamination situation of
the site, with the following actions:
• increase of the pumping rate to induce higher diﬀusion ﬂux of PCE from the matrix, and
therefore clean-up of the limestone matrix
• relocation of the pumping well to tackle contamination under the building
• relocation of the inﬁltration well to capture eﬀectively the downgradient plume
The redesign of the P&T system should be based on proper numerical modeling based on
the ﬁndings of the pump and tracer test conducted in spring 2016.
Finally, the signiﬁcant
concentrations measured further in the plume, demonstrate the need for quantiﬁcation of the
full extent of the plume and the investigation of the direction of the plume migration.
