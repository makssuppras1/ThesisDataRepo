Abstract
Scale-up often represents a major obstacle to the successful commercialization of bioprocesses,
due to the poor transferability of processes from lab to full-scale. To improve this transfer, suitable
scale-down bioreactors are needed, mimicking full-scale process conditions at lab-scale. Exper-
iments in such scale-down bioreactors may be employed to develop processes and strains better
suited for industrial application, and to predict process performance at full-scale. However, several
methodologies exist to run scale-down experiments and research has shown that different results
occur depending on which methodology has been used, highlighting the requirement to carefully
choose the right methodology. This master thesis focused on the rational design and analysis of
various intermittent feeding designs to scale-down the combined oxygen and substrate gradients
in large-scale bioreactors. An Escherichia coli wild type and a tryptophan producing strain were
used to investigate the effects of glucose and oxygen gradients on E. coli in scale-down experi-
ments. It was demonstrated that oxygen gradients induced the most significant phenotypic changes
in both strains compared to the reference fermentation, while substrate induced the least changes,
highlighting the importance of selecting an appropriate scale-down method that mimics the most
relevant phenomena of the large-scale process of interest. Such rational scale-down experiments
have the potential to be used for high-throughput screening of robust microbical production strains
for industrial application.

Introduction
Limited fossil fuels and the eventual depletion of natural resources, have made it crucial to find
better and sustainable ways to produce chemicals, biofuels, and green energy. Microbial biotech-
nology has emerged as a promising solution. By using the natural abilities of microorganisms,
bioprocessing offers a way to address climate change, reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and se-
cure a greener energy future [1, 2]. Some major products of biological processing are for example
ethanol fermentation in Saccharomyces cerevisae, lactic acid fermentation in Lactobacillus del-
brueckii or also amino acids like phenylalanine fermentation in Corynebacterium glutamicum [3].
The production capacity of the microorganisms from an industrial point of view is of high impor-
tance, however economic viability depends on overall process efficiency defined by the three key
process indicators often referred as TRY values: titer (total amount of product formed per volume
unit), rate (total amount of product formed per unit biomass over time) and yield (total product
formed per unit of carbon source) [4, 5]. In a laboratory set up, those values can be optimized by
engineering the strains, optimising media, growth conditions etc. to produce the required product
from the desired substrate and with the required TRY values. However, when scaling-up these
processes to pilot-plant or production facilities, changes in performance occur due to intrinsic and
extrinsic heterogeneities [6] affecting the economical feasibility of the process [7]. Research has
made advances in finding ways to overcome those issues by for example using scale-down design,
to conduct lab-experiment under conditions that are similar to a large-scale environment [8, 9, 10].
In this master thesis, the goal is to illustrate a workflow for rational scale-down experimental design
starting from batch experiments for the determination of physiological growth parameters of the
targeted strains to simulating concentration gradients in big scale compartment model simulation
and running an established experimental scale-down design in AMBR® 250 (Fig. 1.1).

2.1
Challenges in scaling-up fermentation for industrial production
Industrial bioreactors have volumes ranging from 20-2000 m3, while lab scale volumes range from
microliters in shaken microtiter plate cultivations up to tens of liters in stirred tank bench top biore-
actor experiments [11]. Those differences in scale imply that important operating conditions like
reactor type, feed rate, agitation speed, amount of impellers, etc. have to be chosen carefully as
through scale-up there impact determine the performance of the large-scale bioreactor much more
severally than in small scale [12, 13, 14, 15]. When scaling-up a fermentation most of these factors
are increased to fit the large-scales necessity. Nevertheless, to keep industrial fermentations eco-
nomically feasible the volumetric power input for the mixing of the bioreactor can not be scaled up
proportionally, which results in altered mixing behaviours in large-scale bioreactors. When adding
different compounds needed for the fermentation (e.g. substrate, oxygen, acid or base) during a
fed-batch fermentation for example, if the consumption rates of the compounds are higher than
the transport rates of the different compounds, concentration gradients occur in the bioreactor [16]
which can negatively impact the cell physiology, i.e., lower production rates, which in turn also
impacts the economical efficiency [17, 6].
A significant source of heterogeneity in a large-scale fed-batch fermentation is the substrate con-
centration. In this type of fermentation, the feed is typically added at the top of the fermenter to
control cell growth rate by limiting substrate supply. However, high mixing times of the bioreactor
can result in higher glucose concentrations at the top of the fermenter and lower concentrations at
the bottom because cells eat up the glucose faster than it is mixed, leading to varying growth rates
of the cells throughout the reactor. This variability results from the Monod kinetics that cells ex-
hibit when a substrate is limited. The Monod equation, governing single-substrate-limited growth,
is expressed as equation 2.1.

The response to those heterogeneities in the large bioreactor can happen in a big range of timescales

the substrate concentration surpasses the maximum aerobic utilization capacity, surplus carbon is
channeled towards anaerobic fermentation, leading to the simultaneous production of byproducts
such as ethanol or acetate [23]. This phenomenon is meaningful since it results in a considerable

significant substrate costs compared to the product. In addition, lower product concentrations also
increase downstream processing expenses and reduce productivity. Those outcomes underline the
importance of optimizing metabolic responses for efficient and cost-effective production [16].
2.2
Scale-down design
To tackle the challenges listed in section 2.1 researchers have designed so-called scale-down biore-
actors (SDR) that replicate full-scale process conditions at a smaller scale, enabling the refine-
ment of processes and strains better suited for industrial use, and predicting full-scale performance
(Fig. 2.1) [28, 29, 30]. Those SDR can be categorized in two main classes, namely the single
reactor systems and the multi reactor systems [29].
4

Two of the main examples in single reactor systems are the cyclically pulsed SDR (Fig. 2.1 (a)),
and the multi-compartment single SDR (Fig. 2.1 (b)). In the pulsed reactor system, the approxima-
tion of the large-scale is performed by forcing time-varying feed additions in a cyclic manner. This
pulse mimics for example high and low substrate or oxygen concentration areas of the large-scale
bioreactor, altering the fermentation process outcome. Several cyclically pulsed SDR systems
have already been reported in literature usually showing changes in metabolic profiles, biomass
production and product quality etc. [31, 32, 33, 34]. The pulsed SDR offers an easy, rapid, and
cost-effective setup, but critics argue that it exposes all cells within the SDR to the same fluctu-
ating conditions (substrate and dissolved oxygen (DO) fluctuations), while large-scale fermenters
are known to exhibit distinct zones with different circulation time distributions (CTD) [35]. Fur-
thermore, this model encounters challenges in accurately simulating genuine oscillations at shorter
time intervals [36]. Those issues are addressed with the multi-compartment single reactor system.
This system is built up like a regular stirred tank reactor (STR) with cylindrical disks embedded
in the reactor to increase the mixing times similarly to large-scale conditions. This again alters
metabolic profiles and biomass production [30, 37].
The traditional multi-compartment SDR systems make use of the two most used reactor types
namely the stirred tank reactor (STR) and the plug flow reactor (PFR) in different set-ups. Two
of the most basic multi-compartment systems are the STR-PFR reactor system (Fig. 2.1 (c)) and
the STR-STR reactor system (Fig. 2.1 (d)) [29]. In the STR-PFR system the STR usually has the
bigger volume, therefore the longer residence time distribution and is a well mixed and uniform
environment. The PFR on the other hand represents the poorly mixed section of the large-scale
bioreactor with potential chemical and physical gradients. Observation of cell physiology can eas-
ily be made by sampling along the PFR and the possibility to easily alter the mean residence time
in this reactor makes this set-up quite advantageous [31, 38, 39]. In the STR-STR system one
makes use of having two ideally mixed reactors that can adopt different mixing times by having
different volumes or mixing speeds and therefore account for the different mixing behaviors in
large-scale reactors [31]. In aerobic fermentation the STR-STR-system also offers the possibil-
ity of running one STR aerobically and the other anaerobically, in order to estimate possible side
product formation [40, 41]. However, the main disadvantage of multi reactor system is the require-
ment of pumping the broth from one reactor to another which can lead to deterioration in process
performance thereby concealing the negative consequences of large-scale heterogeneities [42].

To perform such a SD experiment workflow Sweere et al. 1987 proposed an adapted procedure of
Oosterhuis’s et al. 1984 method of essentially four different steps (Fig. 2.2): (1) process analysis
of the production scale, (2) simulation of the rate-limiting mechanisms at a laboratory scale, (3)
optimization and modeling of process at laboratory scale, and (4) optimization of the production
process by translating the optimized laboratory conditions to the production scale [9, 28].
Figure 2.2: Scheme showing the 4 different steps when using scale-down experimental workflow
design for large-scale optimization [9, 28].
The first step of the analysis involves a thorough examination of the production scale, carried out in
three distinct steps. Initially, a dimensional analysis is conducted, encompassing a comprehensive
list of all pertinent parameters and their respective units. Subsequently, a mechanism analysis is
executed, identifying and enumerating all the involved mechanisms and expressing their rates using
the predefined characteristic parameters. These characteristic parameters could include fluxes,
pressures, heights, etc. Finally, the regime analysis takes place, which entails a comparison of the
characteristic times. The characteristic time measures the rate at which a mechanism adjusts to
a specific fractional change. A mechanism with a low characteristic time is deemed fast, while
one with a high characteristic time is considered slow. By comparing these characteristic times, it
becomes evident that certain mechanisms within microorganisms exhibit timescales comparable to
those of environmental changes. As a result, the behavior of the microorganisms may alter during
the process, potentially impacting their growth and product formation. [9, 28]
In the second step, the detailed analysis of the large-scale fermentation process needs to be adapted
to smaller laboratory reactors while closely mimicking its behavior. Achieving this entails two
approaches: using common scale-down parameters (e.g., power to volume ratio, kLa, OTR, etc.)
and keeping them constant across scales [43], or employing regime analysis based on characteristic
times [9]. In order to accurately predict the behavior of a large-scale system, a scale-down model
must accurately replicate not only the performance of the system at its normal operating conditions,
but also its response to changes in key operating parameters. Directly comparing the two scales
across all possible conditions is impractical, necessitating a scale-down strategy that maintains
intrinsic consistency between them. This scale-down model serves as a valuable tool to study how
variations in operating parameters impact process performance and product quality [8].
The third step involves optimizing and modeling the lab-scale fermentation process and/or screen-
ing for a suitable strain, to achieve better fermentation outcomes. To achieve a better laboratory-
scale process, it is crucial to consider how the optimized conditions will be applied at the produc-
tion scale. The search for optimal parameters can be conducted through a single-parameter rang-
ing study, which helps identify key operating parameters and determine their acceptable ranges in
cell culture manufacturing. The acceptable range of a parameter might fall within or outside the
characterization range, determined using a risk assessment method called failure mode and effect
analysis (FMEA). Once the key operating parameters are identified, an interaction study, such as a
6

fractional factorial design using design of experiment (DOE), is performed to assess the impact of
different parameter combinations on process performance [9, 8]. This method can also pinpoint the
worst-case condition to be avoided, representing a combination of operating parameters that yields
the poorest performance during manufacturing. The scale-down approach also allows screening
for a suitable strain using a high throughput screening system at the low scale [44, 45, 46].
The fourth and final step centers on transitioning the optimized laboratory conditions and/or strain
to a production scale. This transfer is achieved by employing the models developed in the second
step in order to scale up the experimental conditions, much like they were used for scale-down.
The effectiveness of this scale-up process hinges on the precision of the representative scale-down
experiments, as they play a crucial role in guiding the design of the scaled-up conditions. [9]
The desired information for the first step of a scale-down design can be acquired ideally through
commercial production or, if not available, by examining pilot-scale performances. In cases where
practical experiments do not provide the required data, valuable insights can be collected from
large-scale reactor simulations, as demonstrated in Bach’s work [8, 47].
2.3
Simulation of large bioprocesses
Einsele 1978 found that the liquid phase mixing time increases significantly with increasing reactor
scale, leading to oxygen concentration gradients in highly viscous fermentation broths even in
pilot-scale reactors [48]. Therefore, Oosterhuis et. al 1984 proposed a two compartment model
to calculate the oxygen transfer capacity of a reactor and compared it to measured values [28].
Oosterhuis et. al suggested dividing the compartments spatially in a mixed zone, close to the
impellers, and a bubble zone above and below the impellers. In the bubble zone, the gas bubbles can
rise freely, and for the calculation of the OTR, this part of the reactor can be assumed to be a bubble
column. In the mixed zone, the broth is assumed to be ideally mixed for mass transfer purposes.
The gas itself is also ideally mixed because of the shorter mean gas residence time compared to
the oxygen transfer time and the liquid flow between the two compartments is assumed to be equal
to the pumping capacity. To calculate the OTR, equations for the oxygen transfer coefficient, the
gas hold up, the power input, and the pumping capacity are determined in Oosterhuis et. al’s work.
The compartmentalization of large-scale bioreactors was, after this work, used more and more;
to predict oxygen concentrations in different parts of the bioreactor [18], to simulate different
bioreactor set-ups and choose the suitable one when scaling-up [49], to add unstructured microbial
kinetics to the compartment model for simulation of concentrations of the reaction components in
a large-scale bioreactor [50] or to compare simulated values to an experimental set-up [51].
Eggels 1996 first proposed a large eddy simulation of a stirred tank reactor. The study showcases
snapshots of the flow field, offering an unparalleled perspective of the turbulent flow patterns in the
tank [52]. The results align well with the experimental data. Nevertheless, it’s important to note
that the study solely compared the results to phase-averaged velocity measurements. To unveil
the most intriguing flow subtleties such as the trailing vortex structures near the impeller, phase-
resolved data is indispensable, which was done by Derksen et. al 1999 [53]. With the increase of
computational resources more and more complex computational fluid dynamics simulations were
performed to better comprehend vortices behind impeller blades in large-scale bioreactors [54],
to elaborate on the role of imperfect mixing in the context of shortstopping of a runaway reac-
tion [55] or to understand the impact of parallel, merging, and diverging flow configurations on
gas dispersion in a stirred vessel [56].
Implementing a kinetic model of a microorganism to the computational fluid dynamic (CFD) sim-
ulation was stated to be an essential part of rationally scale-up industrial processes [7]. Lapin et. al
2004 first proposed to utilize an Euler−Lagrange approach to characterize the behavior of a diverse
cell population in a stirred-tank reactor, revealing how intracellular conditions and turbulent flow

2.4
Escherichia coli kinetic models
The kinetic model that are to be analyzed with the compartment model simulation has to capture the
metabolic changes in which one is interested to analyze. In this study an aerobic fermentation of
E. coli including overflow metabolism was the point of interest. Two models have been described
in the literature, one by Xu et al. 1999, the other by Anane et al. 2017. Both are described in the
following sections.
2.4.1
Xu et. al kinetic model
Xu et al. 1999 proposed a kinetic model of E. coli that focuses on dynamic modeling of acetate
formation and reutilization in both batch and fed-batch cultures (Fig. 2.3). Previous research has
shown that glucose represses the cellular utilization of exogenous acetate, with its consumption
occurring only after glucose depletion in batch cultures [60]. This growth on acetate necessi-
tates inducing gluconeogenesis to supply the cell’s anabolism with precursors from the glycolytic
pathway. However, in the presence of limited glucose, as seen in fed-batch cultures, acetate is con-
sumed much more rapidly compared to when glucose is entirely absent [61]. The acetate uptake
system being present in glucose-grown cells, shows no catabolite repression [62]. When acetate
is present, it is transformed back into acetyl-CoA, which then seamlessly enters aerobic energy
metabolism. This process occurs without requiring the induction of gluconeogenesis, as long as
the cell receives an adequate supply of anabolic precursors from a low flux of glycolysis.

Compartment-model simulation

SD pulse design
The SD pulse was also simulated in python with the same kinetic models and ODE’s as the compart-

The ODE’s were solved in a time interval from 0 to 10 min using the odeint solver from the inte-

by adding conditions that either glucose or oxygen addition is stopped after a certain amount of
time. The duration of the pulses (substrate and oxygen) were defined in section 4.2.3.

Pre-culture preparation
For the pre-experiment and the SD-experiments the pre-cultures were prepared by first inoculating
5 mL of seed medium in a culture tube with 100 µL of a 15 % glycerol stock of respectively WTS
and TPS. After growing for approximately 8 h, 0.5 mL of the culture in the tube was used to inoc-
ulate a 250 mL shake flask containing 20 mL of seed medium for the pre-experiment and 5 mL of
the culture in the tube was used to inoculate a seed reactor in the AMBR® 250 containing 160 mL
of seed medium. Both cultivations were performed at 30 ◦C and a shaking speed of 225 rpm.
3.4
Batch pre-experiments
The kinetic parameters necessary for each kinetic model, Xu et al. 1999 and Anane et al. 2017,
and for each strain, WTS and TPS, were determined via a batch fermentation in the AMBR® 250
system that was run in duplicates for each strain. The batch experiment consisted of three different
phases: an aerobic fermentation with glucose as substrate, an anaerobic fermentation with glucose
as substrate and an aerobic fermentation with acetate and other byproducts produced during the
first two phases as substrate. During the fermentation the air flow was set to 100 mL
min. In the first
phase a DO cascade, controlled by increasing stirrer speed, was set to 40 % air saturation. The
anaerobic phase was then achieved by setting the upper limit of the stirrer speed to 1650 rpm thus
limiting the oxygen transfer into the liquid available to cells in the fermenter in this phase. For the
third phase stirring and oxygen are kept at the same settings and the DO cascade enables to have a
constant DO at 40 %.

Discussion
The aim of the thesis was to complete a full workflow from simulating cellular heterogeneities
in a large-scale bioreactor by integrating cellular kinetics with compartment model to the de-
sign of appropriate scale-down (SD) protocol based on intermittent feeding regime (IFR). The
design of scale-down protocol was implemented in the high-throughput fermentation platform
(AMBR® 250, Sartorius) and the production strain physiology was assessed. An initial batch
experiment was designed and performed with high frequency sampling to infer the physiological
growth parameters of both E. coli (the tryptophan production strain (TPS) and the parent swild-
type strain) strains. The batch fermentation protocol contained three phases: aerobic fermentation
on glucose (I), anaerobic fermentation on glucose (II) and aerobic fermentation on by-products
produced during the two other phases (III). It was observed that the TPS has a very similar growth
rate than the WTS, which is surprising as one would expect a lower growth rate for the TPS as it
uses glucose for the production of one additional compound namely tryptophan.From the concen-
tration profiles it was observed that the WTS and the TPS have a similar but not identical pathway
responses in regards to the conditions (corresponding to the different phases in this thesis). The
most interesting difference was how much higher the concentration of pyruvic acid reached for the
TPS compared to the WTS at the end of phase (II). In the central metabolism of E. coli during the
anaerobic fermentation, glucose is converted to the intermediate pyruvic acid and then to lactate
or ethanol requiring 1 and 2 mol of NADH respectively. In the case of the WTS, pyruvic acid was
not present in the WTS but it was present in the TPS fermentation (Fig. 4.7). This can be due to
the fact that the WTS converts the intermediate pyruvate faster in the central metabolism during
anaerobic fermentation to lactate than the TPS to ethanol, since twice as much energy is necessary
to produce ethanol than to produce lactate [69]. This also results in E. coli first using pyruvic acid
as carbon source in phase (III) of the fermentation for the TPS as can also be seen in the RQ values
in Figure 4.6b. Apart from the yield of biomass on glucose in the overflow phase, all the yields and
maximal specific uptake rates from the batch experiment show lower values compared to previ-
ously reported experimental values [26, 70]. This can be due to the fact that the batch experiment
was run at a temperature of 30 ◦C instead of 37 ◦C resulting in a lower maximal growth rate of ap-
proximately 0.34 h
−1 with the WTS compared to 0.56 h
−1 reported previously [26]. Furthermore,
the absence of biomass formation during phase (III) of the batch experiment could be attributed to
the fact that these particular strains may require a higher substrate portion for maintenance, imped-
ing further biomass growth. Consequently, this excess substrate is converted into carbon dioxide,
which accounts for the observed increase in carbon dioxide production during this phase [70].
The carbon balances only closed between 73-94 % in the batch experiment, because the substrate
concentration was possibly overestimated by analytical measurements. Small concentration differ-
ences in the batch medium can alter the amount of glucose that has been used significantly enough
to change the carbon balance between 5-10 % which in this case, would result in a much better
carbon balance.
The experimental data was found to be in good comparison with the model simulations (Fig-
ure 4.15a and 4.16a). For both models simulated, the experimental DO concentration was only
aligned with the simulation during the anaerobic phase as during the aerobic phases a DO cascade
was set to 40 % air saturation (as explained in section 3.4), which was not considered in the simu-
lations. The overestimation of acetate production for the TPS with the Xu et al. 1999 model could
come from the fact that the decrease in biomass formation in the anaerobic phase was not simu-
lated and therefore more biomass could produce more acetate in the simulation compared to the
experimental data. The parameter pA,max is directly correlated to the production of acetate (pA),
but also to the specific uptake rate of glucose in the overflow phase qS,of which is correlated to the

growth rate µ and all compound concentrations. The parameter pA,max was not determined exper-
imentally and was fitted to the experimental data so that the glucose, oxygen and biomass profiles
fit better to the simulation neglecting the fit of acetate. Because the fitting of the experimental data
to the simulated data was worse for Anane et al.’s 2017 model and some ambiguities were found
in some equations of this model, Xu et al.’s 1999 model was further used in this work.
The compartment model simulations of a fed-batch fermentation in a 90 m3 reactor with 4 Rushton
turbines gave a great understanding on how the two strains would behave in a large-scale biore-
actor. Both the strains have similar concentration maps and are only different in absolute values.
From the results, the substrate concentration gradient was found to be larger in comparison to
the oxygen concentration gradient, similar to the simulation results reported previously [47, 12].
The acetate concentration was high in the top right corner compartments as the least oxygen is
available to the cells and a lot of glucose is present to be metabolised anaerobically. This is also
reflected with the specific uptake of glucose in the overflow showing the highest values in the same
compartments. The specific uptake rate of glucose in the oxidative pathway is the highest in the
compartment right below the top ones. This is because glucose but also oxygen is necessary to
grow in the oxidative metabolism. In the complete top compartments the glucose concentrations
is in fact high, but the oxygen concentration is lower compared to the compartments below. Going
down in the compartments, less glucose was available, as seen from the substrate compartment
map, to enable the cells to grow aerobically on glucose which is in accordance to Nadal-Rey et
al.’s 2021 study [59]. From the parameter selection (section 4.2.2) profiles of rates, yield and
growth rate over biomass concentration and feed-rate were obtained. Those profiles behaved as
expected. The parameters qS,ox and qS,of are related to qS by qS = qS,ox +qS,of. Therefore, when
for example qS,of increases because the biomass concentration increases as well but the feeding
rate stays constant, qS,ox has to decrease proportionally (Fig.4.18a). This same behaviour occurs
when the feeding rate is increased and the biomass is kept constant (Fig.4.18b). The biomass yield
on substrate and the growth rate also show expected profiles as they decrease when biomass con-
centration is increased with the same feed rate while they increase when biomass concentration is
constant with an increasing feed rate. This is because when the biomass concentration is increased,
less glucose is available per cell due to the necessary maintenance and when the feed rate is in-
creased but the biomass concentration is kept constant enough glucose is fed to the cells for them to
increase in biomass growth. Interesting is also to see that the growth rate decreases exponentially
when increasing the biomass while the biomass increases linearly with the increase of the feeding
rate. This is however expected as the dependence of the growth rate on biomass concentration is
exponential and the dependence of the feeding rate on the growth rate is linear.
For the scale-down design in this work, a pulse cycle time of 10 min was chosen. Lower cycle
times tested in pre-experiments (data not shown) did not allow to reach anaerobic regimes quickly
enough when turning off the oxygen, and were therefore disregarded. A cycle of 10 min can be
questioned, since cells experience shifts of metabolic regimes much quicker in large-scale reac-
tors [71]. Nevertheless, this design was used as a first test to see the outcome of a combined oxygen
and glucose SD and how SD experiments could be designed better in the future.
Another disadvantage of this design is that the overflow regime occurs only for a specific amount
of time during the pulse feeding, which results in a feast phase that is split in two; growth on
glucose with sufficient oxygen and growth on glucose without oxygen where the complete overflow
metabolism occurs. The SD design could be optimized by enabling a certain amount of overflow
during the substrate pulse by setting a specific stirrer speed value to allow a fixed, limiting amount
of oxygen to enter the bioreactor (Fig. A.3 in appendix A.4). This would better resemble the
scenario of a large bioreactor (Fig. 4.17 and A.1), where partial aerobic uptake of glucose and
partial anaerobic uptake of glucose occur at the same time. Although planned, due to technical
complications it was not possible to tightly control the exact amount of oxygen entering in the SD

reactors (data not shown in this thesis).
The SD experiment was designed to simulate 4 different scenarios; a reference bioreactor, a sub-
strate SD, an oxygen SD and a combined substrate and oxygen SD. From the SD experiments it
was shown that the highest phenotypic changes of the cells compared to the reference fermenta-
tion occured in the oxygen SD and the lowest in the substrate SD for both strains. The less amount
of biomass produced in the combined substrate and oxygen SD was redirected to carbon dioxide
production suggesting that E. coli needs more glucose for maintenance due to the stress condi-
tions which was also reflected in carbon balances (Fig. 4.24) [14]. The oxygen SD did produce
less biomass but did not show an increase in carbon dioxide production as less glucose was con-
sumed from 21 h in the CDR phase (Fig. 4.23) and therefore, less carbon was being transformed to
biomass, carbon dioxide or other by-products, also shown in the carbon balance because the abso-
lute values are lower then in all other cases (Fig. 4.24). Janoska et al. 2023, performed a scale-down
experiment with Corynebacterium glutamicum fluctuating dissolved oxygen and glucose alone and
combined in a fed-batch fermentation [10]. They found out that the glucose fluctuation resulted
in the same decrease in penicillin production than in the combined substrate and oxygen fluctua-
tions, while only the oxygen fluctuations showed less decline in penicillin production. That this is
not in accordance with the results shown in this work could be due to the fundamental differences
between the two microorganisms C. glutamicum and E. coli.
Kaboré et al. 2019 studied the impact of dissolved oxygen fluctuation alone in C. glutamicum fer-
mentation and found that higher anaerobic ratios, resulted in lower growth rates and macroscopic
yields, which is in accordance with the results reported in this study [72]. In their two compartment
model scale-down experiment, Sandoval et al. 2005 also showed that E. coli had production losses
of biomass and product when being limited with oxygen [73]. From the organic acid concentration
profiles (Fig. 4.23) it can be assumed that the cells experience TCA cycle flux alterations when
exposed to oxygen limitation. As in Lara et al’s 2009, more organic acids were produced when
the cells were exposed to oxygen limitations of any kind (only oxygen SD or combined substrate
and oxygen SD) [74]. The increase in acetate concentrations mainly occurred due to the lack of
oxygen in both SD cases where acetate concentrations are higher than the reference [75]. This in-
crease in acetate concentration over 8 mM leads to stagnant cell growth, which also explains why
less glucose is taken up by the cells [25]. The flux changes might also explain why formate, then
succinate and then lactate are used as substrate. From flavoproteins lactate can be converted to
pyruvate, which might explain why pyruvic acid concentrations go up when the lactate concen-
tration goes down for the WTS [76]. This is even further confirmed as the TPS does not show
any production of lactate and therefore pyruvic acid is also not produced. The TPS in oxygen SD
shows a decrease in tryptophan concentration that could occur because succinic acid is being as
substrate and tryptophan is not produced when E. coli grows on succinic acid [70]. The carbon
balances confirm the above mentioned observations. Comparing the yields of the SD experiments
to the batch experiment it was seen that the biomass yield was higher in the fed-batch compared
to the batch experiment for the WTS but not for the TPS. A higher yield from the fed-batch ex-
periment was expected as fed-batch enable to reach higher cell densities [77, 78]. The CO2 yield
coefficient YSC is very similar for both strains in the fed-batch experiment but higher compared to
the batch experiment phase (I) which could imply that the cells need more glucose for maintenance
in fed-batch cultures compared to batch cultures. The acetate yield coefficient YSA is much lower
for reference and substrate SD in fed-batch compared to batch experiment [79].
Scaling-down a large-scale bioreactor process comes with the challenge to chose an appropriate SD
method. Different SD approaches have pros and cons and have been shown to differently impact
scale-down results [9, 80, 40]. A fundamental distinction between single compartment scale-down
reactors and multi-compartment scale-down reactors lies in how cells are exposed to scaled-down
stress factors. In single compartment reactors, all cells experience identical conditions simultane-

==PAGE:62==
ously, which deviates from the conditions observed in large-scale reactors [31]. In contrast, opting
for multi-compartment systems introduces additional complexities such as compartment number,
compartment flow rates, and compartment time distribution to align with the conditions in the final
vessel. Similarly, when selecting a single compartment scale-down reactor, consideration of the
cycle time of oscillations becomes crucial. Moreover, the impact of the scale-down reactor itself,
like the continuous pumping in multi-compartment systems, must be thoroughly evaluated. This
study therefore, didn’t confirm how accurate the SD design mimic a large-scale process but in-
stead shows how E. coli would respond in those different SD designs and how they could be used
to screen for strains with industrial robustness. If assuming that the combined substrate and oxy-
gen SD is the SD representing the large-scale simulation the best, the TPS for example shows great
behaviour in large process scale as only a little less biomass was produced during the fermentation,
but a little more product was produced compared to the reference.

Conclusion and outlook
A complete workflow of SD experiment was successfully performed in this study. First defin-
ing the physiological growth parameters of the two strains used for the SD characterization in a
three phase batch fermentation. Then, simulating large-scale glucose and oxygen gradients via
compartment-model-based simulation with an E. coli kinetic model. To finally, use the two de-
termined SD parameters, the percentage of glucose starvation and the anaerobic ratio, from the
large-scale process simulation to design an IFR SD experiment and perform it in the AMBR® 250
system from Sartorius.
The compartment model simulations provided insights into the behavior of both strains in a large-
scale process and showed that the concentration gradient of substrate was more significant than
that of oxygen. The SD experiments showed that the oxygen SD induced the highest phenotypic
changes in both strains compared to the reference fermentation, while the substrate SD induced
the lowest changes. The choice of a suitable SD method is critical in scaling down a large-scale
process, as different approaches yield varied impacts. While the present study did not precisely
replicate large-scale conditions, it provided valuable insights into strain behavior under different
IFR SD designs and their potential use in screening robust strains for large-scale fermentation.
In future, analyses of the transcriptome through RNA sequencing could bring insights on which
genes are up- or down-regulated as stress response to concentration gradients in substrate or oxygen
in the SD setup. This could be used to modify genetically the strains to delete the genes inducing
those responses and therefore unnecessarily use up energy of the cells. In connection to that the
engineered strains could then more easily be screened in a high throughput fermentation system
as the AMBR® 250 system from Sartorius to confirm the robustness of the strain in SD conditions
and therefore maybe also in large process scale.
