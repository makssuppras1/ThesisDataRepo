Abstract
Multi-port extruded (MPE) tubes are a crucial component in the automotive industry fre-
quently being used as heat exchangers to optimize the efficiency of both combustion engine-
and electrical vehicles. Hydro Extrusions produce several variants of MPE tubes some of which
exhibit abnormal grain growth (AGG) after the final brazing treatment. In this work, the mi-
crostructural evolution of MPE tubes expected to develop AGG upon brazing was charac-
terized before and after annealing, and compared to that of MPE tubes which do not nor-
mally exhibit AGG. The longtitudinal section in an internal wall was investigated using electron
backscatter diffraction (EBSD) and traditional light microscopy to reveal the microstructure
and obtain data on crystallographic orientations. Additionally, the distribution of dispersoids
and intermetallic particles were characterized in the same cross-section. No influence of raw
material nor presence of zinc-arc spray coating was found, but significant differences were ob-
served in the spatial distribution of texture components and grain sizes. For the specimens
expected to exhibit AGG a distinct symmetric layered structure was discovered. This was not
apparent in the specimens which were not expected to exhibit AGG. The layers were defined
by varying dominant texture components and additionally exhibited an oscillating grain size
behavior across the specimen width in the normal direction. The texture component variations
did not follow the same oscillating pattern as the grain size. Likewise, the distribution of dis-
persoids were observed to vary, however not exhibiting symmetry. The varying distribution of
dispersoids and texture components were speculated to influence AGG in certain layers of the
examined specimens.

1. Introduction
1.1. Motivation
1.1.1. Background
For many years, cars have been the most versatile form of transportation humans have had ac-
cess to. They have long range, decent maneuverability and work well in almost any climate.
Many aspects contribute to this flexibility - one being the components in charge of heating,
ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) in the car. These components not only provide com-
fort during use by heating up the cabin to pleasant temperatures, but also ensure efficiency
when driving the car in temperatures that are sub-optimal for its mechanical aspects.
Cars, whether they are electric or of the traditional combustion type, generate heat when oper-
ating. If thisis not removed, the efficiencydropsandifsevereheatisgeneratedeithertheengine
or the batteries risk getting damaged. In a traditional combustion engine car this issue is solved
by placing a condenser heat pump in the front of the car where there is access to plenty of cold
ambient air. The condenser tubes are then connected to so-called evaporator tubes that are
placed on the warm parts of the engine block. The cold ambient air cools down the refrigerant
thereby condensing it such that when it passes over the engine block where it is evaporated, the
pressure drops taking away heat from the engine in the process. Cycling refrigerant through
the system like this thus results in removal of heat from the engine block. A similar system can
be installed on the battery packs in electric vehicles to either cool them down or heat them up,
optimizing efficiency in all types of weather.
1.1.2. Project aim
The condenser and evaporator are commonly made from multi-port extruded (MPE) tubes
such as those shown in Figure 1.1. These tubes have high heat conductivity due to their large
surface area, and are brazed together after extrusion to form the larger HVAC component. A
schematic overview of the production steps of such component is shown in Figure 1.2 where
steps in green squares indicate the state of the material and the processing method to get to that
state is given together with the arrows leading up to it [1]. The red square indicates the state of
the material examined in the present thesis.
One of the most common materials for such MPE tubes is aluminium. A multitude of alloys
exist specifically for heat exchanger purposes and many of these do not follow the standards for
naming Aluminium alloys. In the present study, a so-called AA9108 produced by Hydro Extru-
sions (from hereon called ’Hydro’) which is used specifically for MPE heat exchanger tubes is

1.2. Overview of the multi-port extrusion technique
Contrary to regular extrusion, multi-port extrusion produces linear profiles with several chan-
nels. This entails a more complex die design usually consisting of an upper- and a lower die as
well as a removable mandrel [3], [4]. These three components together make up a porthole die.
The upper die has two portholes separated by a port bridge which holds the mandrel. When the
raw material is heated, and upon yielding forced into the extrusion die, it splits in two sections
which flow around the port bridge entering the two portholes. Upon flowing in between the
mandrel teeth the internal walls, called webs, are formed, and where the two halves meet the
material is seam welded forming a region which is known to have a different microstructure
than the rest of the tube [3]. After seam welding, the material exits through the bearing in the
lower die, which forms the final outer geometry of the extrudate. For micro-channel tubes it is
not uncommon to see extrusion ratios (defined as the cross section area of the container over
the area of the extrudates) on the scale of 1000. This introduces a plethora of problems such as
high extrusion loads, and large die deflection which negatively impact the geometry of the ex-
trudates and shortens the life-time of the die. Therefore, several porthole dies may be inserted
into a die holder, all to be fed by a single billet of raw material thereby lowering the extrusion
ratio [4].

1.3. Annealing phenomena in polycrystalline materials
1.3.1. Fundamental annealing phenomena
As the extrusion takes place at elevated temperatures, and the MPE tubes are subsequently
brazed together essentially adding an annealing step, thermally activated processes take place
during all manufacturing steps. When subjecting a metal to such elevated temperatures a wide
range of annealing phenomena can occur such as recovery, recrystallization and grain growth.
For rolled materials, these processes usually take place statically, lowering the free energy of the
material by removing dislocations and interfaces which were introduced during the plastic de-
formation. For aluminium and its alloys however, dynamic recovery and recrystallization are
likely to occur during extrusion due their high stacking fault energy enabling rapid dislocation
climb [3], [5].

the boundary has some intrinsic mobility, M, the velocity is often described as [6]:

the mobility and the pressure can be affected by numerous factors. Among the most influential
are temperature, texture, solutes, and particles [6], [7]. In thepresent thesis, especiallytheeffects
of texture and small particles will be examined.
1.3.2. Abnormal grain growth
Abnormal grain growth (AGG) is a phenomenon in which some grains in a polycrystalline ma-
terial grow to become abnormally large, compared to the rest of the grains. This should be
averted as it can adversely affect the properties, especially the yield strength as dictated by the
Hall-Petch relationship [8]. But while it may be trivial to avoid in some scenarios, the low ex-
trusion temperatures and high deformation required to maintain a sustainable productivity has
been observed to promote AGG [9]. Thus, if the features leading to AGG can be controlled, it
is possible to maintain a high productivity in the extrusion plant while preserving the strength
of the MPE tube. Therefore, understanding the mechanisms and factors that influence AGG in
aluminium alloys is important for optimizing the MPE manufacturing process and improving
the performance of these micro profiles.
1.4. Working principle of light optical microscopy
In the present work light optical microscopy (LOM) will be utilized which is one of the most
fundamental characterization techniques for materials scientists. Able to quickly reveal in-
formation about the surface of a material it is both incredibly versatile and easy to use. In the
present study, a technique called polarized light microscopy will be utilized to enhance the con-
trast of the optically anisotropic aluminium oxide layer formed on the surface of the MPE tubes

1.5. Measuring textures using electron backscatter diffraction
In materials science the term texture is used to describe the distribution of crystallographic ori-
entations in a polycrystalline material such as a metal. If a metal has no texture the distribution
appears random but this is rarely the case in finished products since manufacturing metals usu-
ally gives rise to some preferential grain orientations, yielding a significant texture. This is of
importance since the macroscopic properties depend on the texture of the material and may
become anisotropic when strong textures are present.
Being able to quantify the texture of metals has proven extremely important, and today a com-
mon technique for doing so is utilizing Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD) [11]. This
method is performed in a scanning electron microscope (SEM) where a stationary high energy
electron beam is aimed at the sample. The setup is shown schematically in Figure 1.5. Some
of the electrons generate, among others, backscattered electrons (BSEs), some of which will
satisfy the Bragg condition given by equation 1.2. This gives rise to constructive interference
which can be detected on a phosphor screen [12]. Equation 1.2 is a fundamental relationship
that stipulates the condition for diffraction of a set of crystallographic planes where 𝜃is the
Bragg angle, 𝑛is the order of reflection, 𝜆is the wavelength of the electron, and 𝑑ℎ𝑘𝑙is the
interplanar spacing [12]–[14].
𝑛𝜆= 2𝑑ℎ𝑘𝑙sin 𝜃
(1.2)
Due to rotational symmetry of the planes the diffracted pattern is in fact a cone shape, giving
rise to two parallel lines on the detector as schematically illustrated in Figure 1.6a. Together
these parallel lines form what is known as a Kikuchi band which is the reason the technique has
been referred to by several other acronyms besides EBSD, such as EBSP (referring to the pattern
arising) and BKD (backscatter Kikuchi diffraction) [12]. The angles between different bands
correspond to the angles between diffracting planes and the angular width of the band {hkl} is
twice the Bragg angle as indicated in Figure 1.6b and thus related to the interplanar spacing by
Equation 1.2 [12]. Using this information, computer indexing of Kikuchi patterns can be used
to relatively quickly obtain delicate information about the texture, the grain structure, grain
boundaries (GBs) etc [12].

2.2.4. Considerations when mounting the specimens
The samples were initially thought to be mounted using a regular Struers Prontopress, but sev-
eral issues may emerge from embedding when using the common thermoplast powders avail-
able at DTU. Firstly, the temperatures reached during such an embedding can be as high as
180◦C which may induce changes in the microstructure. This is undesirable for obvious rea-
sons. Secondly, when observing the TD/ND section, previous experience has shown that the
powder is unlikely to properly enter small cavities such as the channels present in that plane.
Thus dirt and grime from the subsequent grinding and polishing procedure will be drawn into
thecavitiesbycapillaryaction, makingitextremelydifficulttoprepareasurfacefineenoughfor
EBSD measurements. Thirdly, when preparing specimens for observing the ED/ND section,
the grinding procedure is complicated since the embedding material is opaque which makes it
difficult to determine how much material is left to grind away. Therefore, traditional warm-
embedding was foregone and cold-embedding using a curable resin was considered as the al-
ternative. However, this solution also presents issues. While it would be possible to carbon coat
the samples or drill holes in the embedding to make it conductive enabling anodizing and use
in SEM, the resin cannot easily withstand the vacuum in the SEM. Therefore, EBSD data from
specimens embedded in such a way cannot be obtained without contaminating the microscope
considerably. Besides the additional cleaning of the SEM, this would likely also lead to many
unindexed points and mediocre data at best, so for these reasons, cold-embedding was also
deemed impracticable. It was however used for the few specimens in bold in Table 2.3 which
were to have the TD/ND section prepared for LOM investigations since the main issues related

2.2.5. Grinding and polishing procedure
Grinding method
Grinding away the volume marked in yellow in Figure 2.1a proved to be somewhat challenging.
Since the specimens had not been cut in exactly the same location and the webs of these MPE
tubes are so thin, the error tolerance during grinding was quite small. Therefore, grinding was
done by hand one specimen at a time until the correct central web had been exposed using SiC
papers of 500, 1000, and 4000 grits. Specimens were cleaned using soap, water, ethanol and
ultrasound in between each step. A technique was developed where a miniature clamp was laid
on a block of paper and the specimen was inserted face down. By pushing gently on the back-
side of the specimen when tightening the clamp, the area exposed to grinding could be finely
controlled. In this way, every specimen was successfully ground exposing the desired web. For
a few specimens, the cut had gone through the channel next to the central web meaning the
center-most web had been directly exposed to the heat during annealing. Because this could
induce variations in the microstructure, e.g. from this web heating up faster, it was decided to
12
==PAGE:28==
Jan-Jun 2023
Masters thesis
Tobias H. Johansen
remove the central web and expose the neighbouring one for examinations instead. After each
specimen had been individually ground to 4000 grit, the four specimens of each sample were
mounted as described in section 2.2.4. The mounts with specimens were then subsequently
ground on 4000 grit until the specimens were perfectly level with the mount to ensure the best
condition for later polishing.
Polishing steps
Polishing was performed in a series of steps on a Struers Rotopol-22 with a RotoForce 4 at-
tached. The first step utilized a Struers MOL disc, 3μm diamonds and red lubricant, and the
second one was done on a Struers CHEM disc applying a polishing agent consisting of 50%
OP-S+50% water. Results from examinations at this stage hinted that more polishing would
be needed to obtain useful results so a third step was added utilizing the CHEM disc applying
OP-U as a polishing agent instead of OP-S. For all procedures, the machine was set to 150rpm
and the downforce was regulated during the procedure as described in the following.
Extensive examinations revealed that for the procedure on the MOL disc, anything below six
minutes was insufficient as a few scratches on some specimens were preserved even after six
minutes. Therefore all specimens were polished for 12 minutes with 3μm diamonds. The first
four minutes was done with 15N, the next four with 10N and the final four with 5N. This is
more force than is commonly utilized when polishing in this way, but since the samples were
mounted in metal holders and not in a polymer encasing, it was decided to increase the down-
force compared to that from common instructions. On the CHEM disc, the specimens were
polished for 6 minutes with OP-S and OP-U respectively following a similar varying of the
down force as on the MOL disc. The process is summarized in the following points:
1. MOL disc: 12 minutes (4min 15N, 4min 10N, 4 min 5N), 3μm diamonds, red lubricant.
2. CHEM disc: 6 min (2min 15N, 2min 10N, 2min 5N) with OP-S followed by 1 min at 5N
with water.
3. CHEM disc: 6 min (2min 15N, 2min 10N, 2min 5N) with OP-U followed by 1 min at 5N
with water.
After each step, the specimens were cleaned with water and a mixture of soap and water. Using
a gentle thumb, residue was then washed away which proved important as it served to remove
both residual polishing agent and hairs from the MOL disc which would occasionally get stuck
in between the specimens. The holders were then cleaned with ethanol and dried. Extensive
drying was needed, especially after the final procedure, to make sure liquid in the MPE tubes
and between the specimens had evaporated. After polishing, the specimens were kept in exica-
tor chambers for at least 24h before being used in the SEM.

Grain reconstruction
Grain reconstruction was performed using the in-built function in MTEX, calcGrains, which
subdivides the specimen area into grains based on a user-defined misorientation threshold.
This threshold defining a high- and low-angle grain boundary (HAGB and LAGB) is commonly
set between 10◦-15◦and was set to 10◦for all maps in the present work. As it will be seen
the percentage of indexed points was relatively high for the investigated samples with most
reaching upwards of 90%. Therefore, while MTEX offers several options with regards to how
to handle unindexed points, it was decided to let the majority of them be assigned to the closest
grain with the exception of unindexed points near the map edges. Small grains (<4 pixels) and
inclusions were removed. Finally, grain boundaries were smoothed to avoid stair-case effects
which may artificially increase boundary length.

4. Discussion
4.1. Scope of discussion
There are any aspects of a large project such as the present one that deserve to be discussed. In
relation to the main motivation behind this project, the following chapter will primarily focus
on discussing the parameters that may be important in explaining why AGG occurs in some
of the Hydro MPEs and not in others. It is clear that without obtaining data from the ED/ND
section of a web that exhibits AGG, the correlation between the collected results and the AGG
observed in Figure 1.4 and 3.13 comes with a caveat. That said, knowing that the abnormal
grains after annealing are located in the region between S=∼0.16 and S=∼0.80 a foundation
for speculating upon their origin is provided.
4.2. Speculations regarding the effect of dispersoids on AGG
4.2.1. Zener pinning from small dispersoids
The reported abnormally grown grains appear to originate from somewhere between S=±0.16
and S=±0.80. All but those in the rounded part of the MPE halt their growth upon reaching
the seam weld region surrounding S=0. In the seam weld band it was found that the size of
dispersoids is comparatively small. This may explain why boundary migration is lower in that
region as it agrees with the known relation dictating that smaller dispersoids exert a larger drag
force, P, on a moving boundary according the to the relationship in Equation 4.1 [16].

by particles of radius, r and volume fraction f [16]. The same agreement is found in the band
S=0.3 to S=0.67 where the dispersoids are large allowing the grains to grow bigger. However,
this does not concur with the observations in the band from S=-0.3 to S=-0.67 in which the
dispersoids are significantly smaller than in the seam weld, while the average grain size is still
large. One possible explanation might be that the number density of dispersoids in that band is
so high, that despite their smaller radius, the net drag force exerted by them is still larger than in
neighbouring bands effectively reducing the local boundary mobility. A different speculation,
which is supported by the lack of a presumed symmetry in dispersoid distribution, could be that
the measured area was not aligned with the specimen, meaning the center of the measured area
was not the center of the specimen. If this was the case, the measured area of approximately

4.2.2. Addressing dispersoid-assisted abnormal grain growth
In 2015, Holm et al. [17] proposed a new mechanism termed ’particle-assisted abnormal grain
growth’ in which they simulated grain growth in 3D volumes using a Monte Carlo Potts model.
They found that with a volume fraction of 8% pinning dispersoids some normal grain growth
would occur until an approximately Smith-Zener limited grain size distribution was achieved.
However, increasingthevolumefractionto10%, forlonganneals, afewgrainboundarieswould
thermally fluctuate away from its pinning dispersoids, and if the parent grain was inclined to
grow, this would do so abnormally as the neighbouring grain boundaries remained immobile.
Since very little grain growth occurred in the specimens examined in the present study, it is
speculated that the local number density and size of dispersoids in the investigated specimens
were at a level such that rather than AGG, these specimens upon annealing obtained a stable
Smith-Zener limited grain size distribution. From the earlier discussion, this means that in
local regions with either lower or higher volume fractions of pinning dispersoids, AGG could
occur due to either reduced pinning force or these fluctuations. That there might be such local
variations is very likely, and would explain why the same webs exhibited AGG in Figure 3.13 as
both the H30_BY+ and H60_BY+ came from the same tube and thus are likely to have similar
dispersoid distributions. In the model Holm et al. [17] reported they found no single marker to
indicate which grains would grow abnormally, and suggest further study of the local density of
dispersed particles which is seconded by this study.
4.3. Possible effects of solutes on AGG
Besides Zener pinning, one could reasonably expect that solute drag may affect the forma-
tion of AGG. Commonly, solute atoms are preferentially located in grain boundaries where the
increased presence of free volume means that the solutes can reduce their energy by being lo-
cated here. Comparing the misorientation in the intermediate bands from which the AGG is
suspected to originate in the BY+ sample, it becomes clear that the S=0.3 to S=0.67 band in BY+
contains considerably more LAGBs (defined here as <10◦) than the same band in sample AY+
as illustrated in Figure 4.1a-d. This is true for both the annealed and as received specimens and
may contribute to preferential growth of some grains as the lower amount of HAGBs could
mean that the BY+ specimen experiences a reduced solute drag. This however, requires further
investigations to confirm.

4.4. Effects of local variations in grain size and texture components
From the results it is clear that there are especially large differences in the spatial distribution
of extrusion texture between sample AY+ and BY+. In all the specimens from the BY+ sample
distinct bands were observed between which the dominating texture components varied sig-
nificantly. This was in stark contrast to what was observed in the AY+ specimens in which the
spatial distribution of extrusion texture seemed considerably more random. It is known that
the local texture can affect grain growth as it may create a nonuniform distribution of bound-
ary energies and mobilities [16]. As HAGBs have significantly higher mobilities than LAGBs
[6], and since large grains have a growth advantage compared to small grains, some of the large
grains at the boundaries of the S=0.3 to S=0.67 band in specimens from the BY+ sample are in-
clined to grow. They do so preferentially because those large grains are surrounded by bands of
small grains of nearly identical crystallographic orientation meaning growing grains can con-
tinue to form HAGBs upon growing into the bands with small grains. This would not be the
case in the AY+ sample and may explain why BY+ exhibits AGG while AY+ does not.

5. Conclusions
MPE tubes are a crucial component for use in the automotive industry, and will remain impor-
tant as they are used in both combustion engine cars and electrical vehicles. With continuous
downgauging of the MPE wall thickness new challenges arise in many aspects of the field, and
one of them is the formation of AGG after brazing treatments. The findings from the present
work are summarized below.
• ThegeometryoftheMPEtubesmadesamplepreparationsdifficult. However, onemethod
for preparing specimens was discovered enabling studies of the microstructural evolu-
tion in the ED/ND section of an internal web. The method involved individual grinding
ofspecimensuntiltheappropriatewebhadbeenexposedafterwhichmechanicalmount-
ing was utilized to hold a series of specimens.
• EBSD was found to be a good technique for obtaining data on crystallographic orienta-
tions and grain sizes in the specimens, with most scans reaching upwards of 90% indexed
points.
• Few differences were found between samples AY÷, AY+, and AX+. Both grain sizes, and
texture appeared similar regardless of raw material and presence of zinc-coating.
• A significant difference between AY+ and BY+ was observed in terms of the spatial distri-
bution of the texture components. Contrary to observations from specimens pertaining
to the AY+ sample a distinct layered structure was observed in all BY+ specimens. In
these layers, or ’bands’, the average grain size was found to oscillate and be very depen-
dent on the distance to the seam weld. Additionally, the dominant texture component
varied greatly between the bands. Howeverthetexturevariationsdid notmatchthegrain
size oscillations.
• Upon annealing, the average grain size in the BY+ specimens was found to slightly in-
crease in all bands except the central band in which the seam weld was located where the
average grain size remained largely stable. Overall, this lead to the average grain size in
each specimen increasing only marginally for all the examined annealing conditions.
• In the central band, a sharp <001> texture along the ED was observed in all specimens
from sample AY+ and BY+, both before and after annealing. Bands closer to the surface
had varying dominant texture components with large differences between specimens
from sample AY+ and BY+ being observed in the intermediate bands between S=0.14
and S=0.80. After annealing, the dominant texture component in the band S=0.14 to

S=0.3 was similar in both samples, but the differences between texture components in
the remaining bands persisted.
• IMPs were characterized in the ED/ND section of the central web of the AR and H30
specimens from samples AY+ and BY+. An apparent symmetry was observed for the IMP
size and number density distribution. The IMP number density in each band was found
to oscillate following the same pattern as the grain sizes and the average IMP size was

• Dispersoids were characterized in the same region as IMPs for the H30_BY+ specimen.

• No AGG was observed in the ED/ND section for any of the short annealing treatments

longer annealing times of 30 and 60 minutes at 600◦C and only in the TD/ND section.
The abnormally grown grains were systematically located in the intermediate bands.
• The migration of the abnormal grain appeared to stop upon reaching the seam weld band
and the surface band. In the seam weld band the dispersoids were found to be smaller

mobilities, which may be a contributing factor in the observed AGG.

6. Outlook
Examining AGG is no trivial task as the abnormal growth events are rare and stochastic. Ob-
viously, with more time it would have provided great value to find AGG in the ED/ND section
and examine that further using the methods used in the present thesis. In addition to the work
done in the present project, the following additional studies are likely to further increase the
understanding of AGG formation in the examined MPE tubes.
(i) Transmission electron microscopy investigations could provide valuable insights into the
local variations of dispersoid number density- and size at a level of detail not possible
with SEM techniques. These parameters are likely to affect the grain growth behavior so
obtaining this data would thus provide further insight into the formation of AGG. Ad-
ditionally, one could investigate the dislocation density at grain boundaries suspected to
grow abnormally as gradients in dislocation density even after recrystallization has been
shown to lead to AGG [18].
(ii) A multimodal workflow combining EBSD using a small step-size and BSE imaging such as
thatproposedin[19] couldfurtherilluminatetheinteractionsbetweendifferentdominant
texture components and dispersoids. In particular, one could relate misorientation data
to Smith-Zener drag which may aid in revealing from which band the AGG originates.
(iii) 4D scans (x,y,z,t) using techniques such as laboratory diffraction contrast tomography [20],
could provide detailed knowledge of the local grain boundary migration not obtainable by
EBSD. By following a small number of select grains one could obtain novel information
of the parameters that determine whether a grain is inclined to abnormally grow or not.
