Abstract
The world aquaculture production of macro algae is growing at a rate of 7.4% in the past
decade, but the production is very limited to Asia and only few advances have been done
in Europe. Nevertheless the potential of cultivating and utilising this valuable resource is
enormous, and many interesting products can be extracted or derived from it.
The macro algae Saccharina latissima is of special interest due to its high amounts of mono-
and polysaccharides, glucose, mannitol, laminarin and alginates, making it a great source for
bioprocessing in fermentation plants. The ease of cultivation of this algae in ropes in the
open sea makes it a promising candidate for large scale production in Europe.
A cost-eﬀective and biosustainable protocol for the extraction and production of bioproducts
from this algae remains to be elucidated, despite many proposals from the scientiﬁc com-
munity. The purpose of this study was to use the bioreﬁnery approach - placing Saccharina
latissima as the biomass source for extraction of pigments and subsequent treatment of waste
for the production of interesting compounds, in this case lactic acid - with the goal of adding
value to the biomass.
Common solvent extraction with ethanol was used to remove the pigments from both good
quality and fouling epiphyte-rich algae. For the remaining powders, composed of the algae
polysaccharides, a hydrolysis process was developed to hydrolyse it to monosaccharides. The
media was then used for the cultivation of four strains of lactic acid bacteria in screw cap
bottles with gentle magnetic stirring. Lactic acid production was assessed by HPLC and
optimal combinations of strain and media composition was evaluated.
Pigment extraction showed overestimated numbers compared to the literature, and it is sus-
pected the extraction was not performed correctly. Hydrolysis of the residual biomass with
sulphuric acid alone was not suﬃcient, specially in regards to hydrolysis alginate, but with
addition of enzymes the monosaccharides concentrations achieved acceptable levels.
The
cultivation of the strains in the hydrolysate media was not viable but when supplied with
sugar-free MRS, the best performing strain, Lactobacillus pentosus AH240, could produce
11.77g/L lactic acid when stationary phase was reached. Statistical analysis showed that the
use of fouling biomass resulted in considerably lower lactic acid production compared to the
good quality algae samples.
In conclusion, the work presented throughout this report proves the possibility of using
S.latissima to produce both pigments and lactic acid, but further research is advisable to
establish optimal conditions and maximise yields.

Introduction
1.1 Problem Statement
Seaweeds are a relatively unexplored resource in Europe, despite their large presence and
abundance throughout the coastlines of many countries. Asia holds the great majority of the
seaweed production/capturing and uses it mainly as food products and additives. In Europe,
research has focused a great deal on using the biomass as the basis for bioethanol production.
Though bioethanol is a great potential product to derive from it, more value-added products
could be explored.
Tons of seaweed in both good and bad quality are washed ashore each year and depending on
the environment and nutrient conditions, the seaweed might undergo a fouling process, mak-
ing it unattractive to direct human consumption. Finding alternative uses of this biomass,
specially the one not suitable for human consumption, is key to a more biosustainable utili-
sation of marine resources.
The aim of this study is to investigate the feasibility of using Saccharina latissima, a polysac-
charide rich seaweed, as the substrate for the production of food ingredients. Speciﬁcally, the
use of fouling plants is investigated, and in order to add more value, a bioreﬁnery approach
is employed. Firstly the pigments are extracted, and later the residual biomass, waste from
the process, transformed into the product of interest - lactic acid.
The project has the following targets:
• Characterisation of the residual biomass after pigment extraction, regarding the algae
monosaccharides and amino acid proﬁle.
• Degradation of polysaccharides through hydrolysis with acid and enzymes to create a

==PAGE:12==

media based on residual biomass.
• Screening of interesting fermentation organisms to transform algae biomass into food
ingredients.
• Determine the feasibility of using the residual biomass for fermentation of potential
products.
It is hypothesised that the residual of pigment solvent extraction of the macroalgae (white/-
light brown powder) would be a polysaccharide and ﬁber rich material and thereby a good
resource of monosaccharides if hydrolysed.
Many researchers have studied the hydrolysis
of macroalgae, but to our knowledge none have looked into this waste product that could
pose a lot of potential. Further we hypothesize that by applying a bioreﬁnery approach and
generating more products from the macroalgae, the value added to the biomass could enable
sustainable production.
1.2 Algae resources
In this section the state of algae production is described to give an overview of the market.
Both the situation worldwide (mainly Asia) and in Europe, including the Danish potential,
is discussed.
1.2.1 Production worldwide
The production of aquatic plants is monitored by the United Nations’ agency Food and Agri-
culture Organization (FAO) and reported every second year in their report "The State of
World Fishery and Aquaculture".
According to FAO’s latest report [10] the world production in 2012 accounted for 24.9 mil-
lion tonnes of wet weight of aquatic plants. The statistic includes: macroalgae cultivation
in marine/brackish waters, which is the dominant part of the production; microalgae culti-
vation, specially Spirulina sp.; as well as capture of wild seaweed, accounting for 1.1 million
tonnes.
China is by far the greatest producer with over 50% of the world’s share. China’s production
has had an almost 10-fold increase over the past 25 years, with about 1.5 million tonnes in
1990 to 12.8 million tonnes in 2012. The development of a Japanese kelp strain that could
withstand the warmer waters of China’s coast has played an important role. Most impres-
sive is Indonesia’s production, currently the second largest, that accounted for small 100,000
tonnes in 1990 but through a national policy of blue growth in the 2000’s has increased its
production to 6.5 million tonnes in 2012. Together the two countries detain 81% of the world
production. Other Asian countries are big producers as the Philippines, Korea, Japan and
Malaysia.

A great deal of the cultivated algae is directed to human consumption in products recogniz-

1.2.2 Seaweed in Europe
There has not been a strong tradition for eating seaweed in Europe. Though seaweed have
been collected from the coast of France, Spain and Ireland for a long time, and they do have
played a role in hunger times throughout the centuries [6]. In the occidental world the con-

sumption of whole seaweed will probably continue to be limited due to culture acceptance,
therefore requiring large marketing strategies if they are to become common consumer goods
[7].
Seaweed cultivation in Europe is still taking it’s ﬁrst steps, not many algal farms are estab-
lished and most of production is still gathered from the wild. In Europe the seaweed market
is also a very much research-driven aﬀair, more than a marked based on consumer demand
[18]. This is specially true when it comes to bioactive compounds that can be extracted
from seaweeds and used in cosmeceuticals and functional foods, and even pharmaceuticals.
Researchers and the industry are looking into interesting compounds in hope of developing
products to be marketed as functional and healthy, and later be accepted by the consumer.
The latest focus in the european market is transforming seaweed from a commodity to value
added products [18].
The countries leading the production in Europe are Ireland, Norway and France. Ireland had
a macroalgae production of 36,000 wet tons in 2008, where most seaweed were collected from
wild resources and only 5 wet tons estimated to be produced by aquaculture. The main part
of this production goes into extraction of hydrocolloids [7]. If activities related to algae based
functional products are to be increased, it is likely that an expansion of aquaculture would
be necessary. There is much research into strain development, hatchery conditions, cultiva-
tion methods, mechanisation of production, such that the sector could be better established
in Europe, but nevertheless there has been a production decrease in the past decades [33].
Another major factor holding the industry is regulatory aﬀairs, and there is a need for clearer
regulations in the ﬁeld.
1.2.3 Potentials of seaweed in Denmark
Denmark has always taken much advantage of the sea in many diﬀerent ways, but seaweed
has not been an exploited resource until recently. Even tough seaweed are generally not in
the menu, Denmark do have a tradition in utilising the biomass. From the 60’s there has been
production of hydrocolloids in the country, and main companies in the ﬁeld have a connection
to Denmark, as Danisco, FMC Biopolymers and CP Kelpo. The last mentioned company
holds the biggest carrageenan factory in the world just outside of Copenhagen, importing
7,000 tons of semi dried algae yearly to be processed [12].
Despite having processing sites, as mentioned, there are no real large-scale industrial aquacul-
ture production in Danish waters. Recently a few algal farms have been stablished, but much
is still in small scale (Hjarnoe Havbrug A/S) and/or are still small "family-based" companies
(Ocean Rainforest Sp/F in the Faroe Islands). Other companies either collect algae directly
from the sea and the beach by hand or import from other European countries to further
process or retail in Denmark. The research and interest in algae is nevertheless present and

1.3 Macroalgae
Several products can be obtained from seaweed (macroalgae). There is a great variety of
species and their cellular composition can vary, being much inﬂuenced by cultivation condi-
tions, nutrient availability and season of the year. In this section the classiﬁcation will be
described, along with the interesting compounds relating to the focus of the project, pigments
and polysaccharides. The biomass used in the project, Saccharina latissima, is also described.
1.3.1 Classiﬁcation
Algae are classiﬁed into two main groups: microalgae and macro algae. The ﬁrst being com-
posed of small unicellular eukaryotes, and the second of multicellular organism, that can vary
from a few mm to more than 30m in size. Macro algae can be further divided according to
their pigment contents into: brown algae, Phaeophycea; red algae, Rhodophyta; and green
algae, Chlorophyta [2]. Examples of them can be seen in ﬁgure 1.3. In Denmark there are
about 500 species of macroalgae, but only a dozen or so would actually be collected and
consumed [32].
Figure 1.3: Example of Danish macroalgae: red algae Palmaria palmata, brown algae Saccharina
latissima, and green algae Ulva lactuca. Pictures from [2]
1.3.2 Compounds of interest
Pigments
There are three groups of pigments: Carotenoids, chlorophylls and phycobiliproteins. They
can exhibit many beneﬁcial properties, as antibacterial, antifungal, antioxidative, antiin-
ﬂamatory, anti-tumor and antiviral. These health promoting characteristics and especially
the antioxidative activity make them very valuable in the food and cosmeceutical industry [7].

linear polynes that harvest light and have antioxidant activities, inactivating reactive oxygen

Polysaccharides
The total polysaccharides content in interesting macroalgae from Northwest Europe ranges
from 4% to 76% of dry weight [19], varying much between species and season of the year.
Most of it is dietary ﬁbres, not readily taken up by the human gut. The cell wall in algae is
generally composed of cellulose and hemicellulose and lignin is only found in Ulva sp.
Apart from their structure and energy storage roles, polysaccharides from algae are also sus-
pected of having many health promoting properties. But despite the suspicion, up until 2011
no claim has been supported by EFSA for algae polysaccharides[7], though much research
have shown anticoagulant activity as well as antibacterial, along with prebiotic properties
that support the growth of beneﬁcial gut micro ﬂora.
Algae polysaccharides are already widely used in the food industry as mentioned above and
often referred as hydrocolloids, such as agar and carrageenans - sulphated galactans. These
are extracted from red algae and used specially for their gelling, thickening and stabilising
properties. Carrageenan have also been investigated for its antiviral and anticoagulant ac-
tivity, though it is also suspected of provoking an inﬂammatory reaction and ulceration of
the colon, possibly associated with its degraded compound, therefore limits of < 5% of small
molecular weight molecules in food have been recommended by the European Comission [7].
Brown algae contains alginic acid, laminarin(beta-1-3-glucan), sargassan and fucoidan (sul-
phated fucose). In addition, the monosaccharide mannitol is also present in Laminaria and
Saccharina species. The fucoidans are rich in L-fucose and are investigated as an alternative
to heparin due to their anticoagulant activity, also its anti-inﬂammatory and antiviral activ-
ities are investigated. Alginate can be found in both acid form (alginic acid) and salt form,
they are widely used in the food industry together with the other phycocolloids. This dietary
ﬁbre has been shown to decrease colesterol concentrations and body weight, and in addition

1.3.3 Saccharina latissima
The biomass used in this study is Saccharina latissima and it can be seen in ﬁgure 1.4. It is a
macroalgae very much present in the sea of northern Europe and already being cultivated at
a small scale in Denmark. It belongs to the family Laminariaceae, being formed by a lamina,
a ﬂexible stipe and a holdfast, that diﬀerently from land plants is not a root. The individuals
can live 2-5 years and reach a length of 4m, the age of the algae can be assessed by annual
growth rings in the stipe, that is perennial, whereas the old lamina is shed when a new one
starts growing from the stipe in the winter.

1.4 Bioreﬁning - platforms to optimise biomass utilisation
In this section a brief description of the bioreﬁning concept and the current status of the
techniques used in the project is addressed, such as the extraction of pigments and the fer-
mentation of waste biomass.
9
==PAGE:20==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Bioreﬁnery has traditionally been using edible crops as the biomass base for the synthesis of
valuable compounds, but the pressure on the global food stock puts other biomass in focus,
such as the micro and macro algae. The macroalgae as described above, detains many highly
valuable compounds and a high carbohydrate content, though the seasonal variation is a
drawback compared to land plants. If the seasonal diﬀerences can be managed it also oﬀers
many possibilities.
Maximizing the biomass utilization is the aim of bioreﬁning, thereby reducing the waste and
increasing proﬁtability. Goncalo et al. [28] points out the importance for the cost-eﬀectiveness
use of the biomass that it should be able to provide both low-value high-volume and also high-
value low-volume products in a bioreﬁnery set-up.
Determining the products of interest is the ﬁrst step in designing a platform, the unit oper-
ations must then take into consideration the physicochemical properties of the compounds
downstream. This is a challenge as the individual yields might be compromised by the pro-
cess’ choice that best suits all interesting products [28].
1.4.1 Extraction methods
In this study the ﬁrst step in the process is the extraction of pigments. The following is a
brief review of this technique.
From the 1950’s up to 1971 the traditional method used to extract pigments from algae was
by using acetone alone as the organic solvent. In 1972 Seely et al [36] developed a method
using dimethyl sulfoxide followed by acetone, which improved extraction. Their method was
subsequently the preferred one to extract pigments from brown algae throughout the 70’s
and 80’s. Following, methanol began to be investigated as an option for the second solvent
in the extraction, showing better results than acetone [8] in the extraction of phytoplankton.
Later more techniques have been developed in the quest to ﬁnd a nontoxic and more envi-
ronmentally friendly method as an option to the harsh organic solvent used.
To extract pigments, the alga has to be processed readily after harvesting, washed from im-
purities, then dried and milled. In the case of this study, the algae was rapidly stored at
−20◦C without salt removal. Further it needs to undertake a pre treatment to disrupt the
cell wall. This can be in the form of mechanical, thermal, physical, chemical or enzymatic
treatments.
Methods used vary from autoclaving, alkaline lysis, homogenization at high pressure, ultra-
soniﬁcation, microwave, bead mill, freezing and thawing, osmotic shocks, and sulfuric acid
treatment. Enzymes as the agents for hydrolysis are not widely used yet due to their high
costs, but they are a very advantageous method with their degradation selectivity power.
10
==PAGE:21==
The choice of treatment will vary according to the organism chosen and the compounds of
interest [30].
The next step after pre treatment is the extraction itself, which has conventionally been
performed in three ways; in a Soxhlet apparatus, as a Solid-Liquid Extraction or a Liquid-
Liquid Extraction. The conventional organic solvents can be petroleum ether, hexane, cyclo-
hexane, isooctane, toluene, benzene, diethyl ether, dichlormethane, isopropanol, chloroform,
acetone, methanol, ethanol, and more. Novel techniques involve less harsh chemicals and
more a combination of pressure, temperature, ultrasound and microwaves [30]. They are
being developed not only since the bioactive compounds of interest might get degraded in the
traditional solvent extraction, but also because later application of these compounds in the
food and pharmaceuticals industry requires the extraction to be performed in a non-toxic way.
Supercritical ﬂuid extraction put the solvents at temperatures and pressures above their
critical points, thereby their density is altered and their viscosity is lowered along with an
increase in diﬀusibility. A common solvent used is CO2, which shows great aﬃnity for volatile
compounds and is easily separated from the extract. Though its low polarity do not allow
for polar substances extracts. This can be overcome by the addiction of co-solvents. Another
technique is Microwave-assisted extraction, where the vibrations of water molecules causes
an intracellular increase of temperature, which exerts a pressure on the cell wall as water
begins to evaporate. These newer techniques posses a great potential for the extraction of
pigments in a less harsh way.
1.4.2 Fermentation
Fermentation is a thousand year old technology that man has utilised in many ways to pro-
duce food, from beer, bread to later cacao and yogurt. In the mid 19th century scientists
ﬁnally discovered that it resulted from the action of microorganism.
The fermentation of seaweed started to become a discussed topic in the 70’s and 80’s, where
investigations for methane production were being conducted. Then later in the 90’s lactic
acid fermentations with food in mind began to develop. In the 2000’s much technology was
developed for ethanol production based on seaweed, which has also pushed the industry of
enzymes products, as many biomass and processes need a sacchariﬁcation step prior to fer-
mentation [40].
Latest investigations on fermented algae include their suitability for biogas reactors, lipid
or pigment production, the enhancement of biological active compound contents, lactic acid
production, antioxidant properties of fermentation products, healthy foods and others [3]
[14] [16] [23] [35]. Organisms used are varied and might be a combination, as well as the
techniques used diﬀer and can be i.e. submerged fermentation, simultaneous sacchariﬁcation

2.2 Extraction
The extraction of pigments was based on an in-house protocol for the analysis of pigments
in microalgae and modiﬁed to ﬁt the demands of this project. It was carried out using 96%
ethanol as the solvent. Extraction were performed at diﬀerent days and variable amounts of
wet algae weight were used ranging from 100g to 200g. The frozen algae was transferred to a
beaker and washed with ion free water one time to remove salt and ice crystals. Mussels from
Saccharina2013 samples were removed but the bed of epiphytes was allowed to stay as it was
not possible to scratch it out. The samples were triturated in a blender (Waring Commercial
Blender) for 1 minute with 750mL to 1L 96% ethanol. Afterwards the slurry were placed in
a dark chill room in 5◦C overnight.
The next day the slurry was submitted to 40 minutes sonication bath (Branson 2510) and
then centrifuged for 15 minutes at 7500rpm in 4◦C. Supernatant was saved and a further
750mL to 1L 96% ethanol was added to the pellet. The slurry was again submitted to sonica-
tion bath for 40 minutes and centrifuged for 15 minutes at 7500rpm in 4◦C. The process was
repeated one more time. The ﬁnal pellet after the three extraction rounds was allowed to dry
out in a ﬂow hood overnight. When the ethanol had completely evaporated the remaining
powders were placed in the refrigerator.
For the analysis of pigments amounts, ethanol samples from each of the three extraction
rounds, along with a sample of all the ethanol from the extractions (denoted ’Total’) was
gathered. They were submitted to spectrophotometer analysis and wave lengths were read
at 470nm, 664nm, and 648.6nm.
2.3 Hydrolysis
Several treatments of the biomass were tested. The main goal was to hydrolysate the remain-
ing polysaccharides in the Residual2013 and Residual2015 samples. Diﬀerent acid hydrolysis
protocols with varying concentrations of sulphuric acid were used and enzymatic treatment
tested.
2.3.1 Heat treatment
Based on the method used by Gupta et al. [17], the algae samples and the residuals in water
solution were submitted to heat treatment by autoclaving in 121◦C for 15 minutes.
2.3.2 Acid hydrolysis
All acid hydrolysis were performed with sulphuric acid. The concentration varied from 1,25%
H2SO4 in one test, another protocol used 72% followed by a dilution to 4% H2SO4, and
a third method investigated the change in the concentration from 20mM through 100mM
H2SO4. Each individual treatment is described below.

2.3.2.1 Treatment 1: 1,25% H2SO4
The protocol suggested by [31] on spent grain hydrolysis was followed.

2.3.2.2 Treatment 2: 72% H2SO4
Based on the NREL method of determining structural carbohydrates and lignin in biomass

for brown seaweeds was tested. It demands a 2-step hydrolysis, the ﬁrst with 72% H2SO4
followed by a dilution to 4% for the second step.
In 100ml blue screw cap bottles a 10% w/v of Residual2013 biomass in 72% H2SO4 was
prepared. It was then placed in water bath at 30◦C for 60 minutes. The solution was diluted
to 4% by addition of 850ml water and subjected to autoclaving in 121◦C for 40 minutes. The
sample was ﬁltered and investigated by HPLC.
2.3.2.3 Treatment 3: 0mM H2SO4 to 100mM H2SO4
A third method of acid hydrolysis common in the literature is subjecting the biomass to weak
acid solutions. The impact of the biomass amount in the acid solution was tested, for this
purpose two levels of biomass were prepared: 10%w/v and 20%w/v of the Saccharina2013
and Saccharina2015 samples and ﬁve times less weight for the Residual2013 sample was used
to account for the dehydration on the extraction step 2.2.
The concentration of sulphuric acid used were in the following 4 levels; 0mM H2SO4 (pure wa-
ter), 20mM H2SO4, 40mM H2SO4 and 100mM H2SO4. The algae in solution was triturated
in a blender (Waring Commercial Blender) for 1 minute and then subjected to heat treat-
ment in an autoclave at 121◦C for 20 minutes. Samples from the treatments were analysed
by HPLC for monossacharides.

2.5.2 Fermentation of algae hydrolysates
A ﬁnal fermentation experiment was designed to compare the performance of the media based
on the extraction residual against fresh algae, and good quality against bad quality seaweed.
The performance of hydrolysate media from Saccharina2013 and Saccharina2015 along with
their respective Residual2013 and Residual2015 were compared. 150g of Saccharina2013 and
Saccharina2015 were subjected to extraction as described in section 2.2. They were thereafter
named Residual2013 and Residual2015, 30g and 32g of each respective sample were used for
the subsequent hydrolysis, as well as 150g of Saccharina2013 and Saccharina2015.
100mM H2SO4 was added to the biomass up to a ﬁnal volume of 1L in a volumetric cylinder
and then transferred to 1L bottles. They were autoclaved at 121◦C for 20 minutes. The pH
was adjusted to 5 −6 with NaOH and H2SO4 and enzymes added as following: 2, 5% [E/S]
cellulase solution 700 units/g (Sigma-Aldrich) in the form of 3.15ml of solution to each media
(too little cellulase available at the time of experiment) and 15mg of alginate lyase added
to each media in aqueous solution. Bottles were placed in 40◦C water-bath with magnetic
stirring and allowed to stay for 24hrs.
The enzyme treated media were ﬁltered through cellulose acetate ﬁlters starting at 20µm
and ﬁnishing at 0.2µm ﬁlters. 0.5L algae media was then mixed with 0.5L 2X concentrate
MRS media without sugars (that had not had precipitate), such that the cultivation media
contains all MRS components in standard concentrations. 50ml of each of the four ready
MRS-algae fermentation media were poured into 100ml green screw cap bottles, triplicates
for the two strains investigated, and placed in water-bath at 30◦C.
Inoculums for the fermentations were prepared in MRS media 2% glucose (own blend) and
grown in 30◦C water-bath overnight. Biological triplicates prepared. Prior to inoculation
they were centrifuged for 5 minutes at 4000rpm in 30◦C, supernatant discarded and pellet
ressuspended in 40ml of MRS without sugars. Following, less than 1ml was added to each
fermentation ﬂasks to an initial OD600 of 0.01.
Samples were taken frequently in the ﬁrst 14hrs of the experiment and at 26hrs. Parallel to

2.6.4 Statistical analysis
One way ANOVA and multi ANOVA analysis were performed in Matlab for the growth rates
of cultivations and lactic acid production. An Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is used to com-
pare diﬀerences in mean values for multiple groups of data. It can be seen as an extension
of the t-test, which generally compares the mean of one or two groups of data, to multiple
groups, without performing the (many) individual comparison of the groups two by two. The
statistical beneﬁt of this is that every test has a risk of Type I error (false positive), usually
set to 5%, and with multiple tests this risk adds up to unacceptable levels. You could com-
pensate for this by choosing a more strict level for each test, but this would make the tests
less sensitive. The ANOVA tests the null hypothesis that all means in the groups are equal,
but the drawback is that the alternative hypothesis does not tell you which group is best.
This must be analysed by comparing the individual groups after the ANOVA has proven a
signiﬁcant diﬀerence.
The one-way ANOVA is an analysis of a single factor (independent variable) with multiple
levels. This is the case for the comparison of the growth rates of the various bacteria strains.
The N-way ANOVA on the other hand analyses ’N’ factors at diﬀerent levels. This is used for
the analysis of growth rates and lactic acid production of the hydrolysate cultivations where
three factors with each two levels are analysed.
The ANOVA compares the mean values of the groups by an F-test, in which the variance
between the factors is compared to the variance within the factors. This ratio is compared to
an F-distribution to conclude if the factor is responsible for the possible diﬀerence in mean
values. The result of an N-way ANOVA can be presented in the form of a table of F-values for
the diﬀerent factors, and the mean values for diﬀerent combinations of the levels of each fac-
tor can be plotted to visualise any diﬀerences. As the ANOVA assumes the error within each
group to have same variance, the plot will show identical conﬁdence interval for the mean of
the diﬀerent groups. This makes a visual comparison of the means among the groups possible.

4.1 Pigment contents are overestimated
Extraction samples usually show some turbidity and need to be ﬁltered or well centrifuged
prior to measurement in the spectrophotometer. The light scattering and turbidity may lead
to higher absorptions and thereby an overestimation of the pigment levels. The turbidity
can be checked by measuring the absorbance at wavelength 750nm, which should be zero as
chlorophyl a and b and carotenoids do not absorb in this region [24].
The results presented earlier show very high pigment concentrations (i.e. 180ug/g algae) and
it is most probable that these concentrations are not true as reported values in the litterature
indicates just a few ug/gDW. The calculations have been investigated, but they appear to
be performed correctly according to the protocol [24]. It is suspected that the measurements
from the spectrophotometer are insuﬃcient and a procedure error might have occurred there.
The spectrophotomether was zeroed with ethanol and 1ml of unﬁltered sample was read, but
the 750nm wavelenght was not checked. Another critical point is that during the extraction
the material was not kept consequently cold and away from light as prescribed, and it was
only centrifuged and not ﬁltered prior to reading.
The pigment extraction is not the focus of this study, and an optimisation of the extraction
procedure would be a whole investigation on its own. This study modiﬁed the original pro-
cedure of using 0.1g and 50ml solvent to ﬁt the needs of producing residual biomass in large
amounts at once, using limited solvent, generally 150g with 3L ethanol.

When looking into the best way of extracting valuable products with intentions of utilising
the waste it is important to have in mind the consequences of each unit operation and deter-
mine the right order of products for extraction. It might also be needed to consider milder
treatment even if those compromise the yield of a speciﬁc product but enriches the process as
a whole. The choice of ethanol was based on its GRAS status, but nevertheless other solvents
could be used, or better but more expensive methods as supercritical extraction.
4.2 Interferences and challenges in the hydrolysis procedures
The hydrolysis of the residual biomass after extraction was tried with diﬀerent techniques,
i.e. acid and enzymatic treatment. Focus was in the beginning to only use sulphuric acid as
it was reported to work ﬁne with algae tissue in general, which has none or a very low content
of lignin. References where optimisation of acid concentration and operational parameters
were used as guidelines, but reports of macroalgae feedstock sacchariﬁcation with dilute acid
hydrolysis are limited [41].
Jang et at. 2012 [22] can report a sacchariﬁcation of L.japonica, equivalent brown seaweed in
Asia, to be optimal at 1% H2SO4 and 3% biomass material achieving highest mannitol yield
of 9.74g/L when autoclaved at 140◦C for 30min. In another study [21] they tested simulta-
neous sacchariﬁcation and fermentation to produce ethanol and found the best conditions for
the sacchariﬁcation were 10%(w/v) seaweed slurry, 40mM H2SO4 and 1g dcw/L of Bacillus
sp. that reached a reducing sugar concentration of 45.6±5.0g/L. Wang et al 2011 [41] tested
a two-stage hydrolysis of Glaciara salicornia and found 120◦C and 2% H2SO4 for 30min to
be optimal but only yield 4.3g glucose/kg fresh algae, the combination of adding enzymes
increased it to 13.8g.
Manns et al. 2015 [26] investigated the eﬀect of milling and hydrolysing Laminaria digitata
with enzymes alone, and found that milling did not play a role on the yield but using a
combination of cellulase and alginate lyase enzymes was important as the removal of alginate
apparently improved the laminarin and cellulose degradation by the cellulase. Previously
Manns et at. 2014 [25] had investigated diﬀerent hydrolysis methods of brown seaweed, in-
cluding S.latissima, and found that high dosage of the CellicCTec2 (Novozymes, Denmark)
released more glucose than any of the sulphuric acid methods tested, but no alginate degra-
dation took place.
An important factor to look into is the eventual release of inhibiting
compounds that can be released or formed during hydrolysis, Manns et al. 2014 [25] checked
all acid hydrolysates for 5-HMF as a degradation product of hexoses and it was not detected
in any of the mildly treated sulfuric acid samples. After hydrolysis, the residues potentially
consist of inorganic compounds, condensed proteins and insoluble polysaccharides from in-
complete hydrolysis, in particular alginic acid.

The Saccharina2013 biomass contained a bed of epiphytes, which are organisms very much
composed of calcium carbonate, and these were suspected to buﬀer the action of sulphuric
acid and enzymes in the hydrolysis. Studies by Shi et al. [37] on lactic acid production
from paper mill sludge found that the ash content interfered very much with the enzymatic
hydrolysis, speciﬁcally the CaCO3, acting as a buﬀer. It was observed that some acid treated
samples of Residual2013 had very high pH right after heat treatment in the autoclave, which
demanded large amounts of more acid to reach the optimum for the subsequent enzyme treat-
ment. But on the other hand Goncalo et al. 2015 [29]) reports that the presence of epiphytes
did not incur problems in the fermentation-based succinic acid production from seaweeds.
4.3 Lactic acid production
Lactic acid production is a large industry with an estimated demand of up to 150, 000tons/year,
with expectation to grow much in coming years due to biopolymer applications. It can be
produced by chemical and microbiological synthesis, the latter being more advantageous [15].
In the project beginning lactic acid bacteria received greater attention in the considerations
for which compound to produce. It had previously been shown to grow on algae hydrolysate
or algae products and a large study of many strains showed their ability to use the monosac-
charides derived from seaweed [20]. Strains were readily available, so L.plantarum and later
a strain identiﬁed as potential producer L.pentosus was chosen.
Gupta et al. 2010 [17] have investigated the growth and kinetics of L.plantarum in brown
seaweeds, their focus was on producing a probiotic product based on seaweed, so entirely
disrupting of the cells and extraction of compounds prior to fermentation was not performed.
They heat treated small chopped pieces of S.latissima and obtained 2.1g/L of sugars, after
16hrs fermentation maximum concentrations of lactic acid obtained was 2.5g/L, with still
40
==PAGE:51==
1.4g/L sugar in media.
Hwang et al. 2012 [20] subjected many LAB strains to diﬀerent sugars and assessed their
growth. L.plantarum performed very well in all the relevant sugars, glucose, mannose, man-
nitol, and fucose, showing very high yields (>90%, apart from fucose). Following, the strain
L.casei and L.salivarius also performed well with the mentioned monosaccharides. Mannitol,
the alcohol form of mannose, is the ﬁrst product of photosynthesis in brown macroalgae, and
it was therefore important that the strains could utilise it.
The concentrations of lactic acid obtained with the fermentation of algal hydrolysates varied
much depending on the diﬀerent factors, the best performing combination was the L.pentosus
AH240 with the newest and clear of epiphytes algae material, achieving concentrations of
11.77g/L for the not extracted sample and 9.03g/L for the residue sample.
These value
are signiﬁcant higher than the achieved with the older biomass Saccharina2013 and Resid-
ual2013, with maximum production at 1.66g/L and 2.4g/L. Their growth curves indicated
that a shortage of an essential component, most probably the sugars, took place. It is no-
table how the growth rate itself is not much aﬀected in the Saccharina2013 samples, only the
Residual2013 material seen to aﬀect growth rates for both strains.
It is shown that the seaweed media supplied with MRS without sugars could support the
growth, but MRS serve to provide all essential salts and nitrogen sources to LAB. Further
investigation to uncover what could be removed from the supplied media in order to make it
cheaper are needed. It was shown that in contrary to the reference [17], the hydrolysate itself
might not support the growth of LAB, but this might be due to centrifugation and removal
of the pellet.
The amino acid analysis revealed a very high concentration in the Residual2013 sample,
211.73 mg/g(sample) which is more than 20% of the dry weight (the residual biomass is
dried), the corresponding Saccharina2013 only showed an amino acid sum corresponding to
4.7% of the sample, but the biomass is wet. Based on the extraction experience the wet
biomass weights about 4 to 5 times less after dehydrating. Thereby the amino acid amounts
are comparable. Goncalo et at. 2015 [27] found for samples from the same period concen-
trations of about 14% of DW. The results produced in this study are thereby comparable.
The interest of looking into the amino acid content in the media was to evaluate how aﬀected
they were by the hydrolysis process, and assuming the results are comparable between the
groups (the procedure is a bit diﬀerent whether it is pellet or media) than it can be observed
that the great majority of amino acids remain in the pellet. It is suspected that the lack of
amino acid, and thereby nitrogen might be the reason why fermentation with hydrolysates
alone was not successful.

5
Conclusion
Macro algae cultivation is indeed a growing market in South-east Asia, with half of the
world’s seaweed produced in china alone. Europe has not followed the developments in the
same manner, perhaps because of the culture diﬀerences regarding consumption of algae.
Nevertheless it is a biomass that have in recent years gained a lot of attention due to the
many interesting compounds it detains and the possibilities it provides in a biosustainable
perspective.
This study investigated the possibility of using the macro algae Saccharina latissima as the
biomass in a bioreﬁnery platform, where ﬁrstly the pigments are extracted and the residue
hydrolysed and transformed into lactic acid through fermentation. The biomass investigated
was both in good quality, readily available for human consumption, and in bad quality, foul-
ing algae full of epiphytes.
Solvent extraction with ethanol as the solvent was employed and the pigments were success-
fully extracted, but concentrations were not possible to determine precisely. The residual
was allowed to dry and then subjected to acid and enzymatic hydrolysis, where monosaccha-
rides were determined. The algae biomass was also directly subjected to hydrolysis, without
prior extraction. Glucose and mannitol concentrations were not impressive, being in the best
biomass, good quality and prior to extraction, up to 5.52g/L, and in the fouling biomass it
was very low at 0.45g/L, not even being detected in the fouling biomass that had undergone
solvent extraction. Mannitol concentrations were also very low at about 0.75g/L, only a little
higher in the good quality algae prior to extraction, 3.40g/L.
Regarding the performance of the strains investigated for production of lactic acid, the max-
imum amounts were produced by L.pentosus AH240, 11.77g/L, when reaching stationary
phase in algae hydrolysate supplied with MRS media fermentation. Hydrolysates of the foul-

ing algae lacked much more sugars and strains did not produced much, about 2.2g/L. None
algae media could support substantial growth of LAB alone, which might be related to the
low amino acid concentrations found in the media.
Statistical analysis could reveal the signiﬁcance of strain choice, along with the possibility
of the interaction between fouling algae and extraction having much impact in the growth
rate of the strains in the cultivations. Additional experiments are recommended to further
analyse the algae quality eﬀects on the process.
The study demonstrated the concept of extracting pigments and afterwards using the residue
for lactic acid fermentation, yet more investigation is needed in order to reveal the full poten-
tial of utilising good and fouling seaweed as source for pigments and lactic acid production.
