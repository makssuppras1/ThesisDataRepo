Abstract
The goal of this project is to investigate the inﬂuence of the coarse aggregate on the
elastic modulus of concrete. A series of experiments was performed where the compres-
sive strength and the modulus of elasticity were determined. Mortar samples with a glass
rod simulating coarse aggregate in the center were used to simulate the behavior of con-
crete. The rods were varied in size and different surface treatments were applied, such
as sandblasting and coating with anti-adhesive. Mortar mixes with two different values
of stiffness were used to investigate the effect of modulus variation between aggregate
and cement mortar. Local deformations were determined by using digital image correla-
tion method and from those deformations, localized moduli of elasticity were determined.
Crack development around the aggregate was also investigated. The analysis showed that
surface treatment of the aggregate had a noticeable effect on the localized modulus of
elasticity of the interface between the aggregate and cement mortar. The increase of sur-
face roughness resulted in stiffer interface and smaller cracks around the aggregate while
the decrease of the roughness caused the opposite. Smaller aggregates produced higher
interfacial stiffness and smaller crack development. Higher difference in elastic modulus
between cement mortar and aggregate caused larger crack development and larger drop
in the local modulus of the interface.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
The subject
The project area lays on the border of two disciplines of engineering as it concerns
both civil engineering as well as material science. It can be narrowed down to analysis of
concrete and its behavior as a composite material consisting of cement paste and coarse
aggregate. Today concrete is one of the most commonly used construction materials in the
world and as such deserves and in-depth understanding. While the current understanding
of the material allows for multitude of applications, the interaction between the individual
components of concrete is an area rich with research opportunities.
The stiffness of concrete is an area which is difﬁcult to quantify precisely because of
the random nature of concrete. It is inﬂuenced by the individual stiffness of the compo-
nents as well as their interaction and bond. The issue is further complicated by the ap-
pearance and inﬂuence of cracks during loading of concrete. The analysis of the inﬂuence
of the aggregate-matrix interface on the stiffens will allow for the better understanding of
the action of concrete under loading since the Young’s modulus is one of the primary
parameters used to determine the response and the internal actions of any structure.
Knowledge of the interaction between the cement matrix and aggregate and its in-
ﬂuence on the stiffness of concrete could potentially lead to the improvements in the
production process of aggregate which could improve the performance of concrete struc-
ture.
The presented work aims to determine how the properties of the interface between the
coarse aggregate and the surrounding cement paste affect the stiffness.

Description of the interfacial transition zone
Concrete is considered to be a composite material which is composed of two or three
phases depending of the applied model. The two phase model presented for example by
Zhou, et. al. in [15] consists of the cement paste and the coarse aggregate and represents
a more dated model of considering individual components of concrete. The more novel
approach considers concrete to be three phase materiel consisting of the cement paste,
coarse aggregate and the interfacial transition zone. This transition zone (ITZ) is about
30 µm from the contact zone between the aggregate and the cement paste as presented
by Rangaraju at. al. in [12]. The cement paste at this zone exhibits complete hydration
and larger Ca(OH)2. This interface paste presents different properties than the normal
cement paste as it is more porous than the "normal" paste. The interface zone exists not
only in the vicinity of the coarse aggregate but also in the contact zone of sand and the
cement paste. [11]
Zijian et. al. tried to assigned quantitative properties to the interfacial transition zone
around coarse aggregate in [8]. They used three different mixes with varying w/c and
made the investigation constant by ﬁlling 2/3 of the mold and adding a single piece of
coarse aggregate to each sample. The aggregate sizes varied form 5 to 20 mm. They
used backscattered electron images to analyze the the porosity of the concrete around the
inserted aggregate. Since the ITZ is characterized by higher porosity, they were able to
determine the width of the transition zone for the different aggregate sizes. They deter-
mined that the size of the interfacial transition zone increases with the increase of the
aggregate. For limestone aggregate and w/c of 0.35 it was between 25 µm and 50 µm.
[8]
Zijian et. al. also investigated the elastic modulus of the interfacial zone depending
on the distance from the aggregate using nanoindentation. The variation of the elastic
modulus can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1. The elastic modulus of the ITZ is high very close to
the aggregate because of the stiffness of the stone itself. It decreases with distance since
it enters the interfacial zone and ﬁnally levels as it enters the cement matrix. They found
that the elastic modulus of the ITZ at 7 days was 18 GPa. Value which is much lower than
the 27-30 GPa present in the matrix. They also found that the ITZ thickness increased
with the increase of the w/c. [8]

Relationship between elastic properties of cement matrix and
aggregate
The elastic modulus of concrete is depended on elastic modulus of the individual
components of the material. If the two element composite model is considered it can
be subdivided further into two categories. These are presented by Neville in [11]. The
ﬁrst model presents the cement paste as having high stiffness with embedded particles of
low stiffness. The second model consists of the opposite, where the cement matrix has
a low modulus of elasticity and the embedded particles have a high stiffness. The latter
is most applicable when considering a normal strength concrete where the aggregate is
in-fact stiffer then the surrounding cement matrix while the former can be applied to high
strength concrete where the stiffness of the paste is much higher. Another case that can
be considered, is the case of lightweight aggregate. It is usually made of material which
exhibits lesser stiffness than conventional aggregate which results in a case where the
moduli of both materials are similar.
When considering both the high strength and light weight concrete it can be observed
that the difference between the moduli of the paste and aggregate is reduced. Such state
is beneﬁcial as it leads to reduction of concentrated stresses in the interface between
the cement-matrix and the aggregate. In both types of concrete this results in a steeper
stress-strain curve but also in a more brittle failure mechanism. In high strength concrete
this can be explained by the fact that the concrete fracture occurs through the aggregate
and thus the they cannot act as crack arresters. In the lightweight concrete, the rough
texture of the surface of the aggregate results in some mechanical interlocking between
the cement paste and the particles of aggregate. This results in the absence of early bond
microcracking. [11]
The inﬂuence of aggregate properties on the properties of concrete has been studied
to some extent in publications such as [2] or [9] where they describe the overall effect

Inﬂuence of surface roughness of aggregate on elastic modulus of
concrete
The research presented by Hong et.al. in [7] draws a relation between the roughness of
the aggregate surface and mechanical properties of concrete. They performed two exper-
iments aimed to investigate the effect of the roughness of the aggregate on its mechanical
properties. The roughness was quantiﬁed as average distance between the surface of the
aggregate and a ideal surface. Two tests were performed. The ﬁrst investigated the shear
bond strength of stones with different roughness. They varied from a polished surface to
sandblasted and notched surfaces and presented in ﬁgure 1.3. They were cast in mortar
of constant strength and cured in uniform conditions. Thus, the tensile bond strength was
determined and correlated with the roughness of the aggregates and a relation showing
the increase in bond strength with the increase of roughness was obtained. It tended to
an ultimate value because at high roughness the failure occurred in the aggregate or the
mortar.
Figure 1.3: Aggregate used by Hong et. al. (a) fully polished surface; (b) medium
polished surface; (c) saw-cut surface; (d) sandblasted surface; and (e) notched surface..
Source: [7]
The second experiment they performed involved casting concrete with three differ-
ent types of glass marbles acting as an aggregate presented in ﬁgure 1.4. The three types
used were: polished, sandblasted and notched. The compression test of the concrete cubes
showed that the roughest aggregate produced highest ultimate stress while there were no
differences in the values of ultimate strain. It was also observed that the sandblasted ag-
gregate provided highest elastic modulus of concrete According to their analysis smooth
aggregate caused the cracking to start in the area of the interface while in the concrete
with rough aggregate cracking occurred either in the cement matrix or the aggregate it-
self. Such conclusion is conﬁrmed in [7] where glass marbles of various roughens were
used as aggregate and the compression test was performed. Presented results show that
with the increase of roughness of the marbles the compression strength, splitting strength

The differences in elastic modulus of concrete with relation to different aggregates
has also been tested by Giaccio and Zerbino. They claim that cracks begin to form at the
interfaces while the aggregate itself arrests the growth of the cracks. This is in line with
the information presented by Neville in [11]. They also state that mechanism of crack
development is greatly correlated with both the shape and surface texture of the used
aggregate. They proceed to compare two concrete mixes of different strengths and four
different aggregate types. The aggregates used were granitic crushed stones characterized
by irregular shape, rough surface texture and low absorption; quartzite crushed stone
characterized by irregular shape, rough surface texture and high absorption; siliceous
river gravel characterized by a smooth surface and high strength and river gravel which
was a mix of different rock types. They discussed the process of crack formation and
since cracks are an indicator of strain in concrete they can be correlated to the elastic
modulus. [5]
The resulting pattern of cracks varied depending on the type of coarse aggregate used.
In the high strength concrete the siliceous river gravel caused the cracks to start from
the interface and spread through out the concrete matrix. This resulted in the failure of
the aggregate-matrix bond while the aggregate itself remained intact. The rougher mixed
river gravel showed some of the cracks passing through the aggregate itself and ﬁnally
the rough quartzitic crushed stone provided a high bond strength which resulted in cracks
starting and propagating through the matrix and the aggregate. In the conventional con-
crete a large amount of bond cracks could be observed. The measured elastic modulus
was the highest for the siliceous river gravel which was the smoothest of the used ag-
gregates, however, it was also the strongest of the used materials. The elastic moduli

Test methods
The current standard for determining the elastic modulus of concrete in Europe is
the EN 12390-13 and in outlines two methods of determining the secant modulus of
elasticity in compression. They are called Method A and Method B. The methods are
considered equivalent by the standard, however, the have some differences in execution.
The principle of both methods is loading the specimen under axial compression, recording
both the stress and the strain of the sample. The loading is repeated and the secant slope
is the secant modulus of elasticity of concrete under compression. The specimens can be
either cubical or cylindrical. [4]
Method A consists of three cycles of preloading, checking the instruments and car-
rying out three loading cycles for stress-strain measurements. Figure 1.5 presents the
loading cycles. It allows for determination of both the initial secant modulus of elasticity
as well as the stabilized secant modulus of elasticity.

The differences between the two testing methods were analyzed by Domagała and
Dobrowolska in [3]. They tested 4 mixes with various mixes with different w/c ratios.
Two of the samples were normal weight concrete and two of the samples were the high
strength concrete. The test results of the method A and method B on the same specimen
showed that method A consistently shows higher values of both the stabilized and the
initial secant modulus. The proposed explanation for this is the stiffening of the concrete
during the preloading phase in method A. Also, the value of the stress used as a lower
stress in method A is higher which might also improve the performance of concrete under
compression. [3]
Another method of determination of elastic modulus of concrete is using a digital
correlation. Santos et. al. presents a comparison between the classical strain gauge
method of determination of elastic modulus of concrete and the above mentioned digital
correlation in [13]. The technique consist of using cameras for analysis of patterns of
speckles scattered on the surface of the specimen. Then the patterns before and after
deformation are compared and the strain can be determined. The steps involved in the
digital analysis involves preparation of the sample surface, recording of the images and
ﬁnally using an algorithm to determine the displacements in time. The preparation of
the surface involved sanding and spraying black and white dots. The paper analysis two

1.3
Hypothesis
Based on the literature review three main properties have been selected for investiga-
tion of the of the interface. The ﬁrst is the surface roughness of the aggregate. It can be
hypothesized that the increase of the roughness of the aggregate will result in better bond
and thus the stiffness of the interfacial zone will be higher which in turn should result
in higher overall stiffness. Furthermore, the better bond should result in smaller crack
development around the aggregate. Thus, the decrease of the roughness should result in
a worse bond, lower stiffness and more cracking in the vicinity of the aggregate.
The second interface property is the size of the aggregate which in turn determines
the size of the interface. The increase in size of the aggregate should result in more
pronounced effects, thus, more cracking which in turn should result in lower interfacial
and overall stiffness.
The ﬁnal property which shall be investigated will be the effect of the difference
between the moduli of the aggregate and the cement matrix. Higher difference should
result in higher stress concentration in the interface area and thus produce more cracks
which will result in a decrease of local elasticity.

2.1
Mortar Design
The investigation of the effect of the difference in stiffness between cement matrix and
aggregate required a mean to differentiate the E-modulus of cement paste. The stiffness of
the aggregate remained constant as a homogeneous glass rod was used. The objective was
to develop six different mortar mixes which were divided in to two groups representing
high and low stiffness. Each group contained three mixes distinguished by their maximum
aggregate size consisting of 2 mm, 3 mm and 5 mm.
The objectives for the mix development were as follows:
1. Achieve good workability to allow for the penetration of the mortar around glass
rods.
2. Achieve a relatively low air content after vibration to create a good surface for the
digital image correlation software.
3. Avoid segregation during vibration required to achieve the low air content goal
4. Achieve a signiﬁcant difference in stiffness between the two mix designs.
The following materials were used for the the mixes:
• Portland cement 52.5R
• Sand 0.1-2 mm
• Sand 0.1-3 mm
• Sand 3-5 mm
• Dolomite ﬁll
• Silica fume
• Superplasticiser
• Quartz powder

The design process assumed a constant water-cementious ratio for both mixes. The
w/c of the stiff mix was set to 0.3 and of the soft mix 0.6. Thus, the workability was ad-
justed by changing amount of superplasticizer and other additives, such as quartz powder
and dolomite ﬁll. Silica fume and quartz powder were used only for the stiff mix because
they provided a ﬁll for pores which in turn increased the stiffness and strength of the
mix. The dolomite ﬁll was used in the soft mix to provide a similar effect, however, since
dolomite is a softer material it did not increase the stiffness as much as silica fume and
quartz powder. It was observed that the ingredient with most noticeable effect was the
superplasticiser. Increasing the amount of it produced a more liquid mixture, however,
to obtain a mix design which represented a concrete used in practice, its use limited to
an extent. Instead, proportion of ﬁlling material was used to adjust the worakbility of a
higher degree.
The design process was based on trial and error and visual evaluation. The process
was as follows:
1. Proportion of ingredients for 1 liter of mortar were designed for the sand with max-
imum fraction of 3 mm.
2. Dry ingredients were mixed together for 1 minute
3. Water was added and the mortar was mixed for 3 minutes.
4. The mixer was stoped and the sides of the mixing bowl were manually scraped to
remove any residual dry material and ensure proper mixing of the mortar.
5. The workability of the mix was assessed. If it was deemed sufﬁcient the mortar
was cast in to prisms of size 40x40x160 mm and vibrated on a vibration table.
Otherwise the mix was discarded and proportions of the ingredients adjusted.
6. Preliminary assessment of air content was performed during vibration and sec-
ondary when the prisms were demolded after 24 hours.
7. After obtaining a mix of sufﬁcient parameters the process was repeated for the two
reaming sand fractions.
The samples were cured in water of approximate temperature of 30°C for two weeks.
Compression tests were performed to determine the ultimate compressive strength of the
mortar. Once the strength of the samples was determined, modulus of elasticity was
tested.
For the investigation of the effect of the interface on elasticity of concrete the two
mixes with maximum aggregate sizes of 0.1-5 mm were selected in order to represent ﬁne

2.3
Specimen casting
The samples were prepared to simulate a single piece of aggregate cast into mortar.
In order to avoid variability in of different size, shape, surface and chemical composition
of the aggregate which is unavoidable in natural aggregate a man-made substitute in the
form of glass rod was selected. Such aggregate allowed for a homogeneous conditions
of the bond and the aggregate itself because of constant shape of the rod as well as the
isotropic properties of glass.
==PAGE:28==
16
CHAPTER 2. METHOD FOR EXPERIMENTAL INVESTIGATIONS
Three different sizes of glass rods were used, namely with diameter of 20 mm, 16
mm and 12 mm. Also three different surface treatments were investigated on the glass
rod with diameter of 20 mm; an untreated surface, a surface with improved bond and
surface with worse bond. The improvement bond was achieved by sandblasting the rod.
The rods were cut into a desired length before the sandblasting process. Special attention
was given to assure equal treatment throughout the length of the rod. The decreased bond
quality was achieved by coating the glass rod with an anti-adhesive in the form of oil
before casting.
Figure 2.4: Glass rods used as simulation of the aggregate. From the bottom: a) 12 mm
b)16mm c) 20 mm d) sandblasted 20 mm
The sample preparation process consisted of the following steps.
1. Preparation of the rods: The glass rods were manufacture with the length of 1.5
meters and as such had to be cut into a 39.5 mm pieces to ﬁt them inside the mold.
The cutting was done using a small diamond coated bladed. The rods were then
placed inside the mold and were kept in the desired position by wooden end-plates
of the height 15 cm and width of 6 cm located on two opposite ends of the mold
with holes drilled in the size of the rod. After being placed inside of the mold and
before being cast into the mortar, the rods were cleaned with alcohol to prevent
introduction of any external agents to the interface between the cement matrix and
the glass rod.
==PAGE:29==
2.3. SPECIMEN CASTING
17
2. Preparation of the mortar: The mortar mix was prepared according to the recipe
presented in chapter 2 scaled to 9 liters. Such volume provided a sufﬁcient amount
of mortar to ﬁll two molds. All of the desired ingredients were prepared and their
desired amounts weighted. The dry ingredients were placed in the bowl of the
concrete mixer and mixed together for 2 minutes on medium speed. Then, the
mixer was stopped and water was added to mixer. The mortar was then mixed for
5 minutes after which the mixer was stopped again and the sides of the mixer bowl
were manually scraped to ensure equal distribution of water throughout the mix and
afterwards the mixing process continued for additional 5 minutes.
3. Casting of the specimen batch: The rods were cast into concrete prisms with the
height of 15 cm and width of 6 cm with their center located 6 cm from the bottom
surface. The total length of the used mold was 40 cm. The mold was placed on
a vibration table. The concrete was placed in the mold in two layers and vibrated
after placing of each one. The ﬁrst layer was placed to such height that it covered
the glass rod and thus, prevented it from moving during the vibration process, the
vibration table was turned on and the mold was vibrated until no air was visibly
exiting the mortar. The process was repeated with a second layer of mortar placed
to completely ﬁll the mold.
4. Demolding: The specimens were demolded after 24 hours and placed in water
with temperature of 41.5 °C. The conditions of the water were monitored and are
presented in appendix A
5. Cutting: After 4 days from casting of the sample it was removed from the water
and cut using a saw equipped with a diamond blade. The ends were removed and
the prism was cut along its length every 5 cm. Finally, 3 cm of mortar from the top
of the sample were removed resulting in the ﬁnal specimen of 12 cm in height, 6
cm wide and with the length of 5 cm with the center of the glass rod in the middle
of the specimen.
6. Curing: The specimens were placed back in warm water until testing which was
performed no sooner than 14 day from the day of casting of the specimen batch.
According to [10], the strength of of the specimens cured in 46 °C a for 14 days
reach almost 100% of the strength of concrete cured for 28 days in 21 °C.

2.4
Specimen preparation for testing
The specimens were tested in compression test and their deformation was measured
using the digital correlation method further described in section 2.6. To facilitate proper
analysis of the deformation by the GOM Correlate software each tested surface of the
specimen had to be uniformly painted with white paint and random pattern of black points
had to be created on the surface. The white surface was created using white chalk spray
paint. Special care was given to evenly coat the surface with a thin layer to assure that the
deformation of the paint corresponds to the deformation on the surface of the concrete.
Throughout the testing process it was determined that the size and distribution of the dots
had a decisive effect on the results of the performed tests, thus, a series of improvements
were introduced over the span of the testing period.
The pattern of the ﬁrst samples was created using black sprayed chalk paint sprayed
from approximately 0.5 m away over the the sample. The pattern was then assessed
visually and either accepted or the paint was washed off and the process was repeated.
It was observed that manual skill of the painter was an important factor in producing
a working pattern. Such process usually created a pattern of sufﬁcient quality for the
photos that included the entire sample, however, such was not the case for the photos
encompassing a small area of the concrete.

2.5
Testing procedure
Compression strength tests were performed on the samples to determine their ultimate
compression strength. It was later used for set up elasticity test, as the test is carried out
to the percentage of the ultimate strength. Out of each batch of specimen produced, three
were used to determine the strength. The test was carried out using the TONI Technik
300-TON machine with the loading speed of 1.8 kN/s which for the specimens of the
dimension 60mm by 50mm corresponded to 0.6 MPa/s. The test was carried out until a
fracture of 0.150% was detected.
The elasticity tests of the samples were carried out by cycling compression in a MTS
machine. The machine was equipped with two different type of compression heads. The
bottom head, was rigid and was used as the direction of loading. The top head consisted
of a ball bearing which allowed for it to adjust to deviations from the ﬂat surface which
in turn should result in a more even stress distribution.

2.6
Analysis Method
Digital Image Correlation software called GOM Correlate was used to analyze the
deformations of the sample. The program determines a facet based on the structure of the
point pattern sprayed on the sample. Two main parameters describe the facet, namely,
the its size in pixels and the point distance. Each facet results in a measurement point
of the sample. Finally, all of the measurement points are combined to a create a surface
component. [6]
Thought the testing process the default surface properties recommend by the software
was chosen which was the facet size of 16 pixels and the point distance of 19 pixels. The
cameras used photographed the specimens in the resolution of 7359x4912, which means
that that each point on the photograph taken with the close camera was a square with a
side length on the order of magnitude of 0.1 mm. The far camera produced pictures with
measurement points in the form of square with a size of approximately 0.4 mm.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
This work presents the results and analysis of the effect of the interface between the
aggregate and the cement matrix on the stiffness of concrete. Samples with various aggre-
gate surface treatments, sizes and different cement mortars were tested for both elasticity
and ultimate compressive strength. The elasticity test was performed in three loading cy-
cles. The entire testing process was photographed and using the digital image correlation
software GOM, the behavior of the sample was analyzed. Virtual extensometers were
placed on the sample and the local deformations were obtained based on which the local
stiffness of the various parts of the sample were calculated. The crack behavior around
the interface was also investigated with regards to opening as well as sliding of the cracks.

It was hypothesized that the increase of the roughness of the aggregate will results

and bottom interface respectively, than sample with untreated aggregate. This validates
the hypothesis that the roughness increases the local stiffness of the interface.

however, the effect could be further enforced by the lower strength of the cement paste.
The soft mix L5 had a signiﬁcantly lower compressive strength in relation to the H5 mix
and thus it would be more prone to crushing locally. To ﬁnd unequivocal reason for the
behavior, further testing would be required.

a indication of locations where the stiffness is decreased.

Giaccio an Zebion claimed in [5] that the cracks begin to grow from the aggregates

aggregate had no visible debonding around the aggregate however some cracks were oc-
curring in the paste below the aggregate. No cracking caused by the load was detected
during testing. Such behavior partially conﬁrms conclusions presented in [7] where the
claim that smooth aggregate caused the cracking to start in the area of the interface while
in the concrete with rough aggregate cracking occurred either in the cement matrix or the
aggregate it-self is presented. The cracking through the aggregate could be observe in the
samples destroyed in the compression testing.
4.1
Further work
The research presented in this thesis is meant as an introduction to further research
into the effects of concrete components on the elasticity of concrete. As such it suffered
from a number of procedural errors during testing that should be avoided in the future
to provide more accurate results. The natural extension of this work is the repletion
of the presented tests on a larger number of samples. This would eliminate the inherit
randomness which comes with working with material such as concrete and reduce the
signiﬁcance of errors during testing.
The sample preparation method should also be improved to accommodate creation of
samples with ideally orthogonal surfaces. The main source of the uneven sample surfaces
during this testing process was the cutting method. The use of mechanized saw is recom-
mend to decrease the source of human error and manual skill. Furthermore, the surfaces
could be ground to provide a more even surface.
Testing machine which allows for perfectly axial compression should be used because
the uneven stress distribution introduces large errors in the calculated local moduli. The
swivel presented in ﬁgure 2.8 should not be used. It introduced an additional surface to
==PAGE:67==
4.1. FURTHER WORK
55
aligned withe the surface of the sample which propagated errors in the testing process and
increased the eccentricity of the load in the sample.
The simulation of coarse aggregate of concrete with glass rods is a good starting
point in the investigation, however, it does not fully represent the behavior of natural
gravel aggregate. While glass is perfectly isotropic, most natural aggregates are not.
Furthermore, glass does not represent any chemical reaction that might occur between
the surface of the aggregate and the cement matrix. Thus in the later stages of research it
would be recommended to replace the glass rods with natural material of similar shape.
Finally, the investigation presented in this thesis considers only the two dimensional
behavior of the sample. However, during the tests to determine the compressive strength
it could be observed that the failure mechanism was usually three dimensional. Therefore,
in order to fully capture the action of the sample the photographs should be taken from
all 4 sides. This in turn would require a testing machine which would allow unobstructed
placement of cameras on all sides.
