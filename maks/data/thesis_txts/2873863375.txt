Abstract 
Glulam is increasingly adopted as a more sustainable construction material, yet its hygroscopic 
properties pose critical challenges related to moisture-induced deformation and cracking. This 
thesis investigates the effects of moisture gradients in wetted glulam joints subjected to various 
drying conditions, using a combination of controlled laboratory experiments, full-scale monitor-
ing from a case, and numerical simulations. 
Five series of glulam specimens, including two with slotted-in steel connections, were wetted and 
subsequently tested under different drying conditions. All specimens developed moisture-in-
duced deformations, and cracking occurred under all tested conditions. Aggressive drying (40 °C 
/ 31% RH) led to more severe cracking than milder conditions (23 °C / 40% RH and 24 °C / 60% 
RH). However, the number of specimens, the range of drying conditions tested, and the duration 
of the drying periods were limited in this project; therefore, the results should be viewed as in-
dicative. 
WUFI® 2D simulations, calibrated against experimental data, were used to estimate moisture dis-
tributions and drying times. Results estimated that doubling the cross-sectional width increased 
drying time by more than a factor of six. However, it must be emphasized that the simulations 
represent a simplified approximation of reality, and further calibration is needed to improve the 
reliability of the results. 
Case-based evaluations demonstrated that moisture damage can occur even when moisture man-
agement strategies are implemented. The most critical finding is that moisture becomes trapped 
primarily due to inadequate detailing, prolonged wetting, and insufficient understanding of dry-
ing behavior, including moisture monitoring. The thesis highlights the importance of preventive 
design detailing and calls for the development of evidence-based drying protocols for glulam con-
nections exposed to unintended moisture.

1. Introduction   
With increasing sustainability demands in the construction sector, the use of timber as a load-
bearing material is expected to grow in the coming years. The Danish Government’s agreement 
on new climate regulations for the building sector sets a target that, by mid-2025, new construc-
tions must not exceed an average of 7.1 kg CO₂/m2/year[1]. As a renewable resource, timber not 
only helps reduce the substantial carbon emissions associated with construction, but also acts as 
a carbon sink, storing CO₂ throughout the building’s lifetime. 
Modern large-scale buildings often demand long spans and high load capacities, increasing the 
relevance of large mass timber structures with complex joints. Unlike steel and concrete, mass 
timber structures are highly susceptible to moisture during construction, particularly at connec-
tions where water accumulation and limited drying potential can pose challenges. Maintaining 
dry conditions within these assemblies is critical for long-term performance, yet often difficult to 
achieve under practical on-site conditions.   
Moisture exposure in mass timber joints can lead to moisture induced stress. These stresses may 
compromise the mechanical performance of the joint, potentially reducing its load-bearing capac-
ity below design expectations.  
This project investigates the moisture-induced deformation behavior of glulam dowel joints with 
slotted-in steel plates through a series of controlled laboratory experiments. The experimental 
work includes MC measurements at multiple depths, correlated with dimensional changes under 
varying drying conditions. Furthermore, different drying strategies will be evaluated to under-
stand their influence on moisture gradients and mechanical response. The objective of the thesis 
is to: 
• 
Evaluate the influence of different drying strategies on the behavior of partially restrained 
glulam joints following moisture exposure. This includes analyzing moisture-induced de-
formations under controlled conditions and discussing the underlying mechanisms driv-
ing these changes. 
• 
Identify drying conditions that most optimally reduce internal stress development and 
minimize the risk of crack formation in restrained glulam connections. This includes dis-
cussing prevention strategies through case-studies and assessing the required drying 
time for glulam using simulations in WUFI® 2D. 
A case study, referred to as Case A, serves as the primary reference for the design of the experi-
mental setup. The work presented in this study is thus designed to replicate and isolate the key 
parameters observed in Case A - including one-sided wetting, limited drying surfaces, and partial 
mechanical restraint. The project aims to adhere closely to these boundary conditions in order to 
achieve the objectives.

2. Theory  
2.1. Hygrothermal Properties 
This section outlines the fundamental hygrothermal properties of wood that are essential for un-
derstanding moisture behavior in mass timber construction.  
2.1.1. Softwood and Wood Structure 
Wood is generally categorized into two main types, known as softwood and hardwood. In the 
temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, hardwoods are typically broadleaf, deciduous 
trees such as maple and birch, while softwoods are usually evergreen needle-leaved trees like 
pine and spruce. Softwood is commonly used in construction, for example, in applications such as 
glued laminated timber, making it the primary focus of this report. 
When observing a tree stump, several distinct concentric layers can be identified: outer bark, in-
ner bark, vascular cambium, sapwood, heartwood, and pith as shown in Figure 2.1. 
The outer bark provides mechanical protection and reduces water loss, while the inner bark 
(phloem) transports sugars from the leaves to other parts of the tree. The vascular cambium, lo-
cated between the bark and wood, generates both bark and wood tissues. Sapwood is the active, 
living part of the wood, responsible for conducting water from the roots to the leaves. Sapwood 
has not yet accumulated the chemicals that give heartwood its darker color and make it noncon-
ductive. The pith, located at the center of the trunk, consists of the remnants of the tree's early 
growth, before wood formation began [2]. 
 
Figure 2.1: Cross-section of a tree trunk [3]. 
The vascular cambium, located between the inner bark and wood, produces wood inward and 
bark outward through cell divisions. The cells formed during a specific time period are called 
growth increments or growth rings [2]. Diameter growth in trees from temperate zones occurs in 
two stages, forming the earlywood and latewood portions of tree rings. In early spring, rapid cell 
formation results in large, thin-walled earlywood cells. As the season progresses, cell formation 
slows, producing fewer, thicker-walled, and smaller latewood cells as shown in Figure 2.2[4]. 
==PAGE:10==
 
3 
 
 
Figure 2.2: Illustration of softwood cellular structure [5]. 
Softwood is an anisotropic cellular material defined by the radial, tangential, and longitudinal di-
rections. The structure of softwood is shown in Figure 2.2 and is consisting mainly of axial tra-
cheids in the longitudinal system and ray cells in the radial system. Tracheids, which make up 
over 90% of softwood volume, are long, narrow cells that serve both conductive and mechanical 
functions, contributing to water transport as well as structural support in the tree. In cross-sec-
tion, they appear as square or rectangular cells arranged in radial rows. Their walls are thinner 
in earlywood and thicker in latewood. For water to flow between tracheids, it must pass through 
circular bordered pits, primarily located at the tapered ends of the cells. Flow also occurs in the 
two other directions through the interconnecting pits. Generally, there are fewer and smaller pits 
on the tangential surfaces of all longitudinal tracheids. As a result, in the absence of flow through 
the rays, tangential permeability would be expected to exceed radial permeability, since most of 
the pits are located on the radial surfaces of the tracheids. However, observations have shown 
that radial permeability in certain softwoods can be greater than tangential permeability, which 
is attributed to fluid movement through the rays [6]. 
Ray cells, appearing as faint dark lines in Figure 2.2 form the rays in softwood. These brick-like 
cells primarily function in biochemical synthesis, storage, and permit radial flow of water in the 
wood [2].

2.1.3. Relative Humidity (RH) 
When wood is kept dry and shaded from sunlight, its MC below the FSP depends on the relative 
humidity (RH) and temperature of the surrounding air. In use, wood is exposed to both long-term 
(seasonal) and short-term (daily) variations in humidity and temperature, causing changes in MC. 
These changes are typically gradual, with short-term fluctuations mainly affecting the wood's sur-
face [7].

vapor pressure. As the temperature increases, the air’s capacity to hold water vapor also rises, 
causing the saturated vapor pressure to increase accordingly.

The diagram shown in Figure 2.5 is a standard diagram, since it does not account for different 
wood species or distinguish between adsorption and desorption. 
The ability of wood to adsorb or desorb water depends on the number of available sorption sites. 
At a given RH, the availability of these sorption sites can vary depending on the wood’s moisture 
history. Previous fluctuations in RH, referred to here as moisture history, influence the number 
of accessible sorption sites. This effect is the cause of the phenomenon known as hysteresis.  Dur-
ing adsorption (moisture uptake), the swelling of the wood material disrupts existing hydrogen 
bonds within the cell walls, creating additional sorption sites where water molecules can bind. 
Conversely, during desorption (moisture release), the process is reversed as water is released. 
However, because water molecules continue to occupy these sorption sites, the original hydrogen 
bonds cannot fully reform until the wood is completely dehydrated [13]. 
Due to this effect, the EMC differs during adsorption and desorption, with adsorption typically 
resulting in a lower MC at the same RH. This phenomenon is called hysteresis and is shown in 
Figure 2.6 for spruce. Two boundary curves form a hysteresis loop, where the desorption iso-
therm consistently lies above the adsorption isotherm at the same RH. The system can reach dif-
ferent equilibrium states depending on whether adsorption is interrupted and followed by de-
sorption or desorption is interrupted and followed by adsorption. These intermediate paths are 
referred to as scanning curves [14]. 
 
Figure 2.6: Adsorption and desorption curve for spruce [14]. 
The difference in EMC varies with RH, typically ranging from 2–3 percentage points at 50–60% 
RH, and increasing to 4–5 percentage points at 70–80% RH.  
2.2. Impact of Water Ingress and Egress on Timber  
2.2.1. Strains and Deformations 
Wood is anisotropic in terms of dimensional stability, meaning it shrinks and swells differently in 
different directions. This moisture exchange leads to shrinkage and swelling strains. It undergoes 
the greatest shrinkage tangentially (along the growth rings), about half as much radially (across 
==PAGE:15==
 
8 
 
the rings), and very little longitudinally (along the grain). The difference in radial and tangential 
shrinkage, combined with the curvature of the growth rings, can cause distortion in wood pieces, 
leading to common deformation types, as illustrated in Figure 2.7 [7].  
 
Figure 2.7: Shrinkage and distortion in wood pieces depend on the growth ring direction.  
Tangential shrinkage is about twice that of radial shrinkage, leading to characteristic deformations [9]. 
For pine, the total dimensional shrinkage from the green condition (28% MC) to 0% MC is ap-
proximately 8% in the tangential direction and about 4% in the radial direction, while longitudi-
nal shrinkage is only around 0.3% as shown in Figure 2.8 [9] [15].  
However, the shrinkage curve at different moisture levels is influenced by several factors - pri-
marily the size and shape of the timber, the specific wood species, and the drying conditions ap-
plied [7]. 
 
 
Figure 2.8: Radial and tangential shrinkage of spruce as a function of MC during drying [16] 
==PAGE:16==

The shrinkage per 1% change in MC, provided that the MC is below the FSP, can be expressed as 
αr≈0.14% and αt≈0.28% for spruce [17] [16]. For most engineering purposes, however, it is un-
necessary to differentiate between the two transverse directions, and transverse movement is 
often taken as the average value [18].  
The reduced shrinkage in the radial direction can be attributed to two main factors: (a) the limit-
ing effect of ray cells on radial shrinkage and (b) the alternating bands of low-density earlywood 
and high-density latewood. These two factors work together to minimize shrinkage in the radial 
direction. However, in the tangential direction the denser and stronger latewood compresses the 
earlywood tangentially, causing it to shrink more than it would if it shrank independently [6]. The 
bending of microfibrils around the cell wall pits in the radial walls of the wood fibers may also 
contribute to why wood shrinks less in the radial direction than in the tangential direction[19].  
In addition to moisture-induced strain, which includes free shrinkage and swelling strain, the to-
tal strain in timber consists of three primary components: elastic strain, viscoelastic strain, and 
mechano-sorptive strain. Elastic strain refers to the reversible deformation that occurs under me-
chanical loading and as a simplification, it is often assumed that wood behaves elastically under 
low stress or strain levels and over short periods of time. The elastic response of wood corre-
sponds to the recoverable deformation upon unloading and is commonly described using Hooke’s 
law, which states that stress and strain are proportional within the elastic range. Viscoelastic 
strain (creep) represents a time-dependent deformation that develops under sustained stress. 
Creep in wood is increased at high MC [20].  The mechano-sorptive strain describes an additional 
deformation occurring when wood is simultaneously subjected to mechanical loading and fluctu-
ating MC. As moisture fluctuations induce shrinkage and swelling while mechanical loads con-
tribute to time-dependent creep, mechano-sorption can never occur in isolation [21]. 
2.2.2. Cracks 
The anisotropic shrinkage behavior of wood, especially the difference between tangential and ra-
dial shrinkage, plays a central role in the development of cracks during the drying process [16]. 
There are three primary ways to apply force to facilitate crack propagation, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.9. Mode I occur when a crack opens due to tensile stress perpendicular to the crack plane. 
Mode II involves in-plane shear stress directed along the direction of the crack. Mode III results 
from transverse shear stress caused by shear forces acting out of the crack plane, also leading to 
crack shearing (see Figure 2.9). 
With the three principal directions R, T, and L, representing the radial, tangential, and longitudi-
nal directions, the orthotropic nature of wood allows for six possible crack propagation direc-
tions, as illustrated in Figure 2.9. A common assumption is that the crack plane aligns with one of 
these principal material directions. 
The primary crack growth systems are denoted as TL, RL, LR, TR, RT, and LT, where the first letter 
represents the direction perpendicular to the crack plane, and the second letter indicates the di-
rection of crack propagation. In wood, crack propagation is generally unaffected by the crack’s 
initial orientation or the degree of mixed-mode loading. Consequently, cracks typically propagate 
along the grain [22].

Figure 2.9: Fracture modes I, II and III (left) and the six main crack orientations in wood (right)[20][23]. 
Ray tissue has been identified as a vulnerable area in wood during the drying process, leading to 
the propagation of drying cracks primarily in the radial and longitudinal direction referred to as 
TR and TL, where tangential shrinkage creates high stress in those planes [24]. 
2.2.3. Moisture Transport Mechanisms and Drying Dynamics in Wood 
The drying rate of wood is influenced by several key factors, including the RH of the surrounding 
air, the steepness of the moisture gradient, and the temperature of the wood. Lower RH acceler-
ates diffusion by reducing surface MC, thereby steepening the moisture gradient and increasing 
the moisture transport within the material. Elevated temperatures further accelerate this process 
by facilitating the movement of moisture from the wetter interior toward the drier surface [25]. 
The diffusivity generally increases with increasing MC and temperature, as the bonding forces 
decrease under these conditions [26].  
Additionally, drying time is significantly impacted by thickness of the material, increasing at a 
rate greater than proportional to the thickness itself. Doubling the thickness results in a drying 
time that is more than twice as long [25]. 
The flow direction also plays a crucial role in drying rate. Longitudinal permeability is signifi-
cantly greater than radial and tangential permeability because the primary fluid-conducting ele-
ments, such as tracheids in softwoods, are oriented along the wood’s length. In the radial direc-
tion, ray cells facilitate lateral fluid movement, though to a lesser extent than in the longitudinal 
direction [6]. The drying rate of wood is approximately 20 times faster in the longitudinal direc-
tion than in the radial direction, and about twice as fast radially compared to the tangential direc-
tion [9]. 
2.2.4. Moisture Induced Cracking in Timber  
During the desorption, wood undergoes differential shrinkage due to moisture gradients between 
the outer shell and the inner core. Initially, moisture evaporates faster from the surface, causing 
the outer layers to shrink while the core retains higher MC (see Figure 2.10).  
==PAGE:18==
 
11 
 
 
Figure 2.10: To prevent damage to the wood, the main task in the drying process is to ensure that moisture 
moves from the inner layers of the wood to the surface in a controlled rate [9]. 
Differential shrinkage between the shell and core of wood is a major factor contributing to drying 
defects. During the initial stages of drying, the outer fibers (the shell) lose moisture and begin to 
shrink, while the inner core retains its MC and remains dimensionally stable. Since the core re-
stricts the shell from contracting freely, tensile stresses develop in the shell, while the core is 
subjected to compressive stresses, as illustrated in Figure 2.11. 
If the shell dries too rapidly, it may exceed its elastic limit, resulting in permanent set, where it 
remains in a stretched state without achieving full shrinkage. This can lead to surface checking, 
where cracks appear along the wood fibers.  
As drying progresses, the core eventually begins to lose moisture and shrink. However, because 
the shell has already dried in an expanded state, it inhibits the core’s natural contraction. This 
stress reversal places the core under tensile stress and the shell under compression. If these in-
ternal stresses become too severe, they can lead to internal cracking (honeycombing), in which 
the wood fails internally in tension across the grain. The checks follow the rays, which act as lines 
of weakness [25]. 
 
Figure 2.11: End grain view of board showing development of drying stresses early (a) and later (b) in drying 
[25]. 
The slow diffusion of moisture within wood further exacerbates these issues, creating non-uni-
form moisture distribution. This imbalance is particularly problematic in larger timber elements 
where differences in MC between the core and surface are more pronounced.  
Brandstätter et al. [27] used finite element simulations to investigate moisture-induced cracking 
in three beam types: two solid wood and one glulam. Drying was simulated from an initial uniform 
MC of 15 %, with exposure on all sides. The results showed that moisture gradients develop from 
the core to the surface, leading to surface cracks on the drying faces. Cracking was more severe 
when the moisture gradient was steep, especially in larger cross-sections. A strong correlation 
==PAGE:19==
 
12 
 
was found between moisture gradient and crack depth, and cracks in glulam initiated in nearly 
every lamella, particularly near the edges. Gradual drying by stepwise reduction of RH was effec-
tive in reducing cracking in small cross-sections but less so in larger ones. 
Autengruber et al. [28] used finite element simulations to investigate how climate-driven mois-
ture variations cause cracking in solid wood and glulam over a 14-month outdoor exposure cycle. 
In glulam, drying primarily caused cracks along glue lines and edges due to tensile stresses, while 
wetting induced cracking near the center as a result of swelling. The study showed that larger 
cross-sections are more prone to internal cracking due to slower progression toward EMC, while 
smaller sections primarily develop surface cracks. These findings align with those of Brandstätter 
et al., who similarly observed that crack location and severity are strongly influenced by cross-
section size and moisture gradients. Autengruber et al. further concluded that both drying and 
rewetting phases can contribute to damage, and that crack formation results from a combination 
of stress concentration, moisture transport, and anisotropic material behavior. 
Toivanen et al. [29] investigated the influence of different crack types on the load-bearing capac-
ity of glulam beams by testing 50 specimens (115 × 270 mm) across ten defined crack scenarios. 
They concluded that “crack depth is the most critical factor affecting the shear strength of glulam 
beams, as it directly correlates with the concentration of shear stresses. While crack length influ-
ences shear strength in high-stress areas, its overall impact is less significant.” Although moisture-
induced cracks, particularly near adhesive joints, were observed, the study did not determine 
their specific effect on load-bearing capacity. The authors noted that further testing is needed to 
more accurately assess the structural implications of such cracks. 
2.2.4.1. Steel fasteners 
Another important factor is the restriction of moisture-induced deformations, which can occur 
when certain parts of the timber are fixed in position by embedded fasteners. This restraint can 
lead to the development of internal stresses within the timber. To mitigate this issue, a draft for 
Eurocode 5 (2023) provides guidelines for dowel-type fasteners, specifying in section 11.3.8.5 
that if swelling and shrinkage of the wood are restricted - such as by steel plates in a connection 
- the distance between the outermost fasteners perpendicular to the grain should not exceed 400 
mm [30]. Risk of splitting at the fasteners can occur as illustrated in Figure 2.12.

When wood is restrained from shrinking, as in dowel-type joints with slotted-in steel plates, dry-
ing induces stresses perpendicular to the grain. This can result in loose fits, dowel deformation, 
and cracks, typically forming along dowel holes and parallel to the grain [18]. 
Sjödin investigated the behavior of multiple dowel-type glulam beam joints with embedded steel 
plates subjected to controlled climate variations. The embedded steel plate and dowels were in-
stalled in the specimens once they had reached an EMC of 12%. Subsequently, the climate condi-
tions were adjusted from 20°C and 65% RH to 20°C and 30% RH, corresponding to an EMC of 
approximately 12 % and 8 %, respectively. This climate was maintained for approximately 110 
days before the specimens were tension tested to failure. Cracks were observed in the specimens 
just before failure, and shrinkage deformations perpendicular to the grain were measured at 
three different locations on the glulam cross-sections, showing a 0.5% reduction in the cross-
sectional dimension perpendicular to the grain.  
When compared with the expected free shrinkage of spruce using the average transverse shrink-
age coefficient (αₜ ≈ 0.21%), calculated as 4 ∙ 0.21% ≈ 0.8%, the measured shrinkage appears in-
consistent with unrestrained behavior. This indicates that shrinkage was partially restrained by 
the steel plate and dowels, inducing tensile stresses perpendicular to the grain - likely the cause 
of the observed cracks. 
Sjödin also found that joints with a greater height (372 mm compared to 252 mm) and a larger 
spacing between dowel rows (60 mm compared to 36 mm) experienced slightly higher stress 
levels. This increased stress may have contributed to the higher occurrence of moisture-induced 
cracks in the larger joints. Sjödin also observed that large steel-to-timber dowel joints lost 
strength due to restrained shrinkage - an effect not seen in small joints [33].  
The tendency to cracking is generally more pronounced the larger the cross-section and the faster 
the drying rate [18]. Dietsch explains that shrinkage cracks can be attributed to: “Prevention of 
free shrinkage or swelling deformation of the cross-section by restraining forces, e.g. from con-
nections covering larger heights or dowel-type [connections]. In these cases, equilibrium of ten-
sile and compressive moisture induced stresses is impeded, resulting in stresses of higher mag-
nitude and eventually in deep shrinkage cracks” [34]. 
While most existing studies address moisture intrusion in timber and its direct effects on the ma-
terial, limited attention has been given to the behavior of moisture exposed glulam joints involv-
ing fasteners and brackets. There remains a lack of knowledge regarding the effects of moisture 
gradient and drying on such structural connections. 
2.2.5. MC Affecting Wood Strength 
The MC in wood affects its mechanical properties. When the MC is below the FSP, a reduction in 
absorbed water leads to increased strength, as the cell walls contract and the wood structure 
becomes denser. Conversely, as the MC increases toward FSP, the cell walls swell, the internal 
structure loosens, and the wood’s strength decreases. Once FSP is exceeded, additional water ex-
ists only as free water in the cell lumens and does not further affect the wood’s strength [8]. The 
relationship between MC and mechanical strength is illustrated in Figure 2.13.

2.3. Literature Review - Drying Methods for Moisture-Exposed Mass Timber 
This chapter reviews drying methods for moisture-exposed mass timber. Due to limited research 
on drying of water-exposed glulam, studies on cross-laminated timber (CLT) are included. A full 
overview of the literature study is provided in the Appendix A. 
Udele et al. tested two drying schedules on 3-layer CLT bracket connections fully saturated by 
four weeks of water submersion, reaching moisture levels above FSP. The mild schedule involved 
drying at 30 °C for 300 hours followed by 35 °C for 200 hours, while the aggressive regime used 
60 °C for 300 hours followed by 82 °C for 200 hours, intended to simulate industrial kiln condi-
tions. RH was not reported in either case. The mild schedule preserved mechanical performance 
better, while the aggressive regime led to increased brittleness. Udele et al. noted that checking 
was less apparent in specimens subjected to the mild or air-drying schedule, indicating that 
slower drying reduced the development of internal stresses. However, no reduction in load-bear-
ing capacity was observed regardless of drying method. Moisture measurements were taken at 
50 mm depth using a hammer probe, and all specimens reached EMC after drying period. In ad-
dition to the above regimes, Udele et al. also tested outdoor air-drying with roof cover over a nine-
month period, from September to June, encompassing humid winter months. This method repre-
sented the mildest drying condition tested in the study. Complete drying reaching EMC was 
achieved by the end of the period. It further found that while air-drying was the most conservative 
method in terms of damage, it required significantly more time, posing a potential risk of biodete-
rioration.  
Bora et al. exposed 3-layer CLT bracket connections to rain cycles that raised the MC to approxi-
mately 20–25% measured using a Delmhorst surface probe. Their findings showed that repeated 
wetting and redrying had no measurable effect on the mechanical performance of the connection. 
Post-drying damage, such as cracking, was not investigated. The results indicate that the level and 
duration of moisture exposure prior to drying play a critical role in whether damage develops 
during the drying phase. 
Schmidt et al. studied 5-layer CLT floor panels exposed to repeated wetting cycles in a rain cham-
ber simulating 13.3 cm rainfall per week, at 85–90% RH and temperatures of 9–10 °C. The panels 
==PAGE:22==
 
15 
 
were subjected to a sequence of wetting and drying cycles: 11 days of wetting, 82 days of drying, 
3 days of wetting, 48 days of drying, and a final 2-day wetting. Drying was conducted in a climate 
chamber at 21–24 °C and 30–40% RH. Despite no layer exceeding 16% MC, cracking, checking, 
and delamination occurred, especially at the edges and half-lap joints, indicating that damage was 
initiated in the end grain where moisture ingress was greatest. 
Fan-assisted drying was tested by Shirmohammadi and Faircloth on 3-layer CLT panel sub-
merged for 120 hours. Core cut-outs from the panel were dried under 20 ± 5 °C, 62 ± 23% RH, air 
velocity 5.1–6.6 m/s. Surface drying proved efficient, but internal MC remained above 20% after 
72 hours, illustrating the challenge of uniformly drying mass timber sections. The study did not 
report on material degradation or time to full drying. 
In a field study, Lima et al. monitored 5-layer L-bracket CLT connections subjected to outdoor 
exposure for approximately five months, beginning in winter and ending in spring. No active dry-
ing was applied. Moisture measurements were conducted in distinct zones within the connec-
tions. During spring, when drying rates increased (due to warmer weather), drying cracks devel-
oped, particularly in the end-grain zones, which were the most exposed. These findings link high 
drying rates in critical zones with increased risk of cracking and damage. 
Across the reviewed studies, a consistent gap in knowledge emerges regarding the critical thresh-
olds for safe drying of moisture-exposed mass timber. While some studies link rapid drying to 
increased damage, there is no consensus on the maximum allowable drying rate before damage 
occurs. Furthermore, key drying parameters such as RH are in some studies unreported, limiting 
the ability to compare findings or establish guidelines. Most studies focus on specific timber prod-
ucts (primarily CLT) and specific connection test setups, with limited transferability to other con-
figurations or materials such as glulam. Overall, current research on optimal drying methods for 
wetted mass timber remains limited, and the optimal balance between drying time, damage risk, 
and moisture uniformity remains unresolved.  
2.3.1. Guidelines 
Several guidelines emphasize the importance of preventing moisture ingress in mass timber 
structures. 
Canadian Wood Council refer to Wang’s on-site study about moisture management of wood con-
struction: [35] Regarding water ingress in glulam and CLT, he notes: “When glulam and CLT are 
subjected to long periods of wetting (e.g., weeks) enabling deep water penetration […] the subse-
quent drying will be very slow. This can lead to excessive damage and must be avoided.” 
Preventing moisture uptake and moisture traps in connections, and thereby reducing subsequent 
damage, is addressed in the Norwegian Limtreboken [36]: 
“Carefully designed details that prevent moisture uptake are of utmost importance; in particular, 
it is important that joints are designed so that free water is not supplied to the wood (for example, 
by limiting the area of exposed end grain, avoiding narrow gaps that may enable capillary uptake 
of water and reduced ventilation possibilities, etc.).” 
However, recommendations on how to proceed once moisture damage has occurred are frag-
mented and inconsistent. The following presents an overview of the available guidelines related 
to drying procedures. 
==PAGE:23==
 
16 
 
No publications were found that directly address drying times for moisture trapped within mass 
timber elements. However, general guidance on the drying of wood materials - primarily refer-
ring to planks and boards - is provided in the Danish SBi Guideline 278 [37], which states: “The 
drying time increases with the dimension of the construction. […] As a rule of thumb, for all ma-
terials, a doubling of the material thickness results in approximately four times longer drying 
time.” 
SBi 278 [37] also outlines the advantages and disadvantages of different drying methods, includ-
ing natural drying, heating with ventilation, and the use of dehumidifiers. It notes that excessive 
dehumidification can lower air humidity to the extent that materials such as wood may suffer 
damage. 
Swedish Wood’s The Glulam Handbook – Volume 4 [38] notes that: “If the glulam has become 
damp, it must be allowed to dry out slowly to prevent cracking.” 
Similarly, the Swedish guide Moisture-proof CLT construction without a full temporary shelter 
[39] suggests that drying can be accelerated using dehumidifiers or heating combined with in-
creased ventilation. However, it stresses that the process must be carefully controlled to prevent 
checking and that applying excessive heat too early should be avoided. 
The Norwegian Byggforskserien [40] recommends a controlled drying process, warning that 
rapid drying increases the risk of cracking. It highlights fan-assisted drying as faster and more 
uniform than ambient drying. However, it also acknowledges that while ambient drying is slower 
and less efficient, it may be necessary over extended periods to reduce MC in the deeper layers of 
timber elements. 
The Danish Træ 80 - Træbyggeri med CLT [41] addresses the importance moisture monitoring 
and provides guidance on moisture monitoring in case of moisture exposure but does not include 
guidance on drying methods. 
In summary, current guidelines focus primarily on preventing moisture ingress rather than 
providing clear procedures for drying once exposure has occurred. Recommendations are frag-
mented and rely on general advice, such as avoiding rapid drying, without offering specific pa-
rameters. This highlights a clear gap in practical, evidence-based guidance for drying moisture-
exposed mass timber.

2.4. WUFI® 2D theory 
Hygrothermal simulations in this thesis are performed using WUFI® 2D. WUFI stands for 
"Wärme- und Feuchtetransport instationär", which translates to "Transient Heat and Moisture 
Transport". The software is developed by the Fraunhofer Institute for Building Physics (Fraunho-
fer IBP). The underlying calculation model is described in the publication “Simultaneous Heat and 
Moisture Transport in Building Components” [42]. 
WUFI® 2D is a numerical simulation tool developed to model the two-dimensional transport of 
heat and moisture through multi-layered building assemblies. This is particularly useful in ana-
lyzing areas with multidirectional moisture movement. WUFI® accounts for: 
• 
Material-specific properties, including moisture storage function, density, porosity, capil-
lary transport and vapor diffusion resistance. 
• 
Multi-layered constructions, where each layer can have unique properties and interac-
tions. 
• 
Boundary conditions such as temperature, humidity, rain, solar radiation, adiabatic sur-
faces and contact with water [42]. 
Figure 2.14 presents a flow chart illustrating the simulation process in WUFI® 2D, including input 
data, model processing, and post-processing of results.

2.4.1. Limitations of the Software 
While WUFI® is a powerful tool, it has some known limitations and assumptions that are relevant 
for this project: 
Material homogeneity: Each material layer is assumed to be homogeneous. Complex internal 
structures, such as fiber orientations or lamella configurations, as in glued laminated timber, are 
==PAGE:25==
 
18 
 
not captured unless explicitly modelled as distinct layers or zones. Materials are treated as iso-
tropic. This means that the natural tangential and radial directions in wood are not accounted for 
in simulations. 
Material parameters and database limitations: WUFI®'s material database compiles data from 
different research institutes, each using different test methods, assumptions, and conditions. This 
requires a deep understanding of the origin and relevance of these parameters to modify them 
realistically.  
Some researchers note that WUFI® is best suited for comparative analysis rather than exact pre-
diction. It is a decision-support tool that can reflect material and climate behavior - provided the 
input and assumptions reflect the real-world setup [44] [43] [43].

3. Case A  
The moisture-related issue arising from a construction project referred to as Case A forms the 
basis for the design and methodology of the experimental testing conducted in this project. 
In Case A, glulam elements were exposed to prolonged rain due to an open building envelope.  
The glulam beams (GL30H), measuring 500 × 1640 mm and spanning 16 meters, support verti-
cally mounted CLT panels at their upper edges, which carry inclined skylights in the roof struc-

joint area. The primary drainage path for the accumulated water was through the slotted-in steel 
plate connection at the beam ends, allowing moisture to pass through the slots. This configuration 
and the resulting water exposure are illustrated in Figure 3.1. 
As a consequence, the beams were exposed to moisture both from above and through the slot. 
Although the exact duration of the exposure is unknown, suspected elevated MC due to this situ-

The calculated dimensional changes correspond to free swelling and shrinkage, e.g. unrestrained 
hygroexpansion. In Case A, the glulam beam is restrained by steel dowels that prevent vertical 
(transverse) expansion during wetting. This restraint leads to the development of internal 
stresses in the joint area, as the material attempts to expand but is mechanically confined. 
Given these conditions, Case A serves as the primary reference for the design of the experimental 
setup. The work presented in this study is therefore designed to replicate and isolate the key pa-
rameters observed in Case A - including one-sided wetting, limited drying surfaces, and partial 
mechanical restraint - in order to investigate moisture induced deformation and long-term risks 
under controlled conditions. 
Based on these findings, the study further aims to evaluate how different drying strategies affect 
the behavior of restrained timber joints after wetting. In particular, it seeks to identify which dry-
ing conditions that most optimally reduce internal stresses and limit the risk of crack formation 
in restrained glulam assemblies, such as the one observed in Case A.

5. Methodology 
5.1. Experimental Plan 
The experimental program is divided into two main parts, each comprising different test series 
with distinct objectives: 
- 
Test Series 1–3: Moisture Gradient without Mechanical Restraint 
- 
Test Series 4–5: Moisture Gradient with Mechanical Restraint 
Although the boundary conditions differ, all test series are based on the same fundamental setup: 
Each specimen consists of a full-scale glulam beam segment representing half a beam. This ap-
proach was selected to optimize the available material resources while maintaining a cross-sec-
tional size sufficient to develop and capture a moisture gradient. The configuration allows for 
investigation of hygro-mechanical behavior across the width of the section, which would be diffi-
cult to reproduce in smaller-scale specimens. 
The exact duration of water exposure in the Case A specimens is unknown. Achieving a similarly 
developed moisture gradient in full-scale glulam beams likely requires long-term moisture 
transport within the timber. Due to time limitations associated with this thesis project, it was not 
feasible to replicate such prolonged conditions. The experimental approach has therefore been 
adapted to accelerate moisture uptake while still aiming to reproduce the key characteristics of 
the observed gradient. 
5.2. Test Series 1–3: Moisture Gradient without Mechanical Restraint 
Test Series 1, 2 and 3 examine how glulam beams behave when they are exposed to a moisture 
gradient without any kind of mechanical restraint, such as dowels. The goal is to understand how 
changes in moisture alone affect the material. 
Because the setup is simple and clearly defined, it is also within the scope of this project to simu-
late the setup using moisture transport software such as WUFI®. This enables direct comparison 
between experimental measurements and simulation results. 
To replicate the conditions observed in Case A, specific surfaces of the beam specimens were 
sealed using tape: 
- 
The top (T) was sealed to represent a surface covered with roofing felt (as in Case A), 
where moisture cannot escape upward. 
- 
S3 was sealed to simulate a long, continuous beam, where moisture escape in the longitu-
dinal direction is restricted. 
- 
S4 was sealed to reflect how the beam in Case A is placed directly against a column or 
another beam, preventing moisture exchange through the end grain. 
- 
S2 and the bottom (B) were left unsealed, as these surfaces are freely exposed in the real 
structure and allow moisture to exit. 
- 
After the moisture conditioning phase, S1 was sealed as well, to simulate a full cross-sec-
tion and prevent moisture from escaping through this side. 
This sealing configuration creates a realistic boundary condition based on Case A. An overview of 
the specimen geometry and sealing configuration is shown in Figure 5.1.

5.10. 2D Simulations of the Experiments 
Two-dimensional simulations were carried out using WUFI® 2D to analyze the moisture behavior 
of wood specimens subjected conditions similar to those in the laboratory tests. Although mate-
rial calibration was initially not within the scope of this thesis, it proved to be an unavoidable 
factor in approaching realistic results. 
5.10.1. Calibration of the WUFI® 2D Simulation Model 
To ensure that the simulation results accurately reflect the behavior observed during experi-
mental testing, the WUFI® 2D model was carefully calibrated. The aim of this calibration was to 
match the simulated moisture dynamics with the measured data from the wood specimens, with 
particular attention to the moisture distribution pattern. The calibration was performed for both 
the wetting and drying phases to ensure that the model accurately represents the initial moisture 
uptake as well as its subsequent redistribution and release over time. The sensitivity of key pa-
rameters, such as material properties, water absorption, and the depth of moisture exposure, was 
evaluated. 
5.10.1.1. Description of Geometry  
The geometry applied in the calibration is based on the surface referred to as S3 and represents 
a glulam specimen with dimensions of 112 × 399 mm, which corresponds to the average initial 
geometry of all specimens prior to moisture exposure. This orientation reflects a cross-sectional 
view of the lamellas. However, to reduce model complexity, the lamellar structure is not modeled. 
Instead, the specimen is represented as a homogeneous material with uniform properties as seen 
in Figure 5.21. 
5.10.1.2. Climate Conditions  
The temperature and humidity conditions for the wetting simulation were based on measure-
ments from the room where the water bath experiment took place, which was maintained at 21°C 
and 45% RH. A moisture source was applied on side S1. To represent the taped top surface (T) of 
the specimen, a membrane with an Sd-value of 40 m was included in the model, matching the 
diffusion resistance of the Rothoblaas tape used in the physical experiment. After the wetting 
phase, a membrane was also added on side S1, and the simulation continued under drying condi-
tions consistent with those used in experimental Series 1, 2 and 3. The simulation setup during 
the wetting and drying phases is illustrated in Figure 5.21.

5.10.1.5. Calibration of Moisture Source Width 
During the wetting phase of the experimental test series, the specimens were covered by approx-
imately 5–7 mm of water on the water-exposed surface. However, due to observed warping, it is 
likely that some areas, particularly near the center, were exposed to water depths of up to 10 mm, 
while the top and bottom edges may have experienced less exposure.

To evaluate the sensitivity of the model to changes in the applied moisture source width, simula-
tions were conducted with source depths ranging from 1 mm to 10 mm. The results indicated that 
the applied width had an impact on total moisture absorption: narrower moisture source widths 
led to substantially lower simulated water uptake, falling well below the total water uptake meas-
ured in the experimental work.  
A 10 mm moisture source width was ultimately selected as shown on Figure 5.26, as it was con-
sidered a realistic estimate of the average submersion depth during testing, and it produced mois-
ture distribution most consistent with those measured experimentally. 
5.10.1.6. Other Choices 
The numerical grid setting Fine was selected in WUFI®, as it offers the highest available resolution 
and enables more accurate simulation of moisture gradients. The maximum number of grid ele-
ments was set to 150 × 150. 
WUFI® automatically subdivides components with an even number of layers between material 
interfaces and adjusts the grid density accordingly. This results in a denser grid near material 
transitions - where moisture gradients are expected to be steep -and a coarser grid in more uni-
form regions to optimize computational efficiency. In general, small grid elements are required 
in areas with high moisture dynamics, while larger grid elements can be used in more stable re-
gions [57].

7. Discussion 
The discussion presents an uncertainty assessment of the comparison between experiments and 
simulations, an evaluation of the optimal drying climate based on test results compared to condi-
tions in Case A, and a discussion of strategies for preventing moisture accumulation in glulam 
joints as well as approaches for managing damage when it occurs. 
7.1.1. Uncertainties and Errors: Experiments vs. Simulations  
The following section presents an assessment of uncertainties for Series 1, 2, and 3, based on 
experimental data and observations, as well as simulation results. This focus is chosen because 
only these series, which were not subjected to restraint or influenced by dowel connections, were 
included in the simulations. As the wetting and drying procedures, as well as measuring methods, 
in Series 4 and 5 match those in Series 1 and 2, the associated experimental uncertainties are 
considered applicable. 
7.1.1.1. Experiments 
Resistance measurements (2%) 
Delmhorst Europe [59] state that: “Electrical accuracy of all Delmhorst meters is better than 0.5 
percent between 6 and 12 percent MC; better than 1 percent between 12 and 20 percent MC; and 
better than 2 percent between 20 and 30 percent.” 
The uncertainty is therefore set to be ±2%. 
Damage in pin insulation (2%) 
Repeated hammering of insolated pins caused small scratches that led to insulation damage. This 
poses a risk of measurement deviations, as electrical charge may be detected through a scratch 
rather than at the pin tip. 
Before measurements were conducted, the insulation on the pins was carefully inspected for 
scratches or delamination. In cases where the insulation was damaged, the pins were coated with 
one layer of Belton acrylic lacquer spray. 
Since pins were carefully inspected before measurements the uncertainty is set to ±2%. 
Monitoring through tape (3%, only applied to lower bound) 
To evaluate the effect of measuring through tape, preliminary tests were conducted before the 
specimen series.  
Measurements were taken through tape and were compared to measurements on the same wood 
surface without tape. It was found that measurements through tape were up to 10% higher than 
those taken without tape. 
Since this uncertainty only applies to areas covered by tape, which account for approximately 33 
percent of all measurements, the combined impact is estimated as:  
100% × 33% ≈ 3% 
As measurements through tape consistently yielded higher values than those without, the asso-
ciated uncertainty is applied only to the lower bound of the uncertainty range. 
The measurement uncertainty is assumed to be -3%. 
==PAGE:90==
 
83 
 
Monitoring in same holes (10%, only applied to upper bound) 
Repeated use of the same pin holes can reduce measurement accuracy due to enlarged holes and 
poor pin contact. However, the same pin holes were used to enable consistent comparison over 
time and to minimize material damage, as creating too many holes could introduce other uncer-
tainties, such as unintended ventilation paths. A comparison between repeated measurements in 
the same holes and in newly made holes showed that MC readings in new holes were generally 
10% higher. 
As measurements in same holes consistently yielded lower values than those in new, the associ-
ated uncertainty is applied only to the upper bound of the uncertainty range. 
The measurement uncertainty is assumed to be +10%. 
Uncertainty associated with tape coverage (5%, only applied to upper bound) 
Minor drying was suspected to occur through end grain at screw holes and in air pockets formed 
due to warping of the specimen. Screw holes were also observed to enlarge as a result of vertical 
deformation during the test. These uncertainties are illustrated in the photos in Figure 7.1. 
  
  
 
Figure 7.1: Left: Enlarged screw holes. Middle and right: S3, the end-grain side, showing folds in the tape 
caused by warping. 
However, any exposed areas, never exceeding 3 mm, were resealed with new tape upon detection. 
Unintentional leakage paths cannot be represented in the simulation but may help explain dis-
crepancies between measured and simulated moisture distributions.  
As measurements will always show lower values in case of leakage compared to perfectly sealed, 
the associated uncertainty is only applied to the upper bound of the uncertainty range. 
The associated uncertainty is estimated at +5%. 
Potential capillary uptake through taped side surfaces (1%) 
During water exposure, the sides of the specimens were sealed with tape. It remains unknown 
whether the tape permitted capillary absorption, as shown in Figure 7.2, as this was not tested.  
A minor uncertainty is therefore acknowledged and estimated at ±1%. 
==PAGE:91==
 
84 
 
 
Figure 7.2: Illustration of capillary suction through taped side surface. 
Bent pins (2%) 
With repeated hammering into the material, the pins could occasionally become slightly bent. 
Variations in the distance between pin tips can affect measurement accuracy, as resistance read-
ings depend on the distance between the pins. It was observed that bent pins typically resulted 
in a reduced tip distance. Bent pins were straightened using a rubber hammer to avoid damaging 
the insulated coating or were replaced if straightening was not possible.  
A comparison between measurements using straight new pins and bent pins showed a variation 
of approximately 5%.  
Since pins were straightened or replaced when bending was observed, the measurement uncer-
tainty is set at ±2%. 
Variation in measurement placement (1%) 
Measurement points within each layer were marked to ensure consistent placement across all 
specimens. However, in some cases a knot was located at the intended position, requiring the 
point to be shifted by up to 10 mm. As a result, some measurements may have been taken closer 
to the edge of a defined layer rather than precisely at its center, though they are still classified as 
mid-layer measurements. This may have caused slight deviations from the true values at the exact 
center. However, this occurred in only a few specimens and affected a limited number of points.

Material parameters (10%) 
First and foremost, the experimental work is based on nine quite different specimens, each with 
varying MC values. While they reveal a general trend, they do not show identical behavior. This 
highlights the variability of the material and how it responds differently to moisture - both during 
wetting and drying. Consequently, calibrating a simulation model to reflect this behavior is inher-
ently challenging. 
Another source of uncertainty lies in the use of average material parameters for sorption behav-
ior. In WUFI®, the model relies on a single, combined sorption curve and does not differentiate 
between adsorption and desorption processes. This simplification neglects the hysteresis effect 
and may lead to deviations in both the wetting and drying phases.  
When comparing the applied curve for spruce radial with reference sorption data from [19], it is 
evident that the material’s behavior falls between the adsorption and desorption curves, with 
deviations ranging between 0 and 5 percentage points MC, depending on the humidity range. 
Another source of uncertainty in this study arises from the material parameters used in the 
WUFI® simulations - specifically, the liquid transport coefficients for suction (DWS) and redistri-
bution (DWW), as well as the water vapor diffusion resistance factor (µ). Richter et al. report that 
the μ-factor for spruce varies widely- from 1 to 1000 depending on RH. Their comparison of lit-
erature values, WUFI® material data, and experimental results highlights substantial variation, all 
of which are considered valid within their respective contexts [56]. 
Fraunhofer states in the WUFI® database under the spruce radial material entry: “To be used for 
wooden parts in direct contact with rain water and for analyzing drying processes at high MCs. 
Should, however, be used with caution and only for approximative calculations, because of large 
uncertainties. The liquid transport coefficients have been determined from the moisture behavior 
of specimens exposed to natural weather. The moisture-dependence of the µ-value is already in-
cluded in the liquid transport coefficients” [61]. 
This indicates that the transport coefficients are interdependent, and that altering individual pa-
rameters may disrupt other parameters and the internal balance of the model. By changing indi-
vidual parameters, there is also a risk of moving away from physically realistic modelling and 
instead relying on empirical adjustment - where the model matches the measurements but no 
longer reflects the true material behavior.

Krus [55] investigates the simulation of moisture transport in spruce along the longitudinal di-
rection and highlights several fundamental challenges when attempting to calibrate WUFI® to 
match experimental results for wood. His findings help explain the calibration difficulties encoun-
tered in this study and support the need for caution when interpreting simulation output for 
wood materials: 
• 
Unusual Transport Behavior in Wood: Krus points out that transport coefficients in wood 
often need to be set unnaturally high in order to achieve agreement between simulated 
and measured drying curves. In fact, drying transport coefficients must typically be higher 
than those used for absorption. In WUFI®, these coefficients are typically set equal for 
materials such as spruce, meaning they must be evaluated and adjusted manually during 
calibration. 
• 
Presence of an Additional Transport Mechanism: Krus explains that the high moisture 
movement observed during drying cannot be explained solely by classical mechanisms 
such as vapor diffusion or capillary suction. It is hypothesized that this is due to wood's 
ability to swell when wet. As wood dries from a swollen state, internal mechanical 
stresses may create pressure-driven transport that effectively "pushes" moisture out - a 
mechanism not accounted for in the WUFI® simulation model. 
• 
Distinction Between Redistribution and Drying: Krus also emphasizes the difference be-
tween redistribution (movement of moisture within the material) and drying (moisture 
leaving the material). During redistribution, moisture attempts to move from wet to dry 
regions, but if the dry regions are not swollen, the capillary suction is limited. In contrast, 
drying begins in a swollen state, enabling more efficient transport. WUFI® does not dif-
ferentiate between these two transport modes. 
• 
Model Accuracy and Moisture Profiles: While Krus concludes that simulations may achieve 
a reasonable match with average MCs, he notes that accurately replicating detailed mois-
ture profiles - such as those measured in this study - remains a challenge due to the above 
limitations. 
In light of these findings and the model’s ability to reproduce realistic moisture transport, the 
uncertainty associated with the calibrated material parameters is estimated as ±10%.

7.3. Evaluating Case A  
When evaluating Case A - and the simulation’s potential to predict future conditions of the beams 
- it is important to consider uncertainties related both to both Tector sensor’s measurements and 
the validity of the simulations. 
7.3.1. Tector Measurements Uncertainties 
First, irregular fluctuations were observed in the measurement data from Case A. The MC data 
from layer 1 showed a sudden jump in late March, with values increasing abruptly from one day 
to the next. The reason for this is unclear, but the change amounted to 1.5% MC - equivalent to 
the total decrease observed in the first layer over nine months from June 2024 to March 2025. 
One possible explanation is thermal disturbance caused by solar radiation. Because the beams 
support roof windows, direct sunlight may cause localized heating when the sun is high in the 
sky. This heating could affect the sensors and lead to inaccurate readings. Since calibration of a 
simulation model depends on accurate input data, such measurement uncertainty also under-
mines the validity of the simulation results. 
Second, it is difficult to determine whether the insulation around the insulated screws has been 
damaged. During the experiments, it was observed that screwing through knots in the wood in-
troduces large resistance. When inserting screws there is a risk of hitting knots, which are denser 
and can cause additional wear on the insulation. Furthermore, measurements taken near knots 
or potential cracks may also be less accurate due to local material irregularities. 
Third, the physical accuracy of screw placement introduces another layer of uncertainty. For ex-
ample, when sensors must be installed at a depth of 220 mm, it is difficult to ensure perfectly 
perpendicular insertion relative to the surface. Even slight deviations can alter the distance be-
tween screw tips, which affects resistance measurements and therefore the derived MC. 
Finally, according to Tector, it is not possible to measure directly in glue lines, which further limits 
the representativeness of the measurements [44].

7.3.2. Limitations of Simulations on Full-Scale Conditions 
A limitation lies in the use of a two-dimensional simulation domain. Some processes in Case A 
occur in three dimensions, but the WUFI® simulations are restricted to a two-dimensional do-
main. Specifically, moisture transport through end-grain and the redistribution of water along the 
longitudinal direction of the beam cannot be captured in a 2D simulation. In Case A, moisture is 
observed to accumulate near the joint region, while drier conditions are found further along the 
beam. Although this redistribution toward drier zones occurs slowly, it plays a role in the overall 
moisture balance but is excluded from the simulations. 
In addition, the end-grain surfaces of the beams are unsealed, and small gaps (ranging from 0 to 
10 mm) were present between the beam ends and adjacent timber components (see Figure 7.6). 
These gaps allow moisture to migrate or evaporate through alternate paths not accounted for in 
the simulation model. 
 
 
Figure 7.6: Left: Visible gap between two timber elements. 
Right: Beam studied in Case A. 
Uncertainty is also introduced through simplifications in the initial MC profile. The initial profile 
was based on Tector readings at three depths, taken at the beginning of the monitoring period. 
As the full moisture distribution was unknown, these measurements did not permit the construc-
tion of a continuous or fully representative MC profile. It remains unclear, for example, whether 
higher moisture concentrations were retained near the slot. 
In addition, the simulation excludes the overlying CLT elements, which in reality could affect the 
drying dynamics by limiting evaporation or altering local humidity conditions around the beam. 
This additional simplification may further contribute to discrepancies between the simulated and 
actual moisture distribution. 
The simulated MC remains consistently below the measured Tector values for most of the period 
beginning in June 2024, only rising slightly toward the end of the observation. This may suggest 
model drift, potentially due to underestimated drying rates, although this cannot be confirmed 
with certainty.

One possible explanation lies in the material parameters used in the simulation. As shown in the 
experimental simulations, accurate calibration of these parameters is highly challenging without 
direct experimental determination [55]. 
Previous studies support this observation. McClung et al. [62] found that WUFI® Pro (1D) simu-
lations aligned reasonably well with measured values at low MC in CLT panels but tended to over-
estimate core moisture and underestimate to RH changes. Kordziel et al. [63] reported similar 
underestimations in CLT layers with higher MC values between 20–30%, whereas lower values 
near 15% were more accurately simulated in WUFI® Pro. 
Lepage [43] conducted a comprehensive calibration study using WUFI® Pro for CLT walls and 
concluded that matching simulations to measured MC curves required substantial and sometimes 
non-physical adjustments to input parameter - such as modifying suction and redistribution 
curves beyond physically realistic values. The calibration process was largely based on trial and 
error.  Even then, discrepancies persisted between simulated and measured MC profile.  
Notably, the majority of simulation studies using WUFI® focus on CLT, while very few address 
glulam. Currently, very few known WUFI®-based studies have specifically modelled moisture 
transport in glulam beams, highlighting a significant gap in literature. One study models surface 
moisture transport in glulam beams to a limited depth of 25 mm using WUFI® Pro [64]. Another 
study investigates glulam beams using other numerical simulation methods [43].  
7.3.3. Comparison of Experimental Results, Simulations, and Full-Scale Conditions 
Although the experimental results are not directly comparable with Case A due to differences in 
geometry and boundary conditions, their simulations reveal a consistent trend: Prolong drying 
time. 
Using Series 3 as a reference for the beam in Case A is relevant, as both appear to have been 
wrapped in plastic and exposed to similar climate conditions (23 °C and 60% RH). In the long-
term simulation of Series 3 EMC was reached after roughly one year. By comparison, the beam in 
Case A did not reach EMC after six years. However, this comparison should be interpreted with 
caution. As earlier discussed, the Case A simulation indicated a model drift of the moisture gradi-
ent, likely leading to a conservative estimate of the drying time. The actual time to EMC in Case A 
may therefore be shorter than indicated by the simulation.  
According to SBI 278 the drying time increases 4 times when doubling the material thickness. 
This is due both to the fact that materials with smaller cross-sections have a greater surface area 
relative to their volume - enabling more efficient moisture exchange - and to the increased dis-
tance from the material core to the surface, which prolongs the diffusion path required for mois-
ture transport [37]. Based on the long-term simulation of Case A, compared with simulations of 
the experimental setup, the results indicate that approximately doubling the specimen width - 
and thereby the moisture transport path - results in a drying time more than six times longer. 
This exceeds the fourfold increase in drying time indicated by SBI. 
When comparing Case A to the laboratory experiments, it is essential to account for scale effects. 
The specimens in Series 1–3 represented only a quarter of the beam height in Case A, and those 
in Series 4 and 5 approximately one-third. In width, all specimens covered less than half the full 
beam. These smaller dimensions, combined with the steeper moisture gradients observed during 
==PAGE:102==
 
95 
 
wetting and drying, result in more pronounced internal differentials and faster drying rates, po-
tentially exaggerating crack formation compared to full-scale conditions. 
That said, it remains plausible that the full-scale beams in Case A were also subjected to steep 
moisture gradients shortly after wetting - conditions that could have generated internal stresses 
not visible at the surface [58]. Moreover, it is well established that elevated MC increases creep 
deformation, implying that the prolonged drying time in this case may contribute to increased 
long-term deflection [20]. 
Finally, the structural configuration should be taken into account. While the beams in Case A are 
taller than the experimental specimens and have more closely spaced dowels, which may contrib-
ute to a more even stress distribution, the span between the outermost dowels still exceeds 
400 mm. As a result, a substantial section of the beam height remains restrained, limiting free 
deformation and potentially increasing the risk of crack formation due to moisture-induced in-
ternal stresses. 
7.4. Discussion on Prevention of Moisture Traps in Large Glulam Joints by Plan-
ning and Moisture Monitoring 
7.4.1. Planning and Design 
In Case A, a key issue was the placement of the dowel slot on the top of the beam, which allowed 
water to enter the joint. In the same project, a control beam with a bottom-placed slot and no 
openings or cuts on the top surface was moisture-monitored at three depths to assess whether 
this alternative design reduced moisture problems. The beam was exposed to the same weather 
conditions as the others in Case A, yet measurements showed that it maintained a MC of 12% ± 2, 
which is within an acceptable range. The monitored control beam is shown in Figure 7.7. 
This suggests that placing slots or slots on the bottom, where rain exposure is minimal, results in 
a more robust design. It also highlights that moisture risk in mass timber construction is strongly 
affected by detailing and early design decisions. In general, designers aim should be to minimize 
details where water can accumulate, particularly recessed cuts or openings on upward-facing 
surfaces or areas where water may become trapped and unable to escape. Identifying high-risk 
areas during the design phase and defining inspection points may help contractors better manage 
moisture exposure during construction.

The contractor [65] acknowledged in retrospect that the approach could have been improved and 
that insufficient prioritization of moisture control may have contributed to the problems ob-

The contractor notes that although the intentions behind moisture control efforts may be good, 
they are often deprioritized due to a limited understanding of the long-term consequences. For a 
moisture strategy to be effective, it is essential not only to establish it early in the project but also 
to communicate it clearly and ensure that on-site workers receive proper training. Without this, 
even a well-designed strategy can fail in practice, as was the case in Case A. 
The issue identified in Case A illustrates a specific risk of moisture damage in mass glulam timber. 
Despite this, the project involved systematic monitoring and evaluation of beam conditions 
throughout the drying phase. The following sections 7.4.3 and 7.4.4 present insights from inter-
views with two additional timber construction projects, each revealing distinct challenges related 
to moisture monitoring. 
7.4.3. Case B: Challenges in Long-Term Moisture Monitoring Due to Inadequate Sensor 
Planning 
In October 2021, a total of 77 wood moisture sensors were installed in glulam elements of a large-
scale timber construction during the final stages of the building process. The primary objective of 
the installation was to monitor moisture levels within the structure, as there were concerns that 
certain elements may have been exposed to elevated MC due to the absence of weather protection 
during construction. 
However, according to the current facility manager [66], there were uncertainties as to whether 
a systematic plan for sensor placement had ever been developed. Furthermore, no comprehen-
sive documentation was carried out to record the exact installation locations or the rationale be-
hind their positioning. It remains unclear which types of joints or structural elements were pri-
oritized, how deep the sensors were embedded in the timber, or what criteria guided the selection 
of measurement points. Additionally, several sensors were embedded within the structure and 
are not visible, which poses a significant challenge in the eventually event of a high-MC alarm, as 
their precise locations were not properly recorded during installation. 
Furthermore, the sensors were not connected to a gateway capable of transmitting data, resulting 
in a complete lack of data collection from the time of installation until February 2025. This has 
created a substantial data gap during a critical period when continuous monitoring could have 
been essential for detecting and mitigating potential moisture-related damage. Current readings 
indicate that all measurement points are within the range of 8–13% MC. 
A visual inspection of the accessible sensors reveals that many are located at the base of columns 
and at the ends of beams - areas likely selected due to suspected vulnerability to moisture ingress. 
Examples are shown in Figure 7.8. However, the rationale behind these specific placements could 
not be clarified.

This case highlights how insufficient planning, inadequate documentation, and a lack of system-
atic knowledge transfer between contractor, client, and following facility management teams can 
lead to significant challenges in the operational phase of a building. This highlights the need for

7.4.4. Case C: Lack of Moisture Monitoring Knowledge 
A former employee [67] from a contractor company involved in a major timber construction pro-
ject in 2024, comment that an urgent need for enhanced moisture monitoring arose during the 
construction of the structural frame.  
The former employee expressed frustration over the performance of the sensors used on-site, 
noting that they short-circuited when exposed to water. This suggests that the sensors were in-
adequately protected against moisture ingress but also indicates that the timber elements they 
were intended to monitor may have been subjected to significant water exposure. 
The former employee had further noted that the sensors were only capable of measuring up to 
25% MC, and that readings beyond this threshold were unreliable. This limitation was perceived 
as problematic, particularly in situations where high MC levels were suspected. 
In addition, the responsible for installing the sensors experienced that the shrink-flex insulation 
covering the pins could detach, which may have introduced further uncertainty in the measure-
ments. The former employee described that the sensors were fragile and not well-suited for the 
rough conditions of a construction site. They were prone to damage or displacement, and in some 
cases, interfered with other installation work. Similar issues were reported by the contractor in 
Case A, where sensors had to be replaced during construction due to being knocked out of align-
ment or physically damaged. 
These comments highlight a broader issue: personnel responsible for on-site monitoring during 
the construction phase lacked a clear understanding of how to measure wood moisture and why 
an MC of 25% is considered critically high. This points to a need for better training and awareness 
among site teams regarding the implications of elevated MC and the technical limitations of the 
monitoring equipment.

7.4.5. Evaluation of Moisture Monitoring Practices and Industry Guidelines 
The findings from Case A, Case B, and Case C demonstrate that sensor installation alone is not 
sufficient for effective moisture monitoring. Accurate interpretation requires in-depth knowledge 
of moisture behavior in timber, and without a clear understanding of relevant indicators, early 
signs of moisture-related damage may be overlooked. The cases also highlight the importance of 
monitoring large structural elements at multiple depths, as MC can vary significantly within mass 
timber. Relying solely on surface or single-point measurements may result in a misleading repre-
sentation of the internal moisture condition.  
To improve the prevention of moisture traps and related damage in mass glulam timber joints, 
the presented Case A, Case B, and Case C illustrate that effective moisture monitoring requires 
planning, knowledge, and practical considerations. The cases highlight several limitations in cur-
rent moisture preventing, moisture monitoring and drying practices that can compromise their 
effectiveness. 
As demonstrated by the reviewed cases and current monitoring practices, there is a clear imbal-
ance between guidance on preventing moisture ingress and handling moisture once it has entered 
mass timber elements. As highlighted in Section 2.3.1 both national and international guidelines 
- such as Limtreboken [36], SBi 278 [37], and Moisture-proof CLT Construction Without a Full 
Temporary Shelter [39] - strongly emphasize the importance of preventing moisture ingress 
through careful detailing. However, there is a lack of consistent and practical advice for drying 
after exposure has occurred.   
Drying recommendations are fragmented, and typically refer to solid timber components (e.g., 
boards or planks) rather than to large structural elements such as glulam beams or CLT panels.  
SBi 278 [37] outlines drying time principles and methods such as ventilation and dehumidifica-
tion but does not provide parameters specific to mass timber. Similarly, Swedish and Norwegian 
guidelines emphasize slow and controlled drying to avoid cracking but offer no clear thresholds 
or timeframes applicable to mass timber elements [36] [39]. 
This fragmentation is particularly problematic given the evidence from the case studies, which 
shows that moisture can become trapped deep within structural joints, from where drying is 
slower. The absence of drying benchmarks or procedures makes it difficult for practitioners to 
assess the severity of moisture exposure or to determine when action is necessary. 
Træinformation’s publication Træ 80 – Træbyggeri med CLT [41] includes a section on moisture, 
offering practical guidance and identifying areas where moisture monitoring is especially im-
portant during construction. These recommendations provide a useful starting point for deter-
mining where and why monitoring may be required. However, some of the proposed methods 
present challenges in practice. One example, illustrated in Figure 7.9, suggests inserting a ham-
mer electrode vertically into the wall 20 mm from a wall–floor connection. In the experimental 
testing of this project, the hammer electrode method was found to pose a high risk of equipment 
damage, including broken insulation, bent pins, and inaccurate readings due to contact with wet 
surfaces on the pin threads. Furthermore, sideward hammering proved difficult to perform con-
sistently by hand without bending the pins, raising concerns about the method’s practical relia-
bility under site conditions.

Additionally, as noted in Case C, even permanently installed sensors proved fragile and interfered 
with other construction activities, further complicating moisture monitoring efforts under site 
conditions [67]. Notably, the publication by Træinformation is heavily inspired by Swedish 
Wood's Moisture-proof CLT Construction Without a Full Temporary Shelter [39]. 
 
Figure 7.9: Suggested measurement point using hammer electrode at the base of a CLT wall element from

Despite its recent publication, Træbyggeri med CLT [41] does not address drying or damage mit-
igation in CLT, which remains an oversight given the susceptibility of CLT joints to internal mois-
ture accumulation as reviewed in Section 2.3. Furthermore, with the exception of Swedish Wood 
[38], dedicated guidelines currently exist that specifically address moisture prevention in glulam, 
and no published guidelines or studies provide drying strategies or estimated drying times for 
glulam structures. Most existing research on drying behavior, methods, and consequences con-
tinues to focus primarily on CLT. 
While prevention is a focus in current guidelines, there is still an urgent need for practice-ori-
ented, evidence-based drying procedures tailored to mass timber construction. Without such 
guidance, the industry's ability to manage post-exposure scenarios remains limited, increasing 
the risk of undetected damage as observed in the cases and experimental work presented in this 
thesis. No publications were found that directly address drying times for moisture trapped in 
mass timber elements. This underlines in general the importance of preventing moisture ingress, 
particularly into the inner regions of the material.

8. Further Research 
This thesis has identified several key areas that require further investigation, which are summa-
rized in the following sections. 
8.1. Further Experimental Work 
Increase the number of specimens to reduce statistical uncertainty: The current test series 
include only three specimens per series, making it difficult to draw statistically robust conclu-
sions. A minimum of five specimens is recommended to improve the reliability of the results and 
to capture variability more accurately [33]. 
Extend the testing period to capture long-term behavior: The present drying durations (19 
and 40 days) offer an initial understanding of moisture effects but are insufficient for capturing 
long-term behavior. Extended testing would also provide a better basis for validating simulation 
models over time. 
Apply embedded sensors for continuous monitoring: The implementation of embedded mois-
ture sensors would allow for continuous data collection, improving accuracy and enabling more 
detailed temporal analysis of moisture behavior. 
Monitor deformations using strain gauges: Deformation monitoring through strain gauges 
would provide insights into stress fields over time and help determine the precise moment of 
crack initiation. 
Refine material parameter measurements: Hygrothermal material properties should be de-
termined through systematic, small-scale testing. This would improve input quality for simula-
tion models.  
Use of the slice method: In the present experiments, moisture gradients across the cross-section 
lead to internal restraint, hindering direct measurement of the material’s natural deformation. By 
isolating thin wood sections using the slice method and eliminating external constraints during 
wetting or drying, it becomes possible to measure hygro-expansion strain. In Figure 8.1 the prin-
ciple of the slicing method is shown [58].

strain data, supporting both model validation and a deeper understanding of deformation pat-
terns.

8.2. Further Simulation Work 
Simulations with more thorough calibration: Accurate simulation of moisture transport re-
quires well-calibrated material parameters. Calibration should be extended to longer drying pe-
riods to evaluate whether long-term simulation results align with experimental findings.  
Simulations with other numerical programs for hygrothermal analysis: Future work could 
explore additional modelling tools such as ANSYS, which has shown promising results in simulat-
ing moisture distribution in glulam [68]. Cross-validating with WUFI® may improve reliability. 
Implementation of Abaqus: Incorporating Abaqus could introduce mechanical simulations un-
der moisture-induced stress, offering stress distribution analysis.

9. Conclusion 
Mass timber has emerged as a sustainable alternative to conventional construction materials due 
to its low carbon footprint. However, its hygroscopic nature poses challenges, particularly in re-
lation to moisture-induced damage and the absence of established drying protocols once expo-
sure has occurred.  
This thesis specifically aimed to evaluate the influence of different drying strategies on the be-
havior of partially restrained glulam joints following moisture exposure. The work involved an-
alyzing moisture-induced deformations under controlled laboratory conditions and examining 
the underlying mechanisms responsible for these responses. Furthermore, the thesis sought to 
identify drying conditions that effectively reduce internal stress development and minimize the 
risk of crack formation in restrained glulam connections. This included a discussion of preven-
tion strategies based on case studies, as well as an assessment of required drying durations 
through hygrothermal simulations using WUFI® 2D. 
It can be concluded that: 
• 
Moisture-induced deformations were observed in all specimens and cracking occurred 
under all climate conditions. More severe cracking was observed under aggressive drying 
conditions (40 °C / 31% RH) compared to milder conditions (23 °C / 40% RH) in both 
restrained and unrestrained specimens. Specimens with slotted-in steel connections and 
a 448 mm restraint spacing between the dowels exhibited vertical deformation around 
the dowel holes and cracking as a result of moisture-induced gradients.   
• 
The mildest drying condition (24 °C and 60% RH) resulted in reduced cracking in unre-
strained specimens compared to the other tested climates (40 °C / 31% RH and 23 °C / 
40% RH). However, the number of specimens was too limited to support any general con-
clusion about this climate being the most optimal. Further study is recommended to in-
vestigate the long-term effects of this drying condition on restrained glulam joints.  
• 
While damage was effectively already initiated by the moisture uptake itself, the risk of 
further cracking can be reduced through controlled drying. Ideally, such wetting should 
be avoided entirely. However, this project concludes that the extent of damage can poten-
tially be mitigated through appropriate drying strategies. 
• 
WUFI® 2D long-term simulations showed that specimens dried at 24 °C and 60% RH  
reached EMC after approximately one year, whereas the beam in Case A had not reached 
EMC after six years. The simulations suggest that doubling the cross-sectional width in-
creases drying time by more than a factor of six. However, these results are indicative 
only, as the simulations were calibrated with limited data.  
• 
Cases A, B, and C demonstrate that despite existing moisture strategies, inadequate design 
with moisture traps, prolonged wetting, and insufficient knowledge of moisture monitor-
ing can lead to damage. This underscores the importance of preventive detailing, compe-
tent execution, and, crucially, the need for clear drying guidelines for mass timber if mois-
ture intrusion occurs.

D Further Work – Microscopy 
This study proposes that future research should examine whether wood cells around dowel joints 
deform in response to changes in moisture - specifically after wetting and drying. When wood 
absorbs moisture, it tends to swell. In dowel joints, this swelling is limited by the surrounding 
dowel, which may create compressive stress in the nearby cell structure and lead to local defor-
mation of the wood cells. 
Studying these changes could provide insight into how moisture-related deformation affects the 
mechanical strength of the joint and whether the risk of cracking increases in the area around the 
dowels because of deformed cells.  
This preliminary investigation proposes a comparative analysis of the wood microstructure sur-
rounding a dowel in a dowel joint at three stages: following joint assembly, after moisture expo-
sure, and after subsequent drying. 
D.1 Methodology 
Microscopy was carried out following the guide by Friis-Grigonczka [80]. Microscopy was per-
formed on two specimens representing two stages of the experimental process: 
- 
A control specimen with a dowel, prior to moisture exposure 
- 
A control specimen after moisture conditioning  
Prior to microscopy, precise cuts were made in the wood surrounding the dowel using a fine-
toothed saw. The cut sections examined from the wetted specimen are illustrated in Appendix 
Figure D.1. 
 
Appendix Figure D.1: Illustration of cut-out sections below each dowel, both facing the wetted surface  - one 
toward the adjacent dowel, the other toward the free end. 
The dowels were released by making precise cuts close along their sides, followed by careful use 
of a chisel to separate them from the surrounding wood as illustrated in Appendix Figure D.2. 
After removal, a 3 mm thick section was cut beneath each dowel. These sections were used for 
microscopy to observe the fiber structure below the dowel hole - both in the direction of the ad-
jacent dowel and toward the free edge of the specimen. 
==PAGE:157==
 
150 
 
 
Appendix Figure D.2: Releasing the dowel using a chisel after side cuts. 
The wood specimens were first sanded using 240-grit sandpaper to remove roughness and ma-
chining marks. This was followed by fine sanding with 1000-grit sandpaper, following procedures 
inspired by [81] and [82]. This fine-grit sanding ensured a smooth and uniform surface, sufficient 
to observe the cell structure under the microscope. Since grit 1000 sandpaper - primarily in-
tended for wet sanding - was used, there is a risk that loose abrasive particles on both the paper 
and the material surface may cause surface scratching during dry sanding. To minimize this risk, 
the sandpaper was frequently replaced, and the surface was regularly cleaned using a fine dust 
cloth. Appendix Figure D.3 illustrates the sanding setup and the positioning of the specimen dur-
ing microscopy. 
 
 
 
Appendix Figure D.3: Left: Sanding with P1000 sandpaper. Middle: Specimen under stereomicroscope Nikon 
SMZ 25 for imaging. Right: Olympus BX43 Microscope  
Microscopy was performed using a stereomicroscope with a motorized x,y-stage, and images 
were captured with a Nikon SMZ 25. Each specimen was placed in a Petri dish under the micro-
scope lens. Images were taken of the fiber structure around the dowel, both at the interface and 
in the surrounding material, with particular attention to any signs of cracking as well as to the 
shape and arrangement of the cells. In addition, microscopy was also conducted using an Olympus 
BX43 to evaluate whether it provided improved image clarity.  
==PAGE:158==
 
151 
 
D.2 Results 
The microscopy results are shown in Appendix Figure D.4. It can be observed that individual cells 
are visible under the microscope, and both earlywood and latewood can be distinguished.  
  
 
Appendix Figure D.4: Microscopic images captured using a Nikon SMZ 25. Left: Section located beneath the 
dowel. Right: Reference area from an arbitrary location within the specimen. 
As shown in Appendix Figure D.4, no visible difference can be observed between the section be-
neath the dowel and a randomly selected area in the specimen. This suggests that the microscopy 
method lacks sufficient clarity or sensitivity, likely due to the comparison not being made on the 
same area of wood.  
To improve image quality, an alternative microscope was also tested to determine whether 
clearer structural differences could be detected. The resulting images are presented in Appendix 
Figure D.5. While the cell structure appears slightly more defined than in Appendix Figure D.4, 
the image quality remains insufficient to draw any firm conclusions. 
   
 
Appendix Figure D.5: Microscopic image of wood cells in cross-section. Left: showing a transition between 
earlywood and latewood structure. Right: Sample after water exposure. 
D.3 Discussion and Conclusion 
Even if a suitable microscopy method is identified, it remains uncertain whether meaningful 
conclusions can be drawn by comparing images taken in the neutral, moisture-saturated, and 
dried states. At this stage, no method has been established that allows for clear visualisation and 
comparison of the wood fibres across the three specimens - prior to wetting, after wetting, and 
after drying. Furthermore, it is unclear whether any general patterns can be identified from such 
a limited area around the dowel. The microscopy specimen itself can only be approximately 200 
==PAGE:159==
 
152 
 
× 100 mm, and must be further divided into image fields of 200–1000 μm in order to achieve 
sufficient resolution 
To compare cell-level deformations before wetting, after wetting, and after drying, a method is 
required that enables tracking of the same piece of wood throughout the process. One possible 
approach is to use a camera system capable of monitoring changes during the drying phase. How-
ever, capturing corresponding observations during the wetting phase presents greater chal-
lenges.
