Abstract 
Greenland is the world’s largest island, and with a population of roughly 60,000 people, it’s 
looking to improve and expand its cities. This means building new houses, new roads, 
airports, harbors, etc. However, due to the lack of local materials, almost all the constructions 
materials needed for building are imported from Denmark or Sweden. Despite trying to 
manage waste in a sustainable way, through incineration, there are still large quantities of 
residual waste being landfilled. All these together mean that a considerable amount of money 
is being spent, with major negative environmental impact. 
This project focuses on the use of incineration ash, particularly bottom ash, as sand 
substitute in the making of mortars. The ash was taken from two of Greenland’s six 
incinerator plants, located in the two biggest cities: Nuuk, the capital, and Sisimiut. Samples 
with up to four different substitution percentages (5%, 10%, 20%,100%) were tested. The 
main properties studied were the compressive strength and the leaching behavior of the 
mortars containing ash, in comparison to a reference mix. 
First, the characteristics of both ash samples were determined. Both showed to have a pH 
level of around 12, meaning that they are strong bases and are suitable for mixing with  
cement. Heavy metal content tests done on the ash alone showed that the Sisimiut ash 
contained high amounts of lead and zinc, while the Nuuk ash contained high levels of copper 
and chromium.  
Leaching tests done on the bottom ash showed that significant leaching of lead and zinc 
occurs from the Sisimiut ash. For the Nuuk ash, high levels of chromium and copper leaching 
were measured. The size distribution of the ash, as well as the water and organic content 
were also determined. 
For the making of the test specimens, mortar samples with 5%, 10% and 20% sand 
substitution were made using both ash types, at a 0.5 water/cement ratio. Test samples with 
100% substitution were also tested with the Sisimiut ash, at 0.67 w/c. Besides the ash 
samples, reference samples were cast and tested at the same time intervals, for a better 
comparison.  
The characteristics of the fresh mixes were also tested. Flow table test showed that the 
mortars have a high workability, considered to be because of the glass particles in the ash, 
which have smooth faces and poor bonding. Setting time measurements revealed that the 
bottom ash mortars have an initial setting faster than the reference. 
The compressive strength of all the samples was measured after different curing times: 7, 
14, 28 and 90 days. Results showed that the ash samples have a lower compressive strength, 
but are still similar to the reference. At higher percentages, comparing the Sisimiut 20% and

100%, no significant strength difference was found, while maintaining a strength of almost 
70% of the reference.  
Density and volume measurement of the samples showed that the density of the sample 
decreases as the ash percentage increases. However, a significant increase in volume is 
observable on the sample with 20% substitution, which is considered to be due to the 
reaction between metallic aluminum and cement.  
Leaching tests were also done on all the mortar samples. Results show that the level of heavy 
metals in the eluate are significantly lower, compared to those from tests done directly on 
the ashes.  
Overall, results show that the ashes do contain high level of heavy metals and that including 
them into a mortar matrix reduces the leaching significantly. In addition to this, the use of 
bottom ash as aggregate appears to have high potential in the making of mortar, with 
strength values approaching those of the reference mixes. The Sisimiut bottom ash mortars 
have shown higher strength and lower leaching than the ones containing Nuuk ash. 
Fortunately the differences are small.

1. Introduction 
The desire to develop and expand is ever present in modern society. As a result, the 
production of materials and, consequently, waste, is increasing, which may cause serious 
environmental problems.  
Municipal solid waste in Europe has reached levels of over 250 million tons every year 
(Eurostat, 2018). Waste management strategies have been developed and achieving the 3 
R’s: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, has become a priority. 
Among used strategies, municipal solid waste incineration has seen an increase in 
Europe in the past years, with roughly 25% of waste being incinerated (Pecqueur, Crignon 
and Quénée, 2000). This method has become very popular due to the resulting reduction of 
mass of approximately 70% and a reduction in volume of up to 90% (del Valle-Zermeño et 
al., 2013). In addition to this, energy recovery is available. This process, however, also 
produces residual waste, which is divided into 2 types: bottom ash and fly ash. The fly ashes 
are fine in grain size and contain hazardous substances, such as heavy metals and organic 
compounds. Bottom ashes are coarser in particle size and have a very low content level of 
hazardous materials. Bottom ash also serves as the majority of the byproduct, with nearly 
80%.  
To further decrease the amount of waste produced, the valorization of the bottom ash 
has been studied. In the past years, bottom ash has been used as secondary material in road 
construction due to its similarity to natural aggregates, with successful results when using a 
maximum of 10% bottom ash (Toraldo and Saponaro, 2015).  
In concrete production, the use of pre-treated bottom ash has been tested as aggregate, 
with promising results and possible uses in non-structural elements (Jansegers, 1997; Ginés 
et al., 2009; Cioffi et al., 2011; Sorlini, Abbà and Collivignarelli, 2011; del Valle-Zermeño et 
al., 2013). The properties of bottom ash in cement was also studied and have shown that the 
inclusion of pre-treated ash had little effect on the strength of the created cement (Filipponi 
et al., 2003; Cheeseman, Makinde and Bethanis, 2005; Pan et al., 2008). 
For finding an efficient use of the bottom ash in concrete, the replacement of aggregate 
with ash has been studied at different ratios. Zhang and Zhao, 2014, tested at 0, 30, 50, 70% 
by weight. Pera et al., 1997, tested at 50% and 100% aggregate replacement. They concluded 
that compressive strength decreases as we increase the percentage. In addition to this, 
swelling and cracks were visible on the samples, possibly due to the reaction between 
metallic aluminum and cement. A pre-treatment of the ash is recommended. 
Waste strategy was implemented in Greenland in the late 1980s. The country 
transitioned from disposal sites to MSWI incineration in 6 of its major cities for the same

attempting to use the energy created. Annually, Greenlandic incinerators produce roughly

The possibility to use MSWI ash as a construction material would provide an alternative

In this study, Greenlandic MSWI bottom ash from the incinerators of the two largest 
towns, Nuuk and Sisimiut, was characterized and tests were done to determine the usability 
of the ash as sand substitute in mortar production. The main questions asked when doing 
this research were what do the ashes contain; can we use them as a construction material, 
both from a structural and an environmental point of view; how can we valorize the 
maximum amount of ash?

2. Theory 
1.1 Concrete 
Concrete is the most commonly used construction material in the world and the second most 
consumed material, after water. This is mainly due to its simplicity of creation, possibility for 
reuse and overall versatility- whether we talk about strength, workability or shape. 
Worldwide concrete production was estimated to be of almost 30 billion tons. (CSI, 2009) 
There are four primary ingredients needed to make concrete, to which admixtures can be 
added to improve its performance: water, cement, fine aggregate, coarse aggregate.  
Mortar is another highly used construction material, used mainly to connect brick elements. 
It is a constituent of concrete, so a lot of its properties are similar to those of concrete. 
However, mortar is much finer and has a lower strength. There are only 3 primary 
ingredients in the making of mortar: water, cement, fine aggregate. 
 
1.2 Aggregates 
Sand and gravel are the main raw materials used in the construction industry. Together, they 
represent more than 70% of the global non-metallic mineral extraction, followed by 
limestone, for the making of cement, and clay (Schandl et al., 2016).  
Sand is a very important ingredient in the making of concrete and mortars. Sand proportions 
usually vary between a half and a third of the total volume of the mix. As a result, the 
properties of the sand used greatly affect the properties of the resulting concrete or mortar. 
The size distribution of the sand particles has the role of making sure the voids forming in 
the concrete or mortar mix have a minimum volume and that the amount of cement paste 
used to bind the particles is optimal. The particle shape greatly influences the workability 
and strength of the mix, as the roughness and roundness of the sand affect the bonding 
capabilities of the cement paste. 
Beside usage in concrete, sand has an important role in a variety of other sectors, such as 
land reclamation, glass production, asphalt production. As the need for infrastructure 
expansion increases, so does the need for this basic material. However, not all types of sand 
are usable in these fields and suitable sand is becoming more and more scarce, as mining it 
has a large environmental impact on habitats and ultimately species, in areas of extraction 
(Sutherland et al., 2017).

1.3 Waste management 
One of the many problems of this continuously developing society we live in is the fact that 
as we increase our standards of living, the demand for products, and as a result, of waste, 
also increases. This is a major problem as both have a great environmental impact.  
It is a well-known fact that the best way of dealing with waste is to minimize it. However, 
waste must be dealt with once it has been created. That is why the incineration of waste is a 
very common way of waste management adopted by communities. This is due to its 
advantages of volume and mass reduction of waste, which decreases the current need for 
landfilling and, as a result, the risk of chemicals leaching into the environment. Another 
advantage of incineration is that it offers the possibility of energy recovery, reducing the 
future need for fossil fuels and other means of energy production. Out of almost 250 million 
tons of waste produced in Europe in 2016, almost 30% was incinerated, while 25% was 
landfilled. (Eurostat, 2018). However, one of the main disadvantages is that a considerable 
amount of residual waste is being produced because of incineration. 
 
1.4 Municipal Solid Waste Incineration Ash 
MSWI ash is the byproduct of the treatment process named waste incineration. This process 
consists of the combustion of waste material. As an alternative to direct landfilling, the 
incineration of waste prior to landfilling is sometimes preferred due to the considerable 
reduction in mass and volume of the waste. An energy recovery system can also be added to 
further take advantage of this process.  
The resulting residual waste is composed of two types of ashes: fly ash and bottom ash. Fly 
ash is a fine-particle residue. It is a very hazardous material and requires special treatment 
before landfilling or use. Bottom ash is a non-hazardous material, with a larger particle size 
than the fly ash. Depending on the quality of the waste recycling before the incineration, the 
bottom can contain large quantities of steel, glass or ceramics.  
Despite not being classified as hazardous, bottom ash still contains high amounts of heavy 
metals. Leaching is a significant factor when discussing landfilling or valorization of the ash. 
As the bottom ash significantly depends on the waste fractions incinerated, the components, 
and ultimately the properties of the ash may differ between batches. Kirkelund, Dias-
Ferreira and Jensen, (2016) tested the use of Greenlandic fly ash and bottom ash as a local 
secondary resource. They concluded that ash samples should be tested individually as they 
differ. However, there is potential in using the ash in the construction industry, with fly ash 
showing similarities to cement and bottom ash to sand, in terms of behavior and 
characteristics.

1.5 Use of MSWI ash in construction materials 
A solution for utilizing this residual waste was found by using it as a resource in the 
construction industry. In this way, the demand for the making of new materials is also 
decreased. Cheeseman, Makinde and Bethanis, (2005) tested lightweight aggregates made 
from the sintering of incinerator bottom ash. The ash under 8 mm in size was rapidly 
sintered at temperatures between 1000 and 1050 °C. The strength of the resulting pellets 
was similar to commercial lightweight aggregates. However, leaching of the heavy metals 
was not significantly reduced.   
The use of MSWI bottom ash was studied as possible replacement in cement products. Pan 
et al., (2008) studied the replacement of the clay component in cement production with 
MSWI bottom and fly ash. Chemical analysis showed a high level of chlorine and sulfates, 
which may corrode the cement kiln, but by prewashing with water and acid, this problem 
can be solved.  
Despite having a longer setting time, caused by the presence of heavy metals, the 
compressive strength of the mortar samples did not change with the addition of the ash. Li 
et al., (2012) also tested the addition of MSWI BA in cement. Results showed delays in the 
hydration of the cement and a decrease in the mechanical properties of the ash-cement 
matrices as we increase the ash percentage, up to 30% mass. Replacement of 50% cement 
with ash showed considerable deterioration of the mix strength. Overall, bottom ash was 
noted to have a very low reactivity when used as cement replacement (Filipponi et al., 2003).  
Ferraris et al., (2009) studied the effects of vitrified ash and its advantages. After vitrification 
at 1450 °C, the ash was ground and sieved into different sizes, to be used as filler, sand or 
coarse aggregate. Results showed that the replacement of up to 20% mass of filler and up to 
75% volume of coarse aggregate does not change the mechanical properties of the concrete. 
Replacement of the sand was done for 4 different percentages: 25, 50, 75 and 100. This 
showed significant reduction of the strength of the concrete with increase of the ash 
percentage.  
Toraldo and Saponaro, (2015) tested the use of bottom ash in road pavement layers. 
Stabilized bottom ash was used in all the layers of the road: 20% in foundation, 10% in 
subbase, 10% mass of sand in asphalt base. Results on compressive strength showed values 
within required limits, however increased curing time was observed. The mechanical 
performance was noticed to decrease in the asphalt base layer, which was considered to be 
due to the compaction methods presently used. Leaching measurements showed values far 
below limit values.  
However, the use of bottom ash as aggregate replacement in concrete showed overall 
success. Zhang and Zhao, (2014) studied the replacement of coarse aggregate with different 
percentages of bottom ash; 30%, 50%, 70%. Pera et al., (1997) also studied the use of bottom 
ash as replacement for coarse aggregates in concrete. For samples with a 50% volume 
replacement of gravel with bottom ash, the compressive strength remained equivalent to

concrete class C25, suitable for use in low grade elements, such as foundations or road 
leveling. However, tests done on 100% volume replacement of gravel showed that the 
aggregate strength is not enough to comply with the local standards. Ginés et al., (2009) 
tested the combined use of fly ash and bottom ash. Mixtures with combinations ranging from 
75/25% to 100/0% BA/FA were made. The results showed that the most promising mix was 
with 95/5% as aggregate. In addition to this, leaching tests were done. The ash was washed 
prior to mixing. Results showed no significant change in pH. Levels of Pb and Zn remain 
within the limits mainly due to the solidification/stabilization process caused by the mixing 
with cement. Ba shows an increase proportional to the amount of FA. Sorlini, Abbà and 
Collivignarelli, (2011) tested the replacement of natural aggregates with bottom ashes 
varying in size (fine, coarse), as well as types of treatment (unwashed, washed). Results 
showed that the ash replacement reduces the strength of the concrete, however the 
reduction is smaller when using washed ash. Leaching tests show that by washing the ash 
prior to use, the levels of leaching are lower than the limits imposed by the standards, with 
lead levels close to the limit.  
Other tests done on aggregate replacement with bottom ash showed that the setting time of 
the mixture increases with the addition of the ash. Also, the mechanical strength of the 
concrete decreases. (Jansegers, 1997; Cheng, 2012; Reiterman et al., 2016) Overall results 
showed that partial replacement of up to 50% with bottom ash is recommended for use.  
One problem encountered in multiple researches was that the samples showed an increase 
in volume and a development of cracks. (Pecqueur, Crignon and Quénée, 2000; Quenee et al., 
2000). Pera et al., (1997) observed that when substituting the sand with ash, gaseous 
emission occurred, creating a lower strength, porous concrete. The causes were reduced to 
two possibilities: the creation of ettringite and the reaction of metallic aluminum with 
cement. Aubert, Husson and Vaquier, (2004) studied this volume increase. Swelling 
measurements showed that this happens during the first 10 hours of curing, while XRD 
analysis showed that ettringite formation happens after 24h. They also discovered that the 
formation of ettringite increases the strength of the mortar. They concluded that the swelling 
was caused by the release of hydrogen gas from the presence of metallic aluminum, and that, 
overall, the strength of the mortar is lower than the reference due to the cracks and voids 
formation.  
 
1.6 Waste management in Greenland 
Waste incineration was introduced in Greenland as a first step in waste management taken 
to address the problem of the over 50,000 tons of mixed waste produced every year. The 
first incinerator was built in the capital Nuuk, and has a capacity of 10,000 tons per year, and 
a maximum energy recovery of around 70%. Five more incinerators were opened in the 
2000s, including one in Sisimiut, the 2nd largest town. The Sisimiut incinerator has a capacity 
of 4,000 tons and a maximum energy recovery of 62% (Eisted and Christensen, 2011). Both 
incinerators have flue gas treatment, with electrostatic precipitators. However, this can only

remove the particulate materials, so the gaseous contaminants, organic pollutants and 
dioxins are released into the atmosphere (Kirkelund, Dias-Ferreira and Jensen, 2016). 
Overall, the incinerator in Nuuk goes through 9,300 tons of waste per year, while the one in 
Sisimiut only incinerates 2,700 tons per year. This contributes to a total of 6,000 tons of 
bottom ash created in Greenland every year, which is ultimately landfilled.  
 
1.7 Use of Greenlandic MSWI ash 
Kirkelund et al., (2016) studied the use of Greenlandic incineration fly ash and bottom ash 
as resource in the making of mortar. Results showed that substitutions of 5% cement with 
fly ash reduces the compressive strength of the mortar only slightly, and that similarities 
between fly ash and cement, and bottom ash and sand indicate a potential use as a secondary 
material. The study also showed that the workability of the mortar is reduced and the setting 
times delayed, proportional to ash replacement. Kirkelund et al., (2012) found that the use 
of Greenlandic bottom ash as subbase in road construction is possible, with small 
modifications to the size distribution done prior to use. The sorting of waste prior to 
incineration is recommended. However, leaching results showed high levels of copper and 
chromium from small grain sizes.

The Sisimiut BA was further divided into 2 halves. The first one was left unchanged. The 
second half was separated into seven size groups: >2, >1, >0.25, >0.125, >0.08, >0.063, 
<0.063, in mm. The size groups were then mixed in such a way as to create an ash mixture

3.3 Characterization 
The water content was tested for ashes from both incinerators. The test was done on three 
samples of each bottom ash and the reported value was taken as the average, expressed in 
percentage of weight of ash before drying. Nuuk ash water content was tested twice, to make 
sure an accurate measurement had been done.  
The organic content was determined by an equivalent loss of ignition at 550 °C. Three sample 
of each ash type were tested. The pH was measured on three batches of each sample, on both 
plain ash and hardened mortar. Soluble salts measurements were done using IC analysis. The 
heavy metal content of the ashes was determined by digestion. 
Leaching of heavy metals was done on the ash samples, as well as the mortar sample. Three 
samples of 20 g from each mortar combination were crushed by hand to a size smaller than 
4 mm, to simulate the worst conditions of leaching. The samples were then mixed with 40 g 
of distilled water and placed on a shaking table for 24 h prior to measurement of pH and 
filtering. The leaching was measured using ICP analysis.  
To determine the levels of metallic aluminum, the method described in Aubert, Husson and 
Vaquier, (2004) was considered. However, the lack of details regarding the procedure and 
the difficulty in obtaining the materials necessary resulted in replacing the procedure with a 
XRD measurement.  
The size distribution of the ashes as well as the reference sand was made using a sieve 
analysis. The mesh sizes used were: 4, 2, 1, 0.25, 0.125, 0.08 and 0.063 mm.  
The methods used are further described in Appendix A.

batches of each mixture containing the Sisimiut bottom ashes and the reference were made, 
to be tested at four different curing periods: 7, 14, 28, 90 days. Only three batches of Nuuk

3.5 Physical testing

whole assembly was then manually jolted for 15 times, at a rate of 1 per second, on the jolt 
table, seen in Figure 3.4. Calipers were used to measure the spread of the fresh mortar. Two 
measurements were done for each mixture.

samples with the sorted ash shows greater expansion. Overall, the sorted ash shows a

4.3 Errors 
As with any experimental research, a certain level of inaccuracy should be expected. This can 
be either because of the equipment not functioning within optimum standards, materials 
differing from assumptions taken or insufficient knowledge and experience from the part of 
the person performing the experiments and interpretation. Some of the more frequent error 
causes are: faulty calibration, incorrect reading by the user. 
Regarding this project, the lack of experience with laboratory testing was of influence. 
Mortar mixing and casting for the first batches made have been impacted by this and it is 
why some of the mixtures have been remade.  
In addition, the weight measurement of the dry mortar samples was done after removal of 
the water from the surface of the sample. The remaining water inside the sample may have 
altered the real value of the weight.

5. SUGGESTION FOR FUTURE RESEARCH 
 
39 
 
5. Suggestion for Future Research 
The present study focuses on trying to continue a research done by Kirkelund et al. in 2016. 
However, only some of the basic characteristics of the bottom ashes and a fraction of its uses 
in mortars and concrete were discovered.  
Considering the level of technology we have available today, the ash could be subjected to 
different pretreatments to purify and increase its performance as sand substitute. In 
addition to this, admixtures can be added to the mortar or concrete mix, to further improve 
the overall properties of the final product. Washing of the bottom ash prior to use is one of 
the most common way of pretreatment which has shown success in the past. 
As more data is gathered and, specifically, more information about the incinerators and the 
characteristics of the MSWI bottom ash are determined, a machine learning algorithm could 
be implemented to optimize the pretreatment process and mixtures required for a desired 
concrete. By creating an input where the basic properties of the bottom ash are known, such 
as water content, organic content, chemical composition, the machine learning algorithm 
could be used to determine, for example, the bottom ash substitution required to create a 
mixture of a specific strength or density.  
Another possible use for the mortar containing MSWI bottom ash could be the making of 
autoclaved aerated concrete. By taking advantage of the reaction between the aluminum, 
calcium oxide and water, the mortar is used to make ultra-light concrete elements. These 
elements play a double role, as they provide structural support while at the same time acting 
as insulators (Saiyed et al., 2014). This could prove valuable in the arctic region, where 
temperature fluctuations are high.

6. CONCLUSIONS 
 
40 
 
6. Conclusions 
The present study was performed to determine the potential use of Greenlandic MSWI 
bottom ash as a sand substitute in the making of cement mortar and to answer a few 
questions concerning ash properties and possible uses for the ash mortar. 
Tests on the bottom ash taken from Sisimiut showed that it contained low levels of water 
and organic matter. The Nuuk bottom ash, however, had high levels of water content, 
probably connected to the way the ash was stored on site. For both ashes, levels of heavy 
metals were very high, especially for cadmium, copper, lead and zinc. The level of metallic 
aluminum could not be determined, however the ash did contain a large number of 
aluminum compounds. A considerable volume increase was observed for samples with 20% 
BA and, as a result, washing of the ash prior to use in mortar is recommended.  
There appeared to be no significant improvement from the size sorting of the Sisimiut ash. 
Taking into account the amount of work necessary to create the sorted ash and the strength 
results given by the mortar, the use of the size sorted ash proved to be unsatisfactory. 
Mortar density for ash substitution up to 20% classified the mixtures as normal-weight, 
while 100% BA mortars fell into the light-weight category. The mortar mix strength showed 
promising results. At 5% substitution, the mortars had a compressive strength equivalent to 
that of concrete class C35/45, for Sisimiut BA, and class C30/37, for Nuuk BA. Even at higher 
percentages, of 100% Sisimiut BA, the mortar showed a strength of almost 32 MPa. The 
immobilization of heavy metals in the mortar matrix seems to reduce the leaching 
considerably, with only lead showing high values. This is, however, expected to further 
decrease with the prewashing of the ash. 
Overall, the use of bottom ash as substitute for sand in the making of mortar has proved to 
be an acceptable solution to the problems posed by waste landfilling. The strength values of 
the mortar fall within acceptable limits for the use as structural elements in the construction 
industry, with significantly lower impact on the environment than the current situation.
