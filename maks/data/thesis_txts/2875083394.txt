Abstract
This thesis investigates two novel extensions of Variational Diffusion Models (VDMs)
for generative modeling.
The first contribution, the forward iterative variational
diffusion model (FI-VDM), reinterprets hierarchical variational autoencoders as dif-
fusion processes and introduces a new class of variational processes that improve the
performance of the generative model.
The second contribution, time-consistent variational diffusion model (TC-VDM), ad-
dresses the question of wether it is possible to train a diffusion model that can do
inference in variable number of time steps, and what would be the performance draw-
back of doing so.
Both models have successfully been implemented and tested in a proof of concept on
binarized MNIST data. Empirical results show a clear performance boost with the
FI-VDM, while some cost is introduced by the increased flexibility of the TC-VDM,
over the static-time diffusion model.

Preface
What was
This thesis is the product of a slightly winding learning process which I will sketch
here before diving into the actual content of the thesis.
I initially began the project with an interest in generative modeling using stochastic
differential equations. I was especially interested in understanding how results from
the continuous-time Stochastic Differential Equations (SDE) framework can be used
in discrete-time sampling processes, as is done in score-matching with SDEs [1].
This led me to implement the Neural Flow Diffusion model and the SDE-matching
models by Bartosh [2, 3]. In this process, I worked on a kind of multi-output Gaussian
Process called linear model of coregionalization as variational process [4].
This was interesting, but it was a good answer without any really good question to
match it. Maybe something will come out of it in the future.
At this point, my supervisor suggested that we should look at consistency models [5],
but in the framework of Variational Diffusion Models (VDM) [6]. That is: take this
concept that is built on the framework of generative modeling with SDEs and modify
it to use it in VDMs. That is what we call a time-consistent variational diffusion
model.
In the beginning of that process, I was still using Gaussian Processes as variational
distributions, since I had already implemented it in the earlier period of the project.
They work great in SDE-matching, but are a bad idea when working with Markov
processes, as I also show in the thesis.
Luckily, my supervisor came to rescue again, with the suggestion to look at a vari-
ational diffusion process that works backwards. Yes, it is a bit diﬀicult to get your
head around, but I will try to explain below. This is what I call the forward iterative
variational distribution.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis explores two novel ideas in diffusion modeling. The first idea improves
performance for Variational Diffusion models, while the second explores the possibility
of sample generation with variable number of time steps.
The first contribution introduces the forward iterative variational diffusion models
FI-VDM, which reinterpret Hierarchical Variational Autoencoders as diffusion pro-
cesses. Training is carried out through a novel class of variational processes, designed
to improve the eﬀiciency and expressiveness of the generative model.
The second idea is simple: can we train a diffusion model such that sampling can
be done in variable number of time steps, and at what cost? The time-consistent
diffusion model is the answer to this.
Both models have successfully been implemented and tested in a proof of concept
on binarized MNIST data. The empirical results show a clear performance boost
with the FI-VDM, while some cost is introduced by the increased flexibility of the
time-consistent diffusion model, over the static-time diffusion model.
Source code will be made available on https://github.com/simoneiriksson/Mast
ersThesis
1.1
Content overview
In the following a brief overview of the content of the thesis is given.
In chapter two, I will present generative modeling in abstract terms, and then narrow
in on some specific classes of models. The models in this thesis are all based on vari-
ational approximations, where the distance between two distributions is minimized
based on the KL-divergence that is introduced first. Then the basic building block,
the VAE is defined, with the hierarchical counterpart HVAE, with some discussion
of the diﬀiculties in training deep hierarchical models.
==PAGE:10==
2
1 Introduction
In the subsequent chapter, the class of VDMs is introduced. These models can be
perceived as an extension of the class of HVAE models, but is originally thought of in
terms of stochastic processes. The neural network embedded in the generative distri-
bution of VDMs can be reparameterized in multiple ways, enabling training toward
different prediction objectives. Two common formulations are introduced: data-point
prediction and ε-prediction. Finally, I present how VDM’s can be perceived as mod-
eling a stochastic differential equation in the continuous limit.
In the fourth chapter, I present the two novel ideas that are the true subject of this
thesis. Both have been developed in collaboration with my thesis supervisor. The
first idea is the FI-VDM, where time is reversed in the diffusion process. The second
idea is the time-consistent variational diffusion model, which allows to train a model
to generate samples in a variable number of time steps.
Then follows a chapter with implementation details and architecture of the various
models used in the experiments: the variational distributions, the generative models
and finally the emission distribution used in the VDM models. The time samplers
used in the training process are also presented.
Chapter six briefly presents the experimental setup, including the data set used in
the experiments and hyper parameters etc. The two evaluation criteria used in the
results section are also introduced.
Finally the chapter with the Empirical Results. Here I show how the FI-VDM beats
the baseline Ladder VAE model slightly [7] and the VDM models by a large margin.
I also investigate the question of the trade-off between models with fixed time steps
and the time-consisten models.

CHAPTER 2
Generative Modeling
Preliminaries
This chapter introduces some key concept in generative modeling that will work as
building blocks in the following chapters. First the concept of generative modeling
with latent variables is introduced. This is followed by a the definition of the KL-
divergence that measures the distance between two distributions. This measure is
used for optimization of the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) in the following section.
Hierarchical VAEs are then introduced, with some discussion of the complications
involved in training them. One suggestion for a solution to those issues is the the
Ladder VAE [7], which we will use as base line for the empirical results in the later
chapters.
2.1
What is generative modeling
Recent years have seen a surge in literature on generative machine learning – both
within the machine learning research community and in the culture at large. In this
thesis, I will present a certain class of generative models that are predominant in
image generation, but also used in many other fields, such as audio generation, and
generation of synthetic molecules and proteins [8–10].
From an abstract and probabilistic point of view, generative modeling aims to train
a probabilistic model of an underlying (highly complex) distribution that allows one
to sample from it. The model can be trained on a database of literature (in the case
of language models), embeddings of protein structures, the set of all images on the
Internet, or any other large dataset. If the modeling succeeds, a sample from the
distribution will in some sense be similar but not equal to the actual data samples
from the dataset.
In this work, we will deal with diffusion models which are especially good at generating
independent identically distributed samples. Other types of generative models exist,

such as (autoregressive) language models which typically are trained to guess the next
word in a series of words, and thus word-by-word build sentences and paragraphs.
Other examples are flow-based models which transform a random variable through a

a distribution that is close to the data distribution [11], and Generative Adversarial
Networks, in which two networks compete: one generates samples and the other

growth, so many other classes of generative approaches exist by now.

2.2.1
VAEs as generators
The definition of the ELBO suggests the concept of Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
[14]. Variational Autoencoders are the probabilistic cousin of the much older deter-
ministic Autoencoder (AE) which dates back to the late 1980s [15, p. 499]. The
AE is a coupled pair of neural networks: an encoder f and a decoder g, such that
f(g(z)) ≈z for all z in the domain of g. The idea is that f encodes a data point into
a latent representation in another space (typically of lower dimension) and then g
decodes this back into the observation space. The VAE gives this a probabilistic turn:
Instead of deterministic mappings, the VAE is defined by a coupled pair of models p
and q given by a generative distribution pθ(x | z), a prior distribution p0(z), and an
approximate posterior distribution qϕ(z | x). Because the true posterior pθ(z | x) is
generally intractable, qϕ is trained to approximate it. The degree of approximation
is quantified by the KL-divergence, and maximizing the ELBO then simultaneously
decreases the distance from qϕ(x | x) to pθ(z | x) and increases the likelihood of the
observed data under the model pϕ. This means that The quality of a given pair of
distributions can be measured using the ELBO as a reward function.

2.2.2
Hierarchical VAEs as better generators
One problem in this approach is that if pθ(x) is a highly complex distribution and
pθ(z) a simple one, then the model pθ(x | z) has to be equally complex, and very deep
models may be diﬀicult to train. One way to remedy this is to consider a chained
model where z is split up into K parts z = [z0, z1, . . . , zK]. In the following, the
notation z0:i will refer to the z’s with indices 0 . . . i, that is, z0:i = [z0, . . . zi]. Then
following the chain rule,
pθ(x, z) = pθ(x | zK) · pθ(zK | z0:K−1) · · · pθ(z1 | z0) · pθ(z0).
Note that the generative process begins with the variable z0 and ends with the vari-
able zK which contains most information about x. In the following, the probabili-
ties will be subscripted by their parameterizations: pη is the emission distribution
parametrized by η, pθ is the generative distribution parametrized by θ, and p0 is
the prior distribution which in general is not parametrized but set to a standard
multivariate Gaussian. Thus, the above expression for pθ will be
pθ(x, z) = pη(x | zK) · pθ(zK | z0:K−1) · · · pθ(z1 | z0) · p0(z0).
This generative process is depicted in Figure 2.3, where the conditioning on all pre-
vious latent states is indicated. The idea here is that modeling burden is distributed
across multiple latent layers. Instead of requiring one latent variable to capture all
structure in x, each layer models progressively finer details.
If we furthermore assume that the chain is Markov, each of the links in the chain only
depends on the previous latent variable and we get:

2.2.3
Ladder VAEs
Ladder Variational Autoencoders (LVAEs) are a class of models which attempt to
resolve this problem by sharing information between the encoding and decoding path
at each link in the chain [7]. Briefly explained, this happens by defining a generative
model
pθ(zi | zi−1) = N(µθ(zi−1), σθ(zi−1))
with µθ(zi) and σθ(zi) being neural networks. A variational model
qϕ(zi−1 | zi) = N(µϕ(zi), σϕ(zi))
is then defined, but here the mean and covariance are defined as
σϕ(zi) :=

where ˜σϕ(zi)−2 and ˜µϕ(zi) are neural networks. This is a precision-weighted combi-
nation of the generative network outputs and the outputs of the variational networks,
and allows for interaction between the diffusion process and the generative process.
The Ladder VAE model does not share parameters across the layers of the model
which allows each of the levels in the hierarchy to have different sizes. With LVAEs,
it is possible to train a model to use up to around 5 latent layers. In the next chapter,
we will propose a model that match the performance of the LVAE and can be trained
beyond the 5 layers mentioned by [7].

CHAPTER 3
Variational Diffusion
Models
In 2020, Ho et al. introduced Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) [18]
which avoid many of the optimization issues of hierarchical VAEs, such as posterior
collapse by replacing the latent-variable bottleneck with a gradual noising–denoising
process.
This chapter introduces the Variational Diffusion Model (VDM) which is an extension
or more abstract formulation of the DDPM. The training algorithm for matching the
generative distribution with a variational distribution is presented, together with the
corresponding sampling algorithm. Following this, I present how VDMs – when taken
to the continuous limit – can be perceived as modeling an SDE.
In the original VDM paper [6] the variational distribution is parameterized in a spe-
cific way, which is then presented.
The neural network that resides in the generative distribution in VDMs can be re-
parameterized in different ways, such that they are trained to approximate various
prediction targets. A few of those re-parameterizations are introduced: data-point
prediction, and ε-prediction.
Finally score matching is introduced with a brief link to score-based diffusion modeling
with SDEs.
The overall takeaway from this chapter is that it is possible to do more advanced
generative modeling with deeply layered models, and that when the number of layers
in VDMs are theoretically taken to the continuous limit, then they approximate the
same stochastic process as in score-based modeling with SDEs.

CHAPTER 4
Contributions
In this chapter, two novel ideas are presented. Both have been developed in collabo-
ration with my thesis supervisor.
The first concept is the Forward Iterative Diffusion Model (FI-VDM), which is an
alternative parameterization of the variational process q(z | x). In its canonical form
used in the VDM models, this process adds noise to a data point and runs from 1
to 0 but is formulated in a way that allows us to derive the reverse-time conditional
distributions zt ∼q(zt | zs). In the FI-VDM process, these conditionals are instead
parameterized directly using neural networks.
The second idea is the Time-Consistent Variational Diffusion Model (TC-VDM),
which allows for a generative model that is trained to produce samples at variable
number of time steps.
4.1
Forward iterative variational diffusion process
The VDM diffusion process assumes a variational distribution that can be “turned
around” from its canonical formulation qϕ(zt | zs) to a forward-time formulation
qϕ(zs | zt, x). It also requires the variation process to be formulated in such that
way that is possible to sample from the unconditional distributions qϕ(zs | x). That
has the advantage that it makes it possible to sample zt at any time point, without
having to iterate through each step in the qϕ-chain. These requirements impose limits
on the expressivity of the variational process.
Instead, we propose the forward iterative variational process.
Under this process,
first z0 is sampled directly from qϕ(z0 | x), and then subsequently for each s < t, the
next step is sampled zt ∼qϕ(zt | zs, x). Since these distributions are parameterized
using general, nonlinear functions, this approach precludes the possibility of sampling
directly from the marginal distributions qϕ(z0 | x) for any t ̸= 0.
The FI-VDM directly parameterize the transition probabilities of the Markov chain
zt ∼qϕ(zt | zs, x). This differs from the approach of the previous VDM-style models
==PAGE:37==
4.2 Time-Consistent Variational Diffusion Models
29
which parameterize a more general description of the process (in terms of the uncon-
ditional marginal distribution) that permits explicit reasoning about the marginal
distribution of zt at any point in the process. The VDM-style processes only allow
the transition distribution to be an linear combination of the previous data point
and some noise. By contrast, the FI-VDM process allows for non-linear transition
distributions. This also means that the iterative diffusion variational process is not
necessarily a Gaussian process, and that the marginal distribution q(zt | x) need not
to be Gaussian for t > 0. 1
Using this approach, the training algorithm is iterative in the number of time steps.

4.4
Related work
The chapters 2 and 3 generally situates the suggested models in the literature of
Variational Autoencoders, Diffusion models and generative modeling with SDEs. The
motivation for the FI-VDM is simply performance improvement within the class of
VAEs.
Progressive model distillation [31] aims to use a VDM-style model pη, that is trained
to take many small steps, as a “teacher” to train another “student” model pθ. The
training process is reasonably simple. A time ti = i/K is sampled from the unit
interval. Then a state zti is sampled from the variational distribution q(zti | x). Now
two single steps are taken by the teacher along the pη-path resulting in a state at
time ti+2, while one double step is taken by the student model along the pθ-path,
also resulting in a state at time ti+2:
zti+1 ∼pη(zti+1 | zti)
zti+2 ∼pη(zti+2 | zti+1)
˜zti+2 ∼pθ(˜zti+2 | zti)
Then the distance between the states is minimized. Note that in [31], the authors
minimize the distance in the data-point targets rather than the intermediate states.
This differs, however, from what we are proposing with the TC-VDM, in that our
model is trained to synchronously produce samples at variable number of steps, while
the progressive model distillation – or any other distillation method for that matter
– retrains a new model with another fixed number of sampling steps.
Our time-consistent VDM was originally inspired by consistency models [5]. This
class of models are based on score based models. Score based models can be trained
to perform inference along a deterministic path [1] in what is called Probability Flow
ODE (PF-ODE).
Broadly speaking, the idea in consistency models is to train a neural network fθ()
to follow the deterministic path generated by the PF-ODE. Or more formally: A
==PAGE:41==
4.4 Related work
33
model fθ(zt, t) that approximates the target data point x is said to be consistent
if it maps any zt, t from the same sampling path onto the same target point. Like
in the progressive model distillation, the training is carried out in a teach-student
like fashion. Consistency models also allows for variable step-size sampling, by first
sampling an initial state z0 ∼p(z0) then predict a terminal sample with fθ(z0, 0),
then iteratively adding noise and then predict a new sample. A drawback for these
models is that they are no longer likelihood based.
As to my knowledge, nothing similar to the time-consistent VDM has been proposed
– but like many other fields in machine learning, this sees an explosive growth and
especially in 2024 and 2025, many papers have been published on model distillation
and similar topics.

5.1
Model architectures
All models used in the experiments are based on a neural network structure that allow
for the best comparison with the results in the Ladder VAE paper [7]. The Ladder
VAE model does not share parameters across the layers of the model which allows
each of latent states in the hierarchy to have different sizes. In their experimental
setup, 64 dimensions are used for the last latent layer, then halving the dimensionality
of zt for each deeper layer t.
In the present work, the variational (and generative) model shares parameters across
time. That means that rather than having one separate model for each time step as
the LVAE model has, here the model takes the time step as input for evaluation. This
means that the latent space must be equal across time steps. In all the experiments
performed here, the latent space is 64-dimensional.
All models are trained on MNIST data [33] which have been flattened from 1×28×28
to 784 dimensions – again, in order to conform to the work by [7].

5.2
Prediction targets
As discussed in Section 3.1.2, 3.2.2 and 3.2.1, various papers have implemented differ-
ent prediction targets for the generative model. The calculation of the ELBO takes
place in the class that implements the variational distribution, and depends on which
target is chosen. The main variational distribution presented in this thesis is the itera-
tive diffusion process which only allows for the prediction target to be the variational
distribution – that is, predicting both mean and covariance of the next zt.
Apart from this, ε-prediction, data-point prediction, and distribution prediction have
been implemented for the VDM variational distribution.

5.3
Time samplers
The training process of the classic VDM requires sampling an integer i ≤K, where
K is the fixed number of time steps in the model. From this i, the noised data point
zi ∼q(zi | x) is sampled and then the denoised zi+1 ∼q(zi+1 | zi, , x). The variety
of models implemented in this work requires a strategy that is a bit more advanced.
The FI-VDM require all time steps in a partition of the [0, 1] to be sampled at once, so
as to traverse the variational path, while the VDM-style models can exploit training
at random time steps. In both cases here, the time samples must be immediately
preceding each other (in some sense).
On the other hand, the time-consistent models are trained on partitions or pairs of
time points that do not necessarily follow any inherent structure.
Two time samplers for training non-time-consistent (static-time) models have been
implemented, one for the iterative and one for VDM-style learning. In this class, a
K ≥2 is chosen beforehand, for the number of time steps that the model is going to
be trained on. Then for each step in the training loop in the VDM-style training, an
integer i is sampled from a uniform distribution i ∼U[0, K −1], and then si = i/K
and ti = (i + 1)/K are defined.
In the case of the iterative training loop, the time sampler generates the full trail
{0, 1/K, · · · , 1} which is then used for training.
The time-consistent models are trained with two different kinds of time samplers.
The first time sampler is the time-consistent equidistant time sampler that uses a
fixed maximal number of time steps M ≥2. Then an integer K is sampled from a
uniform distribution K ∼U[2, M], and subsequently, the time steps are sampled like
in the static-time models. For the VDM-style training, an integer i is sampled from a
uniform distribution i ∼U[1, K], and then si = (i −1)/K and ti = i/K are defined.
For the iterative diffusion models, the time-consistent equidistant time sampler gen-
erates the full trail {0, 1/K, · · · , 1} which is then used for training.
Furthermore, a time sampler have been developed that aim to emulate random par-
titioning of the interval. The time-consistent Dirichlet-based time sampler generates
partitions of the interval for the iterative diffusion models and for the VDM-style
models. It relies on a fixed maximal number of time steps M, and like in the previous
setup K is sampled K ∼U[2, M]. Then a vector of delta-times ∆t with length K is
sampled from a standard Dirichlet distribution. Defining ΠK = {ti}i for 0 ≤i ≤K

CHAPTER 6
Experimental Setup
The models have been trained on 60.000 binarized MNIST images [33] for 2.000 epochs
using the Adam Optimizer [38] with an initial learning rate of 0.0004, a 100 epochs
warm-up period, and 200 epochs of fine-tuning. During the warm-up, the diffusion
loss has been suppressed using linear progression. In practice, this has been done by
multiplying the Ldiffusion loss by a weight function w(t) = min{t/100, 1}, where t is
the epoch number:
L = Lreconstruction + Lprior + w(t)Ldiffusion.
The fine-tuning is implemented with an exponential learning rate scheduler, reducing
the learning rate from the initial 4e-4 to 1e-7. In all experiments, gradient clipping
with a max norm 1 has been used.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis has explored two novel extensions of variational diffusion models: the
FI-VDM and the time-consistent variational diffusion model. The first was motivated
by limitations in existing VAE architectures and the latter by the need for models
that can generate samples at varying time horizons.
The two novel models have been successfully implemented, and empirical results on
binarized MNIST indicate that the FI-VDM approach consistently outperforms both
Ladder VAEs and conventional VDM formulations.
Furthermore, analysis of the
diffusion profile suggests that directly parameterizing forward transitions alleviates
the KL-weight collapse observed in Ladder VAE models.
The results on the time-consistent models demonstrate that it is feasible to train
models capable of sample generation over variable time horizons, though the cost of
this flexibility comes at a cost in terms of performance degradation for the iterative
variational model.
The work has clear limitations. Results are confined to the MNIST domain, leaving
open whether the proposed methods scale to more complex data such as natural
images.
Evaluation relied primarily on ELBO and IWAE bounds, which capture
likelihood but not perceptual quality.
Future directions include extending the iterative approach to continuous-time formu-
lations and testing scalability on high-dimensional datasets.
In summary, this thesis shows that rethinking the variational process opens new
avenues for improving diffusion-based generative models.
The FI-VDM and time-
consistent approaches provide complementary perspectives: one strengthens optimiza-
tion and expressivity, while the other introduces flexibility at the cost of eﬀiciency.
Both highlight the central role of variational parameterization in generative modeling
and point toward promising directions for future research.
