Abstract
This thesis investigates the optimal model-based feedback control. The idea is to combine model
predictive control (MPC) and Youla–Kucera parameterization to improve reference tracking, tuning trans-
parency, and unknown disturbance rejection, while preserving the key advantages of MPC and Youla–Kucera
parameterization.
Firstly, the linear quadratic Gaussian (LQG) control structure is analyzed to gain some insights into the
control problem. However, this structure alone is not effective in attenuating unknown disturbances. Incor-
porating an integrator into the LQG structure lays the groundwork for rejecting such unknown disturbances.
Nevertheless, tuning the LQG controller remains unintuitive and lacks transparency.
Subsequently, the unconstrained MPC framework is introduced to enhance tuning transparency. The
control law is systematically decomposed into feedback and feedforward components. Specific integrator
implementation methods for low-frequency disturbance rejection are discussed in terms of their impact on
closed-loop performance.
Next, the feedforward component is regathered and integrated into a quadratic programming (QP) solver,
enabling the recovery of the full constrained MPC framework. This reformulation allows the feedforward
term to explicitly account for system constraints, which bridges the gap between the unconstrained analytical
formulation and practical implementation of constrained MPC.
Lastly, the feedback component is incorporated into the Youla–Kucera parameterization framework to
facilitate unknown disturbance rejection, while the feedforward component injects its signal parallel to the
output of Q parameter. This framework enables a clear separation between the feedback and feedforward
components, allowing independent design of reference tracking and disturbance rejection. Such structural
decoupling provides greater flexibility in controller design. Several other properties, such as model mismatch
and sampling time requirement, are also discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Project Description
1.1
Introduction
The objective of the thesis is to examine the optimal model-based feedback control. The thesis mainly
concentrated on the linear systems, but a linearization is provided such that this methodology can be
applied to nonlinear systems that can be approximated as linear systems around the linearization point. The
methodologies employed in the thesis are applicable to multi-input-multi-output (MIMO) systems, although
the case study exclusively presents results for single-input-single-output (SISO) systems for the sake of
simplicity.
The thesis aims to present, implement, evaluate, and analyze methodologies for attaining optimal model-
based feedback control. The optimal model-based feedback control consists of two essential components:
feedback control and feedforward control. Feedforward control may serve as a reference signal or a control
signal parallel to the feedback control signal, depending on its injection point. The feedback signal affects
the closed-loop dynamics of the system, associated with the stability of the controlled plant, whereas the
feedforward signal pertains to reference tracking. Combining the two signals, a two-degree-of-freedom
(2DOF) controller can be obtained. The diagram of a 2DOF controller K controlling a plant P is shown in
Figure 1.1.
Σ
Figure 1.1: The diagram of a 2DOF controller controlling a plant.
In order to achieve optimal control, the theory of model predictive control (MPC) is applied. One primary
advantage of MPC is its ability to take anticipated references into account and subsequently optimize based
on the objective function, enabling the controller to respond proactively prior to changes in the reference.
An additional benefit of MPC is that constraints can be defined as conditions for the solution, ensuring that
the control scheme adheres to the physical limits of the controlled system.

CHAPTER 1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
Initially, the unconstrained MPC is examined. By decomposing the unconstrained MPC, linear com-
ponents are identified and categorized into feedback control and feedforward control. After obtaining the
feedback part of MPC, it is integrated with a Kalman filter to stabilize and change the closed-loop dynamics
of the controlled plant. Through the decomposition, the feedback signal is given by the feedback gain Kfb.
Therefore, the stability of the closed-loop feedback system can be guaranteed by analyzing the closed-loop
poles. Furthermore, to achieve the function of attenuating unknown disturbance, augmented Kalman filters
or integrators are designed to remove the influence of disturbance from the system. Different methodologies
are proposed, implemented, and evaluated.
Nevertheless, the decomposition conducted on the unconstrained MPC discards one of the advantages of
the MPC, i.e., the capability to obtain a solution that accounts for physical constraints. As a 2DOF controller

CHAPTER 2
Introduction to Model Predictive Control
Model predictive control (MPC), a well-established technology in the process industry, forecasts the
future behavior of the controlled system and implements suitable actions through optimization algorithms [1].
Utilizing a receding horizon of future references, Model Predictive Control (MPC) employs mathematical
models of controlled systems to forecast future dynamics and subsequently optimize control strategies by
minimizing the cost function [2]. Moreover, constraints can also be introduced to optimization problems,
such that the control will not break the physical limitations of the controlled system [2]. With the capability to
handle complex, constrained, high-dimensional processes in multi-input and multi-output (MIMO) systems,
MPC is gaining more popularity in industry use [3].
This chapter examines the theory of linear MPC. Initially, the fundamental structure and working
principles of MPC are analyzed, which includes an overview of how MPC predicts future system behavior
based on a model and computes optimal control actions by solving an optimization problem at each time
step. Subsequently, the mathematical model of the controlled system is presented, forming the basis of
MPC. The mathematical model comprises state-space equations that characterize the dynamic behavior of
the system. To obtain information regarding the states of the plant, the observer, specifically the Kalman
filter, is introduced. The Kalman filter facilitates state estimation, supplying the current state information as
the initial state for the MPC predictive algorithms. By following the aforementioned procedures, the MPC
optimization problem can ultimately be resolved utilizing the function available in MATLAB.
2.1
MPC structure
The structure of an MPC and the controlled system is illustrated in Figure 2.1, where ¯Zk is the reference
setpoint, ¯Uk is the control signal setpoint, ˆxk is the state estimation, uk is the control signal, zk is the output
of the plant, vk is the measurement noise, and yk is the measurement output. In this setup, the mathematical
model of the plant is discretized to match the sampling time of MPC. In practical application, the continuous
plant output must be sampled to form zk, and the discrete control signal uk should be converted to a
continuous signal using reconstruction methods such as zero-order hold (ZOH). To simplify, the discrete
mathematical plant model is used in the structure.
For the MPC, it is composed of two main parts, namely the state estimation using the observer and the
optimization problem solver. For the observer, it takes the measurement output and the control signal as
inputs and outputs the state estimation ˆxk. For the optimization problem solver, it subsequently incorporates
the state estimation ˆxk, enabling the MPC to know the current states of the plant, which acts as one of the
initial conditions for the prediction process. Besides, external signals, for example, the reference setpoint ¯Zk
and the control signal setpoint ¯Uk, are incorporated into the optimization problem solver to enable the MPC

2.2
MPC Optimization
During the optimization process, the MPC regulator considers state information, forecasts the dynamics
of the controlled system, and subsequently optimizes the manipulated variables (MVs) to achieve the
optimality of the cost function.
For a linear MPC, the optimization problem can be formulated as a linear-quadratic (LQ) problem. In
this formulation, the objective function typically incorporates multiple penalty terms to balance competing
control objectives. Take the penalty terms of set-point tracking ϕz, control input deviation ϕu, and control
input rate-of-change ϕ∆u for example. Term ϕz penalizes deviations of the predicted system outputs from
the desired reference trajectory ¯z, ensuring that the system closely follows the specified set point over the
prediction horizon. Term ϕu penalizes large magnitudes of control inputs, preventing the controller from
applying excessively large actuation efforts. Term ϕ∆u penalizes rapid changes in control inputs between
consecutive time steps, promoting smoother control actions and reducing wear and tear on actuators. Their

2.4
State estimation
In practical control systems, the internal states of the plant are often not directly measurable. This
limitation arises particularly in cases where physical sensors are not available for certain state variables or
where it is impractical or too costly to place sensors for every state component. As a result, direct access to
the full system state is generally not feasible.
To overcome this challenge, state estimation techniques are employed to reconstruct the unmeasured
states based on the available input and output signals of the system. By using a mathematical model of
the plant together with measured outputs, an observer can provide real-time approximations of the internal
states.
State estimation is based on observers. Observers can be categorized as linear observers and nonlinear
observers. As this thesis is mainly concerned with linear systems, it is desirable to only consider the linear
observers. For linear observers, Luenberger observers and Kalman filters are possible candidates. However,
for the Luenberg observer [6], it does not take the noise into consideration. Consequently, it is advisable to
evaluate the Kalman filter, which explicitly accounts for process noise and measurement noise to attain an
optimal estimate of states.
The Kalman filter has garnered significant attention in the industry since the 1970s [7]. In this section,
the derivation of Kalman filter and implementation methods are introduced.

2.4.2
Kalman filter algorithm
For linear time-invariant (LTI) systems, the estimation performance of a stationary Kalman filter, in
which the Kalman gain is precomputed and held constant, does not significantly differ from that of a
dynamic Kalman filter, where the gain is updated at each time step based on the evolving error covariance
matrix P. This is because, in LTI systems with time-invariant noise statistics, the time-varying Kalman
filter converges asymptotically to a steady-state gain, at which point the dynamic and stationary filters
behave identically in terms of state estimation accuracy. Given this convergence behavior and the reduced
computational complexity of the stationary implementation, it is sufficient to adopt the stationary Kalman
filter in linear system applications. Additionally, the stationary Kalman filter facilitates the use of Youla-
Kucera parameterization to be done offline, which will be discussed in Chapter 3. Therefore, the stationary
Kalman filter is used as the primary example throughout this work.

CHAPTER 3
Introduction to Youla-Kucera
Parameterization
Youla-Kucera parameterization is a technique that was first developed in the 1970s [8,9]. Youla [10,11]
and Kucera [12] proposed a parameterization that provides all linear stabilizing controllers for a given linear
time-invariant (LTI) plant in a feedback loop, where the Youla-Kucera parameter Q is implemented to modify
the closed-loop dynamics [13]. The essence of Youla-Kucera parameterization is through the parameter
Q, whose stability guarantees the stability of the closed-loop system while changing the dynamics of the
closed-loop system. The stability of the entire closed-loop system can then be simplified to the stability of
the Youla-Kucera parameter Q.
In this section, Youla-Kucera parameterization is introduced for both feedback systems and feedfor-
ward/feedback systems. The theoretical foundation of this chapter can be found in [14].
3.1
Stabilizing controller
A stabilizing controller is one that ensures internal stability of the closed-loop system when interconnected
with a given plant, where all internal signals of the closed-loop system remain bounded for bounded inputs,
i.e., bounded-input and bounded-output (BIBO). In this section, the stabilizing requirement of the controller
is discussed. It begins with an introduction to the concept of BIBO stability, followed by a discussion of the
conditions needed to guarantee the internal stability of a closed-loop LTI feedback system.

3.3
Q-parameterization
Q-parameterization, also known as Youla–Kucera parameterization, is a fundamental concept in control
theory that characterizes all stabilizing controllers for a given plant. Specifically, if a plant G(s) is stabilized
by an initial controller Kn(s), then all controllers that internally stabilize G(s) can be expressed as a linear
fractional transformation involving a stable and proper transfer function Q(s).
In this section, the structure of Q-parameterization will be introduced and the realizations will be
implemented.

weight matrices will affect the closed-loop behavior.

5.5
Summary
In this chapter, the design and analysis of LQG control with and without integrator have been thoroughly
discussed. To effectively reject unknown disturbances, the inclusion of an integrator is necessary, as it
enables the controller to compensate for persistent exogenous inputs by accumulating the error between
the output and reference over time. However, the integrator must be carefully designed and tuned, as an
improperly selected integrator gain can destabilize the closed-loop system or significantly degrade transient
performance, especially in systems with slow dynamics.
Furthermore, one of the key challenges in implementing LQG control lies in its limited tuning trans-
parency. The relationship between the selected weighting matrices in the cost function and the resulting
closed-loop performance is not transparent, making it difficult to intuitively adjust the controller’s behavior.
This lack of direct interpretability can hinder systematic controller tuning and may require extensive trial-
and-error or optimization-based approaches to achieve desired performance specifications, especially when
the system grows more complex.

After the knowledge gained and experiments conducted in Chapter 5, it is desirable to consider using
MPC as the controller for the plant, which offers a flexible and intuitive framework for controller design. For
MPC, the objective function of it optimizes the predicted output and control signals over a finite time horizon,
which is more intuitive than the objective function of LQR. Potentially, the tuning process in MPC can be
more transparent and manageable compared to LQR, as designers can directly relate tuning parameters to
performance metrics such as reference tracking and control signal cost. In this chapter, the structure of
unconstrained MPC will be studied to reject the unknown disturbance.

6.4.2
Discussion
In conclusion, a standard MPC scheme equipped with an ordinary Kalman filter is inadequate for
handling unknown disturbances, as it lacks the necessary mechanism to compensate for such unmeasured
inputs.
To address this limitation, an augmented Kalman filter can be employed, wherein an integrator is
introduced to estimate the effect of the unknown disturbance on the system states. Importantly, the design
of the integrator within the augmented Kalman filter can be decoupled from the controller design, offering
greater flexibility. This separation allows for more targeted tuning of the disturbance estimation dynamics
without interfering with the control law. However, the performance of the augmented Kalman filter in
stochastic scenarios is generally inferior compared to that of the ordinary Kalman filter or MPC with
integrator.
For MPC with integrator, the overall control structure can be interpreted as a combination of two
coordinated control actions working together. Specifically, one part is the MPC with oridnary Kalman
filter, which optimizes future control actions based on a prediction model to track the reference. The other
part is the integrator, which introduces an additional degree of freedom to eliminate steady-state error and
reject unknown disturbances. However, this dual-action framework introduces additional complexity into
the control design process, as simultaneous presence of both MPC and integral action requires careful
coordination to avoid instability.

From the comparison above, it can be concluded that the feedforward MPC is capable of retaining the
dynamics of the unconstrained MPC, as the simulation result of the feedforward MPC proposed in this
chapter corresponds with the simulation result in Chapter 6.
Furthermore, one of the key advantages of the feedforward MPC framework is its flexibility in incorpo-
rating system constraints. As discussed in detail in Section 7.1.3, the method allows for the reintroduction of
constraints—such as control input limits, output limits, etc. This makes the feedforward MPC not only a
theoretically robust approach for reproducing the behavior of the unconstrained MPC but also a practically
viable solution for real-world applications, where constraints are inevitable and must be respected.

8.4
MPC as feedforward signal
In previous sections, the feedforward signals are referenced with a gain matrix Nr. To include the
anticipating properties of MPC, the feedforward signal is replaced by the MPC control signals. In Chapter
7, the feedforward MPC is discussed. Especially in Section 7.4, the feedforward MPC and unknown
disturbance attenuation are separated. In the structure of Youla-Kucera parameterization, the disturbance and
the feedforward signal are also separated, sharing a similar structure to the feedforward MPC with integrator.
By changing the feedforward signal to the feedforward MPC, the MPC regulator provides optimal control
action with the future information about reference, and thus the controller is able to react early to optimize
the behaviors of the controlled plant. Additionally, the controller is capable of taking multiple constraints
into consideration, featuring the controlled plant with full features of MPC.
According to the experience in Chapter 7, the information that the MPC regulator has access to is
significant. According to the experience obtained in Section 7.3, where MPC considering the integrator’s
state fails to reject the unknown disturabcne, the MPC should only take care of the nominal system, while
the disturbance attenuation is handled by the implemented Qimp filter.

8.7
Asynchronous feedforward and feedback control
From previous examples in this chapter, it can be observed that the feedforward signal is relatively steady
compared with the feedback signals. This steady property leaves room for asynchronous feedforward and
feedback control, where the MPC regulator does not need such frequent optimization processes.
The idea of asynchronous feedforward and feedback control is to separate the feedforward and feedback
control and place them in different sampling times. The feedforward control is associated with the MPC. It
contains more complex algorithms that might require more time for calculation. While the feedback part
is associated with traditional control, which does not require over-complex control algorithms, and thus is
capable of running in a faster sampling time.
In the following subsections, the feedback system has a sampling time of Ts, while the feedforward
control has a sampling time of Tasyn. In the examples of this section, the prediction horizon is chosen as
Tpredict = 10Ts, and the feedforward sampling time is chosen to be Tasyn = 5Ts.
8.7.1
MPC prediction extension
The predictive solution of MPC actually provides the optimized control signal for the whole time horizon
Tpred. However, before separating the feedback part and feedforward part, the control signal requires a
prompt control signal, where the prediction of future dynamics might not be an appropriate estimation of
the controlled plant’s dynamics in the future. Due to the separation of feedback and feedforward signals,
the stability issue is then governed by the feedback part, and the feedforward can be governed by the MPC
with slower sampling time Tasyn. Then even if the feedforward signal is calculated based on the predicted
dynamics of the controlled plant, the performance loss is acceptable.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion and Discussion
9.1
Summary of work
This thesis primarily contributes to the decomposition of MPC and its potential integration with the
Youla-Kucera parameterization framework. Prior to proposing this integration, related theories are reviewed
to establish the theoretical foundations.
Firstly, the LQG control framework is discussed, where the control signal offers an optimal feedback
gain KLQ through the Kalman filter. The integrator placement in this control structure provides a conceptual
foundation for the unknown disturbance attenuation methods developed in subsequent chapters. However,
during the parameter tuning process, it is difficult to intuitively predict how changes in the weighting
matrices of the cost function in the LQR design will influence the overall closed-loop behavior. This lack
of transparency in parameter tuning can complicate the controller design process, especially in a complex
MIMO system.
To address the challenge of tuning difficulty inherent in LQG control, MPC is then considered to address
the tuning problem. The MPC is decomposed into several linear terms that can be placed in an augmented
state-space system. These linear terms can be categorized as feedback term and feedforward term, where the
feedback term replaces the LQG gain KLQ. The tuning difficulty of LQG control is solved by introducing
weight matrices of reference tracking, control cost, and input change-of-rate cost in MPC.
Subsequently, the focus shifts to the attenuation of an unknown disturbance. Two primary strategies are
evaluated. The first approach involves introducing an integrator within the Kalman filter, aiming to enhance
the estimator’s ability to account for low-frequency disturbance. The second method applies an integrator
directly to the error signal between the system output and the reference input, thereby improving steady-state
accuracy and disturbance rejection through feedback correction. Both approaches are tested to assess their
respective performances, where each method exhibits distinct advantages and limitations.
The preceding work is based on the assumption that an unconstrained MPC is implemented. However, as
one of the major advantages of MPC is its ability to explicitly handle physical system constraints and generate
feasible control strategies accordingly, it is essential that the control schemes be capable of accounting
for constraints. The feedforward term, serving as the excitation signal, is considered to take the role of
introducing the constraints. The approaches for implementing the feedforward MPC are discussed, where
the constraints can be given in the feedforward signal.
Lastly, the integration with Youla-Kucera parameterization enhances the performance of attenuating
unknown disturbance. The disturbance’s influence can generate the residual signal in the Youla-Kucera
parameterization structure, which uses the residual signal to detect the discrepancies in the system. The
key advantage of the setup is that the Youla-Kucera parameterization structure separates the unknown
143
==PAGE:160==

disturbance from the feedforward signal. The feedforward signal serves as the reference tracking, while
the plant-controller pair (P −K pair) serves the stabilizing and unknown disturbance attenuation. Then
the disturbance attenuation can be achieved by designing a Q parameter filter such that the disturbance in
the closed-loop system can be attenuated. As the feedforward signal is not influenced by the disturbance
attenuation parameter filter Q, the unknown disturbance attenuation and reference tracking can elegantly be
separated.
The proposed structure exhibits several favorable properties. For instance, the feedforward signal tends
to be more stable than the feedback signal, which allows it to operate with a slower sampling time without
significantly affecting performance. This characteristic enables the use of asynchronous MPC, where
the feedforward and feedback components can operate at different sampling times, thereby relaxing the
computational speed requirements for the MPC.
9.2
Conclusion
The unconstrained MPC solution can be systematically decomposed into two distinct components: a
feedback component and a feedforward component. The feedback component is responsible for stabilizing
the closed-loop system and can be naturally embedded within the Youla-Kucera parameterization framework,
which separates the feedforward design and feedback design. In this context, the Youla parameter Q serves
as a tunable element that is designed to effectively attenuate unknown disturbances acting on the system.
Moreover, the stability of the closed-loop system is guaranteed if Q is stable, which simplifies the designing
process.
On the other hand, the feedforward component captures the predictive and reference-tracking behavior
of MPC. This component can be restructured to form an optimization problem that incorporates system
constraints such as input bounds, and the result will be given by a quadratic programming (QP) solver. The
feedforward component is placed at a place in the Youla-Kucera parameterization structure such that it will
not influence the plant-controller pair, which uses feedback to stabilize the system. Then the feedforward
component can focus on the reference tracking only, as the unknown disturbance attenuation is handled by
the feedback loop.
Over the structure proposed in the thesis, Youla-Kucera parameterized MPC exhibits the best perfor-
mance over the others in unknown disturbance rejection and model mismatch handling. This structure has
demonstrated strong performance in rejecting unknown disturbances and robustness to model mismatch. In
conclusion, the proposed structure offers a reliable and modular control solution that enhances disturbance
rejection and robustness, paving the way for more resilient and high-performance control systems.
9.3
Future work
In this thesis, it is assumed that the underlying system dynamics are linear, or the controlled system can
be effectively regulated around the linearization point. However, if the controlled system exhibits strong
nonlinear behavior or operates over a wide range where one-time linear approximations are no longer valid,
the linear control framework may become insufficient. Through the iterated and adaptive parameter Q and S
design, the non-linear model can be approximated and controlled by iterating parameter Q and S.
Additionally, the design of parameter filter Q using H2 method can also be refined. In the thesis, the
noise is assumed to be white noise, which has a constant power spectral density across all frequencies.
However, this idealized property rarely holds in practical scenarios, where disturbances often exhibit colored
noise characteristics with frequency-dependent behavior. Then, the loop-shaping techniques in H2 might
have a better performance if the specific dynamics of the controlled system are given.
==PAGE:161==
9.3. FUTURE WORK
145
Furthermore, the constraints applied in the thesis are considered to be hard constraints. In future work,
a broader set of constraints specific to the characteristics of the controlled system could be incorporated.
Moreover, as hard constraints may lead to infeasibility, soft constraints can also be taken into account. By
introducing slack variables and penalizing constraint violations within the cost function, soft-constrained
formulations allow the controller to handle conflicting objectives.
Last but not least, the Youla-Kucera parameterization structure can also be used for fault diagnosis. The
residual signal is generated by the discrepancies in the model, which can be used as key indicators in fault
diagnosis. For instance, changes in the residuals beyond a predefined threshold can trigger fault-tolerant
control mechanisms or system reconfiguration. Incorporating fault diagnosis into the control architecture
enhances system reliability, particularly in safety-critical applications where early fault detection and
mitigation are essential.
