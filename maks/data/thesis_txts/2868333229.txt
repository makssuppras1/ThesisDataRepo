Abstract
The impact of weather variations on the electricity market was simulated using Balmorel
for 30 weather years (1986-2015). These market conditions were then applied to test the
financial robustness of hybrid power plants (HPPs) in HyDesign.
Prices follow a seasonal pattern and are significantly influenced by weather, exhibiting a
negative correlation with solar and wind generation. Additionally, solar and wind resources
themselves are negatively correlated. When one resource (e.g., wind speed) decreases,
the other (e.g., solar irradiation) tends to increase, and vice versa.
HPPs demonstrate greater financial robustness over the long term, exhibiting less varia-
tion and outperforming single-technology plants in terms of net present value (NPV). This
increased stability is primarily attributed to reduced production variability, greater partici-
pation in energy markets, more efficient utilization of grid infrastructure, and lower costs
achieved through synergistic effects. The main driver behind the reduced variability is the
decreased dependency on a single renewable energy source.
Several HPP design configurations with varying shares of wind and solar capacity can
achieve comparable long-term financial performance and outperform single-technology
plants. Notably, each weather year may have its own optimal HPP design. However, plan-
ning HPP designs based on multiple weather years is crucial, as the optimal configuration
can vary significantly over time, ranging from pure solar or wind setups to various hybrid
configurations. Over the long term, HPP designs consistently demonstrate stable and
robust financial performance.
However, this conclusion assumes sufficient wind and solar resources at the plant location
and the possibility of overplanting the point of connection (POC).
Under current battery technology costs and tested electricity market conditions, no battery-
based design was selected during optimization.
Not all potential aspects of HPPs have been explored, such as participation in various
electricity markets like automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) and Frequency
Containment Reserve (FCR), or all possible design configurations of HPPs, including
combinations of solar, wind, hydropower, biomass, and hydrogen production.

1
Introduction
Climate change is primarily driven by the increasing levels of carbon dioxide (CO2) in
the atmosphere. Human activities, such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation, have
significantly accelerated this process, leading to a 50% increase in atmospheric CO2
concentration within the past 200 years [7].
The European Commission has set ambitious climate goals to mitigate this crisis, target-
ing a reduction of net greenhouse gas emissions by at least 55% by 2030 in comparison
to 1990 and achieving carbon neutrality by 2050 [8].
Every sector plays a crucial role in reducing total emissions. In Europe, the Transport
sector accounts for the largest share, contributing 43% of total emissions. The Electricity
and Heat Production sector follows, responsible for 20% of emissions [9].
The EU’s decarbonization targets must align with objectives such as energy market inte-
gration, competitiveness, security of supply, and energy affordability. However, achieving
these targets is challenging, particularly in light of the growing share of intermittent re-
newable energy sources, increasing electricity demand, more small-scale decentralized
generation, and more flexible generation, storage, and demand [10].
Hybrid Power Plants (HPPs), such as wind-solar combinations with integrated storage or
hydrogen production, offer a potential solution to many of these challenges. They pro-
vide several benefits, including more efficient land and grid utilization, scheduled power
dispatch, mitigation of energy variability, and reductions in both CAPEX and OPEX [1].
Since industrialized Hybrid Power Plants with long-term operational data are rare due to
the relatively new concept, energy systems modeling tools like Balmorel and HyDesign
are essential. Those software tools are utilized in the master thesis project to evaluate
HPP robustness.
Balmorel simulates future energy systems and generates day-ahead market prices based
on diverse input data, including historical weather patterns. These prices are then used
in HyDesign to simulate the design and operation of Hybrid Power Plants to evaluate the
financial performance under 30 years of weather and price data.
This thesis aims to answer the research question:
Does a Hybrid Power Plant offer greater long-term robustness compared to standalone
wind or solar farms regarding financial stability under fluctuating market conditions and
variable weather scenarios?
Answering this research question provides valuable insights into the benefits of Hybrid
Power Plants, motivating further research and investment in these systems. This, in turn,
contributes to the acceleration of the energy transition and achieving climate goals.

1.1
Structure
Chapter 1 introduces the topic of HPP and outlines the scope of the master’s thesis.
Chapter 2 reviews the current literature on HPP functionality, discussing its technical ad-
vantages and disadvantages. It also covers the history, operation, and future outlook of
the European electricity market.
Chapter 3 describes the methodology and analytical framework used in the thesis, intro-
ducing the software tools Balmorel and HyDesign employed for the analysis. It also ex-
plains basic weather concepts relevant to the study. In Chapter 4, the case study locations
Denmark, Germany, and France are introduced. The scenario and system configuration
for Balmorel are presented, and the parameters and variables for HyDesign are detailed.
Chapter 5 examines the impact of weather variations and their connection to market price
trends. Furthermore, it links the output from Balmorel with the results from HyDesign to
analyze the financial robustness of HPP designs compared to single-plant designs in the
long term. Finally, it characterizes robust HPP design principles. Chapter 6 provides a
detailed discussion of the findings, limitations, and suggestions for future studies, while
Chapter 7 concludes with a summary of the thesis results.
1.2
Scope
The primary focus of this masters thesis is to evaluate the robustness of HPPs under
long-term weather and market fluctuations. The analysis is based on three European
case studies: Denmark, Germany, and France. The research utilizes the Balmorel and
HyDesign software tools to conduct robustness testing. The scope of the thesis is di-
vided into three research objectives. These objectives, along with their motivations and
constraints, are outlined below. A detailed explanation of the process is provided in the
methodology section.
- Analyze the impact of long-term weather and market fluctuations: This objective in-
vestigates the relationship between day-ahead bidding zone electricity prices and weather
variables such as solar and wind generation profiles, as well as electricity demand profiles.
The analysis aims to identify the causes of price variations and how weather conditions
and demand influence these changes. The findings will highlight the challenges faced by
renewable energy sources and their impact on both current and future electricity markets.
- Evaluate the robustness of HPPs: This objective assesses the resilience of HPPs to
market and weather fluctuations in comparison to single renewable energy plants. The
analysis will provide insights into the long-term financial performance of HPPs and their
ability to withstand external variability.
- Recommend design strategies for resilient HPPs: The final objective focuses on
identifying the key factors that contribute to the robustness of HPP designs. This objective
aims to provide actionable recommendations to improve the resilience and performance
of HPP systems.

The literature review introduces the concept of Hybrid Power Plants (HPPs) and provides
an overview of the European eletectricity markets. It defines HPPs, highlights their impor-

A comprehensive overview of European electricity markets is then presented, starting

and the increasing integration of renewable energy sources.
2.1
Hybrid Power Plants
Hybrid power plants (HPPs) combine two or more types of energy generation, such as
wind, solar, hydrogen, or biomass, with storage capacities or end-use technologies like
electrolyzers [11]. These systems often integrate complementary energy sources, such
as wind and solar, to provide more stable power generation.
A typical configuration might involve solar and wind power for energy generation, lithium-
ion batteries for energy storage, and an electrolyzer for hydrogen production as an end-
use technology. Other storage technologies, such as pumped hydro or flow batteries, can
be employed alongside renewable energy sources to mitigate the variability in renewable
generation.
However, HPPs currently lack a well-defined regulatory framework or standardization,
which poses challenges for market integration, grid compatibility, and economic incentives
[1].
2.1.1
HPP Advantages
The need for hybridization of renewable energy sources is caused by the limitations and
challenges of individual renewable sources such as variability, limited market participa-
tion, and reduced utility maximization of the land [12]. Figure 1, sourced from the lob-
bying agency WindEurope, illustrates the advantages for various stakeholders, including
society, the power system, developers, and owners.

2.1
Hybrid Power Plants
Figure 1: Overview of advantages for hybrid power plants [1].
The society benefits from more efficient land use, cost-effective grid utilization, and en-
hanced system stability. The power system benefits from reduced variability, fewer power
and voltage peaks, and greater overall stability. For developers, HHP facilitates higher uti-
lization of grid connection capacity and streamlines the permitting process by consolidat-
ing contracts, eliminating the need for separate agreements for different energy sources
like wind and solar. Furthermore, HPP reduces uncertainty and provides more consistent
power production for owners [1]. In summary, an HPP improves efficiency and utilization
across multiple sectors.
Additionally, HHP mitigates the disadvantages of individual renewable energy sources.
By combining sources with different peak times and weather patterns such as solar and
wind or by integrating storage technologies, HHP improves reliability, stability, and energy
availability. This reduces energy wastage, as excess energy can be stored for use during
periods of low production or when grid demand increases. Moreover, energy storage not
only supports grid stabilization but also allows participation in various energy markets
[12].
Overall, HPPs, in combination with storage, reduce dependency on conventional elec-
tricity generation. More broadly, renewables decrease reliance on foreign energy sources
and significantly lower carbon emissions compared to fossil fuel technologies. This makes
them essential for addressing climate change and accelerating the transition to a sustain-
able energy system.

2.1.2
HPP Disadvantages
Shifting to the challenges of HHP, several key issues can be categorized into technical,
economic, environmental, and regulatory aspects [13].
From a technical perspective, HPP are more complex than traditional plants in areas
such as control and management, energy output predictions, selecting appropriate bat-
tery storage, and ensuring high power quality. Additionally, economic challenges arise
due to higher CAPEX and increased uncertainty regarding return on investment (ROI).
Environmental assessments also become more complicated, particularly when measur-
ing the yield for an entire site with multiple renewable sources. Furthermore, regulatory
and policy challenges include inconsistent policies and the lack of standardization, which
complicate the deployment and operation of HHP [13].
Focusing on the volatility of renewable energy sources, caused by weather fluctuations
and geographical dependencies, presents significant challenges for grid stability. While
site assessments can mitigate some of these issues, the average number of full load
hours (FLH) for renewables remains significantly lower than for fossil fuel-based power
plants [13].
Moreover, the expansion of individual renewable energy projects increasingly consumes
more space, which is becoming a growing limitation. In contrast, the spatial efficiency
of thermal power plants measured by power output (MW) or energy production (GWh)
relative to land use is much higher [13]. This discrepancy is particularly problematic in
densely populated and industrialized regions, such as Germany, where efficient land use
is critical.
2.2
European Electricity Markets
Historically, energy was managed by national monopolies [14]. However, the national
energy monopolies within the European Union (EU) have been liberalized over time. In
1986, with the Single European Act, the EU launched several policies to liberalize the
market, enhance supply security, and improve price transparency [15]. The development
of the legislation behind energy or electricity and gas markets started on the European
level in 1996 and continues until today. Since 1996, five directives were passed.
2.2.1
Historical Development
In 1996 and 1998, the directive First Energy Package was published to liberalize electricity

2.2.2
Current Electricity Market Overview
Electricity is a unique commodity in the market, as it is difficult to store in large quantities
and requires a constant balance between supply and demand at all times [16].
The
balance between electrical supply and demand is essential to maintain the European
electricity grid frequency at a stable 50Hz, ensuring that all appliances and electrical
equipment operate correctly [17]. Significant frequency fluctuations can cause damage
to electrical devices or even result in blackouts.
An oversupply of energy to the grid
increases the frequency, while an undersupply decreases it. Large deviations from the
reference frequency trigger control actions and lead to high costs [18].
Germany, for example, a deviation of more than 0.2 Hz sustained over a longer period will
activate protection mechanisms, disconnecting networks and plants from the grid [19].
Several stakeholders including producers, traders, markets, transmission system opera-
tors (TSO), and distribution system operators (DSO) work within the framework of appro-
priate regulations to maintain a constant frequency [16].
Electricity, like any other good, is traded in various markets. Figure 2 illustrates the mar-
kets where electricity is traded and the mechanisms that help stabilize frequency. These
markets include forward, day-ahead, intraday, balancing, and continuous day-after mar-
kets. This masters thesis focuses on the largest electricity market, the day-ahead whole-
sale market. Due to time constraints, other electricity markets, such as the automatic
Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) and Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR), are
not covered.

As electricity is difficult to store, supply and demand must be continuously balanced. This
makes the short-term spot power market, particularly the day-ahead wholesale market,
critically important [16].
The day-ahead market is an auction-based platform where electricity is traded for each
hour of the following day [20]. Pricing in each bidding zone is determined by the merit
order principle [16].
Figure 3 illustrates the merit order principle. Generation bids are aggregated for each hour
in the day-ahead market to form a supply curve. These bids reflect the marginal costs of
the generating units. Typically, wind and solar energy sources have the lowest marginal
costs, while fossil fuel-based generation, particularly gas, has the highest marginal costs
[21].
Similarly, demand bids are aggregated to form the demand curve. The intersection of the
supply and demand curves determines the clearing price and volume.
The market-clearing power plant receives a price equal to its marginal cost, while the
rest of the power plants receive a clearing price higher than their marginal costs. The
difference between the clearing price and the marginal cost is known as the inframarginal
rent.
Inframarginal rents contribute to covering fixed generation costs.
A technology
becomes attractive for investors only when the market clearing price consistently exceeds
the sum of its marginal and fixed costs [21].

Summarizing, each country typically has one or several bidding zones. In the day-ahead
market, the price in each bidding zone is set on trading platforms such as EPEX SPOT

European bidding zones are interconnected through the transmission system, and the
European Commission established the Single Day-ahead Coupling (SDAC) to create a
unified cross-zonal day-ahead electricity market.
SDAC optimizes allocating scarce cross-border transmission capacity by coupling re-
gional wholesale electricity markets through a common algorithm, considering transmis-
sion constraints to maximize social welfare[22].
SDAC is operated by a single algorithm that takes bids, offers, and network data from
Nominated Electricity Market Operators (NEMOs) and Transmission System Operators
(TSOs) as inputs. The outputs such as matched trades, clearing prices, and scheduled
exchanges are sent back to the TSOs and NEMOs. This process ensures efficiency and
reliability within strict timelines [22]. The market agents participating in the SDAC are
listed in Appendix A.

Methodology
This section outlines the methodology used to evaluate the long-term performance of
HPPs under variable conditions. It begins with a general overview of the research pro-
cess and describes how the research objectives are achieved. Weather data and basic
weather concepts are explained to provide sufficient context for the thesis project. The en-

adjustments made to each model for this thesis.
The evaluation of HHP performance relies on Balmorel, HyDesign, and publicly available
data, each involving numerous input and output parameters. Rather than explaining every
model in detail, the focus is on the models key functionalities and the adjustments made,
with an emphasis on the resulting insights into long-term HHP behavior.

ERA5: Weather Data
The meteorological dataset utilized by CorRES is ERA5, provided by the European Center
for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). ERA5 is the fifth generation of ECMWF
atmospheric reanalysis for the global climate, covering the period from January 1940 to
the present. This dataset is produced by the Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S)
at ECMWF.
ERA5 delivers hourly estimates of numerous atmospheric, land, and oceanic climate vari-
ables. It offers global coverage with a spatial resolution of 30 km and resolves the atmo-
sphere across 137 vertical levels, extending up to 80 km in height. ERA5 also includes
uncertainty information for all variables at reduced spatial and temporal resolutions [29].
The simulation distinguishes resource locations for energy generation into three cate-
gories: A, B, and C, where Class A represents locations with the best energy resources
and Class C represents the least favorable.
For higher resolution wind data, the Global Wind Atlas (GWA) is used, while for higher
resolution solar irradiance data, the ERA5-Land dataset is applied [28].

3.3.1
Introduction to Balmorel
The general introduction to Balmorel is based on a paper by DTU’s Department of Man-
agement and Department of Wind Energy [30], as well as the user guide provided by
Ea Energianalyse [31]. Ea Energianalyse is a Danish consulting company that provides
consulting services and has many years of experience working with Balmorel.
Balmorel is an open-source energy system model written in GAMS. It encompasses var-
ious aspects including energy, environment, economics, and policy regulations, and it
covers the technical components of power and combined heat and power (CHP) sys-
tems. Balmorel model offers solutions based on least-cost economic dispatch and capac-
ity expansion, focusing on the European energy system. The results are interpreted as
representing an efficient market under the assumption of perfect competition.
Balmorel analyzes several relevant issues for the energy system, such as electricity sup-
ply and demand, natural gas, energy transmission and expansion, heat planning, and
pricing. Each issue can be analyzed from different perspectives, including market design
analysis, policy implications and regulatory design, technical and economic cost-benefit
feasibility, operational simulation, and scenario analysis, such as building an energy sys-
tem without fossil fuels.
Geographically, Balmorel is divided into countries, regions, and areas. At the country
level, policies and aims can be defined, while at the regional level, the electrical system
and transmission are defined. Areas contain information about generation capacity and
investment options for generation capacity, full load hours, variation profiles for wind, solar,
or hydro, and fuel prices. Heat demand is also defined per area.
Balmorel has simplifications and limitations. For instance, transmission between regions
is modeled with a single capacity connection, ignoring voltages and grid stability, and fo-

HyDesign: Hybrid Renewable Energy System Model
HyDesign is an open-source tool designed for optimizing the utility sectors of hybrid wind,
solar, and storage power plants [36]. The tool addresses a nested optimization problem,
also referred to as bilevel optimization [37].
In HyDesign, there are two optimization problems: an outer sizing optimization and an
internal operation optimization. The solution to the internal (inner) operation optimiza-
tion problem depends on the outcome of the outer sizing optimization. The outer sizing
optimization maximizes the net present value (NPV) by considering capital expenditures
(CAPEX) and or comparing the results to the results of a standard design that minimizes
the levelized cost of energy (LCoE) [36].

Several of these design variables, such as the wake loss surrogate, degradation factors
for wind, solar, and battery, the wind power plant (WPP) model, the photovoltaic (PV)
power plant model, and the associated cost and financial models, are not elaborated in
detail here. Information about these specific models is available in the official DTU paper

The inner optimization problem, or energy management system (EMS), aims to maxi-
mize the revenue of the HPP by determining the optimal amount of battery charging or
discharging and power curtailment.
The objective function is to maximize the product of electricity price and the generation
from the HPP (solar, wind, and battery storage), while accounting for opportunity costs
when generation does not meet demand during periods of high prices, and penalties for
inefficient battery use. The penalties discourage poor charging and discharging behavior,
ensuring that large fluctuations in battery activity are only allowed when electricity prices
are high. Moreover, the system discourages discharging the battery when prices are low,
to minimize battery degradation [36].

• Batteries are only installed for NPV/Ch optimization because they increase cost but

5.3
HHP Design Characteristics
The results are summarized and interpreted to identify key factors contributing to HPP
design robustness. Based on these insights, recommendations for HPP design strategies
are proposed. Four key observations stand out regarding the robustness of HPP design:
1. HPP Design Functionality: The design of the HPP is influenced by the availability
and variability of wind and solar resources, as well as market prices. HyDesign op-
timizes grid integration through overplanting strategies and leveraging the negative
correlation between solar and wind generation. This reduces production variability,
ensuring steadier cash flow and a more stable business case. In contrast, single-
technology plants are more directly affected by the fluctuations of their specific re-
source and market prices.
2. HPP Design Variations: The design of HPP plants varies depending on the weather
year, with different allocations of shares of solar and wind. Consequently, evaluating
an HPP design across multiple weather years is essential rather than relying on a
single year. Financially, however, the variations between different HPP designs are
relatively small, especially when compared to the significantly larger variations seen
in pure wind or solar plant designs.
3. Resource Requirements: Developing an HPP that integrates solar and wind tech-
nologies requires sufficient renewable resources at the chosen location to achieve
high capacities factors for both technologies. Additionally, the site must support
overplanting, which is critical for establishing an attractive business case. If solar or
wind resources are inadequate, a single-technology plant may be a more practical
and efficient option. As a result, not every location is suitable for constructing an
HPP.
4. Battery Storage Feasibility: Under current costs for lithium-ion battery storage
systems, investments in such systems are not attractive across any weather or price
scenarios.
However, declining costs could make battery storage attractive busi-
ness cases, particularly in scenarios with more frequent negative electricity prices
or through participation in different electricity markets, such as the FCR.

6
Discussion
The chapter discussion is divided into three parts. First, the analysis results are sum-
marized and discussed. Second, the limitations and assumptions of the study are high-
lighted. Finally, future research directions are outlined.
6.1
Study Discussion
The 2030 day-ahead market prices vary hourly, weekly, and annually across all study
cases for 30 weather years. Prices follow a seasonal pattern, peaking in winter and dip-
ping in summer, significantly influenced by weather, especially wind resources. Market
prices negatively correlate with solar and wind generation, while solar and wind genera-
tion are also negatively correlated. In this framework, wind has a greater price influence
than solar. These trends suggest that as renewable energy expands, price volatility will
become increasingly weather-dependent, with even greater fluctuations driven by chang-
ing weather conditions.
The HPP assessment over the long term is analyzed for the optimized HPP design under
the weather scenario year 2012. HyDesign selected a hybrid design only for the case of
Germany. For Denmark and France, single-plant designs are preferred, primarily due to
the availability of local renewable resources. However, in the German case, a comparison
of single-plant designs with HPPs over 30 weather years demonstrates that the HPP
design is financially more robust. This robustness is reflected in a lower standard deviation
and superior long-term average financial performance.
Analyzing design outcomes under varying weather scenarios and price conditions, it is
notable that batteries were not chosen in any case. This outcome is likely due to the high
costs of batteries and low energy prices, which make battery HPP designs economically
unfeasible.
In Germany, a diverse range of HPP design options has been identified, varying in total
installed capacity (MW) and the proportional contributions of wind and solar energy. The
long-term average composition for Germany consists of 65% solar and 35% wind. Addi-
tionally, the analysis underscores the critical importance of considering multiple weather
years for plant design and operational assessments, as opposed to relying on data from
a single year, to ensure robust and reliable performance under varying conditions.
For France, a purely solar-plant design is consistently selected, characterized by sig-
nificant overplanting. The optimization results showed minimal differences in financial
performance between single-plant and HPP designs.
In Denmark, wind was the dominant resource selected, as expected due to favorable wind
conditions. Solar was chosen as a secondary technology in 11 years, with significant
overplanting also observed. Germany was the only country where a hybrid design was
consistently selected across all weather years, emphasizing its suitability under the given
conditions.

6.2
Study Limitations
The sensitivity analysis highlights the identification of various HPP designs with different
share allocations that achieve similar financial stability. However, the level of NPV is more
dependent on the degree of overplanting. Achieving this requires sufficient renewable
resources and legal permission to overplant the grid connection capacity.
The absence of battery installations across all case studies, despite 30 years of varying
price data inputs, is notable. However, potential trends and the impact of cheaper storage
technologies were not included in the analysis. Furthermore, the study focused solely on
day-ahead market prices, excluding other markets such as automatic Frequency Restora-
tion Reserve (aFRR) and Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR), which could represent
attractive business opportunities for HPPs.
Putting the study results into context with recent market developments, such as negative
electricity prices or price peaks reaching 900 EUR/MWh during "Dunkelflaute" conditions,
it becomes clear that these factors were not considered in the analysis. Such trends intro-
duce greater uncertainty for single-plant designs and highlight the increasing relevance
and potential of HPPs.
6.2
Study Limitations
This study has several limitations, categorized into the aspects of HPP technology, de-
signs, and markets, historical weather data, price data, technology cost data, sensitivity
analysis, scope, and simulation limitations.
- HPP Technology, Designs, and Markets: Not all technologies were considered when
analyzing price variations under weather fluctuations. Additionally, not all potential HPP
designs were explored, such as future configurations combining HHP with hydrogen,
biomass, or alternative storage technologies. Furthermore, the business case analysis
was limited to the day-ahead market, excluding other electricity markets such as the FCR
and aFRR, which could present new business opportunities.
- Historical Weather Data: The simulation data used in Balmorel and HyDesign is based
on historical weather data, which does not account for recent climate change trends, such
as rising temperatures, an increased likelihood of extreme weather events, and potential
shifts in weather patterns.
This limitation introduces uncertainty regarding how these
changes might influence electricity prices, HPP design, and operations.
- Price Data: Day-ahead market prices for 2030 were adjusted by +15 EUR, as the
initial prices, given in real 2016 EUR, resulted in exclusively negative business cases.
However, Balmorel generally produced low prices due to its perfect market simulation,
raising concerns about financing new grid infrastructure and incentivizing developers to
build adequate generation and storage capacities. How can new heavy CAPEX energy
projects be financed without subsidies? Who assumes the risk if future designs continue
to rely on the merit-order principle, where participants bid at marginal costs?
- Technology Cost Data: Cost parameters significantly influence HPP design. However,
no sensitivity analysis has been conducted to evaluate how variations in cost parameters

farm layouts. Additionally, the cost data was sourced exclusively from the Danish Energy

- Scope: The broad scope of this master’s thesis limits the depth of analysis regarding the

transition and market development are heavily influenced by national and European po-
litical priorities, as well as external factors such as tariff conflicts or wars. Additionally,
international developments, such as recent policies in the United States, can significantly
impact the renewable energy industry.
6.3
Future Studies
Future research directions are derived from the limitations of this study and can be sum-
marized into two key areas:
Sensitivity analysis: A detailed sensitivity analysis addressing several aspects of the
study limiations such as HPP technology, designs, and markets, historical weather data,
price data, and technology cost data could provide valuable insights. Exploring varia-
tions in these parameters could help identify the characteristics of optimal scenarios for
HPPs. This includes potential design concepts, price targets for battery business cases,
minimum day-ahead market prices, validation of cost inputs, required weather resources
combined with technology to operate an HPP, and identifying exceptional land resources.
A core research question could be: What are the key characteristics of best-case HHPs
in the European context?
Real-world case studies: Addressing the study’s limitations in terms of scope and sim-
ulation constraints, future research should focus on real-world HPP case studies with
specific constraints on a global scale, utilizing data from energy developers. Alongside
this, conducting interviews and surveys with HPP developers would provide insights into

7
Conclusion
This study investigated the robustness of HPP under long-term weather and market fluc-
tuations. For this purpose, 30 weather years (1986-2015), combined with price data from
Balmorel, were analyzed. The performance of HPP designs was compared with single
plants in HyDesign to evaluate their financial robustness in operation.
The research question: Does an HPP offer greater long-term robustness compared to
standalone wind or solar farms regarding financial stability under fluctuating market con-
ditions and variable weather scenarios? was divided into three research objectives: un-
derstanding the connection between long-term weather and market fluctuations, evaluat-
ing the robustness of HPP designs, and providing recommendations and strategies for
resilient HPP designs.
In summary, prices follow a seasonal pattern and are significantly influenced by weather,
exhibiting a negative correlation with solar and wind generation. Additionally, solar and
wind resources themselves are negatively correlated.
Focusing on HPPs demonstrates greater long-term financial robustness, with less vari-
ation and higher NPV compared to single-technology plants. This financial stability is
largely achieved through the optimal use of grid infrastructure, overplanting strategies,
and leveraging the negative correlation between solar and wind generation creating a
balancing effect. As a result, HPPs produce steadier cash flows throughout the year,
benefiting from reduced production variability and enhanced participation in energy mar-
kets. In contrast, single-technology plants are more directly affected by the fluctuations of
their specific resource and the associated market prices.
Several HPP design configurations with varying shares of wind and solar capacity can
achieve similar long-term financial performance and outperform single-plant design. No-
tably, each weather year may have its own optimal HPP design. Nevertheless, conducting
HPP design planning based on multiple weather years is crucial, as the optimal design
can vary significantly over time, ranging from pure solar or wind setups to various hybrid
configurations. Under current battery technology costs and electricity market conditions,
no battery-based design was selected during the optimization process.
However, this conclusion is valid under certain conditions, such as the presence of suffi-
cient wind and solar resources at the plant location and the possibility of overplanting the
point of connection (POC).
HPPs address key challenges in renewable energy systems, including rising electricity
demand, the need for flexible generation and storage, and better utilization of limited
land and grid infrastructure. To scale up HPP deployment, it is essential to manage their
complexity through policy standardization and identifying best-case operational scenarios.
Further research can advance HPP adoption by conducting sensitivity analyses on costs,
prices, weather data, site locations, and technology design. Additionally, surveys with en-
ergy developers to evaluate existing HPP projects would help identify current bottlenecks
and barriers to HPP implementation.
