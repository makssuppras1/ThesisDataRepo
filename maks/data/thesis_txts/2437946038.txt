Abstract
This project investigates electrodeposited copper on a substrate copper wire.
Mi-
crostructure of the deposited material as well as the substrate is characterized qual-
itatively in light optical microscopy, and texture is analyzed by electron backscatter
diffraction. Mechanical properties are examined by hardness measurements and ten-
sile tests. The effect of texture on the modulus of elasticity is examined by a finite
element (FEM) program developed for the purpose. This research concludes that the
electrodeposited copper appears in 4 microstructures, depending on the electrodepo-
sition parameters. All deposited microstructures are harder than the substrate, and
the hardness is specifically linked to the microstructure in all cases. The texture anal-
ysis shows a high density of twins for low current densities (around 2 A/dm2), and
a preferred orientation of ⟨1 1 0⟩in the growth direction; the preferred orientation is
not shown all around the radius, and more research is needed to clarify this. The
FEM-program finds that the modulus of elasticity should be within 123 GPa - 126
GPa for a simplified model of the composite wire with a substrate and a radially pre-
ferred direction in the deposited layer. The tensile test finds a much lower modulus:
71 GPa, and this is estimated to come from local softening contributions of plastic
strain. The plastic properties of the deposited layer is estimated to be affected mainly
by grain boundaries.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
For metals the material properties are relevant for many purposes.
In structural
mechanics, in electrical engineering and in general for design of products whether
performance or elegance are in focus, material properties are considered; and these
properties are tailored for the purposes. Mechanical properties can be affected by
chemical composition, deformation history, temperature treatment and other manu-
facturing characteristics. Some processes are linked to certain textures, grain shapes
and grain sizes and this affects the material properties. Texture is linked to elastic
deformation and to crystal plasticity (dislocations slipping in slip systems) and the
microstructure is linked to the resistance to plastic deformation as grain boundaries
hinder dislocation movement.
Dislocation density also plays a role in mechanical
properties, but is only considered briefly for this investigation.
In the following, a copper wire is considered. This copper wire is made by electrode-
position of copper on a copper substrate wire.
The substrate wire is drawn and
recrystallized before hand meaning it has characteristics well described in literature.
The deposited layer however is less well described and in focus for this investigation.
Both texture (crystallographic orientations) and microstructure have effects on the
mechanical properties and are investigated. The texture has a theoretical influence
on elastic deformation and can also be related to the plastic deformation through
orientation dependant slip systems. The microstructure is relevant for only plastic
properties, but the microstructure has dramatic effects on the local hardness of a
section.
Different microstructures are investigated for different electrodepositing parameters,
and the microstructures are linked to hardness measurements.
The texture is not only investigated experimentally, but also simulated in finite ele-
ment modelling, and the elastic response of the finite element model is compared to
tensile test results.
In order to examine microstructure qualitatively, light optical microscopy (LOM)
is used, and looking into texture, electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) becomes
relevant. EBSD results are analyzed by MTEX Toolbox [Hie+], a package to MAT-
LAB.
The link between microstructure and plastic properties is found in the hardness maps.
Hardness measurements are done on a micrometer scale, giving in impression of the
plastic properties for local microstructure and texture.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In order to describe the mechanical properties of the produced wire, some effects of
the microstructure and texture has to be accounted for. The effects taken into account
are mainly grain size/grain boundaries and crystal orientation, as these influence the
mechanical properties in the plastic domain. The crystal orientation also effects the
elastic properties. In the following, the effects of grain size and crystal orientation
are considered separately under the main categories elastic and plastic deformation,
after an introduction to orientation measurements.
2.1
Measuring crystal orientations in EBSD
Electron backscatter diffraction is used for finding crystal orientations. This is done
by subjecting a plane surface to a beam of electrons, and letting the beam be scattered
back in all directions in the material. The scattered electrons are then diffracted by
lattice planes. The diffracted electrons give a pattern, where Bragg’s law, is satisfied,
[ER10].
The Bragg angles are used to determine the crystallographic orientation,
which can then presented in various ways ([ER10]). In this investigation, inverse pole
figures are used to show the results. An inverse pole figure presentation is illustrated
in Figure 2.1 from [ER10].
Figure 2.1: Presentation of crystallographic orientations in an inverse pole figure
(from [ER10]).

Orientations in a sample can be presented by assigning a color to each orientation,
and then coloring the cross section corresponding to the assigned orientations. In
terms of quantifying orientations relatively, another presentation is done by coloring
the orientations in the inverse pole figure map (Figure 2.1) according to the density
of orientations. Quantifying orientations relatively like this is compared to a random
texture, meaning that a high occurrence of a specific orientation does not mean that
other orientations are not present.
2.2
Elastic deformation
Loading with a corresponding elastic deformation implies no persistent change of the
crystal lattice after unloading. There are no dislocations that move, and so lattice
defects have no influence on this. In that sense, grain boundaries do not influence
elasticity, assumed that the grain boundaries are thin and have no significant volume
fraction with different elastic properties than the interiors of the grains.
Elastic
deformation links stress to elastic strain:
σ = C ϵ
(2.1)
In this equation, σ is stress and ϵ is strain, both in Voigt vector notation as introduced
by Zenkert and Battley, [ZB09]. C is the material stiffness matrix linking stress and
strain. Later, another matrix, K is introduced. K is linking displacement and force,
and therefore has different units etc. To avoid confusion, C is denoted the material
stiffness matrix and K us denoted the structural stiffness matrix.
2.2.1
Influence of Crystal orientation
The elastic deformation of a crystal varies with crystallographic orientation. In the
following, a single face centered cubic (FCC) unit cell is considered, and crystallo-
graphic orientations are given in a Bravais lattice notation, [a b c]. In such a unit
cell, loading along directions [1 0 0], [1 1 0] and [1 1 1] will give different strains.
The crystal is not isotropic. In order to calculate the strain and consequently the
elasticity modulus for a given direction, the stiffness matrix for a cubic system has to
be transformed into a stiffness matrix for the coordinate system in which the loading
is defined. In the following, every grain will have the same material stiffness matrix
in its local coordinate system. Physically, this just means that all the grains have the
same elastic properties, if the load is applied equivalently in the local coordinate sys-
tem, i.e. crystallographic coordinate system. If load is not applied equivalently in the
coordinate systems, either the coordinate system of the load has to be transformed
from one grain to another, or the stiffness of each grain should be transformed into
the loading coordinate system. Considering structures consisting of many grains, the
deformation is calculated by solving a system of equations:
F = K · D
(2.2)
==PAGE:15==
2.3 Rotation matrices
5
Here, F is a vector, being the load, D is a vector of displacements and K is the struc-
tural stiffness matrix containing all the grain contributions to the stiffness. Equation
2.2 is the foundation of the Finite Element Method (FEM) used in this project, and
it will be derived at a later point. Assuming that all points of a structure are in-
cluded in an evaluation, there are infinitely many equations. If the load is known
in a global coordinate system for the whole structure, the displacements are solved
in the global coordinate system, and the stiffness matrix then has to be in the same
coordinate system as the load. For this reason, transformation of the material stiff-
ness matrix is relevant, as the force will be kept in its coordinates; the coordinate
system of the load is then denoted the global coordinate system. In opposition to the
global coordinate system (the coordinate system used for the whole structure) stands
the local, crystallographic coordinate system, which for each grain is the coordinate
system where the material stiffness matrix C has the properties of a stiffness matrix
for a cubic system, being described by only three components, (C11, C12, C44) [ZB09].
In the following, transformation of the crystal material stiffness matrix by rotation of
the coordinate system is described, keeping in mind the application for finite element
method (FEM).
2.3
Rotation matrices
When building a finite element code that is capable of accounting for grain orientation,
transformation matrices are needed to transform stiffness, stress and strain from
grain coordinate systems to global or in the reverse direction.
The Euler angles
used to describe grain orientation give rotations around the z-axis and the x-axis
of the global coordinate system, and so transformation matrices for these rotations
are calculated. In the following, rotation around y is included and used in the FEM

2.4
Crystallographic orientations and deviations from
directions through EBSD
When analyzing crystallographic orientations, the orientations are given in Euler an-
gles. In this section, the Euler angles are ϕ1 (around z), Φx (around x) and ϕ2 (around
z). These Euler are measured and can be used to describe any crystallographic ori-
entation. The rotation matrices are made from the same considerations as in section
2.3, and the procedure is then as follows:
1. Build rotation matrix: R = Rz(ϕ1) · Rx(Φ) · Rz(ϕ2)
2. Multiply rotation matrix by normalized direction, a, in which crystallographic
orientations, b, are wanted: b = R · a
3. Normalize the crystallographic orientation: bnorm = norm(b)
4. Make a vector taking the absolute values of elements: bnorm,abs = abs(bnorm)
5. Sort the elements of vector bnorm,abs,sort = sortbnorm,abs from lowest to highest.
Now the crystallographic orientation b in direction a is given in families of
directions.
6. The angular deviation, θ, between bnorm,abs,sort and a certain orientation, cnorm
is found:
θ = cos−1
( bnorm,abs,sort · cnorm
∥bnorm,abs,sort∥∥bnorm∥
)
Step 4 is possible because of the cubic symmetry, meaning that the orientation (1,1,1)
has the same properties as (1,-1,1). With the procedure above, the angular deviations
will show how well defined a preferred orientation is, i.e. how big a deviation there
is from the preferred orientation in absolute numbers. This is especially relevant for
modelling stiffness in FEM.
2.5
Plastic deformation
While elastic deformation is in equilibrium with the load, plastic deformation happens
when the positions of atoms are changed. From energy considerations, work done on
plastic deformation is not stored as potential energy in the structure and is not freed
during unloading, meaning that the structure remains deformed when load is removed
again.
==PAGE:20==
10
2 Theory
2.5.1
Deformation by dislocation slip in FCC lattice
Copper atoms are positioned in a face centered cubic lattice, meaning there are close
packed planes. In these close packed planes, there are close packed directions, and
a close packed plane along with a close packed direction is called a slip system. For
FCC, plastic deformation is tied to dislocation slip, and dislocations slip in the slip
systems. The Burgers vector is always in the slip direction - the slip will be in a close
packed direction in a close packed plane. This means that the stress in a crystal has
to be evaluated in the direction of potential slip. For FCC there are 12 slip systems
(4 slip planes with each 3 slip directions), meaning that dislocation slip can happen
in more than one plane during loading. In order to evaluate what slip systems are
active, the resolved shear stress for each slip system is compared to a critical resolved
shear stress, which is the stress at which the dislocation slips.

2.5.3
Polycrystal plasticity
For Finite Element Modelling of material non-linearities by the forward Euler solver,
Sachs model is convenient because the plastic strain increment is found at every stress
increment. The Sachs model is simply the single crystal plasticity applied to all slip
system within every single grain. This means that every grain can have different
strains, but in the finite element model, this is used to affect the tangential stiffness
of every stress increment. For every element, the stiffness matrix is modified by the
amount of plastic strain increment in the previous stress increment, meaning that the
deformation of each grain may not take into account the overall strain, but at least
all grains are still coherent as elements. The implementation of this is described more
thoroughly later on.
2.6
Resistance to plastic deformation
The investigation of the microstructure and texture of the electrodeposited copper is
done to characterize mechanical properties and the behaviour of plastic deformation.
The elastic deformation is possible to test and to do a simulation of in the linear
domain only depending on grain orientation, but the plastic behaviour is affected by
other parameters. In the following, the most significant are considered.
Effects on plastic deformation
1. Crystallographic orientation (affects elastic properties too)
2. Dislocation density in the material
3. Amount of grain boundaries/grain size in the material
From microscopy and EBSD, it is possible to determine the grain size and the texture,
but also to see the number of twins and other grain boundaries. Nuance variations
within grains can give a qualitative impression of the amount of dislocations. Grain
boundaries and dislocations adds to the resistance to plastic deformation, for even
though plastic deformation is carried by dislocations in the FCC lattice of copper,
they also cluster and hinder dislocation motion. Grain boundaries also hinder dislo-
cation movement as slip stops when the lattice changes. This grain boundary effect

2. Grinding 4 minutes on diamond-600 plate. Force: 5 N.
3. Grinding 4 minutes on diamond-1200 plate. Force: 5 N.
4. Polishing 4 minutes on paper 1000. Force: 5 N.
5. Polishing 4 minutes on paper 4000. Force: 5 N.
6. Polishing 2 minutes on mol plate with 3 µm diamonds. Force: 5 N.
7. Washing 2 minutes on mol plate, no diamonds, running water. Force: 5 N.

Finite Element
Modelling
In order to model the effect of crystal orientation, a finite element program is de-
veloped. The scope of the finite element model is to predict displacements of the
structure and calculate stresses in the material.
The finite element model is implemented in FORTRAN, and is rooted in a classic
2D-FEM model for elastic, isotropic materials. The FORTRAN code is able to take
an input file from ANSYS, so that a mesh can be made. The FEM-model is developed
to be able to handle both anisotropy in elastic deformation and plastic deformation.
This contains both implementation of crystal orientation for the anisotropy, and an
incremental solver for the non-linear plastic deformation. Furthermore, the model
has to be developed for three dimensions in order to contain the correct strain energy
in the grains.

5.2.1
The stress state in polycrystaline, anisotopic materials
In isotropic materials, a beam of constant cross section in tension in the longitudinal
direction will have the same stress component in all positions of the cross section,
assuming that the tensile load is distributed equally over the cross section. In the
case of isotropic materials, there is no distinction between grains, and a normal load
will only invoke normal stress for equilibrium. For anisotropic single crystals, not
only normal stresses will appear, but still the whole cross section will have the same
stress components.
For polycrystalline, anisotropic materials, the stress state over the cross section is not
constant. If the cross section is composed by two crystals, one being stiffer in the
loading direction than the other, the stiffest crystal will take up more load than the
compliant crystal. This means that not all crystals are equally stressed. An analogy
can be made to composite fiber materials, where a stiff fiber material is embedded in
a compliant resin with fibres distributed evenly in the resin matrix. The elongation
in the fibre direction is constant over the cross section, but the stiff materials have a
much higher Young’s modulus and hence carry almost all the load.
For polycrystals, the difference is that the stiff and compliant ones are not distributed
evenly.
This means that if one side of the cross section is stiff and the other is
compliant, the beam will bend to the side of the stiffer crystal which is also the most
stressed crystal.

5.3
Anisotropic plasticity model
In contrast to the fully elastic model, a plastic model does not rely on a linear relation
between force and displacement. In order to describe a nonlinear behaviour, several
incremental solvers can be used, but to decrease computational needs, a simple Euler-
solver is used (described by Süli & Mayers in [SM03]).
5.3.1
Assumptions
Assumptions
1. The product of the elastic strain increment ˙ϵel and the stress increment are in
equilibrium with the force increment.
2. The load increment ˙p is constant both in magnitude and direction (geometrically

3. The stress increment is the product of the elastic material stiffness matrix and
the elastic strain increment and it is equal to the product of the tangential
material stiffness matrix and the total strain increment (Tvergaard, [Tve01]).
4. The stress evaluated in the center of each element is sufficiently accurate for
small elements.
5. The research done by Tvergaard and Hutchinson [TH99] (presented in the fol-

5.3.4
The issue of evaluating stress in centroids
As stated in the flow chart, the stress state of each element is based on the values
of strain in the center of the element. The tangential stiffness matrix is calculated
from this centroid stress. The stress is not homogeneous over the element volume,
and the stiffness of the whole element is decided by the centroid stress, meaning that
the described procedure is an approximation. For few elements, the approximation
is worse than for many. An expansion could be evaluating the stress in the Gauss
points, and then building the tangential stiffness matrix for every Gauss point for the
Gauss integration.
5.3.5
Limitations of model
The total plastic increment can be added up, and so can the rotation increment. The
problem with this model is that the visco plasticity is used to find the plastic strain,
but not to modify the element stiffness matrix. This has the consequence that the
deformation (which leads to the stress and in turn the strain increment) does not ac-
count for the visco-plastic effect. The method of course is useful in the linear domain,
and up to the first plastic increment. This means that the model can be used to pre-
dict what element reaches plasticity first, and the rotation increment ˙ω at initiation
of yielding can also be estimated. Figure 5.7 shows the force-displacement curve with
transition from elastic to plastic deformation, but is not used for further research, as
the connection between visco-plastic strain increments and element tangent stiffness
is not made.
Methods of linking the plastic strain increments to the tangential stiffness matrix have
been investigated, but no useful solutions were found. Figure 5.7 is focused on the
linear deformation and the transition to plastic deformation. The principle of virtual
work is not satisfied for large plastic strains, because the visco-plastic formulation is
not included in the manipulation of the tangential stiffness matrix, L.

Analysis of Tensile test and simulations
The tensile test gives a result that the modulus of elasticity is similar from the wire
with deposited layer to pre-stretched wire with no deposit. This indicates that the
measurements are not able to detect any differences in Young’s modulus between a
drawn wire and an electrodeposited wire. From the result, the linear pieces do not
show any difference in modulus of elasticity, and this modulus is lower than expected.
The lowest possible modulus of elasticity is in the ⟨1 0 0⟩direction, and this is found
to 67 GPa in simulations (Table 5.2). From the tensile test, the modulus appears to
be 70 GPa. This would mean that the crystallographic orientation in the drawing
direction is very close to ⟨1 0 0⟩everywhere in the cross section; but from EBSD, it is
known that this is not the case. Hence, there must be a softening effect, even when
the deformation seems linear elastic.
During the tensile test, the grips holding the ends deform the wire, and at fracture,
one end of the wire shows a ductile fracture right at the grip, meaning that this
part is exposed to larger plastic deformation than the rest.

plastic deformation, there may be a short region in each end of the wire, where
plastic deformation initiates early in the process, giving a lower force response for a
prescribed displacement. If for each end, the plastic strain rate ˙ϵpl

8.1
The electrodeposition process
As described in the experimental details, electrodeposition is done in two different
baths and using different initial current densities. Different results are seen in the
microstructure from various current densities, and the baths also give an overall
difference in the surface of the wire (the cross section circumference). The difference
in baths mean a difference in additives, which affects the growth of the deposited layer.
From LOM of wire 3, it appears that one side is different from the other. A reason for
this could be that several wires were in the bath, and despite distance between them,
one side had a different current than the other. The amount of deposited copper is
approximately the same all the way around, but the cross section is not symmetric
over all axes. The effect of the current density on the microstructure may be related
to a lack of symmetry in the test setup, if the current is not equally strong around
the bath.
The characteristics from the low current densities are not necessarily the same all
the way around either. For the specimen in the raw bath, the surface is rough, and
this roughness means that the current density or at least the deposition rate is not
the same everywhere. From the microstructure in different regions in the deposited
layer of wire 4 and 6 (an intermediate region between the striped/feathery region
and the substrate wire), the effect of changes in current density is seen. It looks like
for a certain current density (1.6 A/dm2 for wire 4, 1.3 1.3 A/dm2 for wire 6), the
arrangement of grains changes. Surprisingly, the microstructure in the inner part of
the deposited layer of wire 5 is not equivalent to that of wire 6. The striped/feathery
region starts right away in wire 5, even though the initial current density is supposed
to be the same for these wires. Assuming that the current density may only be slightly
different from what is stated by the producer, the microstructure is very sensitive to
changes in current density.
Development of microstructure from high to low current density
Looking at wire 2 (initially 6 A/dm2), the average current density at the outermost
of the deposited layer is well below those 2 A/dm2 that trigger the striped/feathery
region for wire 1 and 5. This suggests that not only the current density, but also the
substrate or previous deposited material may have an effect on the microstructure.

some grains have a change in nuance within the defined grain boundaries. A grain
boundary is a well defined change in lattice orientation, but the misorientation from
grain to grain is not necessarily large. Low angle boundaries have a misorientation
below 15o, due to dislocations lining up.
The variation of nuance within a grain
means that the lattice is slightly rotated over a distance. The rotation is a product of
lattice distortion/strain, coming from lattice defects. As there are no foreign phases
present, the distortion must come from either vacancies or dislocations. Vacancies
distort the lattice locally while dislocations add a small rotation. For edge dislocation,
the inserted half-plane adds a rotation, for screw-dislocation the screw itself gives a
rotation. The color gradient in each grain can be seen as an indication of lattice
defects, caused by high dislocation density.
These dislocations are strengthening.
Additional strength in the deposited layer can therefore be a product of not only
grain boundaries, but also dislocation density.
8.4
Hardness measurements; variations and patterns
From the hardness measurements it is very clear that the substrate and deposited layer
have different characteristics. It also becomes clear within each sample if any areas
have different characteristics than the rest. Generally, the hardness measurements
show the same hardness range for the substrate wire, but in one case, wire 5, there are
some higher values in the substrate wire than for the others. The absolute difference

is only of approximately 5 HV, but the instrument is used at the same settings, and so
there must be a reason for the variation. Three causes are discussed in the following:
• The effect of sample preparation and surface conditions.
• The effect of indent interaction.
• The effect of indent reading.
The relevance of the sample preparation and surface comes down to three factors;
local hardening by scratches, plane surface and clean surface. In order to minimize
the effects of surface hardening from scratches, a fine preparation is done. A planar
surface should be obtained in this process, and hardness measurements should be
taken before oxidation of the surface has an influence. For wire 1, after EBSD, some
hardness measurements in the substrate are excluded because the surface is rough and
gives disqualified geometries of the indents. Regarding the oxidation or impurities on
the surface, it should be visible and checked for.
The effect of indent interaction should not be present, because the standard ISO
6507-1 point 7.6 is followed, leaving more than a distance of 2.5 diameters between
indents. Even though the standard is used, the interaction could still be there on
a small scale; wire 5 is after all the sample with the indents closest to one another.
Because the DuraScan reads the indents more roughly than an operator, there may
be a tendency that the DuraScan readings are slightly lower than it would be for an
operator. In the case of wire 5, the indents are closer to each other than for the other
wires. They are so close that DuraScan does not separate them from each other at
the chosen magnification, and therefore many of the indents of wire 5 are read by
an operator. This fact may contribute to a slightly higher hardness average (5 HV),
which is seen for wire 5.
The difference from wire 5 to the others should only be a slightly higher hardness
average. The profile within wire 5 should still show the true overall tendency. When
comparing wire 5 and wire 6, Figure 6.45, the hardness difference is more than what
can be explained by reading differences, and since the preparation is the same, it
should be valid that wire 5 has a higher hardness in the deposited layer.

8.6
Finite element method for anisotropy
As stated in subsection 5.3.5, the plastic model developed for this investigation has
limitations after plastic deformation is initiated. For a simple structure with well
known orientations, the model could be of use in estimating what elements to yield
first, and what initial rotation would be seen. This is not investigated, because the
texture of the actual wire is complex. Instead, the elastic analysis is in focus.
The elastic anisotropy is developed because grain orientations affect the material
stiffness matrix, and the results from this is held up against the results of a tensile
test. The FEM-program can handle pseudo-3D problems, meaning that a mesh is
given in 2D, and then the problem is expanded in the out-of-plane direction. This is
necessary for full anisotropy. Throughout this investigation, there is only 1 element
in the out-of-plane direction. This is sufficient to simulate a tensile test (at least in
the elastic regime), but for more complex geometries, modifications must be done.
In this investigation, no computational improvements are done, which means that a

8.6.1
Centroid stress state and force equilibrium
In Figure 5.6 stress equilibrium is shown. For every column, the horizontal stress
integrated over the cross section area must be in equilibrium with the tensile force.
Equilibrium can only be shown by centroid stress for single crystal configurations,
because the stress within each element is not constant. Picturing two elements with
different stiffness in the tensile direction (Figure 7.1), the stress is different in the
two elements, but in the lower element, the stress is higher along the top edge, where
the element has the largest strain. As for the upper element, the stress is also higher
in the top, but the ratio between stresses in the upper and lower are not trivial,
and therefore equilibrium cannot be illustrated by centroid stresses for a polycrystal
material as it can for a single crystal in Figure 5.6.
Estimating the modulus of elasticity, 2 elements in y-direction
In estimating the modulus of elasticity for a beam in tension, the structure is in some
cases found too stiff for only 2 elements in the transverse (y) direction. The modulus
of elasticity estimated from FEM uses the horizontal end point displacement, and
there may be a energy stored in deformation contributing to displacements in other
directions. In order to get around the effect illustrated in Figure 7.1, three solutions
are considered.
1. Fixing some or all center line points in the transverse direction. This is not
done.
2. In order to estimate the modulus of elasticity, the total elongation of the center
line of the beam could be investigated. This is not done either.
3. Increasing the number of elements in the transverse direction.
The third of the above options is taken, but still the result does not match Reuss and
Voigt bounds - it even becomes stiffer for the random texture. Looking at a result
from literature, Hill [Hil52], finds Reuss and Voigt bounds that match the FEM results
better. An option here is that the numeric integration done in section 6.5 does not
correspond to the wanted distribution of orientations. Another possibility is that the
MATLAB function integral3 is insufficient for estimating the moduli.

8.7
Mounting for tensile tests
The fact that the stress-strain curves from tensile tests do not correspond to expected
moduli of elasticity are assumed to come from plastic strain in the mounting region,
see Figure 8.5. The mounting region is where grips are pushing onto the wire. The
wire must be held by friction force, and therefore the high normal force is applied. A
way around the plastic strain could be to deposit more copper in the ends where the
grips are used. This would higher the stress needed to induce plastic strain during
tension. Another option is to develop more appropriate grips. The grips used are
pushing from each side as shown in Figure 8.5, but if the grips were covering the
surface all around the wire in the mounting region instead, the squeezing effect would
be less pronounced. A way to obtain this could be to produce grips with a circular
hole as the grip-surface.
This hole should fit the wire diameter, and therefore it
becomes quite a project in itself. Another effect lowering the measured stiffness is the
fact that the displacement measured is the displacement of the upper part of the grips,
not accounting for elastic strain in the structure of the machine used for tensile test.
The thinner the wire, the less effect from this elasticity of the structure. A way around
errors from plastic regions and stiffness of structure is to mount a strain gauge on
the wire, enabling measurement of only the elastically strained region without effects
from structural stiffness or plastically strained gripping regions. Because the wire is
so thin, this is complicated and not done. The most obvious improvement would be
to find grips with a normal force distributed around the wire so lower stress would

8.8
Further work
On the electrodeposition
From the inverse pole figure results, a preferred direction in the deposited layer is seen.
The deposited layer is full of grain boundaries and have variations in microstructure
(stripes and feathery regions). In order to explore these differences further, more
wire should be produced, using other setups for electrodeposition.
The academic
bath with no additives is easy to control since there is no content of additives that
has to be constant. In the academic bath, the effect of the electric field could be
controlled by adding more anodes surrounding the cathode, distributing the electric
field equally around the cathode. An alternative could be rotating the cathode during
electrodeposition. In this way there should be symmetry around all axes; however, it
is possible that the rotation direction could affect the growth.
For all wires in this investigation, the current is constant. For growing radius, current
density decreases. It could be interesting to have a development of current correspond-
ing to the development of the area, meaning a constant current density. In that case
the microstructure could be defined specifically for various current densities, and the
amount of twins, grain size, hardness could be characterized. The expectations here
is that there would be different classes of microstructures appearing at certain current
densities, as seen for the feathery/striped and bright microstructures.
On the EBSD
For a better EBSD analysis, it is necessary to do a full scan of the deposited layer at
a high resolution. The resolution should be as high as possible (limited by the inter-
action volume), and this would take a week only in scanning time. The following data
treatment is computation and time consuming. In order to have less time consuming
analysis, the deposition should be on a smaller substrate wire. Such a substrate wire
could be obtained by deforming the drawn and recrystallized wire additionally by
drawing, followed by recrystallization, repeated until the wire is as thin as wanted.
If the final deformation is not followed by recrystallization, the strength of the wire
is high, and it is already straight, which is an advantage for the electrodeposition
as no stretching is needed to avoid curvature (Figure 4.1). For a thin substrate, the
total area scanned in EBSD is smaller for a deposited layer of a certain thickness,
than for a thick substrate with the same layer thickness. A thin substrate is less time
consuming in the scan.

On improving hardness resolution
A higher resolution in the hardness scans are definitely possible, but using DuraScan
by Struers, an operator will have to read many indents when using a lower load and
a higher magnification in microscopy reading of indents. A hardness map could be
made excluding the substrate, saving some work.
On the FEM-simulation
In order to compare the modulus of elasticity to measured grain orientations, the FEM
model should be made so that crystallographic orientations (or at least distributions
of orientations) could be loaded into the FEM. For high numbers of elements (>
1200), the computational time is a whole work day. The code can be optimized by
implementing the structural stiffness matrix K as a band matrix as explained by Süli
and Mayers in [SM03]. In that way the need for memory and computation decreases,
and simulation of a more realistic texture is possible.
Regarding the crystal plasticity model, a better link between plastic strain increments
and the manipulation of material tangent stiffness matrix L is needed for this to be
reliable.
On the tensile tests
As already mentioned, better grips are needed to avoid effects of plasticity in the ends.
Further more, digital image correlation could be used to see the elastic strains, to
eleminate effects from plastic strain and structure stiffness of the tensile test machine.
As the simulations show, the variation may be within a couple of GPa for differences
in texture.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
The conclusion of this investigation is that the electrodeposited layers appear in 4
different microstructures depending on the initial current densities and other elec-
trodeposition parameters. The link between microstructure and plastic properties of
the deposited material are found in hardness maps.
• Very fine grained structure seen for high initial current density wires. Hardest
deposited microstructure seen (210-250 HV0.02).
• More coarse grained than the above structure seen for outer parts of high ini-
tial current density wires. 2nd hardest deposited microstructure seen (130-150
HV0.02).
• The feathery/striped regions rich on twins seen for low current densities (some-
times occurring for a special current density). 3rd hardest deposited microstruc-
ture seen (100-150 HV0.02).
• The intermediate region (between substrate and feathery/striped), softer than
the feathery/striped, but formed at a higher current density. 4th hardest de-
posited microstructure (100-110 HV0.02).
Light optical microscopy also reveals that the transitions from one microstructure to
another occurs with some sort of symmetry. For the lower current densities (wire 1,
4, 5, 6) transitions occur rather sharply all around the wire approximately at same
radius, while for higher current densities the microstructure changes at different radial
distances to the substrate around the wire. When shifting from the intermediate to
the feathery/striped, the change is persistent, while for the higher current densities,
the microstructure can shift from the fine grained to the more coarse grained and
back. The feathery/striped microstructure could be considered as two different, but
they appear together and are considered one in this investigation.
Texture is investigated for low initial current densities, finding that there is a preferred
orientation in the growth (radial) direction. This preferred orientation is ⟨1 1 0⟩, and
is linked to the striped region. From grain boundary analysis (using MTEX Toolbox)
twins are seen in abundance in the deposited layer for a high resolution. Twins are also
seen in the substrate. Hardness is not linked to texture but more to the appearance
of grain boundaries when a higher hardness is determined in the striped area than in
the feathery areas.
==PAGE:116==
106
9 Conclusion
Tensile tests suggest a modulus of elasticity of 71 GPa both for the substrate wire
and for a wire with deposited layer, and local plastic strain is assumed to be the main
reason for such low values. Using the preferred orientation in a simplified wire model
in FEM, the modulus of elasticity is estimated to 123-126 GPa, and it is concluded
that changes in texture only has a small effect. Looking at the data from EBSD,
it is reasonable that no difference is seen. Even though there is a preferred radial
orientation, there is still a large presence of other orientations in the tensile direction.
From literature it is suggested that hardness increase and grain size decrease with
increasing current density in the same range as for this investigation. However this
investigation shows that hardness can increase with decreasing current density when
microstructure changes.
