Abstract 
The advantages of peptide therapeutics are its high selectivity, potency and low toxicity. 
Liposomes have emerged as a prominent delivery system for peptides due to their low toxicity, 
biocompatibility and biodegradability. However, the reported cellular permeability for peptide 
drugs is rather poor for most peptides due to a high polar surface area and many charged 
hydrophilic moieties. Peptide cyclization can reportedly in some instances increase the 
permeability of cellular bilayers. A solution for efficient delivery of peptide drugs could be in 
the form of liposomes. Some of the challenges of peptide loaded liposome therapeutics are 
poor bioavailability, short in vivo half-life, chemical and metabolic instability, and low loading 
efficiency which reduces the therapeutic outcome. Therefore, this project focuses on 
establishing efficient methods for loading Endomorphin 1 (Endo1), its analog and Veber 
Hirschmann peptide into liposomes with high loading efficiencies. The project will start with 
synthesizing and characterising liposomes and then remote loading Endo1 using ammonium 
sulphate gradient. After achieving optimal encapsulation efficiency for Endo1, Endo-1 analog 
and cyclic peptide, Veber Hirschmann, will be remote loaded with the same method. The 
factors explored during remote loading experiments are remote loading time, remote loading 
temperature, D/L ratio and number of times dialysis is done. The remote loaded peptides 
yielded a 63% encapsulation efficiency for endomorphin-1, 37% for End-R analog with 
arginine and 28% for the cyclic peptide. Stability tests were done for liposomes and a remote 
loaded sample by analysing size, PDI and concentration over a period of 8-12 weeks. Analysis 
was done using RP-UHPLC with ELSD and DLS.

1. Introduction 
The development of peptide drugs has made a great progress over the last decade due to 
improvement in the production processes and analytic technologies. There is a continuous 
advancement of the field because of the high interest and demand of peptide-based 
therapeutics. There are various therapeutic applications of peptide and  the most used peptide 
drugs are for type 2 diabetes, obesity, acromegal, multiple sclerosis, osteoporosis, breast and 
prostate cancer. [1], [2] Peptide drugs are popular for their low toxicity, high potency and 
selectivity, and for broader range of targets. The main disadvantage of peptides as therapeutics 
are their limited oral bioavailability, short half-life, low metabolic stability and poor membrane 
permeability.[2], [3]  
Some of the problems can be overcome by synthesizing a cyclic peptide as they have greater 
metabolic stability, higher selectivity and improved membrane permeability. [4] Novel drug 
delivery systems for the peptides have helped to overcome the drawbacks of therapeutic 
peptides and one such system is liposomes, which consist of encapsulating drug into the 
liposome. Compared to traditional drug delivery system liposomes have controlled release of 
the drug, have targeted delivery, protects the drug from degradation and clearance. [2] 
To date, most of the peptide drugs are administered parenterally. Injections are the most 
common route of administration which causes the patients pain and discomfort. The oral route 
is more preferrable because of its high level of patient compliance. The liposome system has a 
great potential of overcoming this challenge and improving the oral bioavailability of peptide 
therapeutics. However, there are several hindrances in loading peptides into liposomes such as 
low encapsulation efficiency (EE), low drug-to-lipid (D/L) ratio, non-uniform and unstable 
drug loading, which increase the production costs and reduce the therapeutic outcome. 
There is a need to make a stable and efficient peptide delivery system by establishing an 
optimized method of loading therapeutic peptides into liposomes to achieve maximum EE%. 
Remote loading (RL) can be used to load peptides into liposomes. There are several factors 
such as RL temperature, RL time duration, and drug-to-lipid ratio that can be optimized to 
achieve a maximum EE%. In this project the liposomal delivery system is optimized for a linear 
peptide Endomorphin-1 and a cyclic peptide Veber Hirschmann. Both peptides are synthesized 
and characterized by MSc. student Emil Skovgaard Kristensen. This leads to the following 
project aims: 
• Synthesize and characterize stealth HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/cholesterol liposomes 
as a delivery system for Endomorphin-1 peptide. 
• Synthesize and characterize a liposome formulation as delivery system for cyclic 
peptide Veber Hirschmann. 
• Develop and validate the analytical methods used for lipid and remote loaded 
liposomes quantification. 
• Conduct experiments to evaluate the effects of RL time duration, temperatures and 
drug-to-lipid ratio for Endomorphin-1 and Veber Hirschmann on the EE%

2. Peptides as therapeutics 
Short peptides are more efficient therapeutical agents with reduced side effects. Therapeutic 
peptides act by binding to specific cell surface receptors, where they trigger intracellular 
pathways.[3] Their structural diversity combined with the conformational flexibility can be 
used to control interactions with particular receptor sites. The advantage of short peptides 
compared to their larger analogues is that they have cost-effective synthesis, easy modification 
and structural optimization, high bio-activity, absorbability, high selectivity and specificity, 
biodegradability and biocompatibility. They also have low toxicity due to their safe 
metabolites-amino acids so there is limited possibility for accumulation in the body. Peptides 
have diverse bio-functionalities of their amino acids and good biomolecular recognition. As a 
consequence, they have high binding affinity for a wide range of specific targets.[5] 
Peptides also have limitations, such as high conformational flexibility which can lead to poor 
target specificity. Due to strong interaction between peptide back bone and water molecules, 
peptides have poor transport properties through biologic membranes. One of the most 
important downsides of peptides to consider is the short half-life in vivo. It is because the 
peptides are rapidly degraded by peptidases in the gastrointestinal tract. This leads to fast 
clearance from the first-pass metabolism by the liver and kidneys. Due to this, peptides have 
low oral bioavailability and rapid excretion. Therefore, it is important to find an effective 
method of delivery for peptides. Development of orally active preparations have been 
experimented by encapsulation of peptides in nanoparticles such as liposomes and synthetic 
polymers, which shields the drug from protease digestion until required, therefore increasing 
stability. [3], [5] 
Structural modifications in peptides can improve the physicochemical properties and simple 
modifications can lead to greater general stability. Variations in the sequence length and side 
chains, peptide backbone modification, C-terminal amidation, N-terminal acetylation, addition 
of stabilizing and chelating agents such as polyethylene glycol (PEG), or fatty acids can be 
applied. In this way, we can stabilize peptides in their bioactive conformation, increase 
efficiency, reduce kidney clearance, improve membrane permeability and target selectivity. [3], 
[5] It is important to have understanding of the possible modifications for peptides but it is not 
the main focus of this project. In this project the linear short peptides used are endogenous µ-
opioid receptor modulator endomorphin-1 and two analogs of this, End-K and End-R, with 
altered peptide sequences. These peptides were synthesiszed by Emil Skovgaard Kristensen.

2.1. Solid-phase peptide synthesis 
Solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) is a common technique used for peptide synthesis. In 
SPPS peptides are synthesised from the carbonyl group side (C-terminus) to amino group side 
(N-terminus) of the amino acid chain, which is the opposite direction of peptides synthesized 
biologically in cells. The method is flexible as there are many different coupling agents, 
protecting groups and resins to select from. The success of SPPS depends on the choice of the 
solid support, linker, appropriately protected amino acids, coupling methodology, and protocol 
for cleaving the peptide from the solid support. [7], [8] 
In Figure 4, in the first step of SPPS, the protected amino acid is bound by a linker to 
the insoluble polymeric resin which is the solid support. Then the amino group is deprotected 
using a base such as piperidine and reacted with the carbonyl group of the next N-protected 
amino acid. This cycle is repeated until the required peptide chain is achieved. The coupling 
step is done through an activation of the carboxyl of amino acids using various coupling 
reagents, often derived from 1-hydroxybenzotriazole (HOBt). After the final deprotection, the 
synthesised peptide is cleaved from the resin.[7], [8] 
 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
 
 
 
Figure 4: Scheme of solid phase peptide synthesis[7] 
In recent SPPS procedure, amino acids with amino groups protected by 9-
fluorenylmethoxycarbonyl (Fmoc) group are used. This protecting group allows for better 
control of the synthesis, as it caps the peptide chain when the amino acid has been attached, 
only allowing elongation once deprotected. The use of Fmoc allows for milder conditions as 
this group is base labile and can be removed using piperidine. In order to cleave the peptide, 
TFA is used. For this thesis, all amino acids used for synthesis of linear peptides were Fmoc-
protected.[7], [8] 
2.2. Peptide cyclisation  
Linear peptides can also be cyclised as there are several advantages of cyclic peptides. Cyclic 
peptides reduce peptide backbone flexibility and this limits availability for enzymes. It is also 
a way to lock the active conformation of the peptide. Cyclic peptides have better chemical and 
enzymatic stability, pharmacodynamic properties and receptor selectively in comparison to 
linear peptides. Examples of some cyclic peptides are insulin, penicillin and cyclosporin. 
Synthesis of cyclic peptides is a step toward orally administered peptides.[5] For this project a 
cyclic peptide has been synthesized by Emil Skovgaard Kristensen and the structure can be 
seen in Figure 5. This cyclic peptide will be used to remote load it in liposome and analyse the 
drug loading possibilities of a cyclic peptide.

3. Liposomes 
Liposomes are nano-sized drug delivery systems composed of phospholipids that form a 
spherical structure. They consist of amphiphilic phospholipids with hydrophilic head and 
hydrophobic tail and this aids liposomes in self-assembly in aqueous media. Liposomes in the 
size range of 50-200nm are used for encapsulations of hydrophobic or hydrophilic drugs into 
the aqueous phase or the bilayer of the membrane for drug delivery applications.[11] 
The advantages of using liposomes for drug delivery are prolonged drug half-life, targeted 
delivery, biocompatibility, biodegradability, low immunogenicity and systematic toxicity, 
protection of drug and enhanced pharmacokinetics.[11] 
Liposomes are classified based on size, liposomal composition and number of bilayers. Some 
of the examples of liposomes can be seen in Figure 6. These factors impact the loading capacity 
of the liposome. Liposome size plays an important role in efficient drug delivery in the body 
and have significant effect on the pharmacokinetics of liposomal drug. Some types of 
liposomes based on size and number of lamellae are given in Table 1.[11]

Liposomes smaller than 200nm have increased circulation, residence time in the blood and 
enhanced in vivo drug release from liposomes. So, for drug delivery SUVs and LUVs are 
mostly relevant. 
Phospholipids are classified as phosphatidylcholine, phosphatidylinositol, phosphatidylserine, 
phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidic acid, and phosphatidylglycerol which is because of 
substitution at head group.[12],[11] The variation in length of the nonpolar moieties produces 
different phospholipids. The type of bonding also forms different types of phospholipids such 
as ether or ester between glycerol and aliphatic chains will form different phospholipids. To 
achieve charged vesicles, charged phospholipids like stearyl amine can been used. [11]

Phospholipids alone form liposomes with defects in the bilayer packing leading to release of 
captured drug due to unsaturation of fatty alkyl chains or low phase transition temperature 
during storage. To avoid the defects various other bilayer excipients are added to the liposome 
composition such as cholesterol, one of the most widely used excipient. The aggregation of 
liposomes because of electrostatic effect and the fluidity of phospholipid bilayers can be 
stabilized by adding cholesterol. Changes in the phospholipid bilayer composition also 
influences the EE%. The lipid type and cholesterol affect the thickness of the lipid bilayer 
which affects the drug release kinetics. There are various liposomal modifications that vary 
depending on the type of drug and target, an overview of the modification is seen in Figure 7. 
 
 
Figure 7: Overview of liposomal modifications and drug incorporations[13] 
Theranostic liposome consist of a nanoparticle, a targeting element, an imaging component, 
and a therapeutic component. Theranostic nanoparticles has gained interest as it is both a 
therapeutic and diagnostic tool all-in-one.  
Polyethylene glycol (PEG) on the liposome surface offers longer circulation time, protects the 
captured drug from metabolic degradation, enhances stability and improves intracellular intake. 
PEG also helps in decreasing aggregation of liposomes and improves the stability on storage. 
Cellular intake of PEGylated liposomes are enhanced by ligands such as antibodies, vitamins, 
proteins and nucleic acids on the receptor surface. 
3.1. Liposome Preparation  
There are various conventional and novel methods to prepare liposomes. In the following 
chapter a few of the most used methods are shortly described. 
3.1.1. Thin Film-Hydration Method  
The thin film hydration method is the most used method for liposome preparation and is 
beneficial for loading the lipophilic drug. In this method, an organic solvent such as 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
 
dichloromethane, ethanol, chloroform, or chloroform–methanol mixture is used to dissolve 
lipids. Then a thin film is created by evaporating the organic solvent under vacuum. MLVs 
suspension is formed by adding the aqueous media to hydrate the lipid film. The liposome size 
is reduced to obtain homogenised SUVs in which the drug can be passively or actively loaded. 
Thin film hydration method can be used for all kind of lipid mixtures and is a simple and 
straightforward process. The downsides are that the EE% is low for hydrophilic drugs, and 
there are difficulties in scaling up the method, as it is challenging to remove organic solvent, 
sterilization is required, and size reduction needs to be done. [11], [14], [15] 
3.1.2. Solvent Injection Techniques 
In this technique, lipids are dissolved in a water-miscible organic solvent such as diethyl ether, 
ether-methanol mixture or ethanol, and then the organic phase is injected into aqueous buffer, 
where the material is encapsulated at 55–65ºC or under reduced pressure to form liposomes. 
At the end small unilamellar liposomes are formed spontaneously. The organic solvent is 
removed by evaporation, dialysis, or diafiltration. The liposomes can be concentrated to the 
needed volume. Ethanol is commonly used as an organic solvent. This method is simple, fast, 
and reproducible. However, improper mixing may produce heterogeneous liposomes. It is also 
challenging to remove ethanol because it forms azeotrope with water. Biologics or peptide may 
become inactive in presence of ethanol. [11], [14], [15] 
3.1.3. Reverse Phase Evaporation Method 
In this method lipids are dissolved in an organic solvent and desired drugs are dissolved in an 
aqueous media; further the mixture is sonicated to produce w/o emulsion or inverted micelles, 
followed by slow removal of organic solvent using rotary evaporator, leading to conversion of 
these micelles into viscous state or gel product. In this process, at a critical point, the gel state 
collapses, and some inverted micelles were distributed. The excess phospholipids form a 
bilayer around the residual micelles and this results in the formation of liposomes. It is a simple 
process and is used to encapsulate small, large and macromolecules. This process is not suitable 
method for fragile molecules such as peptides. It uses a large quantity of organic solvent and 
removal of organic solvent is also challenging.[11], [15] 
3.1.4. Freeze Drying Method 
Freeze drying is one of the novel methods. In freeze drying method lipid and drug are dissolved 
in tert-butyl alcohol at 45ºC, and lyo-protectant dissolved in water at 45ºC. Both solutions are 
mixed to form third identical monophasic solution. Then it is followed by filtration, and freeze 
drying and hydration of the solution to produce liposomes. This technique is used for 
commercial scale production. It also increases the shelf-life of the liposomes by preventing the 
physical degradation of liposomes during storage. However, it can induce physical alterations 
to size and/or structure of liposomes.[11], [15] 
3.1.5. Size Reduction Techniques 
Size is a critical factor for the performance of liposomes and once the liposome has been made 
using the methods mentioned above, it is important to homogenize and reduce the liposome 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
 
size to a required size. Extrusion and high-pressure homogenization (HPH) are the most 
popular size reduction techniques used in pharmaceutical manufacturing. [14] 
In extrusion, liposomes of large sizes pass through the polycarbonate membranes to become  
smaller liposomes with a fine size distribution. This method is reproducible, and is done at 
moderate conditions. During size reduction the MLVs are ruptured at the entrance of the 
membrane pore and are rearranged during the membrane passage. The critical process 
parameters, such as the pore size of the polycarbonate membrane, the number of passage 
cycles, pressure, and flow rate, can influence the size and liposomal lamellarity. [14] 
In HPH the MLVs suspension is passed through a narrow gap under high pressure, broken 
down by means of shear force, turbulence, and cavitation of fluid generated by the velocity 
gradient, and then rearranged into smaller liposomes. The parameters of the homogenization 
process determine particle size and size distribution. The parameters are pressure, process 
cycles, and flow rate. Particle size and size distribution is also affected by  the composition and 
viscosity of the bulk medium and initial size distribution of particles. Increasing the pressure 
and process cycles, decrease the particle size and polydispersity index (PDI), but also result in 
a decrease in the EE%.[14]

4. Liposome Formulation Parameters 
The important parameters are any physical, chemical, biological, or microbiological properties 
or characteristics that affect the liposomes pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic 
performance. The parameters that affect pharmacokinetics and the properties of liposomes are 
particle size and size distribution, morphology, lamellar structure, surface properties, zeta 
potential, PEGlated thickness, and targeting molecules, such as a ligand, the phase transition 
temperature of the lipid membrane, drug loading efficiency and release rate. There are many 
factors and in this part few of them will be discussed.[14] 
4.1. Particle size and size distribution 
The whole pharmacokinetic process of liposomes, such as systemic circulation and the 
clearance of drugs, the extravasation into tissue interstitium, and cellular uptake and 
intracellular trafficking, are size dependant processes. The particles with size less than 200 nm 
decrease the opsonization by serum proteins and clearance of mononuclear phagocyte system. 
Smaller liposomes have higher anti-tumor efficacy. Niu et al. study shows that for 150 nm and 
400 nm insulin-loaded liposomes for oral administration exhibited slower and prolonged action 
up to 24 h, whereas liposomes with a particle size of about 80 nm and 2 µm had a transient and 
no pharmacological effect. [14] 
4.2. Surface modification 
One of the important modifications for liposome is adding flexible PEG chains to create a 
hydration layer, which reduces the clearance by mononuclear phagocyte system, prolongs the 
circulation lifetime, and prevents aggregation of liposomes. Using ligands for active targeting 
is a common surface modification of liposomes. The coating thickness of nanomaterial is 
described in the dossier because the coverage density and thickness of the layer affects cellular 
uptake and control nanoparticle transport through biological matrices.[14] 
4.3. Phase transition temperature 
Hydrated phospholipid membranes change between different phases, characterized by different 
positions and orientation. The two extreme phases of bilayer’s phospholipids are the gel phase 
also known as solid ordered phase and fluid phases. The gel phase is characterized by the 
formation of a very compact bilayer with a minimum mobility since hydrocarbon chains 
display an all-trans configuration favoring their maximum elongation Figure 8.[16] 
==PAGE:17==
 
16 
 
 
Figure 8: Phase transition temperature diagram[16] 
Above phase transition temperature (Tm), the lipid bilayer undergoes a gel–liquid transition, the 
lipid chains change configuration and have less compact structure. Depending on the chemical 
structure such as length of the acyl chains, type and ionic force of the dispersion medium, 
different lipids will have different Tm.[16] 
The length and degree of unsaturation of the acyl chains have the most significant effect. The 
longer the length of the acyl chains or the lower the degree of unsaturation, the higher the Tm of 
the phospholipid. Moreover, the incorporation of cholesterol in the bilayers causes a 
broadening, or elimination, of the Tm because it exerts an ordering effect on the liquid phase 
that assumes physical characteristics in common with the solid-ordered gel phase. In fact, the 
bilayer configuration in presence of cholesterol is defined liquid-ordered phase. [16] 
A higher Tm than physiological temperature of 37 °C is required of lamellar. Therefore, the rate 
of drug molecules passing through the gel state of the membrane is slow. The burst release and 
drug leakage from liposomes in vivo can be better prevented in order to reduce the risk of 
systemic toxicity.[14] 
4.4. Hydrophilic-Lipophilic balance (HLB) 
HLB is an indication of the solubility of the surfactant molecule. It presents the balance 
between the hydrophilic portion to the lipophilic portion of the non-ionic surfactant. The HLB 
range is from 0 to 20 for non-ionic surfactants. The lower HLB refers to more lipophilic 
surfactant and the higher HLB to more hydrophilic surfactant. For preparation of vesicle a 
surfactant with a HLB between 4-8 can be used. Hydrophilic surfactants with a HLB value 
ranging from 14 to 17 are not suitable to form a bilayer membrane due to their high aqueous 
solubility.[17] 
The surfactant effects the size, charge, stability, entrapment efficiency, pharmacokinetics, and 
pharmacodynamics of the lipid vesicular systems. Increase in the surfactant concentration, 
carbon chain length, the hydrophilicity of the surfactant head group, and the hydrophilic-
lipophilic balance decreases the size vesicles. The stability of the system can be enhanced by 
==PAGE:18==
 
17 
 
increasing surfactant concentration which leads to an increase in charge, and consequently 
reduces vesicle aggregation. The EE% depends on the surfactant properties and the 
encapsulated drug. Encapsulation of a lipophilic drug can be enhanced by using a surfactant 
with a low hydrophilic-lipophilic balance value. The membrane permeability of vesicles 
mainly depends on the surfactant's carbon chain length and transition temperature. In addition, 
surfactants have a clear influence on pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics such as 
sustaining drug release, enhancing the circulation time of vesicles, improving targeting and 
cellular uptake.[18] 
4.5. Critical packing parameter (CPP) 
Phospholipids tend to aggregate spontaneously when they are dispersed in water, to form 
bilayers. The shape and size of the formed micellar aggregates depend on the molecular 
geometry of its component surfactant molecules and the solution conditions such as 
surfactant concentration, pH, ionic strength, and temperature. There is a possibility to develop 
and manipulate nanostructures architecture because of the control over the shapes of 
liposomes. Estimates of shape and size can be made with the analysis of the critical packing 
factor (Cpp), which are shown in Figure 9. 
For packing parameters larger than one (Cpp > 1) inverse micelles can form. In this case, 
hydrophobic chains radiate away from centrally aggregated headgroups that surround the 
water solvent.[19] The value of CPP allows to predict the type of self-assembled structure 
and molecular interactions, which play a significant role in physiological and pathological 
conditions.[20]

5. Drug Loading 
5.1. Loading strategies 
High drug loading is desirable to minimize the amount of excipient used, reach the desired 
concentration of therapeutic agents, decrease the dose volume, and reduce dosing time. Two 
primary techniques are routinely used for drug loading; passive and active drug loading.[14] 
5.1.1.  Passive Drug-Loading Approach  
The passive drug-loading method involves encapsulating the drug during the preparation of 
liposomes by dispersing lipid and drug in an aqueous buffer. The method is shown in Figure 
10. Then the drug is entrapped within the inner aqueous space or embedded in the bilayer of 
liposomes by means of covalent, ionic, electrostatic, non-covalent, or steric interactions 
between drug molecules and lipids. [14], [22] The hydrophilic drug is encapsulated in the 
aqueous core of liposome and the hydrophobic drugs are entrapped in the small-sized 
hydrophobic lipid bilayer. [12], [23] 
 
Figure 10:Passive encapsulation method[23] 
There are variable factors that affect the trapping efficiency of passive loading such as drug 
solubility, vesicle size, lipid concentration, and preparation procedure. In general, the 
encapsulating efficiency of the passive loading is low, and it has a rapid drug release. A large 
amount of drug is released in burst form instead of controlled release, which is preferred. 
Passive loaded drugs are not stable due to weak association between drugs and the liposome 
which leads to poor drug retention and storage stability.[12], [14], [23] 
5.1.2. Active Drug-Loading Approach  
In active loading approach, also called remote loading (RL), the drug is incorporated after the 
liposome preparation. The transmembrane gradient of pH or ion concentration is the driving 
force to promote the drug diffuse across the membrane into the inner core of liposomes, 
presented in Figure 11. During remote loading, drug diffuses through the lipid bilayer into the 
liposome resulting in a stable drug complex. The chemical modification that happens inside 
prevents the removal of drug from the membrane. The drug-entrapment process has a high 
loading efficiency of more than 90% but it cannot be used for all weak acid or base drugs.. 
[11], [12], [14], [22]

6. Materials and Methods 
6.1. Materials and instruments 
All the chemicals, solvents and materials were purchased from commercial sources. Milli-Q 
water was tapped from a Milli-Q purification system from NOION AQUA (Magliaso, 
Switzerland). Ammonium sulphate and Phosphate buffered saline (PBS) were from Sigma-
Aldrich (St. Louis, USA). Lipoid HSPC/MPEG-2000- DSPE/Cholesterol (3:1:1) stock and 
cholesterol (ovine) stock were both from Avanti Polar Lipids (Alabaster, USA). Dialysis 
tubing, high retention seamless cellulose tubing MWCO 12400 99.99% retention from SIGMA 
ALDRICH (USA) was used. 
High-pressure extruder connected to a Lauda Ecoline E100 heating system from Gemini 
(Apeldoorn, Netherlands) was used. Drain discs and membrane filters for high-pressure 
extrusion were from Whatman (Freiburg, Germany). 
Analytical RP-UHPLC ELSD was performed on Shimadzu Nexera LC-2040C system (Tokyo, 
Japan) coupled to a SEDEX-100 ELSD detector. Heptafluorobutyric acid (HFBA) used was 
from SIGMA-ALDRICH (St. Louis, USA). Solvent A used for UHPLC was 50ml MeCN, 
20mM HFBA in MQ and solvent B is 20mM HFBA in MeCN. As for the column, XTERRA 
RP8 5microm was used. 
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) measurements were performed using Malvern Zetasizer Nano 
ZS (Malvern, UK). For Zeta poetential HEPES 10mM and 5% Glucose solution was used. For 
size PBS was used for dilution. 
6.2. Methods 
The following methods were used for the characterization of liposomes and quantification of 
encapsulated drug. Synthesis of peptide was done by Emil Skovgaard Kristensen and will 
therefore not be included in the following part. 
6.2.1. Liposome synthesis and characterization 
The lipoid HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/Cholesterol with (3:1:1) mass ratio was used which is 
used for Doxil™ and is known for its stealth properties. To make liposomes, stealth mix is 
made by mixing mass of HSPC, MPEG-2000-DSPE and Cholesterol with mass ratio of 3:1:1. 
In this case an already prepared stealth mix from December 2011 was used. A 40mM liposome 
is prepared in 250mM ammonium sulfate solution (pH 5.2) at 70°C and is stirred for 1hr. Then 
high-pressure extrusion was performed at 70 °C to get the desired size and PDI of liposome. 
First it was extruded through 3x800nm, then 3x400nm and at last 3x100nm filters. To establish 
a gradient for RL, the liposomes were dialysed overnight in 500ml of 10mM PBS solution (pH 
7.6) with continuous stirring at room temperature. 
The liposomes were characterized with respect to size, PDI, and zeta potential with DLS. The 
analytical RP-UHPLC-ELSD method was also used to determine the lipid concentration after 
high pressure extrusion as there is lipid loss during extrusion process and for accurate remote 
loading exact liposome concentration is required. For RP-UHPLC-ELSD analysis the liposome 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
 
samples were diluted 100-fold to prevent overloading of the ELSD detector. The liposome 
concentration was measured before each batch of remote loaded experiments and the RL 
calculations were done each time to minimise errors. Over the period of few weeks multiple 
size, PDI and concentration values were taken to do a stability test of the liposomes. 
6.2.2. Lipid quantification 
RP-UHPLC-ELSD was used for the quantification of the lipids in the stealth mix. A standard 
curve from dilution series of the lipid stealth mix was made and the linearity was assessed. For 
the standard curve the area under the curve (AUC) was plotted as the function of the known 
standard concentrations. Linear regression was applied to obtain the equation of the straight 
line and regression correlation coefficient (𝑅2). Triplicate measurements were taken from 
UHPLC, and the data is presented as mean ±standard deviation.  
6.2.3. Remote loading 
For remote loading, the mass of the target drug was calculated based on the D/L molar ratio 
desired. The exact mass of the drug was weighed and added into a 10mM PBS solution. The 
drug solution and the liposomes with ammonium sulfate-PBS gradient that were dialyzed were 
heated at remote loaded temperature. After heating they were combined, and the mixture was 
stirred at the remote loaded temperature for a specific RL hour. Then it was refrigerated at -
4°C to stop the loading process and to cool the solution for the dialysis process. Then it was 
left overnight for dialysis in 10mM 500ml PBS solution at 4°C to eliminate the free non-loaded 
compounds. Lastly the loaded liposomes were characterized with respect to size, PDI and zeta 
potential at RT with DLS. The quantification of the loaded drug in liposomes was done by 
running samples in RP-HPLS-ELSD. The samples were diluted 50-fold to run in UHPLC to 
avoid overloading of the detectors. The respective standard curve of the drug and liposome is 
used to know the concentration of the liposome and the drug in the loaded liposome. For remote 
loading of Endomorphin-1 at optimal condition, the D/L ratio was 1:20 molar ratio, RL 
temperature was 65°C and it was remote loaded for 3hrs. Control loading were obtained by 
doing the experiment with same RL condition but without gradient. The first step of dialysing 
liposome, which creates the gradient is not done and rest of the steps of loading are the same. 
Triplicate experiments were done at the optimal conditions for cyclic peptide only and rest of 
the experiments were only done once because of lack of time and material. 
6.2.4. Quantification and encapsulation efficiency 
RP-UHPLC method was used for quantification of EE%. Linear regression was used to obtain 
the EE% based on the standard curves for the respective peptides. 
𝐸𝐸% = (𝐶𝛼/𝐶𝛽) · 100 
The EE% was calculated using the equation above, where 𝐶𝛼 is the concentration of drug 
encapsulated in the liposomes (mg/mL) and 𝐶𝛽 is the total concentration of drug in the RL 
mixture.

7. Results and discussion 
In this section the results obtained will be analyzed and discussed considering the hypothesis, 
problem formulation, and the literature. 
7.1. Liposome characterization 
The properties and physical stability of the synthesized liposomes were evaluated by analyzing 
their size, PDI and the concentration of the liposomes over time. The physical instability of the 
liposomes was evaluated by analyzing the size of the liposomes, if the size increased over few 
weeks as a result of aggregation then the liposomes were unstable. Unstable liposomes have 
shorter in vivo half-life and lower therapeutic efficacy of the drug. Replicate Zeta potential data 
for HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/Cholesterol was 10.8±1.4mV and for HSPC/MPEG-2000-
DSPE it was 6.8±2.0mV. In general this zeta potential means that liposomes will have limited 
flocculation.[25] 
The stability of liposomal formulation was analyzed over a few weeks during which liposomes 
were stored at 4°C. For HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE/Cholesterol stealth mix liposomal 
formulation an average liposome size was 132.58±1.30nm with PDI of 0.034±0.018. With no 
significant changes over 12 weeks as seen in Figure 19 and Figure 20, respectively which 
means that the liposome is stable.. More readings should have been taken in between week 1 
to 8 for more accuracy of liposome size, PDI and liposome concentration data. Some of the 
data from DLS and UHPLC for stability of liposmes is given in Appendix 1.

not significant and 150nm size is comparatively ideal for a therapeutic drug than 400nm. The 
experiment was only done once so for more concrete conclusion triplicates should be done.

7.3. Encapsulation Efficiency of Vhpep-cyc 
Experiments were performed to remote load the cyclic peptide Veber-Hirschmann peptide to 
analyse the effects of cyclization on a peptides ability to remote load. Cyclic peptides should 
have better permeability through cellular membranes due their restrained structure. But this 
does not necessarily guarantee high permeability or EE% because cellular membrane is more 
complex system then the liposomes. In the first attempt the same remote loading conditions 
were used as for End-1 with stealth liposome HSPC/DSPE-mPEG2000/Cholesterol (3:1:1) and 
only the D:L ratios were varied to find the optimal D:L ratio for cyclic peptide but no 
encapsulation was seen with RP-UHPLC data. Another explanation of no remote loading might 
be cholesterol in the liposome formulation. There is likely not enough gap in the bilayer for a 
relatively big peptide Vhpep-cyc to pass through. So remote loading was tried with 
HSPC/DSPE-mPEG2000 (3:1) liposome formulation with D:L ratio as a variable and the

8. Conclusion 
Experiments were done with different remote loading factors as variables. For some there was 
significant result such as RL time and for some the results were inconclusive and more data 
was required to come to a conclusion. The factors that were analysed in the project were D:L 
ratio, remote loading time, liposome size, remote loading temperature and number of times 
dialysis was done. End-1 was successfully remote loaded in stealth mix and the optimal remote 
loading conditions were 150nm liposome size, D:L ratio of 1:20, RL time of 3 hrs and, dialysis 
was done once. The encapsulation efficiency for the End-1 in stealth mix liposomes was 63%. 
When same optimal conditions were used for analogs none of them were remote loaded. Then 
End-R was successfully remote loaded in HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE liposome without 
cholesterol using a pH gradient based on ammonium sulphate. This means that cholesterol 
creates some blockage or difficulty for End-R in encapsulation. For the analog, End-R in 
HSPC/MPEG-2000-DSPE liposome the encapsulation efficiency was 37%. It proved that 
substituting the arginine with proline made the diffusion of peptide across the bilayer more 
difficult. The analog End-K could not be remote loaded with either of the liposome 
formulations. The cyclic peptide was also proven to remote load, reaching an encapsulation 
efficiency of 28% on average. The optimal conditions for remote loading of cyclic peptide were 
D:L ratio of 1:25, RL temperature was 60°C, remote loading time was 3hrs, dialysis was done 
once with liposome size of 150nm. Further studies need to be done in which more factors can 
be analysed to optimise the method and EE% further.

9. Future Perspectives 
Studies have been done about peptide drugs and liposomal formulations but there is not enough 
data on remote loading of peptides in liposomes especially for cyclic peptides. More research 
needs to be done for remote loading of peptides with different formulations of liposome. There 
are so many factors that are of interest such as liposomal properties, peptide modification and 
remote loading parameters. The data from this paper only shows the effects of D:L ratio, 
Liposome size, RL temperature and time. Further experiments with a wider range of each 
variable can give data for a deeper analysis using CRYO-TEM, zeta potential and doing 
replicates. More experiments can be done so there is enough data to do ANOVA and other tests 
to analyse the data. The remote loading experiments were done with one cyclic peptide in this 
paper and experiments can be done with more modified cyclic peptides.
