Abstract (English) 
The biological processes within the Sludge Treatment Reed Bed were analyzed from the change in 
gas compositions for the different depths and emitted gas flux. The gasses measured in depth were 
CH4, CO2, O2 and residual gasses. The residual gasses are assumed to mainly consist of nitrous gasses.  
The emitted fluxes were measured for CH4, N2O and CO2. The gas flux measurements were done 
using static chambers.   
Aerobic conditions are the optimal environment for a STRB when trying to minimize the amount of 
emitted GHG and simultaneously have an efficient biodegradation of organic matter.  It was found 
that weather conditions and temperature had a significant impact on the biological processes. 
Rainfalls and low temperatures increases anaerobic conditions and thus gives higher levels of emitted 
methane, CO2 and nitrous oxide. Additionally the total depth of the STRB was also observed to have 
a great impact on the amount of emitted gases. STRB 4 with a depth of ~1.2 m in general emitted a 
greater amount of gasses compared to that of STRB 2 with the depth of ~0.6 m. With regard to global 
warming potential the deeper STRB 4 was also shown to have a greater impact on the climate changes 
than STRB 2.  
A scatter plot of emitted gas flux and the gas composition was made to see if any correlation could 
be found as assumed.  However no direct linear correlation was found. Furthermore the study suggests 
that methanogenesis is the primary anaerobic biological process.

Abstrakt (Danish) 
Biologiske processer i slam mineraliseringsbed blev analyseret ud fra gas koncentrationer i 
forskellige dybder og samt den udledte gas flux. I dybden af slambedet blev forholdet mellem CH4, 
CO2, O2 og resten af de tilstedeværende gasser målt. Her var de ”resterende” gaser forventet at være 
kvælstofholdig gaser.  Udledt CH4, N2O og CO2 flux blev målt med statiske flux kamre.  
Det mest optimale forhold for slam mineraliseringsbede er aerobe forhold. Her bliver mængden af 
udledte drivhusgasser reduceret og samtidig øger nedbrydelsen af organisk materiale. Det blev fundet 
at vejrforhold og temperatur har en betydelig indflydelse på de biologiske processer.  Regn og lave 
temperature øger de anaerobe forhold og fører til større mængder af udledt metan-, kuldioxid- og 
lattergas. Yderligere havde den totale dybde af slam mineraliseringsbedet en betydelig effekt på 
mængden af udledte gasser. Bed 4 som havde en dybde på ~1.2 m udledte mere gas ud end Bed 2 
som havde en dybde på ~0.6 m. Med hensyn til global varme potentiale havde det dybere Bed 4 også 
den største påvirkning på den global opvarmning. 
For at se om der var en sammenhæng mellem de udledte gasser og gas koncentrationer i dybden blev 
et scatterplot lavet. Dog blev ingen direkte sammenhæng fundet. Yderligere indikerer undersøgelsen 
at methanogenesis er den primære anaerobe biologiske proces i mineraliseringsbed.

Introduction  
In Denmark large amounts of wastewater is treated every day. Wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) 
remove pollutants so that the effluent water can safely be reentered into the environment without 
harmful effects (e.g. bio toxicity and eutrophication). Some of the removed pollutants precipitate and 
end up as part of the sludge during the anaerobic and aerobic reactions in the reaction tanks at the 
WWTP.  
Annually 1.3 million tons of sludge is produced from WWTPs and it is thus a significant waste 
product (Kirkeby et al., 2013). Active sludge contains many bacteria, nutrients, particles (primarily 
organic substances) and pathogens. To reduce sludge volume and decrease concentrations of volatile 
solids as well as stabilizing organic matter the sludge has to be treated (Uggetti et al., 2010). Before 
agricultural use the sludge furthermore must be treated to remove the pathogens and hazardous 
organics left (Nielsen & Bruun 2014). The purpose of decreasing the sludge volume is to reduce the 
costs of sludge handling, transportation and final disposal (Uggetti et al., 2010).  
The surplus sludge from WWTPs is conventionally treated with anaerobic digestion (AD) or 
stabilized by composting (Uggetti et al., 2010) as well as mechanically treated with decanter 
centrifuges or screw-presses (Nielsen 2015).  
As an alternative and more ecofriendly sludge treatment Reed Bed Systems (RBSs) with Sludge 
Treatment Reed Beds (STRBs) have been used increasingly since 1988 (Nielsen 2005). Sludge 
treatment reed beds (STRBs) are a combination of a traditional sludge drying bed and natural 
wetlands (Peruzzi et al., 2011). More than 120 Reed Bed Systems (RBS) are currently in operation 
in Denmark (Nielsen 2011). 
In the STRBs, the sludge is stabilized by mineralization of organic solids as well as dewatering via 
drainage and evapotranspiration (Nielsen 2005). The environmental advantages of STRBs such as 
use of natural biologic processes make it a good alternative to the conventional sludge treatments. 
Apart from low maintenance, costs and operation no additional personal or advanced mechanical 
processes are used (Olson et al., 2014). The environmental advantages are low energy consumption 
and that no chemicals or polymers are used (DeMaeseneer 1997). Furthermore the quality of the 
sludge residue is often good enough to be used in agriculture and the STRB systems are quite easy to 
build (Nielsen and Willoughby 2005).  
Various microbial reactions can transform or remove organic matter and nutrients in the sludge. From 
these reactions gasses such as methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O) and carbon dioxide (CO2) can 
enter the atmosphere (Uggetti etal., 2012). These gasses are known greenhouse gasses and contribute 
to the greenhouse effect and thereby impact the global warming.  
Much is still not known about the amounts of emitted greenhouse gasses from reed bed systems (RBS) 
and their effect on climate change, due to RBS being a young sludge treatment method.  As such only 
a few studies on the specific biological processes within the STRB accumulated sludge have been 
done (Olson et al., 2014). The relevance of studies like this on STRBs are thus high.

Aim  
This study focusses on the biological processes that produce greenhouse gasses in Sludge Treatment 
Reed Beds (STRB). Gas composition in different depths of the STRB in Helsinge and gasses emitted 
from the surface were measured during a measuring campaign in the fall 2015 (M2). Another 
measuring campaign was also done prior to this in the summer (2015) (M1) where only the emitted 
gas flux was measured.    
 
Based on the gathered information in the field the following questions will be answered: 
 
- 
How do biological processes affect the emitted gas flux and gas composition in depths during 
the resting period of an STRB?  
- 
How does the composition of gasses in depth correlate with the emitted gas flux?  
- 
Where there any difference between gas flux measurements taken during the summer and fall? 
- 
How does the water removal affect the biological processes?  
- 
How do the emitted gasses contribute to the greenhouse effect?

Theory 
In the following first part of the theory section the origin and composition of the WWTP sludge is 
described in general together with the basics on sludge treatment reed beds and later with specific 
focus on the Helsinge Reed bed system.  
Wastewater treatment 
Urban wastewater contains high concentrations of biodegradable organic matter and if it was directly 
led in to water streams eutrophication could occur. Microorganism would deplete the oxygen in the 
water as these degrade the organic matter. Moreover unpleasant odors could occur if oxygen 
concentrations are reduced in the sediments so that hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and other organic sulfur 
compounds can be formed (Nazaroff & Alverez-Cohen 2001).   
 
The biological treatment of wastewater is divided into a primary and secondary treatment (Nazaroff 
& Alverez-Cohen 2001). The primary treatment of wastewater consists of a sedimentation basin that 
removes solids and oil/grease. The secondary process is where most of the organic matter is 
biodegraded. Here the natural biodegrading processes are optimized in the WWTP by utilizing 
optimal conditions for the required microorganism in the activated sludge with regard to both aerobic 
and anaerobic processes. Typically WWTP have a removal efficiency of over 90% of the suspended 
solids and biodegradable organic matter. 60% of the organic matter is degraded in the activated sludge 
(Nazaroff & Alverez-Cohen 2001).  As a final step, before the effluent wastewater is led out it is sent 
to secondary settling tanks where the leftover suspended solids can precipitate. 
Waste water sludge  
Residual sludge has a general high content of nutrients (nitrogen and phosphor) and can therefore be 
used as agricultural fertilizer (Nielsen & Bruun 2014). Due to the too high content of heavy metals, 
hazardous organic compound and pathogens (Bacteria and viruses) the sludge though has to be treated 
to a point where the final concentrations follow the Danish regulation limits. Studies have shown that 
the quality of the final STRB product (after 10 years) make it possible to recycle the bio solids as 
agricultural fertilizer (Nielsen 2007).  
Heavy metals mainly bind to particles in the sludge residue and thereby limits the amount transported 
out with the reject water. Additionally, mineralization of organic compounds may leave heavy metals 
behind and consequently increase the concentration of heavy metals in the STRB (Nielsen & Bruun 
2014). Toxic effects on plant growth (Phytotoxicity) and bioavailability of heavy metals in the sludge 
should thus be estimated before agricultural use (Peruzzi et al., 2011).  
Heavy metals (Cr, Cu, Ni, Cd, Pb and Zn) found in sludge can be divided into four fractions: 
Exchangeable (most mobile and toxic to plants), Reducible (bind strongly to Fe an Mn oxides, 
unstable in anoxic and acidic environment), Oxidizible (bind to organic matter and form stable humic 
matter with high molecular weight - considered bio-available.) and Residual (inert form considered 
not extractable). A previous study done on sludge from Helsinge showed 63% of the heavy metals in

Some of the organic compounds found in the sludge have been shown to have harmful effects on the

A reduction of pathogens (Salmonella, Enterococci and Escherichia coli) in the STRBs over time has 
been observed in earlier studies and the reject water show very low amounts of pathogens (Nielsen 
2005). Earthworms present in the STRBs are also contributing to the reduction of pathogens as they 
digest organic matter. Some microorganisms in the sludge can even produce organic substances such 
as antibiotics and thereby act as a growth inhibitor for other microorganisms. The population of 
microorganisms will decrease if the organisms’ reproduction rate is lower than the decaying rate. 
Food and nutrients as well as temperature are important for the bacterial growth. Most of the 
microorganisms are furthermore sensitive to low or high pH as well as ultraviolet radiation. Moreover 
desiccation can inhibit the growth of some organisms (Nielsen 2007).  
 
Sludge Treatment Reed Beds (STRBs) 
In 2009 12% (17000 tons) of the produced sludge in Denmark was treated in STRBs. Same year 52% 
of the produced sludge was treated by conventional treatment methods, 24% was incinerated and 12% 
exported or landfilled (Kirkeby et al.,2013).  
In STRBs sludge volumes are reduced via dewatering (draining and evapotranspiration) as well as 
biological degradation of organic matter. Aerobic conditions are improved by additional oxidation 
from the bottom of the STRB and oxidation from the roots of the imbedded reeds (Nilsen 2011). 
Only sludge with a certain wetness (dry matter content between 0.5% to approximately 3-5%) is 
loaded onto STRBs. This can either be the surplus of activated sludge alone or a mix of surplus 
activated sludge and digested (mesophilic) sludge both coming from the WWTP. Loading rates for a 
RBS vary from 25-2200 ton of dry matter per year over a period of 10 years. Wastewater treatment 
plants (WWTP) with the capacity between 2500 to 125000 in population equivalence (PE) can use 
RBS with around 1-18 STRB basins to treat all their surplus sludge (Nielsen 2003).  
Before sludge is loaded onto the STRB basins it is transferred to a buffer tank from the activated 
sludge plant, final settling tank, concentration tanks or digester. Here it is stirred into a homogenous 
mixture and aerated if necessary (Nielsen 2007). A simplified version of these steps is illustrated in 
Figure 1.

The loading rate is area specific and range from 50 - 60 kg of dry matter/m2/year (Nielsen & Bruun 
2014). The loading of the sludge furthermore also depends on the age of the sludge reed system. With 
regard to the final number and size of the basins in a RBS the dewatering capacity of the STRB is the

production, type, quality and climate (Nielsen 2007). After 8-10 year the STRB is emptied when the 
total aggregated sludge has reached a height of 1.2 -1.5 m with a dry solid content of 30-40% (Nielsen 
2005).  
 
Furthermore in previous studies on CH4 and N2O emissions from conventional sludge treatment 
methods compared to sludge treatment with reed beds have shown RBS have a significantly lower 
atmospheric impact in terms of CO2 equivalence emissions (Uggetti et al., 2012). 
Helsinge Reed Bed System  
The WWTP in Helsinge is a mechanical-biological active sludge facility with nitrification and 
chemical phosphor removal. Helsinge WWTP treats household waste water from 42000 PE. After 
treatment the wastewater is led out into Maglemose Å (Gribvand Spildevand A/S, 2015).  
 
A reed bed system (RBS) was designed and added at Helsinge WWTP in 1996. Before that the plant 
was a conventional WWTP. The treated sludge consists of surplus activated sludge from the activated 
sludge plant and from final settling tanks (Peruzzi et al., 2013) 
 
The two sludge types are mixed before they are added to the reed bed system (RBS) where each basin 
area at the filter surface is 1050 m2 (Nielsen 2005; Peruzzi et al., 2013).  After deposition the sludge 
is left to rest for a period of approximately 40-60 days (most often around 42 days) as pollutants are 
naturally removed and the water is drained.

The STRBs at Helsinge are designed as seen in Figure 2 above. An underdrain system is placed at 
the bottom of the STRB in the filter layer of sand and gravel. The underdrain system allows effluent 
water from the accumulating sludge to be drained. Air from the surface also enter the filter layer via 
the underdrain system (Nilsen 2005). To prevent leaching, a water proof membrane is placed at the 
bottom of the STRB. To ease leachate collections from the bottom of the STRB, the bottom layer 
slopes ~1% (Uggetti et al., 2010). 
 
Reeds (Phragmites australis) are initially planted in a growth layer on top of the filter layer. The roots 
of the reeds will travel upwards as the height of loaded sludge increases.  Hence, the reeds will self-
sufficiently continue to grow in the STRB (Nielsen & Willoughby 2005). If weeds or other unwanted 
plants appear in the STRB, the basin is flooded with water. The weeds are not designed to handle the 
stress of the water and dies in contrast to the aquatic reeds. 
 
Water is also removed from the sludge via increased evapotranspiration from the reeds (P. australis) 
Additionally, a pore system is created in the sludge when roots decay through which drainage water 
can run and thus increase the drainage of water.  The reject water is returned to the WWTP. The reeds 
also contribute to the mineralization of organic matter in the sludge through rhizosphere oxidation 
(Nielsen 2007).   
 
Aerobic conditions are favored in the design and operation of STRBs. Aeration from the filter layer, 
sludge surface and rhizomes in the roots contribute to the oxygen diffusion as seen in Figure 2.

During resting periods deep cracks are formed as the sludge dries. This increases the run-off capacity

Biological processes 
In the next part of the theory section the biological processes within the STRB will be explained with 
regard to degradation of organic matter and the present nutrients. A specific focus will be on the 
origin of the emitted greenhouse gasses of interest in this study (CH4, CO2 and N2O).  
Degradation of organic matter  
Gasses such as CH4, N2O and CO2 can be formed by microbial reactions in the sludge. Aerobic 
respiration, denitrification, sulphate reduction, fermentation processes and methanogenesis are some 
of the microbial reactions that remove organic matter in wetlands and sludge beds (Uggetti etal., 
2012). 
Most xenobiotics degrade best under aerobic conditions (oxygen as the electron accepter). Although 
some chlorated compounds (mainly non-aromatic) tend to degrade faster when aerobic conditions are 
greatly reduced (Helweg et al., 2003). Dead organic material derived from dead soil microorganism 
and plants that have resisted the decomposition end up as humus. Some humus components have a 
long turnover time of several decades where other humus components decompose more quickly 
(Madigan et al., 2006).  
Methane and CO2 production 
CO2 emissions from wastewater management are defined as biogenic, as they have a short turnover 
time in the carbon cycle (USEPA 2011). The carbon cycle depends on phototrophic organisms (plants 
and microorganisms) which are only found where light is available. Through photosynthesis the 
phototrophic organisms remove CO2 from the atmosphere. The CO2 is returned to the atmosphere 
through respiration from animals and chemoorganootrophic microorganisms. Photosynthetic fixed 
carbon is degraded by microorganisms (methanogens and chemoorganotrophs) to methane (CH4) and 
carbon dioxide (CO2). Anoxic produced methane is insoluble and flow to aerobic environments, 
where it is oxidized to CO2 by methanotrophs. (Madigan et al., 2009) 
CH4 is formed in anoxic soils and sediments. Factors such as oxygenation, water table, plant species 
and temperature can regulate the production of methane (Uggetti et al., 2012). Microorganisms that 
produce methane CH4 are known as methanogens. Methanogens are archaea and a part of the group 
called Euryarchaeota. Methanogenesis is a fundamental part of the anaerobic biodegradation of 
organic matter. Here the substrates methane and CO2 are formed by the methanogens (Madigan et al., 
2009)

Methane is a chemically stable molecule. However, some aerobic bacteria known as methanotrophs 
can also oxidize it.  With the enzyme methane monooxygenase methanotrophs form methanol by 
combining CH4 with O2. With continued oxidation, methane can be oxidized to CO2 (Madigan et al., 
2009). Oxidation of CH4 is summarized in steps below. 
CH4(methane) → CH3OH (methanol) →CH2O(formaldahyde) →HCOO− (formate) → CO2  
Nutrients in the STRB 
Sludge from a WWTP has a high content of nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus) which is wanted 
when later applied as fertilizer. Nitrogen content typically decrease with depth of the STRB as well 
as duration. The nitrogen decrease is due to microbial mediated processes of nitrification and 
denitrification. Some of the nitrogen is also removed via drainage water (primarily in the form of 
nitrate) (Helweg et al., 2003). Phosphorus on the other hand can interact with iron and other 
constituents in the STRB and is thereby bound to the sludge residue and is thus not easily removed. 
Hence, phosphorus concentrations tend to increase with both depth and time (Nilesen & Bruun 2014). 
Both nitrification and denitrification can lead to formation of nitrogen oxides. Nitrous oxide is an 
intermediate product of denitrification or a byproduct of nitrification. These processes are regulated 
by environmental conditions such as oxygen, carbon and nitrogen availabilities (Uggetti et al., 2012). 
Nitrification 
Nitrification is an aerobic chemoautrophic process (Uggetti et al., 2012). Nitrification is the microbial 
oxidation of ammonium (NH4+) to Nitrite (NO2-) and then further into Nitrate (NO3-) (Helweg et al., 
2003). Nitrifying bacteria (chemoorganotrophs) which are abundant in soil and water oxidize 
inorganic nitrogen (NH3 and NO2-) aerobically. Ammonia and nitrite are oxidized respectively to 
nitrite and nitrate by the microorganisms of the genus Nitrosomonas and Nitrospira. The reduction of 
ammonium (NH4+) to Nitrate (NO3-) is shown in the following reaction. 
NH4+ + 2 O2  NO3- + 2 H+ + H2O 
 
To breakdown nitrogen compounds enzymes in and around the membrane of the microorganism is 
essential.  Ammonia monooxygenase (integral membrane protein) oxidizes NH3 to NH2OH + H2O. 
Hydroxylamine oxidoreductase (periplasmic) then oxidizes NH2OH to NO2-. When nitrite-oxidizing 
bacteria reduce nitrite to nitrate the enzyme nitrite oxidoreductase is used. The Archaea 
Nitrosopumilus is another nitrifyer (autotrophic chemolithoph) and can produce the enzyme ammonia 
monooxygenase similarly to the nitrosomonas (Madigan et al., 2009). 
Denitrification 
Denitrification is an anaerobic heterotrophic microbial process and is known as the most dominant 
process of N2O productions (Uggetti et al., 2012). 
Denitrification is microbial respiration without oxygen (O2), here Nitrate (NO3-) or Nitrite (NO2) are 
mainly reduced to the gasses dinitrogen (N2) and nitrous oxide (N2O) (Helweg et al., 2003).

Generally, NO3- is first reduced to nitric oxide (NO), then to N2O and finally N2 (Madigan et al., 
2009). The reduction of NO3- to N2 is shown in the following reaction. 
5 {CH2O} + 4 NO3- + H+  5 CO2 + 7 H2O + N2 
({CH2O}is a fraction of carbohydrate representing the biomass) 
Under anoxic conditions, specific purple anoxygenic phototrophic bacteria can oxidize nitrite to 
nitrate. Ammonia can also be oxidized under anoxic conditions by anaerobic bacteria. This is known 
as an anammox process. Here NH4+ and NO2- are oxidized together and yield nitrogen gas (N2) 
(Madigan et al., 2009).  
Gas emissions 
In the last part of the theory section the effects of the emitted greenhouse gasses (CH4, CO2 and N2O) 
on climate change will be described with regard to global warming potential and CO2 equivalence. 
Greenhouse gasses (GHGs)  
Nitrogen and oxygen are the two most abundant gases in the atmosphere with volume mixing ratios 
of 78.1% and 20.9 %. The atmosphere further consists of other gasses such as argon, helium and 
radiative active greenhouse gases (e.g. CO2 (0.35%) and ozone) as well as water vapor and aerosols. 
Increased levels of GHGs will affect the greenhouse effect and carbon cycle and thereby contribute 
to climate change (IPCC 2007). Carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), 
hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), Perfluorocarbons (PFCs) and Sulphur hexafluoride (SF6) are known as 
greenhouse gasses. They all have different impacts on the global heat balance (Fenger & Tjell 2009).  
In 2010 the total GHG emissions originated from the following: Heat and power production 24%, 
industry 21%, transport 14%, agriculture 14%, forestry and land use 11%, other energy 11% and 
buildings 6.3% (IPCC 2014). 
Global warming potential (GWP) 
Global warming potential (GWP) is a simple way to compare the potential climatic impact of different 
greenhouse gasses (GHGs). GWP for a GHG is based on the weight of the molecule, the effects of 
the atmospheric lifetime (e.g. 100-year period) and the absorbents of outgoing thermal inferred 
radiation as well as radiative properties of the mixed greenhouse gases in the atmosphere (IPCC 
2007). The global climate is mostly governed by the emissions of CO2. In 2004 CO2 accounted for 
82% of the total emitted gasses in Europe. Where CH4 and N2O were each responsible for 8 % of the 
total emitted GHG in Europe 2004 even though they have much higher GWP values than CO2 (Fenger 
& Tjell 2009).

Method

Field work /raw data 
Gas compositions and emissions from two sludge treatment reed beds at Helsinge WWTP were 
measured. Two measuring campaigns were done; summer (M1) and fall (M2). During M1 the 
measurement were taken from STRB 4.  The second (M2) campaign was done on STRB 2. In this 
study the main focus is on the data from measuring campaign 2 (M2) where both gas flux emissions 
from the surface and gas compositions in the depth were measured. Campaign 1 only included the 
gas flux emission measurements. 
 
The first day of both campaigns wooden planks, flux chamber and nests of pipes were installed in the 
respective reed bed. Flux chambers were numbered with white tape on the side and the steel pipes 
were color coded to the respective test depth prior to positioning the pipes in the bed: 10cm=red, 
20cm=yellow, 40cm=green, 60cm=blue, 80cm=white and 100cm=blank, see Figure 4.

chambers and nests of pipes were installed in reed STRB 4 for M1. For M2 only 4 flux chambers and 
nests of pipes were installed in STRB 2 as illustrated in Figure 3 above.

INNOVA 
The INNOVA is a photoacoustic gas monitor and measures the gas concentrations of CO2, CH4 and 
N2O in the flux chamber over time. Approximately one sample is drawn every minute. (CO ppm was 
also measured but not used). 
Before the INNOVA was connected to the flux chambers the flux chambers were checked to be tightly 
submerged in the sludge and that the lid was sealed tight so no gasses could escape. Two holes in the 
lid allows a glass thermometer and the connecting plastic tube from the INNOVA to enter the 
chamber. The INNOVA was plugged into to a field computer to display and save the incoming data. 
All heights of the flux chambers were recorded as well so that their volume could be calculated from 
the diameter. 
BIOGAS 5000 
BIOGAS 5000 is a portable biogas analyzer and measures the gas 
compositions of CO2, CH4, O2 and “Bal”. “Bal” is the Balance of the 
residual gasses in the depth samples. These are mainly expected to be 
nitrous gasses (NH3, N2O and N2). Smaller fractions of the residual gasses 
are believed to be CO, H2, and H2S. 
The BIOGAS 5000 is very sensitive, so to avoid water or other larger 
molecules to enter the sensitive electronics a filter was placed in the 
connecting plastic tube. Condensation in the tube between the filter and 
apparatus also needs to be avoided.  
To take samples from the steel pipes a 100mL syringe was used. A small 
rubber tube was used to connect the syringe with the steel pipes and the 
plastic tube of the BIOGAS 5000. Before a sample was drawn the pipes were emptied for stagnant 
gas with the syringe. To stop atmospheric gas from the surface to reenter the pipes the rubber tuber 
was pinched together, while the syringe was emptied and the real sample could be drawn.

Data processing / calibrations 
Gas concentrations and gas compositions gathered from the flux chambers and nests of pipes were 
processed as follows. 
Gas composition 
The gas compositions were directly given by the BIOGAS 5000. Additionally the standard error of 
the mean (SEM) was calculated for each depth over time. This was done as follows: First the standard 
deviation - σ - was calculated from the data points, then the standard error was calculated using the 
following equation standard error = standard deviation. / sqrt(N) - where N is the number of samples.  
 
Flux calibrations  
The gas concentration in the closed (or static) flux chamber will accumulate and increase. So to 
calculate the gas flux a linear regression curve of the measured concentrations was done to see if the 
data followed a linear pattern. The slope of the linear curve then gives the gas flux. The first samples 
taken were generally not used for the curves as they from experience always diverted a bit. This could 
be due to disturbances from setting up the equipment and putting on the lid on the chamber. Hence, 
gas production is not stable at the beginning of the measurements. 
The raw data for CO2, CH4 and N2O collected with the INNOVA show the gas changes over time in 
ppm. In order to calculate the flux of the gasses, the ppm concentrations were converted into mol/L 
using the ideal gas law (eq. 1) to calculate the total number of molecules per volume.

and denitrification. Hence the largest fractions of CO2 are released from anaerobic zones in the sludge.  
From the STRB 2 CO2 flux emissions shown in Figure 15, a great diversity between the chambers 
can be observed.  
Small similarities can be observed when comparing the emitted CO2 in Figure 15 to the CH4 emitted 
in Figure 13 but with no obvious related trend. However when comparing the CO2 emission with the 
emitted N2O in Figure 14 many more similarities in emission can be seen. This point towards a 
correlation between CO2 and N2O emission. Hence a considerable amount of the emitted CO2 seems 
to be linked to the denitrification and production of N2O.  
The reeds in the beds may use some of the CO2 to produce O2 and thus the amount of vegetation in 
the static chambers could have an impact on the measured data.  In Appendix A6 it can be seen that 
the vegetation in chamber 1 and 4 are is denser than in chamber 2 and 3. There seems to be no direct 
relationship between the amount of reeds in the chambers and the data shown in Figure 15. Thus the 
amount of reeds is assumed not to have a significant impact on the measured CO2. Furthermore the 
chambers are closed when measurements are taken so that photosynthesis cannot take place.

each other as it may be assumed. Scatter plots were done for both CH4 and CO2.  
No correlations were found for either of the plots (see Appendix 7) as the data points were scattered 
all over and the respective trend lines could not be made to fit the data. No linear trends were seen 
(R2 < 0.2).

Results from STRB 4 compared to STRB 2 
From the campaign M1 flux measurements of STRB 4, the emitted gas flux was also calculated into 
CO2 equivalences. The data is shown below in Figure 18. When comparing data between the two 
STRBs the fact that STRB 4 was deeper (~1.2m) than STRB 2 (~0.6m) should be kept in mind. 
Furthermore the temperature in fall was almost 10 degree Celsius lower than the summer 
temperatures. Both campaigns were subjected to relatively equal amounts of rain.  
 
Figure 18 - Mean CO2-eqvivalences emission from STRB 4 
 
Because STRB 4 is deeper than STRB 2 it can be assumed that STRB 4 produces more gasses than 
STRB 2 and thereby has a higher global warming potential.  It can moreover in Figure 18 be seen 
that more N2O and CO2 are emitted from STRB 4 than STRB 2. The emission pattern created by the 
two gasses are quite different. In STRB 4 the CO2 emission peaks within the first week and N2O 
emission peaks twice within the first two weeks.  The first peak of N2O correlates with the peak of 
CO2, whereas the second and largest peak happens one week later with no direct correlation to a CO2 
peak. After the first two weeks, the emission of the two gasses has decreased and stabilized a bit more 
compared to the prior weeks.  
As the emitted CO2 from a sludge bed is biogenic, it does not contribute to the global warming. The 
emitted CH4 and N2O from STRB 4 almost have the same global warming potential, where the emitted 
N2O is highest during the second week and CH4 emission is higher in the third week.  
In general it can be seen that the methane production increases when CO2 and N2O decreases and 
reversed. The same trend and relation in emission of CH4 and N2O was also observed by Uggetti et 
al., 2012.  In their study it was observed that the loading of fresh sludge enhanced anaerobic 
conditions and thus the amount of emitted CH4.  The increase in methane productions lead to a 
decrease in N2O emissions. As nitrous oxide production is based on anaerobic denitrification the 
production is dependent on available nitrate. Thus a low availability or lack of nitrate will lead to low 
N2O productions. When anaerobic conditions are extensive enough N2O can be reduced to N2 before 
emitted out. Heavy rainfalls as seen in both campaigns of this study increases anaerobic conditions 
in the STRBs.

When comparing the emitted flux concentrations of the CO2, CH4 and N2O in STRB 2 to STRB 4 
(see Appendix 8) it can be seen that the emitted concentrations in STRB 4 are generally higher when 
looking at CO2 and N2O. In STRB 4 the emitted CO2 and N2O are more unstable than in STRB 2 and 
high emission peaks are seen for both CO2 and N2O in the beginning of the summer campaign.  When 
comparing the emitted CH4 for both STRBs the emitted gas flux is higher in STRB 2 than in STRB 
4.  The high CH4 concentration emitted from STRB 2 is most likely caused by continues rainfalls 
during the campaign. Together with the lower temperature during the second campaign (M2) optimal 
conditions for methane producing microorganism was created. During the first campaign (M1) it also 
rained a lot but the temperatures were 10ºC higher than during the second campaign (M2).  
Consequently more water was evaporated from STRB 4 during M1 giving more aerobic conditions 
compared to M2. Lower amounts of methane were thus produced when the STRB 4 was dry. At the 
end of the campaign (M2) the frequency in rainfall was lower resulting in less CH4 emitted from 
STRB 2 as can be seen in Figure 16.  
Climatic factors and weather conditions can strongly affect the production of gasses in the sludge. 
The locations and extension of aerobic and anaerobic processes are primarily determined by humidity 
conditions. Other studies have shown that summer conditions led to higher emissions of CH4 and 
N2O than during winter conditions (Uggetti et al., 2012).  
Another noticeable effect of the CH4 emissions in both basins are the parallel double peaks. The 
emitted methane in both basins seem to follow the same pattern of double peaks. The first peak in 
both cases occurs after just one week. The second peak in both basins occurs after 3 weeks. Uggetti 
et al. 2012 measured gas emissions from a sludge treatment basin over 13 days in nine locations. 
From the emitted flux they also observed a tendency of an initial peak in methane emissions a few 
days after the sludge loading. This initial peak is a consequence of the fresh organic matter and 
anaerobic conditions that favors methanogenesis.  However they also observed that no significant 
correlation was seen between the emitted CH4 flux and humidity of the sludge.  The initial peak 
position in time varied when compared to other similar studies, which also saw the initial CH4 peak, 
probably due to differences in pH, substrate and temperature for the studies (Uggetti et al., 2012). 
The second observed CH4 peak in this study for both measuring campaigns is assumed to be a 
secondary response to movement of anaerobic conditions in the bed enhanced by the continued 
rainfalls. In the gas compositions Figures for each depth over time for the second campaign a 
secondary peak was observed in the two middle layers (20cm and 40 cm) see Figure 9 and 10. 
When comparing the two basins with regard to emitted gas in CO2 equivalences it can be seen that 
STRB 4 contributes a little more to the greenhouse effect than STRB 2, even though STRB 2 has a 
significant methane production. However STRB 4 produces more N2O which has a much greater 
global warming production than methane.  This is based on the mean of the emission per day in both 
campaigns. The mean CO2 equivalences per day of the campaign (M2) for CO2, CH4 and N20 are 
165, 980 and 146 respectively. Together CH4 and N2O from STRB 2 have a total emission of 1126 
CO2 equivalences day.  For STRB 4 the mean CO2 equivalences per day of the summer campaign 
(M1) CO2, CH4 and N20 are 1051, 1013 and 586 respectively. The total emission of CH4 and N2O 
from STRB 4 per day is 1599 CO2-eqvilances.

Conclusion  
In this study it was shown that the biological processes in the STRB are greatly governed by the 
surrounding environment which greatly determines how aerobic or anaerobic the conditions are. 
During anaerobic conditions unwanted GHG are produced and emitted from the STRB. Instead 
aerobic conditions are optimal as here most of the organic matter is degraded.  An increase in 
anaerobic conditions is found to be a consequence of the humid weather conditions. Heavy rain 
creates larger anaerobic zones and the continued wet weather experienced during the second 
campaign in the fall kept the STRB 2 surface layer wet and thereby decreased the aeration from the 
top.  
The biological processes within the depth of STRB 2 were analyzed from the gas composition 
measurements in depth and the emitted gas flux of CH4, N2O and CO2.  The gas productions are a 
result of different biological processes. Where CH4 and CO2 are products of methanogenesis and N2O 
is mainly produced during denitrification as an intermediate product. It can also be produced via 
nitrification.  
From this study it is suggested that methanogenesis is the primary anaerobic biological process. An 
initial peak in methane production was found for the emitted gas flux. This was also observed for the 
gas flux emissions from STRB 4 taken during the summer campaign as well as other studies. This 
initial peak is believed to be a result of the resent loaded sludge volume that is nutrient rich and create 
greater anaerobic conditions. A second peak in methane productions were seen as well in both the 
first and second campaign gas flux measurements. The secondary peak and increase of methane 
production took place after the third week in both cases. This secondary peak is believed to be a 
secondary response to the relocation of anaerobic zones amplified by the continued rainfall. When 
looking at the gas compositions in depth the secondary CH4 peak is most noticeable in the middle 
layer (20 and 40 cm). The first methane peak was primary produced in the top layers and the second 
methane peak in the middle layers of the STRB.     
Denitrification depends on the availability of nitrate to form N2O or N2. The content of nitrate in the 
loaded sludge can vary. In addition, nitrate can be formed during aerobic nitrification. The emitted 
gas flux pattern can thus vary between studies. In this study the produced N2O first started to increase 
at the end of the second campaign in STRB 2. This was most likely due to low nitrate availability in 
the beginning and as nitrification occurred in the aerobic zones through the campaign more nitrate 
was formed.     
A scatter plot of emitted gas flux and the gas composition was made to see if any correlation could 
be found as assumed.  However no direct linear correlation was found. 
The depth of the STRB affects the biological processes within the STRB. This could be seen from 
comparing STRB 2 to STRB 4.  The depth of STRB 4 was almost twice as deep (~1.2 m) and 
produced considerably more gas compared to STRB 2 (~0.6 m). Here the differences in weather 
conditions also had an impact, as the STRB 4 measurements were done during the summer and STRB 
2 measurements was done during the fall. Thus water could evaporate faster from STRB 4 and

optimizing the aerobic conditions. On the other hand, the summer campaign was also very wet. Here 
the combination of heat and humidity gave optimal conditions for small flies to hatch their eggs, 
making them a pestilence for the city of Helsinge.   
From the dry matter content in depth and the core sampling pictures in Appendix 3 and 4, volume 
reductions were seen in both STRBs. Higher temperatures during summer resulted in a higher 
evaporation and water removal from the STRB. This led to a faster aeriation so that nitrification could 
occur and consequently lead to denitrification in anaerobic zones where N2O could be produced. 
During fall very little N2O was found suggesting that a lesser degree of nitrification and denitrification 
happened due to reduced evapotransporation. 
When comparing STRB 2 to STRB 4 the two main differences were the temperature and the depth of 
accumulated sludge.  STRB 2 had a high CH4 production and a low but stable N2O production whereas 
STRB 4 had a more unstable and generally higher N2O production and a slightly lower CH4 
production. As N2O is a much more potent GHG than CH4. The deeper STRB 4 was shown to have 
a greater impact on the climate changes than STRB 2. Generally, the anaerobic conditions were 
reduced over time for both STRBs so that CH4 and N2O ended up having approximately the same 
climatic impact at the end of the campaigns for both STRBs.
