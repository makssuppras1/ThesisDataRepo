Abstract
Tungsten as a PFC in fusion reactors is recently an active field of interest and investigation owing
to it’s properties including a very high melting point, high density, high young’s modulus, high
thermal conductivity, low thermal expansion, low sputtering yield and high plasma sputtering
erosion resistance. Given all the advantages, one major disadvantage of tungsten as a PFC is its
almost nonexistent ductility at room temperature and its high DBTT. Deformation of tungsten will
increase ductility and decrease the DBTT at room temperature. However, during operation at high
temperatures, deformation structures are thermally unstable and the microstructure of materials
will undergo three different restoration processes: recovery, recrystallization, and grain growth.
An analysis of tungsten-carbide reinforced tungsten and potassium-doped tungsten sheet mate-
rials as PFCs is presented in this work. Strengthening the tungsten matrix by dispersion with
ceramic nanoparticles is known to retard dislocation motion during recovery and limit recrystal-
lization and grain growth by pinning of GBs. The presence of small gas bubbles in potassium
doped tungsten materials is also known to pinn the grain boundaries, thus increasing resistance to
recrystallization and grain growth. The thermal stability of the materials was investigated using
hardness testing, SEM EBSD topographical, phase, grain area, and crystal orientation analysis fol-
lowing isothermal annealing at several temperatures. In addition, in-situ XRD was carried out on
the potassium doped tungsten samples during isothermal annealing.

1
Introduction
Among its unique properties, tungsten exhibits the highest melting point of any element, high
elastic modulus, high density, high thermal conductivity and outstanding mechanical properties
at elevated temperatures. Due to its high melting point, low sputtering yield, and high plasma
sputtering erosion resistance, tungsten has been identified as one of the candidate materials for
PFCs in fusion reactors. Nonetheless, tungsten suffers from high DBTT and its almost nonexistent
ductility at room temperature. As a result of its poor ductility, tungsten has serious problems both
in terms of its workability and its performance in demanding applications. A substantial impact
on the manufacture and potential uses of tungsten could be achieved by improving its mechanical
properties [8], [14].
One of the PFC approaches investigated is a combination of conventional powder-metallurgy
processing of potassium-doped tungsten followed by severe plastic deformation, i.e., rolling. In
potassium-doped tungsten materials small gas bubbles are pinning on the GBs and thereby in-
creasing the recrystallization and grain growth resistance.
Although these materials have en-
hanced mechanical properties at room temperature, prolonged exposure to high temperatures can
cause recrystallization and extensive grain growth.
Another PFC approach is the concept of dispersion strengthening of the W-matrix with W2C parti-
cles to prevent the W grain growth (again by applying pinning forces on GBs). The manual process
uses conventional powder-metallurgy processing and reactive sintering of tungsten with different
carbon precursors in a high-temperature vacuum furnace [15] [18].
This study investigates the microstructural evolution in the aforementioned tungsten alloys during
high-temperature isothermal annealing, as well as their resistance to various annealing processes.

Theory
This chapter will provide a background into some of the properties of tungsten and why it is
potentially an excellent PFC material. The following subsections present a brief summary of tung-

2.1
The history of fusion energy
Fusion is when nuclei of lighter atoms, such as hydrogen isotropes, collide and fuse together,
nuclei of heavier atoms, such as helium, are formed, releasing immense amounts of energy in the
process. Fusion is a form of nuclear energy, since the energy is derived from nuclear reactions. It is
the process of fusion that powers and drives the production of energy in stars, such as our Sun [4].
Nuclear fusion science and physics became apparent only in the 1920s following research by British
astrophysicist Eddington. Eddington’s theory was published in 1926 and laid the groundwork for
modern theoretical astrophysics. Eddington’s paper led to the first calculation of nuclear fusion
rates in stars by Atkinson and Houtermans. At the same time, Rutherford examined the structure
of the atom. Rutherford observed in 1934 that ”an enormous effect was produced” during fusion
of deuterium into helium. His student, Oliphant used an upgraded version of the equipment and
fired deuterium instead of hydrogen to find helium-3 and tritium, proving that heavy hydrogen
nuclei can react. Thereby, Fusion was demonstrated for the first time in a laboratory. In 1938 Bethe
used the understanding of nuclear fusion in his work on stellar nucleosynthesis to show that the
Sun and stars release energy through proton-proton chain reactions [2].
In 1950, Soviet scientists Andrei Sakharov and Igor Tamm designed a magnetic confinement fu-
sion device called tokamak. A tokamak reactor is a torus-shaped vacuum vessel in which plasma
is produced by subjecting gaseous hydrogen to extremely high temperatures and pressures. Mag-
netic coils are used to control and contain the plasma so that it does not come into contact with the
walls [2].
As the 1980’s saw the iron curtain being lifted a group of industrialized nations agreed, to work
on developing a new, cleaner and more sustainable source of energy. The ITER project was con-
ceived at the Geneva Superpower Summit in November 1985, when General Secretary Gorbachev
of the Soviet Union proposed to US President Ronald Reagan the idea of developing fusion en-
ergy for peaceful purposes through cooperative international efforts. A year later, a joint effort
was launched by the European Union, Japan, the Soviet Union, and the United States to develop
ITER, an international fusion facility. ITER’s conceptual design was initiated in 1988, followed by
increasingly detailed engineering design phases until 2001, when the final design was approved
by the members. As of 2003, The People’s Republic of China and Republic of Korea have joined
the project, followed by India in 2005. The site for ITER was selected after an extensive selec-
tion process that was concluded in 2005 when all ITER members unanimously voted in favor of a
proposal by the European Union. The ITER facility would be built in southern France near Aix-en-
Provence. During the subsequent negotiations, the ITER Agreement was developed covering the
construction, exploitation, and decommissioning of ITER, as well as the financing, organization,
and staffing of the organization. To summarize, 35 countries are collaborating to build the world’s
largest tokamak fusion reactor. One of the goals of ITER is to generate 500 MW of fusion power
from 50 MW of input heating power, thereby producing a 10 times return on investment (Q = 10)
[1], [5].
In 2014 a European joint fusion research program, known as EUROfusion was launched by the Eu-
ropean Commission and European fusion research laboratories. Located in the European Union,
the United Kingdom, Switzerland and Ukraine, EUROfusion is a consortium of national fusion

2.2
Tungsten
2.2.1
History of Tungsten
Tungsten’s history dates back to the Middle Ages (mid-16th century), when German tin miners
discovered a mineral accompanying their ore in the mountains near Saxony-Bohemian Erzge-
birge. During the smelting process, this mineral reduced tin yield and increased slagging. Tin
melt formed foam, and a layer of heavy slag formed in the smelter to retain the tin. The unknown
mineral was described at the time as ”tearing away the tin and devouring it like a wolf devours a
sheep”. Due to the mineral’s black colour and hairy appearance, the miners nicknamed it wolform,
wolfshar, and wolfram (which means wolf froth).
Axel Fredrik Cronstedt, a Swedish chemist and mineralogist, discovered an unusually heavy min-
eral in Bispberg’s iron mine in Dalecarlia in the mid- to late-1750’s. He called this heavy mineral
”tung-sten”, which is Swedish for ”heavy stone”. Cronstedt believed that this mineral contained
an element that was as yet unknown.
Carl Wilhelm Scheele, a fellow Swedish chemist, isolated tungstic acid in 1781, which he called
tungsten trioxide.
In Uppsala, Professor Torben Bergman suggested reducing the acid with charcoal to produce the
corresponding metal.
Spanish Nobleman Juan Jose’ de D’Eluyar studied metallurgical chemistry with Professor Bergman
and wrote a review of Scheele’s work on Tungsten in 1781/82. After returning to Spain in 1783,
Juan Jose analyzed a sample of wolfram obtained from a tin mine in Saxony and found it contained
the same acid that Scheele had gotten from Tungsten. As Professor Bergman had proposed, the ox-
ide was then reduced to a new metal with charcoal. ”Wolfram” was then named after the mineral
it was derived from [6].
2.2.2
Tungsten as a PFC
As mentioned already plasma is magnetically confined within the torus shaped vessel within the
tokamak reator. Approximately 85 % of the fusion occurs in the inner surface covered with blanket
modules. The surface of these modules facing the plasma is called the ”first wall”. The heat load
will be around 2 MW/m2 and 800 ◦C under normal conditions of operation (Fig.1).
Continuous operation requires the plasma to be cleaned of unavoidable hydrogen isotopes, he-
lium, and impurities. Magnetic fields are used to direct these ions away from the burning plasma
towards cooled target plates, known as divertors. Once the ions have impact, they recombine into
neutral atoms that can be removed using vacuum pumps. Divertors are subject to extraordinary
sputtering as a result of particle impact and are the highest thermally loaded components of fu-
sion power plant. Divertors receive about 15 % of the total fusion power, while peak loads of 10–20
MW/m2 and 1100 ◦C must be taken into account (Fig.1).
Both the first wall protection layer and the divertor parts are intended to be made of tungsten
based material. The minimum service-life of the first wall protection layer and divertor parts are 5
and 2 years, respectively [7].
Tungsten as a PFC in fusion reactors is recently an active field of interest and investigation owing
to it’s properties including a very high melting point, high density, high young’s modulus, high
thermal conductivity, low thermal expansion, low sputtering yield and high plasma sputtering
erosion resistance. Some of the properties at room temperature are given in Table 1.

Given all the advantages, one major disadvantage of tungsten as a PFC is its almost nonexistent
ductility at room temperature and its high DBTT. Deformation of tungsten will increase ductility
and decrease the DBTT at room temperature. However, during operation at high temperatures,
deformation structures are thermally unstable and the microstructure of materials will undergo
three different restoration processes: recovery(2.8), recrystallization(2.9), and grain growth(2.10).
The energy contained in defects introduced by plastic deformation (primarily dislocations) is the
driving force that enables them to be eliminated, thus restoring the properties that existed before
plastic deformation. The DBTT thus increases again. As a result of the poor ductility, workability
and performance in demanding applications tungsten faces severe challenges [11].
A comprehensive understanding of brittleness is necessary to improve ductility. The two primary
factors contributing to brittleness in tungsten which are generally accepted are the intrinsic lack of
close-packed planes and the poor GB cohesion. The lack of close-packed planes is due to the BCC
crystalline structure of tungsten. Having a BCC crystalline structure means plastic deformation is
governed by the movement of non-planar screw dislocations. Hence, movement of dislocations
along slip planes is difficult.
The poor GB cohesion seems to be the cause of inter-granular fracture in tungsten. There has been
recognition that impurity accumulation at grain boundaries are significant for this inter-granular
fracture, but also the brittleness. Carbon, oxygen, potassium, and phosphorous are some of the
most notable impurities in literature. Generally, it is believed that segregation of contaminants,
such as potassium and nickel, within grain boundaries can weaken grain boundaries, increase
inter-granular fracture and lead to tungsten embrittlement. At the same time, investigations have

2.4
Potassium doped tungsten sheets
In potassium doped tungsten materials small gas bubbles are pinning on GBs, hence increasing
resistance against recrystillization and grain growth as already described in 2.3. Volatilization of
potassium during the sintering process results in the formation of the bubbles, which can grows
due to diffusion-controlled potassium transport. The samples investigated in this work are potas-
sium doped tungsten sheets (WK80) which are prepared by conventional powder-metallurgy pro-
cessing of potassium containing tungsten followed by plastic deformation, in this case rolling.
During rolling the bubbles become elongated into ellipsoids in line with the shape change. Upon
subsequent annealing the ellipsoid bubbles reach a critical aspect ratio breaks up into multiple
aligned bubble rows due to minimization of the surface energy [17], [18].
2.5
Deformation Structure
The deformed state of a material is a result of the plastic work done and increased stored energy
due to dislocation (line defect) accumulation. This is achieved by plastically deforming a material.
The Taylor equation, Eq.[2.1] describes the relationship between flow stress (instantaneous value
of stress required to continue plastically deforming a material) and dislocation density.
τ = αGb√ρ
(2.1)
Where τ is the flow stress, G is the shear modulus, b is the Burgers vector magnitude and α is a
coefficient related to the geometry of interaction between dislocations which are the obstacles that
determine the flow stress.
Deformation largely defines the subsequent microstructural evolution in terms of grain size, shape,
and orientation, especially when it comes to recrystallization. Metals undergo various changes in
their microstructure during deformation. First of all, the grain shape changes, which results in an
increase in the grain boundary area. Deformation creates the new grain boundary by incorporat-
ing the dislocations that are continually created during the deformation process. An additional
feature that is only visible with TEM is the presence of a dislocation cell substructure within the
grains which results from the nonuniform dislocation accumulation. Dislocations and new inter-
faces make up most of the stored energy from deformation, except for a small amount from the
point defects consisting of vacancies and interstitials. Interstitials are so mobile that they do not
contribute significantly to the stored energy of deformation, except in the case of deformation at
very low temperatures. Another relevant deformation outcome is that when single crystals or a
polycrystalline metal is deformed, the orientation of the individual crystals and grains changes
with respect to the applied stress. Changes in this manner are not random, cause lattice rotation
and are directly related to crystallographic slips and/or twins that facilitates plastic deformation.
Thus, the grains obtain a crystallographic preferred orientation that becomes stronger with further
deformation.
During deformation the rate of increase in GB area and grain shape depends largely on the defor-
mation mode. See Fig. 3. When a sheet is rolled, the grains become laths, when a wire is drawn,
needles, and when a specimen is compressed, discs.

2.6
Annealing Phenomena
The annealing phenomena consist of restoration of an unstable thermodynamically state intro-
duced by plastic deformation. As mentioned earlier, plastic deformation introduces defects and
a stored energy in the material by mainly dislocation accumulation. This stored energy consti-
tutes the driving force of restoration. The processes of annealing are recovery, recrystallization
and grain growth. Although the driving force in all annealing processes is the reduction in stored
energy the mechanisms by which they take place are different. The annealing mechanisms are
briefly described as following [11]:
• Recovery: The stored energy declines gradually as defects are mutually annihilated and dis-
locations rearrange without any long-range motion of HABs.
• Recrystallization: Occurs when HABs move, eliminating the defect content in the regions
they sweep.
• Grain Growth: When grains grow, boundaries move in response to their boundary energy,
causing grains to coarsen.

2.8
Recovery
The driving force of recovery is reduction in stored energy. Recovery occurs by reducing the non-
equilibrium concentration of lattice defects, usually by annealing. The defects recovered can be
both point and line defects i.e. dislocations, with the latter being the most relevant as the former
are usually annihilated at relatively low temperatures below 0.3Tm due to their very high mobil-
ity. Recovery by dislocation annihilation requires a combination of kinetics consisting of diffusion,
climb and sub-boundary migration and coalescence. Despite its importance, recovery is often dif-
ficult to quantify. Recovery can take place both immediately after deformation and dynamically
during it. In addition, neither the optical microstructure nor the crystallographic texture is altered
by recovery. The recovery process influences properties such as hardness and structural features
such as dislocation density, subgrain size, and misorientation. However, these changes are very
hard to spot, resulting in difficult statistic quantification [12].
The mechanisms of recovery includes the following:
• Point defect annihilation by diffusion.
• Mutual dislocation annihilation - dislocation of opposite signs or dipoles.
• Organisation of dislocation into walls or sub boundaries (polygonization)
• Coalescence of sub boundary walls.

2.9
Recrystallization
HABs and recrystallization are facilitated by atomistic jumps between both sides of a HAB. The re-
sult is that recrystallization is a thermally activated process with an activation energy comparable
to that of self-diffusion, either through the bulk distributions of the grains or along short circuit
pathways. The process of recrystallization can be described by two microstructural processes: For-
mation of new defect-free nuclei and their growth. Nuclei of recrystillization are not nuclei in strict
thermodynamic sence but must pre-exist in the deformed structure. Recrystillization requires pres-
ence and movement of HABs which have a significantly higher mobility than LABs. HABs only
move when facing deformed structure, no further movement can occur when recrystallizing grains
impinged on each other. The growth possibility of a nucleus depends particularly on the presence

2.10
Grain Growth
Upon completion of the recrystallization process, the structure is not yet stable, and recrystallized
grains may grow further. This is driven by a reduction in the amount of energy that is stored in
the material in the form of grain boundaries. There is typically a driving pressure of ∼100 MPa for
grain growth, which is normally two orders of magnitude less than for recrystallization. Therefore,
grain boundaries will have slower velocities at a certain temperature during grain growth than
during recrystallization, and boundary migration will be affected more by solutes and second-
phase particles. Grain growth has technological importance due to its impact on the properties
of metals, and particularly the impact of grain size on mechanical behavior. Optimal strength
and toughness are usually achieved with small grain sizes in materials for structural use at lower
temperatures. A large grain size, however, is needed in order to improve high-temperature creep
resistance. Therefore, understanding grain growth is fundamental to controlling the microstruc-
tures and properties of metals when they undergo solid-state processing.
Grain growth can be categorized into two types: normal growth of grain and abnormal growth
of grain.
A continuous process is considered to be Normal Grain Growth, in which the microstructure
coarsens uniformly. Here the scaled grain size distribution is usually independent of time and has
a relatively narrow range of sizes. Upon reaching a transient phase of growth, the microstructure
reaches a quasistationary state in which the distribution of grain sizes scaled by their mean value
is invariant, and only the scale varies with time. When Abnormal Grain Growth occurs, which is
a discontinuous process, a few grains consume the matrix of smaller grains, resulting temporarily
in a bimodal distribution of grain size. These large grains impinge eventually, allowing normal
grain growth to resume. See Fig. 6.

curved only in one direction in this situation rather than two, reducing the driving force. By ther-
mal etching, GBs may also develop grooves at their intersections with the surface, which inhibit
grain growth. Finally, a highly textured material will unavoidably contain a high proportion of
low-angle boundaries with low energy, which will result in a decreased driving force for grain
growth.

ECP
The thermal contraction causes cracking of the ampoules during extraction from the furnace due
to rapid cooling.
Studies have shown that oxidation of tungsten starts when oxygen or air is present. Whether it is
formed in oxygen, dry or humid air, tungsten trioxide (WO3) is always formed. Up to 200 ◦C, the
oxide layer gradually thickens, but at a very slow rate, and can be considered insignificant. Above
200 ◦C, however, the oxide layer rapidly thickens. An increase in humidity also accelerates the ox-
idation process. From 327-400 ◦C, a firmly adherent blue and thin oxide film will be formed. This
film acts as a protective layer. WO2.75 represents the film composition. At temperatures above 500
◦C, the oxide layer cracks, and at temperatures above 600 ◦C, WO3 forms on the blue layer, because
oxygen can pass through WO3. Sublimation of WO3 starts at 750 ◦C and becomes significant above
900 ◦C, which causes metal degradation to become more pronounced [21].

3.6
SEM
With a SEM the microscopic structure is examined by scanning the surface of materials at a rel-
atively high resolution and a wide depth of field. An SEM can provide information about the
topography and elemental composition of microscopic structures and images are created by scan-
ning a sample’s surface with a focused electron beam. Fig. 15a illustrates the components of a SEM.
As can be seen, the SEM is composed of an electron gun and a series of electromagnetic lenses and
apertures. For surface scanning, the electron beam emittet from the electron gun is condensed into
a fine probe. Electron guns are either thermionic or field emission guns, and the acceleration volt-
age for producing an electron beam typically ranging from 1-40 kV.
As shown in Fig. 15a, an optical path in SEM passes through numerous electromagnetic lenses,
including condenser lenses and an objective lens. The electromagnetic lenses are used for electron
probe formation. The condenser lenses reduce the crossover diameter of the electron beam. Fi-
nally, the objective lens focuses the electron beam to nanometer-scale. The resolution of an SEM is
determined by the size of the electron probe scanning the sample. With a probe size of 10 nm, SEM
systems can typically produce magnifications of 20000x.
The incident beam produces electron signals when it scans over a sample surface area. A detector
collects electron signals from each pixel in the grit in order to create a corresponding point-to-point
image on a display screen. Inelastic or elastic scattering occurs when high-energy electrons strike
a sample. In elastic scattering, incident electrons are scattered by atoms in the sample, resulting in
backscattered electrons. During inelastic scattering, electrons are ejected from atoms in the sample,
resulting in secondary electrons. Backscattered electrons are typically deflected from samples at
large angles and with little energy loss and retain 60–80 % of the incident electron energy. The
secondary electrons, on the other hand, tend to be deflected at small angles and are significantly
lower in energy in comparison to incident electrons. As a result of inelastic scattering, an inci-
dent electron transfers kinetic energy to an electron inside a sample atom. A secondary electrons
will emerge from the sample atom if an electron has sufficient kinetic energy to leave its orbital.
Secondary electrons energies usually range from 3-5 eV. Signals with secondary electrons are pri-
marily used to achieve topographic contrast while signals with back scattered electrons determine
elemental composition contrast. Backscattered electrons and secondary electrons escape from dif-
ferent locations in the sample. Under the sample surface, electron scatter in the interaction zone
is illustrated in Fig. 15b. The probe produces a zone that is pear-shaped and grows in size as the
energy of the incident electrons increases [24].
This work used SEM to acquire topographical micrographs and orientation maps of the tungsten
alloys. A TableTop Phenom SEM was used to acquire these micrographs. All micrographs were
obtained at a 15 kV acceleration voltage.

3.7.1
Recrystallization analysis by EBSD
EBSD orientation data is used for understanding the nucleation and growth of recrystallization in
WK80 samples. Using orientation data (or orientation maps), the recrystallized volume fraction X
(Eq.[2.14]) can be calculated by taking the area fraction resulting from the scanned recrystallized
grains, assuming that area fraction and volume fraction are the same. Recrystallized grains must
be distinguished from all other grains in order to identify recrystallized regions. For this purpose,
recrystallized grains are identified as follows:
1. The only grains which are considered potentially recrystallized have apparent areas greater
or equal to 10 µm2. Those below this size are excluded from consideration. Inclusion of such
small grains would cause too much of the deformed matrix to be mistaken for recrystallized.
2. To be classified as recrystallized, more than 25 % of the grain’s outer boundary must consist
of HABs, as only those are mobile enough to move into the deformed/recovered matrix
Page 26
==PAGE:37==
Master Thesis
Thermal stability of tungsten alloys
when recrystallization occurs.
3. For excluding grains having internal deformation structures, the total inner boundary length
must be below 10 % of the outer boundary length to be considered recrystallized.
As a result, two distinct subsets of grains can be distinguished as recrystallized grains and recov-
ered grains. Recovered grains still retain a deformed structure after some recovery.
As a result of identifying recrystallized grains (rx) and recovered grains (rc), different subsets of
GBs can be identified. The inner boundaries of grains are those within the grains while the outer
boundaries are those surrounding the grains. A further subdivision of these leads to rxrx bound-
aries separating recrystallized grains, rxrc boundaries separating recrystallized grains and recov-
ered matrix, and rcrc boundaries separating recovered grains. By further moving into the recov-
ered region, the rxrc boundaries can carry on recrystallization. They can be utilized to identify the
regions likely to be invaded by the recrystallization front. Thus, rc grains are further subdivided
into rcrc grains that belong to the recovered matrix in contact with the recovered grains and the
rcloc grains that are recovered grains that border with recrystallized grains. Likewise, every grain
subset has its own boundaries. A rcloc grain possesses both rcloc and rxrc boundaries, whereas a
rcrc grain possesses only rcrc boundaries, and a rx grain has both rxrc and rxrx boundaries. More-
over, grains of all subsets are distinguished by the misorientation angle and are either HABs or
LABs [27].

XRD data representation
With XRD a diffractogram is obtained. Diffractometers measure diffraction intensity as a function
of 2θ. From a low 2θ to a high 2θ, diffraction intensity is recorded. The X-ray diffractogram of a
powder mixture of Si and Al2O3 is shown in Fig. 25. Several intensity peaks located at different 2θ
provide a distinctive ”fingerprints” for a crystalline solid. Each peak represents diffraction from
a particular crystallographic plane. One can identify the crystallized substances in a specimen by
matching a peak sequence with a standard diffractogram.

==PAGE:45==

Figure 25: Diffractogram of Si and Al2O3 mixture [24].
A match of the diffractogram is determined by the positions of the diffraction peak maxima and the
relative peak intensities among them. Computers are used to acquire and process data in modern
diffractometers. For obtaining sufficient data for the identification of a material, the following
factors are necessary:
• 2θ range: In the case of the strong textured tungsten sample analyzed in this work, the ranges
are already known to be between 54-62.5◦and 142-160◦, respectively, thus defining the 200
and 400 peaks.
• X-ray radiation source: Due to its short wavelength and low absorption, Cu Kα is the most
popular radiation source used in laboratory diffractometry. The work presented here also
uses Cu Kα.
• Step size: Scanning in the 2θ range is not continuous, but rather progressed incrementally.
Choosing the right step size means balancing peak position accuracy with peak intensity.
To use about 10–20 data points with intensity above half the maximum, follows the rule
of thumb. For the 200 and 400 peaks, the presented work uses step size[◦]/time per step [s]
values of 0.005/0.01 and 0.01/0.25, respectively. This results in about 20-30 data points above
the FWHM for both peaks.
FWHM is sensitive to variations in microstructure and stress-strain accumulation. The FWHM
provides information on dislocation density as peaks become wider when microstrains are present.
The FWHM increases with plastic strain while decreasing with stress relaxation resulting from the
reduced dislocation density. The FWHM gradually decreases with an increasing grain size at a
higher annealing temperature. The peak shifts to the left when the material is undergoing tension
as the crystal spacing, d (Eq.[3.6]) increases, thus decreasing 2θ. The opposite is true when the
material is undergoing compression. A peak is represented in Fig. 26a.
As an approximation of the Voigt function, the pseudo-Voigt function is a superposition of Gaus-
sian and Lorentzian functions. It is frequently used in powder diffraction for peaks that are not
well described by a pure Gaussian function or Lorentzian function. The Gaussian contribution
reflect the micro strain in the material, whereas the Lorenzian contribution reflect the grain size.

5.4
In-situ XRD results
In this section, the results of in-situ XRD experiments are presented. In-situ XRD experiments
were conducted at 1050 ◦C, 1100 ◦C and 1150 ◦C for a period of up to ∼21.5 hours. A scan of both
the 200 and 400 peaks at 30 ◦C was conducted at the beginning and the end of each experiment.
The FWHM position drifting, FWHM evolution and Pseudo-Voigt function are shown in Fig. 88,
Fig. 89 thru Fig. 92 and Fig. 93, respectively. In-situ experiments have the obvious advantage
of allowing real-time tracking of the FWHM evolution as the materials undergo annealing. The
parameters used in this work for in-situ XRD experiments are provided in 3.8.1. For the 1100 ◦C
experiment, a slit width of 0.6 mm was used, and for the 1050 ◦C and 1150 ◦C experiments, a
slit width of 0.2 mm was used. Peak intensity and peak shape are influenced by the size of the
slit. Small slit widths reduce the intensity of the X-ray beam, the length of the beam hitting the
sample, but produce sharper peaks. The sharper peaks will ultimately improve the resolution of
the instrument.
LaB6 Standard Reference Material (SRM) 660c was used to account for the instrument-induced
broadening. Because SRM 660b consists of strain-free LaB6 crystalline particles with an average
size of 10 µm, no size or strain broadening is expected to contribute to the total peak width. As a
result, the standard measurement reflects only the peak broadening contribution that is attributed
to the instrument-induced broadening 39. The instrument-induced broadening is then filtered
from the total broadening. The standard was measured at room temperature using of course the
exact same parameters as those of the sample. Due to the fact that the 1100 ◦C experiment was
conducted with a different setup, two different LaB6 standards were measured, one covering only
the 1100 ◦C experiment and the other covering the 1050 ◦C and 1150 ◦C experiments. The 1100 ◦C
experiment instrument-induced broadening of the low and high 2θ peaks was found to be [0.0867
◦; 0.0883 ◦] and [0.1526 ◦; 0.1740 ◦], respectively. The 1050/1150 ◦C experiment instrument-induced
broadening of the low and high 2θ peaks was found to be [0.0470 ◦; 0.0482 ◦] and [0.1289 ◦; 0.1680
◦], respectively. The fits are shown in Fig. 86 and 87.

Annealing
It was not possible to verify the temperature in the Nabertherm RHTH 50-150/18 furnace because

On the W7WC samples, the maximum standard deviation of hardness measurements is 10.6 HV1,
but it generally ranges between 4 and 7 HV1. The standard deviations of the averages are between

6.1.3
EBSD
It was confirmed that there was an equipment tilt correction error during the scanning of the 21
days annealed sample. The error was related to a faulty vertical measurement of the sample at
70◦tilt. The error was discovered accidentally, when measuring predefined distances between
nearby hardness indentations, which were set to 600 µm both horizontally and vertically. In the
tilted position, the vertical indentation distance measured 533 µm, while in the untilted position it
correctly measured 600 µm (with a neglectable error margin within 5 µm), resulting in a 65+ µm
compression of the area of investigation. Fig. 94 shows the measurements in the tilted position.
The error could be caused by an improper installation of the sample in the sampleholder. However,
this was checked and found to be in order. It was found that the error was present in some of the
later samples scanned.

6.2.2
X-Ray Diffraction
While at the target temperatures of 1050 ◦C, 1100 ◦C or 1150 ◦C peak position drifting towards a
decrease in 2θ value was observed. After gaining access to the sample, it was discovered that the
sample had bended upward and was misaligned over the heating band. As a result, the two laser
beams did not coincide at the center of the sample as they did in the original setup. See Fig. 97.
As a means of investigating the magnitude of this error source, the bended sample platform was
adjusted vertically until the laser beams again coincided, then another scan was conducted and the
peaks compared with the last scan of the in-situ test. This concluded the sample bending did not
cause the peak drifting in the in-situ test since the two peaks ended up completely overlapping
and identical. On the other hand, as a result of the bending, heat transfer from the band to the
sample occurs only in the regions of the fixation rods, resulting in non-uniform heat distribution
and hence an error source, since the temperature of the thermocouple is different from the incident
points of the laser beams.
As can be seen in Fig. 27b and 97, the WK80 sample is attached to the heating band using two fix-
ation rods of alumina, Al2O3. While this occurs, a spring-loaded piston is acting upon the sample
beneath it by applying a constant upward force through a base plate of aluminum nitride, AlN.
Since both Al2O3 and AlN are insulating materials, the local temperature in the two regions com-
mon to the fixation rods, sample and baseplate will be higher than the remaining sample area. One
theory explaining sample bending is that it occurs due to thermomechanical effects caused by the
different states of thermal expansion. Another more likely theory is that the bending occurs due to
relaxation (during annealing) in sample surface tensions. This might as well explain the peak drift-
ing. In metal rolling operations, residual stresses are left in the formed sheet due to nonuniform

7
Conclusion W7WC
W7WC hardness data that pertain to this work will be discussed only in this chapter. In the absence
of any deformation, the sintered samples have not stored any energy resulting from a deformation
processes in their microstructures. As a results of the reduction in grain boundary area, the W7WC
samples experience grain growth during annealing. The objective of the work on the W7WC alloy
was to examine the resistance (caused by the tungsten carbide particles) to grain growth, as well
as the evolution of the grain area. Although it is not possible to determine whether the tungsten
carbide phase affects grain growth from the EBDS analysis, it was interesting to note that tungsten
and tungsten carbide phases were seen not to grow at the same time throughout the successive
annealing stages. Performing isothermal annealings resulted in a decrease in the hardness values,
as anticipated (according to Hall-Petch relation). As shown in Fig. 29, the graphs seem to approach
a stabilization in hardness at around 478 HV1. It is especially evident in the graph of 1600◦C
annealing. An exception to this is the hardness increase from 28 to 37 days following the annealing
process at 1400◦C. While supported by the tungsten/tungsten carbide mean grain area evolution
shown in Fig. 42 and the SEM micrographs shown in Figures Fig. 31 thru 33, this hardness increase
was rather unexpected. As can be seen the tungsten mean grain area decreased while the tungsten
carbide mean grain area increased. As the temperature in the RHTH 50-150/18 furnace could
not be verified with a thermocouple, a simple explanation for this might be an offset in furnace
temperature (to lower temperatures than indicated) during the insertion of the 37 days sample.
In general, in order to be able to provide a more definitive conclusion regarding the hardness of
the W7WC samples, longer annealing times would have been necessary, which was unfortunately
not possible due to lack of time and ampoule creep limitations during long-therm annealings at
1600◦C.
8
Conclusion WK80
As can be seen from the hardness plot in Fig. 49, it is believed that thermally activated dislocation
motion occurs during annealing in accordance with Kuhlmann’s static recovery as described in
2.7, resulting in a decrease in defect density and, consequently, a decrease in measured hardness.
As shown in 4.2, SEM micrographs indicate that the grain structure is evolving to become slightly
larger and more equiaxed, nevertheless the appearance of a deformed, yet coarsened microstruc-
ture persists, ruling out the possibility of recrystallization as a restoration process. EBSD results in
5.3 and Fig. 75 demonstrate that the observed coarsening is a result of the elimination of HABs,
which leaves a network of immobile LABs in their place. EBSD results in 5.3.2, however, demon-
strated remarkably unchanged grain areas throughout the successive annealing steps in contrast
to what was observed from hardness results and SEM micrographs. In spite of the fact that 5.3.2 is
considered trustworthy with respect to ruling out recrystallization, its results regarding grain area
evolution appear to be less reliable. Additionally, the EBSD results indicate that the WK80 samples
exhibit a strong rotational cube texture that increases even further with the successive annealing
steps.
In 5.4, in-situ XRD results are in agreement with Kuhlmann’s static recovery results, thus ruling out
recrystallization. Except for the 1100◦C 200 peak, all FWHM graphs demonstrate good Kuhlmann
(Eq.[2.7]) linear recovery fits.
