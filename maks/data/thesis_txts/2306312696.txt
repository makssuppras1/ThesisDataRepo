Abstract
Advanced concepts like bioreﬁnery can help making sustainable technologies more economical. Mi-
croalgal cultivation is especially interesting in such concepts. Fast growth and a large biochemical di-
versity makes them interesting for producing biofuels and high added value products.
In the ﬁrst part of the thesis a bioreﬁnery concept is described and developed: lutein and sub-
sequently total proteins are extracted from green freshwater microalga Chlorella sorokiniana and
the resulting debris are used for the production of methane. A novel lutein extraction method
based on the organic solvent tetrahydrofuran was successfully developed and tested. Subsequent
total proteins extraction was performed via hydrolysis driven by the enzyme alcalase. Once these
extraction protocols were optimized, a large scale extraction was conducted in order to validate
the entire strategy. The biomass used for this ﬁnal validation had been generated at the pilot
plant microalgae facility that is located in the wastewater treatment plant of Kalundborg municip-
ality, where algae are grown on anaerobically treated industrial wastewater. The overall concept
proved to be valid and therefore it was possible to generate three products out of the same bio-
mass.
The second part of the thesis adds an extra dimension on the bioreﬁnery strategy described above.
It focused on batch cultivation of Chlorella sorokiniana in the same industrial wastewater from
Kalundborg municipality, with biogas injected into the system to test the ability of the microalgae
to remove CO2 in such conditions. Laboratory scale photobioreactors were used for the experi-
ments. Biogas compositions (%) of CH4/CO2 of 60/40, 80/20 and 95/5 and replacements of CH4
by N2 were tested. CO2 removals of 90% were achieved on average for all conditions. No neg-
ative impact of high CH4 or CO2 concentrations were found on ﬁnal biomass concentrations or
maximum growth rate.
Composition analysis of algal biomass was also performed i respect to
carbohydrates, proteins and pigments. Again no particular inﬂuence trend or inﬂuence of diﬀer-
ent gas concentrations or presence of CH4 were observed. Nutrient removal eﬃciencies of COD,
ammonium and phosphate were also measured and comprised 30-40%, 40%, and 90% on aver-
age.
By combining the two parts it is possible to identify and prove the overall validity of a novel, feasible
and cost eﬃcient bioreﬁnery concept based on the cultivation of C. sorokiniana in industrial wastewater.
Lutein, total proteins, methane, partially cleaned wastewater and biogas mostly free from CO2 are gen-
erated as ’products’.

1 Introduction
With the world relying on fossil fuels and climate change proceeding more and more, it becomes
also more and more important to ﬁnd appropriate alternatives. Sustainable technology, biofuels and
biotechnology are keywords when trying to solve these problems. The current low price of fossil
fuels (may 2016) also makes problems of these rising industries and technologies visible: high costs
of production and operation, unsustainable feedstock and many more. Therefore new or improved
concepts need to be developed and introduced - concepts like bioreﬁnery.
A bioreﬁnery makes use of primary, secondary and residual products from biological processes. This
is done in order to minimize costs, optimize value and lower environmental impact. The concept
originally was used mostly for energy related products. But now it is also increasingly used for
high added value products (HAVPs) like basic chemicals and biochemicals like feed, food and use in
cosmetics and medicine [22].
Microalgae can be especially interesting in such concepts. Microalgae are a diverse group of microorgan-
isms, capable of autotrophic photosynthesis. This group of organisms has a large biochemical diversity,
which makes them interesting for producing biofuels and HAVPs. [15]
The microalga Chlorella sorokiniana was used in this thesis to evaluate a bioreﬁnery concept based on
photoautotrophic growth in industrial wastewater with simultaneous CO2 removal from biogas. Addi-
tionally, the pigment and antioxidant Lutein - available in elevated levels in this microalga - and sub-
sequently proteins were extracted from C. sorokiniana. The resulting debris was used for biomethane
production. This enhanced bioreﬁnery concept should improve economics and environmental impacts
of microalgae cultivation. As side eﬀect of production of HAVPs, nutrients are removed from wastewater
and CO2 is removed from biogas.

2 Overall Goal and Scope
The main goal of this work is to evaluate diﬀerent bioreﬁnery strategies to be integrated with the cultiva-
tion of the microalga Chlorella sorokiniana in industrial wastewater.
The thesis is divided into two main chapters.
In Chapter 1 a strategy is described and developed in which lutein is ﬁrst extracted from the microalgal
biomass. Then the total proteins are extracted and ﬁnally biogas potential of the remaining debris is
evaluated. For the lutein extraction a novel protocol based on the organic solvent tetrahydrofuran was
developed. The total proteins extraction was performed using the enzyme alcalase. The optimization
of the protocol for lutein and subsequent total proteins extraction was performed on biomass grown
at the microalgae facility located within the wastewater treatment plant at Kalundborg municipality,
where C. sorokiniana is grown at pilot scale on industrial wastewater. For testing the eﬀect of adding
enzyme alcalase for total proteins extraction, fresh biomass grown at the laboratory using synthetic me-
dium was used. The extraction debris of these processes was used to evaluate the biomethane potential.
The second chapter aims to give extra dimensions to the bioreﬁnery strategy described above. C.
sorokiniana was grown in lab-scale photobioreactors on the same wastewater from Kalundborg and
synthetic biogas (CH4 and CO2) of diﬀerent compositions was injected into the batch system. The
aim of this set of experiments was to determine if this species/wastewater combination could be used
to remove CO2 from the biogas with the vision of using the resulting information for future biogas
upgrade strategies. Tests were performed to determine the CO2 removal, the inﬂuence of biogas on mi-
croalgal growth, nutrient removal eﬃciency and cellular composition (carbohydrates, proteins and pig-
ments).
Overall this work should proof the feasibility of this bioreﬁnery concept, identify weaknesses and provide
improvements on conceptual, biotechnical and engineering levels. For a graphical overview of goal and
scope, see ﬁg. 2.1.

3 Basics
3.1 Microalgae
Microalgae are a diverse collection of unicellular organisms that are found in marine and freshwater
environments, as well as sediments. Their common feature is the conduction of oxygen-evolving photo-
synthesis. Microalgal cellstructures can be prokaryotic or eukaryotic. Their shape, reproduction and
speciﬁc biochemical composition can diﬀer vastly. A high biochemical diversity and ability to produce
many diﬀerent kinds of metabolic compounds made microalgae popular in biotechnology. Generally
these organisms can be divided into many diﬀerent groups, of which some cannot only grow autotrophic-
ally but also mixotrophically or heterotrophically [9].
3.2 Microalgae cultivation
Microalgae are grown for many diﬀerent reasons, among which are feed, food, biofuel production, and
carbon capture, see section 3.3. The systems used for commercial exploitation can be divided into three
main groups:
1. Natural habitats
2. Closed photobioreactors (PBRs)
3. Open pond reactors
Large-scale microalgae production is generally limited to species that can be cultivated in open
ponds (main genera Arthrospira, Chlorella, Dunaliella). In such systems, risk of contamination
is high and the species either need to have high growth rates or need to be adapted to a speciﬁc
growth medium that also prevents contamination. For these reasons most microalgae cannot be
grown for longer periods in open pond systems. Another drawback of such systems is the relat-
ively high water use due to evaporation. Especially in regions with warm temperatures and high
light intensities, which are favoured for microalgae cultivation due to longer natural photoperiods
this does play a role. Closed PBR systems on the other hand can provide stable and selective
conditions. These systems create a selective environment for microalgal growth on a smaller scale
than open pond systems. Selective parameters include light intensity and period (if artiﬁcial light
source is used), pO2, pCO2, temperature, pH, mixing and substrate addition (for mixotrophic /
heterotrophic growth). Recent developments were aiming for minimizing costs (investment and
operation), energy consumption, substrate usage or generally environmental impacts and maximizing
volumetric productivity and yield of the cultures or simpliﬁcation of operation and handling. There
are many diﬀerent types of PBRs trying to meet these goals, among which are ﬂat or tubular,
horizontal, inclined, vertical or spiral, manifold or serpentine, hybrid, ﬂoating or bioﬁlm reactors.
For a small selection of PBRs, see ﬁg. 3.1. All information above are taken from Zitelli et al. (2013)
[56].

3.3 Biotechnology and microalgal diversity
One of the most discussed topics on microalgae in the public is biofuel production. Microalgae are
seen as secondary biofuel producers. They are a promising alternative to ﬁrst generation biofuels,
which are produced from food crops. Main problems of the development of this ﬁeld are costs,
as fossil fuels are still cheap, and sustainability issues. In biofuel production, microalgal lipid or
carbohydrate accumulation should be maximized. These compounds can be converted to fuels like
biodiesel (from lipids, proteins) or bioethanol (from carbohydrates). Combining primary production
and further usage of residues is called a bioreﬁnery. Other economically feasible markets for microalgal
products are mentioned below. However, a complex task is ﬁnding the most economical and feasible
combination of diﬀerent processes for upscaling of bioreﬁnery strategies. Another sustainability and
cost issue is the substrate used for microalgal growth. However, ertain species can grow on wastewater
and other waste streams, making the concept economically and environmentally more attractive.
[13]
Diﬀerent microalgae are used mainly for nutritional, nutraceutical, dietary supplements or pharmaceut-
icals, like cyanobacterium Arthrospira (Spirulina) (proteins, carotenoids, phycocyanin, vitamin B12)
[16], Duniella salina (β-carotene) [18] or Haematococcus pluvialis (astaxanthin) [32].
Generally high levels of high quality proteins make microalgae (especially Chlorella, Scenedes-
mus, Spirulina) a valuable alternative to protein as feed or food from animals or land based
plants and crops.
Other applications of proteins from microalgae are biodiesel or biofertilizer
[27].
The same goes for high quality lipids: ﬁsh oils (long chained polyunsaturated fatty acids) like omega-3
fatty acids can be found in high quantities in diﬀerent microalgal species.
5
==PAGE:16==
Another important nutritional quality of microalgae is the richness in vitamins (A, B1, B2, B6,
B12, C, E, niacin, biotin, etc.), even though for some vitamins, nutritional value is questionable
due to limited uptake by humans. Pigments - part of the lipid content of microalgae - like chloro-
phyll, carotenoids (astaxanthin, lutein, zeaxanthin) and phycobiliproteins are found in many mi-
croalgal species like Chlorella or Spirulina and promise a wide range of potential applications in
medicine (antioxidant, anti-inﬂammatory, neuroprotective, etc.) and industry (food dye, cosmetics).
[15]
Manipulation and selection of algal strains that are most beneﬁcial for the desired purpose is widely
applied in biotechnology. Selection criteria can be e.g. resistance to photoinhibition, high temperature
ranges, tolerance to high pH and alkalinity ranges, nutrient utilization eﬃciency or compatibility to
substrate and water composition. These selection criteria were designed for Arthrospira (Spirulina),
but can easily be adapted to other species [16]. Selection can be done by using e.g. microplate
screening [49]. Genetic manipulation is another important tool to further improve economical aspects
and to facilitate production of certain products from microalgae. Feasibility has been shown for
microalgae used in biofuel production, in order to enhance biofuel production [48] and certainly is
soon applied to other ﬁelds of microalgal cultivation.
Another important aspect to mention is that microalgae can alter their composition according to
environmental conditions, which can be used as beneﬁt in industrial production processes. Most of the
industrially important microalgal species grow autotrophically when exposed to light. However, some
algal metabolites of industrial importance are light regulated, e.g. uptake of glucose in some strains of
Chlorella is suppressed by light. [38] Also, higher algal growth rates lead to increased P uptake, due to
more P-rich molecules (like nucleic acids and energy carriers) [29]. For a general overview of products de-

3.4 Microalgal growth
Microalgal and bacterial growth are comparable in many respects. The main diﬀerence is, that
light is the energy source of metabolism during photo-autotrophic growth. Light as energy source
leads to a number of implications: Figure 3.3 shows the diﬀerent growth phases of a microalgal
batch culture. As the algae grow, light limitation becomes more and more important until there
are so many cells in the culture, that no light can penetrate through the biomass and growth
becomes stationary. Of course, other growth factors do also play a role. The main nutrients are
nitrogen and phosphorous, which have to be bioavailable, e.g. as NH4+ and PO43-, and diﬀer-
ent micronutrients like trace metals, vitamins, and others. Availability and concentration of CO2
and its dissolved equivalents (H2CO3, HCO3- and CO32-) do also play an important role in mi-
croalgal growth. Chemically, algal growth on NH4+ as nitrogen source can be simpliﬁed by eq. (3.1)
[26].

3.5 Microalga Chlorella sorokiniana
The non-motile green freshwater microalga Chlorella was chosen for this project because it has
a high growth rate, is tolerant to growth in waste-streams like wastewater and ﬂue gases [42].
Additionally, the strain of Chlorella sorokiniana was used in previous studies at DTU (PhD van
Wagenen [53] and others). It has been shown to resist high light intensities, high temperatures
and was extensively studied for biomass production and growth in wastewater [24]. C. sorokiniana
grows optimally at 37-38°C, pH 6.5-7.0 and biomass composition is approx. CH1.63O0.385N0.165P0.011
[37].
C. sorokiniana has many commercial purposes. It can produce up to 30% dry weight (DW) of
lipids, making it interesting for biodiesel production [41]. The microalga is able to withstand high
temperatures and light intensities. It produces protection agents to withstand such conditions such as
antioxidants, pigments and others. These are potentially interesting for commercial use. Relatively
large amounts of pigments lutein and β-carotene and antioxidant α-tocopherol (part of vitamin E)
can be found in C. sorokiniana [45]. A characterization study showed that proteins, carbohydrate,
lipids and total carotenoids comprised 68.5, 11.9, 10 and 0.69% of dry weight in C. sorokiniana [45].
Lutein is considered the most important product from C. sorokiniana, considering concentrations of up
to 10 mg g DW-1 [53]. Chemically it belongs to the group of xanthophylls, a carotenoid, and functions
as an accessory pigment in the photosystem and as photoprotective agent. It is used as food dye and
feed additive in agri- and aquaculture, but is also used as neutraceutical for treatment of macular degen-
eration (eye disease) and shows potential for prevention of certain cancers, skin UV-protection and early
stages of atherosclerosis [23].
The current main source of lutein is marigold ﬂowers. However, microalgae such as C. sorokini-
ana can be a promising alternative. The lutein content in microalgae in general aﬀected by many
diﬀerent parameters, among which are temperature, irradiance, pH, availability of nitrogen, salin-
ity, presence of oxidizing substances, and speciﬁc growth rates [15, 23]. It has been speculated,
that environmental stress like increased CO2 concentration or high light intensities enhances the
production of antioxidants like lutein in C. sorokiniana [45]. Also selection and manipulation of
algal strains can lead to increased lutein production of C. sorokiniana. C. sorokiniana is able to
grow autotrophically, heterotrophically or mixotrophically. Heterotrophic growth showed the highest
growth rate by far followed by mixotrophic and autotrophic growth [35]. As for other microal-
gae, the chemical composition can be inﬂuenced by many parameters, of which studies showed
inﬂuences of C/N substrate ratio and aeration on lipids [20], eﬀects of mixotrophy on lipid con-
tent [54], lutein content enhancement due to mixotrophy and random mutagenesis [23] and many
more.

3.6 Biogas
Biogas is a mixture of mainly CH4, CO2, and small amounts of H2O, N2, H2S, H2, NH3 and other
trace gases. It is the ﬁnal metabolic product of anaerobic digestion, a complex food web of anaerobic
organisms degrading organic substrates under exclusion of O2. The food web can roughly be divided
into hydrolysis, fermentation, acetogenesis and methanogenesis, see ﬁg. 3.5. Typical substrates are
organic wastes (e.g. municipal waste, agricultural waste), wastewater treatment sludge or energy
crops.
Figure 3.5: Methanogenic anaerobic food web, by Angenent et al. (2004) [11], letters indicate involved
type of species: (a) fermentative bacteria, (b) acetogenic bacteria, (c) acetogens and
homoacetogens, (d) hydrogenotrophic methanogens, (e) acetoclastic methanogens
The speciﬁc composition of biogas is depending on the composition of the substrate and can diﬀer vastly:
40-75% CH4, 25-55% CO2, 0-5% N2, 0-2% O2, 0-10% H2O, 0-1% H2, 0-1% NH3 and 50-50000 ppm H2S
[1].
Typical biogas from anaerobic digestion (63% CH4) has a caloriﬁc value of 6.5 kWh Nm-3 (lower
heating value [47]). Before it can be used directly in an engine or injected into the natural gas
grid, biogas has to be upgraded and cleaned, see table 3.2 for the Swedish standards for nat-
ural gas grid injection. A wide range of technologies is available for providing the requirements
for grid injection or other uses, e.g.: pressure swing adsorption (PSA), water scrubbing or mem-
brane separation [14]. All processes have energy losses in common. Usually, the energy expense
of CH4 puriﬁcation is accounted for 0.2 - 0.3 kWh Nm-3 [14], which is quite low in relation to

the energy potential.
CO2 with the largest fraction of undesired gases in biogas can also be
utilized for algal growth: it is a free carbon source and available in relatively high concentra-
tions.
3.7 Carbon ﬁxation by microalgae
Growth of C. sorokiniana (and other microalgal species) can be utilized for biological carbon ﬁxation
when grown autotrophically or mixotrophically. Diﬀerent types of gases containing diﬀerent amounts
of CO2 have been studied in combination with algal growth, like ﬂue-gas, exhaust-gas, or biogas. High
levels of CO2 in combination with even low amounts of H2S were shown to have inhibitory eﬀects on
growth of C. sorokiniana and C. spirulina [36, 33]. But in general, studies revealed feasibility of carbon
removal from biogas and ﬂue gas [43].

1 Goal and Scope
Chapter 1 provides a bioreﬁnery strategy, in which lutein and subsequently total proteins are extracted
from biomass of microalga C. sorokiniana. Algal biomass grown on industrial wastewater at an
algae pilot plant located at Kalundborg municipality was used. A novel lutein extraction method
based on the solvent tetrahydrofuran was developed and tested. The method was compared to a
dichloromethane based extraction method, followed by total proteins extraction using the enzyme
alcalase. For testing the eﬀect of adding enzyme alcalase for total proteins extraction, fresh biomass
grown at the laboratory using synthetic medium was used. The extraction debris of these processes
was used to evaluate the biomethane potential.
2 Materials and Methods
2.1 Kalundborg municipality - pilot plant description
The algal pilot scale photobioreactor wastewater treatment plant at Kalundborg municipality was
built in 2008. It is fed with anaerobically treated eﬄuent of enzyme producing industry Novozyme.
It is part of Kalundborg Symbiosis, an industrial symbiosis network, where diﬀerent companies
collaborate so that environmental impacts are minimized by using each others by- and waste-products
[6]. The pilot plant is a modular daylight harvesting system of 10 PBRs, each 4 m3 of volume,
can produce 10-40 kg dry weight (DW) per day and be operated in batch or continuous mode
[25].
2.2 Laboratory-scale microalgae growth
Chlorella sorokiniana was grown in a cylindric 20 l photobioreactor using 2 high power halogenic lamps
and 10 l MWC+Se growth medium, see appendix A. Harvesting was done after stationary growth phase
was reached by transferring the liquid to 50 ml Falcon Tubes, solid-liquid separation by centrifugation at
4500 rpm for 10 min. and freeze drying the algal biomass. Approximately 7.3 g of dry matter were har-
vested.

2.3 Lutein extraction from C. sorokiniana
C. sorokiniana was reported to contain 4.3 - 10 mg lutein per g DW [45, 23, 53]. These values were set as
benchmark values for the experiments conducted. The two methods were compared on the basis of mg
lutein g DW-1 and on lutein purity.
2.3.1 Method 1: dichloromethane as main solvent
The extraction of lutein using dichloromethane (DCM) as solvent consists of multiple steps and
was performed according Li et al. (2002) [40] using some minor adjustments. DCM is a colourless
liquid of sweetish, chloroform-like odour. It is highly volatile and can induce acute or chronic health
hazards and is hazardous to the aquatic environment [2]. Costs are currently 107 Euros l-1 [3] (May
2016).
First 2.5 ml of 10 M KOH solution containing 2.5% (v/v) ascorbic acid was added to 1 g of lyophilized
algal cells and incubating for 10 min at 60 °C and cooling it to down to room temperature. This
step converts lutein fatty acid esters to lutein. Ascorbic acid as an antioxidant is needed to prevent
lutein from oxidation. In the second step 10 ml of DCM were added to the mixture and the solution
was centrifuged for 5 min at 4000 rpm and the supernatant was collected. This step was repeated
until the extract is light yellow and all extracts are combined. Afterwards distilled water of the
same volume as dichloromethane was added and stirred in a beaker for 4-5 minutes. This step
was repeated until the water phase was transparent. The upper green part was removed and the
lower yellow part was transferred to a rotary evaporator, set to 40°C until all liquid was removed.
The resulting pellet was redissolved in 7 ml 85% aqueous ethanol (v/v). To remove lipid impurit-
ies, 1.75 ml hexane were added and removed afterwards. This procedure was repeated two more
times using 2 ml hexane each time. The upper phase containing hexane and the lipid impurities was re-
moved.
The extraction benchmark from Li et al. (2002) [40] was 90-98 % lutein purity.
2.3.2 Method 2: tetrahydrofuran as main solvent
The second method for lutein extraction was based on United States Patent No. US 6,262,284 B1 (2001)
by Frederick Khachik [34] using tetrahydrofuran (THF) as main solvent and was adjusted for use with
microalgae. THF is a colourless liquid with a ethereal odour. It is highly ﬂammable, vapours are explos-
ive in mixtures with air. It is mixable with water, highly volatile, light and air favour peroxide formation
and there are acute or chronic health hazards from the substance [7]. Cost are currently 141.5 Euro l-1
[8] (May 2016).
In the ﬁrst step, 2.5 ml of 10% KOH (m/v) ethanol solution was mixed with 10 ml THF and was
added to 1 g of lyophilized algal cells and left in a shaker for 2 hours. Afterwards, 2.5 mL 10%
KOH (m/v) ethanol were added, centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 10 min. and the liquid phase was
collected. The residual solids were redissolved in 10 ml THF, centrifuged again and the liquid was
collected and mixed with the previous supernatant liquid. The liquid then was evaporated using
a rotary evaporator at 40°C. The resulting pellet was redissolved in 7 ml of 85% (v/v) aqueous
ethanol. To remove the lipid impurities, 1.75 ml hexane were added, mixed well and removed again.
This procedure is repeated two more times by adding 2 ml hexane each time and remove the hex-
ane.

2.3.3 Quantiﬁcation of lutein
A high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) device (Thermo Scientiﬁc Dionex UltiMate
3000 UHPLC, USA) was used to quantify the lutein concentrations in the samples and to com-

compounds and their individual concentrations in mixtures by comparing the samples to stand-
ards of the desired compounds.
Lutein standards were supplied by DHI Lab Products (Hoer-
sholm, Denmark). The HPLC used a c8 reversed phase column (Eclipse Plus C8, Agilent Tech-
nologies) for pigment separation. Mobile phases were methanol/28 mM TBAA (tert-Butyl acet-

Samples and standards

2.4 Total proteins extraction from C. sorokiniana after lutein extraction
In order to extract total proteins from the lutein extraction debris, 9 ml of distilled water were
added to 1 g of lyophilized debris. Additionally, 0.24 ml of enzyme alcalase (Novozymes, Den-
mark) was added to the algae / water mixture, this accounts for 0.6 AU per g DW. The addition
of enzyme should increase organic matter solubilisation [44].
The pH was adjusted to 8 using
0.1 M HCl and 0.1 M NaOH. The mixture then was heated to 50°C for 3 h in an oven, stirred
every 15 min using a magnetic stirrer and pH was checked and adjusted every 30 minutes. After-
wards the mixture was transferred to 50 ml Falcon Tubes and centrifuged for 20 minutes and 4°C
at 10000g to separate solid and liquid phases. The liquid phase is collected and contains the pro-
teins.
To test the inﬂuence of adding alcalase for total proteins extraction, biomass grown in the laboratory
using synthetic medium (MWC+Se), see appendix A, was used. Quadruples of lutein extraction
debris using the THF method with addition of alcalase, quadrubles without addition of enzyme
alcalase and duplicate blanks containing only water and alcalase were tested for protein content.
As alcalase is a protein, the amount added has to be subtracted from the total proteins extraction
samples.

2.6 Biomethane Potential (BMP)
The BMP is a common tool in order to quantify the amount of CH4 that is being produced from a
certain substrate during anaerobic digestion. In principle, the procedure is mixing anaerobic digestion
inoculum, substrate and water in a bottle of speciﬁc volume, ﬂushing it with N2 to remove all other
gases, placing it in an incubator and measuring the CH4 evolution over time. The procedure was
adapted from Angelidaki et al. (2009) [10]. Thermophilic (55°C) BMPs were chosen to do because
of time constraints, as they are usually ﬁnished in a shorter amount of time. More precisely, BMPs
were done in BMP speciﬁc 1 l bottles, closed with a rubber cap and a metal lid. All BMPs were
done in triplicates: blanks (no substrate), control (Glucose), debris of algae after lutein and total
proteins extraction, microalgae grown in wastewater at Kalundborg municipality, a mix of algal treated
wastewaters, and mixes of both total proteins extraction debris and treated wastewater and microalgae
grown in wastewater and treated wastewater. For more information on the BMP and the setup, see ap-
pendix B.1.
The theoretically achivable BMP of glucose was calculated to be 373.55 ml CH4 g VS-1 (at 273.15 K)
using Buswell equation [19] applied to glucose. Additionally, the theoretical BMP of crude algal bio-
mass, using the formula CH1.63O0.385N0.165P0.011 [37], was calculated to 545 ml CH4 g VS-1 (at 273.15
K).
Gonzales-Fernandez et al. (2012) [28] reviewed methane production using microalgae as substrate
and found values of 160 to 600 ml CH4 g VS-1 in batch and at temperatures from 35 to 41 °C for
Chlorella.

4 Discussion
4.1 Lutein extraction method comparison
The THF based procedure introduced before is considered the better extraction method for lutein. It
had a 78% higher lutein yield compared to the DCM based procedure. Additionally to the worse lutein
extraction yield, the DCM based procedure caused handling issues.These problems did not occur when
using THF as solvent. The DCM extraction showed a decreasing lutein content over the course of the ex-
traction, which the THF extraction did not. Additional, THF is considered less harmful to human and
environment. Also much less solvent is needed per g biomass (33% saving for 3 repetitions of DCM addi-
tion). The DCM procedure on the other hand can be improved using more repetitions of DCM addition,
which would result in higher lutein yield, yet also resulting in using more solvent and increasing costs of
the extraction.
4.2 Total proteins extraction
A promising result was achieved by using the enzyme alcalase. More protein was yielded from the same
biomass. However, low protein yields from lutein extraction debris in comparison to crude biomass can
be explained by neutral pH values used for the total proteins extraction. Ursu et al. (2014) [50] found
that organic matter solubilisation is increased at pH values 12 and mechanical pre-treatment to over
50%.
4.3 Large scale extraction
The results of the large scale extraction were promising, too. The concept of lutein and subsequent total
proteins extraction from C. sorokiniana did work in principle. However, yields of both extraction meth-
ods are improvable. The low yield on total proteins could be impoved by increased pH values during ex-
traction, see above [50].
4.4 Biomethane potential
The quantitative values of the BMP presented in this thesis must be questioned. This is due to low CH4
production in the inoculum, leading to an overestimation of the control (glucose) BMP. Additionally,
most of the BMPs were not ﬁnished until the last measurement of CH4. The theoretical BMP of crude
microalgae of 545 ml CH4 g VS-1 was not achieved, indicating that the experiments were not ﬁnished.
Of course, the chemical sum of microalgae can also be a false assumption. The use of a constant
factor to correct all data based on the overestimation of the control BMP does not results in correct
quantitative values. It was used in this case to give a rough assumption on what values are to expect,
if the inoculum would not be unsteady and inhibited in terms of methane production. The qualitative
value of the BMP analysis is high though: it could be shown that these speciﬁc algal treated industrial
wastewaters inhibit CH4 production, which was also observed when mixes of wastewater and both
total proteins extraction debris and crude microalgae were tested. Towards the end of the experiments
though, the inhibitory eﬀect weakens and CH4 production started to increase. The underlying cause of
19
==PAGE:30==
inhibition of anaerobic digestion in this context remains unknown. A possible reason could be inhibitory
substances found in the anaerobically treated industrial wastewater. On the other hand it was shown
that algal debris after lutein- and total proteins extraction and crude microalgal biomass produce
similar amounts of CH4. However, the relatively high CH4 production of the debris can be explained by
low yields of total proteins. This increases the residual fraction of proteins and other compounds in the
debris.

1 Goal and Scope
Chapter 2 improves the bioreﬁnery concept introduced in the previous chapter by adding an ex-
tra dimension.
The main goal was to proof feasibility of growing microalga C. sorokiniana in
wastewater, injecting biogas as carbon source for autotrophic growth and check if diﬀerent biogas
compositions change algal growth parameters, nutrient removal eﬃciencies and cellular composi-
tion (carbohydrates, proteins and pigments). Simple laboratory scale photobioreactor systems were
set up to grow the alga. The same wastewater from the algae pilot plant at Kalundborg muni-
cipality was used as nutrient source. Simultaneously synthetic biogases of diﬀerent compositions
and a mix of N2 and CO2 for control experiments as carbon source were injected into the sys-
tem.
2 Materials and Methods
2.1 Wastewater characterization
The wastewater used as nutrient source for microalgal growth in the photobioreactor (PBR) experi-
ments is an anaerobically treated industrial wastewater from Novozymes wastewater and biogas plant at
Kalundborg Symbiosis [6]. The wastewater was ﬁltered (0.2 µm) before storage in order to remove tur-
bidity, which potentially hinders light penetration and thereby algal growth. The reason for using this
rather special wastewater is that at Kalundborg Symbiosis this wastewater is used for microalgal growth
on a pilot-scale level by the municipality. Wastewater composition was tested for ammonium (NH4-N),
phosphate (PO4-P) and chemical oxygen demand (COD) using Dr. Lange Hach cuvette kits LCK 303
(NH4-N), LCK 349 (PO4-P) and LCK 114 (COD). Concentrations were determined using a spectro-
photometer. Biological oxygen demand during 7 days (BOD7) was tested using WTW Oxitop devices.

2.2 Correlation of optical density and biomass dry weight
Optical density (OD) measurements are a reliable tool for determining algal biomass concentrations
[30]. In order ﬁnd the correlation between OD and algal DW, optical densities at 680 and 750 nm of
diﬀerent dilutions of inoculum algal biomass of C. sorokiniana and the DW of these dilutions were
measured in triplicates. The OD was measured using a Thermo Spectronic Helios ϵ Photospectro-
meter. The inoculum culture was grown in wastewater in the same system that was used in the
experiments.First, the weight of the dry ﬁlters was determined. Then for each dilution, the DW was
determined using 10 ml of the solution, which was ﬁltered with ﬁlters (GE Healthcare Whatmann
glass microﬁber GF/F ﬁlters, 0.7 µm poresize). The ﬁlters then were dried in 105°C for 24 h and the
DW of the biomass was determined by subtracting the weight of the dry ﬁlters and the DW of the
raw wastewater from the total mass of the ﬁlters after drying. Triplicates of one dilution step were
averaged and a linear regression was performed for both OD at 680 nm and 750 nm, see ﬁg. 2.1, with
adjustments made due to distilled water usage to reference the OD measurements (lack of wastewater).

2.3 Microalgae cultivation in photobioreactors
For the experiments of microalgae growth in wastewater and biogas injection a simple, closed
reactor system was set up, in which the biogas was circulated. For reasons of handling and to
prevent gas loss of the system, batch mode operation was chosen. One batch cycle was ﬁnished
when microalgae growth reached stationary phase. A 500 ml pyrex glass bottle was used to grow
the microalgae in wastewater and placed in front of 6x24 LEDs for constant light radiation. The
light intensity level was set to the highest setting of 2697 µmol Photons m-2 s-1 measured using
a light meter (LI-COR LI-250 A). At ﬁlling level 400 ml, the illuminated area of this bottle is
approx. 117.5 cm2. This part of the system is called the photobioreactor (PBR). The gas was
circulated using a rotary pump, another pyrex glass bottle (size depending on the CO2 content of
the gas, see below) was added to the system to increase the gas volume of the system and the gas
was injected into the wastewater using a ceramic diﬀusor. Other parts of the system include a gas
and a biomass sampling port, a gas inlet valve and a valve to connect the system to a vacuum pump, see
ﬁg. 2.2.

==PAGE:34==
Figure 2.2: Photograph and schematic of CO2 removal from biogas experiments
Before starting a batch, 390 ml of wastewater were added to the 500 ml pyrex glass bottle. Then all
valves were closed and a vacuum pump was attached to evacuate all gases from the system. After 10-20
minutes of evacuation, the valve connecting the system with the vacuum pump was closed and the valve
connecting the desired biogas to the system was opened until atmospheric pressure was re-established
and the valve was closed again. Afterwards 10 ml of inoculum microalgae grown in MWC+Se Medium,
see appendix A, were added to the system and the batch was started. This procedure also made it neces-
sary to use strong glassware and not algae growth speciﬁc reactor designs and materials (e.g. ﬂat-panel
PBRs).
2.3.1 Biogas injection experiments
The PBR system was used to conduct a series of batch experiments. The wastewater used for the exper-
iments was taken from a single 15 l tank, stored in 500 ml bottles and frozen at -20°C until it was used.
Synthetic biogases containing CH4 and CO2 of three diﬀerent compositions were injected into the PBR
system. These were:
• 60% CH4, 40% CO2
• 80% CH4, 20% CO2
• 95% CH4, 5% CO2
Additionally, all experiments were also done replacing CH4 with N2, consisting of the same levels as
before. All experiments were done in duplicates. Depending on the gas-composition, diﬀerent sized
gas-equalization bottles were added to the system in order to have about equal total amounts of CO2
available. For the 60/40 experiments, a 1 l bottle was added, for the 80/20 experiments a 2 l bottle
was added and for the 95/5 experiments a 10 l bottle was added, due to lack of a 8 l bottle. Biomass
concentration was measured approximately three times per day using the OD 750 nm. Gas samples of
5 ml were taken using a 6 ml plastic siringe with needle by using the gas sample port and transfered
to 6 ml gas vial (Gas Vac LBL exotainers, Labco Limited Lampeter, UK) only after gas injection
in the beginning and when the experiment was ﬁnished in order to minimize gas exchange with the
atmosphere. Previous test runs showed strong gas exchange with atmosphere if too many samples
were taken due to siringe handling etc. Wastewater composition was tested for ammonium (NH4-N),
phosphate (PO4-P) and chemical oxygen demand (COD) before and after each experiment using Dr.
Lange Hach cuvette kits LCK 303 (NH4-N), LCK 349 (PO4-P) and LCK 114 (COD). Concentrations
were determined using a spectrophotometer. Biological oxygen demand (BOD) could not be tested for
treated wastewater, as the amounts of wastewater needed for this test exceeded the amounts available.
No online measurements of pH could be installed into the system, so that no pH control was possible.

The pH value was additionally tested in the end of each experiment.
A carbon massbalance for each experiment was calculated. Parameters taken into account were CCO2,
Cbio and CCOD before and after each experiment. The massbalance in general analyses carbon fate and
is a quality tool for all measurements involved. For diﬀerent reasons, the quality of the carbon massbal-
ance in this particular system is considered low, so that it is not presented in results. For details and cal-
culations see appendix D.
2.4 Gas analysis
The gas samples were analyzed using a gas chromatograph (Thermoscientiﬁc Trace 1310 GC) using
two diﬀerent columns for diﬀerent gases. An HP-Plot/Q column (Agilent Technologies, USA, length
15 m, diameter 0.320 mm, ﬁlm 20 µm) was used for CH4 and CO2 analysis and an HP-Molesieve
column (Agilent Technologies, USA, length 30 m, diameter 0.530 mm, ﬁlm 50 µm) was used for
analyzing N2, O2 and CH4. Calibration was done manually using 4-6 diﬀerent mixes of standard
gases injected into the same 6 mL gas vials that were used for the experiments gas samples. The
6 ml gas vials contain 1 ml of air. This has to be accounted for when analyzing N2 and O2 by
testing at least 2 vials into which 5 ml of pure CH4 were injected and subtracting the resulting
values for N2, O2 for all vials. Linear regression was performed using the Data Analysis toolpack of
Microsoft Excel software. The data received from gas analysis and measurement was used to calcu-
late:
• CO2 reduction eﬃciency [%] is the percentage of CO2 removed during each experiments
• molar ratio O2 evolved / CO2 consumed
For CO2 reduction eﬃciency and absolute gas removal / evolution only the gaseous fraction of species
were taken into account. For removal / evolution of gases, the dissolved species were considered negli-
gible. For molar ratio O2 evolved / CO2 consumed, additionally to the gaseous fractions, also the dis-
solved species at Henry equilibrium were taken into account.
2.5 Biomass composition analysis
Microalgal biomass was tested for total carbohydrates, proteins and pigments for each of the experi-
ments.
2.5.1 Total carbohydrates
To extract total carbohydrates from lyophilized algal biomass, 5 ml of 95-97% H2SO2 were added to
50 mg of lyophilized algae, mixed and left for 30 minutes in 50 ml pyrex tubes. Afterwards, 45 ml
of MiliQ water (deionized water) are added and the mixture is autoclaved for 30 minutes at 120°C.
After ﬁltration using 0.2 µm, the supernatant is collected and the procedure is ﬁnished. To quantify
total carbohydrates, the supernatant was diluted 50 times using MilliQ water and transferred to 2
mL ion chromatograph (IC) vials. Then the vials were tested using an IC (Thermo Scientiﬁc, Dionex
ICS-5000, USA), equipped with an autosampler set at 10°C using a 70 min. retention time with a
Carbopac column (Carbopac PA1, Dionex, Thermo Scientiﬁc, USA). Detection was achieved with a
gradient ﬂow combining solvent A (MilliQ water), solvent B (Sodium hydroxide 1M) and solvent C
(Sodium hydroxide 100 mM) at a ﬂow rate of 1.0 ml min-1. For quantiﬁcation, the IC was calibrated
using carbohydrate mixtures of mannitol, fucose, rhamnose, arabinose, aalactose, glucose, xylose,

mannose and fructose (concentration 0.1 g l-1 each) with dilutions of 100%, 50%, 25%, 10%, 5% and
2.5%.
2.5.2 Total proteins / aminoacids
Aminoacid extraction was done using hydrolysis. For total proteins quantiﬁcation method, see chapter 1
section 2.4.1.
2.5.3 Total pigments
For determination of total pigments, ﬁrst 10 mg of lyophilized microalgae were mixed with 15 ml of 90:10
methanol:water (v/v) solution, mixed well and sonicated (Bransonic Ultrasonic Cleaner 3510E-MT)
for 10 min in an ice bath. Supernatant and residual biomass were separated using centrifugation for 10
min at 4000 rpm. The supernatant was collected, ﬁltered and analysed by HPLC (Thermo Scientiﬁc
Dionex UltiMate 3000 UHPLC, USA) using 75 min separation time. For calibration and quantiﬁcation
of pigment concentrations, pigment standards for fucoxanthin, neoxanthin, violaxanthin, zeaxanthin,
lutein, chlorophyll A and B and β carotene, by DHI Lab Products (Hoersholm, Denmark) were used.

3.2 Biomass composition analysis
3.2.1 Total carbohydrates
Total carbohydrates of C. sorokiniana grown in PBRs using diﬀerent gas compositions account
for 100-150 mg per g dry matter, or 10-15 % of algal dry weight, see ﬁg. 3.2. The detailed car-
bohydrate composition is shown in appendix G. On average, a high 8-10% of the algal DW is
glucose, other minor carbohydrates were fructose (1-2%), mannitol (1-2%) and galactose (1%). No
distinct diﬀerences between diﬀerent gas compositions are recognizable except for 95% N2, 5%
CO2 experiments: here the microalgae consist of 20-30% less total carbohydrates in comparison
to the other gas compositions. This is mainly due to a much lower glucose level for this experi-
ment, but it is not considered an important feature. All other carbohydrates tested have a similar
level to the other experiments. No negative eﬀect of CH4 on carbohydrate composition is observ-
able.

3.2.2 Total proteins
The protein content of C. sorokiniana accounts on average for 15%-25% of the microalgal dry matter. A
decreasing trend for decreasing CO2 is visible for CH4 and CO2 experiments, but not for the control ex-
periments, see ﬁg. 3.3.
3.2.3 Total pigments
The pigment composition of C. sorokiniana grown in PBRs using diﬀerent gas compositions is found in
appendix G. Chlorophyll a and b, followed by lutein accounted for the largest fractions of pigments in
the microalga. A relatively high amount of lutein of up to 2.1 mg lutein g DW-1 was observed. Small
amounts of β carotene, focuxanthin, zeaxanthin and violaxanthin were found, too. No vast diﬀerences
are found between diﬀerent gas compositions.

4 Discussion
4.1 Biomass and growth parameters
Considering biomass parameters, Cend and µmax did not show large diﬀerences between the diﬀerent ex-
periments. The parameters Cend and µmax are considered to be more reliable than Y and PV due to their
dependence on individual samples, see also appendix E. In comparison with van Wagenen et al. (2015)
[52], values of Cend are much lower. Possible reasons for these ﬁndings could be the non-ideal growth
conditions (no ﬂat-panel PBRs, no pH-control, etc.). Values of µmax were found to be consistently
high, indicating a well adapted culture to growth in wastewater and combined carbon uptake from bio-
gas.
4.2 Biomass composition analysis
The biomass characterization experiments did not show vast diﬀerences between the diﬀerent ex-
periments either. Concerning the pigments, a relatively high amount of lutein and other pigments
- compared to biomass from Kalundborg municipality - has been found in the PBR and biogas
injection experiments. According to Matsukawa et al. (2000) [45], it can be speculated, that an

increased lutein (and generally antioxidant) content in this context is found due to environmental
stress: high CO2 concentration, high light intensities and dynamic pH values. For protein content, no
ﬁnal statement on a trend of decreasing protein content with decreasing CO2 concentration can be
made, as control experiments showed diﬀerent results than CH4 and CO2 experiments. Overall, the
carbohydrate content of C. sorokiniana is a bit higher than proposed by Matsukawa et al. (2000) [45]
and protein content is much lower in comparison. Lipids were not tested in this work due to time con-
straints.
4.3 Nutrient removal from wastewater
An interesting feature of C. sorokiniana growth is the relatively high nutrient removal eﬃciency for
phosphoros. On average around 90% of phosphorus was depleted. The relative ammonium removal
eﬃciency is low, but when considering the absolute values, with inﬂuent wastewater ammonium
concentration being 325 mgN l-1, C. sorokiniana is considered to have a high ammonium uptake during
the batch experiments. Also the ability of C. sorokiniana to resist high ammonium concentration has
to be mentioned. In comparison to van Wagenen et al. (2015) [52], who used the same wastewater
(but with diﬀerent inﬂuent composition), the nutrient removal eﬃciency is lower. In their study over
99% removal eﬃciencies for both NH4-N and PO4-P could be achieved. The N:P ratio of 54.8:1 in the
inﬂuent wastewater in this thesis was much higher compared to N:P of 16.5:1 in their study, which
explains the diﬀerence in nutrient removal, especially for NH4-N. Low P availability was shown to
potentially limit microalgal N uptake [17], which supports the argumentation above. Concerning
overall nutrient uptake and removal it is also likely that low amounts of PO4-P were potentially
limiting algal growth.
The COD removal indicates mixotrophic growth of C. sorokiniana during the experiments. Mixo-
trophic conditions were expected to occur during light limited growth, after biomass has exceeded a
certain concentration when individual cells start shadowing other cells.
The COD removal on average is higher than the available BOD in the inﬂuent. This suggests that in
this system, parts of inert COD could be made available for uptake by microalgae. The observations
were made throughout the experiments. Hydrolysis performed by microalgae has not been observed,
hence diﬀerent sources for hydrolysis come into question as well: temperature, high light intensities,
pH dynamics, other involved organisms, freezing/unfreezing of inﬂuent wastewater and many more.
But no deﬁnitive statement of the source of the hydrolysis can be made.
The diﬀerent CH4, CO2 and N2 concentrations in the gas phase did not seem to have a high inﬂuence
on nutrient removal, though COD removal of N2 and CO2 gas injection in general is ca. 10% lower
than the CH4 and CO2 counterpart.
Control of pH in algal cultivation is usually done by injecting CO2 or bases/acids, as usually algae
are relatively sensitive to this parameter. For operational reasons, this could not be done in this set
of experiments. Suprisingly the dynamics of the pH value, which were expected due to high CO2
concentrations in the beginning and low concentrations in the end, did not seem to inﬂuence the
growth of C. sorokiniana. Even at very high partial pressures of 40% CO2, the resulting low pH did
not aﬀect microalgal growth. The pH increases during the experiment due to decreasing partial CO2
pressure.
Considering these ﬁndings, wastewater (and anaerobic treatment eﬄuent) is assumed to be a suitable
medium to grow C. sorokiniana, as also shown in other studies [52, 24, 43, 46, 55, 12]. Especially auto-
trophic microalgal growth is a suitable tool to make use of nutrients found in anaerobically treated eﬄu-
ent.

4.4 Gas parameters
CO2 removal is considered to be a reliable overall parameter. Removal eﬃciencies of over 90%
(average) could be achieved, proving the concept to work.
Only gas compositions of 5% CO2
seem to have lower eﬃciencies. This eﬀect can be explained by more CO2 being available in com-
parison to the other experiments see section 2.3.1. When assuming roughly similar CO2 uptake
rates for all experiments, the higher residual CO2 for 5% CO2 experiments makes sense.
The
molar O2 evolution to CO2 consumption ratio was calculated as gas measurement quality tool.
Ideally, a ratio of one for each experiment should have been reached ideally. On average though,
higher ratios than one were resulting for most experiments. A possible reason for that is the in-
trusion of air into the system or incomplete gas evacuation in the beginning, both increasing the
O2 content in the end or beginning of each batch, respectively. This eﬀect increases for larger
equalization volumes as chance for leaks or incomplete evacuation increases, too. This explanation
ﬁts well with the increasing molar ratio towards lower CO2 concentrations and higher equalization
volumes.
In comparison to other CO2 removal experiments using microalgae, reduction rates of ca. 90% can be ac-
counted for being high [46, 55, 21], even though other systems have been used in other studies (continu-
ous reactors, no diﬀusor used, 2-stage systems). Ideally, real biogas (including compounds like NH3 or
H2S) should have also been tested, as even minor concentrations of H2S were shown to have inhibitory ef-
fects on algal growth [33].
Overall, diﬀerent compositions of synthetic biogas and mixes of N2 CO2 did not seem to aﬀect the CO2
removal after all. Also no growth inhibition occured due to high CO2- or CH4 concentrations, which is
supported by Kao et al. (2012) [33].
A remark has to be made on the evolution of O2. In this case, no biogas upgrading (high purity
CH4) was achieved. The main purpose was to show that microalgae can be used as biological CO2-
sequestration process, which basically could be transfered to any gas containing CO2. For the case
of biogas, a relatively dangerous mix of CH4 and O2 is resulting from algal CO2 removal. This has to be
taken into account when designing any larger scale application.

Overall conclusion and outlook
33
==PAGE:44==
The overall experiments and strategies developed in the beginning of the thesis were successful. Con-
cerning the ﬁrst part, in which the extraction of lutein and total proteins and production of methane
from the resulting debris of biomass from microalga Chlorella sorokiniana were in focus, the following
conclusions can be made:
1. development of a novel method to produce three products lutein, proteins and biomethane
subsequently from microalgal biomass
2. a novel lutein extraction method based on organic solvent tetrahydrofuran showed higher yields
and purity compared to a generally used extraction method based on dichloromethane
3. a total proteins extraction method using the enzyme alcalase increased total proteins yield from
algal biomass
The second part, which included the cultivation of C. sorokiniana in industrial wastewater and
simultaneous CO2 removal from biogas, also was encouraging. The most important conclusions
are:
1. CO2 removals of 90% on average
2. diﬀerent concentrations of CH4, CO2 or N2 did not aﬀect growth parameters or biomass
composition to a large extent
3. relatively high levels of lutein and other pigments were present in algal biomass
4. high phosphorus removal, moderate ammonium removal due to high N:P inﬂuent ratio and
moderate COD removal
Findings of both chapters can be combined and integrated into an advanced bioreﬁnery concept.
This concept makes use of the waste streams industrial wastewater as nutrient source and bio-
gas as carbon source.
Potential products of the bioreﬁnery were found to be lutein, proteins,
biogas, nutrient removal from wastewater and CO2 removal from biogas. Alternative pathways
and methods for individual steps of this complex system can always be improved and will even-
tually lead to an optimized bioreﬁnery strategy with speciﬁc products and beneﬁts. All involved
processes help to improve economical beneﬁts and minimize environmental impact of microalgal cultiva-
tion.
The entire bioreﬁnery concept can be further investigated and improved:
• focus on additional HAVPs and co-products for increased economic feasibility
• inﬂuence of individual extraction steps on subsequent extractions
• optimization of presented extraction methods
• use of life cycle analysis and cost evaluation in order to identify weaknesses and strengths and
to choose trade-oﬀs carefully
• integration of oxygen removal strategy to increase feasibility of the bioreﬁnery strategy
• check tolerance of microalgal species to H2S
• inﬂuence of CH4 presence on all growth parameters
• test of other microalgal species, select using e.g. microplate screening [49]
• adress legal issues and public acceptance of products derived from biomass grown on waste-
streams

B.1 Materials and Methods
To each of the bottles, 67.45 g of thermophilic inoculum were added. The inoculum was taken on
april 8th 2016 from a biogas plant treating mixes of pig- and cattle manure and industrial organic
wastes. It was pre-incubated for 7 days before application for BMP at 55°C. For an overview of
the BMP setup, see table B.1. The amount of substrate added to the bottles was calculated in
order to reach an organic loading (OL) of 2.7 g VS l-1, which could not be reached for the treated
wastewater, see table B.2. Finally, the bottles were ﬁlled up to 300 ml working volume using tap
water or wastewater, depending on the experiment. The headspace volume is accounting for 850
ml.
To calculate the exact amounts of substrates needed to reach the OLR, ﬁrst the TS (total solids)
and VS (volatile solids = organic fraction of TS) of the diﬀerent substrates needed to be tested.
TS and VS were tested by weighing samples of protein extraction debris, microalgae grown in
wastewater (lyophilized), treated wastewater and thermophilic inoculum together with their con-
tainers. Then these containers were left in an 105°C oven for 24 hours and weighed again. The
diﬀerence in weight divided by the initial (= wet) weight gives the TS (%).
For the VS, that
dried sample is put in an 550°C oven for 2h, in which all organic compounds are volatilized. The
ﬁnal samples are weighed again and the VS (in % of the TS) can be calculated by subtracting the
ﬁnal weight after 550°C from the weight after 105°C and dividing it by the weight of the initial
sample.

CH4 from then on three times per week in the beginning, and two times per week towards the end of
the experiments. A Thermoscientiﬁc Trace 1310 GC with a HP-Plot/Q column (Agilent Technologies,
USA, length 15 m, diameter 0.320 mm, ﬁlm 20 µm) was used. The calibration was done each time
using min. triple measurements of standard gases 100% CH4, 60% CH4 and 5% CH4. Samples of 0.2
ml were injected into the GC. The calibrated GC then gives calculated values of %CH4 and has to be

D Appendix D: Carbon massbalance
D.1 Calculations
In order to calculate the carbon massbalance, carbon in CO2, COD and biomass were taken into
account. The mass balance was calculated on molar basis.
Carbon in CO2 was calculated as the sum of CO2 in the gas-phase and dissolved CO2 at equilibrium
with the gas-phase, using Henrys law. The moles in the gas-phase were calculated using the ideal
gas-law, the diﬀerent gas volumes, temperature, pressure and ideal gas constant.
Carbon in COD was calculated on the assumption of COD consisting only of glucose (C6H12O6) and
being completely oxidized according to eq. (D.1). Per mole of C in glucose, one mole of O2 is needed.
The amount of O2 can easily be calculated from the COD in mg O2 l-1, using the molar mass of O2 (32 g
mole-1).
C6H12O6 + 6O2 →6CO2 + 6H2O
(D.1)
To calculate carbon in biomass, the approximate biomass composition of Lamers [37] CH1.63O0.385N0.165P0.011
was used. This formula was used to translate the biomass dry weight concentration to moles carbon,
using the molar mass of 20.9 g molC-1. A sensitivity analysis on biomass composition was done addition-
ally using the chemical composition C106H263O110N16P for microalgal biomass of Ebeling et al. (2006)
[26]. The latter composition results in a molar mass of 33.5 g molC-1.
D.2 Results and Discussion
For results of the carbon massbalance calculations using the biomass composition based on Lamers
(2013) [37] see table D.1.
Table D.2 shows the biomass massbalance calculations using the biomass composition based on Ebeling
et al. (2006) [26].
The contribution of COD to the total carbon massbalance is considered neglible. The most important
contributors to the carbon massbalance are CO2 and biomass.
For a comparison of the ﬁnal mass balance using the diﬀerent biomass compositions see table D.3.
Using the biomass chemical composition of Lamers (2013) leads to a slight overestimation of the

the carbon content in biomass is higher, resulting in the overestimation of the output due to high
biomass concentrations. When using the biomass composition of Eberling et al. (2006), the opposite
is true: here the carbon in CO2 in the input of the massbalance has the largest share in the massbal-

The CO2 ﬁxation rate and the O2 evolution rate are not considered to be reliable parameters and were
calculated to give an idea about the magnitude of expected values. The unreliablity is due to sampling
technique: In order to minimize chances of gas leakage or air-intrusion during gas sampling, only one
sample in the beginning and in the end was taken. So the calculated rate is an overall rate over the
entire length of one batch and does not take into account any dynamics during diﬀerent growth phases.
Also, absolute gas concentrations in the beginning and end of each set of experiments (N2, CH4,
CO2 and O2) can be found in ﬁg. H.1. For CH4, CO2 and O2 the behaviour is not unexpected. For
experiments of CH4 and CO2, the concentrations ﬁt well to the predicted injected concentrations
(60/40, 80/20 and 95/5). Small amounts of N2 and O2 show small leaks, handling mistakes or
incomplete vacuum evacuation.
For N2 and CO2, sometimes small but recognizable amounts of CH4 were measured. No CH4 was
injected into the system and systems were evacuated before new gas was injected. An explana-
tion could be that small amounts of CH4 left in the siringe that was used to inject gases into the
GC. Another possibility is small amounts of CH4 being injected from leftover CH4 in the siringe
used for transferring the gas from the system to the gas vials. In these experiments especially
unexpected N2 concentrations can be observed. This indicates intrusion of air or (and more prob-
able) problems using the gas vials, in which the samples were stored. These vials contained small
amounts of air, which was accounted for during GC measurements and calibration. But as can be
seen, especially for N2 often the results do not seem to make much sense. In these experiments
though, observable CO2 reduction and O2 evolution do make sense. As N2 is not assumed to be
produced or consumed by microalgal growth, these ﬁndings were considered are not important for
the outcome of the experiments. But it should be considered when a new set of experiments is
planned.
