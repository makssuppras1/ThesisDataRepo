Abstract
This project focuses on the housing realm in Denmark, with the aim to evaluate its life cycle impacts and
main determinants. In the current situation where residential buildings represent around a third of the
carbon emissions of Denmark (Andersen et al., 2024), the need for improvements in the building sector is
rising, bringing attention to both the use phase, with the heating consumption, and the embodied impacts
comprising construction, materials, transport and end-of-life management.
In order to assess the environmental impacts with accuracy, a large amount of real-life energy data is
collected, covering the whole of Denmark, complemented with environmental data from Ecoinvent (2024)
and combined with occupancy data, centered on the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality. Using data analysis
techniques to assess this data, we confirm its validity through statistical comparisons, and progressively
develop an equation to determine the environmental impact per capita of any Danish dwelling based on
its energy label, building living area, heating system, building type and living area per capita.
We first establish regressions to estimate the heating consumption of a dwelling according to its energy
label and building living area. Afterwards, the impact intensity of the 5 heating systems encountered in
the municipality is assessed to determine the heating impacts of the municipality’s dwellings. Despite
their low consideration in most of the current regulations, the embodied impacts are also estimated using
simplified methodologies supported by the existing literature. The building type appears to be the key pa-
rameter in this determination. The combination of both life cycle stages leads to an overall environmental
analysis highlighting the prevalence of the heating impacts from a climate change perspective although a
majority of impact categories is mainly affected by the embodied contribution. If the equation evaluating
the environmental impacts of housing per capita allows for a simple identification of the parameters in-
volved, a variability analysis is implemented to understand the most influential ones. Strengthening the
results with a qualitative and a quantitative assessment, we conclude that the energy label and the living
area per capita are the determining factors in the overall carbon impact per capita. Finally, this work leads
to identify the most sustainable dwellings of the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality and their characteristics,
but a question remains: Is it good enough? The absolute environmental sustainability approach gives
an absolute reference to answer this question; Although close to the allocated carrying capacity, the most
sustainable dwellings already overshoot the planetary boundaries.
As a conclusion, new improvements are required to reach an absolute sustainable housing realm in Den-
mark, trajectories targeting mostly the energy quality and the surface per occupant are to be designed,
without excluding the other parameters which still have a significant importance in the overall life cycle
impacts.

Introduction
In the current situation where Denmark is one
of the world’s biggest polluter and consumer, as
the 6th country to overshoot the world’s resources
(Global Footprint Network, 2024), there is a need to
address the environmental impact associated with
the Danish lifestyle.
If Denmark wants to reach its objectives concern-
ing carbon emissions - be at net-zero emissions by
2050 (Danish Council on Climate Change, 2023) -
actions have to be taken to reduce the environmen-
tal impact of all realms, and housing should not
be forgotten in the process. Indeed, the building
sector represents 40% of Europe’s energy demand
as well as 37% of the energy and process-related
CO2 emissions in 2021 (UNEP, 2024), it is therefore
a major lever to the climate change mitigation chal-
lenge ongoing. An additional issue raised by cur-
rent tendencies is the population growth inducing
an increasing demand for housing.
To face this challenging situation, various enti-
ties get involved, including the government which
progressively regulates the building construction
field.
With the development of Building Regu-
lations, new houses have to follow stricter rules,
with a main focus on the energy consumption.
The latest regulation, BR18 (Ministry of Transport
Building and Housing, 2018) requires the construc-
tion of buildings rated with an energy label at
least A2015 which corresponds to a maximum of
30 + 1000
area kWh/m². These procedures underline the
efforts done by the Danish government towards
lower environmental impacts in the construction
sector; However, the focus being solely on the en-
ergy consumption, the relative weight of the em-
bodied impacts increases and will soon become
a critical point.
Consequently, it is of high im-
portance to consider all life cycle stages when as-
sessing the environmental impact of a residential
building.
Overall, the interest of this project is to study the
environmental impact of the current Danish hous-
ing sector, through real-life heating data linked
with occupancy data, and approximate it using key
parameters identified as highly influencing both
embodied and heating impacts.
Some publications already assess the Danish heat-
ing consumptions of households, but they mainly
focus on some dwellings (Ohms et al., 2019) or use
statistical values (Lavagna et al., 2018).
The ex-
isting literature concerning both heating consump-
tions and impacts therefore lacks of an individual
perspective on a large scale and support from real-
life data. Moreover, in most studies, heating con-
sumptions are approximated with general values
(Baron, 2023; Zimmermann et al., 2021) without
any real justification. This project also intends to
compare the energy label and the energy consump-
tion associated in order to assess and justify the use
of one or the other in the study.
When looking at the environmental impact, the
building construction sector has been deeply stud-
ied by Aalborg University, with the development
of the LCAbyg software and numerous articles
published (R¨ock et al., 2022; Zimmermann et al.,
2021). Most of them, however, concentrate on the
global warming potential and do not include de-
tailed data or analysis about other impact cate-
gories which gives an incomplete picture of the en-
vironmental impact of the building realm.
This
project aims to go further and provide a full
overview of the impacts associated with housing
on a large scale.
To bring the individual perspective, most projects
associate heating data with occupancy statistics
(Baron, 2023; Brejnrod et al., 2017) which leads to

interesting studies but still inaccurate compared to
using real-life data. Indeed, the main difficulty is
often to directly link the different kinds of informa-
tion collected which is done in this project to bring

In addition, parameters having a part to play in the
environment impact assessment of housing have
already been singled out such as the energy label or

Following this assessment of the current status
concerning the relationship between housing and
environmental impact in Denmark, the primary
objective of this project is to determine the envi-
ronmental impact per capita of housing in Den-
mark and the main parameters influencing it
through a thorough understanding of the life cy-
cle impacts of this realm considering embodied
and heating impacts. To achieve this goal, the fol-
lowing intermediate objectives need to be met:
1. Establish in detail the distribution of heating

collected.
2. Determine the environmental impact of heat-
ing a dwelling and study the main levers
which influence this impact by assessing the
heating consumption collected as part of ob-
jective 1.
3. Define simplified methodologies to evaluate
embodied impacts, keeping a broad perspec-
tive with various impact categories. Combine
heating and embodied results to estimate the
overall life cycle impacts per square meter.
4. Bring an individual perspective to the analy-
sis by combining heating and embodied re-
sults with the occupation data to estimate
the combined impacts per capita of housing
and identify the main parameters enabling to
approximate this impact knowing the main
characteristics of a dwelling.

Methods
The environmental assessment of housing in Den-
mark through real-life data involves computer pro-
gramming to collect and analyze the data on a
large-scale.
In terms of embodied impacts, a
large pan of literature already exists and statis-
tical values found in previous publications such
as the Build Report from 2021 (Zimmermann et
al., 2021) are used along with Ecoinvent databases
(Ecoinvent, 2024) to evaluate the environmen-
tal impact.
To give an estimation of the envi-
ronmental impact per capita per year of hous-
ing in Denmark, we developed Equation 1 com-
posed of multiple elements determined during this
project. The energy consumption per unit area (in
kWh f inal/m2/yr) is multiplied by the heating type
specific impact intensity per energy consumed (in
e.g.
kg CO2, eq/kWh f inal) summed with the em-
bodied impact per square meter and year (in e.g.
kg CO2, eq/m2/yr), all being multiplied by the liv-
ing area per capita (in m2/cap) to obtain the envi-
ronmental impact per capita per year of a Danish
dwelling. The different parts of this equation, iden-
tified by the colors encountered throughout the re-
port, correspond to the coverage of each of the 4
objectives.

Data collection
This project being based on real-life data, it re-
quires the collection of data.
The starting point
of such a task is the definition of the data sought
and necessary for the full implementation of the
project.
To assess real heating consumptions with the per-
spective of an individual analysis, we need the fol-
lowing data:
• Individual energy information: Energy label,
energy consumption
• Living area associated with the energy infor-
mation

An energy label is an indicator of the energy per-
formance of a building, determined based on pa-
rameters such as the insulation, the windows and
the heating system, currently from A2020 (the best
label) to G (the worst label). Denmark is a front
runner in the energy labelling of buildings with
a first requirement in 1997 (Laustsen, 2001) with
the aim to inform the current and future owners
or renters of a dwelling of its energy condition.
Identification information of the dwellings is not
required for the analysis itself but has revealed use-
ful to link the different databases as all the data
cannot be obtained directly from the same source.
Concretely, identification numbers such as the BFE
number1, the BBR number2 or the label identifier3
can be used; The address is another possibility
although identification numbers are preferred for
their uniqueness and the greater ease of use.
Obtaining such detailed real data can induce chal-
lenges as this might be considered as personal in-
formation. Public and governmental entities were
targeted as potential holders of this type of infor-
mation.
Energy data collection
A first database was studied, provided by a pre-
vious Master’s Thesis’ student (Baron, 2023), and
obtained from a contact with the Danish tax en-
tity SKAT (SKAT, 2024).
This database, de-
spite including almost all the necessary informa-
tion, was proven non-reliable after several char-
acterizations as it contained non-coherent data.
Apart from SKAT, the Danish Energy Agency with
the SparEnergi website (Energistyrelsen, 2023c) as
well as the BBR website (Bygnings- og Bolgreg-
istret) from the Danish Valuation Agency (Vurder-
ingsstyrelsen, 2024) are two interesting entities as
they both display building-related information on
their website. The EMO data service from Ener-
gistyrelsen (2019) finally provided us with a so-
lution: Collecting data using an Application Pro-
gramming Interface (API) through a Python code.
An API allows to communicate with a website and
collect information from it with a programming
software.
Multiple collections have been implemented with
APIs, geographically focusing on the Lyngby-
Taarbæk municipality to begin with as a contact
had already been established:
• A first one to obtain all the energy labelled
dwellings of the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipal-
ity providing the municipality number.
• A second collection to access additional infor-
mation such as the address, the living area,
building type and heating type associated
with each BBR number.
• A third collection adding the calculated en-
ergy consumption to the collected database
with the label identifier as an entry.
The calculated energy consumption is defined by
Energistyrelsen (2023c) as “a fairly accurate indica-
tor of the building’s energy quality” independently
of its use. It is calculated by an energy labelling
company and used afterwards to associate an en-
ergy label.
After collecting data for the Lyngby-Taarbæk mu-
nicipality, the same APIs have been used during
two months for the whole of Denmark resulting in
a database of 923 841 rows.

2.3
Environmental impact of heating
The first step to evaluate the environmental im-
pact associated with the heating consumption of
housing is to know the useful energy. Indeed, if
the heating consumption obtained with the data
collection is the final energy (energy transported
to the consumer, bought and received by the con-
sumer, in kWh), the environmental impact is calcu-
lated based on the heating energy really produced
by the heating system, meaning the useful energy
(in MJ).
From final to useful energy
The article by Sakai et al. (2018) focuses on the en-
ergy conversion chain and identifies the conver-
sion factor from final to useful energy as the ther-
modynamic efficiency. This efficiency depends on
the heating system used.
First, the district heating has an efficiency of 1 as
the heat received by the consumer is also the heat
he will actually consume, the losses occur earlier
in the process. To determine the thermodynamic
efficiency of the heating systems using fuel, gas or
oil, natural gas and wood pellets and be as consis-
tent as possible with the origin of the environmen-
tal data, we used Ecoinvent 2 (Ecoinvent, 2024).
This database provides the amount of primary
non-renewable energy for both burnt and non
burnt resources. The ratio of these values gives the
thermodynamic efficiency as the burnt scenario in-
cludes the burning process occurring between the
final and useful energy. Finally, SimaPro gives ac-
cess to the amount of electricity consumed for 1 MJ
of heat produced by a heat pump which enables
to calculate the thermodynamic efficiency of this
heating system. The conversion factors selected are
gathered in Table 2.

From useful energy to environmental impact
The transition from useful energy to environmental
impact is made using the Ecoinvent v3.10 database
(Ecoinvent, 2024) for both APOS and cut-off meth-
ods. APOS stands for Allocation at the Point Of
Substitution and is an allocation method from life
cycle assessment (LCA) which shares the burden
of a waste over the producer and the next user
whereas the cut-off method cuts the environmen-
tal impact of a waste which becomes burden free
(Ecoinvent Support, 2024).
The comparison be-
tween both methods is relevant in this project as
the district heating uses waste, among other re-
sources, to generate heat and is therefore handled
differently by the two methods.
Among the 24 000 processes detailed in the Ecoin-
vent databases, only 5 interest us to assess the im-
pact of the heating systems. The parameters taken
into account in the choice of processes are: The
heating process itself, the geographical location
(preferably Denmark, if not, Europe) and the scale
of the heating system (small scale for the boilers,
the furnace and the heat-pump, large scale for the
district heating). The processes selected are gath-
ered in Table 3.

Embodied impacts
If obtaining the heating impacts of many Dan-

sumption and the heating system, getting pre-
cise information concerning the embodied impacts
is more complicated.
Indeed, many parameters
come into play when looking at these impacts,
among which the type of building, the materi-
als used or the construction year.
The existing
literature mainly differentiates the embodied im-
pacts according to the building type (Zimmermann
et al., 2021); A handful of publications also fo-
cus on the energy quality of the building (R¨ock
et al., 2022) or the construction year (Lavagna et
al., 2018). Even though some are more complete,
most of these publications single out the carbon im-
pacts and make it more complicated to find rele-
vant papers to use. Overall, a precise assessment
of embodied impacts requires a case-by-case study,
therefore, the analysis of the embodied impacts is
hereafter a simplified assessment.
Two methods are presented and compared leading
to the choice of the method employed for the com-
bined environmental analysis.
Method 1

method is based on the Build Report from 2021
(Zimmermann et al., 2021) and the Ecoinvent pro-

cording to the LCAbyg software: Production and
transport of construction products, replacement of

Absolute environmental sustainability assess-
ment (AESA)
To complete the analysis and compare the results
obtained, we assess the environmental absolute
sustainability of the housing realm. This method
brings an absolute perspective by comparing the
actual results with planetary boundaries, a mea-
sure of safe activities keeping the earth in a sta-
ble state. The article by Brejnrod et al. (2017) uses
the egalitarian and the economic allocation prin-
ciples to define absolute thresholds for 12 impact
categories. Two categories concern climate change
impacts: Climate change (CC, temps.) and climate
change (CC, rad.). The first category considers a
temperature increase of 2◦C while the second one
limits to a 1 W.m² increase in radiative forcing, a
more stringent condition. These are the two thresh-
olds against which the global warming potential
per capita is compared in this work. Brejnrod et
al. obtain respectively, for the two climate change-
related categories, the allocated carrying capacity
of 287 and 152 kgCO2, eq/yr.
To calculate these
thresholds expressed in kgCO2, eq/yr, they multi-
ply the carrying capacities per capita by the aver-
age number of occupants in a dwelling, 2.6, esti-
mated by Statistics Denmark (2024).
In order to have the allocated carrying capacities
per capita, we divide their results by this same
average occupancy of 2.6 occupants per dwelling.
This results in the following allocated carrying
capacities:
110 kgCO2, eq/cap/yr for the climate
change (CC, temps.) and 58.5 kgCO2, eq/cap/yr for
the climate change (CC, rad.).
The analysis is done through a visual assessment,
by plotting the global warming potential per capita
of each dwelling against the living area per capita,
adding straight lines representing the allocated car-
rying capacities selected.

Data distribution
To characterize the data collected and have an in-
sight into its representativeness, we looked at the
population of the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality.
Out of around 57 800 inhabitants in 2022 (Ener-
gistyrelsen, 2024), the occupancy database covers
40 643 inhabitants, representing 70% of the munic-
ipality’s population.
The representativeness of the data also depends on
the fraction of the building types, gathered in Ta-
ble 6. The Danish Statistics entity (Statistics Den-
mark, 2024) gives access to the distribution of the
dwellings in the municipality in 2024 (Table 20
in supporting information S.2.1) which allows to
compare. Overall, both tables contain similar or-
ders of magnitude. The difference between the per-
centages for the student hostel category can be ex-
plained by the approximated categorization, many
student apartments are considered as part of multi-
dwelling houses instead of student hostels. It is
important to keep in mind that the datasets com-
pared are not exactly the same ones, the data col-
lected only concerns the energy labelled buildings
whereas the statistics are about the whole munici-
pality.
Comparing the distributions of energy labels is
also useful for assessing representativeness. As ex-
pected, most energy labels in the municipality fall
into the intermediate categories (C and D). Similar
trends are observed when compared to the distri-
butions from SparEnergi (Energistyrelsen, 2023c)
(Figure 32 in supporting information S.2.1).
Al-
though the comparison is not as good for the other
building types, the charts for detached houses
show a high resemblance. Once again, the datasets
compared present differences: If SparEnergi con-
siders only the buildings which have a valid label
(less than 10 years old), the collected data includes
all buildings which have been labelled since 2006.

Temporal evolution
The understanding of the collected data also in-
volves a temporal analysis, supported by Figure 4.
The cumulative curve when looking at the energy
labelled buildings has a regular slope which argues
in favor of a regular energy labelling of buildings,
with a slight increase in the last years. The com-
parison with Figure 33 in supporting information
S.2.1 which counts the dwellings, sheds light on the
type of building labelled: The significant increase
in recent years illustrates that a larger number of
multi-dwelling houses have been labeled recently
compared to single-family houses. Moreover, both
graphs highlight the recent emergence of A labels
and the gradual decrease in F and G labels.
We concluded from this short statistical analysis
that the data collected is well-representative and
can be used to assess the whole municipality.

Environmental impact of heating
After applying the method detailed previously, the
heating impacts are known for each dwelling of the
database. Boxplots are efficient ways to present re-
sults for such large amount of data as it displays
both the mean, the median and the first and third
quartiles.
Ecoinvent database: APOS or cut-off?
Figure 13 compares the global warming potential
from ReCiPe of the dwellings according to their
heating installation, when employing the APOS
or the cut-off methodology.
As expected, very
few differences can be identified but for the dis-
trict heating. The cut-off methodology does not at-
tribute any burden to the waste used for the Dan-
ish district heating, hence its carbon impact of 0
kgCO2, eq/m2/yr in this case. Apart from the dis-
trict heating, the other heating systems are handled
similarly for the carbon impacts.
The comparison was also made for other impact
categories, the plots can be found in supporting in-
formation S.3.1 for the freshwater eutrophication
(Figure 34), the ozone depletion (Figure 35), the
terrestrial ecotoxicity (Figure 36) and the land use
(Figure 37). If the freshwater eutrophication cate-
gory leads to a very similar conclusion as the global
warming potential one - the district heating is the
only heating system revealing a significant differ-
ence between both methods -, the land use cate-
gory makes another difference visible. Indeed, the
heating based on wood presents a significantly dif-
ferent land use when analyzed with the APOS or
cut-off methodology. The allocation of wood pro-
duction impacts differs and the APOS methodol-
ogy associates a larger share of responsibility to the
heat production with wood pellets than the cut-off
does. Moreover, Figures 35 and 36 show a change
in the electricity production, with a lower impact
associated with the cut-off methodology.
The APOS methodology was selected for the whole
environmental analysis in order to associate some
environmental burden to the district heating which
should not be considered as a zero-impact process.

Embodied impacts
The simplified methods implemented associate to
each building type an embodied impact, for multi-
ple impact categories. In the first method, this im-
pact is corrected using a randomly selected house
from the Build Report 2021 analysis (Zimmermann
et al., 2021) which introduces variability in the
results.
Tables 8 and 9 respectively display for
method 1, the impacts associated with the three
building types (detached house, terraced house
and multi-dwelling house) using the median of
the Build Report 2021 for the correction, and for
the method 2, the impacts corresponding to the
two building types (single-family and multi-family
houses). Colors have been added to highlight the
lowest impact (in green) and the highest impact (in
red), for each impact category.
Overall, for both methods, the multi-dwelling
houses are at the origin of less embodied impacts
per square meter which can be explained by the
lower proportion of impactful parts per square me-
ter for larger buildings, such as the roof. Never-
theless, the distinction between single-family and
multi-family houses is less pronounced in method
1, where values closely resemble those of terraced
houses, a phenomenon not observed when using
method 2.
To verify and compare the results of
method 1, the cut-off methodology has also been
applied and the results are gathered in supporting
information S.4.1 Table 23. The same trend is visi-
ble with detached house bearing most of the envi-
ronmental burden while the multi-dwelling houses
seem to impact less from an embodied perspec-

Selection of the method
Both methods present advantages:
• Method 1 can be applied to the same impact
categories as for the heating impacts
• Method 2 is based on a complete LCA which
is more consistent in terms of source
• Method 1 uses more recent data
But also drawbacks:
• Method 2 is based on a European study com-
pared to method 1 focused only on Denmark
• The representativeness adopted in the pa-
per used in method 2 corresponds to the EU
housing stock of 2010 (Lavagna et al., 2018)
• Method 1 is based on two sources using not
entirely coherent processes
• The Build Report 2021 used in method 1 con-
siders only houses built between 2013 and
2021 which have been A-labelled
• Method 2 differentiates only two building
types

warming potential and the energy label is clearly

previously described. Nevertheless, both labels E

describing the impact categories terrestrial acifica-

(Figure 38c), human toxicity, freshwater eutroph-

Key parameters of Equation 1
After discussing the overall LCA results over mul-
tiple graphs and analysis, the focus is now on the
determination of the key parameters and the eval-
uation of the life cycle impacts of housing in Den-
mark. This involves Equation 1 developed to this
end, giving an estimation of the environmental
impact per capita per year (Idwelling) knowing the
main parameters.
This equation can be applied to any dwelling in
Denmark to approximate the life cycle impacts as-
sociated with it. Knowing the energy label, the liv-
ing area of the building, the heating and building
types and the living area per capita, it is possible
to evaluate this impact, keeping in mind that the
establishment of this equation is built upon several
assumptions.

Variability: Quantitative analysis for the global

After visually identifying some influential mecha-

components of Equation 1 using statistics. These
have only been applied to the global warming po-

As explained in the methods section 2.5, we made
the assumption that the environmental impacts per
capita as well as the components of Equation 1 are
log-normally distributed. The actual distribution
of the components of the equation, for the global
warming potential impact category is displayed
Figure 27. This underlines the limitations of the
assumption made. Indeed, while this assumption
seems coherent for the living area per capita, the
thermodynamic efficiency and the impact intensity
are far from being log-normally distributed.
This allowed the use of Equation 3 from Hong et al.
(2010) to determine the geometric standard devia-
tion square (GSD²) of the total environmental im-
pact, corresponding to the variability, as well as
the contribution of the elements involved in the
formula. We started by comparing (ln(GSD))² of
the global warming potential per capita consid-
ering each occupant, directly evaluated from our
database, with (ln(GSD))² calculated with Equation
3, to evaluate the validity of the formula.
The two evaluation methods gave consistent re-
sults:
0.40 for (ln(GSD))² evaluated from the
database and 0.42 for (ln(GSD))² calculated with
Equation 3.
This validates the use of Equation
3 with a small discrepancy attributed to the log-
normal approximation of the components.

Conclusion
Main achievements
In conclusion, this work covered the Lyngby-
Taarbæk municipality in a detailed analysis of
the life cycle impacts associated with the housing
realm.
With a 70% coverage of the municipality’s in-
habitants, a large amount of data has been col-
lected: Energy data as well as occupancy data.
The combination of both led to precise informa-
tion about 19 605 dwellings and their 40 643 occu-
pants. In addition, the energy collection has been
extended to the whole of Denmark, representing
a 923 841-row database, to improve the quality
of the energy-related analysis. The data collected
was deemed representative after multiple statis-
tical comparisons with Danish sources (Statistics
Denmark, 2024; Energistyrelsen, 2023c).
To complement the use of the energy label, we es-
timated the heating consumption through an opti-
mization process. This led to the environmental as-
sessment of the heating part, using the Ecoinvent
APOS database, which emphasized the influence
of energy labels and heating systems.
If the heating consumption is considered as a
main driver of environmental impacts of housing,
the embodied contribution is not forgotten in this
work.
After comparing 2 simplified methodolo-
gies, the building type appeared important to ac-
count for in the study of the embodied impacts.
The combination of heating and embodied im-
pacts, first with a square meter perspective, under-
lines the importance of the heating consumption
although with a global warming potential around 7
kgCO2, eq/m2/yr, the embodied impacts are not to
be generally neglected as the heating impacts range
from 2.5 to 160.5 kgCO2, eq/m2/yr.
Bringing the individual perspective with the oc-
cupancy data, we arrived to the conclusion that
the most impactful dwellings are the ones hosting
only one occupant with a large living surface. If
a worse energy label increases the risk of high im-
pact per capita, this is not the only determining fac-
tor. Moreover, if the correlation between energy
label and global warming potential is clear, other
impact categories behave differently and demon-
strate the relevance to broaden the analysis. Com-
paring the relative importance of the two life cy-
cle stages considered, the influence of the energy
label was pointed out once again, a better energy
label inducing a lower share of heating impacts.
Despite this tendency, the average repartition bal-
ances with a relative weight of 53% for the em-
bodied impacts.
Although this relative analysis
informs on the share of responsibility across the
impact categories, the damage assessment brings
an absolute perspective putting forward the preva-
lence of the climate change categories, dominated
by the heating impacts.
Overall, the understanding of the life cycle impacts
of housing in Denmark went through Equation 1.
This sums up the components to account for and
the parameters involved, and allows for the esti-
mation of the impact associated with any Danish
dwelling.
To go further, two analysis were implemented: A
variability assessment and an absolute environ-
mental sustainability assessment.
Qualitatively, the energy label and the living area
per capita were identified as the most influential
parameters in the environmental impact par capita
determination although the heating type also came
up, with different intensities according to the im-
pact category. The quantitative analysis brought
validation to this visual assessment: The living
area per capita and the energy consumption are

respectively responsible for 47.8 and 41.2% of the
variability of the global warming potential while
the other components have a lower share of re-
sponsibility. The variability has not been quanti-

Finally, the absolute environmental sustainability
assessment highlighted the high requirements as-

None

perspective, considering the applied methodology
and allocation principles. Even if A2020-labelled

kgCO2, eq/m2/yr for the construction part would
be an interesting improvement compared to the
current situation, to complement by energy reno-
vation measures gradually bringing the building
stock to better energy labels.
Areas of improvement
This report is the result of 5 months of work, mul-
tiple aspects could be further developed with more
time allocated.
If the overall results apply to the whole of Den-
mark, the detailed analysis focused solely on the
Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality.
Obtaining occu-
pancy data for the 97 other municipalities would
enable a more thorough assessment of housing per
capita in Denmark.
In addition, as described in the methods section,
the embodied impacts have been estimated fol-
lowing simplified methodologies. The results un-
derscore the role played by the embodied impacts
which, although dominated from the carbon point
of view, deserve real attention considering the
other impact categories. Consequently, deepening
the embodied impacts’ analysis would be benefi-
cial and enable to have more insights into the influ-
encing parameters. This would require additional
data collection to obtain more detailed information
about the buildings such as the construction year
or the materials used. Getting in contact with LCA
commissioners could be an interesting solution as
LCA is now required for new buildings in Den-
mark. Having access to these LCA results would
add a case-to-case perspective for the construction
aspects.
In terms of analysis, the conclusions could be
strengthened implementing the quantitative vari-
ability assessment on other impact categories. In-
deed, assumptions and expectations were asserted
following the qualitative variability assessment but
the change in behavior between the impact cate-
gories could bring unexpected results.
Concerning the absolute environmental sustain-
ability assessment, the threshold considered was
taken from Brejnrod et al. (2017) based solely on
one sharing principle and using an approximation
of 2.6 occupants per dwelling. The existing liter-
ature mainly implements AESA for buildings per
square meter or per dwelling unit, and the result-
ing limits present a high contrast. It would be in-
teresting to establish a per capita allocated carry-
ing capacity, for different allocation principles, as
an absolute basis for comparison.
Eventually, reduction trajectories aiming at re-
specting the planetary boundaries for the hous-
ing realm in Denmark could be designed offering
guidance to policy makers.
The future-oriented
perspective could be included in the environmen-
tal analysis considering both energy grid improve-
ments as well as prospections into the future heat-
ing systems and building methods.
