Abstract
Segmentation of volumetric images is an active and highly relevant field of study, due
to the increased availability of volumetric imaging methods. However, as the imaging
quality improves the increased data sizes cause issues for voxel based segmentation ap-
proaches. Thus, deformable meshes become an attractive alternative, as they allow for a
sparse representation of the structures of interest and easy incorporation of geometrical
prior information.
In this thesis we explore how to use deformable meshes to perform volumetric seg-
mentation with a focus on the popular graph cut framework first introduced by Wu
and Chen. We investigate the limitations of the current approaches via numerical ex-
periments and an application real data. Furthermore, we modify the current methods
to handle the segmentation of multiple non-overlapping objects using quadratic pseudo
boolean optimization. Moreover, we extend the methods to fit hypersurfaces in 4D for
the segmentation of volumetric time series data. The extensions are evaluated with nu-
merical experiments and applications to real data.
The graph cut based method were shown to be very flexible and highly robust to
noise. Furthermore, the extension to multi object segmentation was found to perform
well on both artificial and real data. However, while the extension to 4D showed promise,
it was not able to give a good segmentation of the real test data. Nevertheless, more
work and experimentation is required to definitively determine the usefulness of this
approach.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The segmentation of volumetric (3D) images is an interesting and difficult task which is
still an area of active research [1, 2]. This has long been the case for the medical com-
munity [3], but in recent years 3D X-ray computed tomography (CT) has also become
widely used within the field of materials science [4]. As a result, good segmentation
methods are now more relevant than ever.
However, despite many advances in the
imaging process used to generate volumetric images, there is still a lot of room for im-
provement in current analysis approaches [4]. Furthermore, due to the high resolution
of modern data sets, they are often very large [4] – often requiring several gigabytes of
memory – which can make more classical voxel based approaches impractical [2]. This
is especially relevant for volumetric time series data1, which consists of a sequence of
several volumetric images, each of which needs to be segmented.
The task of volumetric segmentation is, for the purpose of this thesis, to partition a
volumetric image into multiple distinct regions. Usually, the purpose is to extract the
location and shape of one or more objects of interest – such as an organ in the context
of medical images. An extensive body of work already exists on this, and many methods
have been developed to solve the task of segmentation [1–3]. These range from simple
thresholding methods to more advanced models incorporating statistical and geomet-
rical prior information. Recently, the use of deep learning have also become popular
for volumetric segmentation, but still faces a number of challenges due to the size and
complexity of volumetric data [5, 6].
An attractive approach for volumetric segmentation is the use of deformable meshes.
Here, an explicit mesh is fitted to the surface of an object of interest, and the segmenta-
tion is then given by the regions inside and outside the mesh [2]. Since meshes only need
to describe a 2D surface, they allow for a very sparse representation volumetric objects,
which can significantly reduce the computational and memory costs compared to voxel
based approaches. Additionally, meshes make it relatively simple to incorporate geo-
metric prior information in the segmentation model, and are well suited for additional
processing such as object registration or finite element modeling. A popular method
to fit surface meshes is based on graph cuts and was introduced by Wu and Chen [7]
and later popularized by Li et al. [8]. The original method has since been the subject
of multiple extensions and has been applied to a wide array volumetric segmentation
problems [9].

Thesis Outline
In this thesis we will perform an exploratory study of the use deformable meshes for seg-
mentation of volumetric images. The main focus will be on the graph cut based method,
where we will investigate its performance for a set of challenging segmentation problems.
Moreover, we shall also explore how to extend the graph cut method further in order
to solve a wider array of segmentation tasks. Specifically, we will develop a variation
which can be used to segment multiple distinct non-overlapping objects. Furthermore,
we will extend the current formulation of the graph cut method to volumetric time se-
ries data. This is done by modifying the method from fitting 2D surfaces in 3D data
to fitting 3D hypersurfaces in 4D (space + time) data. To evaluate the performance of
the developed methods we will perform a series of numerical experiments which applies
methods to artificially generated data where the ground truth segmentation is known a
priori. Finally, we shall also apply the methods to a number real data sets in order to
assess their applicability to real world problems.
The structure of the thesis will be as follows; in Chapter 2 we give a general introduc-
tion to the topic of meshes from both a mathematical and computational point of view.
Next, in chapter Chapter 3, we review how graphs and graph cut algorithms can utilized
to fit meshes to objects in volumetric images. Here, we will cover both the standard
graph cut method, as well as an alternative approach based on quadratic pseudo-boolean
optimization which is able solve a wider range of segmentation problems. In Chapters 4
and 5, we detail our extensions of the graph based methods for first static and then dy-
namic (time series) data. Both of these chapters are concluded by a series of numerical
experiments which explores the limitations of the developed methods. Next, we apply
the methods to a number of real data sets in Chapter 6 and reflect on the quality of the
results. Finally, in Chapters 7 and 8 we provide a discussion of the work and results
developed in the thesis, suggest directions for future work, and give our conclusions.

Quick Note on Terminology
In order to ease the understanding of this thesis, we shall briefly clarify the meaning of
the most important terms here:
• Vertices and edges will be reserved to describe, respectively, the points- and
point connections of meshes.
• Nodes and arcs will used exclusively when describing the elements of a mathe-

• Volumetric images and volumes will be used interchangeably to describe a

0 ≤y < ny, and 0 ≤z < nz.
• Volumetric time series will be used to denote a series of volumetric images
I = {I0, I1, ..., Int−1} each acquired at different time points t0 < t1 < ... < tnt−1.
We will model I as a set of scalar values indexed by their (x, y, z, t) location where
t ∈{t0,t 1, ..., tnt−1}. Unless otherwise specified, we assume that tk = k.
• Time slices will refer to the individual volumetric images of a volumetric time
series.
• Voxels will refer to a single element of a volumetric image or volumetric time
series – e.g. a voxel is the value of the volumetric image I at (x, y, z).

CHAPTER 2
Meshes
In this chapter, we outline the basic definitions and notions needed to work with triangle
and tetrahedral meshes, as these will form the basis of the methods explored in this thesis.
We will begin by providing a mathematical description of what a triangle- or tetrahedral
mesh is. Then, we describe some common mesh operations and properties, which will
allow us to work with meshes in a rigorous framework. Finally, we will review some data
structures which will be used to represent meshes for computational purposes.
2.1
Triangle and Tetrahedral Meshes
In general, meshes can be seen as nothing more than a collection of points, edges, and
faces which describe the geometry of some surface, volume, or other higher dimensional
entity [10, 11]. For this thesis, we shall mainly focus on triangle and tetrahedral meshes,
due to their simplicity and high flexibility. Examples of triangle and tetrahedral meshes
are shown in Figure 2.1. We shall define triangle and tetrahedral meshes using simplices.

2.1.1
Topological operations
We now introduce the notion of neighborhood and incidence [12]:
• Two vertices vi, vj are neighbors if they are connected by an edge.
• Two simplices σi, σj of dimension p > 0 are neighbors if they share a face of
dimension p −1. For example, two triangles are neighbors if they share an edge.
• For a p-simplex σp and q-simplex σq where p < q we say that σp is incident with
σq (and vice versa) if σp is a face of σq. For example, a vertex is incident with an
edge if it is an endpoint of that edge.
Next, we define star, closure, and link [12]:
• The star, St(σ), of a simplex σ is the set of all simplices that have σ as a face. See
Figure 2.3(a).
• The closure, Cl(σ), of a simplex σ is the union over the simplex itself and all of
its lower dimensional faces.

2.1.2
Manifoldness
We now turn to the concept of Manifoldness. In its most general form, a topological
space1 M is a manifold if every point of M has a neighborhood which is homeomorphic
to an open ball in Rn [11]. If the space has a boundary, the above definition holds for
the interior points, while some care must be taken at the edge [13]. For triangle meshes,
we have the following theorem:
Theorem 2.1 (Theorem 5.2 in [10]). A triangle mesh is a manifold if:
• Faces only meet along edges or vertices.
• Every edge is shared by two faces, or one face if it is a boundary edge.
• Faces around a vertex form a single cycle.
Similar conditions exist for tetrahedral meshes [14, 15].
Thus, manifold meshes
correspond well with the intuitive understanding of a surface or volume, since we cannot
have self intersections or singularities as depicted in Figure 2.4. For the remainder of this
thesis, we shall not distinguish between manifold meshes with and without boundary.

2.1.3
Orientation
Lastly, we introduce the property of orientability and orientation. We shall focus on the
combinatorial definition of orientation, given in [12], as they easily apply to meshes. More
general definitions can be found in [13]. For surfaces, orientability relates intuitively to
whether we can assign an ‘inside’ and ‘outside’ to a surface. This is important, since it
allows us to assign a normal vector to every point on the surface which is consistent – i.e.
the normal vector does not suddenly flip direction when moving around on the surface.
Moreover, many operations and measures performed on (both triangle and tetrahedral)
meshes rely on orientability [12]. It should also be mentioned that not all surfaces are
orientable even though they may be manifolds. The most famous example of this is
likely the Möbius band [13] – shown in Figure 2.5 – which only has one side.
Figure 2.5: A möbius band, which is an example of a non-orientable surface.
For meshes, we can assign an orientation to every n-dimensional simplex σ. Let
⟨p1, p2, ..., pn+1⟩be the ordered list of vertices σ. The ordering of the vertices then
induces an orientation of σ [12]. Furthermore, we say that another ordering induces the
same orientation if it is an even permutation of the first ordering - i.e. we exchange an
even number of points. For example, for a triangle given by ⟨p1, p2, p3⟩, the ordering
⟨p3, p1, p2⟩induces the same orientation (we have done two exchanges), but ⟨p2, p1, p3⟩
does not (we have done one exchange). For any n-dimensional simplex there are only
two possible orientations. We will let [p1, ..., pn+1] denote an ordered n-simplex and
−[p1, ..., pn+1] denote the same simplex with the opposite orientation.
Furthermore, an oriented n-dimensional simplex also induces an orientation of its
(n−1)-dimensional faces. Letting ⟨p1, ..., pi−1, pi+1, ..., pn+1⟩be some (n−1)-dimensional

2.2
Data Structures for Meshes
In this section we describe the data structures we use to store the triangle and tetrahe-
dral meshes. In its most simple form, a data structure might simply be a list of vertex
coordinates and a list of which vertices span each triangle or tetrahedron. However, this
is impractical since no connectivity information is stored so we would have to perform
expensive computations in order to retrieve this. On the other hand, storing all con-
nectivity might also be prohibitively expensive in terms of memory use. Therefore, the
point of these data structures is to have an efficient way of storing both the connectivity
(or some sufficient subset of it) and vertex positions.
2.2.1
Half-edge Data Structure
Several different data structures for representing surface meshes have been developed,
see [10] for a short overview. The half edge data structure2 is a widely used data structure
for representing 2-manifold meshes. It is not restricted to triangle meshes - which is what
we will use it for - but can handle faces made of arbitrary polygons. It works by storing
each edge as two half edges, where each half edge is associated with a single face. For
each half edge we then store pointers to:
• The next half edge for its associated face.
• The previous half edge for its associated face.
• Its twin half edge.
• Its associated face.
• The vertex it points out of.
Figure 2.6 illustrates how half edges point to each other. It is convenient to require that
half edges for a face point in a counter clockwise direction - dependent on the choice
of face orientation. In addition, each face stores a pointer to (only) one of its incident
edges, and every vertex stores a pointer to (only) one of its outgoing half edges. This
data structure makes it easy to perform many operations which are common in mesh

2.2.2
Incidence Simplicial Data Structure
Like triangle meshes, there exists a multitude of data structures for storing tetrahedral
meshes [16, 17]. Inspired by the Deformable Simplicial Complex (DSC) method [12]
we will use the Incidence Simplicial (IS) data structure proposed by De Floriani et al.
[18]. This data structure allows efficient navigation - e.g. for retrieving neighbors - and
can store simplicial complexes of arbitrary dimension. However we restrict ourselves to
only storing 3-simplices (tetrahedrons) and lower. Additionally, we will only use the
data structure to store manifold meshes, in which case the memory requirements will be
linear in the number of total simplices stored [18].
Concretely, we store a list of every n-simplex for n = 0, 1, 2, 3, and a list of vertex coor-
dinates. Furthermore, every n-simplex σ stores a pointer to all of its (n−1)-dimensional
faces. Additionally, we store a pointer to an incident (n + 1)-dimensional simplex from
each connected component of the link of σ. For manifold meshes this relation has a
particularly simple structure:

CHAPTER 3
Graph Based Segmentation
In this chapter we outline the methods which will form the basis of our segmentation
approach. Generally, an object to be segmented will be represented with a mesh, and
the objective is then to deform this mesh to fit the object as well as possible. Most
researchers seem to achieve this using one of two methods [1, 3]. The first is deformable
models, where a mesh is iteratively deformed to minimize an energy functional, which
causes it to fit an object of interest. The second approach, which will be the focus of
this thesis, is based on graph cuts. Here, vertex positions are modeled as nodes in a
weighted graph, and the mesh deformation is then computed via a partitioning of this
graph using a graph cut algorithm.
3.1
Graph Cuts
We now detail how to use graph cuts to perform segmentation. This approach was orig-
inally introduced by Wu and Chen [7] and was later popularized by Li et al. [8]. Due to
the high flexibility of the method it has since seen numerous extensions – see e.g [9] for
a recent survey – but the basic premise remains the same. Our description will closely
follow the one by Li et al. [8], which details how to perform segmentations with so-called
terrain-like surfaces, but it can readily be extended to arbitrary surfaces [19]. We will
elaborate more on this in Section 3.1.1.
Let I : Ω⊂N3 →R be a volumetric image (henceforth referred to as a volume)
which can be viewed as a scalar function sampled on a discrete grid of size nx × ny × nz.
Furthermore, let x = {0, 1, ..., nx −1}, y = {0, 1, ..., ny −1}, and z = {0, 1, ..., nz −1}.
A terrain-like surface is then defined as a height function H : x × y →z which maps
each (x, y) coordinate to a height value. Thus, the surface will intersect each z-column
exactly once, and will have nx × ny vertices, see Figure 3.1. In order to control the
surface smoothness, we introduce a parameter ∆and require that the height difference
of neighboring surface points is at most ∆. Specifically, we want

3.1.1
Generalization to arbitrary surfaces
The description from the previous section is restricted to work with flat terrain-like sur-
faces. However, as described by Egger et al. [23] and Wang et al. [24], the method
can be readily extended to work with arbitrary surfaces. In fact, the only thing which
is needed is a set of points, a point neighborhood relation, and a sampling direction
for each point. Using a mesh, the vertices, surface normals, and vertex neighborhood
relation naturally fill these roles – as long as the mesh represents an orientable surface.
Specifically, to construct the graph G = (V ∪{s, t}, E) used for the minimum s-t cut,
we proceed as follows. Let every mesh vertex w correspond with the bottommost node
of an associated column C(w). Instead of a column being aligned with the z-axis it will
now be aligned with the vertex normal nw at w, and we shall henceforth refer to these
columns as normal-aligned columns. Furthermore, the nodes in the column, which also
represents candidate positions for w, will be placed along the direction of nw as follows

3.2.1
Minimizing non-submodular energy functions
Energy functions of binary variables are examples of pseudo-boolean functions, which
are functions from {0, 1}n to R. Pseudo-boolean functions which can be written using
only constant, unary, and binary terms – i.e. having the same form as the energy func-
tion in (3.17) – are denoted quadratic pseudo-boolean (QPB) functions. Minimization
of such functions is known as quadratic pseudo-boolean optimization (QPBO). For this
thesis we shall only consider the minimization of QPB functions, as it is known that the
minimization of any pseudo-boolean function can be reduced to the minimization of a
QPB function [30].
In general, the minimization of a quadratic pseudo-boolean function is NP-hard [27].
However, a lot work has gone into finding approximate or partial solutions to these [25,
27, 31] due their wide applicability. Many of these methods are based on the concept
of roof-duality, which we will not elaborate on further in this thesis – instead referring
to [32, 33] for a detailed theoretical description. We shall make use of the method origi-
nally developed by Boros et al. [34], as it is described by Kolmogorov and Rother [27].
This method – which is also based on roof-duality – is specialized for the case where the
energy function is applied to a graph. It should be noted that these methods are not
guaranteed to find a solution x which is a global minimum [27]. Instead, it is possible
that they will only find a partial assignment of x where the value of some entries remain
undetermined. However, for any entry xv which is assigned a value, we are guaranteed
that for any globally optimal x∗we will have xv = x∗

Extensions for Static Data
In this chapter we describe our extensions of the graph cut based segmentation for static
volumetric data. A large body of work already exist on this [9], but in this thesis we shall
focus on multi object segmentation, where we represent each object with a separate mesh.
Generally, we shall assume that the objects we are segmenting are convex – or at least
star convex – and have a smooth boundary. Thus, the basic procedure will be to first
build a graph for each surface mesh, as described in Section 3.1.1, and then optionally
add edges which represents inter-object interactions. The segmentation is then done by
minimizing the corresponding energy functions using either the graph cut or the QPBO
method.

this part, it will be more convenient to work with the QPB energy function E(x), as
described in Section 3.2. Let p be a point on the plane. Since the normal vector of the
plane, nP, points from cA to cB we can define that a point v is in front of the plane if
(v−p)TnP ≥0 and vice versa for behind. Now, iterate over all candidate plane positions

CHAPTER 5
Extensions for Dynamic Data
In this chapter we extend the graph cut method to work with dynamic volumetric time
series data, where data is represented as an ordered series of volumetric images indexed
over time. Several methods for segmentation of objects in 4D (space + time) data have
already been developed [48–54], including applications of the graph cut method [7, 56–
59]. These methods are all either voxel based, or process the data time slice by time
slice format. Specifically, the graph cut based methods place a separate mesh in each
time slice, and then add edges between nodes at different times to enforce temporal con-
straints. While this strategy has shown good results, it has the weakness that it requires
a good mesh initialization at all time slices, and it cannot handle topology changes (e.g.
objects merging over time). In this chapter, we will present a different approach, still
based on the graph cut method, which potentially solves both of these problems.
To motivate our approach, we will first consider a simple example. Assume we have
a series of 2D images from t = 0 to t = 100 showing two growing disks. As the disks
grow, they merge into a single object, as shown in Figure 5.1(a)-(d). To segment the

the object boundary at the given time. The details of this step will be explained in

aspects of the smoothness constraints affect each other is not currently understood.

We will describe two candidates for meshes which can be used as an initial hypersurface
in the above graph cut method. The first is a subdivided 600-cell which works as a

and its surface normals are therefore evenly distributed in all directions. The second
option is a hyperdisk which is a 3D solid ball contained in a hyperplane. The disk can
be aligned to only exist at one time slice whereupon all of its normals will point either

5.2.2
Hyperdisk
A hyperdisk is a 4D analog of a disk in 3D. We can define a disk in dimension n as an
(n −1)-dimensional ball contained in a hyperplane. For 3D this clearly results in the
usual 2D disk, and in 4D we have a solid 3D ball. The motivation for using a hyperdisk
is that we may want to place the initial mesh at the first or last time slice, since the
graph cut method is restricted to segment objects in the star-domain [19]. Going back
to the simple example in Figure 5.1, it is clear that the initial mesh must be placed at
a time where no subsequent object merges will occur. Therefore, the last time slice (or
first, if the tendency is for objects to split) is a simple but safe choice. However, if we
were to use a 600-cell, half of the vertices are essentially redundant as they have nowhere
to move. Thus, if we are only interested in one direction of movement, a hyperdisk can
be a better choice.

5.3
4D Cross Sections
We now turn to the task of computing cross sections – i.e. intersections with hyperplanes
– of tetrahedral meshes in 4D. We shall assume without loss of generality that we always
use the xyz-hyperplane at t = 0. Then, the problem is similar to that of isosurface
extraction where we consider the time coordinate as a function of the xyz-coordinates.
Several algorithms already exists for this problem [64], with many being based on the
popular marching cubes algorithm by Lorensen and Cline [65]. This algorithm has sub-
sequently been generalized to work with tetrahedral meshes [66, 67] and has been used
to visualize 4D data [61].
We shall use a slightly modified version of the marching tetrahedra algorithm. For
every tetrahedron in the mesh, we first test for intersection by computing whether each
vertex is above (t > 0), below (t < 0), or on the hyperplane (t = 0). Since the time co-
ordinates are stored as floating point numbers we say that a vertex is on the hyperplane
if |t| < ε for a predetermined threshold ε. If the tetrahedron intersects the hyperplane,
we then compute the intersection as one of the eight cases shown in Figure 5.6. The
fact that these are the only possible cases for tetrahedron intersection can be verified by
enumerating all permutations of vertices being either below, above or on the hyperplane.
After removing equivalent cases – e.g. one above and three below is equivalent with one
below and three above – one is left with eight distinct cases.
After computing all of the tetrahedron intersections, their union then gives the cross
section of the hypersurface [66]. It should be noted that the cross section can consist
of multiple disconnected components – something which may need to be handled before
doing additional processing. Our method differs from the regular marching tetrahedra
algorithm [61, 66] in that we handle vertices being on the hyperplane as a separate
case – i.e. we do not merge the cases in Figures 5.6(b) to 5.6(e) and we treat the cases
in Figures 5.6(g) and 5.6(h) as legal intersections. Furthermore, unlike in [61] we also
include the case where the entire tetrahedron is contained in the hyperplane, which is
modeled by counting all triangle faces as intersections.

6.1.2
Segmentation Pipeline
In order to use the developed methods we must first locate a rough center point for each
impurity, which is done with the following pipeline:
1. Threshold volume at τ = 1.25·104. The threshold value was determined manually
by visual inspection.
2. Find connected components of background voxels, discard largest connected com-
ponent, and fill the remaining ones with a voxel value of 2τ. This removes (most
of) the internal porosities of the impurities.
3. Perform a morphological opening with an approximated sphere [70] of radius 18.
This removes the fibers from the volume, leaving the impurities.
4. Threshold opened volume, and find connected components.
Each of the connected components (should) now correspond to one of the impurities,
and a total of 188 objects were detected. Due to the large shape variations, we also
need an estimate of the size and shape of each impurity. This is done by estimating the
image covariance matrix Σ [71] for each connected components. The eigenvalues and
eigenvectors of this matrix then gives a description of the object shape.
For segmentation we place a subdivided icosahedron at the centroid of each connected
component. To compare the effect of initialization we perform the segmentation with
meshes initialized as both spheres and then ellipsoids.

6.2.2
Segmentation Pipeline
As with the stone wool data set, we must first determine a rough center point for each
bubble. Furthermore, we must specify which bubbles are neighbors in order to enforce
non-overlap. We perform both of these tasks using the pipeline below:
1. Normalize to the range [0, 1].
2. Perform voxel-wise division with an estimated background image computed as the
median of every z-column.
3. Renormalize to the range [0, 1].
4. Smooth with an isotropic Gaussian kernel with a standard deviation of 1 voxel.
5. Binarize with a threshold selected by Otsu’s method [77].
6. Find center points as maxima of the Euclidean distance transform (EDT) of the
binary volume.
7. Remove all center points where the value of the EDT is less than 1 voxel.
8. If the distance of two center points is less than 0.8 times the sum of the EDT at
the points, remove the point with the smallest EDT value.
9. Mark all center points whose distance is less than 1.2 times the sum of their EDT
values as neighbors.
In total, 2922 center points were found with 14127 neighboring pairs. A subdivided
icosahedron was placed at all center points with a radius of half the value of the EDT at

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
In this chapter we discuss the findings of this thesis and the performance of the developed
methods. We will review the methods for static- and dynamic data in two separate
sections. In each section we first evaluate the performance of the method, review the
major weaknesses, and finally discuss directions for future work.
7.1
Segmentation of Static Data
In this section we discuss the methods developed for segmenting static data – i.e. the
graph cut method and the separating planes method using QPBO. The numerical ex-
periments, as well as the application to the stone wool and foam data, showed that
both methods were able to give good segmentations, even in the presence of signifi-
cant noise. Furthermore, the separating planes method was successfully able to perform
segmentations for multiple non-overlapping objects in sparse data. The computational
performance of both methods was also found to be satisfactory. Even when applied
to large problems, such as the stone wool and foam data set, a segmentation could be
performed in minutes.
However, both methods do require a good initialization. One aspect of this relates
to the fact that the shape of the final segmentation cannot deviate significantly from the
initial shape, as illustrated in the numerical experiments and in the application to stone
wool. Thus, it can be necessary to either perform a pre-segmentation of the objects
or incorporate a priori knowledge of the object shapes. Furthermore, one also need to
establish an approximate location for every object one wishes to segment. As this infor-
mation is not always easily available it represents one of the major weaknesses of the
graph cut based approach. Indeed, the data sets considered in this thesis required the
development of extensive initialization pipelines. However, it must be mentioned that
this property can also be a benefit since it allows a lot of control over the segmentation
output.
Another aspect of initialization is the fineness of the mesh, which ties into the more
general question of how to select the parameters for the graph cut based methods. As
shown in Section 4.3.1, the choice of a good smoothness parameter ∆is very dependent
on the mesh fineness. However, due to the way the segmentation graphs are constructed,
there is also a connection between ∆and the other graph parameters δstep and nstep.
Generally, the trend in the literature seems to be that parameters are either selected
69
==PAGE:78==
7 Discussion
manually [37, 39, 40], like in this thesis, or learned from a set of training data [38]. While
these strategies for parameter selection have proven to work well, they do represent a
weakness of the methods. The reason for this is that good parameter selection currently
requires either a lot of trial and error, good a priori knowledge, or a set of representative
training images. All of these narrow the applicability of the developed methods, and a
more robust approach may be highly beneficial in the future.
7.1.1
Directions for Future Work
As hinted in the previous section, an interesting direction for further research may be to
establish a more robust and automated way to select the segmentation parameters. A
good first step in this direction would be to reformulate the smoothness constraints for
the graph cut method in a way which is invariant to the object scale and the fineness of
the mesh. This would make the parameters less interconnected which in turn could make
it easier to select good parameters. Another benefit of this, is that it would allow one to
use meshes with varying- or adaptive fineness. As the computational work required for
the graph method is directly related to the number of vertices, decreasing the amount
of these in smooth regions would make it possible to segment larger object with many
fine details.
Regarding the separating planes method, it would be beneficial to extend the ap-
proach to more general surfaces. This would be necessary to handle more general seg-
mentation problems where the assumption that objects are (mostly) convex may not be
sufficient. Indeed, the liquid foam example considered in this thesis is technically already
an example of such data, since the walls which separate two bubbles is in general not
flat [75]. Currently, the only requirements is that the separating surfaces are orientable
as we need a way to test if a vertex is on one or the other side of the surface. The major
difficulty is how to incorporate deformation and / or movement of the separating surface
into the graph cut problem.
Finally, it should be investigated why the QPBO method seems to work well for
these problems even though several authors report problems with it [26, 27, 31]. One
approach may be to study other formulations of the graph problem, such as a one of
the reformulation as a linear optimization program from [32]. Here there is a direct
connection between the cases where QPBO gives a full solution and when the associated
linear program has integer solutions.
Since integer linear programming already has
an extensive body of work, it may be possible adapt existing results in a way which
establishes conditions on when the QPBO method will work.

7.2
Segmentation of Dynamic Data
We now discuss the 4D graph cut method for segmenting volumetric time series data.
While the numerical experiments performed in Section 5.4 showed promise, the appli-
cation to real data was not as successful. The main problem seemed to be that the
temporal resolution of the data was too poor, which meant that changes from one time
slice to another were too large for the 4D graph cut method to handle – even when
using an iterative approach. This does reveal a weakness with the method in that high
temporal resolution may not always be available. In these case it may be more beneficial
to use methods such as those in [55] which explicitly place a separate surface mesh at
every time slice.
However, if the temporal resolution of the data is high enough that a good segmen-
tation is possible, the 4D graph cut method does present a number of advantages over
existing methods. As mentioned previously, the 4D graph cut method is able to handle
adaptive topology, as was demonstrated in the numerical experiments and when applied
to the metal foam data. Furthermore, it is also possible to interpolate the shape of
segmented objects between time slices, since a 4D cross section can be computed for any
time value.
Finally, it is possible that this method might be better suited for an iterative approach
similar to a classic deformable surface, similar to the work in [48].
The graph cut
framework is of course attractive since it provides an efficient way to compute globally
optimal segmentation surfaces. Yet, the restriction that the object to segment must be
in the star domain [19] may be too restrictive. Indeed, it was observed for the metal
foam data that an iterative approach, which refined the initial segmentation, lead to
better results. Of course, such a conversion is not trivial, and we expand on this in the
next section.
7.2.1
Directions for Future Work
As mentioned, it could be of interest to convert the 4D graph method to an iterative
approach. This may be done using a strategy similar to [19] where the graph cut algo-
rithm is applied repeatedly, or as in [48] where a more traditional deformable surface is
used. Although, as explained in [12], iterative deformation of a tetrahedral mesh is far
from trivial if one wishes to avoid degenerate meshes. Despite this, we deem that the
potential benefits from an iterative approach may outweigh such difficulties.
Another important direction for future work will be to better understand how the
smoothness constraints affects the segmentation results w.r.t. to its spatial and temporal
smoothness. This should be investigated from both a theoretical point of view, but also
with additional numerical experiments. Furthermore, the smoothness constraints should
ideally be split into a separate spatial and temporal part, since we may have different
71
==PAGE:80==
7 Discussion
requirement on smoothness in space and time. Potentially, this could also allow us to
augment the segmentation approach by incorporating models describing the excepted
evolution of an object.
Finally, it would interesting to generalize the method of separating planes to 4D,
such that multiple non-overlapping objects could be segmented. However, the size of
the resulting graphs might mean that the current graph cut algorithm is no longer
practical. Thus it would make sense to explore algorithms such as the one in [78] which
can leverage parallel processing. Indeed, this may be necessary in all cases, if the 4D
graph cut method is to remain practical for larger objects over longer time scales.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis we performed an exploratory study of the fitting of deformable meshes with
graph cut based algorithms for the use of segmenting volumetric images. We explored
the limitations of existing methods by subjecting them to both a set of numerical experi-
ments with artificially generated data and applying them to real data. Furthermore, we
modified the current methods to be able to segment multiple distinct non-overlapping
objects using quadratic pseudo-boolean optimization. Moreover, we extended the cur-
rent method from 3D to 4D for the purposes of segmenting volumetric time series’, which
is an example of 4D (space + time) data. Based on the results of this thesis, we make
the following conclusions:
• Generally, the graph cut based methods were found to have excellent robustness
to noise. Even for objects with high levels of details or significant distortion, the
amount of noise seemed to have little to no effect on the quality of the segmentation
until the magnitude of the noise approached that of the data.
This was also
demonstrated with real data, where the methods were to give good segmentation
despite the noisy data sets.
• The graph cut based methods were also found to have good computational perfor-
mance. Even for large data sets with many object interactions, the segmentation
time never took more than a few minutes.
• The initialization used for the graph cut methods was found to be very important
and had significant impact on the quality of the resulting segmentations. Thus,
initialization had to be done either manually or by developing extensive pipelines
for each data set.
• The separating planes method with quadratic pseudo boolean optimization was
able to perform good segmentations of multiple distinct objects in sparse volumet-
ric images while ensuring no overlap. This improved the results of the standard
graph cut method significantly, which did not keep the objects separated.
• The 4D graph cut method showed promise when applied to simple artificial data.
Yet, it was not able to give a good segmentation of the real volumetric time
series data considered in this thesis. However, the temporal resolution of the data
was found to be too poor to give a conclusive judgment on the usefulness of the
4D graph cut method. Thus, more work and experimentation is needed for this
method.

APPENDIX C
Sufficient Condition for
Tranversal Hyperplane
Intersection
Let M be a manifold tetrahedral mesh in 4D an let P be a hyperplane. Assume without
loss of generality than P is the xyz-hyperplane at t = 0. Assume that no vertex of M
is on the hyperplane, i.e. that all vertices have non-zero time coordinate. We shall now
give an argument for this being a sufficient condition that the intersection of M and
P results in a manifold triangle mesh. Recall that a triangle mesh is manifold if (cf.
Theorem 2.1):
• Faces only meet along edges or vertices.
• Every edge is shared by two faces, or one face if it is a boundary edge.
• Faces around a vertex form a single cycle.
Since we require that no vertices of M are on the plane, all tetrahedron intersections
will be either a triangle or a quadrilateral – in the latter case, assume it has been split
into two triangles along an arbitrary diagonal, see Figure C.1. Henceforth, we shall refer
to these triangles as intersection triangles to distinguish them from the triangle faces of
the tetrahedra.

the tetrahedron is on the hyperplane. If the intersection is a quadrilateral, we assume it has
been split into two triangles.
By the above argument every tetrahedron contains either zero, one, or two coplanar
triangles sharing an edge. Since M was manifold, and therefore does not intersect itself,
this must mean that all triangles can only meet along edges or vertices. Thus, the first
