Abstract 
Nasal carriage of S. aureus is associated with an increased risk for nosocomial infections. Around 20% of the 
human population is persistent carriers of S. aureus, while the rest of the population never or only 
intermittently carries the bacterium. While the mechanism behind nasal S. aureus carriage is still unknown, 
both human and bacterial factors may play an important role. Studies have reported that culture 
supernatant of some S. epidermidis can inhibit S. aureus biofilm and thereby contribute to eradicate S. 
aureus from the nasal cavity. 
 In this project 56 S. aureus and 27 S. epidermidis isolates were obtained from frozen nasal swab samples. 
The isolates were identified by a multiplex PCR targeting the tuf gene and confirmed by three phenotypic 
tests; gram test, catalase test and coagulase test. Moreover were 40 of the 56 S. aureus isolates spa typed. 
The 56 nasal S. aureus isolates were screened for their ability to produce biofilm. Supernatant from 27 
nasal S. epidermidis isolates were tested on their effect on 10 of the best biofilm producing S. aureus 
isolates. Furthermore small drops of all 56 S. aureus isolates and 27 S. epidermidis isolates were spotted 
against each other in order to examine if there were any interaction patterns between the strains.  
The spot test of S. aureus and S. epidermidis showed that none of the S. aureus isolates were affected by 
the presence of any of the S. epidermidis isolates. Some isolates of S. epidermidis did however seem to be 
affected by S. aureus and showed very weak growth and lysis, while other isolates of S. epidermidis grew 
well. Those strains that showed weak growth were consistent and did not distinguish between which 
isolate of S. aureus was present. Biofilm formation was detected in all 56 S. aureus isolates, but the amount 
of biofilm measured varied from absorbance ~ 0.2-4. The effect of S. epidermidis supernatant from the 27 
isolates on biofilm formation in the ten chosen S. aureus isolates varied a lot. While previous studies have 
only observed inhibition of S. aureus biofilm formation by S. epidermidis supernatant, this project show that 
some S. epidermidis supernatants can also stimulate biofilm formation. The supernatants can be divided 
into inhibiting or stimulating supernatants although the results are not explicit. While some supernatants 
inhibit some S. aureus isolates they stimulate other isolates. Based on OD measurements and a spot test 
experiment with S. aureus culture and S. epidermidis supernatant, the S. epidermidis supernatants are not 
believed to modify biofilm formation of S. aureus by influencing growth rate. This however needs further 
and more thorough testing. The stimulation or inhibition is therefore believed to be caused by something 
affecting the biofilm production in the cells by a compound that is still unknown. 
The importance of S. epidermidis in S. aureus nasal carriage is still unknown. While some strains of S. 
epidermidis previously was believed to have a negative effect on S. aureus colonizing the anterior nares, 
this thesis consider that the opposite could also be the case for other strains.

Introduction 
Staphylococcus aureus is a facultative anaerobe, gram-positive cocci. It is part of the genus Staphylococcus, 
a genus containing more than 30 species, in which S. aureus is the most pathogenic species toward 
humans. It is a commensal bacterium, being a natural inhabitant of skin and mucosal surfaces in some 
humans. It is primarily found in the skin and the nasal cavity but can also be found other places at the body, 
here among in axillae, pharynx and vagina (Lowy, 1998). S. aureus is a versatile pathogen that is able to 
cause a wide range of infections. The bacterium is notoriously known for its ability to acquire resistance 
towards antibiotics, and today still new multi-resistant strains are emerging. Especially the methicillin-
resistant S. aureus (MRSA) has been an increasing problem in hospitals settings since methicillin was 
introduced in the 1960’es (Chambers & Deleo, 2009). Since MRSA carries resistance genes toward 
methicillin and often also other antibiotics, vancomycin has been one of the last resort treatments for 
MRSA. Unfortunately both vancomycin resistance and increased MICs (minimal inhibitory concentration) 
for the drug is seen at hospitals, creating new concerns about how to treat MRSA (Gould et al., 2012). 
S. aureus carriage 
The human nose encompasses a habitat for a complex microbial community. The micro-environment in the 
anterior nares is dominated by the three phyla Actinobacteria (~50%), Firmicutes (~25%) and 
Proteobacteria (~20%) in which the species Corynebacterium, Staphylococcus and Propionibacterium is the 
most prevalent (Yan et al., 2013). S. aureus are most often found in the anterior nares, or the so called 
“nose picking area” of the nose. The epithelium of the anterior nares is stratified, fully keratinized and non-
ciliated, and contains sebaceous glands and sweat glands. S. aureus adhere to the stratified epithelium cells 
directly or to the mucus associated with these cells (Kluytmans, Belkum, & Verbrugh, 1997). The 
transmission of bacteria to the nose often comes from the hands, and there is a strong correlation between 
nasal carriage and hand carriage. Beside direct contact, dispersal of S. aureus also occurs via airborne 
transmission, for example from individuals suffering from rhinitis from which high loads of bacteria are 
dispersed into the environment. One carrier in a household can impose their carrier state onto other 
members of the household. Studies show that 65% of carrying individuals within one household share 
identical genotype (Heiman F L Wertheim et al., 2005; Yan et al., 2013). 
Three pattern of S. aureus nasal carriage have been defined for healthy individuals; persistent carriage, 
intermittent carriage and non-carriage. The exact definition of a persistent carrier varies from study to 
study, although requires several positive cultures to be found in the same individual over time. 
Approximately 20% of the population is persistent colonized with S. aureus in the nasal cavity, while 
approximately 30% carries the bacteria intermittently and 50% are non-carriers (Heiman F L Wertheim et 
al., 2005). 
A study from 2009 by A. van Belkum et. al. suggests that only two S. aureus carriage patterns exists; carriers 
and non-carriers. In this study, 51 volunteers, here among persistent carriers, intermittent carriers and non-
carriers, were decolonized with mupirocin treatment and afterwards artificially colonized with a S. aureus 
inoculum mixture. They were then able to compare intranasal survival of S. aureus the groups in between. 
They found two interesting things; first of all the persistent carriers obtained and reselected for their 
originate strain, suggesting that S. aureus is highly host-specific. Moreover they could distinguish for only 
two carriage patters, not three, on the basis of the duration of the carriage and the S. aureus loads from the 
swap samples (van Belkum et al., 2009). 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
The study explained above is a good example showing that persistent carriers somehow are distinguished 
from the rest of the population, although the reasons are still unclear. Non-carriers appear to have an 
ability to clear the bacteria from the nasal cavity that carriers do not. Several attempts have been made in 
finding genetic factors associated with S. aureus nasal carriage. No definitive answers have been provided 
in these genetic studies, although a few genetic factors have been proposed to have an effect in individuals 
carrying S. aureus. It has been suggested that lower concentrations of interleukin 4 (IL-4) in serum may 
result in lower mucin production and decreased mucociliary clearance. Individuals with single nucleotide 
polymorphism in genes encoding IL-4 may therefore be predisposed for S. aureus nasal carriage (Emonts et 
al., 2008; Weidenmaier, Goerke, & Wolz, 2012). Other compounds of the immune system may play an 
important role in S. aureus nasal carriage. The carriage rate is much higher for immunocompromised 
people than for healthy individuals, indicating that the immune system could be an important factor for 
nasal carriage. For example several polymorphisms in the gene encoding glucocorticoid receptor are 
associated with variation in glucocorticoid sensitivity, insulin sensitivity and some autoimmune diseases. 
They also found a correlation between these polymorphism and S. aureus nasal carriage, probably due to 
some form of immune suppression (van den Akker et al., 2006). 
Not only genetic factors, but also bacterial determinants have been shown to either increase or decrease 
the amount of S. aureus, which could be important for whether a person is a persistent carrier or not. Since 
the nose is a natural habitat for a complex microbial community, S. aureus must compete with other 
species for a successful colonization. It has been described that colonization of S. aureus is inhibited by 
other bacteria, here among Streptococcus pneumonia and Corynebacterium. Moreover it has been 
suggested that Staphylococcus epidermidis can eradicate S. aureus, a mechanism which will be described 
later (Weidenmaier et al., 2012). 
 
S. aureus pathogenesis 
Infections caused by S. aureus, are ranging from mild skin and wound infections to severe ones such as 
septicemia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis, meningitis and toxic shock syndrome. The ability to infect all types 
of human tissue makes the bacterium an important pathogen. S. aureus infections are prevalent in both 
community and hospital settings due to its ability to survive outside the body for a longer period. Most 
often the infection begins from the skin or a wound and can from there spread to the blood stream and 
additionally other tissues. Except for the toxinoses such as toxic shock syndrome and food poisoning, 
virulence of S. aureus is considered multifactorial due to the combined action of cell surface proteins and 
secreted components (Lowy, 1998). 
Because of the enhanced use of antibiotics, especially in the health care system, antibiotic resistant strains 
have emerged. Especially methicillin resistant S. aureus (MRSA) have become a major problem in the 
hospitals all over the world, causing severe infections that are difficult to treat because of resistance 
towards all β-lactam agents. The resistance occurs via a penicillin-binding protein PBP2A encoded by the 
mecA gene. The mecA gene is located on a mobile genetic element called the staphylococcal cassette 
chromosome mec (SCCmec). Many MRSA strain have emerged by receiving this mobile element through 
horizontal gene transfer (Gordon & Lowy, 2008). Hospital acquired MRSA (HA-MRSA) have been an 
increasing problem over the past 40-50 years, and resistance to more antibiotics such as macrolides, 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
linosamides, tetracyclin and gentamicin have made some strains multi-resistant. Today, the risk of an MRSA 
infection in not only confined to patients with known risk factors or persons associated with the health care 
environment. Several small epidemic outbreaks have been observed in healthy individuals, by the so called 
community-acquired MRSA (CA-MRSA). The number of CA-MRSA infections have been increasing, and the 
S. aureus strains responsible for these infection are starting to enter the hospitals causing new problems to 
solve (Boucher & Corey, 2008; Gordon & Lowy, 2008). 
Although colonization of S. aureus of the nares is asymptomatic, nasal carriage have shown to be an 
important risk factor for invasive nosocomial infections. In a study by C. von Eiff et. al. from 2001, they 
tested nasal swaps obtained from hospitalized patient with S. aureus bacteremia. In 82 % of the patients 
they found the same strain in the anterior nose that had infected the patients’ blood (von Eiff et. al. 2001). 
Often is the bacteria introduced into the human body in hospitals during surgery, insertion of an indwelling 
catheter or by aspirations. Several studies have examined whether the use of mupirocin nasal ointment and 
chlorhexidine soap before surgery reduced surgical-site infection (Bode et al., 2010; Konvalinka, Errett, & 
Fong, 2006). The results have been contradictory, but no statistical significant conclusions have been made 
since the numbers of patients in the trials have been insufficient. A systematic review in which they 
collected result from studies testing the effect of mupirocin nasal ointment has been made. It seemed that 
the effect of mupirocin only applies to S. aureus carriers, in which they found a strong reduction. They are 
therefore recommending a screening process for nasal carriage prior to the given surgical procedure (van 
Rijen, Bonten, Wenzel, & Kluytmans, 2008). 
The strain of carriage is also important when considering the risk of infection.  A recapitulation of 10 
studies, in which the correlation between S. aureus infection and colonization with either MRSA or MSSA 
(methicillin susceptible S. aureus) was investigated, they found that colonization by MRSA increased the 
risk of infection by 4-fold compared to colonization of MSSA strains (Safdar & Bradley, 2008).  Although the 
number is increasing, several studies have shown that S. aureus nasal carriers are rarely (0,3-5%) colonized 
with MRSA strains (Kenner et al., 2003; Monecke, Luedicke, Slickers, & Ehricht, 2009; H. F L Wertheim et al., 
2004). 
 
S. aureus virulence factors are located on the accessory genome 
The ability to infect all sorts of organs and tissues are a result of the variety of virulence factors that the 
bacteria possess. Even though the bacteria has a relatively small genome of 2,5-2,9 Mbp, the genome is the 
reason for the high versatility of the bacteria. Around 75% of the S. aureus genome is highly conserved 
among isolates, and is called the core genome. Genes in this part of the genome are mostly housekeeping 
genes associated with central metabolism and maintenance of the cell.  The remaining ~25% of the genome 
is called the accessory genome. The accessory genome is highly variable and consists of mobile genetic 
elements such as bacteriophages, pathogenicity islands, chromosomal cassettes, transposons and plasmids. 
These are elements once obtained from other species via horizontally gene transfer. The frequency at 
which these elements move also varies, from almost no movements to frequent movement. It is on these 
elements that genes associated with virulence and antibiotic resistance are found (Lindsay & Holden, 2004). 
An example of one of these genetic elements is the aforementioned Staphylococcal cassette chromosome 
(SCC), a 21-67 kb genetic fragment located in the chromosomes of staphylococci.  There are five types of 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
SCCmec types carrying antibiotic resistance genes and four types of SCC non-mec without resistant genes. 
The most notorious are the SCCmec that carry antibiotic resistant genes, but the elements are also 
containing several other virulence factors. These SCC’s are highly developed mobile elements used for gene 
exchange among staphylococcal species (Hanssen & Ericson Sollid, 2006). Studies have shown that the 
emergence of CA-MRSA may be due to horizontal gene transfer of SCCmec from S. epidermidis to some of 
the less virulent strains of S. aureus, giving rise to new pathogenic strains for humans to fight (Michael Otto, 
2009). 
S. aureus virulence factors can be divided into three functional categories; those that mediate adhesion to 
cells and tissues, those involved in tissue damage, and those that protect the bacteria from the surrounding 
environment. 
Binding and adherence to host tissue 
Binding of S. aureus to nasal epithelial cell or other host tissue is mediated by cell surface adhesins. Most of 
these adhesins are in the protein family termed microbial surface components recognizing adhesive matrix 
molecules (MSCRAMMs). These proteins are covalently linked to the cell wall peptidoglycan of S. aureus. 
They all have a common structure; a N-terminal ligand-binding domain exposed on the outer side of the 
membrane, a membrane-spanning hydrophobic domain, and a C-terminal LPXTG motif which is responsible 
for anchorage of the protein to the cell membrane. S. aureus contains more than 20 genes encoding 
MSCRAMMs (Foster & Höök, 1998). 
S. aureus can bind and adhere to cells and component of the extracellular matrix, such as fibronectin, 
fibrinogen, elastin, collagen and von Willebrand factor. After surgery with insertion of medical devices, the 
implanted material will be coated with plasma, palettes and extracellular matrix proteins for the bacteria to 
adhere to (Foster & Höök, 1998). Examples of MSCRAMMs are the fibronectin binding proteins FnBPA and 
FnBPB that most strains express, which besides fibronectin also bind fibrinogen and elastin. They are of 
great importance during infection causing invasive forms of disease and persistence within host cells  
(Hauck & Ohlsen, 2006). S. aureus also express several proteins that can bind fibrinogen, best known are 
the clumping factors A and B (ClfA and ClfB), named clumping factors because they mediate cell clumping 
when in connection with human plasma. They are able to bind and aggregate platelets, which are 
important for the bacteria in some infections. ClfB also bind nasal squamous epithelial cells and to 
cytokeratin, which may be an important factor in S. aureus nasal colonization (Foster & Höök, 1998). 
In a study by Lindsay et al. they compared genes from S. aureus isolated from the nose and from invasive 
infection by a microarray analysis. They could divide the isolates into 10 linages that especially varied in 
genes associated with virulence. They saw no consistent differences in the gene content from the bacteria 
isolated from the nose compared to the isolates from infection. They could however see a difference in 
genes encoding MSCRAMMs and regulators that control their expression in the 10 bacterial linages. This 
means that each bacterial linage presents a unique combination of surface components and therefore must 
interact with the human host in unique ways. Most human carriers only carry one linage of S. aureus, which 
may be due to specific host factors well fitted for the particular S. aureus. They speculate that host 
variation may have driven the evolution of the linages (Lindsay et al., 2006). This tally with the observations 
made by van Belkum et al., in which the carriers always selected for their originate strain after eradicating 
and inoculating with a S. aureus mixture (van Belkum et al., 2009).

Toxins 
S. aureus is able to produce and secrete a variety of toxic compounds. Toxins are poisonous proteins that 
cause harm to the host. Toxins of S. aureus are virulent factor that can be difficult to associate with specific 
diseases or medical conditions. Examples of conditions that are certainly caused by toxins are toxic shock 
syndrome (TSS), staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) and necrotizing pneumonia. Most genes 
encoding toxins are located on mobile genetic elements, although a very few amount are found on the core 
genome (Grumann, Nübel, & Bröker, 2014). 
The mechanisms of which toxins harm the host vary from toxin to toxin and can therefore be divided into 
groups by how they function. 
The membrane-damaging toxins are toxins that causes pore-formation in the host membrane, and thereby 
leading to efflux of molecules and metabolites. Some toxins are general while others need binding to 
specific membrane receptors to function and therefore are more target-specific. An example of a 
membrane-damaging toxin that binds to a receptor is alpha-toxin or hemolysin-alpha. This toxin is encoded 
by the hla gene, which is located on the bacterial chromosome and it is therefore a toxin released by 95% 
of S. aureus strains. It binds the Hla receptor which is located on a variety of host cells including epithelial 
cells, erythrocytes, fibroblasts, monocytes, macrophages and lymphocytes. Upon binding the toxin  can 
cause disruption of epithelial and endothelial layers, which could be an important step in host invasion of 
the bacteria (Grumann et al., 2014). 
Another group of toxins are the enterotoxins. These are toxins that interfere with receptor function, but 
unlike the alpha-toxin do not cause damage to the cell membrane. The enterotoxins are so-called 
superantigens which mean that they cause a massive immune response.  They trigger a major T-cell 
response and release of cytokines directly by binding to MHC-II in antigen-presenting cells. Because the 
toxins interact with an element on the T-cell receptor which is relatively frequent, some toxins can activate 
up to 20 % of all T-cells. The enterotoxins are often involved in staphylococcal food poisoning, but one of 
the most severe toxin is the one causing toxic shock syndrome, which in worst case can end up with 
multiple organ failure (Grumann et al., 2014; Michael Otto, 2014). 
Besides toxins, S. aureus also secrete enzymes, mostly proteases that can either degrade host molecules or 
interfere with host signaling cascades. These enzymes are lipases, proteases and hyaluronidases that by 
their function may be involved in pathogenesis, facilitating the spread of infection by tissue destruction. An 
interesting example of virulent enzymes secreted by S. aureus is the coagulases staphylocoagulase and von 
Willebrand factor-binding protein. They trigger the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin which leads to fibrin 
clots on the bacterial cell surface. This is important during device-related biofilm infections, because the 
fibrin clots prevent the bacteria from being phagocytized and instead give rise to clumping and adhesion to 
the device  (Michael Otto, 2014). 
 
Biofilm 
Biofilm is defined as an aggregate of microorganisms embedded in a self-produced matrix of extracellular 
polymeric substances (Vert et al., 2012).There are several reasons why bacteria live in biofilms. First of all, 
the extracellular matrix is able to sequester and concentrate nutrients from the environment, for the 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
benefit of the bacteria living inside the matrix. Moreover will biofilm protect the cells against surrounding 
environment, such as UV exposure, metal toxicity, acids, dehydration and salinity, phagocytosis and 
antimicrobial agents. Even though some antibiotics can penetrate through biofilm it is not always efficient 
against the cells. Some cells living in biofilms change their physiological state, to starved, stationary phase 
dormancy. This is especially effective against antibiotics such as β-lactams which are effective only against 
dividing cells (Hall-Stoodley, Costerton, & Stoodley, 2004). 
Several forms of S. aureus infection are associated with biofilm formation on either host tissue, most often 
on bone or cartilage tissue, or on implanted material such as catheters or orthopedic devices. S. aureus can 
produce a multilayered biofilm, in which the bacterial cells live in a slime layer consisting of teichoic acids, 
proteins and extracellular DNA (Archer, Mazaitis, & Costerton, 2011). Biofilm formation takes place in three 
steps, see Figure 1. Once inside the body, the first step is attachment to human extracellular matrix 
proteins such as fibrinogen and fibronectin. This is done via MSCRAMMs, which as previously described is 
molecules expressed by S. aureus that are able to bind the host matrix molecules. This step is followed by a 
maturation phase. In this step the S. aureus cells accumulate and aggregate in a matrix that forms the 
characteristic mushroom-shaped structure. The main component of the extracellular matrix responsible for 
the cell adhesion is the exopolysaccharide PIA (polysaccharide intercellular adhesion) also known as 
polymeric N-acetyl-glucosamine (PNAG). Together with teichoic acids and other host and bacterial proteins, 
PIA forms the slimy extracellular matrix that the bacteria are embedded in. Not all biofilm is dependent on 
the production of PIA. Some strains produce biofilm without PIA by instead producing S. aureus protein A 
(Spa), fibronectin-binding proteins and biofilm-associated protein (Bap) (Archer et al., 2011).When the 
biofilm has been formed, single cells or cell clusters will detach from the biofilm. This step is very important 
during infection since it allows the bacteria to spread and colonize in other sites and tissues. The 
mechanism behind the detachment is not completely understood, but it most likely a combination of shear 
forces and production of phenol-soluble modulins (PSMs) and proteases that cleave MSCRAMMs  (Archer 
et al., 2011; Cassat, Smeltzer, & Lee, 2014; M. Otto, 2008).

Regulation of virulence factors 
The expression of S. aureus virulence factors is dependent on several environmental components such as 
peptide pheromones, temperature, nutrition and pH. These components are all signals that control 
different regulatory systems. The accessory gene regulator (agr) system consists of two transcription units 
expressed from the promoters P2 and P3, see Figure 2. From P2 the quorum sensing (QS) locus agrBDCA is 
expressed. It is a classical two-component system consisting of a histidine kinase (AgrC), a response 
regulator (AgrA) and a signaling molecule (AgrD) which in this case is a small peptide pheromone also called 
auto-inducing peptides (AIPs). AgrB is believed to be a protein processing the peptide hormone before it is 
excreted to the extracellular environment. Upon binding of the pheromones, AgrC auto-phosphorylates 
and hence activate AgrA. AgrA then activates transcription from P2 and P3.The P3 transcript of the agr 
system is the effector molecule RNAIII. Most virulence factors in S. aureus are controlled by the agr locus. 
RNAIII upregulates transcription of secreted proteins and down-regulates transcription of cell-surface 
proteins such as MSCRAMMs (Arvidson & Tegmark, 2001). The staphylococci peptide pheromone and its 
receptor can be divided into groups with different specificities. For S. aureus four different subgroups have 
been identified (group I-IV). These pheromone subgroups can cross-activate the agr signal within the same 
group and moreover cross-inhibit the groups in between (Jarraud et al., 2000; G. Ji, Beavis, & Novick, 1997). 
 
Figure 2: The agr system of S. aureus. The agrBDCA locus encodes a two-component system transcribed from promoter P2. agrD 
encodes the signaling molecule, a peptide pheromone, which is modified by AgrB. The pheromone is detected by the histidine 
kinase AgrC, which upon binding auto-phosphorylates and activates the response regulator AgrA. When activated, AgrA induces 
transcription from the promoters P2 and P3. From P3 the effector molecule RNAIII is transcribed which up-regulates excreted 
proteins and a lot of virulence factors. Figure from: (Cegelski, Marshall, Eldridge, & Hultgren, 2008)

The staphylococci accessory regulator (sarA) locus encodes the DNA-binding protein SarA. This protein is 
under some growth conditions required for increased expression from the agr and RNAIII promoters. 
Transcription of sarA operon is initiated from two multi-σ-dependent promoters, which allows SarA to be 
expressed during all growth phases. SarA also work in an agr-independent manner, binding to conserved 
regions called Sar boxes within promoters of several cell wall-associated proteins and toxins, either up-
regulating or down-regulating their expression (Bronner, Monteil, & Prévost, 2004). 
All three steps in the biofilm formation are regulated and influenced by a vast of regulatory mechanisms. 
Adhesion factors such as MSCRAMMs are up-regulated in S. aureus when the cell density is low and is 
under negative regulation by the agr system. The production of PIA is very important in the maturation of 
the biofilm formation. PIA is transcribed from the icaADBC operon. Studies have shown that the expression 
of the icaADBC operon are enhanced by SarA, and that biofilm production of sar- mutants are reduced 
(Archer et al., 2011; Beenken et al., 2004). Even though the agr system is very important in virulence 
regulation, it is not the case for biofilm formation. It has been shown that cells within a biofilm repressed 
the expression of agr under some environmental conditions (Yarwood, Bartels, Volper, & Greenberg, 2004). 
The expression of agr has on the other hand been shown to be important for biofilm dispersal. It has been 
speculated that the agr system functions as a switch between planktonic and biofilm lifestyle (Boles & 
Horswill, 2008). 
 
Staphylococcus epidermidis 
Staphylococcus epidermidis is a commensal microorganism of the human skin, colonizing epithelial cells 
predominantly from the axillae, head and the nares. S. epidermidis belong to the coagulase-negative 
staphylococci (CoNS), distinguished from S. aureus by its inability to produce the enzyme coagulase 
(Michael Otto, 2009). 
S. epidermidis is an opportunistic pathogen that predominantly causes infection in immunocompromised 
individuals. Infection of otherwise healthy people only happens after penetration of the skin, and S. 
epidermidis is the most frequent cause of infections of indwelling medical devices such as catheters. Since 
the bacterium is a big part of the normal skin microbiota, it can relatively easy be introduced into the body 
during inoculation or implantation of medical devices. Even though the infections are rarely life-
threatening, they can in worst case lead to septicemia and endocarditis (Vuong & Otto, 2002). 
The most important virulence factor for S. epidermidis is its ability to form biofilm. The biofilm formation of 
S. epidermidis resembles what is previously described for S. aureus. As for S. aureus, the biofilm formation 
makes the infection of S. epidermidis difficult to treat. Moreover has 10 different SCCmec structures been 
identified in S. epidermidis. Approximately 80% of the strains isolated from nosocomial infections are 
resistant to methicillin, and most of them are multi-resistant (Michael Otto, 2009; Vuong & Otto, 2002).

Interspecies interaction 
The pathogenesis of different bacterial species within medical microbiology has been studied since the 
famous Koch’s postulate. Since then, most researchers have focused on studying only single 
microorganisms, which has been providing a lot of knowledge within single species pathogenesis. With 
increased knowledge about human infectious diseases, scientist became to realize that not all infections 
are caused by a single species alone but also can be polymicrobial. Although many infectious diseases can 
be initiated by a single pathogen, others are originated or attributed to a complex environment of 
microorganisms. Virtually all microorganisms live in complex polymicrobial communities, often in biofilms 
attached to biotic or abiotic surfaces (Murray, Connell, Stacy, Turner, & Whiteley, 2014; Peters, Jabra-Rizk, 
O’May, William Costerton, & Shirtliff, 2012). The microorganisms in these communities have evolved 
synergistic relationships giving them a competitive advantage. Several types of interactions contribute to 
the synergistic relationship between bacterial species, see Figure 3. One mode of interaction is metabolite 
cross-feeding in which metabolic end-products of one microorganism is consumed by another member in 
the community. Another very important way for the microorganisms to interact is by signaling systems 
(quorum sensing), a mechanism by which many bacteria communicate and coordinate their activities, often 
in relation to upregulating virulence factors during infection. Also direct contact within microorganisms is 
promoting synergy. Via adhesins and specific receptors expressed on the bacterial surface they are able to 
aggregate, a function that is very important in biofilm formation (Murray et al., 2014). 
 
Figure 3: Interspecies interactions causing synergistic relationship between microorganisms. Figure from: (Murray et al., 2014). 
Interspecies interactions can be an important factor for pathogenic bacteria to cause infections. The 
interaction may be complex involving both pathogens, commensal bacteria and the host.

Interaction between S. aureus and S. epidermidis 
Synergistic relationships may also be important in nasal carriage of S. aureus. Commensal bacteria are 
known to be a part of the immune system, inhibiting colonization of pathogenic bacteria. A negative 
correlation between S. aureus and S. epidermidis has been reported, indicating a competition in the nasal 
microenvironment (Frank et al., 2010). 
In 2010 T. Iwase and his co-workers discovered that a subset of S. epidermidis strains secreted serine 
protease Esp that inhibited the biofilm formation of S. aureus and cleared the bacterium from the nasal 
cavity. The investigators isolated S. aureus and S. epidermidis from the nasal cavity of 88 volunteers, from 
which ~32% was S. aureus carriers. They could divide the S. epidermidis strains into two groups; those that 
inhibited S. aureus biofilm formation and those that did not. They found that the active compound in 
biofilm inhibition was in the supernatant of S. epidermidis. They compared the supernatants from inhibitory 
and non-inhibitory strains, and found that the non-inhibitory strains did not excrete a serine protease 
encoded by the gene esp. They confirmed their findings in several ways. First of all did they create an esp 
mutant and saw that it was unable to inhibit biofilm production. They also used a serine protease inhibitor 
and they observed that the biofilm destruction was blocked. Also purified Esp alone added to S. aureus 
biofilm showed destruction. They then introduced the inhibitory strains of S. epidermidis into the nasal 
cavity of voluntary S. aureus carriers and were able to eliminate the S. aureus. They also tried with purified 
Esp, and it also cleared the bacteria from the nasal cavity (Iwase et al., 2010). 
As a follow-up study a research group in Norway tried to find the correlation between S. aureus nasal 
and/or throat carriage and S. epidermidis carrying esp. They isolated 114 S. epidermidis and 74 S. aureus 
from healthy adolescents. They detected the esp gene in all 144 S. epidermidis isolates. They sequenced the 
gene from seven S. epidermidis isolated from the nose and seven invasive strains isolated from blood 
culture and compared to the published genome of the community strain S. epidermidis ATCC 12228. All 
seven nose isolates and the four blood isolates were identical to ATCC 12228, but three of the clinical 
strains had up to nine nucleotide differences. They then tested the supernatant from S. epidermidis on S. 
aureus biofilm formation and they used a semi-quantitative reverse transcriptase PCR to analyze the 
expression of esp. They found that 2/3 of the S. epidermidis supernatants inhibited the biofilm of S. aureus, 
but did not see a correlation between expression of esp and biofilm inhibition (Fredheim, Flægstad, 
Askarian, & Klingenberg, 2014). 
 
Project aim 
Understanding of the biological interactions between S. aureus and other bacterial strains may provide 
important information in development of antimicrobial agents or other treatment strategies against S. 
aureus. The purpose of this project was to study the bacterial interaction between different strains of S. 
aureus and S. epidermidis colonizing the human nose. The main focus was to investigate the effect of S. 
epidermidis supernatant on S. aureus biofilm and see if the pattern correlates with the previous studies by 
Iwase et al. and Fredheim et al. (Fredheim et al., 2014; Iwase et al., 2010). This included isolating bacterial 
strains from frozen nasal swab samples and verifying the bacterial strain, screening S. aureus isolates for 
their ability to produce biofilm and testing the S. epidermidis supernatant effect on the biofilm production.

Materials and methods 
Isolation of S. aureus and S. epidermidis 
In cooperation with SSI, bacterial strains of the species S. aureus and S. epidermidis were isolated from 
frozen samples of nasal swabs. The nasal swabs samples were taken in the anterior nares with a nasal E-
swab and transported to carrier medium. The swabs was a part of a large microbiome study conducted by 
SSI (Andersen et al., 2012). 
Since the nasal swabs samples were frozen at -80°C without added glycerol, obtaining sufficient amount of 
S. aureus and S. epidermidis isolates was difficult and took several attempts. In first attempt, scrapes of the 
frozen samples were taken with an inoculation loop and streaked on S. aureus chromID plates. On these 
plates S. aureus colonies will become blue and S. epidermidis colonies will not change color. 
Later, in the intent of obtaining more samples, the samples were defrosted on ice and aliquots of 40-80 µl 
were spread on both S. aureus chromID and blood agar plates. On blood agar, S. aureus become brown and 
S. epidermidis grey. Only samples in which DNA from S. epidermidis were found was plated. The DNA was 
obtained from the swab samples by making a chelex extraction, see protocol in appendix B. 
From each sample two colonies were chosen for further testing. If it looked like both S. aureus and S. 
epidermidis appeared from one sample two colonies of each bacterium were chosen. These colonies were 
restreaked on LB agar and were further tested. 
 
Identification of S. aureus and S. epidermidis 
To identify the bacteria isolated from nasal swab samples from SSI, four tests were done; PCR, catalase test, 
gram test and coagulase test. Both S. aureus and S. epidermidis are gram positive and catalase positive. S. 
aureus differ from other staphylococci by being coagulase positive. See protocols for the phenotypic tests 
in appendix B. 
All samples were subjected to a multiplex PCR made by S. Delgado et al. (Delgado et al., 2009). The PCR is 
build up by three primers targeting the tuf gene encoding an elongation factor; tuf-e which binds on S. 
epidermidis, tuf-a which binds S. aureus and tuf-g which binds both bacteria. The primer pair tuf-g/tuf-e 
results in a 370 base pair fragment identifying S. epidermidis and tuf-g/tuf-a result in a 530 bp fragment for 
S. aureus. See PCR protocol in appendix B. 
Esp gene identification 
All S. epidermidis isolate were analyzed for containing the gene encoding Esp serine protease. This was 
done by PCR with the primers gseA F157-177 and gseA R660-640  (Ikeda et al. 2004).  See PCR protocol in 
appendix B. 
spa typing of S. aureus isolates 
All 56 isolates of S. aureus were sent to SSI to be spa typed. This is done by sequencing the spa gene 
encoding the surface protein Spa (S. aureus protein A). Of the 56 isolates sent to typing, 40 of the isolates 
were managed to be typed, while 16 is still lacking to be typed.

Spot test 
To test the interaction between S. aureus and S. epidermidis a spot test were conducted. All S. aureus were 
spotted against all S. epidermidis. The isolates were grown overnight in 96 well microtiter plates. A pin-
replicator was used to spot the S. aureus isolates against the S. epidermidis isolates on LB agar in omni 
trays. First one isolate was spotted and dried for a few minutes, and then the other isolate were spotted as 
close to the other as possible, without the isolates touching each other. The trays were then observed for 
three days. 
Because S. epidermidis grows a little slower than S. aureus, the same test was made with that variation that 
the S. epidermidis isolates were spotted 24 hours before the S. aureus isolate. 
To see if quorum sensing was important in the interaction between the two species, a S. aureus QS-
deficient mutant (agrC mutant) and its wild-type strain S. aureus JE2 were spotted against the 27 S. 
epidermidis isolates. 
 
Crystal violet biofilm assay in 96 well microtiter plate 
When all isolates of S. aureus were verified, they were screened for their ability to produce biofilm. This 
was done in a microtiter plate setup, see protocol in appendix B. A total of 56 S. aureus isolates were 
tested. The isolates were grown over night in TSB media. Dilution to OD600 ~0.05 was made for all isolates 
in TSB supplemented with 1% glucose. 200 µl samples were added to the wells of the microtiter plate and 
left at 37°C for 24 hours. Empty wells were filled with TSB with 1% glucose as control and background.  
Hereafter the non-adherent cells were removed and the plate was washed three times in PBS (phosphate-
buffered saline). The biofilm was fixed in 60°C for one hour prior to staining with crystal violet (CV). After 
staining for 15 minutes the plates were washed four times with water and then air-dried. To quantify the 
amount of biofilm produced of each isolate, ethanol was added to the wells and the absorbance at 595 nm 
was measured on a spectrophotometer. 
The assay was made in triplicates for each isolate and the assay was made three times. 
 
S. epidermidis supernatant assay 
This assay was made to analyze the effect of S. epidermidis supernatant on S. aureus biofilm production. 27 
S. epidermidis isolates were grown overnight and supernatant were harvested by spinning the culture down 
and filtrating the supernatant through a 0.2 µm filter. Wells in the microtiter plate were filled with 100 µl 
supernatant and 100 µl S. aureus overnight culture. S. aureus overnight culture was diluted to an OD600 = 
0.1, hence the concentration in the well was OD600=0.05 as for the biofilm assay. The plates were left in 
37°C for 24 hours, and were then washed and dyed as described in the biofilm assay above. Before washing 
the plates, OD595 was measured to identify if increased biofilm was due to higher growth rates. 
The assay was made in triplicates for each combination of S. aureus and S. epidermidis and the assay was 
made three times.

Spot test with S. epidermidis supernatant 
To test if S. epidermidis supernatant altered the growth of S. aureus, a spot test was made. The ten S. 
aureus isolates used in the supernatant assay were also used in this assay. Five S. epidermidis supernatant 
were picked for this assay, two that generally enhanced biofilm formation (from isolate 53A and 58A), two 
that generally decreased biofilm formation (from isolate 34A and 61A) and one that did both (from isolate 
8/9B), see results section. Aliquots of 10 µl of S. epidermidis supernatant were spotted on top of an LB agar 
plate and dried. Then 5 µl S. aureus culture diluted to an OD600 ~ 1.0 were spotted on top of the 
supernatant spot and incubated.

Discussion 
Experimental improvements 
Isolation of S. aureus and S. epidermidis turned out to be more difficult than first expected. As 
demonstrated by many empty S. aureus chromID and blood agar plates, I believe most bacteria in the 
frozen nasal swab samples are dead due to incorrect freezing without glycerol. The selective plates for S. 
aureus did not provide many bacteria in general and only very few S. epidermidis. The use of blood agar 
increased the appearance of colonies from the nasal swap samples hence increasing the number of samples 
from which bacteria could be isolated and also the diversity since more S. epidermidis appeared. Blood agar 
is a very rich medium which is highly preferred in Staphylococcal species. It is possible to tell the difference 
between S. aureus and S. epidermidis on blood agar, making the medium a better alternative in isolating 
these bacteria from these swab samples. 
The multiplex PCR used in this project was a smart and quick way to identify the bacteria isolated from the 
frozen nasal swab samples. Since this PCR has not been used in our laboratory before and we therefore do 
not have any experience with it, the credibility of the experiment should be questioned. The primers gave 
positive results with known S. aureus and S. epidermidis strains from the laboratory, and the experiment 
was therefore proceeded with, with the nasal isolates. In order to confirm the results, gram test, catalase 
test and coagulase test was also made for the isolates. Since sequencing is the state of the art within 
bacterial identification the typing of the S. aureus strains could however provide an extra conformation of 
the results obtained by the multiplex PCR is reliable. Out of 40 isolates being typed, four gave negative 
results. This could mean two things; either the bacteria is not S. aureus or else something went wrong in 
the experiment along the way. For isolate 3A and 3B, which gave negative results in both spa typing and 
coagulase test, I believed it is a false positive in the multiplex PCR. Even though the supernatant assay 
conducted with S. aureus isolate 3A probably cannot be relied on, it is however optimistic that spa typing 
results did confirm seven of the ten isolates in this experiment. 
The idea behind the spot test is to test the hypothesis that some strains of S. epidermidis are capable to 
inhibit the growth of S. aureus. Moreover could it be used to see if any interesting interactions between the 
two strains occur.  Done properly equal amounts of bacteria should be spotted against each other, but 
since I had 56*27=1512 combination to look at this would take an enormous amount of time. The use of a 
pin-replicator is and easy and fast way to screen all isolates of S. epidermidis against all isolates of S. aureus, 
although there are few source of errors by this. First of all the drop-size varies a lot, meaning that 
sometimes a big and sometimes a small aliquot are added to the plate. Moreover the cultures are grown 
overnight in a microtiter plate, the culture should be outgrown, but the OD still vary the strains in between. 
This means that not the same amount of bacteria is added to spots, giving some spots the advantage from 
the beginning. Since some of the S. epidermidis that lysed on the spots managed to live a little better when 
grown 24 hours in advance, I believe that the OD in the spotted cultures are different in the microtiter 
plates. Diluting to the same OD and spotting the same aliquots would make the experiment more precise 
and give a better picture of whether some S. epidermidis is inhibited by S. aureus. Furthermore it is most 
crucial to always to have controls. In this case to see how S. epidermidis grows alone and to see if it would 
have lysed anyway after a few days of growth. 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
The biofilm assay made in this study is an efficient method to screen for biofilm production of many strains 
at the same time. The assay gives a good measurement for how good a biofilm producer the strains is and 
also of what can affect the biofilm as in this case S. epidermidis supernatant. You are however obliged to 
make a lot of replications to give a full picture of the truth. The assay is very sensitive due to the many 
steps in which small variations can happen, and due to human error the replicates are never 100% similar. 
Examples to these errors than are most likely to vary; precise dilutions (culture OD, glucose level, CV 
solution) and laboratory techniques (pressure applied to pipette, flickering the plates during washing steps 
etc.). 
The role of Esp in S. aureus biofilm degradation 
Two previous studies show that some strains of S. epidermidis are able to produce soluble factors that can 
inhibit S. aureus biofilm formation (Fredheim et al., 2014; Iwase et al., 2010). The Japanese research group 
found that the inhibitory compound in the S. epidermidis supernatant is the serine protease Esp. They 
compared the supernatant from inhibitory and non-inhibitory S. epidermidis and found that the varying 
factor was Esp (Iwase et al., 2010). The gene encoding Esp has been demonstrated to exist exclusively in S. 
epidermidis and moreover that the gene exist in all S. epidermidis strains (Fredheim et al., 2014; Ikeda et al., 
2004). The Norwegian research group found that the expression and activity of Esp varied among the 
isolates of S. epidermidis, and they found no correlation between Esp expression and S. aureus biofilm 
reduction. They do however agree that something in the supernatant of some S. epidermidis strains are 
capable of inhibiting the biofilm production in S. aureus (Fredheim et al., 2014). Regardless of these 
findings, the Japanese researchers have carried on their research on Esp. What they recently have 
discovered is that Esp degrades a total of 75 proteins in which 11 are specific proteins in the biofilm matrix, 
here among extracellular adherence protein, FnBP-A and Spa. Moreover does it degrade several human 
receptor proteins that S. aureus binds such as fibronectin, fibrinogen and vitronectin. This still indicates 
that some strains of S. epidermidis are able to disrupt S. aureus biofilm and therefore could be important 
for eradicating S. aureus from the nasal cavity in non-carriers  (Sugimoto et al., 2013). The major differences 
in the Esp experiments in the Japanese and Norwegian groups is that the Japanese look at the protein Esp 
production and the Norwegian look at esp expression (RNA transcript). It should therefore be taken into 
account in the Norwegian study that a lot of translational and post-translational regulations could have an 
impact of how much Esp is actually produced and that it does not necessarily correlate with the 
transcription rate.   
What I find in my project on the effect of S. epidermidis supernatant on S. aureus biofilm is not seen in any 
of these articles, namely that not only can the supernatant inhibit biofilm production of S. aureus biofilm, it 
can also stimulate it. The effects of the supernatants vary a lot depending on which S. aureus isolate is 
growing in it. Some of the supernatants are primarily inhibiting while others are primarily stimulating the 
biofilm production of the 10 S. aureus isolates. The gene encoding Esp was found in all S. epidermidis 
isolates except isolate 6A and 56A. It should be noted that the PCR was made from colonies, and that the 
gene might have been found if the DNA was extracted prior to PCR. In order to compare my results with the 
results obtained from the two research groups it would be interesting to look into the expression of the esp 
gene in the 27 isolates of S. epidermidis.

Inactive agr system stimulate biofilm production 
Whether the excretion of Esp is important in biofilm destruction or not, I also see that the biofilm 
production is stimulated for some of the S. aureus isolates by some S. epidermidis supernatant, a subject 
that has not been touched upon before. There is something in the S. epidermidis in most of the 27 isolates I 
have been working with that either stimulate or inhibit biofilm formation. The biofilm production can be 
influenced by several things. The first thing to look into is whether the increased biofilm is due to increased 
growth or something else affecting the biofilm. The growth of S. aureus in this project may be affected a 
little by S. epidermidis supernatant compared to the controls, but the variations in OD are small, especially 
compared to the CV absorbance measurements. When looking at biofilm production per cell density the 
charts appears as the absorbance measurements and are therefore not shown. Also the spot test with S. 
aureus culture and different supernatants did not show any difference in colonies. To conclude more 
specific if the influence of biofilm formation is due to the growth is increased or decreased when S. 
epidermidis supernatant is added better growth experiments need to be conducted. But the results in this 
study indicate that the biofilm production is not affected by growth modifications. 
If growth is not the reason for the big variation in biofilm production when S. epidermidis supernatant is 
added, something else is triggering the increase or decrease. For the destruction of biofilm, it could be a 
mechanical compound such as Esp, that by cleaving biofilm components decrease the amount of biofilm. 
This does not explain why the biofilm is sometimes increased. Since biofilm production and destruction is a 
highly regulated process in S. aureus one explanation could be that S. epidermidis excretes something that 
can trigger a regulation mechanism in S. aureus, for example quorum sensing molecules interfering with 
the agr response in S. aureus. S. aureus can be divided into four subgroups in which the sequence of the 
pheromones vary completely. Within S. aureus subgroups corresponding pheromones can inhibit the agr 
response of foreign subgroups. Also agr pheromone from S. epidermidis has been shown to inhibit the agr 
response in S. aureus. Because of the variation in the four subgroups, pheromone produced by S. 
epidermidis are only inhibiting S. aureus subgroup I-III and not subgroup IV (Michael Otto, Echner, & 
Voelter, 2001). The agr response has been shown to be important in biofilm dispersal and that low agr 
activity is stimulating biofilm formation in S. aureus. Dispersal of S. aureus is protease-mediated and the agr 
system is among other things responsible for expression of excreted proteases  (Boles & Horswill, 2008). If 
S. epidermidis pheromone can inhibit the agr system in S. aureus subgroup I-III, the biofilm dispersal would 
be inhibited which could be an explanation for the stimulation biofilm seen in some of the S. aureus 
isolates. This would not happen in S. aureus subgroup IV. Therefore it could be very interesting to classify 
the S. aureus isolates in this project into the four subgroups, to identify if quorum sensing is an explanation 
for my observations. This hypothesis would also explain the lower amount of biofilm in the controls 
compared to the biofilm screening, because the control in this assay was culture of S. aureus in its own 
supernatant. When screening all 56 isolates for their ability to produce biofilm, the 10 isolates used in the 
supernatant assay produced much more biofilm than the control did with supernatant added. For control 
the 10 S. aureus isolates were grown in their own supernatant. Because the supernatant were harvested 
from an overnight culture the cell density and thereby the amount of pheromones is likely to be high. This 
could explain why the supernatant from the same bacteria still would decrease the amount of biofilm 
measured compared to the biofilm screening see Figure 7. One way to test this is to repeat some of the 
experiments done by Otto et al. in which they investigated the effect of S. aureus virulence by inhibiting the 
agr system with S. epidermidis pheromone. They were capable to both extract pheromones from S. 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
epidermidis and to synthesize them. The concentration of the pheromones was measured by HPLC. To test 
the effect on the S. aureus agr system, they also used HPLC to measure the concentration of δ-toxin, a 
translation product of RNAIII (M Otto, Süssmuth, Vuong, Jung, & Götz, 1999). 
 
High diversity between staphylococcal isolates 
56 nasal isolates were tested for their ability to produce biofilm. Often the threshold for biofilm production 
is absorbance above 0.1-0.2, which all isolates in this study are above. In another study they found that 
only 1 out of 10 nasal isolates produced biofilm (Askarian et al., 2014). They speculate in the article 
whether S. aureus nasal colonization is biofilm-associated, which indeed correspond to their results. It 
should be noted that they only tested ten isolates. This does however not correspond to my findings that 
nasal isolates of S. aureus are all producing biofilm in greater or lesser extent. 
The biofilm production of the 56 nasal isolates gives a picture of how diverse the nasal environment can be 
from person to person. The sampling of these individuals was only taken once, and there is therefore no 
knowledge about whether these individuals are carrying the same strain persistently.  S. aureus from this 
study are isolated from 35 individuals. The biofilm production for these isolates under the same condition 
varied from absorbance 0.2-4, also for isolates within same CC. How the S. epidermidis supernatant 
influenced the biofilm formation of the S. aureus nasal isolates also differed. Furthermore the S. 
epidermidis showed different persistency when spotted against S. aureus, indicating that isolates of nasal S. 
epidermidis also show diversity. 
Even though studies are considering S. aureus to be a highly clonal population, the diversity of the strains 
carried in the nasal cavity from person to person has before been observed. As mentioned in the 
introduction S. aureus appear to have a high degree of host specificity. Normally strains of S. aureus are 
divided into linages generated by MLST (multilocus sequence typing) that detects point mutations in seven 
housekeeping genes. Isolates with an identic allelic profile are considered to be clonal and those isolates 
with at least five out of seven matching genes belong to the same clonal complex (CC). The genome of S. 
aureus can be divided into the stable core genome and the accessory genome. Previously only the 
accessory genome was believed to differ between the strains, but in a study by Lindsay et al. they found a 
third group of genes varying between isolates called core-variable genes. Each linage of S. aureus carried a 
unique combination of these core-variable genes suggesting a common ancestor but evolutionary deviation 
(Lindsay et al., 2006). In another study they compared 155 nasal isolates of S. aureus obtained from 
asymptomatic carriers. These could be divided into 20 clonal complexes and had a huge variation in gene 
content (Monecke et al., 2009). The most prevalent CCs in this thesis are also the most prevalent seen in 
that study, in which CC8, CC15, CC30 and CC45 are the most detected CC from nasal isolates.  
Since the diversity of the colonizing strains appears to be wide in different individuals, perhaps it would be 
worth looking more into this variation when studying nasal carriage. When correlating nasal carriage of S. 
aureus with either human genetics or nasal microbiota it may play a role which linage of S. aureus is 
present. Maybe some genetic variations may provide good conditions for some S. aureus strains and not for 
others, and maybe some strains of S. epidermidis are able to eradicate S. aureus in the nose while others 
rather stimulate it.

S. aureus nasal carriage 
Determinants affecting nasal carriage of S. aureus seem to be many and studies conducted in which 
evidence for either genetic or microbial importance for S. aureus nasal carriage has been very convincing. 
The relationship between S. aureus and S. epidermidis has already been discussed. The studies made with 
the two strains of bacteria were conducted because a negative correlation between them was observed 
(Frank et al., 2010). In another study, a higher relative abundance of S. epidermidis was found in S. aureus 
carrier compared to non-carriers. So the impact of S. epidermidis on S. aureus colonization is still unclear. In 
that study they instead found a correlation between S. aureus and Corynebacterium. By looking at co-
occurrence in the nasal cavity and the interaction between these bacteria they found that S. aureus and C. 
accolens occurred together and that C. accolens enhanced the growth of S. aureus. Another 
Corynebacterium, C. pseudodiphtericum, occurred more often in non-carriers and they found it to inhibit 
the growth of S. aureus (Yan et al., 2013). In another study they tested the binding affinity of S. aureus, S. 
epidermidis and Corynebacterium sp strain Co304. They discovered that Co304 had higher binding affinity 
to nasal mucus than S. aureus and were furthermore able to supplant S. aureus in the anterior nares by 
inoculating this strain into the nose of persistent carrier (G. Ji et al., 1997). Several studies have also been 
made in which the human factors have been the focus. As mentioned in the introduction genetic variation 
in genes associated with the human immune system. Other studies have also worked with immune factors 
and have alongside genetic studies also focused on nasal secretions obtained from persistent carriers and 
non-carriers. Nasal secretion from non-carriers displayed a bactericidal activity not seen in persistent 
carriers although they did not identify the compound responsible this activity. They conclude in this article 
that it is not enough to look at genetic variations alone but gene expression profiles and proteomic 
screening should be included for better understanding the mechanism in S. aureus carriage status (van 
Belkum et al., 2007). 
It is still unknown why a part of the human population is able to avoid S. aureus colonization, and thereby 
decrease the risk for nosocomial infection. Probably both host factors and bacterial factors are influencing 
carriage status of S. aureus in the nose. Human genotypes may influence the numbers of receptors in the 
nose for S. aureus to adhere to and how well the immune system tolerates S. aureus. Factors such as 
chronic diseases that cause immunodeficiency may also influence the carriage state. Bacterial factors that 
influence S. aureus carriage is determined on which strain is carried. Expression of adhesins and the ability 
to biofilm formation may influence if the strain is capable of adhere to the human tissue. Moreover is 
bacterial competition presumable a very important determinant for S. aureus carriage and the normal 
microbiota in the nose may be able to clear the bacteria. 
Future studies 
In this master thesis I have tried to illustrate some of the interactions that take place between S. aureus and 
S. epidermidis, namely by focusing on S. epidermidis effect on S. aureus biofilm production. While this 
project emphasizes that S. aureus can be affected by S. epidermidis in its ability to produce biofilm, this 
project raises many questions regarding how these bacteria interact and what influence these interaction 
can have on the nasal environment and carriage of S. aureus. This is a description of how to pursue some of 
these questions in order to answer them. More experiments than those mentioned below could also be 
conducted; some involving genetic studies of the carriers, nasal secretion studies etc. Here are only 
mentioned experiments on the basis of the isolates used in this project.

To test if the influence of S. epidermidis supernatant on biofilm production is due to increased or decreased 
growth of S. aureus, growth experiments of S. aureus with S. epidermidis supernatant can be conducted. 
This experiment will determine if the growth rates of S. aureus grown with S. epidermidis supernatant is 
different from S. aureus grown alone.  
The S. epidermidis supernatant had an effect on S. aureus biofilm production when added in the beginning 
of the experiment. Since biofilm is difficult to treat once it has been established, it could be interesting to 
find out if the effect of the supernatant also was seen if added to an already established biofilm.  
To test the hypothesis that S. aureus biofilm production in this experiment is affected by pheromone cross-
inhibition, some experiments can be conducted. If some subgroups of S. aureus agr system are inhibited by 
S. epidermidis peptide pheromones, it would be interesting to see if the same happens for S. epidermidis 
biofilm production. Therefore I would make the supernatant assay in which S. epidermidis culture grows in 
S. aureus supernatant. As mentioned in the discussion I would also classify the strains of S. aureus into the 
four agr system subgroups by sequencing the agr locus. By extracting S. epidermidis pheromones or 
synthesizing them, it would be possible to check the effect on S. aureus agr system and biofilm production 
of the nasal isolates used in this study.  
Whether or not the active compound in S. epidermidis supernatant is secreted Esp or peptide pheromones, 
several methods could be used in the discovery. Protein extract of the supernatant can be analyzed using 
proteomics techniques such as mass spectrometry or SDS-page. Another way to discriminate important 
compounds produced by S. epidermidis is by sequencing RNA from the cells. This will provide a 
characterization of gene expression in the cells.  
 In this thesis the nasal isolates have been screened for either their biofilm producing abilities or the effect 
on biofilm production. The mechanism behind is still unknown. Using a flow-cell chamber biofilm setup 
could provide visualization of how S. aureus form their biofilm using a confocal microscope. By adding S. 
epidermidis supernatant to the medium, the effect on the biofilm could also be seen. Looking more in to 
the bacterial interactions it would be interesting to make a multi-bacterial biofilm using both S. aureus and 
S. epidermidis and observe how they influence each other.   
To wrap all these experiments up, the idea behind them is to gain knowledge about the bacterial 
interaction happening between S. aureus and S. epidermidis, because the interaction might influence 
human nasal colonization. Therefore would it also be relevant to look more into the role of biofilm in 
human nasal colonization. Is there perhaps a correlation between biofilm and persistence of carriage? How 
to examine this is difficult, but it could perhaps involve mammalian cell based experiments or other in vivo 
studies.

Conclusion 
In this study 56 S. aureus and 27 S. epidermidis were isolated from swab samples taken in the nasal cavity 
of 49 individuals. The use of blood agar plates demonstrated to be useful in obtaining both strains but 
especially S. epidermidis that did not appear very often at S. aureus chromID plates. To verify the identity of 
the bacteria a multiplex PCR was conducted that yielded different gel bands from S. aureus and S. 
epidermidis. Also gram tests, catalase tests and coagulase tests were made to support the verification. The 
27 isolates of S. epidermidis were screened for the gene encoding Esp, a serine protease, which all but two 
isolates were positive for. 40 out of 56 S. aureus isolates were spa typed, showing that A and B isolates 
were the same. This also revealed that the multiplex PCR are not 100% reliable.   
All S. aureus isolates were grown next to all isolates of S. epidermidis in a spot test. The appearance of the 
S. epidermidis varied for the 27 isolates in between but did not differ depending on which S. aureus they 
were spotted against. No difference was seen in any of the 56 S. aureus and the growth were not affected 
by the presence of S. epidermidis. 
Screening of biofilm production of the 56 S. aureus showed a great diversity in ability to produce biofilm. 
Supernatant was harvested from 27 S. epidermidis and tested for their ability to inhibit S. aureus. The ten 
best biofilm producers were used in this experiment. As for previous studies some S. epidermidis secrete 
something into the supernatant that inhibits biofilm formation of S. aureus. Surprisingly was not only 
inhibition of biofilm in S. aureus strains seen, but some supernatants were also stimulating biofilm 
production. 
Whether S. epidermidis is important in S. aureus nasal carriage is still uncertain. What can be concluded 
from this project is that the diversity in staphylococcal strains colonizing the human nose are substantial 
and that S. epidermidis has the ability to modify biofilm production in S. aureus.

Appendix B - Protocols 
Isolation of S. aureus and S. epidermidis from SSI frozen nasal swaps 
 
Defrost nasal swap samples on ice 
 
Plate 10-20 µl of sample on ¼ of both a S. aureus chromID plate and on blood agar plate, more if 
you are using the whole plate 
 
Incubate overnight at 37 °C. Leave the SAID plates on the tables for a few more days. 
 
Colonies from S. aureus turn blue on S. aureus chromID plates. 
Colonies that look brown on blood agar could be S. aureus. Moreover can S. aureus be hemolytic on 
blood agar which can be seen as clearing zones on blood agar. 
S. epidermidis look grey or silver on blood agar. 
 
Chelex DNA extraction 
 
Defrost nasal swap sample on ice 
 
Mix 75 µl 20% chelex in TE buffer with 25 µl of the nasal swap sample 
 
Vortex for one minute 
 
Put on heating block at 98°C for 10 minutes 
 
Spin the sample for 5 minutes at 20000 g 
 
Freeze until use 
 
Before use: defrost the sample and spin for 5 minutes at 20000 g. Aspirate DNA from the top. 
 
PCR: 
Targeting the tuf gene (Delgado et al., 2009): 
tuf-g (5'-GGTGTACCAGCATTAGT-3') 
tuf-a (5'-TTCAGTATGTGGTGTAA-3') 
tuf-e (5'-TTCGTGCATACCGATGA-3') 
The primer pairs tuf-g/tuf-e and tuf-g/tuf-a result in a 370 bp (S. epidermidis) or a 530 bp (S. aureus) 
fragment 
 
Suspend  bacterial colony in 20 µl TEN buffer 
 
Heat 10 min in 98 °C 
 
Add 180 µl sterile H2O to dilute cell components that can interfere with the PCR 
 
Use as template for PCR

Phenotypic tests 
Both S. aureus and S. epidermidis are gram positive and catalase positive. The only tests besides the PCR 
that differentiate between the two bacterial strains are coagulase test in which only S. aureus is positive. 
Gram test 
 
Add a drop of 3% KOH on a glass slide 
 
Take a 1-3 colonies with a toothpick and smear into the drop. Mix for 1 minute. 
 
Look at the mass: 
1. If the mass turns slimy and strings can be drawn the bacteria is gram negative 
2. If the mass does not become slimy and strings can’t be drawn the bacteria is gram positive. 
 
Catalase test 
 
Dissolve a colony in a drop of saline on a glass slide 
 
Add a drop of H2O2 
 
If bobbles occur in the drops, the bacteria is catalase positive 
 
Coagulase test 
 
To test tubes, add 500 µl rabbit plasma and 100 µl overnight culture (alternatively take 3-5 colonies 
and dissolve in a little LB) 
 
Incubate at 37°C 
 
Positive reaction: clumping/gelling of the plasma that cannot be re-suspended by shaking 
Negative result: no clumping is seen after 24 hours of incubation 
 
Biofilm assay in microtiter plate 
1. Grow strains  of S. aureus in TSB at 37°C overnight 
2. Dilute the overnight culture to OD600 = 0.05 in TSB supplemented with 1 % glucose 
3. Add 200 µl samples into wells of a 96-well microtiter plate and incubate for 24 h at 37°C 
4. Wash the wells thrice in 300 µl PBS (phosphate-buffered saline) to remove non-adherent cells 
5. Fixate biofilm for 1 hour in 60°C 
6. Add 150 µl 0.1 % CV for 15 min 
7. Remove CV and wash with water 3-4 times 
8. Air dry 
9. For quantification, add 150 µl ethanol and measure absorbance at 595 nm 
Test of S. epidermidis supernatant, change step 3. 
3. Add 100 µl sample diluted to OD600=0.1 and 100 µl S. epidermidis supernatant into wells of a 96-
well microtiter plate and incubate for 24 h at 37°C overnight
