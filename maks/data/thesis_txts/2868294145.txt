Abstract
Efficient baggage chute allocation is a critical component of airport logistics, ensuring smooth
baggage handling while minimizing disruptions. This thesis investigates the optimization of chute
allocation at Copenhagen Airport, focusing on reducing penalties associated with chute opening
times while maintaining operational feasibility. With increasing passenger volumes and limited
infrastructure, optimizing chute usage is essential to prevent congestion, reduce delays, and en-
hance overall airport efficiency. Traditional exact optimization methods struggle with large-scale
scheduling problems, necessitating heuristic-based approaches that balance computational effi-
ciency with solution quality. This thesis formulates the chute allocation problem as a Mixed In-
teger Programming (MIP) model but demonstrates its computational infeasibility for real-world
scenarios. Instead, heuristic and metaheuristic methods are applied, including a greedy heuris-
tic, Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP), and Simulated Annealing (SA).
Sensitivity analyses further examine the impact of increasing chute capacity, adjusting ground
handler constraints, and introducing minor flexibility in chute opening times. The results confirm
that SA consistently outperforms the other heuristics, effectively escaping local minima and min-
imizing penalties. Additionally, flight density proves to be a stronger determinant of scheduling
difficulty than the number of flights alone. While expanding chute capacity provides long-term
improvements, short-term gains can be achieved by adjusting ground handler constraints or allow-
ing limited flexibility in Minimum Opening Time (MOT). These findings offer actionable insights
for optimizing baggage chute allocation at Copenhagen Airport and similar large-scale airport op-
erations.

Introduction
Copenhagen Airport (CPH), Denmark’s largest airport, serves as a key international gateway con-
necting the country to the rest of the world. Copenhagen Airport (2024) reports that during the
summer of 2024, the airport experienced a significant surge in passenger traffic, with nine million
travelers passing through its terminals between June and August — a 13% increase compared to
the previous year — despite operating fewer flights. Pisinger and Rude (2020) mention that a study
done by SITA (2016) have shown that the positive emotions of the passengers drop below 54% if
the waiting time at reclaim belts is exceeding 30 minutes. On the other hand, if the waiting time is
less than 10 minutes, the positive emotions among the passengers shoots to above 88%. The rise
in passenger numbers in CPH directly correlates with increased luggage volumes, amplifying the
demand for efficient baggage handling infrastructure.
The baggage handling infrastructure in airports consists of multiple components. Pisinger and
Rude (2020) mentions that in general, three main baggage processes exist:
• Departures (outbound);
• Arrivals (inbound);
• Transfer (inbound/outbound).
This thesis focuses on the departure process, specifically the assignment of chutes to departing
flights. The problem is approached as deterministic, where flights scheduled for a given day are
known in advance. The objective is to assign these flights to valid chutes within predefined time
periods, optimizing the schedule while adhering to operational constraints. Whilst it is important to
keep passenger satisfactions high, optimizing the chute assignment is also crucial for capacity and
cost reasons. Although some airports have already implemented algorithms for the chute assign-
ment, both Pisinger and Rude (2020) and Syltevik et al. (2018) argue that significant opportunities
for improvement remain, which motivates this study.
2.1
Background of the Problem
As one of the busiest airports in Northern Europe, CPH operates within a unique framework that
influences its operational challenges, particularly in chute allocation. The airport’s evolution from
a fully government-owned entity to a partially privatized, publicly traded company highlights the
complexity of its current operations. Established in 1925 as the Copenhagen Airport Authority,
CPH transitioned to a limited liability company in 1990, marking the start of its privatization.
Over the subsequent decade, the Danish government gradually reduced its ownership, selling 25%
of shares in 1994, 24% in 1996, and 17% in 2000 (TV2 Nyheder, 2017). This shift brought about
significant investment and innovation, though it also introduced new challenges in balancing op-
erational efficiency with profitability and stakeholder expectations.

As the owner of the airport infrastructure, CPH provides essential facilities, including baggage
handling systems, chutes, and runways, while airlines and ground handlers lease access to these
resources. This decentralized structure necessitates effective collaboration among stakeholders
with differing priorities, particularly in managing the logistics of baggage and cargo. Ground
handlers, responsible for allocating chutes, must navigate the constraints of limited resources while
ensuring fair and efficient operations for the airlines they serve.
Adding to the complexity, Danish aviation laws mandate that CPH accommodates all flights re-
questing access, as long as runway slots are available. This legal requirement, coupled with the
operational autonomy of airlines over their flight schedules, means that CPH must dynamically
adapt its resources to accommodate varying demands. The airport has no authority to refuse ser-
vice to flights or influence their departure times, which further underscores the need for a robust
allocation system capable of handling unpredictable schedules with minimal conflicts.
These interdependencies, arising from privatization, infrastructure ownership, independent stake-
holder operations, and legal obligations, create a challenging environment for resource allocation
at CPH. The development of an efficient chute allocation model is crucial to address these chal-
lenges, minimizing operational conflicts, enhancing throughput, and ensuring the airport’s capacity
to adapt to growing passenger volumes and complex logistical demands.

2.3
Scope of the Project
2.3.1
Objectives
This thesis aims to develop an optimization model for baggage chute allocation at Copenhagen
Airport, focusing on minimizing reductions in chute opening times while ensuring operational
feasibility. The study begins with a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) formulation to establish
a theoretical benchmark for optimal allocation. However, given the computational challenges of
exact methods for large-scale scheduling, the approach transitions to heuristic-based strategies.
The first step involves implementing a greedy heuristic, which constructs an initial allocation based
on predefined sorting strategies. This provides a baseline for evaluating more advanced heuristics.
To introduce a degree of flexibility, the Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP)
is applied, incorporating controlled randomness and local search to explore alternative allocations.
Finally, Simulated Annealing (SA) is implemented as a metaheuristic approach, designed to iter-
atively refine solutions by allowing controlled modifications over multiple iterations.
The study aims to evaluate how well these methods perform in balancing solution quality and
computational efficiency, ensuring that the proposed approaches remain practical for large-scale
real-world scheduling at Copenhagen Airport.

Literature review
The literature reviewed in this chapter focuses primarily on the chute allocation problem. However,
much of the existing research explores broader optimization models that encompass areas such as
luggage transportation, passenger flow, and the GAP. While these aspects are not the scope of
this thesis, the heuristics and methodologies proposed in these studies offer valuable insights and
inspiration. Although the GAP differs from chute allocation by requiring the assignment of a
single gate rather than a sequence of gates, the shared challenges in resource allocation make these
studies highly relevant to the development of effective heuristics for this project. However, the
constraints in chute allocation differ from those in GAP, particularly due to the need for adjacent
chutes, handler restrictions, and multiple chute assignments per flight, which introduce additional
complexities not present in standard gate assignment problems.
3.1
The Chute Allocation Problem
As mentioned in Chapter 2, optimizing the baggage handling is a crucial aspect for airports as it is
directly impacting important aspects such as passenger satisfaction and operational costs. For the
operational costs, Huang et al. (2016) highlights some of the core challenges associated with this
problem: ”The major decision of the outbound BHS is to assign the outbound flights to the chutes.
If a flight is assigned to a far-away chute, the BHS workers need more time and effort to process to
the corresponding aircraft. If more than one flight is assigned to the same chute at the same time,
the baggage handlers will need to sort the baggage manually for the respective flights, which is
quite labor-intense.” This concern aligns closely with the operational requirements set by CPH.
CPH has explicitly emphasized that ensuring each flight has its designated chute without conflicts
is a fundamental priority, further underscoring the importance of an effective allocation strategy.
Huang et al. (2016) furthermore highlight that Lin et al. (2015) mentions that, in accordance with
Pisinger and Rude (2020), the classical process of the Baggage Handling System (BHS) consists
of depatures, arrivals and transfer. The departures process is described as: ”The destination of
a baggage can be either a buffer zone where the baggage will be re-sorted later or a baggage
unloading zone, which is also called a chute. Usually, the chute for a specific outbound flight is
assigned before the departure time.”
Huang et al. (2016) says that while their literature addresses the chute assignment problem, the
majority of solutions developed rely heavily on heuristic approaches. This underscores the com-
plexity of achieving optimal allocations through exact methods, paving the way for the exploration
of heuristics and metaheuristics as practical and effective alternatives. The following section delves
into these techniques, examining their role in solving large-scale optimization problems like chute
allocation.

3.2
Heuristic Methods for Chute Allocation
3.2.1
Chute Allocation using Construction Heuristics
Huang et al. (2016) states that Ascó et al. (2012, 2014) address the chute allocation problem by
using heuristic-based approaches such as construction and greedy algorithms. Ascó et al. (2012,
2014) consider the problem of assigning available chutes to flights which have already been sched-
uled and allocated stands. From this they learn that appropriate algorithm selection is highly de-
pendent upon whether or not reductions in service time are permitted and upon the flight density
in relation to the number of chutes. From these constructive approaches that produce different
solutions which are better for different trade-offs, they utilize these as initial solutions for an evo-
lutionary algorithm.
The constructive algorithms presented by Ascó et al. (2012, 2014) shows a significant difference
in the solutions produced. They describe their flight ordering methods as follows:
1. Order by Starting Time (OST). This orders flights into ascending order by their values.
2. Order by Departure Time (ODT). This order flights into ascending order by teir values.
The OST will be the corresponding value for the STD/MOT used in this problem. The ODT is
the equivalent STD. Then, based on three different methods for chute selection, namely First In
First Out (FIFO), Last In First Out (LIFO), Closest (shortest distance for the luggage to travel), the
chute to be allocated was chosen. As the structure and constraints of problem presented here and
the problem examined for this thesis are different, it is important to weigh the overall idea of the
heuristics used in the solutions. So even though this thesis is not tracking when luggage is being
checked in, other selections in how to sort flights and allocate chutes will have a huge impact on
the solutions produced.
Ascó et al. (2012, 2014) conclude by stating that the behavior of flight assignments to chutes -
specifically flight ordering, chute assignment algorithms, and selection methods - depends on the
relationship between the number of flights and the number of sorting stations. They highlight that
when a dataset has high flight density (i.e., a large number of flights requiring service simulta-
neously), achieving an optimal schedule becomes more challenging. This holds true even if the
overall number of flights is lower compared to another dataset with lower flight density.
3.2.2
Chute Allocation using Metaheuristics
A significant body of research exists on the GAP, which closely parallels chute allocation in airport
baggage handling systems. GAP deals with assigning one flight to one gate at a time, aiming to
minimize ungated flights and reduce passenger walking distances. Chute allocation differs as this
is two-dimensional, as it involves assigning flights to multiple, adjacent chutes over overlapping
time windows, focusing on minimizing operational conflicts and maximizing efficiency.
One foundational study by Obata et al. (1979) established that the basic gate assignment problem
is a quadratic assignment problem (QAP), formally classified as NP-hard. This complexity un-

derscores the necessity for heuristic and metaheuristic approaches to find feasible solutions within
reasonable computational times.
Cheng et al. (2012) has explored the use of metaheurstics for the GAP. They considered the Genetic
Algorithm (GA), SA, Tabu Search (TS), and a hybrid approach based on SA and TS. They mention
that Xu and Bailey (2001) reported good results using TS. Ding et al. (2005) consider the use of
SA, and introduced a combination of SA and TS to tackle the GAP. Their approach began with a
greedy algorithm designed to minimize ungated flights, providing an initial feasible solution. This
was followed by a neighborhood search leveraging a novel ”Interval Exchange Move” technique,
enabling the exchange of consecutive flight intervals between gates to reduce congestion and im-
prove gate utilization. This approach proved particularly effective during peak scheduling periods,
where simple pairwise exchanges often failed to yield improvements.
The greedy algorithm presented by Ding et al. (2005) operates by sorting flights based on departure
times and assigning them iteratively to the gate with the latest available departure slot. If no gates
are available, flights are assigned to aprons, analogous to chute overflow zones in baggage systems.
The greedy solution not only optimizes initial gate allocation but serves as the starting point for
the subsequent metaheuristic search.
Daş et al. (2020) has made a review on the GAP, highlighting that Xu and Bailey (2001) designed
three neighborhood moves used in relation to the GAP. The three moves are described as:
• Insert Move: A flight, previously assigned to a gate, is reassigned to another gate.
• Exchange Move 1: Two flights exchange gates.
• Exchange Move 2: The gate of a flight pair is exchanged with the gate of another pair.
In their Tabu Search framework, Ding et al. (2005) observed that Exchange 1 and 2 did not al-
ways produce feasible solutions. Therefore, they design another move called Interval Exchange
Move, where all the flights in gate k whose arrival and departure times are between flight i and j
are exchanged with the flights in gate l whose arrival and departure are between g and h. This is,
a set of flights scheduled to gate k exchange gates with another set of flights at gate l (Daş et al.,
2020). This neighborhood search allowed for significant performance gains, outperforming ex-
isting approaches by achieving more optimal solutions, particularly for larger, denser scheduling
scenarios. The relevance of such techniques extends directly to chute allocation, where interval-
based exchanges can minimize chute overlaps and ensure smoother baggage flow.
3.3
Conclusion
The literature highlights the complexity and significance of chute allocation in airport operations,
underscoring its critical role in minimizing operational costs and enhancing passenger satisfaction.
While direct studies on chute allocation are limited, parallels drawn from the GAP offer valuable
insights. Research demonstrates that heuristic and metaheuristic approaches, such as greedy al-
gorithms, SA, and TS, provide effective solutions for large-scale, NP-hard optimization problems

Data Description
The data for this thesis, provided by Copenhagen Airport, forms the foundation for addressing the
chute allocation problem. Section 4.1 includes three original instances of outgoing flights requiring

of these instances that are used for further analysis.
4.1
Original Instances
Table 4.1 summarizes the key characteristics of the three instances, Set A, Set B, and Set C in this
study. Set A consists of 306 flights, with 282 flights belonging to flight group C, representing the
largest share of flights in this dataset. Flight group E contributes 22 flights, while flight group D
has just 2 flights. Ground handler 1 handles the largest workload with 167 flights, followed by
ground handler 4 with 56 flights, ground handler 3 with 55 flights, and ground handler 2 with 28
flights.
Set B includes a total of 310 flights. Similar to Set A, flight group C dominates with 291 flights,
while flight groups E and D account for 17 and 2 flights, respectively. Ground handler 1 again
manages the majority of flights at 187, with ground handlers 4, 3, and 2 handling 48, 47, and 28
flights, respectively.
Set C features 316 flights, making it the largest dataset. Flight group C remains the largest category
with 293 flights, followed by 20 flights in flight group E, 2 flights in flight group D, and a single
flight in flight group B. Ground handler 1 handles 180 flights, ground handler 4 manages 56, ground
handler 3 manages 53, and ground handler 2 handles 27 flights.
These instances provide a diverse and realistic basis for evaluating the proposed optimization meth-
ods, capturing variations in workload distribution and flight group characteristics across different
days of operation. The structured representation of flights and handlers in these sets ensures a
robust analysis of the chute allocation problem. However, to test the scalability and robustness of
the heuristics in this thesis, variations of these instances will be presented in Section 4.2.

Mathematical Formulation
This chapter presents the mathematical formulations of the chute allocation problem at CPH. Two
models are introduced: Model 1 and Model 2, each addressing the problem with different objectives
and constraints.
In Chapter 3, it was established that the classical assignment problem is NP-hard. To illustrate
this complexity, Model 1 was formulated as a MIP model, with the objective of maximizing the
number of flights assigned to chutes. This exact formulation ensures that as many flights as possi-
ble receive a chute assignment while strictly enforcing MOT constraints. However, Copenhagen
Airport aims to not only satisfy MOT but also minimize reductions in SOT, ensuring longer chute
availability when possible. As the number of flights increases, the computational complexity of
the MIP formulation in Model 1 quickly becomes intractable, making it impractical for large-scale
real-world scheduling. Running Model 1 for increasing numbers of flights demonstrates this issue,
as solver runtimes escalate rapidly, ultimately reaching scenarios where no solution is found within
reasonable time limits.
To address these limitations, Model 2 is introduced — not as an exact optimization model but as
a heuristic-driven approach. Unlike Model 1, which relies on strict constraints and infeasibility
checks, Model 2 allows for controlled reductions in SOT and, through heuristic methods, may pro-
duce solutions where some flights experience MOT reductions. However, Model 2 itself does not
inherently allow MOT reductions; it is the heuristic implementation that introduces these ”illegal”
solutions. These solutions remain feasible in the sense that all flights receive a chute assignment,
but they are heavily penalized to discourage deviations from MOT. True infeasibility only occurs
if a flight cannot be assigned a chute at any point, which the heuristic methods seek to avoid.
To model this flexibility, binary decision variables y and y′ are introduced, where:
• y ensures that the chute is open during MOT, which is mandatory.
• y′ allows the chute to open earlier during SOT, but this is optional.
Since Model 2 is not formulated as a MIP, it does not solve the problem to proven optimality but in-
stead relies on heuristic methods to construct and refine solutions efficiently. This heuristic-driven
approach balances computational feasibility and operational realism, enabling practical large-scale
scheduling while ensuring that solutions remain as close as possible to the ideal allocation.

Construction Heuristics
This chapter introduces construction heuristics as a method for generating initial chute allocations.
Since exact methods proved computationally infeasible for large instances, heuristic approaches
provide a scalable alternative. The chapter begins with an introduction to greedy heuristics in
Section 6.1, followed by an analysis of different sorting strategies in Section 6.2. Finally, Sec-
tion 6.3 presents a comparison of results, evaluating the effectiveness of each approach and laying
the foundation for further refinement through metaheuristics.
6.1
Introduction to Greedy Heuristics
As presented in Chapter 3, a greedy heuristic is a good approach when obtaining an initial solu-
tion. This chapter explores the use of greedy heuristics as a straightforward approach to the chute
allocation problem. These heuristics are particularly appealing due to their simplicity and compu-
tational efficiency, offering feasible solutions within a short time frame. These properties make
them well-suited for operational environments, such as chute allocation.
The greedy heuristic operates by assigning flights iteratively, one at a time, according to a prede-
fined sorting order. For each flight, the heurstic identifies all valid chute sequences and selects
the one that incurs the lowest penalty at the moment of assignment. This process does not involve
iterative refinements or global re-optimizations, making it computationally inexpensive but inher-
ently suboptimal. Despite its limitations, the greedy heuristic provides a useful initial solution for
evaluating the performance of more advanced optimization techniques.

6.2
Sorting Strategies
As highlighted in Chapter 3, the order in which flights are processed significantly impacts the
quality of the solutions generated. To explore this impact, four distinct sorting strategies were
implemented, each prioritizing flights based on specific criteria:
• Earliest Flights, Smallest Flights (EFSF): Flights are ordered by their STDf in ascending
order, followed by their resource requirements in ascending order (e.g., shortest SOTf).
• Earliest Flights, Biggest Flights (EFBF): Flights are prioritized by their STDf in ascending
order, followed by their resource requirements in descending order.
• Latest Flights, Smallest Flights (LFSF): Flights are prioritized by their STDf in descend-
ing order, followed by their resource requirements in ascending order.
• Latest Flights, Biggest Flights (LFBF): Flights are ordered by their STDf in descending
order, followed by their resource requirements in descending order.
Each strategy was designed to test the interaction between flight ordering and resource allocation
under different priorities. The comparison between these strategies provides valuable insights into
the trade-offs and effectiveness of prioritizing flights based on temporal and resource considera-
tions.

A key feature of GRASP is its integration of both greedy and probabilistic decision-making in the

include elements whose cost is no more than α% greater than the cost of the best element. From
this list, one element is randomly selected for assignment.

7.2
Simulated Annealing
This chapter presents the implementation of SA for the chute allocation problem. As a metaheuris-
tic, SA incorporates probabilistic acceptance of worse solutions, allowing it to escape local min-
ima and explore a broader solution space compared to purely greedy approaches. Unlike GRASP,
which constructs diverse solutions but remains constrained by its deterministic local search, SA
enables a more flexible search process by occasionally accepting suboptimal moves to avoid stag-
nation.
Section 7.2.1 begins by outlining the general SA framework, detailing its probabilistic acceptance
criterion and temperature-based exploration mechanisms. Following this, Section 7.2.2 demon-
strates its specific application to the problem, including the definition of the neighborhood structure
and move operators. Section 7.2.3 discusses the parameter tuning process, where different cooling
schedules and acceptance thresholds are evaluated to optimize performance. Finally, Section 7.2.4
presents the results of the SA implementation, comparing its effectiveness against the previously
tested heuristics and analyzing its ability to improve solution quality.
A key aspect of this study is the comparison of two initialization strategies: one starting from the
greedy solution obtained in Section 6.2.2, and another using a completely random allocation. This
approach investigates whether SA benefits from a structured starting point or if its exploration
mechanisms are robust enough to optimize a randomly generated solution efficiently. By sys-
tematically analyzing these aspects, this chapter aims to establish SA’s role as the most effective
heuristic for the chute allocation problem.
7.2.1
The Simulated Annealing Framework
SA explores the solution space by iteratively generating and evaluating ”neighbor” solutions de-
rived from small modifications to the current solution. A defining characteristic of SA is its prob-
abilistic acceptance criterion, which occasionally accepts worse solutions based on a temperature
parameter. This feature allows the algorithm to escape local minima, particularly in the early stages
of the search when the temperature is high. Over time, the temperature decreases according to a
predefined cooling schedule, controlled by parameters such as the initial temperature T and the
cooling rate α.
The algorithm proceeds as follows: at each iteration, a new candidate solution is generated, evalu-
ated, and either accepted or rejected based on its quality and the acceptance probability. The best
solution encountered during the search is returned as the output.
Algorithm 4 outlines the key steps of a general SA algorithm, illustrating how the cooling schedule
and acceptance criteria govern the search dynamics.

7.2.2
Applications to the problem
Random Step
The random step part, central to the optimization process, ensures that any new solution respects
group-specific chute constraints and produces a feasible allocation. The random step plays a cru-
cial role in this process by generating neighbor solutions through Insert-Move operations, where a
randomly selected flight is reassigned to a different chute while maintaining feasibility. Unlike the
previous heuristic, which accept only immediate improvements, SA occasionally accepts worse
solutions to escape local minima. The probability of accepting such moves decreases as the al-
gorithm progresses, controlled by the temperature parameter T. This allows SA to first explore
widely and later converge toward a high-quality solution.
The Insert-Move heuristic embedded in the random step function ensures that each neighbor solu-
tion respects fundamental constraints while introducing variations in the allocation. By iteratively
refining solutions and penalizing excessive reductions in SOT and MOT, SA provides a flexible
and scalable approach to handling large-scale chute scheduling, making it a strong candidate for
real-world implementation.

8.4
Preliminary Conclusion
The analysis explored three key strategies to improve baggage chute allocation at Copenhagen
Airport: allowing controlled MOT reductions (FMOT15<), relaxing ground handler constraints,
and increasing the number of chutes. Each approach was evaluated in terms of its impact on

solution quality, feasibility, and operational practicality.
The first approach, allowing MOT reductions up to 15 minutes per chute, showed that CPH can
feasibly schedule up to 300 flights before excessive reductions become a concern. The results
indicate that permitting small reductions in MOT provides greater scheduling flexibility while still
ensuring operational feasibility. However, as the number of flights increases, the number of flights
exceeding this threshold also rises. While this approach is simple to implement, it does not provide
a long-term structural improvement to the allocation problem.
The second approach, relaxing ground handler constraints by increasing the number of FLEX
chutes, demonstrated a clear improvement in scheduling efficiency. The removal of rigid han-
dler restrictions allowed better utilization of available resources, significantly reducing penalty
values across all instances. However, this strategy is only practically viable if CPH can negoti-
ate adjustments to ground handler operations, as a daily reallocation of chutes is not feasible. In
the short term, expanding the FLEX chute pool appears to be a realistic, cost-effective solution,
requiring only policy changes rather than infrastructure expansion.
The final approach, increasing the total number of chutes, provided the most direct way to im-
prove scheduling outcomes. A 20% increase in chute capacity eliminated all MOT reductions in
Set A, while smaller increases still provided significant penalty reductions. However, this method
requires physical infrastructure expansion, making it the most resource-intensive solution. While
adding more chutes would offer the most substantial long-term improvements, it is likely not fea-
sible in the short term due to cost and space constraints at the airport.
Based on these findings, the most practical short-term solution is relaxing ground handler con-
straints by increasing the number of FLEX chutes. This adjustment demonstrates clear improve-
ments in scheduling while requiring minimal operational changes. Additionally, allowing con-
trolled MOT reductions up to 15 minutes per chute could serve as an adaptive measure to ac-
commodate peak scheduling demands without major disruptions. In the long term, increasing
the number of chutes remains the most effective strategy for completely eliminating scheduling
conflicts. However, given the significant investment required, this option should be evaluated
alongside other airport expansion projects to determine its feasibility. Ultimately, a combination
of increased scheduling flexibility, strategic infrastructure investments, and minor adjustments to
operational policies will likely yield the best results for CPH.

Discussion
9.1
Discussion of the Results
The results of this study highlight the trade-offs between computational feasibility and solution
quality in chute allocation. The transition from exact optimization (Model 1) to heuristic methods
(Model 2) demonstrated that exact methods, while theoretically optimal, are impractical for large-
scale scheduling. Conversely, heuristic and metaheuristic approaches provided computationally
feasible solutions, with SA emerging as the most effective method.
Model 1, formulated as a MIP, aimed to determine whether an exact solver could efficiently allocate
chutes while respecting all constraints. However, as problem size increased, its scalability rapidly
diminished. While the solver could handle small instances, solving times increased exponentially.
For 200 flights, the solver was unable to find a feasible solution within five hours, illustrating
the computational intractability of exact methods for large-scale scheduling. This confirms that
exact optimization, despite its theoretical guarantees, is not a viable option for CPH’s real-world
operations, as the computational burden far outweighs any potential benefits.
To address these limitations, Model 2 introduced heuristic methods. The greedy heuristic served
as a fast but myopic allocation strategy, where flights were assigned based on sorting criteria with-
out considering long-term feasibility. Among the four tested criteria, Earliest First, Biggest First
(EFBF) yielded the best results, prioritizing flights with early departures and large chute require-
ments. However, the results confirmed that while greedy heuristics are computationally efficient,
they lack mechanisms to refine solutions, often leading to suboptimal chute utilization.
To mitigate this, GRASP was introduced, incorporating controlled randomness via the Restricted
Candidate List. This improved performance by avoiding strictly greedy decisions, allowing for
more diverse allocations. However, the local search component (Insert-Move) was largely inef-
fective, as it failed to meaningfully refine solutions. This suggests that the local search operator was
too restrictive, making only small-scale perturbations that did not allow for meaningful structural
improvements.
Recognizing this limitation, SA was introduced to explore the solution space more effectively.
Unlike GRASP, SA probabilistically accepted worse solutions, enabling it to escape local min-
ima and converge toward improved allocations. The results demonstrated that SA consistently
outperformed both the greedy heuristic and GRASP, yielding solutions with significantly lower
penalties. Additionally, testing SA with a greedy initialization provided insights into whether a
structured starting solution accelerated convergence. While this approach led to faster improve-
ments for lower-density schedules, the overall results remained comparable, suggesting that SA’s
effectiveness is primarily driven by its probabilistic acceptance mechanism rather than its initial

solution. This indicates that for low-density cases, hybridizing greedy initialization with SA may
be beneficial, but for high-density cases, SA’s inherent search capability is sufficient.

Beyond heuristic comparisons, the results emphasize that flight density is a key determinant of so-
lution quality. During peak periods, chute shortages became inevitable, and no heuristic could fully
eliminate reductions in SOT or MOT. This reinforces the need for dynamic scheduling strategies
that proactively manage congestion. The penalty results showed that the number of flights alone
does not indicate the scale of the problem — high-density instances incurred greater penalties de-
spite having fewer flights than some lower-density counterparts. This confirms the observation
made in the literature that flight density is more influential than the total number of flights when
evaluating congestion levels.
Overall, the results validate heuristic and metaheuristic methods as effective alternatives to exact
optimization. The greedy heuristic provided speed but lacked flexibility, GRASP introduced ran-
domness but remained constrained by local search, and SA delivered the most robust results by
effectively navigating the solution space. These findings suggest that CPH should prioritize SA
for large-scale scheduling while considering hybrid methods for lower-density cases where greedy
initialization provides a head start. Additionally, since flight density significantly influences penal-
ties, future work could explore density-aware allocation strategies to further improve robustness
in high-demand scenarios.
9.2
Extensive Use
The optimization model developed in this thesis assumes a deterministic scheduling environment
where all flights are known in advance, and disruptions such as delays or baggage flow fluctua-
tions are not explicitly accounted for. This simplification ensures computational efficiency and
allows the proposed allocation strategies to be practically implemented. However, real-world bag-
gage handling is subject to uncertainty, which can impact chute utilization and overall system
performance. A key consideration for future extensions is the inclusion of buffer times to better
accommodate operational variability. Currently, chutes are assigned based on predefined SOT and
Optimization of Baggage Handling in Copenhagen Airport

MOT requirements, but in reality, flights may experience delays that disrupt the planned sched-
ule. Implementing dynamic buffer mechanisms, where chutes are scheduled with additional slack
time, could mitigate the risk of cascading delays when flights do not operate precisely as expected.
Additionally, integrating real-time adaptability into the scheduling process could enhance respon-
siveness to operational disruptions. While the current model optimizes allocations based on static
data, a dynamic system that updates chute assignments throughout the day could allow for adjust-
ments based on incoming flight delays or changes in baggage volume.
Beyond scheduling flexibility, further refinements could be introduced to account for baggage flow
efficiency. Currently, the heuristic and metaheuristic methods aim to minimize penalties related to
chute shortages, but they do not explicitly consider the impact on baggage transfer times. Future re-
search could incorporate constraints that optimize baggage movement within the terminal, ensuring
that luggage is routed efficiently and minimizing transit delays. This would be particularly relevant
for hub airports, where efficient baggage connections between flights are crucial for maintaining
tight transfer schedules. While this thesis focuses exclusively on chute allocation, an interesting
avenue for further study would be integrating this model with broader airport logistics, such as
baggage belt operations and ground handling processes. A multi-objective optimization approach
that simultaneously considers chute utilization, baggage flow, and operational constraints could
provide a more comprehensive scheduling solution. Although the model developed in this thesis
effectively addresses the chute allocation problem in a deterministic setting, real-world operations
require adaptability to changing conditions. By incorporating buffer times, dynamic scheduling,
and baggage flow constraints, future work could extend this model into a more flexible framework
capable of handling the complexities of large-scale airport operations.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the baggage chute allocation problem at Copenhagen Airport, aiming to
develop an optimization model that minimizes reductions in chute opening times while ensuring
operational feasibility. Given the complexity and scale of the problem, an exact MIP approach
was initially explored but proved computationally infeasible for large-scale instances. As a result,
heuristic and metaheuristic methods were introduced to provide practical solutions within reason-
able computational times.
The research demonstrated that while greedy heuristics offer a fast initial solution, their myopic
decision-making limits their effectiveness. Expanding on this, the GRASP introduced controlled
randomness, allowing for better allocations. However, its local search component was unable to
consistently improve solutions. SA emerged as the most effective approach, leveraging probabilis-
tic acceptance of worse solutions to explore a broader solution space and consistently outperform-
ing both greedy heuristics and GRASP in minimizing penalties.
Beyond algorithmic performance, the study analyzed key operational constraints affecting chute
allocation. The findings confirmed that flight density is a stronger determinant of scheduling dif-
ficulty than the sheer number of flights, as higher-density scenarios resulted in greater penalties
despite having fewer flights overall. Sensitivity analyses explored different mitigation strategies,
including increasing the number of chutes, relaxing ground handler constraints, and allowing lim-
ited flexibility in chute opening times. Among these, minor flexibility in MOT provided immediate
scheduling improvements with minimal operational impact, whereas increasing chute capacity sig-
nificantly reduced penalties but required substantial infrastructure investments.
In practice, these insights offer valuable recommendations for Copenhagen Airport. While expand-
ing chute capacity would yield the most substantial long-term benefits, immediate improvements
can be achieved by adjusting the ground handler constraints or allowing limited flexibility in chute
opening times. Implementing SA as the primary heuristic ensures efficient, scalable scheduling,
providing a balance between computational efficiency and solution quality.
Future research could explore real-time adaptability by incorporating stochastic elements to handle
last-minute schedule changes, such as flight delays or baggage handling disruptions. Addition-
ally, hybrid approaches combining heuristic methods with selective exact optimization for critical
flights could further enhance the robustness of the allocation process.
This thesis establishes a strong methodological foundation for optimizing baggage chute allocation,
demonstrating the feasibility of heuristic-based scheduling while offering actionable insights for
airport operations. The results contribute to both academic research in combinatorial optimization
and practical improvements in large-scale airport logistics.
