Abstract
In this thesis an indoor monostatic Radar Cross Section (RCS) measurement facility
is made for small scattering objects. The facility is established in a room which
has no Radar Absorbing Material on the walls, ceiling, or ﬂoor, i.e.
scattering
of electromagnetic waves will be strong at all surfaces.
The measurement setup
consists of a single antenna measuring the reﬂection coeﬃcient, S11, and the RCS
can be extracted from the measurements by the radar range equation.
Well known RCS measurement techniques are implemented to reduce reﬂections.
The Cancellation Method, the Background Nulling Method, the Null Position Method,
the Signal Subtraction Method, and the Time Gating Method have been imple-
mented, and the quality of each method have been assessed. A best practice for
measuring the RCS is to reduce reﬂections from the rooms by the Background
Nulling Method and the Null Position Method, and then digitally implement the
Signal Subtraction Method and the Time Gating Method.
With the best practise the measurement facility is able to estimate the RCS of
metallic spheres down to 0.25 cm in radius. It was observed that the measurement
results at the limits of the frequency range yielded wrong results, but corresponded
well for frequencies between 4.6 GHz and 5.6 GHz
A circular double split ring resonator (DSRR), also known as a unit cell, was mod-
elled in the computer aided design tool Computer Simulation Technology (CST)
Microwave Studio (MWS), and numerically calculated to resonate at 4.15 GHz.
The monostatic RCS was measured in the facility and the results compared well in
the frequency range above 4.4 GHz to 5 GHz. Below 4.4 GHz the unit cell deviated
by up to 10 dB from the numerical values. The deviation between experimental
data and numerical data is most likely caused by the manufacturing process, which
created discontinuities on the surface of the conducting material of the unit cell.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Much work have been done in the area of metamaterials (MTM) in the last two
decades. The aim of this work is to be able to construct bulk MTMs that can exhibit
either single negative- or double negative materials, so the physical phenomena
occurring for single negative- or double negative materials can be utilized.
The resonant behavior of the unit cells is important for creating bulk MTMs, and
diﬀerent types of unit cells have been investigated for this purpose. In literature the
resonance frequency is often determined by numerically calculating the transmission
coeﬃcient, S21. The numerical calculations are then validated through experiments
[4][11][26].
Investigations of the total- and diﬀerential radar cross section (RCS) have also been
done [3][12] since scattering is stronger at the resonance frequency. Experimental
investigations of the RCS of MTMs have been done in [29], but this type of investi-
gation is not easily found in literature.
This thesis aims to provide experimental data of the monostatic RCS for a well
known unit cell. In particular is a circular double split ring resonator (CDSRR)
designed in the computer aided design (CAD) tool computer simulation technol-
ogy microwave studio (CST MWS) and the diﬀerential RCS is acquired through
numerical calculations.
In order to be able to measure the monostatic diﬀerential RCS of the CDSRR, a
RCS measurement facility is needed. The room available for measurements is not
anachoic, so well known RCS measurement techniques is applied. A best practice is
given in terms of getting precise and accurate measurements of S11, from which the
RCS can extracted.
The thesis is divided into 7 chapters. Chapter 2 describes the theory of scattering
of electromagnetic waves with emphasize on RCS. Techniques for measuring the
diﬀerential RCS is given aswell. Chapter 3 contains a description of the instruments
used for the RCS measurements. In chapter 4 the focus will be on digital signal
processing and what theorems and phenomena are important for this thesis. Chapter

2.4
Antenna Ranges and Object Dimensions
Antennas are instruments that transmits or receives electromagnetic waves. When
the antenna transmits energy in the form of electromagnetic waves, the transmitted
ﬁeld intensity changes as a function of distance and angle. The space surrounding
the antenna can be divided into 3 regions based on ﬁeld distribution. These regions
are called: Reactive Near-Field Region, Radiating Near-Field Region (also called
the Fresnel region), Far-Field Region (also called the Fraunhofer region). From the
IEEE standards, [16], the regions are deﬁned in the following way.
Reactive Near-Field Region: "That portion of the near-ﬁeld region immediately
surrounding the antenna wherein the reactive ﬁeld predominates."
Radiating Near-Field Region: "That portion of the near-ﬁeld region of an antenna
between the far ﬁeld and the reactive portion of the near-ﬁeld region, wherein the
angular ﬁeld distribution is dependent upon the distance from the antenna."
Far-Field Region: "That region of the ﬁeld of an antenna where the angular ﬁeld
distribution is essentially independent of the distance from a speciﬁed point in the
antenna’s region".
The far ﬁeld distance is deﬁned as
R = 2D2/λ

wavelength. This quantity comes from the derivation of the phase error of a wave at

observation point is R = 2D2/λ or farther away. The regions are shown in ﬁgure

2.5
General overview of RCS measurements
RCS measurements can be done on outdoor- and indoor ranges. Each range presents
challenges and advantages, which can be useful or not for a given measurement
system. Outdoor ranges are generally in an open space, which means weather can
aﬀect measurements, and unauthorized sources can observe the measurements.
Indoor ranges are shielded from weather, but suﬀers from reﬂections from the bound-
aries of the indoor location. These reﬂections can be reduced by forming the walls
of the room, and the room itself [18]. For most indoor RCS measurements Radar
Absorbing Material (RAM) is used on surfaces, to reduce the amplitudes of the
reﬂections. Indoor locations where reﬂections have been reduced are referred to as
an-echoic chambers. In addition are an-echoic chambers is usually shielded from
outside signals.
Measurement systems are characterized by how energy is transmitted, and we will
brieﬂy describe the following: Continuous Wave (CW) systems, Doppler systems,
Pulsed systems, and Frequency Modulated (FM) systems. Thorough explanations
of the systems are given in [25] and [18]. A variety of measurement techniques exist
for measuring the RCS of objects. The techniques imply a speciﬁc measurement
system and a speciﬁc measurement setup.
The CW system transmits continuously at a single frequency, ft This means the
receiver measures continuously. The CW system can not measure range, but an
advantage of this system is that it is simple and component wise not very costly.
The CW system can be used for both indoor- and outdoor ranges, but is most often
used for measuring moving targets, i.e. making use of the doppler eﬀect.
11
==PAGE:25==
CHAPTER 2. RADAR CROSS SECTION
A doppler system utilizes the Doppler eﬀect, which means the object we want to
measure has to be moving. The velocity of the object will cause a a shift in frequency,
±fd, where the minus indicates the object is moving away from the receiver and the
plus indicates the object is moving towards the receiver. For the doppler system to
work, the object must be moving.
The pulsed system transmits a pulse of energy and then waits for the return of that
pulse. This requires the receiver to activate at exactly the time, when the return
response appears at the receiver antenna. This makes it less suitable for indoor
ranges as the recovery times has to be very small in order to switch from transmit
to receive. In outdoor ranges this is useful, as unwanted reﬂections can be ﬁltered
out by the close time of the receiver.
The output signal in this thesis is FM system, which allows for range determination.
This system is useful for indoor ranges, because the same antenna can be used for
transmit and receive, and the range is short. Caution must be taken as we have to
ensure the reﬂection of the swept signal is received before transmitting a new signal.
This problem will be addressed later.
2.6
RCS Measurement Techniques for Indoor Ranges
It is the intention to measure the reﬂected energy from the object, when the antenna
transmits energy. The received energy, however, is a combination of the reﬂected
energy from the room and the object. The reﬂections from the room are unwanted
and will be referred to as unwanted signals. There are various methods to reduce
the unwanted signals. The methods mentioned in the following are common in RCS
measurements, and they are listed in no particularly order.
2.6.1
Cancellation method
The cancellation method is about measuring the pure object response by cancelling
out the response of the room. The cancellation of the room response is done by
generating a signal with equal amplitude of the room response, but 180◦out of
phase.
The cancellation signal can be generated by using a magic T waveguide
which is terminated in a load. The load can be made as a piston for purposes of
tuning the cancellation to a speciﬁc frequency.
The received response, s, is thus not contaminated by the response of the room.
The signal s, however, contains not only the response of the object, so, but also the
response of the object scattered wave, se, that is reﬂected back to the receiver from
the boundaries of the room. The response received after cancellation is s = so + se,
where se is due to multipath reﬂections and is small compared to so.

The Background Nulling method is about shaping or forming the background, i.e.
the room, so the response of the room cancels itself. The idea of this method is
similar to the idea of the Cancellation method, but the Background Nulling method
is less adjustable but can work for several frequencies.
The Null Positioning Method is about making the nulls of the radiation pattern of

the antenna direct towards the most signiﬁcant background scatterers in the room.
Thus the reﬂections from these scatterers are reduced.
The Subtraction Method is a mathematical process. A reference measurement, sr,
of the room is made. Next, is the object placed in the room, and the response, so,
is measured. The measurement consist of two elements. The pure object response,
so,1, and the room response when the object is in it, so,2. This last response is
diﬀerent from sr, hence sr −so,2 = se. This is due to multipath reﬂections and the
shadowing eﬀect from the object.

(2.30)

The Background Nulling Method, the Null Positioning Method and the Subtraction
Method are not stand alone methods, since the RCS of an object can not be derived
by using only them.
2.6.4
The Time Gating Method
The pulsed system is basically a hardware Time Gating Method. The pulse systems
only receives signals at time instances that equal the duration of expected signals.
This means signals appearing at the receive antenna at all other time instances than
the expected signal are not measured.
It was mentioned, that pulse systems are unideal for indoor ranges, because the
demands of the receiving mechanism is too high. The Time Gating Method can
also be applied digitally. A signal in the frequency domain can be transformed to
the time domain by the Fourier Transform (FT), and the object response can be
digitally time gated to ﬁlter out unwanted reﬂections, and then transformed back
to the frequency domain. The RCS can then be found from the ﬁltered signal.
The digital time gating method is suitable for indoor ranges because of the low
requirements to the setup.
Furthermore is it an absolute method, that can be
used to get the RCS for a large range of frequencies. The Cancellation Method,
Background Nulling method, Null Positioning Method, Subtraction Method, and
the Time Gating Method will be investigated.

4.1
Sampling and Sampling Phenomenons
In the present work the data consists of discrete samples of continuous signals, i.e.
we get a discrete representation of the continuous signal. The discrete representation
will be called the discrete signal. For the discrete signal to represent all frequency
components of a continuous signal, the sampling frequency, fs, must be at least
twice as high as the highest frequency component inherent in the continuous signal,
fh, i.e. fs ≥2fh.
When fs = 2fh we have the minimum sampling frequency required to get acceptable
information from the signal. This frequency is called the Nyquist frequency. Un-
dersampling is fs < 2fh and oversampling is fs > 2fh. Undersampling can lead to
aliasing, which is when high frequency components is represented as low frequency
components.
The time resolution, dt, is the measure of the capability to distinguish signal values

in the frequency domain.

of the time domain signal.

When signals are sampled they are truncated. Transforming a truncated time do-

appears for transforming abrupt changes in the frequency domain.

Reﬂections from the room
The antenna transmits a wave, which will propagate in all directions with amplitude
characteristics determined by the radiation pattern. The strongest reﬂections will
come from surfaces, that has form of strong reﬂectors, e.g. ﬂat surfaces that the
wave is perpendicular on. Another strong type of reﬂector that is present in the
room is the corner reﬂector. There are reﬂections from the walls, ﬂoor, and ceiling,
and some of the critical paths from the room is depicted in Figure 5.2.
Figure 5.2: Trace lines of critical paths in the MR. The middle of the SS is the point
in space, where the object is at the far ﬁeld of the antenna. A - Antenna, SW -
SideWall, O - Object, BW - BackWall, and C - Corner.
The path of the reﬂection from the object is given by A-O-A, and is the relevant
reﬂection. The backwall forms a ﬂat surface, that will yield a strong reﬂection. This
path is given by A-BW-A. The intersection between backwall and ﬂoor/ sidewall/-
ceiling, forms a corner reﬂector, which is referenced C. The path for this reﬂection
is given as A-C-A.
More complex reﬂections are also present. The transmitted wave can be incident on
the side wall, ceiling, or ﬂoor, be reﬂected in the direction of the object, and then
will the reﬂection from the object be intercepted by the antenna. A path like that
could be A-SW-O-A. This is known as multipaths.
This response is unwanted, since that path is not accounted for in the radar range
equation. Ultimately we want to reduce these reﬂections or be able to separate them
from the object impulse. Radar Absorbing Material (RAM) was chosen as a starting
point to reduce amplitudes of the strong reﬂectors.

Signal Subtraction Method
In the following the empty room measurement will be referred to as the reference
signal or reference measurement, the object measurement will be referred to as
the object signal or object measurement, and the signal that is the result of the
subtraction will be referred to as the result signal.
In the time domain, the diﬀerence between the reference measurement and the
object measurement is not only at the position of the object. This is because the
electromagnetic wave is scattered in all directions by the object and the scattered
waves interfere constructively or destructively with the reﬂections from the room.
Thus there will be diﬀerences at other positions in the time domain as well. The
object also blocks the wave, and the transmitted wave will not interact directly with
what lies behind the object as for the reference measurement. This eﬀect is called
the shadowing eﬀect, and will add to the diﬀerences in magnitudes.
The measurement data of the sphere of radius 18 cm sphere is used for the inves-
tigation. In Figure 5.15 we have plotted the signal before subtraction and after
subtraction.
The subtraction technique might not have a big eﬀect for strong object reﬂections,
since the diﬀerence in magnitudes are large. For weak object reﬂections the signal
subtraction technique can have a big eﬀect. The signal subtraction method will be

Metamaterials and Experimental
Results
Materials are characterized by their constitutive parameters: the relative permittiv-
ity, ϵr, and the relative permeability µr. For naturally occurring materials these are
positive and above 1. The exception is vacuum/free space which has the relative
constitutive parameters of 1, i.e. ϵr = 1 and µr = 1.
Metamaterials (MTM) are characterized by having negative constitutive parameters.
MTMs have become a widespread topic of interest, because materials with negative
ϵr and µr can be used as components in electromagnetic cloaking, super lenses, and
other not realizable with positive ϵr and µr, i.e. natural materials.
The procedure of designing and constructing MTMs relies on arranging unit cells
in a periodic pattern or grid to have a volume that is eﬀectively a (meta)material,
for which there can be found an eﬀective set of constitutive parameters. This is
called homogenization, and is imitating the atomic structure of natural materials.
An example of a bulk MTM is shown in Figure 6.1, where the small squares are the
unit cells.

Chapter 7
Summary and Conclusion
In this project we have established a monostatic radar cross section (RCS) measure-
ment facility, where reducing unwanted reﬂections and getting accurate results was
of great diﬃculty. The operational frequency range of the system is from 3.8 GHz
to 6 GHz. The measurement facility is used to measure the monostatic RCS of a
circular Double Split Ring Resonator (DSRR) over the frequency range 3.8 GHz to
5 GHz.
The circular DSRR is modelled and simulated in Computer Design Technology Mi-
crowave Studio (CST MSW). The transmission coeﬃcient S21 is simulated for a
waveguide environment, and the bistatic RCS is simulated in a free space envrion-
ment, from which the backscattered RCS is extracted.
The conducting material of the circular DSRR is copper and the substrate is Rohacell
HF 51, and it was manufactured in a fotographical process.
The fotographical
process did not manage to corrode the cobber in the correct places, and the unit cell
was in a bad condition as a result of this. We want to improve this in future work.
The measurements of the circular DSRR and the numerical calculations corre-
sponded very well with the measurements for frequencies above 4.4 GHz. Below
4.4 GHz the measurements had the same curve shape as the numerical data, but
deviated with up to 10 dB.
The measurements facility was tested with metallic spheres as objects. The facility
proved to be able to measure metallic spheres down 0.25 cm, with some inaccuracies
in the frequency limits of 3.8 GHz to 6 GHz.
It was seen from measurements of RAM in front of the antenna, that reﬂections
from the waveguide and antenna are quite big. The RAM would reduce ripples in
the frequency domain, and in the time domain there were large responses close to
the reference plane. In furture work we want to adress this problem, and we want to
implement the Cancellation Method by a magic T waveguide with a tunable load.
Further could the Standing Wave Method be implemented easily.

Appendix D
Data Accuracy and Precision
In order to acquire accurate, precise, and enough data for making data processing,
the VNA has to be set so frequency domain resolution is acceptable, time domain
resolution is acceptable, and the measurements are stable and robust. The operation
of the hardware in the VNA will not be explained here, but those parameters which
have an eﬀect on on the quality of the data will be brieﬂy explained.
A block diagram of the system operation of the HP8753 network analyzer is shown
in ﬁgure D.1, and is taken directly from the user manual.
Figure D.1: System operation of HP8753
An RF signal is generated at the source block, and is applied to the test-set block,
which contains hardware to route the signal to the device under test (DUT) and into
the receiver. A a small portion of the source signal is led into the receiver, input R,
for frequency- and phase reference purposes. We are concerned only with the data
acquisition, and so the system operation will not be explained any further.
Measured signals are incident at input ports R, A, and B and will be called carrier
signals. The receiver samples the carrier signals and downconverts them to a ﬁxed
intermediate frequency (IF) of 4 kHz. The digital signals are then processed by the
analyzer microprocessers.
The following user settings have an impact on the quality of the digital data: fre-
quency span, number of data points, output power, sweep time, sweep type, IF
73
==PAGE:87==
APPENDIX D. DATA ACCURACY AND PRECISION
bandwidth, averaging factor and smoothing.
The output power is set to 10 dBm to get the largest reﬂection from the object in
terms of amplitude. This means the amplitudes of the unwanted reﬂections also
will be the largest. The sweep type determines how the VNA outputs the range of
frequencies to be measured. The sweep type is set to "Linear Frequency Sweep",
because we are measuring a large range of frequencies and we want good resolution
for each sample. This means the frequency of the output signal will start low and
increase linearly. One such cycle will be referred to as a sweep.
Number of data points ranges from: [3 11 26 51 101 201 401 801 1601], and is the
measure of resolution in the frequency domain. The number of data points is linearly
proportional with the duration in the time domain. The eﬀect of changing number
of points will be discussed below for values of 201, 401, and 801.
The averaging factor determines how many consecutive sweeps are averaged. The
averaging process will reduce noise and make measurements more consistent. The
factor ranges from 1 to 999, and the higher the number the longer it takes to get a
fully averaged data set. The eﬀect of an increasing averaging factor will be investi-
gated in this section.
When the carrier signal is downconverted to IF, intermodulated signals are generated
alongside. These must be ﬁltered out and thus a IF bandpass ﬁlter is used. The
IF bandwidth (BW) is usercontrolled and has a big eﬀect on the noise level in a
signal. A narrow bandwidth IF ﬁlter will ﬁlter the IF component and contain little
noise, while a large bandwidth will not ﬁlter the signal perfectly and contains more
noise. A narrow IF BW is preferred, because noise is reduced. The trade oﬀis,
measurement times are longer for narrower IF BWs. A factor 10 in diﬀerence for IF
BWs are investigated in the following.
Another method of processing the data directly on the VNA is by smoothing the
trace. The smoothing operation, is a function that can be turned on or oﬀmanually
on the VNA. This operation performs a running average on an acquired data point
with a band of adjacent data points. The band can be as large as 20% of the swept
span (linear frequency span is swept). The smoothing operation will not be used,
because it can mask signal amplitudes.

CST uses and adaptive mesh reﬁnement method. When calculating a parameter
within a speciﬁed frequency span, the mesh will increase locally when the calculation
of a parameter is done. Then the mesh of the model will increase, and the parameter
is calculated again based on the new increased mesh. The two parameter values are
compared and if their values do not diﬀer more than the threshold value (set by the
user) the adaptive mesh reﬁnement method marks 1 pass.
