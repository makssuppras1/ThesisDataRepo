Abstract
Antimicrobial properties are desired characteristics of a lactic acid bacteria (LAB) to be
used in food production. There is a need to develop starter cultures speciﬁc for camel
dairy with satisfying acidiﬁcation rates and good characteristics, to ensure high quality
and food safety.
Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis strains isolated from camel milk in Ethiopia have shown
to be able to decrease and eliminate multiple common food pathogens. Many antimicro-
bial factors can be inﬂuencing as lactic acid, the redox potential, bacteriocins, diacetyl
and hydrogen peroxide. Previous studies have shown that especially H2O2 may have a
high contribution to the antimicrobial potential identiﬁed in the L. lactis subsp. lactis
MS22333 and MS22337 strains.
Using bioinformatic tools three genes ahpF, lctO and sodA known to be inﬂuencing H2O2
accumulation were identiﬁed in both strains, with high similarity to relevant gene se-
quences. A hit against pyruvate oxidase was also identiﬁed, but mutations have caused
multiple stop codons within the protein domains. The NADH oxidases ahpC and noxE
was also identiﬁed, as important genes for the redox potential.
The project also included identiﬁcation of bacteriocins, but it was not possible to identify
any known peptides. Possible bacteriocin-like-inhibitory substances (BLIS) was found as
multiple hits were identiﬁed when BLAST against lactococcin A and B.
Finally primers applicable to both strains were designed for each gene involved with H2O2
accumulation, so future studies will be able to study the inﬂuence of each individual gene
on the total accumulation concentration by knock-out.

1
Introduction
Microorganisms are essential for the dairy industry. Many dairy products rely on fermentation
technologies to produce taste, texture and to ensure food safety.
Camel milk is a crucial diet constituent in African countries. Multiple studies have recently been
focusing on developing dairy products using camel milk, which have shown to be a challenge
[1–4]. Lack of hygiene and good manufacturing practices might also be a problem in African
countries. A study found 108 (85.7%) camel milk samples contaminated with multiple known
pathogens as Staphylococcus spp., E.coli, Salmonella spp, Klebsiella spp., Streptococcus spp.
and Enterobacter spp. [5]. There is thereby a need for starter cultures speciﬁc for camel dairy
with optimum acidiﬁcation rates and antimicrobial properties limiting the growth of spoilage
and pathogen microorganisms possibly present to increase the quality and safety of the milk.
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are known for their antimicrobial properties, mainly related to the
production of lactic acid, diacetyl, ethanol and hydrogen peroxide [6, 7]. Antimicrobial sub-
stances may have technological characteristics and health-promoting eﬀects but most essential
may be used as a food preservative in the food industry to extend the shelf life of food products
[8].
Multiple studies have shown that hydrogen peroxide alone or in combination with lactoperox-
idase, an enzyme naturally present in raw milk, can inhibit growth of multiple pathogens in
milk and dairy products [9–12].
This project concerns LAB isolated from fermented camel milk in Ethiopia [13]. Two interest-
ing Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis strains were identiﬁed with antimicrobial potential, as they
could inhibit and kill E.coli in raw camel milk [14]. Both strains showed production of H2O2
in contrast to two similar strains without any antimicrobial eﬀect against E.coli and H2O2
production. It is known that the antimicrobial potential may be a combined eﬀect of multiple
factors. The eﬀect of H2O2 have been documented in multiple studies, but only a few have
researched and described the genomics behind [10, 12].

1.1
The aims of the project
One aim of this project is to map out the genes possibly involved in hydrogen peroxide pro-
duction and identify their presence in L. lactis subsp. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 using
bioinformatic tools. Other genes inﬂuencing H2O2 catalysing systems will be included as well.
Especially the alkyl hydroperoxide reductase (AhpR) system and the NADH oxidase NoxE.
A second aim is to compare the identiﬁed genes with other relevant known protein sequences
from both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. Furthermore, their individual contribu-
tion to the overall H2O2 accumulation is interesting. Due to the circumstances it cannot be
tested in the laboratory in this project, but unique primers will be constructed, so knock-out
mutants can be created in the future.
The AhpR system contains two enzymes, AhpF and AhpC, where AhpF catalyse the produc-
tion of H2O2, and is assumed to have an essential role regarding the antimicrobial potential.
Some of the genes contains disulphide centers, and their importance and conservation between
bacterial species will be investigated using relevant studies and amino acid alignments.
A third aim is to study other relevant antimicrobial agents. The antimicrobial potential may
be a combination of multiple agents produced by the strains. Other L. lactis strains have
demonstrated production of bacteriocins.
Using genomic search algorithms the genomes of
L. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 will be studied to identify genes of known bacteriocins or
promising genes of possible novel bacteriocins.

Theory
2.1
The environment in camel milk
Camel milk is produced by the genus Camelus. Species living Africa, having one hump, is called
Camelus dromedarius, where camels with two humps normally living in Asia and referred to as
Camelus bactrianus [1]. The global Camelus population is estimated to be 35 millions [15].
Camel milk is comparable to bovine milk in terms of lactose, fat and protein, but the relative
composition of both caseins and whey proteins is diﬀerent [1]. Bioactive compounds such as
peptides from camel milk proteins have been reported in a study to confer both antimicrobial
and antiviral properties. Multiple Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria were inhibited,
and especially lactoferrin combined with lactoperoxidase showed antiviral eﬀects against hep-
atitis C [1].
2.2
Lactic Acid Bacteria
Lactic Acid Bacteria refers to the metabolic ability to produce lactic acid from a carbon source.
Multiple genera belongs to the group of LAB microorganisms as Aerococcus, Carnobacterium,
Enterococcus, Lactobacillus, Lactococcus, Leuconostoc, Oenococcus, Pediococcus, Streptococcus,
Tetragenococcus, Vagococcus and Weissella [16]. LAB are used dominantly in the dairy indus-
try but also in other fermented food industries, as they have gained the status of both generally
regarded as safe (GRAS) and qualiﬁed for presumption of safety (QPS) [17]. LAB both con-
tribute to food ﬂavouring, texturing and shelf life. Furthermore, some are capable of producing
antimicrobials such as hydrogen peroxide, bacteriocins and other peptides [18]. They can either
be homo- or heterofermentative depending on their metabolism of carbohydrates.
Lactococcus lactis are mesophilic and homofermentative Gram-positive bacteria.
The main
qualities found in many L. lactis strains are the production of lactic acid from lactose, diacetyl
from citrate and its proteolytic activity, which are the main qualities for aroma in dairy pro-
duction [19].
L. lactis is categorized into two subspecies L. lactis subsp. lactis and L. lactis subsp. cremoris.
[18–20]

2.3
Metabolism in Lactococcus spp.
The most fundamental metabolism in LAB is the carbohydrate catabolism generating energy for
biosynthesis. Both L. lactis MS22333 and L. lactis MS22337 are homofermentative LAB. Ho-
mofermentative bacteria ferment carbohydrates to lactic acid producing adenosine triphosphate
(ATP). The most abundant carbohydrate in camel milk is lactose in concentrations of 2.4-5.8
% [22]. Lactose is a disaccharide consisting of a glucose and galactose linked by a β(1-4) gly-
cosidic bond, that is cleaved internally by β-galactosidase in most LAB [23]. Uptake of lactose
and galactose require further metabolism before the glycolysis. The pathways responsible for
catabolism of lactose and galactose into intermediates ﬁtting the glycolosis, is the Leloir and the
Tagatose-6-Phosphate pathways. The end-product of the Leloir pathway is glucose-1P, which
can be interconverted to glucose-6P by phosphoglucomutase and enter the glycolysis as shown
in Figure 1. The end-products of the Tagatose-6P pathway is glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate and

2.4
The Redox potential and its inﬂuence in dairy
Lactic bacteria are normally characterised by their acidiﬁcation rates but other parameters
may be important. The redox potential (Eh) is a parameter describing the physico-chemical
environment in the milk. Eh is contributing to the microbial quality and the formation of
ﬂavour compounds during fermentation [27]. It has been found to aﬀect the growth of spoilage
and pathogenic microorganisms and expression of virulence factors [28]. The redox potential
can be deﬁned as measure of a biochemical system to lose electrons (oxidise) or gain electrons
(reduction). A positive mV value describes a system capable of oxidising, while a negative mV
value describes a reducing system [28, 29]. LAB can be diﬀerentiated by the Eh value during
growth, where some as Streptococcus thermophilus results in a positive mV [29]. L. lactis is
one of the LAB that produces a negative Eh, especially during the fermentation. The level of
oxygen have shown to be inﬂuencing Eh [28].
Figure 2: Reduction of O2 inside the L. lactis cell by NoxE and the alkyl hydroperoxide reductase
system (AhpR) composed by AhpC and AhpF. The ﬁgure is borrowed from Tachon et al. (2010) [28].
The complete mechanism behind the redox potential have not yet been fully described, but
the level of dissolved oxygen present and the rate of reduction have been thought to be related
[30]. L. lactis does have the genes coding for the electron transport chain, but lacks the genes

2.5
Oxidative stress in L. lactis
L. lactis can persist multiple environmental changes [31]. One change may be between aerobic
and anaerobic growth conditions, where oxygen is present. Oxygen can not do any harm to the
bacterial cell itself, but through the metabolism O2 may lead to reactive O2 intermediates. L.
lactis have developed several pathways to protect itself from superoxide and hydrogen peroxide,
which is crucial as the species is catalase negative. [31, 32]
One of the primary reasons for its survival during aerobic conditions is due to a group of enzymes
as superoxide dismutase (SodA) and multiple NADH oxidases. SodA is an enzyme found in
many LAB as a resistance mechanism to transform oxygen radicals to H2O2. NADH oxidases
(AhpR and NoxE) are important for regenerating NAD+, survive aeration and both resist
and produce H2O2. Especially the production of hydrogen peroxide is of interest. The alkyl
hydroperoxide reductase system (AhpR) consist partly of nox-1 (AhpF) and partly peroxidase
(AhpC). Nox-1 is catalysing the formation of H2O2, where AhpC is decomposing H2O2 to
water. Furthermore has NoxE shown to be essential for early oxygen consumption [28]. The
enzymatic catalysed reactions are as shown:

2.6
Antimicrobial agents produced by lactic acid bacteria
LAB produce multiple compounds that are crucial for food safety and shelf-life of especially
fermented foods. Some of the agents produced by these bacteria are organic acids, bacteriocins,
12
==PAGE:14==
hydrogen peroxide, lactoperoxidase and diacetyl [33]. Lactic acid is the most relevant organic
acid concerning inhibition of pathogens. Lactic acid is a weak acid with a pKa of 3.86, but
during fermentation the pH of the milk drops to 4-4.5 due to a high concentration. The high
concentration of lactic acid outcompete many microorganisms, who are inhibited by the low
pH [34].
2.6.1
Hydrogen peroxide
Bacteria are often studied alone but in natural environments multiple bacteria will coexist and
compete for colonisation. Bacteria may produce bacteriostatic and bactericidal substances to
inhibit or even kill competitors. Hydrogen peroxide have been studied in several bacteria, as
multiple species have shown increased production levels and antibacterial properties [10–12].
Hydrogen peroxide may either be a bacteriostatic or bactericidal depending on the concentra-
tion. The mechanism behind is oxidation of cell material both at the cell wall surface and
internal. H2O2 oxidise chemical compounds especially thiol groups (-SH) from DNA, proteins,
enzymes and at the cell wall [35]. Thiol groups are mainly derivatives from cysteine residues
and crucial for protein and enzyme activity [36, 37]. Misfolding of enzymes or changes in the
active site due to oxidation may lead to cell inhibition or even cell death, making H2O2 toxic
to many microorganisms without any active defence mechanism as catalase.
Multiple genes are involved with hydrogen peroxide production in LAB. The most essential
genes are sodA, ahpF, L-lactate oxidase (lctO) and pyruvate oxidase (poxL) [31].
SodA is
a common resistance enzyme removing oxygen radicals to produce H2O2.
Most LAB have
superoxide dismutase activity, and results shows that especially under aeration are sod mutants
growing slower [31].
Many LAB can produce both isomers of lactic acid namely D(-) and L(+)lactate. L-lactate
oxidase is a ﬂavoprotein consisting of 383 amino acids. LctO converts L-lactate and O2 to
pyruvate and H2O2 and seem to be induced by the concentration of L-lactate [38].
Pyruvate oxidase (poxL) is another enzyme found in LAB. PoxL can be triggered to convert
pyruvate, phosphate and O2 to acetylphosphate, CO2 and H2O2. Limited information is known
about this enzyme in Lactococcus strains. Acetylphosphate can further be converted into acetyl-
CoA for further metabolism [31].

2.6.2
The lactoperoxidase system
Lactoperoxidase (LP) is a peroxidase enzyme found in raw mammalian milk [39]. LP catalyse
the oxidation of thiocyanate to hypothiocyanate (OSCN −) using H2O2. Hypothiocyanate is
antimicrobial with a wide spectrum of organisms such as fungi, viruses, Gram-positive and
Gram-negative bacteria [9]. Activation both require thiocyanate (SCN −) and H2O2. Thio-
cyanate is naturally present in the milk at concentrations around 1-15 ppm, depending on feed,
species and health of the mammalian [39]. As described earlier H2O2 can be produced by vari-
ous lactic acid bacteria and thereby trigger the LP system. The LP system have been reported
to be important for raw milk, cheese and yoghurt. Especially in areas where cooling is limited,
activation of the LP system by adding 10 ppm H2O2 is recommended [10, 40].
2.6.3
bacteriocins
Lactic acid bacteria produce diverse types of ribosomal synthesized peptides varying in size,
inhibitory spectrum, heat stability and structural conformation. Bacteriocins are mostly color-
and tasteless and their mode of action are often in terms of pore formation or inhibition of
cell wall synthesis [41]. Gram-positive bacteriocins are overall characterised in three classes.
Class 1 are bacteriocins which are modiﬁed post translation, containing lanthionine, β-methyl
lanthionine and dehydroamino acids. Examples are Nisin (A,U,Z) and subtilosin A. Class 2
are unmodiﬁed peptides that can both be circular or linear and both consist of a single or two
peptides. Examples of class 2 bacteriocins are lactococcin (A,B), carnocyclin A and lactacin
F. Class 3 are peptides longer than 30 kDa and often heat unstable. To compare is the size of
bacteriocins in class 1 and 2 smaller than 10 kDa. Examples of class 3 bacteriocins are Caseicin
80, and Enterolisin A.[41, 42]
Lactococcin
Lactococcin is a group of bacteriocins belonging to class 2 and produced by Lactococcus lactis to
inhibit other lactococci. The ﬁrst lactococcin were reported back in 1991 and named lactococcin
A (LcnA) [43]. Since, a close related bacteriocin has been identiﬁed as lactococcin B (LcnB).
The structural diﬀerences are shown in Figure 3 as an amino acid alignment. The 21 amino
acids from the N terminal is the signal peptide, which is conserved between the two peptides.
The glycine-glycine position (pos. 20-21) is the proteolytic processing site, which have shown

to be general for multiple class 2 bacteriocins [44]. Both lactococcin A and lactococcin B are
hydrophobic with a positive charge [44, 45]. The activity of lactococcin B has shown to be
controlled by the cysteine residue at position 24 of the active peptide.

2.7
Gene knock-out using homologous recombination
Homologous recombination (HR) is a genetic tool to modify the genome. Genetic tools have
been developed a lot the last years and many opportunities for genome editing is available
in some bacteria and yeasts. Tools developed for LAB have been limited due to strict rules
within the food industry and a negative consumer perception towards gene modiﬁed organisms
(GMO).
Even without the GMO ending up in the ﬁnal food product, genetic tools are important for
research and evaluation of genes and their expected trait. Improved understanding of the micro-
bial metabolism and production of speciﬁc compounds is of great importance, when developing
modern food products.
Homologous recombination is a plasmid-based genome editing tool using the natural damage
repair system present in bacteria. To start the recombination in a bacterial cell homologous
sequences are required. The most simple procedure is to include a homologue sequence from
the start of the gene followed by an antibiotic resistance gene and then another homologue
sequence from the end of the sequence as shown in Figure 4. The antibiotic resistance gene will
be used as a positive selection marker, as the placement between the two homologue sequences
will cause antibiotic resistance in bacteria where the HR is successful. The easiest way to screen
for resistant bacteria is to include the antibiotic in the growth media on a agar plate. Many
diﬀerent methods and procedures using the HR principle have been developed and is available
[46].

3
Methods
3.1
Genomic analysis of DNA and protein sequences
The DNA sequences of MS22333 and MS22337 were imported into CLC Main Workbench
(version 20.0.3 CLC bio, Qiagen, Aarhus, Denmark) and saved before further use.
3.1.1
Identiﬁcation of genes
Identiﬁcation of genes were performed using both local BLAST inside CLC Main Workbench
and BLAST at Uniprot [47]. The gene of interest were downloaded and imported into CLC
Main Workbench followed by a BLAST against all the contigs from both strains. Signiﬁcant
hits were evaluated and the open reading frame (ORF) were identiﬁed. The ORF were then
transferred into the Uniprot server for a second BLAST evaluating the best gene hit and its
origin. The result of the BLAST were imported for annotation in CLC Main Workbench.
3.1.2
Primer design
The design of the primers were carried out by making an amino acid alignment of the genes in
each strain using the tool in CLC Main Workbench. The tool Primer Design were chosen and
regions with 100% identity were annotated as forward and reverse primer regions. The primer
parameters were speciﬁed to be: a length between 15-20 bases, minimum melting temperature
of 50◦C and a maximum melting temperature of 55◦C. The primers were chosen based on their
overall primer score, which is calculated using a CLC Main Workbench algorithm.
Primer candidates were validated by a local BLAST against all contigs from both strains to
check for speciﬁcity.
3.1.3
Identiﬁcation of bacteriocins
The presence of bacteriocins were assessed by the Bagel4 [48] web-based server and the Rapid
Annotations using Subsystems Technology (RAST)[49] server. Both servers identiﬁed area of
interest (AOI) based on both BLAST and motifs of known bacteriocins. The resulting AOIs
were imported to CLC Main Workbench and the complete ORF where identiﬁed. The ORF
were copied and used for a second BLAST at Uniprot, to identify if the hit belonged to a known
17
==PAGE:19==
bacteriocin. ORF without any speciﬁc hits at Uniprot where further assessed by looking both
up- and downstream for bacteriocin like structures.
3.1.4
Amino acid alignments
Amino acid sequences were compared using the alignment tool in CLC Main Workbench. Stan-
dard parameters were chosen. Conservation in percentage were added together with matching
residues shown as dots to visualize similarities.
3.2
Data availability
The bacterial sequences have been stored at Genbank and published [50]. Both raw reads and
the assembled contigs are available. L. lactis subsp. lactis MS22333 contigs can be retrieved
under the accession no. WWDI00000000 and L. lactis subsp. lactis MS22337 can be accessed
under WWDK00000000.

5
Discussion
This project aimed to identify genes known to be involved in H2O2 production in similar strains,
and look for their presence in L. lactis subsp. lactis MS22333 and MS22337.
5.1
H2O2 genes identiﬁed
Three genes responsible for H2O2 production were identiﬁed in both L. lactis strains using
bioinformatic tools. Both AhpF, L-lactate oxidase and superoxide dismutase were found to be
very similar to other Lactococcus ssp.
The amino acid alignment across all three genes showed high conservation between L. lactis
strains, but it was also noticed that the overall structure of the genes was very similar to other
species as B. subtilis, S. aureus and E. coli.
ahpF showed very high conservation at both active sites (C-X-X-C), which have been reported
previously [32]. The same study found that AhpF was active at moderate H2O2 concentrations,
where higher concentrations of either NAD+ or H2O2 inhibited the enzyme. AhpF the nox-1
oxidase is a part of the AhpR system composed of AhpC and AhpF. ahpC is normally found
very close to ahpF upstream, which was also seen in these strains. The overall AhpR reaction
is similar to NoxE, converting O2 to H2O, while oxidising NADH to NAD+. Tachon et al. [28]
found that NoxE is essential during oxidative stress at aerobic conditions in the early phase,
while the inﬂuence of AhpF on the level of dissolved oxygen and decrease of the redox potential
was limited. The results indicate that even though both NoxE and AhpR have the same overall
reaction, the L. lactis strains may use them for diﬀerent purposes. One possible explanation
could be that NoxE is responsible for O2 consumption, while AhpF is expressed suﬃcient to
produce an antimicrobial environment by H2O2.
The L-lactate oxidase have been isolated in multiple bacterial species. The enzyme was ﬁrst
identiﬁed and characterised in L. lactis by Toda and Nishyia in 1998 [38]. They found that
L-lactate oxidase relative activity is highest at pH 7 and as low as 20% at pH 5 and 8.5. Based
on their ﬁndings L-lactate oxidase in MS22333 and MS22337 would be expected to have most
activity during the very early stage of fermentation. The idea of having enzymes most active
during the early stages, responsible for making the environment unfavourable for other bacteria
30
==PAGE:32==
would be a sound strategy to ensure proliferation and nutrients for growth.
The amino acid alignment in Figure 7, showed large variations between bacterial species, indi-
cating that there are diﬀerences, at least structural between species but maybe also in terms
of function.
As mentioned earlier superoxide dismutase was identiﬁed with 100% similarity to hits from
known L. lactis spp.. Very little is known about SodA and the inﬂuence on H2O2 accumula-
tion, but another study found that SodA is crucial for growth during aerobic conditions due to
reactive oxygen species [52]. The identiﬁed enzyme is a metalloenzyme requiring manganese as
a metal cofactor, which is naturally present in milk in low concentrations. The results obtained
showed high conservation at manganese bindings sites, indicating the importance of the general
structure of the enzyme across many bacterial species. SodA removes oxygen radicals during
aerobic conditions, which otherwise can be fatal for bacteria lacking a defence mechanism.
The identiﬁcation of multiple genes involved in H2O2 production, may be promising for the
development of a starter culture with good characteristics. Hydrogen peroxide can trigger the
LPS system present in raw milk. LPS have shown to have a very broad antimicrobial spectrum
including both Gram-positive bacteria, Gram-negative bacteria, fungi and viruses [39]. The
eﬀect have been ranging from inhibition of sugar transport, oxygen uptake, enzyme inhibition,
growth inhibition to bactericidal [39]. This ability would be very useful in African areas to
improve the quality and safety of milk, as the microbial load and pathogen incidence in camel
milk have been reported to be very high [5].
The gene expression of the individual genes identiﬁed and their contribution to H2O2 accumu-
lation is impossible to investigate using bioinformatics. More research will be needed to adress
this. Knock-out of the relevant genes using the designed primers will most likely lead to answers
to some of the questions raised. The primers have designed to ﬁt both strains as conservated
domains where chosen.
Multiple studies have shown that aeration results in higher H2O2 levels and thereby stronger
inhibition of the pathogen [11, 54–56]. The reason behind have been identiﬁed to be due to
drastic modiﬁcations of the transcriptiome leading to metabolic adaptions [55]. The expression
31
==PAGE:33==
of the relevant genes in MS22333 and MS22337 and the H2O2 levels may thereby be dependent
on the amount of available oxygen and distribution of molecules by aeration.
While L-lactate oxidase, SodA and AhpF may be responsible for H2O2 accumulation in the
extracellular medium, another study found that in Lactobacillus acidophilus spp. deletion of
these genes did not aﬀect the accumulation but two very similar genes (LJ0548,LJ0549) did
[57]. The results could be similar in MS22333 and MS22337, and there may be other genes
involved in H2O2 production in MS22333 and MS22337, which have not been identiﬁed and
isolated yet.
5.2
NADH genes and their inﬂuence
Both NADH oxidases AhpC and NoxE were identiﬁed with very signiﬁcant hits in both MS22333
and MS22337. Both genes are important for the growth and survival in aerobic conditions. It
was noted that both genes was placed on a contig close to a gene involved with H2O2 production.
ahpC was upstream 64-66 amino acids from ahpF, while the distance between noxE and sodA
was longer but still on the same contig. The NoxE oxidase have been reported to be inactivated
by hydrogen peroxide and acidic pH, but have also been reported to be crucial for the early
consumption of O2.
A study found that noxE mutants signiﬁcantly had a higher level of
dissolved O2 [28], compared to intact functionally noxE variants. The redox potential have
notably eﬀects on the food quality, but a prerequisite for reduction showed to be complete
removal of dissolved O2 from the milk.
Tachon et al. [28] studied the reduction of O2 and its inﬂuence on the redox potential, and
found that other compounds must be reduced, to reach a ﬁnal Eh of -220 mV, which was seen
with the wildtype strain. When knocking-out genes involved with the electron transport chain
it was seen that the milk was reduced to around -65 mV [28].
5.3
The partially identiﬁed bacteriocins
Multiple short sequences with homology to lactococcin A and lactococcin B were identiﬁed, but
none of the matches could be identiﬁed as any known bacteriocins. Some of the matches had
very high sequence identity to the propeptide of lactococcin A/B (Figure 13,14 and 15). Even
though it was impossible to recognize any known genes, the hits may be relevant as candidates
32
==PAGE:34==
for novel bacteriocin-like-inhibitory substances (BLIS) discovery and should be evaluated in
the laboratory. Short peptides with antimicrobial activity is often referred to as a BLIS, which
means an uncharacterised bacteriocin.
A study found new lactococcins by having homology to the propeptide of lactococcin A, B and
M [58].
Lactococcin A and B antibacterial spectrum is speciﬁc to L. lactis strains [58]. Identiﬁcation of
novel bacteriocins only showing antibacterial activity against L. lactis would result in limited
use, as the overall goal in the Haramaya Dairy project is to develop starter cultures speciﬁc
for camel dairy with beneﬁcial characteristics as limiting the growth of pathogens. On the
other hand would identiﬁcation of lactococcin like bacteriocins but with broader antimicrobial
spectrum as Nisin be favoured. The use of novel BLIS would be easy to implement as BLIS
originating from L. lactis is expected to be safe agents to be used in the food industry, due to
the GRAS status of Lactococcus species [7, 58].
Having L. lactis strains with a combined antimicrobial pool consisting of H2O2 accumulation,
lactic acid, a negative redox potential and novel BLIS would be strong candidates for a starter
culture speciﬁc for camel dairy.

6
Conclusion
The two strains included in this project L. lactis subsp. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 have
shown to include genes, possibly crucial for the antimicrobial properties shown in previous
studies. The combination of both lactic acid, the LP system and the compounds produced by
the identiﬁed genes, could be promising evidence for their future use in camel dairy.
Using genomic information from homologue sequences gathered from both close related and
more distant bacterial species, three genes involved with H2O2 production were identiﬁed in
both strains. Both L-lactate oxidase, AhpF and superoxide dismutase were found to be very
similar to known gene sequences from L. lactis subsp. lactis IL1403 and L. lactis subsp. cre-
moris MG1363. Amino acid alignments of the genes derived from both very similar species
and other both distant Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria revealed that especially the
active sites were conservated between the species.
An open reading frame with very high similarity to a known sequences of pyruvate oxidase could
be identiﬁed, but it turned out that a mutation were present in both MS22333 and MS22337.
The mutation were placed in either two of the three domains and resulted in a stopcodon.
No genes coding for any known bacteriocins could be identiﬁed, but several hits to especially
the propeptide of lactococcin A and B could be identiﬁed with high identity. The hits could
potentially be BLIS, which should be researched further in the future.
The identiﬁcation of promising genes involved with hydrogen peroxide production and genes
coding for possible BLIS, could lead to development of a starter culture with demonstrated
antimicrobial characteristics for camel dairy.

Abstract 
22 
The genome sequences of four Lactococcus lactis strains were isolated in fermented camel milk in 
23 
Ethiopia and sequenced using paired-end illumina MiSeq. The genome size of all strains was about 
24 
2.6 Mb and three of the strains were annotated with tet(S) coding for tetracycline resistance.  
25 
Announcement 
26 
Lactococcus lactis is a well-known acidifying gram-positive bacterium, approved as Qualified 
27 
Presumption of Safety (QPS) by the European Food Safety Authority and used in starter cultures to 
28 
make dairy products (1). 
29 
Here, we report the draft genome sequences of L. lactis strains MS22314, MS22333, MS22336 and 
30 
MS22337. All strains were isolated from camel milk in Ethiopia. The new strains demonstrate 
31 
fermentation performance qualities in camel milk, as exponential cell growth, acidification and 
32 
decrease in redox potential, comparable to what other strains have shown in bovine milk. Starter 
33 
cultures used for bovine based products have shown poor fermentation results in camel milk (2, 3). 
34 
 
35 
Camel milk samples (n=29) were collected from several farms in the Babile area, Ethiopia, and 
36 
incubated at 30°C, 42°C or 48°C for 48h to stimulate fermentation. Samples with a pH < 5 after 48h 
37 
were plated and re-streaked 5 times on MRS, M17 containing 0,5% lactose or PB agar, all 
38 
containing 20 µg mL-1 natamycin for fungal inhibition (4, 5).  
39 
Single colonies from 114 isolates on either one of the agar plates were chosen for 16S rRNA 
40 
sequencing. DNA was purified (NORGEN milk bacterial DNA Isolation kit 21550) following 
41 
manufacturers protocol. Five microliters of purified DNA mixed with five microliters (5 pmol µl-1) 
42 
of a specific forward primer (27F, AGAGTTTGATCMTGGCTCAG) were sent to GATC Biotech 
43 
(GATC Biotech AG, Cologne, Germany) for Sanger sequencing. Sequence data were imported to 
44 
==PAGE:46==
 
3 
CLC Main Workbench (version 7, CLC bio Qiagen, Aarhus, Denmark) and trimmed manually, 
45 
before correlated with 16S data at the NCBI BLASTN database. (6) 
46 
 
47 
Based on phenotypic characterisation (6), four isolates respectively MS22314, MS22333, MS22336 
48 
and MS22337 were clean streaked at 30°C for whole genome sequencing on M17-lac agar plates. 
49 
DNA was extracted following the manufacturers protocol (NORGEN milk bacterial DNA Isolation 
50 
kit 21550). 
51 
DNA concentrations were measured on the Qubit double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) HS assay kit 
52 
(Invitrogen). Libraries for paired-end sequencing were constructed, using the Illumina NexteraXT 
53 
Kit (Illumina, California, USA) Guide 15031942v01. The Pooled NexteraXT libraries were loaded 
54 
onto an Illumina MiSeq reagent cartridge using MiSeq reagent kit v3 and 500 cycles with a 
55 
standard flow cell. Both sequencing and assembly were done using default settings unless other is 
56 
indicated. Sequencing were carried out using an Illumina MiSeq benchtop sequencer with an 
57 
average read length of 210, which yielded 1,868,468 to 2,187,598 reads. The coverage ranged 
58 
between 130.2 to 202.8.   
59 
Raw reads were assembled using Assembler v1.0 (https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/Assembler/), 
60 
resulting in 233-340 contigs for each strain >200 bp in size, GC content (%) ranging between 35.0-
61 
35.1 and a genome size of 2.6 - 2.7 Mb shown in Table 1. We are aware that these draft genomes 
62 
contain a high number of contigs, but other draft genome announcements(7) especially concerning 
63 
Lactococcus spp. dairy strains contain similar numbers (7, 8), probably due to the high amount of 
64 
plasmids and IS-elements (9, 10). 
65 
Annotation of the contigs were performed using the RAST server (11).We identified with 99.8% 
66 
identity to a SWISS-PROT (12) entry ( Q48712), a gene coding for tetracycline resistance tet(S) in 
67 
==PAGE:47==
 
4 
MS22314, MS22336 and MS22337, which should be considered when developing starter cultures 
68 
specific for camel dairy applications.  
69 
The draft genomes of L. lactis strains MS22314, MS22333, MS22336 and MS22337 are valuable 
70 
for the future manufacturing of effective and safe starter cultures specific for the camel dairy 
71 
industry.

Abstract:
The present study investigated inhibition of multiple food related pathogens in raw and
pasteurised camel milk during fermentation with four novel  Lactococcus lactis  strains.
All pathogens alone in camel milk reached about 8.0 log  10  CFU/ml. When the
pathogens were cultivated with either  L. lactis  MS22333 or MS22337 they were
reduced between 0.9-6.0 log  10  CFU/ml.  L. lactis  MS22314 and MS22336 showed
no antimicrobial activity.
Bad hygiene, lack of cooling facilities and ancient traditions have resulted in camel milk
being mostly spontaneously fermented in African countries with high incidence of
contamination with pathogens. Starter cultures promote food safety by controlling the
fermentation. Commercial cultures developed for bovine milk have shown to acidify
poorly in camel milk due to among others the different composition. A need for
bacterial strains showing fast acidification and inhibitory effects against food pathogens
remain.
To our knowledge, we have for the first time demonstrated that some  L. lactis  strains
isolated from camel milk can inhibit the growth of food related pathogens in both raw
and pasteurised camel milk within 75 hrs. Future studies should explore and determine
the mechanism of inhibition. The mechanism should be utilised to increase the food
safety of camel dairy.

1 Introduction  
38 
The quality and safety of milk is highly influenced by the rate of contamination by pathogens. 
39 
Staphylococcus spp., Escherichia coli, Salmonella spp., Klebsiella spp., Campylobacter 
40 
jejuni, Bacillus cereus and Listeria monocytogenes have been reported multiple times with 
41 
high frequency of contamination in milk (Ismaili, Saidi, Zahar, Hamama, & Ezzaier, 2019; 
42 
Oliver, Jayarao, & Almeida, 2005; Tetili, Bendali, Perrier, & Sadoun, 2017).  
43 
==PAGE:56==
 
3
A study by Abera et al. (2016) demonstrated bacterial contamination in 108 (85.7%) samples 
44 
from camel milk. Collection of the samples were in the Ethiopian Somali region where poor 
45 
water quality, bad sanitation, uncooled preservation and transportation traditions were some 
46 
of the main factors promoting contamination(Abera et al., 2016)(Abera et al., 2016). 
47 
Food poisoning is relatively harmless in developed countries where a medical system can 
48 
support fast recovery and hydration. In African undeveloped countries, diarrhoea, 
49 
salmonellosis, dehydration and vomiting can be fatal. In Africa food poisoning is estimated to 
50 
kill 137.000 persons yearly (Bisholo, Ghuman, & Haffejee, 2018; Rebgui et al., 2013). 
51 
Camelus dromedarius is an important milk producer in sub-Saharan and East African 
52 
countries, where raw and non-heat-treated dairy products are consumed. Camel milk is 
53 
mainly consumed directly after milking or as sour milk, often due to spontaneous 
54 
fermentation (Abera et al., 2016).  
55 
Camel milk have been reported to be difficult to acidify due to relative higher concentrations 
56 
of antibacterial and antiviral substances compared to bovine milk (El Agamy, Ruppanner, 
57 
Ismail, Champagne, & Assaf, 1992). Commercial starter cultures designed for bovine milk 
58 
have been tested in camel milk and they were able to acidify camel milk to comparable final 
59 
pH but with slower acidification rates (Berhe et al., 2018). Starter cultures designed specific 
60 
for camel milk with optimum acidification rates to inhibit growth of spoilage and pathogenic 
61 
microorganisms to be developed.   
62 
Lactococcus lactis are used in many starter cultures to ferment foods and thereby prevent 
63 
growth of pathogens or spoilage bacteria by producing multiple antimicrobial substances 
64 
(Cizeikiene, Juodeikiene, Paskevicius, & Bartkiene, 2013; Jay, 1982; Kondrotiene et al., 
65 
2018; Mufandaedza, Viljoen, Feresu, & Gadaga, 2006; Roessland, Borge, Langsrud, & 
66 
Sørhaug, 2003).  
67 
==PAGE:57==
 
4
Studies of inhibition of several common food pathogens by L. lactis strains have been 
68 
reported in cow milk and cheese products (Kasra-Kermanshahi & Mobarak-Qamsari, 2015; 
69 
Kondrotiene et al., 2018; Roessland et al., 2003; Tetili et al., 2017). Only very few studies 
70 
have, to our knowledge described inhibition of food pathogens using lactic acid bacteria 
71 
(LAB) isolated from camel milk (Benmechernene et al., 2013; Rahmeh et al., 2019). Rahmeh 
72 
et al. (2019)(Rahmeh et al., 2019)(Rahmeh et al., 2019) characterised antimicrobial activity 
73 
of multiple genus within lactic acid bacteria and the distribution of LAB in raw camel milk 
74 
from western Asia. Benmechernene et al. (2013) have shown that two strains of Leuconostoc 
75 
mesenteroides subsp. mesenteroides isolated from Algerian camel milk could be used as a 
76 
bioactive strains against L. monocytogenes and S. aureus. 
77 
The aim of this study was to identify potential L. lactis candidates for a starter culture with 
78 
antimicrobial properties demonstrated in camel milk to enhance food safety in African 
79 
countries.

2 Materials and methods 
81 
2.1 Bacterial strains 
82 
L. lactis strains were obtained from the strain collection at the National Food Institute at the 
83 
Technical University of Denmark. Originally, the strains MS22314, MS22333, MS22336 and 
84 
MS22337 were isolated from spontaneous fermented camel milk in Ethiopia (Fugl et al., 
85 
2017). Genome sequences of the strains can be accessed at the Genbank under BioSample 
86 
numbers: SAMN13701540, SAMN13701541, 76 SAMN13701542, SAMN13701543 
87 
(Bragason, Svendsen, Guya, Berhe, & Hansen, 2020). The cultures were grown for 24 hrs at 
88 
30 °C in M17 broth (Oxoid, Thermo Scientific, Hampshire, UK) containing 0.5% lactose.  
89 
Klebsiella pneumoniae (MS22380) was obtained from DSMZ - German Collection of 
90 
Microorganisms and Cell Cultures (DSM 30104). 
91 
==PAGE:58==
 
5
Salmonella Typhimurium DT12 (MS20842) and E. coli O:157 VT- (MS21811) were obtained 
92 
from the strain collection at the National Reference Laboratory for Salmonella and zoonotic 
93 
Escherichia coli at the Technical University of Denmark. S. Typhimurium DT12 was 
94 
originally isolated in cow faeces. All pathogens were grown for 24 h at 37 °C in brain-heart 
95 
infusion (BHI) broth (Oxoid, Thermo Scientific, Hampshire, UK). 
96 
2.2 Media Preparation 
97 
Unpasteurized camel milk provided by Kamelenmelkerij Smits (Berlicum, Netherlands) was 
98 
sent within 24 h after bottled on 0.5L plastic bottles and immediately stored at – 40 °C until 
99 
use. The milk was placed in the fridge 2 days before use at 5 °C to thaw.  Enzymatic 
100 
inactivation of the milk was carried out by heating the milk for 10 minutes at 96 °C in a 
101 
VWR water bath (Grant Instruments Ltd., Cambridge, UK) followed by cooling to room 
102 
temperature before further use.  
103 
Preparation of Prussian blue (PB) agar plates were made following the formulation described 
104 
by Saito, Seki, Iida, Nakayama, & Yoshida (2007). M17-lac agar were made using M17 agar 
105 
provided by SSI Diagnostica (Hilleroed, Denmark) in 200 ml bottles and sterile 10% lactose 
106 
solution were added to a final concentration of 0,5%. Xylose-Lysine-Desoxycholate (XLD) 
107 
agar plates were made following the directions by the manufacturer (Oxoid, Thermo 
108 
Scientific, Hampshire, UK). MacConkey no. 3 agar plates were provided by SSI Diagnostica 
109 
(Hilleroed, Denmark) ready to use. All agar plates kept at 5 °C until use.   
110 
 
111 
2.3 Antimicrobial activity of L. lactis 
112 
50ml raw or pasteurised camel milk were poured into a 50ml falcon tube. From overnight 
113 
cultures, 5l and 50l for respectively the specific pathogen and the L. lactis strain were 
114 
==PAGE:59==
 
6
inoculated to the milk samples. Falcon tubes were homogenized using a Vortex mixer and 
115 
incubated at 30 °C for 75 h. Sampling intervals were planned for the next three days.    
116 
2.4 Viable count of bacteria  
117 
At each sampling interval the milk was homogenized and 100l were removed for serial 
118 
dilutions. Maximum Recovery Diluent provided by SSI Diagnostica (Hilleroed, Denmark) 
119 
were used to dilute samples.  Adequate dilutions were chosen and 40l were pipetted on a 
120 
half agar plate. M17-lac agar plates were incubated for 48 h at 30 °C and used for L. lactis 
121 
strains, XLD agar plates incubated for 24 h at 37 °C and used for S. Typhimurium DT12 and 
122 
MacConkey No.3 incubated for 24 h at 37 °C and used for K. pneumoniae and E. coli O:157 
123 
VT- . Colonies were counted using a Stuart SC6+ Colony Counter (Cole-Parmer, 
124 
Staffordshire, UK) and colony-forming units (CFU) per millilitre were calculated using a 
125 
weighted mean.   
126 
2.5 Measurement of Hydrogen Peroxide  
127 
Determination of H2O2 concentration in samples were carried out using CDR FOODLAB 
128 
(CDR s.r.l, Florence, Italy) photometric analyser following their method and using their 
129 
specific reagent kit.

3. Results 
131 
In this study L. lactis strains were inoculated into both raw and pasteurised camel milk at 
132 
around 106-107 CFU/ml to simulate the fermentation. Results are presented in Figure 1 and 2. 
133 
All L. lactis strains reached levels of 108-109 CFU/ml both in raw and pasteurised milk with 
134 
pH ranging between 4.0 - 4.5. 
135 
3.1 Inhibition of E. coli O:157 VT- in raw camel milk 
136 
==PAGE:60==
 
7
The inhibitory effect varied between L. lactis strains MS22314, MS22333, MS22336 and 
137 
MS22337. The inhibition is shown in Figure 1. The pathogenic E coli O:157 VT- was 
138 
inoculated at about 4.7104-5.0105 CFU/ml and after 8 h inhibition effects were detected. 
139 
MS22333 and MS22337 decreased the concentration of E coli O:157 VT- with 0.9 - 4.8 log10 
140 
CFU/ml within 54 h at 30C. In contrast did MS22314 and MS22336 promote the growth of 
141 
E coli O:157 VT- at early stages of fermentation. After 8 h of incubation the concentration of 
142 
E coli O:157 VT- was 0.5-1.0 log10 CFU/ml higher in milk samples containing either 
143 
MS22314 or MS22333 compared to E coli O:157 VT- growing alone in raw camel milk. 
144 
After 54 h all three samples reached a stable E coli O:157 VT- concentration of 5.5 - 7.5107 
145 
CFU/ml.  
146 
In the absence of L. lactis strains and E. coli O:157 VT- the raw camel milk sample showed 
147 
both growth on M17-lac and MacConkey no. 3 agar plates indicating both natural LAB and 
148 
unknown Enterobacteriaceae. 
149 
3.2 Inhibition of Salmonella Typhimurium DT12 and Klebsiella pneumoniae in 
150 
pasteurised camel milk 
151 
The effect of L. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 to inhibit the pathogens in pasteurised camel 
152 
milk is presented in Figure 2B and Figure 2C. Both S. Typhimurium DT12 and K. 
153 
pneumoniae were inoculated at concentrations about 6.0 log10 CFU/ml, while L. lactis strains 
154 
were about 7.0-7.5 log10 CFU/ml. Overall L. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 inhibited both 
155 
pathogens and a mixed starter culture composed of both did not show a significant difference. 
156 
No inhibitory effect on S. Typhimurium DT12 were seen the first 21 hrs of the fermentation. 
157 
After 47 hrs, it was impossible to detect S. Typhimurium DT12 in milk samples containing 
158 
either MS22333 or MS22337 or a 50:50 mixed culture.  
159 
==PAGE:61==
 
8
Similar results were seen with K. pneumoniae at 21 h. At 47 h no growth on MacConkey no. 
160 
3 agar were detected in the L. lactis MS22337 inoculated milk. The mixed L. lactis culture 
161 
and L. lactis MS22333 showed concentrations of respectively 4.5 and 3.8 log10 CFU/ml at 
162 
same sampling interval. After 75 h, K. pneumoniae were impossible to detect in all milk 
163 
samples inoculated with a L. lactis strain. 
164 
 
165 
3.3 Production of hydrogen peroxide 
166 
Characterisation of H2O2 producing L. lactis strains were carried out using PB agar. Prussian 
167 
blue colour formation was seen as a blue halo around positive colonies. MS22333 showed 
168 
strong blue colour formation around each colony, while MS22337 had small light blue halos 
169 
around most colonies (data not shown). L. lactis MG1363 were included as a negative 
170 
control. L. lactis MS22314 and MS2236 looked like L. lactis MG1363 with no clear colour 
171 
formation. 
172 
Quantification of H2O2 levels in camel milk is shown in Table 1. Overall, none of the L. lactis 
173 
strains showed high concentrations of H2O2 < 24 ppm. The concentration of H2O2 in both 
174 
raw and pasteurised camel milk were at the beginning below detection level (DL) < 1.5 ppm. 
175 
Similar results were obtained for bovine milk. After 24 h at 30 °C, all samples showed an 
176 
increased H2O2 concentration. 23.8 ppm were the highest concentration detected, which was 
177 
in pasteurised camel milk inoculated with L. lactis MS22337.

4 Discussion 
179 
Milk and processed dairy products are consumed globally by all social classes. Contaminants 
180 
can occur through the food chain from milking of the animal to the final consumers. Bacterial 
181 
contamination of milk should be minimal, as contaminated milk is a threat to public health. 
182 
As the majority of camel milk producers lack cooling facilities, the camel milk often acidifies 
183 
==PAGE:62==
 
9
by spontaneous fermentation (Holzapfel, 2002). We have been studying LAB in spontaneous 
184 
fermented camel milk to isolate the most beneficial strains based on antimicrobial activity 
185 
and rate of fermentation. Our findings of two strains inhibiting E. coli O:157 VT- in raw 
186 
camel milk, demonstrates antimicrobial activity of L. lactis strains with comparable 
187 
acidification rates and pH levels as commercial starter cultures (Berhe et al., 2018). A study 
188 
by Charlier et al. (2008) demonstrated that low-acidifying L. lactis strains efficiently could 
189 
inhibit the growth of S. aureus in milk. The inhibitory effect seen in L. lactis MS22333 and 
190 
MS22337 and not in MS22314 and MS22336 is thereby not due to lactic acid production as 
191 
they all had similar acidification curves (data not shown) and similar pH levels. Same strains 
192 
showed H2O2 production on PB agar but only low levels < 24 ppm could be detected using 
193 
the CDR FOODLAB analyser. Multiple L. lactis strains isolated from food samples have 
194 
earlier been reported to accumulate more than 300 ppm H2O2 when the suspension were 
195 
aerated (Ito et al., 2003). The results shown in Table 1 could be residue levels, from an 
196 
incomplete utilisation of hydrogen peroxide potential caused by anaerobic conditions. 
197 
Furthermore, H2O2 is known to have a short lifetime in milk, and in raw milk, H2O2 may 
198 
activate the naturally occurring lactoperoxidase (LP) enzyme system (Martin et al., 2014). 
199 
Detection of H2O2 in a complex media such as milk can be a challenge. H2O2 concentrations 
200 
as low as 60 mg/L administered along with 28 mg/L thiocyanate have been reported to 
201 
activate the LP system and extend the shelf life of raw ovine, bovine and caprine milk for 
202 
several days (Boulares, Mankai, & Hassouna, 2011).  
203 
To our knowledge, inhibition of pathogens in pasteurised camel milk have not been 
204 
demonstrated before. L. lactis MS22333 and MS22337 showed complete inhibition of both S. 
205 
Typhimurium DT12 and K. pneumoniae within 75 h shown in Figure 2B and 2C. The reason 
206 
for the 4.07 log10 CFU/ml decrease of K. pneumoniae in fermented camel milk is unknown, 
207 
==PAGE:63==
 
10
but may be due to growth of unknown bacteria inhibiting and utilizing lactose, which was 
208 
seen as growth on M17-lac agar plates reaching 6.0 log10 CF/ml at 47 h (Figure 2A).   
209 
Pasteurisation of camel milk inactivates the LP system (Sharma & Rajput, 2014). The 
210 
inhibition of S. Typhimurium DT12 and K. pneumoniae by L. lactis MS22333 and MS22337, 
211 
indicates that other antimicrobial mechanisms are happening during fermentation. According 
212 
to literature, L. lactis have shown to inhibit pathogens by producing bacteriocins and other 
213 
low molecular compounds (Armas, Camperio, & Marianelli, 2017; Cardinal, Meghrous, 
214 
Lacroix, & Simard, 1997; Enan, Abdel-Shafi, Ouda, & Negm, 2013; Loh, Lim, & Ting, 
215 
2017; Millette, Smoragiewicz, & Lacroix, 2004). 
216 
We have previously published the genome sequences of L. lactis MS22314, MS22333, 
217 
MS22336 and MS22337 (Bragason et al., 2020), where annotation of the contigs showed that 
218 
MS22333 were the only strain without any genes coding for antibiotic resistance. Starter 
219 
cultures containing resistance genes can possibly be a critical source of spreading antibiotic 
220 
resistance, and studies have found multiple starter cultures with resistance genes (Kastner et 
221 
al., 2006; Katla, Kruse, Johnsen, & Herikstad, 2001). Future studies should explore the 
222 
mechanism of inhibition and develop L. lactis MS22333 into a starter culture specific for 
223 
camel dairy.  
224 
5 Conclusions 
225 
Our present study shows that L. lactis MS22333 and L. lactis MS22337 isolated from 
226 
spontaneous fermented camel milk have antimicrobial abilities and can be applied as a starter 
227 
culture to promote food safety in African countries. We have demonstrated for the first time 
228 
that S. Typhimurium and K. pneumoniae can be eliminated in pasteurised camel milk by L. 
229 
lactis strains. Further work has to be done to explain the mechanism of inhibition.
