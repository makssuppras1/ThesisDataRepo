Abstract
A systematic evaluation for a well-founded model setup for the Coupled-Ocean-Atmosphere-
Wave-Sediment Transport Modeling System COAWST is performed with focus on the
simulation of rough weather events over the North Sea and on its utilization for oshore
wind energy applications. Research work dealing with investigations of impacts of grid
parameters, initial and lateral boundary conditions and parameterization schemes on the
simulation of dierent characteristics of severe weather events (among others intensica-
tion, track of storm center, precipitation) points out a dissent in the importance of certain
factors depending on the investigated case. This dissent emphasizes the necessity for a
closer and systematic examination focused on a specic application. Within the scope of
this work, an evaluation criteria catalog is developed, which is listing demands for the per-
formance in the determination of storm characteristics, namely storm track, intensity and
atmospheric and oceanic parameters. The catalog is integrated in an evaluation scheme,
developed to provide a consistent and systematic procedure to determine a proper model
setup. Six initial setups and derived modied setup congurations are designed and the
evaluation scheme is applied on two exemplary storm events which are rstly simulated in
the uncoupled conguration (i.e running only the atmosphere model WRF). The results
are evaluated both qualitatively and quantitatively, according to the evaluation criteria
catalog. It is found that too small simulation domains are not able to capture the rele-
vant storm characteristics while too big simulation domains develop their own dynamics
inside the domain diverging from the forcing data. Splitting the total simulation in sev-
eral shorter individual simulations is shown to reduce this eect. Underestimation of the
storm intensity is detected when the storm enters the domain too close to the corner of the
domain and misinterpretation of the general trend of the wind speed is present in setups,
which are not passed by the storm center. Spectral analysis showed that a spin-up time
of 12 h is sucient to build up also high vertical layers while a horizontal resolution of at
least 2 km is necessary to reconstruct high frequency components in the wind speed mea-
surements. Recommendations for a suitable domain size and resolution are given and a
setup is selected and used for the coupled simulation of three storm events. It is observed
that atmospheric conditions are represented well while the performance with regard to
the estimation of signicant wave height and peak wave period is case sensitive. Based
on the output of the coupled simulation, exemplary possible applications of the spacial
distributed atmospheric and oceanic parameters are presented.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Knowledge about atmospheric and oceanic conditions at oshore wind turbine sites can
be crucial, since they are not only inuencing power production but also determine the
loads which have an eect on foundation, tower and blades.
Already the stability of
the atmospheric boundary layer is signicantly inuencing the power output of oshore
wind farms [2] or has an impact on the blade and tower fatigue loads in wake aected
inows [3].
This inuence will increase when the weather conditions become rougher
for example during the presence of cyclones, which are responsible for unstable and wet
weather conditions with strong winds and high waves. Extreme events like Hurricanes
can cause irreparable damages on oshore structures, but even less extreme events have
already an inuence on the blades and the foundation [4]. Especially in the North Sea,
where the amount of installed oshore wind capacity is very high, the eects of rough
weather situations are already noticeable.
An investigation of the impact of cyclone
Xaver 1 on oshore wind power generation showed a sudden rise of oshore wind power
production in Germany respectively shutdowns of turbines in Denmark due to high wind
speeds during the event (cf. [5], [6]). Steiner et al. (2016) [7] showed that during the
period 2012-2014 60% of days characterized by large day-ahead wind power forecast errors
in Germany can be connected to rough weather events in the North Sea and Baltic Sea.
Together with the break o of power supply due to the joint shut-down of wind farms under
extreme wind conditions, forecast errors pose additional stress to the transmission system
operators to stabilize the grid [8]. Because of the rise of installed oshore wind power
capacity in the North Sea and worldwide [9], the above mentioned impact becomes more
and more relevant. Besides the impact on power production, knowledge about atmospheric
and oceanic parameters is crucial for installation processes and operation and maintenance
(O&M) purposes. Atmospheric parameters (like the wind speed and wind direction) and
wave parameters (like signicant wave height and wave period) determine the time frame
in which the sea state and the weather conditions are within certain boundaries (weather
window) to perform installations and maintenances of wind turbines and substructures
[10]. An accurate prediction of weather windows can help to reduce costs due to a more
optimal selection vessel eet for a certain task and eective scheduling. Furthermore,
suitable weather models can be used as a foundation to determine oshore wind turbine
design parameters like extreme values for signicant wave height (fty year return period)
1also known as cyclone Bodil by Danish Meteorological Institute
1
==PAGE:18==
[11] and extreme values for the wind speed (fty year return period) [12].
All the above mentioned issues point out that estimations and predictions of rough weather
events should be as accurate as possible to reduce power forecast errors, to avoid unex-
pected load peaks on wind turbines and to reduce O&M costs. In the last decades, numer-
ical weather modeling has improved remarkably and results are obtained in increasingly
higher resolutions due to modern computing technologies. However, the accuracy and
reliability of the predictions depend not only on the model resolution.
White et al. (1999) [13] and Cacciamani et al. (2000) [14] suggested that inadequate model
resolution, insucient physical parameterizations, lack of detail or inaccuracies in the ini-
tial and boundary conditions or any combination of the three are capable reasons for a
poor forecast. This is supported by recent investigations in dierent locations. Landman
et al. (2005) [15] investigated the inuence of domain location and size on the simulation
of cyclone-like vortices on the African west coast (Indian Ocean) using the National Cen-
ter for Atmospheric Research (NCAR) Regional Climate Model. They observed that the
placement of the domain borders and domain size has an inuence on the development
of the simulated vortices. Li and Pu (2009) [16] performed a numerical simulation of the
intensication of Hurricane Emily (2005, Gulf of Mexico) using the Weather Research &
Forecasting (WRF, [17]) model with focus on its sensitivity to parameterization schemes
and horizontal grid size. It is indicated that cumulus schemes inuence the simulated
minimum sea level pressure (MSLP) at grid spacings of 9 km but have no noticeable im-
pact on storm intensity or storm track for grid spacings of 3 km. It is also found that the
selection of planetary boundary layers (PBL) parameterizations schemes have an impact
on the determination of the sea level pressure.
Dierences between simulations using
dierent PBL schemes and the National Hurricane Center best track data become more
visible using 3 km grid spacings than using 9 km grid spacings. Mohanty et al. (2010)
[18] investigated the inuence of initial conditions (that is the initialization time of the
simulation) and boundary conditions (that is the origin of the global analysis data, which
is induced at the boundaries of the model domain to drive the model).
The authors
performed four case studies of cyclones which took place in the Gulf of Bengal using
WRF. Each case was performed with dierent initialization times with a time dierence
of 24 h and with dierent global analysis resources at boundary conditions. It is shown
that the dierent initialization times and dierent global analysis data have strengths and
weaknesses with respect to the determination of track detection, mean track and landfall
errors. Raju et al. (2011) [19] performed a parameterization study to identify a proper
combination of parameterization schemes in WRF for tropical cyclone prediction based
on cyclone Nagris in the Bay of Bengal. Nasrollahi et al. (2012) [20] investigated the
WRF performance with regard to predicted track, precipitation and landfall time of Hur-
ricane Rita which took place in September 2005 in the Gulf of Mexico. 20 combinations
of microphysics and cumulus schemes are investigated. It is shown that no combination
could be considered as an ideal setup to model precipitation, hurricane track and the time
of landfall. A large dependency of the results on the selected microphysics and cumulus
parametrization is detected. Kanase and Salvekar (2015) [21] studied the sensitivity of
cumulus, planetary boundary layers and microphysics parameterizations and initial and
boundary conditions. Their investigations base on the estimation of track and intensity
of cyclone LAILA (May 2010) in the Bay of Bengal. The authors used WRF numerical
model and performed studies using four convective parameterization schemes, two plan-
2
==PAGE:19==
etary boundary layer parameterization schemes and two microphysical parameterization
schemes and performed four sets of setups. It is presented that the estimated cyclone
track and intensity forecast is strongly sensitive to the cumulus parameterization. The
investigation showed also that the microphysics parameterization and planetary boundary
layer parameterization has an impact on the intensity while the impact on the cyclone
track is insignicant. The sensitivity of track and intensity of the storm to initial and
boundary conditions is also shown to be highly signicant. Du et al. (2015) [22] examined
the impact of grid resolution of the atmosphere model and the wave model in the coupled-
ocean-atmosphere-wave-sediment transport (COAWST) model on the estimation of wind
and wave characteristics during storms in the North Sea. It is shown that the signicant
wave height strongly depends on the model resolution. Strong winds are better estimated
using high spatial resolution. Ren et al. (2016) [23] used COAWST system to investigate
the inuence of dierent parameterizations in WRF on the atmosphere and the ocean.
Tropical storm Nock-ten (July 2011, Southeast Asia) was examined in this study. The
authors found that the parameterizations inuence the results especially around the storm
center area.
The above mentioned work suggests that an investigation of grid specic parameters (grid
size, grid location and grid resolution), parameterizations and initial and boundary con-
ditions is important. Therefore, a proper selection of the parameter is necessary to obtain
a suitable numerical weather setup which estimates atmospheric and oceanic conditions
as accurate as possible. However, it is also shown that there is dissent about inuencing
factors and a closer and systematic examination for specic applications is needed.
This systematic evaluation builds the objective of this Master Thesis. Within the scope
of the thesis, a suitable numerical weather simulation setup using the Coupled-Ocean-
Atmosphere-Wave-Sediment Transport Modeling System (COAWST, [24]) is developed
by investigating grid specic parameters. It is aimed to answer the following questions,
which are seen as leading questions for the development of this thesis:
1. Which physical characteristics of a severe weather event are especially important
for wind energy applications and should be estimated as accurate as possible and
which steps are necessary to consider for developing well-founded simulation model
setup?
2. Which conditions for grid specic parameters (size, location and resolution) and
simulation time have to be applied then to achieve this well-founded simulation
model setup?
After a short presentation of the COAWST environment and its subroutines in Chapter 2
and the used reference data in Chapter 3, the systematic investigation of dierent setups
is performed in Chapter 4.

This chapter provides a description of the models used within the scope of this thesis.

1
Coupled-Ocean-Atmosphere-Wave-Sediment Transport
Modeling System (COAWST)
The Coupled-Ocean-Atmosphere-Wave-Sediment Transport Modeling System (COAWST,
[24]) is a system, which is designed to establish data exchange between the mesoscale atmo-
sphere model Weather Research and Forecast (WRF,[17]), the Regional Ocean Modeling
System (ROMS , [27]), which includes a sediment transport model, and the wave model
Simulating Waves Nearshore (SWAN , [28]) by mean of the Model Coupling Toolkit (MCT,
[29]). Developed to examine coastal processes, it provides a suitable tool to analyze the
interaction between the ocean and the atmosphere.
Within the scope of this thesis, a reduced coupling conguration is used. Reduced coupling
conguration means that not all coupling features provided by COAWST are used. The
focus lies on the coupling between the atmospheric model WRF and the wave model
SWAN with the ocean model ROMS not being integrated. A schematic overview of the
reduced coupling structure is given in Figure 2.1. Velocities U10 and V10 at 10 m above
ground are determined within WRF and are transmitted via the Model Coupling Toolkit
to SWAN. SWAN determines and passes wave specic parameters (signicant wave height
Hm0, wave period Pwave and wave length Lwave) back to WRF. The determination of the
surface roughness in SWAN is based on the wave boundary layer model presented in [30]
and transmitted to WRF.

2
Atmosphere Model: Weather Research and Forecast
(WRF)
The Weather Research and Forecast Model [17] is a state-of-the-art numerical weather
prediction system tool and an atmospheric simulation tool. It can be used for a wide
range of applications within research and operation due to its modular code, its broad
operational range from global scales to mesoscales (> 1 km) and its variable physics,
numerics and dynamics options. WRF is widely used including wind energy applications
and it is the topic of a variety of investigation related to dierent aspects within wind
energy over the past decades (e.g [31], [32], [33], [34]).
Although the used parametrization schemes will have an inuence on the performance of
WRF as shown in several investigations (e.g [21], [19], [35]), the investigation of the impact
of dierent physics parameterization schemes is considered as next-step check point after
the eort proposed in the thesis. The used parameterizations are based on the settings
used in XWiWA1 and are only mentioned for the sake of completeness and reconstruction
purposes.
Precipitation is parameterized by mean of the enhanced bulk scheme based on [36] and
the Rapid Radiative Transfer Model for general circulation models, [37]) is activated to
model shortwave and longwave radiation. Eects of clouds on the radiation are neglected,
meaning clouds are considered as being fully transparent. The radiation scheme is called
every 18 minutes. The unied Noah land surface model ([38]) with four soil layers is
chosen and the Mellor-Yamada-Nakanishi-Niino scheme is applied as surface layer and
planetary boundary layer scheme. The Kain-Fritsch convective parametrization scheme
[39] is activated only in domains with a grid resolution lower than 10 km following the
recommendation in [40]. The convective scheme is called every 5 min.
Initial and lateral boundary conditions are given by the 6-hourly Climate Forecast System
Reanalysis data set (more details in Section 3).
The land cover input data inclusive
lake information are coming from the Coordination of Information on the Environment
(CORINE) land cover 2000 data [41].

Wave Model: Simulating Waves Nearshore (SWAN)
The third-generation spectral wave model Simulating Waves Nearshore (SWAN,[28]) is
designed for the estimation of wind waves in shallow water regions like coastal areas. It
is based on solving the spectral action balance equation [42].
SWAN is currently used in its uncoupled version with regard to oshore wind energy,
mainly with focus on the impact of oshore wind farms on the development of waves at
certain locations [43], [44].
Similar to the settings in WRF, the settings and parameterizations in SWAN are taken
over from XWiWA and are only mentioned for the sake of completeness and reconstruc-
tion purposes. SWAN is set up as a non-stationary and two dimensional run with a spacial
directional resolution of 36 directions equidistantly distributed over the full circle and a
frequency resolution of ∆f/f = 0.1 with the lowest frequency bin of 0.03 Hz and a total
number of 62 frequency bins. JONSWAP wave spectrum with peak enhancement param-
eter of γf = 3.3 (SWAN default) is applied as initial wave spectrum. Depth-induced wave
breaking is considered by constant breaker index of γ = 0.8. Description of the source
function for wind-input follows [30], while white-capping dissipation is considered accord-
ing to [45]. Quadruplets non-linear wave-wave interaction is described by the Discrete
Interaction Approximation presented in [46].
All other settings are following the default settings in SWAN and can be found in [47].
The simulation time step in SWAN and the exchanging rate with WRF is set to 5 min.
WRF are using the same grid.
2EMODnet Digital Bathymetry (DTM) [48] with a spatial resolution of 0.125 arc minutes
is used as bathymetric data.

1
Evaluation Criteria Catalog
To obtain a decent representation of rough weather events, the model must be set up in
such a way, that it is able to capture relevant weather characteristics reasonably. Since
these characteristics strongly depend on the purpose of the simulation, an evaluation
criteria catalog with focus on the simulation of severe storms is developed in the rst
step. These criteria should be fullled as good as possible by the simulation in order to
determine its suitability. Keeping the usability for oshore wind energy applications in
mind, the following criteria are chosen:
1. Proper estimation of storm track and intensity
Storm track and intensity are key parameters, since they contain important infor-
mation about the aected areas and the severity of rough weather events. Wind
park operators can determine if wind turbines or wind parks within their area are
aected and adjust maintenance schedules.
11
==PAGE:28==
2. Proper estimation of wind speed characteristics
A proper representation of wind speed characteristics is crucial, since the wind speed
is not only inuencing loads and power production of the wind turbines, but it is also
a key parameter in operation and maintenance, for example in the determination
of weather windows for oshore constructions [10]. It is expected that the model
setup is able to estimate in a rst step (location and size analysis) the general trend
of the wind speed and should detect especially the following three characteristics as
accurate as possible:
(a) The time and duration, when the wind speed on hub-height at a certain location
exceeds the cut-out wind speed for a typical wind turbine. A good estimation
is very useful, because it provides a clear time frame in which individual wind
turbines or whole wind parks will shut-down due to the high wind speeds.
This information can be used by transmission system operators which have to
organize alternative capacities to balance the loss of wind power.
(b) The maximum wind speed at a particular height at a certain location. This is
an important feature as well, since the maximum velocity is an indicator about
the maximum wind induced forces on oshore structures and gives an idea
about the loads and even damages which can be expected. This is especially
crucial when the wind speed exceeds the cut-out wind speed and becomes close
to the design limit of a turbine.
(c) The general wind speed direction. A good estimation can be especially useful
for transmission system operators and wind farm owners, since the general wind
direction can give an overview about the amount of turbines operating in the
wake of other turbines in front which inuences directly the power production
performance of a wind farm [54].
In a second step (resolution analysis), the resolution of the domain has to be selected
in such a way, that also the high frequency components in the measurements are
represented.
3. Reasonable computational costs
Besides the performance of the simulation with respect to the estimation of relevant
physical parameters, the computational costs have to be considered as well. They
should be in a reasonable frame and improvements in the results should be weighted
against their computational eort.
The computational costs are not calculated
explicitly but are evaluated qualitatively based on the resolution and size of the
used domains compared to their outcome.

good as medium size domains or will fail completely in the worst case. Very big domains
will start to develop their own dynamics which could diverge from the reanalysis data with
increasing time. Very small domains might be too small to capture the storm structure
or the storm passes the domain too fast to be properly resolvable. It is also assumed
that the domains, which are not passed by the storm center will perform less well due
to the missing information about the storm structure or misinterpretation of the forcing
data. Concerning the storm intensity, it is expected that the determined minimum sea
level pressure will decrease with time during the development of the storm and reaches its
minimum when the storm is fully developed. Subsequently, the minimum sea level pressure
will increase again during the resolving process of the low pressure system. Generally, it is

higher resolutions. Concerning the wind speed, a sudden increase within the presence of
the storm is expected in company with increasing pressure gradient. The magnitude and
time of appearance of the peak will depend on the relative position between storm center
and measuring point and the storm intensity. A change in wind direction depending on
the position between the storm center and the measuring point is expected as well.
4.1
Storm Track Detection
Following the evaluation criteria catalog, the domain setups are rstly analyzed with
respect to their ability to estimate the track and intensity of a low pressure system.
Setup 05WC and Setup 06WC are not part of the analysis since the storm track is not
passing the domain.
The algorithm, which is developed to track the storm, is based on a feature tracking
method using the minimum sea level pressure as key variable. However, WRF is using
terrain following vertical coordinates and sea level pressure information is not directly
available over land. Therefore, the sea level pressure has to be reconstructed rst based
on other available WRF output parameters which is done by mean of the NCAR Command
Language (NCL , [57]). The interpreted language provides functions for scientic data
processing and data visualization and wrf_user_getvar is especially designed to extract
data from the WRF model. The basic idea behind the function is the extrapolation of
temperature and surface pressure down to sea level.
This constructed sea level pressure is the input data for the developed storm track de-
tection algorithm. Firstly, the obtained sea level surface pressure eld is investigated to
see if regions have to be excluded from the analysis. This is necessary when the low
pressure system of the storm is not the most intense system in the domain, because the
algorithm is driven by the detection of the minimum sea level pressure in the region. It is
implemented in the algorithm as a matrix of boolean entries which ags grid points to be
considered or ignored. Afterwards, the time frame in which the storm can be tracked is
determined according to the domain size and is based on the XWS track data. As starting
date, the time stamp of the rst XWS data point inside the domain is selected as long
as the time stamp does not collide with the spin-up time. Otherwise, the rst data point
available after spin-up is chosen. The longitude and latitude coordinates of the selected
point are at the same time the initial condition for the storm track detection. The end
date of the tracking is either determined by the end of the simulation or the time stamp
of the last point inside the domain. For each time step within the time frame, the follow-
ing calculations are performed: Firstly, an elliptical focus area around the initial point
is created. Within the focus area, the minimum sea level pressure is determined and the
detected location is used as new initial point for the next time step. The same procedure
is used to determine the storm track from the CFSR data. However, the focus area is
selected bigger due to the bigger time step between two consecutive available CFSR sea
level pressure elds. Tabel 4.4 states the parameter used for the elliptical focus area. The
algorithm can be directly applied to the CFSR data, since the sea level pressure eld
is directly available (i.e. no reconstruction is needed). It must be noted that there are

grid points is performed).

onshore site, but the closest grid point is a water grid point. Comparing the measurements
at Høvsøre a water grid point might be representative for wind coming from westerly
directions (dominated by water) but might be less representative for wind coming from
easterly directions (dominated by land mass).

4.5
Summary and Conclusion from Case Study I
Eight setups (six initial setups plus two modications) are investigated in the current case
study.
Setup 01 failed the storm track analysis (storm track generally too far north) and is
excluded due to the quality of the results. The obtained maximum and mean distance
between the estimated storm track and the CFSR and XWS reference data are too high
to justify the high computational costs of the biggest domain in the investigation.
Setup 06 is excluded due to its complete failure in the storm track detection. The size of
the domain is too small to properly follow the storm.
The discovery that the storm track has to pass the domain to represent the general trend
in wind speed yield to the exclusion of all setups with a domain size smaller than Setup
32
==PAGE:49==
04, since the Setup should be able to cover storms aecting the whole North Sea area.
Therefore, Setup 05, Setup 05WC and Setup 06WC are excluded.
Setup 02, Setup 03 and Setup 04 passed all investigations since they showed good per-
formance in the storm track detection. Setup 02 and Setup 03 have a low mean absolute
error (Lmean < 100 km). The statistics of Setup 04 are founded on a very low amount
of data points and are therefore not interpretable. Furthermore, they underestimate (on
average) the minimum sea level pressure of the reference, which shows the ability of the
setups to resolve small scale features of the storm which are not present in the larger scale
forcing data. The setups have also relative low absolute errors compared with wind speed
measurements of MAE ≤2 m/s (resp. MAE = 3 m/s for Setup 03 at Høvsøre).
Generally the case study of cyclone Xaver revealed two main characteristics with regard
to the size and location of domains:
1. The storm track analysis suggests that the domain has to be big enough to cover not
only the storm center but also a main part of the storm structure itself. Otherwise,
the storm track detection fails.
In the case of cyclone Xaver, a domain size of
180 km × 180 km was not sucient enough.
2. The comparison of the wind speed and wind direction suggests that setups, which
do not cover the storm center path due to their location yield to a dierent trend in
determination of the wind speed and wind direction and yield to an great underes-
timation of the peak velocity. This indirectly traces back to the size of the domain,
since it is more likely that a small domain is not able to cover the storm path than
a big domain. The case study of cyclone Xaver suggests that the minimum domain
size should cover widely the area of interest.
Based on the above mentioned points, Setup 02, Setup 03 and Setup 04 passed the eval-
uation of cyclone Xaver.

Summary and Conclusion from Case Study II
Four initial setups based on the domains used in the case study of cyclone Xaver are
tested (Setup 02 - Setup 04). Furthermore, two modied versions of Setup 03 and Setup
04 (Setup 03s respectively Setup 04w) and a new developed Setup 01r are investigated.
Additonally, Setup 02 and Setup 01r are run with the initially introduced long term
simulation time (1 × 72 h) and with the newly introduced short term simulation time
(3 × 36 h).
Setup 02 showed good results in the storm track determination with low mean distance
and low maximum distance from the reference data (cf. Table 4.12). The storm inten-
sity was determined best compared to Setup 03 and Setup 04, but it showed partly an
overestimation of the minimum sea level pressure. It also performed insucient in the
determination of the maximum wind speed (underestimation of more than 5 m/s at the
two oshore sites, cf. Table 4.19) and showed questionable drop in the end of the simula-
tion. Because of that, this setup is excluded during wind speed analysis. The short term
simulation Setup 02t showed highly improved results in the determination of the mean
and maximum distance in the storm track detection and in the determination of the min-
imum sea level pressure (strong underestimation of the reference data). Furthermore, the
60
==PAGE:77==
underestimation of the maximum wind speed is reduced and the physically questionable
behavior in the wind speed estimation is not visible. Since it also showed comparatively
good results in the wind direction analysis (MAE between 15 deg and 22 deg), it passed
the evaluation.
Setup 03 and Setup 04 failed in the determination of the storm intensity because of a high
overestimation (on average) of the reference data as seen in the highly positive values of
the mean error (cf. Table 4.15). The modied versions Setup 03s and Setup 04w, which
were created to avoid the entering of the storm center in the corner of the domains, yield
to a reduction of the overestimation in the case of Setup 03 and to no signicant change in
the case of Setup 04 (cf. Table 4.15 and Figure 4.23). They revealed that storms entering
the corner might cause problems. However, Setup 03 and Setup 04 and their modied
versions were excluded due to insucient results in the storm intensity investigation.
Setup 01r was introduced to avoid the corner issue on a bigger scale. The long term run
showed a slightly better performance in the detection of the storm track compared to Setup
02. It has a lower maximum distance compared to the CFSR and XWS reference data
and a lower mean distance compared to the CFSR data (cf. Table 4.18 and Table 4.12).
However, its performance in the storm intensity showed a higher overestimation of the
minimum sea level pressure compared to the reference in the beginning of the simulation
than Setup 02 (cf. Figure 4.26). The performance with respect to the determination of the
peak in the wind speed showed very bad results with underestimations between 8.9 m/s
at Høvsøre and 15.8 m/s at Fino 3, (cf. Table 4.19) and showed the same questionable
velocity drop as Setup 02. Because of the bad performance in the wind speed analysis, the
setup is excluded from further analysis. The 3 × 36 h simulation run (Setup 01rt) yields
to strongly reduced mean and maximum distances in the storm track detection and to
improvements in the enhancement of the low pressure eld (ME decreased signicantly,
cf. Table 4.22). Furthermore, the underestimation of the peak in the wind speed is highly
reduced as well and the questionable velocity drop in the wind speed estimation is not
visible. The wind direction is also detected suciently with a MAE between 13 deg at
Fino 3 and 20 deg at Høvsøre compared to Setup 02t (cf. Table 4.24). It passed the
evaluation as well.
The results of the case study of cyclone Christian showed the following main points:
1. The simulation time is a very important parameter. A repetition of the simulations
with a 3 × 36 h simulation time instead of 1 × 72 h simulation time improved the
results for bigger domains. The peak in the measurements is less underestimated.
This suggests, that the simulation time is also a quite important parameter which
should be considered in the development of a proper setup.
2. The entering point of the storm center can inuence the results signicantly. The
wind speed analysis showed that the maximum wind speed is highly underestimated
when the storm center is entering very close to the corner of the domain. Therefore,
it is recommended to place domains in such a way that the possibility of entering in
one of the corners is low. This refers indirectly to the size of the domain and favors
bigger domains, because the probability of entering in the center is reduced and it
is more probable that the storm develops within the domain, which avoids the issue
completely.

Based on the above mentioned points, Setup 02t and Setup 01rt passed the evaluation.
6
Final Conclusion and Recommendation based on the
Case Studies
Based on the criteria formulated in the evaluation criteria catalog, the case studies of
cyclone Xaver and cyclone Christian suggest to consider four main points when selecting
the outermost domains for rough weather events.
1. The domain has to be big enough to ensure that storms, which are going to be ana-
lyzed are passing the domain. Otherwise, a dierent trend in the velocity estimation
is visible. Furthermore, it should be ensured that the domain is covering not only
the storm center but big parts of the structure in order to avoid failure of the the
storm track detection.
2. The domains should be big enough to avoid the issue of storms entering close to
the corner of the domain. The domain should be either placed in such a way that
the probability of entering in the corner is minimized (e.g by analysis of storm
tracks of previous storms in the relevant areas) or be big enough to ensure that the
storm is developing within the domain (avoids the issue completely). Otherwise, a
misinterpretation of the forcing data is possible.
3. The domain size should be not too big, since it is not only computationally expensive
but also the quality is not increasing after reaching a certain size. It is even more
likely that the storm track detection is less accurate (cf. Setup 01).
4. The simulation time should not be too long. Simulations using a running time of
36 h tend to perform much better than long term simulations of 72 h. Therefore,
several shorter simulations should be preferred to one long simulation.
The last two points reinforce the inuence of the large-scale forcing data on the simu-
lations. Considering all this points, the tested Setups 02t and Setup 01rt are equally
qualied for further analysis, since they showed the best overall performance. In the end,
it is decided to use Setup 01rt for further investigations since its placement was designed
to reduce the corner issue according to storms extracted from the XWS catalog. This
makes the Setup more exible towards the investigation of many storms (cf. Figure 4.24
and Figure 4.25).

7.1
Spacial Spectral Analysis
A spacial spectral analysis is performed to obtain information about the distribution of
the kinetic energy within the atmosphere model. This does not only provide information
about the eective resolution of the model [67] but it is also a useful tool to analyze if the
model reached a physically meaningful state i.e. the spin-up time is selected properly [55].
The eective resolution describes the wavelength where the determined kinetic energy
spectrum starts diverging from an observed spectrum or a spectrum determined from a
simulation with higher resolution [67]. The term gives an idea of the actual resolution of
the model, which is generally much lower than the theoretical limit determined by the
Nyquist frequency (i.e. 2∆x). The spectral slopes of k−3 (large-scale) and k−5/3 (mesoscale
and smaller), observed in the fundamental study of [68] and discussed in detail in [67] and
[69], are depicted as reference spectrum. They provide an indication about plausibility of
the results.
The spacial spectral analysis is performed in six dierent vertical levels, which are specied
by the terrain following hydrostatic pressure variable η:
η = p −pT
ps −pT
(4.3)
with ps being the surface pressure and pT being the top layer pressure which is dened
as pT = 50 hPa. That means, that η varies between 0 (top level pressure) and 1 (surface
pressure level).
The following steps are performed to calculate the spectrum at a certain η-level and a
certain time step. Firstly, spacial data is extracted row-wise in Northwest-Southeast direc-
tion and column-wise in Northeast-Southwest direction. The rst and last 15 data points
in each row/column are ignored to minimize the eect of the boundary. Subsequently, the
data obtained in each row respectively column are detrended, Fourier transformed and
the results are averaged over all rows respectively columns. This yield to one spectrum
in each direction (Northwest-Southeast and Northeast-Southwest) per time step and per
η-level.
Figure 4.38 shows the results obtained at six dierent η-levels in Northwest-Southeast
and Northeast-Southwest direction at 0 h, 3 h, 6 h, 9 h and 12 h after initialization. It can
be seen that the model layers at the top (orange and yellow) need more time to build up
energy than the lower levels at the surface (bluish colors). The build up process is mainly
taking place in medium and higher angular wave numbers ( k > 10−5 m−1rad) which can
be explained by the transition of the model from resolving only the large scale elements
provided by the forcing data to a detailed resolution of small scale elements up to the
eective resolution of the model with time. The change of the relative energy distribution
with time gives an idea about the needed spin-up time.
Relative energy distribution
means the relative dierence in kinetic energy between the individual vertical layers. It
is observed that dierences in kinetic energy between the higher levels and lower levels in
the area between the eective resolution and the Nyquist frequency decreases. It takes
between 6 h and 9 h, which is less than the selected spin-up time, to reach a consistent
level. One exception build the highest model layer at η = 0.006, which does not reach

unstaggered grid of the WRF model. The results obtained so close to the border of the
model could be less signicant. The slopes in the kinetic energy spectra are comparable
to the theoretically expected slopes of k−3 and k−5
3. All in all, the investigation shows,
that the model reached a physically meaningful state and that the spin-up time is selected

Summary and Conclusion from the Resolution Analysis
The spacial spectral analysis showed that a spin-up time of 12 h is long enough to build
up all model layers up to the top and to reach a physically meaningful state. It could be
shown that η-levels high above the surface need more time to build up than η-levels closer
to the surface. It is observed that the kinetic energy follows approximately the expected
slopes of k−3 and k−5
3 as observed and examined in several studies for dierent ranges.
The eective resolution at around 7∆x is also visible.
The temporal spectral analysis showed the strong connection between the spacial and
temporal resolution. It is presented that the higher the spacial resolution, the better
are the high frequency components in the measuring signal represented. The temporal
spectrum followed the expected slope of f −2
3 in the case of cyclone Christian while the
high frequency components in the measurements during cyclone Xaver follow a dierent
trend due to the presence of the open cell structure (cf. Figure 4.12). This suggests that
the assumption of Taylor might be not valid in this case. Both analyses strongly suggests
a resolution of at least 2 km to represent also high frequency components in the measuring
signals.

Summary and Conclusion
The evaluation showed the wave parameters in all investigated cases are (overall seen)
estimated well with relative low mean absolute errors. However, all cases showed dif-
culties in estimating the peak value in the measuring signals. Furthermore, the wave
parameter estimation of cyclone Xaver and cyclone Ulli were characterized by relative
dominant underestimation of the measurements (mean absolute error mainly caused by
underestimation), while cyclone Christian is not showing this eect.
The evaluation of cyclone Ulli showed a good performance in the determination of the
storm track and the enhancement of the sea level pressure. Notable is the good estimation
of the atmospheric parameters wind direction and wind speed while the performance in
the estimation of the wave parameters is less accurate. This is especially visible with
regard to the maximum values in the time series.
All in all, the investigation revealed that the performance of the coupled simulation is
case sensitive, which emphasizes the importance of simulating a statistically signicant
amount of rough weather events to draw powerful conclusions on the model performance.
However, it could be shown that the model is able to reconstruct storm track, enhance
the sea level pressure eld and shows an generally good agreement with measurements.

Chapter 6
Applications
Beyond the application for the determination of location and intensity of storms and the
possibility of extracting wind and wave parameters at a certain location, this chapter states
applications within the area of oshore wind energy. Since the coupled simulation has
the huge advantage of providing a spacial distribution of wind and wave characteristics,
the focus in this chapter lies in the usage of the spacial elds. The aim of the following
chapter lies in the exemplary depiction of possible applications and can be extended in
principal to any severe storm event. Within the scope of this analysis, the results of the
coupled simulation of cyclone Ulli are used. Section 1 deals with the presentation of sea
state and wind conditions at several locations during the storm event while Section 2 will
focus on the detection of endangered areas based on recommendations in "Guidelines for
Oshore Marine Operations" [1]. Section 3 presents the spacial distribution of the peak
wave period which is a key parameter in wave induced fatigue loads and the motion of
vessels.
1
Sea State and Wind Conditions at Certain Locations
Even though the sea state and the wind conditions of an individual storm case are not
representative from a climatological perspective, the knowledge can be used to classify
the severity of the storm event or categorize it according to dominant wind and wave
directions, dominant wave heights and dominant wind speeds.
Wave roses are created from the simulated signicant wave heights of the two simulation
blocks of cyclone Ulli. For the directional component of the wave rose, the peak direction

increases if the peak wave period is close to the rst natural frequency or its harmonics
since it increases the excitation of structures. Furthermore, it has also inuence on vessel
motions during transits [71], for example when causing excessive roll motions due to waves
running close to the natural roll period of the vessel [26].

Summary and Outline
1
Summary and Final Conclusion
This work presents a systematic evaluation procedure for the design of a proper model
setup to simulate rough weather events over the North Sea based on the numerical mod-
eling system COAWST with focus on oshore wind energy applications.
The need of accurate and reliable storm reconstructions for operation and maintenance
purposes and the determination of oshore wind turbine design parameters is pointed
out. An overview over relevant literature dealing with the investigation of the impact of
grid parameters (grid size, grid location and grid resolution), initial and lateral boundary
conditions and parameterization schemes on dierent aspects inuencing the simulation
of severe weather events is given. Dissent in the literature about inuencing factors is
revealed, pointing out the necessity for a closer and systematic examination focusing on
the broad picture of storm simulations adjusted to the specic application for wind energy
application purposes.
This aim of developing a well-founded model setup for the simulation of rough weather
events with focus on wind energy applications is achieved by formulating and answering
two leading questions:
1. Which physical characteristics of a severe weather event are especially important
for wind energy applications and should be estimated as good as possible and steps
are necessary to consider to develop a well-founded simulation model Setup?
2. Which conditions for grid specic parameters (size, location and resolution) and
simulation time have to be applied then to achieve this well-founded simulation
model Setup?
To address the rst leading question dealing with the key factors which should be esti-
mated well by a proper model setup, an evaluation criteria catalog (Chapter 4, Section
1) is developed to list important characteristics which are especially important for wind
91
==PAGE:108==
energy applications. The proper estimation of the storm track and the storm intensity
(represented by the minimum sea level pressure of the storm center), relevant charac-
teristics of the wind speed at hub-height (time and duration above cut-out wind speed,
maximum wind speed during the event) and the general trend in wind direction are se-
lected as being most relevant. Based on this catalog, an evaluation scheme (Figure 1.1)
is created to provide a consistent and systematic procedure to determine a proper model
setup.
To answer the second leading question addressing the conditions for grid parameter and
simulation time for a well-founded model setup, the evaluation scheme is applied to two
exemplary storm events in 2013 (cyclone Xaver and cyclone Christian) which are simu-
lated in the uncoupled conguration (i.e. WRF is ran only). The results are evaluated
qualitatively and quantitatively according to the evaluation criteria catalog (Chapter 4,
Section 4 and Section 5).
The investigations reveal that model domains have to be large enough to ensure that the
storm center is passing the model domain but should not be too big in order to prevent
the development of own dynamics diverging from the forcing data. It is shown that this
eect can be reduced by splitting the total simulation time in several shorter individual
simulations.
Furthermore it is revealed that the location where the storm enters the
model domain yield to an underestimation of the storm intensity if it is very close to the
corner. Spacial spectral analysis showed that a spin-up time of 12 h is suciently large to
build up the top vertical layers and reach a consistent status while the temporal spectral
analysis revealed that the resolution in the frequency domain strongly depends on the
spacial resolution and that a domain resolution of at least 2 km is necessary to represent
also the high frequency components in the measuring signal of the wind speed.
Based on the above mentioned conditions, a recommendation for a suitable setup is given
and is selected for the coupled simulation of the two known cases cyclone Xaver and
Christian and the randomly selected case of cyclone Ulli. It is shown that the atmo-
spheric conditions are represented well while the performance with regard to signicant
wave height and peak wave period indicate a tendency in underestimating the peak val-
ues (cyclone Xaver and cyclone Ulli). However, due to the limited amount of coupled
simulations, a suciently supported conclusion addressing the underestimations cannot
be drawn.
Finally, the coupled simulation of cyclone Ulli is used to show exemplary possible applica-
tions of the spacial distributed wind and wave parameters in Chapter 6. This includes the
depiction of sea state and wind conditions at four dierent locations during cyclone Ulli,
a marking of endangered areas characterized by high signicant wave heights and high
wind speeds based on recommendations in "Guidelines for Oshore Marine Operations"
and a spacial depiction of the development of the peak wave period with time.
2
Outline and Further Work
The presented work provides a solid foundations for the investigation of rough weather
over the North Sea using COAWST, but the evaluations in Chapter 4, Chapter 5 and
92
==PAGE:109==
Chapter 6 showed that further investigations in dierent areas are necessary.
Firstly, Chapter 5 showed that a good estimation of atmospheric condition in COAWST
does not guarantee a similarly good estimation of oceanic parameters. This clearly sug-
gests further investigations towards the interaction between the atmospheric model and
wave model, for example towards the simulation time and initial conditions for the wave
model. Currently, only one conguration of the wave model SWAN is investigated (ini-
tialization 24 h before the initialization of WRF). This might be an anchor point for
improvements. It must be noted as well the Evaluation Criteria Catalog focuses on the
best representation of atmosphere parameters.
Another anchor point is the amount of investigated cases.
The simulation of a large
amount of severe storm events is necessary to lift the current investigation from a plain
detection of case specic issues to the next level, which is the separation in systematical
problems caused by improper settings and in storm case specic problems due to very com-
plex atmospheric and oceanic conditions during some rough weather events. Currently,
only three events are analyzed. Exemplary applications for individual storm cases could
be shown (Chapter 6) but the limited amount of simulations do not provide a statistically
signicant foundation to abstract the results obtained from the individual case.
A large amount of simulated cases also has great potential considering the advantage of
COAWST to provide combined spacial elds of atmospheric and oceanic parameters. The
simulation of a large number of storm cases distributed over several decades could then be
used for example for joined wind and wave hint-casts with the advantage of a combined
extreme value analysis of atmospheric extremes like 50-year magnitude of the mean wind
speed and oceanic extremes like the 50-year magnitude of the signicant wave height at
locations, where the coverage of long term measurements in the dimension of decades is
poor or the resolution or time span of available combined wind-wave hint-casts e.g. [72]
(North Sea, Norwegian Sea, Barents Sea) is too low respectively too short.
