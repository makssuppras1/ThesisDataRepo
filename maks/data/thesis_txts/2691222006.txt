Abstract
Additive manufacturing provides the possibility of design freedom to make optimal de­
signs a reality. This opens up the possibility to obtain efficient and robust structural de­
signs, that can perform even if damage occurs. However, this technology adds geomet­
rical restrictions such as the need of a piece to be self­supported. If not possible, support
material can be utilized, although increasing resource consumption. Some of the current
approaches incorporate the manufacturing restrictions within the topology optimization
problem. Other methods, rely on the use of a virtual skeleton, a self­supported truss
topology that influences material distribution towards its elements.
Current efforts for developing continuum domain fail­safe optimizations, have resulted
in expensive computational methods. In contrast, a few methods are available for truss
fail­safe optimizations that are efficient and robust.
The work performed in this project deals with reducing the demand of computational
resources to obtain fail­safe designs that can be additive manufactured. A methodology
is proposed to reduce the amount of support material for structural designs considering
fail­safe requirements.
For this, the virtual skeleton approach is utilized and expanded. The method consists in
solving two problems. First, a truss topology optimization, taking into account fail­safe
and manufacturing requirements. Then, the output design is projected onto the con­
tinuum domain, guiding the following continuum optimization (second problem). The
projected topology (virtual skeleton) encourages material distribution towards its mem­
bers, leading to layouts with reduced unprintability.
In this thesis, the method is thoroughly explained and applied in 2­D examples. More­
over, the methodology is expanded towards transient load applications and some pre­
liminary results are presented. The numerical results indicate that a reduction of unprint­
ability is achieved at the expense of performance drops. The transient load preliminary
results suggest that additional studies need to be made on implementation.
Keywords: Additive Manufacturing, Topology Optimization, Virtual Skeleton, Fail­Safe
Optimization, Transient Optimization

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Additive manufacturing (AM) is a promising technology with the potential to revolutionise
the manufacturing industry. Most techniques work similarly, where parts are created by
adding one fine layer of material at a time. The machines replicate the geometry from
a computer­aided­design (CAD) model, following the manufacturing directions from a
stereolitography (STL) file. This opens up the possibility of creating complex geometries
that could not be conceived under current methods. Optimal designs could potentially
be used without geometrical constraints, improving the performance of pieces while
reducing the use of resources when manufacturing.
Frequently, engineering intuition is used to optimize structural designs. However, an
even better approach is to rely on structural optimization methods. One of such meth­
ods is Topology Optimization (TO), a mathematical technique which distributes the ma­
terial efficiently over a design domain. This approach leads to high performing layouts,
obtained after searching iteratively for optimal solutions from finite element analyses.
The resulting designs, however, usually comprise complex features that are difficult to
manufacture. Therefore, there is a good match between TO and AM technologies, as
the latter may overcome the issues related to the manufacturing of complex geometries.
Nonetheless, optimal layouts usually lack redundancy. This could be hazardous in case
a structural member fails, possibly leading to the total collapse of the structure. Fail­safe
design is a method that accounts for either losing elements (total damage) or reducing
their contribution to the load carrying capacity of a structure (partial damage). Thus,
by including this concept into the structural optimizations, the safety of a final layout
gets increased. This approach results in robust designs with a layer of redundancy,
which ensures the safety of the structure even if damage occurs. That being said, the
current fail­safe design methods for continuum structures usually lack the considera­
tion of ”structural members”, relying on the removal of a pre­established group of finite
elements instead.
The combination of the aforementioned approaches and the mass adoption of AM could
lead to substantial changes in terms of design methods and material consumption.
Some industries have already benefitted from the application of these technologies such
as the automotive and aerospace industry, where weight reduction and high structural
performance are constantly sought. Even the wind power industry could take advantage
of them, either in the design of turbine components or jacket substructures.
1.2
Objectives and Approach
The main objective of this project is to develop a method to deal with the TO of con­
tinuum structures with fail­safe requirements, that need as minimum support material

==PAGE:10==
as possible for AM. This means that robust structural designs are obtained as output,
which are safe even after some damage occurs and that require a minimal amount of
support material to be printed.
The approach used to fulfill the objective is based on solving two consecutive problems:
first a discrete truss­based optimization followed by a continuum­domain optimization.
In the first problem, the AM requirements and the failure scenarios are considered simul­
taneously. A working­set approach (Stolpe (2019)) is implemented to solve the fail­safe
design problems, in an attempt to reduce the computation times. The optimal truss
topology is then projected to the continuum domain (virtual skeleton) to encourage ma­
terial distribution towards truss elements throughout the continuum optimization. The
virtual skeleton approach is based on the work found in Mass and Amir (2017).
The second problem consists in solving the continuum TO influenced by the optimal
truss topology and computing the post processing evaluation parameters from the out­
put. The continuum TO is based on the top88.m script from the TopOpt group, and its
details can be found in Andreassen et al. (2011).
1.3
Scope
The project relies on TO algorithms for discrete and continuum domain elements. The
discrete optimizations are based on a linear problem, not considering any sort of pe­
nalization. The continuum optimizations are based on a SIMP model using unit length
finite elements.
The AM manufacturing constraints and fail­safe requirements are only introduced in the
discrete problem. Therefore, the continuum domain optimizations do not include any
explicit information about these requirements (only effects from the virtual skeleton).
Both static and transient load optimizations are implemented. Only linear elastic anal­
yses are considered. In the dynamic problems, the damping matrix gradients are only
estimated. Due to the limited amount of computational resources, only numerical ex­
periments subjected to static loads are carried out for the consolidated algorithms.
1.4
Outline
The rest of the thesis is organised as follows: Chapter 2 contains a short literature review
on the main topics related to this project, namely AM, TO and fail­safe design. Chapter 3
develops on the methodology associated to the different steps followed in this project. It
displays the problem formulations, governing equations and explanations are provided.
Chapter 4 displays the results from initial problems used to validate the individual scripts
developed for the final consolidated algorithm. Some transient load optimization results
are included. Chapter 5 elaborates on the results of the application of the fully coupled
algorithms for a static problem. Finally, in Chapter 6 some conclusions are elaborated
and a discussion about the results is treated.

Literature Review
This chapter gives a rough overview about the existing work related to concepts and
techniques relevant to the project.
2.1
Topology Optimization
The first publication regarding TO of continuum structures can be found in Bendsøe
and Kikuchi (1988), where the methodology of introducing small holes to determine the
optimal shape of structures was defined. Since then, the method has developed in
multiple direction. An overview of TO approaches can be found in Sigmund and Maute
(2013).
2.1.1
Density Approach
One of the most widespread methods is the density approach. This method consists in
solving a discrete domain (finite elements) TO by means of continuous variables (densi­
ties). These variables define whether should be assigned to each element (for instance,
with values between zero and one). The optimal topology is determined by all elements
with (or near) unit values. To discourage material distribution towards elements with low
densities some penalization methods have been proposed. Bendsøe (1989) suggested
the use of the Simplified Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP), which is an artificial
power law affecting the stiffness of elements. This method effectively improves the con­
vergence of values towards binary solutions. An alternative approach was presented by
Stolpe and Svanberg (2001), the Rational Approximation of Material Properties (RAMP)
method. This method introduces another interpolation scheme that influences material
properties, but ensures the concavity of the model. The RAMP method also ensures
the existence of a gradient larger than zero when the densities reach a null value, in
contrast to the SIMP model, where it is equal to zero.
TO was not free of complications, and in the early days a common ”checkerboard” prob­
lem was usually encountered. This refers to the appearance of alternating black and
white (solid and void) elements in the topologies, resembling the patterns of a checker­
board. This happens due to numerical instabilities that induce an artificial high stiffness
to the checkerboard layout (Díaz and Sigmund (1995)). This leads to the convergence
of that kind of topology for compliance minimization problems. One way to solve this
problem is to use a density filter as presented in Bruns and Tortorelli (2001). This method
introduces an additional density field that filters the original design variables. The re­
sult is that a length scale is imposed on the designs and mesh­independent layouts
are obtained. The application of this filter eliminates the checkerboard problem due
to the mesh­independency of the solutions. However, the use of this filter also brings
the problem of generating regions with intermediate densities at the boundaries of the
topology.

To deal with the intermediate densities associated with the aforementioned filtering tech­
nique, a third density field can be introduced. These formulations are known as pro­
jection methods, as they project the densities towards zeros and ones by means of a
smooth Heaviside function. One of these functions can be found in Guest, Prévost,
and Belytschko (2004), where a non­linear Heaviside projection is presented in terms
of exponential functions and a parameter β. The latter which determines the shape of
the Heaviside approximation. Its application relies on a continuation scheme, updating
β during the optimization. However, this leads to a temporary violation of the volume
constraint, which can lead to perturbations on the topology. Xu, Cai, and Cheng (2010)
works on this, presenting a volume­preserving function and introducing a parameter η
that controls the threshold of the smooth Heaviside projection. All densities above η are
projected to one and all those below to zero. Another function worth mentioning is that
developed by Wang, Lazarov, and Sigmund (2011), based on the use of a hyperbolic
tangent and similar parameters β and η. The method relies on a robust method (RM)
(Sigmund (2009)) where three design versions are obtained: a dilated, intermediate and
eroded versions. Each is computed by means of the proposed Heaviside projection us­
ing different threshold values η. Moreover, each design plays a different role during the
optimization where the intermediate version defines the final designs.
2.1.2
Dynamic Optimizations
The dynamic problems introduce a higher degree of difficulty due to the introduction of
time­dependant constraints. The sensitivity analysis becomes more complicated too.
Moreover, the demand of computational resources is considerably higher, as for every
iteration a second order differential equation (equation of motion) needs to be solved
and a large number of constraints satisfied. A brief overview about these topics can be
found in Kang, Park, and Arora (2006).
The algorithms used to solve optimization problems rely heavily on the use of gradients
of both the objective function and the constraints. Therefore, finding a way to reduce
the number of time constraints is relevant. One way to do this, is to reduce them to an
equivalent parameter that acts as the objective function. In one of the first publications
about transient load TO (Min et al. (1999)), the optimization problem is based on the
minimization of a mean dynamic compliance. Another option is to utilize aggregation
techniques. This means that multiple constraints are converted into one single estima­
tion of the maximum response. Some aggregation functions that have been used are:
the Kreisselmeier­Steinhauser (Kreisselmeier and Steinhauser (1979)), P­norm and P­
mean (Duysinx and Sigmund (1998)) functions. All three of them depend on some
parameters that affect the quality of the estimation. These kind of approximations de­
crease considerably the computation times of the optimizations.
Another challenge about transient load optimizations is the sensitivity analysis. The
classical method to compute the gradients used to be finite differences, however the
computational costs are too high. Hence, other methods have been developed over
time, such as the Direct Differentiation Method (DDM) and the Adjoint Variable Method
(AVM). For static problems the adjoint method is trivial, however, for transient problems
4
==PAGE:13==
it becomes more complicated. A few publications developing on the sensitivities of
transient problems are available (Kang, Park, and Arora (2006), Jensen, Nakshatrala,
and Tortorelli (2014)). Moreover, the definition of which method to use is not irrelevant,
and some publications with details about the implementation of DDM (Hsieh and Arora
(1985)) and the AVM (Michaleris, Tortorelli, and Vidal (1994)) are available.
2.1.3
Fail­safe design
Optimization algorithms can also be used to obtain robust structures in terms of redun­
dancy. These so­called fail­safe designs, mean that the final topology can still perform
correctly after some damage occurs.
One of the very first publications on truss optimization problems considering redundancy
can be found in Mohr et al. (2014). The problems under consideration consist of the
minimization of volume under stress constraints. The proposed approach also considers
multiple load cases. The concept of redundancy is applied to truss structure inspired
by the coding theory. Thus, the fail­safe optimizations are carried out by removing bars
from the structure.
In Stolpe (2019) a working­set algorithm is introduced to reduce the demand of com­
putational resources during fail­safe optimizations. The method consists in solving a
series of relaxations instead of the standard problem. These are obtained by temporar­
ily removing constraints and re­introducing them strategically for the benefit of speed.
Three different minimum compliance problems are solved by means of the working­set
approach and timed for different configurations. The results show that the application
of the method converges in a few outer iterations. In Pollini (2020), the working­set al­
gorithm is also utilized for a different kind of application (optimal distribution of viscous
dampers). The application of the method also results in a significant computational cost
reduction.
TO can also be used towards fail­safe design of continuum structure as first described in
Jansen et al. (2014). The proposed idea is to minimize the maximum compliance sub­
jected to a volume constraint. The compliances are computed for each damage scenario
by removing square­shaped patches from the 2D continuum domain at a time. The max
function is approximated by means of the KS­function (Kreisselmeier and Steinhauser
(1979)) in order to find the optimal layout of the structure. Even though the aggregation
function is used, the approach is very time consuming, as a number of finite element
analyses equal to the number of patch removal scenarios needs to be solved for each
iteration. Since not all patches are critical, a potential active set strategy is suggested
as an approach to decrease computation times. A similar method can be found in Zhou
and Fleury (2016), where cube and sphere shapes are used to model damage instead,
for a 3D problem. However, none of these methods are capable to directly removing
members from the final design, since it is updated in each iteration until convergence.

2.2
Additive Manufacturing
2.2.1
General
Multiple AM technologies have been developed over the years. These technologies
have a large potential to change current manufacturing procedures, as it offers many
new capabilities. They range from fabricating almost any shape to the possibility of
creating complete mechanisms or embedding other components within a printed part
(Rosen (2014)). Thorough overviews about AM can be found in Gao et al. (2015),
Wong and Hernandez (2012) and Gibson et al. (2021), where the main technologies
are described. Moreover, in Gao et al. (2015) and Gibson et al. (2021) a wide range of
current applications are presented, including products within the medical and aerospace
industries, among others. Not only do they address the advantages of AM, but also the
challenges to become a standard in the manufacturing industry.
2.2.2
Overhang Features
One of the main limitations of AM technologies is that the inclination between the outer
surface of a member and the horizontal plane should be above a threshold value to be
self­supported. This limitation has already been studied for different technologies and
some authors have established a critical angle around 45°(Leary et al. (2014), Thomas
(2009)). If the part to be manufactured contains patterns below the critical angle (over­
hang features), design is not fully printable as it is. To be manufactured, the design has
to be either modified or printed by using temporary support material. The former option
introduces a reduction in performance and the latter an increment in material consump­
tion, which later has to be removed. Both alternatives involve an increase in time and
costs.
Optimal topologies for general applications usually exhibit overhang features, as AM
restrictions are not included. Thus, some authors have put effort into solving this ge­
ometrical problem. Leary et al. (2014) deals with the manufacturing requirements in a
post­processing step. The methodology modifies an optimal topology by adding mate­
rial to make the design totally printable. This increases the total volume and mass of the
structure, reducing the efficiency of the topology. Another approach is to include the AM
design limitations within the TO problem. Gaynor and Guest (2016) introduces a new
support variable that defines whether material can be assigned to an element or not,
depending on the fulfillment of the AM constraints. The support variable is involved in a
series of projection functions that take into account the critical overhang angle. Addition­
ally, an overhang support neighbourhood is defined, consisting of a wedge­shaped re­
gion below a given node. Langelaar (2017) proposes the use of a filter that encourages
the topologies to comply with the AM requirements. This is carried out by means of two
density fields (blueprint and printed). The former acts as the design variable whereas
the latter as the actual design. According to these definitions, the method seeks the sur­
rounding elements for support material simulating the printing motion (layer by layer). Its
application leads to designs that in general avoid the generation of overhang features.
An extension of the algorithm to 3D was also developed and some results are available
in Langelaar (2016).
6
==PAGE:15==
A new interesting method to deal with overhang constraints in the optimization of struc­
tures, conceived for AM, is presented in Mass and Amir (2017). In this approach, the
author suggests using a ”virtual skeleton” to encourage the material distribution during
the continuum optimization. This virtual skeleton is obtained from a previous step: a
truss optimization, which takes into account the overhang constraints and printing ori­
entation. Then, the truss topology is projected into the continuum domain, being used
as starting point for the final optimization.

Methodology
The following chapter describes the methodology used throughout the simulations. It
begins with a brief explanation of the general problem followed by details about the
implementation used to solve the static and transient problems. Finally, a section re­
garding the evaluation of final designs is presented, both in terms of performance and
printability.
3.1
General
The goal of this project is to develop an approach for TO of continuum structures with
fail­safe requirements, that need a minimum amount of support material for AM. Thus,
the output topologies are more robust in the case of certain damages (fail­safe designs)
and can be 3D­printed without wasting much material (temporary supports for AM). For
this purpose, the TO problems are divided into two subsequent optimizations. First, a
2­D truss structure is optimized while considering fail­safe design requirements. The
available bars are defined in a ground structure where the inclination of members com­
ply with the overhang limit angle. The resulting topology is used as a virtual skeleton
in the next problem: a continuum optimization. To accomplish this, the truss topol­
ogy is projected into the continuum domain, encouraging material distribution towards
the location of truss elements throughout the optimization. Both static and transient
dynamic loads are considered in this work. Particular attention is directed to the as­
sociated problem formulations and their implementations. A common TO approach is
taken in all problems, minimizing an objective function (different for static and transient
problem), subjected to a volume constraint. The design variable is an artificial density
ρ, which determines whether material exists or not within each bar or finite element.
3.2
Detection of Overhang Features
The detection of overhang features is carried out before starting the truss optimization.
In this study, the ground structure is defined within a rectangular­shaped domain. The
ground structure is filled by a regular distribution of equidistant nodes, each connected
to multiple neighbouring nodes by a series of bars. Having defined the ground structure,
all geometrical properties are established, namely node coordinates and bar topology.
Therefore, all the information to evaluate the inclination of bars is available. A subset
B of the ground structure is filtered by means of a subroutine, which identifies the el­
ements that do not comply with the overhang limit angle (see Algorithm 1). Then, all
elements within subset B are assigned a small cross section and their corresponding
upper boundary of the design variable is set to a small number. By doing this, the op­
timization is carried out considering all elements in the ground structure, but the final
topologies only include elements that satisfy the AM constraint.

3.3.3.1
Working­set algorithm
The problem formulation displayed in (3.8) could result in a large amount of constraints
in case the number of damage scenarios becomes relevant. This results in significant
computation times and therefore, an alternative method is also implemented in an at­

consists in solving a series of problem relaxations instead. Assuming that a small num­

from considering only a sub­set of damage scenarios, defined as the working­set W

continuum optimization. This is performed following the procedure presented in Mass
and Amir (2017), by means of a matrix T which maps each bar from the truss topology
into the continuum domain. The purpose of the virtual skeleton is to influence the ma­
terial distribution towards the truss elements. An illustration of an input topology and an
output T­matrix mapping is depicted in Figure 3.1.

3.4
Transient Problem
In this work, not only static loads are considered, but also transient dynamic loads.
To analyze the effects of such excitations, it is necessary to resort to the methods of
structural dynamics, which consider the response of the system over time. This new
approach leads to substantial modifications to the problem formulations. First of all, the
equilibrium of the system is described by the equations of motion:
M¨u(t) + C ˙u(t) + Ku(t) = f(t)
(3.43)
where M, C and K are the mass, damping and stiffness matrices, ¨u(t), ˙u(t) and u(t),
the acceleration, velocity and displacement vectors and f(t) an external dynamic force
vector. It is worth noting that expression (3.43) is a second­order differential equation
over time, adding a higher level of complexity in comparison to the static equilibrium
equation (see Equation (3.1)). Other relevant features are that the external load can
change over time, enabling the modelling of more complex phenomena, and that the
outcome of the problem consists of a time history of the state variables (u, ˙u, ¨u). Since
the methods used to solve the equations of motion rely on discrete time steps, it is
relevant to display it in its discrete form:

3.5
Solution Methods for Optimizations
There are different methods to solve TO problems, which are used depending on the
type of problem to be solved. Some methods are very fast but only applicable to simple
problems, whereas others are more robust but take a longer time. In this section, the
methods used in this project are presented briefly.
28
==PAGE:37==
3.5.1
Truss Problems
For a single static optimization the Optimality Criteria method can be used to solve
the problem, using a heuristic update rule, adopting a parameter η = 0.8 and based
on a bisection algorithm to find the solution. This method produces fast and accurate
results. More complex problems such as the fail­safe examples (both entire problem
and working­set approach) and all the transient optimizations are solved by means of
the fmincon solver, provided by the optimization toolbox available in MATLAB® version
R2020a (The MathWorks Inc. (2020)). An interior­point optimization algorithm is used,
supplying the gradients of both the objective function and constraints.
3.5.2
Continuum Domain Problems
For the case of the baseline static problem designs, the Optimality Criteria method is
used. Similar settings as for the static truss optimizations are adopted, but with a pa­
rameted η = 0.5 instead. However, all other problems (T­matrix mapping and transient)
make use of the Method of Moving Aymptotes (MMA) developed by Svanberg (1987).
This is a robust algorithm that can handle a vast variety of optimization problems. The
application MMA is carried out with the default parameters and leads to smooth solu­
tions when using the virtual skeleton approach as well as for the case of transient load
optimization problems.

6
Conclusion
The work in this thesis proposes a methodology to deal with the design of structures
that comply with fail­safe and AM requirements. The procedure is an extension of the
method found in Mass and Amir (2017), which accounts efficiently for overhang features
by means of a virtual skeleton. The extension consists in using a virtual skeleton ob­
tained by solving a fail­safe optimization. The results show that it is possible to obtain
robust structures where a high level of redundancy is achieved in the final topology.
Moreover, the designs possess an enhanced printability, although at the expense of
performance. In contrast to what is recommended in Mass and Amir (2017), the first
method of the T­matrix implementation yields better results. Furthermore, a high value
of the parameter α is required to achieve suitable topologies rather than a small one. It
is worth mentioning that only static problems were solved using this approach.
A few advantages of this method are that the overhang feature detection and fail­safe
requirements are only considered during the truss optimization. This is relevant since
additional constraints in the continuum optimization can become very expensive in terms
of computational costs. Additionally, it is straightforward to account for different printing
orientations, as it only involves rotating the ground structure and applying the corre­
sponding element filtering.
Transient load optimizations were implemented on the discrete and continuum domain
optimizations. Two methods were used to compute the gradients: DDM and AVM. Both
provide reliable results but AVM is faster for the type of problems solved in this project.
However, the transient load problems also introduce additional challenges. First, their
computations are very time consuming, specially the fail­safe and continuum domain
simulations. Next, the gradient of the damping matrix C is not trivial. Only estimations
were implemented in this project: considering C as constant and using finite differences.
The latter approach is very time consuming. Hence, an efficient approach to compute
the gradient of C should be investigated. One option could be to use constant parame­
ters α and β when building the matrix. Additionally, the output seems to be dependant
on the input load frequency. Thus, a method to get frequency­independant solutions
should be implemented. Having said that, more thorough analyses should be carried
out before reaching conclusions.
Different problem formulations can be developed for the dynamic optimizations. In this
project, the objective functions were expressed by means of the KS­function in order
to control the maximum displacements. However, the objective functions could also be
defined in terms of accelerations or velocities. Moreover, constraints could be formu­
lated to limit the the state variables. Other options to simulate the max function are the
P­norm and P­mean.

The working­set algorithm was implemented to solve the truss fail­safe optimizations.
Even though it provides fast computations for the static problems, it did not hold true
for the dynamic analyses. In the latter the standard problem formulation resulted in
better computation times. That can be explained by the amount of relaxed problems
needed to solve the optimization, which all together sum longer times. This is true for
both implementations of the C­matrix gradient. Nevertheless, provisional results using
constant parameters α and β when dealing with the C­matrix gradient, seem to benefit
from the working­set approach.
Future research can be directed towards the application of this method to transient load
optimizations. It would also be a valuable contribution to study the effects of an excitation
containing a set of different frequencies. An extension towards a multiple­load case
optimization or optimizing structures subjected to random vibrations could work towards
that goal. An extension towards a 3­dimensional domain is another logical step for this
project.
