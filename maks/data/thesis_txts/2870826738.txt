Abstract
Background: Soil viromes play a vital role in microbial ecology, yet their diversity and
taxonomy remain poorly understood.
Objective: This study aimed to characterize RNA viral communities across different soil
types by identifying RdRp gene after high-throughput sequencing.
Methods: RNA viruses were extracted, sequenced, and analyzed using PalmScan to
identify RNA viral contigs. HMM-based tools were employed to detect the RdRp gene.
Community analysis, including alpha and beta diversity assessments, was conducted
to evaluate viral distribution patterns. Additionally, phylogenetic analysis based on pro-
tein sequences was performed to explore evolutionary relationships among the identified
viruses.
Results: Grassland soils exhibited the highest viral diversity, with negative-strand Negar-
naviricota as the potentially dominant phylum, whereas agricultural soils showed lower
richness and diversity, with a possible dominance of positive-strand Kitrinoviricota. The
forest soil virome demonstrated considerable diversity, reflecting the complexity of its
broadly diverse ecosystem. Comparisons with previous studies suggest that the discov-
ery of novel viruses is particularly likely within the Negarnaviricota phylum.
Conclusion: These results underscore the impact of soil type on viral community diver-
sity, suggesting that host availability and environmental factors play a key role in shaping
soil viromes.
Keywords: Soil virome, viral diversity, viromic, RNA viruses, hmm(Hidden Markov Mod-
els), alpha diversity, beta diversity, viral-host, high-throughput sequencing, microbial ecol-
ogy, phylogeny.

INTRODUCTION
Soil viruses are the most abundant biological entitties, which drive soil microbial commu-
nites, and ecosystem processes. They infect all domains of life in soil from bacteria, fungi
and others. Soil viruses regulate microbial populations, and by doing so, they contribute
to soil health, carbon cycling and overall ecosystem.Since the outbreak of the COVID-19
pandemic, public awareness of viruses has increased significantly, driving a substan-
tial expansion of virus-related research across various fields. While previous study have
evaluated soil virus richness under different soil conditions [1], many aspects remain un-
explored. Soil conditions and biocommunities interact intricately, influencing each other
in complex ways. Soil organisms can contribute to soil erosion or, conversely, reduce
soil loss through their biological activities [2]. In turn, soil conditions globally affect micro-
bial communities by distrubing the soil microbial food web. Soil viruses, along with other
microorganisms such as fungi, archaea, and protists, interact dynamically under varying
physicochemical properties, including soil texture, water content, and pore size [3].
To comprehensively and systematically investigate soil viruses and their environmental
interactions, recent developments in bioinformatics and machine learning are essential
to fill existing knowledge gaps. The bioinformatics pipeline for virome data analysis pre-
dominantly supports or favors DNA viruses, however, in recent years, interest in RNA
viruses and tools to support RNA virome has been growing. A novel pipeline has recently
been used to study RNA viruses, with a particular focus on double-stranded (ds) RNA
viruses [4]. Similarly, this study takes an inclusive approach by examining all types of
RNA viruses across three distinct soil environments. It encompasses double-stranded
(ds) RNA viruses, positive-sense single-stranded (ps) RNA viruses, and negative-sense
single-stranded (ns) RNA viruses, providing a broad perspective on RNA virus diversity
within these ecological niches.
1.1
The Soil Environment
Compared to aquatic ecosystems, soil virology remains a relatively underexplored field [5].
Viruses in soil play pivotal roles in nutrient cycling, food web interactions, gene transfer,
and other essential ecological processes. These viral activities are influenced by a range
of environmental factors, including temperature, soil moisture, the presence of aerobic
microorganisms, virus adsorption to soil particles, resin-extractable phosphorus levels,
exchangeable aluminum, and soil pH. Among these, temperature and virus adsorption to
soil particles have emerged as primary areas of research focus [1]. Furthermore, studies
have demonstrated that the specific composition of soil ecosystems can create a robust
spatial structure that significantly restricts viral spread [6].
1.2
The Soil Virome
The soil virome is highly diverse, active, dynamic, demonstrating rapid adaptability to
changing environmental conditions. Viral-host interactions and the role of viruses in the
soil food web have become central topics in this field. The primary hosts of soil viruses in-
clude prokaryotes, such as bacteria, and eukaryotes, including vertebrates, invertebrates,
plants, protists, and fungi. Under varying physicochemical conditions, these organisms
form complex soil ecosystems, with viruses acting as key regulators of carbon, nitrogen,
and phosphorus cycling [7].

Certain bacteriophages, such as those infecting Actinobacteria, are significantly enriched
in low-moisture grassland soils [6].
Other viruses, including those infecting animals,
plants, and fungi, persist across diverse soil environments, influenced by distinct eco-
logical factors. Notably, environmental conditions have a greater impact on the abun-
dance and diversity of non-animal viruses than on animal viruses [8]. Recent studies
have identified bacteriophages, such as Leviviricetes, and plant-infecting viruses, such
as Ourmiavirus, as dominant viral groups in specific soil types [9].
The soil virome consists of both DNA and RNA viruses, with RNA viruses only recently
becoming a major research focus. Within the kingdom Orthornavirae, which includes all
RNA viruses containing the RdRp gene, the RNA virome can be categorized into five phyla
based on evolutionary relationships: Lenarviricota, Pisuviricota, Kitrinoviricota, Duplor-
naviricota, and Negarnaviricota [10]. Lenarviricota comprises mostly positive-stranded,
capsid-free viruses that primarily infect eukaryotes, including invertebrates, fungi, plants,
and protists. Interestingly, a class within this phylum, Leviviricetes, was recently found
to infect prokaryotic bacteria [11]. Kitrinoviricota includes exclusively positive-stranded
viruses, with eukaryotic organisms serving as hosts. Pisuviricota is predominantly com-
posed of double-stranded viruses, although some members are positive-stranded. These
viruses mainly infect eukaryotes, with no confirmed evidence of bacterial hosts. Duplor-
naviricota viruses are all double-stranded and infect eukaryotic hosts. Negarnaviricota
consists of negative-stranded viruses that are closely associated with arthropods, with a
few also capable of infecting vertebrates.
1.3
RNA Virome Analysis
RNA virome allows to study viral diversity and evolution of RNA viruses in different ecosys-
tem including soils. Bioinformatics tools such as SPAdes [12] and MEGAHIT [13] can be
used for de-novo assembling of viral contigs. Theses contigs can be further subjected to
tools such as VirFinder [14] or Virsorter2 [15] to identify viral reads. Also widely used tools
like BLAST(Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) [16] and HMMER [17] can be employed to
annotate viral regions. Currently, the primary approach to identifying RNA viral genomes
relies on detecting the presence of the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) gene
1.1, an essential enzyme required for genome replication and transcription [18]. While
RdRp gene sequences and structures vary among viral phyla, they exhibit notable simi-
larities within the same phylum [10]. By comparing RdRp sequences from obtained con-
tigs with those of known RNA viruses in currently established databases—such as NCBI,
PalmScan, and NeoRdRp—researchers can infer key information, including viral taxon-
omy and potential host associations. This method has significantly expanded the global
RNA virome, facilitating the identification of diverse RdRp sequences. Beyond soil RNA
viruses, this approach has successfully uncovered viruses from various environments.For
instance, novel viral species within Kitrinoviricota were discovered in the intestinal envi-
ronment of Red-Back [19], and the known RNA virus diversity in aquatic systems was
nearly doubled [20]. On a global scale, the number of identified bacteriophages has also
increased through similar pipelines [21].

1.4
Phylogenetic Analysis
Phylogenetic trees are fundamental tools in evolutionary biology, offering a visual frame-
work for depicting evolutionary relationships among organisms based on genetic, morpho-
logical, or molecular data. These trees trace species lineages, illustrating their divergence
from common ancestors over time. By analyzing phylogenetic trees, scientists can un-
cover evolutionary patterns, systematically classify organisms, and identify key processes
such as gene duplication, horizontal gene transfer, and speciation. In microbiology and
virology, they are instrumental in tracking pathogen evolution, shedding light on viral mu-
tations, antibiotic resistance, and disease outbreaks. Additionally, phylogenetic trees are
widely used in metagenomics to classify novel microbial species and assess ecological
diversity. As sequencing technologies advance, these trees remain indispensable for un-
derstanding biodiversity, reconstructing evolutionary history, and informing conservation
efforts in the face of rapid environmental change.
Phylogenetic tree construction is a crucial step in RNA virome research, providing in-
sights into the evolutionary relationships, diversity, and classification of viral communities
in environmental samples. The workflow typically begins with metagenomic sequencing,
followed by quality control, assembly, and viral identification using tools like VirSorter2
[15], VirFinder [14], or HMM-based searches [17]. Identified viral contigs encoding RNA-
dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) or other hallmark genes are then aligned using mul-
tiple sequence alignment (MSA) tools such as MAFFT [23] or MUSCLE [24]. Next, phylo-
genetic trees are constructed using Maximum Likelihood (ML) or Bayesian Inference (BI)
models in programs like IQ-TREE [25], RAxML [26], or FastTree [27]. The choice of an
evolutionary model (e.g., GTR, LG+Γ) influences tree topology, impacting taxonomic clas-
sification and viral diversity assessments. The significance of phylogenetic tree analysis
in RNA virome studies lies in its ability to trace viral evolution, identify novel viral lineages,
and infer potential host-virus interactions. By integrating phylogenetic approaches with
community ecology metrics, researchers can better understand how environmental fac-
tors shape RNA virus diversity, particularly in ecosystems like soil viromes, where viral-
host dynamics are still poorly characterized.
For phylogenetic inferences in this study, known protein sequences containing the RdRp
gene from previous studies—including those by Hillary et al. [9], Wolf et al. [10], and
Starr et al. [28]—were integrated with local viral contigs to construct phylogenetic trees.
Vertebrate and plant viruses, such as Kitrinoviricota and Pisuviricota, are expected to be
found in grassland soil, consistent with findings from previous studies under similar con-
ditions [9]. The analysis is also expected to reveal novel RdRp sequences within specific
phyla. Identified RdRp sequences assigned to specific phyla but lacking classification at

1.5
Hypothesis and Objectives
This study aims to deepen our understanding of the soil RNA virome and investigate
how viral communities vary across different soil types. By conducting phylogenetic and
community analyses of identified viral contigs from three distinct soil environments, this
research provides valuable insights into viral diversity and ecosystem interactions. These
soil types exhibit variations in plant cover, water content, pH, and soil organic matter
(SOM), creating a diverse framework to assess how environmental factors shape viral
community composition.
Key expectations and observations include:
• Agricultural soil is predicted to contain the fewest viral segments, likely due to agri-
cultural practices that reduce soil biodiversity through land management, pesticide
use, and monoculture cropping.
• Forest soil is expected to support moderate viral diversity, reflecting the intermedi-
ate complexity of its microbial and nutrient dynamics.
• Grassland soil is anticipated to harbor the highest viral abundance, as suggested
by previous studies [9], due to its relatively undisturbed ecosystem and higher mi-
crobial richness.
By analyzing how viral communities differ across these environments, this study con-
tributes to a broader understanding of RNA virus ecology, host-virus interactions, and the
role of environmental factors in shaping virome structure.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1
Sample Collection and Library Preparatioin
A total of 21 soil samples containing soil viromes were collected from four sites in Den-
mark: Boserup, Risø, Foulum, and Højbakkegård. Eight samples were taken from forest
soil in Boserup, characterized by beech and pine cover. Four samples originated from
grassland soil in Risø, which is predominantly covered by grass. Nine samples were
collected from agricultural soils in Foulum and Højbakkegård, both cultivated with winter
wheat. For each soil sample, basic characteristics—including water content, pH, and soil
organic matter (SOM)—were recorded and visualized using R [29].
During the sampling process and virus-like particle extraction, total RNA was isolated us-
ing the NucleoBond RNA Soil Mini Kit(Macherey-Nagel, Düren Germany) according to the
manufacturer’s instructions. Within a few days, DNase treatment was completed using the
DNase Max Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) as per the provided protocol. Complementary
DNA (cDNA) synthesis was then performed using the NEBNext Ultra II Directional RNA
library Prep Kit (New England Biolab), adhering to the recommended procedure. Initial
cDNA fragmentation was conducted at 94°C for 7 minutes, followed by 18 cycles of PCR
amplification. In total, 21 cDNA libraries containing paired-end raw reads of 151 bp were
prepared, pooled, and sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq 500 platform.
2.1.1
Illumina Sequencing
During library preparation, the Illumina NextSeq 500 was employed as a high-throughput
sequencing platform for gene data analysis. It utilizes sequencing-by-synthesis (SBS)
technology, which generates raw read data by capturing emitted fluorescent signals as
each base is incorporated into the growing DNA strand. The system employs a two-
channel detection method, where two fluorescent colors represent the four DNA bases:
red for adenine (A), green for cytosine (C), no signal for thymine (T), and a combination of
both colors for guanine (G). Each base is assigned a quality score during sequencing. Af-
ter capturing the fluorescent signal images, FASTQ files containing sequence identifiers,
paired-end nucleotide sequences (150 bp in length), and base-specific quality scores
(Phred scores) were retrieved as raw read files from the Illumina NextSeq 500.
2.2
Data Preprocessing
Initial quality control and adapters trimming of raw reads from each library were conducted
using FASTQC [30] and TrimGalore [31]( –paired -j 20 –basename trim –trim-n). Prior
to trimming, FASTQC outputs were integrated and visualized using MultiQC [32] to as-
sess read quality. Reads shorter than 35bp were discarded, and the remaining trimmed
reads were pooled into a single fasta file using bash command. To remove deduplicate
reads, clumpify.sh from BBtools package (v37.76: sourceforge.net/projects/bbmap/) was
applied with the parameters(dedupe subs=0 passes=2). Assembly was performed using
MEGAHIT (–k-min 21 –k-max 81 -1) [13], and only contigs exceeding 300bp in length
were retained for downstream analysis.
2.2.1
Quality Control
FASTQC was used for quality control of raw reads stored in FASTQ files, while MultiQC
aggregated all the reports generated by FASTQC. Figure A.2 presents one of the sum-
mary tables from MultiQC. The metric used to assess read quality is the Phred score,
which quantifies the probability of an incorrect base call during sequencing. The Phred

quality score is calculated as Q = −10 log10(P), where P represents the probability of an
error. For example, a base with a Phred score below 20 indicates a 1 in 100 chance of
being incorrect. Generally, bases with Phred scores below 20 are considered low quality
and are subsequently removed.
TrimGalore, a wrapper for Cutadapt [33] and FASTQC, was used to remove adapter se-
quences and low-quality bases from raw FASTQ reads. Adapter sequences, such as
AGATCGGAAGAGC, were recognized and removed by the tool. Additionally, bases with
an average Phred score below 20 (by default) were filtered out using a sliding window ap-
proach. After trimming, reads shorter than 20 bp were discarded before further analysis.
2.2.2
De Novo Assembly
The trimmed reads in FASTQ format were assembled into contigs in FASTA format using
MEGAHIT, an iterative de novo assembler that constructs contigs from high-quality raw
reads based on overlapping k-mers. MEGAHIT first builds a de Bruijn graph using short k-
merss, which capture more connections but may introduce false links in repetitive regions.
It then iteratively increases the k-mer size to refine the graph, resolving repeats while min-
imizing fragmentation in low-coverage regions. For this assembly, k-mer sizes ranging
from 21 to 81 were used, with input reads averaging 150 bp after trimming and deduplica-
tion. Although read lengths varied slightly post-processing, the majority remained around
150 bp, with only a small fraction reduced to 140 bp Figure A.3.
2.3
Identification of Viral Sequences
Viral contigs were identified using Palmscan [34]. The headers of resulting amino acid
contigs were extracted, and their corresponding nucleotides contigs were retrieved using
custom python scripts. Broad viral classification of each contigs was achieved by extract-
ing the phylum information from Palmscan output file, which was subsequently used for
community analysis.
Gene prediction was performed using Prodigal (-p meta -q) [35]. The presence of RdRp(RNA-
dependent RNA polymerase) [36] was detected using HMMER’s HMMSearch (–cpu 40 -E
1e-3) [37], with NeoRdRp [38] as the reference HMM database. Contigs matching RdRp
hits with E-values < 0.01 and scores > 50 were extracted and dereplicated using custom
R scripts, retaining only unique contigs with the highest E-value.
Taxonomy assignment for each contig was achieved by integrating data from InterPro-
scan-pfam-inter-pro annotations-description, combined with hmmsearchRdRp-scan-description-
of-target in NeoRdRp and Palmscan outputs. During this process, classification discrep-
ancies were anticipated, as NeoRdRp potentially identified broader RdRp hit regions com-
pared to Palmscan under the same filtering conditions, given that Palmscan detects only
a single RdRp hit per contig. Taxonomic information at the viral family level was also
retrieved from NeoRdRp. Four viral phyla were identified through taxonomy assignment
in this study: Negarnaviricota (negative-sense single-stranded RNA viruses), Kitrinoviri-
cota (positive-sense single-stranded RNA viruses), Lenarviricota, and Duplornaviricota
(double-stranded RNA viruses).
2.3.1
Viral Identification Tools

that utilize different approaches to detect and classify viral sequences 2.1.
VirFinder
and DeepVirFinder employ machine learning-based k-mer frequency analysis to distin-
guish viral from non-viral sequences, with DeepVirFinder using a deep learning frame-
work for improved accuracy [14, 39]. VirSorter2 applies Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)

and expert-curated reference databases to classify viral sequences based on genomic
features and sequence similarity [15]. VIBRANT integrates HMM-based functional an-
notation and neural networks to classify viral sequences and predict viral genome com-

maximization.

2.4
Community Analysis
Trimmed reads from each library were mapped to viral contigs using BBwarp.sh from the
BBtools package. The resulting SAM files of each library, were then converted and sorted
into BAM files using SAMtools [43] for subsequent extraction of coverage and FPKM val-
ues. Pileup.sh from the BBtools package was used to obtain coverage and FPFM files
for each sample. The analysis, incorporating sample metadata and TPM(Transcript per
kilobase million) alongside coverage and fpkm values, was conducted using various pack-
ages from Tidyverse R. Additionally, codes provided by Hillary.L [44] on GitHub, written
in Base R, was referenced for generating plots.
Prior to analysis, the coverage and FPKM output files for each sample were concatenated
into unified long-format coverage and FPKM dataframes, respectively. Taxonomy from
Palmscan and those from HMMSearch were then integrated into these dataframes based
on the query name of viral contigs. Only viral contigs with Covered Percent > 50% were
considered present in each library and this threshold would be applied in subsequent
analysis. Upset plots, illustrating viral abundance similarities across three soil types, were
generated using UpSetR package from Tidyverse R at coverage of 25%, 50%, 75%, and
90%, respectively. TMP values for each contig were calculated using the fpkm2tpm()
function from Hillary.L’s code. Basic statistical metrics of TPM values, including mean,
median, and percentage, along with taxonomy information, were then utilized to assess
relative abundance, alpha-diversity, and beta-diversity.
Alpha and beta diversities are commonly used in community analysis to assess diver-
sity within and between communities. In this study, three indices of alpha diversity were
applied for within-sample comparisons: Richness, Shannon, and Simpson indices. Rich-
ness measures the raw count of different species in a sample, while Shannon index con-
siders both species abundance and the evenness of their distribution. The Simpson index,
on the other hand, measures the probability that two randomly selected individuals belong
to the same species. For cross-sample comparisons, beta diversity was evaluated using
the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity index. To visualize differences in community composition be-
tween samples, a non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) plot was generated. In
addition to alpha and beta diversity, relative abundance is also considered. This metric
calculates the proportion of a particular species relative to the total species count within
a sample, facilitating comparisons of different species to the ecosystem.
In this study, the Kruskal-Wallis test, a a non-parametric statistical method, was applied as
a general measurement for comparing groups when there are more than three categories,
such as for each index of alpha diversity. When a statistically significant difference among
groups was detected(p-value < 0.05), Dunn’s test, a post hoc test, was performed to
identify specific between-group differences.

Phylogenetic Analysis
Phylogenetic trees of RdRp sequences were generated by aligning sequences using
MAFFT (–auto) [45] and constructing trees with FastTree [46]. The RdRp sequences
included a combination of those identified in this study along with sequences from Hillary
et al. [9], which also incorporated data from Starr et al. [38] and Wolf et al. [10]. Protein
contigs assigned to the same viral phylum based on previous annotations were pooled
together and concatenated with corresponding sequences from Hillary et al. [9]. The final
phylogenetic trees for each phylum were annotated and visually enhanced using iTOL
[47], with color-coding applied to improve clarity and facilitate interpretation.
2.5.1
Multiple Sequence Alignment
In this study, MAFFT was used for RdRp sequence alignment. MAFFT accelerates pair-
wise sequence comparisons by converting sequences into frequency spectra using Fast
Fourier Transform (FFT). Based on these similarity calculations, a guide tree is con-
structed to determine the order of progressive alignment, where closely related sequences
are aligned first, followed by more distant ones. To enhance accuracy, MAFFT provides
iterative refinement, realigning misaligned regions multiple times to improve consistency.
It also offers various alignment strategies, such as G-INS-i (global alignment with itera-
tive refinement) for closely related sequences and L-INS-i (local alignment for distantly
related sequences), allowing users to balance speed and precision. This combination of
FFT-based distance estimation, progressive alignment, and iterative refinement makes
MAFFT a highly efficient tool for large-scale multiple sequence alignment.
2.5.2
Approximately Maximum Likelihood Trees Construction
In this study, FastTree constructs phylogenetic trees using a maximum-likelihood (ML)
approach, applying the JTT+CAT model by default for protein alignments unless speci-
fied otherwise. The JTT (Jones-Taylor-Thornton) model defines amino acid substitution
rates based on empirical evolutionary data, while the CAT (Category) model accounts for
site-specific evolutionary rates, allowing different positions in the alignment to evolve at
different speeds.

FastTree begins by reading a multiple sequence alignment (MSA) and computing pair-
wise evolutionary distances using the JTT model. It then applies the CAT model to clas-
sify sites into discrete rate categories, enhancing accuracy compared to a uniform rate
assumption. The initial tree is constructed using a neighbor-joining (NJ) approach and
subsequently refined through maximum-likelihood optimization. This refinement involves
nearest-neighbor interchanges (NNI) and subtree-prune-regraft (SPR) moves to improve
tree topology. Branch lengths are assigned based on JTT substitution probabilities and
CAT site-specific rates, ensuring an efficient yet accurate representation of sequence evo-
lution.
To assess confidence in the tree topology, FastTree estimates local support values us-
ing the Shimodaira-Hasegawa (SH) test. Since JTT+CAT is the default model, running
FastTree on a protein alignment without specifying parameters automatically enables it.
However, for more precise rate variation modeling, the JTT+Gamma (Γ20) model can be
used with the -gamma flag. The JTT+CAT model, however, strikes a balance between
computational efficiency and accuracy, making it particularly well-suited for large-scale
phylogenetic and metagenomic studies.
• Heuristic Neighbor-Joining (NJ): FastTree first constructs an initial tree using the
neighbor-joining (NJ) method, clustering sequences based on pairwise distances
calculated from the BLOSUM45 [48] similarity matrix.
• Tree Refinement: The initial tree is optimized by minimizing its total length through
nearest-neighbor interchanges (NNIs) and subtree-prune-regraft (SPR) moves. By
default, FastTree performs 4×log2(N) rounds of NNIs and two rounds of SPR moves
to refine the topology.
• Distance Model: FastTree assigns branch lengths based on an evolutionary dis-
tance model. For protein sequences, it uses the BLOSUM45 similarity matrix, whereas
for nucleotide sequences, it applies either the Jukes-Cantor (JC) or the generalized
time-reversible (GTR) model.
• Likelihood Maximization: The tree topology is further refined by maximizing its like-
lihood. For nucleotide evolution, FastTree supports the JC or GTR models, while
for amino acid evolution, it employs the JTT (Jones-Taylor-Thornton) [49], WAG
(Whelan-Goldman) [50], or LG (Le-Gascuel) models [50].
• Local Support Values: To assess the reliability of each split in the tree, FastTree
estimates local support values using the Shimodaira-Hasegawa (SH) test [51].
In addition to maximum-likelihood methods, other tree construction tools employ different
approaches, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. For example, PAUP [52]
uses Maximum Parsimony to identify the tree requiring the fewest evolutionary changes,
though it often yields only moderately accurate results. Meanwhile, MrBayes [53] utilizes
Bayesian inference by calculating probability distributions, gradually converging on highly
accurate phylogenetic trees.
2.5.3

were constructed using various methods 2.2. These methods differ in their approach, rely-
ing on distance matrices, likelihood calculations, or Bayesian probability models. Distance-
based methods cluster taxa by iteratively grouping sequences with the smallest evolu-
tionary distances. In contrast, maximum parsimony (MP) and maximum likelihood (ML)
methods take both pairwise distances and individual sequence positions into account. MP

Evolutionary models provide mathematical frameworks in phylogenetics to describe how
nucleotide or amino acid sequences change over time due to mutations, substitutions,
insertions, and deletions. These models are categorized into two types: nucleotide sub-
stitution models and amino acid substitution models 2.3.
For nucleotide substitution models, the Jukes-Cantor (JC69) model [54] is a simple and
commonly used method in nucleotide tree construction. It assumes equal base frequen-
cies (A, T, C, G) and uniform substitution rates across all sites, meaning that all nucleotide
changes occur with the same probability. However, JC69 is often too simplistic for real-
world evolutionary scenarios. The Kimura 2-Parameter (K2P) model [55] provides a more
realistic alternative by distinguishing between transition (A-G, C-T) and transversion (A-
C, A-T, G-C, G-T) rates, while still maintaining equal base frequencies. The Generalized
Time-Reversible (GTR) model [56] is the most flexible and widely used in ML and Bayesian
tree construction, as it accounts for both unequal base frequencies and distinct rates for
transitions and transversions.
For amino acid substitution models, the biochemical properties of amino acids must be
considered instead of base frequencies and transition/transversion rates. Some amino
acids are more likely to substitute for one another due to their similar biochemical proper-
ties. The Jones-Taylor-Thornton (JTT) model is widely used in protein phylogenetics, as
it reflects empirical observations of amino acid substitutions based on protein alignments.
The Whelan & Goldman (WAG) model, derived from a larger dataset, offers improved ac-
curacy over JTT. Additionally, rate variation across sites is a crucial factor in protein tree
construction. This variation is commonly modeled using a gamma distribution to better
reflect differences in evolutionary rates among amino acid positions.

DISCUSSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES
The viral communities in grassland, forest, and agricultural soils exhibited distinct diversity
patterns influenced by environmental conditions and host availability. Grassland soils
demonstrated the highest viral diversity, predominantly featuring Negarnaviricota, known
to infect both vertebrates and invertebrates. The unique and diverse viral community
in grassland soils was likely driven by favorable conditions such as high water content,
alkaline pH, and elevated soil organic matter (SOM). Forest soils showed moderate viral
diversity, with the highest number of viral contigs but no clear dominance of a specific
viral phylum. The variable soil conditions—including fluctuations in water content, pH,
and SOM—supported a broad yet heterogeneous viral community, suggesting dynamic
ecological interactions. In contrast, agricultural soils exhibited the lowest viral diversity
and the highest proportion of shared viral contigs, indicating a simpler and more uniform
viral community. Although Kitrinoviricota, which infects fungi, plants, and invertebrates,
occasionally dominated, the overall community composition lacked stability. The reduced
diversity in agricultural soils was likely influenced by low water content, acidic pH, and
decreased SOM, potentially exacerbated by agricultural practices. Overall, grassland
soils served as reservoirs for diverse viral communities, forest soils hosted broad yet
variable assemblages, and agricultural soils reflected the impact of environmental and
anthropogenic factors through reduced viral diversity.
The phylogenetic composition of viral contigs in this study presents a marked contrast
to previous findings by Hillary et al. Here, Negarnaviricota emerged as the most abun-
dant viral phylum, followed by Kitrinoviricota, Duplornaviricota, and Lenarviricota, with no
Pisuviricota contigs detected. This pattern diverges significantly from Hillary’s study [9],
where Pisuviricota dominated, followed by Kitrinoviricota, Duplornaviricota, and Lenarviri-
cota, with Negarnaviricota being the least represented. These discrepancies may reflect
differences in ecological conditions, host availability, or methodological approaches be-
tween the studies. The high abundance of Negarnaviricota in this study aligns with the
predominance of vertebrate and invertebrate hosts, particularly in grassland soils. Con-
versely, the dominance of Pisuviricota in Hillary’s study may indicate a distinct ecological
niche or host preference, potentially influenced by soil management practices, vegetation
types, or climate. This contrast underscores the significant role of environmental factors in
shaping soil viral communities and highlights the need for comparative studies to explore
the ecological drivers of viral diversity across diverse ecosystems.
A notable discrepancy was observed among the results of the UpSet plot, alpha diversity
indices, and relative abundance, underscoring the impact of sample size. Despite the
significant differences in Shannon and Simpson indices between grassland and agricul-
tural soils, the number of identified viral contigs in these soils was relatively similar (423
and 354, respectively), as shown in the UpSet plot Figure 3.3. Interestingly, forest soil
contained more identified viral contigs than grassland soil, yet the latter exhibited a sig-
nificantly higher Shannon index, which accounts for both abundance and evenness. This
discrepancy can be attributed to the difference in sample sizes—forest and agricultural
soil libraries were nearly twice as numerous as grassland soil libraries. While a larger
number of libraries increases the total number of detectable viruses, the diversity of viral
community composition within each individual library remains unaffected.
In previous study [9], normalized read abundance was often reported using the term CPM
(Counts Per Million). However, a closer examination of the underlying calculation meth-

ods indicates that the reported values align more closely with TPM (Transcripts Per Mil-

advantage of accounting for contig length, making it particularly effective for comparing
gene expression or viral abundance across samples. In this study, TPM values were
specifically chosen to provide a more accurate representation of viral contig abundance.
This approach enabled a standardized comparison of viral communities by balancing se-
quencing depth and contig length, thereby enhancing the robustness and reliability of data
interpretation.
This study has several limitations that may affect the interpretation of viral community
composition. First, viral contig identification relied exclusively on PalmScan, which, while
effective, may not capture the full spectrum of viral diversity. The use of a single tool
could lead to the underrepresentation of certain viral groups, particularly those with atyp-
ical genome structures or limited representation in the PalmScan database. Addition-
ally, inconsistencies were observed between taxonomic annotations from PalmScan and
HMMSearch, likely due to differences in the specific genomic regions targeted by each
method. To address these discrepancies, priority was given to HMMSearch results, as
this approach provided broader and more reliable coverage of the target regions. How-
ever, this decision may have introduced a bias toward viral families or phyla that are more
comprehensively represented in the HMMSearch database. Moreover, a notable num-
ber of RdRp contigs remained unclassified despite annotations from both PalmScan and
HMMSearch. These unclassified contigs warrant further investigation, as they may rep-
resent novel viral species or, conversely, false positives arising from the limitations of
current annotation methods. Future studies integrating multiple viral detection and anno-
tation tools could enhance the accuracy and completeness of viral identification, providing
a more comprehensive understanding of soil viromes.
The choice of bioinformatic methods in virome analysis significantly influences the detec-
tion, classification, and interpretation of viral sequences, shaping the overall conclusions
of the study. Different tools utilize distinct algorithms, sensitivity thresholds, and refer-
ence databases, leading to variations in the number and type of viruses identified. For
example, BLAST-based methods [16], which rely on pairwise sequence similarity, are
effective for identifying well-characterized viruses but may fail to detect highly divergent
viral sequences. In contrast, Hidden Markov Model (HMM)-based approaches, such as
HMMsearch and PalmScan, improve sensitivity by capturing conserved motifs across dis-
tantly related viruses, leading to a broader detection of novel viral taxa. Similarly, multiple
sequence alignment (MSA) tools, such as MAFFT or Clustal Omega, impact phylogenetic
reconstruction and evolutionary inferences, where different alignment strategies may al-
ter the placement of sequences in phylogenetic trees. Additionally, variations in assembly
methods (e.g., de novo assemblers like MEGAHIT vs. reference-based approaches) can
influence genome completeness and the accuracy of viral contig reconstruction. These
methodological differences highlight the importance of choosing appropriate computa-
tional tools based on research objectives, as biases introduced by different bioinformatic
pipelines can affect viral diversity estimates, taxonomy assignments, and ecological in-
terpretations in virome studies.
The choice of evolutionary models in phylogenetic tree construction significantly impacts
tree accuracy, topology, and biological interpretation.
These models account for nu-
cleotide or amino acid substitution rates, transition/transversion biases, and rate varia-
tion across sites, all of which influence inferred evolutionary relationships. Simpler mod-
els, such as Jukes-Cantor (JC69), assume equal substitution rates and base frequen-
cies, making them computationally efficient but often unrealistic for real biological se-

quences. In contrast, more complex models, such as General Time Reversible (GTR)
or WAG/LG for proteins, accommodate unequal substitution rates and site-specific rate
variation, producing more reliable trees at the cost of increased computational demand.
Additionally, incorporating gamma-distributed rate heterogeneity (+Γ) better captures evo-
lutionary constraints across genomic regions, affecting branch lengths and clustering pat-
terns. Using an inappropriate model can lead to long-branch attraction, misplacement of
clades, or biased divergence time estimates. Therefore, selecting an optimal evolutionary
model—typically determined through model testing tools such as ModelTest or IQ-TREE’s
ModelFinder—ensures that phylogenetic trees more accurately reflect evolutionary his-
tory, minimizing systematic errors in downstream analyses like viral classification, species
divergence studies, and ancestral trait reconstruction.

CONCLUSIONS
This study analyzes the soil RNA virome across three soil types: grassland, forest, and
agricultural soil. Grassland soil provides a stable, moist, alkaline, and organic-matter-
rich environment for RNA viruses, harboring the most abundant and evenly distributed
viral communities compared to the other soil types. The dominant viral group in this soil
type is Negarnaviricota, which primarily infects vertebrates and invertebrates, such as
arthropods. Other viral phyla, particularly those infecting plants, are present in lower
abundance in grassland soil. Forest soil contained the greatest number of viral contigs,
regardless of richness and abundance, due to its diverse environment with varying pH
levels, high overall moisture, and organic-matter-rich composition. Viruses in this soil type
are distributed across all phyla identified in this study, suggesting a wide range of potential
hosts and highlighting the ecological diversity of the forest soil virome. Agricultural soil
has become a focus of soil virome studies due to its distinct ecosystem. This soil type was
found to have the lowest viral richness and abundance. Its physicochemical conditions
were the driest, most acidic (low pH), and contained the least organic matter. The number
of unique viral contigs was the lowest among the three soil types, alongside the lowest
viral abundance and evenness. Viral hosts in this soil type vary, but Kitrinoviricota appears
to be relatively dominant based on its abundance, even though no single phylum strongly
drives the overall community composition.
Compared to a previous study [9] that similarly investigated the soil virome in grassland,
this study unexpectedly identified a higher relative abundance of Negarnaviricota, while
detecting fewer Lenarviricota and no Pisuviricota. This discrepancy is likely due to differ-
ences in viral identification methods and the presence of novel viral contigs. In this study,
Palmscan was the sole tool used to detect viral contigs, whereas Diamond BLASTx was
employed in the previous study. Although both studies used HmmSearch to identify RdRp
hits, they utilized different HMM databases. Here, the NeoRdRp database was used,
which is a more recent resource expected to provide more accurate and comprehen-
sive results compared to the Wolf database used in the previous study. Notably, during
the annotation process, multiple and occasionally conflicting phylum classifications were
observed for the same HMM seed name when using the NeoRdRp database. While po-
tential biases arising from clustering and annotation must be considered, the discovery of
novel viral families remains an important avenue for further research. RdRp sequences
assigned to a specific phylum but lacking classification at the family or lower taxonomic
levels are highly likely to represent new species within that phylum. Negarnaviricota ex-
hibited the highest number of viral contigs, suggesting that this phylum also harbors the
greatest potential for novel viruses. Further wet-lab investigations, including gene struc-
ture characterization and phylogenetic analysis, are necessary to accurately determine
their evolutionary placement.
Future work in viral identification and phylogenetic analysis will focus on integrating mul-
tiple detection methods and refining tree construction models for improved accuracy and
resolution. Combining alignment-based approaches (e.g., BLAST, HMMER) with ma-
chine learning classifiers (e.g., VirFinder, DeepVirFinder) and k-mer-based methods (e.g.,
VirSorter2) can enhance the sensitivity and specificity of viral detection, particularly for
highly divergent or novel viruses. Additionally, phylogenetic tree construction can be im-
proved by exploring Bayesian inference (BI) models, incorporating site-specific substitu-
tion rates, codon-aware models, and deep learning-assisted topology inference. Future

studies may also leverage long-read sequencing technologies to recover more complete
viral genomes, improving phylogenetic placement and evolutionary insights. Integrating
multiple substitution models (e.g., GTR, LG+CAT) and evaluating their impact on tree
topology will further refine viral classification and evolutionary inference in metagenomic
studies.
