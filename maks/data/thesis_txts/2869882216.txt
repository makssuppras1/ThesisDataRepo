Abstract
Inspired by the critical role of surveillance in military and law enforcement operations, this
thesis explores the development of a portable surveillance and monitoring device using
the Jetson Xavier NX platform. The project aims to develop a surveillance system that
is both resource­efficient and portable, while ensuring real­time feed and rapid response
capabilities.
The system integrates a high­resolution camera for real­time acquisition of audio and
video data. Using technologies like GStreamer and OpenCV libraries, the system achieves
seamless streaming to remote locations, also giving a high level of control to the remote
device(s). The device can detect and track objects based on color, and identify and track
humans using face recognition. The device can have a variety of use cases due to its
audio event detection and speech detection capabilities.
It addresses the challenges in data processing, focusing on minimizing latency and re­
sponse time during acquisition and transmission while maintaining high resolution and
frame rate.
Key contributions include real­time synchronization techniques, low­latency streaming us­
ing RTSP, and advanced data processing for dynamic audio­video control, color detection,
face recognition, and audio event and speech detection. The system’s modular design
ensures flexibility, scalability, and efficient resource utilization, making it suitable for vari­
ous surveillance applications.

Introduction
1.0.1
Problem Statement
Surveillance systems play a crucial role in high­stakes operations, such as military and
law enforcement activities, where real­time intelligence is critical for decision making.
However, these systems often face significant challenges, including high latency, lim­
ited portability, and difficulties in achieving real­time synchronization of audio and video
feeds. These limitations can hinder operational effectiveness, particularly in dynamic or
resource­constrained environments.
While existing surveillance solutions offer high­performance capabilities, they are fre­
quently resource­intensive, requiring substantial computational power and energy[2]. This
makes them less suitable for portable or real­time applications in remote or high­risk ar­
eas. Additionally, many existing systems struggle to meet the demands of seamless real­
time operation, particularly when faced with constraints such as bandwidth limitations or
power efficiency.
The challenges of maintaining effective surveillance systems during conflicts have been
a subject of ongoing discussion among defense and security experts[2]. Recent dis­
course around surveillance practices during conflicts highlights a significant issue: the
sustainability of valuable, long­term surveillance operations. These operations often de­
mand substantial financial investment and a consistent allocation of assets, making them
resource­intensive and difficult to scale without careful planning.
One proposed solution involves rethinking traditional approaches to surveillance by par­
tially outsourcing tasks to allied or commercial platforms. This involves deploying small
teams of specially trained personnel equipped with both military and commercial­grade
sensors on existing allied or commercial vessels[2]. Such an approach aims to leverage
already­available platforms to enhance surveillance capabilities without the need to build
new, expensive, dedicated systems.
Furthermore, this model of outsourcing and integration is viewed as scalable, enabling
it to adapt to different operational demands across the globe. By leveraging distributed
sensors and existing infrastructure, this approach offers the potential to expand global
surveillance capabilities while reducing the financial and logistical burdens traditionally
associated with such operations. However, the high cost of equipping allied platforms and
training specialized teams, combined with the need for global coordination, underlines the
resource­intensive nature of even these innovative solutions[2].
1.0.2
Objective
These discussions underscore a critical point: while advanced surveillance systems are
indispensable in modern conflicts, finding ways to make them more resource­efficient
and sustainable remains a pressing challenge. Balancing capability with affordability and
scalability is central to shaping the future of military and civil surveillance strategies.
To address these challenges, this project explores the development of a modular and
resource­efficient surveillance system optimized for real­time performance. Built on the
Nvidia Jetson Xavier NX platform, the system integrates advanced audio and video pro­
cessing techniques to deliver low­latency streaming while maintaining portability.

2
Literature Review
In modern surveillance systems, the efficient transmission and storage of audio and video
data play a pivotal role in ensuring real­time performance and scalability[3]. Given the
substantial volume of data generated by high­resolution cameras and microphones, en­
coding techniques are essential to compress this data while preserving its quality. These
techniques not only optimize the use of bandwidth but also reduce storage requirements,
enabling the deployment of portable and resource­efficient systems.
Audio and video encoding methods, such as compression standards and codecs, are de­
signed to strike a balance between data size, quality, and processing complexity. This
chapter explores the principles and techniques behind these encoding methods, highlight­
ing their relevance in real­time surveillance applications. By understanding these foun­
dational technologies, we can better appreciate the role they play in the overall efficiency
and performance of advanced surveillance systems.
Both types of inputs, audio and video, can share some similarities in encoding and com­
pression, although they may look like different types of inputs.
2.1
Video Encoding and Compression
A camera achieves video capture by intaking light and converting different intensities of
light into different electrical signals proportional to the relative intensity of light. Hence,
after the reception in to an electrical system, a video data is essentially a collection of dif­
ferent levels of voltage that is making a frame (picture). Video encoding and compression
can be understood exactly like the name itself, as two sub­parts of the procedure, encod­
ing and compression. But these two parts feed into each other to form one algorithm. A
general understanding can be taken from the flow diagram 2.1[3].

2.1.1
Motion Compensation
A video can be represented as a sequence of images organized along the temporal di­
mension. Due to the typically high frame rate of videos (e.g., exceeding 15 frames per
second) and the relatively stable camera parameters, such as focal length, position, and
viewing angle, the content of consecutive frames remains largely similar. Significant vari­
ations between frames occur only in cases of rapid object motion or substantial scene
changes. This consistency introduces temporal redundancy within the video data.
Hence, Instead of encoding every frame independently as a new image, only the differ­
ences between the current frame and other frames in the sequence are encoded. When
the redundancy between frames is substantial, these difference frames typically contain
smaller values and lower entropy, which enhances compression efficiency.
Modern video compression standards, such as H.264, H.265, and later standards, imple­
ment a hybrid coding approach that combines techniques for predicting and compensat­
ing for differences between video frames to eliminate temporal redundancy, followed by
transform coding to process the residual errors.
A simple method of generating these difference frames involves pixel­by­pixel subtrac­
tion between frames, but this is generally ineffective for achieving high compression ra­
tios. Since the primary cause of frame differences is camera or object motion, these
changes are addressed using motion compensation (MC). MC­based algorithms detect
and account for the displacement of corresponding pixels or regions between frames, sig­
nificantly improving compression performance. The process typically involves three key
steps:
1. Motion estimation (motion vector search)
2. Motion­compensation­based prediction
3. Derivation of the prediction error—the difference.

prediction is derived [4].

Each image is divided into macroblocks of size N X N. By default, N = 16 for luminance
images, while N=8 is used for chrominance images when 4:2:0 chroma subsampling is
applied.
In video compression, the current frame being processed is referred to as the target
frame, and the goal is to find the macroblock in the target frame that matches most closely
with a corresponding macroblock from one or more reference frames (previous or future
frames). This process predicts the target macroblock using the best­matching reference
macroblock. The displacement between the target and reference macroblocks is defined
as a motion vector (MV). When the reference frame is a previous frame, the process
is called forward prediction, and when it is a future frame, it is termed backward predic­
tion. The difference between the target and reference macroblocks is refered to as the
prediction error.
In motion­compensation­based video compression, once the first frame is encoded, only
the motion vectors and the difference macroblocks need to be transmitted. This infor­
mation is sufficient for the decoder to reconstruct all subsequent frames by applying the
motion vectors and prediction errors to the reference frames.
Further details on this process, such as the algorithms used to search for motion vectors,
will be explored in subsequent sections.
2.1.2
Motion Vector Search
The process of determining motion vectors (MV )(u, v) involves solving a matching prob­
lem, also referred to as a correspondence problem. Due to the computational intensity of
this process, the search is typically restricted to a small neighborhood around the target
macroblock. The horizontal and vertical displacements i and j are constrained within a
range of [−p, p], where p is a small positive integer. This results in a search window with
dimensions (2p + 1)×(2p + 1). Within this window, the center of the macroblock in the
target frame, located at (x0, y0), is compared to grid positions in the reference frame.
For simplicity, the upper­left corner (x, y) of the macroblock in the target frame is used
as the origin. The pixel values in the macroblock from the target frame are denoted as
C(x + k, y + l) while the corresponding pixels in the reference frame macroblock are
represented (x + i + k, y + j + l) where k and l index the pixels within the macroblock,
and i and j represent the horizontal and vertical displacements.

2.1.3
Encoding Process
Earlier encoding and compression standards employed motion­compensation­based com­
pression, which remains foundational for modern standards. The encoding process usu­
ally involved three types of frames [4]:
1. Intra­frames (I­frames): These are encoded independently using techniques sim­
ilar to JPEG, leveraging spatial redundancy within the frame.
2. Inter­frames (P­frames): These rely on motion compensation, where current mac­
roblocks are predicted based on macroblocks from previous frames, reducing tem­
poral redundancy. Only the motion vectors and prediction errors are encoded.
3. PB­frames: A combined P­frame and B­frame structure that improves efficiency by
allowing bidirectional prediction within a single frame.
For compression, frames were divided into macroblocks (16 × 16 for luminance and 8 ×
8 for chrominance with 4:2:0 subsampling). Each macroblock undergoes:
1. Transform Coding: A Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) converts spatial data into
frequency components.
2. Quantization: Frequency coefficients are quantized to reduce data size.
3. Entropy Coding: The coefficients are compressed further using variable­length
codes.
2.1.4
Compression Efficiency
These standards achieved significant compression by exploiting both spatial and temporal
redundancies. The use of motion vectors and differential encoding for P­frames reduces
the need to encode entire frames. Additionally, these designs supported bitrates as low
as 64 kbps while maintaining acceptable quality for real­time communication.

2.2
H.264
H.264, also known as Advanced Video Coding (AVC), selected for this project, is a highly
efficient video compression standard that significantly improves upon earlier formats like
H.261 and H.263 [6]. Designed for low­latency, high­quality video transmission, H.264
optimizes both spatial and temporal compression while maintaining adaptability across
various applications. This makes it particularly well­suited for real­time surveillance and
monitoring systems, where minimizing latency and maintaining video clarity are critical.
Main Features of H.264/AVC that help increase the precision are:
1. 4×4 Integer Transform – A low­complexity transform applied to small blocks (4×4),
reducing computational overhead while preventing drift errors.
2. Flexible Block Sizes for Motion Compensation – Motion estimation supports vari­
able block sizes, ranging from 16×16 down to 4×4 in luminance images, improving
adaptability to different motion patterns.
3. Quarter­Pixel Motion Estimation – Motion vectors achieve quarter­pixel accuracy
through interpolation, enhancing precision in motion prediction.
4. Advanced Motion Compensation with Multiple Reference Frames – Instead of rely­
ing solely on P­frames and B­frames, H.264 allows multiple reference pictures for
motion estimation, improving efficiency.
5. Directional Spatial Prediction for Intra­Frames – Intra­frame compression is en­
hanced by directional prediction, utilizing neighboring pixels to achieve better spatial
redundancy reduction.

6. In­Loop Deblocking Filter – A built­in deblocking filter reduces blocking artifacts,
improving visual quality, especially at lower bitrates.
7. Advanced Entropy Coding – Implements Context­Adaptive Variable Length Coding
(CAVLC) and Context­Adaptive Binary Arithmetic Coding (CABAC) for more efficient
compression based on statistical context.
8. Improved Error Resilience and Stream Adaptability – H.264 offers greater robust­

H.264/AVC achieves significantly higher video coding efficiency, offering up to 50% better
compression compared to MPEG­2 and 30% greater efficiency than H.263+ and MPEG­4
Advanced Simple Profile, all while preserving the same level of video quality [4].
2.2.1
Encoding Process
H.264 follows a hybrid video coding approach, which balances computational efficiency
with high compression performance. The core encoding steps relevant to our implemen­
tation include:
Intra­Frame (I­frame) Encoding
Each frame is independently compressed using a block­based transform (4×4 integer
transform) to eliminate spatial redundancy. This ensures that every keyframe maintains
a baseline quality, which is important for stability in real­time streaming.
Inter­Frame (P­frame and B­frame) Encoding
Motion estimation is performed using quarter­pixel precision, allowing for more accurate
predictions of moving objects. P­frames store only changes from previous frames, reduc­
ing the bitrate significantly. B­frames (bidirectional prediction) are disabled in our setup
(bframes=0) to prioritize low­latency performance over compression efficiency
Transform and Quantization
H.264 replaces the traditional 8×8 DCT with a more efficient 4×4 integer transform, which
reduces computational overhead and prevents drift errors. Adaptive quantization dynam­
ically adjusts compression levels based on content complexity.
Entropy Coding
Instead of standard Variable Length Coding (VLC), H.264 supports Context­Adaptive Vari­
able Length Coding (CAVLC) and Context­Adaptive Binary Arithmetic Coding (CABAC),
both of which enhance compression efficiency.

2.2.2
Group of Pictures (GOP)
H.264 supports the traditional Group of Pictures (GOP) structure, where a sequence be­
gins and ends with I­frames, with P­frames and B­frames in between. In this setup, P­
frames are predicted from previous frames (forward prediction), while B­frames utilize both
forward and backward prediction, increasing compression efficiency. However, H.264
also introduces additional GOP configurations to optimize performance for different use
cases.
GOP Without B­Frames
In scenarios where low latency is critical, B­frames can be omitted, leaving only I­ and
P­frames. This reduces compression efficiency but minimizes delay and memory usage,
making it ideal for applications like videoconferencing or real­time surveillance, which
align with H.264’s Baseline and Constrained Baseline Profiles.
Multiple Reference Frames
Unlike previous standards, H.264 allows multiple reference frames (N frames) for motion
estimation, improving compression by selecting the best match for macroblocks. While
this approach enhances efficiency, it increases computational complexity and buffer re­
quirements at both the encoder and decoder.
Hierarchical Prediction Structure
H.264 introduces layered hierarchical prediction, where frames are predicted in multiple
levels rather than sequentially. A higher­level B­frame (e.g., B6) is predicted first using
reference I­frames, followed by lower­level frames referencing it. This structure, combined
with adaptive quantization across layers, improves compression efficiency compared to

2.2.3
In­Loop Deblocking Filtering
Block­based compression often introduces visible artifacts at block boundaries due to
less accurate pixel reconstruction. To mitigate this, H.264 applies an adaptive in­loop
deblocking filter on 4×4 block edges, dynamically adjusting filter strength and type based
on:
1. Macroblock type (intra/inter­frame).
2. Reference frame differences and coded coefficients.
3. Spatial characteristics (edge detection to preserve details)
This filtering occurs within the encoding loop, before reference frames are used for pre­
diction, ensuring smoother, artifact­free video. By reducing blockiness without increasing
bitrate, the deblocking filter enhances visual quality, making it particularly useful in real­
time video applications.
2.2.4
H.264 Decoder
The H.264 decoder consists of five key functional blocks, each contributing to the accurate
reconstruction of compressed video:
1. Entropy Decoding – Extracts compressed data using Context­Adaptive Variable
Length Coding (CAVLC) or Context­Adaptive Binary Arithmetic Coding (CABAC).
2. Inverse Quantization and Transform – Reconstructs residual pixel data by reversing
the quantization and integer transform applied during encoding.
3. Motion Compensation or Intra Prediction – Recreates missing details using motion
vectors (for inter­frame prediction) or spatial reference data (for intra­frame predic­
tion).
4. Reconstruction – Combines the residual data with predicted blocks to reconstruct
the video frame.
5. In­Loop Deblocking Filter – Applies adaptive filtering to smooth block boundaries,
eliminating compression artifacts before storing reference frames.
This structured decoding process ensures efficient video reconstruction, preserving qual­
ity while maintaining compression efficiency.
2.2.5
Why not H.265?
H.265 is definitely a step up from H.264 but it was not used because of a few reasons:
1. H.264 is native to GStreamer and has stronger support across plugins compared to
H.265 [7].
2. Many of GStreamer’s hardware­accelerated encoders and decoders are optimized
for H.264 first.
3. H.265 support exists (via x265enc), but not all GStreamer builds include it by default.
4. H.264 is more universally supported across different platforms (Windows, Linux,
Mac, embedded devices).
5. While H.265 is widely adopted in modern streaming, older or lightweight devices
may not support it well without extra software [8].
These are the reasons to choose H.264 over any other later standards.

2.3
Audio Encoding and Compression
Sound, much like color, is perceived as a combination of physical and psychological fac­
tors. Physically, it is a disturbance in a medium that propagates as a pressure wave,
caused by the movement of atoms or molecules. This can be represented as a func­
tion s(t), where the pressure at any given time t defines the sound wave, similar to how
electronic signals are measured.
To store and process sound digitally, it must be converted into a numerical representation,
which involves sampling and quantization. This process forms the foundation of audio
encoding and compression, where data size is reduced while maintaining quality.
The human ear is typically sensitive to frequencies ranging from 20 Hz to 20 kHz, though
this upper limit decreases with age. While children may hear frequencies close to 20 kHz,
most adults have a reduced hearing range.
A key aspect of sound perception is timbre, which distinguishes different instruments and
voices. Interestingly, the attack phase of a note—its transient behavior—is crucial in iden­
tifying an instrument. If the initial portion of a note is removed, sounds from different
instruments producing the same pitch may become indistinguishable.
Sound waves can be graphically represented in two main ways:
1. Time­Domain Representation – A plot of amplitude (pressure) over time, showing
the waveform’s shape and characteristics.
2. Frequency­Domain Representation – A frequency spectrum diagram, where ampli­
tude is plotted against frequency, highlighting how different frequencies contribute
to the overall sound.
2.3.1

2.3.4
Pulse Code Modulation (PCM)
Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) is the most fundamental method of digitally encoding audio.
It serves as the baseline for most modern audio formats, including lossless and lossy
compression techniques, which will be discussed in later sections.
PCM converts continuous analog sound waves into a digital representation by sampling
the audio signal at fixed intervals and quantizing the amplitude values into discrete nu­
merical values. It is a raw, uncompressed format that retains the full detail of the original
sound. PCM stores every sampled value explicitly without any compression. All three au­
dio encoders (MP3, AAC and Opus) considered for this project has PCM as the baseline.

PCM or any other encoding technique requires understanding of two main factors: Sam­
pling rate and Bit Depth.
Sampling Rate
Sampling Rate determines how often the audio signal is sampled per second. Common
rates are 44.1 kHz (CD quality), 48 kHz (video standard), 96 kHz+ (high­resolution audio).
Higher sampling rates capture more details but increase file size.

2.3.5
Psychoacoustics
Psychoacoustics is the study of how humans perceive sound, focusing on the relation­
ship between physical sound waves and our auditory perception. It explains why certain
sounds are more noticeable than others and how we can compress audio efficiently with­
out significantly affecting perceived quality. [13]
Frequency Masking
A loud sound in one frequency range can mask (hide) quieter sounds at nearby frequen­
cies, making them inaudible to the human ear. A loud drum hit may make a faint back­
ground note imperceptible. [10]
Temporal Masking
A loud sound can mask quieter sounds that come immediately before or after it, even if
they are in a different frequency range. A gunshot or clap may momentarily ”deafen” a
person to softer sounds before and after it.
Hearing Range (20 Hz ­ 20k Hz)
Humans can only hear within a certain frequency range, with sensitivity peaking between
2 kHz and 5 kHz (speech range).
Perceptual Coding
Since the human ear does not detect all sound components equally, audio compression
techniques use psychoacoustic models to selectively remove less important data while
keeping essential audio features intact.
Psychoacoustics is important to understand before lossy compression techniques as it is
the baseline for them. Lossless and lossy compression techniques can be easily under­
stood by their names.
2.3.6
Lossy Compression
Lossy compression removes unnecessary and inaudible data to achieve significant size
reduction. It is based on psychoacoustic principles, which exploit human hearing limita­
tions. All the audio encoders considered for this project are lossy. Lossy compression
can drastically reduce file size and is optimized for different applications. However, it can
cause some loss of quality and irreversible compression.
2.3.7
Lossless Compression
Lossless compression reduces file size without losing any audio data. The original wave­
form can be fully reconstructed upon decompression. It provides perfect sound quality
and efficient compression but will have large file sizes compared to lossy techniques.

2.4
MP3 (MPEG ­ Audio Layer 3)
MP3, or MPEG­1 Audio Layer 3, is a lossy audio compression format that became the de
facto standard for digital audio storage and transmission. It was developed by the Moving
Picture Experts Group (MPEG) and introduced in the early 1990s as part of the MPEG­1
standard, later refined under MPEG­2.
The name ”Audio Layer 3” refers to its position within the MPEG­1 audio hierarchy, which
consists of three layers of increasing complexity and efficiency:
1. Layer 1 – The simplest form, used in early digital audio applications
2. Layer 2 – More efficient than Layer 1, still used in broadcasting.
3. Layer 3 (MP3) – The most advanced and efficient of the three, using psychoacoustic
modeling, transform coding, and entropy coding to achieve high compression ratios
while maintaining perceptual audio quality.
MP3’s primary goal is to remove redundant or inaudible information from audio signals
based on human hearing characteristics while retaining an experience that sounds nearly
identical to the original. [15]
2.4.1
Encoding Process
MP3 builds upon the fundamental audio encoding and compression techniques discussed
earlier. It starts with PCM audio data and applies a series of transformations and com­
pression techniques to reduce file size while maintaining perceptual quality.
Transform Coding
Before compression begins, MP3 converts the raw PCM (Pulse Code Modulation) audio
signal from the time domain into the frequency domain. This transformation is crucial be­
16
Master Thesis
==PAGE:24==
cause human hearing perceives audio in terms of frequency bands, not individual samples
in time.
MP3 achieves this transformation using the Modified Discrete Cosine Transform (MDCT).
The MDCT divides the signal into 576 frequency subbands using polyphase filter banks,
allowing the encoder to analyze which frequencies are most important. This step en­
ables more efficient compression, as only the most critical frequency components are
preserved.
Psychoacoustic Modeling
MP3 uses psychoacoustic compression techniques to determine which parts of the sig­
nal can be removed without a noticeable effect as shortly explained above. By removing
inaudible or less perceptible frequencies, MP3 significantly reduces data size without af­
fecting perceived quality.
Bit Allocation and Quantization
After psychoacoustic modeling determines what can be removed, the remaining audible
frequency components are encoded with varying levels of precision:
1. More bits are allocated to critical sounds (like speech and vocals).
2. Fewer bits are allocated to less important sounds (like quiet background noise).
3. Quantization introduces controlled rounding errors, further reducing the amount of
data needed to represent each sample.
Hence, the most degree of compression happens during this step. Lower bitrates (e.g.,
128 kbps) apply more aggressive quantization, which can introduce compression artifacts.
Higher bitrates (320 kbps) allocate more bits to critical sounds, preserving more detail.
Entropy Coding
To further reduce file size, MP3 applies Huffman coding, a form of lossless data com­
pression. Huffman coding replaces frequently occurring bit patterns with shorter codes,
making the final MP3 file even smaller. A sum up of MP3 codec can be understood by
considering Figure 2.10. [15]
Figure 2.10: MP3 Encoder
2.4.2
MP3 and Real­time Applications
MP3 was originally designed for storage and playback, not real­time streaming.
The
higher latency in MP3 encoding and decoding comes from several factors related to its
compression algorithm.

==PAGE:25==
MP3 processes audio in large frames of 576 or 1152 samples, depending on the bitrate
and encoding mode. This means the encoder buffers at least 1152 samples before starting
the compression process and the decoder must wait until an entire frame is received
before it can start playback.
Hence, as MP3 was designed for efficient storage, not for minimal delay, It does not
have a dedicated low­latency mode or adaptive bit control. MP3 uses fixed bitrate (CBR)
or variable bitrate (VBR), but it does not dynamically adjust to network conditions. If
bandwidth drops, MP3 cannot lower its bitrate to maintain a smooth real­time stream.
And since, network conditions vary all the time in surveillance situations, MP3 happens
to not provide exactly what is required for real­time processing.
2.5
Advanced Audio Codec ­ AAC
AAC (Advanced Audio Codec) was developed as an improvement over MP3, aiming for
better sound quality at lower bitrates and lower latency for real­time applications. It was
standardized by the MPEG group as part of MPEG­2 (1997) and later refined in MPEG­4
(1999). [16]
While MP3 was a breakthrough in digital audio compression, it had several limitations:
1. MP3 struggled with low and high­frequency accuracy.
2. MP3’s fixed block sizes made it less adaptable to different types of audio.
3. MP3 had large frame sizes (1152 samples), increasing the delay for real­time appli­
cations.
2.5.1
Improvements in AAC
1. Improved Frequency Handling: AAC uses MDCT with adaptive block switching,
leading to better transient response and high­frequency accuracy.
2. Higher Compression Efficiency: Achieves the same or better audio quality at 30%
lower bitrates than MP3 due to smarter bit allocation and refined psychoacoustic
modeling.
3. Flexible Block Sizes: Uses short blocks (128 samples) for transients and long
blocks (1024 samples) for sustained sounds, eliminating pre­echo and preserving
details better than MP3’s fixed 576/1152 sample blocks.
4. Lower Latency for Real­Time Applications: AAC allows smaller frame sizes (512
or 960 samples), reducing delay compared to MP3, which has large frame sizes
(1152 samples). AAC­LD and AAC­ELD further optimize for low­latency applications
like VoIP and live streaming. Details of AAC­LD or AAC­ELD will not be included in
this report.
5. Wider Frequency Range: Supports up to 96 kHz, compared to MP3’s limit of 16
kHz, resulting in better high­frequency reproduction.
6. More Efficient Entropy Coding: Replaces MP3’s Huffman coding with CAVLC and
CABAC, improving compression efficiency.
2.5.2
AAC and Real­time Applications
AAC was developed as an improvement over MP3, addressing many of its limitations,
including latency. However, while AAC achieves better compression efficiency and sound
quality at lower bitrates, its default encoding modes still introduce noticeable delays in
real­time applications.

2.6
Opus
Opus is a highly flexible, low­latency audio codec designed specifically for real­time com­
munication, making it an ideal choice for applications like live streaming, VoIP, surveil­
lance, and interactive media. Unlike MP3 and AAC, which were primarily developed for
storage and playback, Opus was built from the ground up to handle real­time audio trans­
mission while maintaining high quality and low bandwidth usage. [18]
2.6.1
Hybrid Architecture for Maximum Efficiency
Unlike traditional codecs that use a single compression model, Opus combines two dif­
ferent encoding techniques:
1. SILK (used in Skype) – Optimized for speech, providing high­quality compression
at low bitrates.
2. CELT (Constrained Energy Lapped Transform) – Optimized for music and com­
plex signals, offering low­delay high­fidelity encoding.
Opus dynamically switches between SILK and CELT or combines them, ensuring the
best trade­off between quality and latency depending on the content. Hence, Opus can
handle both speech and full­bandwidth music efficiently, whereas MP3 and AAC struggle
to balance the two.

2.6.2
Differences in Encoding and Compression Techniques
Opus uses the same encoding and compression techniques laid down in earlier explained
codecs. The things Opus does differently are explained below. [18]
Sampling & Quantization
Opus supports sampling rates from 8 kHz to 48 kHz, covering everything from narrow­
band speech to full­range music. Uses floating­point arithmetic, reducing quantization
noise without increasing file size. Unlike MP3/AAC, which have fixed bit depths, Opus
adapts dynamically to different signal conditions making it a great choice for real­time ap­
plications.
Psychoacoustic Modeling & Perceptual Compression
It uses more advanced psychoacoustic masking than MP3 and AAC, improving efficiency
at lower bitrates. And it has custom­tailored masking models for speech vs. music, rather
than a single one­size­fits­all approach. The details of these will not be included in the
literature review but the mention is enough to prove why Opus is better for real­time ap­
plications. If some detail is deemed necessary in later sections, it will be included.
Transform Coding (MDCT vs. LP)
MP3 & AAC only use MDCT (Modified Discrete Cosine Transform) for compression. How­
ever, opus uses a hybrid model. SILK (Linear Prediction for Speech) is ideal for low­bitrate
voice encoding, uses predictive coding like traditional speech codecs (e.g., Speex) and
CELT (MDCT for Music and General Audio) is optimized for low­latency and high­quality
audio, similar to AAC but more efficient.
Bitrate Control & Adaptability
MP3 and AAC use either CBR (Constant Bitrate) or VBR (Variable Bitrate) but do not
adjust in real­time. Opus, however, dynamically adjusts bitrates on the fly (6 kbps – 510
kbps) based on network conditions, providing better streaming quality with fewer dropouts.
Low Latency Frame Processing
MP3 has high latency due to fixed 1152­sample frames. AAC has a relatively lower la­
tency (512–960 samples), but still frame­based. Opus supports 2.5ms–60ms frame sizes,
adapting based on the needs of real­time applications. Which makes opus highly efficient
with lowest latency.

2.7
Networking Layers and Transmission
Our system relies on Wi­Fi for video and audio transmission, networking layers are briefly
defined in this context without going into unnecessary details since networking details
were not the focus point of this project.
The OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) model provides a structured way to understand
how data flows over networks[19]. In the case of Wi­Fi communication, the following
layers are relevant:
1. Physical Layer (Layer 1) – Responsible for radio wave transmission over the wire­
less medium (Wi­Fi signals operating on 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, or 6 GHz).
2. Data Link Layer (Layer 2) – Manages MAC (Media Access Control) addressing and
frame transmission over the Wi­Fi network.
3. Network Layer (Layer 3) – Handles IP (Internet Protocol) addressing and routing,
ensuring that data packets reach the correct destination.
4. Transport Layer (Layer 4) – Uses TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) or UDP (User
Datagram Protocol) to manage data transmission between devices.
5. Application Layer (Layer 7) – Protocols like RTSP (Real­Time Streaming Protocol)
and RTP (Real­time Transport Protocol) operate here, enabling real­time media
streaming.
2.7.1
RTSP and RTP
For real­time audio and video streaming over Wi­Fi, RTSP (Real­Time Streaming Proto­
col) and RTP (Real­time Transport Protocol) provide an optimal combination of control and
efficient data transport. These protocols are designed to reduce latency, ensure smooth
playback, and adapt to varying network conditions, making them ideal for applications like
surveillance, live monitoring, and real­time video transmission. [20]
RTSP Provides Flexible Control Without Overhead
RTSP acts as a control protocol that manages play, pause, stop, and seek commands,
similar to how HTTP handles web requests but optimized for media streams.
Unlike traditional streaming methods, RTSP does not transmit media data itself; it only
coordinates the media stream, reducing unnecessary processing overhead. It operates
over TCP, ensuring reliable session control, meaning stream setup and commands are
delivered without loss.
It allows dynamic stream control without restarting the connection, unlike HTTP­based
streaming solutions that require frequent reconnections.
In surveillance applications,
the ability to quickly start, stop, and control streams without re­establishing connections
makes RTSP efficient and flexible.
RTP Enables Low­Latency, Real­Time Data Transmission
While RTSP handles control, RTP is responsible for delivering the actual audio and video
data in real time. It is designed for low­latency transport of continuous media streams.
It uses UDP (User Datagram Protocol) instead of TCP, meaning it does not require ac­
knowledgment of received packets. This avoids retransmission delays and ensures con­
tinuous streaming hence reducing latency.
It also supports packet timestamping and
sequence numbers, allowing the receiver to reconstruct the stream in the correct order
despite possible network jitter or out­of­order packets. It is designed to handle missing

packets gracefully by allowing playback to continue without stuttering, unlike TCP­based
streams where retransmitted packets cause buffering delays.
Since real­time applications prioritize speed over 100% reliability, RTP over UDP ensures
fast, uninterrupted streaming, even if minor packet loss occurs. [20]

Video Pipeline Description(TX)
The pipeline formulated to capture video has various components and all have specific
purpose. [26]
1. v4l2src is the capture element for the camera connected to Jetson Xavier NX. de­
vice=/dev/video0 is to specify the camera plugged in the first port or the first device
seen in the list of hardware devices in the jetson system should be specifically used
for this capture. name=video_src is just a label which is essentially useful when
color and face recognition are introduced but it is good to add a label in case the
device needs to be addressed later somehow.
2. video/x­raw defines that the video data that is required to be captured should be raw
and uncompressed and then the capabilities and parameters format the data to the
system’s need. format=YUY2 is a YUV format. YUV is preferred in video encoding
techniques because of the lightweight data that it produces since it encodes video
data in terms of Luminance (Y) and Chrominance (U (Color difference between blue
and luminance) and V(Color difference between red and luminance)) components
instead of RGB. YUY2 is a specific format under the YUV family called YUV 4:2:2
packed format. It stores Y, U, V in a single interleaved buffer like | Y0 | U0 | Y1 | V0
| Y2 | U1 | Y3 | V1 | ... Hence, it provides better storage and compression.
It compresses color information while keeping luminance detail high. RGB requires
8 bits to encode each color so one pixel in one frame ends up being 28 bits. This
is not suitable for our purpose and since it is established fact that human eye is
more sensitive to light than to color, a colorspace that works with light and some
information of color is perfect for our case since a surveillance device isn’t always
operating in great network conditions.
Width, height and framerate are experimented on and are set to these values be­
cause throughout experimentation, it was observed that this gives the best results
in the case of transmission.
This can happen because the other device can perceive certain dimensions best and
find the match in these specific parameter setting (concluded after setting various
element properties while testing and formulating). Since 360p is still a well enough
video resolution, this was enough for project’s purposes for now. Framerate is pretty
good and it was luckily accepted well by both devices. 30/1 still means fps=30 but
gstreamer expects this input value to be written with a denominator hence /1 was
added.
3. videoconvert is one of the most useful elements in this case since this allows us
to plainly convert whatever type of input is coming through to a desired output type
without having to mention or figure out input type. It is sort of a middle man and it
works with all the settings tried for this project.
4. videobalance was the element added later and is not essential for the transmission
of video. It is specifically added so the system can see colors better in the case
of color detection and tracking. Contrast, brightness and saturation are variable
parameters and can be adjusted as per needed.
5. x264enc is the H.264 encoder. How the encoder itself works has been explained in
the literature review. However, the gstreamer element and its capabilities are here
for specific reasons. tune = zerolatency is a critical element yet easy to implement.
It minimizes or disables some of the encoding techniques that are a part of H.264
Master Thesis
31
==PAGE:39==
to make compression efficiency better but introduce latency. For example, it dis­
ables lookahead buffer that ”looks ahead” for better quality but introduces latency.
Same as b­frame disabling which can be achieved by zerolatency tuning as well but
bframes are separately disabled as well to make sure.
6. Bitrate = 800 has been experimented with and this was a well suited setting for a
balance of quality and data usage.
7. speed­present = ultrafast controls how aggressively the encoder prioritizes speed
over compression efficiency. It tells the encoder to minimize processing time, mak­
ing it ideal for real­time streaming. It reduces CPU usage by skipping complex com­
pression techniques and prioritizes encoding speed over video quality and compres­
sion efficiency. Hence, it is best suited for real­time applications with zerolatency
setting.
8. key­int­max = 30 sets the group of pictures(GOP) length to be 30. This means that
after every 30 frames, an I­frame will be inserted. Since, the application is set for 30
frames per second, this means that an I­frame will be inserted once every second.
It ensures quick frame recovery if any packets are lost in compression and encoding
settings.
9. rtph264pay is responsible for packetizing H.264­encoded video into RTP (Real­
time Transport Protocol) packets so it can be streamed over a network. It will take
an H.264 encoded stream, split it into RTP packets following the RTP specification
for H.264, add RTP headers that allow the receiver to reconstruct the video properly
and assigns a payload type (pt = 96).
The parameter pt = 96 is a dynamic allocation of payload type. Payload types from
0 to 95 are statically allocated by IANA (Internet Assigned Numbers Authority) and
cannot be used. This payload type is a label that helps identify the payload type on
the receiver end.
Payload is also named as pay0 as an identifier and is used to separate packets at
the receiver.
10. config−interval = 1controls how often SPS (Sequence Parameter Set) and PPS
(Picture Parameter Set) headers are sent in the RTP stream. These headers are
critical for decoding H.264 video streams.
Sequence Parameter Set (SPS) contains global video settings like resolution, frame
rate, and codec profile. Picture Parameter Set (PPS) defines frame­specific param­
eters like entropy coding method and slice groups. It helps receivers quickly sync
and decode the video without waiting for a new I­frame.

4.4
Updating Pipeline based on Receiver Control Signals
There is one thing that requires understanding at this point. The flow of signals and data
is generally understood by now. But how is the control signals effecting the gstreamer
pipeline, really? Or, how does flask influence gstreamer pipeline after it has been updated
by the receiver?
The answer is simple but was a bit tricky to come up with and required a lot of trials, espe­
cially to achieve a lower latency. The pipeline is broken into. We will call this concept of
pipeline, The Edited Pipeline. The edited pipeline is designed to allow dynamic modifi­
cation of the transmitted video and audio stream based on the receiver’s settings. Instead
of simply passing the raw camera feed, the transmitter intercepts frames, processes them
(e.g., applying color detection, face recognition), and injects the modified frames into the
stream. This ensures that real­time changes requested by the receiver (via Flask /control)
take effect without restarting the stream.
4.4.1
Edited Pipeline Structure
The pipeline is split into two main parts:
1. Original Data Capture (appsink): This part captures raw video frames from the cam­
era and audio data from the microphone.
2. Modified Data Injection (appsrc): This part takes processed frames and re­injects
them into the stream before encoding and transmission.
Key components for this setup in the pipeline are:
1. appsink (video_sink) receives frames from the camera and passes them to Python
for processing.
2. appsrc (processed_src) →Receives modified frames and injects them back into the
GStreamer pipeline.

Understand that gstreamer can be tricky about sources and sinks. Here, source is now the
modified source feeding the encoder, not the element before appsrc that is the supposed
actual source of video frames.
The edited pipeline looks like this for video:
”v4l2src device = /dev/video0 name = video_src ! video/x−raw, format = Y UY 2, width =
640, height = 360, framerate = 30/1 ! videoconvert ! videobalance contrast = 1.1 brightness =
1.3 saturation = 1.3 ! appsink name = video_sink emit −signals = true sync =

# Processed video re­injected

The function on_new_sample() is a callback triggered every time a new video frame ar­
rives from the appsink. Instead of directly passing the frame to the RTSP stream, it first
checks the receiver’s settings and applies any requested modifications (like color detec­

The function is triggered when a new frame arrives from appsink and the raw frame is

is generated and sent to the receiver. If color detection is enabled, it applies HSV thresh­

Converting from BGR to HSV
Since detecting colors directly in the BGR format can be unreliable under changing lighting
conditions, the system converts the frame to HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) format.
The selected BGR color is also converted to HSV. The HSV model allows the system to
focus on hue (color type) while ignoring brightness or intensity variations. This conversion
helps in accurate color detection, even when lighting conditions change.

1. CV2 expects color input as BGR but input data is in RGB format.
2. RGB needs to be converted first to BGR and then to YUV (Similar to HSV method.)
3. After converting in to YUV and the processing the frames somehow reswaps the

to or from YUY2 separately in functions for some reason.
4.6.5
Defining an HSV Range for the Target Color
Once the selected color is converted to HSV, the system defines a range of values that
will be considered a match. Since real­world colors vary slightly due to lighting, shadows,
and reflections, the system introduces tolerance buffers.
A lower and upper HSV threshold is set to cover slight variations in hue, saturation, and
brightness. If the selected color is red, a special case is handled because red exists at both
ends of the HSV spectrum (0 and 179). In such cases, the system defines two separate
HSV ranges. This ensures that a wider spectrum of the selected color is detected rather
than a single exact value.
4.6.6
Creating a Mask to Isolate the Color
With the HSV threshold defined, the system applies a mask to the frame. This mask filters
out everything except the pixels that fall within the defined color range.
The mask creates a binary image where White (255) pixels represent areas where the
color is detected. Black (0) pixels represent everything else. This means only the regions
of the frame containing the target color remain visible in the mask.
4.6.7
Refining the Mask to Remove Noise
The initial mask might contain small unwanted noise due to lighting variations or reflec­
tions.
To improve detection, the system applies morphological transformations using
OpenCV. Closing Operation fills small holes inside detected objects, ensuring more solid
detection. Opening Operation removes small isolated noise that may have been mistak­
enly detected as the target color. These transformations help in cleaning up the detected
regions, making them more reliable for further processing.
4.6.8
Finding the Largest Detected Object
After filtering, the system identifies contours in the mask, which represent separate de­
tected objects of the selected color. The contours are sorted based on area size, ensuring
that the largest detected object is prioritized. If multiple objects of the same color are de­
tected, only the largest one is tracked to reduce false detections. If no object of significant
size is found, the system ignores the frame.
4.6.9
Drawing a Bounding Box Around the Detected Object
Once the largest detected object is identified, the system draws a yellow bounding box
around it to visually highlight the detected region.
The bounding box dimensions are extracted from the contour. A rectangle is drawn on
the original frame, surrounding the detected object. A label “Color Detection” is also dis­
played in the frame. At this point, the frame now contains both the original video and the
highlighted detected object.

Audio Related Modes
In order to try and work with audio[37], the two modes chosen were Audio Event Detec­
tion and Speech Detection. Both these modes are specifically tied to the concept of the
surveillance device as perceived initially. This will enable the device to operate specifically
for low network condition or really specific ”mission­critical” conditions.
If the device is needed to be operated in low network conditions, in a specific surveillance
or monitoring operations, these two modes can come in handy. These two modes are
properly explained below.
Audio Event Detection mode enables the device to go in to a standby mode, effectively
turning off audio and video. If a certain threshold of audio event happens, which in this
case in any sound greater than 60dBs, the standby mode will be turned off. This can
serve as an alarm for certain operations and can come handy if the user has to monitor a
remote place.
Speech Event Mode is useful for similar purpose but for slightly different situations. When
the speech detection mode is enabled, device goes in to standby mode, turning off audio

and video. While enabling this mode, user gets a dialog box where they can write a word,
phrase on which the user wishes to be notified. So if the devics picks up the set speech,
the audio and video is turned back on and live transmission is live for monitoring.
To enable these two modes, the first step was to set up a standard standby mode which
enables the user to manually turn on or off, both, audio and video. The user can also
manually turn off any of the standby modes (Standard Standby, Audio Event Detection
and Speech Detection modes). Here is a short explanation of how these three modes are
implemented in the project.
5.2.1
Standby Mode
Standby mode is designed to optimize resource usage by temporarily disabling audio and
video transmission when not needed. Instead of shutting down the pipeline completely, it
preserves the connection while pausing transmission. When enabled, the current audio
and video states are saved. Both audio and video are then disabled until standby mode
is turned off. When disabled, the system restores the previous states, ensuring seamless
resumption of streaming.
5.2.2
Audio Event Detection Mode
In Audio Event Detection Mode, standby mode is automatically disabled when a loud
sound is detected. Instead of manually re­enabling streaming, the system monitors the
RMS (Root Mean Square) audio level and wakes up when a significant noise is detected.
The system monitors the audio intensity using an appsink connected to the audio source
similar to the video pipeline discussed earlier. If the RMS exceeds a predefined threshold,
standby mode is automatically disabled. Transmission resumes without user intervention.
5.2.3
Speech Event Detection Mode
Speech Event Detection Mode expands on Audio Event Mode by introducing keyword­
based wake­up functionality. Instead of simply detecting loud noise, the system listens for
a specific wake­word or a phrase, using speech recognition to reactivate streaming. The
receiver user provides a custom keyword or phrase, if audio is received, it is processed
through a speech recognition model. If the detected speech matches the wake word,
standby mode is disabled and transmission resumes normally.

remote­controlled from the receiving system.

7.2
Approaches that Didn’t Work
There were a couple of things tried in the process of course and either they didn’t work or
other reasons didn’t allow them to be part of the final version. However, two of these are
worth mentioning because it would have been worked but didn’t due to mere compatibility
issues. Hence, they should be included in order to have the record of what else can work
for such a system if compatibility isn’t an issue.
7.2.1
Color Detection using YUV instead of HSV
Achieving color detection using YUV colorspace would have made more sense but as
explained earlier, there were some issues. BGR can itself be converted to YUV but since
we are using cv2 to achieve color detection, we needed to consider how cv2 expects color
to be given to it. CV2 takes color as BGR but camera picks color as RGB. By converting
frame color format RGB to YUV, the color sequence is essentially shifted. Same happens
when HSV is being used but HSV, for some reason, after shifting the color to BGR format,
keeps them like that and moves on with color detection.
In case of YUV, for some reason, even after converting RGB to BGR and then conversion
to YUV takes it back to its original format. Which means in the end, the colors are shifted
and are detected that way. If red is selected, blue is being detected. If YUV could have
been used, that would have made the latency at color detection time even lower.
7.2.2
Audio Event Detection
Audio Event Detection could have been much more easily done by using a GStreamer
parameter level with audio element[40]. It automatically detects level of audio and then
we can directly take audio level from here and use on_bus_message. Which is directly
connected to gstreamer pipeline this way.
This method wasn’t used because parameter level was not compatible with the GStreamer
version installed on Jetson Xavier NX. And it couldn’t be upgraded as per specific instruc­
tions to not change or update the board by supervisors.
7.3
Conclusion
This thesis presents the design and implementation of a real­time, controllable surveil­
lance/monitoring system that integrates audio, video, color detection, face recognition,
and dynamic standby mechanisms. The system is built around an RTSP­based transmis­
sion pipeline using GStreamer on an NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX platform, with Flask and
Tkinter providing real­time control and user interaction.
The core objective was to develop a flexible and responsive multimedia transmission
framework, allowing a receiver to dynamically control audio, video, and processing fea­
tures without interrupting the ongoing stream. This was successfully achieved through a
Flask­based REST API that enables external devices to send control commands to the
transmitter. The system dynamically injects modified frames back into the pipeline, en­
suring smooth and uninterrupted streaming.
Furthermore, the standby mechanisms introduced in this system significantly improve en­
ergy efficiency and user convenience. The implementation of three standby modes: Sim­
ple, AudioEvent, and SpeechEvent ensures adaptive power management by disabling
media transmission when unnecessary. These modes also serve as very specific moni­
toring choices to build upon specially within a mission critical situation. The Vosk speech
