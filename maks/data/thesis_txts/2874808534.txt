Abstract
Modern vehicles rely heavily on Controller Area Network (CAN) bus systems for ECU com-
munication, yet these networks lack built in security mechanisms, making them vulnerable to
cyberattacks. This thesis investigates the development and evaluation of machine learning based
Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) for automotive CAN networks, addressing the critical need
for effective security measures in increasingly connected vehicles. This thesis presents and con-
tributes to the solution of three main problems: First, successful reverse engineering of CAN
signals from an Opel Astra H to create reusable signal interpretation. Second, development of a
custom Denial of Service attack dataset with realistic injection patterns, addressing limitations
in existing public datasets where attacks often exhibit unrealistic deterministic patterns. Third,
comprehensive evaluation of three distinct machine learning approaches Long Short Term Mem-
ory (LSTM) networks, Gaussian Naive Bayes (GNB), and Isolation Forest (IF)—across multiple
attack scenarios including DoS, fuzzing, and spoofing attacks.
Evaluation on the widely used HCRL Car Hacking Dataset revealed concerning characteristics:
attack patterns were highly deterministic with identical payloads and predictable timing, en-
abling artificially high detection rates. When tested on the custom dataset with more realistic
attack patterns, model performance varied significantly. LSTM demonstrated superior robust-
ness, maintaining 99% accuracy even on challenging scenarios by effectively capturing temporal
dependencies.
GNB, while computationally efficient and achieving perfect scores on HCRL
datasets, suffered from high false positive rates (68%) on realistic data. Isolation Forest offered
unsupervised detection capabilities but showed inconsistent performance and high sensitivity to
parameter tuning.

Introduction
With the rise of autonomous driving and connected cars, the attack surface of vehicles is ex-
panding rapidly. Tomorrow’s cars are not only transport machines but also mobile data hubs,
tightly integrated with external networks and cloud services. As a consequence, cybersecurity
becomes a prerequisite for safety: a successful cyberattack can directly endanger passengers and
other road users.
It has been just about thirty years since the introduction of the Controller Area Network (CAN)
bus, which has become the de facto standard for in vehicle communication. Modern vehicles
are no longer simple machines but complex networks of interconnected Electronic Control Units
(ECUs).
Where only two decades ago a car might have contained a handful of ECUs for
engine and braking control, today’s vehicles can feature over a hundred ECUs responsible for
everything from infotainment to advanced driver assistance systems. All of these units rely
on CAN to exchange short, periodic messages; however, CAN was designed for reliability and
simplicity rather than security.
The CAN protocol’s broadcast nature, lack of built in encryption, and absence of message
authentication leave it vulnerable to a variety of attacks. As vehicles become increasingly con-
nected to external networks (cellular, WiFi, smartphone tethering), these theoretical weaknesses
have become practical attack vectors, for example, security researchers demonstrated in 2015
that remote access through an infotainment system could lead to control over vehicle functions.
Such demonstrations highlight the pressing need for practical and deployable mechanisms to
detect and mitigate malicious manipulation of vehicular networks.
One promising class of countermeasures is Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) tailored for CAN
traffic. An IDS monitors bus traffic continuously and detects deviations from normal behavior,
enabling detection without requiring invasive changes to the legacy CAN protocol. This thesis
investigates machine learning based IDS approaches for CAN networks, evaluating them on
both public benchmark datasets and a custom dataset collected from an Opel Astra H on which
realistic denial of service (DoS) attacks were injected. The custom experiments were designed
to emulate attacker behavior under realistic timing and priority conditions so that resulting
datasets reflect the temporal and statistical characteristics that a deployed IDS would face.
Because CAN traffic is highly structured (message IDs, periodicity, short payloads), the IDS
problem can be framed in multiple ways: supervised classification when labeled attack traces are
available, and unsupervised or semi supervised anomaly detection when only benign data exist.
This thesis evaluates both paradigms. Feature extraction and preprocessing steps transform raw
CAN frames into representations suitable for learning (examples include ID frequency counts,
inter arrival times, payload entropy, and sliding window aggregate statistics).
The machine
learning models considered include classical classifiers (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting),
anomaly detection algorithms (Isolation Forest, One Class SVM), and deep learning approaches
that capture temporal patterns (autoencoders, LSTM and transformer based sequence models).
Each approach is assessed not only for detection accuracy (precision, recall, F1, ROC AUC) but
also for robustness to noise, detection latency, false positive rate, and computational feasibility
for in vehicle deployment.

Problem statement
This project aims to answer three main research questions:
1. Can we reverse-engineer real automotive CAN signals (e.g. brakes, speed) into a reusable
signal interpretation?
2. How does injecting a controlled DoS attack into a self-collected driving dataset affect IDS
detection performance?
3. Which of three ML models (LSTM, Gaussian NB, Isolation Forest) offers the best trade-off
between detection accuracy, false-positive rate? How do the specific models perform on a
public dataset versus the dataset created in this project?

Theory
4.1
Electronic Control Units (ECUs)
Electronic Control Units (ECUs) are embedded systems within modern vehicles, each dedicated
to specific functions such as engine control, transmission management, braking systems, and
infotainment. These units process data from various sensors and execute commands to actuators,
ensuring optimal vehicle performance and safety.
Modern vehicles can contain up to 150 ECUs, depending on their complexity and feature set [20].
This increase has led to higher system complexity, necessitating robust communication proto-
cols.
The Controller Area Network (CAN) is the dominant protocol facilitating inter-ECU
communication due to its efficiency and reliability [3]. However, depending on manufacturer
requirements, other protocols like Local Interconnect Network (LIN) and FlexRay are also em-
ployed. LIN offers a cost-effective solution for simpler subsystems, while FlexRay provides higher
data rates and deterministic communication for time-critical applications [7].
The interconnected nature of ECUs introduces cybersecurity challenges. Vulnerabilities in one
ECU can potentially compromise the entire network, posing risks to vehicle safety and user
privacy. Notably, research has demonstrated that attackers exploiting a single compromised
ECU can gain access to critical vehicle systems [6]. Such findings underscore the importance of
implementing robust security measures across all ECUs.
Any modern vehicle is by law required for the onboard ECU’s to support diagnostic functionali-
ties through interfaces like the On-Board Diagnostics II (OBD-II) port, allowing external devices
to access operational data. While essential for maintenance, these access points can also serve
as potential attack vectors if not adequately secured.
In response to these challenges, the automotive industry is transitioning towards centralized
computing architectures. By consolidating multiple ECU functions into fewer, more powerful
units, manufacturers aim to reduce system complexity, improve performance, and enhance se-
curity [9]. This shift also facilitates over-the-air updates and supports the evolving needs of
connected and autonomous vehicles. While the consolidation of ECUs represents one approach
to managing system complexity, the fundamental challenge remains: how do these numerous
computing units coordinate their actions in real time? This question leads us to examine the
Controller Area Network (CAN), the communication backbone that has enabled the increase of
distributed automotive electronics for over three decades.
4.2
CAN
The Controller Area Network (CAN) protocol is a robust vehicle bus standard designed to allow
microcontrollers and Electronic Control Units (ECUs) to communicate without a host computer.
Developed by Bosch and first introduced to the Society of Automotive Engineers in 1991 [1], it
has since become the de facto standard for intravehicle communication.
Following its introduction, CAN was formalized as an international standard under ISO 11898
in 1993 [8]. It is particularly valued in automotive systems for its low latency, deterministic
message delivery, and minimal overhead features that support real time data exchange between
safety-critical components.
CAN supports data rates of up to 1 Mbps and a maximum data payload of 8 bytes per message
frame. While the standard CAN protocol remains in widespread use, CAN-FD (Flexible Data-
rate), standardized in ISO 11898-1:2015, extends this capacity to payloads of up to 64 bytes and
allows for faster communication rates, improving performance for modern vehicle applications.

4.3
Application of CAN Bus on the OSI Model
The Controller Area Network (CAN) bus is a message-based protocol. CAN operates predom-
inantly across the physical and data link layers of the OSI model, as illustrated in Figure 1.
These layers are responsible for the accurate and reliable transmission of messages across the
CAN network.
Figure 1: CAN Bus application across the OSI model [8]
4.3.1
Physical Layer
The physical layer of the CAN bus handles the actual transmission of raw bitstreams over a
physical medium. It is defined by ISO 11898-2 (for high-speed CAN) and ISO 11898-3 (for
low-speed or fault-tolerant CAN). Key characteristics include [25]:
• Differential signaling: CAN uses two wires (CAN High and CAN Low) with differential
voltage levels. This technique improves noise immunity by ensuring that external inter-
ference affects both wires equally, allowing the receiver to ignore common-mode noise. It
makes the bus robust in the electrically noisy automotive environment.
• Half-duplex communication: In half-duplex systems, nodes can either transmit or
receive data at a given time but not both simultaneously.
This design simplifies the
hardware and allows all ECUs to share the same physical bus without collisions, thanks
to the arbitration logic in the data link layer.
• Bus topology: All ECUs are connected to a shared bus, meaning any device can attempt
to transmit. This topology supports scalability, allowing new nodes to be added without
changing the fundamental communication infrastructure.
• Asynchronous communication: The CAN bus does not rely on a global clock. Each
message is self-synchronizing, allowing nodes with different internal clocks to communicate
effectively. This is essential for distributed control systems in vehicles.
CAN transceivers convert digital signals to physical voltages. A dominant bit is transmitted
when CAN H (High) is higher than CAN L(Low), overriding any recessive bit being sent by an-
other node. This mechanism allows for deterministic arbitration and reliable collision resolution,
crucial in safety-critical systems.

The On Board Diagnostics II (OBD-II) port is a standardized diagnostic interface that has been
required on most passenger vehicles since 1996. Its introduction was driven by emissions regula-
tions and the need for standardized access to vehicle diagnostic data. Located inside the vehicle

4.3.3
Data Link Layer
The data link layer ensures reliable communication between nodes through error detection, frame
formatting, and arbitration mechanisms. It is standardized in ISO 11898-1 and is split into the
Logical Link Control (LLC) and Medium Access Control (MAC) sublayers. Key responsibilities
include:
• Message framing: CAN messages are transmitted in structured frames consisting of
fields such as the identifier, control bits, data, CRC, and acknowledgment. These well-
defined formats ensure consistency and allow all nodes to parse messages correctly (see
Figure 2).
• Arbitration: As described in the standard ”The arbitration fields of the different frame
formats are constructed to enable resolving collisions even between frames of different
formats so that only one data frame transmitter continues transmitting.” [8]. CAN uses a
non-destructive bitwise arbitration method. Nodes monitor the bus while transmitting; if a
node detects a dominant bit when it transmitted a recessive bit, it stops transmission. The
message with the lowest identifier continues, ensuring priority without data corruption.
• Error checking: Multiple mechanisms help maintain integrity:
– CRC: Detects errors in the data payload and header.
– ACK: Receivers confirm successful frame reception by overwriting the acknowledg-
ment slot.
– Bit stuffing: Prevents long strings of identical bits, which could desynchronize
receivers.
• Frame types: The protocol supports different frame formats:
– Data Frame: Carries data from transmitter to receiver.
– Remote Frame: Requests data from another node.
– Error Frame: Indicates a detected error.
– Overload Frame: Adds delay between frames.

4.4
Threat Landscape
The modern automotive threat landscape is expanding as vehicles integrate more ECUs and
expose more interfaces.
Each ECU and connected subsystem can act as an entry point for
attackers via remote update mechanisms, weak isolation between subsystems, wireless interfaces
(WiFi, Bluetooth), or physical access to components and wiring. For example, an infotainment
unit with vulnerable network services can provide remote access to the CAN bus, while physical
access to components such as headlights may yield a path into the vehicle network.
The OBD-II port remains the best known and most direct access point to vehicle networks,
as noted in the Car Hacker’s Handbook, ”the OBD-II port is your easiest access to the ve-
hicle’s communication bus.” [22] In practice, researchers have demonstrated severe real world
impacts: Miller and Valasek remotely controlled steering and braking on a Jeep Cherokee via an
infotainment vulnerability (2015) [17], and projects such as Comma.ai show how aftermarket de-
vices can interact with vehicle CAN buses, illustrating both legitimate innovation and potential
abuse. Conference demonstrations (DEFCON, Black Hat, etc.) have further showcased remote
takeovers, denial of service attacks, and ECU spoofing, underlining that these vulnerabilities
are actively exploitable.
Below are the principal types of CAN-bus attacks considered for developing the IDS:
4.4.1
Replay
The Replay attack is executed by capturing legitimate CAN messages during normal vehicle
operation and retransmitting them at a later time. In this scenario, the attacker initially operates
in a passive mode, listening to the CAN bus to collect and analyze traffic without disturbing
system behavior. Once specific message patterns that trigger functional behaviors (e.g. door
unlock, engine start) are identified, these messages are stored and later replayed into the network.
Since the replayed messages are structurally valid and conform to expected formats, they are
difficult to detect using conventional filtering or integrity checks. This makes replay attacks
particularly stealthy and dangerous, especially in systems lacking freshness mechanisms such as
timestamps or sequence counters. Replay attacks might seem like the perfect attack, but ECU
manufacturers have built in system to prevent some dangerous commands from being processed.
For example the steering might require both the torque and speed signals to match. This is not
necessarily made with attacks in mind, but mechanism to improve the safety of the car, due to
faulty sensors or ecu. These security measures still get in the way of successfully executing a
replay attack.
4.4.2
Fuzzy
The Fuzzy attack involves sending a stream of malformed or random messages onto the CAN bus
to test how ECUs respond to unexpected or out of spec inputs. Unlike structured attacks, fuzzing
does not require detailed protocol knowledge; instead, the attacker generates a wide range of
arbitrary message formats, data lengths, or IDs. This can expose software vulnerabilities, logic
flaws, or fault handling weaknesses in ECUs. Fuzzing may lead to system crashes, abnormal
behavior, or even permanent damage if critical thresholds are not handled correctly
4.4.3
DOS
A denial of service attack on the CAN involves exploiting the aberration system to cripple all
traffic on the network. This involves sending a flood of messages with the lowest arbitration id,
which corresponds to the highest priority normally reserved for critical system functionalities.
The DOS attack is trivial to execute as it does not require any knowledge other than how to
send a CAN packet on the network.

4.4.4
Masquerade
The Masquerade attack is performed by injecting CAN messages using the identifier of a legiti-
mate ECU, thereby impersonating it. This type of attack exploits the lack of built in authen-
tication in the CAN protocol, where any node on the network can send messages using any ID.
After eavesdropping on the bus and identifying message patterns, the attacker sends forged mes-
sages with spoofed IDs (e.g. mimicking the Automatic breaking system (ABS) or engine control
module). These impersonated messages can be used to issue commands, alter vehicle behavior,
or manipulate sensor data. Because the receiving ECUs trust messages based solely on their ID,
masquerade attacks are highly effective and difficult to detect without additional authentication
layers or intrusion detection systems. Some ECUs mitigate this with sanity checks or cross
verification from multiple signals, but these defenses are inconsistent and often bypassable.

4.5
Intrusion Detection Systems
Modern vehicles are increasingly connected and rely heavily on in vehicle networks such as the
Controller Area Network (CAN) bus. This connectivity broadens the attack surface and makes
vehicles potential targets for cyberattacks. Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) have therefore
emerged as a key defensive mechanism to monitor automotive networks for signs of malicious
activity. Lampe and Meng [12] emphasize that “Intrusion detection, while not a panacea, can
be a cost effective solution to many automotive security issues.”
Designing an automotive IDS requires careful consideration of the environment and expected
attack vectors. Constraints such as limited computational resources, strict real time require-
ments, and the broadcast nature of the CAN bus all influence what is feasible. Moreover, an IDS
must be capable of detecting subtle and stealthy attacks—such as spoofing, replay, or message
injection—while introducing minimal latency and without disrupting normal vehicle operation.
For these reasons, lightweight, real time, and non intrusive detection mechanisms are gener-
ally preferred, with anomaly detection approaches being particularly prominent in the vehicular
domain.
4.5.1
Anomaly Based vs. Signature Based Detection
Intrusion detection methods are commonly divided into two categories: signature based and
anomaly based. Signature based IDS rely on known attack patterns (signatures) to identify
intrusions. They are effective against well understood threats but require frequent updates to
remain effective, and they cannot detect previously unknown (“zero-day”) attacks.
In contrast, anomaly based IDS construct a model of normal behavior and flag significant
deviations as suspicious. These methods are well suited to CAN networks due to their regular
and predictable message structures. Anomaly based systems can detect message injections and
zero day attacks that do not match any predefined signature. Although they may suffer from
false positives, their adaptability makes them a more future proof choice in the automotive
context [12,21].
4.5.2
Network Based vs. Host Based IDS
IDS can also be categorized by their deployment location as either host based or network
based. A host based IDS (HIDS) runs on an individual electronic control unit (ECU) and
monitors its local activity as well as incoming messages. This allows detection of attacks that
specifically target a given ECU, but is limited by the ECU’s restricted computational resources.
A network based IDS (NIDS), in contrast, monitors the traffic flowing across the CAN bus. This
approach offers several advantages:
• Bus visibility: A NIDS observes all messages broadcast on the CAN bus, providing a
system wide perspective [15].
• Non intrusive deployment: A NIDS can often be installed via existing interfaces (e.g.,
the OBD-II port) without modifying ECUs [12].
• Resource efficiency: More complex detection algorithms can be executed centrally
rather than burdening individual ECUs [21].
• Security separation: Because a NIDS operates independently of the ECUs it monitors,

Isolation Forest
Isolation Forest (IF) is an unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm based on the principle
that anomalies are easier to isolate than normal data points. It constructs random decision
trees (isolation trees) and measures how many splits are required to isolate a point. Anomalies
generally require fewer splits and therefore have shorter average path lengths across trees [14].
This makes IF suitable for CAN bus intrusion detection, where the goal is to learn normal
message behavior and flag unusual traffic as potential attacks.
IF can detect anomalies in
payload content, message frequency, or timing without requiring labeled attack data.
When applied to CAN traffic, features such as CAN IDs, payload bytes, DLC, and timing (e.g.,
inter arrival time ∆t) are extracted. Each CAN message is treated as a data point. Attacks
like fuzzing and DoS typically produce highly irregular data, leading to low path lengths (high
anomaly scores). Replay attacks may evade detection unless temporal or contextual features
are included.
IF is effective in detecting novel or unknown attacks and can operate in real time on embedded
automotive platforms.
Its simplicity and low overhead make it a strong candidate for IDS
deployment in modern vehicles.

5.6
Feature Engineering
Delta T
CAN messages are typically sent at regular, periodic intervals. The time gap between consecutive
frames (∆T) for each message ID under normal conditions is relatively consistent. Analyzing
these time differences helps establish a baseline of ‘normal’ timing behavior.
Attacks often
disrupt this regularity: for example, a flooding or Denial of Service (DoS) attack injects messages
far more frequently than normal, while certain spoofing or replay attacks may cause unexpected
delays or bursts. Monitoring ∆T can therefore reveal anomalies in message frequency.
An example of where ∆T can be effective is in the detection of DoS attacks. These attacks
dramatically reduce the timing difference for the injected frames and can cause genuine periodic
messages to be delayed or completely overridden. This creates irregular timing patterns that
deviate from the nominal schedule. An IDS can flag abnormally high message rates or detect
when expected messages do not arrive on time. These observable differences suggest that ∆T
serves as a valuable feature for helping machine learning models distinguishing malicious traffic
from legitimate CAN communication.
In later sections the ∆T feature will be subject to scrutiny for its potential for temporal leakage.
Temporal leakage occurs when a machine learning model inadvertently uses future information
to make predictions about past or present events. In real time intrusion detection, this creates
artificially inflated performance metrics that do not translate to practical deployment.
For the ∆t feature, temporal leakage could manifest through forward-looking calculations (e.g.,
using ∆T between message i and future message i + 1 to classify message i) or through normal-
ization parameters computed from the entire dataset including future observations.
Numerical encoding
CAN IDs and data payload bytes are often represented in hexadecimal format (e.g. ID 0x100,
data byte 0xFF). Numerical encoding means converting these hex values into standard integers
(or binary features) before feeding them to a machine learning model. Essentially, each byte or
ID becomes a numeric feature (e.g. 0xFF →255) instead of a string.
Most machine learning algorithms require numeric feature vectors. Converting CAN fields from
hex to decimal makes the data machine readable and mathematically comparable. This has also
been done in other research [13].

Of note, lower arbitration IDs appear with much higher frequency across most datasets. This

the concentration of activity in low-ID ranges highlights the vulnerability of the CAN protocol’s
priority-based arbitration scheme.

8.2
Identifying and Interpreting CAN Signals for Vehicle Parameters
The first step in reverse engineering is to identify which CAN message (frame) corresponds to a
given vehicle parameter. A common approach is to link physical events to CAN IDs by observing
the bus traffic while triggering one function at a time. For example one would record CAN bus
data of them using left turn signal, then replay it and try to decipher which bits are changing.
Craig Smith shows this process
Figure 10: Flow diagram of decoding process [22]
Although this is a simplified example, as this can be done in many ways, for example, to isolate
an engine RPM signal, one can log CAN data while revving the engine and note which message’s
data bytes vary with the tachometer. Tools like SavvyCAN or the recently developed Cabana [2]
help filter and highlight changing messages in real time, allowing users to gradually eliminate
irrelevant frames and find correct bits and IDS.
Once a potential CAN frame for desired signal is identified, the next task is to interpret the
signal’s encoding within that frame’s data bytes. A CAN message is at most 8 bytes (64 bits),
and often contains multiple signals packed into different bit fields. The reverse engineer must
determine which bits correspond to the target parameter. A good strategy is to observe patterns
in the value, comparing with what can be expected. Some patterns to look for are: the value
increasing linearly? Does it rollover or stay within a range? For on/off signals such as a door
lock or a headlamp status, a single bit often toggles between 0 and 1. Some of these signals will
also be tricky, for example there might be a brake signal that is both a toggle (0,1) and a brake
pressure signal 8 bit value.
For analog or continuous values (speed, RPM, steering angle), the raw data usually needs to
be scaled to convert to physical units. For example, if a speedometer shows 50 km/h while the
corresponding CAN bytes read as 0x32 (50 in decimal), it suggests a 1:1 scale with no offset;
but if the bytes read 0xFA for 250 RPM, that might indicate the raw value is 4 times the RPM
(since 250 * 4 = 1000, representing 1000 RPM).

8.3
The Role and Structure of DBC Files in Mapping Signals
Once CAN signals have been deciphered, the information is systematically recorded in a DBC
file (CAN database). A DBC file is a standardized text based format that describes how to
decode raw CAN frames into human readable signals. This is critical because without a DBC
(or a similar database), raw CAN logs are just streams of hex values with no obvious meaning.
8.4
Using Reverse Engineered Mappings to Inject Control Commands
Reverse engineering CAN signals not only enables passive monitoring, but also opens the door
to active control via CAN injection. Once you know the CAN message and data that correspond
to a particular action or state, you can craft and send that message on the bus to invoke the
effect essentially simulating the component or sensor that originally sends it. This capability
is powerful for hobby users, but also attackers, for example one could tell the power steering
module on modern cars to lock while on a highway.
It should be noted that while injecting commands can replicate many functions, modern vehicles
sometimes implement security countermeasures.

Evaluation Metrics
To assess the performance of each intrusion detection model, several standard classification
metrics were computed based on the confusion matrix. A confusion matrix provides a breakdown
of prediction results into four categories [27]:
• True Positives (TP): Instances where an attack was correctly classified as an attack.
• False Positives (FP): Instances where benign traffic was incorrectly classified as an
attack (false alarm).
• True Negatives (TN): Instances where benign traffic was correctly identified as benign.
• False Negatives (FN): Instances where an actual attack was missed and classified as
benign.
From these values, the following metrics are derived:
• Accuracy:
Accuracy =
TP + TN
TP + TN + FP + FN
(6)
This reflects the overall proportion of correctly classified samples.
• Precision:
Precision =
TP
TP + FP
(7)
Precision quantifies how many of the predicted attacks were actually attacks.
• Recall (Sensitivity or True Positive Rate):
Recall =
TP
TP + FN
(8)
Recall indicates how many actual attacks were correctly detected.
• F1-Score:
F1-Score = 2 · Precision · Recall
Precision + Recall
(9)
The F1-Score provides a balance between precision and recall, especially useful in imbal-
anced datasets.
These metrics allow for a comprehensive evaluation of each model’s ability to detect intrusions
while minimizing false alarms and missed detections.
To ensure the reported results were not artificially inflated due to data leakage or overfitting,
several measures were taken during data processing and model evaluation. Specifically, many
different ways of splitting training and test data were tried. Although it turned out to have
little to no impact on the performance of the models, the model for splitting used in the final
code is Groupshufflesplit. GroupShuffleSplit is based on calculated time windows, ensuring
that samples in the training and testing sets did not overlap in time. This technique helps
evaluate the model’s ability to generalize to unseen periods of CAN traffic.
Additionally, feature leakage was evaluated by selectively removing certain input features like∆
T, which could potentially carry information that correlates too strongly with attack labels.
Even after removing such features, the Gaussian Naive Bayes model consistently achieved very
high scores on most datasets. These validations suggest that the high performance is not solely
due to overfitting, but suggests that the dataset has strong patterns in attack insertion.
Upon closer inspection of the datasets—particularly the spoofing (Gear and RPM) and DoS
scenarios, it became clear that the attack patterns were highly distinct. For example, in the

Interpretation of findings
The evaluation of Gaussian Naive Bayes (GNB), Isolation Forest (IF), and LSTM models across
different CAN bus attack scenarios (DoS, fuzzy injection, gear spoofing, RPM spoofing and
custom DOS) reveals distinct performance patterns that shed light on each model’s strengths
and limitations. This section examines how each model fared on each attack type, analyzes what
the results imply for the research question of effective CAN intrusion detection, and discusses
the impact of testing in both simulated and real car environments.
10.0.1
Model performance across different scenarios
Each attack dataset had unique characteristics that influenced how well the models could detect
intrusions. Overall, the supervised learning approaches (GNB and LSTM) showed extremely
high metrics on most attack types, whereas the unsupervised Isolation Forest had more mixed
and perhaps realistic results.
DoS Attacks:
The DoS dataset was characterized by a flood of CAN frames with a low
arbitration ID (0x000) at a constant, high frequency. This created a very clear distinction from
normal traffic. Both GNB detected DoS attacks flawlessly, achieving precision and recall of 1.00
for both normal and attack classes as seen in tables5 and 4. Even a simple classifier like GNB
could perfectly separate the classes, indicating that the DoS attack introduced features that
were trivially distinct. In contrast, the Isolation Forest struggled with DoS, it failed to flag
any of the DoS attack frames13(c).
This likely occurred because the flood of attack frames dominated the dataset to such an extent
that an unsupervised model expecting 10% anomalies did not consider them outliers, effectively,
the attack traffic blended into what it perceived as normal by sheer volume. This is further
supported by the model performing remarkably 0.85% accuracy on my custom DoS dataset,
which aims for a more realistic injection pattern.
Tuning the contamination rate for IF is
challenging, with the contamination fixed at 0.1 (10%), the model was simply not calibrated to
catch a flooding attack that constituted a much larger portion of the bus traffic. Once the attack
frequency exceeds the expected anomaly budget, IF either misclassified many attack frames as
normal or raises the threshold to label some as anomalies, potentially missing the majority. This
result underscores that while DoS attacks are obvious to supervised models, an unsupervised
detector needs struggles with these types of attacks.
Fuzzy (Random) Attacks
Fuzzy injection attacks introduce random CAN frames on various
IDs and data bytes, aiming to produce unexpected inputs. Here, the separation between normal
and attack is less tidy than in DoS because some injected frames might coincidentally use IDs
or data patterns that overlap with legitimate traffic. Accordingly, GNB’s performance dipped
slightly on this dataset (relative to its perfect scores on other attacks). It still achieved high
accuracy (around 97%), but for the attack class GNB’s precision was about 0.88 and recall 0.86.
Practically speaking this means GNB caught most attack frames but did raise a few false alarms
and missed some attacks, likely due to the varied nature of fuzzy attacks. Although the results
of LSTM are put into question, specifically on the fuzzy attack the LSTM did not hit 100%
accuracy, it still performed exceedingly.
The Isolation Forest, interestingly, handled the fuzzy attack relatively well after tuning. IF
achieved perfect recall (1.00) on the fuzzy attacks, meaning it caught all injected frames, al-
though its precision was around 0.60. In other words, it labeled a number of normal frames
as attacks (false positives) but was sensitive enough to mark all true fuzzy intrusions. The
fuzzy attack’s randomness likely produces some frames that are extreme outliers. For example,
completely new CAN IDs or unusual data payloads never seen in normal driving) which IF can

Model evaluations
Gaussian Naive Bayes (GNB):
GNB proved to be a very strong baseline on the HCRL
benchmark datasets, reaching perfect detection scores for DoS, gear spoofing, and RPM spoofing.
In these cases, the attacks were so distinct from normal traffic that even a simple model could
separate them. However, when faced with the custom Opel Astra H DoS dataset, which emulated
a more realistic attack, the limitations of GNB became clear. The model achieved high recall
on the attack class (1.00), but at the expense of very low precision (0.32). In practice, this
means that GNB would detect most attacks but at the cost of constantly raising false alarms
by misclassifying traffic. In a deployed car system, frequent false positives would erode driver
and operator trust in the IDS, potentially leading to alarms being ignored or disabled. The
advantage of GNB is that it is computationally lightweight and easy to train, making it feasible
for embedded ECUs. Still, its simplistic assumptions about feature independence mean that it
cannot generalize well to nuanced or noisy traffic. Overall GNB could not as a standalone model
be used as an IDS.
Isolation Forest (IF):
The Isolation Forest, being unsupervised, offers an appealing advan-
tage for in vehicle IDS: it does not require labeled training data and can, in theory, adapt to
new environments. However, the evaluations showed very mixed outcomes. On structured at-
tacks such as DoS floods or spoofing (gear, RPM), IF often failed to flag any anomalies at all,
achieving near zero recall. This suggests that if an attacker crafts packets that blend into normal
distributions, IF will not recognize them. On the other hand, IF handled fuzzy injection attacks
better, achieving perfect recall, and showed reasonably good accuracy (85%) on the custom DoS
dataset. The model’s effectiveness is tightly coupled to the contamination parameter (set to 0.1),
which governs how many anomalies it expects. In real CAN traffic, the proportion of anomalies
is highly variable and context dependent, making such calibration difficult in practice. In an in
car deployment, this sensitivity could lead to either too many missed attacks (if contamination
is too low) or too many false alarms (if too high). Thus, while IF provides valuable insight into
anomaly detection without labeled data, its reliability in a car system is questionable without
adaptive tuning mechanisms.
Long Short Term Memory (LSTM):
LSTM networks demonstrated the strongest and
most consistent performance across all datasets, including the challenging custom DoS dataset.
After only a few training epochs, the model reached accuracies exceeding 99.9%, indicating that
it can capture the sequential and temporal patterns inherent in CAN traffic. This temporal
modeling is a natural fit for IDS in cars, since many attacks are not just about the content
of frames but about their timing and frequency.
For deployment in a vehicle, LSTMs hold
significant promise. They can distinguish subtle deviations in message sequences that static
models miss. However, challenges remain. LSTMs are computationally heavier than GNB or
IF, which may pose constraints for real time deployment on resource limited ECUs. Despite
these concerns, the results suggest that with careful optimization and validation, LSTM based
IDS could serve as a highly reliable detection layer in modern vehicles, particularly as automotive
ECUs become more powerful.
Summary:
In the context of real world vehicle deployment, the three models represent differ-
ent trade offs. GNB is fast and lightweight but risks overwhelming operators with false alarms
when faced with realistic noise. IF avoids the need for labels but is highly sensitive to con-
figuration, making it less dependable in dynamic traffic conditions. LSTM provides the most
robust detection capability, leveraging temporal dynamics to catch even subtle attack patterns,
but requires greater computational resources and careful generalization testing. Taken together,
these evaluations highlight that while deep learning offers the strongest detection power, simpler
models like GNB and IF may still play a role as complementary systems in car network.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the development and evaluation of intrusion detection systems for au-
tomotive CAN bus networks, addressing critical security vulnerabilities in modern vehicles.
Through comprehensive analysis of both public datasets and real-world vehicle data, the research
provides insights into the effectiveness of different machine learning approaches for detecting var-
ious attack types. The project successfully demonstrated the feasibility of reverse-engineering
CAN signals from a physical vehicle (Opel Astra H) and creating reusable signal interpretation.
This practical experience revealed the challenges of working with real automotive systems, in-
cluding the presence of gateway ECUs and segmented network architectures that limit external
access to critical functions. While full control was achieved in the ICSIM simulation environ-
ment, the physical vehicle tests highlighted important security mechanisms already present in
production vehicles, even in older models from 2008. The creation of a custom DoS attack
dataset with controlled injection parameters addressed a key limitation in existing research,
the lack of realistic attack patterns. By implementing burst-based attacks, the dataset better
represents how real-world attacks might manifest on a CAN bus. This custom dataset proved
invaluable for exposing the limitations of models that performed nearly perfectly on the HCRL
benchmark datasets.
The comparative evaluation of three distinct machine learning models
LSTM, Gaussian Naive Bayes, and Isolation Forest, the evaluation revealed significant perfor-
mance variations across different attack scenarios. LSTM emerged as the most robust solution,
consistently achieving 99% accuracy even on the more challenging custom dataset. Its ability
to capture temporal dependencies makes it particularly well suited for CAN bus intrusion de-
tection, where attack patterns often manifest through timing anomalies. However, this superior
performance comes at the cost of increased computational requirements, potentially limiting de-
ployment on resource-constrained ECUs. Gaussian Naive Bayes, while computationally efficient
and achieving perfect scores on the HCRL datasets, demonstrated significant weaknesses when
faced with realistic attack patterns. The model’s high false positive rate (68%) on the custom
dataset would likely result in frequent false alarms in production systems, potentially leading to
alert fatigue and reduced trust in the IDS. Isolation Forest presented a middle ground, offering
unsupervised detection capabilities without requiring labeled training data. However, its heavy
dependence on the contamination parameter and inconsistent performance across attack types
(failing entirely on certain spoofing attacks while performing well on fuzzy injections) raises
concerns about reliability in dynamic real-world conditions.
A critical finding of this research concerns the characteristics of the widely-used HCRL dataset.
Analysis revealed that attack patterns in this dataset are highly deterministic, with identical
payloads and predictable timing patterns that make classification artificially simple. This dis-
covery underscores the importance of evaluating IDS systems on diverse, realistic datasets that
better represent the complexity of real-world attacks.
This work provides a comprehensive comparison of IDS approaches, highlighting the importance
of realistic attack modeling, and demonstrating the practical challenges of implementing security
measures in real vehicles.
The findings suggest that effective CAN bus security will likely
require a layered approach, combining multiple detection methods to address the diverse threat
landscape facing modern connected vehicles.
As vehicles continue to evolve toward greater
connectivity and autonomy, the security of in-vehicle networks becomes increasingly critical.
This research provides a foundation for developing more sophisticated and practical intrusion
detection systems that can protect against both current and emerging threats to automotive
cybersecurity.
