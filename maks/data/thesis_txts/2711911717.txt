Introduction
Over the last years the world have witnessed a huge leap in development of the artificial
intelligence, and the machine learning in particular. A huge impact on the machine learn┬¡
ing field was made by introduction of such models as AlexNet [1] or gradient boosting
[2], which showed that machine learning models can be efficiently used to solve complex
problems and opened the gates for the rapid development in that area. Some problems,
such as image or voice recognition, media generation, recommender systems, etc. be┬¡
came possible to implement with high efficiency, which was unimaginable before.
However, even while being very efficient, there are still challenges that most of the modern
machine learning models face. Most methods used in the machine learning algorithms
heavily rely on the Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) principle. This assump┬¡
tion is based on the data samples for a given dataset being independent of each other,
and they are identically distributed due to different samples coming from either the same
mathematical function or a model where the underlying processes that generate the data
are equal for each sample. This has been one of the most important principles for ma┬¡
chine learning and its ability to generalize properly. For example, when training a machine
learning model, we wish to have the same type of data with similar distribution for both
the training dataset and the evaluation one. Thus, we expect the data that the model will
be working with to be IID for the model to work correctly with it.
However, in practice, the IID approach is not always applicable. It is practically impossi┬¡
ble to cover all the possible data variation while training a machine learning model, and
many times the real┬¡world data comes out of the IID assumption. In their paper, Bernhard
Sch├Âlkopf et al. discuss the IID and some challenges the machine learning models based
on this assumption face, some of which being weak robustness, problems with learning
reusable mechanisms, and lack of causality perspective. [3]
There are existing solutions as mentioned by Jingkang Yang et al. in their survey, that
face the challenges brought by out of distribution data when dealing with machine learning
algorithms, such as anomaly and novelty detection, outlier detection, and out of distribu┬¡
tion detection methods. [4] However, all these methods for the most part deal with the
destructive impact of out of IID or previously unseen data on the models, but they donÔÇÖt
necessarily change the design or philosophy of these models.
In the same paper, Bernhard Sch├Âlkopf et al. argue that the machine learning models
based on the statistical correlation are weaker compared to the causal models. This is
due to the aforementioned issues as well as the fact that most statistical models just
learn associations and joint distributions of variables they work with without taking into
consideration any underlying processes or real connections between the variables. Of
course, this is the strength of statistical machine learning models in the way that they can
learn on data without any external involvement and approximate it pretty well, however, if
our goal is to develop a system that can be robust and accurate in as many situations as
possible, this approach starts facing significant issues.
Lets consider a simple parabolic function f(y) of some variable x. An example of how
a statistical model can fail is if we train it on a portion of data that lies only in a part of
the function where 0 < x < x _max, where x _max is the maximum value in the training
dataset for the model. A visualization of the function can be seen in the figure below,
where the function is in red. As result, there is a possibility where the model can predict

Background
In this section, I will provide the background and general concepts to fully understand the
definition of the problem and later approaches to solve it. The main purpose here is to
give a reader a general intuition and grasp of concepts of causality, and why I chose to
use causality in this work as opposed to the statistical correlation. Once all the concepts
will be introduced and briefly explained, I will state the scope and the definition of the
problem for this work, as well as introduce the main hypothesis.
2.1
Statistical Correlation and Underlying Issues
As mentioned previously, the classical machine learning algorithms rely heavily on sta┬¡
tistical correlation. And even though it has been proven over the last years how efficient
that approach can be at solving complex tasks, it has some core underlying issues, which
will be explored further.
There are some really ridiculous examples of the statistical correlation and how it can be
unreliable. In the figure below, we can see how the number of people dying by drowning in
a pool is directly correlated to the number of movies starring Nicolas Cage. The correlation
is there, and itÔÇÖs pretty strong, however, I highly doubt that these two things are connected
in any way and itÔÇÖs just a coincidence. However, if we were to submit this data to a machine
learning model and train it, most probably it would agree with what we see on the graph.
Figure 2.1: Example of ridiculous correlations [5]
The two main issues with the correlation are that itÔÇÖs blind and symmetric. The ÔÇØblindnessÔÇØ
of the correlation can be seen in situations where two absolutely unrelated variables or
phenomena all of a sudden show correlation. Usually, examples of this are ridiculous
pseudo correlations that people find, like in the fig. 2.1 figure, however, in the context
of machine learning, this can be tied to two or more variables seemingly correlate, while
not being related to each other at all. This can happen by accident, or by an unbalanced
dataset that is used to train a model. This can result in a machine learning model learning
wrong correlations and being wrongly biased, which will lead to a big impact on perfor┬¡
mance once it starts working with new data without such accidental correlation.

The symmetry issue lies in the fact that with the statistical correlation when two variables
are correlated, changes in either of them will result in a change in the other one. This
means that given two variables X and Y, a change in X will result in a change in Y ac┬¡
cording to how they are correlated, but at the same time, the same change in Y will also
correlate with a change in X. While normally it wouldnÔÇÖt be an issue, when we try to con┬¡
struct models that represent the real world surrounding us as closely as possible, this can
be a problem. An easy intuitive example of why this can be the base of faulty logic can
be this: ÔÇØwhen it rains (X), the ground is wet (Y)ÔÇØ, which is correct, but the opposite is ÔÇØif
the ground is wet (Y), it rains (X)ÔÇØ is not always necessarily true.
2.2
Causality
Now that the issues with the statistical correlation have been described, I will introduce
the concept of causality and some of its advantages. These advantages are the main
argument to using the causality with machine learning models in this work.
A variable X is said to cause variable Y when an intervention in X causes change in Y, but
intervention in Y does not necessarily cause change in X. Coming back to the example
from before, in the context of causality we can say that ÔÇØwhen it rains (X), the ground
is wet (Y)ÔÇØ however, ÔÇØwhen the ground is wet (Y), it does not necessarily mean that itÔÇÖs
raining (X)ÔÇØ. This example involves only two variables, however, it is possible to draw
causation between more variables: maybe there can be other factors that can cause the
ground to be wet or other effects of rain, or maybe we can find some other effects of the
ground being wet, like people slipping and falling more often. We can chain the causes
and effects like that until we have a full picture of the problem and underlying processes.
Compared to the statistical correlation, causality offers a way more robust and complete
picture of how things are.
2.3
Structural Causal Models
Usualy, to describe the causality and causal relations, the Structural Causal Models (SCM)
are used. An SCM consists of two components: Structural Equation Model (SEM) and
Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). SEM is a set of equations that describe every causal re┬¡
lation of an SCM that is related to the variables. An important note on SEM equations
is that they are non┬¡symmetrical, meaning that gives a function /( Y = f(X) /), it cannot
be inverted to find X. The DAG component consists of a directed graph that represents
the causal relationships between variables. The graph is directed, which means that if a
variable X has an edge pointing to a variable Y, this relation is not reversible.
2.4
Interventions
While itÔÇÖs very simple to see if two variables are correlated or not, itÔÇÖs a bit more compli┬¡
cated to see the causality relation between two or more variables. Usually, when we want
to test causality, we can perform empirical tests to see if the cause┬¡effect relationship is
present, but sometimes itÔÇÖs impossible to perform such tests physically.
That is in the context of causality there is a concept of interventions. An intervention is
a forced change in a variable state or a process to see how it reflects on the rest of the
system. For example, coming back to the Nicolas Cage example shown on fig. 2.1, we
can test if there is a causality between these two events. Unfortunately, we canÔÇÖt control
how many people drown in a swimming pool annually. However, if Nicolas Cake retires
and stops starring in movies, chances are the amount of drowned people wonÔÇÖt change.
This can show that these two events donÔÇÖt actually have a cause┬¡effect relationship.

2.5
Confounders
Sometimes, there can be a variable in the causal graph that affects two or more other
variables at the same time. In that case, the parent variable is called confounder. An
example of a graph including a confounder can be found below where X is a confounder
of both A and B variables.
Figure 2.2: An example of a confounder
In the context of causality, the confounders are generally viewed as not desirable for a
couple of reasons. The biggest issue comes up when two variables affected by a single
confounder are statistically correlated, and then itÔÇÖs important to distinguish if this correla┬¡
tion is caused purely by their confounder, or if there is actually some connection between
them besides the confounder. Normally, we can figure this out by our expert knowledge
of the situation or performing interventions, however, a true problem appears when the
confounder is ÔÇØhiddenÔÇØ. Sometimes we might overlook components of the causal struc┬¡
ture of a problem, or maybe we may lack some data for the full picture, so some elements
of the graph may be missing.
A popular example of a such situation is the Ice Cream and Shark Attacks. [6] This exam┬¡
ple shows the direct correlation between how eating ice cream increases your chances
of getting attacked by a shark. The correlation is there, and at first sight, it doesnÔÇÖt make
any sense. And it shouldnÔÇÖt. The cause of the correlation is actually a third factor that
is overlooked in data, which is the season, or temperature. During summer or when itÔÇÖs
warm people tend to eat more ice cream, as well as people swim and dive more, which
leads to more shark attacks. So the consumption of ice cream doesnÔÇÖt cause the sharks
to attack people despite these two variables being correlated. However, both of them are
affected by their confounder which is not included in the data and is hidden.
Situation like this can be really confusing. Going back to the fact that most of the machine
learning algorithms rely on the statistical correlation, if we try to train a model on the data
without the temperature factor, it will just associate the ice cream consumption with the
shark attacks without a second thought. This shows how the problem of confounders is
very significant and how the current statistical machine learning models are vulnerable to

2.6
Problem Statement and Scope
The main objective of this project is to try to solve or improve the issues related to the sta┬¡
tistical machine learning models that were mentioned before by introducing the causality
to the machine learning concepts. The way it will be done is by designing the architecture
of machine learning models around the causal structure of a problem. A similar approach
was described by Di Wu and G. Gary Wang [7] in their work where they describe an archi┬¡
tecture of an artificial neural network based on the causal relations. This approach and
the details of model implementation will be discussed in further chapters.
The main hypothesis of this work is that the causal models are more robust to
the data distribution shifts and have better performance when working with out┬¡of┬¡
distribution data. To test this hypothesis, I will describe, develop and train a number of
causal models, as well as a number of non┬¡causal models that are based on statistical
correlation. Then, I will perform a number of equal tests with out of distribution data and
compare the performance of all the models to prove or reject the proposed hypothesis.
The further work is split into stages, and the hypothesis will be tested using a prototype
model with a proof of concept problem, and later with a more complex problem. The
complex model will be split into simplified and full versions, with different models being
developed for both of them. In the end, all the models and their performance will be
compared to draw conclusions.

3.2
Model and Causal Artificial Neural Network
As a prototype causal model, IÔÇÖve decided to use a Causal Artificial Neural Network (C┬¡
ANN) described in the paper by Di Wu and G. Gary Wang. [7] They describe an archi┬¡
tecture of an artificial neural network that is based on the causal structure of a problem.
The causal structure is represented with SCM, and the architecture is based on the DAG
component of the SCM, which is a causal graph of the problem. The causal structure can
be either deducted by expert knowledge of the underlying processes, or by causal dis┬¡
covery methods. Since I will be using a simulator where all the processes are predefined
and known, the causal structure of the problem is defined by this knowledge in this case.
Once the causal structure is defined and known, a C┬¡ANN can be constructed. ThereÔÇÖs
a key difference between the C┬¡ANN and a regular ANN: while the regular ANN is a
singular network that takes all the input variables at once, passes them through hidden
layers, and predicts the output variables, the C┬¡ANN consists of multiple subnetworks that
produce the intermediate and target variables. Each subnetwork is based on a singular

causal relationship in the causal structure of the problem, where the input variables of
a subnetwork are causes in a causal relationship, and the output variables are effects.
Then, the outputs of subnetworks are passed forward according to the causal structure,
where in case an output of one subnetwork is a cause for another causal relationship in
the causal graph, it is used as an input for the respective subnetwork. The data is passed
forward through the subnetworks based on the causal structure until the target variables
are reached. This architecture fits the purposes of this work perfectly. It is following the
causal structure of the problem, and its architecture is directly connected to that structure.
We have already defined the SCM which contains DAG for the simulator in fig. 3.1, which
can be used to define the architecture of the model. Altogether, the C┬¡ANN will consist of
3 subnetworks for a and V, which are intermediate variables, and for x, which is the tar┬¡
get variable. The structure of each of the subnetworks is following the respective causal
relation in the causal structure of the problem. The data is propagated through the sub┬¡
networks in such a way that a single network takes in all the required input parameters
and produces an output variable, which is passed as an input to the following subnetwork
until the target variable is reached. The target variable is the output of the entire C┬¡ANN
network.
3.3
Scope of the Problem
The main goal of this stage is to test the theory that a causal model is more robust to
changes in the distribution of data, and that the modular structure of the model that is
based on the causal graph would perform better than a regular non┬¡causal neural network.
To test this theory, I will construct a dataset with the kinematics simulator and train both
models using it.
After training the training process, the original dataset will be modified and the models will
be evaluated using it. The change IÔÇÖve decided to make is to shift both x0 and x variables
by adding the same value dx to them. That results in the distribution in data being changed
for both of those variables, but the processes, the rest of the variables, and the distance
traveled overall will remain unchanged. Essentially, as a test, the reference point of the
coordinate system will be shifted, but the object in the coordinate system and its behavior
will remain the same as in the original data. This is a perfect test since there is no need
to generate any new data or change any processes, and it can clearly demonstrate the
effects of data distribution changes on the performance of the models. The shifts will be
randomized across the dataset rows but will be the same per sample for both x0 and x.
Both models will receive the same data for both training and testing. The comparison
of the performance of the models will be made by matching the performance scores of
training loss, evaluation, and the altered data performances. The metrics to measure the
performance will be R2 metrics.

4.1.1
Modifications on the Original Simulator
For the purpose of this thesis, some modifications have been made to the original sim┬¡

in the form of milk being added to the coffee, which essentially means that a colder liq┬¡

The addition of milk to the coffee is mentioned in the paper by Downey when he talks
about the scope of the problem, however, it is not implemented or taken into account later

an intervention that drops the temperature outside the scope of the cooling cycle.
Then, instead of returning just temperature data as in the original version, the simulator
now also returns the all data that participates in the process, like environmental temper┬¡

4.2
Causal Approximation Models
The first model I will test will be similar to the one used with the kinematics simulator,
and will be used on a causal structure of the problem. However, since there is a cyclic
process in the simulation, the causal models, like C┬¡ANN, would be difficult to apply in this
case. That is why for this stage IÔÇÖve decided to simplify the process, and instead of using
a model to predict every step of the process and temperature change, I will use models
to approximate the temperature at certain time intervals that are defined by the causal
structure of the problem.
Of course, this approach skips many steps and is not able to predict temperatures at
intermediate steps of the cooling process, but for the purpose of this thesis, the scope
would be limited to predicting the final temperature after the cooling process given the
input variables. The main goal is not to recreate the accurate simulation or recreate the
underlying processes, but rather to approximate the end result and compare different
approaches and see how they perform compared to each other.
4.2.1
Causal Structure of the Problem
The simulator gives us a very detailed picture of what is going on at every time step of the
cooling process, however, the overall cooling process can be broken down into two sub┬¡
processes: the cooling of the liquid determined by the equation eq. (4.2) and the addition
of milk determined by the equation eq. (4.3). The milk is added at a random point in the
cooling process t _inter. From that, in the current scope we can split the entire process into
three parts: cooling before adding milk, the addition of milk, the cooling after adding milk
till the end. This split requires the introduction of a new intermediate temperature variable
Temp _before _inter which is the result of the first part and represents the temperature
right before adding the milk to the coffee.
Apart from that, since at this stage we are not dealing with sequential models, we can
treat the time variables t _inter and t as regular input variables for the models. However,
since the whole process has been split into three parts, two of which are the actual cooling
sub┬¡processes, the total time variable t becomes obsolete as an input variable due to not
being directly associated with any of the sub┬¡processes. Instead, a new variable t _rem
has been introduced for the third sub┬¡process which represents the time remaining after
adding the milk. This variable is calculated by simply subtracting time of intervention t
_inter from the total time of the process t.

4.2.2
Baseline and Non┬¡Causal Models
To see if the causal models actually perform better and if they are more robust compared
to the conventional approach, we need to train both types of models on the same data
and perform a set of equal tests that involve some kind of distribution shift. There will be
three baseline models for this stage that will be trained on the same data. The method
of choosing these baseline models is trying to pick ones that would potentially solve the
problem outside the scope of causality, and at least one model that will most certainly fail
during the tests to make sure that the other models have an adequate performance by
comparing them to this failing baseline model.
Linear Regression
The first baseline model that will be used for this stage is linear regression. Linear re┬¡
gression is a pretty weak model to approximate complex data. While linear regression
might fit the training data relatively well in some cases, its generalization capabilities are
extremely low. This is pronounced even more when we are dealing with distribution shifts
in data that a linear regression model hasnÔÇÖt been trained on.
The reason behind choosing a linear regression as a baseline model is that we expect it
to perform extremely poorly on the validation data and the experiments with distribution
shifts with the data. We expect all the other models to perform at least better than the
linear regression as a sanity check.
Artificial Neural Network
The second baseline model is going to be a fully connected ANN. ANN models are known
to be very good at generalizing data, so we assume that they would be a good competitor

The ANN model would take in all the input variables from the data and try to predict the

4.2.3
Causal Models
Apart from the classical models, two causal models will be trained and then compared.
When selecting the causal models, I tried to pick ones that would be direct counterparts
to the non┬¡causal models, but it would also be interesting to see how they perform against
every non┬¡causal model regardless of their relation.
Causal Artificial Neural Network
The first model is going to be a C┬¡ANN that works on the same principle as in the previous
chapter with the kinematics simulator. We know the causal structure of the problem and
its DAG from fig. 4.1, so we can design the architecture of the network and its subnet┬¡
works according to that. The architecture of the C┬¡ANN model for the current problem will
consist of three subnetworks in total. Each of them calculates the temperature at different
intervals of time, which are before the milk addition, after the milk addition, and the final
temperature.
The first subnetwork is responsible for calculating the temperature all the way up to the
milk addition point. The input variables are Temp _0, t _inter, Temp _env, r, and the
output is Temp _before _inter. Then the output of the first subnetwork is passed to the
second one, which is associated with the milk addition. Apart from the temperature input,
the second subnetwork takes in vol, Temp _milk, vol _milk parameters, and the output is
Temp _inter. Then the final subnetwork takes in the same parameters as the first one,
except instead of Temp _0 it takes the output of the second subnetwork, and the time
is a substitute for the t _rem variable. The output of the third subnetwork is the final
temperature Temp _end, which is the target variable.
C┬¡ANN is able to use the same datasets as all the other non┬¡causal models, but it has to
be split into parts according to the inputs of each subnetwork of the model. Thus, it would
be fair to compare it with the non┬¡causal models, and it allows for fair testing.

So far IÔÇÖve been dealing with a very simplified causal structure of the coffee simulator,
and all the models used so far only approximate the target variable or certain intermedi┬¡

The cyclic process which makes this problem complex and interesting in the first place

4.3.1
Cyclic Causal Structure of the Problem
The previous causal structure was very simplified and treated the cyclic processes inside
the simulator as one big step: from the start to the milk addition, and then from the milk
addition to the end. This has worked perfectly well for the models described so far, and
theyÔÇÖve managed to achieve a decent performance even with simplified structure and data
related to it. This time, I will define the full structure that includes the cycles.
The full structure is very similar to the one already described in the previous stage. We can
still split the entire process into three parts: the cooling before the milk addition, the milk
addition and temperature change after the mixture, and the cooling after the milk addition
which results in the final temperature. However, this time instead of treating the cooling
process as one big step, we will treat it as a continuous cyclic process. The process is
cyclic since the temperature for the next time step depends on the temperature in the
current time step and the temperature of the environment and the heat coefficient r, as
seen in eq. (4.2). The milk addition process remains unchanged.
This new structure requires the introduction of two new variables Temp _before and Temp
_after that represent the cyclic process of cooling and change accordingly for each time
step. Practically, we can say that itÔÇÖs one variable that represents the current temperature
of the coffee, but for the simplicity and for the purpose of the causal structure IÔÇÖve decided
to split it into two aforementioned variables.
In the previous stage all the models have been dealing with only the general data, but this
time the time series dataset with the temperature at every time step will be used as well.
This will give the new models the opportunity to deal with the cyclic processes.

with inclusion of expanded inputs. The middle block is responsible for the milk addition
and is essentially a fully connected ANN subnetwork and it works in the same fashion as
the one already described in the C┬¡ANN architecture. The middle block doesnÔÇÖt deal with

4.3.6
Segmented ANN
One potential problem that can occur when using RNN as a model or part of a model is that
it might happen that RNN only learns the sequence, which would lead to the model always
predicting the temperature difference with the same rate, no matter the input. That can
lead to a huge problem where the model canÔÇÖt adjust to the extreme shifts in the starting
temperature or other conditions where the rate of temperature change drastically changes
from the typical one.
To address these potential issues, I will introduce the last model for this stage, which is
called Segmented Artificial Neural Network (SegANN). This model combines the capabil┬¡
ity of RNN to work with time series data and approximation from models from the previous
stage, such as C┬¡ANN. The core idea behind this model is that instead of using an RNN to
predict every step of the process, a number of connected ANN subnetworks will be used
to predict the temperature at certain predefined time intervals.
The way this architecture works is by dividing the total time of the process t into small
intervals t i and assigning each ANN block to the respective interval. In the scope of
the current problem, it is established that the time of the process t in every simulation is
always 40 sec. From that, the total time has been split in 5s intervals, meaning there are
8 ANN blocks in total in this model, each responsible for time intervals from 0 sec to 5
sec, then from 5 sec to 10 sec, etc.
The input to an interval block is the temperature Temp i, the environmental temperature
Temp _env, the heat coefficient r, and the output is the temperature at the end of the time
interval Temp i+1. The input for the first block is always the starting temperature Temp _0
and the output for the last block is always the final temperature Temp _end, which is the
target variable.
In addition, there is one ÔÇØfloatingÔÇØ ANN block that is responsible for the milk addition and
is similar to the same block in C┬¡RNN and C┬¡ANN models. It is floating because the milk
is added at a random time for each sample in the dataset, and itÔÇÖs impossible to define its
28

position in advance. The data is passed through the floating block between the interval
blocks when t _inter is between time intervals t i and t i+1 for the respective interval blocks.
That way the floating block ÔÇØinterceptsÔÇØ the data when itÔÇÖs propagated forward between
the interval blocks. Of course, most of the time, the moment of the milk addition t _inter
will be inside the time intervals and the actual time of the floating block prediction will be
different, but since this model estimates the temperature at the time intervals, we can say
itÔÇÖs also an estimation.
The training process of such architecture is done in clusters of data grouped by the time
of the milk addition t _inter. The data is grouped up in chunks where t _inter lies inside
intervals, in the case of the current model, between 0 sec and 5 sec, then between 5
sec and 10 sec, etc. This is done to enable the model to train on batched data with the
inclusion of the floating block. As mentioned before, the milk addition for every sample is
random, thus without grouping, the training would have to be done sample┬¡by┬¡sample so
it would be able to pass through the floating block in between appropriate intervals.

Discussion and Conclusions
Causal and Non┬¡Causal Models Comparison
When comparing the results of all the experiments across all the models used with the
coffee simulator, both approximating and cyclic, we can notice certain trends in the perfor┬¡
mance of the models. Apart from the baseline models, which were the linear regression
in the approximation stage and the base RNN in the cyclic stage, in most cases, the reg┬¡
ular ANN has the worst performance. In most experiments, ANN is outperformed by all
the models, both approximating and the cyclic. The regular gradient boosting in some
experiments has similar or even better results than the rest of the models, but overall its
performance is far from the best. This means that the non┬¡causal approximating models
are in general the worst models in this work. The same applies to the base RNN model,
which in some cases performed better than the ANN and the gradient boosting, and in
some worse, but the results for the RNN are far inferior compared to both approximating
and the cyclic causal models.
This result leads us to a very crucial conclusion: in the scope of the current work and
experiments conducted to measure how well the models can adjust to the changes in data
distribution, all non┬¡causal models in general have worse performance than all the causal
ones. This means that the main hypothesis in the frame of this work is confirmed.
This was also confirmed by the proof of concept models using the kinematics simulator.
Approximating and Cyclic Models Comparison
Between the causal models, there were two types developed and tested: approximating
and cyclic models, where the SegANN is both cyclic and approximating in nature. From
the compiled results, itÔÇÖs difficult to tell precisely which type of model is superior. How┬¡
ever, in every experiment at least one of the approximating models including the SegANN

Limitations of the Work
The main objective of this work is completed successfully: the causal and non┬¡causal
models were developed, tested, and compared, which allowed us to see a trend in perfor┬¡
mance and test the main hypothesis proposed at the start of the paper with further proof
backed by the results of experiments. However, when it comes to the direct comparison
of all the models one by one and trying to pick the best performing one, it can get quite
difficult to do that. I tried my best to make the conditions and experiments as fair and equal
as possible for each model, but a big factor in the performance is also the implementation
details of the models, such as the hyperparameters, training process, external parame┬¡
ters like the learning rate, and so on. As mentioned before, my goal wasnÔÇÖt to develop the
perfect models that can ideally solve the problem, but rather to develop models that can
adequately predict the data and see a general trend.
Another limitation comes from the coffee simulator itself, especially for the cyclic models.
While having cyclic processes is a complex task to solve, cooling is still a single process
that is repeated multiple times. The addition of milk made things a bit more complex, but
that totals to two process types. This leads to the causal structure being relatively simple,
which limits the scale of the causal modelsÔÇÖ application. That is why it was important for
me to note that the results for certain models are true for the scope of this project, but may
vary for other problems. Despite that, I was able to capture a trend in model performance
that helped to prove the main hypothesis, and I believe it would stay true for most of the
problems.
