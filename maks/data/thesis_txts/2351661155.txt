Abstract
As the ships become larger and more ﬂexible they can no longer be regarded as ﬂexible bodies
when computing their response to water waves since the natural frequencies of their hulls are in
the same range as the incoming wave frequency. The traditional rigid body analysis must be ex-
tended to ﬂexible modes in order to capture the physical behavior. The objective of this master
project was to extend the ﬁnite diﬀerence linear potential ﬂow solver OceanWave3D-Seakeeping1
to a ﬂexible body solver using the generalized modes formulation for both the zero speed and
forward speed problem.
The program was validated using three test cases chosen based on the limitations of OceanWave3D-
Seakeeping: A ﬂexible barge at zero speed in head seas, a ﬂexible prismatic barge at zero speed
in head seas validated against experimental data and ﬁnally a modiﬁed Wigley hull at diﬀerent
forward speeds in head seas. The latter enabled a study of the eﬀect of forward speed on the hy-
drodynamic coeﬃcients which showed that forward speed have a large inﬂuence on the solution
to the ﬂexible radiation problem and a negligible eﬀect on the ﬂexible diﬀraction problem.

Introduction
In order to assess structural strength and fatigue life of a ship hull the loads acting on the ship
during operation must be determined and modelled during the design phase. Which loads to
consider and how is depending on the ship type. For most cargo ships it is still suﬃcient to assess
the fatigue life of the hull structure using either empirical formulations based on a structural
analysis or by ﬁrst making a hydrodynamic load and motion analysis for the rigid ship which is
then used as an input in a Finite Element Analysis according to Class Societies2.
However with the development within shipbuilding in the previous years with ever-larger ships
(especially container ships) the hull structures become so ﬂexible that their natural frequencies
are so low that they can be excited by the periodic impact of the waves, <0.5 Hz. This inﬂuences
both the fatigue life of the vessel and the comfort onboard3. Then it is no longer appropriate to
use the simple approach where the hydrodynamic and structural analyses are carried out separ-
ately. The ﬂuid-structure interaction is instead best modelled using a hydroelastic approach.
There are the usual three ways to obtain a hydroelastic response: Numerical simulations, model
tests and full scale measurements. Model tests are expensive and are hence limited in the num-
ber of cases. Full scale measurements are limited by the number of operating conditions which
in itself can be diﬃcult to measure (e.g. problems with determining speed through water) and
by measurement diﬃculties when it comes to isolating the mode shapes. These issues in turn
limit the development of numerical tools due to lack of real-life validation.
There are diﬀerent approaches to solving the hydroelastic problem numerically. The ultimate
modelling of hydroelasticity is obtained using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) with a
Finite Element Modelling (FEM) coupling. But since the analysis requires 3-5,000 regular wave
cases and several diﬀerent combinations of ship speeds and wave headings the method is not ap-
plicable for routine work in the design process due to the computational time. Instead a method
such as generalized modes where the number of degrees of freedom are extended to several more
ﬂexible modes in the hydrodynamic problem based on potential theory can be used.
In this thesis the generalized modes will be implemented as an extension to the linear po-
tential ﬂow time-domain seakeeping solver, OceanWave3D-Seakeeping, which was developed by

The validity of using generalized modes to represent the ﬂexible deformation of structures was
presented by Newman in [4] where it was concluded that the superposition of the response of
the mode shapes obtained from an analysis of the ship structure outside of the water were suﬃ-
ciently accurate. The implementation is done both for the zero and forward speed case. The zero
speed implementation will enable validation using a benchmark from the commercial software
WAMIT developed at MIT. The forward speed solver will make it possible to address the eﬀects
of forward speed on the hydroelastic added mass and damping of a ship hull.
The OceanWave3D-Seakeeping solver is based on high-order ﬁnite diﬀerence schemes on an
overlapping grid. This makes it diﬀerent from other state of the art methods which are all based
on panel methods. This diﬀerence is considered throughout the report.
The report is structured as follows: The ﬁrst chapter introduces the concept of hydroelasticity
and generalized modes and presents the theoretical background. The second chapter describes
the implementation of generalized modes into the OceanWave3D-Seakeeping solver and the al-
gorithmic framework used in that respect. The third chapter validates the implementation by
comparing the solutions obtained to ﬁrst WAMIT and then experimental results for a prismatic
ﬂexible barge at zero speed. The fourth chapter presents the hydrodynamic coeﬃcients at dif-
ferent forward speeds for a modiﬁed Wigley hull. Future work is discussed at the end of the
thesis.

Chapter 1
Theoretical Background
This chapter is comprised of two parts. First the history and theoretical formulation of the
hydroelastic extension is presented. Second the mathematical formulations on which the
OceanWave3D-Seakeeping solver are based are summarized. The latter is relevant for a later
comparison between solutions obtained from diﬀerent solvers as well as for the perspective of
implementation in real life as discussed towards the end of the thesis.
Hydroelasticity refers to the study of the interaction between a deformable body and the sea-
state it is operating in.
The interaction occurs since the ﬂuid pressure acting on the body
changes its dynamics and hence its resulting motions and distortions which again changes the
pressure ﬁeld. In other words a coupling between a structural analysis and hydrodynamic ana-
lysis is required to obtain a solution to a hydroelastic problem which is present when the ﬁrst
natural frequencies of the hull structure are in the same range as the impacting wave frequencies.
The overall methodology of a hydroelastic analysis is outlined in ﬁgure 1.0.1. Here it can be seen
that the analysis needs three inputs: The geometrical model of the hull, the mass distribution
and a structural model. These are then used in three diﬀerent blocks - dry natural frequencies
and mode shapes are computed from the structural model and this block is subsequently used
in both the hydrostatics and the hydrodynamics block. Finally the wet natural frequencies and
modes are obtained by combining the results from the aforementioned blocks. These are the
natural frequencies of the hull in operation and can hence be validated against full scale or model
test experiments.
Both the input and the computation blocks can be varied as described in the following two
sections.
The history of ﬂuid-structure interaction modeling is outlined in section 1.1 where the current
state of the art is presented as well. Furthermore an overview useful for choosing the correct con-
ﬁguration of the analysis is presented. Next the theoretical formulation of generalized modes -
the method used for modeling the ﬂuid-structure interaction in the present project - is presented
in section 1.2.

1.1
Litterature Review
1.1.a
Historical Background and State of the Art
As described by S. E. Hirdaris in [7] the study of hydroelasticity began in the late 1970s when
Bishop and Price introduced their new numerical model to the naval architect community in
their book [2]. Their model used beam theory to respresent the ship’s structure by a few dry
natural modes and strip theory to represent the ﬂuid.
The next leap was made by the same group in the 1980s when a 3D panel model was developed
to facilitate the analysis of non-beamlike marine structures such as catamarans and monopiles
at zero speed. The mode shapes were computed using Finite Element Modeling (FEM) and the
hydrodynamics were computed in a 3D potential ﬂow analysis using a pulsating source distribu-
tion over the hull.
The increase in computer power created the momentum needed for further research. Amongst
these were Newman who in the early 1990s introduced generalized modes as a method for com-
puting the ﬂexible response of ﬂoating structures, [3] and [4]. This approach is currently being
extended to forward speed problems by e.g. Kashiwagi in [14] using both a 3D time-domain
4

Green-function method and a frequencydomain Rankine panel method. The diﬀerence between
Kashiwagi’s approach for representing the ﬂuid and the approach used in this thesis is described
in section 1.1.c.
Finally as described in the introduction a new leap is in the making as well.
The state of
the art within the research of hydroelasticity is to use 3D Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD)
or Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes (RANS) solvers in direct combination with FEM calcula-
tions. This will enable the analysis of non-linear eﬀects. However this approach is very costly
computationally so the industry will beneﬁt from simpler models which can be used in the early
design stages, as described in the next section.

1.1.c
Linear Potential Flow Solver
From ﬁgure 1.1.1 it can further be concluded that a linear potential ﬂow model is suﬃcient for
respresenting the hydrodynamic problem at the early design stage of normal ship hulls. Now the
ﬁnal choice is which approach to use to solve this problem as described in section 1.1.a with two
methods currently being used: The panel method or the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver used in this thesis.
The panel method is described by Newman in [6]. It is also known as the boundary integ-
ral equation method (BIEM) and the basis is a form of Green’s theorem where the velocity
potential everywhere in the ﬂuid is represented by surface distributions of singularities over the
boundary surfaces which is divided into panels. This leads to an integral equation which must
be solved for the unknown source strength or dipole moment on each panel. From here the
pressure is evaluated and integrated to compute the forces and moments.
The ﬁnite diﬀerence solver discretizes the entire ﬂuid domain and solve the continuity equa-
tion by approximating the derivatives. The theory behind is presented in section 1.2.a and the
numerical implementation in 2.1. So unlike the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver - where the entire domain
has to be discretized - the only part of the domain that needs to be discretized for the panel
method is the body surface and sometimes the free surface.

1.2
Formulation of the Problem
This section will after a short introduction to generalized modes ﬁrst describe the hydrodynamic

Chapter 2
Extension of the OceanWave3D-Seakeeping Solver
The generalized modes computation was implemented as an extension to the existing linear
potential ﬂow ﬁnite diﬀerence solver OceanWave3D-Seakeeping developed by Mostafa Amini Af-
shar during his ph.d. studies at DTU [1]. The framework used for the solver is the open source
framework Overture which consists of C++libraries for solving partial diﬀerential equations on
overlapping grids and has a built-in overlapping grid generator Ogen. Both the Overture lib-
rary and Ogen will be described in more detail in section 2.3.
The algorithm was implemented successfully using the following 7-step procedure which was
employed to minimize possible causes of errors for the ﬁnal validation. The steps are as follows:
1. Input generalized mass and stiﬀness matrix plus extra mode in input ﬁle
2. Allocate space for extra mode (modeled after rigid mode) in computation and output
3. Create function for computing mode shape
4. Implement above function in program
5. Validate against WAMIT zero-speed computation
6. Add forward speed: M-terms
7. Extend to more modes
This chapter is organized such that each proceeding section will describe the solver in more
detail. The sections 2.2 and 2.3.b contains essential information for understanding the conclu-
sions in the rest of the report.
The ﬁrst section introduces the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver. The next section outlines both the ori-
ginal code and the extension to the it using a pseudocode representation. Then the Overture
library is described with an emphasis on the overlapping grid generator Ogen.

2.1
Finite Diﬀerence Implementation
A complete overview of the numerical tools used in the solver is given in section 3.2 of [1].
The three-dimensional continuity equation is approximated using a fourth-order ﬁnite diﬀerence
scheme leading to a linear system of equations on the form:
Aφu = b
(2.1)
Here A contains the ﬁnite-diﬀerence coeﬃcients for both the continuity equations and the
boundary conditions, φu is the desired solution consisting of the velocity potentials and b con-
tains the forcing from the boundary conditions since the continuity equation equals zero. The
ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme requires a grid that covers the entire domain as opposed to panel meth-
ods where only the body surface is discretized.
A centered ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme is used in this solver. The ghost point method has been
used at the boundary of the domain to be able to use the same stencil everywhere.
Three boundary conditions are needed as described in section 1.2.a: A homogeneous Neumann at
the sea bed, a non-homogeneous Neuman at the body surface and a non-homogeneous Dirichlet
boundary condition at the free surface. The body and the free surface boundary condition are
time dependent. But whereas the body boundary condition is known ahead of simulation from
the impulse described in 1.2.a the velocity potential for the free surface boundary condition is
obtained using the fourth-order Runge-Kutta time integration scheme.
2.2
Structure of the Solver
The OceanWave3D-Seakeeping solver is divided into two parts: A RUN part where the boundary
value problem is solved in time domain as described in 1.2.a and 2.1 and a POST part where
the transform to the frequency domain and the subsequent post-processing is carried out. The
interface between these two solvers consists of the binary output ﬁles from the RUN execution
and the input options ﬁle with corresponding extraction of data.
The solver can handle both 2D and 3D problems at either zero or forward speed. Unfortu-
nately a current limitation to the solver is that it can only compute results for head or following
seas.

FirstOrder
The time-domain solutions - consisting of the ﬁrst-order velocity potential and the surface elev-

2.3
Overture Library
The classes and functionalities of the Overture library that have been used in the Ocean-
Wave3DSeakeeping solver are described in the sections below.
2.3.a
Arrays
All solutions in the solver are created as A++/P++arrays since that means that the programmers
do not have to worry about allocation and de-allocation of memory and the data is more easily
accessible using the indixes (i,j,k).
2.3.b
Grids and Mappings
The overlapping grid is generated and analyzed using ﬁve diﬀerent tools. First a CAD program
such as Rhinoceros is used to deﬁne the surface of the geometry and exporting it as an IGS
ﬁle. Second the rap program from Overture is used to clean up the CAD model and build a
global triangulation of the geometry. Third the Mappingbuilder class is used in the program
Mbuilder to construct component grids using hyperbolic mapping. The hyperbolic mapping
generate volume grids by marching from a given reference curve and using geometrical boundary
conditions such as a ﬁxed maximum vertical coordinate. The mappings should have physical
overlaps in order to complete the next step.
Fourth the overlapping grid is generated using Ogen by reading in the grids generated by the
previous step as well as creating the ﬂuid domain which in this case is deﬁned as a cylinder to
save up to 1/2 −2/31 computational time compared to a rectangular mesh. The resolution for
each mapping is deﬁned and it is possible to stretch each grid to have a ﬁner resolution at near
the body and at the bow and stern of a vessel. The boundary conditions or each mapping is set
here as well with the options being: Periodic (Value less than zero), Interpolation (Value zero)
1Estimate given by Mostafa Amini Afshar.
19
==PAGE:29==
and Physical boundary such as an exciting surface or a wall (Value greater than zero). A share
ﬂag is used for each boundary for each mapping to specify which boundaries are coinciding.
Finally the grid is saved. The ﬁfth and ﬁnal step is to evaluate the grid using Ogen’s plotStuﬀ
to output the grid statistics.
For each component grid the Overture Mapping classes are used to transform the physical
grid to a unit cube (or square in the 2D case) with uniformly spaced mesh elements. This is
useful for the ﬁnite-diﬀerence discretization schemes since the ﬁnite-diﬀerence coeﬃcients are
constant on a grid with uniform spacing for each stencil.

Operators
This class builds the coeﬃcient matrix for the combined Laplacian operator and boundary
conditions.

3.1.a
Description of WAMIT
WAMIT is a radiation and diﬀraction computer program for analyzing the eﬀect of ocean waves
on ﬂoating or submerged bodies based on linear and mean second-order potential ﬂow theory.
The radiation and diﬀraction potentials on the wetted surface of the body are computed using
Green’s theorem with the free-surface source-potential as the Green’s function. The solution
is computed using a 3D panel method with either a low-order (the geometry is represented by
quadrilateral panels with constant velocity potential on each panel) or a high-order method (the
geometry can be represented in multiple ways and the velocity potential is represented by con-
tinuous B-splines). The higher-order solution will be used for comparison in this section.
The input ﬁles necessary for running the test case can be found in E. The input that needs
to be speciﬁed by the user is the same as for OceanWave3D-Seakeeping which will be presented
in section 3.1.c.

Discussion
Comparison to Panel Methods
The beneﬁts of the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver OceanWave3D-Seakeeping are not obvious when com-
paring the computational eﬀort and accuracy of the simple test cases presented in this thesis.
The simple rectangular barge for instance takes a few minutes to set up in WAMIT and the
same time is used to compute the ﬁrst eleven modes using a ﬁne resolution for the evaluated
frequencies.
In comparison generating grids in Ogen is very complicated and includes a ﬁve-step approach
with the consequence that it can take weeks to generate a good grid even after you have worked
with the programs for a long time. And computationally: A grid with a 100,000 point dimension
(which is the limit for the direct solver) uses approximately 4 hours per mode and has to be
run on a cluster to avoid memory errors. Furthermore the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver is unstable at
sharp corners in the geometry so they will have to be rounded before generating the grid creating
an inherent deviation from reality from the beginning. Ogen can currently only create proper
ghost points inside the body if the walls of the body are perpendicular to the free surface in the
water plane. This has not been a problem with the three computed cases but in this report will
be a problem if the solver were to be applied to a real ship with diﬀerent loading conditions.
Finally the evaluation of the three surface integrals over the mean wetted surface to obtain
the displacement of the submerged body shows a relatively large deviation in the output com-
pared to WAMIT. This in turn aﬀects the center of gravity where the displacement is used in
the computation.
Of course it is not quite fair to compare the computational time to WAMIT since the pro-
gram can only consider zero speed problems.
The advantage is expected to be found when
computing the response of complex structures at forward speed where a high spatial resolution
is required. Here the panel methods are at a disadvantage since they have to interpolate between
all panels on the body surface whereas the grid points in the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver only inter-
polates between nearest neighbors leading to more zero elements. So even though the matrix is
smaller for the panel method it scales badly at high resolution grids.
44
==PAGE:54==
Validity of Generalized Modes Results
As mentioned in the conclusion of the literature review the motivation for creating a general-
ized modes solver lies in the application of the tool in the early design phases of large ﬂexible
vessels where only the global loads are of relevance and not the local stresses. Disregarding the
geometrical inaccuracies and large computational time described in above section the method
still faces challenges in general. The problems with obtaining good benchmark cases from model
testing and full scale measurements makes it diﬃcult to validate the code to such an extent that
you trust the output as a basis for decision making of design changes early on. This method
uses the assumption of a uniform mass and stiﬀness distribution which is an approximation at
best. It could be interesting to see an extensive – experimental – study of the eﬀect of especially
stiﬀness distribution and vertical center of gravity on the ﬂexible responses.
45
==PAGE:55==
Conclusion
It has been possible to extend the potential ﬂow ﬁnite diﬀerence solver Oceanwave3D-Seakeeping
to include ﬂexible motion for both zero speed and forward speed. Creating the grids necessary
for validating and applying the code proved to be a prolonged aﬀair that has impacted the
compliance to the relevant benchmarks.
Two other sources of inaccuracy are present: One
comes from the grid generator which shows inconsistencies in volume evaluation the other comes
from the limitation for the ﬁnite diﬀerence solver to have well-rounded mesh corners.
Future Work
The two low-hanging fruits would be to extend the solver to solve for multiple wave heading
angles and to set the solver up for parallel programming for the diﬀerent modes in the RUN
part. Also, an iterative solver should be implemented to reduce memory requirements and CPU
time.
To keep up with the state of the art in the industry it could be interesting to investigate the
possibility for including non-linear eﬀects in both the hydrodynamic and the structural part.
And for the structural part an extensive study of mass and stiﬀness distribution coupled to
hydroelastic experiments could help with the validation of the approach.
Finally a written guide to mesh generation in Ogen would be an invaluable asset for future
projects.
