PREFACE 
“Without decisive action, energy-related greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions would lead 
to considerable climate degradation with an average 6°C global warming” [1]. 
 
Denmark has a goal for a fossil free energy sector by 2050 and to reach this goal it is 
assumed that there is a need for a wide variation of sustainable energy technologies, 
solar cells being one of them. Photovoltaic (PV) technology is a sustainable energy in 
high growth and with its competitiveness getting better it is expected to have a signifi-
cant influence in the future electricity system. In Technology Road Map – Solar Photo-
voltaic Energy from 2014 the prediction for PV technologies share of global electricity 
will rise from 11% up to 16% by 2050 [1]. 
The price for solar energy plant and large integrated systems are dropping, this tendency 
seems to continue the following years to come. The efficiency of the technology will 
increase as well, and this will enhance the economic benefit of installing PV systems.   
 
This thesis has been developed in the period August 2017 to January 2018 and is based 
on solar technology theory and Copenhagen international school (CIS) in nordhavn. 
Copenhagen and Denmark are focusing on sustainable energy sources here solar energy 
is one of them. Therefore, it is very interesting to do research and investigate vertical 
building integrated PV systems as installed at CIS. Studying sustainable energy, it has 
been an interesting process to work with this subject and a building as CIS with such a 
large complex integrated PV system. I would like to thank all the many people that I 
have been in contact with and EnergyLab Norhavn which is working with Norhavn as a 
research area. Also, a thanks to my supervisor Per Nørgaard for motivation and con-
structive guidance and to friends and family for support during this process.

ABSTRACT 
A variation of sustainable energy technologies is important if the plan for a fossil free 
electrical system by 2050 is to be achieved. Photovoltaic (PV) technology is expected to 
be one of the most important technologies in the future electricity system [1].  
This paper investigates the performance of vertical solar PV modules installed on the 
building facades of Copenhagen international school (CIS) located in Nordhavn, Co-
penhagen. To evaluate the energy yields of vertical PV systems facing different orienta-
tions the CIS building has PV panel covering the entire building facade. There are in 
total around 6048 square meter PV modules orientated North, East, South and West on 
the four towers of the building. The yield data of the PV systems analysed in this paper 
is for one day, on July 6th 2017 and are used to analyse the performance. This paper 
presents an analytic evaluation based on one-day energy yields for the PV system in-
stalled on CIS, which help to understand a system of a vertically installed PV modules 
mounted on a complex build and what the energy production is.  
Facade mounted PV system in urban areas are hard to optimise regarding minimising 
losses coursed by shadows due other building structures. The architectural concept of 
building integration PV (BIPV) which is used at CIS has a positive change in how 
buildings are conceived as a structure. Using PV integration as an architectural the 
building goes from being just an energy consuming unit to be an energy efficient unit. 
PV facade integrated system installed at CIS it may not be seemed efficient, but the PV 
modules replaces other material while at the same time producing electricity. There 
square meter of opportunity for architects and designer to integrate good PV solution on 
buildings. Similar to that of east/west PV power plant solutions the vertical PV systems 
is becoming an interesting subject for urban location. The main issue there can be made 
from observing the yield solar production from CIS is losses caused by shading and the 
orientations of the building itself and surrounding buildings. These areas are crucial 
when planning and investing in BIPV systems. After investigating the potential of verti-
cal PV system and analyzing the yield data from CIS the conclusion is that well-
designed BIPV system has high potential for both new buildings and renovations of 
older building.

INTRODUCTION 
In this chapter the aim is to present the motivation and background behind the work 
done in this thesis. Furthermore, the main objective of the investigation as well as meth-
odology and the thesis outline are presented in this chapter. 
The report will be a technical scientific rapport explaining findings, method and prob-
lems related to Potentials and challenges of facade mounted PV in urban areas. This is 
done by investigating the Copenhagen International School as case study. 
The project objective is to analyse energy production data from the PV solar panels in-
stalled on the facade of CIS and look at how different conditions as shading, angles and 
facade orientation have an influence on the electrical output. 
 Motivation  
In the future the demand for electricity is expected to increase as the global population 
is growing and as an electrification of industries will continue. This will mean that the 
need for generating energy will increase. The European Union (EU) energy policy is 
one of the more ambition policies. In the EU energy demand and production is expected 
decrease, the energy production is predicted to decrease from 8,84 million GWh in 2015 
to 7,66 million GWh in 2050 [2]. The EU predicts a high decline in all fossil fuels (oil, 
gas and coal) to reach the 2050 energy strategy. To meet this goal of reducing green-
house gas emissions by 80 to 95% by the year 2050 [3] the EU predicts an increase of 
solar and wind energy from 17% in 2015 to 36% in 2050 in the renewable energy mix   
One of the key elements in the renewable energy mix is the conversion of light received 
from the sun to electrical energy by using photovoltaic (PV) cells. In a world where 
urbanization is growing, and the energy demand is concentrated in the cities a local pro-
duction of clean energy is therefore needed. In the cities, a large part of people is living 
in high-rise building and here the available roof area is rather limit and the use of this 
area for roof terrace has high priority for investors and resident. One solution to solve 
this energy demand is by using PV cells these PV cells could be integrated into facades 
and other building parts as an energy producing element, building integrated PV BIPV 
systems. It is therefore interesting to consider and investigate PV cells integrated as 
building facades in urban environment and how these system acts as an electrical energy 
generating element in the renewable energy mix.

 Background 
Solar energy is radiant light and heat coming from the solar radiation. It is a renewable 
and green technology since the methods used to transform the solar energy into electric-
ity does not emits any pollutant gasses or toxics. However, since the energy generated 
by this source comes from the sunlight, it cannot be used during the night, and even on 
some days when the weather is completely cloudy, rainy, snowy or of other natural fac-
tors the production is close to zero. Solar energy can mainly be divided into two main 
sources of use, it can be exploited for various applications through the solar thermal or 
solar photovoltaic (PV) methods. This research is focused on the solar energy being 
used through the photovoltaics principle.  
Solar photovoltaic modules are manufactured by semiconductor materials and they turn 
the radiant energy coming from the sun into direct current and therewith, electricity. 
The competitiveness of this field is increasing: in 2013, for the first time in more than a 
decade, solar was over all other renewable energy technologies in the sense of new gen-
erating capacity installed with an increase of 29 percent compared with 2012. [2]. 
Worldwide the total PV installations represented 1.8 GW in 2000 and 71.1 GW in 2011 
with a growth rate of 44%. [3]. 
This has led to a situation where the electricity from solar panels costs as much or is 
even cheaper than electricity purchased from the electrical grid. Solar energy generation 
does have some problems as the conversion efficiency of solar cells is low and the out-
put of PV modules is highly affected by irradiation and temperature. [4]

But one of the most important and critical problems is the shadowing effect on photo-
voltaics cells. Shaded conditions are sometimes unavoidable due to several factors such 
as clouds, the time of the day, the season of the year or even shadows from neighbour-
ing objects or building, this will course the PV system or parts of the PV system to re-
ceive less solar radiation [5]. The lower solar radiation results in a lower energy output 
from the PV system. 
 Formulation of the problem 
The aim of this report is to analyse and discuss the potential of building mounted or 
integrated PV systems for electrical energy production in urban areas. In the investiga-
tion the newly build Copenhagen International School (CIS) will be used as case study.  
PV systems on buildings can have a big potential in energy production in cities because 
of the large area available for PV installation but is it suitable for PV installation due to 
it vertical slope, odd orientation and partial shadowing from surrounding building. One 
of the key elements of the future energy supply is the conversion of solar energy to elec-
trical energy by using photovoltaic (PV) cells. In a world where urbanization is grow-
ing, and the energy demand is concentrated in the cities a local production of clean en-
ergy is needed. In the cities, a large part of people is living in high-rise building and 
here the available roof area is small and the use of this area for roof terrace has high 
priority, so to fulfil a local energy demands the facades can play a key role in meeting a 
fraction of this demand. It is therefore very interesting to consider if and how PV cells 
on the facade of buildings in urban environment can meet the electrical energy demand.

THE SUN AND SOLAR RADIATION 
In this chapter the solar radiation will be assessed. Both in general and for danish condi-
tions where the case study is located. First the sun as a resource will be explained, from 
the energy origin to the irradiation that is received at earth surface. The different factors 
influencing PV systems will be evaluated and discussed and a simulation model of the 
expected electricity production from a vertical PV located in Copenhagen city area 
Nordhavnen will be assessed. Later a comparison between the simulated electricity pro-
duction and the electricity data from CIS Nordhavn on july 6 2017 is done. 
 
In this report the term radiation will be used to describe the power from an object and 
the term irradiation the power transmitted on an object. 
 Solar energy 
The sun has high potential as a renewable energy source. The sun is centred in the mid-
dle of our solar system and emits electromagnetic radiation as energy. This energy is 
extremely large and reaches earth at a relatively constant rate at 24 hours per day and 
365 days of the year. If the energy coming from just 10 hectares of the surface of the 
sun could be collected it would be enough to supply the worlds energy demand. This is 
not possible for 3 reasons, energy spread, the earth’s rotation and the earth’s atmosphere 
which can account only 30% reduction of the sun’s energy at best. The weather condi-
tions can, as known, almost completely reduce the so-
lar radiation from reaching the earth’s surface. The 
distance from the sun to the earth varies between 147 
and 152 million km. [5] The solar energy that reaches 
the earth is called solar irradiation or insolation and are 
the rate at which solar energy hits a unit area, (in this 
paper solar irradiation will be used). Irradiance are 
measured in watts per square meter (W/m2) but in PV 
systems and other energy system designs the energy 
that reaches a collector over a period is a more conven-
ient to know. This summation over time is called solar 
radiation and is measured in joules per square meter 
(J/m2) or watt-hours per square meter (Wh/m2).

PHOTOVOLTAIC TECNOLOGY AND PRINCI-
PLE 
The history of photovoltaic (PV) starts in 1839, when Alexandre-Edmund Becquerel 
observed that “electrical currents arose from certain light induced chemical reactions”. 
Later in the 1940s the development of the first solid-state devices paved the way for the 
development of the first silicon solar cell with an efficiency of 6% [15].  
The photovoltaic (PV) effect is the process of converting light to electricity in a solar 
cell. Basic description is that light enter the cell and transfer enough energy to some 
negatively charged atomic particles (electrons) to be free. A barrier in the cell creates a 
potential for the electrons to produce a voltage, the so-called photovoltage. This photo-
voltage can be used to drive a current through a circuit and this can be used as electrici-
ty [20]. This conversion of light into electricity in a PV cell can be comprises into three 
processes: 
1) Absorption of light in the solar cell: an electron is lifted to an energy rich state, an 
excited state. 
2) Charge separation: The electron 
is separated from the nuclei at the 
junction between a positively doped  
semiconductor (p-type) and a nega-
tively doped semiconductor (n-
type). The negative electron is 
transferred from the p-type layer to 
the n-type layer leaving a positively 
charged hole behind. 
3) Diffusion of charge carriers: The 
whole and electron diffuses out to 
opposite electrodes creating a poten-
tial difference of typical 0.5 – 1.0 
volts. 
In general, a photovoltaic (PV) installed system is composed of several solar cell mod-
ules and typically includes an inverter, cables and the mounting profile-system for the 
installation.

20 
There are three main generations of PV cells which is: Crystalline silicon solar cells are 
the most common ones with about 90% of the world market. Then there are Tin film 
solar cells with a very flexible application use and about 10% of the world market. 
There are a lot of research in developing the Third-generation solar cells as low cost. 
Power Conversion Efficiency (PCE) is the ratio between the generated electrical energy 
and the incoming solar radiation. The efficiency for three generation of PV cells is as

might give an opportunity to getting insight and knowledge about energy production

monitored and logged so it can be used for electricity production monitoring and sci-

from the PV panels and their potential as electricity supply.

 Shadows cast and effect 
Shadow covering part of the PV panel has a high effect on the yield output. The cells 
there are in the shadow will have a lower current output and because of the series con-
nection of the cells all the cells are drawn down to the same current level and the entire 
PV panel has a low yield output. A PV cell can be seen as a combination of a current 
generator and a diode. In solar modules the cells are connected in series and the shading 
of a single solar cell causes the current in the string to fall to the level of the shaded cell. 
This is because when a solar cell in a string is partially shaded the other cells will try to 
push current through the shaded cell. But because the cell acts as a diode in the reverse 
direction this is not possible, and the shaded cell will limit the current in the string [22]. 
 
The CIS building has a lot of variation in the facade which make shadow casting on PV 
panels doing the day. CIS is accommodating this by have micro inverters per 8-16 PV 
modules, this also discussed in section 4.2, to insures that the PV module strings do not 
get to large. An example of how this effect is shown by using a panel on the south side

CONCLUSION 
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the potentials of facade mounted PV 
panels in urban areas. To achieve this objective the PV solar production from Copenha-
gen international school was investigated. It is my understanding that to get a better 
view on the behaviour and components of PV solar systems, as the one installed at CIS, 
active monitoring and performance assessment should be implemented. By having this 
active monitoring, a collection of data can be made and a numerical analysis of the data 
for new system installation or improving already installed systems. EnergyLab 
Nordhavn are in the process of doing just that with the data from CIS PV Production. 
Analysis of monitored data from large system should be done for time-series over long-
er time intervals, more than one year. This provides means for assessment of the tech-
nology performance.  
Facade mounted PV system in urban areas are hard to optimise regarding minimising 
losses coursed by shadows. In a PV power plant, the panel are mounted in landscape 
fields and position in rows to minimise the impact of shading of panels. This takes up a 
large area and are not possible in urban areas. 
 Results 
In the last decade the interest for sustainable and green architecture has been increasing 
and there has been a larger attention to the integration of the PV modules into the struc-
tural elements of the buildings. This is the concept used in the PV system at CIS and 
this concept has done that the photovoltaic technology is starting to play an important 
role in building architecture. This architectural concept of building integration PV 
(BIPV) has a positive change in how buildings are made and making them more energy 
efficient. This change of the building facade from architectural envelop there gives pro-
tection from the weather to PV system there with this technology give a multi purposes 
to the architecture. Analysing the PV facade integrated system installed at CIS it may 
not be seem efficient when comparing it to a PV power plant installed in a field due 
south. But the PV modules at CIS replaces other material that would be installed for 
weather, noise and light protection while at the same time producing electricity. 
The glass technology used at CIS give PV modules this multi-functionality and an ar-
chitectural possibility with low loss of PV efficiency because of the KROMATIX tech-
nology. The lowering of efficiency due to coloured glass is an issue with BIPV because 
the natural colour of PV cell does not have an aesthetic architectural look. With BIPV 
==PAGE:54==
 
52 
there are prospects for larger system installations due to the large surface areas of fa-
cade. There are a lower efficiency kWh/m2 than that of roof integration PV system but 
also additional square meter. Not only PV has part of the facade is a tool for architects 
and designer but also PV shading system is a good solution for buildings effected from 
high amount of daylight, as ex office buildings. It’s an easy setup using PV modules as 
the shadow component and at the same time producing electricity. The glass material on 
PV modules for building integration is one on the costly parts and at CIS they have cho-
sen an expensive solution but in comparison that it replaces another covering material 
that would have to be installed there are a lower cost difference. Vertical PV systems is 
becoming an interesting subject for investigation and shows potential similar to that of 
east/west PV power plant solutions.  
With BIPV at CIS performance of the electrical production are observed and these sys-
tems have losses caused by shading and the orientations and these areas should be in-
vestigated when planning BIPV systems. A vertical system has a better performance in 
winter months but have a very narrow period of production over the course of a day. 
After investigating the potential of vertical PV system and analyzing the yield data from 
CIS the conclusion is that well-designed BIPV system has high potential for both new 
buildings and renovations. Furthermore, having main drivers as decreasing prices for 
PV systems and the increasing demanding legislation related to energy performance in 
buildings BIPV system is expected to have an annual growth of 18,7% the coming years 
[23].  
 Perspectives 
The project shows that the influence of shadowing and orientation has a high effect on 
the PV energy production therefore these subjects are important to investigate when 
installing BIPV. At CIS an architectural integration of reflective mirrors on the north 
and west side of the building. This would extend the hours where the modules on north 
and west side receiving direct irradiation and higher the annual yield output. Additional 
incentives or subsidies as other countries have implemented could be a way to get more 
newbuilding and renovations to invest in building-integrated. 
 Future work 
Getting the data sorted and collected in a database will help to do further analyses on the 
data from CIS. As mention an analyse should be made on at least one year of yield-data. 
The database should be built so the extraction of yield data can be done by cardinal di-
rection and not only by tower and floor. A further investigation on the linear connection 
between the Azimuth angle, Zenith angle and the yield output from the BIPV modules. 
To see if the construction of a data lookup table or program for each day of the year 
could be made for use in future BIPV or similar PV installation in Denmark. 
An engaging from architects in using BIPV in project should be in focus and knowledge 
about this technology should be spread in the industry and projects as CIS does just this

MATLAB CODE FOR CALCULATING SOLAR AN-
GLES 
Latitude 
L = 55.4242; 
%Longitude 
LL = 12.3554; 
  
% d = day number, January 1 = day 1 
d = 187; 
B = 2*pi*(d-1)/365.242; 
  
% Equation of Time (EoT) 
EoT = 0.258*cosd(B) - 7.416*sind(B) - 3.648*cosd(2*B) - 
9.228*sind(2*B);  
  
% Local Time (LT) 
LT = 7; 
GMT = LT-2; 
 
% The (LSTM) 
LSTM = 15*(LT-GMT); 
  
% Time Correction Factor (TC) 
TC = 4*(LL - LSTM) + EoT; 
  
% The net Time Correction Factor (in minutes) 
LST = LT + TC/60; 
 
%Hour angle 
HRA = 15*(LST - 12); 
 
%Declination angle 
D = 23.45*sind(360*((284 + d)/365)); 
  
The altitude angle or elevation angle 
beta = asind(sind(L)*sind(D) + cosd(L)*cosd(D)*cosd(HRA)); 
  
% The zenith angle 
theta = acosd(sind(L)*sind(D) + cosd(L)*cosd(D)*cosd(HRA)); 
a = 90 - beta; 
  
% The azimuth angle, negative before solar noon, and positive after 
solar noon. 
Az = asind((cosd(D)*sind(HRA))/cosd(beta));
