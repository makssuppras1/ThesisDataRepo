Abstract
The focus of this dissertation is the development, fabrication and testing of
microfabricated chips that can function as a testing platform for experiments
involving 2D materials, such as graphene, when they are performed within a
transmission electron microscope (TEM). Three metals; platinum, tungsten
and tantalum, are tested with various thicknesses and conﬁgurations to de-
termine their maximum temperature, stability and failure mechanisms. During
the project it was found that a spiral and a rectangular heater using 100 nm
platinum reached temperatures of 750 ◦C while thicker 200 nm platinum reached
temperatures of 600 ◦C. It was shown that a tungsten spiral heater can reach
temperatures of 550 ◦C. The tantalum chips’ temperatures were unmeasurable.
The chips were found to be stable for a duration of 24 hours at diﬀerent temper-
atures; 100 nm platinum was stable at 310 ◦C, 200 nm platinum was stable at
407 ◦C and tungsten was stable at 351 ◦C. The failure mechanisms of the chips
were found to be due to membrane failure, such as blistering and buckling, and
not metal degradation eﬀects. It was shown that graphene could be transferred
to the chips defect free using the wedging transfer method. The defect count,
generated by a two minute TEM investigation at room temperature, was found
to be 1.77 · 1010 cm−2. Lastly, it was shown that using the produced micro-
heaters to heat graphene to above 200 ◦C while imaging them in the TEM lead
to an elimination of defects.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the discovery of graphene in 2004 [1][2], graphene has been shown as a
very interesting strictly two-dimensional material with exceptional crystal and
electronic properties [3][4]. It is a ballistic conductor with a long mean free
path at room temperature [1][2]. Graphene can be shaped into a ribbon which
makes it behave as a semiconductor [5][6][7]. It can also be gated locally [8] and
can carry Josephson supercurrent, which is a current that ﬂows indeﬁnitely long
without an applied voltage [9]. Graphene is a 2D material of a single layer with
carbon atoms arranged in hexagonal lattice. The crystal lattice of graphene
gives it a noteworthy band structure, where the valence and conduction band
meet in only two points known as Dirac points. At these points the energy bands
form circular cones which causes the dispersion relation to be linear. This leads
to a zero-energy band gap and the charge carriers can be described as massless
fermions giving rise to some remarkable electronic properties [4]. It results in
graphene not having a band-gap which gives rise to issues when attempting to
incorporate graphene into the semi-conductor industry, as the industry is based
on band-gap technology. Many proposals have been made in attempting to open
up band gaps in graphene e.g. opening a band gap by adsorption of hydrogen
[10], using bilayer graphene and breaking the symmetry by use of a transverse
electrical ﬁeld [11], patterning graphene into a nanoribbon, eﬀectively acting
as a 1D structure conﬁning the charge carriers [6], patterning of holes in the
graphene layer [12], creating nanobubbles by using strain [13], creating holes by
use of hexagonal boron nitride (hbN) [14] or making graphene antidot lattices
[15].
Han et. al has demonstrated experimentally that it is possible to engineer
band gaps in nanoribbons by using lithography processes. The band gap they
engineered was found to scale inversely with the width of the nanoribbon, the
highest gap being 20 meV with a 15 nm wide nanoribbon [6]. Richard et. al
observed the opening of a bandgap in hydrogenated graphene, which was done
by hydrogen covered regions that created a conﬁnement potential for the carri-
ers in the pristine graphene regions. [16]. Hunt et. al has shown that stacking
graphene and hexagonal boron nitride, thereby creating van der Waals het-
erostructures, creates a bandgap [17]. Numerous methods for creating graphene
nanoribbons have been proposed; plasma etching, e-beam lithography, scanning
tunneling microscopy (STM), standard lithography and chemical stripping [18].
Whether the nanoribbons behave quasi-metallic or semiconducting depends pri-
4
==PAGE:8==
marily on the edge state: zig-zag or armchair [19] [20].
One of the ambitions for graphene is to construct atomically precise inte-
grated circuits.
Graphene nanoribbons have shown that they are promising
candidates, as nanoribbons down to a width of 20 nm have been fabricated
using e-beam lithography and can be incorporated as channels in ﬁeld eﬀect
transistors [7]. Being able to atomically change the properties of the graphene
could pave the way for future nano electronics. One machine that shows great
promise within this ﬁeld is the Environmental Transmission Electron Micro-
scope (ETEM). This TEM allows for the graphene, or other 2D materials to be
atomically imaged while under the inﬂuence of diﬀerent gasses introduced into
the TEM chamber. Furthermore it can be used to have high resolution in-situ
live images of graphene, or other 2D materials, while they are being imaged in
the TEM, expanding upon the data gained from the experiment. A TEM can
be used to either image or sculpt the graphene layer depending on the energy
of the electron beam [21] [22] [23]. To successfully knock out a carbon atom
from graphene, an energy of 80KeV is required [22]. The concentrated electron
beam also enables shaping of the graphene into tightly controlled sizes and con-
ﬁgurations for experiments [24] [25] [26]. Monolayer graphene can be sculpted
down to near atomic precision at high temperatures of 600 ◦C [23].
Imaging graphene using an electron beam will eventually introduce defects into
the graphene lattice. As such the self-repair of graphene is an important mecha-
nism to help avoid damaging the sample during analysis and thereby increasing
the quality and control of the experiments [27]. Allowing constant heating in
the TEM chamber can enable a constant self-cleaning of the sample, making
Electron Beam Induced Deposition (EBID) much easier to avoid and thereby
limiting the defect introduction [28].
To enable the execution of similar experiments to the above mentioned, a proper
heating platform for heating and supporting graphene is required. This platform
or chip must contain both heating capabilities and the ability to do electrical
measurements. The goal of this report is to fabricate and improve upon such
a platform by testing diﬀerent metals and process steps to obtain higher and
more stable temperatures than the original design oﬀers.
1.1
Transmission Electron Microscopy
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) was ﬁrst demonstrated in 1931 by
Ernst Ruska and Max Knoll. TEM works by having an electron beam generated
and then focused by a condenser lens, which limits high angle electrons as can
be seen in ﬁgure 1.1. The focused beam is then sent through the specimen and
the transmitted electrons are focused by an objective lens onto a charge coupled
device thereby creating the image [29]. This enables high resolution images of
thin materials such as graphene and other 2D materials. Furthermore, the TEM
can be used to image over time, creating movies and sequences of activity of
ones sample. An example of a TEM used at DTU CEN can be seen in ﬁgure
1.2.

1.2
Microheaters
Microheaters are microscale high power heaters that can accurately heat small
areas by the use of converting electrical work to heat. These characteristics
make them well suited for use with experiments requiring speciﬁc heating prop-
erties. Platinum is the most used metal when fabricating microheaters, both
because it has a linear Temperature Coeﬃcient of Resistance (TCR), is easy
to deposit onto wafers and is fairly common. Platinum microheaters can reach
611 ◦C, which is near the temperature where graphene starts to self-repair, but
this requires speciﬁc heater conﬁgurations [32]. The most eﬀective heater con-
ﬁguration is a spiral heater conﬁguration. Other conﬁgurations can be used as
well though lower temperatures will be reached since the surface to heater ratio
is higher. Alternative materials such as molybdenum and TiN [33] [34] [35],
have also been tested, showing higher temperatures compared to platinum [36].
Tungsten has been tested as well as a possible heater material, showing higher
attainable maximum temperatures compared to platinum. Moreover the failure
mechanisms of tungsten are more related to the delamation of the membrane
and less to degradation eﬀects [34].
Prasad et al. has created a low power double spiral microheater of platinum
capable of reaching 611 ◦C. This was achieved using an SiN membrane, and it
was shown that the microheater could remain stable at 300 ◦C for 1.8 hours.

Goals
The goal of this project is to fabricate functional microheaters using plat-
inum, tungsten and tantalum, enabling in-situ heating of 2D materials such
as graphene. Self repair and self cleaning of the specimen, will clear oﬀcon-
tamination yielding measurements with a much larger resolution. Furthermore,
self-healing will help with keeping the 2D material strong and uniform.
The overall goal can be divided into a series of subsidiary goals:
• Fabricate heaters using platinum heater metal, showcasing that such is
possible using the process steps and machines at the DTU Danchip clean-
room. Calculate the TCR, maximum temperature and failure mechanisms.
• Design and test fabrication methods for creating tungsten and tantalum

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter covers the relevant theory in regard to electrical circuits, resistance
and heat generation. Raman spectroscopy is also covered as it has been shown
to be an excellent tool for investigating the quality and type of graphene. This
theory lays the foundation for designing and investigating the chips to ensure
high functionality and experimental data.
2.1
Electrical circuit
To calculate the resistance of the heater, Ohm’s law is used
U = R · I
(2.1)
Where U is the voltage, R is the resistance and I is the current. This law
is only applicable when the circuit contains resistive elements such as a diode
or a resistor. The current must be direct current, as using alternating current
has a time dependent resistance. As such it is important to ensure that the
circuit does not contain capacitative inductive elements that would disrupt the
ideal state by which Ohm’s law can be applied. For this project, the heater
element acts as the resistor while no other part is acting as neither a capacitor
nor inductor.
2.2
Thermal Coeﬃcient of Resistance
Assuming that the coeﬃcient only has minuscule variations with changing tem-
perature, the thermal coeﬃcient of resistance (TCR) can be calculated from the
linear approximation
R = R0(1 + α(T −T0))
(2.2)
Where R0 is the initial resistance and T0 is the initial temperature and α is
the TCR.
The TCR is a measure of how much the resistance of a material changes as a
function of temperature. Determining this number for the produced samples
will enable an easy way to calculate the ﬁnal temperature of the samples. This
can be done using nothing but a 4 point probe system and a vacuum chamber
12
==PAGE:16==
after having acquired the speciﬁc TCR of each sample. In order to calculate the
temperature of the sample, the equation for the TCR can be rewritten as
dR
R = α · dT
(2.3)
Where dR is the change in resistance between the initial resistance and the
current resistance, dT is the corresponding change in temperature from the ini-
tial to the current and R is the current resistance.
The resistance of a conductor is dependent upon the number of collisions be-
tween the atoms and electrons. As such it is intuitively given that increasing the
temperature will increase the number of collisions within the conductor, thus
increasing the resistance.
2.3
Microheaters
Miroheaters utilise Joule heating, which describes how current running through
a conductor generates power given by
P = I2 · R
(2.4)
Where I is the current, R is the resistance and P being the power.
Joules law is based on the fundamental consideration that when electrons move
through an electrical circuit, they will collide with atomic ions making up the
body of the conductor. These collisions causes the charged particles to scatter
with random motion which is characterised as thermal. Hence when increasing
either the current (which increases the number of electrons passing through
the circuit), the voltage (which increases the energy of the electrons) or the
resistance of the material (which increases the number of collisions), they will
all lead to a larger deposition of thermal energy in the circuit and thereby more
energy per second.
2.4
Raman spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is a technique used to investigate vibrational, rotational
and other low frequency modes in the sample. This is typically done by shining
a laser light on the sample. The electromagnetic radiation interacts with a solid,
liquid or gaseous molecule’s bonds and electron density, as they are polarizable.
The radiation causes a form of inelastic scattering with the sample, where the
photon excites either the molecule in the ground state or in the rovibronic
state, which is a form of rotational and vibrational state within the electronic
state. This causes the molecule to enter a virtual energy state for a short time
before scattering inelastically, which means that it can be of either higher or
lower energy of the incoming photon.
This is compared to elastic Rayleigh
scattering where the scattered photon has the same energy as the incoming
photon. As the scattered photon has changed energy, the molecule has entered
a diﬀerent rotational or vibrational state. The change between the initial and
ﬁnal rovibronic state causes a shift in the frequency of the scattered photon,
making it diﬀerent when compared to the incoming frequency or the Rayleigh
line. This is to maintain the total energy of the system, whereby the scattered

==PAGE:17==
photon moves to a new frequency and thereby a new energy state. If the change
in energy level between the initial and ﬁnal rovibronic state of the molecule is
higher, the scattered photon will shift to a lower frequency as to maintain the
total energy. This shift is called a stokes shift or downshift. An anti-stokes or
upshift is when the energy diﬀerence of the molecule is lower and as such the
scattered photon will have a higher frequency. The energy levels can be seen in
ﬁgure 2.1.

Here ∆w is the Raman shift, λ0 is the excitation wavelength and λ1 is the
Raman spectrum wavelength. The unit is most often cm−1.
2.4.1
Raman spectroscopy and graphene
When performing Raman spectroscopy on graphene, there are two main peaks:
G (1580 cm−1), a primary in-plane vibrational mode, and 2D (2690 cm−1), a
second-order overtone for a diﬀerent in-plane vibration D (1350 cm−1). Both
D and 2D are dispersion dependent, meaning they are dependent on the laser
excitation energy. These positions are for a 532 nm laser.

2.4.2
I2D/IG
As the number of AB stacked graphene layers increases, the added force causes
the 2D peak to split into an increasing number of modes, which combine into
wider, shorter and higher frequency peaks. The G peak can also experience a
small red shift from increasing the number of layers. As such the ratio I2D/IG,
as well as the position and shape of the peaks, contain information about the
number of layers. For rotationally decoupled graphene layers, the 2D peak can
still be singular and hence only the FWHM and position can contain information

Doping of graphene has a large eﬀect on the ratio I2D/IG. Increased doping
in graphene leads to a lower ratio as the doping causes an upshift, narrowing
and stiﬀening of the G and the 2D peaks [42]. It has also been shown that
the ratio decreases upon either electron or hole doping [43] and that pristine
graphene has the highest ratio. Raman spectroscopy can be used to investigate
15
==PAGE:19==
the carrier mobility in graphene. The 2D peak is very sensitive to the impurity
concentration while it has no eﬀect on the G peak, showing that graphene with
a high I2D/IG ratio has a much higher carrier mobility [44].
2.4.3
ID/IG
The ﬁrst-order D peak is not present in pristine graphene due to crystal sym-
metries. In order for the D peak to appear, a charge carrier must be excited
and inelastically scattered by a phonon followed by an elastic scattering of a
defect or a zone boundary to create a recombination. Meanwhile the 2D peak
is always visible, as it is the result of inelastic scattering from a second phonon.
Using the ratio ID/IG, the disorder of the graphene can be characterised. With
increased disorder, the ratio displays two diﬀerent behaviours. The ﬁrst regime
is one of low defect density; here the ratio will increase as the number of elastic
scattering increases with higher defect density. The second regime is one of high
defect density; here the graphene becomes a more amorphous structure, causing
an attenuation of the Raman peaks. This relation can be seen in ﬁgure 2.3.

3.4
Heater chips
Three versions of the heater platform were created: a 2 point heater seen in
ﬁgure 3.3, a 4 point heater seen in ﬁgure 3.2 and a spiral heater seen in ﬁgure
3.4. To successfully fabricate these chips, ﬁve mask layers are required. Three
are used in UV lithography using a positive resist: contact openings, membrane
holes and membrane openings. Two are used in metal deposition using lift-oﬀ
processes with a negative resist: heater electrodes and measure electrodes. The
heater feature a large SiN membrane and an embedded Ti/Pt, Ta or Ta/W/Ta
heater loop. The heaters are either 3 µm, 6 µm or 9 µm wide and 100 nm thick
(5 nm Ti / 95 nm Pt), (5 nm Ta / 95 nm W) or (100 nm Ta).
The 2 point and 4 point heater loops are placed at the same distance from the
boundary of the membrane in order to create uniform boundary conditions, so
that the same amount of material has to be heated everywhere. This symmetry
21
==PAGE:25==
is only broken at the end of the membrane where the loop enters the membrane.
Here it will naturally be colder, although due to using a rectangular membrane
the middle part should not be aﬀected by the colder end.
This ideally should resemble a system of two inﬁnite wires placed a distance
from a hole. As such this motivates having a rectangular membrane, such that
the middle part where the holes are and the investigation is happening, is not
aﬀected by the boundaries of the membrane. The design strives to achieve the
ideal case which would be to make the membrane as large as possible with-
out causing the membrane to lose structural integrity and break by becoming
too fragile. The wires could also be moved closer together at the expense of
investigation area and hole integrity.

Another important parameter is the width of the heater loop wire. Increasing

degradation eﬀects that occur as a result of a high surface to volume ratio.

resistances, a lower total current is required to achieve the same power, giving
the wires a much higher stability.

Platinum height
The last parameter is the height of the metal, which was only tested with plat-
inum. As well as with the heater wire width, it greatly decreases the surface to
volume ratio as well as decreasing the required current.

etchrate in PECVD nitride was 122.5 nm/minute while in LPCVD it was 26.67
nm/minute. This met the expectation, as a grown nitride has a much higher
etch resistance compared to a PECVD nitride [37].
4.8
Step 5 - Backside SiN
AZ MIR 701 was spincoated onto the front side of the wafer to act as a protective
layer while the backside was etched. Afterwards photolithography was done on
the backside with AZ MIR 701. Three wafers were etched for 13 minutes using
the OH
POLYA recipe and 2 minutes using an SiN etch recipe, and then
measured in a stylus surface proﬁler to ensure that the SiN was etched all the
way through. The SiN etch recipe turned out to be unnecessary for etching
through SiN, and the time was then reduced to 12 minutes as an over-etch of
1400 nm had occurred.
4.9
Step 6 - KOH
Having created the SiN etch mask and manually spun 2 µm ProTEK photoresist
on the front to protect the metal, the backside was then etched using KOH; 1
kilogram of potassium pellets and 2 litres of water kept at 80 ◦C to etch through
the wafer in around 4 hours. About halfway through the wafer was taken out
of the KOH and the etch depth was measured using a stylus force proﬁler. The
resist was then stripped as described earlier in section 4.2.2 with care taken to
not break the membranes.

investigation of substrates placed on the chip.

5.2.1
Mechanical exfoliation
To be able to transfer the graphene onto the TEM chips, it must ﬁrst be
extracted.
In this project, it was extracted using mechanical exfoliation of
graphite, which is also known as the Scotch tape method. This was ﬁrst done
by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov in 2004 [1]. The method exploits the
fact that only weak van der Waals forces hold the diﬀerent layers of graphene
together.
Mechanically exfoliated graphene has the highest quality in terms
of electronic properties such as electron mobility and defect density compared
to more conventional growth methods, for instance epitaxial growth and CVD
growth.

Chapter 6
Results and Discussion
The microheaters have been designed to be used as platforms for experiments
with 2D materials, such as graphene, as well as other materials ideal for TEM
investigation. Because of this, it is a priority that the chips are reliable, so
they do not fail during the experiments. Therefore, the chips must be tested
thoroughly so the limitations and performances are known. These tests include:
TCR measurements, maximum temperature measurements, stability over time
with a high current and failure mechanisms when the current is continuously
increased. Lastly graphene will be transferred onto the chips, using the wedging
transfer technique, where they will be analysed with Raman spectroscopy both
before and after TEM imaging to investigate the defect generation of a TEM
beam.
6.1

To investigate the temperature possibilities of the created samples, their TCR
must be measured.
Using the experimental setup described in the previous
chapter, the TCR can be measured by placing the chips in the Linkam stage.

measured using the two probes not supplying the current. When investigating

than what is recorded in this project, but that due to the temperature, a fault
appears. The fault is likely due to either imperfect fabrication methods or the
membranes delaminating to create an empty space next the wire, which causes
the breakdown eﬀect seen here.
The breakdown investigation seems to allude to failure mechanism other than
metal degradation and more towards membrane failure due to either fabrication
errors or damage caused by the adhesion layer. This suggests that much higher
temperatures can be reached if the membrane can be strengthened.

6.8
Summary
The 100 nm platinum microheater chips reach the highest temperatures (700-
750 ◦C), while the 200 nm platinum chips reach 600 ◦C and the tungsten chips
reach 400-550 ◦C. The chips were found to be stable at temperatures between
310-407 ◦C for up to 20-24 hours. The breakdown mechanism was investigated
both with SEM and microscope imaging, and it was found that it was not the
degradation of the heater loops themselves, but either the membrane, insulation
layer, adhesion layer or other materials in the chip that caused the structural
failure of the membranes. Furthermore it was found with SEM imaging, that
an insulation layer blocked the tantalum chip from being used to its fullest
potential, leading to troublesome measurements of the tantalum chips. At last
pieces of graphene were successfully transferred onto the thoroughly investigated
microchips. They were then imaged with both Raman spectroscopy and TEM
to show the functionality of the produced chips and their eﬃciency at limiting
defect generation during TEM imaging.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and Outlook
The goal of this dissertation was to design, fabricate and characterise a TEM
heater platform, for use with in-situ TEM experiments involving 2D materials
such as graphene. The platform was designed to facilitate both the heating of
the sample, as well as enable electrical measurements. The project concerned
itself with three major areas regarding the TEM chip: Fabrication of the chip
in the cleanroom, characterising the stability, temperature limits and the failure
mechanisms behind and ﬁnally transferring graphene onto the chips to test their
functionality and eﬃciency as heaters in TEM imaging.
The original mask design have been made by Tim Booth and Joachim Dahl
Thomsen. In this project the sizes of the membrane, as well as the wire thick-
nesses, have been altered. Some optimisations and improvements were made
during the fabrication phase. Working with tantalum and tungsten caused some
diﬃculties in regard to their deposition. As a result the metals were not de-
posited using a lift-oﬀprocess normally used with platinum, as the depositions
of tantalum and tungsten burn the resist. Instead deposition using an etchback
technique was used, where the chips were instead etched using a photoresist
mask. To successfully etch the metal while retaining the photoresist, and by
extension the metal beneath, a ﬂuorine etch program was decided upon, as it
was deemed to have high selectivity with the photoresist. The ﬂuorine etch pro-
gram was then improved so that the etch rate in both tantalum and tungsten
was optimised to limit over-etching of the SiN layer beneath. Furthermore the
diﬀerent etch rates of sulphur hexaﬂuoride dry etch in LPCVD grown nitride
and PECVD nitride was investigated. The etch rates found for PECVD nitride
was 122.5nm/minute and 26.67nm/minute for LPCVD nitride, and the recipe
was changed accordingly.
Two 100 nm platinum chips reached maximum temperatures of more than
750 ◦C, while the remaining platinum chips only reached temperatures of around
600 ◦C. For 200 nm platinum, most of the chips reached 600 ◦C, while none
reached the same temperatures as the 100 nm platinum chips. For tungsten a
single spiral type heater reached temperatures of 550 ◦C, while the remaining
microheaters reached temperatures of around 400 ◦C. It was impossible to suc-
cessfully measure the maximum temperatures of tantalum, as it was discovered
that the resistance was very high, coupled with SEM showing an insulating layer
66
==PAGE:70==
on top of the electrode limiting the signal required to determine the TCR. The
maximum temperatures found for the tungsten chips were contrary to what was
initially expected, as the higher melting point and, as shown in literature, less
prevalent degradations eﬀects should yield much higher maximum temperatures
for the chips.
The platinum heaters of the altered design have seen huge increases in per-
formance compared to the original design. The platinum chips of the original
design only reached temperatures of 300 ◦C compared with the chips produced
in this report that reached temperatures of 750 ◦C.
There was only a weak correlation between heater dimensions and maximum
temperature, exclusively seen in the 100 nm platinum microheater chips. Addi-
tionally there was a strong correlation between membrane sizes and power, with
larger membranes using less power. To determine the stability of the chips, the
diﬀerent chips were placed within the vacuum chamber and then kept at 310
◦C for 100 nm platinum, 407 ◦C for 200 nm platinum and 351 ◦C for tungsten
for 24 hours. The chips turned out to be stable at these temperatures.
To further investigate the reason behind the failure mechanisms of the plat-
inum and tungsten microheaters, the chips were imaged live while the current
was slowly increased. It was discovered that for both chips, the metal was not
destroyed, but the membrane broke as blisters formed and destroyed the struc-
tural integrity of the membrane. It indicated that much higher temperatures
could be reached if the membrane could be strengthened.
Finally graphene was transferred onto the chips using the wedge transfer method.
Raman spectroscopy was performed on the samples before exposing them to a
TEM electron beam for two minutes. During TEM exposure, the chips were
heated to diﬀerent temperatures and it was shown that the chips could heat the
graphene and thereby avoid defect generation as a result of TEM imaging.
In conclusion it was found that the 100 nm platinum chips were capable of
functioning as in-situ microheater platforms in a TEM.
7.1
Suggestions
The standard lift-oﬀtechnique worked for heater fabrication, but when using the
etchback technique to create the tantalum and tungsten heaters, several prob-
lems regarding the subsequent deposition of SiN occurred. As such, a deeper
study into the eﬀects and methods of the etchback process is suggested to im-
prove the deposition of high melting point metals such as tungsten and tanta-
lum.
The failure mechanism investigated in this dissertation indicates a structural
failure of the membrane instead of metal failure. Thus an investigation into the
adhesion of the metals to the membrane and optimisation of the membrane and
insulation layer is suggested. The investigation could be made by cutting the
destroyed membranes in half to investigate them more suﬃciently in an SEM.
Furthermore it is suggested to investigate how uniform the temperature distribu-
tion is, as well as whether the microheaters are correctly reaching the calculated
temperatures. For this a thermal camera could be used. Another method of
temperature determination could also be used: Tim Booth et. al [60] has deter-

mined the crytallographic etching of graphene by Ag nanoparticles in an oxygen
environment. From [60] it was found that the velocity of the particles depends
on the temperature in an Arrhenius form. Knowing the activation energy, the
temperature can be acquired from the velocity of the particles.
