Summary
We present KL inducing points Linearized Laplace Approximations (KiLLA), a framework de-
signed to scale up the linearized Laplace approximation by addressing 2 major challenges: 1.
linear scaling of runtime cost with the size of the dataset, 2. quadratic scaling of memory cost of
the posterior covariance matrix with the number of neural network parameters. 1. is addressed
via an inducing point approximation, optimized via a Kullback-Leibler divergence objective –
hence the name – and 2. is addressed via matrix free algorithms for inducing point optimization
and sampling, built on linear operator oracles and various approximations that bypass the need
to materialize the covariance matrix.
KiLLA is shown to work well for 2-dimensional classification on various accuracy and calibra-
tion metrics. It also shows promise for more complex problems in higher-dimensional domains,
demonstrated in image classification on MNIST and FashionMNIST, where it performs especially
well in out-of-distribution detection.
Finally, KiLLA is demonstrated to run on a 1 million parameter ResNet model trained on
CIFAR10, although further research is needed to properly gauge performance, since such large-
scale experiments exceed our current computational and memory resource budget for the current
state of the framework.

setting in chapter 1, we dive into the high-level theory of linearized Laplace approximations

Introduction
1
A certain challenge of modern deep learning. The advent of deep learning has taken neural
networks from classifying handwritten digits (LeCun et al., 2002) to playing at superhuman levels
in games (Silver et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2019), powering state-of-the-art chatbots (Brown
et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018, 2019), and predicting protein folding (Jumper et al., 2021).
However, at a fundamental level, neural networks suffer from poor calibration to unseen data
(Guo et al., 2017). This means undesirable behavior such as overconfident predictions in clas-
sification or regression, and “hallucinations” in generative modeling (Manakul et al., 2023). In
critical applications such as medical diagnostics, autonomous driving, and other high-stakes
decision environments, these failure modes can potentially be catastrophic.
Reliable uncertainty quantification in model predictions would allow us to flag low-confidence
predictions, detect out-of-distribution inputs, and in general make deep learning systems more
robust.
Enter Bayes.
In an effort to tackle this, we therefore adopt the Bayesian approach.
The
Bayesian approach is a probabilistic modeling framework in which we model uncertainty as
probability distributions; specifically, distribution variance is what quantifies uncertainty during
inference. We compute predictive uncertainty by integrating over plausible weight configurations,
leading to provably better-calibrated posteriors (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020).
Bayes’ theorem can be expressed as

In words, Eq. 1.1 formalizes updating our beliefs given new evidence; 1. The prior is our initial
beliefs about θ before observing any data. 2. The likelihood measures the compatibility of
observing D with θ. 3. The evidence renormalizes with the overall probability of observing D.
4. The posterior represents our updated beliefs after taking the data into account.
Bayesian deep learning (BDL) applies the structure detailed above to neural networks:
We apply a prior over network weights θ and define a likelihood via the loss function. These
combine into a posterior, which in principle produces reliable uncertainty estimates during future
inference.
Writing out the evidence term in Eq. 1.1 as p(D) =
R p(θ, D) dθ reveals why it is also known as
the marginal likelihood. It also reveals a fundamental limitation of the BDL approach described
above: exact integration in the weight space – and by extension exact posterior inference – is
computationally intractable. Practical BDL therefore relies on approximations such as Varia-
tional Inference (Graves, 2011), Markov Chain Monte Carlo (Neal, 2012) or post-hoc methods
like Laplace Approximations (MacKay, 1992), each of which constitutes a tradeoff between
approximation fidelity and computational speed.
We will focus entirely on post-hoc Laplace Approximations, where we first obtain a single set
of parameters from training and then afterwards attach the posterior. This strategy aims to
provide better calibrated predictions without the need to retrain, and is in the “faster, but more
crude” end of the tradeoff spectrum mentioned above.

2.2
Analysis of the GGN matrix
The GGN matrix is central to this thesis, and is worth spending a bit more time on.
Immer, Korzepa, and Bauer, 2021 argue that there is a natural connection between LLA and
the Generalized Gauss-Newton (GGN) approximation to the Hessian. The GGN approximation
can be viewed as an implicit local linearization of the Bayesian Neural Network (BNN), turning
it into a generalized linear model, thus warranting the modified predictive distribution.
Roy et al. (2024) argue that the approximate posterior of LA fails to maintain invariance under
reparameterization, i.e. it assigns different densities to sets of parameters that map to the same
exact functions. This is ascribed to the Gaussian approximation not properly aligning with the
subspaces of the parameter space that correspond to the same functions.
The posterior will not properly assign probability mass around the mode as expected, and our
sample quality and downstream predictive quality will lack.
Linearizing the model aligns the weight space (thus the invariant subspaces) to the Gaussian
approximation, thus achieving the desired invariance under reparameterization, and once again
assigning probability mass as expected. This is illustrated in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Reproduced figure from Roy et al. (2024). We note the difference in how proba-
bility mass is assigned to reparametrization families (different weight configurations that lead
to identical functions) between LLA (left) and classical LA (right). The metric relates to the
Riemannian lens adopted in the original paper.

The challenges of the GGN approximation. While the GGN approximation has clear
benefits, there are some aspects of its formulation that prove challenging when scaling up both
the neural network and dataset. In the written out sum in Eq. 2.9, we explicitly see the linear
runtime complexity scaling w.r.t. dataset size N, and further, the shape of the Jacobians Jθ ∈
RO×D mean that the memory cost of operations involving G ∈RD×D scale quadratically with
number of model parameters D.
The quadratic memory scaling with D is usually handled with a further approximation to the
GGN itself.
Most prominent are structural approximations such as diagonal approximation
which drops all the cross-covariance terms, the last-layer approximation which only considers
the parameters of the final neural net layer, and the Kronecker-factored approximate curvature
method which computes a layer-wise block factorization. See Daxberger et al. (2021) for a survey
on these approximations.
The approach we will take involves matrix free methods. Our matrix free methods use the exact
GGN operator (not the matrix!) and instead approximate the functions we compute with it. For
example, drawing samples from the posterior involves a matrix-vector product with the inverse
square root of the GGN; we will later derive an approximation for this operation (and others)
that never materializes the GGN matrix.
To tackle the linear scaling with dataset size, we will draw inspiration from the Gaussian Process
literature.
2.3
A detour into Gaussian Processes
We will now stray away from the domain of Bayesian Neural Networks for a bit, and delve into
another Bayesian model, namely Gaussian Processes (GPs), since it turns out that there are
quite strong connections between LLA and GPs. Therefore, we will now look in the GP toolbox
to find some inspiration on how to proceed with our challenge of handling the coupling between
the runtime complexity and the dataset size.

2.3.2
Constructing sparse Gaussian Processes
To combat the rampant memory and computation scaling with dataset size, we introduce the
inducing point approximation to GPs. We assume that the full dataset contains a lot of redun-
dancy in regards to informing the GP. The central idea is then to compress the GP f ∼GP(µ, K)
to obtain the sparse approximation fZ ∼GP(µZ, KZ), where in particular KZ ∈RM×M is much
smaller than K ∈RN×N.

2.4.1
Variational LLA using the GP formulation
A natural idea could now be to combine variational GP compression (subsection 2.3.2) with the
GP formulation of LLA (subsection 2.3.3), which is exactly the approach of Ortega et al. (2023).
They first cast the LLA posterior as a GP using the kernel in Eq. 2.22 and then apply the
variational sparse GP bound from Titsias (2009), choosing Z and a variational covariance S by
maximizing the ELBO. They modify the procedure s.t. the predictive mean remains unchanged,
i.e. the original MAP.
Computationally (per iteration) and memory-wise, the method scales as O(M3) per mini-batch
for the GP kernels - thus eliminating the GP formulation’s scaling with dataset size N. In
many experiments, the outperform the structural approximations to full LLA discussed earlier
(diagonal, last-layer, KFAC), with comparable or even lower computational cost. In particular
on CIFAR-10, they outperform (structurally approximated) full LLA with M = 100 inducing
points.

3.2
Matrix free methods
As the name suggests, matrix free linear algebra involves constructing operations that do not
require materializing the full matrix involved. In our downstream program, we want to expose
the matrix as a function that returns matrix-vector products, instead of storing a dense D × D
array. Usually this involves paying a higher computational cost to get a lower memory cost.
Calculating the computational and memory costs is complex for the methods we will present.
As many of the methods build on top of each other, the full picture easily becomes convoluted.
Throughout this section, we will present tables with a practical overview of such costs. These
are meant to give an idea of the asymptotic complexities of the methods, which is useful when
we later use them to build algorithms.
3.2.1
Linear operator oracles
The basic idea is that if we have a linear operator function X : Rn →Rm that computes the
matrix-vector product v 7→Xv without forming X, then we can approximate various functions
of the underlying matrix X ∈Rn×m. Such a function is called an operator oracle, and we will
use it extensively in conjunction with JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018), a deep learning library in
the functional programming paradigm. In addition to just-in-time compilation to both CPU
and GPU, JAX is designed to support eﬀicient matrix free implementations, making it a natural
choice for our code.
Central to the matrix free approach is the change in mindset from thinking in materialized
matrices and dense operations.
Instead, we turn to thinking about which operators we can
compute, and how these might be used to compute matrix functions. If the underlying matrix is
suﬀiciently large, eﬀiciently computing matrix functions and operations with the oracles usually
involves some type of approximation, either numerical or stochastic.
We will discuss these
approximations in the next sections, but first we will introduce our immediately available oracle
functions and how we can combine them to build a GGN oracle.
A Jacobian-vector product or jvp is - as the name suggests - the operator computing matrix-

and after the blocks, s.t. we lower the peak memory usage accordingly. However, we will mainly
use checkpointing at a higher function level (specifically for stochastic trace estimation, see next
section) due to the relatively small neural net sizes we will be using.

since they will turn out to still be constrained by memory budget.

Experiments
4
To quantify the performance of the presented methods, we will first apply them on various toy
examples. For these, we will primarily look at 2-dimensional binary classification in order to
visualize the results. The purpose of the toy examples is primarily to verify that the methods
work, and measure the impact of the different matrix free approximations we utilize.
For larger examples, we will look at image classification, and here we will only be able to run
the matrix free methods. Hence, we will report our metrics and attempt to quantify the scaling
behavior of the methods.
Both the full LLA and KiLLA performance is highly dependent on the MAP estimator we base
it on. Thus we will measure performance of KiLLA in different settings relative to MAP and full
LLA when possible. Direct comparisons with other LLA literature are limited by the fact that
we have to use neural net architectures that fit within the budget of the GPU resources while
still performing well enough to give meaningful results. This turns out to be tricky in particular
for the ResNet/CIFAR10 combination that is common in Bayesian deep learning literature.
Metrics. To quantify performance, we will use the standard metrics of accuracy on a held-out
test set and negative log-likelihood (NLL), which we also used for the MAP training.
To further measure the quality of the full predictive distribution, we will also use calibration
metrics. Expected calibration error (ECE) (Naeini et al., 2015) bins examples by their predicted
confidence and computes the weighted average gap between the mean confidence and empirical
accuracy in each bin; an ECE of 0 indicates perfect calibration. Brier score (Brier, 1950) is
the mean-squared difference between predicted class-probability vector and the one-hot encoded
label; it ranges between 0 (perfect fit) and 2 (every entry is confidently incorrect).
Finally, we will use out-of-distribution (OOD) AUC. Here, in-distribution (ID) samples are
treated as negatives and OOD samples are positives, and we then rank them by confidence
(negative maximum predicted class probability), and compute area under the ROC curve; a
score of 0.5 is equivalent to random guessing, while 1.0 is perfect OOD detection.

Discussion & conclusion
5
In this thesis, we introduced KiLLA, a framework for scalable Linearized Laplace Approxima-
tions (LLA), centered around an Inducing Point approximation to the generalized Gauss-Newton
(GGN) matrix optimized via KL-divergence, as well as matrix free optimization and sampling
algorithms.
While the framework has yielded solid results in the realm of toy examples, it still underperforms
somewhat on the larger datasets.
On MNIST and FashionMNIST, we see a substantial improvement in OOD detection over the
MAP estimator, matching performance in held-out test set accuracy, but a substantial gap in
NLL, Brier and ECE.
Results for CIFAR10 seem to indicate that the current version of the framework is still con-
strained by memory costs, since we can only reasonably afford to use M = 50 inducing points
with a reasonable number of Hutch++ samples and Krylov basis vectors for approximating
trace and log-determinant. This seems to be too few inducing points to approximate LLA for
CIFAR10, and the results are inconclusive.
Additionally, our CIFAR10 experiments used a
scaled down ResNet architecture compared to related works (Immer, Korzepa, & Bauer, 2021;
Ortega et al., 2023).
Thus, optimizations to memory costs are needed to perform large-scale experiments to thor-
oughly quantify performance in high-dimensional domains. An obvious avenue for improvement
is to implement additional checkpointing logic, s.t. we cache less intermediate values during the
forward pass, but rather recompute them during the backward pass.
Another limitation is the problem of selecting a good prior precision α.
We found α to be
crucial for the KiLLA optimization process itself and for downstream performance. α is diﬀicult
to select; with inspiration from Immer, Korzepa, and Bauer (2021), in our experiments, we
sweep over logarithmically spaced candidate values to find the best one, but in practice, a better
mechanism for selection or even a learnable α would be a clear direction of improvement.
A procedure such as the one presented by Immer, Bauer, et al. (2021) is worth exploring in
this regard. This was out of scope for this project, but a procedure that can optimize α while
foregoing the GGN as prerequisite would be of high value to the KiLLA framework.
If the limitations are addressed, we will be able to see if the performance of KiLLA on large-scale
problems matches the potential shown in the small-scale experiments.
