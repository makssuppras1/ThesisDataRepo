Abstract
Shale gas reservoirs, especially liquid rich shale, play an important role in the global oil and gas produc-
tion, meanwhile, they pose many new technical and theoretical challenges. One of the most important is
the poor understanding of the phase equilibrium of multicomponent ﬂuids in extremely tight reservoirs.
The aim of this work is to develop a robust and efﬁcient algorithm for two-phase PT ﬂash in the presence
of capillary pressure. The algorithm is a modiﬁcation of the classical Successive Substitution method
where the Young-Laplace equation is incorporated to describe the difference between the two phases. A
PVT algorithm for Constant Mass Expansion (CME) in the presence of capillary pressure is, then, devel-
oped using the ﬂash algorithm as the main calculation tool. The ﬂash and CME algorithms were tested on
several binary mixtures and more complex ﬂuids and were able to converge in a wide range of tempera-
tures and pressures, even close to the critical point, producing results comparable to the results obtained
from existing literature and other calculation tools. The CME algorithm was subsequently used to study
the inﬂuence of capillary pressure on the mixture PVT properties, components phase molar fractions and
Formation Volume Factors (FVF). The presence of capillary pressure reduces the bubble point pressure,
causing the light components to remain in solution in the liquid phase at lower pressures. This is reﬂected
in the computed increase of GOR, Bo and gas density and the decrease in liquid density and Bg. The in-
ﬂuence of pore size, was also assessed for pore radii ranging from 10 nm to 100 nm, showing that the
capillary effect decreases as the pore size increases, becoming almost negligible for pore size of 50 nm.
Three CME simulation tests were also performed for temperatures ranging from 300K to 400K on a non
volatile oil, showing a decrease in bubble point suppression, phase densities and FVF variations as the
temperature increases. Lastly, three CME simulations were performed for oil with different GOR, showing
a decrease in bubble point pressure and capillary effect when the oil becomes more volatile. However it
was also observed that oil composition has a major effect on phase densities and FVF variations due to
capillary pressure.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Literature Review
Low permeability rocks and shale reservoirs have become an important source of gas and oil in the last
decades. They now play an essential role in the energy matrix of several countries. With the recent drop
in oil price, tight reservoirs have lost most of the interest from the oil industry, however it is reasonable
to assume that with the development of new technologies and the continue demand for oil from the
emerging markets, unconventional resources, such as shale gas and shale oil, will continue to play an im-
portant role in the future energy market. These unconventional reservoirs pose new technical challenges
and they are not entirely understood. As stated by Sandoval et al. (2015) one of the major challenges is to
understand the thermodynamics and the phase equilibrium in conﬁned spaces, with pores on the order
of nanometers. Such understanding is crucial to better estimate the amount of hydrocarbon in place and
forecast its production.
According to Kuila and Prasad (2013), the shale matrix consists of pores that can vary in size from
less than 2 nm up to meso-pores in the range of 2-50 nm. Several other authors have reported similar
results,( Li et al. (2014),Clarkson et al. (2012) cited in Rezaveisi et al. (2015)). In this extremely constrained
space, the capillary pressure cannot be neglected anymore and it will affect the phase behavior of the
ﬂuid. This effect was investigated by several authors and, sometimes, with contradictory results. Fisher
and Israelachvili (1981) successfully managed to apply the thermodynamic basis of the Kelvin equation
to highly curved menisci with a radius down to 4 nm for pure components. Tindy and Raynal (1966)
measured the bubble point pressures of two crude oils in both a PVT cell and a porous medium with
grain sizes in the range of 160-200µm reporting an increase in the pressure of few percents for the latter
case. However when they used a mixture of methane and n-heptane they could not ﬁnd any considerable

difference. Trebin and Zadora (1968) found that, for gas condensate mixtures, an increase in dew point
pressure of 10−15% could be observed. On the other hand, Sigmund et al. (1973) performed a theoretical
and experimental study on the effect of capillary pressure on binary mixtures and concluded that equi-

Other authors tried to address the problem in a numerical way. Brusilovsky et al. (1992) used a ther-
modynamic model to numerically study the phase behavior of multicomponent ﬂuids. The author con-
cluded that when pores size reduces and the interface curvature increases, the bubble point pressure
decreases and the dewpoint pressures increases. Moreover, he noticed that the effect becomes negligi-
ble for very high reservoir pressures (> 20MPa). A similar approach was also used by Pang et al. (2012).
Nojabaei et al. (2012) numerically modeled the inﬂuence of capillary pressure on Bakken oil and sev-
eral other binary mixtures, observing that, for small pores, the bubble point pressure decreases and the
dew point pressure can decrease or increase depending on the region of the phase envelope. They also
found that for Bakken shale a pore size of 10 nm leads to a reduction of up to 900psi in the bubble
point pressure. Similar results were found by Sandoval et al. (2015). In their paper the authors developed
an algorithm for calculating the phase envelope in multicomponent mixtures under a capillary pressure
difference. They found that the capillary pressure modiﬁes the saturation pressure in the whole enve-
lope except at the critical point. They also noticed a shift in the cricondentherm to higher temperatures.
Shapiro and Stenby (2001) made an in depth analysis on the capillary equilibrium problem in the space of
the intensive variables T,P,µ1,...,µnc. In their work, using phase surface geometry concepts, the authors
concluded that, in the space of (P,T,µ), where P is the phase pressure, T is the temperature and µ is the
chemical potential vector, the capillary equilibrium is possible only between the true equilibrium point,
where both chemical potentials and phase pressures are equal, and the points where the compressibility,

1.3
The Isothermal Two Phase Flash
As stated in Michelsen and Mollerup (2007) the isothermal ﬂash calculation, i.e. equilibrium calculation
at speciﬁed T and P, is probably the most important and one of the most widely used equilibrium calcula-
tion. The reason is to ﬁnd in the ability to easily create a robust and reliable numerical algorithm to solve
isothermal problems. Its simplicity and efﬁciency makes the PT Flash ideal as a building block for other
equilibrium calculation problems. One other reason for its popularity is the fact that the correct solution
of the isothermal ﬂash, always represent the global minimum in the total Gibbs energy. There are two
main strategies to develop a PT ﬂash algorithm; the oldest is through the use of the successive substitu-
tion method, where the Rachford-Rice equation is solved for ideal mixtures in an inner loop, while the
equilibrium factors are update in the outer loop computing the fugacities from an EoS. Its popularity can
==PAGE:15==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
6
be ascribed to its simpleness and robustness; giving a reasonable ﬁrst estimate, the algorithm, with rare
exceptions, will converge. However its efﬁciency is strongly reduced close to the critical region, where the
characteristics of the two phases tend to converge. Moreover if the initial estimate is not good enough,
the algorithm may converge to a trivial solution, that is a false, non stable, single phase solution. In order
to avoid this problem, the PT Flash is coupled with stability analysis as it will be explained later in the
section. The second strategy is to treat the problem as a minimization problem. In this scenario the ob-
jective function to be minimized is the reduced Gibbs energy (G/RT ). This method is more efﬁcient near
the critical point, but it requires the knowledge of the fugacity composition derivatives. Before 1980 the
successive substitution method coupled with the Newton’s method for solving the Rachford-Rice equa-
tion was the most widespread algorithm for solving isothermal equilibrium problems (Prausnitz et al.
(1967)).

Stability Analysis

The algorithm developed for the PT Flash is based on the successive substitution method, similar to the
strategy proposed by Michelsen and Mollerup (2007). Moreover the Negative Flash methodology was
used. The major reason to prefer this this strategy to the classical one is that, since no stability analysis
is performed, it allows some control on the stability of the solution in the single phase region. Indeed,
as stated by Whitson and Michelsen (1989), if the algorithm converges to a solution different from the
trivial solution with β < 0 or β > 1, it is reasonably possible to assume the mixture to be stable. The PT
ﬂash equations were coupled with the Young-Laplace equation which accounts for the capillary pressure
difference between the liquid and gas phase. The phase behavior was described using an SRK equation of
state. All the systems considered are of pores larger than 10nm. Thus, the inﬂuence of the solid surfaces
on the ﬂuid, and the conﬁnement effect of the ﬂuid were neglected.

2.1.3
Considerations on Initial Estimates
For this work the equation proposed by Wilson for the equilibrium factors is used. Recalling section 1.3.3
in the previous chapter, Wilson’s approximation behave reasonably well for a wide spread of hydrocarbon
mixtures in several scenarios. Nevertheless the estimation could lead to very erroneous values, especially
in supercritical conditions. It was observed that, sometime and particularly in single phase regions, these
initial estimates would lead the Newton’s method applied to the Rachford-Rice equation to fail, probably
due to the particularly “knee “ form of the equation with very steep asymptotes and an abruptly change of
the function’s gradient close to the stationary point, that will lead the Newton’s method to overshoot. The
same phenomenon was observed in early iteration with the ﬁrst estimation of capillary pressure, leading
to wrong estimation of the second phase pressure and consequently of the equilibrium factors. To avoid
this problem it was decided to make use of the bisectional method in early iterations while solving the
Rachford-Rice equation. In the speciﬁc case 5 iterations of bisection are performed before switching to
Newton. It was observed that the velocity of convergence was not affected in a substantial way.

Chapter 3
Results and Discussions
The aim of this chapter is to test the robustness and reliability of the algorithms developed and, at the
same time, to investigate how the capillary pressure affects phase densities, vapor and liquid molar com-
position, saturation pressure and, consequently, Formation Volume Factors.
In the ﬁrst section the PT Flash algorithm is tested for several binary mixtures ranging from C1 −C3 to
C1 −C7 to a more complex 7-component mixture. This choice allows the algorithm to be tested in sce-
narios with increasing non ideality. The results obtained are compared to the accordant calculations
perfomed by the authors, showing that the program is able to withstand simple and more complex mix-
tures with high accuracy. The linearity of convergence is, then , proved.
In the second section, the CME algorithm is tested using the same 7 component mixture and a binary
mixture of C1-C4 . The test is performed using a very high capillary radius, simulating a system with 0
capillary pressure, and comparing the results computed with a DTU developed simulator called SPECS.
Once the reliability of the algorithm is proven, the program is used to study the inﬂuence of the capillary
pressure on PVT properties and FVF using real oil mixtures described in Whitson and Sunjerga (2012) .
The detailed description of the oils molar compositions and components bulk properties, can be found
in the appendix. Moreover the effect of temperature, pore radius and oil composition are also studied in
the subsequent subsections.

be attributed to the suppression of the bubble point that causes gas to remain in oil solution longer as

leads, as a consequence, to a signiﬁcant underestimation of GOR, Bo and Bg, when capillary pressure is
not taken into consideration, as also mentioned by Firincioglu (2014).

==PAGE:79==

the bubble point variation. It is interesting to observe that ligher oils have higher variations, in absolute
value, for GOR, Bo and liquid density. This phenomenon could, probably, be explained by the higher
solubility for light components that light oils have compared to heavy ones. For this reason an higher
amount of light components, like methane, will remain in solution in the volatile oil liquid phase when
capillary pressure is applied. As a result, the liquid phase will have a higher decrease in density. These
light components will be liberated when the ﬂuid is brought at surface conditions, explaining the higher
variation in GOR and the higher shrinkage of the oil, due to the higher mass lost. It is also interesting to
observe that the variation in Bg and gas density do not seem to be very affected by the variation in oil
composition.

4.1
Summary and Conclusions
The aim of this work was to develop a robust and reliable algorithm for PT Flash in the presence of cap-
illary pressure. The algorithm was then used to develop a constant mass expansion simulator to investi-
gate how capillary pressure inﬂuence the PVT simulation results. The main conclusion of this work are
as follows:
1. Our successive substitution algorithm for negative ﬂash, always intialized by the Wilson equation,
has proven to be reliable and accurate, being able to precisely compute equilibrium solutions even
in difﬁcult regions like the critical one region. Moreover the algorithm is able to handle a variety of
mixtures, ranging from the simple binary to complex oils typical in shale gas reservoirs in a wide
range of temperatures and pressures.
2. The algorithm never converged to the trivial solution during the simulations, however this does
not mean that it is impossible to happen. The use of the negative ﬂash method developed by Whit-
son and Michelsen (1989) gives an indication of the phase stability when β converges to a value
different from the trivial solution.
3. The CME simulator has proven to be reliable and robust too. The results obtained with capillary
radius of 109m found to be in agreement with the results without capillary pressure obtained using
SPECS, a simulator developed at the Technical University of Denmark.
71
==PAGE:81==
CHAPTER 4. SUMMARY
72
4. Capillary pressure has a strong effect on the PVT properties of the mixture, causing a reduction in
bubble point pressure, a decrease in liquid and an increase in vapor density. Indeed it was observed
a relative increase in gas density ranging from 2.83% for 14.5MPa up to 265.79% for pressures as
low as 0.44MPa. In the same pressure interval, a relative reduction of liquid density was observed,
oscillating between −0.72% and −1.46%. This, consequently, heavily affects the FVF . Indeed it
was computed that, for an oil with OGR of 2000ST B/MMsc f (equivalent to a solution GOR of
89sm3/sm3), its GOR could be underestimated from 4.5sm3/sm3 for pressure of tens of MPa up
to 6sm3/sm3 for pressures as low as 1MPa when the capillary pressure is not taken into account.
An increase in Bo was also observed, ranging from 1.28% for 14.5MPa to 2.51% for 0.44MPa. On
the other hand, a reduction in Bg has been observed, and its relative variation greatly increase in
absolute value when the pressure is reduced, ranging from −3.26% to −78.52% at the same pressure
range. This is due to the fact that light components tend to stay in solution at lower pressure,
making the liquid phase lighter. Indeed, as it can be observed from the results, the molar fraction
of very light components, like methane, ethane and propane, is higher in conﬁned liquids when
compared at the same pressure with unconﬁned ones. This is a direct consequence of bubble point
suppression.
5. Capillary radius strongly inﬂuences capillary pressure. It was observed that the effect of capillary
pressure becomes relevant when the pore is of the order of 10nm. For 50nm, capillarity is almost
negligible and at 100nm no difference in numerical results were observed compared to unconﬁned
mixtures.
6. Temperature seems to negatively affect bubble point suppression. Indeed, capillary pressure tends
to reduce at higher temperatures, becoming 0 at the critical point.
7. Oil composition has a major effect on capillarity effect. Lighter oils show a lower bubble point
suppression. This phenomenon is caused by the lower density difference between the vapor and
liquid phase that, as consequence, reduces the interfacial tension. An higher absolute difference in
GOR and relative difference in Bo is observed for lighter oils.
4.2
Discussions and Recommendations for Further Work
1. The algorithm accepts as input only the vapor phase pressure, the liquid pressure is back calcu-
lated from the converged capillary pressure. The reason for this is that Wilson initial estimation
==PAGE:82==
CHAPTER 4. SUMMARY
73
would not accept negative pressures when the equilibrium is solved in the negative liquid pres-
sure region. Moreover, since the capillary pressure is not known a priori, the vapor phase pressure
could become negative too in the ﬁrst iterations, preventing the algorithm to converge. It would
be interesting, then, to investigate other initial conditions for the equilibrium factors and capil-
lary pressure, when the liquid pressure is used as the reference pressure and the computations are
performed in the negative liquid pressure region.
2. In his work, Wang et al. (2013) extended the tangent plane criterion incorporating the liquid and
vapor pressures in the equation. However, the extension is too simplistic and does not have a sound
theoretical background. Further study on a proper stability analysis criterion, probably based on
Helmholtz energy, can be tried in the future for equilibrium calculations with capillary pressure.
