Abstract
The major challenge in many embedded system applications operating in remote areas
is the provisioning, deployment and communication between systems. Most Internet Of
Things (IoT) systems rely on Wireless Fidelity (WI­FI) or satellite network communica­
tion techniques to perform their tasks with a reasonable level of latency and reliability.
However what happens when these techniques cannot be used? For instance commu­
nication between autonomous systems in a designated area where satellite signals are
unavailable, becomes a significant challenge.
Another critical challenge is the absence of Continious Intergration & Continious Deploy­
ment (CI/CD) pipelines for edge systems. CI/CD pipelines are designed for cloud based
environments where stable connectivity enables operations like updates and monitoring.
In remote areas where network availability is limited, deploying updates and maintaining
Artificial Intelligence (AI) models becomes significantly more complex. Without an efficient
CI/CD mechanism updating edge AI models requires manual intervention increasing op­
erational costs and reducing adaptability to new firmware updates.
Using edge AI models in inference mode offers a significant advantage over traditional
cloud based AI solutions in such environments. Their capabilities and reduced latency
improve response time and enhance system reliability even in network constrained or
fully offline scenarios.
The beating heart of edge computing is the embedded system. By leveraging the utilities
of Zephyr Real Time Operating System (RTOS) it is possible to create a robust, precise,
reliable and power efficient embedded system.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Systems working on the edge provide the benefit of instant decision­making which is
crucial for critical applications. In today’s rapidly evolving technological landscape the
optimization and performance of AI models are advancing at an accelerated pace. To
maintain compatibility with these advancements, reliable and efficient software update
mechanisms are essential. In parallel, a monitoring system is critical for tracking system
performance and identifying potential issues in remote or inaccessible locations (predic­
tive maintenance, fault tolerance).
Remote environments where WI­FI and Global Positioning System (GPS) are unavailable
struggle on maintaining data communication between the embedded device and a cen­
tral server. This communication channel is necessary shouldn’t be used for transmitting
sensor data but for enabling over­the­air firmware updates and system monitoring. This
communication enhancements could help improve utility and operational efficiency of AI
applications used in embedded systems on the edge.
The Long Range (LoRa) Point­to­Point (P2P) communication technology can be used for
such conditions. When combined with the reliability and security features of the Zephyr
RTOS, it provides a solid foundation for implementing CI/CD automation on the edge.
1.2
Proposed Solution
This master’s thesis project aims to design and implement an embedded system that in­
tegrates TinyML capabilities with the Zephyr RTOS core functionalities on the Espressif
32 S3 Board (ESP32S3) DevKitC microcontroller. The system will locally process sen­
sor data to enable real time decision making while maintaining low power consumption.
The project will evaluate system performance in terms of latency, energy efficiency and
reliability.
To enable CI/CD automation the project adopts GitLab CI/CD for the continuous training,
validation and deployment of AI models. The CI/CD pipelines powered by GitLab Runners
and Docker containerization will automate the building and configuration of Zephyr RTOS
images, streamlining the integration of updated functionalities into the embedded system.
These images will include integrated functionalities such as the AI inference model, LoRa
communication and other utilities which are offered by Zephyr.
All components will be deployed remotely to the embedded system via LoRa P2P to cre­
ate a low­latency communication with robustness and adaptability in real world operating
conditions.

Theoretical Foundation
2.1
Embedded Systems
An embedded system is a specialized computer system. It combines a processor, mem­
ory, and input/output peripherals to perform a dedicated function within a larger mechan­
ical or electronic system [1]. It is responsible for executing the physical operations of
the device it is embedded in and in most cases, it operates under real­time computing
constraints [2].
Figure 2.1: Embedded system hardware.
Most embedded systems are usually based on microcontrollers but in more complex se­
tups standard microprocessors are also used. Those system are built for specific tasks
where optimization is integrated on the design to reduce size and cost while improving
performance. They are utilized across a wide range of applications including industrial,
automotive, household, medical and military domains. The real challenge starts when
these systems are part of safety critical operations especially in aerospace and defense.
Most of them are deployed with established communication through wired or wireless net­
works or other methods used for IoT monitoring or control purposes. There are also cases
where embedded systems are deployed in remote isolated areas without any communi­
cation utilities, creating a secure and self contained environment for specific applications.
In this thesis project a hybrid profile will be developed, where an embedded system
(specifically a microcontroller) will be deployed in a remote area using a LoRa RF96
module for sporadic communication and monitoring. This semi isolated system design
is suitable for operating in remote locations, while still maintaining the ability to provide
critical communication with a server.

2.2
Microcontrollers
Microcontrollers are ”tiny computers” built on a single integrated circuit. Their basic com­
ponents are, one or more CPUs, some memory and programmable input/output peripher­
als. Program memory usually in the form of NOR flash, OTP ROM or ferroelectric RAM is
often integrated on the chip along with a small amount of RAM. They are built for embed­
ded applications where low power consumption, efficiency and security can be achieved
with minimal resources. Figure 2.2 presents an ESP32S3 microcontroller which is the
core component of this thesis.
Figure 2.2: Espressif 32 S3 DevKitC System on Chip (SoC) microcontroller.
Image © Espressif Systems. Used for educational purposes.
A microcontroller is somewhat similar to a System on a Chip (SoC). However,a SoC is a
more advanced system that includes a microcontroller along with other components such
as a WI­FI module, a Bluetooth module or even additional coprocessors. These powerful
and compact computers are used in the applications described above. Their ability to
reduce hardware size and cost makes them widely applicable. A microcontroller itself
can also be used as an embedded system [3].
These systems must provide Real Time Computing (RTC) and be able to respond to
events in the embedded system they are controlling. They use a functionality called an
interrupt to signal the processor when certain events occur, causing it to pause the current
instruction sequence and begin executing an Interrupt Service Routine (ISR). This routine
also performs the necessary processing based on the interrupt source before returning to
the original instruction sequence.
To operate the microcontroller, programs are stored in the available on­chip memory.
These programs are compiled or assembled into machine code and saved in memory.
Some devices use permanent memory where the code is fixed while others use field­
alterable flash memory that allows the code to be updated whenever a new binary is
flashed.

2.3
LoRa
LoRa was developed by Cycleo a company of Grenoble, France and patented in 2014 [4].
It is based on spread spectrum modulation techniques derived from Chirp Spread Spec­
trum (CSS) technology [5]. LoRa transmits data using radio waves through frequency
modulated chirps which are encoded into symbols representing information. The trans­
mission begins with a preamble consisting of several identical chirps to alert the receiver,
followed by synchronization symbols that help align the reception window. Once synchro­
nization is achieved the data symbols are transmitted [6].
To adjust the data rate when using LoRa technology, those key parameters are taken into
consideration:
• Bandwidth (BW) refers to the frequency range of the transmission band and defines
the width of the transmitted signal. A larger bandwidth enables a higher data rate but
reduces receiver sensitivity, thereby decreasing the effective communication range
as the signal becomes more difficult to detect. LoRa supports channel bandwidths
of 125kHz, 250kHz, or 500kHz.
• Time on Air (ToA) refers to the total time required for a device to transmit an entire
packet over the air.
• Duty Cycle (DC) creates the proportion of time the device is actively transmitting
within a defined time periode expressed as a percentage:
Duty Cycle = Time over the air
Total Time Period × 100%

• Symbol Transmission Period (Ts) is defined by the time required to transmit a
single symbol give by:

the rest are for error correction. Different coding rates balance between data speed
and reliability affecting error correction, data rate, and transmission time. Some

2.4
Zephyr Real Time Operating System
Zephyr is an open­source and lightweight real­time operating system (RTOS) designed
for connected, resource constrained embedded devices which can support multiple hard­
ware architectures [10]. It includes a kernel along with essential components such as li­
braries, device drivers, protocol stacks, file systems and firmware update utilities required
for developing complete application software [11].
Figure 2.5: Zephyr RTOS Ecosystem.
Image © Zephyr Foundation. Used for educational purposes.
For Zephyr Operating System (OS), a subsystem is defined as a logically separate seg­
ment of the operating system that manages particular functionalities or offers specific ser­
vices [12]. These subsystems may encompass elements like networking, communication
protocols, classes of device drivers, file systems, power management, and additional util­
ities. Designed to be modular each subsystem can be configured, tailored, and enhanced
to achieve optimal performance while addressing the unique needs of various embedded
applications.
Zephyr is supported by an open­source community which ensures that its code under­
goes ongoing review and enhancement by developers globally. This collaborative model
fortifies the project through regular evaluations of vulnerabilities and security measures
[13].

Tiny Machine Learning
Generally, TinyML integrates ML with embedded system architecture into a cohesive solu­
tion. TinyML involves the implementation of machine learning models on ultra­low­power
hardware, such as microcontrollers, to facilitate real­time inference on the device.
To begin with, TinyML is a domain within computer science that converts applied statistical
techniques into executable code, allowing systems to identify patterns in data and make
informed decisions. It is a subset of TinyML where a machine, typically a computer, learns
to replicate human reasoning and decision making processes by training on data with the
help of mathematical models and algorithms. Instead of depending on explicitly defined
rules, machine learning empowers systems to enhance their performance as they are
exposed to increasing amounts of data. Figure 2.6 depicts the hierarchical structure of
AI subfields, culminating in deep learning, a specific subset where critical features are
extracted and model generalization often reaches its highest capability.
Figure 2.6: Artificial intelligence and the subfields.
Using TinyML forIoT devices creates a intelligent system that can operate in remote lo­

Low complexity models can be trained on general purpose computers and the resulting
models can be deployed to microcontrollers for on­field inference. In today’s world micro­
controllers are built with dedicated accelerators for tasks required by higher complexity
architectures like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), allowing these small devices to run
more computational heavy models. In some cases they can even support deep learn­

Continious Integration & Continious Deployment
Continuous practices play a key role in many organizations and software engineering
teams during the development of new features and products. These techniques form an
ongoing cycle of product improvement. The concept of CI/CD, though rooted in software
engineering, follows a unique logic that can be applied to various scientific and technical
fields [16]. The core stages of Continious Intergration (CI) are:
• Planning: The initial phase of a product, where all requirements are gathered and
organized to start the development process.
• Code: The stage where human­readable code is written to create a usable compo­
nent or even a complete application.
• Build: The stage where the written code is compiled and packaged into executable
artifacts, including all dependencies, configuration files, and necessary resources
required for deployment.
• Test: The stage where the application is tested and validated to ensure that core
functionalities work as expected and common issues are identified and resolved.
Those four stages represent the CI where the beggining and eventually the testing of
application takes place ready to be released and processed. Furthermore the core stages
of Continious Deployment (CD) are:
• Release: The phase where the application is prepared and made available in con­
trolled environments for final validation before production.
• Deploy: The stage where the application is delivered and deployed into the produc­
tion environment for end users.
• Operate: The stage where the application runs in the production environment, often
orchestrated by tools like Kubernetes [17] to manage performance and availability.
• Monitor: The stage where valuable metrics are generated, and full observability of
the system is established to enable alerting and issue tracking.
In this thesis the foundational software development for the embedded system is based
on Zephyr OS. By applying continuous practices a powerful pipeline for embedded system
development emerges. All these concepts come together to shape the final implementa­
tion in chapter 5.

4.1
Transmission Control Protocol (TCP)
The Internet relies heavily on the TCP protocol. Its distinctive characteristics have made
it a widely adopted. Through TCP different hosts on the Internet can establish reliable
connections for the exchange of data packets. The protocol provides essential guarantees
for reliable data transmission between hosts. The most important features include:
• Stability: Ensures reliability by automatically retrying transmissions when packet
loss occurs.
• Ordered Delivery: Guarantees that packets arrive at the recipient in the exact order
in which they were sent.
TCP establishes a reliable connection between sender and receiver through a three­way
handshake (Synchronize (SYN), SYN­A, Acknowledgement (ACK)) and closes the con­
nection with a four­step process (Last packet from sender (FIN), ACK, FIN, ACK) to ensure
proper termination [20].
This communication method ensures error free and in­order delivery of data using ac­
knowledgments (ACKs) to confirm receipt.
It prevents data overflow by adapting the
transmission rate to the receiver’s buffer size and uses checksums in the header to de­
tect corrupted data and trigger retransmissions if necessary. TCP is commonly used in
applications that require reliable and ordered data transfer such as web browsing, email
and remote login.
Figure 4.1: TCP three way handshake workflow.

RF­96 transceivers used in this thesis. Those concepts will be examined in detail in the

4.2
Reliable data transfers with LoRa
Reliability is a core component in communication systems. Most commonly used tech­
nologies such as WI­FI and Satellite communication ensure reliable data transfer by using
protocols like TCP and others built on top of the physical layer. However in cases where
a stable connection can’t be guaranteed such as remote and isolated areas up to 3 kilo­
meters from any infrastructure LoRa still manages to deliver data effectively offering good
performance with very low power consumption.
It is well know that LoRa faces reliability issues especially due to packet loss when the
communication range exceeds 1 kilometer [21]. Research on the field has shown that a
waiting protocol in comparison with a simple P2P protocol for LoRa communication can
improve packet delivery without needing a gateway. Previous studies show it increased
the packet reception rate for 100­byte payloads from 49.87% to 97.52% [22].
Another research paper shows that using the P2P simple protocol resulted in lower deliv­
ery times but with a high packet loss rate up to 87% at a distance of 3 kilometers. On the
other hand when using a waiting protocol at the same distance the longer wait time led
to a higher packet reception ratio as shown in Table 4.1. This is because more transmis­
sion packets are sent with higher ToA and by introducing a wait period, packet loss was
reduced by 51% [23].
These issues can be improved or even eliminated in some cases by implementing proper
retry mechanisms and using an acknowledgment handshake to confirm packet delivery.
This can be done by creating a TCP­Like packet creator and reader for the communication

Model Optimization and Quantization Techniques
The limited amount of Random Access Memory (RAM) (typically less than 512 KB) in
most microcontrollers introduces a major resource constraint which adds even more re­
quirements on the TensorFlow Lite Micro exporter tool. While TensorFlow models are
initially trained using floating point precision, since it’s the most flexible and is heavily
optimized for CPUs and GPUs, it becomes impractical when those models need to be
deployed on devices with very limited memory. In such cases quantized formats like 8­bit
representations become extremely valuable.
TensorFlow allows floating point models to be converted into quantized versions [28] which
makes them suitable for microcontroller deployment. Although this conversion adds extra
complexity to the model export process. Another useful optimization is constant folding
where constant expressions are pre­computed and embedded into the model even in
complex operations like batch normalization [29]. Operations that are only relevant during
training such as dropout are also removed during export.

Image recognition problems are effectively addressed using Convolutional Neural Net­

regularization techniques and careful data preprocessing are applied to improve general­
ization ability of the model. By training a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with strong
regularization and diverse data it is possible to build a versatile model capable of making
accurate predictions in real­world scenarios [31].

4.5.1
How a CNN Model Works
In this thesis a specific use case is explored in which the microcontroller’s secondary
Central Processing Unit (CPU) core application (APPCPU) runs a CNN model inference
to classify images of grapevine leaves. These images are represented as 3D arrays of
pixel values which serve as inputs for training the model in the initial stage.
The process begins with convolution where small kernels of NxN arrays (weights) slide
over the input image to perform element­wise multiplication and summation to extract lo­
cal features. By applying multiple kernels, a set of feature maps is produced that capture
different aspects of the image such as edges, textures or patterns. Another step is pool­
ing which reduces the spatial dimensions of the feature maps while at the same time it
retains the most important information. Pooling operations help make the network more
robust to small translations and reduce computational load. The convolution and pool­
ing steps may be repeated multiple times and progressively create a more abstract and
compressed representations of the input image. In the final stage called flattening the re­
sulting 2D feature maps are converted into a single 1D vector. This vector is then passed
into one or more fully connected layers which perform the final reasoning and produce
the classification output. At training stage this output is used to calculate a loss metric
and by adjusting the network’s weights through backpropagation a refinement of both the
convolutional and fully connected layers is done to improve accuracy and generalization.
This entire process enables the CNN to learn hierarchical features from low level edges to
high level shapes. It makes it greatly effective for image classification tasks. The trained
glscnn model deployed on the ESP32S3’s APPCPU is used to classify grapevine leaf
images into distinct categories such as Healthy, Black Rot, ESCA or Leaf Blight.

4.6.1
Docker
Docker is widely recognized as a leading software solution for containerization, providing
virtualization at the OS level. This form of isolation facilitates the establishment of various
operating systems within a single host machine. Multiple containers can be instantiated
on the host, each possessing its own distinct file system. Docker streamlines the pro­
cess of continuous integration and continuous delivery CI/CD since a new Docker image
is generated and compiled whenever code is committed to a repository. Architecturally,
Docker simplifies the implementation of a microservices framework allowing each con­
tainer to function as an independent component of the application which can be managed
separately from others[34].
Docker is composed of the following core components:
• Docker Engine This is a client­server application where the server­side component,
known as the daemon, listens for API requests and oversees Docker objects like
images, containers, networks and volumes. The client interacts with the daemon to
execute commands and manage the containers.
• Images These are fundamental building blocks of Docker. An image acts as a
read­only template that encompasses the application code, runtime, libraries and
dependencies necessary for running an application. Images are utilized to create
one or more containers.
• Registry This component stores Docker images and can be either public or private.
Public registries, such as https://hub.docker.com allow users to share and download
images freely while private registries provide secure storage and access control for
proprietary images.
• Containers These are active instances of Docker images. Containers provide iso­
lated environments that contain everything needed to run an application. Depending
on the design of the image, a container may execute one or multiple processes.

5.5.2
TCP­Like packet for reliability and robustness
To establish a robust and reliable communication layer over LoRa a custom TCP­like
structure was developed. These characteristics are implemented at the data level where
each packet is organized according to the architecture shown in Figure 5.9. Every packet
maintains a consistent structure and fits within a 172­byte payload, consisting of:
• Header – A 1­byte field used to identify the communication type.
• Device ID – A unique identifier assigned to the transmitting or receiving device.
• Type – Defines the type of message being transmitted.
• Sequence Number – Identifies the packet’s position within a sequence of transmis­
sions or receptions.
• Payload Length – Specifies the length of the actual data being transmitted.
• Payload – Contains the main data for transmission.
• Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) – A Cyclic Redundancy Check value used to
detect data corruption in each packet.
This custom packet format mirrors the logic of traditional TCP to ensure a reliable and
integrity checked communication channel over the LoRa physical layer.
Figure 5.9: TCP­Like packet architecture.
The packets are created and processed using the packet_creator and packet_reader
functions located in the tcp_packets.c C file. The packet_creator function assembles
the required packet structure by embedding all necessary fields to ensure reliability and
it applies AES­128 encryption to generate a secure packet ready for transmission. On
the receiving side the packet_reader function decrypts the incoming packet, validates all
critical fields (header, device ID, type, sequence number, payload length, payload) and
verifies the CRC for integrity. If all checks pass, the packet is considered valid and ready
to be processed either by the ESP32S3 device or the RPi 4 LoRa server.

5.5.3
Secure Packet Encryption with AES 128
Reliable communication over LoRa requires not only robustness against transmission
errors but also strong protection against eavesdropping and data tampering. To achieve
confidentiality and data integrity, AES­128 encryption is integrated into the communication
protocol.
Each packet initially consists of 172 bytes of structured data. In order to apply authen­
ticated encryption an additional 28 bytes are appended (12 bytes for the nonce and 16
bytes for the authentication tag). This results in a complete 200 byte packet that includes
all the necessary information for secure and reliable data exchange between the edge
device and the server.
Figure 5.10: AES 128 Encrypted TCP­Like packet architecture.
Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) with a 128­bit key length is used to encrypt the
payload before transmission. The encryption key is securely stored within the firmware
image. This symmetric block cipher operates on 128­bit blocks and offers a high level of
security considered extremely difficult to breach while maintaining efficient performance
on the ESP32S3. Each packet includes a randomly generated nonce and a 16­byte au­
thentication tag. The file aes_12_encryption.c is responsible for applying encryption and
decryption, utilizing the gcm, cipher, and random libraries provided by the Zephyr RTOS
within the zephyrproject framework.

5.7.4
Heatshrink stream decompression of binary images
When the 200­byte TCP­like packets are received by the LoRa RF 96 module they are first
decrypted and validated. The resulting 165­byte payload is then extracted, decompressed
and stored in a 4 kilobyte buffer. From this buffer, flash memory write operations store the
data into the slot0_appcpu_partition.
The Heatshrink library is again used on the ESP32S3 to decompress the received binary
image. The packets are written into the 4 kilobyte buffer where a Heatshrink stream de­
coder (configured with the parameters shown in Table 5.4) incrementally decompresses
the binary data as it is received. During this process some buffer bytes are written directly
to flash memory while others are temporarily held in the buffer until a sufficient sequence
of bytes is available for the decoder to decompress and write them correctly to flash. This
streaming approach ensures reliable, memory efficient decompression and storage on
the device. Figure 5.13

amount of RAM during the decompression step. Instead of storing the entire compressed

5.7.5
Flash writings and SHA256 checksum
The flash write operations for an OTA firmware update on Zephyr OS for the ESP32S3 are
performed using a streaming workflow. Each decompressed chunk of the binary image is
immediately written to the appropriate flash slot as the final step in a carefully structured
process.
The transmission of compressed packets in a precise, ordered sequence ensures a re­
liable decompression and flash storage workflow that enables successful updates. The
combination of the TCP­like packet mechanism and the Heatshrink compression method
delivers a highly efficient, lightweight and robust update process. This design is well­
suited to low­power, resource­constrained microcontrollers, effectively leveraging Zephyr
OS features to achieve excellent performance in demanding, remote deployment scenar­
ios.
During each flash write operation a SHA­256 function continuously updates a checksum
based on the current state of the written data, referencing the total size of the flashing
area (slot0_appcpu_partition). Once the entire image has been written the checksum
of the fully received zephyr.signed.bin file is computed and compared to the expected
value to validate the integrity of the firmware update. Upon successful validation an ac­
knowledgment message is sent to the RPi 4 server, confirming the completion of a reliable
update.

In this thesis project a CNN model was created to demonstrate the ability of the ESP32S3
microcontroller to run AI models in designated areas with high performance and low power
consumption. The problem that ESP32S3 must continuously solve is the classification of

5.13.1
Architecture
The architecture used for this CNN model incorporates modern deep learning techniques
while maintaining computational efficiency suitable for mobile or edge deployment (Table
5.6).
Input Layer
The input layer accepts a 64×64×3 RGB image. This moderate input size balances detail
preservation with computational efficiency. The ESP32S3 microcontroller uses only 512
kilobytes of RAM so handling smaller resolution images is crucial.
Stem Block
The stem block serves as the feature extraction stage. It starts with 12 channels to reduce
the number of parameters. It uses strided convolutions with the second convolution using
stride=2 for spatial downsampling. A ReLU6 bounded activation function is used because
it benefits quantization performance. Batch normalization momentum is set to 0.98 for
stable training.
Depthwise Separable Block
The core building blocks use depthwise separable convolutions. They reduce parameters
by separating spatial and channel­wise operations while being computationally efficient.
This reduces the floating point operations per second (FLOPs) compared to standard
convolutions while holding up performance.
Hierarchical Feature Learning
The architecture employs a hierarchical approach with three main processing blocks:
• Block 1 implements feature extraction at 32×32 resolution.
• Block 2 enables mid­level feature learning and abstraction at 16×16 resolution.
• Block 3 provides high­level semantic feature extraction at 8×8 resolution.
Skip Connections
Each block incorporates skip connections following the ResNet paradigm. They improve
gradient flow to address the vanishing gradients problem. A feature preservation mecha­
nism maintains low level features throughout the network while enhancing training stability
and convergence.
Squeeze and Excitation Blocks
Channel attention mechanisms are implemented through SE blocks. They determine
channel importance by weighting channels based on their relevance. They dynamically
adjust feature responses with minimal computational overhead.

Image Preprocessor
To help the CNN focus on the leaf and analyze its shape and unique spots related to leaf
diseases, a simple image preprocessing function is used. It removes the background by
making it black and applies a yellow filter to the image. This yellow filter helps reduce false
predictions of Leaf Blight observed during training and simulates real world conditions
where images may be captured under varying daylight.
Furthermore the preprocessing function operates directly on the static image buffer by
applying pixel corrections and overriding its values. This in­place processing approach
minimizes memory usage which is essential for deployment on microcontrollers.
The algorithm starts by detecting green pixels using simple color thresholds where the
green channel must be stronger than both red and blue. The pixel intensity must fall
between 40 and 200 and the green value should be at least 60% higher than the combined
red and blue values. After the initial green pixel detection, a dilation step is applied to fill
small gaps by marking non green pixels as green if they have green neighbors within a
5×5 window. Then an erosion step removes isolated green pixels if they have less than
3 green neighbors in a 3×3 area, helping to clean up noise. In the final stage a mask
is created by turning non leaf (background) pixels to black and converting the remaining
leaf pixels to grayscale using the average of the red and green channels. This method
segments the leaf area from the background effectively while keeping the computation
low which is important for running on resource constrained embedded systems.

5.16.4
Firmware updates with Raspeberry Pi 4 PyLoRa Server
The Docker container hosts the required Python environment. Inside this environment the
lora_p2p_server.py script is executed to perform the P2P communication. The work­
flow starts by initializing the LoRa RF 96 module and checking whether a new firmware
update is available.
Firmware update files are placed in the update directory of the
RPi4-pySX127X-LoRa-Server repository. These files are deployed automatically at the
end of a complete CI/CD pipeline executed by the GitLab Runners. If a new zephyr.sign.bin
file is detected in the directory, the server sends an ACK message of type UPDATE to the
ESP32S3. The ESP32S3 responds with an ACK message and the firmware update pro­
cess begins.
At the start of the process the zephyr.sign.bin file is compressed using Heatshrink with
the same parameters used in the ESP32S3 decompression process (see 5.4). The com­
pressed file is then split into 165­byte chunks, forming 200­byte packets. Initially a packet
containing the total number of bytes is sent to the ESP32S3 to inform the microcontroller
of how many packets will be required to complete the firmware update. The ESP32S3 re­
sponds with an ACK message after which the actual firmware image transmission begins.
Once all packets have been sent successfully the RPi4 receives a final ACK message and
moves the zephyr.sign.bin file to a backup directory. The process then ends and the
PyLoRa transceiver stops operating. This entire update mechanism is automated every
time a new firmware image is placed in the update directory from a cron job that triggers
the process.
The ESP32S3 microcontroller operates and goes into deep sleep. There is a well defined
operation time­frame for the RPi 4 server and the ESP32S3 microcontroller created from
the begining of the project. Also to compensate miscommunication or malfunction in op­
erations, an idle mode can be used for the RPi4 server where it can wake up when the
ESP32S3 microcontroller wakes up from deep sleep and informs the server with a ACK
message.

6.2
Evaluation of Communication Effectiveness in Remote
Areas
By leveraging LoRa technology the challenge of long range communication in remote ar­
eas is effectively challenged. LoRa’s capability to transmit and receive data over extended
distances adds a crucial advantage, enabling the deployment of IoT devices in locations
where satellite or WI­FI connectivity is either unreliable or entirely unavailable. The pre­
vious tests demonstrate minimal data loss and consistent message delivery underlining
the robustness and efficiency of LoRa.
The results confirm that LoRa communication can be established reliably using software
defined implementations, even without specialized hardware. However communication
range and stability can be further enhanced by integrating well designed, high gain an­
tennas offering a scalable and cost effective solution for extending IoT coverage in remote
or rural areas [44].
This evaluation compares three major wireless communication technologies:
• The current LoRa TCP­like approach developed in this thesis.
• WI­FI HaLow [45].
• Satellite IoT [46].
They will be compared in terms of distance capabilities, reliability and suitability for de­
ployment in remote and low power environments. The focus lies in assessing their ef­
fectiveness in scenarios where infrastructure is minimal and power consumption must be
optimized.
LoRa TCP­like
The implementation of a TCP­like protocol over LoRa demonstrated highly performance
in long range communication. In open environments with clear line of sight and minimal
interference, LoRa achieved a stable communication with distance of up to 1 kilometer.
Its robustness and ability to function in areas without cellular or satellite connectivity make
it an excellent candidate for remote IoT deployments.
WiFi HaLow
While Wi­Fi can deliver high throughput, it generally requires substantial infrastructure and
draws more power than LoRa. Even under optimal configurations achieving consistent
connectivity at distances over 500 meters is unfeasible without power intensive routers
and repeaters.
Satellite Internet of Things
Utilizing satellite communication for IoT applications in remote areas offers the significant
advantage of global coverage even in regions with no terrestrial infrastructure. This ap­
proach comes at a high financial cost both in terms of deployment and subscription based
access [46]. In terms of power efficiency, medium to high energy consumption is typically
required to ensure stable data transmission and reliable communication protocols posing
challenges for energy constrained IoT devices.
By comparing these three state­of­the­art technologies (Table 5.3) the first thing to keep
in mind is distance. The LoRa TCP­like technology incorporates long range data trans­
missions, providing coverage up to 4 km in an open environment when transmitting at
20 dBm with transceivers at only 1.5 meters height [47]. On the other hand WI­FI HaLow

Evaluation of Continious Integration and Continious
Deployment for Applications on the Edge
Creating automation within an application is a challenging process. It provides robust inte­
gration and deployment capabilities essential for critical operations and long term stability
of remote systems.
The adoption of Continuous Integration enables a collaborative and secure development
environment where code is reviewed, tested and merged through structured processes.
This approach ensures that every change is thoroughly validated before integration re­
ducing development time, minimizing human errors and establishing a streamlined path
toward continuous improvement. In consequence Continuous Deployment enables the
automated release of already tested updates directly to remote systems. This minimizes
downtime, reduces reliance on manual intervention and lowers the risk of deployment
related errors.
The use of GitLab and the complete CI/CD pipeline implemented through GitLab Runners,
the DTU GPU server and the RPi 4 PyLoRa server integrated with the Prometheus Man­
ager enables the creation of a fault­tolerant, highly operational system. This infrastructure
manages the entire application lifecycle from code production and testing to deployment,
continuous functionality in the field and monitoring.
Incorporating a complete CI/CD workflow into an edge deployed application is a challeng­
ing task which requires several refinement cycles to ensure secure and reliable updates
especially in remote and critical environments. This thesis only scratched the surface by
highlighting the strong potential of CI/CD workflows to enhance future IoT projects.

Evaluation of Monitoring and Alerting for Application on

The microcontroller operates in remote environments yet when a fault occurs or the CNN

Continuous monitoring and alerting enable critical interventions that can prevent signif­

as an informational layer, while the system itself can autonomously take corrective action

Finally within the CI/CD workflow a robust monitoring system is critical for identifying op­

a direct feed into the Continuous Integration (CI) process allowing for updates that are
tested, deployed and iteratively refined. In general monitoring closes the CI/CD feedback
loop, reinforcing the system and ensuring continuous improvement.
6.10
Limitations
There are limitations in this thesis project that could be addressed in future research.
Some of those are a direct result of the constraints of current technologies and may be
bypassed on future work. One of the most significant challenges is updating a microcon­
troller firmware while it’s deployed at the edge. Remote firmware updates introduce both
technical complexity and potential security vulnerabilities. While AES­128 encryption of­
fers strong protection with 2168 possible keys. It is theoretically breakable given sufficient
computational resources (even Grover’s algorithm brings it down to 264 possible keys) but
unachievable with the current technology.
Another critical limitation concerns long term power autonomy. For such a device to oper­
ate continuously for several years without hardware intervention such as battery replace­
ment. Its power consumption must be rigorously measured, optimized and tested under
realistic environmental conditions. Although LoRa offers an excellent low power commu­
nication solution its limited throughput means that firmware updates of 15–20 kilo bytes
may still require 2–3 hours to complete.
The most notable constraint for running AI models on microcontrollers lies in available
resources, particularly RAM. In this implementation only the 512 KB of on chip RAM is
utilized avoiding the need to activate additional external RAM modules which would in­
crease power consumption. However such memory constraints impose strict limits on
the size and complexity of deployable models. It is well understood that microcontrollers
cannot realistically execute deep neural networks with thousands of parameters and hun­
dreds of layers. Instead engineers must focus on designing efficient problem specific
solutions that can be deployed effectively in constrained environments.

Conclusions
This thesis integrates a range of state of the art technologies into a unified, cost efficient
and highly functional edge application. The work began by deploying an AI model di­
rectly at the edge to enable autonomous decision making without reliance on constant
connectivity. Achieving this required careful consideration of the microcontroller’s power
efficiency. The chosen hardware, the ESP32S3 microcontroller, provided an optimal bal­
ance between low power consumption and computational capability making it well suited
for this thesis objective.
Although deploying embedded systems in remote environments introduces significant
challenges particularly in performing firmware updates, maintaining secure communica­
tions and ensuring reliable monitoring. To address these needs a complementary low
power communication technology LoRa was integrated into the system. On top of this a
custom TCP­like protocol was designed to enhance security reliability and fault tolerance
for critical operations such as firmware updates and alert notifications.
To further demonstrate the system’s capabilities a specific use case was implemented of
detecting leaf diseases in vineyards through a custom trained CNN model. This model
was designed, trained, validated and optimized for deployment on the constrained hard­
ware platform. By leveraging the Zephyr RTOS the project was able to efficiently manage
the microcontroller’s resources coordinate multiple complex tasks and deliver robust real
time performance within strict power budgets.
Beyond the technical implementation the project incorporated a simple yet complete CI/CD
workflow.
This pipeline automated the development, testing and deployment of both
firmware and machine learning models significantly reducing the potential for human error.
A monitoring system was integrated to provide real time feedback and facilitate proactive
maintenance. It’s ability to close the feedback loop, creates a continuous system improve­
ment functionality that kickstarts the hole CI/CD process.
This work was driven by iterative problem solving and pushing a microcontroller’s bound­
aries to the limit. The result is a scalable solution capable of executing AI model inference
and apply decisions on site within milliseconds. It maintains operational stability over long
periods and receives periodic improvements through secure, low power update mecha­
nisms. In essence this thesis demonstrates how edge AI can be effectively deployed
in resource constrained environments combining low power consumption, reliable com­
munications and automated operational workflows to meet the demands of dynamic real
world applications. The system not only solves the initial challenge of running inference
at the edge but also establishes a foundation for future research in sustainable, secure
and intelligent remote sensing systems.

Future Directions
Several avenues remain open for further development and optimization both in terms of
software and hardware capabilities. A key componens of this project is Zephyr OS which
continues to evolve at a rapid pace. Its a relatively new real time operating system and
it is actively maintained/enhanced by a growing community. Future versions will likely
offer expanded functionality, improved scheduling algorithms and better peripheral sup­
port features that can be leveraged to further optimize performance, reduce latency and
minimize energy consumption.
The custom LoRa TCP­like protocol developed in this thesis can also benefit from addi­
tional testing, parameter fine­tuning and optimization in real world deployments. Adaptive
configuration algorithms could be introduced to automatically adjust parameters based on
environmental conditions, network congestion or application requirements.
In the AI deployment phase, this project used TensorFlow Lite with an interpreter em­
bedded into the firmware. This approach consumes additional RAM and couples model
updates with full firmware updates that require bigger updated image sizes. An alternative
method can be the TVM Runtime System which would allow decoupled AI model updates
with minimal data transfer. This way only a few kilobytes will be needed to make the nec­
essarry AI model changes and it will be more practical to adapt to changing environmental
conditions without reflashing the entire firmware on every update.
Another promising research direction involves configuring ultra low power modes like
deep sleep. Investigating capacitor based energy harvesting systems that collect en­
ergy throughout the day and execute a single operation cycle could extend operational
lifetimes dramatically even in harsh or resource limited environments. This would enable
deployments in remote locations for years without battery replacement or maintenance
by utilizing the ability of deep sleep at its finest.
Finally the CI/CD workflows demonstrated in this thesis represent only an initial integration
of a much broader ecosystem. Future work could extend automation to include self testing
firmware in simulated environments before deployment, advanced rollback strategies for
failed updates and tighter integration with monitoring systems for predictive maintenance
and anomaly detection.
