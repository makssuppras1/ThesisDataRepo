Abstract
The human microbiome plays a crucial role in health and disease, being associated to
various physiological functions. While these associations have been established at the
population level, translating this knowledge to individual cases is complex. Moreover,
as research in this field rapidly expands, the volume of metagenomic data being gener-
ated is growing exponentially. In this context, efficiently organizing and querying this vast
amount of information remains a significant challenge, particularly for non-experts seek-
ing personalized insights.
This problem is addressed through the development of microbeGPT, an integrated pipeline
that combines a SQL microbiome database with natural language processing capabilities.
This project focuses on creating a microbiome reference database that consolidates data
from sources like ChocoPhlan, MetaCyc, and CuratedMetagenomicData. A natural lan-
guage querying system is implemented using OpenAI GPT-4 API to generate SQL state-
ments, enabling users to easily formulate and execute complex queries without extensive
technical expertise.
microbeGPT is evaluated using benchmark questions about microbiome composition and
function, testing various prompt engineering strategies and database optimizations to im-
prove its Text-to-SQL performance. Results show significant improvements in query suc-
cess rates and execution times after migrating from SQLite to PostgreSQL and imple-
menting optimized prompting techniques. The combination of few-shot prompting and
natual language questions rephrasing proved most effective in enhancing microbeGPT’s
performance.
These findings have broader implications for both microbiome research and personalized
healthcare. By bridging the gap between easily formulated natural language queries and
structured database queries, this system has the potential to democratize access to com-
plex microbiome data. It enables consumers, healthcare providers, and researchers to
generate answers to personalized questions about microbiome composition and function,
potentially accelerating discoveries in microbiome-related health conditions and assisting
personalized stool sequencing services.
All data and programs used in the project are available at
https://github.com/AnnaMi8/microbeGPT/.

Introduction
1.1
The human microbiome
The microbiome refers to the community of microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi,
archaea, viruses, and eukaryotic microorganisms, that reside in a specific environment.
In humans, the microbiome inhabits various body sites, such as the skin, oral cavity, nasal
passages, and gastrointestinal tract [1].
Overall, the bacterial fraction alone is as abundant as approximately the number of cells
in the human body, with a count of approximately 1014 [2].
The gut microbiome, specifically, is the collection of endogenous intestinal microorgan-
isms. It is shaped early in life, typically within the first one to two years after birth. During
this period, the intestinal epithelium and mucosal barrier develop in a way that supports
the growth of these microorganisms while providing a barrier against pathogenic organ-
isms [3, 4, 5].
It is considered an essential ’organ’ that provides multifaceted functions in human physiol-
ogy [6]. The composition of gut microbiome is host-specific and can undergo changes due
to various endogenous and exogenous factors, including diet, lifestyle [7] and antibiotic
use [8]
The microbiome has been studied for its involvement in digestion, metabolism, and im-
mune regulation. It aids in the breakdown of complex carbohydrates, synthesizes essen-
tial vitamins, and contributes to nutrient extraction. Moreover, the microbiome forms a
protective barrier against pathogens and modulates the host immune response [1].
The intricate relationship between the microbiome and human health is also associated
to different diseases. An imbalance in the microbial community referred as dysbiosis, has
been linked to inflammatory bowel disease (IBS) [9, 10], obesity, metabolic syndrome,
and autoimmune disorders [11, 12, 1]. Emerging evidence also suggests a potential role
of the gut microbiome in mental health, including conditions such as depression and anx-
iety [13].
For these reasons the human microbiome has been considered a critical area of research
in recent years. Specifically, there is currently a lot of focus on defining the ’healthy’ gut
microbiome profile and the link to host physiological functions [11], with the defined target
of being able to develop efficient therapeutics and preventive strategies that could steer
toward such a state. Many approaches have been speculated but for now the most used
are lifestyle modifications, like applying specific dietary regimes and physical exercise,
and the use of probiotics [14]. Despite this, a lot remains to be confirmed with high quality
clinical trials and discoveries to be made regarding microbiome manipulation strategies
[14].
Identification and study of microbiome community members was, in the past, predomi-
nantly conducted through culturing studies, which indirectly prioritized the study of easily
cultivable strains.
The advent of next-generation sequencing techniques has revolutionized this field, en-
abling the study of microbial DNA culture-independently. This approach, known as metage-
nomics, allows for the study of all genomes in an environment rather than just those that
are easily cultivated [15].
Shotgun sequencing is a popular method for generating metagenomic data, though other
techniques like long reads and amplicon-based sequencing are also employed. These
methods yield a considerable quantity of unlabeled reads, each of which represents a
microbeGPT

random fragment of a single DNA molecule within the sample.
Computational approaches are employed to extract knowledge from this complex array
of unlabeled data. Metagenomic reads can be assembled in longer sequences (contigs)
by using reference known genomes.
Alternatively, de novo assembly approaches can identify novel genomic profiles not present
in existing databases. These contigs are then grouped by their genome of origin through
a process called binning, resulting in Metagenome Assembled Genomes (MAGs) [16].
The obtained MAGs or, alternatively, raw-reads enable the identification of both known
and unknown microbes in a sample, allowing researchers to identify and quantify taxa
and genes present in a microbiome through homology (abundances) .
This set of methods enables a comprehensive investigation of the human microbiome and
the discovery of microbial associations with various health conditions and dietary patterns
[17]. They facilitate the linking of taxa to specific functions and the expansion of the known
phylogenetic diversity of bacteria, as well as their characterization.
The growing interest in microbiome research, coupled with these powerful analytical ca-
pabilities, have resulted in a consistent increase in the generation of metagenomic data
over recent years, both in research and commercial settings [17, 18].
Moreover, many fast and easily accessible methods, like commercial kits for microbiome
profiling, have emerged in response to the mounting interest in personal and personal-
ized microbiome research. Metagenomic data can inform about patients´ microbiome
profile, thus providing information on disease predisposition as well as metabolic func-
tions, which can be used to tailor dietary intervention, lifestyle changes, and probiotic
usage (fig. 1.1).Furthermore, researchers and clinicians can use these data to investigate
associations between gut microbiota and various diseases, track treatment responses,
and identify microbial biomarkers [19, 20].
It is worth mentioning that the vast amount of metagenomic data available also presents
limitations due to a lack of standardization in many technical aspects of microbiome analy-
sis [19]. These include variability in sample collection, DNA extraction, sequencing meth-
ods, and bioinformatics pipelines. Inconsistencies in data analysis, interpretation, and
metadata reporting further complicate comparisons across studies [21]. Such issues can
affect result reproducibility and hinder clinical translation. Efforts are underway to develop
standardized protocols and best practices to address these challenges[22, 21].

1.2
Relational Databases
Relational Database Management Systems (RDBMS) are among the most widely used
technologies for storing, manipulating, and retrieving structured data in modern comput-
ing.
In relational databases the data is organized in simple linear files, referred as relations
or tables, which is the most common data structure available. This structure, while sim-
ple, allows for efficient storage, retrieval, and management of data. In these tables, data
is represented as rows (tuples) and columns (attributes), with relationships established
through keys [23].
Every table has always a primary key, which is an attribute (or a combination of more) that
is unique throughout all the rows of the relation. Those ensure data integrity and facilitate
efficient data retrieval and table relationships.
To this extent tables can be linked by foreign keys (fig. 1.2), which are attributes ref-
erencing primary keys in other relations, establishing referential integrity between tables.
Those are crucial components of RDBMS because they enable complex query operations
and support data normalization, distinguishing relational databases from simple linear file
structures [23].
Figure 1.2: Foreign key link between relations. (picture taken from [23])
Structured Query Language (SQL) is a programming language used to query, manipulate,
and analyze data within relational databases. Different variations of SQL are available
based on the database system where it is implemented, although core functionalities are
maintained. Overall, SQL language supports complex database operations and adheres
to ACID properties [24]. This term, defined in 1983, refers to four main properties that
database transactions should have to ensure data validity and integrity. ACID stands for
Atomicity (transactions are all-or-nothing), Consistency (transactions bring the database
from one valid state to another), Isolation (concurrent transactions do not interfere with
each other), and Durability (committed transactions are permanent) [25].
As previously anticipated, different SQL variants are available. SQLite and PostgreSQL
are two of the most popular and used open-source DB systems [26].
SQLite
SQLite is one of the most used database engine [27] and is an in-process database which
was developed in 2000 out of the need of having an isolated (thus ensuring more security)
and easy-to-handle light database that can be simply imported as a library. It is now widely
used where a lightweight, local database is needed and powers mobile apps, desktop
software, and other local applications. This system is in contrast with database servers
microbeGPT
3
==PAGE:12==
running in separate process which are more complex to handle but are optimal for multiple
users and to store and organize very large datasets [28].
PostgreSQL
PostgreSQL is an object-rational DBMS, open-source system available from the Post-
greSQL Global Development Group [29]. Architecturally, PostgreSQL operates using a
client/server model. The server process manages database files, accepts connections
from various client applications (including text-oriented tools, graphical applications, web
servers, and specialized database maintenance tools), and performs database actions on
behalf of clients. The PostgreSQL server can handle multiple concurrent connections by
creating new processes for each connection [29].
Among open-source SQL implementations, PostgreSQL stands out as the most feature-
rich and complex [26].
1.3
Neural networks and deep learning
Machine learning is a field of artificial intelligence. In machine learning, statistical models
are inferred from available data, for this reason they rely on availability and quality of
training data [30, 31].
In machine learning, a problem is typically represented as a vector x ∈Rn, where each
entry xi of the vector represents a distinct feature. Machine learning models can be con-
ceptualized as functions that take these input values x1, ..., xn and associate them with an
output y. During the learning process, these models determine a set of weights w1, ..., wn.
The output is then computed using a function such as: f(x, w) = x1w1 + ... + xnwn.
This formulation represents a basic linear model, where the output is a weighted sum of
the input features. More complex models use non-linear functions or multiple layers of
computation [32].
Overall, machine learning algorithms are categorized in three main types: supervised,
unsupervised and reinforcement machine learning models [32].
Supervised learning methods require the value of the output variable for each training
sample to be known. As a result, each training sample comes in the form of a pair of input
and output values [31].
Unsupervised learning, in contrast, identifies meaningful patterns in the input data without
relying on predefined outputs. This approach directly infers properties of the probability
density function underlying the data. Clustering is the most prevalent unsupervised learn-
ing technique and it is widely used in exploratory data analysis to uncover hidden patterns
or intrinsic structures within datasets of interest [33].
Finally, in reinforcement learning the model learns to make decisions by interacting with
an environment. It aims to maximize a cumulative reward signal, allowing the model to
improve its performance through trial and error, without explicit supervision [34].
In this context, artificial neural networks (ANNs) are machine learning algorithms that were
first conceptualized in the 1950s, and have gained attention again in the the late 20th
century thanks to breakthroughs in algorithm techniques, a higher availability of computa-
tional resources and the ongoing exponential growth of data produced [35]. ANNs derive
their name and baseline concepts from biological neural networks found in animal brains.
While ANNs are a simplified abstraction of biological neural systems, they share key func-
tional principles such as distributed parallel processing and adaptive learning through the
modification of connection strengths .
The basic unit in ANNs is the neuron, also known as perceptron. A neuron receives input
signals, computes weighted sums, incorporates bias terms, and applies activation func-

Neurons are the basic component of deep learning, which is a subfield of machine learn-

ing algorithms in addressing complex AI tasks, such as speech recognition and object
detection. While simple ML algorithms perform well on many problems, they struggle with
high-dimensional data and fail to generalize effectively. Deep learning was specifically de-
signed to overcome these challenges by learning intricate functions in high-dimensional
spaces, improving generalization and mitigating computational costs [37]. Three pivotal
deep learning architechtures are Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neu-
ral Networks (RNNs), and Transformer architectures [35].
1.4
Large Language Models
Large Language Models (LLMs) are advanced machine learning systems that apply deep
learning algorithms to process natural language. State-of-the-art LLMs, such as GPT
(Generative Pre-trained Transformer) and BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations
from Transformers), are distinguished for their ability to interpret natural language, pro-
vide coherent responses and assist with language generation tasks. These models have
significantly expanded the capabilities of Natural Language Processing (NLP), particularly
since transformer architectures have been introduced [35].
Transformer architectures are based on self-attention mechanism for parallel processing
of input data. Input natural language text is here converted in tokens (numerical repre-
sentations), which are converted into vectors (embeddings). Following, multiple layers of
self-attention and feed-forward neural networks allows the model to efficiently interpret the
input. In each layer, tokens are contextualized within the context window, emphasizing
relevant tokens while de-emphasizing less important ones [38].
LLMs like BERT and GPT are pre-trained on vast amount of text data using unsuper-
vised techniques, such as predicting the next word or filling in masked words based on
context [38, 39]. Thanks to the vast and broad amout of information they gained during
pre-training, these models can thus be applied for different tasks without task-specific
fine-tuning. In this context, prompt engineering is a valid approach to enhance model
performance by better utilizing the model’s acquired knowledge. [39].
It is worth to mention that despite these capabilities, these models are not fully reliable

1.5
Natural language querying microbiome data through LLM
models
In the previous sections it has been underlined that:
1. The microbiome field has generated a substantial volume of public metagenomic
data, driven by current research and commercial interests.
2. Relational SQL databases are structured to optimize data retrieval efficiency. Through
complex query operations data retrieval and manipulation is ensured, while foreign
keys facilitate data organization, reducing redundancy and improving integrity.
3. Large Language Models (LLMs) are capable of interpreting and generating natural
language queries.
LLM models are also being employed and evaluated for their ability in generating SQL
code given a natural language question. This process is known as Text-to-SQL task, and
it aims to automate query generation to enhance database accessibility without requiring
SQL expertise[41]. This task presents significant challenges, as it requires the model to
possess knowledge of database structure while handling variable, user-dependent natural
language inputs [42]. The GPT model has demonstrated promising performance in Text-
to-SQL tasks across various databases, even with minimal prompting [42].
This works aims to organize diverse linear files containing metagenomic data and bridge
them with state-of-the-art knowledge from by the microbiome research community into a
robust and efficient SQL database.
The resource obtained this way allows for a centralized and easily reproducible approach
to bridge the gap from metagenome abundances up to metabolic reactions mechanisms.
This in turn provides the basis for looking into mechanistic insights of individual microbial
strains, their functions, or even propose roles for metabolites. In brief the resource can
orient the targets for the early discovery phase of probiotic strains and their mode of ac-
tion.
Moreover, this approach assures a streamlined and sped up process data retrieval, com-
pared to programming each time query-specific scripts that manage and scan the different
files to obtain the information.
Additionally, it is in scope of this elaborate to document and analyze the application of
the GPT model in the Text-to-SQL task, specifically assessing its performance on the
aforementioned SQL microbiome database. This will also investigate performance opti-
mization techniques for automated query generation, aiming to develop a reliable tool for
researchers and consultants.
By integrating diverse information sources, the project (summarized in fig. 1.4) seeks to
enable efficient generation of answers to personalized microbiome-related queries , en-
hancing accessibility and utility of complex microbiome data for both researchers and
consumers.

Materials and Methods
This chapter describes and refers to material visible in the microbeGPT public repository
(https://github.com/AnnaMi8/microbeGPT/). The resource there is divided into two main
directories: one contains the code and files for databases construction (fig. 2.1) and the
other regarding microbeGPT pipeline development and analysis (fig. 2.2). This section
will follow a similar organization.
2.1
Databases creation
2.1.1
Data retrieval and curation
Part of the data used in this work to build the reference microbiome databases is taken
from databases that come with HUMAnN 3.0 pipeline, part of bioBakery 3 platform [43].
From here, ChocoPhlAn database was downloaded [44] and used to extract data to build
Chocophlan table. Reactions and Pathway tables have been obtained with the same ap-
proach and source [44] [43]. Tables containing InterPro domains and EC numbers (ip2ec
and ip2mc) have been created from the correspondent input files reported while data was
retrieved from Uniprot database [45]. These two tables are never used in benchmark-
ing queries because this information is not usually an interest in company’s consultants
questions list. They have been created and added to the database for future aim that go
beyond the scope of present thesis work.
Metagenomic data was taken from CuratedMetagenomicData R package [46]. Data was
obtained using Rstudio (version 4.2.2 [47]), following the procedure described in the pack-
age documentation [48]. Here, relevant information were obtained extracting metadata
and abundance data files from TreeSummarizedExperiment object [49]. Furthermore,
abundance data was processed by adding a columnar indication of the species and piv-
oted to fit the desired input file requirements (script provided in create_SQL_database
folder). Metadata descriptive statistics displayed in table 3.1 in Results section, has been
obtained using table1 R package [50].
2.1.2
SQLite Microbiome Database creation
SQLite database engine [27] was used to create microbiome database by using the python
library sqlite3 [51] in a python (version 3 [52]) script (folder SQLite_databases_scripts),
where SQLite commands to create and fill in the tables are defined.
Three SQLite databases have been created, and the general process used to create
tables and insert data was the same. Each database was built creating empty tables and
filling them with data taken from the input files described above.
2.1.3
PostgreSQL Microbiome Database creation
PostgreSQL (version 13.7) schema has been created inside company’s intranet datalake.
Microbiome database was developed using the psycopg2 [53] python [52] library. The
process was similar to the one used with SQLite as tables were created, and afterwards
data is extracted from the same input files.
Data curation
For the PostgreSQL database, the metadata were derived from the same source as the
SQLite database, with the input file being organized such that the sample identifiers are set
as primary key. Since primary and foreign key settings are more stringent in PostgreSQL,

2.2
microbeGPT pipeline
2.2.1
LLM model used in microbeGPT pipeline
To access and employ a LLM model into the pipeline, OpenAI GPT-4 model [40] was cho-
sen; it was accessed with AzureopenAI [54] python [52] library, thus using a client version
with API key provided from the company.
The main script used to produce SQL queries from a benchmark list of questions is the
same for all SQLite databases, called (”openAI_query_generator.ipynb”), while prompt
differs accordingly.
PostgreSQL database is a client/server model, thus enabling the integration of the whole
microbeGPT pipeline into one script, from which both GPT model and database are called.
For this reason all postgres assessments folders contain a file called ’pipeline.ipynb’,
which is always identical but specifies different input prompt files for each evaluation case
(folders 1-8 in fig. 2.2).
2.2.2
Intra-question variance at different GPT temperatures
Assessments about variance within the same question at different temperature values
were performed applying the main pipeline (described in results) and recording results
is text files. The answers from those assessments have been evaluated using R [47]
scripts to calculate the scores and produce heatmaps. Reference material is found in the
4T_variance_sqlite and 4T_variance_postgres (fig. 2.2) folders.
Temperature has been set by specifying the parameter values as described in Azure Ope-
nAI demo website [54].
2.3

question list. Benchmark dataset for the text-to-SQL task is defined as a pair:
B = (D, (Li, Gi)/i = 1, ..., n), where D is a database and, for i = 1, ..., n, Li is an natural
language question over D and Gi is the ground truth SQL query over D that translates Li
[55].

A microbiome reference database has been generated using the SQLite relational database
engine. Relevant data is organized into five tables, which collectively include information
about pan-genomes, taxa, proteins, reactions, and pathways characterizing all strains

samples. In total, there are seven tables (described in table 3.1) , each serving a specific

database. These relations have been inserted into the SQLite database, with foreign keys
used to define relationships between columns across different tables. A first initial version

3.3.1
Benchmarking questions list and pipeline evaluation
The previously described pipeline has been employed to evaluate different instances of
SQL databases, together with multiple prompting strategies.
A list of 20 questions was used as benchmark to establish how AI-assisted queries would
perform compared to the ground truth (see Methods section 2.3). The best combination
of database and prompting was then identified by the highest amount of correct results
compared to human-evaluated SQL queries.
Benchmark questions, listed in fig. 3.3, were classified according to their complexity, la-

if the corresponding SQL query didn´t use any ”subselection” or ”group-by” operations
and didn´t require joining more than three tables to obtain the result. Moreover, simple
questions were very linear and were self-explanatory.
On the contrary, questions labelled as ”difficult” did not contain an explicit formulation of
the operation to perform during the SQL query. This typically includes joining many tables
and performing ”subselection” and/or ”group-by” operations.

3.5
PostgreSQL database pipeline assessment
3.5.1
PostgreSQL database construction
The database created via SQLite resulted in some critical structural flaws due to several
”many-to-many” relationships between tables. Since SQLite is a serverless embedded
database system, it can be convenient for small applications, while it’s not the ideal so-
lution for storing large amount of data [28]. During the previously described tests, result
generation from the SQLite databases pipeline assessments has been extremely slow
(Figure 3.9), affecting the assessment efficiency.
For this reason, microbiome reference genome database has been built and ameliorated
using the PostgreSQL relational database management system (RDBMS) [62]. The ra-
tionale of using a different RDBMS for this work lies in a better suitability in storing bigger
amount of data and its strictness in defining constrains between relations. Building the
microbiome reference genome database with the strict rules of PostgreSQL resulted in a
higher level of structure, where extra tables have been added to guarantee ”one-to-many”
relations between relations.
These newly added tables resulted in the same role as taxonomy relation, i.e. listing
unique values of other more redundant tables and permit a foreign key constrain between
unique attributes in a parent table, and the same attribute in a child table.
As an example, chocophlan and reactions were previously linked by the ’uniref90’ attribute
as foreign key constrain (fig. 3.5). In the new PostgreSQL database, the unique_uniref90
table was created merging all unique Uniref90 values present in both chocophlan and in
reactions table (fig. 3.8).
This way the two relations are now connected to the parent table (unique_uniref90s) by
a ”one-to-many” constrain, thus aligning with PostgreSQL foreign keys settings require-
ments and ensuring a more robust structure.
The resulting PostgreSQL microbiome reference database is thus composed of 12 tables.
Further details about database construction are reported in section 2.1.3, while its ERD
is shown in fig. 3.8.

connecting the relations according to the schema.(ERD obtained with PgAdmin4 [63])

3.5.4
Pipeline score optimization through prompt engineering
In previous sections, pipeline evaluation using PostgreSQL database resulted in 10 cor-
rect questions in total, while intra-question variance at different temperatures values high-
lighted an optimal temperature value at 0. This temperature has been used to perform
further evaluations of the pipeline using different optimization approaches.
Since PostgreSQL database is located in a company’s datalake, on server, it can now be
accessed from local machine through a python script. Thus, the entirety of the pipeline
has been streamlined by making it run with a single interactive script called ’pipeline.ipynb’
to increase workflow speed.
As previously observed, questions labelled as ”difficult” remained an issue to solve. As a
tentative to overcome the remaining problems, three main strategies have been applied,
and these were tested alone and in all of the multiple combinations.
With the first strategy, all questions that require interpretation, as shown in fig. 3.3, have
been rephrased to describe and explain them more extensively. Figure 3.13 shows the
list of reformulated natural language questions.
The second approach consisted of changing the ”database metadata” file, that is used
to provide database structure in the prompt. To do so, foreign keys definitions section
has been replaced with more explicit comments defining all tables that can be joined and
how. This strategy was inspired by the metadata file structure used with SQLCoder, a
fine-tuned LLM capable of translating natural language into SQL code [64]. As a result,
the alternative version of metadata file defines explicitly the JOINable tables and variables
name they have in common, as shown in listing 3.3.
The third and last strategy is a generally utilized prompt engineering strategy called Few-
shots prompting technique. According to this, one or more demonstrations consisting of
”input-SQL” pairs are provided to help the LLM model completing its task [65]. Our previ-

4
Discussion
A matter of choosing the right SQL database
The initial implementation of the microbeGPT pipeline showed suboptimal performance,
with a very low success rate using SQLite as the database management system. Through
database restructuring and migration to PostgreSQL system, significant improvements in
performance were achieved. A significant enhancement in data retrieval processes has
been realized, with query times about 100 times faster after migration, and success rate
increased to 16 out of 20 questions. Postgres’ referential integrity [62], ensuring logical
dependency of a foreign key on a primary key [68], was crucial in the development of
a more robust database structure. In addition to this, its advanced features including
better indexing and scalability proved key to the speed and performance improvement
[69]. The system also allowed for the consolidation of the pipeline into a single script,
reducing potential errors. Speed and score improvements are crucial for future pipeline
applications, especially as larger datasets are processed.
Prompt engineering strategies improved microbeGPT performance
This work demonstrates that integrating large language models (LLMs) with Text-to-SQL
tasks requires a nuanced approach, beyond simply inputting data and expecting accurate
results.
The substantial improvement in performance that was observed was not solely attributable
to database selection and refinement. It also stemmed from the application of tailored
strategies to enhance LLMs capabilities.
One first aspect analyzed in this work has been about LLM temperature parameter tailor-
ing for the defined task. Literature on the subject suggests to use lower temperatures for
analytical or multiple-choice tasks, while higher temperatures (closer to 1.0) are generally
preferred for creative or open-ended tasks [70]. Text-to-SQL tasks are typically consid-
ered open-ended. Converting natural language queries into structured SQL queries can
indeed vary significantly based on context and user input. The process requires creativity
and understanding of both language and database structures. On the contrary, in this
work it has been chosen temperature 0. This is because the selection criteria that it’s
been decided to apply was to identify the highest performance total score, which resulted
at 0 as shown in fig. 3.12B. Overall in this work we can see that temperature alone is
not being enough to change the performance. The application other factors proved to be
necessary.
Recent guidelines highlight the importance of prompt engineering and model fine-tuning
when integrating LLMs in industrial applications[71].
The current work focused on prompt engineering because it’s a simpler approach than
fine-tuning, without the risk to over-fit the data. Additionally, the process of fine-tuning
can be resource-intensive, demanding substantial training data and a consequent time-
consuming process [72].
SQLCoder is an example of a specialized text-to-SQL model recently developed, whose
performance on Defog’s database resulted promising and outperforming GPT models.
Although, when SQLcoder was applied on a larger database its overall performance was
limited [55].
Moreover, a study from 2023 compared few-shots prompt with fine-tuning approaches on
microbeGPT
33
==PAGE:42==
PaLM 2 model [73] highlighting only a 1% improvement with the latter choice compared
to the former strategy [41].
The ways prompt-engineering has been applied in this work covered different aspects. A
key operation to consider when building the appropriate prompt is to use structured input
and output techniques. This was implemented using Markdown formatting for communi-
cation with the GPT model, according to the documented preferences [71].
Few-shots prompting with in-context learning has been chosen at the beginning of this
work analysis as the baseline prompt provided to the model, reflecting current trends in
LLM interaction. Those above mentioned strategies include providing some background
context via database information such as data schema (tables, columns, and data types),
as well as primary and foreign keys [41]. In addition, some demonstrations (input, SQL)
pairs are provided to instruct the model with some practical examples about how to solve
some of the text-to-SQL tasks [65].
This structure leverages in-context learning, allowing the model to understand without
fine-tuning [74].
GeneGPT is a recently developed tool that teaches LLMs to use NCBI Web APIs through
in-context learning [74]. Here, the prompting strategy has been designed in a very similar
manner to what described in this work. Their prompt design, which proved highly effective
for biomedical question answering, comprised four key components: a general task in-
struction, API documentation, concrete API usage demonstrations, and the test question
[74].
Notably, their findings revealed that providing API demonstrations had a bigger impact
than just including the documentation, and that a streamlined version with just two care-
fully chosen demonstrations could achieve a performance comparable to using a more
extensive prompt [74]. This supports the efficacy of concise, well-structured prompts and
shows the importance of carefully choosing the most representative examples in future
microbeGPT’s optimization steps.
Contrarily to previous knowledge [65], few-shots prompting alone was insufficient to im-
prove pipeline performance by a sensible amount in the present work (fig. 3.14).
Overall, it has been noticed that the most effective implementation was achieved by
rephrasing the ’difficult’ questions in a more descriptive and ’step-by-step’ manner. More-
over, the combination of few-shots prompting technique with rephrased questions showed
the best improvement in performance, thus indicating that both conditions are synergically
contributing.
Natural language question phrasing has proved to be one of the main limitations in text-
to-SQL tasks due to the inherent ambiguity of natural language as it can have different
interpretations or be formulated in a complex manner [41].
For this same reason, many studies focused on finding ways to simplify the questions by
instructing their model to decompose these intricate queries into simpler sub-questions
[72][74]. This approach, known as chain-of-thought prompting (CoT), has shown remark-
able performance improvements on tasks such as arithmetic, commonsense, and sym-
bolic reasoning. By leveraging this method, language models can achieve state-of-the-
art results without requiring a large training dataset, making it an efficient and effective
approach for reasoning tasks [75]. In the geneGPT study, difficult questions are called
’multi-hops’ and are defined as those requiring multiple steps to be answered. By ap-
pending the instruction ”Let’s decompose the question to sub-questions and solve them
step by step” to each test question, they obtained promising results on a benchmark of
complex questions that were decomposed into smaller tasks [74]. Similarly, DIN-SQL
approach demonstrates that complex queries can be solved through simple prompting
34
microbeGPT
==PAGE:43==
techniques ”if the problems are broken down to the right level of granularity” [72]. This
method follows a similar approach to chain-of-thought, but here each query is divided in
four main sub-tasks that are defined as the necessary steps to write SQL[72].
This findings indicate that CoT strategy could be applied also in microbeGPT optimization,
aiming to further improve the handling of questions labelled as ’difficult’.
Another important aspect to be considered for future improvements is the number of ques-
tions that compose the benchmark list. In similar studies mentioned, the benchmark test
set was much larger than the one included in this work [41] [76], where the list was hand-
crafted by one person and was kept short due to time limitations. Increasing the number
and types of queries would be beneficial to ensure a good representation of tasks that
are commonly seen for microbiome, ensuring a more robust evaluation of microbeGPT
performance.
Finally, the industry leading Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) technology could
also be considered in future developments and optimizations of this work.
RAG enhances the language model’s responses by adding relevant external information
in real-time, thus further improving the context and specificity of its outputs [77]. This
approach could increase the model understanding the natural language used for certain
specialized questions. For instance, by accessing a database of microbiome knowledge,
the model could more accurately interpret and respond to queries using terms like ”alpha-
diversity” or ”sample richness”.
The potential of RAG in microbeGPT pipeline was indirectly shown in this study. The
last part of the pipeline using the LLM to contextualize the numerical answer was tested
using either GPT-4 model and the Askmicrobe tool [67]. Askmicrobe approach uses the
same base LLM (GPT-4) together with RAG techniques: when asked a question, the tool
searches through scientific article summaries and book extracts provided by the tool de-
velopers to retrieve relevant context before generating a response [67]. This microbiology-
focused AI chatbot provides more detailed and accurate answers when explaining ques-
tions and results mimicking the same language used by the provided corpus.
These findings suggest that adding RAG to our pipeline could greatly enhance its ability
to handle specialized microbiome queries, potentially improving the overall quality and
accuracy of the outputs.
Applying microbeGPT to tailor-made datasets
The SQL microbiome database created in this project integrates diverse metagenomic
data with current research knowledge, offering a centralized resource for connecting
metagenome abundances to metabolic mechanisms. This tool facilitates the exploration
of microbial functions and may accelerate probiotic strain discovery and elucidate their
mode of action, providing valuable support for microbiome research and applications.
The database’s versatility allows for integration of custom metagenomic abundance data
and expansion with additional microbiome-relevant information, such as gene families
or pathway abundances from sources like CuratedMetagenomicData [46], enhancing its
adaptability for diverse research needs.

Conclusion
This project defines a PostgreSQL microbiome database that organizes metagenomic
data and bacterial genomes, pathways and reactions knowledge. The database’s signifi-
cance lies in its potential applications for both scientific research and commercial endeav-
ors, particularly in elucidating metabolic functions and microbiome-associated diseases.
The SQL-based architecture facilitates rapid query execution, enabling researchers to
address specific questions within seconds. Additionally, it provides a streamlined mech-
anism for the generation and extraction of relevant subdatasets, enhancing the efficiency
of data manipulation and analysis in microbiome research.
The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) for Text-to-SQL tasks further improves
database search speed and effectiveness. This allows even users without SQL expertise
to easily query the microbiome database,expanding its accessibility and potential impact
in the field.
Overall this work proposes an alternative approach to manage and manipulate metage-
nomic data. The microbeGPT pipeline establishes a framework for employing LLMs in
addressing metagenomic data-related inquiries. While this shows promising results, its
performance requires further refinement to enhance robustness. Future work should fo-
cus on exploring alternative prompting strategies and expanding the benchmark dataset
to improve the system’s efficacy and reliability.
