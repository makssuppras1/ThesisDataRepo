Abstract 
The status of the European flat oyster (Ostrea edulis) is listed as threatened by OSPAR mainly 
due to overfishing and Bonamia ostreae outbreak. To increase the natural stocks, active 
restauration of European flat oysters is currently a highly researched topic. Restoration is 
performed by the release of oysters in natural areas. A method for producing these oysters is by 
producing European flat oyster larvae in a hatchery. However, hatcheries are observing issues 
with loss of larvae in larvae crashes. A possible explanation for these larvae crashes is infections 
of harmful Vibrio bacteria. During this thesis it was tested whether a 24h MgCl2 bath (which is 
used for the testing of B. ostreae) or a 24h antibiotic bath had any effects on the Vibrio CFU 
count on European flat oyster shells, tanks and water outflows. Furthermore, it was tested 
whether these treatments affected larvae production. It was shown that antibiotic treatment had 
a short-term effect on the CFUs on the oysters which reduced with time. MgCl2 showed to have 
neither a positive nor a negative effect on the CFU count. It was not possible to determine any 
effects of treatment on larvae production due to a lack of replicates.

1. Introduction 
1.1 European flat oyster biology and ecology 
The European flat oyster, Ostrea edulis (Linnaeus 1758), has been produced and consumed by 
the human population for centuries. It is native to Europe and is found along the European coast 
between Norway and Morocco. Flat oyster populations can also be found in the Mediterranean 
basin and, after intentional introduction, in eastern North America between Maine and Rhode 
Island (FAO, 2023a). 
European flat oysters are protandrous hermaphrodites. This means that all offspring will start 
their life as male. During the reproductive season, some individuals can change their sex to 
female. This sex change can occur back and forth even within one season. Older oysters can 
reproduce multiple times in one season, possibly as either sex (van Onselen, n.d.). During 
reproduction, the male oysters will excrete their sperm in the water column. Female oysters will 
take up this sperm and fertilize their eggs within their cavity. One female oyster can fertilize up 
to one million eggs. After seven to ten days in the mantle cavity, the mother oyster will release 
the larvae into the water column. This process of excretion of living larvae is called ‘swarming’. 
The larvae will live in a free-floating planktonic phase for one to two weeks (Helmer et al., 
2019; van Onselen, n.d.; Lapègue et al., 2006; Colsoul et al., 2021). In this time, the larvae, also 
called a veliger, will metamorphose into an early umbo, an umbo and then into a pediveliger 
larvae. These different larvae stages are illustrated in Figure 1.1 (Acarli & Lok, 2009). After 
the transformation into pediveliger, they will seek a suitable surface to settle on. From then on, 
they are called spat. Spat will grow for three to four years, after which it will be a sexual mature 
adult (Helmer et al., 2019; van Onselen, n.d.). The full life cycle of the European flat oyster is 
shown graphically in Figure 1.2. 
The European flat oyster provides ecosystem goods and services for its surroundings by 
forming biogenic reefs. These reefs provide multiple habitats for many species, for example: 
escape areas, hiding spaces, reproductive habitats and foraging areas. Due to the formation of 
these different habitats, oyster beds are seen to have a high species biodiversity (Kennon et al., 
2023). Another function of European flat oysters is the removal of nutrients from the water 
column. Oysters are filter feeders, which means they filter water to extract their food. Through 
this feeding method, they remove plankton and bacteria from the environment. By removing 
the plankton from the water, oysters decrease eutrophication and create a better environment 
for many species (Perez, 2022; Colsoul et al., 2021; Sas et al., 2020; Smyth & Roberts, 2010).

1.2 European flat oyster threats 
The natural stocks of the European flat oysters have been very low for centuries. In the last 
years, as can be seen in Figure 1.3, the overall production of European flat oyster has been 
extremely low. The convention for the Protection of the Marine Environment of the North-East 
Atlantic, OSPAR, (Oslo and Paris agreement) has determined that the status of European flat 
oyster is currently critical at multiple locations along the coastal waters (OSPAR commission 
2020, 2020b). According to OSPAR there are four main aspects that are a threat to natural 
European flat oyster stocks: overfishing, introduction of the Pacific oyster, climate change and 
disease (OSPAR commission 2020, 2020a).   
 
Figure 1.3: Production of European flat oyster in both fisheries and aquaculture between 1950 and 2021 (FAO, 
2023b; FAO, 2023c) 
1.2.1 Overfishing 
Oysters have been an important food source for many centuries. Production and consumption 
of European flat oysters can be traced all the way back to the Roman times. The European flat 
oyster production has been decreasing for a long time. During the 18th and 19th century the 
population decreased enormously due to a very high fishing effort and fatal extreme winters 
(Ermgassen et al., 2023; FAO, 2023d). Due to the overexploitation of the natural oyster beds, 
the recruitment is very low, slowing down the recovery of the flat oyster community. Since the 
ecosystem services of oysters are dependent on many living individuals, these ecosystem 
services also decreased, causing a reduction in biodiversity (OSPAR commission 2020, 2020b; 
Lapègue et al., 2006; FAO, 2023d).

pathogens, such as bacteria, copepods, fungi, microalgae, polychaetes, protozoa, sponges and

viruses, can harm oysters at different life stages (Colsoul et al., 2021). The natural stocks of 
European flat oysters in Europe have been mainly affected by two main parasites, Bonamia 
ostreae and Marteilia refringens.  
B. ostreae is a protozoan parasite that infects the haemocytes of flat oysters. It causes 
physiological disorders and can eventually lead to death. Currently, the majority of the 
European flat oyster populations are infected with B. ostreae (OSPAR commission 2020, 
2020b; World Organisation for Animal Health, 2021). The main oyster cultivation area in 
Denmark, Limfjorden, has also been infected with the parasite and is, thus, considered a 
Bonamia-infected area. The first case of Bonamiosis, the disease caused by B. ostreae, in wild 
European flat oyster stock in Denmark was found in 1980 in Limfjorden. Bonamiosis, is known 
to cause high mortalities in the natural European flat oyster stocks (Sas et al., 2020). Currently, 
there are only a few locations left in Europe with Bonamia-free natural oyster stocks (OSPAR 
commission 2020, 2020b; Sas et al., 2020; World Organisation for Animal Health, 2021; Von 
Gersdorff Jørgensen et al., 2020). 
The diagnosis of bonamiosis is done with histology, heart or gill imprints and Polymerase Chain 
Reaction (PCR). PCR is shown to be a more sensitive detection technique as it is better to detect 
the parasite at lower levels. For detection by PCR, the common practice is to sample a piece of 
oyster flesh for DNA extraction to run the PCR. This means that oysters have to be harmed or 
even sacrificed to be able to detect disease. Another method of collecting DNA for B. ostreae 
detection is through environmental DNA (eDNA). As the parasite is shown to transfer between 
oysters through the water column, it must be able to exit the currently infected oyster to infect 
others and would therefore be present in the oysters’ environment. This method of DNA 
collection is harmless to the oysters. However, for both detection methods, low infection levels 
of the parasite can go unnoticed (Von Gersdorff Jørgensen et al., 2020).  
The other parasite affecting European flat oyster stocks, Marteilia refringens, is also a 
protozoan parasite. M. refringens is able to infect multiple bivalve species, under which the 
European flat oyster. The parasite induces physiological disorders and can cause death. 
Mortalities in oyster stocks have been observed since the 1970s (Lópex-Sanmartín et al., 2015). 
M. refringens has not been found in the European flat oyster community in Limfjorden (Madsen 
et al., 2021)

1.2.3 Introduction of the Pacific oyster 
Due to low amounts of European flat oysters available, in the 20th century, the Pacific oyster 
(Crassostrea gigas) from Asia was introduced in different European waters (Troost, 2010) to 
sustain the production demand. However, the species has managed to escape their farming areas 
and spread around most of the native habitats of the European flat oyster, potentially competing 
for resources (food and habitat). The first introduction of the Pacific oyster in Denmark was in 
1972 in Limfjorden. In the 1990’s the Pacific oyster was also observed in the Danish Wadden 
sea (Troost, 2010, Stagličić et al., 2020).  
Due to their beneficial traits in the European environment, such as the lack of natural predators, 
the Pacific oyster spread very quickly. It has been hypothesised that with the introduction of 
the Pacific oyster, there is a possibility that they have overtaken habitat and resources of the 
European flat oyster (OSPAR commission 2020, 2020b; Stagličić et al., 2020; Troost. 2010).  
The introduction of the Pacific oyster to Europe, is also a threat to the European flat oyster due 
to co-travellers. Along with the Pacific oyster, other invasive species were introduced, such as 
the Japanese oyster drill (Ocinebrellus inornatus), which is also a predator to the native 
European flat oyster (Lützen et al., 2012).  
1.2.4 Climate change 
Changes to the climate can also have a high effect on oyster fitness. Climate change affects 
water temperature, precipitation, ocean acidification, sea rise and low oxygen areas. European 
flat oysters have strong interactions with many different parts of the aquatic environment as 
they live, feed and have life stages both in the water column and on the seabed. Therefore, 
climate change driven environmental changes can affect oyster growth, survival and, thus, 
productivity of O. edulis (OSPAR commission 2020, 2020b; Kamermans & Saurel, 2022; 
Eymann, et al., 2020). 
1.3 Restoration of European flat oysters 
As European flat oyster beds are important for their environment (as mentioned in section 1.1), 
it is important to battle the decrease in oysters that has occurred (OSPAR commission 2020, 
2020b). Therefore, it is currently a highly researched topic (Colsoul, 2021). One such alliance 
working together to achieve oyster restoration is the Native Oyster Restoration Alliance 
(NORA). Their mission states that they: ‘support the protection and ecological restoration of 
the native European oyster, Ostrea edulis, and its habitat in areas of its current or historical

distribution’ (NORA, n.d.). They provide a platform for collaborations to improve 
conservation, restoration, and recovery of European flat oyster stocks (NORA, n.d.). 
There are currently three methods for oyster restoration: releasing produced larvae in the wild, 
releasing produced spat in the wild or planting adult oysters (Colsoul, 2021). NORA mentions 
three different techniques to produce oyster seed: sea-based collectors, pond-based production 
and hatchery production. Sea-based collectors collect oyster seed from the wild. However, wild 
recruitment is low due to low oyster stocks. Both pond-based production and hatchery 
production rely on the active breeding of oyster spat. In ponds, the parental broodstock is placed 
outside in a pond and settlement substrate is added at the appropriate time. With hatchery 
production, more factors are under control. Hatcheries use broodstock, a possible quarantine, 
broodstock conditioning and spawning, larval production and settlement and spat rearing in 
nursery systems (Strand, et al., 2021). 
Due to the high risk of disease infection of B. ostreae, hatchery production of European flat 
oyster seed would be more optimal. By producing larvae and spat in a hatchery, there is more 
control on disease-free status and genetic diversity. Therefore, hatchery production of oyster 
larvae and spat has become more popular (FAO, 2023a) 
1.4 Hatchery production and culture practice 
There are several challenges with the production of European flat oyster offspring in hatcheries. 
Even with highly skilled staff, proper survival and growth of larvae is hard to achieve. A 
common issue within hatchery production of European flat oyster larvae are so-called larvae 
crashes, which are mass mortality events in larvae batches (Gray et al., 2022).   
There are multiple aspects involved in the production of European flat oyster offspring in a 
hatchery. There are technical factors, such as water quality and biosecurity and the different 
conditions (temperature, salinity, light, feed, etc.) needed for the parental oysters to produce 
larvae and spat. 
1.4.1 Water quality 
European flat oysters, and especially larvae, are highly dependent on their water quality. As the 
water this is their habitat, their place of excretion and source of food, their interaction with the 
water is high. It is important that the quality of their aquatic environment is high (Helm et al., 
2004).

In hatchery practice, most factors affecting the site are under control so the practitioner can 
decide how high they want their water quality to be. Water is often filtered through a required 
mesh (FAO recommends 1-2μm particle size) to limit environmental effects, such as diseases 
and harmful organisms, entering the hatchery. Water might also undergo multiple or finer 
filtrations or disinfection methods like ultraviolet (UV) treatment (Helm et al., 2004). 
Furthermore, temperature is kept under control for each life stage at the optimal levels (Helm 
et al., 2004). 
1.4.2 Biosecurity 
To keep disease and harmful bacteria to oysters away within a hatchery, it is important to 
maintain high biosecurity. For example, to keep mortality low and prevent the spread of disease 
to other organisms, broodstock is often collected from disease-free areas or potential broodstock 
is tested for disease. Disease-infected oysters are often not used for broodstock (Kamermans et 
al., 2023).  
Furthermore, biosecurity is kept high onsite, with entry requirements, high levels of hygiene, 
quarantine, limited animal movement, high water quality, and no spread of water between 
different treatment and suspected individuals (zu Ermgassen et al., 2020).  
1.4.3 Broodstock conditioning 
The preparation for the reproductive season for European flat oysters is called conditioning. 
During the conditioning process, flat oysters will undergo gametogenesis. This process is 
dependent on temperature, food availability and age of the oyster. The optimal temperature for 
European flat oyster gametogenesis depends on the area they originate from. For example, in 
France the start of gametogenesis has been defined as 10°C but in Ireland the beginning of 
gametogenesis is shown at 7°C (Colsoul, 2021). Furthermore, age, size, salinity and 
photoperiod are factors that may impact the gametogenesis (Colsoul, 2021; Maneiro et al, 
2020). 
The temperature for gamete emission is shown to be higher. For this, the temperature in France 
has been shown at 15°C and for Ireland an onset spawning temperature has been observed at 
13°C. For Denmark, a temperature of 20°C has been reported for the spawning of European flat 
oyster gametes (Colsoul, 2021).

1.5 Associated organisms 
In the wild, the water column is shared by many organisms which may have an effect on 
European flat oysters. The calcium carbonate-rich shell of oysters provides a good settlement 
substrate for bentho-pelagic organisms such as barnacles, algae and polychaetes. Many 
different species have been shown to live on the shell of oysters (Guy, et al., 2018). 
As mentioned in section 1.1, the European flat oyster is a filter organism. Due to this feeding 
method, microorganisms in the environment can easily enter the internal organs of the flat 
oyster. Therefore, the effects of the environmental community can have a big impact on oyster 
performance and production.  
Microbiome (community of microorganisms (Segre, 2023)) research is increasing in many 
organisms. Just as research in humans has shown that the microbiome of the body is more 
important than previously expected, microbiome in and on other animals may have similar roles 
in their homeostasis (King et al., 2019). Not many studies are available on the microbiota 
associated with European flat oysters (Maneiro et al., 2020).  
Bacteria that live around the oyster can have both positive and negative effects on oyster 
wellbeing. It is shown, however, that diseases within hatcheries are most commonly caused by 
bacteria (Gray et al., 2022; Helm et al., 2004). The most common genus of bacteria harming 
European flat oyster hatcheries and causing high mortalities are bacteria from the genus Vibrio 
sp. (Prado et al., 2010). They can trigger severe epizootic diseases in hatcheries, closing them 
down due to high mortalities after which enormous cleaning protocols need to be performed to 
remove these bacteria from the system (Gray et al., 2022; Colsoul et al., 2021; Helm et al 2004). 
A list of harmful Vibrio bacteria for European flat oysters has been published by Colsoul et al. 
(2021). 
To be able to avoid high mortalities due to Vibrio infections, the Vibrio bacteria need to be 
detected. An easy method for detection of Vibrio sp. is by using plating samples on agar plates. 
Thiosulfate-Citrate-Bile Salts-Sucrose (TCBS) agar is a medium that is used for the isolation 
and cultivation of Vibrio sp. Different species on this medium show different colonies and 
colours, ranging from small to larger and yellow to green (Aryal, 2022). 
1.6 DTU aqua hatchery – Danish Shellfish Centre 
Danish Shellfish Centre (DSC) is a research hatchery from DTU aqua. Their strategy contains 
a research and production program for both aquaculture and restoration of European flat oysters.

Their hatchery includes research towards genetic diversity, genetic parentage and disease-free 
production of oyster offspring with high biosecurity levels at the hatchery.  
In 2022, DSC observed larval crashes from one of their European flat oysters broodstock, which 
had not received the standard treatment of MgCl2 nor any treatment of antibiotics. As it is 
hypothesised that a potential reason for larval crashes is infection with Vibrio bacteria (Gray et 
al., 2022), this could also be a potential reason for the crashes that occurred with this 
broodstock.  
Based on this observation, it is of interest for DSC to investigate whether this hypothesis is 
correct. Testing this hypothesis would not only help finding a reason for larval crashes but could 
also potentially lower the risk of future crashes by finding the best treatment practices when 
introducing new broodstock to the hatchery. 
1.6.1 Disease testing 
As mentioned, there are currently multiple testing methods available for testing of B. ostreae. 
DSC makes use of the PCR testing method, both with oyster DNA and with eDNA. For the 
oyster DNA collection, DSC uses gill-sampling. For this gill-sampling method, the oysters are 
given a MgCl2 bath as an anaesthetic. The uptake of the MgCl2 relaxes the adductor muscle of 
the oyster and allows it to remain open, which makes gill dissection possible. When eDNA is 
collected, the oyster will not receive any treatment. As the broodstock that produced the larvae 
that crashed was tested using eDNA, it was hypothesised that this MgCl2 bath could possibly 
have affected the bacterial community on the oysters and, therefore, impacted the larvae quality. 
1.6.2 Antibiotics 
As larval crashes are hypothesised to be correlating with bacteria in the hatchery (Gray et al, 
2022), antibiotics could be seen as a solution to get rid of the bacteria. Antibiotics in hatcheries 
are mostly used as a resort to kill the bacteria of the oysters or tools as a treatment method. 
However, antibiotics could also be used upon entry within the hatchery as a precaution. In that 
case, antibiotics would be used to killed to kill the bacteria to target the reduction of Vibrio 
bacteria.  
However, the use of antibiotics is not very sustainable. Antibiotic use can lead to antibiotic 
resistance in pathogens, making them more dangerous as they can now persist even after 
treatment. Therefore, antibiotics are usually only used as a treatment method in the event of

outbreaks and not for routine application. Ideally, antibiotics should be used as minimally as 
possible (Kesarcodi-Watson et al., 2012).  
1.6.3 Aim 
The aim of this thesis is to investigate whether the different testing method, and thus the MgCl2 
treatment, and the use of antibiotics before the entering of the hatchery has an effect on the 
Vibrio species in the oyster environment and whether this could impact the larvae production. 
Furthermore, this research investigates other epibionts around the oyster shell and general 
oyster growth.

2. Materials and Methods 
This experiment was conducted at the Danish shellfish centre (DSC) in Nykøbing Mors in 
Denmark. DSC is a research facility for coastal ecology from DTU (Danish Technical 
University) Aqua. At this facility, there is new hatchery built for European flat oyster

2.1 Hatchery culture practice 
2.1.1 Water filtration and flow 
Water was pumped from Limfjorden to be used in the hatchery. Upon entering the water 
filtration facility, the water went through a sedimentation tank and a drum filter (80µm) to enter 
a buffer tank. From there it flowed through a sand filter (40µm), then a 5μm cartridge filter and 
a 1μm cartridge filter followed by UV light treatment.  The water then travelled to the hatchery 
where it received more treatment depending on the water usage.

were fed with different ratios of food as seen in Table 2.1b. Before the oysters were placed in 
the hatchery, they were fed a random mixture of P, C and R.

2.2 Preparation of broodstock 
2.2.1 Experimental broodstock 
For this experiment, 300 European flat oysters (O. edulis) were fished in Limfjorden in 
Denmark (co-ordinates can be requested). Upon arrival, the oysters were stored in a fridge at 
5°C for two to three days. 150 oysters that were estimated to be between 80 and 100 grams 
were randomly distributed and named OE26-23-1-1 to OE26-23-1-150. The oysters were put 
in plastic trays in groups of ten to twelve and epibionts on the outside of the shell were 
identified. After that, the encrusting epibionts were scraped off with a knife and the oysters 
were brushed off to remove the dirt. The oysters were then placed back into their tray, rinsed 
with freshwater and dipped in a 20,000ppt chlorine bath for ten seconds, after which they were 
rinsed three times with freshwater on each side and placed into individual 1L boxes with 1µm 
filtered seawater. The oysters were fed approximately 10mL of a random mix of the four species 
of microalgae. 
The 150 oysters were tested for B. ostreae using two different methods. Of the 150 oysters, 50 
were selected for Bonamia testing using eDNA collected through water filtration. This was 
conducted by leaving the selected oysters in a 1L container with 1µm filtered seawater for 48 
hours. To sample the eDNA, the oyster was removed from the water and the water was mixed. 
100mL of the mixed water was measured and filtered through a 1.2µm CFC filter. After 
filtration, the filter was rolled with tweezers and stored in a 5mL Eppendorf tube and sent for 
Bonamia testing. The other 100 oysters were selected for Bonamia testing through DNA 
obtained from gill sampling. For this, the oysters were immersed in individual 1L containers 
with 28.75g/L Magnesium Chloride solution at 28ppt salinity for 24hours, following which, a 
small piece of their gill was dissected out. The gill tissue was stored in an Eppendorf tube with 
2mL of 96% ethanol and sent for DNA extraction and Bonamia testing.  
After DNA sampling, all oysters were placed into individual 1L boxes with 1µm filtered 
seawater and approximately 10mL microalgae for approximately three days before being 
transferred to individual 2L boxes with 1µm filtered seawater and 20mL of microalgae. 
When confirmation of Bonamia-negative results for all sampled oysters arrived, 15 oysters from 
the eDNA sampling set and 18 oysters from the gill sampling set were selected for antibiotic 
treatment. 15 oysters from the eDNA sampling set and 15 oysters from the gill sampling set 
were put aside as negatives for the antibiotic treatment. The antibiotic solution was prepared 
one day prior to the treatment and consisted of 0.073g/L Streptomycin sulfate salt and

2.3 Conditioning broodstock 
After 15 European flat oysters were selected from each different treatment group, the oysters 
were put into the hatchery together in their respective groups. The groups were separated by a 
physical barrier and no water was exchanged between treatments. A setup of the system is 
shown in Figure 2.2. 
The experimental setup for the broodstock area worked with a flow-through system. Before 
flowing into the setup, the pre-treated seawater was filtered another time at 1µm and treated 
with UV light. Microalgae was then added to this water to provide feed for the oysters (Table 
2.1). This enriched water then flowed into the oyster tank (Figure 2.2), through the oysters and 
into a cylindrical larvae collector tank. From this tank, the outflowing water passed through an 
80µm filter before being discarded. The oyster tank received air bubbling through an air stone 
and the larvae collector tank received air bubbling with a tube. The oyster tanks had a light 
hanging above them with timers attached to produce a photoperiod initially between 7:00 and 
18:00 and increased to reach 12:12 (between 7:00 and 19:00).  
The broodstock was held in this system at 12°C for 3 weeks. Following that, the water 
temperature was increased at the rate of 1°C every two to three days until the temperature 
reached 20°C.

2.5 Larvae 
When larvae were observed in the larvae collector tanks, they were transported to their own 
larvae tank in a designated section of the hatchery. They were then distributed to be in a 
maximum density of 10,000 larvae per litre. The setup of the larvae tanks functioned with a 
flow-through system. The pre-treated seawater was refiltered at 1µm, UV treated and warmed 
up to 25°C before flowing into the larvae tanks. Microalgae was added to ensure continuous 
food availability in the ratio mentioned in Table 2.1. In the tanks, a continuous upwelling 
current was applied to this water medium with air bubbling at the bottom of the tank. An 80µm 
filter was installed to prevent the larvae from flowing out with the outflowing wastewater.  
Throughout their time in the larvae tanks, the larvae were observed regarding size, survival, 
abnormalities and other organisms living in the water column.  
The larvae were kept in this system until ready for settlement. This was determined by the 
observation of minimally 50% of the larvae having an eye spot (Figure 1.1). When larvae have 
this eye spot, it shows that they have metamorphosed from veliger into pediveliger (Figure 1.2) 
and will start to settle soon. The larvae were then transferred to a settlement section of the 
hatchery, after which they were no longer included in the research for this thesis.

colour change) were sent for identification using a MALDI-TOF.

2.7 Statistical analysis 
For analysis of oyster growth, the program Prism GraphPad, Version 9.5.1 was used. For the 
bacterial analysis, the program JMP® Pro, Version 16 was used.  
2.7.1 Oysters 
For the growth of the oysters, a simple linear regression of the shell height over time was 
conducted. The slopes of the different treatments were compared using a two tailed t-test. 
Before this analysis the data was tested for normality using a Shapiro-Wilk test. 
2.7.2 Bacterial analysis 
To analyse the bacterial data, the amount of colony forming units (CFU) per cm2 had to be 
determined from the data received. For this, the total amount of CFU on each dilution step from 
the 10-fold dilution was determined. From there, the dilution with a total number of CFUs 
between 30 and 300 was determined to be the most correct dilution (if there was more than one, 
an average was taken). From this step the real amount of CFU that was in that step of the 10-
fold dilution was determined and after this was standardized to CFU/cm2 or CFU/mL.  
If no colonies were observed in the first dilution, it meant that <10 colonies were available in 
the sample. As the software required exact numbers, 0 was used to simplify the analysis. 
To determine whether any treatment had a significant effect on the number on CFU on the outer 
oyster shell, the data was fitted to a Generalized Regression model with a negative binomial 
distribution (NBR) (Cristen & Parker, 2020).

4. Discussion 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate whether the treatment of MgCl2 and antibiotics would 
influence the Vibrio bacterial community growing on the oyster and in its environment. 
Furthermore, it was investigated whether these treatments would have an effect on larvae 
production. To test these effects, this thesis researched the growth of the parental oysters, the 
count of Vibrio bacteria in the oyster environment and the number of larvae produced.  
4.1 Oysters 
To determine the effect of the different treatments on oyster reproduction and bacterial 
community, this thesis first looked at the performance of the parental oysters. To determine the 
growth, the shell height was used as a measurement. Weight was not used because during the 
reproductive season the internal organs of European flat oyster change (Helmer et al., 2019; 
van Onselen, n.d). Since the experiment ended before the end of the reproductive season, the 
final weight of the oysters would not be a good representative. Shell height was determined a 
better proxy for oyster growth. However, when the oysters were brushed during the weekly 
cleaning, some of the oyster shell occasionally broke off, which gave a loss of shell height. As 
all oysters were treated the same, it was decided that the comparison could still be made.   
The average growth rate of each treatment lays between 0.08 and 0.11 cm per day. The 
differences in growth rates between the treatments was not shown to be significantly different. 
It can therefore be concluded that the treatments do not affect the growth of the parental oysters. 
One of the oysters of treatment group B died. The cause of death is unknown. This oyster DNA 
has been sent to be re-tested for B. ostreae to determine whether this could be the cause of 
death. This is the treatment group that received the gill-sampling Bonamia testing and, thus, a 
MgCl2 bath. As low infection rates still go undetected, B. ostreae could be an explanation (Von 
Gersdorff Jørgensen et al., 2020). The result was not received in the time to be included in this 
thesis. As this is the only case of death, it is assumed that it is unrelated to the treatment. The 
death could also be due to other natural reasons.  
4.2 Methodology of CFU collection 
4.2.1 TCBS agar and bacterial analysis 
At the start of this thesis, the researching staff at DSC had little knowledge and experience 
concerning the bacterial analysis needed for this thesis work. An example where this became a 
challenge was for the making of the TCBS agar plates used, which are used for the Vibrio CFU

count. The plates were made on site, but due to the lack of experience and proper protocols a 
lot of plates were lost due to too low quality of the plates. This resulted in replicates or dilution 
factors being lost in the experiments, causing an alternated estimation of the Vibrio CFU count 
of the samples. Furthermore, this challenge made the plate counts more difficult. 
In some experiments, data points are observed which is expected to be outliers (Figure 3.4: A 
before cleaning, Figure 3.5: C after cleaning, Figure 3.6a: B, and a lot in the complementary 
broodstock). These outliers affect the data analysis. Outliers in CFU counts like these are 
common, as a one colony on a highly diluted sample, gives a high increase in the final 
calculation for CFU/cm2. In order to be conclude whether this is actual outliers and thereby 
being able to confidently exclude them from the analysis, more replicates of swabs should have 
been taken.  
4.2.2 Water outflow analysis 
The water outflow analysis used in this thesis required a good amount of method development. 
To find the best method for Vibrio CFU count from the water samples three methods were 
tested: 1) Plating the water samples directly on the TCBS agar, 2) Filtering the water samples 
and placing the filter on TCBS agar, and 3) Filtering the water samples, vortexing the filter in 
Marine broth and plating on TCBS agar. The final decision of which method to use was based 
on observations and the exact results are, therefore, not included in this thesis. This paragraph 
contains a summary of why the specific method was chosen. 
The first method, where water samples were placed directly on the agar, did not make it possible 
the count CFU, as they were not visible. The samples had to be concentrated. The second 
method resulted in too high CFU counts even at low amounts of sample filtration. Besides this, 
the build of the filter made it impossible to count as the colonies grew in different directions. 
Therefore, the third method was used for the experiments in this thesis. However, it showed 
that this method did not give the correct amount of CFUs compared to a filter with the same 
filtration amount placed directly on a plate. It is hypothesised that the bacteria might be sticking 
to the filter. Different vortex times and speed did not show to improve the amount of CFU in 
the liquid. Therefore, in this thesis, the exact amounts of CFU/mL cannot be determined. 
However, as the same methodology was applied to all treatments, the treatments can be 
compared.

4.3 Initial cleaning of fished oysters  
The common practice at DSC is that, when oysters enter the hatchery, they are scraped with a 
knife to remove epibionts, brushed with a brush to remove all dirt and rinsed with freshwater. 
Thereafter, they receive a chlorine bath to kill off everything that lives on the shell followed by 
another freshwater rinse. This method is performed in order to have the oysters entering the 
hatchery with a minimum of other organisms. In this research, it was tested whether these 
treatments are effective in cleaning the oysters of epibionts and Vibrio bacterial species before 
entering the hatchery.  
Scrapping was an effective method to reduce the amount of Vibrio and epibionts on the shells 
from a median of 1,836 down to 1.25 CFU/cm2. On the other hand, the levels were so low that 
adding chlorine bath did not have a significant effect (median of  0.6 CFU/cm2 compared to 
1.25 CFU/cm2). As the scraping treatment showed a significant decrease of CFU/cm2 and got 
rid of most epibionts on the oyster shell, this treatment showed to be a good practice to the 
retrieval of oysters from the wild from this area in Limfjorden.  
Since this scraping and brushing treatment shows to decrease the amount of Vibrio CFU on the 
oyster shell, this treatment gives a good indication to be useful during the weekly cleaning. 
The epibionts on the outer shell were observed after the receival of the oysters. After the 
scraping treatment, no visible epibionts were left. During the experiment in the hatchery, some 
of the previous epibionts were observed on 2 oysters out of 60 (in both cases tubeworm in the 
treatment B group, which had only received MgCl2). These reoccurrences could be due to those 
oysters not being scraped thoroughly enough, as over 300 oysters were scraped to prepare for 
the experiments.  
As the chlorine treatment did not show to have a significant impact on bacteria count, it does 
not seem to not be a necessary step to decrease the bacteria on the outer shell before entering 
the hatchery. However, the effect of a chlorine bath on non-scraped oysters was not tested. As, 
the oysters received their chlorine treatment after a very good brush and cleaning of the oyster 
shell with freshwater, which already showed a significant reduction of CFU, it might hide an 
effect of the chlorine treatment. Chlorine treatment might be a more beneficial treatment to 
unthreatened or uncleaned oysters. It would be interesting to test the built up of Vibrio bacteria 
over time in the hatchery broodstock to investigate whether the chlorine treatment would show 
an effect over time. Overall, it is not possible to conclude on the effect of the chlorine treatment 
without having all the information of its effect (effect on Vibrio CFU on non-scraped oysters).

4.4 Effect of MgCl2 and antibiotics on CFU count on European flat oysters 
4.4.1 Experimental broodstock 
After the initial cleaning (scraping, brushing and the chlorine bath), the oysters underwent 
testing for Bonamia ostreae. Some of the oysters were tested by DNA sampling of their eDNA. 
Part of their water was filtered and the filter sent for B. ostreae PCR testing. The rest of the 
oysters were tested by gill sampling. For this testing method, the oysters were immersed in a 
MgCl2 bath for 24 hours, after which a piece of their gill was dissected out. This piece of gill 
was sent for DNA B. ostreae PCR testing. Furthermore, for each of these two testing methods, 
half the oysters underwent an antibiotic treatment in which the oysters were put into an 
antibiotic bath for 24 hours. The other half of each testing method received no treatment. Four 
treatment groups were created with different combination of these two treatments as can be 
seen in Table 3.1 and Figure 2.1. 
To determine the effects of these treatments to the parental broodstock, the amount of Vibrio 
CFU was measured at three different moments in time. Directly after receiving the antibiotic 
treatment, at the start of the conditioning process in the hatchery (21 days after entering the 
hatchery) and during the swarming season (63 days after entering the hatchery). On day 63, the 
amount of Vibrio CFU was also measured on the bottom of the tanks and water outflow of each 
oyster broodstock treatment. 
Due to the lack of resources, only one broodstock could be conducted of each specific treatment, 
which makes the experiment lacking replicates. Therefore, this thesis can only give indications, 
rather than full conclusions. As effects on a specific broodstock could have been affected by 
one individual error/aspect affecting the specific broodstock tank rather than the treatment. 
Further research with replicates should be conducted to draw full conclusions on the effect. 
4.4.1.1 
Antibiotics 
At the first sampling (day 0), oysters that had received the antibiotic treatment showed a lower 
CFU on their shell compared to oysters that did not receive antibiotic treatment. On the samples 
of day 21, the antibiotic treatment, again showed to have an effect on the number of Vibrio CFU 
on the oyster shell. However, the effect seemed to have reversed. Oysters with antibiotic 
treatment showed a higher CFU/cm2 on their shell than oysters that did not receive the antibiotic 
treatment. After 63 days, the antibiotic treatment did not have any more effect on Vibrio CFU 
on the oyster shell as well as the tank samples. The water outflow samples taken on day 63 
showed that the antibiotic treatment resulted a higher CFU count than no antibiotic treatment.

When the data of day 21 and day 63 was combined, the antibiotic treatment was shown to cause 
a significantly higher Vibrio CFU on the oysters for the groups that had not received the 
antibiotic treatment.  
Shortly after the treatment was received, the antibiotics demonstrated a large cleaning effect, 
getting rid of most of the Vibrio bacteria. This reaction was expected since antibiotics kill 
bacteria and should, therefore, decrease the Vibrio CFU. With time, this effect, however, is 
reversed, and oysters that received the antibiotic treatment had higher CFU counts on their 
shells. A reason for this could be that the antibiotics indiscriminately killed off bacteria living 
on the oyster shell and, thereby, also killed any positive effecting bacteria. Some bacteria on 
the oyster shell could possibly help keep the level of the bacterial community under control 
(Verschuere et al., 2000). The oysters that did not receive the antibiotic treatment would, in this 
case, have a sustaining community on their shell (Maneiro et al., 2020; Kesarcodi-Watson et 
al., 2012), keeping the Vibrio levels under control. 
During the sampling of bacteria for this thesis, samples were also taken by DSC to research the 
microbial community around the oyster shells during this period. This information could 
explain whether the rest of microbial community was affected by the antibiotic treatment and 
could, therefore, have caused the antibiotic effect to switch. The overall research on this 
microbial community is still ongoing and has not shown any results at the time of this thesis. 
As no significant effects of the antibiotic treatment was found on the last sampling (day 63) on 
the oyster shells and tanks, this indicates that the effect of the treatment disappears over time. 
However, for the water outflow samples an effect of antibiotics was still observed. It can 
therefore not be concluded that the effect of the antibiotic treatment is completely gone after 63 
days. For future studies, it would be relevant to include more replicates, time points and samples 
from all three areas (shell, tank, and water) to study the antibiotic treatments effect fully. 
In conclusion, antibiotic treatment only has a short-term decreasing effect on the number of 
Vibrio CFUs on the oyster shell. As antibiotic treatment should be decreased due to the risk of 
antibiotic resistance (Verscuere et al., 2000; WHO, 2020; Helm et al., 2004) and from the work 
of this thesis,  antibiotic usage is not recommended as a precautionary method to decrease the 
Vibrio content in oyster hatcheries.

4.4.1.2 
MgCl2 
On the samples of day 0, oysters that received MgCl2 treatment showed to have a higher Vibrio 
CFU count than oysters that did not receive this treatment. On day 21, no effect of treatment 
was shown on the oyster shells. However, on day 63 oysters that received the MgCl2 treatment 
had a lower CFU count on their shells than oysters that did not receive the treatment. Thereby, 
showing the reversed effect compared to day 0. The samples on the water outflow confirmed 
this trend. However, the samples from the tanks showed the opposite effect, where receiving 
the treatment resulted a higher CFU count than not receiving the treatment.  
In the short term, MgCl2 treatments appear to result in higher Vibrio bacteria counts than oysters 
that did not receive this treatment. In the long term, this trend was shown to be inverse, where 
oysters that received the treatment had a lower CFU count than oysters that did not. As the tank 
samples showed contradicting results, it is hard to draw a conclusion for the MgCl2 treatment 
and further research should be conducted to study the long-term effects of MgCl2 treatment. 
As the results of this thesis do not indicate any negative effects on the use of MgCl2 on the 
Vibrio bacterial community, it indicates that this treatment can be used safely in regards to 
biosecurity. As gill sampling is currently the common practice for diagnosis of B. ostreae at 
DSC, this thesis does not indicate any reason to avoid this practice concerning CFU count. 
However, if eDNA turns out to be a preferred sampling method, the MgCl2 treatment can be 
removes if future studies do not show any positive effects. 
4.4.2 Complementary broodstock 
As there were no replicates for the experimental broodstock treatments, due to resource 
limitations, four other broodstock were composed from oysters already at the centre. These 
oysters would be able to give insights on the long-term effects of MgCl2 and antibiotic 
treatments. Two broodstock had been used for breeding the previous year, and two additional 
new broodstock were composed. The complementary broodstock was added to the hatchery 
broodstock area 18 days after the experimental broodstock. The complementary broodstock had 
all been treated with MgCl2 and one of the four also received antibiotic treatment. 
To see whether the effect of antibiotic treatment would still affect the Vibrio CFU on the outer 
oyster shell, these oyster broodstock were also subject to swabbing for bacterial analysis. 
CFU/cm2 on their shell, tank, and water outflow (CFU/mL) was determined on day 70 (with 
day 0 as the moment the experimental broodstock entered the hatchery).

The Vibrio CFU on the shells of the oysters did not appear to be affected by the antibiotic 
treatment. However, for the tanks and water outflow, antibiotic treatment did show to have an 
effect. Oysters that had received the antibiotic treatment showed a lower CFU count. For the 
tanks, this seems to be highly affected by the samples from N as can be seen in Figure 3.9a. In 
the water outflow samples, the results were inconsistent between the different broodstocks.  
For the experimental and complementary broodstock, the CFU data was also combined to 
determine the overall effect of the treatment with MgCl2 and antibiotics. The combined data 
did not show an effect of the two treatments. For the antibiotic treatment, this was expected as 
the experimental broodstock and complementary broodstock both individually did not show 
any significant effect of this treatment. MgCl2 treatment did not show any effects in this 
combined experiment, opposite to the experimental broodstock, where an effect of the treatment 
was found. 
For the complementary broodstock, it is important to note, that there are some outliers, that 
could have affected the data. For the Vibrio CFU on the oyster shell it can be observed that the 
number of CFU/cm2 for the N broodstock seems to be higher than the CFU/cm2 for T and V 
(which had all received the same treatment, MgCl2 and no antibiotics). For both tanks and water 
outflow, the CFU count of N, again, seemed to be higher than the other treatments. A hypothesis 
for the higher counts in N is that this broodstock consisted of older and larger oysters. These 
oysters had more rugous surfaces with areas that were not easy to reach with a brush. Bacteria 
could have been able to build up in these areas and re-establish their growth easily into the rest 
of their environment. 
Altogether, as was shown in the experimental broodstock, it seems that the antibiotic treatment 
effects on CFU count wears off over time, as was expected. For the complementary broodstock, 
the CFU on the shell did not show any more effects. The water outflow and tank samples 
showed different indications in the experimental and in the complementary broodstock. 
Research with more controls, where they undergo the treatments at the same time, should be 
conducted to determine whether the antibiotic treatment effects on Vibrio CFU would wear off 
with time. In this future study, it would be important to include samples from all three areas 
(shell, tank, and water outflow) as these seem to give different indications of the Vibrio levels. 
4.4.3 Weekly cleaning 
During the experiment, the oysters were subject to a weekly cleaning. Every week, the oysters 
were drained of their water, brushed, their tank was replaced and the waterflow was turned on

again. This weekly cleaning treatment showed to decrease the Vibrio CFU count. This shows 
that this weekly cleaning treatment is a good method of regular oyster cleaning. 
By comparing the two different sampling moments, on day 21 and day 63, it was shown that 
there was a significantly higher Vibrio CFU count on day 63 compared to day 21. However, it 
is unclear whether this effect is due to time or the increase in temperature (from 13°C to 20°C). 
It is hypothesised that the increase in Vibrio CFU could be due to a bacterial build-up over time, 
despite the weekly cleaning efforts. The complementary broodstock data before cleaning 
(which had received their treatment earlier than the experimental broodstock) seems to support 
that hypothesis. More research is recommended to determine whether this could be due to time 
or the temperature increase. A possible solution to the decrease in CFU, that can be controlled 
with weekly cleaning (no matter the cause), could be the occasional chlorine treatment.  
As this research only focussed on Vibrio bacteria on the oyster shell, conclusions can only be 
drawn based on this specific bacteria genus. However, just as with antibiotic treatment, the 
weekly cleaning could possibly also remove the beneficial bacteria. Just as for the antibiotic 
treatment, more research will be conducted on the specific microbiome and the effects of 
different treatments.  
4.4.4 Larvae tanks CFU 
To determine whether the Vibrio bacteria would transfer together with the larvae, and whether 
this could influence larval mortality, the CFU count was tested on the larvae tanks. Samples 
were taken by swabbing the tank. Due to limited access with the larvae still in the water, samples 
could only be taken at the top water level. The samples were taken on day 66 of the experiment. 
The larvae were at that point between 10 and 13 days in their respective larvae tank.  
The number of CFU/cm2 showed to be quite low compared to the other samples from the 
parental oysters. The test showed there to be a significant effect of CFU on the tank with 
antibiotic treatment but not with MgCl2 treatment. Tanks of larvae with a parental broodstock 
that received antibiotics showed to have less CFU the larvae with a parental broodstock that did 
not receive this treatment. 
However, it seems that this data could be affected by individual tanks that had a higher CFU 
count than others, and thereby not a cause of which treatment the parental oysters had received.  
The N treatment seems to show a higher count overall than other tanks with this treatment. This 
experiment should be repeated with more replicates to be able to draw a conclusion.

4.4.5 Identification of colonies 
As some technical issues occurred with the transport and storage of the TCBS agar plates, few 
results of the identification of species were available in time to be included in this thesis. Some 
plates of sample day 63 were able to be analysed using a MALDI-TOF. The results stated that 
the species Vibrio splendidus was present on some of these oysters. Treatments containing these 
were from treatment groups A, B and C. From the other treatment groups, no results were 
present. Vibrio splendidus is not on the list of Colsoul et al. (2021) of Vibrio bacteria harmful 
to oyster larvae.  
The following microbiome research that will be conducted at DSC will give a better overview 
of the species living in the oyster microbiome and whether harmful bacterial species, that could 
affect larvae mortality, are present. 
4.5 Larvae swarming 
During this thesis, the aim was also to investigate whether there was an effect of MgCl2 and/or 
antibiotic treatment on the reproduction of the oysters. Therefore, larval swarming was 
observed. Total larvae produced ranged between 210,000 and 14,288,000 larvae per treatment 
group. There was a range between one to six swarming events. Synchrony was observed during 
the first swarming events as seven of eight broodstock swarmed within three days. 
The highest total number of larvae was observed from broodstock A that received both MgCl2 
and antibiotic treatment. The most swarming events were observed from broodstock D that 
received no treatment. The effects of treatment on the number of larvae produced could not be 
tested due to the lack of replicates. However, as the number of larvae from treatment A seem 
higher, the treatments might affect the number of larvae. More research with replicates should 
be conducted to confirm that hypothesis. 
As synchrony is not an observation that is regularly made at DSC, it was an interesting 
observation that seven out of eight broodstock swarmed in a three-day timespan. Maathuis et 
al. (2020) had found that synchronous swarming could have to do with a regular food supply 
as is provided within the hatchery. 
The number of larvae produced in the first swarming events seemed to be higher and decreased 
with the number of swarming events. It is hypothesised that this could be due to exhaustion. 
Parental oysters are using a lot of resources on their reproduction (Colsoul et al., 2021) and it 
is possible that not much energy is left after multiple reproductive cycles.

The number of larvae produced by the experimental broodstock is higher than the number of 
larvae produced in the complementary broodstock. A possible explanation for this could be that 
the experimental broodstock had started their conditioning process in the wild before they were 
fished, while the complementary broodstock was held on-site in the quarantine at 10°C. 
Furthermore, the broodstock entered the hatchery broodstock area 18 days after the 
experimental broodstock.  
The number of larvae produced in individual swarming events was high compared to previous 
observations at DSC. As female oysters fertilize a maximum of around one to two million eggs 
(Lapègue et al., 2006; Colsoul, 2021), it seems that there could be multiple mother oysters. DSC 
will conduct a parental analysis to determine whether this hypothesis is true and to determine 
the sex of the oysters during the reproductive season. No results were available yet at the time 
of this thesis.  
Larvae performance and survival were not taken into account in this thesis. During the 
experiments, the facilities, unfortunately, ran into technical issues concerning water quality.  It 
is expected to have affected the larvae too much to make any conclusions on the effects of the 
treatments.

5. Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate whether MgCl2 and/or antibiotic treatment of oyster 
broodstock before entering the hatchery would affect the amount of Vibrio species surrounding 
the oysters. Furthermore, it was a goal to investigate whether these treatments and possible 
control of the Vibrio species would affect the larvae production. 
The work of this thesis gave strong indications, that the effect of an antibiotic treatment of the 
broodstock only had a short-term effect and no long-term positive effects. It can be said from 
this work that it would not be recommended to use antibiotics for precautionary oyster 
treatments. The treatment with MgCl2 did not show any harmful effects regarding the amount 
of Vibrio species and can therefore, according to these results, safely be used as a part of the 
Bonamia ostreae testing by gill sampling. There is, however, no reason found to continue using 
MgCl2 treatment, in regard to Vibrio control, when other treatment methods are available. 
Larvae production from broodstock receiving the different treatments was observed, but it was 
not possible to conclude on the treatments effect on the reproduction due to a lack of replicates
