Abstract
Asthma is a complex disease meaning that disease-etiology cannot be attributed to a single set of core genes.
Instead, many variants contribute collectively with small eﬀects to confer disease. Asthma, like many complex
diseases, is common and manifests itself across a gradient of symptoms ranging from mild to severe cases with
multiple hospitalizations even in childhood. Family studies show that asthma has a strong genetic basis laying
the foundations for investigations of genomic loci conferring asthma risk, thereby elucidating the molecular
pathway improving in diagnosis, treatment, and prevention. Genome-wide association studies assuming that
each variant increase risk of asthma in an additive manner have discovered many susceptibility loci, but
genome-wide studies of epistasis have not been carried out to the same extent. Investigation of epistasis in
asthma has previously been carried out by considering a subset of variants selected on behalf of prior belief
of association. This approach has high power but risks missing relevant gene-gene interactions due to the
initial selection criteria. This thesis aims to show a proof of concept of a technique to perform a genome-wide
screen for epistasis, without the need for pre-selection of variants. The approach is called Neural Interaction
Detection (NID) and involves ranking pairwise input-combinations proportional to their interaction strength
in a two-step approach.
The ﬁrst step involves training a neural network with genotypes as input and
phenotypes as output.
The second step is a ranking of input-combinations based on overlapping paths
through the network. Data from the COPSACsevere and COPSAC2010 cohort is subjected to NID, using
the newly discovered FUT2-ABO interaction as a benchmark for performance on real data sets. Interaction
candidates ranked highly by NID will be validated with traditional regression approaches, ﬁtting a single
interaction to the phenotype, one at a time.

Introduction
Asthma is one of the most prevalent diseases worldwide and the most common non-transmissible disease
in children. Estimates from 2016 suggest that ca. 339 million people suﬀer from asthma on a global scale
[45]. In the same year, there were 417,918 deaths due to asthma, according to WHO estimates [48]. Asthma
is characterized by recurrent episodes of tightening in the airways, resulting in breathlessness or wheezing.
Asthma is believed to be caused by a combination of environmental exposures and genetic susceptibility
and twin-studies have shown heritability estimates ranging from 70 - 90% [7], indicating a strong genetic
component of the disease. The molecular mechanism of asthma is largely unknown and is speculated to
be inﬂuenced by many low-eﬀect mutations collectively causing asthma. As of now, estimates of genomic
heritability provided by GWAS, for most complex traits, remain low, indicating the interaction between
genotype and phenotype is not fully understood [26]. One of many reasons for the "missing heritability" is
the assumption of contemporary models, that SNPs interact in an additive manner to confer disease risk.
This section presents the theory regarding genetic diseases and epistasis as a non-additive interaction in
regards to asthma.
1.1
Fundamental genetics
DNA is the fundamental piece of genetic information, used to pass traits from parents to oﬀspring. DNA
consists of two intertwined polymers of the bases adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), and thymine (T)
connected by a pairing of the bases, forming a helix-shaped structure, see ﬁgure 1. The human genome
is partitioned in 23 homologous chromosome-pairs collectively containing approximately 20,000 genes and
6 · 109 base pairs. The term "locus" is used to signify, a position in the genome, but is used just as often
to refer to a position in the genome containing a gene. Eukaryotic genes consist of coding (exons) and
non-coding parts (introns). During transcription, the entire gene is transcribed into a long precursor-mRNA
molecule containing a copy of both the exons and introns of the gene, but the intronic regions of the molecule
are spliced away resulting in a ﬁnished mRNA molecule ready for translation. Diploid organisms have two
copies of the same genes, situated on homologous chromosomes. Homologous is not equivalent to identical
and variations in sequences between the two gene copies can occur. To distinguish between two diﬀerent types
of the same gene in a homologous pair, we use the term allele, such diﬀerence can be caused by mutations
having deleterious consequences for the individual, especially if they are located in a coding region of a
gene. Mutations causing severe deleterious eﬀects in an individual are expected to be rare in populations
due to natural selection causing individuals containing these mutations to reproduce at a lower rate than
individuals without the mutation. The rate at which this occurs is highly dependent on the degree of severity
and time of disease-onset imposed by the mutation [13]. The simplest mutations are changes in single base
pairs referred to as SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphism) and is the form of genetic variation studied in

Sexual reproductions begin with meiosis of which the product is haploid cells. In the sex cells,
homologous chromosomes cross over to increase the physical proximity of their sequences, allowing identical
sequence segments to form base pairs, thereby exchanging segments of DNA, in a process called homologous
recombination. These exchanged segments are from the same location of each chromosome and one chromosome
can receive the mutations of the other and vice versa, creating novel variants of genes by combining mutations
yielding a genetic mosaic. The recombined chromosomes are moved to each side of the cell before the division
of the cell membrane into two cells (cytokinesis). After cytokinesis, the two remaining cells each divide into
two cells, yield a total of four haploid sex cells. Homologous recombination is another mechanism for genetic
variations other than mutation and the resulting sex cells are now genetically diﬀerent from the cells from
which they originated. The sperm cells can now fertilize an egg cell, which has undergone the same procedure
of recombination in the ovaries [13].

1.2
How heritable is a disease?
The goal of genetics is to investigate the relationship between an individual’s genes and corresponding traits,
termed genotype and phenotype respectively. If genes are inherited from parents to oﬀspring the heritable
part of disease must be the genes. The deﬁnition of heritability was ﬁrst given by J.L. Lush in the book
Animal Breeding Plans from 1937 and might have been called ’heritability’ due to the historical dogma of
genetics, which stated that the phenotype of an animal was a function of deterministic genetic instructions
combined with an environmental factor. The deﬁnition of narrow-sense heritability is given in equation 2
VP = VG + VE

where VP , VG and VE is the phenotypic, genetic, and environmental variance respectively and h2 is
the narrow-sense heritability which is deﬁned as the proportion of genetic and phenotypic variance, reﬂecting
the proportion of the variation in a trait accounted for by genetic diﬀerences.
1.3
Rare monogenic diseases
The goal of medical genetics is to investigate the relationship between the genotype and a disease or disease-
related trait. Conditions such as Thalassemia, Sickle-cell disease, and Cystic-ﬁbrosis are some of the ealry
disorders in which a set of mutations in "core genes" was associated.
The aforementioned diseases are
inherited in a Mendelian inheritance pattern, meaning that the disease segregates in families in modes of
dominance or recessiveness (mostly recessiveness) [38, 29]. Studies of such diseases have elucidated highly
detailed molecular mechanisms for rare genetic diseases allowing better diagnostics and treatment. Mendelian
diseases have been extensively studied using cohort- and cross-sectional studies. In these studies, researchers
are probing for overrepresentation of disease in individuals with speciﬁc genotypes at the marker locus
or haplotype.
Evidence for a disease-causing marker is obtained if a disease is signiﬁcantly enriched in
individuals carrying the investigated marker. Markers are chosen based on known function, position near
associated region, or position near a region linked to an association signal. Such studies can provide strong
evidence for genotype-phenotype interactions, but they are limited by the fact that can only explore already
known genes. For that reason, previous studies of asthma and allergic diseases were heavily biased towards
immune-associated genes and loci [33].

1.4
Common polygenic diseases
Common diseases without overwhelming evidence of causative "core genes", have been observed to be
inherited in families raising the question of how these diseases are passed on from parents to children. This
category, termed polygenic- or complex diseases, is sometimes explained by an additive polygenic model,
stating that each variant has a minuscule eﬀect on phenotype, and only a large collection of such variants
results in a signiﬁcant manifestation of disease. The "Common Disease Common Variant" hypothesis states
that if a disease is common, it seems probable to reason that the causative genetic variants must also be
common. What follows from this is that if disease-causing variants are common in a population their eﬀect
must be small, since natural selection would have diluted the frequency otherwise, se ﬁgure 2.
A study from 2011 collected 400 susceptibility loci from 13 phenotypes, both qualitative and
quantitative, to analyze the distribution of allele frequencies, eﬀect sizes, and how much of the heritability
these loci can explain for a trait of interest. The frequency-distribution of disease alleles is skewed towards
higher frequencies in patients compared to the general human population. Furthermore, the risk allele in
the susceptibility locus was most likely to be the minor allele for most traits. The distribution of eﬀect
4
==PAGE:12==
sizes for most traits is skewed towards low values, suggesting that many complex traits have a large number
of susceptibility loci with small eﬀect sizes.
The relationship between eﬀect size and allele frequency is
such that when allele frequency diverges away from 0.5, this is often followed by an increased eﬀect size
meaning that no matter if the variant confers protection of risk of disease eﬀect size is expected to increase,
e.g. in the appropriate direction. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 2 showing this inverse relationship between
eﬀect size in absolute terms and allele frequency.
It is hypothesized that there exist a large number of
undiscovered susceptibility loci with low eﬀect size indicated (amongst other things) by the discrepancy
between heritability estimates in GWAS and family-studies. However, since most susceptibility alleles are
expected to have high frequencies, the absolute amount of heritability explained by future discoveries of rare
variants are expected to be modest. However, this conclusion is based on the assumption that the estimated
frequency-distributions of disease alleles are representative of the total population of disease-causing alleles
[34].
1.5
Genome-wide association studies
Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are the preferred study type for investigating causative genes in
common diseases. The starting point of a GWAS is to gather a study population either by measuring a
quantitative trait or deﬁning a case-population and a control population of roughly equal sizes. However,
in medical case-control studies, cases are often much rarer than controls resulting in skewed case-control
populations. The case-population has the phenotype of interest (preferably diagnosed by a physician) and
the control-population are certain to not display the phenotype.
Each individual is genotyped using a
genotyping chip since next-generation sequencing is still too costly to be carried out on a large scale [39].
The goal of GWAS is to determine the eﬀect of millions of genetic variants across the genome by probing for
either case-enriched variants or a proportional relationship between variant enrichment and a quantitative
trait. The variants in question are SNPs. When a single nucleotide mutation has a frequency of more than 1
% in a population it may be considered a SNP but in some cases, mutations with frequencies below 1 % can be
considered rare or low-frequency SNPs. GWAS assumes random mating and if a GWAS population contains
substantial amounts of individuals representing diﬀerent sub-populations, and that these sub-populations
are not equally distributed among cases and controls, population-speciﬁc variants may be falsely identiﬁed
as disease-causing variants. Thus population structure is a confounding element in GWAS, underlining the
importance of explaining why a variant is enriched instead of just stating that it is. It is possible to account
for population structure by including the most important principal components of the genotype matrix in
the regression analysis when mapping genotype to phenotype [35].
An interesting feature of GWAS is that not all investigated variants need to be genotyped. During
meiosis homologous chromosomes overlap and exchange genetic information to create genotypes which is
5
==PAGE:13==
a combination of the genomic material from the sperm cell and the egg.
The chromosomes exchange
information by homologous recombination which enables cross-over between two chromosomes providing and
receiving large chunks of DNA to and from the partner gamete. The consequence is that the child gamete
will have a new combination of variants. On the population level, this happens a lot and the frequency with
which two variants occur in the same individual provides information about the recombination rate between
the two variants. This chunk-wise exchange of DNA by homologous recombination means that some variants
will rarely segregate, producing variants that are inherited together more often than expected by random
chance. The frequency of an allele times the frequency of another allele is the frequency with which the
two alleles are observed in the same individual by random chance. Deviation from this frequency is called
linkage disequilibrium (LD) which can be described as the discrepancy between the observed frequency of
co-occurrence and the expected frequency of co-occurrence between two variants under the assumption of
independence. This quantity is often denoted D, deﬁned in equation 3.

Genomic regions can be deﬁned in LD-blocks consisting of regions in which SNPs are in high LD
with each other. Knowing the genotype of a SNP enables statistical inference of the genotypes of highly
correlated SNPs within the LD-block. In a population with random mating and no population structure, the
relationship between a marker and a trait can be mapped independently with a univariate regression using a
single marker as the independent variable and the trait as the dependent variable. Such a model is additive
in the sense that 2 copies of the same allele have twice as much eﬀect as 1 copy. If an eﬀect is high it is
tempting to assume that the marker in question is a causative allele, it is however important to remember
that spurious associations are expected between markers in LD. A high eﬀect of a marker may therefore be
interpreted as a representative of a causative LD block.
For most complex traits (as well as diseases) the estimated heritability is rather small compared to
what can be observed in twin-studies. As an example, early studies of height suggest heritability-estimates of
80 %. In comparison, the genomic heritability of human height was estimated with GWAS to 3.7, 2.0 and 2.9
% in these respective studies [15, 22, 46]. Also, a study from 2017 [28] showed that a highly complex trait such
as human height, also were associated with variants located in genes, whose mutated types cause monogenic
skeletal growth disorders, suggesting that there might exist a continuous gradient in the distinction between

==PAGE:14==
monogenic and common traits [14].
Many common diseases are known to exhibit phenotypic heterogeneity between patients and it is
not excluded that some complex traits are in fact umbrella-terms containing diﬀerent distinct diseases [31].
A very recent study has shown that Parkinson’s Disease is two diﬀerent sub-types, with one originating in the
brain and one in the enteric region [18]. This underlines the importance of speciﬁc phenotype deﬁnitions as
some diseases considered as monoliths can be umbrella-terms for multiple sub-diseases, reducing the power
of detection and replication in GWAS. Bønnelykke et al. 2013 provide a concrete example of using speciﬁc
phenotypes to obtain higher eﬀect sizes in GWAS with much lower sample sizes than former GWAS. Not
only were eﬀect sizes larger, but a novel association locus was also discovered. The phenotype in question
was asthma characterized by severe exacerbations (worsening of symptoms) between 2 to 6 years of age in
1173 cases and 2522 controls. Five loci were identiﬁed and four of them (GSDMB, IL33, RAD50, and ILRL1)
were already identiﬁed by previous studies, but with lower eﬀect size. Furthermore, a ﬁfth susceptibility
locus, CDHR, was identiﬁed, demonstrating the strength of applying speciﬁc phenotype deﬁnitions in the
search for disease loci in complex diseases [7]. The ﬁnding of CDHR3 as a susceptibility loci for childhood
asthma has also been replicated in a Chinese cohort [23]. Additionally, phenotypes that are functionally
closer to the DNA in terms of biochemical pathways are more directly aﬀected by DNA variation, and SNPs
are thus expected to generally have higher eﬀect sizes on such traits [27].
So far this report has touched upon complex diseases and some of the reasons why contemporary
GWAS fail to explain a similar proportion of heritability as traditional family studies. An additional factor
not described as of yet is the presence of the complex architectures in common diseases comprising of non-
linear relationships between genotype and phenotype.
The phenomenon of epistasis is an example of a
non-additive eﬀect.
1.6
Epistasis; the missing piece of the puzzle?
Epistasis is deﬁned as a departure from an additive combination of two loci in their contribution to a
phenotype [12]. Epistasis is believed to explain some of the missing heritability as conventional GWAS do
not account for it [14] and several studies have investigated the impact of epistasis on predictive power.
One study used simulated data to estimate the increase in discrimination between cases and controls on
BRCA, RA & T2D and found two-way interactions in rather small SNP panels to only improve classiﬁcation
modestly [4]. However, this study only considered pairwise interactions and phenotypes with relatively low
information content as compared to another study which found many higher-order interactions with gene
expression as phenotype [17].
The obstacles in the study of epistasis are multifaceted and are present in both the experimental,
7
==PAGE:15==
statistical, and computational aspects of study design. Experimentally; it is challenging to obtain large
enough sample sizes to get suﬃcient power to detect interactions, since the joint probability of an interaction
is, the product of the frequency of interacting SNPs, thus an interaction will always have a lower frequency
than the individual SNPs. Statistically; the burden of multiple testing weighs heavy since the combinatorial
eﬀect of interactions increases the number of tests and thus the number of false detections. Computationally;
an enormous number of calculations must be performed challenging computer power for larger data sets
providing the need for heuristics. Epistasis can cause derivative consequences for susceptibility loci discovered
in traditional GWAS by letting the main eﬀects be dependent on interacting loci. Variations in frequencies
of interacting loci across populations can be a hindrance in the replication of main eﬀects in diﬀerent data
sets [25].
Searching for epistasis entails expanding the parameter space into two or more dimensions [47].
It is diﬃcult to employ linear models to such large and multidimensional data sets and Machine Learning
algorithms have therefore been proposed as an answer to detect and analyze epistasis. Algorithms such as
Multifactorial Dimensionality Reduction, Neural Networks, Random Forest and Support Vector Machines
have all been reasonably successful in detecting epistasis through exhaustive searches in moderately sized
data sets, however, it is unclear how they will perform on GWAS-data containing up 500,000 SNPs [44].
Several methods have been developed to tackle this problem.
1.6.1

minimum. On the other hand, a very small learning rate can result in never reaching the global minimum
because convergence takes too long.

will occur long before that.
Quantifying overﬁtting can be done by measuring network performance on
independent data and for that reason, the data set is split into a training set and validation set. During
training, the model is simultaneously validated on a validation set, while parameters are adjusted using the
training set. A scenario in which a model is overﬁtted is when the training loss is very low, but the validation
measure is optimal and the optimal model is therefore located at the epoch with the best validation measure.
To ﬁnd this place a patience counter is implemented, which stops the training when the validation metric
ceases to improve for ﬁve epochs in a row. Whenever the validation metric is better than the previous epoch,
the model and its score is saved to a list and when training is stopped, the model with the best-associated
validation loss is selected as the ﬁnal model. Because neural networks are initialized randomly, some noise
is expected due to the unequal starting points of the models. To take this randomness into account each
network was trained until the stopping criterion was reached six times and the best model among was
retained, inspired by Bellot et al., 2018 [5].

2.5.4
How can neural networks generalize?
The architecture employed in this project, a single hidden layer of 50 neurons, will result in 13, 317·50+50 =
665, 900 parameters for the COPSACsevere data. The implication of this is that even for a very simple
neural networks the number of parameters will be more than a hundred times higher than the number of
training examples. For the COPSAC2010 data, this discrepancy becomes even more extreme with 5,833,300
parameters in a dataset with 542 data points yielding a parameter-examples ratio of more than 10,000.
Arpit et al., 2017 show that neural networks learn simple patterns ﬁrst and then rely on memory later in the
learning process. Analysis of correctly predicted labels throughout the learning process showed that networks
trained on the CIFAR10 data set learned simple patterns early in the training process compared to permuted
or randomized data sets. The networks trained on signal-less data learned patterns in the data relatively
late in the process, likely relying entirely on memory. Additionally, it is shown that for datasets with strong
patterns, smaller networks are suﬃcient and deep architectures are needed to be able to memorize data really
well. The proposed conclusion from here is that networks will exploit actual patterns ﬁrst and then afterward
rely on memory [3]. In fact, neural networks can achieve perfect accuracy on data without any meaningful
signal, as shown with permutation testing where networks basically memorize the data. This is also true
on image data in which the images were replaced with random pixels but labels retained. This means that
networks can memorize data, but this also means that they are really good at overﬁtting. but if neural
networks were only doing memorization they would not be able to generalize[52]. In spite of this, neural
networks generalize really well on independent data. Regularization like L1 and L2 norm, dropout, and early
stopping is used to discourage the network from overﬁtting. However, networks generalize decently even
without these explicit regularization techniques. Research indicates that the stochastic nature of gradient
descent introduces explicit regularization during training [10, 49, 37].
A comparative study illustrates the performance of Multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and Convolutional
neural networks (CNNs) with various architectures and showed that performance was highly dependent on
the analyzed trait and model architecture [5]. The same study (Bellot et al., 2018) shows that MLPs with
a single hidden layer generally perform better on predictions of phenotypes with complex traits, providing
the reason for the choosing architectures containing only a single hidden layers in this project.

2.6
Neural Interaction Detection
Neural networks are able to learn any function mapping an input to the output, but the price to pay
for this inherent ﬂexibility is a lack of routine interpretations of how the mapping takes place. For this
reason, neural networks are traditionally regarded as "black box" models in the sense that they are not
easily interpretable. A group at the University of Southern California has published a paper that describes
a method to detect statistical interactions captured by a feed forward neural network. They called this
approach Neural Interaction Detection, abbreviated NID [42] and this section will be heavily based on this
paper. The implementation of NID used in this thesis is based on the one used in the paper with some
modiﬁcations [41].
2.6.1
Deﬁning interactions
A described in the previous section, the weights of a neural network are adjusted to minimize a loss function.
Patterns learned from the data are therefore expected to be embedded in the weights and biases of the
network. Input-features that aﬀect the outcome will follow paths of weights with large absolute values,
propagating through the network. If an interaction between two features exists, the propagating paths are
expected to overlap at some point. This implementation of NID assumes an interaction is manifested in the
ﬁrst hidden layer of a network and input features sharing a hidden unit in deeper hidden layers cannot be
accounted for in this model. The ﬁrst step in deﬁning an interaction is to quantify interaction strength at a
hidden unit of the ﬁrst hidden layer, denoted an intersection. To do this, NID computes the average of the
input weights from each feature, µ(). The developers of NID deﬁned three criteria that an average function
must adhere to in order to provide the best possible interaction ranking.
1. Interaction strength is evaluated at zero whenever an interaction does not exist (one of the features
have zero weight).
2. Interaction strength does not increase with any decrease in magnitude of feature weights.
3. Interaction strength is less sensitive to changes in large feature weights.
The average function providing these properties is the minimum. Whenever one of the input-weights
is zero, the average will be zero. If weights decrease the average will not increase. The average weight will
only increase if the smaller weight increases in magnitude, while the other does not, resulting in stability
to changes in large feature weights. Consider a case with a large and small input-weight share a hidden
unit. A large change in the large input weight will result in a large change in interaction strength if the
arithmetic-mean is used as an average function. Based on these criteria the minimum-function is the preferred
20
==PAGE:28==
average-function when quantifying the interaction at the ﬁrst hidden layer. However, only considering the
inputs-weights of an intersection does not capture the full path of interacting features through the network.
Weighted paths originating from the intersection determines how much an interaction aﬀects the output and

2.7.1
Fitting a Bayesian model
One of the many applications of Bayesian inference is to estimate the distribution of model-parameters given
data. In logistic regression, the likelihood is a Bernoulli distribution with the probability deﬁned as the logit
of a linear model. This is the distribution of the data given the parameters of the linear model:
logit(p) = log(
p
1 −p) = β0 + β1x
(57)
P(D|β1, β2, β3) = Bernoulli(p)
(58)
where p is deﬁned in equation 45 as the logit of the linear model. The prior distribution reﬂects
knowledge about the parameter before we observe the data. In genomics most SNPs are expected to have
zero-to-no eﬀect on phenotype [9]. The prior is therefore deﬁned as such
P(β1, β2, β3) = N(0, 0.22)
(59)
According to basic probability theory, the joint probability of event A & B is the product of the
conditional probability of event B given event A, and the marginal probability of event A. The likelihood
is analogous to the conditional probability and the prior, to the marginal. The likelihood times the prior
therefore equals the joint probability of the data and the parameters
P(D, β1, β2, β3) = P(D|β1, β2, β3)P(β1, β2, β3)
(60)
additionally, the conditional probability of an event given another event is the joint probability of
two events divided by the conditioning event, meaning that the probability of the parameters given the data
can be obtained as such

==PAGE:33==
P(β1, β2, β3|D) = P(D|β1, β2, β3)P(β1, β2, β3)
P(D)
(61)
The following example resulting in equation 61, can be generalized in equation 62. This equation
is called Bayes theorem.
P(θ|D) = P(D|θ)P(θ)
P(D)
(62)
Bayes theorem describes the relationship between the posterior distribution P(θ|D), the likelihood
P(D|θ) and prior P(θ). Bayesian inference can be worded as, "allocation of a priori credibility across all
considered possibilities after observing data" [21]. In the case of logistic regression all considered possibilities
are described by the prior distributions which describe the prior belief of the coeﬃcients β1, β2 & β3. The
evidence provided by the data will, through inference, shift the credibility of possible coeﬃcient-values. The
only thing missing to derive the posterior distribution of the coeﬃcients is the denominator of equation 62
P(D). P(D) is the probability of the data across all possible parameter values and P(D) quickly becomes an
intractable integral to compute meaning that even simple models become impossible to analytically solve.
To estimate the posterior distribution it is possible to sample directly from the posterior, through a class
of sampling-algorithms that approximate the true posterior. These are called Markov Chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC) algorithms.
MCMC starts out in a random position θcurrent and then generate a random value θproposed.
Because the likelihood times prior is proportional to the posterior (P(θ|D) ∝P(D|θ)P(θ)), it is possible to
evaluate the likelihood of the proposed value of θ, compared the current value. An acceptance of value as a
representative sample is called a move and the probability of a move in the posterior space can be expressed
as below
pmove = min(P(D|θproposed)P(θproposed)
P(D|θcurrent)P(θcurrent) , 1)
(63)
This means that the probability of moving to a new position in the posterior space is equal to 1
if P(D|θproposed)P(θproposed) > P(D|θcurrent)P(θcurrent). This means a step to a new location is always
taken if the new location has a higher likelihood (times prior) than the previous location. However if the
current position has a higher likelihood than the proposed, the sampler moves to the new position with
a probability equal to the ratio between the likelihood of the two positions. This ensures a random-walk
property of the trajectory since we want a representative sample from the posterior not just values closely
centered around the mode. This property allows each value of the posterior to be sampled proportional to its
posterior probability. The sequence of steps taken in the posterior space is called a chain and if the chain is
26
==PAGE:34==
run for enough iterations it will eventually explore the posterior comprehensively. However, the time before
a suﬃciently representative sample can be acquired greatly depends on the transition model. The transition
model deﬁnes how the algorithm takes a step, e.g. proposes new values for θ. A Gaussian distribution is
a common transition model with µ = θcurrent, such that θproposed ∼N(θcurrent, σ). The value of σ control
the step size in posterior space, analogous to step size in Gradient descent based techniques. If σ is too big,
many proposed values will be rejected and an eﬃcient exploration will take a long time. Conversely, a tiny
value of σ will result in a slow sampler spending a long time obtaining a representative sample. Bias will
be introduced in the proposed posterior if sampled for too few iterations, as each step introduces very little
new information.
To evaluate the validity of a parameter-estimate with Bayesian inference, it is needed to consider
how much of the posterior overlaps with the values that are practically equivalent to null-values. Null-values
are parameter-values when there is no signal in the data, analogous to the null-hypothesis in frequentist
inference.
The region of practical equivalence (ROPE) is used to determine a reasonable range of null-
values. An interaction with an OR of 1.01 or 0.99 is practically equivalent to the null-value (OR of 1),
indicating no eﬀect on phenotype. The decision to accept or reject the null-value is based on the percent-
wise overlap between the ROPE and HPDI of the posterior. If the HPDI falls completely outside the ROPE,
the null-value can be rejected with great certainty and vice versa. If the ROPE and HPDI overlap with the
ROPE not completely containing the HPDI, then the data is insuﬃcient to make a clear decision of accepting
or rejecting the null-value. Deﬁnition of the ROPE is dependent on the problem and mode of analysis and
the limits of the ROPE depend on its practical purpose [21]. In this thesis, the ROPE is equal ±0.095, since
this equal 0.9 and 1.1 in OR-scale.

2.7.3
Frequentist regression
Interactions are also validated with frequentist regression to compare with results not aﬀected by a prior
distribution.
Frequentist regression only relies on ﬁnding the parameters that describe the maximum
likelihood solution regardless of prior knowledge. Coeﬃcient estimates must be interpreted in light of their
p-values, which describes the probability of obtaining the same coeﬃcients by chance assuming that the
true coeﬃcient is zero. The false detection rate of frequentist methods amounts to a substantial amount of
false positives, when multiple tests are performed. This is often done by adhering to a stricter signiﬁcance
threshold, but this has the downside of increasing the rate of false negatives, thereby missing true eﬀects.
By using both frequentist and Bayesian methods it is possible to get a robust evaluation of the interaction
eﬀects, getting the best from both worlds.
Furthermore, frequentist methods are routinely used, their
implementations in languages such as R, are often easier to use and require less coding to implement and
are thus less prone to be aﬀected by human errors.
2.8
Rank estimation
If bench-marking was to be carried out on the interactions obtained from the COPSAC2010 data set,
searching the entire interaction list would be intractable.
To allow for future bench-marking a method
of estimating ranks based on interaction strength is proposed. NID has a complexity of O(n2), for pairwise
interaction detection, meaning that the resource consumption of the algorithm scales squared with increasing
data size. Running NID on the COPSACsevere data set (13,317 SNPs yielding 8.863 · 107 non-redundant
interactions) is manageable and takes around 40 minutes and consumes approximately 16 Gb. However,
sorting all the possible interactions in COPSAC2010 with 116,665 SNPs (6.805·109 non-redundant interactions)
is not tractable and an alternative approach must be taken since a system with suﬃcient RAM is not
available. In order to avoid holding the entire interaction-list in memory, extracting interactions with the
highest interaction strengths and discarding the rest, seems like a reasonable approach. For example, the
20,000 highest ranked interactions could be extracted disregarding the vast majority of the possibilities, as
shown in algorithm 1. If the top 20,000 interactions are extracted this constitutes 0.0029 % of all possible
pairwise interactions in the COPSACsevere meaning that it is not unrealistic to assume true interactions
may be located outside this very stringent cutoﬀ. When investigating larger data sets like the one in the
COPSAC2010, in which 116,665 SNPs are included, even extracting the top 10 % of all possible interactions
would entail almost 700 million interactions; the still troublesome amount of variables to meaningfully
investigate. Therefore extracting the top interactions may be a possible method of handling this data. To
extract the top interactions, an approach developed by Peter Wad Sackett (DTU, Health Technology) was
utilized. Since this algorithm has no oﬃcial name, it will be denoted "Sackett’s algorithm" as a reference to
the developer. The approach is described in algorithm 1.

Algorithm 1 works by appending each interaction to a list until it reaches a length of a pre-speciﬁed

the number of top-ranked interactions the user wish to extract. When N interactions have been gathered,

changing the global minimum. Memory consumption is signiﬁcantly reduced by this alternative strategy,
compared to sorting the entire list of interactions. Additionally, algorithm 1 can ﬁnd the N interactions with
the lowest strengths, with few modiﬁcations. This will be utilized in rank estimation.

4
Discussion
The purpose of this project is to apply a hypothesis-free approach to genome-wide epistasis-detection and
avoid pre-selecting variants based on prior knowledge of association. Filtering SNPs based on prior knowledge
of association will lead to a higher power, but may also ignore potential novel associations. This section tries
to answer how the results obtained in this project can answer how well NID will perform in genome-wide
detection of epistasis.
4.1
Simulation tests
Out of the 50 model runs, the interaction with a main-eﬀect was always recovered and the interaction without
main-eﬀect had a much lower rate of recovery. A recovery is counted when a ground truth interaction is
found in the top 10,000 interactions (out of 8.86 · 108). Ground truth interactions are placed near the top of
the interaction list most of the time and placements below rank 2500 are extremely rare. This means that of
the 8.86 · 108 possible pairwise interactions, the true interactions are typically found in the interactions with
the 0.0002 % highest interactions strengths. MCC-scores of the networks follow an approximately normal
distribution with a mean around 0.25, never falling below 0.1, but ground truth interactions are nevertheless
highly ranked, indicating that relevant patterns are captured by the networks even though predictions only
show moderate correlations with labels. Permutation tests were performed by shuﬄing phenotypes removing
any meaningful signal from the data and the results show that no interactions were detected. The interactions
didn’t make the top 10,000, and the MCC-distribution is centered around 0, indicating that the obtained
results are caused by the detection of relevant patterns in the data and not random ﬂuctuations. The results
also indicate that interactions with main-eﬀects are easier for NID to recover. Reasons for this may be in the
way that NID computes interaction strength. To compute interaction strength, NID multiplies the smallest
input weight (of the interacting features) with the weight going from the intersection-neuron to the output
layer, see equation 40. Features with both main-eﬀect and interaction-eﬀects can be represented by a neural
network when a hidden neuron is activated by inputs from two input features, while another hidden neuron
in the same hidden layer is only activated by one of the two input features. When interaction strength is
summed across hidden neurons, main-eﬀects increases the interaction-strength, compared to if input features
were silent in some hidden neurons. This is because more non-zero weights are included in the sum. If a
feature has no main eﬀect on the outcome, it may have fewer associated weights with high values, deﬂating
interaction strength. Alternatively, interactions with additional main-eﬀects could also be materialized as
higher weight values, thus also increasing interaction strength.
As indicated by ﬁgure 6, MCC-scores in the range 0.125-0.225 does not inﬂuence the ranking of
interactions, as interactions are ranked equally good. It also shows that networks don’t need to predict the
48
==PAGE:56==
outcome very well, for true interactions to be modeled, in data with few but strong interactions. Since the
simulated data only contains few interactions, it is expectantly easier to rank interactions without interference
by a large number of interactions with similar strength. Therefore, it is too soon to conclude that a better
MCC-score cannot increase the performance of interaction-ranking when more complex patterns are present.
These results indicate that NID can detect interactions in SNP data if there is a meaningful signal mapping
the genotype to a phenotype, represented by a correlation between the predicted true phenotypes, even
in sparse data. Furthermore, patterns can still be modeled in spite of fairly bad predictions. One could
imagine, that longer training time, would increase the performance and this would perhaps allow more
robust detection of the remaining interactions [36].
4.2
Neural networks for genomic prediction
Publications on feedforward neural networks mapping raw genotypes to phenotypes are scarce and many
existing studies use genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs as input to the network, thus performing a GWAS as a
pre-selection step. This means that studies using entire genotyping arrays as input to a neural network are
rare and that the expected performance of a network on such data is hard to determine a priori to analyze,
making bench-marking diﬃcult. Approaches using genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs adhering to a more liberal
signiﬁcance threshold have been shown to increase the predictive power of a neural network. A study used this
approach to classify morbidly obese patients and showed that using a higher number of signiﬁcant SNPs with
a less stringent signiﬁcance threshold increased the performance of the network. Using a p-value < 1 · 10−2
obtained AUC = 0.9908, but using a p-value < 1 · 10−5 yielded AUC = 0.5980 [32]. This is a demonstration
that conventional GWAS with stringent signiﬁcance thresholds like Bonferroni increases the false-negative
rate since many SNPs contributing to phenotype predictive performance are missing when using conservative
p-value thresholds. Inspired by this study (Montañez et al., 2018), neural networks may be used to quantify
how much information is encoded in signiﬁcant SNPs from a GWAS, by observing changes in predictive
power when gradually adding additional SNPs to the input, based on signiﬁcance thresholds. Alternatively,
Bayesian approaches may also be used and a reduction in ROPE-width could be used as an alternative to
relaxing the signiﬁcance threshold. Practically, hypothesis-free interaction-discovery of, say 2000, of the most
signiﬁcant/relevant SNPs would still yield 2 · 106 possible non-redundant interactions. Cumbersome for a
regression approach, but very manageable for NID. The expectation is that NID estimates would be even
more accurate than at present time due to the lower degree of sparsity in the data used to train the neural
network.
COPSACsevere
The neural networks trained on the COPSAC data resulted in descent predictions, with
up to 0.7 correlation in both phenotypes, see ﬁgure 8. This means that the networks learn patterns that
49
==PAGE:57==
enable them to generalize on independent data. One of the regularization techniques used is early stopping,
which entailed that the network stopped the training process if the correlation between predictions and
labels dropped for more than ﬁve epochs. The neural networks were trained for maximally 200 epochs and
ﬁgure 7 show that most networks trained for no more than 60 epochs, due to the stopping criterion. It can
also be seen from ﬁgure 7 that networks with the speciﬁc phenotypes make use of the stopping criterion
much earlier (on average), thus providing better predictions faster than the default phenotypes. The early
stopping combined with the predictions, as indicated by MCC-scores, suggest that at least some general
patterns speciﬁc to the genotype architecture of childhood asthma are learned. However, it is expected that
the network is inﬂuenced by noise in the data. The reason for this expectation relates to the fact that shallow
networks, in practice, tend to converge towards discrimination between redundant and relevant features, at
a much slower speed than deep architectures [36]. This is contrary to the general expectation that simpler
models tend to provide the best generalizations.
The point of this being that a deeper architectures is
expected to achieve better predictions since these architectures will learn to ignore noise faster than the
one-layer perceptrons used in this project. In the end, this will provide more robust interaction estimates
by NID, perhaps moving FUT2-ABO up the rank list as well as allowing more robust regression validation
of the top interactions, to discover novel interactions. Furthermore, using the speciﬁc phenotypes, result in
shorter training processes (on average) and much stabler training curves for both training and validations,
in line with the much narrower MCC-distribution for speciﬁc phenotype, see ﬁgure 8.
COPSAC2010
Validation is a little less robust for the COPSAC2010 data with the NAAG-concentration
as phenotype as shown in ﬁgure 10. When comparing these results with the training process in ﬁgure 9,
it indicates that the networks do not make use of the stopping-criterion. Figure 9 shows the training and
validation process. Fluctuations in MSE may be inﬂated compared to the actual diﬀerence between the
prediction and label since the diﬀerence is squared. This is backed up by the validation loss being relatively
stable despite seemingly large ﬂuctuations in training loss, indicating that the large diﬀerences in MSE have
only a small eﬀect on generalization. However, when inspecting the PCC-distribution of the networks, see
ﬁgure 10, most networks yield correlations comparable to the COPSACsevere-data, but something is causing
instabilities causing some networks to predict badly. This indicates that some networks stop prematurely,
since PCC-values of 0.2-0.4 as shown in ﬁgure 10, is non-existent if the training process completes the
maximum number of epochs, see ﬁgure 9. This inherent instability is also reﬂected in the ability of NID to
robustly rank some interactions in the top, as no interactions consistently rank in the top 20,000 for more
than 5 times.
General conclusions
Figure 8 shows that phenotype speciﬁcity signiﬁcantly improves predictions compared
to using the default asthma deﬁnition. This presents evidence in favor of using speciﬁc phenotypes if the
goal is a robust prediction of phenotype. These results indicate that using a speciﬁc phenotype encourages
50
==PAGE:58==
the network to learn more general patterns from the data, resulting in signiﬁcantly higher MCC-scores
compared to using the default asthma deﬁnition. This trend, before shown to increase the power of linear
models, transfer into the ability of neural networks to generalize in predicting phenotypes from genotypes.
It is not clear, as of yet, if phenotype speciﬁcity increases clarity in feature interpretation of neural networks.
This will be discussed in the following section.
The advantage of neural networks is that they can learn any mapping between input and output
taking non-additive eﬀects into account. On the other hand, this ﬂexibility is a disadvantage because the
interpretation of the model becomes diﬃcult. Neural networks greatly surpass linear models in areas such
as image analysis or computer vision where high-dimensional data is paired with robustly deﬁned labels.
However, in the prediction of complex diseases, a noisy phenotype is the only available label for a dataset. As
explained in section 1.5 many common diseases are not thoroughly understood and potentially unaccounted
sub-types of the disease will create noisy outcomes for models trying to associate a genotype to a phenotype.
Moreover, the phenotype is inﬂuenced by the environment and genes in unison, which means that we will
always expect noisy predictions even if we had perfect labels. Furthermore, genotype calls are much simpler
than pixels in image classiﬁcations which can be continuous, of mixed types, and whose values vary over
a wide range. The extremely complex relationship between DNA sequences and the phenotype has to be
encoded in a very simple data format consisting only of 0, 1, and 2, and allele frequencies can diminish
the eﬀective number of possible combinations as the homozygotes in reality becomes non-existing for rare
alleles. This fact may additionally explain why neural networks using genotype data may be having a hard
time boiling down the intricate nature of complex diseases eﬀectively using a 0-1 bit input [5]. There are
many limitations of deep learning in genomics namely model interpretations, which this project aims to
clarify part of by interpreting interactions. This means that it is diﬃcult to extract the mode of causality
between genotype and phenotype because causative features are not easily interpreted. Another diﬃculty
is when there are more predictors than data points; a recurrent problem in genomics, namely "the curse of
dimensionality". With a large number of predictors, an equivalently large number of data points is needed
to provide accurate estimates. This is especially relevant for the COPSAC2010 data set. A way to deal
with this problem is merging data sets from large repositories of publicly available data sets like the UK
Biobank [19]. However, this will most likely compromise phenotype speciﬁcity. It could therefore be a mode
of improvement, to ﬁnd the optimal trade-oﬀbetween sample size and phenotype speciﬁcity.
4.3
Neural Interaction Detection
The fact that some individual SNPs are over-represented in numerous of the highly ranked interactions
indicates that main-eﬀect SNPs can be modeled as spurious interactions. This limitation is also pointed out
in the paper by Tsang et al., 2017 which proposed an architecture MLP-M, to reduce spurious interactions
51
==PAGE:59==
caused by main eﬀects. MLP-M consists of a neural network with P input features (in a P-dimensional data
set), as well as P networks with a single input layer for each feature. The MLP and the P univariate networks
are trained jointly and summed at the output. This architecture is proposed to encourage the modeling of
main eﬀects away from the main network. However, training 13,315 networks jointly proved too costly and
MLP-M was dropped, showing the same problem in COPSAC2010 data. Furthermore, simulation studies
showed that MLP-M had a consistently higher AUC than MLP in regards to interaction-detection, but not
with a vast amount [42]. A meta-analysis of the AUC values from Tsang et. al., 2017 did detect a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence between the AUC of MLP and MLP-M architectures. Thus it is assessed that the gain of excluding
MLP-M from this project is higher than the loss.
However, it is not known how the complex patterns
of genotype-data may inﬂuence NID to predict spurious interactions and aﬀect the modeling of spurious
interactions, meaning that spurious interactions in NID cannot be disregarded.
Additionally, NID only
considers interactions originating in the ﬁrst hidden layer. If NID could be extended to deeper hidden layers,
deeper architectures may capture more interactions. Because the data is subjected to LD-pruning, intra-locus
epistatic eﬀects are ignored and those are never going to be included in these analyses. However, investigation
of intra-locus gene-gene interactions may cause a problem since LD between SNPs can potentially generate
false positive interactions [17]. However, this could be accounted for in a potential analysis.
COPSACsevere
Comparing the rank of FUT2-ABO to the rank of a randomly drawn SNP-SNP pair from
the data shows that the ranks of FUT2-ABO are much more clustered around high ranks, than expected by
random chance, compared to an almost uniform distribution for a random SNP-SNP pair see ﬁgure 12 and 13.
This indicates that FUT2-ABO drives the network in predicting asthma. Furthermore, of the interactions
that always occurred in the top 20,000, selected interactions, see in table 4 all contain word-associations
such as cancer, type I diabetes, and cytokines, indicating involvement in immune-related functions. This
indicates that NID can extract SNPs which has before been associated with a biologically relevant function.
Word-association has been done manually, but to provide a more quantitative measure of the biological
relevance of the interactions, an automated approach to SNP-ontology must be done. To enable interaction-
wide word-association, rs-IDs need to be paired with locus-name to enable gene-ontology analysis. A more
comprehensive investigation will show if a general enrichment of immune-related functions is present in the
top SNPs.
Contrary to expectation NID performed worse with regards to ranking of FUT2-ABO in the speciﬁc
phenotype. A possible explanation of this is that since the data becomes even more unbalanced (5 % cases),
NID is more sensitive to low MAF alleles, which is also shown in some tests regarding the limitations of NID
concerning MAF, see appendix D. This is because the neural network simply has fewer examples of cases
and their respective genotypes. A strange fact is that the neural network performed signiﬁcantly better on
the data stratiﬁed by severity, but the betterment in phenotype prediction did not transfer into interaction
52
==PAGE:60==
detection. This may be due to the ﬁrst explanation, that the speciﬁc phenotype has fewer cases, increasing
susceptibility to spurious patterns caused by low MAF values.
Another explanation may be that using
the default asthma deﬁnition may result in a slightly overﬁtting network, causing SNPs with strong main
eﬀects to be modeled as spurious interactions with high strengths, resulting in few but strong features easily
detected by NID. If the analysis were to be repeated less stringent MAF ﬁlters, in the initial data pruning
could be used to challenge this problem.
Figure 14 shows the relationship between MCC on validation data and rank assigned to FUT2-ABO.
This indicates that when the network is eﬃcient in classifying asthma-patients, NID assesses that FUT2-ABO
is important for predictions. This also shows that the stable MCC-scores for the stringent asthma-deﬁnition,
provide a more unstable interaction ranking, regardless of the models’ ability to generalize. In this case, it
seems as if NID does not believe FUT2-ABO to be as important in driving asthma-predictions. Since the
network is a binary classiﬁer, the raw output of the network is a probability of the individual belonging to
a class. If the probability is > 0.5, the input is assigned to the case-group and vice versa. This rounding
of the output, discards important information about network predictions since a prediction of 0.51 and 0.9
are considered equals, in calculations of the MCC. Taking the examples of 0.51 and 0.9 there are huge
diﬀerences in how certain the network is, about the classiﬁcation of these individuals. To investigate the
relationship between the certainty of the network and rank, MCC was replaced by the raw output of the
network as shown in ﬁgure 15. The relationship between the certainty of the network and rank assigned
to FUT2-ABO is stratiﬁed by the true case-control status. Stratifying by case-control-status shows that
FUT2-ABO is assigned a lower rank when the raw network output is closer to the true label, both for cases
and controls. Cases display an negative relationship between output value and rank, while controls show
a positive, showing that wrongly predicted labels result in a low rank of FUT2-ABO and vice versa (on
average). The relationship is found to signiﬁcant (P-val: 0.000452, P-val:4.59e-07) with linear regression,
for cases and controls respectively. The P-value for controls is lower than cases due to the increased sample
size of controls compared to cases. Figure 15 provides further indication that FUT2-ABO is detrimental in
classiﬁcation of asthma-patients. Overall, this indicates that NID can detect important drivers for phenotype
predictions and that NID is expected to perform increasingly better on networks with higher predictive power.

4.4
Validation of interactions
Regression analysis was only carried out on the COPSACsevere using the default phenotype since only this
dataset provided consistency in interaction-ranking. Previous analysis showed that FUT2-ABO was placed
high on the interaction list with a modal rank of 187,620. Therefore, interactions that consistently rank at
the very top of the interaction-list may have some robustness and validity in the estimate. For this reason,
interactions that occur in the top 20,000 in 50 out of 50 model runs, were chosen for validation by regression.
Bayesian regression
The results from Bayesian regression are ambiguous and it seems as if the data
cannot suﬃciently reallocate the belief of the model to values away from the prior. Figure 17 shows that
almost all posteriors were completely inside the ROPE, leading to either rejection of the estimate or indecision
about the practical relevance of the coeﬃcients. Furthermore, it shows that the posterior is not aﬀected by
MAF, as lower MAF-values are not overwhelmingly enriched in posterior means deviating a lot from the
null-value (OR=1).
Additionally, the low spread combined with the P(HPDI=ROPE) of the posteriors
indicates a reluctance of the model to conclude that any of the interactions-candidates investigated are true
epistatic interactions. The prior has a σ = 0.04, indicating an informative prior, e.g.strong prior belief about
interaction eﬀect. Informative priors may deﬂate the estimates, indicating that the interaction eﬀect may be
higher. On the contrary, using a more uninformative prior increase the rate of false discovery. This reveals
the resilience of Bayesian methods to spurious results, potentially caused by low-MAF interactions enriched
in cases by chance. This is further underlined in the frequentist analysis.
The power of Bayesian methods also come with a cost, as it is not always straightforward how
to reduce previous knowledge to a prior distribution. There exists an inverse relationship between allele
frequency and eﬀect size, and this could be reﬂected in a prior-distribution.
A model could contain an
adaptive prior, to vary the strictness with varying MAF, such that low-MAF variants are assigned a wider
prior, compared to high-MAF variants. This could be a way of including information about natural selection
in a model since it is not unreasonable to subject lower-MAF alleles to less shrinkage since we expect these
to have a higher eﬀect.
It could also inﬂuence the mean of the prior, but this would require extensive
knowledge about the inﬂuence of MAF on eﬀect sizes.
However increasing the standard deviation with
decreasing frequency may reduce the number of false negatives in a GWAS using stringent priors to account
54
==PAGE:62==
for multiple testing, since the prior used in this project completely discards the information provided in Park
et al., 2011. In practice, this could be achieved with a hierarchical model approach. One of the problems
with this approach would be the increased rate of false positives, as lower-MAF variants are more likely to be
spurious and the degree of regularization would have to be somewhere in between the conservative classical
prior and the liberal frequentist. Such an approach would therefore require a larger sample size, than the
one-size-ﬁts-all prior.
Frequentist regression
Using a frequentist framework many interactions are estimated to have high OR,
even under stringent signiﬁcance thresholds (α = 0.01) of Bonferroni-adjusted p-values. Additionally, the
burden of multiple testing is present (447 tests) but not as immense as in GWAS. A conservative signiﬁcance
threshold was chosen because the analysis yielded many signiﬁcant interactions, giving rise to the suspicion
that false positives may be among the results. Quality control of the frequentist measures, as shown in ﬁgure
??, indicates trustworthy results in that the p-value distribution is very diﬀerent from a random sample of
p-values ??a. Figure ??b shows that estimates need to be exceedingly far away from zero, to be considered
signiﬁcant and that plenty of such estimates exist. Furthermore, it shows that minor alleles are more likely
the disease causing allele, indicated by OR-value having a higher magnitude above 1, than below. This is in
line with the theory of disease-alleles described in section 1.4.
Frequentist methods rely on accepting or rejecting a null-hypothesis. This dichotomous approach
results in sub-optimal properties in large data sets. With increasing sample size, the certainty of small but
practically irrelevant eﬀect sizes increases, thus making them signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from the null-hypothesis
regardless of the magnitude of the eﬀect. Bayesian estimations becomes increasingly accurate with increasing
sample size, and the ROPE+HPDI decision rule can be used with greater conﬁdence. This is because a small
but practically irrelevant eﬀects will fall inside the ROPE resulting in rejection of the parameter, assuming
the ROPE has been deﬁned appropriately [20]. Figure 21 shows OR is highly dependent on MAF, and that
ORs for low-MAF interactions fall inside a much lower range completely below 1.5. Furthermore, none of
the interactions with MAF > 0.2 were statistically signiﬁcant.

The results in table 4 show three of the best regression results, in terms of
both Bayesian and frequentist regression. The last column show that the individual SNPs are associated
with biologically relevant publication-titles, including diseases related to the immune system, suggesting
that SNPs in the highly ranked interactions are involved in immune-related pathways, similarly to previous
variants associated with asthma [7, 50]. Replication of the interactions in COPSAC2010 indicates that the
high frequentist estimates are most likely spurious, due to failure to replicate, see table 3. This suggests that
the relationship between MAF and frequentist eﬀect size shown in ﬁgure 21 can be attributed to spurious
interactions since low MAF-interactions are generally non-signiﬁcant and lower OR. The lowest interaction

frequency would still yield an expected number ≈55 individuals carrying that interaction in COPSAC2010,

Figure 21 shows a clear inverse relationship between eﬀect size and MAF. Large interaction estimates

prior.
A possible explanation as to why no interactions are consistently ranked in the top 20,000 using
the speciﬁc phenotype, could be that the speciﬁc deﬁnition of asthma, reduces the number of cases, thereby
reducing the risk of random enrichment. Furthermore, the same interactions do not consistently rank among
the top 20,000 as in the default phenotype. However, the consistently high-ranking interactions in the default
phenotype turned out to be spurious, indicating a present degree of bias. The consistency of the top-ranked
interactions with the default asthma phenotype, may be due to SNPs with large main eﬀects being modeled as
spurious interactions by NID, explaining why the individual SNPs are involved in immune-related functions,
but not in interactions. This could be caused by a few SNPs with a high main-eﬀect being over-represented
in the top interactions, as indicated by ﬁgure 19. This shows that out of the 74 signiﬁcant interactions,
2 SNPs (rs2643195 and rs17609240) are vastly over-represented, indicating that these are SNPs with large
main-eﬀects causing NID to predict spurious interactions, leaving true interactions further down the rank-
list. This is in line with the fact that FUT2-ABO receives a modal rank of 187,620, which is quite high
considering 80 million possible interactions. rs2643195 and rs17609240 are SNPs with strong main eﬀects
falling completely outside the ROPE, but failing to replicate in COPSAC2010, see ﬁgure 20. The failure of
replication may be caused by the non-speciﬁc phenotype used as dependent variable.

On average rank estimations deviate less than 1 standard deviation from the true ranks, and predicted
ranks display a consistent trajectory with the true ranks, see ﬁgure 16. This is an indicating of a reasonable
heuristic estimate of rank. However, rank estimation seems to overestimate at higher ranks. This is especially
evident in ﬁgure 27 in appendix C. This is not surprising since the model is trained on the 0.002 % top-

5
Conclusion
The results in this report indicate that NID is able to correctly rank interactions if a network can learn
to discriminate cases from control, with high accuracy. This is illustrated by networks with high MCC-
scores on validation data, seem to rank true interactions higher than networks with low scores. A higher
resolution of this analysis shows that when the probability of predicting a case or control is closest to the
label, true interactions get ranked equivalently better. This strongly suggests that NID is able to capture
true interactions in network architecture and that optimized architectures are expected to result in better
performance of NID. Additionally, simulation studies show that strong interactions in which both SNPs have
main eﬀects are more suﬃciently captured and interaction recovery becomes approximately half as frequent
when the individual SNPs do not aﬀect phenotype. However, validation-by-regression indicate that NID
is strongly aﬀected by main-eﬀects in the ranking of the strongest interactions. This is exempliﬁed by a
few SNPs with main-eﬀects being strongly over-represented in the top interactions validated by regression
approaches. Validation by regression thus shows that the top interactions in NID are mostly false positives,
conﬁrming the hypothesis of Tsang et. al., 2017. To further show that the top interactions were spurious,
ﬁndings by NID in the COPSACsevere dataset could not be replicated in the COPSAC2010 cohort.
In regards to neural networks, using speciﬁc phenotypes increases the predictive power, resulting in
a narrower distribution of MCC-scores, additionally increasing the average score signiﬁcantly. The training
process is also more stable using speciﬁc phenotypes, resulting in a faster converge towards a minimum. This
is in line with previous ﬁndings that demonstrate that speciﬁc phenotypes increase the power to discover new
variants [2, 7]. However, the increased predictive power does not seem to concern NID. A possible explanation
could be that the default phenotype relies on a few strong patterns when predicting the phenotype and that
FUT2-ABO is one of them. On the contrary, the speciﬁc phenotype allows the network to learn additional
signals that drive predictions, thus decreasing the relative importance of FUT2-ABO, resulting in ﬂuctuations
in its rank.
Overall NID shows great promise in performing genome-wide interaction screens, but the approach
needs some major improvement before robust interaction detection can be carried out in GWAS data. The
last section of this thesis will touch upon ideas for future improvement of NID to be employed as a tool for
genome-wide detection of epistasis.

6
Future work
This thesis does not provide any deﬁnite solution to how to perform a genome-wide screen for epistasis, but
some ideas for future research is provided. This section presents a set of ideas for possible directions towards
extending and developing a NID-based framework for genome-wide interaction detection.
6.1
Genetic algorithms
Neural networks are limited by their architectures which can critically inﬂuence performance.
Neural
networks have an inﬁnite amount of possible architectures and so far there are no methods of determining
the best ﬁtting architecture to a speciﬁc problem a priori.
In order to avoid searching for all possible
architectures, genetic algorithms have been applied to evolve a population of networks to have the best
possible architecture. Such a method have been implemented in DeepEvolve (Liphardt 2017) [24] which
can discover a new combination of hyper-parameters through mutations and recombination events, further
preventing optimizations from getting stuck in local minima [5]. However, this speciﬁc implementation was
discovered too late in the process of writing this thesis for any thorough application to be realistic. This
could be a future direction for this project to increase the stability and predictive power of the network.
6.2
Main eﬀect detection
NID is a framework for sorting overlapping paths between features according to relevance. It is easy to
imagine that, with simple adjustments, NID could be adapted into a main eﬀect detection tool. A proposed
name for this is Neural main Eﬀect Detection (NED). The advantage of such an approach is that these results
can be readily compared with comprehensive GWAS results, such that genome-wide signiﬁcant SNPs should
be at the top of the ranking list. It is also immune to the burden of exponential growth as NID since data
sets with hundreds of thousands of SNPs would be relatively un-problematic to handle. NED is expected to
be a straight-forward extension of NID, with plenty of results from alternative methods to compare. NED
can also be employed on the same trained neural network as NID and they could perhaps be complementary
approaches supporting or correcting one another.

Appendix B: Network pruning
With the smaller data sets, deeper architectures were utilized.
Deeper architectures have more weights
and therefore also more expected parameter redundancy and weight pruning was hypothesized to reduce
the number of redundant parameters. This is hypothesized to cause a small reduction in network predictive
power but increase the detection rates of ground truth interactions and the removal of irrelevant weights may
limit the rate of main eﬀects modeled as spurious interactions. However using the real data sets, single layer
networks seemed to be the best choice of model architecture, thus limiting the utility of network pruning,
since small amounts of pruning greatly aﬀected network predictive power, indicating low levels of redundancy.
Thus pruning strategies are reserved for deeper architectures. However, these early-stage simulation results
are still valuable since they give an idea about how pruning works, information that could be valuable if
deeper architectures are found through genetic algorithms in future works with NID on genomic data.
