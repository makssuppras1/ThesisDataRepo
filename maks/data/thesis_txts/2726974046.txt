Abstract
Obesity rates have nearly tripled since 1975 according to the WHO. Obese individuals
experience progressively severe disease states with many of them suffering from MetS,
characterised by insulin resistance, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and abdominal obesity.
This huge increase in incidence combined with more severe disease states puts a sub-
stantial burden on society, with the US alone spending 190 billion dollars on obesity-
related healthcare expenses.
Research uncovering the biological mechanisms behind obesity and MetS is of paramount
importance. Herein the need for well-suited animal models also plays a crucial role. The
Ossabaw pig is proving to be one of the best animal models for replicating the disease
states seen in obese humans with MetS. It is hypothesised that the Ossabaw pig’s mutated
leptin receptor contributes to its heightened obesity tendency.
Therefore the aim of this thesis is to map the expression of the leptin receptor in various
different Ossabaw pig tissues. High-quality RNA samples were extracted from blood,
PBMCs, liver, SAT, and VAT from lean and obese Ossabaw pigs. These samples were
synthesised into cDNA and used to perform gene expression analysis on the Rotor-Gene
Q. Additionally, SDS- and Native PAGE experiments were conducted to investigate the
binding of leptin to the leptin receptor.
The results from the experiments indicate that the leptin receptor is expressed across
the different biological sample groups. However, further research is required before any
specific conclusions can be made. The SDS- and Native PAGE experiments showed
leptin binding to the leptin receptor in pure samples, but similar results were not obtained
in tissue samples.
Overall, further validating and improving on the results from this thesis will contribute to
an enhanced understanding of the leptin system in the Ossabaw pig and its possible
implications in obesity and MetS.

1.1
Motivation
Obesity is a growing public health concern, with the World Health Organization (WHO)
reporting a nearly three-fold increase in obesity rates since 1975 [72]. The widespread
prevalence of obesity not only poses a significant threat to the health and well-being of
individuals but also to society and industries.
To understand obesity we first need to know how it is defined. The WHO defines over-
weight and obesity as abnormal or excessive fat accumulation that may impair health [72].
And the most common way to categorise, when an individual is overweight or obese is
based on the body mass index (BMI). BMI is calculated by taking an individual’s weight
in kilograms and dividing it by their height in meters squared [80]:
BMI = weight(kg)
height(m)2

Drug manufacturers are fond of this definition because they can then start to offer treat-

as the Ossabaw pig will be of significant importance since the pigs will help researchers
better understand the underlying nature of obesity.
The market for treating obesity is huge. In 2016, more than 1.9 billion adults (aged 18
years or older) were classified as overweight, and over 650 million were classified as

population were overweight [110], and 37.3% were obese [109]. And in 2016 in Denmark,
55.4% of the adult population were overweight [110], while 21.3% were obese [109].
Looking towards the future it is likely that even more people will get obese. A projection
made by Kræftens Bekæmpelse in Denmark shows that 66% of the Danish population
will be overweight in 2045, while 29% will be obese [62].

often stigmatised and experience bullying and teasing, leading to lower self-esteem in the

1.2
Objectives and Outline
This master thesis builds upon previous studies performed on the leptin system in the
Ossabaw pig. It aims to contribute to the understanding of the Ossabaw pig, obesity, and
metabolic syndrome (MetS).
Previous studies suggest that the gene encoding the leptin receptor (LEPR) of the Os-
sabaw pig differs from that of other pig types. This might contribute to the heightened
obesity tendency of the Ossabaw pig. However, this possible connection has to be inves-
tigated further and more in-depth, which is where this thesis comes into play.
The overall aim of the thesis is to map the expression of LEPR in adipose and liver tissue,
as well as in whole blood and peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), from lean
and obese Ossabaw pigs.
To do this, high-quality ribonucleic acid (RNA) is needed. RNA will be purified from the
above-mentioned biological sample groups and tested for both concentration and purity.
To ensure the highest possible quality of RNA different protocols will be tested to see
which yields the best results.
To map the expression of genes, the RNA samples of the highest quality have to be syn-
thesised into complementary DNA (cDNA). This cDNA can then be used in the quantitative
reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) experiments. The RT-qPCR
experiments will provide a targeted gene expression analysis, where the expression of
the LEPR gene and the gene for the putative ligand alpha-1-acid glycoprotein (AGP) will
be tested in the different biological sample groups from which the RNA was purified.
A validation of previous results from another master’s student will also be done using dif-
ferent polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) experiments. These experiments will
further improve our understanding of the interplay between leptin and LEPR in tissues
and pure samples.
Through these experiments, I will be able to test the working hypothesis that a state of
partial leptin resistance, due to mutations in the LEPR gene, may contribute to the obe-
sity tendency of the Ossabaw pigs. This will help advance the characterisation of the
mechanisms behind leptin resistance and its possible connection with features of MetS.

Background
The cause of overweight and obesity is suggested to be a result of a long-lasting and com-
plex interaction between many different factors. The general public often views it as just
being lazy and eating too much but studies find that a mix of both genetic predisposition,
psychosocial factors, and health behaviours are to blame [62].
2.1
The Socioeconomic Impact of Obesity
Obesity does not only affect the individual in a physical way. In children, it is mostly the
psychosocial problems that cause the biggest issues. This includes them being subject
to bullying, teasing, having a lower self-esteem and a lower quality of life [101]. Adults
are also affected, in their case it is bullying, discrimination, and stigmatisation. In fact,
stigmatisation alone has been proven to contribute to the development of obesity [101].
In Denmark, and elsewhere, there is a social imbalance in the occurrence of both over-
weight and obesity. According to data from Sundhedsstyrelsen: 63% of people with the
lowest educational degree are overweight, while for people with the highest educational
degree, only 39% are overweight. When looking at obese people in the same categories
it is 26% versus 8%. This social imbalance can also be seen in children [101].
As mentioned in section 1.1 obese Danes put a massive workload of 1.3 million extra
contacts to general practitioners in an already overworked healthcare sector [101]. This
is one of the main characteristics of the socioeconomic impact of obesity and it is not only
seen in the Danish welfare model.
The US, which has a massively different welfare model, also experiences this excessive
workload. According to studies evaluated by the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public
Health, in 2005, the cost of obesity on an individual level compared to normal-weight
individuals was between 42 and 150% higher. This means that in the US they spent 190
billion dollars on obesity-related healthcare expenses (given the 150% mark) [40, 18].
Since obesity is caused by both individual and societal conditions it is important to not
exclusively see obesity as an individual matter but also prioritise what society can do to
help [101]. Especially, the massive healthcare expenses provide an incentive for society
to do something to combat obesity.
Reducing overweight and obesity should be manageable since obesity, in most cases, is
preventable. Both we as individuals, society, and large organisations can help drive this
change. Some of the most common socioeconomic strategies to reduce obesity are to
implement policies that promote a healthy lifestyle, such as making consumption of fruits,
vegetables, nuts, whole grain, legumes and water more accessible and affordable [42,
72].
Another way to combat obesity would be to have a better understanding of the underlying
physiological mechanisms. Understanding these mechanisms can prove crucial to know-
ing why some people are more prone to obesity than others. And especially, being able
to tell when obese individuals start to develop MetS is of significant importance.
MetS comes with an increased risk of developing NCDs, more on this in section 2.2.3. Ac-
cording to the WHO, NCDs threaten the progress towards accomplishing the 2030 Agenda
for Sustainable Development. This agenda includes a target of reducing the probability
of death from any of the four main NCDs (cardiovascular diseases, cancers, chronic res-
piratory diseases, and diabetes) by one-third in 2030 in individuals between ages 30 and
70 years [75].

2.2
The Physiological Impact of Obesity
Obesity is proposed to be associated with a chronic low-grade inflammatory condition [36,
83] and many complex factors, herein genetic predisposition, lead to it. Twin, family, and
adoption studies show that the heritability of obesity is between 40% and 70% [61].
Obesity can be classified in two ways with regards to heritability: monogenic and poly-
genic obesity. Monogenic obesity is inherited in a Mendelian pattern, it is usually severe
and rare, only accounting for 5% of severe obesity cases [92, 61]. Polygenic obesity,
however, is inherited in a much more complex way and it is the result of many different
polymorphisms whose effects add up and cause obesity [61].
2.2.1
Measuring Obesity
It is crucial to have accurate and reproducible measurements of overweight and obesity
in order to track the condition.
As mentioned in section 1.1, BMI is widely used as an indicator of overweight and obe-
sity, and thereby also weight-related health risks. However, weight-related health risks
are often more associated with percentage body fat than BMI [77]. Hence, critics argue
that BMI should not be used as a direct measure of body fat percentage since it has some
inaccuracies in doing so. One of these inaccuracies is the fact that having a high muscle
mass will increase body weight and therefore, athletes or fit individuals may appear to
be overweight in the BMI definition but have a perfectly healthy body and fat percentage
[93]. Some other inaccuracies are the fact that muscle and bone density usually declines
with age, meaning that an older individual might have a higher body fat percentage than
a younger individual with the same BMI. Also, women tend to have a higher body fat per-
centage than men with the same BMI. And finally, BMI does not show the distribution of
adipose tissue [93].
There are other ways to measure obesity that can prove to be useful for studies that need
a more accurate measurement of body composition. These include waist circumference,
waist-to-hip ratio, and bioelectric impedance [41]. In general, these methods are inex-
pensive, easy to perform, and either directly measure body fat percentage or correlate
well with it. However, they have some limitations with regards to not being standardised,
which can impede reproducibility. They are also prone to errors in their measurements,
and they are inaccurate in individuals with BMI’s of >35 [41].
The more sophisticated methods such as computerised tomography and magnetic reso-
nance imaging, are the most accurate in analysing body composition but are very expen-
sive, time-consuming, and non-compatible with some individuals [41].
Nevertheless, BMI is still a useful tool at population level, it is inexpensive, easy to mea-
sure, and most studies use the well-defined BMI-based cutoff points for overweight and
obesity [41].
2.2.2
Adipose Tissue
Adipose tissue plays a key role in energy homeostasis and is a major endocrine and
immune organ [79, 31].
Obesity leads to a remodelling of adipose tissue, and it is hypothesised to occur mainly
through an expansion as the body takes on excessive calories. The expansion of adipose
tissue leads to an increased and imbalanced production of adipokines which in turn results
in the pro-inflammatory state that is observed systemically in obese individuals [33, 31].

Adipose tissue can be classified as three different types: white adipose tissue (WAT),
brown adipose tissue (BAT), and beige adipose tissue (BeAT) — see figure 2.1. These
tissues are characterised by their anatomical locations and roles [79].
WAT is distributed throughout the body and it is the body’s main lipid storage, which can
be seen by the fact that it contains large lipid droplets. Its role is to store (and mobilise)
energy in the form of triglycerides. WAT makes up most of the body’s adipose tissue and
can be split into two subtypes: visceral adipose tissue (VAT) and subcutaneous adipose
tissue (SAT). VAT is located around the organs and protects them, while SAT is mostly
located under the skin and insulates from heat or cold [79, 33].
BAT is mainly distributed in the thorax region i.e. the chest, abdomen, and at the col-
larbone. It regulates body temperature through heat generation by oxidising fatty acids,
which gives it an anti-obesity role. BAT is the most vascularised and innervated adipose
tissue, which allows it to respond to sympathetic nerve activity and heat up the blood
which in turn heats up the body. BAT has smaller lipid droplets compared to WAT, and
crucially, BAT has a much higher mitochondrial content than WAT [79].
BeAT is located within WAT and it resembles WAT in the basal state but responds to
thermogenic stimuli similarly to BAT [79].
Figure 2.1: The three types of adipocytes and their cell morphology [111].
As mentioned earlier in this section, obesity leads to a remodelling or rather an expan-
sion of adipose tissue. This expansion can either be an increase in adipocyte numbers
(hyperplasia) or an increase in adipocyte size (hypertrophy) — or a combination of both.
In general, hyperplasia is considered to be a ”healthy” expansion of adipose tissue due
to the fact that it is moderated by the formation of mature adipocytes from preadipocytes
[33]. On the other hand, hypertrophy leads to dysfunctional adipocytes that intensify adi-
pose tissue inflammation. This adipose tissue inflammation is termed adiposopathy or
sick fat. Adiposopathy is more prevalent in VAT than SAT and this could be one of the
reasons why the expansion of VAT is strongly associated with adverse events such as
cardiometabolic diseases [33].
Additionally, adiposopathy leads to an increase in the number of T cells, B cells, macrophages,
and neutrophils located in obese individuals’ adipose tissue, with macrophages being the
most abundant. Macrophages are split into two main groups with opposing effects, called
M1 and M2. M1 macrophages are pro-inflammatory and they promote adipogenesis and
hepatic steatosis, while the M2 macrophages are anti-inflammatory and have the opposite
effect. Interestingly, M2 macrophages are mostly observed in lean individuals’ adipose
tissue, while M1 macrophages tend to be associated with obese individuals’ adipose tis-
sue. Again promoting the inflammatory state of obesity.

2.3
Leptin
The first idea of leptin comes from G. C. Kennedy’s lipostatic theory from 1953 [56]. This
theory states that food intake is controlled by the hypothalamus to regulate body fat. For
this theory to be true the hypothalamus must be able to detect the size of body fat stores.
Subsequent experiments showed that there is in fact a humoral factor that takes part in
the feedback regulation of body fat stores [36]. Around the same time, in 1950, Ingalls

==PAGE:16==
et al. [54] described a mutant strain of mice that were suffering from severe obesity —
dubbed ob/ob mice. And roughly 20 years later, in 1973, Coleman [22] could report that
ob/ob mice gained weight less rapidly and ate less when their circulation was connected
to wild-type mice. This indicated that there was a molecule in the circulation of wild-type
mice that was involved in energy balance and that this molecule was lacking in ob/ob
mice.
Again, around 20 years later this humoral factor was identified as the so-called ob gene
protein in 1994 by Zhang et al. [113], and is now better known as leptin.
Leptin is a 16 kDa non-glycosylated hormone which is encoded by the LEP gene. It sup-
presses feeding behaviour and elevates energy expenditure under normal circumstances.
In humans, leptin’s 167 amino acids are structured as four antiparallel α-helices that are
connected by two disulfide cross-links. Leptin is said to be a helical adipokine and aug-
ments innate immune responses as a proinflammatory adipokine [57, 83, 32].
Leptin is primarily produced in WAT and the expression is proportional to white fat mass
[36]. Leptin is continuously secreted in a pulsatile manner with diurnal oscillations, with
the highest rates in the evening and early morning. This is due to the presence of small
vesicles of leptin in adipocytes and the secretion is to some extent independent of leptin
mRNA expression. The secreted leptin circulates the body’s bloodstream and the circu-
lating leptin concentrations are higher at night than during the day in humans. Circulating
leptin is found both in a free and LEPR-bound form [36, 83].
It is however not only the quantity of adipocytes that equals the amount of leptin. Many
other factors such as estrogen, tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), free fatty acids, hor-
mones, nutritional status, and impaired renal clearance affect leptin levels, independently
of obesity [83]. For example, 4-7 hours after a meal circulating leptin levels usually in-
crease, this is primarily due to insulin-stimulated glucose utilisation, which leads to up-
regulation of mRNA transcription and release of leptin [83].
2.3.1
The Leptin Receptor
LEPR is a membrane-spanning receptor and is sometimes also known as OB-R — only
the LEPR title will be used in this thesis. It is mainly localised in intracellular compartments
such as endosomes, the endoplasmic reticulum, and the trans-Golgi apparatus, but can
also be found on the plasma membrane (estimates show that 5-25% of LEPR are located
on the cell surface) [8]. LEPR is distributed in almost all tissues in the body and and the
LEPR structurally resembles the group of class I cytokine receptors [83, 32].
There are six isoforms of LEPR found in humans and mice, denoted a, b, c, d, e, and f. In
humans, five of these six isoforms are produced by alternative splicing of the mRNA, only
LEPRe is not a product of alternative splicing — instead, it is derived from ectodomain
shedding [83]. All six isoforms of human LEPR can be seen in figure 2.2.
In pigs, only two isoforms have been described. The first one is a short LEPR isoform
that matches the functions of the short LEPR isoforms (a, b, c, and f) seen in humans and
mice. The other isoform is a long isoform, which also matches the long isoform (LEPRb)
seen in humans and mice. The long isoform found in pigs was only quantifiable in the
hypothalamus, while the short LEPR isoform appeared widely expressed across tissues
[82].

The human LEPR isoforms have an identical extracellular domain, which is made up of

The membrane-bound isoforms of the LEPR also share the same 23 amino acid sequence
in their trans-membrane domain and in the intracellular domain they share the first 29
amino acids. These 29 amino acids cover the so-called box 1 motif. The box 1 motif binds
Janus kinase 2 (JAK2), which is an important molecule in the downstream signalling from
the LEPR [83].

sharing the first 29 amino acids. LEPRa, c, d, and f have fairly short intracellular domains
of 32-40 amino acids and they all have unique C-termini. Their main role is to transport
leptin across the blood-brain barrier (BBB), perform internalisation, and lysosomal degra-

be involved in some parts of leptin signalling — namely activation of mitogen-activated
protein kinase (MAPK) pathways, but it is predominantly LEPRb that takes part in sig-
nalling pathways [95].
LEPRb’s intracellular domain is much longer than the others with roughly 306 amino acids
which means that it contains another box motif, giving it two in total. This means that
LEPRb can bind both JAK2, signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT), and
other signalling molecules. Hence, LEPRb is the main isoform responsible for signal
transduction [83] — more on this in section 2.3.3.

LEPRe
The concentration of LEPRe in serum reflects the expression of membrane-anchored
LEPR since LEPRe is exclusively produced through ectodomain shedding in humans [95].
LEPRe is soluble and does not have a trans-membrane domain. It circulates in the blood,
primarily as dimers or oligomers. LEPRe is crucial in maintaining the steady-state level
of free leptin and controlling the bioactivity of leptin by complexing the free leptin. LEPRe
is the body’s main leptin-binding protein, and when complexed, leptin’s degradation and
clearance are delayed. High levels of LEPRe also neutralise STAT3 activation and thereby
inhibit the anorexigenic effect of leptin. Put in other words LEPRe has an antagonistic
effect on leptin [83, 95].
In vitro data suggest that the enzyme class, A disintegrin and metalloprotease (ADAM) is
one of the main groups of enzymes that participate in the constitutive shedding of LEPR.
While many factors such as lipotoxicity and apoptosis might induce the shedding [95].
In vivo data suggest that acute and chronic dysregulation of energy homeostasis might
affect the production of LEPRe. Interestingly, it is observed that obese individuals have a
decreased LEPRe concentration in serum when compared to normal-weight individuals.
And under conditions of energy shortage, seen in anorexic or very lean individuals, there
is an increased LEPRe concentration. Since LEPRe has an inhibiting effect on leptin’s
bioactivity, this increased LEPRe concentration in an energy shortage state promotes
food intake [95].
In vivo data also supports the in vitro data of lipotoxicity and apoptosis-induced ectodomain
shedding. This is seen in obese individuals suffering from NASH that show a fibrosis-
dependent increase in LEPRe concentration [95].
2.3.2
Leptin Resistance
Obese humans and rodents have elevated levels of leptin (hyperleptinemia) without the
expected anorexic responses [33, 60]. This suggests a state of leptin resistance where
the responsiveness to leptin in tissues is reduced, similar to how insulin responsiveness is
reduced in type 2 diabetes [35]. The effect of leptin resistance is complex and not yet fully
understood. Despite that, research shows that leptin resistance can act both systemically
or in a more localised manner e.g. in the hypothalamus [60, 95].
Studies performed on rodents suggest that especially the hypothalamus and adipocytes
are prone to leptin resistance [36]. Additionally, other rodent studies show that LEPR-
associated proteins, such as suppressor of cytokine signalling 3 (SOCS3) and protein
tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP1B) might be involved. Both these proteins terminate the
activation of the long isoform of LEPR, LEPRb, in the hypothalamus and they have been
proven to have increased concentrations in obese mice [95].
Leptin resistance is hypothesised to generally occur in three different ways: circulating
leptin not reaching its targets in the brain, leptin receptor downregulation, and defects in
the leptin signalling pathways [83, 27].
Interestingly, the soluble leptin receptor, LEPRe, has been proven to play a role in two of
these three ways. A model proposed by Schaab and Kratzsch (2015) [95], see figure 2.3,
shows that leptin resistance occurs in two situations. The first situation is when leptin con-
centrations are high and are not being controlled by LEPRe since LEPRe concentrations
are low. This situation leads to leptin resistance because there are not enough membrane-
anchored LEPR to properly mediate the biological action of leptin, since LEPRe concen-
trations reflect the concentration of membrane-anchored LEPR [95, 60].
The second situation is when the LEPRe concentration greatly exceeds the leptin con-
centration. In this case LEPRe complexes leptin and inhibits its effects.

Some obese humans experience that hyperleptinemia worsens their leptin resistance.
This leads to a vicious cycle where they get more obese and leptin levels further increase,

2.3.3
Leptin and the Hypothalamus
High plasma levels of leptin make the brain reduce food intake and increase the body’s
energy expenditure. It also improves glucose metabolism through reduction of the hep-
atic glucose production. This biological action of leptin is mediated by LEPRb which is
expressed in many brain areas including the main leptin target, the hypothalamus [35].
In the hypothalamus it is the arcuate nucleus (ARC) that is the major site of leptin action.
The ARC is located within the mediobasal hypothalamus and thereby lies partially outside
the BBB. The fact that it is partially located outside the BBB makes it more accessible to
hormones from the circulation and cerebrospinal fluid, herein also leptin. The ARC con-
sists of two main neuron populations with opposing effects on the energy balance. The
orexigenic neuron population mainly producing two types of peptides: neuropeptide-Y
(NPY) and the agouti-related protein (AgRP). And the opposing anorexigenic neuron pop-
ulation mainly producing proopiomelanocortin (POMC) and cocaine- and amphetamine-
regulating transcript (CART). Leptin exerts its anorexigenic effects by inhibiting the neu-
rons production of AgRP and NPY while activating the neurons production of POMC and

When leptin binds to LEPRb in the ARC it leads to three main downstream signalling

JAK/STAT Pathway
The JAK/STAT pathway is the predominant pathway in which LEPRb acts through [27].
When leptin binds to LEPRb it leads to conformational changes that promote homo-
oligomerisation, which binds JAK2 in the box 1 domain of the intracellular part of LEPRb.
Once bound, JAK2 autophosphorylates and also phosphorylates four tyrosine residues on
LEPRb: Tyr974, Tyr985, Tyr1077, and Tyr1138. The phosphorylated Tyr1138 recruits STAT3,
which is then phosphorylated by JAK2. Once STAT3 is phosphorylated it dimerises and
translocates to the nucleus. In the nucleus, it induces SOCS3, PTP1B, POMC, and CART
expression, while repressing the expression of AgRP and NPY [83, 32, 35].
SOCS3 inhibits signalling by binding to phosphorylated JAK2 or by interacting directly
with the phosphorylated tyrosine residues of LEPRb. This provides a negative-feedback
loop on the leptin signalling system. Additionally, PTP1B dephosphorylates JAK2 creating
another negative regulation in this pathway [83, 32].
MAPK Pathway
In the MAPK pathway, Src homology-2 domain-containing protein tyrosine phosphatase-2
(SHP-2) binds to the phosphorylated tyrosine residues of LEPRb and is activated. Once
activated it recruits the protein growth factor receptor binding-2 (Grb-2). Grb-2 activates
the Ras/Raf/MAPK cascade which finally leads to the phosphorylation of extracellular
signal-regulated kinases (ERKs). The phosphorylated ERKs lead to the expression of
specific genes that are important for cell proliferation and differentiation [32, 31].
PI3K Pathway
The phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K) pathway starts off with the phosphorylation of in-
sulin receptor substrates (IRSs). This phosphorylation increases IRS’s affinity for binding
other signalling molecules, in this case the regulatory subunit p85 on PI3K. This stim-
ulates PI3K and leads to the activation of Akt. Akt is an important molecule in further
downstream processes, including processes that activate STAT3 [32].
It is also worth mentioning that most insulin-dependent actions involve PI3K activation
and that insulin also targets the same neuron populations as leptin. This goes to show
how interconnected leptin and insulin are [32, 83, 27].

2.3.4
Leptin and the Immune System
Obesity is proposed to be associated with a chronic low-grade inflammatory condition
and it results from changes in both the innate and adaptive immune system. The fact that
LEPR is expressed in human T cells, B cells, monocytes, and macrophages, suggests
that leptin might play a role in the innate and adaptive immune system [36, 83].
The inflammatory state in obese humans is accompanied by elevated levels of pro-inflammatory
cytokines in the bloodstream, such as TNF-α, interleukin-6 (IL-6), and C-reactive protein.
It is observed that pro-inflammatory signalling pathways in adipocytes are activated during
this state. And the production of leptin is also regulated by the pro-inflammatory cytokines.
An increase in plasma leptin levels is therefore seen during acute infection, inflammation,
or sepsis [36, 83].
In the innate immune system of humans, leptin has the following effects:
In monocytes, leptin stimulates the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and induces ex-
pression of cell surface markers which activate resting monocytes. Leptin also promotes
the proliferation and differentiation of monocytes into macrophages. And finally, leptin
has an anti-apoptotic effect on macrophages and promotes phagocytosis [83].
In neutrophils, leptin also has an anti-apoptotic effect and is responsible for enhancing
the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [83].
In dendritic cells, leptin, again, has an anti-apoptotic effect while also upregulating the
production of multiple cytokines, including IL-1β, IL-6, IL-12, and TNF-α [83].
Now looking at the adaptive immune system, in this case in mice:

2.3.5
Leptin and Other Tissues
Adipose tissue expresses both short and long isoforms of LEPR, thus suggesting possible
self-regulation of their leptin expression [36].
In addition to this possible self-regulation, leptin can also exert direct or indirect effects on
other parts of the adipocytes’ metabolism [36].
The direct effects of leptin on adipocytes were investigated through in vitro studies in or-
der to exclude other possible hormonal or neuronal effects.
The major takeaways from these in vitro studies (performed on porcine adipocytes) are
that leptin directly inhibits lipogenesis (formation of lipids) and stimulates lipolysis (break-
down of lipids) [36, 81].
These in vitro observed effects are also supported by in vivo studies where it is noticed
that when leptin is administered to leptin-responsive mice, it reduces their body fat mass,

Leptin exerts its indirect effects on adipocyte metabolism in different ways, including, mod-
ifying the release of hormones, changing the response to the released hormones, and by
increasing the activity of the sympathetic nerves that innervate adipocytes.
As an example, leptin stimulates lipolysis directly, as described above, but also indirectly.
In the indirect route, leptin makes the sympathetic nerves release norepinephrine that

Another indirect effect is caused because leptin interacts with insulin. Prolonged expo-

by binding to LEPR in the pancreatic beta cells. Leptin has also been shown to stimu-

insulin-like effect [36].
Leptin not only affects adipocytes, it also affects the heart and vasculature.
As explained in section 2.2.2, obesity leads to adiposopathy which creates an imbalance
in adipokine levels. This imbalance contributes to a chronic, low-grade inflammatory state.
The inflammatory state is believed to initiate and progress both metabolic and cardiovas-

One of the main cardiovascular diseases caused by obesity and hyperleptinemia is atheroscle-

the risk of cardiac ischemic events [33]. The possible connection between leptin’s proin-
flammatory role and atherosclerosis has been proved in both mice and human studies.
One of these studies, performed in humans, showed that leptin increases the production
of ROS in endothelial cells which is one of the actions that lead to atherosclerosis [13,

Ossabaw Pigs
Ossabaw pigs come from the Ossabaw Island which is a large barrier island (roughly 105
km2) located off the coast of Georgia, USA. The Ossabaw Island is located between the
Atlantic Ocean to the east and the Bear River to the west and is therefore isolated [29].
Since pigs are not native to North America it is believed that the exploring Spaniards
released them in the southeastern part of the US in the 15th or 16th century. It is also
believed that the pigs were introduced to Ossabaw Island around this time [28, 30].
Ossabaw pigs are feral and have lived isolated for over 500 years [28]. They are charac-
terised by their thick black coat, long snouts, and upright ears [30]. But the most interest-
ing characteristic is that they can add 30-40% of their body weight in fat during the winter
months and survive on these fat reserves during the summer when food is more scarce
— a so-called ”thrifty” phenotype [30, 21]. This fact has, through natural selection, led to a
very robust pig which is exceptionally good at building fat deposits [28]. In fact, Ossabaw
pigs have the highest levels of total body lipid reserves of any hoofed mammals [15].

2.4.1
An Ideal Animal Model
Developing reliable animal models that display both similar pathophenotypes and respond
to known disease state modifiers in a similar way to humans is of great importance. Pigs
are known to be good animal models due to their similarity to human physiology. And
the fact that they are omnivores and can take on different dietary programs makes them
super useful in modelling metabolic diseases and MetS [21].
As described in section 2.2.3, MetS is a mix of different metabolic dysfunctions and risk
factors and it is a direct result of genetics, environment, and lifestyle choices. The core
components of MetS are insulin resistance, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and abdominal
obesity. MetS and its associated secondary comorbidities (hepatic steatosis, peri-organ
adiposity, inflammation, diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, cancers, and remodelling of
cardiac tissue) will be one of the greatest challenges to healthcare providers and govern-
ments in the coming century [21]. A reliable animal model to investigate MetS further will
therefore be highly useful.
Many studies have been performed on different porcine models and Cluzel et al. (2022)
[21] reviewed 127 articles describing a porcine model of MetS.
Their overall conclusion is that pigs are powerful animal models for studying MetS. This
is mainly due to the fact that their physiology is so similar to human physiology and that
they accurately reproduce the disease phenotype [21].
Ossabaw pigs are the most commonly used porcine MetS model and of the 127 articles
reviewed, 46 of them were based on Ossabaw pigs. According to the review, the Ossabaw
pig was considered the most useful of the porcine models reviewed. An overview of the
disease profile of the reviewed porcine models can be seen in figure 2.7.
Ossabaw pigs proved to be a well-translatable model, developing all the core parameters
of MetS, as well as, most of the associated secondary comorbidities [21].

Since Ossabaw pigs are a miniature pig breed and not one of the larger livestock breeds
is another reason why it is a better animal model compared to other pigs. A miniature pig
breed like the Ossabaw pig requires less food to keep in an obese state. If we compare
the Ossabaw pig to e.g. the Iberian breed, then the Ossabaw pig generally weighs 40 kg
in a lean state while the Iberian pig weighs roughly 200 kg [21].
On the other hand, it is also worth noting that livestock breeds are more affordable and
available while miniature breeds come at an increased cost to purchase [21].
For research in severe obesity, the Ossabaw pig is the go-to animal model, while the
livestock breeds, such as Iberian pigs, might be used instead of Ossabaw pigs in research
on moderate obesity. This is due to the fact that the Iberian pigs exhibit an overall leaner
phenotype and only reach moderate obesity by increasing their body weight by 10-30%.
Ossabaw pigs develop severe obesity and are known to exceed a body weight of 100 kg,
which is more than double their lean weight [21].

Genomic Data
Zhang et al. (2021) [112] has generated a high-quality assembly of the Ossabaw genome
based on blood samples from a female Ossabaw pig from the European Ossabaw Facility
at DTU. The generated genome has a contig N50 (CN50) of around 6.03 Mb which is
significantly higher than that of comparable porcine models such as the Göttingen minipig
(CN50 = 0.021 Mb). This high-quality genome is really important for future research on
the Ossabaw pig since it makes it possible to perform gene-targeted experiments [21,

When compared to the reference genome of the Duroc pig the Ossabaw pig genome
showed many variations. The Duroc pig genome is used as a reference genome be-
cause it is of a very high quality and has been used continuously since 2017 [23].
There were three main types of variations that might promote the ”thrifty” genotype of the
Ossabaw pig: single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), an inversion, and positively se-
lected genes (PSGs) [112].
The study by Zhang et al. (2021) [112] found 24,617 Ossabaw-specific SNPs and 10,688
of these caused changes in amino acids, thereby potentially affecting the function of pro-
tein products of 3,781 genes.
An approximately 2 Mb inversion was found on chromosome 10 of the Ossabaw genome,
compared to the Duroc genome. Such an inversion can lead to pseudogenisation and it
is believed that four genes are pseudogenised by this inversion. Of these four possibly
pseudogenised genes, CUBN and RAB18 are believed to play a role in the thrifty nature
of the Ossabaw pig. CUBN might play a role in the susceptibility of type 2 diabetes and
RAB18 has a central role in controlling adiposity. Hence, a pseudogenisation of these
two genes might give rise to the Ossabaw pigs’ thrifty nature [112].
214 genes were identified as PSGs when comparing the Ossabaw genome to 13 other
porcine breeds. One of the most interesting PSGs is the LEPR gene. The LEPR gene
expresses the LEPR which mediates the biological activity of leptin, as explained in pre-
vious sections. In fact, two sites in the LEPR gene are positively selected in the Ossabaw
pig (see figure 2.8). The first site changes glutamic acid to lysine at amino acid 417, which
is a non-conservative amino acid substitution. The second site has three potential out-
comes: the first outcome is if splicing occurs after amino acid 664 at site GG, leading to
a deletion of amino acid 665, although this will violate the GT-AG rule. The second and
third outcomes do not violate the GT-AG rule but will lead to frameshifts, thus turning the
gene into a pseudogene [112].

It is hypothesised that these mutations in the Ossabaw pig genome might lead to its ”thrifty”

A Comparison to Human LEPR Mutations
38 distinct LEPR mutations have been described in humans and interestingly none of
these have been linked to the intracellular domain of the LEPR [83].
In humans, LEPRb consists of 1165 amino acids [103, 106]. With the first 840 of them
being extracellular, the next 23 trans-membrane and the final 302 being intracellular [83].
Likewise, in pigs (herein also Ossabaw pigs) LEPRb consists of 1165 amino acids. The
first 838 of them are extracellular, the next 23 are trans-membrane and the final 304 amino
acids are intracellular [102].
Since the mutations seen in the Ossabaw pigs’ LEPR are located at amino acids 417 and
665 it can be concluded that these also lie extracellularly, exactly as seen in the human
LEPR mutations [112, 83].

Ethical Considerations
According to the European Animal Research Association’s (EARA) Directive on animal
research, three requirements have to be met before animals can be used in research.
These are: that there is a convincing scientific justification, that the expected benefits of
the research are greater than the potential risks of animal suffering, and finally that the
scientific objectives cannot be achieved using non-animal methods [6].
The experiments performed in this thesis satisfy these requirements and in general, the
work performed on the Ossabaw pigs cannot be achieved using non-animal methods
since the fact that Ossabaw pigs mimic humans in terms of obesity and MetS is the main
reason why we use them for experiments.
The EARA’s Directive also states that the 3R principle has to be implemented. The 3R
principle consists of replacement, reduction, and refinement [6]. Although we cannot re-
place the Ossabaw pig as an animal model, we can ensure that we reduce the amount
of animals used, by improving our understanding of them. An improved understanding of
the Ossabaw pig as an animal model should lead to an improvement in the studies per-
formed on them. Improved studies will probably be more efficient, and sharing samples
between research facilities will keep the number of animals used at a minimum. Addition-
ally, we can refine the practices used in the studies so that the Ossabaw pigs experience
a minimum of pain, suffering, and distress.
Another key ethical consideration for researchers is to use the species least able to ex-
perience pain and suffering [6]. However, pain can be challenging to define and measure
in animals. Regarding the Ossabaw pigs, it is important to note that their obese state
occurs naturally when they are placed in a situation that allows them to gain weight, such
as being on a weight-inducing diet and not moving much. The fact that they can achieve
obesity fairly naturally suggests that they generally do not feel pain or distress by being
in an obese state.
They could however experience pain or distress in some of the conditions that arise when
being in an obese state — the same way as humans might experience pain in a simi-
lar situation but are not generally in pain or distress by being obese. It is however worth
mentioning that taking blood samples, moving, and isolating the Ossabaw pigs can cause
them pain or distress.
Another way to quantify which species are least able to experience pain and suffering is
to use the evolution continuum. The evolution continuum would suggest that it is best to
use rodents, then pigs, and lastly primates [9, 63]. Rodents have been, and are still, used

3.2
RNA Purification
The purification of RNA is the first step in many downstream techniques. The RNA purified
in this thesis will be used to perform RT-qPCR. Obtaining a high-quality and pure RNA
sample is essential to get precise results in downstream techniques such as RT-qPCR
[53].
3.2.1
Liver Tissue
RNA was purified from liver tissue using the miRNeasy Mini Kit (ID: 217004) from Qiagen
[86].
The procedure is described below:
1. Clean the workspace using a 0.5 % NaOH solution and find clean RNase-free equip-
ment.
2. Approximately 30 mg of liver tissue was dissected and transferred to an M-tube
containing 1 mL of QIAzol lysis reagent [12, 89].
3. The tissue was then homogenised on the gentleMACS Octo Dissociator [11] and
left to settle for 5 minutes at room temperature.
4. The homogenised tissue and QIAzol mixture was then transferred to an Eppendorf
tube and 200 µL of chloroform was added and mixed thoroughly using a vortexer.
5. After waiting for 2-3 minutes the Eppendorf tubes were centrifuged at 12,000 g
(14,000 rpm) for 15 min at 4 ◦C.
6. This centrifugation divides the homogenate into 3 phases; an aqueous phase con-
taining RNA, an interphase containing deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), and an organic
phase containing proteins and lipids. From the top aqueous phase, 300 µL was
transferred into a new Eppendorf tube. 450 µL of absolute ethanol was then added
and mixed with the pipette. The ethanol helps with appropriate binding conditions
for all RNA molecules in the sample (from 18 nucleotides and larger) [87].
7. The mixture was then loaded onto an RNeasy Mini Spin Column and centrifuged at
8,000 g (10,000 rpm) for 15 sec at room temperature.
8. Flow-through was discarded and the column was loaded and centrifuged again until
all of the sample had been loaded.
9. 350 µL of RWT buffer was added to the column and centrifuged at 8,000 g (10,000
rpm) for 15 sec at room temperature. Flow-through was discarded. RWT buffer
contains the salt guanidine thiocyanate which facilitates the lysis of cells, inhibits
RNases, and also removes most of the cellular DNA and proteins from the lysate
through organic extraction [87].
10. 78 µL of DNase I incubation mix was pipetted directly onto the column and a 15-
minute wait started.
11. Once the 15 minutes were up 350 µL RWT buffer was added to the column and
centrifuged at 8,000 g (10,000 rpm) for 15 sec at room temperature. Flow-through
was discarded.
12. 500 µL RPE buffer was added to the column and centrifuged at 8,000 g (10,000
rpm) for 15 sec at room temperature. Flow-through was discarded. RPE buffer is
used to remove traces of salts from previous steps [85].
The leptin system of the Ossabaw pig. Leptin receptor expression and interaction with leptin and
alpha-1-acid glycoprotein in lean versus obese Ossabaw pigs
23
==PAGE:33==
13. A second wash using 500 µL RPE buffer was performed while centrifuging at 8,000
g (10,000 rpm) for 120 sec at room temperature. Flow-through was discarded.
14. The column was then transferred into a new collection tube and centrifuged at 8,000
g (10,000 rpm) for 60 sec at room temperature.
15. The now dry column was transferred into a final Eppendorf tube for elution. The RNA
was eluted from the column by adding 50 µL of RNase-free water and centrifuging
at 8,000 g (10,000 rpm) for 60 sec at room temperature.
16. The column was discarded and the eluate was tested for concentration, A260/280,
and A260/230 ratios on the NanoDrop ND-1000 Spectrophotometer before being
placed at -80 ◦C for storage.

Adipose Tissue
RNA was purified from adipose tissue, both SAT and VAT, using the miRNeasy Mini Kit
(ID: 217004) from Qiagen [86].
The procedure is described below:
1. Clean the workspace using a 0.5 % NaOH solution and find clean RNase-free equip-

2. Approximately 300 mg of adipose tissue was dissected and transferred to an M-tube

3.3
NanoDrop
To check the purity of the RNA samples, the concentration, A260/280, and A260/230
ratios were measured on the NanoDrop ND-1000 Spectrophotometer using the RNA-40
sample type setting.
The NanoDrop only requires 1 µL of sample [49] which is an important feature since
the purified RNA usually is used for further downstream applications and therefore using
as little as possible is crucial so there is more sample for the rest of the downstream
applications.
The NanoDrop works by pipetting a 1 µL sample onto the end of a fibre-optic cable, called
the receiving fibre. A second fibre-optic cable, called the source fibre, is located on an arm
that is brought down into contact with the sample. Due to surface tension, the sample is
stretched out and held in place between the two fibre-optic cable ends. The gap between
the two fibre-optic cable ends is controlled by the machine to both 1 mm and 0.2 mm
The leptin system of the Ossabaw pig. Leptin receptor expression and interaction with leptin and
alpha-1-acid glycoprotein in lean versus obese Ossabaw pigs
27
==PAGE:37==
paths. The light source is a pulsed xenon flash lamp and the spectrometer utilises a
linear charge-coupled device array to analyse the light after it has passed through the
sample [47].
To calculate the concentrations the NanoDrop software uses the Lambert-Beer law:
A = E ∗b ∗c
Isolating for concentration it is:
c =
A
E ∗b
Where A is the absorbance (in absorbance units), E is the wavelength-dependent molar
absorptivity coefficient (in litre/mol-cm), b is the path length (in cm), and c is the analyte
concentration (in moles/litre or molarity) [47].
The absorption is determined by measuring how much light reaches the detector in rela-
tion to the amount of light sent through the sample. Based on the fact that nucleic acids
absorb light at 260 nm, generating a characteristic peak, the NanoDrop can then deter-
mine the concentration and purity of an RNA sample [50].
3.3.1
A260/280 and A260/230 Ratio
Understanding the purity of RNA samples is important before beginning any downstream
applications, which are expensive and sensitive to the purity of the sample. Using the
ratios of the measured spectrophotometric absorbance values at 230, 260, and 280 nm
is a useful way of determining the purity of RNA samples [26].
The A260/280 ratio is one of the ratios used to determine the purity of RNA samples.
This ratio is mostly used to determine if there are protein contaminants in the sample.
A260/280 ratios should be between 1.9 and 2.2 with 2.0 being the ideal value [26].
Another highly used ratio is the A260/230 ratio. This ratio is used to determine if there are
organic compound contaminants such as guanidine thiocyanate. A260/230 ratios should
be between 2.0 and 2.2 [26].
3.4
Bioanalyzer
Once the RNA has been purified from the biological samples and its purity determined, it
is time to look at its quality.
It is important to look at the quality of the RNA since it can be rapidly digested by RNase
contamination from surfaces and solutions in the lab. If the RNA is digested it will present
itself as shorter fragments in the sample, which can potentially compromise the results of
downstream applications [96]. The concentration measurement done by the NanoDrop
does not distinguish between smaller or larger RNA fragments and therefore it is neces-
sary to test for RNA integrity using the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer system.
The Bioanalyzer system is an automated microfluidics-based electrophoresis tool which
is used for sample quality control of biomolecules, including RNA [4, 2]. For the determi-
nation of RNA quality, the Bioanalyzer RNA 6000 Nano assay was used. Samples (up to
12) are loaded on an RNA Nano chip as well as a ladder, dye, and marker. See appendix
B for the Bioanalyzer SOP.
The Bioanalyzer system works by moving the samples individually through microchan-
nels and injecting them into the separation channel. Once in the separation channel, the
sample components are electrophoretically separated. The separated components pass
by a laser which detects the fluorescence. The detected fluorescence correlates with
the amount of RNA of a given size and is translated into electropherograms and gel-like
images that can be used to determine the quality of the RNA [5, 68, 96].

3.4.1
RIN Values
As explained previously the Bioanalyzer system provides the user with a RIN value that
is used to determine whether the RNA is intact or degraded. The RIN value scale goes
from 1 to 10. With 1 being totally degraded RNA and 10 being intact RNA [96].
The RIN algorithm takes the entire electrophoretic trace into account and hence provides
accurate and reproducible results without the need for human interpretation, which is often
flawed [68].

figure 3.2.

28s/18s Ratio and Other Considerations
Simple models assessing the 28s/18s ratio have historically been used to determine RNA
quality [96]. However, this assessment was often done visually on agarose gels. The ri-
bosomal RNA (rRNA) bands are affected by the conditions of the electrophoresis, amount
of RNA loaded, and saturation of the fluorescent [78]. These factors make the visual as-

alpha-1-acid glycoprotein in lean versus obese Ossabaw pigs
29
==PAGE:39==
sessment subjective as well as the fact that researchers might assess bands differently.
According to studies performed by Schroeder et al. (2006) [96] and Mueller et al. (2016)
[68] the RIN value is better correlated with the results of downstream applications com-
pared to the 28s/18s ratio, which only showed weak correlation with RNA integrity.
It is also important to note that even though the RIN value is more accurate than the
28s/18s ratio it might not be accurate for downstream applications such as RT-qPCR that
generate small amplicons. RT-qPCR is very sensitive and the RIN value does not provide
any information on where the RNA might have degraded, so it is possible that the target
site might be degraded while the rest of the RNA is intact — giving a high RIN-value but
poor RT-qPCR results [25].
Additionally, the nature of the RNA molecule being sensitive to temperature, UV light, and
various chemicals means that it might further degrade during downstream applications
and the RIN value is therefore only a snapshot of the RNA integrity at the time of mea-
surement [99].
Also, messenger RNA (mRNA) only makes up 1-3% of total RNA samples and it is not
readily detectable due to its low concentration. rRNA is however readily detectable since
it makes up over 80% of total RNA samples. So determining mRNA quality with the Bio-
analyzer relies on the assumption that the rRNA quality reflects the mRNA quality [78].
3.5
RT-qPCR
RT-qPCR is a method used when RNA is the starting material. It begins with RNA being
reverse-transcribed into cDNA. The cDNA is used as the template for the quantitative
polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) part of RT-qPCR [98].
RT-qPCR is, in this case, used for targeted gene expression analysis and will be run on
the Rotor-Gene Q [91].
3.5.1
Primer Design
The design of the primers is based on a protocol designed by Louise Brogaard from DTU
and the research performed by Zhang et al. (2021) [112].
Zhang et al. (2021) [112] found changes in the Ossabaw LEPR gene at the two amino
acid (AA) sites: AA417 and AA665. Therefore, two sets of primers are designed; one that
covers the site of AA417 and one that covers the site of AA665. To design these primers
the Ossabaw LEPR gene sequence is needed.
The full Ossabaw genome can be found here [34], and based on this my supervisor Peter
M. H. Heegaard provided the full Ossabaw LEPR gene sequence, including introns. All
the exons from this sequence were copied into a separate document and the exon-only
sequence was then copied into Primer3web (v.0.4.0) as the source sequence. Additionally
in Primer3web the product size range was set to 150-200 nucleotides, the primer melting
temperature (Tm) was set to a minimum: 59 ◦C, an optimal: 60 ◦C, and a maximum: 61
◦C. Finally, the table of thermodynamic parameters was set to Breslauer et al. 1986 [14].
AA417 and AA665 were then located in the Ossabaw LEPR gene sequence and marked
so suitable forward primers could be found that covered the AA sites.
The forward primer for AA417 was selected so it covered the site of AA417, had a Tm
within the given criteria (60 ◦C ± 1 ◦C) and a sufficient GC%. The primer sequence was
determined through trial and error in Primer3web. The forward primer for AA417 does
not span the exon-intron boundary and is therefore not over-intron but the reverse primer
found by Primer3web is located in a different exon and thereby an intron is spanned. When
designing primers for qPCR it is important that the primers span an exon-exon junction.
Designing primers this way will reduce the risk of false positives from genomic DNA since
the genomic DNA will not be amplified due to its introns.

3.5.2
cDNA Synthesis
The synthesis of cDNA is the first step in the RT-qPCR workflow [98]. The cDNA synthesis
was performed using the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit from Qiagen [90].
The procedure is described below:
1. Clean the workspace with Microsol and ensure that the equipment is RNase-free.
2. Thaw the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit and RNA samples.
3. Prepare 0.2 mL tubes and mark them with a dedicated cDNA number.
4. 300 ng of RNA is needed per synthesis — so based on the concentration of the RNA
solution the necessary volumes are now calculated.
5. A reverse transcriptase master mix is prepared for all the samples, excluding the
-reverse transcriptase (-RT) controls. For 1 reaction, mix: 0.75 µL reverse tran-
scriptase, 1 µL RT Primer Mix, 4 µL RT Buffer, and 0.25 µL RNase-free water.
6. For the -RT controls the following mix is made, for 1 reaction: 1 µL RT Primer Mix,
4 µL RT Buffer, and 1 µL RNase-free water.
7. 2 µL of gDNA Wipeout Buffer is added to each 0.2 mL tube, followed by the calcu-
lated amount of RNA solution. Then RNase-free water is added to achieve a total
volume of 14 µL.
8. The tubes are placed in a thermocycler and incubated for 2 minutes at 42 ◦C and
then placed on ice.
9. 6 µL of the reverse transcriptase master mix is added to each tube — except for the
-RT controls, they get 6 µL of the -RT mix added. The components are mixed gently
with the pipette.
10. The tubes go back in the thermocycler and are incubated for 15 minutes at 42 ◦C,
followed by 3 minutes at 95 ◦C.
11. The cDNA is now synthesised and can be stored at -20 ◦C.

3.5.4
Rotor-Gene Data Analysis
Two main data plots are generated in qPCR, the amplification plot and the melt curve.
From which, respectively, the threshold cycle (Ct) and Tm can be determined [10].
Generic examples of such plots can be seen in ”b” and ”c” in figure 3.4.
Figure 3.4: How data is generated by the Rotor-Gene [10].
a: fluorescence only shows up when the dye SYBR Green I is intercalated with double-
stranded DNA (dsDNA).
b: how the cycle number is determined (Ct is also known as Cq).
c: how the melting temperature is determined.
It is the measure of fluorescence that makes it possible to generate the data points of
the plots. In the experiments performed in this thesis, it is the dye SYBR Green I that
intercalates with dsDNA [65] (see ”a” in figure 3.4). When free in solution, SYBR Green I
only emits a baseline fluorescence, but when it intercalates with dsDNA the fluorescence
is greatly increased [10].
The fluorescence is measured during the 40 cycles that take place at the beginning of the
Rotor-Gene run. In the beginning, the fluorescence is below the background noise but as
the product accumulates the fluorescence will increase and pass the set threshold level.
The cycle number where it passes the threshold is called the threshold cycle or Ct. The Ct
value is proportional to the initial target concentration and it is therefore used to compare
different samples [10].
The Rotor-Gene run finishes off by analysing the melt curves. For the melt curves, it is

alpha-1-acid glycoprotein in lean versus obese Ossabaw pigs
34
==PAGE:44==
the reduction of fluorescence that is measured. The strong fluorescence seen at the end
of the 40 cycles is reduced when the dsDNA, with its intercalated dye, is denatured due to
the increasingly high temperatures of the melt curve analysis run. Given that the sample
only contains one specific product, the intensity of fluorescence will remain steady until the
temperature approaches this product’s melting point. When the temperature approaches
this point the fluorescence will decrease to a baseline level. The temperature where the
fluorescence has decreased by 50% is the product’s melting temperature. The melt curve
is often transformed using the first-order derivative of the fluorescence intensity over the
temperature change, giving a so-called melt peak where the top of the peak is located at
the Tm [10]. Once obtained the Tm can be compared to historic experiments where the
same primers were used.
For new primers, cDNA that has worked historically will give an indication of where the
Tm is, as well as the temperature where the majority of the samples melt — given that
the RT-qPCR was run effectively.

PAGE is a technique used to separate proteins based on their size and charge [66]. Two of
the most common PAGE forms are the sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) and Native variants
— these two techniques are used in this thesis.
In general, samples are loaded onto a polyacrylamide gel and exposed to an electrical
current which makes the proteins migrate towards the anode since they are negatively
charged. The larger proteins will migrate slower through the gel while smaller proteins
will migrate faster, thus separating them. The general setup for a PAGE can be seen in
figure 3.5.

3.6.1
SDS-PAGE
SDS wraps around the proteins’ polypeptide backbone and together with heat exposure
denatures the proteins. SDS is anionic, and since, in the majority of cases, SDS binds to
proteins in a ratio of 1.4 g of SDS to 1 g of protein, it overrules any charge of the protein
and creates a uniformly negatively charged molecule. This means that the protein-SDS
molecule will strictly migrate according to its mass [76].
The procedure for SDS-PAGE is described below:
1. Prepare samples and buffers.
2. Open the NuPAGE-SDS 10% gel bag and rinse the gel cassette with Milli-Q water.
3. Remove the comb and gently rinse the wells with Milli-Q water.
4. Remove the adhesive tape on the bottom of the gel cassette.
5. Place the gel cassette in the electrophoresis container and lock it in place.
6. Fill the inner and outer chambers with a running buffer.
7. Add 10 µL of the weight marker (WM) and 20 µL of the samples into the dedicated
wells according to your sample setup overview.
8. Put on the lid and connect the electrophoresis container to a power supply.
9. Turn on the power supply and run the gel at 200V for roughly 40-60 minutes.
10. Once finished: turn off the power supply, remove the gel cassette, open the gel
cassette, remove the gel, and proceed to immunoblot — see section 3.6.3.
3.6.2
Native PAGE
The main difference between the Native PAGE and the SDS-PAGE is the fact that the
proteins are not denatured in the Native PAGE — the proteins are in their native confor-
mation. This is important since interactions between proteins can be retained, which is
not always possible when denaturing them [76].
In Native PAGE the proteins are separated based both on their mass and charge. The
gel allows small proteins to migrate faster while large proteins encounter more resistance
and migrate slower. Additionally, proteins have different negative charge densities in the
alkaline buffers used in Native PAGE, and this also affects how well the proteins migrate
through the gel [76].
The procedure for Native PAGE is described below:
1. Prepare samples and buffers.
2. Open the 4-16% NativePAGE Bis-Tris gel bag and rinse the gel cassette with Milli-Q
water.
3. Remove the comb and gently rinse the wells with the cathode buffer.
4. Remove the adhesive tape on the bottom of the gel cassette.
5. Place the gel cassette in the electrophoresis container and lock it in place.
6. Fill the inner chamber with cathode buffer and the outer chamber with anode buffer.

The relative expressions, ∆Ct, are calculated using this equation:

Using the relative expressions, the lowest expressed sample (highest Ct) will have a value

how much the gene that the primer targets is expressed relative to other samples.
The relative expressions are calculated for all three primers and whenever possible the
mean of the technical duplicates’ Ct value was used for the calculations.

The primer targetting AA417 is designed to target the mutated site where glutamic acid
has mutated to lysine [112], as seen in figure 2.8. And the primer targetting AA665 is
designed to target outcome 1 of the potential splice outcomes seen in figure 2.8, leading
to a deletion of AA665.
The fact that MM_417 and MM_665 show expression, and that they are designed to be
mutation specific, indicates that the mutations described by Zhang et al. (2021) [112] have
been proven in this experiment. Given that the data is reliable.
Another condition that has to be true in order for the above to be correct, is that the primers
are so specific that they do not bind to the target sequence if the mutations do not occur.
It is quite likely that MM_417 will still bind since the mutation is only 1 base changing from
A to G, but for MM_665 a different splice outcome will change roughly half of the primer
and it would most likely not bind to the target sequence. In this argument it is also worth
noting that the PBMCs were from Duroc pigs who do not have the mutations seen in the

5
Conclusion
The overall aim of the thesis was to map the expression of LEPR in adipose and liver
tissue, as well as in whole blood and PBMCs, from lean and obese Ossabaw pigs. This
goal was not achieved, however many small achievements were made that will make it
easier to investigate the expression of LEPR in the future.
Successful RNA extractions were made from five different biological sample groups: liver,
SAT, VAT, blood, and PBMCs. For all five groups, high-quality RNA with reasonable yields
was extracted. Liver tissue (harvested from biopsies) and blood gave the best results.
While an updated protocol for the extraction of RNA from PBMCs showed that freshly
harvested PBMCs with a cell count of at least 1*106 gave the best purities and highest
yields.
The best of these RNA samples were successfully synthesised into cDNA that was used
on the Rotor-Gene Q for gene expression analysis. During these analyses, many unpre-
dicted issues occurred, but a successful experiment was achieved using undiluted cDNA.
The final experiment suggests that LEPR is in fact expressed in various Ossabaw pig tis-
sues, which has not been proved before. Additionally, the success of the final experiment
also proves that both the newly synthesised cDNA and the new primers targetting AA417
and AA665 of the Ossabaw LEPR are ready to use in future studies.
The PAGE studies were also troublesome but showed that setups using anti-leptin in
the immunoblot might generate false positive results because of an unspecific binding
between LEPR and anti-leptin. Poor results from the weight markers used in the Native
PAGE also indicate that this is something to be cautious about in future setups.
The overall conclusion of this thesis is therefore that although it has not been possible to
give any significant results on gene expression, the road has now been paved for future
studies using the high-quality RNA purified in this thesis, as well as the functioning primers.
5.1
Future experiments
Validating the Rotor-Gene data presented in section 4.2 would be a good place to start
for future experiments. Most of the experiments performed led up to the point where
consistent results were reached, but there was not enough time to validate them.
Based on this validation new conclusions on the experiments can be made and potentially
interesting results can be further investigated.
Purifying RNA from more biological samples will also be important going forward. Espe-
cially, from the blood of lean Ossabaw pigs and from PBMCs of lean and obese Ossabaw
pigs. This way the expression of genes can be compared in blood and PBMCs between
lean and obese Ossabaw pigs, which will prove important for understanding which genes
are expressed in the immune system.
Additional stimulation experiments of PBMCs and how this changes the gene expression
would also be very interesting to investigate.
Finally, further testing the Native PAGE system with different weight markers will be im-
portant for the use of this method in the future. Once a suitable weight marker has been
found it will also be interesting to further check for leptin binding in other adipose tissue
samples.

SDS-PAGE, Native-PAGE, and Immunoblotting 
 
SDS-PAGE 
 
Sample preparation 
Dilute samples in PBS – aim for 100 µg/mL or OD280 of 0.1. 
For the LEPR used no dilution is needed since it is already 100 µg/mL. 
VAT18 dilute 200 times. Make 200 µl. Take 1 µL VAT18 sample and add 199 µL PBS. 
 
Before electrophoresis all samples are pretreated with reducing sample buffer: 
(total sample volume: 60 µl) 39 µl sample + 15 µl sample buffer + 6 µl reducing agent. 
Boil for 5 minutes or place in 100° heating block for 5 minutes. 
 
Preparation and running of NuPAGE-SDS gel (10%) 
Running buffer: prepare 1L MES  
Dilute MES 20X in H2O Milli-Q, i.e. 
1L running buffer: 50 ml MES + H2O ad 1L. 
 
1) Open the gel bag. 
2) Rinse the gel cassette with H2O Milli-Q. 
3) Remove the comb by carefully pulling it upwards. 
4) Rinse the wells carefully with H2O Milli-Q water. 
Use a disposable Pasteur pipette.  
5) Remove the adhesive tape, which is placed at the 
bottom on the outside, from the cassette. 
6) Place the gel cassette in the electrophoresis 
container, so that the inside of the gel cassette faces the middle of the container. In case only one gel is 
run, place a ’dummy’ in the other gel place. 
7) Fix the gel cassettes with the lock in the container. 
8) Fill the inner chamber with running buffer, so that the buffer covers the wells in the gels. If there is a 
leakage in the container, open and close the lock and re-fill the inner chamber with buffer. Leakage 
between the inner and outer chambers is not acceptable. 
9) Fill the outer chamber with running buffer. 
10) Apply the molecular weight marker (10µl/well) and the samples (20µl/well) in the wells, using long 
pipette tips – according to the overview. 
11) Put on the lid. 
12) Connect the gel apparatus to the power supply, ensuring anode is at the bottom of the gel. 
13) Turn on the power and run the gels at 200V for approximately 40-60 min. making sure that samples do 
not run longer than to 0.5 cm from the bottom of the gel (or until it reaches the “lip”) 
 
When the run is finished, turn off the power supply, open the gel apparatus and remove the gel cassette 
from the container. 
Detach the two cassette plates using the gel knife. Put in the knife between the two plates and break the 
plates gently apart. Use the sharp edge of the knife to cut off the lip. The gel needs to be transferred 
directly into an adequately sized container. Make sure the gel is not folded onto itself. 
Proceed to immunoblot.

Native-PAGE 
Sample preparation 
Dilute samples to around 100 μg/mL. Mix the samples (1:4) with NativePAGE Sample Buffer (4X). One 
sample in well consists of: 15 μL sample and 5 μL Sample Buffer. 
 
Preparation and running of Native-PAGE gel 
1. Prepare sample dilutions.  
2. Prepare Cathode- and Anode Buffer.  
3. Open gel bag (4-16% NativePAGE Bis-Tris gel) and drain away the packaging buffer.  
4. Rinse the gel cassette with Milli-Q water using a Pasteur pipette.  
5. Peel the tape off the bottom of the cassette.  
6. Carefully remove the comb from the top of the gel. 
7. Rinse the sample wells with cathode buffer using a Pasteur pipette. Invert the gel and shake 
out the buffer. Repeat 2 times. In the end fill the wells with cathode buffer. Be sure there are 
no bubbles left in the wells.  
8. Insert the cassette into the tank. Make sure the lower side of the cassette is facing the inner 
chamber. 
9. Fill the Cathode chamber a little bit to prevent the gel from drying out (e.g. 3 cm).  
10. Add the molecular weight marker (5µl/well) and the samples (20µl/well) in the wells, using 
long pipette tips – according to the overview.  
11. Fill the Cathode chamber (inner chamber) with the Cathode Buffer. Fill the Anode chamber 
(outer chamber) with Anode Buffer.  
12. Run the electrophoresis at room temperature 150V for 105-120 min.  
When the run is finished, turn off the power supply, open the gel apparatus and remove the gel 
cassette from the container. 
Detach the two cassette plates using the gel knife. Put in the knife between the two plates and 
break the plates gently apart. Use the sharp edge of the knife to cut off the lip. The gel needs to 
be transferred directly into an adequately sized container. Make sure the gel is not folded onto 
itself. 
Proceed to immunoblot.

Immunoblot 
 
While the SDS- and Native-PAGE gel is running, prepare the following for: 
Pre-wet 2 fiber pads and 2 x 4 pieces of filter paper in transfer buffer for each gel to be blotted (use the lid 
of a plastic box) 
Cut out a piece of PVDF membrane (“Immobilon”), 7 x 8 cm, and place in a large petri dish – again one for 
each gel. Due to the hydrophobicity of PVDF this type of membrane should be “wetted” before use by 
placing it in 70% ethanol before transferring it to transfer buffer (point 6 below) 
 
Assembly (see figure below as well) 
1. Place one sponge on the BLACK side of the blotting cassette 
2. Remove air bubbles from a stack of 4 filter papers using a rolling pin 
3. Place the 4 filter papers on the sponge 
4. Place the SDS-PAGE/Native-PAGE gel on the filter paper 
5. Add a few drops of transfer buffer on the gel 
6. Wet the PVDF membrane (“Immobilon”) with transfer buffer in the petri dish and then place it 
carefully on the gel (NB: it should be pre-wetted in 70% ethanol before this step (see above). 
7. Remove air bubbles from the remaining 4 filter papers using a rolling pin 
8. Place the 4 filter papers on the membrane 
9. Carefully use the rolling pin on the filter papers 
10. Place the remaining sponge on top 
11. Close the blotting cassette and place it in the blotting chamber (white side facing the red half of the 
chamber, black side facing the black half of the chamber) 
12. Put in an ice block 
13. Add transfer buffer up to the edge
