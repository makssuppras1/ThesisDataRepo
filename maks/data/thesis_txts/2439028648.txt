ABSTRACT 
Using biomass gasification to make producer gas is one of the promising sustainable energy options 
available for small scale and rural applications for off-grid technology.  Tar content in producer gas is 
the main problem if it is used directly as a fuel for an engine. A low-tar biomass (LTB) gasifier of 
approximately 30 kW capacity has been developed to solve this. Moving bed gasifier with internal 
recirculation of pyrolysis gas has been the basic principle of the LTB gasifier. The gasifier focuses on 
the concept of mixing the pyrolysis gases with gasifying air and burning the mixture in separate 
combustion chamber. Five tests were carried out with the use of wood pellets and wood chips 
separately, with moisture content of 9-34%. The LTB gasifier offers excellent opportunities for 
handling extremely low-tar in the producer gas. 
The gasifiers producer gas had an extremely low tar content of 21.2 mg/Nm3(avg.) and an average 
lower heating value (LHV) of 4.69 MJ/Nm3. Tar content found in different tests in the ranges of 10.6-
29.8 mg/Nm3. This low tar content makes the producer gas suitable for direct use in internal 
combustion engines, without further tar removal. Using mass and energy balances, the average 
gasifier capacity and cold gas efficiency (CGE) observed 23.1 kW and 82.7% for wood chips, and 33.1 
kW and 60.5% for wood pellets, respectively. Average heat loss in term of higher heating value (HHV) 
observed 3.2% of thermal input for wood chips and 1% for wood pellets, where heat loss was found 
1% of thermal input in term of enthalpy for wood chips. Thus, the LTB gasifier performs better 
compared to typical gasifiers in term of heat loss. Approximately 1.3% of the input energy is lost 
through char-ash generation for wood chips. This char-ash energy can be used as a soil conditioner. 
Equivalence ratio (ER) in the range of 0.29 to 0.41 gives better performance in terms of heating value 
and CGE. The specific gas production yields at the above ER range were in the range of 2.1-3.2 Nm3/kg. 
Gasifier performance with ER of 0.27 and 0.29 were almost the same. Heating value and CGE changes 
proportionally with the producer gas yield. The average gas compositions (H2-19%, CO-19%, CO2-10%, 
CH4-0.7% and N2-51%) obtained for wood chips are higher than the typical producer gas composition. 
Again, the temperature profile of the LTB gasifier observed relatively low temperature compared to 
typical moving bed gasifier. The average partial oxidation zone temperature of 970°C observed for 
wood chips. The use of separate combustor in the partial oxidation zone substantially lowers the bed 
temperature to 750°C. 
The LTB Gasifier was operated with the engine-generator system for over 300 minutes in last two 
tests. During these tests the engine was started and operated completely with the producer gas. The 
engine operated well on the produced gas, and no deposits were observed in the engine afterwards. 
Part of the producer gas flow was used for engine operation and corresponding electrical power was 
found to be 1.5 kW continuously, and maximum power of 2.5 kW was also observed, while maximum 
generator capacity is 3 kW.  
A thermodynamic equilibrium model correctly predicts the equilibrium bed temperature, gas 
composition, LHV of the producer gas and ER with the experimental data, when the heat loss of 4% of 
the energy input is considered. The predicted equilibrium bed temperature of 650°C is only 100°C 
lower than the experimental bed temperature of 750°C, the bed temperature from the experiment is 
qualitatively in good agreement with the model bed temperature. Thus, the thermodynamic 
equilibrium model is good agreement with the experimental results.  
Therefore, being easy to build with low cost materials, this gasifier is an excellent technology for 
thermal and power applications, especially in rural applications.

Danish Abstract 
Brug af biomasseforgasning til at producere produktgas til varme- og/eller elproduktion er en af de 
mest lovende, bæredygtige og tilgængelige løsninger for små-skala decentrale og isolerede anlæg i 
udviklingslande. Tjære i produktgassen er typiske det primære problem hvis gassen skal anvendes i en 
forbrændingsmotor. En lav-tjære biomasseforgasser (LTB) på ca. 30 kW kapacitet er blevet udviklet 
for at løse denne problemstilling. Medstrømsforgasning med intern recirkulering af pyrolysegas er det 
bærende princip for LTB-systemet. Forgasserens koncept er centreret omkring opblanding af luft og 
pyrolysegas og efterfølgende forbrænding af blandingen i et separat brændkammer inde i reaktoren. 
I dette projekt blev fem tests blev udført med træpiller og –flis med fugtindhold på 9-34%. LTB viser 
sig at være meget egnet til at producere en gas med yderst lavt tjærekoncentration. 
Via masse- og energibalancer er produktgassens gennemsnitlige nedre brændværdi (LHV) beregnet til 
4.69 MJ/Nm3, med en ekstrem lav tjærekoncentration på 21.2 mg/Nm3.  Denne koncentration gør 
gassen velegnet til direkte brug i forbrændingsmotorer uden yderligere gasrens. Den gennemsnitlige 
forgasserkapacitet og koldgasvirkningsgrad (CGE) er fundet til 23.1 kW og 82.7% hhv. for træflis og 
33.1 kW og 60.5% hhv. for træpiller. Varmetabet ift. indfødt på øvre brændværdi (HHV) basis er fundet 
til 3.2% og 1% for træflis og –piller hhv., og den tilsvarende værdi af varmetabet ift. enthalpi er 1% for 
træflis. Det ses herved at LTB-forgasseren er markant bedre end typiske modstrømsanlæg på denne 
paramter. Omkring 1.3% af energien i det indfødte brændsel tabes som kulstof i asken. Denne aske 
kan bruges som jordforbedringsmiddel eller som koks. 
Systemets optimale drift i forhold til gassens brændværdi og CGE findes ved Equivalence ratio (ER) 
0.29-0.41. Gasproduktionen ved disse ER er 2.1-3.2 Nm3/kg. Forgasserens driftstilstand ved ER 0.27 og 
0.29 ses at være tilnærmelsesvis ens. Brændværdi og CGE ses at ændre sig proportionalt med 
gasproduktionen. Den gennemsnitlige gassammensætning (H2-19%, CO-19%, CO2-10%, CH4-0.7% and 
N2-51%) for træflis er mere energirig end typiske gassammensætninger i sammenligning. 
Temperaturprofilen for LTB-forgasseren viste sig at være relativt lav i forhold til typiske 
medstrømsforgassere. Den gennemsnitlige partiel oxidations temperatur var 970°C for træflis. Brugen 
af et separat brændkammer til den partielle oxidation sænkede koksbedtemperaturen betragteligt til 
750°C.  
LTB-forgasseren blev koblet til en motor i mere end 300min over to tests. Disse tests inkluderede start 
af forgasser og drift med produktgas. Driften var succesfuld og motorens performance var som 
forventet. Motoren fungerede efter hensigten med gassen og der blev ikke fundet nogle aflejringer 
ved efterfølgende undersøgelse. Kun en del af produktgassen blev brugt til motordrift og den 
elproduktionen var 1.5 kW nominel og 2.5 kW maksimum. Motorens maksimale ydeevne fra 
producenten er 3 kW. 
En termodynamisk ligevægtsmodel, der ved 4% varmetab matcher de eksperimentelle værdier for 
koksbeddens/gassens ligevægtstemperatur, gassammensætningen, gassen LHV og systemets ER er 
opstillet. Den modellerede bedtemperatur er 650°C, kun 100°C lavere end den eksperimentelt 
observerede værdi. Modellens parametre sammenlignet med de eksperimentelle er tilfredsstillende. 
Da forgasseren er relativt nem at konstruere, gør brug af billige materialer og er relativ i simpel i drift 
ses den som en effektiv løsning og mulighed for varme- og/eller elproduktion i små, decentrale anlæg 
der ikke er forbundet til et eksisterende netværk.

CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background 
Because of environmental safety, reducing level of oil and coal fuels and arising issue of climate 
changes, there is a need to replace fossil-based fuels to sustainable energy. It continues to play an 
important role to power applications due to the awareness and concern about energy dependency 
and global change to ensure the clean energy sources. Therefore, future-proofing sustainable energy 
is an essential component in safeguarding the welfare society of the future. For this, the entire worlds 
energy system is transitioning from fossil to renewable energy, which is a huge technological challenge 
requiring new technological solutions. This needs to install new decentralized energy plant around the 
world where electricity is not available from national electric grid systems.  
Among all renewable energy sources, biomass is the main source of energy that used significantly for 
a large number of small, rural, and cottage industries along with the majority of rural households 
where electricity is not available. Biomass gasification is an affordable promising renewable energy 
system for supplying thermal energy and generating electric power. Less than 22% of primary energy 
demand mix is currently met by biomass or biomass derived fuels [1]. Heat and electricity are two 
forms of primary energy derived from biomass [1]. In the area of low population densities and under 
difficult to reach geographical conditions, it is not economically feasible to supply national electricity 
grid to ensure electricity among the entire areas. Therefore, the energy requirement of these un-
served remote areas has to be met by means of off-grid technologies. 
Among off-grid renewable technologies, it is cheap and affordable to use biomass energy for 
standalone power generation in remote areas where the national electricity grid is not available. To 
support these isolated rural areas with power, it is necessary to replace the use of traditional oil 
powered systems, and inefficient traditional use of biomass by an efficient de-centralized source of 
biomass energy as a sustainable green fuel at an affordable cost.  
Use of biomass energy is transformed into producer gas that consists of CO, CO2, H2, CH4 and N2 by 
the gasification process. By making the producer gas tar free, it can be used directly in an engine 
generator system to produce electricity and heat. However, tar content of producer gas is the main 
problem if it is to be used directly in an engine. Unlike heat applications, power generation demands 
low tar producer gas, as downdraft gasifier generally produces low particulate matters and producer 
gas with low tar content, specially co-current moving bed gasifiers (thrustless), it is preferred for 
producer gas generation for power applications. [2][3].  
Therefore, the challenge has been in establishing a gasification technology package using CO2 neutral 
biomass as a fuel towards generating producer gas of desired quality that can be piped directly to an 
internal combustion engine to replace oil-based fuel to a significant extent.  
1.2 Previous Study 
A moving bed gasifier with an internal recirculation and separate combustion of pyrolysis gas offers 
excellent opportunities for solving the problems met in scaling-up the conventional co-current 
downdraft gasifier [2]. In a study by Susanto and Beenackers [2], the following results were obtained. 
The recirculation systems greatly reduce the tar content of the producer gas, to below 100 mg/Nm3 
[2]. The tar content decreases with increasing recycle ratio to 0.6 (ratio of recirculation gas to gasifying

air, v/v at s.t.p). At recycle ratios greater than 0.6, no further tar reduction was observed. Figure 1.1 
shows the schematic view of modified co-current moving bed gasifier.

The use of separate combustor lowers the highest temperature of the bed to 973 K, compared with

Again, some researchers suggested that the tar content should certainly be less than 50 mg/Nm3 to 
guarantee lasting performance of the complete gasification system including an engine generator. This 
requirement can be fulfilled in the down-draft co-current gasifier if properly designed [4]. So, there is 
a scope to investigate and reduce tar content level lower than that above-mentioned range. 
1.3 Research Scope 
This master thesis is based on the operation of a small scale (30 kW) low-tar biomass (LTB) gasifier 
with internal recirculation of pyrolysis gases. The LTB gasifier was built at the DTU Risø campus based 
on the original design developed by Susanto [2]. The modified co-current moving bed gasifier 
developed by Susanto works on the principle of a moving (downdraft) bed gasifier with internal 
recirculation of pyrolysis gas and separate combustion of pyrolysis gas in the oxidation zone. The 
gasifier has a multiple air nozzle or a ring type of oxidation zone while maintaining low tar content in 
producer gas.  
The LTB gasifier focuses on the concept of mixing the tar loaded pyrolysis gases with the gasifying air 
and burning the mixture in an internally separate combustor instead of multiple nozzle type oxidation 
zone to achieve a low-tar content in the producer gas.  
==PAGE:13==
3 
 
This master thesis project is carried out to study the LTB gasifier performance with the aim of low tar 
content in the producer gas by commissioning, testing and evaluation. Again, this study is aimed to 
identify characterization of the gasifier parameters and to optimize the performance parameters of 
the LTB gasifier. Furthermore, modeling of the gasifier operation is done using an equilibrium-based 
model and this model is validated with the experiment data.  
1.4 Objective 
The objective of this master thesis is to commission, test, evaluate and optimize the low-tar biomass 
gasifier for rural applications. The more specific objectives are the following:  
1. To optimize/test of the nozzle design for proper recirculation and mixing of the pyrolysis gas. 
2. To investigate and evaluate the influence of the pyrolysis gas recirculation in the operation of the 
gasifier. 
3. To test the gasifier’s performance with wood pellets and wood chips and identify characterization 
of the gasifier parameters in terms of mass and energy balances, gasifier capacity, energy efficiency, 
producer gas composition and tar content. 
4. To optimize the operation parameters of the gasifier. 
5. To test the engine performance with the gasifier producer gas.

CHAPTER 2: REVIEW OF LITERATURE 
2.1 Biomass 
Various gasifier fuels are commonly used for gasification process, namely wood pellets, wood chips, 
wood residues, municipal waste, manures, charcoal, agricultural residues and agricultural wastes. 
Chemical, physical, thermal and morphological property of these fuels demand different gasification 
technologies or gasifier designs in order to smooth functioning the system.  
Among these gasifier fuels biomass is used increasingly now-a-days. The share of biomass in meeting 
current worlds primary energy mix is at a modest level of 10% [5], but given the rising concern about 
global warming and sustainability, this percentage is very likely to rise. Thermal design of biomass 
utilization system needs to know the composition of the biomass as well as its energy content. In this 
aspect two types of compositions are mostly identified: proximate and ultimate analysis. 
2.1.1 Ultimate or Elemental Composition 
The composition of biomass is expressed in term of its basic elements except for its moisture content 
(MC) and inorganic constituents, ash. A typical ultimate analysis is [1] 
 
𝐶+ 𝐻+ 𝑂+ 𝑁+ 𝑆+ 𝐴𝑠ℎ+ 𝑀𝐶= 100% 
(1) 
In which C, H, O, N, S are the mass percentages of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen and sulfur, 
respectively in the biomass fuel. Not all the biomass fuels contain all of these elements. From the 
ultimate analysis, high amount of hydrogen and oxygen in biomass results in relatively low heating 
value [1]. 
2.1.2 Proximate Analysis 
Proximate analysis gives the composition of biomass in terms of gross components such as moisture 
content, volatile matter (VM), ash and fixed carbon (FC).  
Moisture Content (MC): High moisture content is a major characteristic of biomass. MC of biomass 
differs for biomass species from 7% to 95% [1]. High MC of biomass reduces the thermal efficiency of 
gasifier because high moisture drains much of the deliverable energy from a gasifier, as the energy 
used in the evaporation is not recovered which is otherwise used in reduction zone in converting 
thermal energy into chemical energy or heating value of gas. This important input parameter for 
design must be known for assessment of the energy penalty in drying the biomass. 
The moisture in biomass can remain in two forms, namely free or external and inherent or equilibrium 
[1]. Free moisture is that which is above the equilibrium MC.  It generally resides the cell walls. 
Inherent MC is absorbed within the cell walls.  
Volatile Matter: The volatile matter of a biomass is the condensable and non-condensable vapor 
released when the biomass is heated up [1]. This amount depends on the heating rate and the 
temperature developed in the pyrolysis zone of the gasifier. High volatile matter of the biomass might 
demand a special design of the gasifier and combustion system in order to remove the tar content. 
Fixed Carbon (FC): Fixed carbon in a fuel is the amount of solid carbon calculated from the following 
equation, where MC, VM, and ash stand for moisture content, volatile matter and ash, respectively 
[1].

analysis, FC is an important parameter because in most gasifiers the conversion of fixed carbon into 
gases determines the rate of gasification and its yield. This conversion reaction, being the slowest, is 
used to determine the size of the gasifier. 
Ash Content: Ash is the inorganic solid residue left after the biomass is completely burned. Ash 
content of biomass is generally very small but plays a significant role if it contains alkali metals such 
as potassium or chlorine. These compounds may lead to melting, agglomeration, fouling or corrosion 
in gasifier results in slagging or clinker formation [1]. This may lead to excessive tar formation or 
blocking the gasifier with the risk of explosion even.  
2.1.3 Bulk Density 
Bulk density refers to the weight of material per unit of volume. It differs for different types of 
biomass. Bulk density is an important parameter for the performance of biomass gasification as a high 
voidage tends to result in channeling, bridging, incomplete conversion and a decrease in the capacity 
of the gasifier [6]. Biomass with high bulk density indicates high energy content per unit volume and 
it means less amount of biomass is needed in gasification process. When bulk density is low results 
difficult to flow under gravity and produces low heating value of gas.  
To overcome these drawbacks of biomass characteristics suitable pretreatment of biomass is needed 
to obtain higher heating value of gas. This needs the biomass with uniform size, low moisture content 
and densification for low bulk density biomass.

2.3 Gasification Process 
Gasification is a thermochemical process in which solid biomass reacts with a limited supply of 
gasifying air to completely convert all the carbonaceous materials (e.g. biomass and bio-waste) into 
gases rich in CO and H2 at a temperature higher than 700°C [9]. Thermo chemical characteristics of the 
biomass plays an important role in the performance of gasification process. Thermal treatment of 
==PAGE:16==

biomass is dependent on gasification temperature and gasification agents (air, stream or oxygen) 
which contributes to producer gas outcome, tar species in the producer gas, and solid products such 
as char, ash and soot [10].  
These processes contribute a wide range of flexibility in product applications, especially for thermal 
and power production. The gasification process can be divided into four principle process taking place 
in different zone in the gasifier- the drying zone, pyrolysis zone, partial oxidation/oxidation zone and 
reduction zone [10]. Figure 2.1 represents heat flow and chemical reactions in downdraft gasifier.

2.3.1 Drying Zone 
The conversion of moisture to water vapor occurs during drying process and no thermal 
decomposition of the biomass taking place during this process. The major portion of the surface and 
inherent moisture in the biomass evaporates in the drying process [10]. Evaporation of water is an 
endothermic reaction which requires heat transferred between hot gases from the oxidation zone to 
biomass in the drying (see Figure 2.1). Biomass is introduced at the top of the gasifier and drying of the 
biomass takes place at the top of the gasifier resulting formation of water vapor, which partly leads to 
the production of H2 by the reaction of steam-char reaction and the remaining goes with the producer 
gas. The amount of moisture released is equal to the water vapor formed and this can be expressed 
in term of mass balance as shown in the following equation

in producer gas. The biomass with higher moisture content will produce higher H2 and this leads to 
higher H2 percentage within the producer gas that requires more heat for drying. One kilogram of 
moisture in biomass needs 2260 kJ extra heat from the gasifier to vaporize water at an atmospheric 
pressure [1]. Usually suitable moisture content of biomass for downdraft gasifier is about 5-35% [11].  
Typically the drying zone temperature is about 100- 200°C [9].

2.4 Gasifier Designs 
Gasifiers are divided mainly on the basis of the product gas-solid contacting mode [1]. Based on the 
gas-solid contacting mode, gasifiers are divided into three principle types- fixed bed gasifiers, fluidized 
bed gasifiers and entrained flow gasifier. Fixed bed gasifiers are for the smallest size applications while 
entrained-flow gasifiers are for the largest size applications. The entrained flow gasifier requires a very 
small particle size due to the need of extensive pretreatment. It is normally not used for biomass and 
used for coal dust combustion at large capacities [1]. The details of fluid bed gasifier and entrained 
bed gasifier can be seen in Appendix D.  Figure 2.3 shows the range of applications for different types 
of gasifiers [1].

2.4.1 Fixed Bed Gasifier 
The fixed bed gasifier is also known as moving bed gasifier because the fuel moves down in the reactor 
[1]. Biomass is fed from the top of the gasifier, and air and producer gas pass up or down through a 
bed of solid fuel. In the bottom of the gasifier, there is a fixed bed and grate which hold solid char, 
hence the name is fixed bed gasifier. Both mixing and heat transfer within the moving bed are rather 
poor, which makes it difficult to achieve uniform distribution of fuel, temperature and gas composition 
across the cross sectional of the gasifier. Besides these, fixed bed gasifiers are the simplest type of 
gasifier and can be built inexpensively in small scale. The main advantages of the gasifier are the higher 
carbon conversion efficiency, the wide range of ash content in the biomass and the possibility to melt 
ash [1]. There are mainly three types of fixed bed gasifier used in the production of producer gas. 
2.4.1.1 Updraft gasifier 
In this gasifier, gasifying air is introduced at the bottom of the reactor and the product gas produced 
moves up to the top through the bed of biomass. The heat is generated in the oxidation zone which is 
utilized for drying and pyrolyzing the biomass through the reactor. This means that the volatiles from 
the pyrolysis and drying zone are not thermally oxidized before exiting the reactor. That’s why the 
producer gas has a very low exit temperature and subsequent high energy efficiency. This makes 
updraft gasification suitable for high moisture content [10]. On the other hand, the producer gas 
contains high level of tars and water, and therefore this gas cannot be used directly in the engine that 
requires tar free gas [1]. But tars in the producer gas condense afterwards and use them as fuel for a 
water heater. A good example of successfully running of an updraft gasifier is at Harboøre, Denmark 
for the application of district heating with a capacity of 3.5 MW (biomass input) on Woodchips with a 
total efficiency of 94% [12].

2.4.1.2 Downdraft Gasifier 
A downdraft gasifier has a cocurrent reactor where gasifying air enters the reactor at a certain height 
below the top. The product gas from the pyrolysis zone flows downward through the oxidation and 
reduction zone, that’s giving the name downdraft [1]. Therefore, all the product gas including volatiles 
are oxidized or cracked down into gaseous products including CO, H2, CH4 and CO2. The producer gas 
with high temperature (700-900°C) is drawn off at the bottom of the gasifier and results in an 
increased concentration of hydrogen and almost tar free producer gas.

gradient along the height ( right) [1]. 
The downdraft gasifier work well with the internal combustion engines (ICE) that needs cleaner gas. 
Low tar content (0.015-3 g/Nm3) in the producer gas is the main advantage for their use with ICEs. A 
downdraft gasifier requires a shorter time (10-30 min) to ignite and bring the working temperature 
compared to the time required by an updraft gasifier [1]. There are two types of downdraft gasifier:

This makes the downdraft gasification suitable for applications of clean gas which uses directly in an 
internal combustion engine for the production of power and heat simultaneously [14] or can be used 
for solid oxide fuel cell applications [15]. However, the only drawbacks of downdraft gasifiers can be 
identified as limitation to operate with low density fuels due to flow problems and excessive pressure 
drop.  
2.4.1.3 Crossdraft Gasifiers 
In this gasifier, biomass moves downward while the gasifying air is supplied from the side at the 
combustion zone, and the producer gas exit the gasifier on the opposite side, at the same level, hence 
the name cross-draft. The combustion zone has a very high temperature (>1500°C), and the 
gasification zone is just before the gas outlet [1]. As the combustion zone forms around the entrance 
of air, thus results in gases leaving the gasifier at a high temperature, hence lowering the overall 
efficiency.   
 
Figure 2. 6: Layout of a crossdraft gasifier [1]. 
Crossdraft gasifier is generally used in small scale biomass units. The advantages of this gasifier are 
relatively small reaction time with low thermal capacity, which gives faster response time than that of 
any other moving bed gasifier and requires less startup time (5-10 min). The gasifier produced tar 
content is low (0.01-0.1 g/Nm3) [1], so a crossdraft gasifier requires a relatively simple gas-cleaning 
system. Besides this, the gasifier can be very light and small (<10 kWe) [1]. The crossdraft gasifier is 
less suitable for high-ash or high tar fuels, but it can handle high moisture fuels.

2.5 Gas Applications and Engine Performance  
Producer gas is a highly versatile fuel with a variety of possible different applications, including the 
production of biofuels, solid fuels and fertilizers, as well as for cogeneration of power and heat (CHP) 
with gas engines or modified diesel engines [16]. For downdraft gasifier, the main applications are 
divided into two groups, namely thermal application and engine application. 
2.5.1 Thermal Applications 
Thermal applications do not require clean gas due to direct burning of the producer gas, as this kind 
of application can be operated with high tar content and dust without cleaning. Efficiency of thermal 
application of gasification is in the range of 90% [1].  
2.5.2 Engine Applications 
For applications with the producer gas, the Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) does not require any 
additional gases or diesel to start as they can run completely on producer gas directly [17]. Downdraft 
gasifier is the best choice because of the low tar content in the producer gas. However, diesel engines 
must be converted by reducing the compression ratio and installing spark ignition system in order to 
run on producer gas alone [17]. But up to 90% of producer gas can be achieved by duel fuel mode on 
diesel engines without any modifying the engine [17].  
Engine applications needs to be clean gas with low tar content in order to ensure proper functioning 
of the engine-generator systems. The key factor is that the tar must not condensate. Thus, the 
concentration of tar needs to be low enough to avoid this. The energetic output for engine-generator 
systems is one third of electricity production and two thirds of heat production, being gasification 
efficiency over 93%. The net electricity efficiency from biomass gasification is usually low, ranging from 
20% to 40% [18]. The actual efficiency of engine varies with design, size and running condition of air 
fuel ratio.  
2.6 Gas Cooling and Cleaning 
Producer gas exits the gasifier in a mixture of combustible and non-combustible gases along with tar 
vapor, water vapor and dust. Sulphur compounds such as hydrogen sulphide (H2S, COS) and nitrogen 
compounds (NH3, HCN) in the producer gas are undesirable because of their condensates are corrosive 
and pollutants in exhaust gases [19]. The amount of NH3 and HCN in the gas depends on the nitrogen 
content of the fuel. The generation of H2S is of little importance in gasification of biomass as long as 
sulphur content does not exceed 0.5% dependent on the specific legislation [19]. Producer gas with 
nitrogen content less than 2% is safe for gasification [19]. Silicon oxide (SiO2) and iron oxide (Fe2O3) in 
dust are important because of their abrasive nature. 
Depending on the design of the gasifier and the type of biomass used as a fuel, there can be more or 
less of the mentioned above components [6]. These contaminants can reduce the efficiency of the 
producer gas to use in engine or burner and also causes to damage the engine or the burner. Thus, 
these impurities need to be removed before the producer gas to be used in engine or burner. The 
removal of tar from gas producer is one of the most difficult problems in gas cleaning. Depending on 
the type of applications, different cleaning and cooling processes are needed to be performed [20]. 
Gas cooling prior to engine applications is very much essential for improving volumetric efficiency and 
condensing the tar or liquids. Direct and indirect wet scrubbing used to cool the gas and remove the 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
tar by condensation and NH3 and HCl by adsorption, and finally an electrostatic precipitator for dust 
and tar aerosols. The tar concentration goes down to 20-40 mg/Nm3 for a high efficient scrubber 
system [21]. It has been shown that a water scrubber can reduce a tar concentration as low as 20-40 
mg/Nm3 and particulate level of 10-20 mg/Nm3 [21]. The gas cleaning systems basically include cyclone 
separators, biomass filters and bag filters for dust removal and water separators for water droplet 
removal after gas cooling. 
2.7 Gasification Parameters 
The performance of biomass gasifier could be characterized by several parameters. Design and 
operation of downdraft gasifier requires understanding of biomass characteristics, process parameter 
such as air-fuel ratio, temperature, and biomass consumption rate etc. These parameters play an 
important role for performance of gasifier as well as to optimize in terms of producer gas and gasifier 
efficiency.  
2.7.1 Biomass Characteristics 
2.7.1.1 Biomass Size 
In fixed bed gasifier, there is a limitation in biomass size [22]. Smaller sized biomass increases the 
energy efficiency of gasification process [23] as well as yields in more producer gas compare than large 
sizes biomass for particular gasification time. Heat transfer area will increase with particle size 
reduction, thus enhances volatile released during the pyrolysis process [24]. However, gasification of 
small size biomass has a tendency of higher pressure drop in the reactor due to lower bed porosity as 
well as high dust content in the producer gas. On the other hand, large size biomass reduces the 
reactivity of biomass feed stock and causes starting and bridging problems [24] which in terns reduces 
the production rate of gas [25] as well as yields more char and tar. So, homogeneity of biomass size 
affects the performance of gasifier. Increasing the level of biomass homogeneity increases the gasifier 
efficiency [26].  
2.7.1.2 Moisture Content (MC) 
Moisture content of biomass affects the quality of producer gas in term of heating value. Biomass with 
high moisture content reduces the heating value of the producer gas. Biomass with more than 30% 
moisture content reduces heating value due to incomplete pyrolysis processes during gasification [27]. 
Heat required for complete pyrolysis process is insufficient because more heat is needed for driving 
off the moisture during the drying process. Therefore, biomass with less moisture content is favorable 
to produce higher heating value as well as increases gasifier efficiency. 
2.7.2 Gasification Temperature 
Gasification temperature is directly affected by the air flow rate. The temperature increases with the 
increasing air flow rate due to the enhancing combustion process in the oxidation zone [28]. The high 
temperature in gasifier demands for tar cracking and increases endothermic char gasification rate that 
yields producer gas with increased heating value [29]. Generally, the partial oxidation zone has the 
highest temperature among the all zones, while the temperatures of all zones oscillate during the 
gasification process. The oscillation happens due to the self-regulating nature of the downdraft 
gasifiers [30]. Air flow rate differs during gasification which is depended on the bed porosity and 
suction rate of the producer gas. The variation of air flow alters heat released during oxidation process 
thus gasification temperature oscillates. The percentages of CO and H2 increases with the increasing 
==PAGE:24==
14 
 
gasification temperature [29], while heating value of gas increases at a certain level and opposite 
phenomena occurs when temperature reaches above 1300°C. 
2.7.3 Biomass Consumption Rate 
Biomass consumption rate is directly affected by the air flow rate and biomass size. Biomass 
consumption rate is proportional to the air flow rate and inversely proportional to the biomass size 
[31]. Oxygen availability increases with the air flow rate which improves oxidation rate results more 
biomass consumption rate. Smaller size biomass is favorable for oxidation process due to larger total 
reaction area per unit volume of biomass.  
2.7.4 Equivalence Ratio (ER) 
Equivalence ratio (ER) is the most important performance parameter for a gasifier.  Equivalence ratio 
is the ratio of the actual air used in the gasification to stoichiometric air needed for complete 
combustion  [32]. The air supply is only a fraction of the stoichiometric amount in the gasification 
process. The stoichiometric amount is calculated from the ultimate analysis of the biomass. For 
effective gasification, typical equivalence ratio is between 0.2 to 0.4 [22], while it gives the best yield 
for 0.25 [32]. Figure 2. 7 below shows the effects of equivalence ratio on mole fraction of the producer 
gas at an atmospheric pressure [9].  
 
Figure 2. 7: Computed mole fraction as a function of the equivalence ratio [9]. 
The quality of producer gas strongly depends on the value of the ER. At ER = 0.25 all the char is 
converted into gas giving the highest energy density of the gas [6].  An excessively low ER value (0.2) 
results in problems including incomplete gasification, excessive char formation and a low heating 
value of the producer gas. On the other hand, too high ER value (0.4) results in excessive formation of 
complete combustion, such as CO2 and H2O, other than the desirable products, such as CO and H2, and 
the temperature also increases. This results a decrease in the heating value of the producer gas and 
the highest CO and lowest CO2 yield. That’s why in practical gasification systems, the ER value is 
normally maintained about 0.20-0.30 [1].

tar destruction. 
Tar content is the main problem if the producer gas is used directly in an engine. Tar content in the 
producer gas is primarily depends on the oxidation temperature, air fuel ratio and the biomass

3.2 Process principle of LTB Gasifier 
Figure 3.9 shows the principle of the gasification process in the modified co-current moving bed 
gasifier [2]. The process principle of the modified co-current moving bed gasifier has been the basic 
principle of the low-tar biomass (LTB) gasifier.  
In the study of Susanto and Beenackers [2], the biomass is fed at the top and producer gas leaves at 
the bottom of the gasifier. The gasifying air is supplied in the center of the combustor through the air 
supply system. The recycle gas containing pyrolysis products is sucked from the bed by an internal 
injector and mixed with the gasifying air. This mixture is burnt in a pre-combustor chamber installed 
in the reactor.  
 
Figure 3.9: Process principle of modified co-current moving bed gasifier [2] 
From the Figure 3.9 above, the feedstock mw first undergoes drying and decomposes in the pyrolysis 
zone to produce char mc and volatiles mV. The volatiles are sucked off upwards together with some 
gas mI from the countercurrent reduction zone. In the injector, the recycle gas mR consisting of mv and 
mI is mixed with the gasifying air mA and injected into the combustor. The heat generated in the 
combustor during the combustion is used for the drying, pyrolysis and reduction processes. The flue 
gas mF from the combustor is split into two streams: the downdraft blast mB1 into the co-current 
reduction zone and the updraft blast mB2 into the countercurrent reduction zone. The ratio mB2/mB1 
depends on the recycle ratio applied. Recycle ratio is the ratio of recycle gas divided by the gasifying 
air (v/v at s.t.p.). Then the updraft blast mB2 reacts with the char mC to produce the injected gas mI. 
Part of the char from the pyrolysis zone, mC2, is consumed in the countercurrent reduction zone, 
whereas the remaining char, mC1 moves further down into the co-current reduction zone. This 
remaining char mC1 then reacts with the downdraft blast mB1 to produce a clean producer gas. 
==PAGE:32==
22 
 
The volatile gas flow containing tar content is mixed with the air prior to combustion, all tars from the 
volatiles gas must pass in the hot flame through the oxidation zone. Therefore, tars are cracked down 
in the oxidation zone and then further reacts with the char in the char bed. Besides this, any tars that 
might have escaped from the hot combustion zone will subsequently be converted over the hot char 
in the char bed. As a result, complete tar removal can be expected.  
If most oxygen is consumed in the combustor, the bed does not reach the extreme high temperatures 
observed in the oxidation zone of a conventional co-current gasifier. Primary air is supplied in the 
oxidation zone by air inlet nozzles (21.1 mm, inner diameter). The air inlet nozzle is connected to the 
top of the gasifier reactor and connected just below 128 mm from the top of the gasifier according to 
Figure 3.1. This nozzle is connected to an air inlet manifold which is controlled by a gate valve. The air 
supplied can be regulated according to the operation needs of the gasifier.

3.5 Operation and Testing of the Gasifier 
The gasification tests were carried out outdoors in an open place. The gasifier was placed on a flat 
surface and connected to the blower (see Figure F.1). The blower was used to suck the air out from 
the gasifier. LPG burner was placed near the reactor and fixed it with a stand. The gas flow through 
the top of the reactor was turned on to heat up the reactor. A hand-held gas burner (Bernz-O-Matic 
TS 7000) was introduced to ignite the LPG burner to produce flame inside the gasifier and gasifier 
reactor was heated between 800-900°C. After reaching the stable temperature the LPG burner and 
the blower were detached from the gasifier (see Figure F.1). The reduction zone below the combustor 
was filled with hot char and then fresh cold char was put into the reactor char bed. The whole reactor 
was filled with the biomass. The valve of the air supply system was then turned on to supply the 
pressurized air into the reactor, and the rate of air supply was monitored with the help of a flow meter. 
After few moments the producer gas came out in the form of a thick white smoke through the gas 
outlet pipe. The clean producer gas was observed after few minutes. A part of the producer gas was 
cooled down in the long pipe. Subsequently, it was passed through a filter to get rid of the particulate 
==PAGE:34==
24 
 
matters. After filtering, the cool producer gas was set to ducting in the engine (see Figure 3.10). The 
rest of the producer gas was ignited with a hand-held firing torch at the outlet of pipe (see Figure F.2).  
The gasification process was terminated by the turning off the valve of air supply system to cut off the 
air. After stopping air supply, N2 was introduced into the gasifier to quench rapidly for cooling and 
preventing further pyrolysis of the remaining char (see Figure F.2). The gasifier was then left in an 
open place until it cooled down and all gases were ejected to the atmosphere (more than 5 hours).  
After every test, the char left in the gasifier and ash in the ash bunker were collected and weighted 
for the calculation of mass and energy balance. The char samples were taken from the top, bed and 
layer to layer of the gasifier in order to observe the gasification performance.  
Before starting operation, all the thermocouple ports connected to the different zone of the gasifier 
were properly tightened. Gate valve of the air supply system was kept switched off. During the test, 
the temperature of different zones of the gasifier were measured with the help of thermocouple that 
is connected to the data logger of a computer. A temperature measurement software, NI DIAdem 
2015 was used to record the measured temperatures continuously. The gasifying airflow rate was 
adjusted in order to get the optimum temperature, and then it was kept at a constant flowrate. Air 
flow rate was measured continuously by air flow meter. The total wood consumption with hot char 
was measured by a digital weight meter. The two liquid column manometers were installed at a point 
of combustor and producer gas outlet to measure the pressure drop during the testing. In order to get 
gas composition in the producer gas a GC analyzer was installed at the beginning of the test. Gas 
samples were taken by means of SPA sampling from the producer gas outlet, pyrolysis zone and 
combustion zone to get quantity and composition of tars. 
3.6 Improvements after Test 1 and 2 
The gasifier was tested with the wood pellets for test 1 and 2. During the gasification process of the 
test 1, wood pellets were decomposed in the drying zone due to the release of moisture content and 
then decomposed wood pellets were reacted with the liquids (mostly tars) produced from pyrolysis 
zone, which results to form stickiness between wood pellets and it causes difficulty to fall down easily. 
Again, all the steams from drying and pyrolysis zone deposited at the colder part of the reactor top. 
When there was a need to fill in biomass again, fuel hopper of the reactor was opened, and all the 
steams and white smoke came out together in the same time. This was the problem to cause 
personnel damage when the test was running. Wood pellets are very dense and small, thus have a 
higher fuel to open space ratio than desired. They create a heavy dense bed that is much more difficult 
to penetrate with air and causes high pressure drop, this was observed during the test period. That’s 
why decision was taken to test the gasifier with the wood chips for next all tests. Also, the top of the 
reactor was increased about 280 mm to solve this problem. 
Another improvement was done to avoid accidental issue during biomass filling. When there was a 
need to fill the biomass again, fuel hopper was opened, and all the gases came out from the rector 
top. To avoid this issue, blower was installed at the gas outlet pipe during filling period of testing. The 
biomass filling time of 3-5 min was needed for the 1st version of the gasifier, because of this time 
needed to open the fuel hopper and close the fuel hopper tightly with the rector. To minimize filling 
time and for trouble-free operation, new fuel hopper was introduced that can be seen in Figure 3.11

3.11 Safety assessment during testing 
Safety assessments were carried out during testing of gasification process. As the gasifier operated at 
high temperature in the oxidation zone and the producer gas contains a flammable CO, CH4 and H2, 
which are also toxic, so a number of safety issues were taken to avoid toxic, fire and explosion hazards. 
These are listed below: 
a. All tests were carried out in an open place and the combustible gases were burned at 
atmosphere.  
b. Before starting all the joining point and equipment used in gasification process was checked 
properly and tightened to avoid leakage. 
c. Hand gloves, safety shoes and safety glass were used when filling the biomass in to the 
gasifier.  
d. The gas flow for the LPG burner was controlled by a valve to control temperature of the 
reactor when the reactor was heated up. Reactor temperature is not needed over 1000°C 
because steel begins to soften and loses about half of its strength over 1000°C. In order to 
minimize this issues security valve is introduced to avoid explosion from high temperature. 
e. The blower sucked the hot air and gases from the gas outlet pipe during heating up the gasifier 
and also sucked the producer gas once the biomass was filled up. The blower has a valve, and 
a personnel is adjusted the rotation speed so that the suction is just enough to avoid smoke 
raising while filling biomass in the gasifier. If the person filling the biomass to the gasifier 
smells smoke, person will stop the filling and leave the gasifier immediately. 
f. A personal CO detector is worn always during the test.  
g. Most hot elements are insulated to avoid personal damage (except the LPG burner flame and 
torch). Avoid looking into the ignition opening when introducing flame. 
h. Since the gasifier system operate at negative pressure, safety valve is installed to avoid any 
accidental explosion due to higher pressure difference inside the gasifier.

Figure 4.7: Producer gas composition in volume percentage in dry basis. 
The high H2 content observed in the producer gas in the test 3 and 4 because of mainly the biomass 
with high moisture content used to produce more water vapor in the drying zone and this reacted 
with the char to form steam reforming and water gas reaction in the reduction zone to produce more 
H2. At relatively low temperature, H2 will be formed by water gas shift reaction. Higher H2 content in 
the producer gas also can be seen at high temperature which predominantly water-gas reaction.  For 
the test 5, the moisture content in the biomass is comparatively lower, that produces comparatively 
low H2 content due to the water gas and water gas shift reaction in the gasification process and high 
percentage of CO in the producer gas.  
All the CO formation reactions in reduction zone are endothermic and reactions in partial oxidation 
zone are exothermic. To produce higher amount of CO, more air is needed and subsequently high 
temperatures favor CO formation. From the Figure 4.7 above, CO2 and H2 have similar trends while 
CO has opposite trend to CO2 and H2 for test 5. Therefore, exothermic water-gas shift reaction is 
predominant in the reduction zone. This may be due to considerably low temperature in that zone.

reaction, that’s why high CO found in the producer gas.

ratio (ER), because excess air is needed to combust the biomass, which needs more energy and causes 
more heat loss. The heat loss is found to be quite low, which is confirmed experimentally from the 
measurements of the wall temperature of the gasifier during testing. It is to be noted that the outside 
surface of the gasifier was well insulated, which minimized the extra heat loss and thus improved the 
gasifier performance. Only the upper part of the gasifier and fuel hopper was open during the 
operation period. Heat loss has a significant negative effect on the gasification efficiency.  
In Table 4.6, heat loss is showed in negative value for test 2 in term of enthalpy. This may be due to a 
wrong assumption to calculate the heat loss. Char and solid wood pellets are left in the gasifier after 
test 2. The char and solid wood pellets are not considered in the analysis after the test. It is assumed 
that initial char and residual char are the same with the same properties and considered to be 100% 
C. In this situation, enthalpy for steam is not possible to predict correctly, and this is included in the 
output enthalpy. That’s why, output energy is lower compared to the input enthalpy.

4.9 Evaluation of Pyrolysis Gas Recirculation System 
Recirculation ratio is the measure to evaluate pyrolysis gas recirculation system during the operation 
of the LTB gasifier. The recycle ratio is calculated by dividing the amount of recycled pyrolysis gas 
(volatiles gas) to the gasifying air. Minimum recycle ratio value is needed to recycle pyrolysis gas prior 
to mix with the incoming gasifying air. For analysis, the pyrolysis gas recirculation system is started 
instantaneously at the supply of gasifying air, although circulation system is not effective before the 
pyrolysis zone was not hot enough to produce volatiles gas. The combustor zone temperature is high 
enough due to the conduction of heat from the co-current reduction zone and the recycle gas-air 
mixture could burned itself in this condition [2]. The ignition is started immediately after the supply 
of air, and the steady state conditions is reached based on the production of pyrolysis gas composition 
corresponding to the gasifier temperatures achieved in the combustion zone. Minimum recycle ratio 
needed to recycle pyrolysis gas is 0.4 and above, and recycle ratio between 0.6 and 0.9 is needed to 
get a better gasifier performance [2].

CHAPTER 5: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
5.1 Conclusions 
Limited tests are performed in this research study on power applications using the producer gas 
derived from the Low-tar biomass (LTB) gasifier. Four tests are considered to calculate gasification 
parameters. More tests over a wider operating span are needed to develop LTB gasifier as a mature 
technology. Based on the analysis of results the following conclusions can be drawn, that relevant to 
the basis of the design for the gasification reaction. 
The LTB gasifier is commissioned, tested and evaluated during this project. A low-tar biomass (LTB) 
gasifier with an internal recirculation and combustion of pyrolysis gas in the partial oxidation zone 
offers excellent opportunities for handling extremely low-tar content in the producer gas. Here, 
average tar content of 21.2 mg/Nm3 in the producer gas is obtained for wood chips. This value is lower 
compared to the tar content observed in a previous analysis for a moving bed gasifier. Again, the 
gasifiers producer gas had an average lower heating value (LHV) of 4.69 MJ/Nm3. Furthermore, the 
average gas compositions (H2-18.78%, CO-19.22%, CO2-10.34%, CH4-0.69% and N2-50.71%) in the 
producer gas obtained for three different tests are comparable with typical producer gas composition. 
Moreover, at a recycle ratios 0.3, tar content of 10.6 mg/Nm3 observed in the producer gas.  
Using mass and energy balances, the average gasifier capacity and cold gas efficiency (CGE) observed 
23.1 kW and 82.7% for wood chips, whereas 33.1 kW and 60.5% for wood pellets, respectively. 
Average heat loss in term of higher heating value (HHV) observed to be 3.2% of thermal input for wood 
chips and 1% for wood pellets, whereas heat loss was found to be 1% of thermal input in term of 
enthalpy for wood chips. Thus, the LTB gasifier performed better compared to a typical moving bed 
gasifier in term of heat loss. Approximately 0.38 kW is lost through char-ash at the maximum char-ash 
generation rate for wood chips. This char-ash energy can be used as a soil conditioner or as charcoal. 
The gas outlet temperature of the gasifier is around 676°C, therefore the producer gas can be used 
directly in heating applications in order to enhance the performance. The temperature profile of the 
LTB gasifier observed relatively low temperature compare to typical downdraft gasifier. The average 
partial oxidation zone temperature of 970°C observed for wood chips. The use of separate combustor 
substantially lowers the bed temperature to 750°C, compared to 1000°C in conventional co-current 
moving bed gasifier.  
There is a clear variation of gasifier performance with equivalence ratio (ER) for all the tests. ERs in 
the range of 0.29 to 0.41 give better performance and the heating value and CGE were almost same.  
The specific gas production yields at ER of 0.29 and 0.41 were 2.16 Nm3/kg and 3.20 Nm3/kg, 
respectively. Higher gas production yield is obtained with higher equivalence ratio. Heating value or 
CGE changes proportionally with the gas production yield. Since the equivalence ratios were obtained 
at the end of the tests, it cannot be justified as the actual optimum point of the low-tar biomass 
gasifier. The optimum points valid only within the range of tested equivalence ratios. 
The LTB Gasifier was operated with the engine generator system for over 300 minutes in last two tests. 
During these tests the engine was started and operated completely with the producer gas. The 
operation was well successful, and the output was as expected. The engine operated well on the 
produced gas, and no deposits were observed in the engine afterwards. Part of the producer gas flow 
was used for engine operation and corresponding electrical power was found to be 1.5 kW 
==PAGE:64==
54 
 
continuously and a maximum value of 2.5 kW is also observed while the maximum generator capacity 
is 3 kW.  
The thermodynamic equilibrium model correctly predicts the bed temperature, gas composition, LHV 
of gas and equivalence ratio with the experimental data. In the model, predicted bed temperature of 
650°C is found, which is only 100°C lower than the measured temperature. The equilibrium bed 
temperature from experiment is qualitatively in good agreement with the thermodynamic equilibrium 
model bed temperature. Thus, thermodynamic Equilibrium model is in good agreement with the 
experimental results.  
5.2 Difficulties 
Since the biomass wood chips are prepared automatically, it was difficult to maintain uniform size and 
shape of the chips. This is an essential requirement for gasifiers because the chips should flow down 
smoothly, and gasification operation is very sensitive to biomass size [38]. The ambient humidity 
affects sometimes the moisture content of woodchips, so it is difficult to maintain the moisture 
content of woodchips at the required level. Sun drying was used as the drying method and the weather 
conditions frequently affected the drying of woodchips. In this research sun drying method used in 
the green house, so moisture content could be maintained in a certain level, specially 11.57 
percentage for test 5.  
Since biomass filling in the gasifier is practiced manually, the work is tough and time consuming. Non-
uniform size of woodchips, specially bigger sized wood chips, filled inside the gasifier and caused 
blockage. Due to this blockage sometimes the wood chips could not fall automatically. The gasifier 
was built for batch operation and not for continuous operation. If refueling is needed, the top cover 
should be opened and this caused a lot of air leakage and heat loss. Also when the hopper is opened, 
gases inside the gasifier comes out and safety issues arise. Therefore, gasifier needs to be operated in 
continuous basis to avoid above mentioned problems. 
5.3 Recommendations for Further Improvements 
During the operation of the project, several experiences have made a basis for projecting further 
research on the project. The proposed further work needs to be envisaged. The proposed future works 
can be carried out to learn more about the gasifier performance. 
Electrical efficiency is one of the most important performance parameters of the gasifier. Here only 
for two test conditions the electrical power through power generator system was measured where a 
part of the producer gas was used. The calculations on electrical efficiency is not conducted, thus in 
future analysis electrical efficiency can be calculated.  
Usually gasification process is operated with the hot char, in future the gasification operation with 
cold char can be analyzed by using cold char, especially for agricultural purposes or in remote rural 
areas where heating arrangement is not available.   
As the combustor installed inside the gasifier causes blockage and prevents the biomass to fall easily, 
so it is needed to be researched on how the biomass can easily fall down to the reduction zone. To 
avoid this blockage, a stirrer can be installed to stir the combustor, which will make the biomass fall 
easily.  
Further experiment is needed to know more accurate prediction of the height of the reduction zone. 
Again, it is also important to identify the optimal combustor height and optimal position of the 
==PAGE:65==
55 
 
combustor as a function of recycle ratio. This is also required to know the behavior of the pyrolysis 
gas channeling. 
The separate combustor used for lowering the highest bed temperature. The LTB gasifier substantially 
lowers the bed temperature to 750°C. So, it is clear that the use of separate combustor has a positive 
effect to lower the bed temperature. Therefore, it is needed to be further research to lower the 
equilibrium char bed temperature to a certain level as this temperature affect the overall efficiency of 
the gasifier.   
A last suggestion, this study does not analyze other operating parameters such as types of fuel wood 
and optimal size of the biomass. Optimal size of the biomass needs to be predicted as it affects the 
maximum production of the producer gas as well as the increase of the heating value of the producer 
gas. In addition, the effect of wood chip size was not studied completely. Therefore, someone can 
continue this study for the issues discussed above.  
These all suggestions indicate that further improvement of the LTB gasifier, including modification of 
the current design is required. In addition, the suggested future works can be performed to get more 
interesting results.

D  Review of Gasifier Designs  
D 1 Fluid Bed Gasifier 
Fluid bed gasifier is more suitable for large scale application and for biomass with small particle size 
with low density. This type of gasifiers is used in biomass pyrolysis due to their very high heating rates. 
Fluid bed gasifiers are noted for their excellent mixing and temperature uniformity throughout the 
entire gasifier. There are two main types of fluid bed gasifiers namely- the bubbling fluidized bed and 
the circulating fluidized bed. Circulating fluidized bed gasifier is the best option in term of considering 
wide range of applications.  
In circulating fluidized bed (CFB) gasifier, a very fine sand particles are used as bed materials and 
fluidized by aerating from nozzles at the bottom of the reactor. This gives the characteristics of a fluid 
which ensures uniform mixing of the biomass particles and a high mass transfer between gas and 
solids. Sand also has a high thermal capacity which increase the heat transfer and ensures an even 
distribution of heat in the reactor. The advantages of having a large thermal inertia in the sand bed 
offers using a wide range of low-density fuel combinations or quality in this gasifier. The problem 
associated with low density fuel contain high amount of ash causes sensitive to ash agglomeration and 
sintering at a high temperature. The operating temperature range of fluid bed is usually between 700-
900°C which makes it suitable for biomass gasification [6]. 
 
Figure D.1: Circulating fluid bed (CFB) gasifier [1]. 
A circulating fluid bed (CFB) gasifier has a special appeal for biomass gasification because of the long 
gas residence time it provides. It is especially suitable for fuels with high volatiles. A CFB reactor 
typically comprises of a riser, a cyclone, and a solid recycle device that can be seen in Figure D.1 above. 
The riser serves as the gasifier reactor [1]. The functions are similarly to the CFB gasifiers, but it is 
operated with high fluidization velocity of 3.5-5.5 m/s [1] and therefore much more turbulent fluid 
velocity. This means that gases with hot sand reaches the cyclone and is therefore separated and 
circulated back into the fluid bed with the char and ashes. Due to higher velocity, CFB gasifier are less 
prone to bed agglomeration and can therefore be operated at higher temperature.

D 2 Entrained-Flow Gasifier 
Entrained flow gasifier is the most successful and widely used type of gasifier for large-scale 
gasification of coal, petroleum coke, and refinery residues. The gasifier is ideally suited to most types 
of coal except low-rank coal, which, like lignite and biomass, is not attractive because of its large 
moisture content.  
The suitability of entrained-flow gasification for biomass is questionable for a number of reasons. The 
residence time in the reactor is very short. For the reactions to complete, the biomass particles must 
be finely ground. Being fibrous, biomass cannot be pulverized easily. Molten ash from biomass is 
highly aggressive because of its alkali compounds and can corrode the gasifier’s refractory or metal 
lining. Given these shortcomings, entrained-flow gasifiers are not popular for biomass. Entrained-flow 
gasification of biomass is therefore rather limited and has not been seen on a commercial scale. 
However, there is at least one successful entrained-flow biomass gasifier known as the Choren 
process.  
 
Figure D. 2: Schematic view of Choren process. The biomass is gasified in an entrained flow gasifier, facilitated 
by a rotary-type partial gasifier (stage 1). 
The Choren entrained-flow biomass gasifier is comprised of three stages that can be seen in Figure D. 
2 above. The first stage receives biomass in a horizontal stirred-type low-temperature reactor for pre-
gasification at 400-500°C in a limited supply of air. This produces solid char and a tar-rich volatile 
product. The latter flows into the second chamber (stage 2), an entrained-flow combustor, where 
oxygen and the product gas from the first stage are injected downward into the reactor. Combustion 
raises the temperature to 1300-1500°C and completely cracks the tar. The hot combustion product 
flows into the third chamber (stage 3), where the char is gasified. 
The solid char received from the first stage is pulverized and fed into the third stage of the Choren 
process. It is gasified in the hot gasification medium produced in the second stage. Endothermic 
gasification reactions reduce the temperature to about 800°C [1]. Char and ash from the product gas 
are separated and recycled into the second-stage combustor. The ash melts at the high temperature 
in the combustor and is drained from the bottom. Now the molten ash solidifies, forming a layer on 
the membrane wall that protects the wall against the corrosive action of fresh molten biomass ash. 
The product gas is processed downstream for thermal and power applications or Fischer-Tropsch 
synthesis.

INTRODUCTION 
Biomass fuels continue to play an important role both in the domestic and industrial sector in developing countries. 
To support isolated rural people with power, it is necessary to replace the use of traditional liquid powered systems, 
and inefficient traditional used of biomass with a producer gas as a sustainable green fuel. Tar content of producer 
gas is the major problem if it is used directly as fuel for engine. To solve this problem low-tar biomass gasifier has 
been developed at BGG, Chemical Engineering, DTU. 
 
METHODS 
Moving bed gasifiers with internal recycle of pyrolysis gas has been the basis of principle in this gasifier. This 
gasifier focuses on the concept of mixing the pyrolysis gases with the gasifying air and burning the mixture in an 
internally installed separate combustion chamber. The schematic diagram of this small-scale gasifier is shown in 
figure 1 and figure 2. The biomass (Danish pine wood chips) are filled up in to the gasifier and gasifier top is fitted 
tightly with the reactor. The released producer gases are burned afterwards by the torch.

Combustor; G, Ash grate; H, Ash bunker.  Figure 2. Gasifier prototype 
 
 
RESULTS & CONCLUSION 
Five tests were done for this experiment. The average gas production and yield of producer gas was found to be 
19.41 kg/h (wb) and 2.31 Nm3/kg, respectively. low tar content of 22.2 mg/Nm3 was observed. Producer gas with 
this low tar content has to be used directly to the engine for the production of heat and electricity. A lower heating 
value of 5.5 MJ/Nm3 was observed. The gasifier capacity was found to be 25.2 KW. The cold gas efficiency was 
found as 58% where thermal efficiency was found as 70% if producer gas has been used directly in thermal 
purpose. The air equivalence ratio was found as 0.272 which was close to the optimal value. So, it’s pointed out 
that this gasifier offers one of the most promising renewable energy production system. Finally, it is noted that, 
being comparatively easy to build with low cost materials, this gasifier could be the attractive developed technology 
for thermal and power applications, especially for developing countries.

Description 
Purpose 
The setup is used for gasification of biomass at high temperatures. The purpose of 
the setup is to study the characterization of gas composition of producer gas and 
tar content using wood pellets and wood chips by thermochemical process. The 
setup is also used to evaluate mass and energy balance to figure out gasifier 
capacity, gasification efficiency, energy efficiency and optimization of operation 
parameters.   
Setup Description: 
The gasification tests are carried outdoors. Wood chips with 32.28% moisture content are filled up into the 
gasifier. The gasifier reactor is heated externally by a LPG burner and gasifier is heated up gradually. The blower 
connected in the producer gas outlet pipe is turned on and hot air is sucked continuously and discharge it into 
the atmosphere.  Once the gasifier is heated at 800oC-900oC, the blower is detached from the outlet pipe. The 
biomass is filled up in to the gasifier and gasifier top is fitted tightly with the reactor. The released producer 
gases are burned afterwards by the flare. Figure 1 shows the Gasifier setup. 
A simplified operation procedure can be written as: 
➢ Blower and LPG burner setup: After placing the gasifier reactor in outside, blower is setup in the right 
place, and it can be turned on to suck hot air from the reactor. LPG burner is placed near the reactor 
and is fixed it with stand. The gas flow can be turned on and LPG burner can be ignited. 
➢ Warm-up: The LPG burner is ignited with a hand-held gas burner (Bernz-O-Matic TS 7000) and gasifier 
reactor is heated at 800oC-900oC. After reaching stabilize temperature, LPF burner and blower are 
detached from the gasifier which is represented by figure 1(a). The char bed of gasifier is filled up with 
glowing char and after few moments fresh cold char is put into the reactor char bed. Then whole 
reactor is filled up with the biomass. The valve of air supply system is then turned on to supply 
pressurized air in to the reactor. Gasifier temperature and pressure difference at different zone are 
monitored continuously with the help of thermocouple (T1-T6, T_top and T_outlet) and U-tube 
manometer, respectively.  
➢ Producer Gas: The producer gas is produced immediately, and the producer gas is ignited by the torch 
which is represented by figure 1(b).  
➢ Shut-down: Stopping the following order: 1) turned off the valve of air supply system to prior to cut off 
the pressurized air, 2) turn on the value of N2 supply (see figure 1(b)) in to the reactor.

Safety mechanisms: 
• 
The reactor is covered in isolated materials which prevent the personal damage. The non-covered 
areas are the flame of the LPG heater, pipe of producer gas outlet and flame of producer gas.  
• 
The personnel always wearing the hand gloves, safety shoes and safety glass in head when filling 
the biomass in to the gasifier. 
• 
The blower is sucking from the gas outlet pipe. The blower has a VLT and personnel is adjusting the 
rotation speed so that the suction is just enough to avoid smoke raising against the person filling 
biomass in the gasifier. If the person filling the biomass to the gasifier smells smoke, he will stop 
the filling and leave the gasifier immediately. 
• 
The setup is operated outside, and a personal CO detector is worn always during the test.  
• 
The gas flow for the LPG burner is controlled manually by a valve to control temperatures of 
reactor. Reactor temperature will not exceed over 1000°C because steel begins to soften and loses 
about half of its strength over 1000°C.  
• 
Top of the gasifier is tightened with the top cover by well-sealed materials, so it prevents to leak of 
toxic pyrolysis gases.   
• 
Security valve is installed to the top of gasifier so that it makes sure to avoid in any accidental 
problems.
