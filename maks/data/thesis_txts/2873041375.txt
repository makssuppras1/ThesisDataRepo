Abstract
This thesis presents the design and development of an autonomous paraglider, utilizing
a Pixhawk 4 flight controller to autonomously execute a mission plan. It is a work that
analyzes the capabilities of such a UAV by conducting both computer­simulated flights
and practical real­world flights, thereby bridging the gap between them. This work can
be divided into five discrete parts, which can be categorized as follows: 1. Modeling and
design of a paramotor, 2. Analysis of the PX4 Autopilot software stack, 3. Integration of a
paraglider vehicle into the PX4 Autopilot, including parameterization and development of
new modules, 4. Setting up the simulation environment, and finally, 5. presentation and
analysis of flight results. The primary objective of this paper is to create a simulation that
is accurate enough for testing purposes, while the secondary objective is to develop a ro­
bust controller that accurately follows positional and angular setpoints. The experimental
results demonstrate that the autonomous paramotor can effectively navigate through way­
points with stabilized altitude control. This paper highlights the potential of autonomous
paragliders as a viable alternative to traditional UAVs, particularly in applications requiring
energy efficiency and the ability to carry heavier payloads.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Autonomous aerial vehicles have seen widespread adoption in various domains, with
fixed­wing and rotary UAVs dominating both research and commercial applications. How­
ever, powered paragliders—or paramotors—represent a largely underexplored yet promis­
ing class of aerial platforms, given their simple mechanical structure, energy efficiency,
and potential for long­endurance missions [1]. Unlike fixed­wing aircraft or rotary drones,
paramotors use a flexible paraglider wing, also known as a canopy or parafoil, and a com­
pact propulsion unit to enable low­speed, low­altitude flight with high energy efficiency.
Despite these advantages, paramotors are not widely adopted, and the amount of aca­
demic and technical research is limited compared to fixed­wing or rotary UAVs [2]. Being
unpopular as a UAV solution means they also lack support from standard frameworks used
in the industry, making the entry barrier greater for researchers and developers aiming to
deploy autonomous paragliders for specific use cases. Another major barrier to the wider
adoption of paramotors in autonomous applications lies in their inherently complex flight
dynamics, sensitivity to wind conditions, and slow response time [3]. These limitations
have made robust autonomous operation of paramotors a difficult and often overlooked
challenge.
Nonetheless, the unique flight characteristics of paramotors open doors to a broad spec­
trum of mission­critical applications across diverse domains. In civil contexts, they offer
a compelling solution for persistent aerial surveying, infrastructure inspection, agricultural
monitoring, and low­cost communications relays, particularly in regions with limited ac­
cess or infrastructure [4]. Their compact form factor and minimal launch requirements
also make them valuable assets in humanitarian missions, such as rapid aid delivery
and search and rescue in disaster­affected regions [5, 6]. In the environmental sciences,
paramotors enable low­impact, long­endurance ecological monitoring, wildlife tracking,
and atmospheric data collection without disturbing sensitive ecosystems [7]. These di­
verse use cases underscore the potential superiority of paramotors in scenarios that de­
mand endurance, energy efficiency, and flexible deployment. Compared to fixed­wing
UAVs, which require runways or launch mechanisms, and multirotors, which are con­
strained by limited endurance and high power consumption, powered paragliders offer an
optimal balance of payload capacity, mission duration, and terrain independence [2].
1.2
Problem Statement
Despite the promising capabilities of powered paragliders, their integration into the broader
UAV ecosystem remains underdeveloped. One of the primary obstacles is the absence
of a standardized, open­source control architecture tailored to the dynamic behavior of
paragliders. Existing autopilot frameworks such as PX4 have been extensively devel­
oped and validated for multirotor and fixed­wing platforms, but they do not natively sup­
port the control paradigms or actuator configurations specific to flexible­wing aircraft like
paramotors [2].
This lack of built­in compatibility not only complicates the process of developing autonomous
paraglider systems but also discourages experimentation and innovation in this field.
Overcoming this barrier requires both the adaptation of existing frameworks and the vali­
dation of these adaptations through systematic simulation and real­world testing. Bridging

the gap between simulation and physical flight testing is essential for ensuring that novel
control strategies can be safely and efficiently evaluated before deployment.
1.3
Objectives and Scope
The primary objective of this thesis is to explore and validate the use of PX4 Autopilot—a
widely adopted, modular flight control framework—for the control of a powered paraglider.
The work is structured around three key goals:
• PX4 Integration: Analyze and document the PX4 software architecture in detail, fo­
cusing on how to extend its fixed­wing control pipeline to support powered paraglid­
ers through appropriate control allocation, actuator configuration, and flight mode
adaptation [8].
• Simulation Environment Development: Build a high­fidelity simulation environ­
ment using PX4 SITL, Gazebo, ROS 2, and Micro XRCE­DDS to test autonomous
waypoint navigation for powered paragliders. The aerodynamic characteristics of
the test platform are derived and approximated based on published models [7] [3,
9].
• Model Validation and Analysis: Validate the fidelity of the dynamic model and
control implementation by comparing simulation outcomes against real­world flight
test data. Highlight the limitations of using fixed­wing controllers for flexible­wing
platforms and propose control customization strategies for improved tracking per­
formance.
This thesis is focused on achieving stable autonomous waypoint navigation under nominal
wind conditions using a lightweight paramotor. It does not address obstacle avoidance,
adaptive control, or high­wind disturbances.
1.4
Thesis Structure
The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows:
• Chapter 2 – Modeling and Design: Introduces the principles of powered paraglider
flight, describes the physical test system, and develops a mathematical model of the
dynamics suitable for control and simulation.
• Chapter 3 – PX4 Autopilot: Describes the PX4 software stack, including its ar­
chitecture, control loops, and actuator mappings. The chapter explains how PX4’s
fixed­wing flight stack is leveraged and adapted for controlling a powered paraglider.
• Chapter 4 – Simulation: Details the implementation of a simulation framework
using PX4 SITL, Gazebo, QGroundControl, ROS 2, and Micro XRCE­DDS. It also
discusses the aerodynamic modeling and simulation validation process.
• Chapter 5 – Results: Presents and analyzes the results from both simulation and
real­world test flights. The chapter compares performance metrics and identifies
gaps between model predictions and observed behavior.
• Chapter 6 – Conclusion and Future Work: Summarizes the key findings and
contributions of the thesis and outlines possible directions for future improvements
in autonomous powered paraglider systems.

2.1

This chapter presents the theoretical and practical foundations of the powered paraglider
system developed in this thesis [10]. It provides a general overview of powered paraglid­
ers, including their structure and flight behavior. Additionally, the paraglider used in the
experiments is described in detail, including its airframe, propulsion system, sensors, and

Finally, the chapter discusses important design considerations related to autonomy and
control, including actuation constraints and dynamic stability characteristics.
2.2
Overview of Powered Paraglider Systems
2.2.1
Structure and Components
Powered paragliders, often referred to as paramotors, consist of a flexible canopy (also
known as a parafoil), a suspension system, and a payload module typically housing the
propulsion unit and electronics (in the case of an autonomous paramotor). The canopy is
usually constructed from lightweight fabric shaped into an airfoil profile via a series of ribs
and cells. Unlike rigid­wing aircraft, the aerodynamic shape of the canopy is maintained
entirely by internal pressure and airflow, resulting in a dynamically flexible flight surface
[11].
The suspension system includes a network of risers and lines that connect the canopy
to the payload. These lines distribute aerodynamic loads and serve as control surfaces
by modulating the canopy surface through servo­actuated brake lines. The payload of
an autonomous paramotor consists of an electric motor with a propeller, flight control
electronics, sensors, and communication modules. Unlike traditional aircraft, paramotors
are launched on foot (see figure 2.1 or with minimal takeoff assistance, making them
particularly useful for operations in remote or undeveloped areas [2][4].
Figure 2.1: Launch of a paraglider on foot
2.2.2
Basic Principles of Flight
The flight mechanics of powered paragliders differ significantly from those of fixed­wing
or rotary aircraft. The flexible canopy generates lift primarily through ram­air inflation and
forward motion. Forward thrust is provided by the motorized propeller, which induces
relative airflow over the canopy. Steering is achieved through asymmetric brake inputs,
which alter the trailing edge shape on one side of the canopy, inducing both roll and

Costello, 2003). Apart from asymmetric brake deflection to control roll and yaw, pulling
both brake lines symmetrically induces a pitch­up moment, as shown in Figure 2.2.

motor, the center of mass will move forward, and if brakes are applied again, it will cause
the paraglider to pitch up again. This behavior of pitch oscillating can be seen in figure 2.3

2.3.1
Wing and Suspension System
The paraglider wing employed in this work is a small­scale ram­air parafoil with a surface
area of 0.5 m2. It is constructed from nylon and features multiple internal cells that inflate
during forward motion, forming a stable airfoil profile. The wing is suspended from the
payload via the suspension lines, organized into groups. These lines include left and
right brake risers, which terminate at the trailing edge of the canopy and are connected
to the servos, allowing them to be controlled.
Pulling on the brake lines, to alter the aerodynamic profile to induce roll, yaw, and pitch,
is performed by two high­torque digital servomotors [6], each connected to a brake riser.
The servos are rigidly mounted to the body frame as shown in ?? and receive PWM
commands via a breakout board interfaced with the Pixhawk 4 flight controller.
2.3.2
Payload and Propulsion Unit
The propulsion system initially used a 1400kv brushless motor, selected to provide suf­
ficient thrust for sustained flight. However, after increasing the weight of the payload by
appending a 3D­printed case for the hardware, the motor was replaced with a 2200kv
brushless motor. The motor is driven by a compatible electronic speed controller (ESC)
and powered by a 4S LiPo battery. This configuration results in efficient powered climbs
and navigation at low speeds.

2.3.3
Sensors and Electronics
The system’s electronics and hardware are centered around the Pixhawk 4 flight con­
troller, running PX4 open­source firmware.
The controller features integrated inertial
measurement units (IMUs), a magnetometer, and a barometric pressure sensor. Sup­
plementary electronic components include:
• GPS: u­blox M9N module, providing high­rate position and velocity data via UART.
• Telemetry: 915 MHz Holybro telemetry module for bidirectional MAVLink commu­
nication.
• RC Receiver: FrSky receiver paired with a Taranis transmitter for manual override
and tuning.
• Power Module: Standard Pixhawk­compatible unit for voltage and current sensing.
• PWM Breakout Board: Holybro bridge for routing PWM signals from the Pixhawk
to the servos and ESC.
All sensor data is fused onboard using PX4’s extended Kalman filter (EKF2), which esti­
mates the system’s position, velocity, and attitude in real­time. Flight data is logged both
onboard and externally via MAVLink messages for post­flight analysis. The system archi­
tecture is designed for rapid prototyping, modular testing, and debugging, with interfaces
available for both QGroundControl and ROS 2 during simulation and field trials.
2.4
Mathematical Modeling
The development of a mathematical model for the paraglider is essential for simulation,
control design, and performance analysis. Due to the flexible­wing structure and coupled
translational and rotational dynamics, modeling a powered paraglider is significantly more
complex than for rigid­body UAVs. The mathematical model used assumes a simplified
6­DOF model, where the canopy and payload are treated as a single rigid body. This
section presents the kinematic and dynamic modeling of a powered paraglider, along with
its control allocation, which utilizes motors as control surfaces.
2.4.1
Assumptions and Simplifications
To make real­time control feasible and reduce the model complexity, the following as­
sumptions are applied:
• The canopy and payload are treated as a single rigid body, neglecting suspension
line dynamics.
• Wind is assumed constant, and turbulence is neglected.
These simplifications are consistent with prior works on parafoil modeling [3], [9] [12] and
allow integration with standard autopilot firmware such as PX4.

2.11
Design Considerations for Autonomy
Developing an autonomous control system for a powered paraglider presents unique chal­
lenges due to the platform’s inherent flexibility, underactuation, and slow response dynam­
ics. Unlike fixed­wing or rotary UAVs, powered paragliders are typically controlled using
a single pair of trailing­edge brake lines. This results in only two degrees of actuation—
left and right brake inputs, which simultaneously influence roll, pitch, and yaw due to the
coupled aerodynamics of the flexible canopy.
Furthermore, the paraglider’s large surface area and low wing loading make it highly sen­
sitive to disturbances [12]. The system exhibits:
• Slow longitudinal dynamics, resulting in delayed pitch and forward speed re­
sponses.
• High lateral coupling, where brake inputs generate both roll and yaw with minimal
decoupling.
• Limited damping, which can lead to oscillatory motion, especially during step inputs
or abrupt maneuvers.

3.2
Controllers
In general, PX4 has developed different controllers for multicopters, fixed­wing, and VTOL
vehicles. In this section, the fixed­wing control stack will be explained, as it serves as the
foundation for the tests conducted with the powered paraglider. This means that the con­
trol loops remained the same as those of the fixed­wing plane but with necessary modi­
fications to the parameters used in these controllers to accommodate the dynamics and
actuator layout of the powered paraglider. The reason for choosing fixed­wing controllers
for the paraglider use case is that they share two common traits: they generate lift from
forward motion and use flaps to change their heading.
3.2.1
Lateral Navigation Controller
For fixed­wing vehicles to follow paths, such as waypoints and loiters, a control loop with a
guidance algorithm, such as the L1 guidance law or Nonlinear Path Following Guidance
(NPFG), is used. Before diving into the specifics of these two controllers, we should
first specify the need for such a controller and its role in the overall control architecture.
The lateral navigation controller, which is part of the position controller, must output a roll
setpoint that the attitude controller uses to steer the vehicle toward the desired location.
The L1 guidance law, as implemented in PX4, is used for smooth and reliable trajec­
tory tracking in autonomous fixed­wing aircraft [14]. The core principle introduces a look­
ahead point along the desired path at a fixed distance, also known as the L1 distance,
and calculates the lateral acceleration required to steer the vehicle toward that point. This
approach ensures convergence to both straight­line and circular paths with minimal error
and stable dynamics, even under varying speeds [14]. PX4’s implementation of the L1
controller builds on this theoretical foundation and incorporates ideas developed by the
ArduPilot community (Paul Riseborough, Brandon Jones, and Andrew Tridgell, L1 control
for APM). These ideas included 1) explicit control over frequency and damping, 2) ex­
plicit control over track capture angle, and 3) the ability to use a loiter radius smaller than
L1 length. These ideas were implemented as modifications to the existing L1 controller,
Design and Development of an Autonomous Paraglider
15
==PAGE:21==
which included explicit tuning parameters for the guidance loop’s period and damping.
This allowed for more precise control over system responsiveness and stability. Addition­
ally, the controller was modified to support a loiter radius smaller than the L1 distance
through proportional­derivative (PD) control, improving flexibility in tight maneuvering. Fi­
nally, another improvement is the ability to directly define the track capture angle, en­
hancing trajectory capture behavior in constrained airspace or during sharp turns. These
refinements make L1 a highly effective guidance law for a wide range of UAV missions,
particularly in fixed­wing configurations. However, its reliance on a fixed look­ahead ge­
ometry limits its adaptability in extreme wind conditions or low­speed scenarios where
nonlinear, wind­aware alternatives like NPFG provide superior performance.
The Nonlinear Path Following Guidance (NPFG) controller implemented in PX4 repre­
sents a significant advancement over the traditional L1 guidance law, particularly for low­
speed aircraft operating in strong or variable wind conditions, thus making it an effective
control mechanism for a paraglider as well. Building on the work of Stastny and Sieg­
wart [15], NPFG addresses the fundamental limitation of L1: its assumption that the ve­
hicle’s airspeed is always sufficient to overcome wind and reach the desired trajectory.
This assumption is no longer valid in conditions where wind speeds exceed or closely
match the vehicle’s airspeed, causing the vehicle to diverge from the path and, in worst­
case scenarios, fail the mission due to the inability to follow the path accurately. NPFG
introduces a curvature­based trajectory tracking strategy that optimizes the lateral accel­
eration command based on real­time wind and kinematic feasibility analysis. Unlike L1,
which maintains a constant look­ahead distance and produces fixed path curvature, NPFG
continuously adjusts the path geometry using nonlinear control laws that consider air­
craft dynamics, heading feasibility, and energy efficiency. The work by Stastny [2] further
refines this framework by integrating wind­aware constraints and proposing local trajec­
tory re­planning strategies for obstacle avoidance and terrain interaction, making NPFG
a robust and adaptable solution for modern UAV navigation. For powered paragliders—
characterized by low airspeeds, slow response, and high wind sensitivity—NPFG offers
improved path­tracking reliability and mission robustness under challenging environmen­
tal conditions.
3.2.2
Total Energy Control System
Part of the position controller’s work is to orchestrate the longitudinal control of the vehicle,
and for that, it uses the Total Energy Control System (TECS). TECS aims to control the true
airspeed and height; therefore, it receives as input an airspeed setpoint and an altitude
setpoint and generates throttle and pitch setpoints as outputs. These two outputs are
then passed into the fixed­wing attitude controller, which implements the attitude control
solution. To understand how TECS works, it is essential to explain why PX4 chose to
decouple this controller into two independent control loops: one for throttle and another
for pitch.
A change in pitch or throttle affects both airspeed and altitude. For example, when the
pitch increases, the airspeed decreases, and the altitude increases. Conversely, an in­
crease in throttle produces an increase in both airspeed and altitude due to increased lift.
To simplify the control system, PX4 represented the original setpoints as energy terms in
two independent control loops. As also described in the PX4 documentation, the total en­
ergy of an aircraft is the sum of its kinetic (speed) and potential (height) energies. Given
arbitrary combinations of potential and kinetic energies, one can achieve a total energy
state. That being said, thrust can be used to regulate the total energy of the vehicle,
whereas pitch can be used to maintain a balance between kinetic and potential energy.

3.2.3

PX4 employs a cascaded control architecture for fixed­wing attitude stabilization, consist­

This design supports high modularity and simplifies tuning across different aircraft types,
including adaptations for platforms without conventional aerodynamic control surfaces—
such as powered paragliders.

Airspeed Dependency and Open­Loop Behavior:
When a calibrated airspeed sensor is available, the controller dynamically adjusts the PI
gains in the rate loop based on the measured true airspeed VT . This scaling compensates
for changes in control surface authority that occur across varying flight speeds, where
lower airspeeds result in reduced aerodynamic response to deflection inputs. The gain
scheduling is typically linear with respect to airspeed and is defined by user­configurable
parameters, such as FW_ARSP_SCALE and FW_AIRSPD_TRIM.
In the absence of airspeed sensing, as is often the case with lightweight platforms like
powered paragliders, the attitude controller falls back to fixed (pre­scaled) gain values.
This effectively turns the control into an open­loop system with respect to airspeed, mean­
ing that the control surface deflection effectiveness is assumed to be constant. While this
simplifies implementation, it can degrade control precision across a wide envelope of flight
conditions, particularly during climb or descent phases where apparent airspeed changes
significantly due to throttle or brake input.
Feedforward Compensation:
To improve response characteristics, PX4 includes feedforward terms in the rate control
loop. These directly scale the angular velocity setpoints to generate actuator commands
without waiting for error accumulation. This is especially useful in aircraft with slow re­
sponse or high damping characteristics, helping to counteract known system dynamics
preemptively.

3.2.4
Control Allocation
The control allocation framework was initially introduced to replace the static mixers used
in PX4, and it was an attempt to align with the highly modular and dynamic system it
provides. More specifically, they decoupled mixing from the attitude/rate controller, and
the core controllers no longer require special handling for each airframe geometry. The
outputs of torque and thrust setpoints from the rate controller specifically are fed into the
control allocator module, which translates them into actuator setpoints. In other words,
mapping abstract control commands, such as roll, pitch, yaw, and throttle, into specific
actuator outputs in the form of PWM signals occurs in the control allocator.
A linear model governs this mapping:
τ = F · δ
Where τ represents the vector of requested torques and thrust from the controller, δ is the
vector of actuator deflections (e.g., servo positions), and F is the actuator effectiveness
matrix. Each element Fij of the matrix quantifies how effective actuator j is at generating
control effort along axis i (roll, pitch, yaw, or thrust).
PX4 obtains F from the ActuatorEffectiveness library, which defines effectiveness pro­
files based on the configured airframe type. For instance, a flying wing with two elevons
will define roll effectiveness as ±0.5, meaning that the combined deflection of the elevons
can generate a unit roll torque when acting in opposition. These values represent relative
scaling between actuators, not absolute torque production.
To compute the actuator deflections necessary to realize a desired control effort, PX4
inverts the actuator effectiveness matrix using a Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse:
δ = F−1 · τ
This inverse operation distributes the control effort across all contributing actuators in a
least­squares optimal way. More effective actuators (with higher normalized effective­
ness) will deflect less, and the matrix inversion allows automatic compensation for control
20
Design and Development of an Autonomous Paraglider
==PAGE:26==
surface interactions. For example, if rudder deflection induces undesired roll, the allocator
can preemptively add aileron correction to cancel it out.
Effectiveness Normalization:
The values defined in the control effectiveness matrix
are normalized—usually with respect to the most effective actuator or to the maximum
torque the entire system can deliver. This means that a value of 1.0 corresponds to the
actuator (or actuator combination) that can generate the greatest torque per unit deflec­
tion, and other values are scaled relative to this. For example, if a left elevon generates
twice the pitching moment of a right elevon due to moment arm differences, their pitch
effectiveness values might be 1.0 and 0.5, respectively.
Significantly, this normalization does not affect control authority. Instead, it informs the
allocator how to proportion deflections among available actuators. The total available
torque is scaled as needed by actuator limits (typically defined in ±1 range), and the re­
sulting control signals are passed to the mixer.
Application to Powered Paragliders:
In the context of powered paragliders, actua­
tors such as the left and right brake lines and the motor throttle do not align directly with
conventional fixed­wing surfaces. Yet, by defining a custom actuator effectiveness con­
figuration, PX4 can treat asymmetric brake inputs as sources of yaw torque, symmetric
brake inputs as sources of pitch torque and throttle as a thrust and pitch­affecting input.
Control allocation allows this flexibility without modifying the underlying controller logic.

Middleware
PX4 features a robust middleware infrastructure that supports internal and external com­

Each uORB topic in PX4 represents a specific message type (e.g., vehicle_imu) and sup­
ports multiple publishers and subscribers. When a module subscribes to a topic, uORB al­
locates a dedicated ring buffer (a circular queue) for that subscriber to manage latency and
synchronization. New messages are pushed into this buffer as they are published, allow­
ing the subscriber to process them in a first­in, first­out (FIFO) order. However, because
the buffer has a fixed size(although it can be changed via the ORB_QUEUE_LENGTH
constant), subscribers that fail to consume messages in time may miss updates due to
overwriting. This mechanism ensures efficient memory use while maintaining timely data
delivery, which is critical for reactive behavior in real­time flight control systems. PX4
modules operate as independent processes scheduled by the system scheduler, allow­
ing them to run at different rates depending on update frequency and priority. For exam­
ple, the navigator is updated less frequently compared to modules subscribed to drivers,
such as IMU drivers. A significant advantage of utilizing uORB for handling inter­process
communication and message synchronization is that it enables loose coupling between
system components. This modular architecture greatly simplifies the extension of PX4 to
new vehicle types, such as powered paragliders, where core navigation and estimation
logic can be reused, and only the actuator and control interfaces require customization.

Mavlink
MAVLink is a lightweight, open communication protocol that facilitates data exchange be­
tween the PX4 flight stack and external systems such as ground control stations (GCS)
and companion computers. Within PX4, the MAVLink module serves as a bridge be­
tween internal uORB topics and external MAVLink messages, enabling real­time telemetry
transmission, mission command reception, and parameter configuration. Telemetry data,
including attitude, GPS position, battery status, and mission progress, are streamed to
QGroundControl, which also supports mission planning and in­flight parameter tuning.
Conversely, when MAVLink messages are received from the ground control station (e.g.,
flight commands, parameter updates, mission uploads), these are interpreted and pub­

external systems. Additionally, PX4 implemented the events interface, which is an API for
notifying users of events. These events are also published via the MAVLink events service
to the ground control station, delivering structured event messages and enabling aware­
ness of the flight status, warnings, and failures. MAVLink is integral to both real flight and
simulation (SITL), as it serves as a handy tool for monitoring system performance in real­
time, tuning controllers, accessing sensor data, and tracking and assessing the vehicle’s
flight behavior in general.

Gazebo
Gazebo is an open­source robotics simulator that supports realistic physics, sensor mod­
eling, and 3D rendering. It is particularly well­suited for developing and testing autonomous
aerial systems in a controlled, repeatable environment. Gazebo operates using an ex­
tensible plugin architecture and uses the Simulation Description Format (SDF) to define
models, environments, and their interactions. The simulator supports multiple physics en­
gines, such as ODE, Bullet, and DART, with ODE being the default and most commonly
used. Through its integration with PX4 and ROS 2, Gazebo enables real­time sensor
simulation, dynamic actuation, and hardware­in­the­loop compatibility, making it ideal for
testing autonomous behaviors prior to deployment in real­world conditions. In this sec­
tion, the decisions behind the structure of the SDF files for the paraglider model and the
simulation world are discussed in detail, along with the physical and sensor plugins used
to achieve realistic behavior.

barometer) and actuators (e.g., servos, motors), allowing PX4 to operate as if it were con­

4.3.3
Control Allocation Modifications
To support a novel vehicle type, such as a paraglider, within PX4’s control allocation sys­
tem, a series of code­level modifications and definitions must be introduced. This enables
the PX4 flight stack to map desired body torques and thrust into actuator commands as
discussed in chapter ??, in a vehicle­specific way. The following steps outline the com­
plete integration pipeline.
The first step involves extending the enumeration of supported airframe types within the
parameter system. In the module.yaml file (typically located in src/modules/control_allocator/),
a new entry must be appended to the CA_AIRFRAME parameter, assigning a unique ID to
the new vehicle type. This enumeration enables PX4 to distinguish between different
effectiveness models at runtime (see Listing A.4).
Each actuator’s influence on the roll, pitch, yaw, and spoiler is configured using a per­
actuator list of torque and control parameters. The range of allowed values for these
parameters is declared in the same configuration file module.yaml, allowing per­servo
definitions for torque in each axis(see Listing A.5).
The mixer entry for the new airframe should then be added to the airframe mixer defini­
tions, which are found again in the same configuration file (see Listing A.5). The defini­
tions declared in the mixer entry are also what QGroundControl uses to show the actuator
configuration, as shown in figure 4.9.
Figure 4.9: Actuator configuration for the paraglider vehicle type
To accurately reflect the unique control behavior of a paraglider system, it is necessary to
define a new actuator effectiveness matrix and, consequently, an allocation matrix, rather
than relying on the existing fixed­wing allocation matrix. Although both vehicle types share
the same actuator types —namely, two servos and a motor —their aerodynamic effects
differ significantly. For instance, in fixed­wing aircraft, servos actuate discrete control
34
Design and Development of an Autonomous Paraglider
==PAGE:40==
surfaces such as ailerons, elevators, or rudders with predictable, decoupled effects. In
contrast, brake line deflections in a paraglider generate coupled responses across multiple
axes: symmetric deflection induces pitch, while asymmetric deflection causes both yaw
and roll.
Moreover, in a paraglider, the motor not only produces forward thrust but can also induce
pitch torque due to its position relative to the center of mass. These behaviors are not
accommodated by the standard ActuatorEffectivenessFixedWing logic.
To support these dynamics, apart from adding new control surface types (see Listing A.5)
and updating the mixer configuration, a new subclass of ActuatorEffectiveness should
be implemented, directly linked to the new airframe type, the paraglider.
The new ActuatorEffectivenessParaglider class is required to define how actuator
commands map to body torques and forces. This class must override core methods,
such as getEffectivenessMatrix() and allocateAuxiliaryControls (), to compute
the control effectiveness based on parameters tailored to the paraglider.
Additionally, this new handler must be registered in the ControlAllocator to ensure cor­
rect instantiation at runtime (see Listing A.8).
Through this approach, PX4’s control allocation framework can be extended to handle the
coupled and unconventional control dynamics of paragliders, enabling a more realistic and
stable control in simulation and flight performance, mainly because the allocation matrix
is more representative for the case of a paraglider.

Results
This chapter presents the experimental validation and performance evaluation of the au­
tonomous powered paraglider system developed for this study. The results are structured
to compare simulation­based predictions with actual flight test outcomes, providing insight
into the effectiveness of the control strategy utilized. Both simulated and real flight perfor­
mances are analyzed with a focus on trajectory tracking, disturbance rejection, and overall
guidance behavior. This analysis will primarily base its insights on simulation testing, due
to the limited trials and, therefore, poor data collection in the outdoor environment. A
consideration for the flight tests is that the flight stack used corresponds to the fixed­wing
vehicle, with modifications to the airframe configuration file (see parameters used in Ta­
ble A.3) and the different parameters used to tune the control behavior closer to that of a
paraglider system. Part of this analysis involves experimenting with the allocation matrix
to improve performance in the simulation. The mission plan analyzed in both simulation

5.3
Sim vs Real: Comparison and Analysis
In this final section of the results chapter, the observed differences and similarities be­
tween the simulation environment and the real flight data are discussed, providing insight
into the simulation fidelity and the extent of the sim­to­real gap.
Trajectory and Path Following
In both environments, the guidance controller success­
fully followed the loiter pattern. In simulation, the cross­track error remained under 2
meters even with wind, while in real flight, the error occasionally exceeded 3 meters,
particularly during wind disturbances. This discrepancy can be attributed to unmodeled
realistic environmental conditions, as it is observed that wind speed appears to oscillate
much more aggressively in real flight. Additionally, modeling the paraglider in the simu­
lation and specifying its flight dynamics can introduce a slightly different flight behavior
compared to testing in real conditions. Additionally, another reason for discrepancies can
be potential delays in the real system’s response due to hardware constraints or com­
munication latencies. This argument can be supported by Figure 5.19, which compares
telemetry metrics between real and simulated flights, showing that communication quality
was notably degraded in the real flight compared to the simulation. The implications of ac­
cumulating communication delays, telemetry dropouts, and estimator uncertainty in real
flight can result in inaccurate control responses. More specifically, these communication
delays can cause overshoot and oscillations due to lagged corrections as well as reduced
tracking accuracy.

(b) Simulated telemetry performance.

• Persistent Message Loss Rate: A non­zero ‘rx_message_lost_rate‘ confirms a

main higher and less stable than in simulation, indicating lower estimator confidence,
especially horizontal variance is significant and very unstable.
In contrast, the simulation (Figure 5.19b) shows clean telemetry with zero overruns and

Altitude Regulation
Altitude control was closely matched between simulation and re­
ality. The TECS controller maintained height within a tight envelope in both cases. How­
ever, small amplitude oscillations appeared more frequently in real flight, potentially due
to propeller­induced disturbances or airframe flexibility. This problem also states the im­
portance of a new custom control allocation and control logic explicitly refined for the case
of a paraglider. Due to its lower center of gravity compared to a fixed­wing vehicle and a
slower response rate, motor­generated thrust introduces high pitch variations.
Attitude and Control Response
The pitch­motor coupling behavior was consistently
reproduced across environments. More specifically, decreasing the motor throttle causes
the paraglider’s pitch to decrease, and similarly, increasing the throttle causes the pitch to
increase. However, roll behavior differed significantly, especially under wind. Real­world
behavior involved more abrupt changes in brake inputs and less predictable roll response,
indicating limitations in actuator authority and brake asymmetry.
Wind Disturbance and Heading Behavior
Simulated wind effects induced crab angles
and heading offsets that were similar in trend to those observed in real flights. However,
real wind introduced higher variability and unsteady behavior. The heading tracking, while
conceptually similar, revealed slower correction and greater lag in the real system. Crab
angles were higher overall, suggesting real disturbances were more challenging to over­
come.
Sources of Mismatch
Based on the analysis conducted above, the main issues that
widen the sim2real gap and require further work are the following:
• Unmodeled disturbances (gusts, thermals, turbulence)
• Sensor noise and estimation drift in a real environment
• Differences in airframe dynamics not fully captured in the SDF model
Despite these differences, the simulation proved valuable for tuning and validating key
behaviors.

Conclusions and Future Work
6.1
Summary of Contributions
This thesis presents a comprehensive workflow for designing, implementing, and validat­
ing an autonomous paraglider system, utilizing a Pixhawk 4 flight controller and the PX4
Autopilot framework. One major contribution of this work is that by combining rigid­body
dynamics with lift and drag coefficients derived from the analysis in XFLR5 and supple­
mented by empirical data, a solid foundation was established for controller design and
simulation fidelity. The extraction of key parameters—including mass properties, inertia
tensors, and servo characteristics—was achieved through precise CAD measurements,
ensuring that the physical system could be as closely replicated as possible in the simu­
lated environment.
Building upon this model, the second contribution involves modifying fixed­wing flight
stack to facilitate flexible­wing control into the existing PX4 architecture. This required
a redefinition of the actuator mixer to support brake­line inputs, adjustment of the thrust­
elevator mixer, and retuning of the PID gains in the thrust, elevation, and attitude control
loops. Extensive software­in­the­loop experiments guided the selection of control gains
that eventually yielded satisfactory results.
The third and very crucial advancement is the establishment of a simulation environ­
ment that unites PX4 SITL, Gazebo, ROS 2, and Micro XRCE DDS under a unified soft­
ware infrastructure. A detailed SDF model incorporating the paraglider canopy, suspen­
sion lines, and payload geometry was constructed, and Gazebo plugins were adapted
to emulate flexible￿wing aerodynamics. This platform supported mission planning, soft­
ware￿in￿the￿loop testing, and rapid prototyping of guidance laws, all within a repro­
ducible framework.
Finally, a series of waypoint navigation experiments, conducted in both simulation and
outdoor flight trials, validate the system’s performance. In simulation, cross￿track errors
remained below two meters over multiple loiter loops, while real￿world flights exhibited
trajectory deviations under three meters despite wind perturbations and minor sensor de­
lays. Altitude maintenance was similarly robust, with deviations contained within one me­
ter of the setpoint at all times. These results confirm the feasibility of leveraging low￿cost
hardware and open￿source autopilot software to achieve reliable autonomous navigation
with a paraglider platform.
6.2
Limitations
Despite the encouraging outcomes, the work reported here is subject to several limita­
tions. Foremost, it was obvious the need for a unique flight stack for paragliders, as
the fixed­wing flight stack introduced limitations. Paraglider’s dynamics differ significantly
from the ones found in fixed­wing aircrafts, therefore the control of it was not optimal.
Aerodynamic coefficients were obtained from XFLR5 analyses and literature values rather
than through wind￿tunnel measurements or in￿flight system identification. As a result,
the stall characteristics and nonlinear lift￿curve slopes at high angles of attack remain
approximate. Similarly, the assumption of steady, uniform wind in simulation does not ac­
count for the spatial and temporal variability of natural wind fields, including vertical drafts
and gust fronts. In outdoor trials, these environmental factors manifested as sporadic de­
viations and controller oscillations that the current guidance law and estimator could not
48
Design and Development of an Autonomous Paraglider
==PAGE:54==
fully mitigate. Therefore, in order to improve th simulation realism these problems should
be addressed.
Another, limitation specific to this thesis was related to the weather. Not many outdoor test
cases were conducted due to rain, wind, and time limited to this semester work, therefore
making it harder to analyze further the real world flight behavior.
6.3
Recommendations for Improvement
To advance the robustness of the autonomous paraglider system, a huge improvement
would be to have onboard wind estimation to enable wind￿aware guidance. The fusion of
airspeed, ground speed, and attitude measurements can support real￿time wind vector
estimation, which in turn allows the guidance law to adapt heading commands to com­
pensate for crosswinds and gusts. Coupling this capability with a new custom control al­
locator, specifically developed for the case of the paraglider, that accounts for brake￿line
coupling effects on roll, pitch, and yaw would reduce actuator saturation and improve
trajectory tracking under challenging conditions.
In the simulation domain, incorporating atmospheric turbulence models and thermal up­
draft representations in Gazebo would better emulate real atmospheric conditions, thereby
supporting more realistic SITL testing. Additionally, a continuous tuning of the aerody­
namic parameters should happen in light of new data and to further improve the dynamics
of such complex aerial vehicle in a simulated environment.
6.4
Future Work
Extensive field testing across a diverse range of environmental conditions is essential to
validate and refine the system fully. A structured flight campaign should include missions
flown in varying wind speeds, directions, and thermal profiles, with systematic logging of
sensor data and vehicle responses. This dataset would support iterative tuning of both the
dynamic model and control parameters, closing the loop between simulation and reality.
Research into adaptive and learning￿based control methods offers a promising avenue
for enhancing autonomy. Techniques such as model reference adaptive control (MRAC)
or reinforcement learning (RL) could enable the paraglider to adjust its control gains ,
compensating for parameter uncertainties, canopy wear, or changing payload configu­
rations. Additionally, the integration of vision or LiDAR sensors would enable obstacle
detection and avoidance, opening up the possibility of autonomous missions in cluttered
environments, such as urban canyons or forested areas.
By addressing these directions, future work will further bridge the gap between simula­
tion and field performance, advancing the state of the art in flexible­wing UAV autonomy
and unlocking energy­efficient, low­cost aerial platforms for a wide range of scientific and
commercial applications.
