Abstract
This paper explores the uses of Unreal Engine 5 as an indoor daylight assessment tool,
in order to evaluate the game engine’s potential usefulness during the design process in
architecture.
During the writing of this report, a plethora of different sources and documentations were
examined, regarding light propagation in space, daylight analysis methods, as well as
ways to visualize those in computers. This means that a lot of emphasis was given into
exploring computer graphics methodologies and algorithms, such as ray­tracing and the
rendering equation.
The argument we are trying to resolve, involves coming to a conclusion about the poten­
tial use of game engines in architecture, which have become increasingly powerful and
realistic in recent years, due to the rapid evolution and development of new technologies.
There will also be comparisons to other, widely used software for lighting analysis, in order
to evaluate the potential benefits of using game engines during the design process.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In this day and age, the climate crisis is universally acknowledged as a pressing concern.
Notably, the Architectural, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) sector stands account­
able for a substantial 37% of the global carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions1. Recognizing
the importance for limiting these emissions, considerable efforts have been dedicated to
this cause in recent times.
Within the lifecycle of a building, different factors contribute to CO2 emissions, with energy
efficiency being one of the most prevalent ones. In the realm of lighting, the adoption of
daylighting is one of the practices used to address this.
Daylighting in architecture is a deliberate strategy aimed at optimizing a building’s ex­
posure to natural sunlight. Beyond its role in reducing the reliance on artificial lighting
and associated energy consumption throughout the lifecycle, daylighting also significantly
contributes to the occupants’ well­being2.
The customary approach to conducting daylight analyses for new or existing buildings
requires engineers utilizing simulation tools such as Radiance3, Ladybug4 for Grasshop­
per5 and DiaLux EVO6, among others. Despite the established use and validation of these
tools for light propagation simulations, the drawback lies in the time investment required
for their computations. This is particularly important in engineering practices, where the
rapid development of different design iterations is very important during the early design
phase.
As a result, there is a growing demand for more accurate and time­eficcient software
and technologies for building simulations. Alongside the rapid advancements in com­
puter graphics in recent years, especially by NVidia’s RTX series Graphics Processing
Units (GPUs) since 2018, several gaming engines have emerged boasting unprecedented
photorealism. This paper seeks to explore the feasibility of leveraging one such gaming
engine, specifically Epic Games’ Unreal Engine 5’7, to perform real­time daylight analysis
simulations.
1.2
Objectives of the Study
The primary objective of this study is to evaluate the capabilities of Unreal Engine 5 and
explore its potential utility as a daylight analysis tool. The study aims to comprehensively
examine the features and functionalities of Unreal Engine 5 to assess its performance in
accurately depicting lighting scenarios.
Furthermore, an in­depth examination of various techniques and methodologies com­
monly employed in lighting simulations and analyses will be performed. This involves
an analysis of Unreal Engine’s approach to light propagation simulations, using meth­
ods such as Ray­Tracing. To achieve this, a historical exploration of advancements in
computer graphics will be conducted, exploring the link between lighting simulations and
computer­generated images.
Lastly, we will touch on the difference between quantitative and qualitative analyses re­
garding lighting, how each of those can help guide the design process in their own way
and how Unreal Engine can be leveraged to perform them.

1.3
Scope and Limitations
Regarding the study’s limitations, they mostly revolve around hardware and software con­
straints. The intricate nature of light as a phenomenon, as will be explained later, requires
computationally intensive solutions that demand sophisticated hardware for timely and
efficient results. Unreal Engine 5, being a resource­intensive software, proves to be chal­
lenging for older systems to use.
Moreover, an additional constraint arises from the subjective nature of light perception,
which will be further investigated in subsequent sections. It is noteworthy that human
perception of light can exhibit considerable variability among individuals, as evidenced
by differences in preferences for optimal lighting conditions during various occupational
tasks.

Basics of Light
2.1
Nature of Light
Light, the fundamental entity that enables vision, is a fascinating phenomenon with diverse
properties that range from its spectral composition to the intricacies of human perception.
Exploring the properties of light involves delving into its various spectrums, understand­
ing how the human eye perceives these spectrums, recognizing the significance of RGB
(Red, Green, Blue) in visual technology, and navigating the quantitative aspects of light
measurement.
2.1.1
Light Spectrums
Light is composed of electromagnetic waves with varying wavelengths, and this range of
wavelengths constitutes the electromagnetic spectrum. The visible spectrum, the portion
perceivable by the human eye, spans from approximately 380 nanometers (nm) for violet
light to 750 nm for red light. This rainbow of colors illustrates the diversity within light,
each color corresponding to a unique wavelength.(2.1)
Source: https://learn.inside.dtu.dk/d2l/le/lessons/126155/topics/496123
Figure 2.1: Visible light spectrum
Beyond the visible spectrum, we encounter ultraviolet (UV) and infrared (IR) light. UV
light has shorter wavelengths than violet light, while IR light has longer wavelengths than
red light. Understanding these spectrums is crucial in scientific fields such as astronomy
and medical imaging.
2.1.2
Human Vision
The human eye serves as a remarkable instrument capable of perceiving the intricacies
of the visible spectrum. Light enters through the cornea and passes through the lens,
where it is refracted and focused onto the retina. Photoreceptor cells in the retina, called
cones and rods, are responsible for detecting and transmitting light signals to the brain.

2.2.1
Reflection
The reflection of light is the process by which light bounces off a surface. It can be further
categorized in diffuse and specular reflection (2.4).
Source: https://www.sciencelearn.org.nz/images/45-types-of-reflection

As shown in the image above, an example of a perfect specular reflection is the common
mirror, in which the angle of incidence of the light, to the normal of the reflective surface,
is equal to the angle of reflection. An example of a diffuse surface would be a matte
workspace like a desk. These types of reflection play a very important role in techniques
used in simulating light that will be discussed later.
2.2.2
Transmission
Light transmission refers to the passage of light through a medium, allowing it to propagate
from one point to another. Generally, when light hits a surface, some amount is reflected,
some is absorbed and some is transmitted, depending on the hit surface (2.5).

Colorimetry
Colorimetry is the science used to quantify and describe human perception of color. It
aims to replace the subjective responses of the human eye to colors with an objective
numerical equivalent. One of the most common uses of colorimetry, is the definition of
color representation methods, such as the RGB and XYZ models, which are widely used
in today’s televisions and computer screens. For the purposes of this research, we will
mostly focus and use the RGB model.
Another property of light that finds uses in the science of colorimetry, is color tempera­
ture. Color temperature is often used alongside other parameters of colorimetry, such as
chromaticity coordinates, in order to accurately describe the spectral properties of light.
However, for the purposes of this research, we are mostly interested in the subjective
appearance of light, based on its temperature.
Colorimetry and the use of colors, in general, finds many applications in architectural
design, as different colours can have a large impact on people’s perception of lit spaces
and well­being. Furthermore, it is widely used in computer graphics, which are important
when simulating light.

Importance of Computer Graphics
Computer graphics plays a very important role in lighting simulations. Exploring the evo­
lution of computer graphics as a science in the past years would be beneficial, in order to
better understand how a lighting simulation works and to verify its results.
It is important to understand that the biggest issue when generating a realistic computer
image, is the propagation of light in the scene. The fact that this problem exists, creates
a strong connection between the sciences of computer graphics and lightning analysis.
For decades, computer scientists have been researching light propagation, in order to
create more realistic visuals. The first steps were taken by James Kajiya, one of the
pioneers in the field of computer graphics, in 1986, when he introduced the rendering
equation9. This equation became the basis of all computer graphics simulations, as it
describes the foundamental issue that they are aiming to solve, which is how light moves
around a virtual scene.
4.1

The first step in understanding how a realistically lit computer generated image is made,
is by understanding the rendering equation.

Even though understanding the Rendering Equation can become a simple task, solving it
is generally cannot. Imagine looking at a point in space, from which we want to calculate
the amount of light that is transmitted towards our eye. In order to do that, we need to
solve the rendering equation at that point. As stated above, in order to find the outgoing
radiance from that point towards us, we need to find the incoming radiance towards that
point from its surroundings. This means that we need to use the rendering equation at
that point, and figure out how much light arrives at it from the space around it. This
process technically repeats infinitely. This is why the Rendering Equation is a recursive
equation. Thus, there is no simple way to solve it analytically. In order to solve it, one
must resort to using algorithmic approximation methods. One of the most famous ways
to do it, and the way that Unreal Engine 5 uses to do it, is the Monte Carlo approximation.
The mathematical analysis of this method is beyond the scope of this paper, but the main
idea is it relies on probabilistic distribution functions (pdfs) to reach a solution.
4.2
Ray­Tracing
The way that the Rendering Equation is solved in computer graphics, in order to simulate
light propagation on a scene, is mainly through the use of Ray­Tracing. Ray­Tracing
as a general physics term has been used since the 1600s. Its main physical function
is to describe the path of particles or waves through a system with varying absorption
characteristics or surface reflectances. Some of the sciences that use a form of ray­
tracing are astronomy, acoustics and seismology. However, it wasn’t until 1968 that the
first form of a shaded image was generated with a computer using Ray­Tracing by Arthur
Appel10.
Tracing the path of the light particles (photons) emitted by a light source, involves drawing,
or casting, rays from it in all directions around it, following those rays as they move around
the environment, until they eventually reach a sensor (4.4)

==PAGE:30==

Ideally, this is what we would use in order to perfectly simulate light propagation in a
space. However, the reality is different. While it is technically possible to use this method
in a small enclosed environment with a limited amount of light sources, as we increase
the size of the rendered space, as well as the number of the light sources, we can see
that the amount of rays we are required to trace increases exponentially. For this reason,
Ray­Tracing is an extremely computationally expensive method. If we also consider that
in order to create perfectly accurate lighting simulations, we would need to model the
entire world, it is easy to realize that a simplified method of Ray­Tracing was needed.
In reality, only about 1% of the total rays traced by a light source will ever reach the sensor.
This lead us to conclude that it is not necessary to cast infinite rays from a light source,
but it is enough to cast only those that reach the sensor. But how can we know which
ones they are? The answer is reverse Ray­Tracing.
Reverse Ray­Tracing is the method that modern computer ray­tracers use. What this
means, is that instead of casting rays from the light sources and following them all the
way to the sensor, which in our case is the pinhole camera behind the screen pixels, we
instead cast the rays from the camera itself through every single pixel, and then follow
these rays to see how they move around the environment (4.5).

4.2.1
The Whitted Algorithm
The first meaningful improvement of the Ray Tracing algorithm, as introduced and used by
Appel, came by Turner Whitted in 198011. Whitted proposed that the rays going through
each pixel of the screen, perform subsequent bounces towards each light source, from
each intersection point. If any of those rays collided with any other geometry before reach­
ing the light source, that meant that the intersection point was shaded.
At this point, it is important to note that Turner Whitted, during his early career, was a
researcher in the nuclear power industry and had studied the behaviour of light photons
in space. This knowledge that he had previously acquired is what helped him to create
an algorithm, commonly referred to as the Whitted algorithm, which is the foundation of
modern computer graphics and is widely used to this day as an educational milestone.

Influence on Lighting Simulations
Ray­Tracing, and consequently computer graphics, has played a very important role in
makibg simulating light propagation possible. While it is theoretically possible to simulate
light propagation in space with absolute accuracy, one would need to simulate every sin­
gle light photon from every relevant light source, account for an infinite number of light
bounces around the scene and ideally model the entire world. However, this is obviously
impossible in practice.
The reason why Ray­Tracing is so important, is because it is an algorithm that is constantly
being improved and iterated upon, in order to make it more efficient, as seen in the case
of reverse Ray­Tracing.
Lighting simulation software, such as DiaLux EVO or Radiance, rely on some form of
Ray­Tracing to perform their simulations. As soon as a simulation is started, the software
begins to solve the rendering equation using Ray­Tracing and some form of algorithmic
approximation to calculate the lighting of the scene. This is where the computationally
intensive nature of Ray­Tracing, and consequently the need to approximate it and make
it faster becomes apparent. Depending on the scale and complexity of the performed

This challenge is addressed by implementing Ray­Tracing techniques and solving the
Rendering Equation using Monte Carlo approximation, especially through the Bidirec­
tional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) component. The BRDF approximates
the behavior of light photons as they interact with various surfaces. However, this so­
lution is not perfect, particularly when simulating scenes where there are no visible light
sources, and lighting mostly relies on indirect illumination.
As mentioned earlier, modern computers employ reverse Ray­Tracing to render scenes.
Consequently, only a fraction of the traced rays from the screen pixels actually intersect
with present light sources, resulting in an inaccurate estimation of indirect lighting con­
tribution. To mitigate this issue, many Ray­Tracers increase the number of ray bounces
within the scene before sending them towards light sources. However, this approach
introduces another challenge.
4.3.2
Computational Demands
As previously mentioned, performing lighting simulations through Ray­Tracing calls for
significant computational capabilities, presenting challenges in terms of time and hard­
ware requirements. Complex simulations require extensive computational resources and
substantial processing time, often relying on expensive and powerful computer systems.
Furthermore, enhancing simulation accuracy by increasing the number of ray bounces to
account for indirect lighting exponentially increases computational needs.
For instance, a modern workstation capable of efficiently managing such tasks typically
costs more than $4000. Thus, many consultancy firms rely on only one or two high­
performance machines to handle their workload efficiently. However, there is a need for
a more accessible and cost­effective approach to perform simulations across multiple
lower­end computers, which could result in savings on both hardware expenses and time
investment.
4.3.3
Real­Time Illumination
Lastly, one of the main challenges in lighting simulation pertains to real­time illumination
rendering, which involves the immediate computation of light data using ray­tracing tech­
niques.
Traditionally, ray­tracing in simulation software involves importing a scene from a different
modeling software and adjusting lighting parameters, such as sun intensity and position,
as well as incorporating artificial lighting effects. Subsequently, the user begins the ren­
dering process, also known as ”baking,” during which the software performs the necessary
ray­tracing calculations to display the lit scene. However, modifying the scene afterward,
whether by altering architectural features, adjusting lighting settings, or adding new ele­
ments, requires repeating the entire process, leading to significant time waste, particularly
for complex scenarios.
Real­time illumination rendering addresses this challenge by enabling instant modification
of the scene with simultaneous lighting calculations. This approach streamlines workflow
efficiency by eliminating the need for repetitive rendering cycles, allowing engineers to
rapidly iterate through design changes and evaluate various solutions without prolonged
waiting periods. As a consequence, real­time illumination offers a substantial enhance­
ment to productivity in lighting simulation tasks.
The drawback of this method however, is the credibility of the physical accuracy of the
results. Due to the many simplifications and optimizations of the ray­tracing algorithm, it
is crucial to test whether the physical accuracy of the lighting results becomes diluted.

meaning that the closer the camera gets to the mesh, the higher the number of polygons
used to render it. Since it has been developed by Epic Games specifically to work with
Unreal Engine 5 and Lumen, using it provides many benefits, such as an increase in visual
quality without sacrificing performance, as well as an increase in visual results for Lumen.

5.3
Significance in Lighting Simulation
As mentioned before, the way Unreal Engine 5 handles global illumination is through a
framework called Lumen. Lumen uses multiple ways of Ray­Tracing techniques in order
to handle indirect illuminations in real time. For example, the first bounce of the rays
through the pixels is handled separately from the rest since it is the most important one,
and is sampled independently16.
In general, a lot of optimization and simplification techniques have been used in Unreal
Engine 5’s Lumen, in order to massively improve performance and reduce computation
times. This has lead to the creation of a real­time illumination system that processes
environment changes almost instantaneously, accounting for both direct and indirect illu­
mination and creating very photorealistic images (5.6).

7.2
Extracting Lighting Information from Unreal Engine 5
Extracting the needed information from Unreal Engine, proved to be more challenging
than originally anticipated. The engine itself includes an illuminance evaluation tool, called
the HDR eye adaptation histogram, which does in fact show the lux value of the area that
the cursor points to. The histogram was used to calibrate the created tools to the engine’s
results, in order to try and calculate lux values, as will be discussed later. Furthermore,
we relied on the pixel inspector, which is an in­engine debugging tool that can display the
RGB values of a specific pixel.
7.2.1
Source Code Modification
The first attempt included downloading the source code of the engine, which is free and
can be found on GitHub. The main idea was figuring out the location of the used ray­
tracing algorithm, finding the relevant C++ function, which was responsible for using the
lighting information and converting it into pixel color, then exposing that value per pixel
inside of the engine, in order to be used as a post process shader and create an accu­
rate representation of the illuminance values in real­time. This, however, proved to be
impossible, due to the fact that, as was mentioned in chapter 3, the ray­tracing algorithm
includes many simplifications and optimizations, which did not allow for a singular result
to be found.
7.2.2
Polynomial Regression
The next attempt involved accessing the RGB pixel data directly inside of the engine.
Using the HDR eye adaptation histogram with the pixel inspector, a collection of data was
gathered which correlated in­engine calculated illuminance values to specific pixel RGB
values. This set of data was subsequently used to train a simple machine learning model,
in order to perform a polynomial regression analysis and find a formula that can be used to
calculate the illuminance from the RGB. The data set (which can be seen in the Appendix)
included a set of values for 244 different color, illuminance and pixel combinations.
While running the script for the 244 combinations and testing the results inside of Unreal
Engine, it became apparent that it would not work. The result of the created shader was a
completely wrong combination of different colored pixels which constantly alternated were
unusable. This happened due to the fact that, since the engine is running in real­time and
continuously performs calculations, the exact RGB values of each pixel where not static
and would change on every tick. Another issue was the fact that when measuring the
RGB for illuminance values higher than around 4000 lux, a slight change in RBG value
would lead to significant lux changes. Lastly, using this data set, the regression model
provided an enormous average RMSE, which was the main way that the results were
verified before being implemented inside of the engine.
This led to a number of different implementations of the model, which subsequently led to
the use of only 104 of the collected data points. The model then produced the following

8
Conclusion
In conclusion, a lot can be said about Unreal Engine 5. While the software was developed
primarily as a game development engine, it has become apparent that it has its applica­
tions in the AEC sector. In fact, Epic Games has a department that specifically addresses
and improves the Engine’s AEC applications.
Regarding lighting simulations specifically, it appears that it is currently not suited for quan­
titative calculations. This, however, may easily change in the near future, as the engine
is further developed and upgraded. The fact that the lighting information actually exists
in the Engine but is not easily accessible, seems like a missed opportunity. If there is a
demand from the users and if Epic Games finds real value in it, I believe that it would not
be impossible for the source code to be modified in order to display this information.
On the other hand, an aspect of the engine that really shines, is its qualitative analysis
potential. As was evident during this research, the photorealistic capabilities of the engine
are unparalleled. As of the writing of this paper, there is currently no other free software
available in the market that is capable of implementing real­time global illumination in a
similar fast, cheap and easy manner.
With that in mind, it seems that Unreal Engine would not be suited for a highly detailed,
high LOD model lighting analysis, since the results would simply be inaccurate. It could
however find potential uses in the early stages of the design phase, were rapid iterations
and a better understanding of the spatial constraints are more valuable than accuracy.
Lastly, looking outside of lighting simulations, we can easily see the engine’s potential for
the AEC sector. Niagara offers physically based fluid simulations that can potentially be
used for cooling and heating solutions, VR integration, as well as AR applications, can
greatly enhance the multidisciplinary, collaborative aspect of the AEC sector and stream­
line workflows, designers and engineers can freely walk and discuss about their structure
and identify potential clashes, before the foundation is even laid out and so much more.
The only thing that limits the software’s capabilities is our imagination, and one needs only
to be willing in order to utilize it.
