Abstract.
It is challenging to formalize and verify privacy properties of cryp-
tographic protocols, and it is desirable to have specifications that
are both formally precise and yet simple and intuitive and this may
give rise to discover problems that have not yet been discovered
earlier due to complexity. The recently proposed method of (α,β)-
privacy[1] aims at making models and proofs simpler. This frame-
work has been applied at modeling and proving[2] properties of the
voting protocol FOO92. This includes the crucial property of receipt-
freeness: a voter cannot prove to a third party, how they have voted.
The main motivation is to avoid corruption and coercion of voters.
Also in other privacy-sensititive areas, receipt-freeness is inter-
esting.
For instance, in an eHealth system where a doctor can
make electronic presciptions for patients, we similarly want a receipt-
freeness property to prevent coercion between pharmaceutical com-
panies and doctors.
The goal of this thesis is to formally model
and analyze an existing e-Health protocol[3] that was made with
the receipt-freeness in mind. The modeling will be made using the
alpha-beta-privacy approach. This involves conceptual work in for-
malizing the components and their privacy properties.

DLVV08 Protocol Description
The proposed e-health protocol aims to protect the privacy of participants like
patients and doctors. Therefore we will focus on the commitunications where
the two participants are involved. And we will also focus on describing all the
details such as what cryptogrphic primitives has been used and how we can rep-
resent those cryptographic messages in a formal way with the (α, β)-privacy[1].
Because these are important for protocol specifications in the next chapter.
1.1
Roles
The participants of the e-Health protocol are patients, doctors, pharma-
cist, Medical Prescription Administration, Health Insurance Institute, Social Se-
curity Organization, Medical Certification Authority and Central government-
approved Certificate Authority. We will use italic font alphabets with upper-
case alphabet initials to represent those participants, for instance:
A,B,C,M,H,R
may represent those participants mentioned above. The two Certification Au-
thorities are stand for trusted government in the e-Health protocol. We will
use sans-serif font alphabets with lower-case to represent the trusted authori-
ties. For e.g. s can be used for representing the Certification Authorities. For
simplicity, we can assume there is exactly one trusted third party, because they
always follow the protocol. We will repeatly use role to represent the finite sort
of the participants of the e-Health protocol.
1.2
ID, Pseudonyms
The role such as doctors or patients, possesses some privacy sensitive iden-
tity attributes. These identity attributes include role identifier and pseudonym
and so on. In e-Health protocol, the link between identifier and pseudonym of
doctors are required to be only known to the “College” of Physicians”. We have
privacy statements of the form as follows:
DrID = {d1, . . . , dn}
where DrId is the domain of doctor identifiers and d1, . . . , dn are the constants
represents the identifiers.
We have privacy statements of the form such as
follows:
⋆A ∈DrID
This represents the intruder only gets the information of A is one of the doctors
in the domain.

For the role such as pharmacists, Medical Prescription Administration (MP-

1.6
Anonymous Credential
The e-health protocol uses anonymous credential to anonymously authen-
ticate role such as doctors and patients.
The Certification Authority issues
anonymous credential based on assertion of role identity attributes. The anony-
mous credentials contain different identity attributes regarding to the different
role. For example, doctor has its identifier and pseudonym, but patient has
some more attributes such as Health Insurance identifier and Social Security
identifier. We will use function symbols such as credD/2, credP/3 ∈Σop and
A, B ∈V to construct the identity attributes for doctors and patients as follows.
For instance:
credD(A, nym(A))
credP(B, nym(B), hi(B), ss(B))
represents the identity attributes respect to a doctor and a patient. The func-
tions are public for role, i.e. every role can use them to form their own credential
attributes and send to credential issuing organization. However, in the cryp-
tographic implementation, the plain text must be orgnized in a particular way
to allow for a zero-knowledge proof about the content of the attributes without
submitting the credential. Therefore, we will only provide destructor for those
function with out verifier for instance:
getName(credD(A, nym(A))) ≈A
getNym(credP(B, nym(B), hi(B), ss(B))) ≈nym(B)
We can use the same idea of the digital certificate for representing the assertion
of the identity attributes by Certification Authority. For instance,
sign(priv(s), credD(A, nym(A)))
sign(priv(s), credP(B, nym(B), hi(B), ss(B)))
where the two terms represent anonymous credential for doctors and patients.

the prescription. The prescription includes doctor’s commititment of doctor’s

prescription identifier. These information is publicly released. Moreover, the
