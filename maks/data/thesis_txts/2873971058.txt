Abstract
Source separation and diarization with multi-microphone arrays is challenging in noisy,
conversational scenes. This work presents a fully classical spatial-audio pipeline centered
on spherical-harmonic (SH) minimum variance distortionless response (MVDR) beam-
forming and direction-of-arrival (DOA) estimation, with tracking and hidden Markov model
(HMM) smoothing. DOA-driven MVDR forms spatially selective beams to enhance indi-
vidual speakers; speaker diarization supplies the essential “who spoke when” to estimate
noise-only cross-spectral matrices (CSM), so diarization accuracy directly limits the effec-
tiveness of the spatial filter.
We evaluate a controlled loudspeaker corpus and a multi-speaker conversation corpus
recorded with a 32-channel Eigenmike (EM32), using 300 Hz and 900 Hz bands. Per-
formance is strongly frequency-dependent: on the loudspeaker corpus, diarization error
rate (DER) is typically 10–20% in the 1.7–4.7 kHz range (excluding 2.3–3.5 kHz, where
beamformer instability and limited HMM training cause errors). In conversation, DER is
generally 39–50%, with a best of 36% at a 3.05 kHz center frequency using a 900 Hz
bandwidth; wider bands help selectively rather than uniformly. Under strong background
noise, DER degrades to 46–70%. With a 5.3° separation, the system attains 10% DER
within the recommended band, demonstrating useful spatial separation.
These findings show that diarization performance depends on frequency selection, robust-
ness to background noise, and spatial resolution at small angular separations. Future
gains are expected from broader-band, conversation-conditioned HMM estimation, re-
fined MVDR regularization with frequency-dependent SH-order gain control, and lightweight
hybrids pairing SH–MVDR spatial cues with DNN-based diarization for robustness.

Introduction
Noisy environments with multiple speakers are commonplace in everyday life, while ob-
serving single sounds by itself is very rare. To separate a single source in a noisy en-
vironment is one of the hardest task for the auditory system, particularly for individuals
with hearing impairments. Humans with normal hearing can follow a single voice in noisy
multi-speaker settings (“cocktail-party problem”) with remarkable ease, but replicating this
technologically remains a significant challenge [1, p. 192]. Separating speech sources
using microphone arrays offers substantial benefits, such as enhancing the audio signal
from video conferences by focusing on the speaker, improve hearing aids and hands-free
telephone calls by suppression of background noise and more [2, p. 169]. To develop and
evaluate such a system, this work investigates a dataset of table-top, multi-speaker con-
versations recorded with the Eigenmike 32 (EM32), a 32-channel spherical microphone
array. While DNNs dominate many modern source separation methods, they demand ex-
tensive training data and computational resources [3]. In contrast, classical approaches
like delay-and-sum (DAS) beamforming and MVDR beamforming rely on acoustic mod-
els of the microphone [4, pp. 140, 160]. These methods continue to play an important
role, particularly as components of hybrid systems in state-of-the-art beamforming [3].
This study explores a fully classical pipeline, integrating spherical harmonics (SH) based
beamforming with hidden Markov model (HMM)-based diarization to deliver robust per-
formance without DNNs.
1.1
Problem Definition and Objectives.
The primary objective is to design and evaluate a classical pipeline for multi-talker source
separation using the EM32, incorporating prior knowledge of speaker configurations and
estimated positions. Key challenges include accurate direction of arrival (DOA) estimation
through peak detection and azimuth tracking, speaker segmentation, and voice activity
detection (VAD), assessed via the diarization error rate (DER).
1.2
Research Questions:
RQ1. Which frequency range is optimal for beamforming on speech signals with the
EM32?
RQ2. What performance levels can be achieved on our datasets?
RQ3. How does the pipeline perform under high background noise and small angular
separations?
1.3
Contributions.
This work introduces the first EM32-based classical diarization system incorporating seat
priors, azimuth tracking, and HMM refinement for enhanced voice activity masks.
1.4

2
Related Work
This section reviews foundational concepts and prior research on beamforming tech-
niques, S processing, classical diarization methods, evaluation metrics, and gaps in ex-
isting approaches that motivate this work. Experts familiar with these areas may skip this
section.
2.1
Beamforming Techniques.
Beamforming enhances signals from desired directions while suppressing interference.
It is a key technique in microphone arrays for applications such as hearing aids, telecon-
ferencing, and acoustic scene analysis [2, p. 169]. For example the DAS beamformer
aligns signals from multiple microphones by compensating for time delays, maximizing
signal gain in a desired direction [4, p. 138]. The MVDR beamformer minimizes the out-
put power while preserving the target signal through a distortionless constraint, using the
cross-spectral matrix (CSM) of the array signals to suppress interferers [4, p. 161]; how-
ever, it requires accurate DOA estimates and regularization to mitigate noise amplification
[5, pp. 162–166]. The linearly constrained minimum variance (LCMV) beamformer ex-
tends MVDR with multiple constraints for enhanced control over beamwidth and sidelobe
suppression [4, p. 163] but needs to identify the location of the disturbance [5, p. 174].
2.2
Spherical-Harmonic Arrays.
The sound field around a spherical microphone array can be modeled by decomposing
it into orthogonal spherical harmonic basis functions [5, p. 51]. This approach allows
for beamforming in the SH domain, where the order N determines the complexity of the
modeled field. Higher orders provide better spatial resolution but increase sensitivity to
noise and aliasing [6, pp. 9–12]. For example, arrays like the EM32, with 32 capsules
on a rigid sphere, support orders up to N = 4, balancing aliasing, spatial resolution,
and noise amplification [6, pp. 9–12]. Prior studies highlight challenges in dynamic multi-
talker scenarios with time-varying or unknown DOAs [7], where frequency-adaptive SH-
order selection [6, p. 9] and regularization improve robustness [4, p. 157]. Sub-band
processing can be used [8], allowing setups that control beam width over frequency using
regularization [9]. Our pipeline integrates MVDR in the SH domain with per-frequency-
band optimization of SH order and regularization parameters to maintain consistent beam
patterns.
2.3
Classical Diarization Methods.
Classical speaker diarization segments audio to identify “who spoke when” by detecting,
clustering, and labeling speech activity without deep learning [10]. To solve this problem,
DOA-based diarization tracks speaker positions via power maps and probabilistic assign-
ment [11] and HMMs provide a useful tool by modeling temporal state transitions (ac-
tive/inactive) for robust segmentation [12]. However, parameter tuning, such as expected
burst/pause durations and detection/false-alarm probabilities, is critical [13]. Informed
methods use room/array geometry priors for robust tracking in reverberant, dynamic en-
vironments [14].
Our method uses exponential moving average (EMA)-smoothed 3D
power maps, Gaussian assignment to metadata-informed seats (occupation) using ta-
ble geometry priors for initial DOA-estimates, and a two-state HMM smoothing DOA-data
via Bayesian-optimized parameters [15] to enhance VAD.

2.4
Evaluation Metrics.
The DER is commonly used in diarization studies to measure false alarms, misses, and
speaker confusions per active time frame relative to ground truth (GT), often with a 250
ms collar to forgive boundary errors [16]. For beamforming, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
measures target signal energy against noise energy [17], while white noise gain (WNG)
assesses sensitivity to noise or error [4, p. 229], and half power beam width (HPBW)
expresses main lobe width [4, p. 235]. The main metric in this work is DER. Signal en-
hancement using MVDR beamforming ideally constructs the CSM from noise-only frames
and points the steering vector at the active source [8]. To identify noise-only frames, we
use VAD, which is evaluated implicitly through DER by false alarms (FA) and missed
speech (MS), and the source position through speaker error (SE). SNR was used during
an early project phase to select a suitable beamforming technique but the results are not
published.
2.5
Gap Analysis.
While deep-learning methods dominate recent source separation advances [3], they re-
quire large amounts of training data [18] and are often trained on specific datasets, which
limits their generalizability to unseen data, and lack interpretability [19], [3]. Classical
pipelines are vital when computational resources are limited [5, p. 7] or to improve gener-
alization of DNN-driven approaches [20]. Deep learning-based approaches often outper-
form classical methods [19], but we aim for competitive results, because using spherical
arrays like the EM32 in combination with a SH beamformer should provide strong spa-
tial cues, compared to typical microphone setups in datasets used for comparative test-
ing (e.g., AMICorpus, NISTMeetingPilot, CHiME6Data, DiPCoDataset, DIHARDIIIPaper).
Our work fills this gap by proposing an end-to-end classical pipeline: SH-MVDR beam-
forming with per-band optimization, DOA estimation, HMM diarization with Bayesian-
optimized parameters, and evaluation on real multi-talker corpora, enabling efficient di-
arization and source separation in challenging acoustics.

Methods
In this chapter, we introduce the methods used in this study, including dataset descrip-
tions, simulation for beamformer design, GT generation, and the processing pipeline for
diarization. All algorithms were implemented in MATLAB R2021a.
3.1

3.2
Datasets
The datasets used in this study include a loudspeaker corpus for controlled testing and
a conversation corpus for real-world evaluation. The loudspeaker corpus was recorded
in a standard meeting room using a 32-channel EM32 rigid-sphere microphone array at
48 kHz (24-bit depth). It consists of six two- to four-minute mixtures, each generated
by replaying two audio source files through two loudspeakers at three azimuthal positions
(Position 1, 2, and 3) with angular separations of 5.3°, 23.0°, and 54.6° between a station-
ary speaker (Loudspeaker A) and a moved speaker (Loudspeaker B), both 34 cm above
the table. The EM32 was centrally placed 10 cm above the table. Source files include
mix1_FM (female and male speech), mix2_MM (two male speakers), and mix3_MN (male
speech and noise). Recordings were made with and without background noise from an
audiobook played through eight surrounding speakers at 55 dBA (see Fig. 3.2 for setup).
The conversation corpus derives from table-top conversation scenarios with 7 subjects

3.3
Generating Ground Truth
To generate GT activity labels, we processed each corpus separately using a heuristic
approach with energy-based thresholding followed by postprocessing. For the conversa-
tion corpus, three mono audio files (boom mic-1, 7, and 9, sampled at 48 kHz) were first
processed by manual silencing of inactive sections to remove unwanted sounds. Then
the cleaned audio files were analyzed using a frame length of 4000 samples (≈83 ms) and
hop size of 1000 samples (≈21 ms), with Hann windowing and EMA smoothing (α = 0.05)
applied to frame energies. A fixed energy threshold of 1 · 10−7 was used to detect activity
in each file. Gaps ≤0.85 s between active frames were filled, followed by binarization us-
ing a moving mean (> 0.5 threshold) over a 0.85 s window. For the loudspeaker corpus,
the stereo source files (sampled at 44.1 kHz) were processed per channel (left and right
independently) using an adjusted frame length of 3675 samples (≈83 ms) and hop size of
919 samples (≈21 ms) for temporal equivalence with the processing of the conversation
corpus, with Hann windowing and EMA smoothing (α = 0.9). Energies were thresholded
at the 50th percentile of the values (detecting the upper 50% of energies as active). Gaps
≤0.25 s were filled, followed by binarization using a moving mean (> 0.5 threshold) over
a 0.25 s window.
3.4
Processing Pipeline Overview
The processing pipeline is the key contribution of this work, addressing the diarization
problem posed by the MVDR beamformer. Before a spatial filter can be calculated, it
must be known when the target source was active (e.g., subject talks) and where the
location of the target is, to ensure the steering vector points in the right direction and the
CSM is calculated only from noisy frames (i.e., frames where the target is not active).
As shown in Fig. 3.4, the process unfolds in four main stages: time-frequency analysis,
beamforming, speaker localization and diarization, and performance evaluation.
3.4.1

All EM32 signals were processed at 48 kHz using a short-time Fourier transform (STFT)
with a 4000-sample Hann window (≈83 ms, periodic), hopped by 1000 samples (≈21 ms,
effective 75% overlap). CSMs were computed per frame via fast Fourier transform (FFT)

3.4.2
Beamforming
Frequency-domain MVDR beamforming was performed in the SH-domain using precom-
puted harmonics up to order N = 1–4 (band-dependent, to prevent overfitting at low
frequencies and model the increasingly complex sound field at higher frequencies) and
rigid-sphere radial filters. Steering vectors were calculated for a 5.625° azimuth/eleva-
tion grid. The CSM was regularized by adding λI, with λ tuned per band by simulation
(e.g., 0.15–4.40) to balance WNG and HPBW. The MVDR pseudospectrum was aver-
aged across bins within each band, producing 3D power maps per frame (the complete
list of parameters can be found in the Appendix A, Tables A.1, A.2, and A.3).
3.4.3
Speaker Localization & Diarization
The 3D power maps were EMA-smoothed (α = 0.85). Peaks were detected via regional
maxima, limited to one per frame, using signals from full elevation arc, a percentile thresh-
old (30th), and a minimum angular distance of 5.625°. Azimuths were offset by −115◦to
compensate for the misalignment of the EM32 to our intended coordinate system (the
8

EM32 was not aligned to a specific direction during recording).
Peaks were assigned to expected seats from metadata using a wrapped normal distribu-
tion (σ = 15◦) [21]. Tracked azimuths were updated via EMA (α = 0.05), limited to ±10◦
from initial positions to prevent drift. A two-pass approach computed mean azimuths first,
then re-tracked. Raw activity maps from assignments were smoothed using a two-state
HMM (Viterbi decoding; burst length 34 s, pause 0.12 s, Pd = 0.49, Pf = 0.31) [22].
3.4.4
Performance Evaluation
To evaluate diarization performance, the estimated binary activity masks from the beam-
former outputs were temporally aligned with the GT masks. The evaluation window started
after the initial 5 s of each segment to avoid influence of claps or burst tones indicating the
start of the segment, and extended to the minimum duration between GT and estimate.
The DER, a standard metric for speaker diarization that measures the proportion of time
where the system incorrectly identifies ”who spoke when” (e.g., [16]), was computed. To
mitigate boundary uncertainties, 250 ms collars were excluded around the start and end
of each GT speech segment. This process was repeated per frequency band (300 Hz
fixed-width bins from 200–6500 Hz), yielding per-speaker activity error rates (AER) and
their combined DER, visualized across bands for comparison.
The AER was used to evaluate single channels individually. It measures the fraction of
false positives and missed speech relative to all frames evaluated, providing insight into
detection performance including silent frames (in contrast to DER, which focuses on active
speech segments).
3.5
Optimization of HMM Parameters
To optimize the HMM parameters for smoothing raw activity maps, Bayesian optimization
was employed using MATLAB’s bayesopt function. This approach minimized the mean
DER across selected frequency bands (800–1100 Hz, 1100–1400 Hz, 1400–1700 Hz,
1700–2000 Hz, 2000–2300 Hz) on a 5 s segment from the loudspeaker corpus (mix1_pos2),
incorporating a 250 ms collar. The results for optimization are: burst length (Lburst) = 34 s,

3.6
Spatial filtering
Spatial filtering using DAS, MVDR, and LCMV beamformers was done early during the
project to determine which beamformer to use. They were evaluated on a 10 s conversa-
tion sample using SNR, where the energy of inactive frames was compared to the energy
in active frames. The positions of the talkers were estimated based on 2D heat maps
and used to point the steering vector to the targets. Active and inactive frames were de-
termined by thresholding the boom mic recording. Results have been used for selection
only and are not published.

5
Discussion
5.1
Optimal Frequency Range for Beamforming with the
EM32
One of the key challenges during development was determining the optimal frequencies
for DOA estimation. Speech energy is highest on average between 200 Hz and 500 Hz,
decreasing at higher frequencies [23, p. 4]. However, beamformer performance tends
to improve with frequency, as shown in the simulation results (Table 3.1). For a DAS
beamformer, the HPBW widens at lower frequencies where the EM32 diameter (8.4 cm)
is small compared to the wavelength (approximately 172 cm to 68 cm), limiting phase
differences between microphones (Figure A.1). The MVDR beamformer can maintain
a constant HPBW through regularization, but this increases noise amplification at lower
frequencies (Table 3.1).
Evaluations of both the loudspeaker and conversation corpora indicate performance degra-
dation above approximately 4400 Hz (Figures 4.2 and 4.4). This may stem from reduced
speech energy at higher frequencies [23, p. 4]. The pipeline employs an average energy
threshold to filter low-energy peaks and suppress noise, potentially discarding weaker
signals. The heuristically defined STFT frame length of approximately 83 ms aligns well
with the recommended 40 ms window for capturing stop bursts using a Hann window [23,
p. 166], allowing short signals to dominate frames. Stop bursts peak between 1000 Hz
and 4000 Hz [23, p. 35], facilitating their detection. Additional factors include greater
amplitude deviations among microphone capsules at higher frequencies, which degrade
performance [24], and spatial aliasing beginning around 5000 Hz [25].
DER results alone do not clearly identify the preferred frequency range, given spikes
observed for mix 1 position 1 and mix 2 position 2 in the 2300 Hz to 3500 Hz band (Fig-
ure 4.2). These increases arise from two issues: (1) misassignment during tracking,
where active frames are allocated to the wrong channel, and (2) HMM smoothing failing
to remove interference from nearby sources, leading to erroneous detection of simulta-
neous speech rather than a single active source. Examples of these errors appear in
Figure A.2.
The first issue may relate to reduced beamformer stability. Sound scattering on the EM32
becomes more pronounced when its diameter approaches the wavelength (15 cm to
10 cm in this band), and sound can be reflected back from surrounding objects to the
microphone and disturb the measurement [5, p. 86]. From 2000 Hz on we increase SH
order abruptly from 2 to 3. Instead, slowly increasing the order by using a gain factor could
help to increase robustness in that band. Also, in the problematic 2.3–3.5 kHz band, our
current design shows WNG declining from about 10 dB to 6.08 dB (Table A.2), which co-
incides with the DER spikes; stronger regularization would trade some beam sharpness
for robustness.
The second issue stems from limited HMM training: the HMM was trained only on mix 1
position 2 between 800–2300 Hz, that is just below the problematic band, so it underper-
forms in 2.3–3.5 kHz and also in conversational speech. This aligns with the removals of
valid short segments seen in Fig. 4.3.
Assuming these issues can be resolved through beamformer fine-tuning, a suitable range
for MVDR beamforming with the EM32 on speech signals is approximately 1700 Hz to
Signal Processing Techniques for Source Separation on Multi-Channel Audio Data
19
==PAGE:28==
4700 Hz. Notably, the 2300 Hz to 3500 Hz band yielded among the best results on the
conversation corpus (Figure 4.4), despite the heightened DER on the loudspeaker corpus
in that range, suggesting signal-dependent challenges. Stabilizing the beamformer and
improving training data selection are likely to enhance outcomes.
Widening the bandwidth from 300 Hz to 900 Hz reduced DER in some bands but in-
creased it in others on the conversation corpus. For instance, DER decreased at 3050 Hz
but rose at 3950 Hz, likely due to inclusion of the poorer-performing 3650 Hz band from
the narrower analysis (Figure 4.4) but also at 4250 Hz the DER increased, even though
neighboring bands showed good results in the 300 Hz bandwidth analysis. Thus, broader
bandwidths do not consistently improve diarization.
Answer to RQ1: The recommended range for MVDR on EM32 speech is 1.7–4.7 kHz;
using 900 Hz bandwidth can help in some bands (e.g., 3050 Hz) but is not uniformly
beneficial.
5.2
Performance on the Loudspeaker and Conversation
Corpora
Pipeline performance on the loudspeaker corpus varies by frequency band. In the recom-
mended 1700 Hz to 4700 Hz range—excluding the 2300 Hz to 3500 Hz anomalies—DER
typically ranges from 10% to 20%. For the conversation corpus with 300 Hz bandwidth,
DER is generally 39% to 50%. For context, the recent CHiME-8 challenge analyzed the
CHiME-6 dataset, featuring four friends in dinner party scenarios. Speakers moved freely
across rooms, recorded by six far-field Kinect arrays (24 microphones total) [26]. Eight
pipelines from five teams achieved DERs of 37% to 69%, under challenging conditions
with limited microphones per room and mobile talkers. The DiPCo dataset, similar to
our conversation corpus, involved four colleagues conversing, recorded by five circular
arrays (35 microphones) plus close-talk microphones per person. The same teams ob-
tained DERs of 20% to 48%. Similar to our setup, which evaluated three of seven talkers
due to limited close-talk microphones, participants like NTT Corporation relied solely on
arrays for diarization, achieving 23% DER [27].
Interpreting the optimized HMM parameters.
The HMM optimization converged to
Lburst= 34 s, Lpause= 0.12 s, Pd = 0.49, and Pf = 0.31. This setting implicitly favors
very long “speech” segments and extremely short pauses, which explains the tendency
to merge adjacent speech and miss brief utterances, which is consistent with the ob-
served removal of valid short segments in Fig. 4.3, while longer segments seem to be
detected more reliable. Re-optimizing with priors or constraints on typical conversational
turn lengths (seconds rather than tens of seconds), and retraining on conversation data,
should reduce these effects.
Results on the loudspeaker corpus demonstrate the pipeline’s potential in controlled con-
ditions with no background noise and only two sources, achieving a best DER of below
10 %. On the conversation corpus, the outcomes are promising, with a best DER of 36%
at 3050 Hz using a 900 Hz bandwidth, though this underscores the challenges of real-life
scenarios and noise [3], indicating room for further improvements.
Answer to RQ2: Loudspeaker: typically 10–20% DER in 1.7–4.7 kHz; best <10%. Con-
versation: generally 39–50% (300 Hz bands), best 36% at 3050 Hz (900 Hz bandwidth).

5.3
Pipeline Robustness under Noise and Small Angular
Separations
Under strong background noise (mix 1 position 2 with noise), DER rose to 46% to 70%.
Although SNR was not measured, auditory review indicated speech was hard to under-
stand. The beamformer cannot differentiate noise from signal, selecting the strongest
peak per frame; if not filtered by the energy threshold, this increases errors like FP and
SE.
The pipeline’s ability to separate sources with small angular distances was tested using
mix 1 position 1. In the recommended frequency band, excluding the unstable 2300 Hz
to 3500 Hz range, DER values of around 10% were achieved even when the loudspeak-
ers were separated by only 5.3°, highlighting the system’s spatial resolution capabilities.
However, the elevated DER in the problematic frequency band indicates room for fur-
ther optimization. For low frequencies (below 1.1 kHz), the pipeline struggles to separate
the closely spaced 5.3° pair, whereas larger angular separations remain resolvable (see
Fig. 4.2). This behavior is consistent with the low-frequency beam characteristics: wider
HPBW and reduced WNG degrade spatial resolution and increase susceptibility to noise
(cf. Table 3.1).
Answer to RQ3: High noise degrades DER to 46–70%; with 5.3° separation the system
attains 10% DER in the recommended band.
5.4
Limitations
Only a small portion of the conversation corpus was evaluated due to the limited avail-
ability of GT data. To enable more robust testing, GT should be generated for additional
portions of the data, including all talkers in at least one block to contain a variety of con-
versation scenarios. Given the brief test segment (about four minutes), additional data is
essential to validate findings.
5.5
Future Work
Further enhancements could include tuning beamforming parameters such as regulariza-
tion, SH order adjustments per frequency with gain factors, and improved HMM training.
Given the non-DNN scope of this pipeline, a practical next step is to evaluate a lightweight
hybrid, for example an MVDR front end paired with a pretrained diarizer or VAD to re-
duce false alarms and missed speech. A hybrid MVDR + DNN diarization has shown
potential [3]. MVDR provides DOA tracks and spatial filtering, while the DNN handles di-
arization; correlating DOA tracks with diarizer streams can assign positions to speakers.
Pre-diarization noise reduction could further improve robustness.

6
Conclusion
This work presented a non-neural diarization pipeline that combines MVDR beamform-
ing, DOA estimation/tracking, and HMM smoothing, yielding promising DERs. Regarding
the optimal operating band (RQ1), the 1.7–4.7 kHz range provides the most reliable per-
formance; the 2.3–3.5 kHz subrange remains unstable unless beamformer tuning and
broader HMM training are applied, and performance generally degrades above 4.4 kHz.
Using a wider 900 Hz bandwidth can be beneficial at specific center frequencies (e.g.,
3.05 kHz) but is not uniformly advantageous across bands.
In terms of achievable performance (RQ2), the loudspeaker corpus typically attains 10–20%
DER within the recommended band, with best results below 10% under controlled, noise-
free two-source conditions. The conversation corpus is more challenging, with DER gen-
erally 39–50% and a best of 36% at a 3.05 kHz center frequency using a 900 Hz band-
width. Concerning robustness (RQ3), strong background noise substantially degrades
performance, raising DER to 46–70%; however, for small angular separations the pipeline
maintains useful spatial resolution—at 5.3° separation it achieves about 10% DER within
the recommended band, though performance worsens in the 2.3–3.5 kHz subrange.
Primary limitations are MVDR fragility in the 2.3–3.5 kHz band, the narrow frequency
span used to train the HMM, limited adaptation to conversational speech, and sparse
ground-truth coverage. Future work will (i) broaden HMM training across all evaluated
bands and include conversation-conditioned data; (ii) refine MVDR regularization and ap-
ply frequency-dependent SH-order gain control to improve WNG and pattern stability; and
(iii) assess targeted pre-diarization noise-reduction. Finally, lightweight hybrids that fuse
SH–MVDR spatial cues with DNN diarization are a natural next step toward lower DER.
