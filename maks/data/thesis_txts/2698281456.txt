Preface
This is a master’s thesis project in environmental pollution as part of the Nordic Master in
Cold Climate Engineering, Land Track - a joint programme between NTNU, DTU and UNIS.
The project was carried out during the spring semester of 2021, under the supervision of
professor Tone Merete Muthanna, with support from co-supervisors Pernille Jensen (DTU)
and Aga Nowak (UNIS).
The idea for this project was brought up after reports from different sources indicated
signs of wastewater contamination in Paaraarsuk bay in Sisimiut. With the intent of protect-
ing the population from the incidence of infectious diseases, the need for assessing recre-
ational water quality in remote Arctic communities was revealed.

Summary and Conclusions
A review on Arctic sewage systems is provided as a way to understand the differences and
limitations faced by northern communities. Because of that, old practices are often still
adopted and pose risks to the population. Untreated wastewater disposal, for example, af-
fects marine life and the public. Exposure to bacteria and viruses present in wastewater can
result in numerous, sometimes even deadly, illnesses. Water security is the ﬁrst possible
source of contamination that comes to mind, but recreational water quality has also proven
to be an important aspect of public health preservation. Recreational waters are checked for
safety according to methods suggested by standards. Checking for the presence of pathogens
is not a feasible approach, reason why indicator organisms such as E. coli are often adopted.
Sewage handling in Sisimiut and Longyearbyen, the studied Arctic communities, is summa-
rized to present the possibility of wastewater contamination, thus bacteria and viruses, in
bathing areas.
In Longyearbyen, literature review was enough to conclude that wastewater is unlikely
to reach the city’s bathing areas. However, presence of wildlife may be a source of fecal
contamination that is still to be assessed. Sisimiut, on the other hand, had little literature
available on the matter and sampling was necessary. Sea water samples were collected from
the Paaraarsuk bay, where swimming and kayaking take place. Even though wastewater dis-
charge is not expected in the area, considerable levels of E. coli were found. This can be
explained by either the existence of illegal discharges or wastewater being transported to the
bay by currents and tidal changes.
In conclusion, recreational water quality can be a source of problems in the near future
if not enough attention is given to the matter. It is still to be assessed if waterfowl can be a
potential source of fecal contamination in Longyearbyen. In Sisimiut, there is a need to verify
what is the source of E. coli. If human originated, ﬁnding the wastewater outlets contributing
the pollution is necessary.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Lack of funds, isolation, soil conditions and low temperatures are few of the obstacles to be
overcome when designing and building a sewage system in the Arctic (Gunnarsdóttir et al.,
2013). As a result, communities rely on primitive and unhygienic practices (Smith and Low,
1996a). Gastrointestinal illnesses caused by inappropriate water, sanitation and hygiene
(WASH) practices is the reason why 700 kids under the age of 5 die every day in the world
(UNICEF, 2020). Similarly, treatment is a challenge for the same logistic reasons, which in
the end is insufﬁcient or even neglected, resulting in untreated wastewater disposal (Smith
and Low, 1996a).
Consequently, public and marine life are negatively impacted. High content of bacte-
ria together with other major pollutants of concern, negatively impacts the environment in
different ways, such as the risk of contamination in recreational waters (Doménech-Sánchez
et al., 2008; Yoder et al., 2008; Mannocci et al., 2016; Leonard et al., 2018; Bourque and Vinetz,
2018). In order to assess if waters are safe, regulations specify methods for quantiﬁcation and
results interpretation (Statens Helsetilsyn, 1994; CEC, 2006). In this report, locations where
people engage on activities will be checked for their risk of contamination, as a way to verify
if the public is vulnerable and try to motivate further studies in this subject area.
1.1
Background
Problem Formulation
People do engage in water-based activities in the Arctic, unlikely as it may sound. The cold
temperatures add extra adventurous and braveness factors to a simple dip. Testing the hu-
man body’s limits in such an exotic environment is reinvigorating and has even proven to be

Monitoring recreational water quality has proven not to be a big point of focus in the Arctic,
given the lack of literature available on the topic. With so many infrastructure limitations and
more urgent needs such as water security and wastewater collection, it is comprehensible.
Locations such as Sisimiut and Longyearbyen, however, are better developed in comparison
to other communities and rely on tourism for at least part of their yearly income. When
tours and activities start being cancelled due to anthropogenic pollution, attention must be
given to the matter. With that in mind, it is necessary that relevant authorities evaluate the
importance of keeping waters safe for bathers: the population and an increasing tourism
industry.
When bacteria and viruses are present and get in contact with the public, numerous dis-
eases and infections can result in mild to death threatening risks. The problem is that this
possibility is not concerning to the authorities, probably due to the fact that the wastewater
outlets are positioned away from bathing areas and no contact with the public is expected.
This project will try to check if this is valid.

Literature Survey
Research was necessary to understand sewage limitations in the Arctic, mostly with deﬁni-
tions and practices that were unknown to the author due to a very singular niche of wastew-
ater engineering. Understanding the processes behind bacteria degradation, indicator or-
ganisms and the impacts of contact with the public was also necessary to acknowledge the
processes involved in recreational water quality assessments.
Available standards describe methods and procedures for evaluating bathing water qual-
ity. The EU Directive 2006/7/EC is valid for the EU Member States, while the IK-21/94 for
Norway. An overview of what each one requires was necessary as a way to better understand
how waters are classiﬁed.
A new suggested approach for E. coli enumeration was adopted. Past literature that fol-
lowed the same or similar approach was checked to verify how valid the results are in com-
parison to other approved methods.
What Remains to be Done?
The studied locations have little to no data available on bacteria presence in wastewater re-
cipient water bodies. Focus is mostly (not less importantly) given on nutrients, heavy metals
and antibiotics, due to their persistence in the environment. Once again, recreational water
quality is not a major concern for Arctic communities, but it can become a potential source
of outbreaks in the future. More focus must be given to this matter, as this study will show
how harmful pathogenic bacteria and viruses can be when in contact with the public, espe-
cially in isolated communities.
1.2
Objectives
The main objective of this project is to verify if the studied locations show a tendency to
be unsafe for bathers to engage on activities. Even if outlets are positioned far away from
bathing locations, low water exchange, currents and tides might still be responsible for bring-
ing risks to the public. If that is the case, which is what this study will try to conclude, mea-
sures must be taken to preserve public health. Other objectives are:
1. Present how sewage is handled in Sisimiut and Longyearbyen.
2. Verify if wastewater disposal poses a risk to bathing waters quality.

1.3
Limitations
The frequency of samples collected could not be higher due to unforeseen events. Recre-
ational water quality assessments are based on samples collected during the bathing season,
usually from the end of May to the end of August. In this study, samples were collected in
December, which is already far from when bathers are expected to engage on activities.
A single sample was collected from each location, which is not enough to assess bathing
water quality according to standards. The adopted practice only reveals how polluted the
locations were on that speciﬁc day and weather conditions.
The Petriﬁlm™methodology adopted for bacteria enumeration in sea water is yet to be
approved by the scientiﬁc community. This approach is currently only veriﬁed for bottled
water and food industry.
Lastly, literature on bacteria enumeration in Longyearbyen is not widely available. Con-
clusions were made from studies recently performed, but more data may be necessary to
ensure that the public is safe to engage in water-based activities.
1.4
Approach
Numerous people were contacted by e-mail, phone or in person for different reasons. Mu-
nicipalities were contacted for more detailed information about the sewage system in their
respective cities. In Sisimiut, tourism operators were met in person for information about
the incidence of wastewater pollution in the bay. Social media proved to be a great tool to
obtain relevant information from the public in Longyearbyen, such as a hint on possible il-
legal discharges. As required, some sources remained as anonymous.
Literature review was necessary to better deﬁne practices that are uncommon in temper-
ate and urban areas, together with unknown processes involved in recreational water quality
assessments. Results from previous studies were used as sources for illustrating existing sce-
narios and supporting conclusions.

Chapter 2
Background
In order to better understand all factors involved in untreated wastewater disposal, the con-
sequences of it and how to verify the extent of risks, a review on available literature was
necessary. This section consists of a summary on why and how pathogens are released to
recreational waters, and how to identify their presence and evaluate the threat.
2.1
Sewage in the Arctic
Due to isolation, lack of infrastructure and harsh climate (Gunnarsdóttir et al., 2013), differ-
ent strategies are adopted to provide a functional system. Even though new technologies are
constantly introduced, implementation and adaptation may differ according to each loca-
tion. These barriers are frequently linked to the reason why sewage systems are still a source
of recurring problems and rudimentary practices in the Arctic.
2.1.1
Collection
Piping. Conventional gravity lines are preferred when possible, either below, above or on the
ground. This is due to the fact that they are normally the most cost effective option and ma-
jor failures are unlikely to happen. Freezing, if unavoidable, is expected to happen in layers
because the pipes are not usually running with full capacity, giving enough time for reme-
diation actions. As another alternative, pressure and vacuum sewers can be adopted when
topography is unfavorable. However, this comes with higher operation and maintenance
costs, as the system is energy input dependant and plumbing ﬁxtures are costly and possibly
hard to obtain. (Smith and Low, 1996a).
Soil conditions. Topography and permafrost are often hindrances to implementation of

gravity lines. Designing a system that matches each location’s terrain (both ﬂat or hilly) and
respects different parameters such as minimum velocity, slope and self cleansing conditions
is not as forthright as in other parts of the world. Above-ground pipes have its advantages
over below-ground pipes. For instance, there is no need of excavation for installing the pipes,
ﬁnding and ﬁxing problems; possibility to easily correct slopes with addition of adjustment
blocks between pipes and piles; and it is a good solution when the soil has high ice content
permafrost, which would require extra machinery and more expertise on designing. On the
other hand, below-ground pipes tend to be a preference for being protected from potential
vandalism and trafﬁc damage, aesthetically less impacting, no need for piles, and elevated
roads or sidewalk crossings are not necessary. Furthermore, slope for private connections
from houses to the main line is easily achievable, whereas with above-ground pipes, houses
would in some cases need to be elevated. (Smith and Low, 1996a).
In case topography is the main challenge on designing a functional layout, this problem
can usually be overcome by the adoption of sewage lift stations. The most noticeable ad-
vantage is that grades and elevation differences in the community do not pose a threat to
operation. Construction costs tend to be lower, as pipes can be positioned below or on the
ground and do not need piling. In contrast, pump units operation and maintenance may be
costly, as it might require specialized workforce. Moreover, stations need to be designed with
special care, especially in regards to insulation and foundation. Sewage pipes can also be po-
sitioned in a structure where other utility pipes such as water and central heating are placed
together, known as utilidors. One of its beneﬁts is that if central heating pipes are included,
heat losses tend to be enough to work as freeze protection. (Smith and Low, 1996a).
Low temperatures. Heat loss is also an important factor when opting between above or
below-ground pipes. As shown in Equation 2.1, heat loss (assuming same material proper-
ties) is directly proportional to the internal and external temperature difference ∆T . This
means that pipes exposed to air temperature are expected to require better frost protection
and insulation than if they were buried.

Heat tracing is deﬁned as the addition of heat, in this case to pipes, to prevent them from
freezing or for thaw recovery. Different strategies are adopted for that, such as glycol loops
and electric heat tapes. Glycol is used as a mixture with water and intended to stay in its liq-
uid form at the lowest expected temperature. It provides a lower freezing point, which keeps
the mixture from freezing in a potential shutdown, and higher boiling point in comparison
to other ﬂuids in heating loops (steam and hot water). With higher temperatures, thawing
is performed faster. Disadvantages of glycol are the need of maintenance, pump capacity
requirements, initial investment and leaking potential. Electric heat tapes are easier to in-
stall and need less space and maintenance. However, their use is not recommended where
electricity costs are high, making it impracticable. In addition to that, operation in below-
ground pipes can be a challenge, as the wires and connections of the circuits need to be kept
dry. Water bleeding is also used for freezing protection where ﬂow is intermittent or too low,
which works by addition of water in the system. This is not seen as a good solution when wa-
ter availability is limited, treatment expensive or water heating necessary. (Smith and Low,
1996a).
Adopting the right pipe is the ﬁrst step to avoid major heat losses and optimize the pipeline’s
functionality. Design and speciﬁcations may differ according to the manufacturer, but the
principle remains the same. The pipe must be involved in insulation material, which has to
be moisture proof, and when necessary have its heat tracing between pipe and insulation,
a setup commonly referred to as “Arctic pipe” (Figure 2.1). Consequently, they must be wa-
ter tight to avoid unnecessary heat losses and most importantly inﬁltration. That would not
only damage the insulation, but also introduce lower temperature water inside the pipes,
increasing the chances of freezing events.

sewage system, some locations either do not have enough budget for construction or con-
clude that it is simply not viable. Therefore, different hauling systems are adopted as a way

2.1.2
Treatment
When treatment is to be adopted in an isolated Arctic community, the ﬁrst and foremost
concern is: how costly and how simple is operation and maintenance? Similarly to the hin-
drances to the implementation of a piped sewage system, feasibility of a treatment plant
is often questionable. Remoteness, permafrost, unskilled workforce and rapid turnover of
operator personnel are among the planning considerations (Smith and Low, 1996b). In the
end, the question remains the same: Is the environmental impact signiﬁcant enough to jus-
tify such investment? Even when all requirements are met, it is not uncommon that ﬁnances
and politics do not allow projects to progress (Hendriksen and Hoffmann, 2018). Before bud-
geting, however, Arctic speciﬁc obstacles need to be addressed to assure the treatment so-
lution will be effective. Insufﬁcient or untreated wastewater disposal, commonly done in
many highly vulnerable Arctic regions, can be harmful to the environment (Gunnarsdóttir
et al., 2013). Treatment is necessary to protect such a fragile ecosystem, but at the same time
many obstacles may prevent implementation.
Lagoons. Due to limitations previously stated, it is ideal that treatment technologies are
as simple as possible, without requiring high implementation costs and constant operation
and maintenance. These are some of the reasons why lagoons are the most common type
of treatment adopted in isolated Arctic communities (Chouinard et al., 2014; White et al.,
2021). Furthermore, land costs are low when high in availability, and energy requirements
together with necessary knowledge for operators minimal (Smith and Emde, 1999). Mechan-
ical treatment plants are known to require less space and be efﬁcient, but on the other hand
require skilled workforce, high energy input and constant operation and maintenance. Sep-
tic tanks are not commonly used in the Arctic due to the impossibility of water percolation in
frozen soil, while activated sludge processes require power that in some cases is not widely
available.

Lagoons are similar to other wastewater treatment strategies. The aim is to provide enough
time and opportunity for physical, chemical and biological treatments to take place, result-
ing in the reduction of organic matter, nutrients, and indicator and pathogenic organisms
(Smith and Emde, 1999). Tundra wetlands downstream of the facultative lakes are respon-
sible for providing signiﬁcant additional treatment (Yates et al., 2012), even though it is not
always the intention and just a consequence (Chouinard et al., 2014).
In the Arctic, sewage in the lagoons stays frozen for up to nine months depending on
the location. The content thaws in the end of spring or beginning of summer, when it goes
through the so-called “treatment season”, undergoing high solar radiation levels and algae
blooms. Before freezing starts in early September, the treated sewage is decanted either to
the ocean or to a natural tundra wetland (Huang et al., 2014).
Facultative and anaerobic lagoons are the usual approach for northern communities. As
lagoons operate mostly under anaerobic conditions due to ice formation and snow accumu-
lation in the winter, aerobic conditions are only present after spring ice melts and during
summer months (Smith and Emde, 1999). Aerated lagoons are only suggested when energy
availability is enough and specialized workforce is present in the community, making main-
tenance of the oxygen supplier possible (Smith and Emde, 1999)
Given the fact that cold region lagoons only offer primary treatment, winter and spring
discharges are not suggested, while fall discharges have shown better efﬂuent quality (Smith
and Emde, 1999). Prince et al. (1994) concluded that fall discharges are preferable in regards
to total and fecal coliform levels, showing better results in comparison to spring efﬂuent
quality. Even though E. coli removal has shown to be efﬁcient at a rate of more than 99.99 %
(Heinke et al., 1988), pathogens removal, on the other hand, may not be as adequate. Huang
et al. (2014) detected the presence of 4 different human bacterial pathogens in 10 mL samples
collected from collection trucks, lagoon and efﬂuent in Clyde River, Nunavut.
Performance on metals removal is also not ideal, with sludge having high concentrations
of metals - considering long retention times and occasional sludge removal (Smith and Low,
1996b). Treatment efﬁciency in Nunavut was studied by Yates et al. (2012), with results sum-
marized in Table 2.1. Bacteria removal proved to be efﬁcient, but pathogens may still be
present as another study revealed presence of Salmonella species in a total coliform level of
less than 10 cfu/100mL (Dutka and Bell, 1973).

2.2
Bacteria in the Environment
Commonly not as industrialized and populous as in other parts of the world, Arctic com-
munities have a different composition in its sewage. Most of the time they tend to be low
in volume, due to low density populations, but high in strength as a consequence of limited
water use (Smith and Low, 1996a). Sewage tends to be essentially domestic, with possibility
of laundry wastes and extra grease and garbage from institutional kitchens.
2.2.1
Indicators Organisms
The number of pathogens that can be harmful to the public are part of an extensive list
(Boehm and Soller, 2013). Therefore, due to costs, potential health hazards, need for large
sample volumes and difﬁculty to grow in the laboratory and isolate, routinely testing water
for all pathogenic microorganisms is impracticable (Odonkor, 2013). Multiple studies have
shown the correlation between the risk of becoming ill with increasing content of intesti-
nal bacteria in bathing waters (Doménech-Sánchez et al., 2008; Yoder et al., 2008; Mannocci
et al., 2016; Leonard et al., 2018; Bourque and Vinetz, 2018). The question is how to iden-
tify their presence. Analysis for speciﬁc indicators must therefore be taken in order to as-
sess the presence of microorganisms and whether their levels are of concern to the public.
Bitton (2011a) described how the perfect indicator organism should be: (1) Present in warm-
blooded animals (including humans) intestines; (2) present when pathogens are present and
not in uncontaminated samples; (3) higher in presence than pathogens; (4) survival at least
as good as pathogens; (5) unable to multiply in the environment; (6) detected by easy, quick
and inexpensive methods; and (7) not harmful to the analyst. Finding the perfect indica-
tor has been a challenge. Each one has its own advantages and disadvantages, as described
below.
Coliforms. Coliform species are commonly used to determine water quality in different
scenarios, according to each case. They are divided in different subgroups, illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.4. Coliforms species such as Salmonella and E. coli (O157:H7) can pose risk to human
and animals health (Singh et al., 2016). Most of them, are however, non-pathogenic and nat-
urally found in the environment as non-fecal in origin (Leclerc et al., 2001) - reason why their
presence can not be blindly linked to fecal contamination. Contrary to recreational waters,
the drinking water industry still adopts total coliforms as an indicator organism, as fecal con-
tamination is not the only concern. Considering drinking water is not a natural environment
for coliforms, its presence indicates that water supply facilities have been compromised and

Indicator organisms that belong to the coliform group of bacteria are widely used to identify
the presence of fecal contamination, as they can be found in the digestive tracts of animals,
including humans, and their waste (Madigan et al., 2014). It must be noted, however, that
several bacteria that are a part of the coliforms group are not necessarily originated from
the intestine, reason why care must be taken when looking for fecal contamination. Numer-
ous coliforms can also be found in plants, soil and the environment as a whole, reason why
detection of coliforms is not essentially a sign of fecal contamination.
Thermotolerant Coliforms. Fecal coliforms is a subgroup of total coliforms that grows
and ferments lactose at elevated incubation temperature, reason why they are now com-
monly referred to as thermotolerant coliforms (TtC) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration,
2020). Commonly found in the intestine and feces of mammals, they have a short life span
in comparison to other coliform bacteria and its presence could be related to sanitary waste
improper disposal (Sengupta and Saha, 2013).
Similarly to coliforms, TtC do not exclusively indicate fecal contamination. Pulp and pa-
per mill wastewater discharges are known to have high levels of TtC, mostly represented by
the Klebsiella genus and E. coli (although in less quantity) (Singh et al., 2016). Therefore, if
the goal is to check for the presence of sewage contamination, TtC analysis results may lead
to misinterpretation.
Duncan and Nigel (2003) defend the claim that TtC are efﬁcient at fecal contamination
detection as good as E. coli, given the fact that over 95 % of excreted total coliforms (TC) are
==PAGE:29==

17
E. coli. On the other hand, Doyle and Erickson (2006) claim that fecal contamination must
not be characterized by the content of TtC, as wrongly done for years. This is based on the
fact that its sources are not exclusively warm-blooded animals intestines, suggesting that
results can often be misleading.
Escherichia coli. Escherichia coli are bacteria that have warm-blooded animals intestines
as their habitat and short survival of 4 - 12 weeks away from it (Edberg et al., 2000), suggesting
that its presence indicates recent fecal contamination (Madigan et al., 2014). Out of the 1 %
of coliforms naturally found in the human body, E. coli accounts for the majority of it (Eden,
2014).
E. coli tends to be harmless and its detection does not necessarily conﬁrm the presence of
pathogenic microorganisms, but simply indicates a higher chance that pathogenic bacteria
and viruses such as Salmonella spp. or the Shiga-toxin producing E. coli (STEC) are present.
There are approximately 200 serotypes producing Shiga toxin, in which more than half of it
has been linked to the cause of human diseases (ECDC, 2017). The most common serotype
is the O157:H7 strain. It can be frequently found in cattle and is transmitted to humans
by consumption of contaminated foods (raw or undercooked ground meat), contaminated
water, raw milk and others (WHO, 2018).
In contradiction to E. coli, total coliforms and TtC detection can be linked to both fecal
and non-fecal sources, which means that they are not useful when checking for fecal con-
tamination. Affordable, quick, reliable and easy methods for enumeration are among the
reasons why E. coli has become one of the main indicators of fecal contamination. Further-
more, exclusive occurrence in feces, difﬁculty on multiplying in the environment, presence
in sewage and in large numbers in faeces of warm-blooded animals enhance its functionality
in comparison to other indicators (Paruch and Mæhlum, 2012).
It must be noted that even though E. coli has proven to be a good indicator of bacterial
pathogens, its performance on identifying protozoan and helminthic pathogens is unsatis-
factory and might not be the best approach in that case (Duncan and Nigel, 2003). In ad-
dition to that, they are less resistant to disinfection than viruses or protozoan cysts (Bitton,
2011a), which suggests that absence of E. coli could still mean that pathogens are present.
Enterococci. Fecal streptococci and intestinal enterococci are considered synonymous
with enterococci (Byappanahalli et al., 2012), but differently from E. coli they are not col-
iforms. In accordance with TtC, enterococci is found in the intestines and feces of humans
and animals, but not exclusively (Byappanahalli et al., 2012). Advantages over TtC bacteria

indicators are: occurrence in higher numbers than bacterial and viral pathogens, more re-
sistant to disinfection than TtC and impossibility to multiply in the environment (Duncan
and Nigel, 2003). A study by Jin et al. (2004) concluded that enterococci can even be a bet-

Expected concentration in the Arctic. The number of total coliforms/bacteria found in
the efﬂuent is different according to the sewage service level in the community. Where honey
buckets are widely adopted, wastewater is undiluted as there is no water to promote dilution.
Improved dilution is noted in communities that have access to limited water availability and
adopt solutions to optimize water use. If piped water and wastewater services are provided,
the efﬂuent is conventionally diluted as elsewhere. Finally, high volume of water is added to
the sewage system when water bleeding is performed, resulting in a weaker concentration
(Smith and Low, 1996a).
As summarized by Hrudey and S. (1981), concentration of total coliforms where water
bleeding is adopted is in the order of 105 CFU/100mL, while 107 for holding tanks efﬂuent
and 5·108 for honey buckets.
2.2.2
Impacts
Pathogens present in coastal waters can be divided in two groups: autochthonous and al-
lochthonous. The ﬁrst one is characterized by pathogens that have an ecological niche in
recreational waters, while the latter come from an outter source, such as human and ani-
mal wastes (Boehm and Soller, 2013). Focus is given to allochthonous pathogens, as they
originate from warm-blooded animals and potentially wastewater.
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
19
Public Health. Bacteria, viruses and parasitic protists are sources of waterborne pathogens
and thallasogenic diseases - infectious diseases caused by wastewater pollution in the ma-
rine environment (Shuval, 1986). When bathers get in contact with any of these, risks can
range from low to death threatening. Health effects include gastrointestinal illnesses, skin
and eye infections, earaches and cough or congestion, which can all be caused by swallow-
ing, having contact with or even breathing in mists or aerosols from contaminated waters
(CDC, 2020c).
A study performed by Hlavsa et al. (2018) revealed that the main source of recreational
waters related reported outbreaks in the period of 2000 to 2014 in the United States were
parasites (45 %), followed by bacteria (26 %), resulting in at least 27219 cases and 8 deaths.
Coastal waters across the world impacted by wastewater are estimated to be the cause of ap-
proximately 120 million gastrointestinal and 50 million respiratory illnesses every year (Shu-
val, 2003). Assessing the true number of impacted people is a challenge, given the fact that
most of the cases are not reported as they tend to be mild and self-limiting (Boehm and
Soller, 2013). It is expected that the true rate of cases is 10 to 100 times greater than what is
reported (Mead et al., 1999).
Autochthonous organisms such as the genus Vibrio are responsible for different infec-
tions. Approximately 100 species have been identiﬁed, with 10 known to be harmful to hu-
mans (FHI, 2019b). Vibrio cholerae, for example, is the bacterium responsible for causing
cholera, transmitted by swallowing contaminated food or water. Dehydration is one of the
results of severe cholera, and if untreated can lead to death within hours (CDC, 2020a). Bac-
teria such as Vibrio vulniﬁcus, Vibrio parahaemolyticus and Shewanella are transmitted by
entering open wounds and further spreading in the body, mostly between May and October
when water temperatures are higher. Symptoms include watery diarrhea, fever, low blood
pressure, vomiting, pain and discoloration, with serious cases leading to amputation or even
death after 1 or 2 days after becoming ill (CDC, 2019). Feces from contaminated people can
carry the pathogens, reason why wastewater can be a potential source of the above men-
tioned pathogens.
Mead et al. (1999) concluded that more than 95 % of non-foodborne illnesses in the USA
are caused by eight allochthonous pathogens: norovirus, rotavirus, adenovirus, Campylobac-
ter, Salmonella, pathogenic E. coli, Cryptosporidium spp. and Giardia lamblia. Symptoms
are gastrointestinal and respiratory illness, and skin and eye infections. Cercarial dermatitis,
popularly known as swimmer’s itch, is a common skin infection example. Cercarie is found
==PAGE:32==
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
20
in blood vessels of its main host - waterfowl. Eggs are produced by the cercarie and released
together with feces. The eggs eventually hatch and liberate miracidia, which then develops
in a molluscan (snails) intermediate host. Finally, the dermatitis-producing cercarie pene-
trate the skin of exposed bathers. As they can not develop in humans, they end up dying
under the skin and in some cases causing an allergic reaction and rash. Considering larvae
presence is higher in shallow waters, kids are more susceptible to be affected (CDC, 2020b).
Increase in water temperature is known to speed up parasite development and transmis-
sion. Together with more time spent in water by people, hot temperatures are responsible
for higher chances to acquire the infection (De Liberato et al., 2019).
In Norway, cases of swimmer’s itch have been reported in all counties, more speciﬁcally
in 163 out of 431 municipalities in the period of 1980 to 2017. In 2014, 61 locations identiﬁed
as recreational waters were responsible for causing swimmer’s itch, the highest since the
ﬁrst reported case in 1980 (FHI, 2019a). Unfortunately, data does not present the number of
people who were impacted by it, reason why it is not possible to evaluate the true extent of
occurrence in the country. High bathing water temperatures in the summer of 2018 was the
reason why outbreaks of Vibrio and Shewanella infections took place in Norway. Six serious
cases of Vibrio vulniﬁcus and 2 of Shewanella putrefaciens were reported, in addition to 50
mild cases of wound and ear infections (FHI, 2019c).
Increase in the number of Vibrio reported infections in northern Europe has been linked
to an increase in sea surface temperature (SST), specially during “heatwave” years (Le Roux
et al., 2015). Survey performed by Vezzulli et al. (2012) showed that an increase in the preva-
lence of V. cholerae in the coastal North Sea for the last 44 years is correlated to warming SST.
Baker-Austin et al. (2013) also concluded that warming patterns are related to the emergence
of Vibrio infections in northern Europe, mostly in the Baltic Sea area.
Sea food is another source of waterborne diseases and infections that must be men-
tioned. Firstly, due to bioacumulation - when a substance is consumed and accumulated
at a faster rate than excretion (Popek, 2018). First order consumers in the food chain acci-

2.2.3
Enteric bacteria survival.
The presence of bacteria in a water body after disposal is determined by the actions of factors
that affect growth, reproduction, survival and mortality. Some of the main environmental
stressors to bacteria survival in marine waters are sunlight, salinity, disinfection, starvation
and predation (Byappanahalli et al., 2012).
Sunlight has proven to be the major factor affecting the survival of TtC (Gameson and
Gould, 1975; Sinton et al., 1999). An experiment conducted by Fujioka et al. (1981) concluded
that sewage-borne coliforms diluted in seawater survived for days when in sunlight absence,
while being inactivated in no more than 180 min with sunlight presence. When not directly
exposed to sunlight (i.e. cloudy days or high turbidity surface layer), the study showed that
fecal streptococci content remained stable even after 4 h, while TtC inactivation still showed
good results (Fujioka et al., 1981). A more recent study by Kay et al. (2005) concluded that
when turbidity is higher than 200 NTU, intestinal enterococci decay is similar to when in
dark conditions. This leads to the conclusion that visible sunlight, instead of its ultraviolet
light spectrum, is responsible for the bactericidal effect.
Predation, as expected, is known to shorten T90 - the time it takes for 90 % of the bacteria
in a sample to decrease (Kay et al., 2005). Effect of pH in coliforms decay is dramatic only in

extreme high or low values (Wilkinson et al., 1995). The process of bacteria attaching to par-
ticles and ﬂocs for further settling is also described by Jenkins et al. (1984), where bacteria is
expected to settle in the sea bed and potentially deteriorating water quality by resuspension.
Salinity represents a threat for bacteria mainly because of the immediate osmotic up-
shock, which if overcame may mean survival in this new environment (Munro et al., 1989).
It is known that coliforms are less persistent than intestinal enterococci in seawater (WHO,
2003). Another shock is caused by the temperature difference. Even though optimal growth

low temperatures (Rozen and Belkin, 2001). Reports have indicated that stability of E. coli is
better at lower temperatures (Carlucci and Pramer, 1960; Vasconcelos and Swartz, 1976) and
elevated water temperatures are known to cause faster degradation rates (Wilkinson et al.,

2.3
Recreational Waters Quality
According to WHO (2003), different sources can be the cause for impacts on recreational
water quality: sewage disposal, diffuse pollution from agricultural areas, urban runoff and
combined sewer overﬂows (CSOs). Regulations have been established in order to identify
possible contamination from these sources and classify bathing waters as a way to preserve
public health.
2.3.1
EU Directive 2006/7/EC
Years ago, when untreated or insufﬁciently treated wastewater disposal was still a common
practice around Europe, high nutrient loads from urban and agricultural runoff was respon-
sible for polluting waters to a point that engaging in recreational activities was not possible.
In addition to a growing tourism industry, environmental awareness and risks to the public
health, the EU started adopting in 1976 the Bathing Water Directive (BWD) (76/160/EEC).
Aiming to improve bathing water quality, the directive included guidelines on sampling fre-
quency and suggested methods for analysis. Limits for a total of 19 different parameters were
set as a way to verify whether a speciﬁc location is safe. The directive was eventually criti-
cized for being too technical and outdated, reason why a new and revised BWD (2006/7/EC)
came into force in 2006 and replaced the previous one in 2014. (Milieu Consulting SPRL,
2019).
The Member States are annually required to identify all bathing waters and deﬁne the
bathing season length. A monitoring calendar must be established before the start of the
season and taken into force no later than 4 days after the deﬁned start date. Samples must
be collected from the area where most bathers or highest risk of pollution are expected. As-
sessment is made on a long-term monitoring of at least 4 years. A minimum of 4 samples are
required to be collected and analyzed for each bathing season, totalling 16 samples. (CEC,
2006).

2.3.4
Tools
Quantitative Microbial Risk Assessment. QMRA is a tool often used to determine how mi-
crobial agents spread in the environment and to characterize the nature of the adverse out-
comes. This approach is based on information and data applied to mathematical models
that in the end reveals the risks involved, as a function of chance, hazard, exposure and con-
sequence. On recreational water quality assessments, QMRA has been used for assessing
pathogen loads from swimmers, sewage, animals and other sources of pollution to estimate
water contamination, exposure, and chances of infection (Haas et al., 2014). According to
Haas et al. (2014), QMRA four pillars can be summarized as:
• Hazard Identiﬁcation: Identiﬁcation of microbial agents and their respective exposure
impacts, together with the context on which they are found. This is obtained from clin-
ical literature and past studies.
• Dose Response: A mathematical characterization used to evaluate the correlation be-
tween the rate of exposure and the chances of infection, disease or death resulted by it.
These models are mostly based on experimental data.
• Exposure Assessment: Evaluation of the types and levels of human exposure to the
speciﬁed microbial agents. By using scenarios, the potential pathways from the con-
tamination source to the human receptor are veriﬁed. Microorganisms persistence and
factors behind it are key to understanding the ﬁnal concentration of doses to the public.
• Risk Characterization: Integrating the data from the previous steps will result in the
expected magnitude of public health problem and how likely exposure will occur. The
output is the probability of infection or death after exposure (e.g. 1/200).
Daley et al. (2018) studied the state of knowledge of necessary data for a reliable QMRA
of wastewater efﬂuent exposure in Inuit communities. According to what was concluded,
there is a need for more information on different topics. In the physical environment, more
research is necessary on fate and transport of wastewater pathogens; their persistence in the
Arctic; and waterfowl, sled dogs and wildlife as contamination sources. There is not enough
research on the levels of pathogens and how transmission of pollutants take place in wildlife
==PAGE:42==
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND
30
and ﬁsh, important parts of the Inuit diet. Ingestion, inhalation and absorption values need
to be adjusted for Arctic communities, especially in regards to raw food consumption.
Microbial Source Tracking. When the source of contamination is known, management
of water quality is made easier. This is the main goal of MST, an important tool specially for
waters impacted by pathogenic microorganisms such as recreational waters (Bitton, 2011b).
The concept is based on the fact that different microbial strains are related to speciﬁc
animal hosts, allowing to differ contamination from human or animal source. Microbiolog-
ical, genotypic, phenotypic and alternative methods are used as approaches to identify the
source. For some time, the fecal coliforms to fecal streptococci ratio (FC/FS) was used as a
MST tool. Ratios over 4.0 would indicate human pollution, whereas ratios less or equal to
0.7 refer to animal pollution (Geldreich and Kenner, 1969). The ratio was created according
to the fact that human feces contain higher fecal coliform counts than animals, while higher
levels of fecal streptococci are present in animal feces. The method however stopped being
recommended by the American Public Health Association (APHA) in 1999, as it was shown
to be unreliable due to different reasons, such as variable survival rates of fecal streptococci
species (Scott et al., 2002).

Adventfjorden is a part of Isfjorden, the largest ﬁord on the west coast of Svalbard. It is 8.3 km
long and 3.4 km wide. Tides and wind-driven surface currents are known to provide good wa-

the dominant currents are expected to behave as shown in Figure 3.8. Longyearbyen out-

Untreated wastewater discharge is an important source of contaminants such as phospho-

Fresh water inﬂow is a major component of the Adventfjorden system. During the win-
ter, there is no expected inﬂow as low temperatures keep the rivers frozen. The ﬁrst inﬂow
happens during the spring, resulted from snow cover melt. The second and highest inﬂow,
between May and September when temperatures are higher, is originated from rivers Ad-
ventelva and Longyearelva (Weslawski et al., 2011), changing the physicochemical condi-
tions on the internal part of Adventfjorden and potentially transporting trace elements from
abandoned mines (Kalinowska et al., 2020). Coal mining activities are an important source
of different contaminants, such as heavy metals, PAHs, polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs),
perﬂuorinated chemicals (PFC) etc. Runoff from closed mines is known to highly contribute
to heavy metals pollution in the ﬁord (Granberg et al., 2017).
Recent study shows that in 2010 the melt season in Svalbard was around 131 days, but
tends to increase to up to 175 days in the future (Kvamstø et al., 2012). Such a difference is
expected to increase the volume of meltwater and precipitation (rain) being discharged in
the ﬁord, having an impact in the hydrology and biota.
Impacts like these are expected due to climate change. Terminologies such as “Arctic am-
pliﬁcation” are usually adopted to refer to the enhanced consequences Arctic regions are
suffering from. A good example is how sea ice loss impacts the temperature: with warmer
temperatures, sea ice melts faster, reducing the albedo effect and consequently increasing
air temperature, a process known as positive feedback loop. There is a fear that warmer tem-
peratures and nutrients from increased wastewater discharge due to tourism will be respon-
sible for local eutrophication, resulting in the development of microbial mats, blue-green
algae and toxic algal blooms (Weslawski et al., 2011).

4.1.1
Sampling
Collecting the samples is a step that requires extra care, as doing it incorrectly can make
results unreliable even if the most precise and accurate analysis methods are adopted. Dif-
ferent analytes of interest may require different collection bottles, minimum volume, preser-
vation and holding times. As microbiology is the focus on this study, the following sections
describe the procedures speciﬁcally for that.
Sampling in Sisimiut was performed in December 07, 2020 as a part of a side project also
carried out by the author. The studied area was Paaraarsuk bay, where recreational activities
such as kayaking and swimming take place (Figure 4.1). Wide mouth plastic (polyethylene)
bottles with a 250 mL capacity were used as containers, with at least 2.5 cm head space left to
allow for proper mixing before analysis. Sea water was collected from 10 different locations
along the shoreline, with most of them being key points where people are expected to get in
contact with the water.
A reference sample was collected far from sewage outlets, approximately 1.5 km from U1,
the closest one. Grab samples were collected from a 10 cm depth in a 50 cm water column
and analyzed for bacteria the same day to avoid the need of preservation and potential in-

4.1.2
Analysis
Previously discussed in subsection 2.2.1, E. coli was adopted as the fecal contamination in-
dicator and the only analysis performed to check for the bathing water quality 1.
Regulations on holding times before analysis is scarce and varies among different litera-
ture. Presentations from lectures where the source is not given suggest that samples should
be analyzed within 8 hours of sampling (Staley, 2011; Dichter et al., 2015). The lack of in-
formation and reasoning for that was previously a topic of discussion studied by Standridge
and Lesar (1977). The study even cites a rumour that 8 hours was the time it took to travel
by horse from the British Minister of Health’s laboratory to the Thames River and back. ISO
19458 (International Organization for Standardiation, 2006) also mentions this fact and other
relevant literature (Geldreich, 1975) to conclude that samples must be analyzed as soon as
possible. EPA suggests a maximum holding time of 8 hours for bacterial tests on Table II
of Part 136.3 of the Code of Federal Regulations - Identiﬁcation of Test Procedures, mean-
ing that incubation should start within 8 hours of collection (EPA, 2021a). The EU Directive
2006/7/EC suggests that the time between collection and analysis must be as short as possi-
ble. If impractical, the samples must be stored in the dark on a temperature of 4◦C ± 3◦C for
no more than 24 hours.

The only available approach was to use Petriﬁlms™for bacteria quantiﬁcation, based
on membrane ﬁltration. Petriﬁlms™are approved for use in the food industry, including
bottled water, but its application to sea water has not been approved yet. An unpublished
work, performed by Technical University of Denmark’s PhD student Judith Maréchal, has
nonetheless shown that Petriﬁlms™may offer comparative results with Colilert and Tecta™
methods (more speciﬁcally for E. coli count plates) (Maréchal, 2020). As concluded by the
study, “there is no statistically signiﬁcant difference between the methods” (Figure 4.3). Due
to low cost and little necessary logistic effort, its use on remote communities has shown to
be less demanding than other techniques.

At the same time, it also allows the possibility of adopting other methods, as long as they
are proven to be efﬁcient and provide results comparable to the suggested ISOs. In this case,
the Petriﬁlm™approach could potentially be used as a bacteria enumeration method after

5.2.1
Wastewater Transport Model
Model setup. A model made with the US EPA software Visual Plumes was performed by
Leikvin and Evenset (2009) in order to verify how wastewater spreads after discharge in Ad-
ventfjorden. This subsection consists of a summary of what was done by the study. The in-
puts consisted of: (1) TtC as the discharge material; (2) wastewater salinity and temperature;
(3) water volumes; (4) vertical proﬁles of salinity and temperature based on conductivity,
48
==PAGE:61==
CHAPTER 5. RESULTS
49
temperature and depth (CTD) data; and (5) currents speed and direction.
By the time the study was performed, no data was available for the number of TtC present
in Longyearbyen’s wastewater. It was then assumed a value of 2·106 CFU/100mL, an approx-
imation made based on personal communication and considering the sewage to be well
diluted. Discharge ﬂow was assumed to be 9.6 l/s, which results in a TtC content of approxi-
mately 2·108 CFU/s being discharged into the ﬁord. The outlet depth was set as 50 m.
Discharge from Longyearelva and Adventelva are included in the summer model as they
considerably inﬂuence the ﬁord’s system. The model was then created by setting different
layers. The upper layer was considered to be 3 m of brackish water, according to previous
CTD measurements. The next layer was set from 3 to 10 m and the last layer from 10 m to
the sea bed, where only tides are expected to inﬂuence the model. A winter model was also
generated, where a single homogeneous layer mainly driven by tides dynamics was assumed
given the fact that there is no discharge from the rivers.
On the surface, winds and tides are the most important driving forces. Falling tides are
expected to make the current go towards Isfjorden, while rising tides do the opposite. Low
and high tides are periods when little inﬂuence on wastewater transport is expected, with
low velocities on the surface water.
Storage of wastewater under the surface occurs according to density in the water mixture.
Sea water density increases with depth. When wastewater is released and starts mixing with
sea water, its density increases until a point where it equalizes with unmixed sea water. The
mixed water mass will then have the same speciﬁc gravity as its surroundings, with no posi-
tive buoyancy anymore. Remaining vertical kinetic energy may still make the mix go slightly
above the storage depth, but will then sink again (Figure 5.1).
Results. The vertical proﬁle results showed that during the summer, storage of wastew-
ater occurs in deep waters, with outer limits from 15 to 47 m. In the winter, wastewater is
expected to go as close to the surface as 2 m, with its upper limits reaching the surface (Fig-
ure 5.2). By being lighter than the recipient water, wastewater tends to go up during winter
months when the sea water is homogeneous. On the other hand, when there is glacial water
inﬂow from the rivers, wastewater ﬁnds a barrier that prevents it from getting to the surface.

5.2.2
E. coli Levels
An investigation by Cochrane and Evenset (2020) was carried out in Adventfjorden to check
for the ﬁord’s water quality. Different analyzes took place, such as chemical (PAHs, heavy
metals) and biological (sediment and benthic fauna). In the present study, however, focus is
given to the bacterium (E. coli) results as that is the bacterium often adopted as the indicator
organism when assessing bathing water quality (see section 2.3). Sediments and sea water
samples were collected in August/September 2020 from the outlet’s surroundings.
The highest level of E. coli in sediments was recorded in the sample collected from the
outlet location - 30 CFU/g. Sea water analysis for E. coli only showed presence of the bac-

(Cochrane and Evenset, 2020). The value is well below the “Excellent quality” value estab-
lished by the EU Directive, 250 CFU/100mL (CEC, 2006). However, a single measurement is
not enough to assess if waters are safe for bathing activities.

6.1
Sisimiut
The numbers show a presence of bacteria in a bay where initially no sewage discharge is
expected. “Bay 4” showed the highest concentration of E. coli among all samples, suggest-
ing that there is either a source of pollution next to that location or that the currents and
tides lead to a contamination concentration in that area. Short-term pollution originated
from precipitation runoff is not a possibility, considering samples were collected when air
temperature was already well below zero.
Presence of wildlife is not noticed in the area, reinforcing the theory that contamination
transport is tidal based. However, a conclusion on the contamination source is not yet pos-
sible. Waterfowl and sled dogs may be potential sources as suggested by Daley et al. (2018),
even though Soller et al. (2010) concluded by initial QMRA that animals (not cattle) contam-
ination represent less risks than sewage disposal. It is also possible that water ﬂows from the
harbor (north east from “Bay 3”) to the bay, another contamination pathway. Even though
there are no direct discharges in the harbor, the location is known to have a higher level of
pollution in comparison to one of the city’s main outlet vicinity (U7) (see subsection 3.1.2).
In a pre-visit to the chosen locations done 2 days before the sampling day, a strong odor
of wastewater could be noticed next to “Bay 8”, which was not observed in the day of sample
collection. This suggests that presence of wastewater in the area may oscillate according
to still unknown factors. Illegal discharges are also possible, as indicated by an anonymous
source but unconﬁrmed by the municipality.
Single measurements, as done in this study, is explicitly not enough for assessing recre-
ational water quality. Nevertheless, as a comparison, all measurements are well below the

6.1.1
Limitations and Data Accuracy
During the project where the Sisimiut samples were collected and analyzed, little was known
about how strict the methods for analyzing are. The 23rd edition of the Standard Methods for
Examination of Water Wastewater (Baird et al., 2017), a constantly updated source with EPA
approved methods, and the ISO 19458 (International Organization for Standardiation, 2006)
revealed possible error sources in the previously adopted procedures:
• Collection should have been done from a deeper water column, at least 1 m.
• Samples were probably not shaken enough to break clumps of bacteria. To be more
speciﬁc, the recommendation is to shake 25 times up and down for 7 seconds, in a
30 cm arc.
• After ﬁltering the sample, the funnel should have been rinsed with distilled water (3x
20 mL) in order to prevent it from retaining bacteria. The mixture should have been
ﬁltered through the membrane. Big inﬂuence not expected, as low concentration was
anticipated.
• The membrane material was cellulose nitrate and not mixed cellulose ester (MCE) as
recommended.
The samples were collected in December, not during the summer season as recommended
by the EU Directive and the IK-21/94. The number of bathers and kayakers in that time are
expected to be little.

is the best approach to ﬁnd accurate results. The method has shown good correlation in
regards to the logarithmic scale, but enumeration comparison tends to be inconsistent. In

Kleinheinz et al. (2012) also compared Colilert and Petriﬁlm™E. coli enumeration meth-
ods, focusing on coastal recreational waters. Results showed that Colilert measurements
ranged from 10 to 337.2 MPN/100mL, while Petriﬁlm™results ranged from 2490 to as much
as 1.7E6 CFU/100mL (Kleinheinz et al., 2012). However, the adopted method was different
than Maréchal’s, with no membrane ﬁltration performed. Count plates were directly inoc-
ulated with 1 mL of sample and incubated following the manufacturer’s instructions (Klein-
heinz et al., 2012). It was concluded that due to considerable overestimation, Petriﬁlm™
should not be used as a E. coli enumeration method in coastal waters, given the fact that
unnecessary closures would consequently occur (Kleinheinz et al., 2012).
To conclude, Maréchal (2020) method for bacteria enumeration has great potential. In
the water security ﬁeld, for instance, identifying the presence of bacteria is enough when
checking for fecal contamination. Little necessary logistic effort makes it a good method
when in isolated communities where laboratory infrastructure is not ideal or not present.
All samples analyzed by Petriﬁlms™revealed presence of E. coli, in accordance to Colilert
18 and Tecta™B4. On the other hand, recreational water quality is evaluated according to
predetermined values, hence the need for precision in the measurements to avoid unnec-
essary actions. Until a higher number of samples are studied, and a better statistical study
performed, it is still early to conclude if Petriﬁlms™are a reliable method when quantifying
E. coli for recreational water quality assessments.

6.1.2
Recommendations for Further Work
MST and QMRA approaches are recommended to identify the source of E. coli in Sisimiut,
whether it is human or animal, and reveal the risks for the community. Furthermore, it is still
unclear to what extent each of the nearby outlets contributes to the transport of microor-
ganisms to the bay. Use of different strategies are encouraged to evaluate how wastewater is
spread after disposal. This may indicate if, which and how much the outlets are contributing
to the bay’s pollution. The presence of E. coli suggests a potential risk for the population.
A better sampling strategy is suggested, with collection done during low, high and ebb
tides and in different weather conditions as suggested by Baird et al. (2017). This will serve
as a way to verify if tidal changes affect water quality in the bay. If that is the case, a water
quality monitoring program is proposed. Together with increasing levels of bacteria, visual
pollution - as reported by citizens, is another important reason to start monitoring water
quality as stated by the EU Directive (CEC, 2006).
The municipality’s plan of building a marine outfall must be evaluated. Near shore dis-
posal, specially of untreated wastewater, offers extra risk to the public as not enough water
exchange, thus dilution, is performed. Furthermore, it has an aesthetic impact, causes bad
smell on the surroundings and restricts the possibility of recreational activities engagement.
Taking Longyearbyen as an example, an outlet positioned 63 m deep and with a population
of less than half of Sisimiut’s, has shown to not be enough to keep the surface safe from
contamination. Hydrological studies, such as the one performed in Longyearbyen, are sug-
gested as a way to simulate how spread of wastewater would take place according to where
the outfall is positioned, in order to optimize its location and verify if it will be beneﬁcial. If
proven not to be workable, another solution such as implementation of a treatment plant
may instead be considered.
6.2
Longyearbyen
As previously discussed, q-tips are sometimes ﬂushed in Longyearbyen and have been found
in Hiorthhamn, supporting the model’s claim that wastewater can periodically end up in that
area. Wind is not included in the calculations but is usually blowing into or out the fjord,
which in both cases is not likely to bring wastewater to Longyearbyen, increasing concentra-
tion towards southeast or northwest of the outlet. Wind direction and strength are expected
to have high inﬂuence on the surface layer, signiﬁcantly increasing the TtC content on the
==PAGE:69==

57
opposite direction. The addition of winds to the model would therefore increase variability
to how transport of wastewater is on the surface layer.
According to the model, no to little presence of wastewater is expected above the 10 m
depth between June and October, when sea water samples were collected (Figure 5.2). This
means that locations where the other 3 sea water samples were collected from (500 m and
750 m northwest of the outlet, in a 10 m depth; and 10 m depth on the outlet location), were
already expected not to show presence of bacteria.
Bacteria are more likely to get to the surface in the period of September to June, when
water proﬁle is homogeneous and there is no or little river inﬂow in the ﬁord. Extensive
presence of sunlight during the summer, when recreational activities are most likely to take
place, is an advantage in bacteria degradation for Arctic locations. High turbidity due to melt
water inﬂow in Longyearbyen may not allow bacteria to reach the surface, but at the same
time limits the sunlight bactericidal effect. Higher risk of infection is therefore expected in
the winter period, when wastewater is more likely to reach the surface and considering the
chance that northeast winds may transport bacteria closer to Longyearbyen.
6.2.1
Limitations and Data Accuracy
Wind, known to be the main driving force on the surface transport of wastewater, is not
included in the model as a variable. This would mean variability in wastewater transport
according to wind speed and direction. Furthermore, the layers were modeled separately,
not allowing the input of layers interactions such as vertical velocity.
Weather conditions during sea water sampling by Cochrane and Evenset (2020) is not pro-
vided, neither the sampling methods. It is also unsure how precise the sea water sample was
collected in regards to the outlet’s location. If collected from the outﬂow’s opposite location,
for example, a low value is possible. Assuming a TtC discharge of 2·108 CFU/s as adopted by
the model, it is unlikely that the samples were accurately collected from the outlet’s outﬂow.
According to the report, samples were analyzed within 24 hours of collection, more than the
recommended of 8 hours as previously discussed.
6.2.2
Recommendations for Further Work
In Longyearbyen, it was proved that wastewater originated fecal contamination on the shore-
line is unlikely, but other potential sources must be considered. Presence of waterfowl can be
a potential source of pathogens (Elmberg et al., 2017). This is enhanced by a possibility that
==PAGE:70==

58
some may feed next to the sewage outlet’s mixing zone, an attractive nutrient rich area, ac-
cidentally ingesting bacteria or viruses and bringing them to the recreational areas through
defecation. Migrating wading birds feed on crustaceans such as the amphipod Onisimus
littoralis (Weslawski et al., 2011), which through its ﬁlter-feeding capacity may accumulate
harmful pathogens and end up in the birds’ intestines. This may result in shallow coastal wa-
ters or melt water channels contamination, which are eventually discharged into the ﬁord.
Additionally, when snow starts melting, water courses carry organic matter, surface runoff
from precipitation events and eventual sewage overﬂows to the ﬁord. A monitoring program
together with QMRA are suggested in order to assess the presence of fecal contamination
and evaluate if levels are high enough to justify adoption of protective measures.
Assessing the presence of E. coli in Adventfjorden as done by Cochrane and Evenset (2020)
can help evaluate how likely wastewater is to reach Longyearbyen’s shoreline according to
different weather conditions (specially wind). However, when sampling, a better strategy
such as by using the model results is suggested. Accurately knowing where the outlet is lo-
cated is also important.
Detection of cercarial dermatitis is difﬁcult, demanding and costly (Tracz et al., 2019;
De Liberato et al., 2019). E. coli tests are not enough to identify the presence of cercarie,
but it represents an initial proneness for their presence. Fecal contamination from birds acts
as a warning signal, that could indicate the need to better evaluate the risks on recreational
water quality. Longyearbyen hospital was contacted for numbers on the incidence of swim-
mer’s itch but no answer was provided.
Through social media, a source that asked for anonymity suggested that 3 different es-
tablishments, excluding the airport, are not connected to the sewage system and directly
discharge their efﬂuent close to the shoreline. According to the same source, high volumes
of soap and oil are part of the efﬂuent. Information could not be veriﬁed as the provided
maps do not clearly state their presence. References used in this report only mention the
city’s and the airport’s outlets. The source could possibly be referring to 3 old outlets that
were deactivated in 2008, before the whole city was connected to the present one (Granberg
et al., 2017). Further investigation is suggested, as shoreline discharges can represent a risk
to bathers.
Collecting and analyzing mussels could also be a good indicator to check how polluted
bathing areas are (Viarengo and Canesi, 1991), even though it is a more advanced procedure
in comparison to other fecal contamination indicators. With their ﬁltering capacity, iden-

the supposedly illegal discharges.
By discharging wastewater next to the shoreline and in a location where currents are likely
to transport the efﬂuent into the fjord, it is also important to evaluate if traces of contami-
nation originated from the airport can be found close to bathing areas. Pathogens may not

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Commonly not a point of concern in small communities, recreational water quality is, among
other reasons, affected by untreated wastewater disposal. Exposure to pathogens found in
the efﬂuent, and consequently in the recipient water body, can be a source of outbreaks
especially where there is water scarcity, resulting in limited hygienic practices. In this sce-
nario, one contaminated person is enough to spread a disease. Considering some infections
require quick and intensive care, insufﬁcient medical assistance, occasionally the case in
isolated locations, can sometimes not be enough and result in fatalities.
In order to assess these risks, Sisimiut required sampling as the Paaraarsuk bay has not
been studied to the author’s best knowledge. Results showed presence of E. coli, a often
adopted fecal contamination indicator. In an area where no wastewater discharge is ex-
pected, this could potentially mean that efﬂuent from outlets away from the bay is being
transported by tidal changes and currents. Further studies are necessary to reveal if and
how transport of wastewater to the bay occurs, with special concern to weather condition
impacts.
A modelling study and recent sampling proved that transport of wastewater from Longyear-
byen’s marine outfall to the city’s shoreline is unlikely but still possible. Long distance, cur-
rents and high water exchange in the ﬁord system, together with close to undetectable lev-
els of E. coli, leads to the conclusion that pathogens and viruses originated from untreated
wastewater disposal do not usually pose a contamination risk to bathers on the city’s shore-
line. Northeast winds together with no rivers inﬂow between October and June, may how-
ever result in harmful pathogens ending on Longyearbyen’s shoreline. The vicinity of the
wastewater disposal is suspected to have excessive presence of TtC and is not recommended
for recreational activities.

In both cases, data was not enough to classify bathing waters according to standards.
Bathing water quality is based on a long-term assessment, either for 4 years (CEC, 2006) or
2 years (Statens Helsetilsyn, 1994). Furthermore, even though E. coli is a good and reliable
indicator organism for fecal contamination, fecal streptococci tests are required by the EU
Directive and in special cases by the Norwegian standard. The IK-21/94 does not clearly
specify if E. coli is the suggested indicator organism. From what is understood, the ther-
motolerant bacteria group as a whole is the chosen indicator, proven to be a questionable
approach by literature review.
The results, however, served as a way to verify if there are any risks of exposure given
the presence of fecal contamination. Paaraarsuk bay does show that there is risk, while un-
treated wastewater disposal in Adventfjorden has shown to be less likely to affect Longyear-
byen shoreline.
Similarly to Longyearbyen, Sisimiut has a conventionally diluted wastewater efﬂuent, ex-
cept for the “chocolate factory” outlet case. In Arctic communities where honey buckets are
the main approach for sewage collection, high strength disposal suggests an enhanced risk
of contamination for local bathers. Combined with limited water access, thus hygienic prac-
tices, bathing on recreational waters could be the source for illnesses outbreaks.
In these two locations, having safe bathing waters is not only important for the popula-
tion, but also for the economy. Both of them rely, at least partially, on the tourism industry
as a part of the yearly income. Tourist trafﬁc is known to be highly affected by environmental
quality (Weslawski et al., 2011). With an increasing number of visitors, it is important that
the cities maintain a good image, warn of potential risks of contamination, and do not face
the need to restrict the possibility of water-based activities engagement.
