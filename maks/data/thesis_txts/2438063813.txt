Abstract 
The future of the energy market is leaning towards a clean-energy direction worldwide. In that 
direction, Denmark has taken a leading role in this future with the decisions towards greener ways 
of covering the country’s electricity requirements, but the forecasting of the prices this electricity 
will have or the mix of generation of energy is full of uncertainty.  
This report studies the forecasting of energy mix, electricity price, gas price and household 
requirements to evaluate the potential market penetration of fuel cell systems as household 
micro-CHP energy solutions. By analyzing the future electricity prices and calculating the 
difference between these and the gas prices hourly, also considering the prognosis of fuel cell 
cost reductions the report evaluates if the superior efficiency of a fuel cell can procure for the 
energy needs of a household with a lower economic and environmental impact than traditional 
household systems such as a boiler or a heat pump. 
The evaluation concludes that the investment in this technology is a household appliance to 
provide for heat and power is feasible in the current conditions by 2025 but it is highly sensitive 
to the electricity prices and the ratio of heat and power consumption, being the investment more 
feasible the lower this ratio is. Furthermore, it is found that if current policies of tax exemptions to 
CHP technologies using biogas were applied to this technology or if biogas prices would decrease 
by at least 25%, the feasibility of this technology would increase significantly and the savings 
obtained would compensate for the initial investment. All of this assuming that the forecasted 
increase in production volumes generates a decrease in price to 3.500 €/kW.

Chapter 1: Introduction  
The transition of the global energy markets to renewable technologies is ongoing and the countries 
of Germany and Denmark are actively investing in leading the change to greener solutions. 
Germany has committed to a 45% share of renewable energies by 2030 while Denmark is aiming 
for a 50% by the same year (1). These statements are being backed up by the major energy 
providers of both countries who are diversifying their generation portfolio not only by adding more 
sustainable technologies such as wind and solar photovoltaic (PV), which are predominant in 
Germany (2) but also by transforming old electricity generation technologies fed by fossil fuels 
such as coal and gas fed power plants to carbon-neutral solutions such as waste-to-energy and 
biomass power plants (3). 
The task of leading this energy transition is a major ordeal that requires both investing in known 
renewable energy technologies and aiding in the research and development of new ones that can 
offer more efficient and/or effective results to achieve the targets previously stated. One of the 
aims of this R&D process is how to deal with the uncertainty of supply that accompanies a portfolio 
of generators with a high percentage of wind or solar PV technologies. Several solutions have been 
proposed to procure for this issue, amongst which we find the technology that is the aim of this 
study: fuel cells. 
The use of fuel cells and the technologies around them have been under thorough research for 
the past decades but they have not yet reached a powerful position in the market as part of the 
energy generation mix. It is the goal of many scientists and engineers worldwide to push the 
current boundaries of this technology to exceed its limited access to the markets, therefore 
making it more efficient and cost-effective. 
Having this in consideration, it is interesting to know how that could potentially influence the 
energy mix of the German and Danish markets and what the economic and environmental 
consequences of that would be. This needs to be contextualized in many aspects such as the 
energy market in which to use this technology (which are also dependent on the climate and 
geography of the area), the political landscape (support schemes, technology preferences and 
regulations) and the industrial context (scale of the production, standardization, macro-economic 
considerations, market routes, etc).

Problem discussion 
The leadership role in the energy transition to a more sustainable future is a political stand that 
responds not only to the planet’s needs but also to ensuring security of energy supply for the 
country. The repercussions of the oil crisis of the 1970s were perceived severely in Denmark and 
they developed an outspoken and widespread critique of the welfare state, ultimately leading to 
the design of the current national energy policy (4). 
This transition is currently trending to exploit the country’s renewable energy sources as the main 
drive of it. Wind, biomass and solar PV are the main technologies that will compose the country’s 
generation portfolio, with wind being the main generator. The electricity generated from this 
technology has risen to more than 10 TWh/year from the early 1990s until now and it is expected 
to grow to nearly 25 TWh/year by 2025 (5), being a 50% of the electricity production by 2020 (6) 
and aiming for 100% by 2050. 
The other main sources of electricity generation for the future Danish grid are biogas, solar PV and 
potentially the use of hydrogen as an energy carrier, with the first one being the biggest contributor 
of them all. The energy independence of a country provides the advantage of not depending from 
the fluctuating oil prices to ensure security of supply in these worse-case scenarios. Nevertheless, 
that security of supply is determined by the dependence in the country’s choice of energy mix, 
which in the case of sustainable technologies means depending in intermittent energy sources. 
There is also an extensive research about the dependence in wind energy and how that affects 
electricity prices, which will be studied in the section “Wind energy and electricity price” of this 
document, along with the future technologies to combine it with. 
Purpose 
The future scenarios studied by the big energy actors from the Danish market are based on the 
premise that the present technologies are the key for the green energy transition. The difference 
between them is the level of commitment to sustainability and the investment of making them 
even more efficient and mature. While this might very well be the future of the energy market, 
there is always the possibility that new technologies appear and take over at least a big part of 
what was considered to be the future energy mix. 
==PAGE:12==
11 | P a g e  
 
The idea behind this study is to analyze that possibility from the perspective of one technology: 
the fuel cell micro-CHP based technologies (onward also called FC-mCHP) focused on the 
household systems which are currently offered in the European market. 
FC-mCHP: concept and premise 
The premise is the following: the energy scenarios described for the green future of this country 
consider a big proportion of it depending on wind and biomass. Both technologies are abundant 
in Denmark and have a low to inexistent environmental impact (being the impact of biomass 
depending on its source). Nevertheless, wind introduces the danger of no security of supply if 
there is no way to store the energy provided, while biogas technologies are subject to the 
thermodynamic limits. 
FC-mCHP systems offer a solution to both problems, offering supply of electricity in seconds if 
already heated up when in lack of wind. They can also provide an electrical efficiency of 60% and 
an overall CHP efficiency of more than 90% (7), almost doubling the efficiency of a state-of-the-art 
CHP plant.

Chapter 2: Theoretical pre-considerations 
To have a better understanding of the conditions that have to be fulfilled for the commercialization 
of fuel cell systems as micro-CHP units for individual households’ certain conditions have to be 
met, conditions that include the amount of energy required by that household, the daily and 
seasonal variations of that required energy, the sources currently and potentially used to provide 
them. 
The following chapter aims to study those conditions and evaluate them as part of the 
assumptions to the economic and environmental impact of the implementation of this technology.

The report is going to focus in the countries of Denmark and Germany, focusing in the markets

requirements and the forecasts of these for the future. The goal is to see how the trend of 
technology changes match with the requirements for a higher proportion of renewable 
technologies in the energy market, keeping always the target in the household requirements.

Fuel cell systems: what are they? 
Fuel cells are currently attracting interest because of their huge potential for power generation in 
stationary, portable and transport applications and the increasing need for sustainable energy 
resources (17). They are far from being a new technology; the concept of the fuel cell was first 
demonstrated in 1839 by William Grove, although he called them “gas battery” at that moment, 
since which they have gone extensive research to improve their efficiency and operational 
conditions.  
 Although the challenge of a high price is known to be the main reason for the low market of this 
technology, the advantages it offers are worth summarizing:   
• Efficiency: fuel cells are more efficient than combustion engines whether piston or 
turbine based. It is also worth mentioning that this is applicable both to small systems 
and large ones, important for local power generating systems.  
• Simplicity: the essentials of these systems are very simple, with few moving parts if any 
at all. This can lead to long-lasting systems with high reliability and low O&M costs (17; 
18) 
• Low emissions: by-products of the fuel cell main reactions depend on the fuel cell used 
(hence on the fuel cell types). They go from pure water (which makes the fuel cell a “zero-
emission” system) to minimal emissions of CO2 (if and when considering the type of fuel 
used or keeping in mind the production of hydrogen, which normally involves this 
emission).  
• Silence: this is very important in both portable power applications and for CHP schemes.

Operation  
The basic principle of fuel cells consists in two electrodes (anode and cathode) and an electrolyte. 
A fuel, such as hydrogen, is fed to the anode and air is fed to the cathode. With the help of catalysts 
that lower the activation energy of the chemical reaction, hydrogen (or another molecule, depends 
on the material of the electrolyte) is separated in the anode and turned into an ionic form and 
travels through the electrolyte to the cathode as it can be seen in the example of Figure 3.  
 
 
Figure 7: Alkaline fuel cell working principle (one of the first fuel cell types used (17) 
 
The reactions are different according to the type of fuel cell discussed, but they all have in common 
the use of hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity and water. However, for these reactions to 
proceed continuously, electrons produced at the anode must pass through an electrical circuit to 
the cathode, while an ion passes through the electrolyte. These ions can be O2-, H+ or OH- 
according to the type of electrolyte and therefore fuel cell being used.  
The structure of the electrode is porous so that both the fuel from one side and the gas from the 
other can penetrate it. This is to give the maximum possible contact between the electrode, the 
electrolyte and the gas.

The SOFC is characterized by their high efficiency, fuel flexibility and suitability for CHP

Working principle of SOFC  
The SOFC is a complete solid-state device that uses an oxide ion-conducting ceramic material as 
the electrolyte, which makes it different from most of fuel cell devices that work with three phases 
(solid, liquid and gas) instead of only two (solid and gas). The anode is usually a zirconia cermet 
(composite of metal and ceramic) with nickel due to its electronic conductivity and low cost 
amongst the highly catalytic materials, while the cathode is normally made of electronically 
==PAGE:23==
22 | P a g e  
 
conducting oxides or mix of electronically and ion-conducting ceramics, with the most typical one 
being a strontium-doped lanthanum manganite. These two ensure a constantly reducing and 
oxidizing enclosed atmosphere respectively.  
The electrolyte is normally composed by yttria stabilized zirconia (YSZ) which reaches its peak ion 
conduction capacity from 700ºC to 1100ºC. The working principle of this fuel cell can be seen in 
Figure 9:  
The operating temperature is principally governed by the nature of the electrolyte, for it determines 
its ionic conductivity, and the thickness of the electrolyte layer. Therefore, the two most likely 
approaches to reduce the high working temperatures: reduce the thickness of the YSZ or search 
for alternative electrolyte materials with higher oxygen ion conductivities (which so far have not 
been found).  
SOFC have proven to offer tremendous potential for clean, efficient and economic energy 
generation, especially for CHP and small-scale stand-alone and remote applications. The ability to 
operate SOFCs directly on a range of practical hydrocarbon fuels, internally reforming the fuel and 
utilizing the high quality heat by-product, to give high system efficiencies and reduced system 
complexity, coupled to their ability to utilize carbon monoxide and their greater tolerance to 
impurities in the fuel, represent significant advantages of the SOFC over low temperature fuel 
cells.

Energy markets and mix 
The tendency to shift to a more sustainable electricity generation is fixed, but the strategy to follow 
is dependent in multiple factors such as national policies, international policies, technological 
developments, private company investments, etc. The Danish Energy Agency (DEA – 
Energistyrelsen) has studied and modelled five different scenarios according to the forecasted 
contribution expected of them (1). These scenarios work have five working assumptions according 
to the development, prices and investment level of renewable technologies (Wind, Biomass, Bio+, 
Hydrogen and Fossil fuels). 
The purpose of describing all scenarios is to have an available scope for Danish energy supply in 
the future and a visualization of how it can be configured. There are other scenarios created by 
the company responsible for the energy transmission in Denmark, Energinet, in which the focus 
is the level of commitment that different countries of Europe have to the transition the green 
energy, having four modelled scenarios according to it. 
It should also be considered that quantitative forecasting in regards of energy market related 
matters is a complex topic as previously stated in the section “The science of forecasting”, with 
the forecast depending not only in the technologies introduced but the policies approved to 
support them (see (21)), the learning curve of new technologies and their economic implication, 
the oil prices, etc.  
The general overview extracted from these studies is that the future of the generation mix in 
Denmark is going to be dominated by wind power, solar power and bioenergy (1). Energy storage 
and hydrogen technologies are also considered to be playing a role, although there is no 
consensus about what that role will look like and how big it will be.

Electricity market, price and forecasting 
A key discovery of this study has been the strategies and legislation behind the security of supply. 
The current design of the day-ahead market has some drawbacks that limit the efficiency of the 
power market. The generation capacity to bid in the electricity market has to be at least of 1 MW 
for the day-ahead market, while the regulatory market requires (24): 
- 
A 10 MW minimum bid size 
- 
A plan for the controllable load: The plan must be followed and must exist with 5-minutes 
values  
- 
Demand must be re-established after activation: In some cases, this may be difficult if 
special staff are needed for re-establishing demand, for example, some forms of 
industrial production. 
- 
Real-time measuring of regulation units: Real-time metering is relevant in relation to 
consumers in the +10 MW class. However, for small consumers, the cost of such a 
requirement is prohibitive 
- 
The bidding process in itself requires several active actions. First, a bid must be made, 
then if chosen the supplier notified, and finally the actual regulation must occur. This is 
an undesirably bureaucratic process for smaller resources and a simpler design might 
attract more participants. 
These regulations limit the benefits of wide implementation of FC-mCHP systems, which could 
provide great assistance in the form of market regulators as mentioned in the Ene.field project 
reports about their smart-grid capabilities (25). The aggregation of 500 2 kW SOFC-mCHP units 
would provide the advantage of being a virtual power plant (VPP) that if enabled with automation 
of the aggregated units could contribute to the needs of the grid operators and customers.

Fuel cell systems - present and future 
The cost of fuel cells has been a matter of debate for the past decades. Efforts have been made 
both by national organizations such as the United States Department of Energy (US DOE) and 
international organizations such as the International Energy Agency (IEA) and the Fuel Cells and 
Hydrogen Joint Undertaking (FCH), with the goal of reducing these costs and making this efficient 
technology market-ready. 
The currents prices of fuel cell systems vary a lot, dependent not only on the type of fuel cell but 
also on the application and the size of the system. They can also be altered by several factors, 
such as the manufacturing scale, the number of suppliers for the components or the devices to 
integrate it with: fuel processors, heat exchangers, power electronics and others. 
There have been numerous academic and industrial estimates about the future costs of this 
technology and the barriers between its current status and a potential market disruption (see (18; 
35; 36), as well as the economic and environmental implications of their implementation as 
domestic systems (see (37; 20). The articles agree in the environmental benefits these 
technologies deliver in the terms of higher energy efficiency and lower emissions (reduced 19 – 
56%). The economic benefits are also given an overview but the consensus is not present since 
they are dependent in the capacity to export excess electricity to the grid and the presence of feed 
in tariff and export tariffs, which depend on the country’s policy.  
Nevertheless, these studies show that the performance of the system is indeed satisfactory, but 
the main barrier is the CAPEX. Elmer et al (20) estimate that the presence of Feed-in-Tariffs of 
0,125 £/kWhe would reduce the minimal capital costs of a 1.5 kW BlueGEN SOFC mCHP system 
from £ 10.300 to £ 2125.

Air-to-water electrical heat pump  
Heat pumps are devices that employ the same technology as refrigerators, based on moving 
thermal energy in the opposite direction of the spontaneous heat flow (from a low temperature 
level to a higher one). Heat energy is not conserved in this process, so in order to provide energy 
for the work that is done, heat pumps require using an external source of high grade energy, which 
they do by following the Carnot cycle using two loops with different heat values.  
The compression and expansion of these cycles and their consequences for the heating system 
can be seen in Figure 17

Fuel cell system as FC-mCHP 
Fuel cell systems are not as used as the two previously described systems, but they are being 
studied for their potential to fill their place. Their capacity to not only provide the heat 
requirements but also supply electricity to the household with a considerably higher efficiency.

generators such as coal or gas power plants have a high level of efficiency but they are still subject 
to the efficiency limit determine by Carnot (see Equation 1) as any technology depending on a 
combustion.

85 – 95% CHP efficiency in an FC-mCHP system (7).

Chapter 5: Methodology 
Premises and assumptions 
The main premise of the study relies on the possibility of an increase of electricity prices and a 
decrease in the cost of producing FC-mCHP systems. The aim is not only to find if these conditions 
will ever happen but also to identify the key indicators that would motivate an investment in this 
technology. To do so, the data obtained is going to be the basis of a model that evaluates the 
economic and environmental impact of supplying a household’s energy needs with a FC-mCHP 
system or using more traditional systems such as a boiler or a heat pump. 
The assumptions that will be considered in order to research this premise are supported by 
previous research elaborated by experts in each of the studied areas. These assumptions are 
listed as follows: 
1. The average energy requirements of a household in Denmark are dominated by heat. 
The average heat:power ratio is 4:1. 
2. The temperatures of the year 2016 is not exceptionally high or low. 
3. The daily variations of electricity consumption in Danish household follow a measurable 
trend, with peaks at night (18 to 21h) and valleys at night (00 – 08). 
==PAGE:44==
43 | P a g e  
 
4. The prices of electricity are more volatile than the prices of gas. 
5. The gas grid is going to substantially increase its proportion of biogas by 2020. 
6. The main obstacle for the commercialization of FC-mCHP systems is their high price 
7. The price of FC-mCHP systems would be significantly reduced by scaling-up the 
production due to the effect of economies of scale. 
8. Smart-grid capabilities are going to be approved before 2025 in order to assist with the 
regulation of electricity, allowing household users to be active market players and 
therefor have access to the day-ahead market prices. 
Economic and environmental savings could be achieved by using an FC-mCHP system if the price of 
electricity is significantly higher than the price of gas. The difference should substantial enough to at 
least provide economical compensation for the difference between the prices of the system and the 
price of a “standard” heating system (boiler or heat pump). This difference in price is assuming that 
household owners are actually able to bid in the electricity markets and have a higher degree of control 
over their demand of electricity from an hourly perspective according to the market price rather than 
a fixed yearly price/kWh. If that were not the case, another study could be carried out regarding the 
seasonal difference in price between electricity and gas, analyzing the feasibility by an average cost 
per month or season.

Scenario simulation 
The study will contemplate the analysis of these costs in three different scenarios, all calculated 
from 2025 to 2035 and assuming the investment in the new system to happen the 1st of January 
of 2025. The scenarios will have to sub scenarios to compare separately the heat pump and boiler 
savings and costs with the fuel cell savings and costs. The scenarios are described in the following 
subsections: 
Scenario 1: Business as usual 
This scenario contemplates the investment on a FC-mCHP system in an electricity market situation 
in which the prices of electricity do not change except for the inflation. The fuel cell would operate 
in natural gas from the current gas grid. The scenario also considers a “frozen-policies” approach 
to the calculations, considering that no substantial change is made in the legislation from the 
current one. 
Scenario 2: Forecasted prices of electricity and biogas 
The following scenario emulates the previous study considering the energy prices calculated by 
the forecasts of the main energy agencies of Denmark as described in Chapter 5: Methodology- 
Forecasted prices. It also considers the substitution of natural gas to biogas as the operation fuel 
for the system and applies a “frozen-policies” approach to the calculations, considering that no 
substantial change is made in the legislation from the current one. 
 
Scenario 3: Forecasted prices of electricity and biogas with tax exemptions 
This scenario considers the same electricity prices of Scenario 2 and a variation of the biogas 
prices. The “frozen policies” approach is not taken, as it is assumed that if the household’s system 
works entirely with biogas, the household owner can apply to exemptions in the energy savings 
and CO2 emissions tax in accordance to the current policy applicable to the users of biogas for a 
CHP system. This would mean a reduction of the price of biogas of 0.3354 €/m3.

Sensitivity analysis 
The parameters considered in this analysis are dependent on forecasts of a very volatile nature 
due to so many factors that the estimations considered in this report are subject to be changes in 
an unpredictable way. Therefore the calculations will consider the calculation of the following 
factors based on the described assumptions:

Higher electricity prices: the average forecasted electricity prices of the energy agencies 
are in average 8 – 18 €/kWh lower than the current prices (considering the methods of 
future price calculation). This optimistic approach can be recalculated and considering a 
higher average of €/kWh give a potential scenario calculation, corresponding to the DEA

Estimated heat and electricity consumption: the estimations of the household’s

to the analysis in order to understand the sensitivity this technology has to undergo these

Chapter 7: Discussion of results 
Scenario 1 
It can be appreciated that the savings of the use of an FC-mCHP are substantial in both cases, 
being higher than the estimated future systems cost of 7.000 € for a 2kW system that could cover 
the energy needs of a household for 8738 h/year, since there is 22 h/year in which the electricity 
needs are higher than 2 kWh. 
The emissions of the Danish electricity grid are very low so the use natural gas to provide for the 
energy needs even with the superior efficiency of the cogeneration can’t compete with an energy 
mix with almost 50% of electricity generated by wind power (according to the current energy mix, 
by 2025 it will be higher than 50%). By evaluating these results, it is fair to mention that if the 
price growth of electricity and gas was to be determined only by inflation, the purchase of a fuel 
cell should be considered from an economic perspective.  
Scenario 2 
The forecasted electricity prices are much lower than the base scenario electricity prices, so it is 
to be expected that they are going to result in a lower spark spread.  
The results show a drastic decrease in hours of use of the fuel cell in comparison to the hours of 
use of the heat pump, with these two systems reaching a ratio of almost 50% – 50% in terms of 
their economic feasibility to provide for the household’s heat requirements. 
The gas boiler is practically ruled out as a feasible system to provide for the household’s needs, 
having by far the higher costs and a total usage time of 0.03% in the 11 years from 2025 to 2035.. 
The electricity generation mix is assumed to have drastically reduced its environmental impact to 
125 gCO2/kWh, but the impact of the fuel cell is considered lower than the grid system when 
assuming the emissions of the fuel cell, nevertheless the consideration of biogas coming from 
overall “zero-emissions sources” as future policies indicate (see: (33)) would see the consumption 
of energy from the grid be less CO2 efficient than emissions of the fuel cell. It should be reminded 
that according to the forecasted energy mixes previously mentioned, in this period of time the 
generation mix will still include fossil fuels in the form of natural gas while the fuel cell is 
considered to run entirely on biogas. 
==PAGE:73==
72 | P a g e  
 
Scenario 3 
This scenario is the most beneficial for the implementation of this technology. The tax exemptions 
associated to the biogas price for household CHP generation make the both systems (gas boiler 
and heat pump) unfeasible almost entirely for the 11 years of use. The only exception is the heat 
pump with a 0.63% of hours of feasible usage. 
The savings in this scenario would make the purchase of the fuel cell more than affordable which 
corresponds with the conclusions of Theo Elmer et al (20) that the only two things stopping FC-
mCHP systems of being an active part of the market are the CAPEX and the lack of Feed-in-Tariffs 
(or tax exemptions, which with the high taxations of Denmark would have the same effect). 
Key indicators and considerations 
The study shows promising results for the implementation of FC-mCHP systems as a household 
energy providing system under the right conditions. The maximum savings obtained in the 
sensitivity analysis have been in the conditions of high electricity prices, low biogas prices and a 
low heat to power ratio of consumption, which corresponds again with the conclusions of Theo 
Elmer et al (20). With the results obtained from the study and sensitivity analysis, the key 
indicators to study the purchase of this household appliance would be the following: 
 
A reduction of the CAPEX of an average FC-mCHP system to the values mentioned by 
Staffell and Green (36), estimated to be 3500 €/kW. 
 
An increase of the electricity price higher than the estimated by the mentioned 
forecasts. 
 
A decrease of biogas prices or a tax exemption to their application as fuel specifically for 
FC-mCHP systems. 
 
An increase in the insulation of households that as a consequence would decrease the 
heat to power ratio allowing the cogeneration to provide for a higher amount of the 
household’s heating requirements without having to use the peak electric boiler 
If these conditions are met, the market penetration of this technology can effectively happen, as 
previously announced by Staffell and Green (36). The market penetration achieved will be 
dependent in the degree in which the key indicators are met, the household’s owner willing to 
invest in a technology with a much higher CAPEX that a gas boiler or a heat pump but with a faster 
return of the investment and the environmental awareness of this same household owner.

Chapter 8: Conclusions 
This thesis has studied the current and forecasted evolution of both electricity and gas prices in 
the Danish energy markets, what energy mix will correspond with those prices and how that 
would affect them. It has also evaluated the implementation of a technology with a lot of market 
potential but also limitations, from which the biggest limitation is the CAPEX, and what the 
forecasts of this technology’s evolution predict. From these studies, a model has been built to 
assess the conditions that should be met in the energy markets and the FC-mCHP’s 
technological evolution to make the purchase feasible and what effect it would have for a 
Danish household from an economic and environmental perspective, which translates directly 
into the effect in the energy market (since the households consume 30% of the energy in 
Denmark). 
The findings of this study are that a reduction in the investment cost of a fuel cell to the levels 
estimated by Staffell and Green (36) would make it feasible with the current electricity and gas 
prices. Nevertheless, this feasibility is sensitive to the forecasted changes to the electricity 
prices. If the electricity prices grow in the average degree they are forecasted (which means an 
average 15 - 20% reduction of the current growth rate) without any decrease in gas prices 
(either natural gas or biogas) or an equivalently favorable tax exemption or feed-in-tariff, the 
purchase of this appliance would not be feasible in the near future. 
Moreover it is also seen that ratio of heat to power consumption is also found to determinant to 
the feasibility of this system’s implementation. The study finds an indirect correlation between 
the heat to power ratio and the feasibility of FC-mCHP systems: the lower the ratio of heat to 
power consumption, the higher the hours the system is feasible. This information, considering 
the increased insulation of the newly built and renovated households, is also showing a potential 
future for the implementation of this technology. 
The conclusion is that the feasibility of FC-mCHP systems to provide for household energy needs 
is going to be determined by the industry’s capability to meet the CAPEX reduction forecast and 
the energy market’s spark spread and heat:power consumption, and that if they follow the 
current trend and not the forecasts this can be expected to happen in a mid-term future.

Future studies 
Considering the future energy scenarios from Denmark described by Energinet (see: (1)), favoring 
one technology in opposition to the other may very well change drastically the calculated SSFC-mCHP 
and the HSFC-mCHP in the Danish energy market. A higher investment in bioenergies could lower the 
prices of biogas, while a higher investment in wind power could reduce the average electricity 
price but potentially increase its volatility (see as suggested by (28; 27; 26)). 
The implications of a higher electricity price volatility combined with a lower biogas prices and 
assuming smart-grid capabilities would make an interesting case to study the possibility of using 
FC-mCHP technologies as a regulating tool in the balancing market 
Other considerations should be taken into account such as not limiting the study to the Danish 
market. The carbon footprint, the spark spread and the support schemes of the UK and Germany 
are more favorable for the implementation of this technology and studies such as (20) show the 
potential of this technology in practical cases if the right policies are applied.
