1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Additive manufacturing of metals has become widely applied in various industries
over the past decades. However, the selection of readily printable steels is still
limited. Such are high-carbon cold work tool steels, like the material under inves-
tigation: AISI D2 / DIN X155CrVMo12-1. The present work aims to investigate
the printability and resulting microstructure of D2, and thus explore the potential
of its commercial use.
1.1.1
Additive Manufacturing
Additive Manufacturing (AM) is a rather young family of technologies based on
the idea of building the parts up, generally in a layer-by-layer process, instead of
subtracting material from a stock until the specifications are met. It has found a
wide range of applications due to its flexibility, high design freedom, wide range of
available materials (e.g. plastics and composites, ceramics, metals, etc.), no need
for tooling and shorter lead times, and relatively low cost. One of its downsides
are the need for post-processing, which, depending on the technology, could be
anything from cleaning the parts, removal of supports, machining and polishing to
final dimensions, sintering, and so on. Another general limitation is the production
volume. AM technologies are mostly used for prototyping, designer products, pro-
duction support (e.g. fixtures), high added value parts (e.g aerospace), complex
geometries, and medical implants. [1, 2]
Laser Powder Bed Fusion
While some Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAM) technologies have been around
for a longer time, such as cladding and spray forming, the most well known MAM
process is Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF). As more and more equipment sup-
pliers entered the market, they all invented their own name for basically the same
technology. Thus, LPBF is also known as Direct Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS),
Powder Laser Deposition (PLD), Selective Laser Melting (SLM), and Direct Metal
Printing (DMP). [3]
The principle of LPBF is presented on Figure 1.1. The main components of the
system are the laser source and optics, the moveable base or build plate, the
recoating mechanism, and the powder supply. As printing is done in an inert
atmosphere, the system is enclosed in a chamber, which also protects its users
from laser damage, powder contamination, and fumes. Additional components
could be sensors, ventilators, base plate, and pre-heating system as examples.
The fabrication process starts by the recoater mechanism spreading a thin layer
of powder (generally >
50 µm) onto the build plate. Following that, the laser
selectively fuses the powder particles together, creating a slice of the part. Then,
the build plate is lowered by the layer thickness, gets recoated, and the process
is repeated until the print is finished. [1, 2, 4]

The process itself is controlled by adjusting the input process parameters. There
are also parameters defined by process physics process, equipment and the ma-
terial used. All in all, over 150 parameters were already identified, which all influ-
ence part quality and process stability. In practice, the most commonly adjusted
inputs, in no particular order, are:
• Laser Power P [W]
• Scanning Speed V [mm/s]
• Hatch Spacing S [µm]
• Layer Thickness T [µm]
• Atmospheric Gas
• Gas pressure [bar]
• Scanning Strategy
• Building Orientation [°]

effective energy input. It has a few different variations, such as linear, areal, and
volumetric ED. As volumetric ED (VED) was found to be the most accurate, only

filled in with paths generated by mathematical algorithms.
In order to introduce some randomness into the texture (to lower anisotropy), each
following layer is rotated, often by 67°. This value found to provide the most layer
orientations before repeating one. [5]
The strategy has a significant influence on part quality and thermal gradients, thus
on residual stresses too.
In MAM, residual stress (RS) is a practically inevitable byproduct of the thermal
manufacturing processing itself. A part might be in a state of stress equilibrium,
however if a small piece is taken out, that could be under tensile or compressive
stress. RS could be induced by either a thermal gradient or by shrinkage during
solidification. Thermal gradients can be quite steep, as cooling rates range from
103 K/s to 108 K/s, most often being around 106 K/s. Solidification is continu-
ously happening with the melt pool ”moving around”. The length of which could
be around 0.9 – 1.4 mm, its depth 0.16 – 0.63 mm, and the width 0.12 – 0.38 mm, all
depending on the process parameters. Melt pools get created on top of already
solidified layers, which contract less, so here they cannot shrink freely. In the
freshly solidified upper layer, tensile stress gets introduced, while compressive
stress in the colder bottom layers. Cracking in printed parts is caused by high RS,
while lower stresses could also cause deformation, delamination, and even pore
generation. While there are simple models to explain the fundamentals, sophisti-
cated finite element models are used to predict these stresses in practice, as its
a highly complex phenomen. [2, 6, 7]
1.1.2
Steels used for LPBF
Some of the most commonly utilised steels are: low-carbon stainless steel grades
316L, 320, and 17-4PH; M300 maraging steel (C: max 0.03 wt%); the relatively
high-carbon H13 hot-work tool steel (C: 0.32 −0.45 wt%), and the high-carbon
M2 high speed tool steel (C: 0.78 −1.05 wt%). Steels with a carbon content below
0.2 wt% are considered weldable, and conforming to this, the mentioned stainless
steels are generally easy to print. H13 and M2, on the other hand, require more
caution, as they are prone to cracking. [4, 8]
Even though researchers have already printed crack-free ultra high-carbon steels
(C: 1.25 −2.0 wt%), e.g. [9], these materials are not used in industry yet because
of the difficulties they pose.
1.2
Motivation
The driving force behind the project is twofold. One is to realize a more sustainable
powder supply by reusing surplus powder from a spray forming process. Produc-
ing high-quality powders for LPBF processes is energy-intensive and costly, so
this way substantial savings could be made. The other motivator is to further ex-
pand the material selection and fill the gaping hole of a high-carbon cold work tool
steel. As of now, no literature has been found on processing D2 by LPBF, so this
project could be one of the first steps to commercialize this powder.

1.3
Research questions
- Can the excess D2 powder from the spray forming process be printed?
- Does the microstructure of printed and conventionally processed D2 differ?
- Is the hardness of printed D2 comparable to that of processed conventionally?
- Is it possible to achieve dense and crack-free parts just by adjusting the process
parameters only?
- If not, what else could be done to avoid critical defects?
- Does heat-treatment have a similar effect on printed samples as in conventional
ones?

2
State of the Art
2.1
LPBF of tool steels
At the time of writing, H13 was the most commonly investigated tool steel, followed
by M2. A rather wide selection of literature was already available about H13,
however other tool steels were not yet so profoundly investigated. Thus, this
section presents findings mostly from works on H13.
First, the two review articles on the LPBF of steels, [2] and [6], were processed.
Many an article were surveyed, yet the only tool steels mentioned were H13 and
M2. Fully dense tool parts were printed, however, these were not up to par with
conventional tools in terms of toughness and fatigue properties.
For LPBF processed H13 and M2 steels, Vickers hardness values in the range
of 408 to 900 HV were reported. It was stated, that moderate residual stresses
could contribute to the high hardness, which then improved wear resistance. The
microstructure was found to be of ultrafine cells or dendrites, and made up of
martensite and small, homogeneously dispersed Vanadium and Molybdenum car-
bides, what could explain the high hardness. On the other hand, a significant
amount of retained austenite was present, probably due to the higher cooling rates
(103 – 106 Ks–1), which supressed the martensitic transformation. With the help
of Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD), cell/dendrite boundaries were iden-
tified as preferential locations for retained austenite. This could be the result of
the segregation of austenite stabiliser elements (e.g. C) during solidification. By
the end of solidification, carbide networks developed, which served as paths for
crack propagation. That, with the addition of high residual stresses led to severe
cracking, unless adequately high preheating temperatures were adopted.
Lastly, the similarity between the LPBF and casting-welding processes was dis-
cussed. In both cases, the solidification process is governed by the temperature
gradient (G), the solidification rate (R), and the undercooling (∆T). Especially im-
portant are the ratio and product of G and R, former (G/R) determining the mode
of solidification, while latter (G × R) the microstructure.
The dependence on carbon-content of the HS6-5-3-8 grade high-speed tool steel’s
processability was investigated in [10].
It was shown, that an increasing carbon content led to the formation of alloy-
carbides at the grain boundaries, which resulted in embrittlement and cracking.
Furthermore, in contrast what was expected, higher carbon contents paired with
lower hardness. This was explained by the stabilisation of austenite, and thus the
suppression of martensite.
In the work [8], a laser power - scanning speed process map was developed for
H13, and the resulting microstructures and melt pool geometries were examined.
It was found, that the various process parameter settings produced different levels
of melt pool microstructure inhomogeneity, which influenced the cracking suscep-
tibility.

The article [11] reported the printing of crack-free H13 tools with low tensile resid-
ual stresses of 300 MPa. This was achieved by preheating the build plate to
200 °C. When no preheating was applied, the parts cracked regardless of pro-
cess parameters. The conclusion was, that build plate preheating is a must for
printing tool steels.
The role of residual stresses and elemental segregation in cracking was investi-
gated in the work [12].
Cracks were observed to propagate along carbide-networks, occasionally chang-
ing direction or branching at crossing points of these networks. Examination of
fractographs revealed dendritic superstructures with particularly fine features.
EDS analysis was performed on an interdendritic area. As expected, it was de-
pleted of Iron, but enriched in Carbon, Chromium, Vanadium, Molybdenum, and
Wanadium. Surprisingly though, no Sulfur nor Phosphorus could be detected,
both of which are typical contributors to hot-cracking. Crack surfaces were also
rich in carbides.
A relation between crack locations and stress relieving processes was evident.
Notches, like pores or sample edges, were the main starting points for cracks. It
was concluded, that thermal stress accumulation led to solid-state cracking.
2.1.1
Heat treatment of printed tool steels
The review articles [2, 6] and ASM International’s book on AM were consulted.
As their presented methods and results overlapped to a high extent, those will be
summarized below together.
Heat treatment after printing was found to be effective in lowering residual stresses
and tailoring the microstructure, especially in the case of martensitic steels, like
H13 and M2. The reason being for that the insufficient tempering by intrinsic heat
treatment (IHT), which led to brittleness.
Around 500 °C, the retained austenite started to dissolve, and in the range 600 – 700°C,
the cellular microstructure got dispersed as well. Austenite-free martensite was
achieved by solution treatment and quenching.
Tempering as-printed samples at low temperatures did not result in a significant
drop in hardness, just like in the conventionally processed material. The transi-
tion of the secondary hardening peak to higher temperatures was the result of
high amounts of carbon in solid solution in the matrix along with carbides, which
stabilized the retained austenite. After the full dissolution of the retained austen-
ite, secondary carbides formed.
Another method investigated was Hot Isostatic Pressing (HIP). It increased part
density by eliminating porosity, and slightly increased the average hardness, even
though the microstructure grew coarser as well. Latter had a softening effect,
which was measurable on the wear resistance, which decreased.

Research on D2
Literature on the MAM of D2 is rather limited as of now, and it mostly involves
spray-forming. No reports on LPBF processing of D2 was found. The most rel-
evant works dealt with laser surface modification, rapid solidification of atomized
particles, and, fortunately, Direct Energy Deposition (DED).

The article [13] investigated the microstructure of a spray-formed D2 ingot. It was
made up of very fine and homogeneous globulitic grains surrounded by even finer
ledeburitic carbide networks.
The effect of laser surface melting on the retained austenite content was studied
in the work [14]. It was found, that melting a layer stabilised austenite in the form
of dendrites. When the laser was pulsed, thus increasing the cooling rate, the
retained austenite content rised from the previous 64 % to 90 %.
During wear testing, strain induced martensitic transformation occured in the re-
tained austenite at the examined surface.
Works [15] and [16] characterised the microstructure of atomized and rapidly so-
lidified particles.
Smaller particles, where the cooling rates were higher, showed finer microstruc-
tures, eutectic carbide lamellae, and spacing between dendrite arms. The primary
phase was identified as retained austenite, and the rest as eutectic (Cr, Fe)7C3
carbides. Regardless of the extent of undercooling, 5 −6 % ferrite was also ob-
served after Rietvald refinement. Primary undercooling lower than Tp = 130 K re-
sulted in dendritic microstructure, while above Tp = 160 K, in cellular. Higher un-
dercooling led to more homogenous dispersion of carbides. Intergranular spaces
were also enriched in Manganese, Molybdenum, and Vanadium too.
The lattice parameter of the metastable austenite was measured to be around
1.5 % bigger than normal, which was attributed to the high amounts of Carbon
and Chromium in solid solution.
The two most relevant articles, [17] and [18], dealt with the investigation of D2
processed by DED.
The primary phase was mostly dendritic austenite along with a little bit of marten-
site. Inter-dendritic spaces were filled with lamellar austenite and carbides of the
M7C3 type. Hot cracking was found to occur in these inter-dendritic zones. Crack
propagation took place there too, along the boundaries between austenite and
carbides.
The reason why inter-dendritic regions acted as initiation and propagation sites for
cracks was found to be the lower melting temperature (Tm) of the eutectic phases
present (austenite with higher Carbon content). Upon depositing the next layer,
a reheating event occured in the already solidified one, and phases with low Tm
melted selectively, forming a liquid film this way. Then, due to stresses induced
by solidification and cooling shrinkage, these films might be torn apart (liquation
cracking).
Liquation cracking vulnerability of the part edge regions was observed, along with
a positive relation between the number of reheating events and maximum crack
length.
Hardness measurements on parts fabricated with various energy input showed,
that the hardness slightly decreased as the energy input increased. This was
mainly attributed to the coarsening of dendrites and the reduction of carbon con-
tent through CO gas formation with a higher heat input.

Before any metallurgical investigation can be made, the samples have to be pre-

6
Conclusions
- The results and observations of present project are overall in good agreement
with the findings of the consulted literature. No significant contradictions were
found between the results.
- Printing defect-free D2 was not possible during the course of this project. Pre-
heating the build plate significantly decreased cracking, but it was not a sufficient
measure alone to completely prevent it.
- Using a higher laser power (200 W) and scan speed resulted in less porosity and
cracking than a lower power (100 W) and scan speed of the same energy density.
- Lower energy densities resulted in finer microstructures and generally higher
hardness values, except when the negative effect of the increased porosity be-
came dominant.
- The effect of the pulsing parameters, Duty Cycle and Frequency, on the hardness
and density of parts was found not to be statistically significant. This is probably
due to the low number and unfit design of experiments.
- Repeatability of the prints was quite low primarily due to the issues with the
Baxter system, but also because of the complex nature of the LPBF process.
- The as-printed microstructure consisted of dendritic austenite and mainly (Cr, FE)7C3
carbides in the interdendritic spaces. No change from the dendritic solidification
microstructure was observed in the investigated process window.
- The heat treated microstructures consisted mainly of martensite and homoge-
neously dispersed fine carbides. Martensite probably decomposed into ferrite and
cementite at higher tempering temperatures, as suggested by the hardness val-
ues. The amount of retained austenite was negligible based on the results of the
XRD investigations.
- Hardness values around 700 −800 HV could be easily achieved by heat treat-
ment. The maximum hardness measured was 831 HV after solution treatment at
1090 °C and tempering at 500 °C thanks to to secondary hardening.
- Printed D2 responded to heat treatments in a similar fashion like its conventional
counterpart. A secondary hardening peak was present after solution treatment at
1090 °C, but not at lower temperatures.
- Measurement of the secondary dendrite arm spacing requires higher magnifi-
cation imaging (e.g. SEM), and ideally single track samples. It proved to be too
difficult to do the measurements based on the LOM images due to the extremely
fine and inhomogeneous microstructure.

7
Future Work
Printing crack-free D2 would enable the production of complex shaped tools, with
even higher hardness and wear resistance than conventional D2. Through fur-
ther optimisation of the printing process, thermal stresses could be reduced to a
non-critical level. High pre-heating temperatures, advanced scanning patterns, or
layer-by-layer heat treatment with the laser or by using multiple lasers are some
of the promising options to explore for lowering the thermal gradient in the part.
Another direction might be to change the composition of the material in such a
way, that the range of temperature over which solidification takes place would
decrease. This could result in lower levels of segregation into the inter-dendritic
spaces, and thus lowering cracking susceptibility. The new alloy could bear the
name ”D-TU”.
Once crack-free D2 could be printed, additional mechanical tests should be car-
ried out. Wear and fatigue tests would be of particular interest. Furthermore,
process maps on parameters - defects and parameters - secondary dendrite arm
spacing relations should be attained.
Heat and cryogenic treatments could be optimized to further increase the hard-
ness to over 900 HV , improve wear resistance, and decrease the retained Austen-
ite content. [31]
Hot Isostatic Pressing treatment could be also employed, possibly in place of the
solution treatment, to reduce porosity and increase mechanical properties.
