Abstract
Stainless steel (SS) receiver tubes are standard products currently utilised in solar
thermal plants, because of their corrosion resistivity, and enhanced service life. Carbon
steels on the other hand are desired substitutes, due to low costs, and higher mechanical,
thermal properties. This study is focused on auditing SS 316L, and Nickel electroplated
standard plain carbon steel tubes in terms of thermal stability, and corrosion resistance.
Both tubes were coated with a thin ’¼ülm multilayer of MoTi/TiAlN/TiAlON/SiO2. The
diŌåĄusion of Aluminium into Molybdenum, and distribution of Titanium within the Mo
layer were clear at temperatures over 450ŌŚ”c. FIB-SEM imaging revealed structural orien-
tations at higher temperatures. The AR SiO2 layer seems to disappear after 168 hours
of salt spray testing. In general polarization data con’¼ürmed the superior resistance of
SS316 coated substrates, with corrosion current densities of 0.01┬ĄA/cm2. However, pe-
culiarly, nickel coated carbon steel samples showed enhanced corrosion resistance with
annealing temperature. XRD, and EDS results proved that structure re-orientation and
some chemical changes have occurred, possibly forming NiMoO4 passive ’¼ülms, and less
likely, NiTiO3.

Introduction
The conversion of solar energy into heat is one of the e’¼ācient techniques practised nowadays in
the area of renewable energy. Solar-thermal systems may be installed for electricty production,
heating/cooling purposes and even augmented in desalination plants. The operating temper-
atures may vary as follows, normal hot water production (20ŌŚ”C to 95ŌŚ”C), industrial (95ŌŚ”C to
250ŌŚ”C), and high temperature operating plants (up to 600ŌŚ”C). The main, and most crucial
component found in such systems is the absorber/receiver tube. Therefore, tailoring absorber
tubes for speci’¼üc applications with cost considerations is of uptmost importance for companies.
[1].
The majority of the absorber tubes receive their IR, absorption, and AR multi layer tandem
coating using a PVD process, enabling repeatable results with precise control of coatings, and
production scalability. Almeco-TiNOx, a german company, produces aluminium, and copper
absorber tubes coated with TiAlON, and quartz (AR coating). The thermal stability of such
coatings are maintained at temperatures reaching 400ŌŚ”C, under vacuum conditions [2]. Oxy-rich
TiAlON coatings intrigued many researchers after a patent ’¼üled by Barshilia et al. concluded
that the tubes were stable at temperatures reaching 650ŌŚ”C [3].
Optimizing thin ’¼ülm coatings in order to achieve maximum absorptivity, and minimum emis-
sivity is de’¼ünitely a target for all competitors, however, selecting substrate materials, based
on service life, and cost, with appropriate pre treatment consideration requires a solid under-
standing as well. Stainless steel substrates for absorber tubes have only intrigued researchers
and commercialized companies from a corrosion performance aspect. Carbon steels are much
attractive candidates due to their high mechanical and thermal properties, with relatively lower
costs comparable to stainless steels. One major issue with plain carbon steels is poor corrosion
resistance, since high contents of Nickel, chromium, and molybdenum are not present, as in
stainless steels. Fortunately, well developed coating solutions have emerged over the last years
raising the life expectancy of carbon steels under harsh environments [4].
Coatings based on aluminium, and nickel have been widely investigated. The latter coatings is
believed to increase the service life of carbon steels by a factor of three relative to aluminium
coatings.
T.Xiang et al.
published an article in 2017, discussing on how to protect plain
carbon steels. The study was focused on protecting steels by nickel electroplating followed
by a myristic acid treatment. This 10 ┬Ąmprotective layer was superhydrophobic, which helps
repel moisture, and thus mitigate corrosion. The mixed corrosion current density recorded was
roughly 2.5┬ĄA/cm2 [5]. A 300┬Ąm thick aluminium coating applied by HVOF provided a mixed
corrosion current density of 9.5┬ĄA/cm2 [6]. Finally, a 12 ┬Ąm Aluminium/zinc/chrome doped
with cerium nitrate coating on steel lowered the corrosion current density to 7.94 ┬ĄA/cm2. This
review was published early 2017 by Ke-bing zhang et al [7].
Selecting components for solar thermal systems requires a great understanding of the surround-
ing environment, application parameters, and service life of systems. The focus of this project
will be to analyse the corrosion resistance of diŌåĄerent carbon steel treated surfaces, based on
three diŌåĄerent pre treatments, coated with a constant multilayer PVD coating (MoTi/TiAlN/-
TiAlON/SiO2). The corrosion performance with respect to conventional 316L substrates will
be reported, and further more the tandem coatings thermal stability in air will be audited.
The viability of nickel, and zinc plated carbon steel tubes in terms of corrosion, and thermal
performance will be touched upon.

2.1
Introduction
The sun, an abundant, clean source of energy distributed globally with a high exergy content,
and free of geopolitical tensions. The annual energy consumption of 5.67 x 1020 Joules, back
in 2013, was merely 0.2% of the total amount of solar energy reaching earth [8][9]. Of course,
harvesting solar energy eŌåĄectively is largely dependent on the system design, material usage,
and location of plant installation. Countries located on the so called sun belt (latitudes of
┬▒40ŌŚ”) could receive 1800kWh/m2/year by surfaces that are designed to maintain their per-
pendicularity relative to the main direction of the sun rays through a tracking motion.
In order to minimize unwanted heat losses, it is essential to keep the absorbing surface area as
small as possible. However, a smaller surface area means less energy capturing ability. To tackle
this issue, concentrators are integrated as a part of the receiver/absorber tube. Concentrators
are basically divided into two type namely, Line focus, and Point focus solar concentrators.
The parabolic trough, and the linear fresnel for line focus, and parabolic dish, and heliostat
’¼üeld-central receiver for point focus. Concentrators simply focus the sun rays onto the receiver,
at a factor depending on the ratio of the respective surface areas of the concentrator and the
receiver, which then converts solar energy to thermal energy. The concept of concentrating
solar rays onto a receiver surface is the reason behind the names ŌĆØConcentrating solar thermalŌĆØ
(CST) or ŌĆØConcentrating solar powerŌĆØ (CSP). The core component responsible for converting
solar to thermal energy is the absorber tube. The surfaces of these tubes are tailored as to
increase the absorption to emissivity ratio. Such tubes are often encapsulated in glass tubes,
connected to a vacuum system , to control heat losses through conduction and convection.
The vacuum system, however, only performs well in low to mid- temperature ranges. At high
temperatures, radiation losses are extremely signi’¼ücant, and thus a vacuum plays a minimal
role [10]. In general, designers may improve the e’¼āciency of CSP systems by the following
guidelines:
ŌĆó Increase the concentration factor
ŌĆó Operate at lower temperatures
ŌĆó Install innovative absorber tubes

2.2
Overview of Absorber tubes
Absorber/receiver tubes lie in the core of a CSP plant, and are responsible for converting solar
to thermal energy. The performance of such tubes have propelled in an unprecedented manner.
Research focus is mainly on improving the tubes absorptivity, while reducing emissivity, and
also increasing its service life. The various components that are present in a solar receiver are
depicted in ’¼ügure 2.
Figure 2: Components of a solar receiver. Copied from [10]
Bellow joints, connecting the glass tube and the metal pipe, prevent thermal shocks by com-
pensating for the diŌåĄerent thermal expansions of the respective materials. These joints are
welded to the glass cover, and protected against solar radiation in order to increase durability.
The selective absorber coatings absorb solar radiation, convert it to heat, and then transfer
the energy to a heat thermal ’¼éuid (HTF) ’¼éowing inside the tube. A vacuum system ensures
minimal heat thermal heat losses.
Apart from durability and maximum operating temperatures, the feasibility of solar thermal
systems rely heavily on the receiverŌĆÖs optical and thermal performance.
Moreover, special
attention is necessary in developing high temperature resistant HTFs. The available HTF are
only applicable to temperatures up to 400ŌŚ”C; passed this temperature, the ’¼éuid medium starts
to degrade, and thus halts the system. Molten salts, or direct steam generation could overcome
such problems, and realise temperatures up to 550ŌŚ”C.

An optimum trade oŌåĄbetween durability and costs of absorber tubes is one major challenge
within the solar thermal energy industry. Interestingly, the main degradation problem arises
due to the diŌåĄusion of hydrogen from the oil based HTF into the vacuum chamber. Hence,
substituting for alternative HTF is one way out. Another solution would be to improve to
e’¼āciency of the tubes as to avoid the usage of a vacuum system. Regarding economic aspects,
it is necessary to perform profound auditing of material usage in relation to the operating
plant. An amalgam of factors, such as operating temperature, service life, corrosiveness of
surrounding environment, and thermal e’¼āciency, should be considered when selecting receivers
for CSP plants [10].
2.2.1
Glass tube
The glass tube serves as a barrier against the harsh environment, and also reduces thermal
losses through conduction - convection. The area between the glass cover and absorber tube is
where a vacuum may be established in order to control the thermal losses, and also prolong the
durability of the selective coating (section 2.3). Absolute requirements for these glass covers
are high solar transmittance, low thermal expansion coe’¼ācient, and good durability.
Borosilicate glass is a favourable material used in glass covers. Basic chemicals, Silica, and boric
oxide (>8%) form borosilicate glass. The expansion coe’¼ācient is adjusted by incorporating
alkaline earth compounds and alumina [11]. In general, higher borate containing glasses with
the aforementioned additives maintain low softening points, as well as low thermal expansion.
Furthermore, most borosilicate glasses posses high solar transmittance, in the range of 0.91 to
0.93.
Anti-re’¼éection coatings (ARC) are responsible for the relatively high transmittance of such
borosilicate glasses. ARC are applied both on the inner and outer surfaces of the glass cover to
ensure optimal optical e’¼āciency. Induced porosity Silicon Dioxide (SiO2) is a popular ARC,
since its porous nature lowers the refractive index. Unfortunately, its porous nature comes with
drawbacks. One, pores tend to absorb water, and other volatile compounds ,which in return
disturb optical properties. Two, a decrease in mechanical properties at the coating/substrate
is experienced, since pores act as weak points within the coating interface [12].
The aforementioned issues entails high quality deposition methods.
A major process prac-
tised industrially is sol-gel dip coating due to its low cost, ease of application, and scalability.
Colloidal solutions or polymeric solutions are methods used to introduce porosity within the
SiO2 coating. However, each method either lacks mechanical adhesion, or absorption of un-
desired compounds. Developing coatings or post treating an already deposited layer, which is
hydrophobic in nature, could resolve soiling problems, in addition to lowering cleaning costs
[13, 14, 15, 16].
2.2.2
Vacuum Technology
Maintaining a low pressure atmosphere, around 10ŌłÆ3 mbar, is an eŌåĄective way to improve
solar-thermal processes, and in the same time increase the durability of the absorber coatings
by preventing oxidation. A glass-metal welding of the metallic ring and glass cover, connected
to a bellow ensures a vacuum atmosphere, and simultaneously compensates for any diŌåĄerential
thermal expansions.

Unfortunately, vacuum maintenance is extremely costly, and critical. For absorbers designed
to perform under vacuum conditions, a failure in such system will degrade the coatings, con-
sequently halting the system before replacing the absorber tube. Moreover, a great loss of
thermal e’¼āciency (x6) will occur during its performance. Therefore, one could imagine the
degree of importance in having a well controlled high vacuum environment at all times.
Operating solar-thermal plants have faced two common problems. One being a failure at the
glass-metal welding area, and another being due to hydrogen diŌåĄusion form HTF to vacuum.
The latter problem may be mitigated by substituting oil based systems with molten salts,
adding a hydrogen diŌåĄusion barrier on steel pipe, or installing ’¼ülters [17, 18].
2.2.3
Bellows
Air tight connections, and controlled diŌåĄerential thermal expansions between the glass cover and
metal pipe could be realised by using bellows. Thus, selecting appropriate materials that have
been processed with care is absolutely crucial. These bellows are kept under good conditions
on the vacuum side, however, exposed to the atmosphere on the outer part. Depending on the
location of installation, more corrosive surroundings may be encountered. Inter-granular stress
corrosion cracking (ISCC) of bellows is one major form of failure reported. Generally, it is agreed
upon that for ISCC to occur a material should be susceptible against the speci’¼üc aggressive
environment, and loaded under stress. These stresses may arise from uncontrolled welding, or

Discussion
Based on the results it is obvious that the zinc/iron electroplated tubes failed beyond control.
The tandem coatings were already peeling oŌåĄusing adhesive tape, at room temperature. Expos-
ing the samples to temperatures of 250ŌŚ”c further weakened the coating/zinc interface through
thermal stresses, and thus layers were removed by very minimal force. Plating on zinc was
never an easy task. The formation of kinetically favourable passive Zinc oxides, and hydroxides
exacerbate the adhesive nature of the potential coating, since it is di’¼ācult to form any chemical
bonds. In this case, the incorporation of iron may have formed other inert oxide compounds
that inhibit adhesion. Hence, when dealing with metals that easily form oxides, pre treatment
is of utmost importance. Due to the aforementioned premature failures, and after design of
experiments, zinc/iron plated samples were eliminated, and further investigations were carried
on the remaining samples.
The ’¼ürst point of discussion is pertinent to the removal of the AR SiO2 layer. Both GDOES,
and EDS data indicate losses of Si, and oxygen. Si on its own is exceptionally reactive as
opposed to SiO2, and therefore any ’¼éaws in coating deposition may promote layer dissolution.
XRD measurements were unable to detect SiO2, which lead to concluding that the layer is
amorphous/nano-crystalline, and thus more reactive. Generally, silicon is inert against water
or water vapours, because of the rapid formation of an oxide layer. Silicon dioxide has a water
solubility of 0.12 g/L. However, there are few examples of silicon compounds reacting with
water. Silicon tetra chloride reacts with water quite violently. Silicides of groups 1 and 2 are
generally reactive forming silanes and hydrogen, based on the below reaction.
Na2Si + 3H2O ŌłÆŌłÆ> Na2SiO3 + 3H2
Silicon compounds vary in solubility. Silicon oxide is considered water insoluble compared to
other minerals. However, reactions may be catalysed in the presence of high Na, Cl contents,
or due to inappropriate deposition techniques. The EDS data also reveal spots concentrated
in Si. These spots may be favourably attacked, and gradually spread throughout the coating.
The following reaction may initiate[88]
SiO2(s) + 2H2O(l) < ŌłÆ> H4SiO4(s)
and further hydrolysed into

The eŌåĄect of temperature on the performance of the coating is imperative. Although no optical
tests were carried out, results obtained from GDOES, and FIB-SEM do indicate changes oc-
curring both chemically, and structurally. As temperature is raised aluminium is diŌåĄused into
the molybdenum layer, and titanium is more evenly distributed within the IR layer. These will
inevitably alter optical properties, whether to a better or worse state, that is not clear yet. At
high temperature, it also seems that Aluminium and/or titanium are pulling oxygen oŌåĄsilicon
causing this well distributed oxygen curve under the GDOES test. Unfortunately, FIB-SEM

During this project great focus was put into comparing conventional SS316 L tubes against
nickel plated carbon steel tubes, especially from a corrosion perspective. Before, delving into a
complete corrosion explanation, the eŌåĄect of substrate topography, and presence of impurities
due to the pvd process will be touched upon. PVD coatings are known to replicate surfaces
very precisely. Inadequate surface treatment will simply worsen the pvd coating quality. LOM
images captured of the SS substrate revealed areas containing ’¼éake like structures, which were
also seen in the coated samples. This replication of a ’¼éake like structure was not observed in
nickel coated samples, for a clear reason that nickel electroplated tubes were extremely smooth
resulting in mirror like ’¼ünish on the ’¼ünal pvd coated samples. Another intriguing aspect was
pertinent to high number of white spots visible in the SEM images. The size, and amount of
white spots present in the coating may be traced back to the PVD target. PVD targets are
produced with alternative methods, of which sintering, and melting are most common. Sintered
targets tend to form greater number of white spots and pores in comparison to melted targets.
These spots lower surface hardness, increase roughness, and lower adhesion, all which down
grade the performance of the coating [89].
To avoid complexity, discussion on the corrosion performance for each sample will be made
separately. In the as deposited form, sample REF constantly records an OCP value of -90 mV,
icorr of 0.014┬ĄA/cm2, however the Ecorr value alters signi’¼ücantly depending on the scan rate,

dramatically before settling, and due to the absence of such behaviour in nickel plated samples,
despite having received the exact same tandem coating. One claim is that driving the potential
very low reduces the passive ’¼ülms, which in return lowers the Ecorr.
At lower scan rates,
repassivation is possible, and Ecorr values reach closer to the OCP potential. Upon stressing
the coating, in the range of 0 - 100 mV, an area of passivation followed by trans passivation

the Mo layer, since the potential range falls well into the MoO2 area indicated in the pourbaix

After auditing the results gained from polarization measurements, it is brave to conclude that

100% red rust formation was visible. The author linked such rust formation to the presence of
pin holes within the coating as shown in the captured micro graph [90]. Apart from the presence
of pin holes the thin ’¼ülm coatings seem to lower corrosion rate by hindering the cathodic half
reactions, since the Ecorr values are shifted to lower values, and corrosion current density is

Conclusion and outlook
Initially, all zinc plated carbon tubes were eliminated after design of experiments.
It was
con’¼ürmed that the tubes were suŌåĄering from adhesion problems, since the received PVD layers
were easily pulled oŌåĄusing adhesive tape before annealing. After annealing, coatings were
simply ’¼éaking oŌåĄas place, in the absence of any force. Focus was put onto SS316, and nickel
plated tubes.
Fortunately, performance of SS 316L, and carbon steel coated tubes in terms of corrosion and
thermal stability was reported. Given that the two tubes experience alternative chemical degra-
dation, corrosion studies were separated to avoid complexity. One shared problem applicable
to both tubes was the removal of SiO2 AR layer after 168 hours of salt spray testing B117.
This problem was associated to the coating itself without any eŌåĄects from the substrate. Tubes
that were coated before December 2016 did not behave as such, since the AR layer was in-
tact. GDOES analysis on the coatings proved that diŌåĄusion does occur to some extent. This
will inevitably alter optical properties. Moving onto the polarization tests, SS316 showed to
maintain its performance before and after annealing, unlike carbon steel nickel plated tubes.
SS 316 tubes recorded corrosion current densities of around 0.01┬ĄA/cm2 As deposited, and
a maximum of 0.04┬ĄA/cm2 was achieved after annealing to 450ŌŚ”c. On the other hand nickel
plated samples recorded astonishing results. It was found that corrosion current densities de-
crease with annealing temperature, recording a minimum of 0.0073┬ĄA/cm2 after annealing to
450ŌŚ”c. Moreover, calculated coating resistance varied signi’¼ücantly depending on tested area.
It is believed that the formation of passive ’¼ülms, especially NiMo4, hinders chemical attack by
forming a barrier layer. As of this studies conclusions, carbon steel tubes are not yet quali’¼üed,
but require further investigation. The formation of passive layers may improve or degrade opti-
cal properties. Hence, absorptivity and emissivity should be measured after annealing samples
in order to understand the consequence on solar to thermal e’¼āciency.
This review was a good starting point for further investigations on nickel plated carbon steel
tubes. Based on pourbaix, and ellingham diagrams, it is clear than Ni is chemically active with
Fe, Ti, and Mo, and thus incorporating Ni in between an iron containing substrate, and a MoTi
IR layer may initiate and form interesting compounds. It seems like the formed compounds
during this study were quite passive, reducing corrosion rate signi’¼ücantly. However optical
properties are also imperative. Improving corrosion resistance at the expense of loosing optical
performance is a no way deal, since absorber tubes are primarily optimised in terms of optical
properties. Hence, optical tests should be carried out. Furthermore, XPS analysis, and TEM
imaging will aid in understanding the formation of nano ranged thick passive ’¼ülms. Preparing
’¼éat samples of tandem coated nickel plated carbon steels will enable GDOES analysis, which
could help in understanding the chemical content changes due to passive ’¼ülm formation.
