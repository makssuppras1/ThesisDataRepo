Abstract
In the evolving landscape of energy storage, halide superionic conductors are becom┬¡
ing a cornerstone for all┬¡solid┬¡state battery (ASSB) technology, owing to their exceptional
lithium ion conductivity, paired with notable chemical and electrochemical stability. This
project encapsulates the latest progress in halide┬¡based solid electrolytes (SEs), partic┬¡
ularly focusing on Lithium Yttrium Chloride (Li3YCl6) a chloride electrolyte recognized for
its stability under high voltages. This project delves into the effect of reducing the content
of Lithium chloride (LiCl) within Li3YCl6 and its influence on the crystal structure and ionic
conductivity of the solid┬¡state electrolyte (SSE).
Using the metal oxide (Y2O3) route of synthesis, developed at DTU, Li3YCl6 was suc┬¡
cessfully synthesized, followed by a detailed examination through X┬¡ray diffraction (XRD)
and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). The results indicate that lowering
the content of LiCl demonstrates a significant improvement in the ionic conductivity of the
electrolyte. These findings suggest that Li3YCl6 could be a game┬¡changer for the future
of battery technology, offering a conductivity of 0.14 mS/cm at room temperature. This
could lead to potential commercial implications of Li3YCl6 if further advancements in the
research to optimize Li3YCl6 occur.

Introduction
The expanding use of electric cars has been a transformative trend in the automotive in┬¡
dustry, driven by a growing emphasis on sustainable and eco┬¡friendly transportation. One
key factor accelerating this shift is the continuous improvement in battery technology. Tra┬¡
ditional Lithium┬¡ion batteries have been the cornerstone of electric vehicles (EVs), but the
development of solid┬¡state batteries is poised to revolutionize the electric car landscape.
Solid┬¡state electrolytes (SSEs) are another major area of innovation today, with rising
patenting activity revealing an emerging trend in the hunt for the next generation of lithium┬¡
ion batteries. Electrolytes form the focus of inventive activity, with efforts underway to find
alternatives to the liquid or polymer gel electrolytes used in current lithium┬¡ion batteries,
which pose a flammability risk. Solid┬¡state electrolytes feature a high level of specific en┬¡
ergy and a high degree of stability but are currently expensive. Patenting activity in this
area has been growing very fast since 2010. Fig 1.1 shows the trajectory of this trend, il┬¡
lustrating that from 2018 onwards, the trajectory of patent families for all countries shows
a significant and sharp increase, indicating a robust global acceleration in the filing of
patents related to solid┬¡state lithium┬¡ion batteries. Innovators are targeting some of the
shortcomings found in existing devices, including low lithium┬¡ion conductivity, high con┬¡
tact resistance at the electrolyte/electrode interface, and reliance on high┬¡cost and scarce
materials. Commercial applications of solid┬¡state electrolytes in electric vehicles are an┬¡
ticipated in the next decade and could generate spillovers that would help to make these
batteries competitive for other applications, too.[1] .

1.1
Background
Lithium Yttrium Chloride (LYC) has emerged as a promising candidate for the electrolyte
in solid┬¡state batteries due to its unique properties. This compound is part of a class
of materials known as lithium halides, and its crystalline structure plays a crucial role in
enhancing the performance of solid┬¡state batteries.[3]
In the crystal formation of Li3YCl6, lithium ions are distributed within a chloride lattice, and
yttrium ions are well positioned to enhance ionic conductivity. The ordered arrangement
of the ions in the crystal lattice promotes the efficient movement of lithium ions, reducing
resistance within the electrolyte. This improved ionic conductivity is a critical factor for
solid┬¡state batteries, as it contributes to faster charging and discharging cycles.
By incorporating the solid electrolyte LYC into the cathode, forming a composite cathode,
the ionic conductivity within the cathode can be improved. This is crucial for solid┬¡state
batteries, where ionic transport through the solid electrolyte is often a limiting factor.
A composite cathode can enhance the interfacial contact between the cathode active
material and the solid electrolyte, promoting better ionic conduction.[4, 5]
Recent studies are delving deeper into the optimization of LYC and similar materials,
the potential for commercializing solid┬¡state batteries with higher energy density, faster┬¡
charging rates, and improved safety features becomes increasingly evident. This progress
marks a significant step forward in the evolution of battery technology towards more effi┬¡
cient and sustainable energy storage solutions.[6, 7, 8]

Aim of the project
The focus at the beginning of the project is on synthesizing LYC and optimizing the syn┬¡
thesis process. The objective is to create a high┬¡performance composite cathode using
LYC as the ion┬¡conducting element. It is planned to incorporate LNMO (LiNi1.5Mn0.5O4), a
high┬¡voltage cathode material developed by Haldor Topsoe. The LYC compounds will be

2.1
The Lithium Ion Battery
The structure of a lithium┬¡ion battery typically comprises three main components: the
anode (often made of graphite), the cathode (which can be made from various lithium
metal oxides), and the electrolyte (a lithium salt in an organic solvent). During discharge,
lithium ions move from the anode to the cathode through the electrolyte, generating an
electric current through the external circuit. The reverse process occurs during charging,
where lithium ions move from the cathode back to the anode.
Figure 2.1: Schematics of the working principle of a lithium┬¡ion battery.[9]
Batteries convert chemical energy into electrical energy through redox reactions. The
anode undergoes oxidation, losing electrons that flow through the external circuit to the
cathode, where reduction takes place, and electrons are gained.

The electrochemical stability window of an electrolyte, like Lithium Yttrium Chloride (Li3YCl6),

battery operation. This window determines the upper and lower voltage limits that the
electrolyte can withstand against the electrodes. If the cell voltage goes beyond this win┬¡

stability window is preferred for a higher voltage operation and, therefore, a higher energy
density battery. Li3YCl6, being a solid electrolyte, generally offers a wider stability window
compared to liquid electrolytes, which contributes to the overall safety and performance

The anode
The interface between the solid electrolyte (SE) and the Li metal anode is a critical aspect
of all┬¡solid┬¡state lithium batteries (ASSLBs) due to potential reactivity issues. Typically,
halide┬¡based solid electrolytes, while having good ionic conductivity and electrochemical
stability, can be unstable when in direct contact with lithium metal. The interface can
degrade, forming a resistive layer that increases impedance, reducing efficiency, and
possibly leading to short circuits.
In a study in 2022 by Kim et al., the LiÔêÆIn alloy anode was used in combination with a
single┬¡crystal LiNi0.8Co0.1Mn0.1O2 (NMC811) cathode and LYC as the electrolyte, deliver┬¡
ing a high discharge capacity of 170 mAh/g at C/5 and capacity retention of nearly 90%
after 1000 cycles.
LiÔêÆIn alloy anode showed advantages over pure Li metal. The LiÔêÆIn alloy anode could
prevent the reduction of the halide solid electrolyte (HSE) and enable intimate contact
between the HSE and the anode, which improved the Li+ ion diffusivity and the cycling
stability of the ASSBs.[5]
The electrolyte
Halide solid┬¡state electrolytes (SSEs), particularly chloride┬¡based ones, offer significant
advantages over their oxide and sulfide counterparts for all┬¡solid┬¡state lithium batteries
(ASSLBs). The main benefits stem from their superior ionic conductivity, moisture stability,
and electrochemical compatibility with cathode materials. Sulfide SSEs, while exhibiting
higher ionic conductivities than oxides, are less stable in the presence of moisture and
can react with water to produce toxic hydrogen sulfide gas (H2S). This is illustrated in
Fig. 2.3. Additionally, sulfides and oxides require an intermediate layer when paired with

oxide cathodes to ensure optimal electrochemical performance, complicating the battery
assembly process and potentially impacting the performance negatively.

In contrast, halide SSEs, particularly chlorides, offer a combination of high ionic conductiv┬¡
ity, good mechanical deformability, and excellent electrochemical stability, making them
increasingly attractive for ASSLB applications. Their chemical composition, often repre┬¡
sented as Li┬¡M┬¡X (where M is a metallic element with variable valence states and X is a
halogen element), allows for a wide electrochemical stability window and excellent com┬¡
patibility with high┬¡voltage cathodes. Furthermore, the structural properties of chloride
ions, such as their larger ionic radii and high polarizability, contribute to high ion mobility
and mechanical deformability. These characteristics facilitate the assembly of ASSLBs
by enhancing the interfacial contact between the electrolyte and electrode materials, thus
improving the overall battery performance and safety.[7]
Following the analysis presented by Changhong Wang et al. [11], halide SSEs are distin┬¡
guished by their exceptional voltage stability and broad electrochemical stability windows,
which are pivotal for high┬¡voltage battery applications. This technical merit, coupled with
strategies aimed at cost reduction through innovative synthesis and material optimization,
positions halide electrolytes as a forefront candidate in solid┬¡state battery research. Their
adaptation promises to address the longstanding challenges of safety and energy density
in lithium┬¡ion batteries, heralding a new era of energy storage solutions.
The article further elucidates the cost┬¡benefit analysis of employing halide SSEs, under┬¡
scoring the economic feasibility of these materials despite the higher initial costs associ┬¡
ated with certain halide ions. By leveraging aliovalent substitution and other cost┬¡effective
manufacturing processes, halide SSEs can be produced at a competitive price point, en┬¡
suring their accessibility for widespread application. This balance between performance
enhancement and cost efficiency underlines the transformative potential of halide SSEs
in pushing the boundaries of current battery technology toward more sustainable and re┬¡
liable energy storage systems.

The Composite Cathode
In the development of solid┬¡state batteries, composite cathodes represent a significant
advancement, offering a pathway to enhance performance and safety. The creation of
composite cathodes involves materials at the micro and nano┬¡scale to optimize the inter┬¡
face between the cathode and solid electrolyte, called the cathode active material (CAM).
Incorporating the solid electrolyte into the cathode aims to improve ionic conductivity and
reduce interfacial resistance, which is crucial for increasing energy density and battery ef┬¡
ficiency. Adding conductive additives to the composite cathode is employed to achieve a
homogenous distribution of constituents, ensuring uniform ion flow and electrical contact
throughout the cathode structure.
Lithium┬¡nickel┬¡manganese┬¡oxide (LiNi1.5Mn0.5O4) is a cathode active material (CAM) man┬¡
ufactured by Haldor Topsoe and used in lithium┬¡ion batteries. LNMO cathodes are com┬¡
posed of manganese dioxide (MnO2), which is readily available and cost┬¡effective. LNMO
is cobalt┬¡free and uses less Li and Ni and doesnÔÇÖt rely on scarce or expensive elements.
Manganese┬¡based cathodes are non┬¡toxic and pose minimal environmental risks. LNMO
is less susceptible to water┬¡related degradation, making it suitable for various applications.
The three┬¡dimensional structure of LNMO, particularly in compounds like LiMn2O4, allows

The Blocking Electrode
A blocking electrode is characterized by its inability to facilitate Faradaic, or charge┬¡
transfer, reactions, thereby functioning as an ideal capacitor. Utilizing such an electrode
is advantageous for EIS as it streamlines the experimental parameters═¥ the observed be┬¡
havior can be reliably ascribed to the capacitive response associated with the electrode┬¡
electrolyte interfacial double layer, devoid of the complexities introduced by Faradaic pro┬¡
cesses. Stainless steel is frequently employed due to its substantial inertness, while alu┬¡
minum is also used for its inherent passivity, attributed to the formation of a protective
oxide surface layer.
When graphite is used in EIS, it is often to study non┬¡faradaic processes, such as ion
transport within the electrolyte or the double┬¡layer capacitance at the electrode┬¡electrolyte
interface. Since no redox reactions are occurring at the electrode, the impedance spec┬¡
trum reflects only the resistive and capacitive behavior of the system. Graphite can be
easily fabricated into various shapes and sizes, allowing for customizable electrode de┬¡
sign based on the requirements of the EIS study. This gives better contact between the
electrolyte and the blocking stainless steel electrodes usually used for EIS.

The Crystal Structure of the Electrolyte
Chemical compounds can crystallize in many different structures, classified broadly into
seven crystal systems: cubic, tetragonal, orthorhombic, hexagonal, trigonal, monoclinic,
and triclinic. Each system has a unique arrangement of atoms and can further be divided
into 14 Bravais lattices that describe the geometric arrangement of the lattice points. The
specific crystal structure adopted by a compound depends on factors like the types of
atoms involved, bonding characteristics, and environmental conditions during the crystal┬¡
lization process.
Li3YCl6 is well known for primarily existing in the trigonal crystal structure. In its trigonal
form, the crystal lattice arrangement displays a threefold symmetry axis, called a P┬¡3m1
symmetry.
A paper by Schlem et al.(2019)[3], discusses the structural transformations of Li3YCl6
and Li3YbCl6 under different conditions. It was documented that when Li3YCl6 annealed
at 310┬░C, Li3YCl6 transformed from its original P┬¡3m1 symmetry to an orthorhombic struc┬¡
ture with Pnma symmetry. This change in structure was attributed to the movement of
Yttrium ions within the lattice. Li3YbCl6 similar to Li3YCl6, annealing Li3YbCl6 at a higher
temperature (500┬░C) instead of the usual 400┬░C also led to a change in symmetry. In this
case, the P┬¡3m1 symmetry was replaced by the Pnma structure. This suggests that the
rare┬¡earth element (Yttrium or Ytterbium in this case) plays a crucial role in the structural
stability and transformation behavior. The paper also stated that decreasing the Li and Cl
contents in Li3YCl6 (Li3ÔêÆxYCl6ÔêÆx), led to the orthorhombic crystal structure. Lowering the
content of Li and Cl by 12% (Li2.65YCl5.65) was the maximum deficiency experimented on
by this paper since higher deficiencies showed impurities in the electrolyte. The results

X┬¡ray Diffraction (XRD)
X┬¡ray diffraction (XRD) is a powerful technique used in materials science and crystallogra┬¡
phy to determine the atomic and molecular structure of a crystal. When a beam of X┬¡rays
strikes a crystal, it diffracts into many specific directions. By measuring the angles and
intensities of these diffracted beams, a crystallographer can produce a three┬¡dimensional
picture of the density of electrons within the crystal. This is essential for understanding
the materialÔÇÖs properties and behavior.
Central to XRD is BraggÔÇÖs Law, named after William Lawrence Bragg and his father,
William Henry Bragg, who first proposed it in 1913. This law provides a simple equa┬¡
tion:
n╬╗ = 2d sin ╬©
(3.1)
where n is an integer, ╬╗ is the wavelength of the incident X┬¡ray beam, d is the distance
between the crystal planes, and ╬© is the angle of incidence at which the X┬¡ray beam is
diffracted. BraggÔÇÖs Law is instrumental in explaining why diffraction occurs only at spe┬¡
cific angles. When the path difference between the incident and diffracted X┬¡rays is an
integer multiple of the X┬¡ray wavelength, constructive interference occurs, leading to a de┬¡

for deducing the crystal structure.
XRD, combined with BraggÔÇÖs Law, thus provides a vital tool in the study of crystalline mate┬¡

is pivotal in fields ranging from solid┬¡state physics and chemistry to materials science and

Experimental Methods
All preparation work for the synthesis and characterization of Li3YCl6 were conducted in
an MBRAUN glovebox filled with Argon, both H2O and O2 levels were kept under 0.1 ppm
at room temperature.
4.1
Synthesis of Li3YCl6
For the synthesis of Li3YCl6 in one step via the metal oxide route, 1.5 x the stoichiometric
amount of NH4Cl (ACS, 99.5% min, Alfa Aesar) was manually ground in a mortar with the
stoichiometric amount of Y2O3 (99,9% (REO) powder, Alfa Aesar). After 5 to 10 minutes
of grounding, the stoichiometric (or the desired) amount of LiCl (< 99.9%, Sigma Aldrich)
was added to the powder mixture and then grounded again for 5 minutes. The powder
mixture was then pressed into 12mm pellets at 200 MPa.
The pellets were then placed in a ceramic crucible ready to be brought to the furnace. To
prevent any contamination or contact with air humidity and oxygen, the pellets were trans┬¡
ported to a tube furnace in a desiccator. The pellets were placed inside a quartz glass
tube mounted to the tube furnace. The pellets were then heat treated in the presence of
an argon flow at 235 ┬░C at a ramp rate of 120 ┬░C/hour for 15 hours. The pellets were then
annealed at 500 ┬░C at a ramp rate of 120 ┬░C/hour for 10 hours. The tube furnace then
cools down to 25 ┬░C at a ramp rate of 70 ┬░C/hour. After that, the pellets were removed
and transported immediately in the desiccator to the glovebox. During the process of op┬¡
timizing the synthesis method, different annealing temperatures and annealing durations
were tested. Further details on the synthesis of Li3YCl6 will be discussed in the results
and discussion section.
For the 2┬¡step synthesis, the same amount of NH4Cl and Y2O3 were grounded and pressed.
The pellets were then heat treated at 235 ┬░C at a ramp rate of 120 ┬░C/hour for 20 hours.
After that, the pellets were cooled down to room temperature and brought to the glove
box. The desired amount of LiCl was grounded with the heat┬¡treated pellets and then
pressed to pellets again. The pellets were then heat treated at 500 ┬░C at a ramp rate of
120 ┬░C/hour for 5 hours.

6
Conclusion
The successful synthesis of LYC in both its trigonal and orthorhombic crystal structure
proves that the one┬¡step metal oxide (Y2O3) route of synthesis is a simple and innovative
approach for synthesizing halide electrolytes with a decent ionic conductivity. Heat treat┬¡
ment at 500┬░C seems to be the appropriate annealing temperature for the metal oxide
route of synthesis. The data indicates that the pure orthorhombic at 20% LiCl deficiency
presents the optimal condition for ionic conductivity and the lowest activation energy.
6.1
Further Development
There is still room for improvement in the synthesis method since the effect of the argon
flow during the furnace cycle is not fully understood. Deeper crucibles could be a solution
to minimize the effect of the argon flow on the pellets inside the furnace. There was
a difficulty synthesizing LYC at a higher deficiency than 30%, an extra amount of excess
NH4Cl could be a solution. The duration of annealing time at 500┬░C for higher deficiencies
could be cut down since there is less LiCl to decompose. Optimizing the assembly of the
full┬¡cell has to be done to explore the full capacity of LYC. Pressing the composite cathode
with less weight could help the cathode┬¡electrolyte interface. The composite cathode was
pressed under 2 tons which could have made the surface too hard for a good contact with
the electrolyte.
