Abstract
In recent years, the world has become more aware of the severe impacts of greenhouse
gas emissions. In this regard, many countries have pledged to achieve carbon neutrality
in the near future. This has led to a rising significance of renewable energy sources, such
as solar and wind, over conventional ones. In the context of remote offshore wind power
plants, converters play a crucial role in enabling decoupled, variable speed operation
of wind turbines and HVDC (high-voltage direct-current) transmission of the generated
electricity over long distances. Two-level converters are one of the common types of
converters and find their application as back-to-back converters in the type-4 wind turbines
in large scale offshore wind power plants.
The thesis specifically focuses on three control modes for two-level converters: PQ, VdcQ,
and grid forming. The aim is to implement and validate the performance of the cascaded
feedback control loops in real-time operation for the three modes. In addition, the study
considers three levels of access / interfaces to the converter’s own internal controls: A, B,
and C. To accomplish this, the controls are implemented in an OPAL-RT real-time simula-
tion platform. The implemented controls are first verified by means of offline simulations
using models to represent the converters. Subsequently, the controls are implemented
on a hardware setup at the AC/DC WindPowerLab comprising the converters and the
OPAL-RT emulating some of their controls. The process involves implementing, building,
loading, and executing the controls on the OPAL-RT platform. An analogue input/out-
put interface is utilized for exchanging signals between the two-level converter and the
OPAL-RT. This enables the transmission of references/commands to the converters and
retrieval of measurements from the converters through the OPAL-RT. The relevant signals
are then recorded for further analysis and study.
The results obtained are found to be in line with the anticipated performance, indicating
that the two-level converters operate as intended in the specified control modes and of
access / interfaces to the converter’s own internal controls.

Introduction
The increasing global concern over climate change and the detrimental effects of green-
house gas emissions have prompted nations to transition towards cleaner and sustainable
energy sources. Renewable power plants, such as solar and wind farms, have gained im-
mense significance in this context. These plants harness the abundant and freely avail-
able energy from natural resources, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and mitigating the
environmental impact associated with traditional power generation. Offshore wind power,
in particular, has gained prominence in Denmark owing to its extensive coastline and
favorable wind conditions.
Figure 1.1: Offshore wind farms in Denmark and the overall capacity [WF_capacity]
The country has strategically utilized its offshore resources to establish large-scale wind
farms, capitalizing on the faster and more consistent wind speeds experienced offshore.
These offshore wind farms have the potential to generate a significantly larger energy
output while utilizing fewer and larger turbines. Denmark’s commitment to renewable
energy has propelled its offshore wind sector, currently having an installed capacity of 1.7
GW, as shown in Figure 1.1 with an additional 7.6 GW planned by 2030 [WF_capacity].
One of the key challenges in connecting offshore wind farms to the grid is the transmis-
sion and distribution losses. To address this issue and overcome the limitation of the
long-distance AC system transmission, choice of transmission system is prominent. Tra-
ditionally, High Voltage Alternate Current (HVAC) systems have been used, however, the
limitations associated with HVAC have prompted the adoption of High Voltage Direct Cur-
rent (HVDC) systems. HVDC systems offer several advantages in the context of offshore
wind power. Firstly, they eliminate the need for reactive power compensation, as DC
systems inherently lack reactive power. Moreover, it does not require frequency infor-
mation of system. This addresses the challenges posed by the capacitive behavior of
long-distance submarine cables, which becomes significant in HVAC systems. Addition-
ally, HVDC systems enable the efficient transmission of power over long distances with
high efficiency, making them well-suited for connecting offshore wind farms located far
from the shore. Figure 1.2 shows Wind farms connected to an AC grid utilizing HVDC
system.
HVDC (High-Voltage Direct Current) systems can be configured in several ways based
on the requirements of the transmission project. The main HVDC configurations include:

Monopolar HVDC:
In a monopolar HVDC configuration, a single conductor is used for the transmission of
direct current. The DC line typically consists of a positive pole (anode) and a ground
return path (cathode). The ground return can be achieved through either earth return or
metallic return.
Bipolar HVDC:
In a bipolar HVDC configuration, two conductors are used for the transmission of direct
current. Each pole consists of a positive conductor (anode) and a negative conductor
(cathode). Bipolar HVDC allows for the transmission of higher power levels by combining
the capabilities of two monopolar HVDC systems.
Homopolar HVDC:
In a homopolar HVDC configuration, all conductors of the DC line are connected in paral-
lel, forming a single-pole transmission. This configuration is typically used for low-power
HVDC systems or specific applications where simplicity is prioritized over power trans-
mission capacity.
Multi-terminal HVDC:
Multi-terminal HVDC systems involve the connection of three or more terminals or con-
verters to a common DC line. This configuration enables the exchange of power between
multiple power systems or points of generation and consumption. Multi-terminal HVDC
allows for enhanced grid flexibility and integration of renewable energy sources[2].
The power electronics converters play a crucial role in HVDC systems by converting the
power from AC to DC and vice versa. Two types of converters commonly used in HVDC
systems are Line Commutated Converters (LCC) and Voltage Source Converters (VSC).
LCC converters are based on thyristor technology and have been extensively used in
the past. However, VSC converters, which utilize semiconductor devices such as Insu-
lated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs), have gained prominence due to their numerous
advantages in terms of control flexibility, modularity, and enhanced performance.
VSC technology offers several advantages in HVDC systems, making it an attractive

choice for connecting offshore wind farms. Firstly, VSC converters enable bi-directional
power flow. They provide precise and fast control over active and reactive power, fa-
cilitating power regulation and grid synchronization. Additionally, VSC converters offer
robustness against fault ride-through capability, allowing the system to withstand and re-
cover from grid disturbances.
In HVDC systems, due to the inherent advantage of VSC described above, the two-level
converter topology is extensively utilized in offshore wind systems.The two-level topology
consists of a series of semiconductor switches connected in the configuration as shown
in Figure 1.3 and are capable of handling high-power levels and providing precise control
over active and reactive power flow. The modular design of Two-Level VSC converters
allows for efficient maintenance and replacement, reducing system downtime and en-
hancing reliability.
To regulate power flow and maintain grid stability, the two-Level VSC converters can op-
erate in different control modes. The PQ (Active and Reactive Power) mode enables the
converter to regulate both active and reactive power flow based on setpoints or grid re-
quirements. The VDC/Q (Voltage-Droop Control for Reactive Power) mode allows the
converter to control the reactive power output by adjusting the DC-link voltage using a
PI (Proportional-Integral) controller. In Grid Forming mode, the converter autonomously
generates the grid frequency and voltage.

Control System Components
This chapter describes the main components of the control system such as PI and PR
controllers, low pass and notch filters, and PLL (Phase locked loop), while also giving an
overview of the reference frames and transformations from one frame to another.
2.1
Reference Frames
Reference frames are used to analyze the behavior of signals and quantities in differ-
ent coordinated systems. Two commonly used reference frames are stationary reference
frame and rotating reference frame.
2.1.1
Stationary Reference Frame
The stationary reference frame is a fixed reference frame that is typically aligned with the
AC grid or the three-phase system. The commonly used stationary reference frame in
power systems analysis is the abc frame, which represents the three-phase quantities in
a three-phase electrical system and the αβ reference frame. In the αβ reference frame,
the α-axis is aligned with the instantaneous value of one phase (usually the A phase), and
the β-axis is orthogonal to the α-axis.
2.1.2
Rotating Reference Frame
The rotating reference frame is a reference frame that rotates at the same angular velocity
as a specific AC machine or system. In the dq reference frame, the d-axis is aligned with
the rotor or magnetic field axis, and the q-axis is orthogonal to the d-axis.
The transformation from the stationary abc frame to the arbitrary rotating frame is shown
in Figure 2.1. The space vector ”x” rotates with an arbitrary angular speed (ω) with respect
to the stationary abc frame.
The space vector ”x” represents a physical quantity such as current or voltage, and it is
composed of three components: xa, xb, and xC. These components represent the pro-

2.2
PI Controllers
PI controllers are widely used in control systems to regulate and stabilize a process or
system. They are a type of feedback controller that combines proportional control and
integral control to achieve desired system performance.
A PI controller calculates a control signal based on the error between the desired setpoint
and the actual output of the system. The controller has two components:
Proportional Control: The proportional component produces an output signal that is pro-
portional to the current error. It is calculated by multiplying the error by a proportional gain
(KP ). The proportional control contributes to the immediate response of the controller, as
it reacts to the current error.
Integral Control: The integral component integrates the error over time and produces an
output signal proportional to the cumulative error. It is calculated by multiplying the inte-
grated error by an integral gain (KI). The integral control component helps in eliminating
steady-state errors and improves the stability of the system.

2.5
PLL
A Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) is a closed loop control system that compares the phase of
an input signal with the phase of a reference signal and generates an output signal that
is synchronized with the reference signal.
The basic components of a PLL include:
Phase Detector (PD): The phase detector compares the phase of the input signal, also
known as the feedback signal, with the phase of the reference signal. It produces an
output signal proportional to the phase difference between the two signals.
Voltage-Controlled Oscillator (VCO): The voltage-controlled oscillator generates an oscil-
lating signal whose frequency is determined by an input voltage. The output frequency of
the VCO can be adjusted by applying a control voltage to it.
Low-Pass Filter (LPF): The low-pass filter removes the high-frequency components from
the output of the phase detector and provides a smooth control voltage to the VCO. It
helps in maintaining stability and reducing noise in the PLL system.
The operation of a PLL can be described as follows:
• The reference signal, typically a stable and accurate clock signal, is fed into the
phase detector.
• The error signal is filtered by the low-pass filter, which removes any high-frequency
noise and fluctuations, providing a smooth control voltage.
• The control voltage is applied to the voltage-controlled oscillator, which adjusts its
output frequency accordingly. The VCO generates a signal that is an integer multiple
of the reference signal frequency.
• The output of the VCO is fed back to the phase detector as the feedback signal,
closing the loop.

Further Studies
The chapter proposes areas for further work in the context of the current Thesis. These
areas include:
• Verification of sequential testing for the two-level converters, Converter-1 and
-2:
This involves conducting thorough testing and validation of the sequential operation
of the two-level converters. The purpose is to ensure that the converters function
correctly and produce the desired output according to the sequential testing proce-
dure.
• Optimization of the MMC (Modular Multilevel Converter) models for RT-LAB:
The proposal suggests optimizing the MMC models specifically for RT-LAB. The
goal is to enhance the performance and accuracy of the MMC models in real-time
simulation scenarios.
• Test and verification of MMC models in RT-LAB:
This step involves conducting extensive testing and verification of the MMC models
within the RT-LAB environment. It aims to validate the models’ behavior, perfor-
mance, and response under various operating conditions, ensuring their reliability
and suitability for real-time simulation studies.
• Adaptation of components currently using C function blocks to Simulink blocks:
This proposal suggests converting the components that currently employ C function
blocks, such as Notch Filters, into Simulink blocks. By doing so, the complexity as-
sociated with programming in the C language can be avoided, and the control sys-
tem’s performance can be improved. Simulink blocks offer a graphical programming
environment that simplifies the implementation of control algorithms and enhances
the overall system performance.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis involves the study of cascaded feedback control loops utilizing an
OPAL-RT based platform integrated with a hardware prototype of a two-level converter. To
analyze the performance of the converter and control loop, the operation of the controllers
are validated in three modes defined as PQ, VDCQ, and Grid Forming. Furthermore,
the investigation is divided into three levels defined as Level-A, Level-B and Level-C.
This improves the simplicity in implementation and addresses the safe interface using the
converter’s internal control.
To achieve the research objectives, the controls were implemented and configured us-
ing an RT-LAB simulator, while keeping the controller interface user-friendly. The offline
simulations with converter models were conducted with an average model of a two-level
converter, which verified the controller performance with the system. Subsequently, the
controller was implemented on an OPAL-RT processor to interface with a hardware pro-
totype at the AC/DC WindPowerLab. Using the OPAL-RT platform, the performance of
the controller is experimentally validated to emulate certain aspects of the converter’s
controls.
During the experimental testing phase, it was observed that the phase-locked loop (PLL)
required optimization specifically for the case when the frequency rose from 50 to 55 Hz
in the Grid Forming control mode. Apart from this issue, the experimental results were
found to be consistent with the expected performance. This indicates that the two-level
converters operated as intended in the specified control modes and with the assigned
access/interfaces to the converter’s internal control.
