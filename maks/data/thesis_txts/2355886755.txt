Abstract 
Furcellaria lumbricalis has been reported to contain proteins, lipids, antioxidants, 
pigments, and polysaccharides with promising chemical bioactivities. Furcellaran, the 
valuable source of hydrocolloids from F. lumbricalis, has been commercially utilized 
since World War II. Currently, F. lumbricalis is only used to produce furcellaran, many 
other bioactives (e.g. protein) are wasted during the production. This project focuses 
on the protein extraction with the enzyme-assisted extraction (EAE) method before 
the production of furcellaran, by exploring optimum conditions which do not damage 
furcellaran in the later industrial production. At the same time, this project analyzes 
amino acids, antioxidants, pigments, ash contents in F. lumbricalis. 
8 different enzymes (Celluclast® 1.5 L, Shearzyme® 500 L, Viscozyme® L, Ultraflo® 
Max, AMG® 300 L, Fungamyl® 800 L, Alcalase® 2.4 L FG, and Flavourzyme® 500L) 
were selected to extract protein. The mixture of Celluclast® 1.5 L and Shearzyme® 
500 L and mixture of Viscozyme® L and Alcalase® 2.4 L FG were chosen to research 
further in different parameters (pH, temperature, time, pretreatment, and 
post-treatment) owning to their relatively higher extraction efficiency (12.45 and 
16.62 % respectively) than the other enzymes. For the combination of Viscozyme® L 
and Alcalase® 2.4 L FG, the best extraction condition was pH 9 which was adjusted by 
Na2CO3, together with a post-treatment (Alkaline and NAC extraction), achieved the 
extraction efficiency of 31 %. For the combination of Celluclast® 1.5 L and 
Shearzyme® 500 L, the best condition was pH 7, at room temperature and 18 h of 
extraction time, with the efficiency of 24 %. The pretreatment of microwave and 
ultrasound assisted did not help increase the extraction efficiency. Fungamyl® 800 L 
was also selected, but as time limited, it was only conducted at 40  and pH 6 with 
different post-treatments. The best post-treatment of Fungamyl® 800 L was another 
enzymatic extraction of Viscozyme® L and Alcalase® 2.4 L FG which was conducted at 
pH 9 or together with alkaline and NAC extraction. The extraction efficiency was 
approximately 22 %.

1. Introduction 
Nowadays, extracting natural products from marine organisms has become a huge 
interest in industry as such products can benefit human beings or animals. Seaweeds 
have been regarded as an important source of bioactive compounds with a promising 
and potential future in food, nutraceutical and/or cosmeceuticals products (Kadam, 
et al, 2013; Jeon, et al, 2012).  
Furcellaria lumbricalis which belongs to the genus of red algae has been 
commercially utilized for its valuable source of hydrocolloids, furcellaran since World 
War II. Furcellaran has been regarded as one of the most promising types of  
carrageenan,  and it is a versatile hydrocolloid, constituting 40-50% of the dry 
weight in F. lumbricalis. Apart from this compound, F. lumbricalis has been reported 
to contain proteins, lipids, antioxidants, pigments, and other polysaccharides. 
Meanwhile the chemical bioactivities in this species are promising, such as 
agglutinant, antifungal, antibacterial, and cytotoxic properties (BLUNDEN, et al, 1975; 
RAGAN, 1984) 
Seaweeds are known to have complicated and rigid cell walls owing to their chemical 
structures, thus it is typically difficult to extract the compounds from their 
intracellular wall efficiently. This is due to the presence of microfibrillar 
polysaccharides of the fibrous algal cell walls (Rodrigues et al., 2015). The traditional 
extraction methods, such as water-based extraction which is cheap, of food grade 
and environmentally friendly, but with a low selectivity and efficiency. The ones 
based on organic solvents have a higher selectivity and efficiency, but are a danger in 
food products and even harmful for the environment, due to their toxic solvent 
residues (Samarakoon & Jeon, 2012). Over the years, many novel extraction methods 
have been researched in industry application, e.g. ultrasound-assisted extraction 
(UAE), supercritical fluid extraction (SFE), microwave-assisted extraction (MAE), 
enzyme-assisted extraction (EAE), and pressurized liquid extraction (PLE) 
technologies. The process of SFE is really expensive and often used to extract dietary 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
supplements from marine algae. MAE and PLE have the limitation for thermolabile 
bioactives, as the two methods are carried out at high temperatures. UAE has been 
adopted to extract bioactive compounds from seaweeds in full-scale commercialized 
extraction. EAE is a promising method with precise selectivity, high efficiency and 
yield. Furthermore the method is conducted in mild reactive conditions which can 
keep the original efficiency to maximum (Hardouin et al., 2016; Rodrigues et al., 
2015; Romarís-Hortas, et al, 2013; Kadam et al., 2013; Wijesinghe & Jeon, 2012). 
The protein sources from marine algae have been studied to potentially be used in 
future pharmaceutical, nutraceutical and cosmeceutical applications, especially their 
peptides have aroused great interests because of their specific health benefits, such 
as antioxidative, antitumor and immune-simulative properties(Samarakoon et al., 
2012). 
Recently, furcellaran is the most utilized product from F. lumbricalis, while the other 
compounds such as antioxidants, pigments and proteins are often neglected and 
wasted during the production of furcellaran. This master project will focus on the 
enzyme assisted extraction of proteins, and study the effect of different parameters 
on extraction efficiency, such as time, pH and temperature. Other bioactive 
compounds, antioxidants and pigment contents are also considered. Furcellaran 
extraction is not a part of the present project, but the enzyme assisted extraction 
must be carried out under conditions that will not damage furcellaran which is 
planned to be extracted in the later production stage.  
1.1 Aim of study 
The aim of this study was to: 
 Focus on the extraction of protein from F. lumbricalis with EAE method 
 Test different parameters (pH, time, temperature and concentration) of the 
selected enzymes to improve the extraction efficiency 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
 Investigate the effects of other treatments together with enzymes or no 
enzymes 
 Verify the presence of other bioactive compounds (pigment, antioxidants and 
ash content) in F. lumbricalis 
2. Theoretical background 
2.1. Furcellaria lumbricalis 
Furcellaria lumbricalis (Huds.) Lamour., also called 
Furcellaria fastigiata or Fucus fastigiata, exists 
mainly in the North Atlantic and Arctic oceans, and 
particularly in the brackish waters of the Baltic Sea. 
This species mostly inhabit and is harvested in the 
colder waters. They are regarded as one of the few 
major and unique perennial rhodophytes to penetrate the hyposaline waters in Baltic 
Sea, moreover they occur endemicaly in northern Europe (C. J. Bird & McLachlan, 
1991). 
2.1.1. Chemical compounds in F. lumbricalis 
F. lumbricalis has been researched to contain chemicals of commercial values, e.g. 
furcellaran, also called Danish agar, constituting 40-50% of the dry weight, as the 
major structural polysaccharide/hydrocolloid (Czapke, 1964). The hydrocolloids 
mostly utilized in the industry worldwide are the stabilizing agents (like carrageenan 
and agar). Floridean starch was found to be abundant and presented conspicuous 
granules within the subcortical cells in the red alga F. lumbricalis (C. J. Bird & 
McLachlan, 1991), its content was reported to rise to 3.6% of fresh weight during 
summer (SCHACHAT & GLICKSMAN, 1959). Cellulose, a structural polymer, was 
reported to occupy 5.7% of dry weight. And galactose accounted more than 80%, as

the main monosaccharide after hydrolysis (Ross, 1953). 
In addition, the red alga F. lumbricalis biomass also contains high amounts of 
nitrogen, which constitutes 13-25% of dry weight, and most of these contribute to 
the protein compounds (Indergaard M & Knutsen, 1990; Krasil'Nikova SV et al., 
1972). 
Pigments concentration is high, mainly proteinic pigments in F. lumbricalis (C. J. Bird 
& McLachlan, 1991; Rando Tuvikene et al., 2010).  
Polyphenols were also extracted and verified from F. lumbricalis (Chandler, et al, 
1977; RÖNNERSTRAND, 1968). Zubia et al. studied the total phenolic content of F. 
lumbricalis and found that it contained one of the highest among the 24 rhodophyte 
species from Brittany coasts (Mayalen Zubia & Deslandes, 2009) 
Meanwhile, fatty acid and sterol contents in this species had been researched in 
1977 (Chandler RF et al., 1977), the major fatty acids are 16: 0 and 20: 5 (n-3), and 
also other significant quantities of 16: 1 (n-7), 18: 1 (n-9) and 20:4 (n-6) (JAMIESON & 
REID, 1972); cholesterol, constituted over 99% of the total sterol, but these varied 
according to different seasons (IDLER & WISEMAN, 1970). 
Inorganic compositions which include mainly the mineral part in F. lumbricalis are 
especially rich in potassium, magnesium, and calcium (Merike, et al, 1998), however, 
its iodine content is around 0.08% of dry weight which is moderately high among red 
algae (C. J. Bird & McLachlan, 1991).  
2.1.2. Cell structure 
When extracting the bioactive compounds from seaweeds, it is important to know 
their cell structure, and thus select the appropriate method. The cell structure of F. 
lumbricalis has not been studied clearly, but some information could be obtained 
from the red algae family which it belongs to. The part of cell wall in the red 
seaweeds accounts for 65% (w/w) of dry matter, and it is mainly composed of fibrillar 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
wall, amorphous matrix and glycoprotein domains. The formation of cell wall is 
reticular with fibrillar polysaccharides and glycoprotein domains linked, and 
amorphous matrix was embedded in them. Fibrillar polysaccharides, regarded as the 
most inert and resistant component in the cell wall, mainly contain cellulose and 
mannans whose backbone is mostly D-glucose units linked by β-(1→4) bonds (Miller, 
1997). The glycoprotein domain includes the kind of glycoprotein which holds the 
characteristics of “cellulose binding domains”, and could improve the crosslinking 
among the polysaccharide fibers. Meanwhile, the amorphous matrix is constituted 
by sulfated galactans, polysaccharides (e.g. carrageenans, agarans and “DL-hybrid” 
(Gomez-Ordonez & Ruperez, 2011) which are also called phycocolloids and able to 
form aqueous gels (Al-Marhubi & Soussi, 2011), and it also could extend to the 
intercellular spaces among the neighboring cells. Figure 1 shows an electron 
micrographs of the red seaweed Porphyra. sp. which presents a rigid cell wall (J. 
CRONSHAW, 1958). And the cross sections of F. lumbricalis through the scanning 
electron micrographs presented the galactans which are the commonest and most 
abundant cell wall constitutes in the Rhodophyta (Cole & Sheath, 1990) and also 
seemed to show a thick cell wall among the neighboring cells in Figure 2. (Vaher 
Merike et al., 1998). 
In the red algal cell walls, the content of proteins is low and they are not distributed 
uniformly, as some of them conjugate with polysaccharide (e.g. xylose) to form 
glycoproteins, it was reported that serine formed the O-glycoside bonds in a 
glycoprotein from F. lumbricalis; and some exist in the protein-enriched fragments 
(e.g. cuticles and pit plugs) (Cole et al., 1990). 
Red seaweeds are well known to contain higher protein content than green or brown 
seaweeds (Galland-Irmouli et al., 1999). Aspartic and glutamic acid are the 
predominant amino acids and represent between 22-44% of the proteins in red 
seaweeds, moreover they are responsible for the unique flavor and taste of 
seaweed.  
The main proteins of red seaweeds are phycobiliproteins which are the main 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
photosynthetic accessory pigments and consist of up to 50% of the total protein 
content. Phycobiliproteins belong to the family of fluorescent proteins and become 
highly bright when released from the cells. They are regarded as valuable substances, 
especially the red coloured phycoerythrin and the blue phycocyanin, in commercial 
application, e.g. cosmetics and medicinal diagnostics .  
Currently, it is relatively complicated to extract phycobiliproteins effectively from the 
red algae as most of them contain cold-water soluble viscous polysaccharides. These 
polysaccharides will make the extraction process more complicated. While F. 
lumbricalis contains mainly the polysaccharides of furcellaran, this polysaccharide is 
difficult to resolve in cold water. Thus, F. lumbricalis could represent a prospective 
raw material to extract phycobiliproteins (Rando Tuvikene, 2016).

2.1.3. Furcellaran production 
Most of the commercial application of F. lumbricalis is on the production of 
furcellaran. Its industry production began in 1943 (C. J. Bird & McLachlan, 1991), and 
is usually produced as followed in Figure 3: Free-floating F. lumbricalis is gathered by 
small vessels and collected by large trawling nets, then preserved through drying. 
The dried seaweed have to be washed before and treated with the boiling water for 
furcellaran extraction. After extraction, the step is to filter or centrifuge the extract 
with potassium salt added (SCHACHAT et al., 1959). Finally, the residue goes through 
roller-drying or gel precipitation, milling and packaging. The furcellaran production in 
Figure 3 is from Est-Agar AS which is one of the famous company to produce 
furcellaran in the world now. 
The seaweed also includes many other bioactive compounds which are of great 
value, but most of them would be decomposed in the boiling step during the 
production of furcellaran. It is of great importance to extract them before the 
furcellaran extraction, thus, not only can these bioactive compounds generate extra 
profits, but also they lead to a further purification of furcellaran product.

Figure 3.  Furcellaran production from Est-Agar AS 
40 kilos of F. lumbricalis could yield 1-1.5 kiolos of furcellaran through this or similar 
technologies (seen Figure 3. ) from Est-Agar AS (WHISTLER, 1973). The registered 
annual consumption of furcellaran in 1973 was about 1200 metric tons in the world 
(WHISTLER, 1973). The demand of its potential application in food and supplement 
has been increasing currently (Jibril, Bello & Ahmad, 2016). According to the 
furcellaran production volumes in 1973, an extra production of protein could be 
obtained more than 72 metric tons proteins, as the protein content in F. lumbricalis 
is among 6-25% of dry weight (Indergaard M et al., 1990).  
Therefore, it is meaningful of this project to extract the protein and also other 
bioactive compounds from F. lumbricalis before furcellaran production. 
2.2. Technical problems 
Protein extraction 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
Protein extraction from most seaweeds has been verified to be very difficult due to 
the complicated cell wall and also the presence of large quantities of anionic cell-wall 
polysaccharides, furcellaran as an example. It is a anionic partly sulphated 
polysaccharide in F. lumbricalis, composed of D-galactose, 3,6-anhydro-D-galactose 
and D-galactose- 4-sulphate, repeated disaccharide units of beta (R1 = R2 = H) and 
kappa carrageenan (R1 = SO3-, R2 = H) as shown in figure 4. Such polysaccharides 
could combine protein during the extraction and also its position in the amorphous 
matrix of cell wall would prevent protein extraction from this species.  
 
Figure 4. Furcellaran repeating disaccharide units 
Many researches has been conducted to extract protein from seaweed by enzymatic 
degradation of the cell walls, but with the precondition of destroying the 
polysaccharides. At 1990 and 1992, Amano and Noda began to suggest the 
enzymatic extraction efficiency of protein from several algae by degrading cell wall 
polysaccharides (Amano H, 1990; Amano H, 1992). Fleurence applied the enzymes, 
carrageenase and cellulose, on the red algae Chondrus crispus, and thereby 
increased extraction efficiency of the proteins 10-fold (Fleurence, et al, 1995). Joel 
Fleurence (Fleurence, 1999), and also Wijesinghe et al. (W. A. J. P. Wijesinghe, 2012) 
discussed the potential of this method to improve protein and also other bioactive 
compounds accessibility. 
The project has aimed to find certain enzymes to assist the extracting of protein 
without damaging the polysaccharides, furcellaran. The limits do not allow the uses 
of polysaccharase, like carrageenase. Meanwhile, the gel strength of furcellaran 
depends highly on pH, the optimum range of pH is from 6-9, and the maximum is pH 
8. The gel strength would lose its good gel function below pH 6. Also it was proven to 
have a maximum gel viscosity at 40   when heating from 20   (SCHACHAT et al., 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
1959). Furthermore, both protein and furcellaran could be extracted at high 
temperatures and they would bind together and become more difficult to be 
separated. Therefore, the enzymatic condition in this project should be conducted at 
least above pH 6, maximum temperature 40 °C, and the cell wall degrading enzymes 
should be selected like cellulase and xylanase which do not effect furcellaran. 
2.3. Selection of appropriate enzymes and optimum extraction 
conditions 
Every enzyme is specific, they do their right job with the appropriate time, 
temperature and pH effectively and efficiently. There are many types of enzymes, 
especially digestive enzymes which have the capacity to break down the foods into 
available materials.  
The cell wall formation in F. lumbricalis, like the normal red seaweed, could include 
fibrillar polysaccharides (cellulose, mannan and xylan ), glycoproteins (cellulose 
binding domains) and amorphous matrix (sulfated galactans, polysaccharides, e.g. 
furcellaran). Proteins exist mainly in the cytoplasm and chloroplasts, a small amount 
of proteins is in the glycoproteins (Cian et al., 2015). The task of the enzymes was to 
break down the cell wall to some extent which can aid extracting the inside proteins 
outside without degrading furcellaran. The special concerns would be put on the 
enzymes which could degrade the fibrillar polysaccharides, the binding between 
proteins and polysaccharides or even cut peptide bonds from a protein structure to 
get proteins out. Therefore, the selected enzymes are like the following, cell wall 
degrading enzymes and protease. They were tested individually or in combination. 
As the researches, Joubert et al. adopted a mixture of cellulase and xylanase to 
isolate protein and DNA simultaneously from Palmaria palmaria (Rhodophyta) 
(Joubert & Fleurence, 2008); Harnedy et al proved the higher efficiency of extracting 
protein from the red macroalga Palmaria palmate with Celluclast 1.5L and 
Shearzyme 500L when used together with alkaline extraction (Harnedy & FitzGerald, 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
2013); and Rodrigues et al. also verified that EAE methods was a promising tool to 
obtain extracts with important biological properties by the enzymes Cellulase and 
Viscozyme (Rodrigues et al., 2015). Heo et al. extracted antioxidants from brown 
seaweeds with the five carbohydrate degrading enzymes (Viscozyme, Celluclast, 
AMG, Termamyl and Ultraflo) and five proteases (Protamex, Kojizyme, Neutrase, 
Flavourzyme and Alcalase). The enzymatic extracts were proven to be promising 
applied in food and pharmaceutical industry (Soo-Jin, et al, 2005). Similar studies of 
extracting antioxidants with carbohydrate degrading enzymes and proteases were 
also conducted (Adalbjornsson & Jonsdottir, 2015; Chang-Bum, et al, 2004; 
Jae-Young et al., 2009; Senevirathne, et al, 2010). Hydrolysis of proteins by protease 
released peptides which could have antioxidant properties have already been 
described in many researches (Adalbjornsson et al., 2015; Beaulieu, et al, 2016; 
Jae-Young et al., 2009).  
2.3.1. Cell wall degrading enzymes 
1. Viscozyme® L 
Specification: endo-beta-glucanase that hydrolyzes (1,3)- or (1,4)-linkages in 
beta-D-glucans 
Viscozyme is a cellulolytic enzyme mixture, including arabanase, cellulase, 
β-glucanase, hemicellulase, and xylanase (Refer to Appendix 1 ). It comes from 
Aspergillus sp. The purpose of this enzyme is to break down the cellulose and 
xylanase which could exist in this seaweed. The application of this multi-enzyme 
complex has been on the extraction of polyphenols from unripe apples and also 
protein from oat bran where Viscozyme showed a higher efficiency (55.7%) on the 
condition concentration 30 FBG/10 g of oat bran, pH 4.6, temperature 44 °C and time 
2.8 h (Guan & Huiyuan, 2008). 
2. Celluclast® 1.5L 
Synonym: 1,4-(1,3:1,4)-β-D-Glucan 4-glucano-hydrolase, Cellulase 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 Enzyme Commission (EC) Number: 3.2.1.4 
Celluclase is also a mixture of several enzymes to catalyze cellulolysis and often 
obtained from fungi, bacteria, and protozoans, especially from a selected strain of 
the fungus Trichoderma reesei (Karlsson, et al, 2002). The main aim of it is to break 
down cellulose and plant fiber. The enzyme is often used to degrade cellulosic 
materials into fermentable sugars, glucose, cellobiose, and higher glucose polymers, 
thus reduce the viscosity and increase the yield of valuable products deriving from 
plant (Yuliarti, et al, 2011).  
3. SHEARZYME 500L 
Synonym: xylanase(endo-1,4-) 
EC Number:  3.2.1.8 
Shearzyme, also called xylanase, effects very well on degrading plant materials into 
valuable nutritients. It can be produced by fungi, bacteria, yeast, marine algae, insect, 
seeds, etc. The main commercial source is filamentous fungi (Gibson LJ, 2013). 
Shearzyme shows better capacity to degrade beta-1,4-xylan (the linear 
polysaccharide) into xylose (Scheller HV, 2010), and then break down hemicellulose 
which plays a major role in protecting plant cell walls (Scheller HV, 2010). The 
commercial applications of it would be in the pulp and paper industry. 
4. ULTRAFLO® MAX 
Synonym: Beta-glucanase (endo-1,3(4)-) ; Xylanase (endo-1,4-) 
Ultraflo Max is a specially designed enzyme including beta-glucanases and xylanases, 
usually derived from Humicola insolens (Smith, 2004). The purpose of it would be to 
break down cell wall materials in cereals like β-glucans, pentosans and other gums, 
and create a better beer filtration rate with low viscosity wort during the mashing 
process of brewing which was described in Novozymes. Shumin Hu et al. studied the  
proteolytic and cytolytic enzymes to degrade starch during brew mashing, Ultraflo

Max was found to improve the starch hydrolysis greatly by degradation of malt cell 
walls (Shumin et al., 2014). 
5. AMG ( amyloglucosidase) 
Synonym: 
1,4-  -D-Glucan 
glucohydrolase, 
EXO-1,4-   -glucosidase, 
and 
Glucoamylase 
EC Number: 3.2.1.3 
AMG aims to hydrolyze 1,4- and 1,6-alpha linkages and removes glucose units in a 
stepwise manner from liquefied starch. It is obtained from Aspergillus niger, and 
applied in the glucose syrups production from starch or dextrin (Rani Das, et al, 
1998).  
6. Fungamyl® 800 L 
Synonym: 1,4-alpha-Dglucan glucano-hydrolase; Alpha-amylase (seen in Appendix 2) 
The enzyme holds the ability to hydrolyze 1,4-alpha-glucosidic linkages in amylose 
and amylopectin (seen in Appendix 2). It is a fungal alpha-amylase producing from a 
selected strain of Aspergillus oryzae (NEBESNY, 1989). Its commercial uses have been 
on food industry. In alcohol industry, Fungamyl 800 L is used for starch liquefaction 
where large amounts of maltose could be formed with a prolonged reaction at low 
temperatures (55-60) (Pandey, et al, 2000). In bread making, it can help increase loaf 
volume, improve the brown crust color of baked goods, and ensure consistent bread 
quality with a fine and uniform crumb structure (Swetha, et al ,2006) ; In maltose 
syrup industry, this enzyme was used to saccharify enzyme-liquefied starch with an 
optimum condition (Temperature 55, pH 5.5) (Souza et al, 2010; M. W. KEARSLEY, 
1995; Nigam, 2013).

Alcalase is an alkaline protease with a very broad substrate specificity and 
submerged fermented by Bacillus licheniformis. It can hydrolyse the amino esters 
and selective esters stereoselectivly, and most peptide bonds of the protein (seen in 
Appendix 3). The commercial application of it mostly focus on the detergent 
formulations which can remove stains of protein-based, such as grass and egg, and 
also in food-industry, pharmaceuticals, for studies in molecular biology and in 
peptide synthesis (Nigam, 2013). 
8. Flavourzyme® 500L 
Synonym :P6110 
EC Number: 232-752-2 
It is a protease/peptidase complex produced by Aspergillus oryzae, and has the 
ability to catabolize proteins by breaking down the peptide bonds with the activities 
of both endoprotease and exopeptidase. It is often used to degrade proteins, 
research protease inhibitors and thermal inactivation kinetics (Hamada, 2000; 
POMMER, 1995). 
Alcalase®2.4 L FG belongs to endoprotease which is capable of cutting peptide bonds 
inside a protein structure. While Flavourzyme®500L is a mixture of endoprotease  
and exopeptidase. Exopeptidase is able to cut peptide bonds from the 
end-positioned amino acids in a protein structure. Thereby the difference between 
endoproteases and exopeptidases would be producing more peptides and free 
amino acids respectively when degrading proteins (Hamada, 2000). Hamada studied 
the effect of rice bran proteins hydrolysis with Alcalase and Flavourzyme, and found 
Flavourzyme which is comprised of endoproteases and exopeptidases enhanced 
more functional properties (solubility, emulsification activity and stability) for 
protein hydrolysates (Hamada, 2000). While, SHU et al. found the optimal proteinase

3.6.1. Kjeldahl method 
The kjeldahl method is used to determine the organic nitrogen content in chemical 
substances quantitatively and it was developed by Johan Kjeldahl in 1883. The total 
kjeldahl nitrogen (TKN) contains all the nitrogen content, like ammonia. And it is 
often used to convert nitrogen into protein content through the conversion factors 
which is known as N factors, different numbers for different materials. A universal 
nitrogen-to-protein N factor was researched to be 5 for seaweed (Angell, 2016).  
The method includes mainly three parts: digestion, distillation and titration. During 
the digestion stage, the sample (about 1 g of seaweed) is heating with sulphuric acid 
which can decompose the organic substance into ammonium sulphate by liberating 
the reduced nitrogen through oxidation. The Kjeltabs which contains potassium 
sulphate in this step works as catalyst to increase the boiling point of the medium. 
The stage is complete when the medium has become clear green, it is usually 2 hours 
for seaweed. And then the distillation part, the solution is distilled with a certain 
amount of sodium hydroxide which can react with ammonium salt into ammonia, the 
ammonia is absorbed by hydrochloric acid again in a conical flask. Finally, the 
ammonium ion is titrated by sodium hydroxide by way of ACAndersen pH indicator 
and then the TKN is obtained through the calculation below. (Methods details seen in

3.6.2. Dumas method 
The principle of Dumas method is same as Kjeldahl method to determine the TKN in 
chemical substances, and it is firstly described by Jean-Baptiste Dumas in 1826. It has 
more advantages which are easy, rapid and automatic than Kjeldahl. 
For this method, maximum 500mg seaweed or 2500 liquid was put into the sample 
plate and then went through sample combustion in a chamber under a high 
temperature (900  ), this oxidation process would release carbon dioxide, water 
and nitrogen. And then the gases pass over the special columns which contain 
potassium hydroxide aqueous solution to absorb the carbon dioxide and water. At 
the end of the column, a thermal conductivity detector separates the nitrogen from 
any residues and the signals were converted into nitrogen content. Before 
measurement, the instrument is often calibrated by a pure material which has a 
known nitrogen concentration, like aspartic acid. One sample usually takes 5 minutes 
in this method.

Shearzyme® 500 L, Viscozyme® L, Celluclast® 1.5 L, Ultraflo® Max, AMG and 
Fungamyl® 800 L belong to the group of “cell wall degrading enzyme”. Based on the 
theories part above, Shearzyme® 500 L and Celluclast® 1.5 L can break down xylan 
and cellulose which could exist in the cell wall of this seaweed, into xylose and 
fermentable sugars respectively. The results from Figure 12 presented no significant 
difference between Shearzyme® 500 L and a mixture Shearzyme® 500 L and 
Celluclast®1.5 L, the reason may be more xylan existing in the cell wall of this species. 
It is coincident with the research that the quantity of cellulose in red algae was lower 
and xylans were reported existing only from algae that do not contain cellulose in 
their cell walls (Cole et al., 1990). But it disagrees with the study that cellulose 
occupied 5.7 % of dry weigh in F. lumbricalis (Ross, 1953). Joubert et al increased the 
protein content by factor of 3.7 from the sample of red seaweed Palmaria plamata in 
April with the enzyme mixture of Shearzyme® 500 L and Celluclast®1.5 L, at 40  , 
pH 5, for 6h of extraction time (Joubert et al., 2008). 
Viscozyme® L contains arabanase, β-glucanase, and hemicellulase, in addition to 
cellulase and xylanase. It is thus supposed to degrade the cell wall more effectively at 
the optimum condition, but the efficiency of it turned out to be around 6.6 % instead. 
Similarly, Ultraflo® Max includes β-glucanase and xylanase, and AMG contains 
β-glucanase, the results were shown to be even worse, only around 2% of efficiency 
for them. The results for AMG was according to the discussion above that cell wall 
materials in red algae did not include β-glucan. The reason for Viscozyme® L and 
Ultraflo® Max may be owing to the function of substrate inhibition (Reed, et al, 2010). 
The extraction yield of enzymatic hydrolysates was reported up to 60 % with Ultraflo 
and Viscozyme for Palmaria plamata, at their optimum condition (Wang, et al, 2010).

40   and pH 6. The enzyme finally arrived when all the other enzymes have been 
finished for testing. The changed condition was to owing to no expected good results 
obtained from other enzymes at the preferred condition. As time was limited, this 
enzyme was not conducted at the preferred condition like the other enzymes. 
Fungamyl® 800 L was reported to have an optimum condition at pH 5.5 and 55   
when producing maltose syrup (M. W. KEARSLEY, 1995). Considering the limitation in 
this study, it was conducted at temperature 40   and pH 6. Meanwhile, this 
species was described to have more starch in the cell wall material from the theory 
background above. Fungamyl® 800 L was the exact enzyme break down starch in the 
cell wall material. But the extraction efficiency of it was only about 4.5 %, not as 
expected in this experiment. Maybe this extraction time was not enough, as it was 6 
h in this study. The reaction time of Fungamyl® 800 L was 20-42 h in maltose syrup 
production (M. W. KEARSLEY, 1995).  
For protease, Alcalase® 2.4 L FG and Flavourzyme® 500 L, Alcalase® 2.4 L FG really 
showed a better result of 16.88 % in efficiency. As Alcalase® 2.4 L FG has an optimum 
function at pH 8 (seen Table 1), the condition of pH 7 here could create a good 
environment for it. Alcalase® 2.4 L FG could hydrolyze the amino esters and selective 
esters. The esters might come from the bond of oligosaccharide and protein, the free 
carboxyl group of aspartic or glutamic is usually regarded as the joint point to form 
ester glycosidic bond type. Meanwhile, aspartic and glutamic are the predominant 
amino acids in this seaweed as described in the theory background. But the enzyme 
Flavourzyme® 500 L did not function in the extraction even though its optimum pH is 
close to 7, the possibility could be substrate inhibition or it was not conducted 
properly.  
As assumed, when the enzyme Viscozyme® L degrade the cell wall to an expected 
extent, Alcalase® 2.4 L FG would have a better efficiency to enter into the cell wall

As reported, alkaline solution (NaOH) can help extract highly water insoluble 
proteins effectively from macroalgae and microalgae (Amano H, 1990; Amano H, 
1992; Barbarino & Lourenco, 2005; Fleurence J et al., 1995; Venkataraman & 
Shivashankar, 1979; Wong & Cheung, 2001a; Wong & Cheung, 2001b), and the 
reducing regent NAC has been used to aid the extraction of macroalgal proteins 
(Harnedy et al., 2013), meanwhile, another reducing agent β-mercaptoethanol was 
proven to increase the extraction yield of alkaline soluble nitrogen from the 
microalga Scenedesmus acutus significantly (Venkataraman et al., 1979), 
but β-mercaptoethanol could not be used in food. The reducing agent NAC normally 
was used as a replacement. Both NAC and NaOH have the source of food-grade, and 
they were appropriate choice in this project. Furthermore, Pádraigín verified NaOH 
and NAC concentration as the critical parameters when extracted alkaline soluble 
proteins from P. palmata. They suggested the concentration of NaOH from 0.08 to 
0.12 mol/l, as the pH in this range changed from 11.50 to 12.68 where protein 
recovery increased. And 0.12 mol/l NaOH, 0.1 g/100 ml NAC were their optimum 
condition (Harnedy et al., 2013). In this study, their concentration of 0.0875 mol/l, 
and 0.08 g/100 ml respectively were among their range to increase the protein 
extraction. 
The analysis shown that the condition of pH 9 adjusted by Na2CO3 improved the 
efficiency of enzymatic extraction, and the post-treatments (Alkaline and the 
reducing agent (NAC) could increase the yield further after this treatment, but not 
the condition of pH 7. Maybe the combination of Na2CO3 and enzymes can really 
help degrade the cell wall matrix to some extent for F. lumbricalis. 
Moreover, it has been reported that the extraction condition at pH 12.68 and also 
the use of a reducing agent may affect the tertiary structure of the proteins 
(Harnedy et al., 2013). Another enzymatic extraction may be placed after this 
post-treatment. At this situation, this treatment degraded the tertiary structure and 
the peptide bonds would be more accessible to proteolytic degradation, thus the 
protease Alcalase® 2.4 L FG could work further during the extraction.

More improvements were conducted to extract protein with this enzyme mixture of

The purpose of microwave and sonication assisted extraction is to help break down 
the cell wall a little bit for the benefits of the later enzymatic extraction. For the 
microwave, it is known that it can cause more porosity in the biological matrix, lead 
to a higher penetration of solvents and facilitate the extraction of target compounds 
(Routray, et al, 2012; Routray, et al., 2012). And ultrasound holds the capacity of 
producing high frequency sound waves which can result in surface peeling, erosion, 
and particle breakdown, thus facilitating the extraction efficiency of bioactives from 
the biological matrix.  
Some references have proven the potential of microwave and sonication assisted 
enzymatic extraction. Kadam et al. (2013) described the advantage of enzyme, 
microwave and sonication assisted extraction as the novel technologies to extract 
bioactives from marine algae (Shekhar U. Kadam et al., 2013). Charoensiddhi et al. 
(2015) improved the extraction yield of the antioxidants by 5-20% from brown 
seaweed Ecklonia radiate through the microwave-assisted Viscozyme extraction for 5 
to 30 min (Charoensiddhi, et al, 2015); Romarís-Hortas et al. (2013) suggested a 
better extraction method to extract iodinated amino acid from edible seaweed with 
ultrasound-assisted enzymatic hydrolysis (Romarís-Hortas et al., 2013; Shekhar U. 
Kadam et al., 2013).

5. Conclusion  
F. lumbricalis has been known as few major and unique perennial rhodophytes 
mainly from Baltic Sea. It has been commercially utilized for furcellaran production 
(valuable source of hydrocolloids) since World War II and the demand is increasing. F. 
lumbricalis was reported to contain protein, fatty acids, antioxidants, pigments, and  
other polysaccharides, their chemical bioactivities are promising, such as agglutinant, 
antifungal, and antibacterial. The proteins content in F. lumbricalis was researched 
high enough from the range of 13 to 25%, the main proteins are phycobiliproteins 
which are regarded as the valuable substances, especially the red coloured 
phycoerythrin and the blue phycocyanin included, in commercial application, e.g. 
cosmetics and medicinal diagnostics. While, most of the chemical compounds are 
lost during furcellaran production, it is important to extract them before and take 
full advantage of them. This project focused on the protein extraction with EAE 
methods and also some other pretreatment and post-treatment based on EAE 
method. The contents of pigments, antioxidant activity, amino acids and also ash 
(inorganic compounds) were tested for the species to prove its potential application.  
8 different enzymes (Celluclast® 1.5 L, Shearzyme® 500 L, Viscozyme® L, Ultraflo® 
Max, AMG® 300 L, Fungamyl® 800 L, Alcalase® 2.4 L FG, and Flavourzyme® 500L) 
were conducted to assist the protein extraction process. The mixture of Celluclast® 
1.5 L and Shearzyme® 500 L and mixture of Viscozyme® L and Alcalase® 2.4 L FG were 
chosen to research further in different parameters (pH, temperature, time, 
concentration, pretreatment, and post-treatment) owning to their relatively higher 
extraction efficiency (12.45 and 16.62 % respectively) than the other enzymes. 
Fungamyl® 800 L was also selected to investigate its effect on protein extraction 
efficiency owing to its possibility of degrading starch in the cell wall material. But as 
time limited, it was not conducted at the preferred condition, only at 40  and pH 6 
with different post-treatments. 
For the combination of Viscozyme® L and Alcalase® 2.4 L FG, the best extraction was 
==PAGE:60==
60 
 
pH of 9 which was adjusted by Na2CO3, and with a post-treatment (Alkaline and NAC 
extraction). The best extraction condition increased the extraction efficiency to 31 % 
from 16 % which was conducted at the preferred condition. The best post-treatment 
of Fungamyl® 800 L was another enzymatic extraction of Viscozyme® L and Alcalase® 
2.4 L FG which was conducted at pH 9 or both the mixture of Viscozyme® L and 
Alcalase® 2.4 L FG and alkaline extraction together. The extraction efficiency was 
approximately 22 %. For the combination of Celluclast® 1.5 L and Shearzyme® 500 L, 
the best condition would be the pH 7, room temperature and 18 h of extraction time, 
with the efficiency of 24 % from 12.45 % in the preferred condition. The 
pretreatment of microwave and ultrasound assisted did not help increase the 
extraction efficiency. 
Furthermore, the treatment of KOH extraction ( concentration: 8%, temperature: 
80 , time: 3h) in water or mixture of alcohol: water 60:40 really increased the 
extraction efficiency up to around 80 %, however, this could destroy the extracted 
protein and also furcellaran bonded with protein may be released into the solution.  
6. Future perspectives 
A further study needed to be researched on this protein extraction. As stated before, 
the protein extraction efficiency has not been satisfactory in this study. The reason 
may be the lower protein content in this batch, or the inappropriate condition for the 
selected enzymes. The future experiments should solve these problems, perhaps a 
new batch of F. lumbricalis which contains higher protein content, of roughly 20 %, 
could solve these problems and provide better results. It is still promising to obtain a 
good protein extraction efficiency, especially with the enzymes of Viscozyme® L and 
Alcalase® 2.4 L FG at pH 9 together with alkaline extraction. Fungamyl® 800 L might 
need more improvements for its extraction condition, as the cell wall material of F. 
lumbricalis was reported to contain much starch. A method to verify if furcellaran is 
released into the extraction solution is also needed. Furthermore, multi-extraction 
on F. lumbricalis can be regarded as an innovative study as no studies have been 
==PAGE:61==
61 
 
performed before. The total phenolic content of it has been studied as one of the 
highest among the 24 rhodophyte species from Brittany coasts (Mayalen Zubia et al., 
2009). F. lumbricalis could also represent a prospective raw material to extract 
phycobiliproteins (Rando Tuvikene, 2016). The extracted bioactives (protein, 
pigments and pheolic compounds) have great potential to be applied in food and 
pharmaceutical industry.
