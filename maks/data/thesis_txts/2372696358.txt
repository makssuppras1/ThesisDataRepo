Abstract 
 
The economic recession in Greece took a heavy toll on dwellers’ ability to heat their houses. 
Hence, Greek citizens burn low-cost wood as fuel for fireplaces and stoves causing 
significant air quality deterioration, resulting in intense smog events. Smoke haze and high 
levels of airborne particulate matter appear during the nighttime in many Greek cities. During 
the last few years there are several publications investigating how economic crisis affected 
the air pollution in the Greek territory, focusing on fine and ultrafine particles, however most 
of them were focusing on the three biggest cities (Athens, Thessaloniki and Patras), 
therefore there are no measurements of ultrafine particles in case of medium sized cities. 
 
A first attempt on measuring the ultrafine particles concentration in a Greek medium sized 
city was made through this study. The framework was to verify that in the nighttime, during 
the cold season of the year, there is an increase of ultrafine particles due to wood burning. 
For this reason, measurement campaigns were conducted in Agrinio, a medium sized city, 
both during day and night. Additional measurements were also conducted in three more 
cities, one bigger (Athens) and two smaller (Nafpaktos and Thiva) so as to test that the 
above hypothesis was happening and in other cities as well. Furthermore, two more single 
measurements were carried out in the port of Piraeus and in Acropolis, in places with high 
and low expected values of particles respectively. 
 
The measurements in Agrinio indicated that the mean particles concentration in the daytime 
was 14817 ± 8760 particles/cm3, while in the night it found to be 35079 ± 10112 
particles/cm3. Hence, the total average value for Agrinio was 24948 particles/cm3. Similar 
pattern was also followed in a diurnal measurement that was conducted in the same place. 
Increased ultrafine particles in the night measurements were observed in the other locations 
as well (Athens, Nafpaktos and Thiva). Moreover, in the additional measurements of Piraeus 
port there were high particles values, while in Acropolis their level was low as expected.  
 
Most of the time, the strong winds resulted in lower particles concentrations, while in some 
other cases, especially during the night hours, the wind enhanced the particles 
concentration, depending on its direction and where the source of pollution (chimney) was 
located. As an additional finding, it was observed that new house buildings emitted lower 
ultrafine particles. Moreover, emissions from local buses were responsible for the highest 
concentrations in Agrinio, while in all the measurement locations motorbikes emitted high 
particles concentrations. 
  
The elimination of particles is impossible, however their mitigation and prevention are 
achievable targets. The Greek government should act properly on the mitigation of wood 
burning aiming at effective measures. For better conclusions regarding the concentration of 
ultrafine particles in Greece, there will be needed further measurements across the country, 
both during the cold and warm months of the year.

1. Introduction 
 
Europe has managed to introduce measures and legislations that improved air quality in 
many cities during the last decades (Castellanos & Boersma, 2012). Athens could not differ 
and several measures have been implemented since the 1980s in an effort to improve the 
existing heavy air quality conditions, caused mainly from vehicles (Valavanidis et al., 2015; 
Fourtziou et al., 2017). From 2008, the pollutants’ reduction was accelerated significantly 
because of the economic recession in Greece, as a result of reduction in traffic and industrial 
activity, however there is an increase during the wintertime due to wood burning (Fourtziou 
et al., 2017). 
 
The economic crisis took a heavy toll on dwellers’ ability to heat their houses. Due to high 
taxation, the price of petrol and gas for heating has dramatically been increased. Until 
recently, burning of distilled fuel oil was the most popular method of residential heating, while 
wood burning has also been used in a number of houses as a supplementary heating source 
(Saffari et al., 2013), however this has changed now. Greek citizens are burning low-cost 
wood and biomass as fuel for fireplaces and stoves in order to avoid paying for heating oil, 
gas and proper wood which are more expensive, since their priority is to ensure food and 
other basic needs for their families (Karamanoli, 2013). This switch from burning of fuel oil 
for domestic heating to burning of uncontrolled wood and biomass appears to have caused 
significant air quality deterioration (Saffari et al., 2013) and the first strong evidence of this 
was observed during the winter of 2012-2013, where intense smog events took place 
(Fourtziou et al., 2017). 
 
Smog (smoke and fog) due to wood burning has evolved into a major air pollution problem 
affecting a lot of Greek cities during winter time (Gerasopoulos et al., 2014). As a result, 
smoke haze and high levels of airborne particulate matter appear during the nighttime and 
morning hours in Athens, Thessaloniki and other cities (Valavanidis et al., 2015). A situation 
that resemble London in the early 1950s, as smoke and the smell of burning wood become 
features of daily life for Greek citizens. As air pollution increased, so did the visits of patients 
with chronic respiratory problems to hospital emergency units (Karamanoli, 2013). 
 
Wood smoke particles usually contain several toxic organic constituents such as 
methoxyphenols and PAHs (Saffari et al., 2013), while smoke that is produced from 
inappropriate wood burning (colored or polished woods), contains chemical substances such 
as ozone, carbon monoxide, nitric oxides and nitric acid that can cause eye, nose, and lung 
irritations, resulting in aggravation of existing respiratory difficulties (Karamanoli, 2013). 
 
Measurements of air samples supported that a substantial number of Greek residents in 
cities burn wood and biomass, causing an increase in the concentration of wood smoke 
tracers (Saffari et al., 2013; Valavanidis et al., 2015). The most common of them are non-
sea salt potassium, levoglucosan, mannosan and galactosan, with significantly higher 
concentrations in the evening period compared with the morning (Saffari et al., 2013; 
Fourtziou et al., 2017).

During the last few years there are several publications (Florou et al., 2013; Saffari et al., 
2013; Gerasopoulos et al., 2014; Valavanidis et al., 2015; Saraga et al., 2015, Fourtziou et 
al., 2017; Florou et al., 2017) investigating how economic crisis affected the air pollution in 
the Greek territory, focusing on fine and ultrafine particles. Most of those researches had as 
a target the three biggest cities (Athens, Thessaloniki and Patras), however there are no 
measurements of ultrafine particles in case of medium sized cities. 
 
 
Aim of the project 
 
The aim of the project is to investigate the ultrafine particles in a Greek medium sized city. 
The framework of the present study is to verify that during the nighttime, in the cold season 
of the year, there is an increase of ultrafine particles due to wood burning. In order to do so, 
measurement campaigns will take place in Agrinio, a medium sized city, both during day and 
night. Additional measurements will be conducted in three more cities, one bigger (Athens) 
and two smaller (Nafpaktos and Thiva) so as to test the hypothesis that the increase of 
ultrafine particles during the nighttime happens and in other cities as well. Furthermore, two 
more measurements will be carried out in the port of Piraeus and in Acropolis, in places with 
high and low expected values of particles respectively.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Air Pollution 
 
According to World Health Organization (2016) approximately 3 million people die annually 
due to outdoor air pollution, while 90% of people worldwide breathe air that does not comply 
with the WHO Air Quality Guidelines. Air pollution is mostly affecting low- and middle-income 
countries in all the regions of the world, however, Western Pacific and Southeast Asia are 
currently the most affected areas.  
 
Air pollution (both indoor and outdoor) is the presence of pollutants in the atmosphere; any 
kind of substance in terms of quantity, concentration or duration, such as to cause adverse 
effects on human health, living organisms and ecosystems (McCabe, 1961). Pollution levels 
are usually expressed in μg/m3, or in parts per million by volume or in parts per million 
(ppm). Although a concentration of 1 ppm sounds small, for many gaseous pollutants it far 
exceeds the usual values found in the atmosphere. For this reason, in many cases 
concentrations of pollutants are measured in parts per billion (ppb) (Melas et al., 2000). 
 
The complexity of this problem is mainly due to air pollutants, which may be released in one 
country and transport in the atmosphere, contributing to poor air quality elsewhere (Ferrante 
et al., 2012). Air pollutants in terms of their mode of production are distinguished in primary 
and secondary. Primary are called the pollutants which are formed in sources of pollution, 
emitted directly into the atmosphere by human activities or by nature itself. Such are Sulfur 
Dioxide, Hydrogen Sulfide, Hydrocarbons, Nitrogen Oxides, Carbon Monoxide, Lead and 
Particulate Matter (PMs). Secondary are the pollutants formed in the atmosphere by the 
primary through chemical reactions. They are produced by complex reactions between 
oxides of nitrogen, oxygen and hydrocarbons under the influence of sunlight. Such pollutants 
are usually Ozone, Nitric Oxides, Aldehydes and Ketones (Gentekakis, 2010; Fenger & Tjell, 
2009). 
 
Furthermore, according to Wiederkehr & Yoon (1998) air pollutants can be divided into two 
group; in major air pollutants and hazardous air pollutants. In the first one, belong Sulphur 
Dioxide, particles, Nitrogen Oxides, Nitrous Oxide, Ammonia, Carbon Monoxide, Carbon 
Dioxide, Methane and Photochemical oxidants. In the latter, they belong Heavy metals, Non-
Methane Volatile Organic Compounds, Benzene and Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

2.2 Particles 
 
Particulate matters (PMs) or aerosols or just particles are complex mixtures of small solid or 
liquid particles, or both, that get into the air (Lazaridis, 2005). They are complex because of 
the distinctive characteristics they appear and the different ways in which they can be 
measured (Morawska, 2010). They belong to either primary (dust, carbon black, sea salt and 
volcanic dust) or secondary (organic compounds, Sulphate and Nitrate) pollutants and they 
are emitted into the atmosphere from human activities, such as combustion and fire, or 
naturally, from volcanic eruptions, desert dust and sea spray (Lazaridis, 2005). 
 
Their distinction can be based on their source, whether it is natural or human-made and their 
size (Gentekakis, 2010). As it can be seen in Figure 2.1, based on their size particles can be 
distinguished in coarse and fine particles. Coarse particles belong to PM2.5-10, with a 
diameter greater than 2.5 μm. Those are usually dust aerosols and aerosols of marine 
origin. Fine particles belong to PM2.5 and they have a diameter of less than 2.5 μm. These 
include mainly human-made and industrial aerosols, as well as soot produced by biomass 
burning. A subclass in small sized particles are the ultrafine particles, having a diameter of 
less than 0.1 μm, while the so-called nanoparticles are those whose diameter is less than 
0.02 μm. As previously mentioned, coarse and fine particles are usually measured in mass 
units per volume (μg/m3), however ultrafine particles are measured by their number of 
particles per volume (particles/cm3) (Fenger & Tjell, 2009). 
 
 
Figure 2.1 Distinction of particles according to their size 
(source: Fenger & Tjell, 2009) 
 
Particles with a maximum of 10 μm enter in the atmosphere as solids, both from the surface 
of the earth and from the sea, while often they are in form of aggregates resulting from 
coagulation and condensation processes, creating a constantly increasing particle size. 
These aerosols, consisting mainly of sand, sea salt and plant particulate matter, are involved 
in a continuous cycle activity, in which small particles are formed and grow gradually as they 
move away from the atmosphere (Gentekakis, 2010).

5 
 
The study of aerosols is particularly important because they not only affect the human 
health, but they affect the climate of the earth, through their influence on the energy balance 
of radiation as well (Seinfeld et al., 1998). Moreover, they have a key role in the atmospheric 
chemistry and in the water cycle (water balance) since their presence is essential for the 
condensation of water vapor and the formation of clouds (Ramanathan et al., 2001; 
Rosenfeld et al., 2008). 
 
Energy balance 
 
Aerosol particles affect the climate of Earth by influencing its radiation energy budget (IPCC, 
2007). This is mainly done in two ways; directly and indirectly. In the direct way, the solar 
radiation is scattered and absorbed, while in the indirect way there are two effects. The first 
one is known as cloud albedo and optical thickness effect, where there is a modification in 
cloud microphysical, macrophysical and optical properties. The second one is known as 
cloud lifetime effect, whereby the amount, the lifetime and the precipitation efficiency of a 
cloud are modified (Fotiadi et al., 2006; Haywood et al., 2009).  
 
Apart from those two methods (direct and indirect effect of aerosols), another one is often 
appeared in the literature; the semi-direct effect (Haywood et al., 2009, Gentekakis, 2010). In 
the semi-direct effect, there is a change in the cloud amount, due to anthropogenic aerosols 
which cause additional solar absorption, heating of the ambient environment and reduction 
to the ambient relative humidity (Haywood et al., 2009). 
 
The impact of aerosols, irrespective of their origin, is the decrease of global warming. More 
specifically, the cooling caused by particles is -0.27 W/m2, fluctuating from -0.77 to 0.23 
W/m2 (IPCC, 2013), causing this phenomenon to have opposite trends in relation to the 
greenhouse effect and as shown in Figure 2.2 their effect on the energy balance is the most 
uncertain factor, something that is also stated in a lot of researches leading to ambiguous 
conclusions (Ramanathan et al., 2001; Liepert et al., 2004; Rosenfeld et al., 2008; IPCC, 
2013). 
 
The physical and optical properties of aerosols are uncertain and the interaction mechanism 
of aerosols with the clouds is still limited, mainly due to the large heterogeneity and spatial-
temporal variability they appear, which is associated with their short atmospheric lifetime. As 
a consequence, this is the reason why they have the greater uncertainty factor regarding 
their effect on the energy balance (Fotiadi et al., 2006). 
 
For the better understanding of aerosols, a global aerosol database was created in the early 
1990s, combining satellite (Total Ozone Mapping Spectrometer - TOMS, Moderate 
Resolution Imaging Spectro-Radiometer - MODIS, Multiangle Imaging Spectroradiometer - 
MISR and Polarization and Directionality of Earth's Reflectance - POLDER) and terrestrial 
(Aerosol Robotic Network - AERONET) measurements (Fotiadi et al., 2006).

Human Health 
 
Particle pollutants are estimated to cause more than 500000 deaths annually (Nel, 2005). 
Even from the early 1990s, epidemiological studies show an association between air 
pollution from particles and acute mortality and morbidity (Oberdörster et al., 1995). Both fine 
and coarse particles have adverse effects on human health and are associated with 
respiratory, cardiac and circulatory problems, causing acute illness and chronic diseases 
(Samoli et al., 2011). Long term studies around the world have shown an increased risk of 
general, cardiopulmonary and cancer mortality (Kappos et al., 2004), something that has 
also been observed in short term as well (Katsouyanni et al., 2001). 
 
The toxicity of particulate matter is influenced by the bulk chemical composition (Sulfate, 
Nitrate, Ammonium, Chloride, Elemental and organic carbon, Crustal materials and 
Biological materials are the most common), the trace element content (Lead, Cadmium and 
Mercury are highly toxic), the content of strong acids, like Nitric and Sulfuric acid, the content 
of Sulfate and particle size distribution (Harrison & Yin, 2000). 
 
The following conditions make people more vulnerable to the effects caused by particle 
pollution; age (infants, young children and people over 65 years old), preexisting chronic 
heart, lung and cardiorespiratory diseases, and diabetes mellitus, all of which are associated 
with oxidative stress and inflammation (Oberdörster et al., 1995; Mar et al., 2004; Nel, 2005). 
Apart from the concentration and exposure time of particles, the damage depends on their 
size (the smaller the size, the higher the toxicity), chemical composition, physical and 
chemical properties, as well as the deposition area (Samoli et al., 2011).  
==PAGE:16==

Coarse particles are deposited in the upper airways, while smaller sized particles may reach 
smaller airways, causing inflammation, leading to asthma and chronic bronchitis, airway 
obstruction and decreased gas exchange (Nel, 2005). Moreover, there is a correlation 
between the concentration of particles and mortality from cardiovascular and lung diseases, 
lung cancer, bronchitis, chronic coughing, cardiopulmonary mortality and atherosclerosis 
(Valavanidis et al., 2008; Fenger & Tjell, 2009). 
 
In order to prevent the above effects, a number of measures have been introduced both at 
global and European level. World Health Organisation (2006) have set Air Quality guideline 
values for PM2.5 and PM10 aiming to protect the human health, however, it should be noted 
that no safe limit values exist below which there are no effects (Fenger & Tjell, 2009). 
According to those guidelines, the limits for PM2.5 are 10 μg/m3 in annual mean and 25 
μg/m3 in 24-hour mean, while for PM10 those limits are 20 μg/m3 in annual mean and 50 
μg/m3 in 24-hour mean. The same restrictions have been applied in European Union under 
the EU Air Quality Standards and its latest Directive (DIRECTIVE 2008/50/EC). Furthermore, 
regarding the limits for PM10 and its 24-hour mean value, it is stated that this value may be 
exceeded up to 35 times per year (European Commission, 2008).  
 
2.3 Ultrafine particles 
 
Ultrafine particles are the most dangerous particles for human health, however they are 
unregulated and the least investigated, while at the same time, they are coming out as the 
most abundant particulate pollutant in urban and industrial areas (Nel, 2005). Their exposure 
has increased rapidly due to human-made sources, such as internal combustion engines, 
power plants and incinerators (Terzano et al., 2010). Their concentration levels in clean 
environments, not influenced by human activities, are usually in the order of a few hundred 
particles per cm3, while in urban environments the concentrations range from a few 
thousand to about twenty thousand particles per cm3, where almost 90% of them originate 
from vehicle emissions (Morawska, 2010). 
 
In general, most of the ultrafine particles are combustion derived, such as soot and diesel 
exhaust particles. Their chemistry is related with combustion and pyrolysis processes, where 
combustion generates transition metals, while pyrolysis generates organic compounds 
(Terzano et al., 2010). Their chemical composition varies and depends on geographical, 
meteorological and source-specific variables, such as dispersion conditions occurred by 
wind speed and direction, temperature inversions, urban morphology and the “breathability” 
of cities (air masses exchange) (Kumar et al., 2014).  
 
It is not only the size of ultrafine which differ from larger particles, but there are also 
substantial differences in their chemical properties and consequently in the toxicological 
effects that may cause (Valavanidis et al., 2008; Morawska, 2010). Fine particles show more 
immediate effects, while ultrafine particles show more delayed effects on mortality 
(Morawska, 2010). Ultrafine particles can penetrate into the deepest parts of the lungs 
(Figure 2.3), reaching the alveoli and be taken up by the blood and transported to the other 
==PAGE:17==
8 
 
parts of the body (Oberdörster & Utell, 2002; Valavanidis et al., 2008), while they may even 
be transported to the brain through the nose and the nerve that signals the sense of smell 
(Oberdörster et al., 2004; Fenger & Tjell, 2009), something that is verified by Maher et al. 
(2016) during their recent research. Therefore, the potential hazards from the inhalation of 
ultrafine particles are very different to those from the inhalation of larger particles 
(Morawska, 2010).  
 
Their surface area is larger among the other particles, in relation to their volume, therefore, 
chemical constituents can react with molecules on the cell surfaces easily and uptake inside 
the cells (Nel, 2005; Fenger & Tjell, 2009). Furthermore, they carry a vast amount of toxic 
compounds on their surfaces, such as heavy metals, dioxins, hydrocarbons and other 
organic chemicals which increase their toxicity (Terzano et al., 2010). Hence, even low mass 
concentrations of inhaled singlet ultrafine particles may have a high pulmonary toxicity, 
because of the large numbers of particles involved (Oberdörster et al., 1995).

Currently, there are no legal limits regarding the exposure of ultrafine particles in the 
environment. The Danish Ecological Council have set recommendations based on worldwide 
measurements in an effort to make a first step for their legal approval. According to them, 
the yearly average value should be 7000 particles/cm3, while the hourly average may be up 
to 20000 particles/cm3, however those values are just recommendations and they are not 
legally applied in any Air Quality Directive yet (Press-Kristensen, 2014).

3. Method and materials 
 
The measurement campaigns were conducted in two periods, the first one was from 5th of 
February until 10th of February 2017 and the second one, from 4th of March until 27th of 
March 2017. Namely, within the first measurement period (05/02-10/02) four day and night 
measurements were conducted in Agrinio, while on the last day (10/02), a diurnal 
measurement was carried out in an apartment in the city center. Within the second period, 
six day and night measurements took place in Agrinio, one day and night measurement in 
Nafpaktos (14/03), one day and night measurement in Thiva (18/03) and three day and night 
measurements in Athens. Additionally, two more measurement sessions took place in 
Piraeus port and Acropolis hill at 27/03 and 28/03 of 2017 respectively.  
 
The measurements in Agrinio took place in five different spots; north, west, south, east and 
in the center, while measurements on the walking paths from place to place were recorded 
as well. The same route was followed for the ten total measurement days. In Thiva, the 
measurements took place in three spots; in the north, in the center and in the southern part. 
In case of Nafpaktos, three spots were selected as well, in the west, in the center and in the 
south. In Piraeus port, the measurement was carried out in two spots, in front of Gate E2-E3 
and in front of Gate E4. In Acropolis, the measurement was conducted in one place, in front 
of the Parthenon. Each measurement session lasted for approximately 30 minutes, apart 
from the measurements in the walking paths in Agrinio. Moreover, in some cases due to 
technical difficulties the measurement lasted for approximately 20 minutes. In cases that the 
measurements had to last for less than 15 minutes due to technical reasons, the 
measurements were conducted again. 
3.1 Measuring device 
 
The measurements were carried out using a P-Trak ultrafine particle counter (model 8525) 
produced by TSI. P-Trak is a portable instrument that detects and counts aerosol particles, 
direct and in real time, in the range of 1 μm to 0.02 μm in size (1000 nm to 20 nm), within the 
temperature range of 0-38°C. It has an upper detection limit of 500000 particles/cm3, while 
the sampling flow rate is 700 cm3/min (TSI, 2007). 
 
P-Trak counts single particles, resulting in more sensitive measurements than other 
technologies, such as photometers, which measure the density of a cloud of particle. 
Particles are drawn into P-Trak using a built-in pump. Upon their entrance in the instrument, 
they pass through a saturator tube where they mix with an alcohol vapor (isopropyl). Then, 
the mix of particles and alcohol passes into a condenser tube where alcohol condenses with 
the particles, make them to grow into a larger droplet. Afterwards, the droplets pass through 
a laser beam, producing flashes of light which are sensed by a photodetector. Therefore, the 
concentration of particles is determined by counting the light flashes. If the particles were not 
grown into larger droplets, they would not scatter enough light to be detected (TSI, 2012).  
 
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
For the purpose of these measurements, the P-Track recorded each value every 1 second 
(log interval: 1 sec), therefore in every session of 30 minutes, there are approximately 1800 
values. The result of each session (in each location) is the average concentration of these 
1800 single values.

3.3 Meteorological data 
 
The weather conditions can influence the dispersion of air pollutants; stable atmospheric 
conditions, such as clear and calm nights, can enhance the concentration of air pollutants by 
trapping them near ground level, while unstable conditions can suppress them (Fenger and 
Tjell, 2009). This is why it is needed to record the meteorological data such as temperature, 
humidity, wind speed and wind direction. In this case, all the above meteorological data were 
provided in real time through the National Observatory of Athens (NOA).

5. Discussion 
 
 
From the above results, it is observed that the average concentration of ultrafine particles, in 
the winter period, in Agrinio is on the same level with much bigger cities, such as 
Manchester (27000 particles/cm3) and Vienna (26200 particles/cm3), while it is higher than 
London (22941 particles/cm3), Copenhagen (19224 particles/cm3), Helsinki (13750 
particles/cm3), Leipzig (17119 particles/cm3), Amsterdam (18260 particles/cm3), Singapore 
(15850 particles/cm3) and Thessaloniki (23162 particles/cm3) (Voigtländer et al., 2006; 
Wåhlin, 2009; von Bismarck-Osten et al., 2013; Kumar et. al., 2014; Vouitsis et al., 2015). 
Furthermore, the mean values of nighttime measurements, in the North and West spots of 
Agrinio (42841 ± 9965 and 44612 ± 10776 particles/cm3 respectively) were in the same level 
with capital cities such as Rome (46799 particles/cm3) and Beijing (38400 particles/cm3), 
while it was higher than Toronto (33867 particles/cm3) and Berlin (28000 particles/cm3), 
cities with heavy industry and several million people (Paatero et al., 2005; Beckerman et al., 
2008; Westerdahl et al., 2009; Birmili et al., 2013). 
 
In a previous study of ultrafine particles (Puustinen et al., 2007) it was concluded that the 
average concentration value in Athens was 20276 particles/cm3, while in this case it was 
found to be 20331 particles/cm3. Although, the measurements were carried out only in one 
suburb for a period of three days, the ultrafine particles concentration is on the same level 
with the previous study. Regarding the additional measurements in Nafpaktos and Thiva, 
they gave a rough view about the concentration of particles in these two cities, since it was 
the first attempt to measure the ultrafine particles in both places. The obtained results are 
not able to give safe conclusions, since the findings are based on only one measurement. In 
order to have safer results, more measurements are needed. 
 
Based on the recommended limits regarding the exposure of ultrafine particles in the 
environment by the Danish Ecological Council, it is observed that all the measured places 
are above the limits except from Thiva. Τhe daily average concentration of Thiva is within the 
limits, both in yearly and hourly average, however these low values occurred from the high 
wind speeds which enhanced the particles dispersion and consequently the good air mixing. 
On the other hand, all the daily average values of Agrinio, Nea Ionia and Nafpaktos are 
above from the yearly average limit (7000 particles/cm3).   
 
The Greek government in order to deal with the extensive wood burning across the country, 
issued in January 2014 a Joint Ministerial Decision. According to this, in days where the 
limits for concentrations of PM10 are exceeded, there will be a discount of 70% on the cost 
of domestic electricity, while in some cases they will provide electricity with no charge at 
those who are in need, as a motivation for citizens to use electricity for heating their homes 
(Papamanolis, 2015). Moreover, the Ministry of Environment and Energy provides guidelines 
and tv spots in order to aware people about the smog phenomenon and how dangerous it is 
for their health.

Additional findings 
 
Taking a closer look to the obtained results from Agrinio, it is observed that apart from the 
center, the east spot appears the lowest mean value among the measuring spots in the 
nighttime, although it is a high populated area. The hypothesis on this, is that low emissions 
occurred from the fact that there are many new house buildings in this area (constructed 
earlier than seven years ago) and probably they have fireplaces with better energy 
performance and more efficient filters. Indeed, after a literature review it was found that since 
2010 the “Energy Performance Buildings Regulation” (in Greek: KENAK) is in effect. This 
regulation aims at the sustainable design and construction in buildings in order to improve 
their energy efficiency, their savings and to protect and preserve the natural environment 
(Papamanolis, 2015).  
 
An additional finding is that the buses of Agrinio are responsible for the highest peaks of 
ultrafine particles concentration, which usually exceed the 200000 particles/cm3. Most of 
them, occurred in the walking path from north to west spot in the afternoon measurements of 
Day 3, Day 4 and Day 7. Namely, in Day 3, two buses passed by causing peaks of 500000 
and 304000 particles/cm3 respectively, in Day 4 there is a recorded value of 239000 
particles/cm3, while in Day 7 the particles concentration peaked at 219000 particles/cm3. 
Other places that the local buses influenced the particles concentration were in the walking 
path from east spot to city center, in Day 4, causing a peak of 196000 particles/cm3 and in 
the walking path from west to south spot, in Day 6, resulting in a peak of 238000 
particles/cm3.   
 
Unlike the situation in Agrinio, both in Athens and in Nafpaktos the buses caused lower 
increase in particles concentration. Searching about this, it was found that in Agrinio as of 
January 2017, there are 21 buses aimed at local services. From those 21 buses, eight of 
them belong in Euro 1 category, according the European Union division on the vehicle's 
emissions (Appendix 1), seven are under Euro 2, one is in Euro 3 and five are in Euro 4. In 
particular, it was found that three buses were manufactured before 1984 and eight before 
1995, which means that they are at least 30 and 20 years old respectively, while all of them 
are diesel powered. On the other hand, in Athens Metropolitan Area it was found that there 
are 2022 buses in total; 228 belong in Euro 1 category, 869 are in Euro 2, 401 are in Euro 3, 
220 are in Euro 4, 100 are in Euro 5 and four double - deck buses are in Euro 5 as well, 
while there are 200 new buses which called EEV (Enhanced Environmentally friendly 
Vehicles). Out of the total of 2022 above thermal buses, 610 are powered by natural gas and 
1412 are diesel powered (OASA, 2010).     
 
Another issue that results in high concentrations of ultrafine particles is the extensive use of 
motorbikes, especially the old ones. High recorded values of ultrafine particles due to this 
reason were recorded in all the measured locations. The utilization of motorcycles is usual 
for Greek citizens, since motorbikes are more favorable in South Mediterranean countries 
due to the relatively mild weather during the winter and their low cost. The problem with this 
is that most of them are not equipped with catalytic converters and they have “old 
technology” (Kassomenos et al., 2008), especially the mopeds (<50cc), resulting in high 
emissions. Furthermore, motorbikes are the most important polluters for non-methane 
organic compounds (Symeonidis et al., 2004). According to Ntziachristos et al. (2009) in 
==PAGE:86==

Greece, in 2005, the annual PM emissions from motorbikes were 852 tons, while the 
respective value for passenger cars was 387 tons.   
 
Recommendations 
 
Dealing with ultrafine particles is not an easy task and their elimination is impossible, 
however their mitigation and prevention are achievable targets. The Greek government and 
the Ministry of the Environment and Energy should act properly on the mitigation of wood 
burning and minimization of ultrafine particles through biomass combustion. One measure 
has already been adopted (70% discount on “dangerous” days), however it is not enough 
since many families can not even afford the discounted price in electricity. Therefore, more 
effective measures are needed. A general discount during the cold days and especially 
during the night hours (not only on “dangerous” days), might be a solution and the Greek 
government along with the Greek energy providers should work closely on that way. 
Furthermore, recently due to pressures from European Union Commission, the Greek 
government cut down the Heating allowances, consequently even less families are now 
entitled for them.  
 
Another measure, that had been suggested in 2013 but never came as a framework, is the 
subsidy for the conversion of conventional fireplaces into energy efficient. As observed 
through this study, the area which is consisted of many new buildings (East spot), has the 
lowest ultrafine particles concentration during the night hours. Hence, the conversion of old 
fireplaces with more sustainable ones or the replacement of old filters, might have a positive 
impact in the minimization of particles concentration. 
 
Further research 
 
Overall, for better conclusions regarding the concentration of ultrafine particles in Greece 
more measurements will be needed across the country, both during the cold and warm 
months of the year. So far, particles measurements in Greece were only carried out in scale 
of PM2.5 and higher, hence the measurements of ultrafine particles are limited. Moreover, 
an analysis on particles nature and their traces is required in order to verify that the 
increased particles concentration during the night hours is due to wood burning, as 
happened in the researches of Saffari et al. (2013) and Florou et al. (2017) who found 
increased traces of Levoglucosan, Mannosan and Galactosan, which are indicators of 
emissions from wood and biomass burning.

6. Conclusions 
 
 
A first attempt on measuring the ultrafine particles concentration in a Greek medium sized 
city was made through this study. The framework was to verify that in the nighttime, during 
the cold season of the year, there is an increase of ultrafine particles due to wood burning. 
For this reason, measurement campaigns were conducted in Agrinio, a medium sized city, 
both during day and night. Additional measurements were also conducted in three more 
cities, one bigger (Athens) and two smaller (Nafpaktos and Thiva) so as to test that the 
above hypothesis was happening and in other cities as well. Furthermore, two more single 
measurements were carried out in the port of Piraeus and in Acropolis, in places with high 
and low expected values of particles respectively. 
 
The results of this study indicate that the hypothesis is verified. The average daytime 
concentration of ultrafine particles in Agrinio is 14817 ± 8760 particles/cm3, while the 
respective night value is 35079 ± 10112 particles/cm3. The same conclusion arose through a 
diurnal measurement that was conducted in the center of Agrinio. Increased ultrafine 
particles in the evening were observed in the other locations as well (Athens, Nafpaktos and 
Thiva). Moreover, in the additional measurements of Piraeus port there were high particles 
values, while in Acropolis their level was low as expected.  
 
Most of the time, the strong winds resulted in lower particles concentrations, while in some 
other cases, especially during the night hours, the wind enhanced the particles 
concentration, depending on its direction and where the source of pollution (chimney) was 
located. As additional findings, it was observed that newer house buildings emitted lower 
ultrafine particles. Furthermore, emissions from local buses are responsible for the highest 
concentrations in Agrinio, while in all the measurement locations motorbikes emitted high 
particles concentrations. 
  
Concerning the mean ultrafine particles concentration in Agrinio during the wintertime is 
24948 particles/cm3 and is on the same level with capital cities with heavy industry and 
several million citizens. Dealing with ultrafine particles is not an easy task and their 
elimination is impossible, however their mitigation and prevention are achievable targets. 
The Greek government should act properly on the mitigation of wood burning aiming at 
effective measures. For better conclusions regarding the concentration of ultrafine particles 
in Greece further measurements will be needed across the country, both during the cold and 
warm months of the year.
