Abstract 
Intumescent coatings as passive fire protection materials are increasingly being used in steel 
constructions because of their advantages, as for example: light weight, good aesthetics, they take no 
space and can be applied off-site. The current design is based on the ISO834 ‘standard fire curve’ and 
it underlines that thermal properties of the intumescent coating can be taken independently in what 
regards the fire conditions. The current study aimed at highlighting that the thermal resistance is 
dependent on the fire scenario and in particular on the heating rate. In serving this purpose, two sets of 
experiments were conducted. The first set was conducted in an electric oven and it highlighted the 
development of the thermal resistance at low heating rates, and the second one took place in a gas 
furnace where the “ISO834” fire curve was used as fire exposure. The used steel profiles were IPE400 
having a DFT of 1mm of commercial intumescent paint. The development of the thermal resistance 
respects a complex pattern which was described in an accurate way by dividing it into relevant phases, 
on the basis of what had been done in a previous DTU project. Building upon those results, a 
mathematical model has been developed, which establishes a relation between the steel temperature 
and the three values of the thermal resistance assessed in the different phases of its variation during the 
heating process. Precise criteria has been defined in order to assess the different values of the thermal 
resistance for the different phases on the basis of the heating rate. A good match of the behavior of the 
paint is obtained by using the model, which has been validated in a range of 3-95℃/min. Ultimately 
the findings in the current study prove the dependency of the thermal resistance on the heating rate, 
thus highlighting the limit of the current design procedure that does not take into account a realistic fire 
exposure. The study also proposes an alternative design procedure that can be easily integrated in the 
current one, by using a series of well identifiable values of the thermal resistance.

1. Introduction 
 
The current chapter presents a literature survey on fire protection engineering and fire protection 
systems, both applied on steel structures. From the vast field of fire protection, the current thesis is 
focused on studying the behavior of steel elements protected by intumescent coatings under different 
fire conditions. 
The aim of the literature survey is to give the reader a general overview of the different topics 
concerning the intumescent coatings, and also provide a vital background in this field. This aspect has 
an increased importance because the reader should be able to understand all the different terms and 
principles presented in the following chapters. 
The current chapter also presents the motivation and the objective of the thesis, together with the 
method that was used to achieve the desired goal.  
1.1. History of intumescent paint 
 
Even since fire was used as a light source and also as a heat source, the concept of fire protection had 
an important meaning for the human being. Unfortunately, the concept of fire protection engineering 
raised concerns only after huge catastrophes occurred, and so the people of that time knew that some 
measures had to be taken in order to avoid future disasters.   
The first measure in what regards fire engineering was taken in the year 64 AD, when Emperor Nero 
had some regulations drawn up; they would enforce the usage of fireproofing materials in the exterior 
walls. This measure can be considered as being the first of its kind, in which the science and 
engineering of that day were used to put in practice the nowadays called fire protection engineering [1]. 
The collapse of the Roman Empire marked a significant break in what regards the evolution of the fire 
protection, and it was only in the 17th century, during the Renaissance, that a technical approach to fire 
protection appeared to be of interest. In the year 1666, after the Great London fire, London adopted the 
first building regulations, in which the usage of fire-resisting partition walls should be used for stone 
and brick houses. 
Throughout the Industrial Revolution (18th century), new industrial processes and material storage 
practices emerged, and so the fire hazards got to a new level. The fire engineers of that time were put in 
a difficult position, and they had to find useful solutions for the newly emerged problems. In the first 
half of the 20th century the fire protection engineering was based on the building and fire codes of that 
time, these standards were obtained on the basis of previous disasters. During the second half of the 
20th century, fire protection engineering as a unique engineering profession was born. With this new

Entering the 21st century, continuity in the improvement of the evaluation of the fire protection through 
computational methods can be noticed. The new computational methods include: fire severity, fire 
properties of materials such as heat release rates, fire spread, smoke development, smoke movement, 
egress flow, sprinkler and detector responses. These methods, together with the computational power

1.2. Fire protection 
 
The continuing economic prosperity and population increase in the urban areas point towards a future 
with increased activity in high-rise construction of residential and office buildings. High-rise buildings 
become economically attractive when the structural engineers have a comprehensive understanding of 
the structural behavior of the different systems, and also a practical sense of the construction problems 
[25]. 
When talking about high-rise buildings, steel is considered as being the preferred material. This choice 
is obvious due to the high strength of the material, fact which facilitates the design of slim structures 
with a relatively low self-weight. This feature makes it a great material also from an architectural point 
of view; when designing structures, the impression of lightweight and airiness are always desired. The 
mentioned architectural feature is also mandatory when designing airport terminals, exhibition halls 
and so on [26]. 
In what concerns steel constructions, without any fire protection measure, the overall collapse of the 
entire structure might be induced rapidly when the surrounding temperatures are up to 800℃-1000℃ 
[27, 28].  
The protection of metals against fire has become an important issue in the construction industry. Steel, 
a non-combustible material with good ductility, begins to lose its structural properties above 450℃ 
when exposed to fire, and due to this reason it tends to distort, and so it leads to the collapse of the 
entire structure [29]. Prevention of the structural collapse of the building in order to ensure the safe 
evacuation of the people from inside the building, is deemed as being the most important requirement 
in building regulations in many countries [30].

1.3. Intumescent Coating 
1.3.1. History and market trends 
 
Intumescent coatings as passive fire protection materials are increasingly being used in steel 
constructions because of their advantages, as for example: light weight, good aesthetics, they take no 
space and can be applied off-site [5]. The architectural benefits are not the primary factors to be taken 
into account when choosing to use intumescent coatings, but the fact that they are designed to perform 
under severe conditions and to maintain the steel integrity between one and three hours when the 
temperature of the surroundings is in excess of 1100℃ [34, 35]. 
Besides the applicability in structural engineering, usage of intumescent coatings has also been 
recorded in military vehicles, the aircraft industry (on components like aviation fuel pumps, hydraulic 
landing gear, cabin interior parts and so on), and also on special structures as oil rigs[40, 41].  
According to [41], the intumescent coatings can be divided into two main categories: 
- 
Category I – Cellulosic fire protection was developed mainly for civil structures. The 
intumescent coatings belonging to this category are called “thin film” coatings, the standard 
thickness being recorded as 1 mm. 
- 
Category II – Hydrocarbon fire protection was developed for buildings situated into a high-risk 
environment, for example oil rigs and refineries. The coatings from this category are called 
“thick film” coatings and their thickness can be anything from 3 to 20 mm. 
Further details about a cellulosic fire and a hydrocarbon fire will be presented in subchapter “1.5.  Fire 
curves”.

1.3.2. Activation and functionality 
 
The coatings, which usually are composed of inorganic components contained in a polymer matrix, are 
inert at ambient temperatures. However, when their temperature raises up to 280-350 ℃, an 
endothermic reaction occurs, which causes the combustion of the coating surface and its expansion. 
The heat absorbed by the reaction partly contributes to the insulating effect of the expanded coating, 
which is mostly due to the decrement of density consequent to the increment of volume and to the 
porosity of the char structure; both lowering the thermal conductivity of the original material [6, 7].  
Therefore unlike inert insulating materials such as gypsum and vermiculite panels, which have a 
constant thickness and density and a thermal resistance (from this point onwards it will be referred to 
as T.R.) directly derivable from the material conductivity, the T.R. of intumescent coatings is difficult 
to assess, as it depends on the development of the reaction, which in turn is affected by the heating 
process, coating and substrate thickness [8], as well as the shape of the steel profile protected by the 
coating [9].

As shown in figure 5, when exposed to flames or radiation, in general, the intumescent coating respects 
the following steps [17]: 
- 
At the incipient stage of heating, the coating absorbs a large amount of thermal energy, this fact 
resulting in the rapid increase in temperature in the intumescent coating; 
- 
At the point where the intumescent coating reaches a critical temperature, a viscous fluid is 
formed by the melted polymer matrix. Usually, at temperatures between 100℃ ÷ 250℃, the 
inorganic acid source is subjected to thermal decomposition; 
- 
A large amount of gas is released at the moment in which the blowing agent (within the 
intumescent coating) undergoes decomposition; some parts of the released gas are trapped 
inside the molten matrix. This phenomena occurs at temperatures between 280℃ ÷ 350℃; 
- 
The formation of char is possible due to the fact that the molten fluid hardens and releases 
residual volatile. 
As the current building fire resistance design is based mostly on the ‘standard fire conditions’ 
(ISO834), the research studies in the literature concerning the performance of intumescent coatings 
exposed to different fire conditions has only a small number of reports. The estimation of the effective 
thermal conductivity of the intumescent char is a procedure developed by Anderson et al. [14]. The 
mentioned procedure was based on a heat transfer analysis of the temperature-time data, which is 
obtained from one-dimensional experiments (see figure 5) of coated steel coupons exposed to fire 
environments, usually using aviation-type fuels. 
1.4. Shortcomings of the current design process, project objective 
 
Nowadays, if a customer wants to use intumescent coatings in order to protect his/her structure against 
the harmful effect of a fire, he/she will talk to a certified manufacturer, and the person representing the 
intumescent coating company will provide tables which will present requirements for the thickness of 
7 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
the coatings. The thickness of the coating will ensure that the covered structural member will not reach 
a temperature which would compromise the structural safety for a certain amount of time. 
The design practice mentioned above presents some faulty aspects which can become dangerous for a 
structure if treated lightly. As mentioned above, the current design is based on the ISO834 ‘standard 
fire curve’, which means that if the design is made in the same way for another type of fire, the results 
cannot be considered valid. The current design underlines that the thermal properties of the 
intumescent coating can be taken independently in what regards the fire conditions. This assumption is 
not true and because the intumescent coating works on the basis of a reaction, it is natural to assume 
that the fire conditions will have a great impact on the activation and functionality of the paint. 
As the critical temperature will be reached at a certain point, the current design is based on moving the 
point forward in time, and so delaying the structural failure of the protected element. This assumption 
simplifies the design in the way in which the evolution of the temperature throughout the entire process 
is consider to follow a pre-established pattern (ISO834), but in what concerns different types of fires, 
the design lacks insurance. 
A previous MSc thesis [9] carried out at DTU also mentioned these shortcomings and a method to take 
them into account was researched. In what concerns the T.R. of the intumescent coating, a variation 
was noted, and a division into well identifiable phases was tried. The phases were identified through a 
simple visual inspection, but for a more accurate description an analytical approach is required. 
The T.R. is defined as the ratio between the thickness of the paint and its thermal conductivity 
(“dp/λp”). As these two parameters can vary, it would not be correct to assume that there is a constant 
value for the T.R. 
On the basis of these previous experimental results [9] and of new tests carried out in the framework of 
this and a parallel MSc project [12], a numerical expression of the T.R. will be derived, which can be 
used for a more reliable design of steel profiles protected by intumescent coatings. 
This expression should account for the dependency of all main parameters affecting the behavior of the 
intumescent coating, identified by the previous and parallel experimental project above mentioned [9, 
12], but should also be simple enough to be easily integrated in current design procedures [13]. For this 
reason, an attempt will be made to define equivalent constant values of the T.R., which can be safely 
used between given temperature ranges and for given profile types and heating conditions. 
With other words, the aim of the current thesis can be divided into two main objectives: 
- 
Identify the relevant values of the T.R. from the experiments 
- 
Relate the found values to the H.R. and see if it is possible to assess the values by not relying on 
the experiments

1.5. Method of reaching the desired objective 
 
A set of fire tests will be performed with the aim of identifying the main parameters that have an effect 
on the T.R. of the intumescent coating. Some of these parameters have been identified in previous 
works, such as the intumescent coating thickness, the steel profile type and the heating rate (from this 
point onwards it will be referred to as H.R.) [9]. However, the extent of the influence that these 
parameters have on the T.R. is not yet clear. The tests will therefore be aimed at investigating the 
sensitivity of the T.R. to the variation of these parameters. To this aim, the tests conducted in a parallel 
MSc project at DTU [12] will also be considered. 
Current simplified design procedures of steel profiles protected by intumescent coatings are based on a 
nominal value of the T.R., derived on the basis of standard fire tests for different coating thickness 
[10]. However, real fires differs from the standard monotonic ISO834 fire curve in several respects, 
such as H.R., cooling rate, and incident radiation. These aspects, as well as the dependency on the steel 
and profile type, are not accounted for in current design procedures, resulting in a possible unsafe 
behaviour of steel structures involved in a fire scenario. 
The most relevant parameters will be selected and used to attempt an expression describing their 
dependency on the T.R. of the profile. The correlation will be calibrated on the basis of the tests 
performed and of the literature results. In order to obtain an equivalent, constant value of the T.R. to be 
used in the design practice, attempts done in a previous work [9]  will be considered and extended to 
the consideration of a given time and type of fire exposure. It is envisaged that a set of design tables 
can be defined, providing a design value of the T.R. to be used for given fire conditions and profile 
types. 
Two distinct sets of experiments were conducted. The first set was conducted in an electric oven and it 
had the purpose of showing the development of the T.R. for H.R. smaller than the “ISO834 standard 
fire curve” [10]. A second set of experiments was conducted in the gas furnace and it had the purpose 
to present the development of the T.R. for the “ISO834 Standard fire curve” [10].  
The results of the experiments highlight a variation of the T.R. of the steel profile. The development of 
the T.R. can be easily divided into four main phases, which describe the behaviour of the intumescent 
coating. A more detailed approach will be needed to correctly define the phases from an analytical 
point of view, the approach should yield valid results in all the considered H.R. 
A detailed investigation will be conducted in order to identify the relevant values of the T.R. from the 
experimental data and to see how they vary with respect to the H.R. The next step will be to discover 
how the newly found values can be related to the H.R. and eventually find a way through which they 
can be assessed without the need of the experiments. These values could then be implemented in the 
current design by means of the formula from the Eurocode [13].

2. Heat Transfer Models 
The current chapter contains the theoretical description of the heat transfer models taken for the 
different experiments having the purpose of investigating the behavior of the intumescent coating 
under different fire scenarios. 
In order to assess the fire resistance of a structural steel member involved in a fire scenario, a 
mathematical model of the heat transfer will be created.  The mathematical model is vital in the 
assessment of the fire resistance and the fact that is it relatively easy to use makes it a convenient 
solution. The model will validate or invalidate the data obtained from the conducted experiments. 
Even if the experiments were conducted into different environments (electric oven and gas furnace), 
the mathematical model remains the same. 
2.1. State of the art and practice 
 
In order to satisfy the requirements demanded by the building regulations, the structural integrity of a 
building has to be maintained for a specific period if involved in a fire event. Traditionally, the design 
codes for structural steelwork have adopted the prescriptive approach in what concerns the fire 
protection requirements, but recently some calculation methods [43, 44] have been introduced. 
The calculation methods are preoccupied with isolated elements and they are based mainly on the 
results of experimental studies. This aspect becomes more and more used because large-scale structures 
are rarely tested against fire, the reason being that there is a high cost involved and also that the 
standard furnaces have certain physical limitations [45]. 
The next step was the usage of artificial fire tests; standardized cribs of wood or coal were ignited and 
the impact on the structural elements was observed [46]. 
After the introduction of city gas, Woolson started applying gas furnaces at the University of Columbia 
[47, 48]. The usage of gas furnaces gave birth and helped the development of the standard temperature-
time fire curve, which is still widely applied. The curve has the purpose of replicating a severe fire in a 
building (dating from the 1900s); the fire conditions could be easily reproduced because they were not 
influenced by the weather or the quality of the fuel [49]. 
Even today, fire exposures are used to test building materials; the only issue is that there needs to be a 
high compatibility for all the standardized experiments conducted all around the world [3]. 
Such a curve can be found in the Eurocode [4], and has the name “ISO834 standard fire curve”.

It is relevant for intumescent coatings to divide the fire environments into two categories, namely 
hydrocarbon fires and cellulosic fires [50]. The two categories take their names from the fuel that is 
feeding the fire, so for a cellulosic fire the fuel would be cellulosic based (paper, wood and so on) and 
for a hydrocarbon fire, the encounter would be realised in the oil and gas industry [51].

A cellulosic fire can be divided into four main phases which describe its behavior throughout its entire 
existence, namely: ignition, a developing fire, a fully developed fire, and a decay phase [53]. 
The downside of the ISO 834 fire curve is that it is fixed and also that it has an infinite increase in 
temperature, aspects which do not relate in a good manner with certain design situations. As an 
example, when designing a steel connection against fire, effects as the high cooling strains in the joints 
induced by distortional deformation of the heated elements during the decay phase are ignored [54]. 
The demand is that a new temperature-time fire curve has to be found, and it has to take into account 
certain parameters as: compartment geometry, amount of fuel, thermal inertia of its boundaries etc. 
2.1.2. Parametric fire curves 
 
Besides the traditional standard fire curves, an ever increasing number of codes are including 
parametric fire curves. The concept of parametric fires provides a design method to approximate post-
flashover compartment fires. In comparison to the monotonic standard fire curve, a parametric fire 
estimates in a more realistic way the compartment temperature which should be used to design 
structural steel members against fire [55].  
As presented above, a standard fire curve is obtained through the usage of a formula (see equation 1) in 
which the input parameter is the time. The formula will generate a temperature-time curve which is 
dependent only on the mentioned parameter. The parametric fire curve is describe as well by a formula 
[49], but the curve takes into account the compartment size, fuel load, ventilation conditions and the 
thermal properties of compartment walls and ceilings [55]. 
The first handbook preoccupied with the design of steel structures against fire that took into account 
parametric fires was presented by Pettersson et al. [15]; factors as the opening factor and the thermal 
inertia of the compartment boundaries were for the first time implemented into a design procedure. The 
down part of the design method presented by [15] is that it had a limitation in what regards the 
compartment area (≤200m2), but the limitation was removed by Hertz [49]. 
Recently, CFD (computation fluid dynamics) calculations give engineers the possibility of modeling a 
fire development in more details, the only impediment is that the input data has to be assessed and 
agreed upon. 
As the earlier designs were based on the standard fire curve, a prescriptive-based design has dominated 
the field. With the implementation of the new computational models (implementation of parametric fire 
curves), a new level was reached, a wider array of compartment geometries could be accessed, 
facilitating even the design of more complex compartment geometries (from a fire safety point of view) 
which would please the architects.

3. Experimental Setup 
Moving from the previous thesis related to intumescent coatings [9], the experiments were conducted 
in a similar manner, but adjustments were needed. 
The usage of two different kinds of paint (for the purpose of the current project, the paints will be 
called Paint A and Paint B) was deemed essential. The idea behind this aspect was to check the 
behavior of two different intumescent coatings (which are currently on the market) for the same H.R., 
compare the results, and find a conclusion which suites both paints. 
Paint A is a water based paint, and paint B is a solvent based paint. 
The coating was applied by professional companies in order to avoid any error concerning the 
application of the paint by the unexperienced staff. This aspect brought the experiments even closer to 
real life scenarios, as the company would be in charge of painting the profiles for a paying customer. 
3.1. Components 
In what follows, the components involved in the experiments will be presented. Through trial and self-
study, the components were brought together and the yielded results were deemed as being correct. 
3.1.1. Setup of thermocouples 
The temperature measurement was obtained in all experiments by using type K thermocouples. The 
thermocouples have two wires, one made out of nickel and the other one out of chromium. The two 
wires are coated with an insulation material which has an exterior diameter of 1.6 mm. 
As it was stated by the company which provided the thermocouples, the maximum temperature at 
which they would work is 1260℃. The target temperature for the experiments was 1000℃, and so the 
thermocouples should yield good results without any problem.  
The two wires were welded by the students in charge with the experiments, and tests were performed 
for each thermocouple before placing it into the oven. In order to perform the test, a standard kitchen 
lighter and a computer were used. The thermocouple was connected to the computer and the welded 
end would be subjected to the flame produced by the lighter until the temperature reading will reach 
800℃. At this point, the test is done and the thermocouple is considered to be working properly. 
In order to safely place the thermocouples and avoid that they could detach during the test, small holes 
were drilled into the steel profile. The technique was developed in a previous project by Andersen J. 
[9] 
The holes were drilled using a column drilling machine with a diamond head. The device was provided 
by the university (D.T.U. campus, building 122,) and due to the simplicity with which the holes could 
be drilled, the entire drilling process was done by me. 
23 
 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
Figure 11 - Method of fixing the thermocouples into 
the profile 
As the exterior diameter of the thermocouples (including insulation) is 1.6 mm, the holes were drilled 
using a head of 1.4 mm. The reason for the smaller holes is that after removing the insulation from the 
end which is supposed to measure the temperature, the diameter will decrease. According to this 
principle, the thermocouples were properly fixed into the holes, limiting the possibility of an error. 
Most of the errors regarding thermocouples were recorded in the stage in which the steel profile is 
fixed on its position into the oven. Pulling the thermocouples out of the oven through the exhaust holes 
(in order to connect them to the computer) has proven to be the main reason for which the 
thermocouples would come out of the holes. 
The depth of the holes was set to 5 mm for the flanges and 4 mm for the web. The web has a thickness 
of 8.6 mm and the flanges have a thickness of 13.5 mm (both excluding the paint). Even if the holes are 
not so deep as to reach the middle of the steel, it is considered that the variation in the temperature is 
insignificant when taking into account the one or two remaining millimeters.   
As mentioned above, the protective coating of the thermocouples is removed from one end and placed 
into the hole. After this step is completed, the thermocouple is fixed in place with the help of ceramic 
glue. 
The glue has an insurance (provided by the producer), that it will keep its properties until 1100℃. For 
the experiments which have a target temperature on 1000℃, this solution is considered adequate. 
There are two main disadvantages in what regards the usage of ceramic glue. One of them is that after 
it was applied, it should rest for at least 12 hours in order to 
reach the optimal state. This aspect is highly time consuming, 
and the experiments had to be prepared one day in advance. The 
second major inconvenience is that after it has dried out, it 
becomes very brittle. This second aspect is very important 
mostly when the profile is moved and set in place for the 
experiment. 
For example, if a thermocouple would be pulled out, then the 
process regarding the ceramic glue would have to be repeated, 
and so the time loss would be significantly higher.

3.2. Experiments conducted in the electric oven 
 
The data used for the current thesis was obtained from two distinct experimental setups (see figure 8): a 
part of the experiments conducted in the electric oven and another part from the experiments conducted 
in the gas furnace. The part regarding the electric oven (D.T.U. campus, Fire Lab, building 122) will be 
explained in what follows. 
The inner chamber of the electric oven has steel wires along all the interior sides. As the electricity 
surges through these wires, they will heat up and the radiative heat produced by them will be 
transferred to all the objects placed inside.

The oven has a controller which enables the manipulation of the end temperature, and so different 
temperature-time curves can be programmed into its software. 
3.2.1. Electric oven 
 
 
The electric oven is made by Scandia (model IW500) and it can heat up to 1000℃ with a maximum 
theoretical H.R. of 16.67 ℃/min. The oven is also equipped with a trap door which can close the 
exhaust orifice. While performing the experiments, the trap door was slightly open so that the 
accumulation of gases will not cause a dangerous experimental environment (some sample presented 
ignition, which together with the dangerous gases can lead to devastating results). The main reasons for 
the low H.R. are due to the open orifice and due to the hood, as it is vacuuming all the heat from the 
oven. 
The fact that the electric oven cannot replicate the ISO834 standard fire curve should not be treated as a 
minus. Slower H.R. would expose the steel profile to a new array of fire scenarios, variation of the 
H.R. and also the exposure length are factors which are of interest. 
The position for the steel element can be seen in figure 15. Measurements were made in order to obtain 
a centered position for the steel elements, also a special care was given to the height at which the 
profile will be standing (the amount of bricks used for the elevation). So the profile is equally distanced 
from all the oven walls.

Figure 16 provides a good overview on the points where the thermocouples were placed. Two more 
thermocouples were used (number 6 and number 7), and they were on both sides of the web with equal 
distances from the flanges and also equal distances regarding the height. The use of two thermocouples 
for the oven temperature is required. The reason is that if the two values are in an acceptable error 
range, the data regarding the oven temperature can be considered valid, and the average between the 
two can give a good overview of the behavior of the temperature in the oven throughout the entire 
experiment. 
In order to keep the thermocouples responsible for measuring the oven temperature in place, they 
would be tied to the thermocouples which are fixed in the steel profile, and the measuring end will be 
facing outwards (from the profile). The reason for which they were tied (see figure 19) is that due to 
the extreme heat, the thermocouples would lose their stiffness and just follow the natural gravitational 
direction. This aspect is seen as a problem as the “oven thermocouples” can end up into the expanding 
paint, and so the measurements are rendered as being incorrect. 
The measurement for the temperature was realized each 10 seconds (∆𝑡𝑡= 10𝑠𝑠). The Eurocode [13] 
states that the time step should not be more than 30 seconds, so the considered time step is valid.

same way. 
An approach to assess the values of the H.R. was made, and namely, the last measured temperature 
divided by the amount of time it was needed to reach that temperature. The only problem with the 
30 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
 
mentioned assessment is that if the temperature-time curve is not linear, the assumption cannot be 
considered as being accurate. 
In order to assess the correct values of the H.R., a division was made. Two intervals will be considered: 
- 
From 20⁰C to 400⁰C 
- 
From 20⁰C to 700⁰C 
In both cases, the data from the experiments will be divided into small intervals of 1500 seconds (25 
minutes) each. An average will be computed for each of the new intervals resulting in an array of H.R. 
After applying the average function for the newly found data, the H.R. will be provided; the only 
remaining step is to set the end point of the interval (400⁰C or 700⁰C). 
By using this process, the values of the H.R. will account for the different slopes and when using a 
H.R. to compare certain points, an increased precision is provided. 
For example: after extracting the value of the activation point of the paint from an experiment, a trial 
will be made to define that point as a function of the H.R. The activation takes place normally before 
the steel reaches 300⁰C, and so the H.R. belongs to the “20⁰C to 400⁰C” interval. 
3.3. Experiments conducted in the gas furnace 
 
The second data set was obtained from the experiments conducted in the gas furnace (DBI Fire 
Laboratory). The steel samples (profiles) will be subjected to the ISO834 cellulosic standard fire curve. 
The main difference between the two experimental setups consists of the fact that the gas furnace is 
able to generate a sufficiently high H.R. and through this the reproduction of the ISO 834 cellulosic 
standard fire curve [10]. The main issue in reproducing the desired curve was generated by the high 
temperature increase in the first 30-35 minutes of the experiment. 
By analyzing the data obtained from the current experiments (from the gas furnace), a better 
understanding of the behavior of the intumescent coating has been provided. A new fire scenario has 
been considered as the fire curves generated by the gas furnace will differ in a lot of aspects from the

meters, a depth of 1.5 meters and a width of 1.5 meters.

The four identified phases are: the inert phase, the transient phase, the steady phase, and the post-
austenitization phase. The division was first presented by Andersen J.[9] but only from a visual 
inspection. An addition was brought to the mentioned phase division, namely a mathematical model

The inert phase represents the incipient phase of the heating process; the intumescent paint did not 
reach its reaction temperature. The transient phase is the phase that defines the formation of the char. 
The steady phase is identified as being the phase during which the T.R. is kept at a constant level. The 
40 
 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
post-austenitization phase is the phase that shows the behavior of the steel after the change in the 
crystal structure (from ferrite to austenite).  
4.1.1. Inert phase 
 
This phase presents the transition from an inactive coating to an active one. The inert phase ends at the 
point where the intumescent coating reaches approximately 280℃. During this phase, the intumescent 
coating does not have any enhanced thermal protection; it behaves just like an ordinary paint. 
From this point of view, in the current phase, the measured temperature of the coated steel is 
comparable (but not equal) to the measured temperature of the unprotected steel. 
4.1.2. Transient phase 
 
As the intumescent coating approaches the temperature of 280℃, the blowing agent within the coating 
decomposes and a large amount of gas is released (endothermic reaction). Gas bubbles are emitted into 
the top layer of the intumescent coating, which due to the increased viscosity permits the gas bubbles to 
penetrate it. The char structure is created in the time of this process, replacing the intumescent coating. 
At the point in which the blowing agent is finished, the reaction will end, and a decline in the T.R. 
starts. The peak point of the T.R. marks the fully developed char structure, which means that the 
reaction is over and that there is no more virgin material left (see figure 5). 
4.1.3. Steady phase 
 
The steady phase is characterized by a constant value of the T.R. The char structure has exhausted all 
its combustibles, and the only T.R. at this point is provided by the white multicellular char. 
In this phase, the two temperature increments (measured steel temperature and measured oven 
temperature) are the same (the temperature-time curves are parallel). 
If the white char structure presents any cracks (the heat can pass through without being hindered), the 
measured steel temperature increment will rapidly increase until the temperature in the steel is the same 
as the temperature in the oven. 
4.1.4. Austenitization  
 
In the steady phase, a local decrease in the T.R. can be seen (see figure 26). The decrease culminates 
with a minimum value of the T.R., after which an increase with the exact same amount as the previous 
decrease is noted. The increase stops at the exact same value of T.R. as before the decrease. When the 
T.R. is at its minimum value, a slight increase in the increment of the measured steel temperature can 
be noted, ending the parallel trend of the increments.

4.1.5. Post-austenitization 
 
The last phase in called ‘post-austenitization’; the name is suggestive, so this phase initiates after the 
austenitization of the steel. 
In the current thesis, this phase is not studied in detail, the main reason consisting of some uncertainties 
which are caused by certain errors (paint application errors, experimental setup errors, etc.). 
As seen in figure 26, after the point of austenitization is reached, the T.R. shows an increase, but not 
more than the values presented in the steady phase. The reason for this phenomenon is that the char is 
still protecting the steel profile even after the molecular transformation of the steel. 
At this point the char is fully developed (has a white color) and extremely brittle. One touch or even a 
gentle blow of air can remove the protective layer, or only parts of it. This level of fragility of the char 
structure proves that the carbon binder (the binder which gives the cohesion of the char structure) has 
been combusted [9].

maximum curvature in this interval.

4.2.2. Two values model 
 
The second method used for describing the intumescent process is based on the usage of two distinct 
values for the T.R. The previous method did not take into account the activation point of the 
intumescent coating, fact which was proven to be a downside and it deemed the method as being 
inaccurate. 
48 
 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
From the above mentioned basis, the second method will divide the intumescent process into two 
significant intervals: 
- 
Pre-activation phase of the paint 
- 
Post-activation phase of the paint 
As their names suggest, the first interval ends at the point where the paint reacts and the second one 
starts with the activation of the intumescent coating. The description of the overall process will be done 
in the same way as for the first method (using equation 14), but two distinct values of the equivalent 
T.R. will have to be found so that the behavior is described more accurately.  
 
Figure 33 - Graphical representation of the two significant phases that define the current method 
For the first interval (the pre-activation of the paint) the task is slightly easier because of the reduced 
amount of changes that the coating is subjected to. Until the activation of the paint, the intumescent 
coating behaves just like a regular paint. The usage of the unprotected steel temperature-time curve for 
the current interval is considered as being inaccurate; the reason is that even if the paint does not react 
it still protects the steel. Even if the provided protection is small, it cannot be neglected and for an 
accurate assessment it has to be taken into account. 
In order to have a better behavior of the temperature-time curve for the pre-activation phase, an 
iteration process was conducted. The iteration process is considered as being the best solution for the

is 0.04 m2k/W. The previously mentioned value is fixed and it was chosen because it presented the 
smallest deviation between the theoretical model and the experimental one. 
So as a conclusion it will be stated that the temperature-time curve used for the current phase (the pre-
activation phase) is described by the usage of equation 14 and an equivalent constant value of the T.R.

4.2.4. Conclusion regarding the three different methods 
 
As a conclusion to the chapter responsible for the presentation of the three methods which were used to 
assess the equivalent T.R. value (or values), it can be stated that the third one (paragraph 4.2.3.) is the 
most accurate.  
When defining the behavior of a steel element in a fire scenario (by using equation 14) a temperature-
time fire curve will be generated. From all the different parameters one stands out as being of a vital 
concern, namely the T.R. The assessment of this parameter was made in several ways, starting from a 
simple value and going to a series of values which take into account certain phases. 
In subchapter 4.2.1. a single value of the equivalent T.R. was implemented in the above mentioned 
formula and the results were unsatisfactory, even unsafe. 
When trying the divide to fire scenario into two phases (pre- and post- activation), a good match was 
found for the pre-activation phase, but unsatisfactory results were found for the second phase. Trying 
to apply only one value for the equivalent T.R. for the post-activation phase (the transient phase + the 
steady phase + the post-austenitization phase) was proven to be inaccurate, as only one value of the 
equivalent T.R. is unable to account for the high variation in the transient phase and also the constant 
behavior in the steady phase. Moving from the current aspects, a third approach was needed. The 
approach would divide the entire process into three main phases (the pre-activation, the post-activation, 
the post-transient), which would account for all the variations in the behavior of the steel elements. 
The three phases approach is based on the usage of three distinct value for the equivalent T.R., each 
belonging to one of the phases. An increased accuracy was obtained by using this approach and in what 
follows, an attempt will be made to define all the relevant points (which describe the behavior of the 
fire curve) without the usage of the experimental results. 
An essential aspect will be highlighted, namely that the temperature-time fire curves obtained by using 
equation 14 and the equivalent T.R. provided by the manufacturers has yielded unsatisfactory results 
throughout all the conducted experiments. The curves yield smaller temperatures then the actual ones 
and so, it can be concluded that using the mentioned values as an equivalent T.R. is unsafe as the 
temperatures are underestimated throughout the entire intumescent process.

4.4. Implementation into the design procedure of the points of interest 
 
The current chapter will present the design procedure which can be used to assess the temperature-time 
fire curve for the steel element. 
4.4.1. Procedure 
 
Equation 14 will be used for defining the fire curve throughout the entire process. The only parameter 
which will change (with time) will be the equivalent T.R., and the change will take place from one 
interval to the other in the way that a constant value of the equivalent T.R. will be generated and 
implemented for the corresponding interval. 
The first step is to establish the intervals. The activation point will be computed (see paragraph 4.3.1.) 
and its temperature value will mark the end point of the first phase (the pre-activation phase). 
The second point of interest is the end point of the second phase (the post-activation phase). The time 
value will be computed (see paragraph 4.3.2.) and so the two points that define the three phases are 
computed. 
The next step is to find the values of the equivalent T.R. for the three newly obtained intervals. 
The first interval will be defined by a constant value of the equivalent T.R. (for all the profiles) of 0.04 
m2k/W (see paragraph 4.2.2.).  
The second interval will be defined by the value of the equivalent T.R. called “the average T.R. for the 
transient phase” (see paragraph 4.3.3.). The value is obtained by following the two custom equations 
(23 and 24). 
The third interval will be characterized by the “average T.R. for the steady phase” (see 4.3.4.). The 
value for the equivalent T.R. will be obtained by using equation 25 and 26. 
By using these steps, the temperature-time fire curve for the steel element will be generated.  
In what follows, a subchapter will present the graphical representation of several profiles and the newly 
found procedure will be compared to the current design procedures.

5. Conclusion 
In order to understand the behavior of intumescent coatings and their performance, two different sets of 
experiments were conducted. Both sets used the same profile type (an IPE400 having a length of 400 
mm), but two distinct coatings: a water based intumescent coating called Paint A and a solvent based 
intumescent coating called Paint B. The paints are available on the market and they have been provided 
by their corresponding manufacturers. 
As the title of the current study suggests, the object of interest is the thermal resistance and the 
performance of the intumescent coatings have been be investigated by means of the thermal resistance. 
By collecting all the data from all the corresponding experiments and by processing it through the 
usage of the formulas from the Eurocode [13], the values of the thermal resistance were found. Despite 
the fact that the values of the thermal resistance were represented by distinct points, a clear trend could 
be established for both paint types regardless of the fire scenario. 
The distinct trend which describes the development of the thermal resistance of the intumescent coating 
throughout the entire fire scenario was divided into four main phases, divided by critical points. The 
division was firstly made in a previous work [9] but only through a visual inspection. One of the 
additions of the current project is that a mathematical model was developed (see paragraph 4.1.6.) for 
the above mentioned delimitation. The phases are called: the inert phase, the transient phase, the steady 
phase and the post-austenitization phase. 
As the functionality of an intumescent coating is highly based on the chemical reaction that needs to 
take place in order to render the paint efficient, a particular interest was also allocated to finding the 
activation points of the paint. In was found out that the activation temperatures for the two different 
paints were described by two narrow intervals, namely: for Paint A the activation takes place between 
280℃ and 295℃ and for Paint B it is 290℃ to 320℃, both for the electric oven. A small difference 
can be recorded as the activation temperature of the solvent based paint is slightly higher than for the 
water based paint, the reason could be the different chemical compositions. 
The current design procedure uses the Eurocode [13] design formula to construct the temperature-time 
fire curve for a steel element, by using key parameters from which the thermal resistance is one of 
them. The current design uses a constant value for the equivalent thermal resistance throughout the 
entire duration of the fire scenario. This value is either provided by the producer of the paint on the 
basis of the “ISO834 fire test” [10], either by the literature, but still on the basis of the “ISO834 fire 
test”. It was proven that the current design procedure is inaccurate and unsafe, as the temperature-time 
fire curves obtained through this model always yield smaller temperatures then the actual ones (see 
paragraph 4.2.1.). Moving from this deficiency, a new model was created (see paragraph 4.2.2.). 
The model was called “Two values model” and, as its name suggests, it is based upon the division into 
two parts of the thermal resistance around the point of activation of the paint. The development of the 
77 
 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
thermal resistance will be divided into two significant phases (pre-activation and post-activation). The 
model is based on the same equation as the current design procedure (Eurocode [13]), but for the two 
different phases it uses two distinct values for the equivalent thermal resistance The evolution of the 
thermal resistance is dependent on the heating rate (see chapter 4.5.) and so the custom values of the 
equivalent thermal resistance which are used for the two phases are also dependent on the heating rate. 
The fact that the activation temperature also varies with the heating rate strengthens the before stated 
affirmation. Even if the current model presents a more accurate behavior then the original one, it still 
yields some discrepancies from the measured data for heating temperatures that fall in the transient 
phase of the intumescent coating.  
In order to overcome this shortcoming, an improved model was developed, which took into account a 
division into three steps instead of just two. The division into the relevant phases was done as in the 
previously presented model, the addition consists in one more border which defined the end of the 
transient phase and beginning of the steady phase. Due to the high variability of the thermal resistance 
in the transient phase, this step is considered as being a great improvement. So with other words, three 
phases are now defined: the pre-activation phase, the post-activation phase and the post-transient phase 
(see paragraph 4.2.3.). The newly found model uses the same formula as the previous two, but for each 
separate phase it presents a different value for the equivalent thermal resistance 
The model yields really accurate results but at the cost of being complex. The model works a lot better 
than the current design procedure because it takes into account the variation of the thermal resistance 
throughout the entire duration of the fire scenario. The design values of the equivalent thermal 
resistance are calibrated in the basis of the tests done in the oven and the furnace with different heating 
rates. An attempt was made to relate these values to the heating rate, so that it is possible to use them in 
designing steel elements against a defined fire scenario. 
As the intumescent coating is highly dependent on the fire scenario (heating rate), a series of equations 
will be constructed as functions. The input parameter would be the heating rate, and the output 
parameter would be the desired point, or value of the thermal resistance. As presented in chapter 4.3. 
and chapter 4.4. of the current thesis, the functions yield really accurate results and the overall model 
works accordingly. The proposed model presents a limitation, in that the obtained functions are limited 
in what regards the values of the input parameter. It is not known whether, by using a different heating 
rate (smaller than 3℃/min or higher than 95℃/min), the obtained results would be accurate enough.  
Two attempts were made in order to assess the development of the thermal resistance for the two 
distinct paints: “thermal resistance development of the same intumescent coating subjected to different 
heating rate” and “thermal resistance development of the different intumescent coatings subjected to 
the same heating rate”, presented into detail in chapter 4.5.  
The two paint types presented two different trends in what regards the development of the thermal 
resistance, namely: on one hand Paint A (water base paint) presents an increase in the thermal 
resistance with a decrease in the heating rate, and on the other hand, Paint B (solvent based) presents an 
increase of the thermal resistance with an increase in the heating rate. The two trends are in essence 
different, fact which made it impossible to create a model that would account for both of them. This is 
78 
 
==PAGE:92==
 
 
the main reason for which the functions mentioned above are different from Paint A to Paint B (see 
paragraph 4.3.). 
Another key difference is recorded when comparing the behaviors of the two paints (the development 
of the thermal resistance) subjected to the same heating rate. It can be seen in detail in paragraph 4.5.2, 
that for all of the electric oven experiments, Paint A has a better behavior then Paint B by developing 
higher values for the thermal resistance. On the other hand, when referring to the gas furnace 
experiments, Paint B developed higher values of the thermal resistance then Paint A. This aspect is the 
second reason for which the two different paint types cannot be treated together and so a separate 
analysis is mandatory. 
So it can be concluded that the solvent based intumescent coating (Paint B) has a better performance in 
case of a severe fire (“the ISO834 fire curve”) while Paint A (the water based paint) is behaving better 
when the fire is less severe. For example the solvent based paint did not activate when the profile was 
subjected to a “Slow heating rate”, fact which can render it useless. 
As a final conclusion it will be stated that the current study has confirmed that the behavior of 
intumescent coatings is influenced in a decisive way by the heating rate. Additionally, the development 
of the thermal resistance is directly influenced by the chemical composition and significant differences 
were highlighted between water- and solvent- based coatings. 
Finally a modification to the EN formula [13] for assessing the steel temperature was proposed, which 
is based on the consideration of the three design values of the equivalent thermal resistance, which are 
defined on the basis of three distinct phases of the coating and can be assessed on the basis of the 
heating rate and the custom equations presented in chapter 4.3. 
5.1. Future studies 
 
First of all, all the conducted experiments from the current thesis should be conducted again, but by 
using different intumescent coatings. The new coatings should be available on the market and they 
should have different chemical compositions (one of them should be water based and the other should 
be solvent based). This set of experiments could validate or invalidate the current findings in what 
regards the different behaviors of the two different paints. In particular it would be interesting to see if 
one paint works better for high heating rates and the other for low heating rates, or if this trend is valid 
only for the two different paints used for this current thesis. 
The model found in the current thesis in highly dependent on the interval defined by the values of the 
heating rate. The interval could be expanded, and more experiments are required in order to make the 
model more solid. There is a significant difference between the heating rates obtained from the electric 
oven and the heating rate from the gas furnace. More experiments could be conducted for heating rates 
ranging from the highest heating rate obtained from the electric oven (the “Fast” heating rate) to the 
one obtained from the gas furnace (the “ISO834”). 
79 
 
==PAGE:93==
 
 
A particular resonance has also to be given to the custom equations obtained in paragraph 4.3. They are 
highly sensitive to the experimental data points which describe the equations.  
A further aspect which could require more attention is the emissivity of the paint. As the architects 
desire a different range of colors for their steel members, it is not known if the intumescent coating 
receives less thermal radiation if for example it is red or white. It would be of interest to see how the 
thermal resistance develops and to record the temperature differences of the steel elements coated with 
the two different coatings (from a color point of view). 
Another interesting aspect would be to conduct longer experiments. An interesting aspect could be to 
find out if the decay is linked to the austenitization point and if it is, then what is the reason for the link. 
 
A “real fire” scenario is also an aspect which should be taken into account. As the current experiments 
were conducted in environments in which the heat source did not present flames, it would be really 
interesting to see the effect of the flames on the intumescent coating. Significant differences would be 
recorded when comparing the temperature-time fire curves presented in paragraph 3.2.2. and 3.3.2. 
with the temperature-time fire curves generated by a real fire. 
In order to apply the model to a realistic structure, the effect of an applied load could be an interesting 
factor to take into account. As the steel profile will lose its strength because of the exposure to high 
temperatures (see figure 2), it will also deform because of the applied load. If for example the element 
is positioned as a beam, the deflection caused by the applied load (with the temperature contribution) 
will produce cracks in the char structure, thus directly influencing the overall thermal resistance

Appendix B 
Analytical validation of unprotected steel temperatures 
 
A series of experiments was needed in order to validate the analytical results obtained through equation 
8. This validation was made by a direct comparison between the results obtained from the formula and

Appendix C 
Analytical delimitation of the four phases 
 
The current appendix moves from the aspects presented in subchapter 4.1.6. The definition of the 
phases can be made in a simple manner, the only point which had an increased assessment difficulty 
was the start of the steady phase. 
The method of evaluating the position of the above mentioned points is done by following the 
algorithm: 
1. An interval is isolated (the starting point is the peak value of the T.R. in the transient phase, and 
the end point is the point of austenitization) (see table 15 for the peak values of the T.R.) 
2. The interval has scattered values, and so an equation has to be found which can describe the 
isolated curve 
3. The problem stated above in the current appendix, can be solved by expressing the curve 
through a 4th degree polynomial equation. 
4. After the equation is settled, the point of maximum curvature of it has to be computed 
5. The point of maximum curvature will be estimated as being the point where the second 
derivative has the maximum value, implicitly the third derivative is zero (the fact that the curve 
is concave or convex does not have any relevance for this step) 
6. As it is a complex curve, it can have more than one point which can yield a maximum curvature 
(the first obtained value does not deliver a relevant point, a new interval will be defined) 
7. The new interval will have as a starting point the newly found value (as mentioned in ‘point 6’), 
and the end point will be the border of the initial interval (as described in ‘point 1’) which is the 
furthest away from the last discovered point 
In order to facilitate the understanding of the above presented rules, a basic example will be given: 
Let there be an interval [2; 10]. After the computation of the point of maximum curvature, the yielded 
value is ‘7’ (another point which has a significant curvature is point ‘5’, but the curvature is less than 
‘7’). The currently found point does not describe the desired phase and so a new interval will be 
established. The new interval will be [2; 7] (which has point ‘5’ included in it). When computing the 
point of maximum curvature (in the same way as it was previously done), the newly found point is the 
one that describes the start of the steady phase (in the current example it is ‘5’). 
The main problem for which this double computation has to be done is that there is a significant 
decrease in the T.R. near the point of austenitization. This point generates a curve that has a significant 
curvature, and that is the reason for which the above mentioned filtering has to be done.

The algorithm used to extract the T.R. from the nomogram is: 
- 
By knowing the section factor, the required thickness of the paint is set to 1mm 
- 
Through an interpolation, the design temperature for a fire resistance class is found (example: 
R30, R45, R60, R90, R120) 
- 
The obtained design temperature is then introduced in the nomogram into the axis called 
‘Temperature’ (y axis) 
- 
The point generated after the introduction of the temperature into figure D.1 will afterwards 
generate a line which will be stretched until it meets with the line generated by the fire 
resistance class (x axis) 
- 
The intersection on the above mentioned lines will generate a point which will be approximated 
to the closes dotted line on the nomogram 
- 
The dotted line has a corresponding value (example: 1500, 1200, 1000 and so on) which 
represents the product of the T.R. with the section factor 
- 
As the section factor is known, the T.R. can be computed from the obtained equation 
The value of the T.R. is assessed by using the above presented nomogram (figure D.1) and the 
corresponding design tables (provided by the manufacturers). The tables which were provided by both 
manufacturers would take into account the section factor as being As/Vs=175 m-1, the DFT of 1mm and 
all the relevant fire resistance classes (R30, R60, R90).

Appendix E 
Custom value of the T.R. obtained from the steady phase by using the point 
of maximum curvature 
 
The T.R. of the steady phase can be assessed in several ways: 
- 
Average of the T.R. on the entire phase 
- 
Take the value of the T.R. at the middle point of the steady phase 
- 
Compute the point of maximum curvature of the curve generated by the values of the T.R. in 
the steady phase 
The current appendix is presenting all the relevant steps which were used to obtain the point of 
maximum curvature. The obtained point will serve as a time coordinate, and by it, the T.R. in the 
steady phase will be the value of the T.R. corresponding to the before obtained point. 
As a first step, the curve will be isolated (the entire steady phase), and as a second step, the first, 
second and third derivatives will be plotted and analyzed. 
An attempt to find the inflection point was carried out; the following mathematical algorithm was 
implemented: 
The second derivative is notably useful in defining the inflection point. A necessary condition for the 
inflection point is that  𝑓𝑓′′(𝑥𝑥) = 0 (second derivative has to be equal to zero). A sufficient condition 
requires that 𝑓𝑓′′(𝑥𝑥+ 𝜖𝜖) 𝑎𝑎𝑎𝑎𝑎𝑎 𝑓𝑓′′(𝑥𝑥−𝜖𝜖) have different sign in the neighborhood of the unknown “x”. 
[21] 
After investigating the obtained results, and the given shape of the curve, it was concluded that the 
inflection point is not a good approach in most of the data sets. The first step will become the analysis 
of the curve, and if it is a well behaved curve, the inflection point can be computed. If the curve is not 
well behaved, then the above presented approach is applied.

Leaving aside the ‘two peaked’ behavior observed in figure F.1, the main parameter which yields if one

because of this reason, the experiments which present the same issue should be excluded from the 
current thesis. 
In what concerns this exclusion criterion, a term called ‘efficiency number’ will be defined. The

-

non noticeable.  The main reason for the exclusion of the current experiment is that the activation is 
nonexistent and overall, the paint does not fulfill its function, the provided fire protection being 
negligible.
