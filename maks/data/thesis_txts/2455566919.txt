ABSTRACT
Denmark have taken actions in order to be independent from fossil fuels by 2050. The
electriﬁcation of the industry and an energy system based in renewable energy sources are
two signiﬁcant paths towards a sustainable development.
In this thesis, a combination of technologies have been studied to supply heat to a dairy
industry for the pasteurization of the milk with temperatures up to 90 °C. The technologies
involved were solar thermal, solar PV, a heat pump with three diﬀerent sources (ground
water, sea water and air), batteries, electric boiler and grid connection. The COP for the
diﬀerent technologies were calculated as well as the solar thermal and PV system were
modelled. In addition, a optimization program were developed in GAMS in order to obtain
the best combination of technologies that could meet the heat demands with the lower
cost.
The best combination found for the studied base case were the air source heat pump
in combination with solar PV and connected to the grid. Also a small fraction of solar
thermal were implemented.
The resulting LCOH demonstrate that this combination of technologies can be more
competitive than electric boilers or combustion boilers fueled by biogas or biomass. However,
natural gas still has a lower LCOH with the current prices and CO2 taxes.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background and motivation
The impact of the global warming is transforming the climate all around the world. Among
other facts, temperatures above the Artic circle were 5 ℃higher than usual in the summer
of 2017 [13]. In addition, extreme climate events such as forest ﬁres, ﬂash ﬂoods, typhoons
and hurricanes cause damages and loss of lives. Some examples of this events are Irma
and Maria hurricanes in the Caribbean in 2017 or droughts aﬀecting Central and Eastern
Europe in recent years.
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change exposed the impacts of global warming of
1,5 ℃above pre-industrial levels and demonstrates that it was already reached 1 ℃above
the pre-industrial levels due to human activity. Without any international climate action,
temperature could increase 0,2 ℃per decade implying an irreversible large-scale climate
impact after 2060 [14]. Extreme climates event such as the exposed above are matters of
concern all around the world as they are the cause of material and human live losses.
For this reason, international agreements where developed such as COP1 in order to prevent
a 2℃temperature above the pre-industrial level and even trying to not exceed 1,5 ℃.
More speciﬁcally, Europeans citizens are really concerned about Climate change [13]. Also
the European Union is taking action in the medium/long-term to reduce the green house
emissions by 40 % in 2030, 60 % in 2040 and 80 % in 2050 compared to levels in 1990 [15].
Following this line of action, the latest Danish national energy agreement states that the
energy system will become independent of fossil fuels by 2050 and the energy system will
run based on renewable energy sources [16].
A large share of the green house emissions come from the fuel combustion to supply heat
for the industrial processes [12]. Therefore, there is room for improvement by using CO2
emission-free technologies. Thus, special attention has to be paid in the most important
industry sectors because around 20 % of the energy in 2016 was used in the industry, where
70 % of this energy was used for the manufacturing industry [17]. In the future, a large
part of the low-temperature heat supply (<100 ℃) should come from heat pumps [18].
For these reasons the electriﬁcation of the industry will be signiﬁcantly important.
More speciﬁcally, in Denmark the production of dairy products has been an important
business area with an intense use of energy [18] [19] . In that context, Bühler et al. [18]
presented strategies for electrifying a milk powder production factory with the aim of
reducing the dependency on fossil fuels by using a central electric boiler and heat pumps.
On the other hand, heat pumps can supply heat for supply temperatures up to 100 ℃
in a sustainable and eﬀective way with a very good performance [12]. Nevertheless, the

industry sector was found to use electric technologies if they are proﬁtable [18] and further
assessment analysis must be carried out.
One of the most important concerns for heat pumps is the COP dependency on the heat
source and heat sink temperatures. Typical heat sources (e.g. air, sea water, lakes and
rivers) are inﬂuenced by ambient conditions.
Therefore, the electricity consumed may vary over time even in an hourly basis. It is
necessary to pointed out that depending on the season the source giving the best COP
could change. H. et al. [20] concluded that the highest system overall performance is
obtained with combination of diﬀerent heat sources where the heat source with the highest
COP for each period was preferred to use. However, a further analysis including investment
cost should be performed in order to decide if a combination of heat sources is preferred to
invest than one single heat source .
On the other hand, among other diﬀerent sustainable energy technologies available solar
thermal systems would be economically feasible, but it would only cover a small fraction of
the heating demand and therefore if will be necessary to combine with other heat generators
[18] [21] .
In addition, a PV system could be integrated in order to power the heap pumps with
clean electric energy and also contribute to the overall electric system sustainable goals.
In this case, the intermittent availability of electricity from RES may aﬀect the amount of
electricity that has to be purchased/sold. Also the electricity prices and its seasonality
(both for selling and buying) can aﬀect the revenues and operation cost derived from
electricity and therefore the decision making on the PV installed capacity. Moreover,
batteries may be included if it is proﬁtable to store electricity in order to allocate the
surplus of electricity generation when the selling prices are low.
Furthermore, solar thermal installation cost were close to PV systems installation cost
in 2016 [15] but PV module costs are still decreasing. If the available space for a solar
system is signiﬁcantly limited (e.g. on a rooftop) and the investment cost is not decisive ,
all the above considerations could aﬀect the decision making on the ﬁnal optimal installed
capacity of each technology. However, the future PV module price is uncertain so it would
be necessary a sensitivity analysis.
Finally, the heat demand can have an impact on the solution adopted if it shows a diﬀerent
proﬁle than the seasonal energy generation from RES (e.g. solar thermal or PV). Similarly,
Rinne and Syri [22] studied a life cycle assessment of heat pumps and combined heat and
power production and suggest the importance of seasonal variations in the heat demand,
electricity price and CHP prodution. For these reasons, it may be important for the energy
system analysis to calculate the COP, heat demand and electricity ﬂows in an hourly
resolution.

1.2.1
Heat pumps
Heat pumps extract heat from a low-temperature level heat source and convert it to a
higher temperature level. The resulting product of this process could be both heating or
cooling. Heat pumps have four main components: evaporator, compressor, condenser and
expansion valve. when it is used for heating, the principle of operation consist on transmit
a low temperature heat from a heat source to high temperature heat by circulating a
refrigerant through the above mentioned main components.
First, the refrigerant is evaporated in the evaporator at low pressure and low temperature.
The energy required for this process is provided by the heat source. Then the refrigerant,
now a gas, is compressed at high pressure in the compressor at constant volume and
increases its temperature because of a property of the gases (Gay-Lussac law). Later on,
the vaporized refrigerant is condensed in the condenser and heat is released in the process
at high temperature that can be used in industrial processes. Once the refrigerant is
condensed at the end of this process it is send to the expansion valve where both pressure
and temperature decreases. At this point the refrigerant is still in a liquid state. Finally,
the cycle is closed when the refrigerant enters again the evaporator. The cycle is shown in
Figure 1.1:
Figure 1.1. Schematic drawing of a heat pump cycle with its main components.
When the heat pump is used for heating it may be in combination with other technologies
that can provide heat. Heat pumps are used for industrial process, district heating and
individual place heating. In Denmark large heat pumps are widely used and its market
can inﬂuence the technology development [3].
There are two types of heat pumps: Compression and absorption heat pump. Compression
heat pump is driven by electric energy (to power the compressor) and the absorption heat
pump is driven by a high temperature source.
For compression heat pumps the coeﬃcient of performance (COP) is deﬁned as the ratio
of total heat delivered to the amount of electricity consumed.

1.2.2
PV system
PV cells converts incident light into electricity by two or more semiconductor layers. In
short, photons or light particles incident the surface of a semiconductor, the electrons ﬂow
creating current and the current is captured and transferred to wires.
The main absorber material utilized are [3]:
• Crystalline silicon: this is the most used material up to 90% and can be divided into
mono-crystalline or multi-crystalline. This technology is expected to be the dominant
one during decades because of its cost and performance.
• Thin ﬁlm solar cells: The absorber can be formed by layers of silicon, cadmium
compounds,etc. This layers are placed one on top of each other to increase the
eﬃciency.
• Monolithic III-V solar cells. this cells are made by elements of group II and group
V of the periodic table. They utilize the direct component of solar radiation and
therefore it is not used en Denmark.
Moreover, a PV system is modular and its modules can be connected in series or parallel
to increase voltage or current. They are also grouped in arrays. This modular system is
one of the most important advantages of this technology as it allows to increase gradually
the capacity when it is considered.
In addition, a PV systems are constituted by other elements such as a Balance of System,
DC-AC inverter, monitoring or even a transformer when the system is large . There are a
wide range of software options that take into account all this components and allow an
accurate estimation of the generated power with an hourly resolution.
Cell performance depends on the irradiation that receives and the module temperature.
This conditions change over time. For these reason, all the features are referenced to

Central solar heating (CSHP) provides heat from solar energy by a system that is connected

CSHP:
• Block plants: Thermal collectors are mounted on the building roof top.
• District heating plants: Thermal collectors are placed in large ﬁelds on ground.
There are three types of solar panels:
• Flat plate collectors (FPC): Can achieve temperatures up to 100 ℃[27].
• Evacuated tubular collectors (ETC).
• Concentrated solar power (CSP).
Flat plate collectors are the most common and the most economical but ETC and CSP are
more eﬃcient at high temperatures. Actually, in order to ﬁnd an optimal balance between
price and eﬃciency a combination of collectors can be developed: Flat collectors are placed
at the beginning of the circuit and preheat the ﬂuid. At the end ETC or CSP heat the
water at high temperatures reaching a greater overall performance (e.g Tårs) [28].

Electricity prices
The Nordic power market is called Nord Pool and the main countries involved are Sweden,
Norway and Denmark. Nord Pool also includes Estonia, Latvia and Lithuania. It is divided

consumption and production for the next 24 hours. The process is an action where the
buyers and sellers send bids and consumption oﬀers without any information about the
other participants oﬀers and bids.

for each hour and each bidding zone where the supply (selling price) and demand (buying

1.3
Objectives, scope and approach
This master thesis aims to ﬁnd a solution for the electriﬁcation of a dairy factory based
on the integration of heat pumps powered by the electricity from the grid, a PV system
and batteries if applicable. In order to reduce the levelized cost also solar thermal will
be included. Heat storage will be included as well for the same reason as it can allocate
the surplus of heat at some periods due to solar seasonality, that is to say, the system
performance will increase because the heat storage can store heat when the production is
cheaper and provide heat when the heat production is more expensive. Also an electric
boiler will be installed in the solar thermal system as showed in 1.4.
The objectives of this master thesis are stated below:
• Find a solution which allows the electriﬁcation of a dairy industry.
• Obtain the optimal heat heat pump source combination taking into account both
the operational cost derived from electricity consumption and investment cost.
• Implementing additional RES technologies.
• Understand the relation between the diﬀerent components of the system and the
behaviour that the system could experience in terms of performance due to variables
(e.g. electricity prices, heat pump COP or ambient temperatures).
The following task will be performed to reach the above objectives:
• Modelling the solar thermal and PV systems in order to obtain the energy output
per square meter.
• Calculate the heat pumps COP for each heat source in an hourly basis.
• Build an optimization modelling which fulﬁlls the heat demand of a dairy industry
and involving all the technologies, its interrelations and working principles.
• Determination of optimum capacities for each technology.
• Sensitivity analysis of key parameters such as ground ratio coverage, investment cost
scenarios or electricity prices.
• Economical analysis by comparing the obtained solution with conventional
technologies.

2.4
Thermal storage
Sensible heat storage is the most common simple, low cost and long-lived heat storage [46].
More concretely, water is used as sensible heat storage material in most of the heat stores
for energy systems that requires an operating temperature from 0 ºC to 100 ºC because it
is harmless, inexpensive and easy to handle and store [47]. For these reasons, this system
was used in this work.
As water density decreases with temperature hot water will move to the top and cold water
will move to the bottom of the tank. A mixture of hot and cold water called thermocline
reduces the heat transfer between hot and cold sides and allow an uniform temperature
in each side (stratiﬁcation) as it can be observed in ﬁgure 2.9. In this model the useful
heat stored in the heat storage tank it is considered to be the upper layer at an uniform
temperature of 90 ºC.
Figure 2.9. Thermal gradient in a stratiﬁed heat storage tank [9].
Thermocline thickness also determines the extent of mixing between warm and cold water.
Charge and discharge process could create turbulence that increase the thermocline layer
due to ﬂuid mixing and therefore lower the high temperature heat. It was assumed that
the heat storage were designed and equipped with technologies that reduced the turbulence
(diﬀusers, valves, thermal diodes, high and slim design, etc) so that the reduction of useful
heat at 90 ºC due to turbulences were negligible from a system perspective.
In addition, heat loss because of heat transfer through the tank walls results in reduced
temperatures [20] and the useful heat at 90 ºC will be decreased. Therefore, the heat
storage level at each time is described by equation 2.9:

• Transmission grid tariﬀ: It is determined by the National System Operator that ﬁxed

providing access and services in the transmission network on high voltage levels and
ancillary services.
• Electricity taxes: It is a state range and there are variations depending on the
customer consumption range.
• Value Added Tax (VAT): It is imposed to the other previous elements and Elspot
price at a range of 25 %. Generally, for industries a full refund of VAT applies [52].

In this section the system behaviour will be explained. Heating demand and supply graphs
will be the main tool to understand the results. As solar thermal and solar PV have a
diﬀerent performance depending on the season, two representative weeks were chosen. One
week of June when the radiation is high and one week in January when the solar generation
is very low. In addition, the graphs will be duplicated in order to avoid saturation of
information when including electricity prices or PV generation. Enlarged graphs can be
found in appendices.
The grey bars represents the heat coming from the heat store hsoutt. The heat stored at
every moment hsint is represented by the semi-transparent bars in negative and it is split
in the heat coming from the heat pump (transparent-green) and the heat from the solar
thermal (transparent orange).
The total heat supplied by the heat pump consist on the electricity energy converted to
heat (blue), and the heat extracted from the heat source in green (both in transparent
green and opaque green). It was split in this way in order to represent the electricity
demand to be compared with the electricity purchased (brown line). The black dashed line
represents the heat pump maximum capacity and allows to know when the heat storage is
used to cover peak demands (grey bars above the dashed black line) and when it is used
to reduce heat pump consumption in moments when the electricity price is high (grey bars
below the dashed black line).
The red dashed line represents diﬀerent concepts depending on the graph. in graph a) the
dashed red lines represents the electricity generated in kWh while in graph b) it represents
the electricity purchase cost in €/MWh.
By projecting the generated electricity of graph a) in graph b) it is possible to know that
the energy sold from PV would be sold at the moment of the day when the electricity
prices are higher (as the electricity is sold at a constant price of 45 €/KWh less than the
electricity purchasing price).

4. Discussion
4.1
Base case
Figure 4.1. Heating demand and supply balance with a)PV generation and b) Electricity purchase
price (One week of June).Base Case.
Figure 4.1 represents one week of June for the base case scenario. The heating demand is
met by the heat pump and complemented by solar thermal when it is available. The heat
from the heat storage tank is mainly used at heating peaks of demand. It is also used at
the end of the week where there is no electricity generation from PV and the electricity
prices are high in order to reduce the electricity consumption.
It should be noticed that peaks in heat demand are higher when there is no solar PV
generation (Fig. 4.1 a)) and the heat pump operates at full capacity. However, during this
period the heat pump is powered with electricity from the the grid at a valley price time
(Fig. 4.1 b)).
During the day when the heat demand is lower and solar thermal heat is available, the
heat pump only uses a fraction of its maximum capacity to meet the heat demand. The
remaining heat pump power capacity is used to ﬁll the heat storage. In total the heat
pump operated 147 per week at full capacity (87,5% of the time). In ﬁgure 4.2 it is possible
to observe that the heat storage is ﬁlled when the heat pump capacity allow to do it at
low heat demand periods.

This strategy allow to reduce the maximum installed capacity of the heat pump close
to the average weekly heat demand by allocating the heat that is generated when the
heating demand is low. It is reﬂected in the reduction of the heat pump investment cost
which depends on the maximum installed capacity. The model found that the optimal
solution is obtained by adjusting the capacity of the heat pump rather than increasing it
to generate most of the heat in periods when the electricity is inexpensive or costless due
to PV generation.
However, the heat pump always operates at full capacity when inexpensive electricity
generated by the solar PV system is available even if the heating demand is the lowest
during these hours. Therefore, most of the self-consumption potential is exploited.
Furthermore, the surplus of electricity generated is sold at peak electricity price time.
The advantage of self-consumption in the hours when buying electricity is more expensive
coupled with the revenues by selling the electricity at high prices explain the feasibility of
covering most of the available area with solar PV at the given base case investment PV
cost. By doing so, it is possible to reach a signiﬁcant reduction of the electricity costs (see
ﬁgure 3.2) which allows the return of the PV system investment.
In addition, more electricity has to be purchased in winter due to the smaller availability
of electricity generated by the solar PV system as it can be observed in ﬁgure 4.3.

4.2
Displaced demand
Figure 4.4. Heating demand and supply balance with a)PV generation and b) Electricity purchase
price (One week of June). Displaced demand scenario.
The main characteristic of this scenario is that the heating peak demand occur when the
electricity prices are higher. However, peak demand matches with the highest solar PV
and solar thermal generation and therefore no electricity has to be purchased during this
period when it is necessary to power the heat pump at its full capacity. The electricity
generated that it is not consumed by the heat pump is sold to the grid.
In the base case scenario, the heat pump stored heat when the heating demand was low
during the day by using the generated electricity of the PV system. In the displaced
heating demand scenario the heating demand is lower at night and therefore the heat
pumps can store energy at this period of time by purchasing energy as no solar electricity
is generated.
Both in the base case and the displaced demand scenario, the heat pump installed capacity
was so reduced that it had to operate at full capacity most of the time to store heat that
will be necessary for peaks of heating demand. In this scenario, the heat pump operates at
full capacity 146 hours per week in summer while in the base case the heat pump operated
at full capacity 147 hours per week in summer. For this reason, the electricity demand

CONCLUSIONS
In this work, a feasibility study for the electriﬁcation of an industry side using a mix
of technologies were presented. An optimization model of the system were performed
consisting on a combination of solar thermal, heat storage, heat pump, solar PV, batteries,
an electric boiler and the electrical grid. It was necessary to model the solar thermal
system, the PV system and calculate the Coeﬃcient of Performance of the heat pump for
three diﬀerent heat sources. In addition, a sensitivity analysis was carried out by changing
key parameters such as average electricity prices, PV module cost, ground coverage ratio,
and heating demand.
In all cases the electric boiler, batteries, groundwater and sea water source for the heat
pump were discarded due to high investment cost.
The optimal conﬁguration for the base case consisted on an air source heat pump in
combination with solar PV, small capacity of solar thermal and around 70 % of the
electricity consumed provided by the grid.
In order to minimize the investment cost, the heat pump capacity were adjusted to be able
to store just the necessary heat to cover peaks in heat demand, even in winter when solar
thermal hardly contributes. For this reason, the heat pump were operating most of the
time at full capacity having low ﬂexibility to apply strategies that would allow to reduce
electricity cost such as produce most of the heat when the solar PV is generating inexpensive
electricity and avoid purchasing electricity from the grid. Even though, electricity costs
are reduced by selling the surplus electric energy production from PV.
However, the price of the heat pump system were high due to its small size. Lower prices
would be expected in a heat pump system for larger applications due to economy of scale.
A future work on larger application could allow to determine if lower price in the heat
pump system would make feasible to increase its capacity and operate at full capacity only
when cheap electricity from the grid is available or the the PV system is generating electric
energy.
In addition, when the LCOE of solar PV were increased in some scenarios, solar thermal
acquire more relevance in the system at expenses of the PV system.
Solar thermal
contributes to the heating supply and allow the heat pump to operate less hours and
choose when it is more economically to do in order to reduce electricity costs. In the reality,
an algorithm would be necessary to optimize the strategy in function of solar radiance
forecasts.
Finally, the lower LCOH was obtained when the average prices are in average 40 €/MWh
followed by the base case and the displaced demand scenario.
The three cases were
economically more competitive than biogas, biomass and an electric boiler. However,
