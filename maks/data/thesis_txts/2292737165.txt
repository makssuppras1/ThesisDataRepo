Abstract 
Investigations of lung microbiome by culture independent techniques are critical to understand its 
role in health and its impact on inflammatory lung diseases such as asthma. Culture independent 
techniques require highly efficient DNA extraction methods for studying the microbiome. The first 
purpose of this thesis was to compare different DNA extraction methods to evaluate the DNA 
quality and quantity from Broncho-alveolar lavage (BAL) and fecal samples as control. The second 
objective of this thesis was to investigate the difference between the microbiome of the lung and gut 
between wild type (WT) mice and surfactant protein SP-D gene-deficient mice 
In this study, a variety of different manual commercial DNA extraction kits (Fast DNA stool Mini 
Kit, Qiagen and  DNA mini kit,  Qiagen, for body fluids and modified  PowerLyzer® Powersoil® 
DNA isolation kit, MO BIO with carrier DNA ) and automated  robotic system  Promega 
Maxwell® 16 (Maxwell® 16 Cell LEV DNA Purification Kit) were used. These extraction methods 
were tested on fecal pellets and BAL samples collected from antibiotic, ovalbumin and phosphate 
buffered saline treated mice. The DNA extracted from fecal and BAL samples were quantified by 
using NanoDrop and quantitative PCR respectively. Subsequently, the quality of DNA obtained 
from these methods were tested and compared on PCR-DGGE.  
Our results show that DNA extracts obtained by robotic system Maxwell 16 ® and Qiagen were 
yield approximately the same from fecal samples. DGGE performed on PCR from these methods on 
fecal samples was very successful with the extraction of high quality DNA. On comparing these 
methods with respect to BAL samples, the results indicated that the Qiagen method was more 
efficient in extracting DNA, than Maxwell 16; having said that we also believe that further analysis 
is needed in this regard.  Later on comparing the methods MObio and Qiagen, the numbers of 
copies of 16S r DNA of samples extracted by Qiagen were significantly higher than that of MObio 
Kit. The results of sequencing from SP-D knockout mice and wild type mice microbiome were not 
included in this study due to the unavailability of the results during the writing period of this thesis.  
In conclusion, the Maxwell 16 ® can be used instead of classical DNA extraction kit (Fast DNA 
stool Mini Kit, Qiagen) for fecal samples. While the best DNA extraction method from BAL 
samples was DNA mini kit,Qiagen when compared to PowerLyzer® Powersoil® DNA isolation kit 
and Maxwell 16. Additionally, the DGGE of BAL samples appeared less efficient when compared 
to fecal samples.

1. Introduction  
 
Study of lung microbiome and its association with lung diseases such as asthma and chronic 
obstructive pulmonary disease COPD is a relatively young field. Our understanding on this subject is 
based mainly on few articles published which showed the lung microbial diversity in individuals 
with asthma differs from that found in healthy individuals. Investigating the complex role of 
microbiome in lung diseases and the relationships between the lung microflora and clinical features 
of lung disease appear quiet interesting and requires further research. However, the study of human 
lung microbiome is restricted because of limitations in availability of samples and potential for 
contamination of samples from the upper respiratory tract. Therefore, mice were used as the animal 
model in this project and its lung microbiome was examined in this study. This model has many 
advantages, since mice are characterized by their similarity to humans in anatomy, physiology, and 
genetics. Moreover, we can flush the whole lung with saline solution after killing the mice, without 
contaminating our sample from nasal and oral bacteria (Upper Respiratory Tract).  
 
 
2. Aim of the study  
 
The overall aim of this project was to understand the nature of lung microbiome and its impact on 
inflammatory lung diseases such as asthma. In order to meet this aim, the objectives of the project 
were: 
 
 Comparison of different DNA extraction methods (Manual DNA extractions kits and 
automated robotic system, Promega Maxwell® 16 ) for PCR-DGGE analysis of microbial 
diversity in mice Broncho alveolar Lavage (BAL) and fecal samples.  
 
 Comparison of the lung and gut microbiome between wild-type (WT) mice and SP-D gene-
deficient mice.

3. Background 
 
The term, microbiome was first coined by Nobel Joshua Lederberg as “the ecological community of 
commensal, symbiotic, and pathogenic microorganisms that literally share our body space” 
(Lederberg and McCray 2001). In recent years, the human-associated microbiota has gained a huge 
interest. Investigations have led us to believe that the human microbiota may have a key role to play 
in our health than previously thought. The number of microorganisms inhabiting the human body 
out numbers human cells in the ratio of 10 to 1.Therfore, the combined gene pool of the microbiota 
exceeds150 times that of the host (Qin et al., 2010; medicalxpress.com; Huang et al., 2013). Hence, 
studying and understanding the relationship dynamics between the host and the microbiota becomes 
crucial. Studying the human microbiome will give us a better understanding of the various 
inexplicable physiological or pathological phenomenons, which cannot be understood by studying 
the human genome alone (Hui et al., 2013). The human microbiome project (HMP), is a project by 
the National institute of health (United States), the aim of this project is to identify and characterize 
the human microbiota and analyze their role in both healthy and diseased humans. This project 
might be helpful in understanding the human microbiome, which can be a stepping-stone in health 
care and might lead to various innovative medical solutions involving manipulating and monitoring 
the microbiota. 
Human microbiota is found in all body surfaces, including the gut, skin, nose, vagina, lungs and so 
on, but the largest number of bacteria is found in gut. Various, investigations show that many of 
bacteria in the gastrointestinal tract (GI) play an important role in human health. For instance, some 
bacteria have the ability to stimulate the immune system and synthesize essential vitamins such as 
vitamin K. (Furusawa et al., 2013). Perturbations of the microbiota are associated with altered 
immune response and may be responsible behind various diseases (Knights et al., 2013). These 
investigations have opened a door to investigate lung microbiome and its association with 
inflammatory lung disease. 
Until recently, healthy human lungs were believed to be sterile, but recent evidence show that 
human lungs are host to diverse microorganisms and have their own distinct microflora (Huang et 
al., 2011; Borewicz et al, 2013; Marsland et al., 2014). Thus, compared to other organ systems such 
as the gastrointestinal tract, the lung microbiome has been less studied (Huang et al., 2013). Recent 
studies conducted under LHMP (lung HIV microbiome project) clearly suggest the relation between 
==PAGE:10==
 
10
lung microbiota and various inflammatory diseases such as asthma, cystic fibrosis, chronic 
obstructive pulmonary diseases (COPD) and so on (Zhao et al.,  2012; Huang et al., 2011). Clear 
signals have been observed in correlating the clinical features of these diseases with characteristic 
microbial communities found in patients (Erb-Downward et al., 2011). Recent study conducted by 
Barfod et al., was performed using animal models in this area, and this study gives the first 
description of murine lung microbiome, and also does a comparative study between the lung 
microbiota from broncho-alveolar lavage, lung tissue, gut and vaginal samples. This study 
confirmed the differences between the lung microbiome and gut microbiota in mice (Barfod et al., 
2013). As a result, we can say that there is a huge potential in understanding the functioning of lung 
microbiota and factors, which associate them with lung diseases.  
 
3.1 Inflammatory lung disease  
 
Asthma and COPD are among the most common lung diseases affecting modern humans and have 
been a growing concern in the modern health care sector. These diseases are characterized by 
airway obstruction and chronic inflammation of the respiratory tract, which in turn is caused by 
increase in expression of inflammatory proteins, cytokines, chemokines, adhesion molecules, and 
inflammatory enzymes (Barnes, 2008). Even though, both the diseases are remarkably similar, there 
are various differences in both the diseases. Inflammation in asthma usually occurs in the larger and 
smaller airways, but the lung parenchyma remains unaffected. In contrast, COPD affects airways 
along with the lung parenchyma. The differences in inflammation patterns in both the diseases can 
be linked to the underlying immunological mechanisms (Barnes, 2008; Barnes, 2000; Jeffery, 
2000). 
Figure1 shows the different steps of the inflammatory response in asthma; Inhaled allergens 
sensitize mast cells by causing surface bound immunoglobulin E (IgE) molecules to release 
bronchocontrictors and cysteinyl leukotrienes and prostaglandin. Epithelial cell released stem cell 
factor (SCF) maintains the mast cells at the airway surface. Allergens are processed by dendritic 
cells (antigen-presenting cells), which are conditioned by thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP). 
The dendritic cells in turn release chemokines CC-chemokine ligand 17 (CCL17) and CCL22, 
which acts on chemokines CC-chemokine receptor   CCR4 and attracts T helper 2 cells (Th2). Th2 
cells play a key role in causing the inflammatory response caused in allergy by the production of

cytokines including interleukin 4 (IL-4), IL-5 and IL-13.  IL4 along with IL-13 regulates IgE 
production.  IL5 is involved in the accumulation of eosinophils and causes eosinophilic 
inflammation and IL9 causes mast cell to proliferate. Epithelial cells release CCL11, which 
employs eosinophils via CCR3. Asthmatic patients usually have a defect in regulatory T cells, 
which in turn causes Th2 cell production (Barnes, 2008).

Additionally, Surfactant protein D (SP-D) is an innate immune protein and plays a central role in 
preventing lung infection, allergy and asthma (Qaseem et al., 2013). This protein belongs to the 
collectin group of C-type lectins that is characterized by its carbohydrate recognition domain 
(CRD). As seen in figure 2, SP-D binds to the pathogen or allergen and increase microbial 
phagocytosis by accumulating bacteria and virus. In addition, SP-D regulates cytokine by binding to 
variety of receptors to modulate immune cell cytokine (Pastva et al., 2007). SP-D can also interact 
with adaptive immunity by binding to T cell and inhibit T cell proliferation, and it is also can 
enhance antigen uptake (Pastva et al., 2007).

It has been observed that the lower concentration of SPD in lung is associated with asthma (Hartl et 
al., 2006). Moreover, it has been shown that the SP-D knockout (KO) mice overexpress IL13, and 
this interleukin is involved in development of asthma (Qaseem et al., 2013).The presence of SP-D 
can protect against allergic challenge in murine model by several mechanisms including inhibition 
of IgE-alergen binding. The study of SP-D and its impact on lung microbiome is very important, 
since this protein has been known to play a key role in causing various lung diseases.  In this study 
the microbiome of SP-D gene-deficient mice will be assessed compared to WT mice, and hence it 
would be a novelty to show that host genetic factors determine lung microbiome.  
 
3.2 The human microbiome of the healthy and disease lung 
 
Multiple studies have  pointed out the difference in the microbiome profile of healthy individuals 
and asthmatic patients (Hilty et al., 2010; Erb-Downward et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2011). The 
most common phyla in the healthy lung from BAL samples are bacteroides, Firmicutes and  
proteobarcteria. The important genera among healthy individual are Pseudomonas (Proteobacteria), 
Streptococcus (Firmicutes), Prevotella (Bacteroidetes), veillonella (Firmicutes) (Dickson et al., 
2013). Asthmatic patients showed an abnormal presence of Proteobacteriae and lower frequency of 
Bacteroidetes when compared to healthier control group (Dickson et al., 2013). 
==PAGE:13==
 
13
A variety of studies suggest a relationship between early childhood antibiotic exposure and 
subsequent development of asthma and allergies, which leads to speculations that disrupting the 
natural microbiota during early childhood might lead to immunological and pathogenic 
complications in adult life.(Dickson et al., 2013; Wills et al., 2001; Risnes et al., 2011). These 
studies clearly suggests that the key to curing asthma might be available in understanding the lung 
microbiome (Dickson et al., 2013; Hilty et al., 2010). Studies involving gut microbiota in humans 
point towards mechanisms via which microbial colonies ‘communicate’ and influence each other. 
Recent studies also point towards host and bacterial communication through various molecules 
which influence bacterial quorum sensing and other factors related to virulence (Dickson et al., 
2013).  
The studies conducted in gut microbiota indicates towards similar mechanisms in the lung 
microbiome, and further investigations in microbial interactions in the respiratory system can help 
shed light on the role of microbiota in inflammatory lung diseases such as asthma. Furthermore, a 
potential link between changes in the gut microbiota causing an allergic response should also be 
kept in mind (Dickson et al., 2013).  
An important hypothesis in the study of lung microbiome is that the lung microbiome can be 
potentially manipulated with the aim of curing or correcting the altered microbiota of the lung, 
which might be causing a dysfunction in the activity of lungs. Additionally, it would show that the 
lung microbiome is a “true” microbiome and not just a reflection of background.  
 
3.3 The murine lung microbiome   
 
As mentioned before, the BAL sample from dead mice is easier and prevents contamination from 
bacterial species present in the upper respiratory tract. Therefore, this model allows clear 
identification of bacterial species present in the lower airways at the time of sampling, and also 
highlights the various species present in the lower airways of different mice populations (Barfod et 
al., 2013; Yun Yet al., 2014).   
Barfod et al. use mouse models for studying the lung microbiome and they have found that the 
murine lung microbiome comprises of Proteobacteria, Firmicutes, Actinobacteria, Bacteroidetes 
and Cyanobacteria (Barfod et al., 2013). Study done on murine lung microbiome also suggest that 
==PAGE:14==
 
14
removal of host tissue or cells from lung fluid during DNA extraction may have an impact on the 
eventual bacterial community profile (Barfod et al., 2013). Barfod et al used the mouse model for 
the first time to study the lung microbiota and proved that the mouse models provides sufficient 
means to obtain mechanistic insights into the lung microbiome and can be studied as a starting point 
to relate it to human inflammatory diseases.   
 
3.4 Culture independent techniques 
 
Novel culture-independent techniques have been widely used today to analyze the human 
microbiome in various body sites since 80% of the observable bacteria within human body cannot 
be cultured with standard media (Suau, et al, 1999). Recently, the use of these methods in the field 
of lung microbiome research have been reported and has been helpful in characterizing the lung 
microbiome in humans and also in mice. (Hilty et al., 2010; Erb-Downward et al., 2011; Barfod et 
al., 2013). Essentially, these methods are based on an analysis of DNA or RNA that are extracted 
directly from the sample followed by amplification of 16s gene. Genetic finger printing techniques 
including denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis has been successful in determining the genetic 
diversity from a specific environment (Zotentendal et al., 2002). This method is characterized by its 
possibility to analyze multiple samples at the same time to compare the genetic diversity of 
microbial community. In recent years, the next generation sequencing technology have been 
developed and led to increase the number of microbial diversity studies (Eckburg et al., 2005; 
McKenna et al., 2008). 
3.4.1 Denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis (DGGE) 
 
Denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis (DGGE) is a genetic fingerprinting technique, which in turn 
provides an analysis of the genetic diversity of a microbial community. DGGE of PCR amplified 
ribosomal DNA have been described and introduced into microbial ecology by Muyzer et al. 
(Muyzer et al., 1993). 
Briefly, DGGE is based on polymerase chain reaction (PCR) - amplification and separation of 
amplicons. The 16 s rDNA is usually used as a target for PCR amplification, because it is the most 
conserved segment of the genome and has hypervariable regions that can be used for bacterial 
==PAGE:15==
 
15
identifications (Ferris et al., 1996). Additionally, it is found in all prokaryotic microorganisms. The 
principle of DGGE is the DNA fragments of identical size but with different sequences that can be 
separated and detected by DGGE (Muyzer et al., 1993) 
 
This separation is based on the ability of DNA molecule to melt in polyacrylamide gel containing a 
linear increasing gradient of DNA denaturants that formed is by a mixture of urea and formamide 
(Muyzer et al., 2004). DNA is partially melted in specific separate regions called melting domains. 
The melting temperature of these domains is sequence specific, e.g. DNA molecules with different 
sequences have different melting temperature. This performance decreases the electrophoretic 
mobility of melted DNA in the acrylamid gel (Muyzer et al., 1993). When the melting temperature 
of the lowest melting domain is reached, the DNA fragment becomes partially melted and migration 
of DNA halt, creating branched molecule by breaking hydrogen bond. To prevent complete 
separation of the strands, 40 base-pair GC-clamp is added to one of the primers during PCR 
amplification (Muyzer et al., 1993). After gel staining with GelRed stain, the DNA bands relative to 
the microbial species present in the samples will be visualized.   
 
3.4.2 Next generation sequencing (NGS) 
 
Recently, high through sequencing of 16 s rRNA gene amplicons, also known as next generation 
sequencing such as those based on 454 pyrosequencing platform have been used for microbial 
analysis (Eckburg et al., 2005;  McKenna et al. , 2008). This method was used since it is sensitive, 
rapid and enable for identification of multiple samples simultaneously. Pyrosequencing method is 
based on the detection of released pyrophosphate (PPi) during DNA synthesis (Ronaghi ,  2001). 
This occurs by hybridization of sequencing primer to a single-stranded DNA template and 
incubated with series enzymes (DNA polymerase, ATP sulfurylase, luciferase, and apyrase) and 
substrates adenosine 5′ phosphosulphate (APS), and luciferin.  
Briefly, Deoxyribonucleotide triphosphate (dNTP) is separated added into the pyrosequencing 
reaction. The incorporation of a nucleotide is associated with the release of pyophosate (PPi). 
Subsequently, the ATP sulfurylase quantitatively converts  
PPi to ATP. Due to the generated ATP, luciferase converts luciferin to oxyluciferin producing 
visible light (i.e., photons).  Detection of this light is achieved by a charge coupled device (CCD) 
==PAGE:16==
 
16
camera and integrated as a peak in a program. Finally, the apyrase enzyme continuously degrades 
ATP and unincorporated dNTPs (Md et al., 2012) (Fig. 3).  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
3.4.3  Quantitative RT-PCR  
 
Quantitaive or real time PCR (qPCR) is a standard PCR technique which can be helpful in 
estimating the quantity of DNA formed after each cycle while using fluorescent dyes or 
fluorescently tagged oligonucleotide probes (Marisa , et al., 2005; Bustin et al. , 2000; Carey et al., 
2007). qPCR results can be obtained faster when compared to standard PCR and are also less 
variable due to the elimination of post-PCR detection procedures. qPCR can be highly instrumental 
in investigating gene expression, viral load, pathogen detection and various other applications 
(Bernd  et al., 2009). During this study, the effect of two different DNA extraction methods were 
evaluated using qPCR.

==PAGE:17==

Figure 4: The PCR amplification curve 
 
qRT-PCR is shown in figure 4, the x- axis stands for the number of cycles and the y-axis shows 
ΔRn, which is an increase of fluorescent signal at each time point. The blue line indicates a negative 
sample without amplification, whereas the red graph shows the positive sample where DNA is 
inserted. This graph is divided to baseline phase, exponential phase and plateau phase. 
 
Baseline shows the first 10-15 cycles where the fluorescence emission is at the same level as 
background. Plateau stage represents the time where reaction components are limited, in which case 
the florescence intensity cannot be used for data calculation. The exponential phase is the phase 
where PCR product quantity can be measured before the plateau stage. The anti stain line in is 
called threshold, which can be adjusted in the region of exponential amplification across all stages 
in the experiment. The point at which the threshold crosses the amplification plot is termed as Ct 
(Cycle threshold) which predicts quantity of input target and provides a quantitative measurement 
of input target sequences. qPCR is capable of two kind of quantifications: Absolute and relative 
quantification. Absolute quantification is based on comparison between target DNA and DNA 
standers using calibration curve. This method provides the exact number of target DNA molecules.  
Relative quantification is based on measuring the changes in sample gene expression by measuring 
internal reference sample, in this case the results are expressed as a target DNA/reference ratio (M. 
L.Wong  et al. , 2005).

3.5 Extraction methods 
 
Many DNA-based techniques including PCR, DGGE, and sequencing require highly efficient DNA 
extraction methods. Recently, different commercial extraction and purification kits have been 
developed and evaluated for the extraction of total microbial community DNA from a wide range of 
samples such as blood, fecal, soil and other samples (Argyrs et al. , 2000; Whitehouse et al. , 2007; 
YuZ et al., 2004; Person et al. , 2011; Mirsepasi et al., 2014). Some of these kits utilize a silica 
surfaces based spin filter membrane to isolate DNA in the presence of Chaotropic salts to assist 
binding of DNA to the silica surface.  
In addition, a number of automated extraction methods were developed to extract DNA including 
the Maxwell 16 System (Promega UK, Southampton,UK). Maxwell 16 instrument is designed for 
rapid purification, this instrument can extract 16 samples or few simultaneously and carried out 
purification steps in approximately 29 min following 15 min for sample preparation. Different 
Promega kits can uses to this instrument depended on samples type (Promega). 
All these extraction methods are based on cell lysis, removal of proteins that can inhibit 
downstream PCR, purification and elution of pure DNA by using combination of chemical and 
mechanical cell disruptors.  Sometimes, carriers DNA are used with these kits to enhance DNA 
extraction by increasing the DNA binding to the silica. However, if these methods are insufficient, 
this will strongly affect the success of culture-independent techniques (Santos et al. 2012)  
Acellular Broncho alveolar lavarge sample (BAL) is considered the gold standard for most studies 
of the lung microbiome (Erb-Downward et al., 2011). To date the effect of DNA extraction from 
BAL sample by manual and automated extraction on the microbial diversity profiles is largely 
undetermined. The microbial DNA extraction from BAL and feces samples can be more 
challenging than the extraction of DNA from other samples, since feces contains different PCR 
inhibitors, such as complex polysaccharides and bile salts (Holland et al, 2000;  Monteiro et al. , 
1979). In addition the DNA yield from BAL samples expected to be low in comparison with feces 
(Segal et al., 2013).

4. Project description  
 
The first step of this study was to compare the different DNA extractions methods. Primarily, we 
wanted to determine the best DNA extraction method so as to identify the various bacterial 
communities in BAL and fecal samples as control. We also wanted to find out if these extractions 
method had an important effect on their profiles. Additionally, the quality and quantity of DNA 
from these samples were evaluated and estimated for further analysis. We used the traditional 
manual commercial extraction kits ( QIAamp fast DNA stool MiniKit (Qiagen), QIAmp mini DNA 
kit (Qiagen)), robot system “MaxwellTM 16 procedure (Promega) and PowerLyzer Poersoil DNA 
isolation kit (MO BIO) with carrier DNA. DGGE and quantitative real time PCR (q PCR) was used 
to evaluate these different extraction methods.  
In this study, these methods were tested on feces and BAL samples collected from different 
treatment groups of mice (Antibiotic, ovalbumin (OVA) and phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) as 
control group). OVA inducing TH2-type inflammation and it was used as allergen. Additionally, the 
total microbial community DNA from lung and gut of wild type (WT) mice and SP-D gene-
deficient mice (knockout mice) (KO) were isolated. Some of these mice were sensitized to OVA 
and other were mock treated with PBS. After extraction, the samples were sent to Copenhagen 
sequencing centre at the University of Copenhagen for amplification of bacterial 16s rRNA genes 
and the sequencing. The results of sequencing is not included in this thesis. The goal here was to 
compare the microbiome of the lung and gut between these mice. Thereafter, different types of 
bacteria found in the lungs of these mice will be assessed, additionally the types of bacteria found in 
the gut will also be determined and their possible impact in causing inflammatory lung diseases will 
be evaluated. As mentioned before in the background section 3.1 SP-D is an innate immune protein 
that plays an important role in human lung disease. The hypothesis is that the effect of SP-D-genes 
on microbial diversity in lung and gut in mice may alter the bacterial communities in these organs. 
These changes may have an impact on the lung disease, since altered innate immune response and 
protein content of the lungs are different between KO and WT mice (Qaseem et al., 2013).

7. Discussion 
 
In this study a manual DNA extraction commercial kit (DNA stool mini kit, Qiagen) and an 
automated DNA extraction robotic system (Maxwell16, Promega) were tested and compared based 
on fecal samples in mice. The manual DNA extraction kit (Qiagen) that we used, produced 
approximately the same yield of DNA on average as automated extraction by Maxwell 16 using 
NanoDop when each  sample was split into equal portions (half  fecal pellet). The varaitions can be 
noticed clearly in individual samples between the two methods. It should also be considered that 
NanoDrop is not the perfect way to measure DNA concentration, since it measures not only DNA but 
also other cellular components such as RNA, proteins etc., which absorbs light at 260 nm (Mirsepasi, 
et al., 2014). An alternative method to quantify the DNA is also qPCR.   
 
Based on our results obtained from the control set (fecal samples ), we can estimate that these 
extraction methods produced high quality DNA that were amplified using PCR and analysed by 
DGGE for comparisons and evaluation of microbiome profiles (Section 6.1, Fig.13). DGGE method 
proved optimal for fecal samples and hence this method provided a quick way of looking at 
microbial diversity (Mirsepasi et al. 2014). When DGGE band patterns were analysed for similarities 
by BioNumerics (Section 6.1, Fig. 14) the results indicated that there were consistency between the 
Robot system and qiagen. Therefore, Maxwell 16 can extract DNA from fecal samples and this 
method can be a good alternative to manual extraction. As we have mentioned previously that the 
Maxwell 16 Instrument (Promega) is designed for rapid purification, while the manual purification 
appeared time consuming. Consequently, the robot system provides an advantage and we can 
perform a large number of DNA extractions from fecal samples in a short period, at the same time 
reducing chances of contamination and variation between samples from manual pipetting. q PCR 
should also be performed on the given samples. 
 
Additionally the antibiotic and PBS treatments of mice were also taken into account. The results 
obtained from PCA (section 6. 1, Fig. 15) show a clear effect of the antibiotic Vancomycin on the 
microbial community, because the PBS and Vancomycin groups were well segregated. In addition, 
one or two very intense bands were observed in Vancomycin samples. These results indicated that on 
treatment with Vancomycin, there were a few species of bacteria which increased in numbers on 
elimination of vancomycin susceptible bacteris. This is probably  due to absence of competition from 
vancomycin susceptible bacteria and the availability of more nutrition for these species. In fact, 
==PAGE:48==
 
48
Vancomycin reduced fecal microbial diversity in humans by reducing the gram-positive bacterial 
strains, e.g. Firmicutes  and increased  the gram-negative bacteria e.g  Proteobacteria  (Vrieze et al.  
2014).    
These extraction methods proved highly efficient and competent when tested on the fecal samples of 
mice i.e., the control set and provided a stable basis on which to conduct our study on BAL samples.  
 
 
The same  extraction methods were also tested on BAL samples and when the quality of DNA of 
BAL  was tested on DGGE, the bacterial diversity of lung was observed with the presence of very 
weak bands. This was probably due to the low DNA concentration in the samples. Another setback 
in this study was the presence of 16s rDNA product in the negative control of PCR reaction and 
DNA extraction kits ( Appendix I, Fig.7 ). The presence of such DNA product could be due to the 
use of contaminated Taq DNA polymerase (Hughes et al., 2008; Maiwald et al., 1994). Another 
issue could be the use of 16S rDNA Primer which is very sensitive and is capable of amplifying any 
bacteria present in the environment. Several methods have been used to eradicate this contamination, 
but none of these methods were found to be 100% competent (Meier et al., 1993; Rand et al., 1990). 
This type of contamination in DNA extraction kits and other laboratory reagents can critically impact 
microbiome analysis, particularly samples containing a low microbial count. These contaminants can 
overcome the low amount of DNA from the required sample and produce untrustworthy results 
(Salter et al., 2014). Therefore, it is of crucial importance that the negative controls in DNA 
extraction controls and PCR reaction should be taken into account and analysed or sequenced such 
that these samples could later be set apart from the study samples (Salter et al., 2014).  
Despite the challenges involved in the DGGE process with the BAL samples. The results indicated 
that the Maxwell 16 method was not as efficient as Qiagen to properly extract DNA from BAL 
samples (section 6.2, Fig.17). The DGGE pictures were not sent for analysis to BioNumerics due to 
absence of suitable pictures because of contamination and negative control bands as described 
previously in the results (section 6.2).  However, it should be noted that due to the lack of time we 
were unable to repeat the DGGE experiments. During the course of this study, 3 experiments were 
performed for DGGE on this comparison, but maybe more experiments will be needed to further 
clarify the difference between methods.  
A new DNA modified extraction method (Mobio) with carrier DNA was compared with Qiagen on 
BAL samples and our results demonstrated that Qiagen provided better results. MObio extractions 
yielded fewer copies of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene than Qiagen method using q PCR. A possible 
==PAGE:49==
 
49
explanation of this could be that different beads and a different protocol was used in both the cases, 
and this probably led to poor lysis of bacteria in MObio which in turn generated low DNA.  Fig 23. 
shows quite a difference between these methods, however it should be noted that the MObio method 
appears quite inefficient when noise is taken into account. The average signal to noise ration is lower 
in MObio whereas the ratio is quiet high when Quiagen method is used. Therefore, the DGGE 
profiles bands acquired from these methods varied and didn’t provide sufficient information (section 
6.3, Fig.  21. 
In addition the microbial community profile of OVA sensitized mice and PBS treated mice were also 
compared. The results of this comparison based on DGGE analysis indicated that there was not a lot 
of difference in lung microbial floral in between these two sets of mice. This could be due to the 
presence of a lot of noise in MObio method and absence of sufficient signal strength, unavailability 
of sufficient MObio samples could also be the reason behind inconclusive results.  Unpublished data 
demonstrate the comparison of OVA sensitized mice with naive mice under vitamin D treatment and 
also without vitamin D (Barfod et al., In prep). Based on 16s sequencing, the comparison showed 
that the lung microbiota changed significantly under OVA treatment while vitamin D supplements 
gave weak correlations. OVA sensitization increased levels of Acinetobacter, Haemophilus and 
Rothia bacterial species in mice whereas Species of Acinetobacter, and Flavobacterium were 
elevated in the absence of vitamin D. Decreased levels of Pseudomonas species were also observed 
in naive male mice deficient in vitamin D. This being repeated in a ongoing experiment. It would be 
important to mention here that while DGGE profiling highlights the dominant majority of bacteria in 
a community, the 16s sequencing provides greater information about any bacteria in a community 
ranging form taxonomy to function. 
 
For future experiments, we would like to perform more experiments on DGGE such that the data 
generated could give more robust results. This would help us further clarify the different data 
generated by the two techniques, namely Qiagen, Maxwell and Mobio. We could also compare all 
the extraction methods used in this study against 16 S pyrosequencing on the samples obtained from 
BAL. DGGE and prysosequencing can together prove to be more versatile in identifying the various 
species in a particular community from a sample. 
It is imperative to the field of lung microbiota that we have robust DNA extraction methods that have 
higher yield than the one we use today, we were hoping that both Maxweelll or MObio could show 
better results. However, for future experiments more “sensitive” DNA extraction methods could be 
compared.

8. Conclusion 
 
Analysing DGGE band patterns by BioNumerics  indicated  that the autoumated extraction by 
Maxwell 16 can be used when dealing with fecal samples.  
The DGGE profiles bands acquired from these BAL samples were weaker compared to fecal samples 
and maybe this is why the BAL samples appeared less efficient compared to fecal samples. 
Additionally, we can also say that qPCR is an efficient method to quantify 16s RDNA from BAL 
samples. This study also revealed that among the three extractions methods, Qiagen method was the 
most efficient with respect to the amount of DNA obtained from the BAL Samples compared to 
PowerLyzer® Powersoil® DNA isolation kit (MoBio) and Maxwell 16.
