I 
ABSTRACT 
It is a fact that offshore wind turbines are becoming more attractive eventually not only in 
Denmark but also all around the world. Due to their relatively clean energy production and 
cheaper marginal costs, it will not be a surprise to see more offshore wind farms soon. That is 
why new researches on offshore wind energy is being conducted by many companies, 
universities and consultants. However, many wind farms are going to be decommissioned in 
the next decade when they come to their end of economic lifetime since revenues by incentives 
are decreasing while the maintenance costs are increasing year by year. Thus, alternative 
projects might be designed with existing foundations to continue to harness the wind resource 
from the existing areas.  
 
Middelgrunden Wind Farm is one of them which has been constructed 3.5 km outside of 
Copenhagen harbour in the autumn of 2000. It consists of 20 turbines and half of them are 
owned by a cooperative called Middelgrundens Vindmøllelaug which makes them world’s 
largest cooperative in terms of ownership of a wind farm. All twenty foundations for the 
turbines are gravity based and located in varying depths between 4.5 and 8.3 meters. Also, the 
turbines on the foundations are 2 MW-Bonus turbines (owned by Siemens Wind power 
afterwards) which produces around 89-90 GWh energy per year.  
 
This study aims to research alternative projects for the Middelgrunden Wind Farm after its end 
of economic lifetime (within three years). To do that, initial models for existing turbines 
including wind resource and load analysis have been created. Because, before investigating the 
alternative projects, the models should have been validated for the existing turbines and 
structures. So, the models have been created and compared to the results of the first analysis 
of Middelgrunden Wind Farm done in 2000.  
 
After creating the models and having considered limitations such as aviation rules, visual 
impact, electrical cables, alternative scenarios have been created. Then, all those scenarios 
have been investigated in terms of technical, economic and environmental feasibility. 
  
In the conclusion part, the best alternative scenario has been determined and discussed with its 
reasons to be the best solution and recommended to the Middelgrunden Wind Farm 
Cooperative as an alternative project to the Middelgrunden co-operative.

INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Offshore Wind Energy 
Offshore wind power is a term for wind turbines located in an offshore area to harness available 
wind resource. The first offshore wind farm has been installed in Vindeby, Denmark in 1991 
[1] and it is becoming more popular all around the world since higher wind speeds are available 
on offshore areas compared to land. Figure 1 illustrates the cumulative offshore wind capacity 
in the world between 2001 and 2016.   
 
Figure 1 - Global cumulative offshore capacity in MW [2] 
Europe has more than 90% of total installed offshore wind turbines in the world especially 
within shallow water depths (<30 m). However, the market is growing in US, China and Japan 
as well [3].  
 
When looking at the countries having offshore wind power, United Kingdom has the first place 
with 5156 MW while Denmark is the fourth with its 1271 MW capacity [4]. It is a fact that 
these numbers are increasing eventually and many other countries are also looking for 
opportunities to harness offshore wind power.

The project costs of offshore wind turbines are significantly higher than onshore wind turbines. 
However, there are premiums available in many countries to balance expensive costs for 
foundation, operations and maintenance and installation [5]. 
 
When deciding to investigate an offshore wind project, there are many sub-tasks to be 
considered such as, turbines, O&M, management, electrical connection and foundation. Figure 
2 illustrates the averaged cost distribution of each task for an offshore wind farm located on 
shallow sea bed (h/L<1/20). If water gets deeper (h/L≥1/20), then foundation prices become 
more expensive and keep an important place as much as the turbines.  
 
Figure 2 - Typical cost breakdown for an offshore wind plant in shallow water [5]

substructures). To minimize the costs for substructures, new concepts are being developed. 
Nowadays, the best concepts for foundations are monopiles, gravity based foundations, tripods, 
jackets and trusses. Besides, floating turbines are also being developed for deep waters.  
 
While monopile structures and gravity based foundations are very convenient and cheap for 
shallow waters, tripods and jacket structures are used for medium (transitional) water depths.

1.2 Repowering 
Repowering means replacing old turbines with modern multi-megawatt turbines to harness 
wind resource better and to decrease the cost of production [6]. Repowering of land based wind 
turbines first came into place in 1990s in Denmark and US, chased by Germany and 
Netherlands during the next years. Besides, Denmark is the first country which supports 
repowering by incentives such as feed-in-tariffs. Denmark has also the largest repowered 
capacity by 2011 with 213 MW capacity [7].  
 
There are two ways of repowering such as full repowering and partial repowering. While full 
repowering means decommissioning of whole turbine systems from the area and introducing 
a new turbine instead of that, partial repowering implies to extend the life time of a turbine 
through renewing some equipment (generator, blades, cooling system, etc.) or introducing a 
new turbine only for a few selected turbines in the wind farm. Also, full repowering becomes 
more attractive between 16.-21. year of life of turbines [8]. It has been revealed that the full 
repowering alternative is more appealing against partial repowering [7] after 20-year use. 
 
Repowering can be beneficial by increasing productivity, reducing visual impact by lowering 
the total number of turbines with higher capacity turbines on the site, decreasing O&M costs 
and decreasing the potential damage to birds and bats thanks to modern engineered turbines 
[7] [8].  It has also been found in Altamont Pass, California that the newer turbines have less 
fatality rate for raptors and for all birds by 54% and 66% respectively [9]. 
 
When looking at their noise, it can be said that modern turbine designs have reduced the 
mechanical sounds such from gearbox, etc. at the receptor point. It can make public acceptance 
of repowering easier when considering environmental impacts.  
 
Besides to above mentioned, wind turbines have a big impact on decarbonisation effect by 
having low emission values when they are being operated. However, unless they are replaced 
by newer turbines after 25 years, green generation capacity will be lower [10].  Thus, it might 
be beneficial to repower existing turbines after their 20-25 years to decrease emissions as a 
total percentage of generated capacity using modernized turbines. However, repowering comes 
with some key problems such as availability of infrastructure, planning permit and policies, 
environmental conditions, potential wake effects for neighbour wind farms, changes on

shipping routes and possible public opposition [10].  
 
Another big issue on repowering is what to do with the existing turbines. Denmark, the 
Netherlands and Germany seem they have solved this problem by selling old turbines to 
Balkans and Eastern European countries since 2003, so the second-hand market is getting 
larger after each decision of repowering [11]. However, since this is method only presents a 
temporary solution, permanent selling or re-using solutions are necessary in the near future. 
 
1.3 Middelgrunden Offshore Wind Farm 
The idea of the Middelgrunden project was born by some visionary people in Copenhagen in 
1993 although the transition process from idea to reality took seven years [12]. The first 
operation date of the wind farm was 6th of May 2001 [13]. It is located only 3.5 km outside of 
Copenhagen, Denmark and it consists of 20 turbines (Bonus, 2MW) which delivers more than 
3% of total electricity of Copenhagen today [14]. In terms of ownership of the wind farm, 
while 50% of the Middelgrunden project was owned by DONG Energy, 50 % of it is owned 
by a large cooperative. There are 8553 shareholders in the cooperative and they are mostly 
single families [15]. It is a fact that cooperatives are playing a vital role for acceptance of wind 
power due to noise and visual impact while there are more than 150,000 families are expecting 
to have some financial benefits through wind energy projects in Denmark [14]. Thus, people 
can play an active role during planning and installation phase of repowering. The turbines are 
placed with a slight curve and a distance around 180 m. The layout of the project was given on 
Figure 4 below. According to the plan, turbines from MD1 to MD10 is owned by DONG 
Energy whereas the 10 southern turbines belong to the cooperative.

All the turbines are designed for a lifetime of 25 years and the yearly production is assumed 
99 GWh per year while the guaranteed production is around 89 GWh per year. Total

cooperative is 44 GWh. Actual productions, predictions and the model analysis for the existing 
turbines will be explained and compared in the next chapters.  
 
1.4 Objectives & Methodology 
Middelgrunden Wind Farm is about to complete its economic lifetime although the foundations 
have been designed for 50 years of technical lifetime. It means that the operations and 
maintenance costs are increasing eventually which gives a reason to have less economic 
benefits each year beside to less payment. Additionally, a co-operative is not allowed to have 
debt unlike to a private company. Thus, the cooperative needs to take an action such as selling 
the turbines or considering partial or full repowering. The aim of the report is to present a best 
repowering alternative for Middelgrunden Wind Farm. All alternatives in the report were 
investigated in terms of technical, economic and environmental.  
 
Based on these, co-operative would like to hold the existing foundations on the site without 
making any changes (a reinforcement may be accepted). Also, it was preferred same energy 
capacity (40 MW) after repowering due to potential increased repowering costs by additional 
cable and grid connection problems. So, these two can be defined as limitations of the model.  
 
To determine alternatives for repowering; existing, modern, brand-new offshore wind turbines 
from leader turbine companies were researched. Then, possible layouts with those turbines 
were offered to the co-operative and best three layouts were chosen in a collaborative way.  
 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
7 
After determining alternatives according to the limitations, wind resource assessment and 
annual energy production of Middelgrunden wind farm were analysed. The logic behind this 
chapter was to create a consistent model with the actual production to calculate AEP for 
alternatives correctly. Consequently, wind resource and annual energy productions of the 
alternatives were calculated by using WAsP software and comparison of productions and wake 
losses were presented.  
 
Additionally, existing wind turbines needed to be investigated and validated to create robust 
structural wind and wave load models for the possible turbines. Thus, load analysis for existing 
turbines was made and compared to the initial load calculations of the wind farm. This is highly 
important because load calculations for alternatives were based on same formulations.  
 
Then, the strength of existing foundations was investigated for all possible alternatives and the 
need for reinforcement was researched. To calculate loads from wind, wave and currents, 
combined load analysis was implemented on MATLAB with some assumptions. The aim of 
the load analysis is to determine if the existing foundations are strong enough to take the loads 
from new turbines. When there is a need to increase the strength of the foundations, then a 
conceptual design which was created by using Rhinoceros-5, CATIA (3D design and analysis 
softwares) and AutoCAD has been suggested. However, it needs to be investigated in detail.  
 
Economic feasibility analysis aims to focus on net present value, internal rate of return and 
pay-back times of each alternatives and compares them each other by both socio-economic and 
private-economic analysis. All the implementations were made on Excel and the Excel sheets 
can be found on Appendix-C.  
 
Then, environmental impact analysis for each alternative layout were researched which include 
visual impact and avoided emissions. To create views from the coasts, Adobe Photoshop was 
used. Also, the avoided emissions part uses the output of the socio-economic analysis and the 
Excel sheet belong to avoided emissions is attached on Appendix-D. 
 
The study aims to investigate alternatives for repowering of Middelgrunden as an initial study. 
Afterwards, the co-operative can develop the models and only investigates the best alternative 
in a detailed way.

1.5 Thesis Outline 
In Chapter 2, repowering alternatives and the selection criterions are determined and the 
reasons why they are selected are explained. This chapter also includes the specifications of 
the selected turbines.  
 
The next chapter, Chapter 3 creates a wind resource model by utilizing today’s wind resource 
for the area and the production numbers. After creating a robust model for wind resource, 
energy productions of the alternatives are presented with possible additional losses and 
uncertainties.  
 
Chapter 4 discusses the structural models for the Middelgrunden and investigates whether the 
alternatives can be applied onto the existing turbines. The load model consists of current, wave 
and wind models and the chapter explains the methodology and formulation for the load model 
used for alternatives. Also, the chapter includes an alternative reinforcement to the existing 
foundations where it is necessary. 
 
Moreover, the electrical grid connection and decommissioning are discussed on Chapter 5. 
Essentially, Chapter 3, Chapter 4 and Chapter 5 can be defined as technical feasibility study 
although they are separated. 
 
Subsequently, financial analysis for each alternative is investigated in Chapter 6 to find net 
present value, levelized cost of energy, internal rate of return and pay-back times. This chapter 
aims to compare all alternatives to each other within 20-year period for private-economic 
analysis and 25-year period for socio-economic analysis.  
 
Chapter 7 reveals environmental and social impacts of each possible layout and suggests 
possible solutions.  
 
In the conclusion part, a best solution is chosen and the reasons are explained in a detailed way. 
Also, some suggestions to improve the study for further development have been made.

WIND RESOURCE ANALYSIS 
3.1 Methodology 
The aim of wind resource analysis is to determine mean wind speed, wind rose of the 
Middelgrunden area and to calculate annual energy production of alternatives. In order to do 
this, first the wind resource model has to be validated by the existing wind turbines of the 
Middelgrunden. 
 
Wind data has been studied with three different observation data from the masts of 
Middelgrunden, Risø and Lillgrund. It is a fact that different mast locations can present highly 
different results than each other. The main aim of studying with different masts is to determine 
and go on with the most reliable data set. It means that the observation data should present a 
similar mean wind speed and direction wind rose which results to the actual annual energy 
production to the Middelgrunden.  
 
During the studies, WAsP 11 (Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program) software has 
been utilized. First, the wind observations data through the meteorological masts have been 
collected then they are handled on Excel and MATLAB for having quality data sets through 
full number of years. Afterwards, the handled observed wind data from different masts were 
imported in WACA and OMWC (Observed Mean Wind Climate) and OEWC (Observed 
Extreme Wind Climate) have been created for each of masts. The purpose for the wind data is 
to a generalize wind climate from an observed wind climate. That is why, OMWC data for 
each of mast has been transformed to a generalized wind climate on WAsP.  
 
WAsP Map Editor has been utilized in order to create a study area map which includes 
roughness lines and elevation contours. Subsequently, both the map and the wind data were 
imported on WAsP. Thus, by using Bonus 2 MW turbine generator, annual energy productions 
of the wind farm have been calculated for different generalized wind climates. Then the most 
realistic one has been chosen. So, by using different WTGs (Wind Turbine Generators), 
alternatives have been investigated. In addition, there might be a need to alter air density in 
order to take the density at the mast height into account. First, the calculations were done with 
standardized air density, 1.225 kg/m3. Then, the air density was changed considering the mast 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
14 
height and the average annual air temperature into WAsP and WAT (Wind Farm Assessment 
Tool).  
 
Besides to these, there might also be needed to apply sector management for the alternatives. 
To apply sector management, first WAsP Engineering 4, then WAT have to be applied. 
Nevertheless, since the sector management is one of the final steps of wind farm analysis and 
it needs for a detailed research, it is only considered as a percentage in additional losses section. 
 
3.2 Coordinate System & Map 
First, the coordinate system was chosen as WGS 84 / UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator) 
zone 32N which is the coordinate system used in “Wake effects on Middelgrunden Wind 
Farm” for the coordinates of each individual turbine [19]. All the data and files which were 
used during this study were transferred to this coordinate system.  
 
For mapping area, it is usually recommended to take a study area of a bit more than 100 times 
the hub height of the wind turbines. In this study, all hub heights are selected as 80 m. so the 
selected area should have been larger than 4 km. for each direction. In addition, different mast 
locations can affect the map since the positions of the masts and the wind farm are not always 
close to each other as in the case of Risø. As Risø mast is placed at eastern side of the 
Middelgrunden, the map is extended on east direction.  
 
Orography map has been imported from SRTM database for the corresponding coordinates of 
the study area via WAsP Map Editor 11. Moreover, the roughness map has been created by 
using Corine 2006 data. Afterwards, they have been both combined into WAsP Map Editor. 
Height contours are represented with 10 m. intervals in addition to coastline, while the 
roughness lines are shown with 14 different lines between 0.00 and 1.00 m. The map is also 
consistent with land cover and roughness lengths according to European Wind Atlas, 1999

5.1 Cabling & Decommissioning 
Offshore wind farm cabling has mainly 3 types of connection for grid connection: 
 
1. Star connection 
2. Ring connection 
3. Central Connection 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
44 
Star connection is the type where each turbine is directly connected to the power plant. If one 
of cables belong to any turbine, other turbines are not affected which presents a robust and 
flexible solution for connection. However, when it comes to pricing, it is the most expensive 
one due to having longer cable length.  
 
When each of the two turbines located at the ends are connected to the grid, it is called ring 
connection. If a cable between two turbines breaks down, all turbines still transfer power to 
the grid. Thus, the solution is quite stable although the capacity of some turbines might be 
limited depending on where the break down is.  
 
Middelgrunden Wind Farm utilizes from central connection. The central turbine is connected 
to the power plant by two 20 MVA, three core XLPE submarine cables while all the turbines 
are serially connected to each other. This method is the cheapest solution, never though if any 
of cables between the turbines breaks down, other connected turbines will be directly affected. 
It can be said that it is the method which has least flexibility comparing to other two solutions. 
The main reason why Middelgrunden Wind Farm uses this type of connection is having smaller 
estimated costs of losing power due to break downs than extra costs for laying two separate 
cables down as in ring connection. 
 
Middelgrunden presents a good condition for cabling since the water depth is quite shallow. 
Thus, additional cable installation for Alternative-II and Alternative-III is expected to be 
cheaper comparing to deeper waters. Figure 28 shows the submarine cable connection of

possible location.

6.1 Introduction & Purpose 
To evaluate the repowering alternatives in terms of economical outputs, a feasibility research 
with sensitivity analysis has been done. Basically, economic feasibility researches if; 
1) the alternatives generate more income than they require expenses, 
2) the pay back times are acceptable and shorter than the lifetime, 
3) the company has enough capital to undertake the project, 
4) the economic risks related to returns are acceptable [33].  
 
Economic feasibility study includes both socio-economic and private-economic analysis. 
While socio-economics analysis focuses on net benefit to the society by using real terms, 
private economics researches if the project is worthwhile to be undertaken. Private economics 
uses nominal terms. The main differences are shown on Table 30 below.

Based on Table 30, it can be said that socio-economics is very simplified version of the private

not have to consider taxes price fluctuations through inflation. The aim to consider both 
approaches is to show the financial benefit of the alternatives to the undertaking companies 
(DONG & co-operative) with private concern and the total environmental benefit through 
socio-economic feasibility.  
 
Financial valuation of the alternatives is going be evaluated by “Net Present Value (NPV)”, 
“Internal Rate of Return (IRR)”, “Payback Time (PBT)” and “Levelized Cost of Energy 
(LCOE)” through cash flows. Detailed explanations and formulations for those tools can be 
found on methodology part. No matter which feasibility study is conducted, it is important to 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
50 
be consistent on the type of money used in the analyses. It means that, both real and nominal 
values cannot be used on a study. Also, real values should belong to the same year, so the 
conversions are needed.  
 
Cash flow based feasibility refers to net movement of money inside (as plus) or outside (as 
minus) of a project or a business within a period. This period is selected as whole year in this 
study. While expenses such as investment, operations & maintenance costs and taxes (for 
private economics) are defined as minus, income through revenue is defined as plus. Then, the 
net number belong to each year are found.  
 
There are basically 3 types of cash flows in feasibility assessment [33]: 1) Cash flow operations 
(i.e. revenues from sales, paid taxes, labour costs, material costs), 2) Cash flow from 
investments (i.e. initial and later occurring capital expenditures for plant, equipment and 
intangible assets), 3) Cash flow from financing (i.e. resource by equity and/or loans, their 
repayments, dividends to shareholders). Because the loan and interest payments, incoming 
cash from equity or loans are not matter of this study, cash flow from financing is excluded. 
Thus, the studies are typically based on “Free Cash Flows”.

6.4 Assumptions 
Life time is one of the direct and most important elements while calculating the revenue. Since 
most of the wind farms are designed for 20 years, it is possible to extend their lifetimes to 25 
years by technical improvements. However, the additional costs affect the project largely after 
its economic lifetime around 20 years. This economic feasibility study uses 20-year lifetime 
economic life time and 25-year for technical lifetime. All the scenarios for the sensitivity 
analysis and feasibility studies are based on the initial energy production would be at the 
beginning of 2021, while the decommissioning and the replacement of new turbines will be 
between 2020 and 2021.  
 
Power production for the alternative projects is based on WAsP calculations and the rough 
yearly energy productions with 93 % total park efficiency for the three alternatives are 115.864, 
120.230 and 122.986 GWh respectively.  
 
OPEX is operating expenditures and it is generally defined by per kWh or MWh in wind energy 
industry. According to the European Wind Energy Association [35], O&M cost for 
Middelgrunden is assumed as 12 €/MWh (2006) based on real accounts. Thus, this amount 
would be utilized for the feasibility analysis.

well as an assumption of installation costs. Average wind turbine price in Denmark was 1147

6.6 Sensitivity Analysis  
The aim of sensitivity analyses is to “determine how different values of an independent 
variable impact a particular dependent variable under a given set of assumptions” [45]. 
Through the sensitivity analyses, it is aimed to observe the effects of certain inputs on the 
outputs. By doing that, uncertainties and risks of the model can be evaluated. 
 
Risks are often categorized in four ways: political risks, economic risks, social risks and 
technical risks. However, this study only concerns economic related risks. Economic related 
risks might be about interest rates, credit risk, currency, labour, land lease, imbalances, support, 
renegotiation, insurance premium, etc. Sensitivity analysis investigates a few of them by 
creating alternative scenarios which are listed below: 
1) CAPEX through decommissioning lower (A) and upper limits (B), 
2) Private discount rate lowest (A) and highest values (B) (5 and 10 %), 
3) Reinforcement of foundations to the second and the third alternatives. 
4) No incentives at all (only market price)

7.5 Decommissioning & Re-use of Materials 
Re-use of the materials is a key for decommissioning if the project aims high sustainability. 
Nacelles, blades and hubs can either be sold as assemblies or as components to other countries 
(or to the other wind farms to be built in Denmark) and re-installed. It can be also possible to 
incinerate glass fiber or plastic materials for energy purposes. That is why, before 
decommissioning action phase, it is must to decide what to do with the nacelles, blades and 
hubs since finding a storage place for them could be hard and expensive. So, all the agreements 
either on recycling or selling should be done before decommissioning of the wind farm.  
Nonetheless, re-use of towers is not attractive as nacelles and blades. That is why, all steel 
structures including towers should be recycled by re-melted. Also, if the foundations will be 
removed from the area, then they can either be re-used on another location (if ballast lifting 
method used), or they can be used as secondary construction materials. At this point, storage 
of the foundations can be quite expensive unless they are going to be directly moved to a place 
to be used again. As it was mentioned earlier, foundations were constructed into B&W Dry 
Dock, so they can be stored there which is the closest place to the Middelgrunden. However, 
storing and waiting for re-use would be highly expensive. So, another feasibility study for the 
storage cost should be added onto the economic feasibility analysis. If it is found not feasible 
to move them with ballast lifting method and to store, then they can be easily broken 
underwater and the residuals can be gathered and moved to the land by ships. 
It is also important to mention about the marine growth around the foundations. Since the 
foundations cause to have a new marine environment around them, it might be ecological 
destruction to remove the foundations from the area. That is why, beside to economic issues, 
it will be environmentalist action to leave the non-used foundations on the Middelgrunden 
area.

CHAPTER 8 
CONCLUSION & SUGGESTIONS 
In the thesis, repowering alternatives of Middelgrunden wind farm has been examined through 
feasibility studies. Three alternatives were selected among eight in a collaboration with the 
wind farm co-operative through criterions such as aviation height restriction line, good visual 
impact and same capacity (40 MW) as it is now. Finalized alternatives are Vestas V90- 2 MW, 
Gamesa G128, 5MW and Siemens SWT-4.0-120.  
 
Technical feasibility consists of three different chapters as wind resource and energy output 
estimations, load analysis and electrical connection including decommissioning. After 
investigating and determining technical capabilities and limits of alternative models, economic 
feasibility analysis has been researched. Moreover, the final chapter investigated the 
alternatives in terms of environmental feasibility.  
 
The wind resource data from Risø, Middelgrunden and Lillgrund have revealed that even the 
official study regarding Middelgrunden wind farm power estimation contradicts with other 
masts. That is why most realistic one has been selected by considering actual approximate 
energy output and the prevailing wind direction. Having determined the wind resource data, 
wind energy outputs from the alternatives have been estimated. According to the models, it 
was found that Alternative-III presents the highest wind energy production while Alternative-
I has the lowest production. In addition, when looking at the capacity factors although 
Alternative-II seems having higher production than Alternative-I, Alternative-I has higher 
capacity factor as it has 38 MW total wind farm capacity. Wind resource analysis has also 
considered sensitivity analysis and uncertainties and it is important to emphasize that all the 
alternatives has 80-meter hub height and extending hub height makes significant increase on 
the productions of all alternatives. Thus, if it is possible to get a permission on extending the 
hub height of the wind turbines, it would be better to harness more wind energy. Limitation of 
turbine height due to airport restricts the production capacity of the wind farm indeed. 
 
Having revealed the wind energy productions of the alternatives, load analysis has been done. 
Load analysis has investigated if the foundations are strong enough to resist to the loads from 
the alternative turbines. The strength analysis included the loads from wave, current and wind. 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
70 
While creating both wave-current and wind models, both non-linear (so called unsteady for 
wind) and linear (so called steady for wind) models have been utilized. According to 
comparison with the models of Carl Bro (engineering company worked on foundations of the 
Middelgrunden), linearized wave and wind models have been selected to go with. Based on 
the linear combined wave-current and wind model, it was found that while the first alternative 
can be directly placed onto the existing foundations, Alternative-II absolutely needs supporting 
structures or reinforcement on the foundations. Also, since Alternative-III is just under the 
limit, then it might be a need to support it as well. Beside to calculations, a simple supporting 
structure concrete-steel model has been added into the chapter as a suggestion if it is decided 
to repower with Alternative-II or Alternative-III. 
 
Electrical grid connection and decommissioning part has researched the actions to be done for 
the repowering alternatives. Since the thesis has only investigated repowering with same total 
capacity as before, there is no need to remove the cables from Amager power station till the 
turbines. However, since some of the turbines will not be used anymore, they should be cut 
from the system. Basically, there are two methods have been recommended for used and non-
used foundations. For non-used foundations, the cables can be cut from underwater near the 
foundations and be re-connected. So, the cables inside non-used foundations would be useless. 
Also, for used-foundations, it can be possible to use the cables until the foundation top by 
keeping existing tower and cables until the top of the foundation and connecting the new tower 
with a flange around the existing tower. 
 
Economic feasibility has researched if the alternatives are investable through some critical 
economic terms such as net present value, levelized cost of energy, internal rate of return and 
simple and discounted payback time. Economic feasibility has investigated the alternatives in 
terms of both socio-economically and private-economically with some sensitivity analyses 
since there has been so many assumptions. When looking at the results of the private-economic 
analysis, it is seen that Alternative-I is the most advantageous one since it is the only one which 
does not surely need extra structural support. However, if there is no any need for a support 
leg for the other alternatives, then they can bring more economic profit. Another crucial result 
from the economic analysis is the importance of support payments. If there is no economic 
support on offshore wind, then all the alternatives become unfeasible. Thus, before giving a 
decision for repowering, support system needs to be clarified.  
 
==PAGE:80==
 
 
71 
Environmental feasibility study has revealed the visual impacts of the alternatives. According 
to visual study of the alternatives with comparison to the Middelgrunden wind farm, 
Alternative-I presented the best view as it saves the curved shape of the original design and it 
has the lowest tip height comparing to the others. However, Alternative-II and Alternative-III 
dominated the view looking from the coast. So, Alternative-I has been decided as the best 
option in terms of visualization. Environmental feasibility also investigated the avoided CO2 
emissions and it was concluded that all the alternatives saves around 100,000 tons of CO2 per 
year increasing with the energy production.  
 
As a result, Alternative-I has been decided to go with since: 
 
• It produces approximately 116 GWh per year which is 30 % higher than today’s 
production. 
• It does not have regulatory problems in Denmark as Gamesa might face since there has 
not been any Gamesa turbine installed in Denmark before. 
• It can be directly placed onto the existing foundations without any additional supports, 
• Its electrical grid connection will be very similar to the existing system, so there might 
not be needed to cut the cables underwater except of removing Turbine-20 from the 
system, 
• It is economically more feasible comparing to the others since there will not be any 
extra foundation support cost, 
• It has the best visual impact with its lowest tip height and full curved layout although 
one of the turbines cannot be used because of aviation height rules. 
 
For the future studies, in order to improve the study, it is recommended to: 
 
• To get the precise and the updated aviation height restriction line form the airport,  
• To study the wind resource with closest mast and higher quality data, 
• To model strength analysis with dynamic loads from the turbines (aero-elastic model), 
• To investigate the economic analysis with narrower and more precise estimations, 
• To know exact permissions regarding to decommissioning and residual foundations 
(so, the visualization can be improved).$

function k=wavenumber(f,h,g) 
k=fzero(@(k) ((2*pi*f).^2-(g*k*tanh(k*h))),((2*pi*f)^2/g)); 
return 
 
function [F_tot_drag_Wheeler,F_tot_inertia_Wheeler,F_tot_Wheeler, Moment_Wheeler]=WheelerForce(f,h,g,D,a,iWheeler) 
global H_s omega k x t ita rho Cd Cm z_still_water A h 
  
z_still_water=zeros(1,length(ita)); 
  
for i=1:length(t) 
z_calc(:,i)=linspace(-h,ita(:,i),iWheeler);  
z_Wheeler(:,i)=linspace(-h,0,iWheeler);  
  
u_Wheeler(:,i)=omega*H_s/2.*(cosh(k.*(z_Wheeler(:,i)+h))./sinh(k*h))*cos(omega*t(:,i)-k*x)+2.5*(1+z_calc(:,i)/h).^(1/7); %horizontal 
velocity [m/s] 
du_Wheeler(:,i)=-omega*omega*H_s/2.*(cosh(k.*(z_Wheeler(:,i)+h))./sinh(k*h))*sin(omega*t(:,i)-k*x); %horizontal acceleration [m/s] 
F_drag_Wheeler(:,i)=0.5*rho*Cd.*D(i).*abs(u_Wheeler(:,i)).*u_Wheeler(:,i); 
F_inertia_Wheeler(:,i)=rho*Cm.*A(i).*du_Wheeler(:,i); 
F_Wheeler(:,i)=F_drag_Wheeler(:,i)+F_inertia_Wheeler(:,i); 
FMoment_Wheeler(:,i)=F_Wheeler(:,i) .* (z_Wheeler(:,i) + h); 
Moment_Wheeler(i)=trapz(z_calc(:,i),FMoment_Wheeler(:,i)); 
end 
  
for i=1:length(ita) 
F_tot_drag_Wheeler(i)=trapz(z_calc(:,i),F_drag_Wheeler(:,i)); 
F_tot_inertia_Wheeler(i)=trapz(z_calc(:,i),F_inertia_Wheeler(:,i)); 
F_tot_Wheeler(i)=trapz(z_calc(:,i),F_Wheeler(:,i)); 
  
end 
end 
 
function [V_rel,F_wind]=Fwind(V_hub,rho_air,D_rotor,xdot_hub); 
  
A_rotor=pi*(D_rotor/2)^2; %[m^2] 
  
for i=1:length (V_hub) 
  
V_Table=4:1:25; 
CT_Table= [0.855 0.856 0.808 0.852 0.869 0.81 0.755 0.678 0.583 0.51 0.438 0.382 0.337 0.301 0.271 0.246 0.224 0.206 0.191 0.1799 
0.167 0.157]; 
  
C_T(i)=interp1(V_Table,CT_Table,V_hub(i),'linear'); 
  
V_rel(i)=V_hub(i)-xdot_hub; %[m/s] 
F_wind(i)=0.5*rho_air*A_rotor*C_T(i)*V_rel(i)*abs(V_rel(i)); 
end 
 
function [amplitude,alfa,Sjs,frequency,l]=jonswap(gammaJS,Hs_50,Tp_50,Tdur,alpha ) 
global Tdur f0 df frequency omega x k sigma Spm Fn jonswap_ita 
  
l=length(frequency); 
  
for i=1:l; 
if frequency <= f0 
sigma=0.07; 
else 
sigma=0.09; 
end 
alfa(i)=exp(-((frequency(i)-f0)^2)./(2*(sigma^2)*((f0)^2))); 
Spm(i) = 5*Hs_50^2/(16*Tp_50^4*frequency(i)^5)... 
*exp(-5/4*(frequency(i)*Tp_50)^-4); 
Fn = (5*(0.065*gammaJS^0.803+0.135))^-1; 
Sjs(i) = Fn * Spm(i) * gammaJS^alfa(i);  
amplitude(i) = sqrt(2*Sjs(i)*df); %wave amplitude 
for ii=1:Tdur+1 
jonswap_ita(ii,i)= amplitude(i).*cos(omega(i).* ii - k(i)*x); 
end 
end 
end
