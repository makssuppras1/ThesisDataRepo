Abstract
Robotic grasping has evolved beyond repetitive tasks to more complex manipulations re­
quiring adaptability in unstructured environments. While vision and tactile sensing en­
hance robotic dexterity, challenges persist in processing sensory data and designing
adaptive controllers for diverse objects and conditions.
This project develops a grasping controller for a two­finger parallel gripper that operates
without prior knowledge of object properties, relying only on real­time tactile feedback.
A robotic arm and a gripper with tactile sensors (Graspian ApS) are used to implement
and evaluate the controller. The grasping controller transitions through predefined grasp­
ing phases, using tactile data to detect key events such as initial contact and slippage,
enabling real­time grip adjustments.
The system’s performance is evaluated through two experimental tests involving eight
different objects. The results reveal inconsistencies in the initial grip, with contact val­
ues varying between iterations, leading to uneven grip pressure. However, the system
reliably detects slippage and adjusts the grip, effectively preventing object drops. These
findings highlight the potential of tactile­based control while exposing challenges in force
estimation and grasp consistency.
This work serves as a basis for improving tactile sensing and adaptive control, with future
efforts focusing on enhancing sensor reliability, refining force estimation, and optimizing
slip detection for more stable and adaptive grasping.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Automation is not only becoming more prevalent in society but also more sophisticated.
Once simple machines designed for repetitive tasks, robots are now designed with capa­
bilities that mirror human cognitive functions. This change is not merely a testimony of
technological progress, but a response to the growing demand for efficiency, safety and
precision in industries that once relied heavily on human labor.
As time has gone on, technology has advanced, and people have become more and
more motivated to delegate repetitive, risky, or precision­intensive tasks. As a result,
the robotics sector has grown and evolved, performing tasks like pick­and­place, surgical
assistance, safe handling of food and material, autonomous driving, and conducting space
exploration or rescue missions.
The incorporation of visual data is a crucial component of these developments, as it has
significantly enhanced robots’ perception of their surroundings. Thanks to cameras and
image processing technologies, robots can now accurately identify objects, evaluate their
surroundings and navigate unfamiliar environments. With this visual capability, robots are
becoming closer to human senses, allowing them to perform tasks more accurately and
adjust their actions in response to real­time visual information.
Building on the foundation of visual data, the emergence of tactile sensors has brought
robotic capabilities even closer to human dexterity [1]. Robots can now modify their ob­
ject manipulation strategies (deliberate interaction with objects to change their state) and
grip strength using tactile sensors, which provide vital information about characteristics
such as object weight, texture, stiffness and contact. This degree of sensitivity is essen­
tial for jobs requiring skilled manipulation, as it greatly reduces the possibility of slipping
or damaging objects and improves the reliability of robotic operations. Additionally, the
combination of tactile and visual data in manipulator robots increases task efficiency and
safety, while creating new opportunities for automation in complex and unfamiliar envi­
ronments [2].
Manipulating objects is a challenging journey. Unlike humans, who can quickly observe
and perceive their environment and instinctively know how to interact with it, robots face a
much steeper learning curve. For a human being, performing simple tasks such as filling
a glass of water, picking up a piece of paper or opening a refrigerator are effortless thanks
to their prior knowledge of objects and how to interact with them. They also have continu­
ous feedback on these interactions through sensory stimuli such as sight and touch. For
a robot, however, every action is a result of a complex process consisting of continuously
taking data from the environment, analyzing it, making a decision and finding a way to
carry it out. The variety of shapes, textures, weights, sizes and materials of objects in­
crease the complexity of robot movements, making object manipulation, especially object
grasping, a challenging task with a low level of adaptability to uncertainties and unknown
environments.
Even though some of these obstacles have been resolved or scaled down as a result of
ongoing advancements in gripper and sensors design as well as the use of Artificial Intel­
ligence (AI), accurately and reliably grasping complex­shaped or delicate objects remains
a challenge.

According to Tong Li et al. [1], robotic grasping can be divided into four stages: grasping
generation, planning, grasping state discrimination and grasping destabilization adjust­
ment (Figure 1.1). The first one is in charge of getting information on the target and ob­
taining the optimal pose of the gripper to grasp the target, also known as optimal grasping
point (OGP), it involves target recognition and target pose estimation but it can also in­
clude grasping force estimation if possible. The second one is focused on planning how
to approach the target. Following, the grasping state discrimination stage is in charge of
assessing the grasping process, which is highly relevant for reliable grasp control, and it
can be divided into grasping stability prediction and grasping slip detection. The last stage
is a consequence of the previous one and it is responsible for the needed adjustment to
provide a secure grasp [1].
Figure 1.1: Overview of the robotic grasping process. Figure inspired on [1].
This project will go through the four aforementioned phases, emphasizing the assessment
of object grasping and identifying any necessary adjustments.
1.2
Problem Statement and Research Questions
While visual data has made relevant advances in robotic grasping possible, including
precise object recognition and positioning, it is still unable to directly evaluate factors like
force or physical contact with the object. This limitation is noticeable when attempting to
detect object slippage, determining the proper force for grasping or verifying that an object
has been securely grasped. These flaws are significant, particularly in industrial settings
where robotic grasping safety, sensitivity and accuracy are critical.
In many real­world applications, detailed information about an object’s properties (e.g.,
complete geometry or object type) may be incomplete, unreliable, or completely absent
due to problems such as poor lighting, occlusions, or sensor limitations. In these cases,
relying purely on visual data can lead to non­optimal grasping strategies. In contrast,
tactile sensors provide localized, real­time information about contact conditions and force
dynamics. Although tactile data does not provide a complete picture of the overall proper­
ties of an object, it can be a key source of information when visual details are insufficient
[3]. This raises important questions about how to effectively exploit tactile information,
either alone or in combination with visual data, to achieve optimal grasping.
Thus, the following research questions arise:
• How can tactile sensing aid to perform optimal grasping without prior information of
an object’s properties (such as weight or material)?

• How can the performance of robotic grasping using tactile sensing be assessed?
1.3
Research Goals and Methods
This project aims to determine the grasping capabilities of the Intelligent Tactile Gripper
built by Graspian ApS by developing a new gripper controller. The proposed controller
dynamically adjusts the grip based on real­time tactile and motor feedback. In this way,
the objective is to determine whether the gripper can achieve optimal robotic grasping
through this control method.
In summary the goals are:
• Perform a literature study of topics relevant to the thesis work.
• Develop a controller for the gripper based on tactile data.
• Test and validate the performance of the new controller on a specific task.
• Formulate conclusions about the grasping capabilities of the Intelligent Tactile Grip­
per by Graspian.
1.4
Thesis Outline
The subsequent chapters are organized as follows. Chapter 2, Literature Review, pro­
vides a concise review of the literature on sensory perception in robotic grasping, focusing
on recent studies on grasp point detection and grasp control techniques using tactile and
visual data. It also introduces a human­inspired control methodology that serves as the
foundation for the project’s system implementation.
The following Chapter 3, System Overview, gives an overview of the system components
used in the project, including the gripper, sensors, supporting hardware and objects used
for testing. Chapter 4, Design and Implementation, details the design and implementa­
tion process, covering tactile analysis, system integration, control strategy and testing
procedures. The experimental results and evaluation of the controller performance are
described in Chapter 5, Results.
Finally, the last two chapters, Discussion and Conclusion, summarize the overall findings
and limitations, address the research questions, and highlight potential areas for future
work.

Literature Review
This chapter analyzes robotic grasping by giving an overview of the various tactile sen­

2.1
Sensory Perception for Robotic Graspings
2.1.1
Overview of tactile sensors
As has been seen in the introduction, the appearance of tactile sensors in the robotics field
has opened a window of new and improved possibilities for robotic grasping. Therefore,
this section explains why tactile sensors are important in this field and reviews some of
the most common types.
Tactile sensors are considered components that can measure the tactile properties of ob­
jects through physical contact between the sensors and the objects [4]. These properties
can include pressure, force, temperature, vibrations, material and texture, although not
all properties can be detected by a single sensor. Furthermore, the type and placement
of these sensors can vary based on the gripper used and the purpose of the application.
For instance, a parallel­jaw gripper used for pick­and­place tasks may have tactile sen­
sors only on the fingertips, whereas a more complex robotic hand might feature sensors
on both the fingers and joints or on the palm.
The use of tactile sensors allows achieving accurate object identification and stable object
handling, which means not only maintaining a good grip but also avoiding potential slip or
object damage.
Types of tactile sensors
A well­structured classification by Tong Li et al., further refined by Mahmoud Meribout
et al., categorizes tactile sensors according to their working principles into capacitive,
piezoelectric, piezoresistive and visual­tactile types [1][5].
• Capacitive sensors detect changes in capacitance caused by the deformation of
conductive plates separated by a dielectric material. When an external force is ap­
plied, the distance between the plates changes, altering the capacitance, which is
then measured to determine the applied force or pressure. These sensors provide
high sensitivity and fast response times; however, they are susceptible to environ­
mental factors such as humidity and can produce errors when interacting with metal
objects.
• Piezoelectric tactile sensors use materials that generate an electrical charge in re­
sponse to mechanical stress (external force). A charge amplifier is used to convert
the electrical charges into an output voltage proportional to the pressure. These sen­
sors are stable, highly durable, and respond quickly to dynamic signals. However,
they are less suitable for measuring static forces and can be sensitive to temperature
variations.
• Piezoresistive sensors measure changes in the electrical resistance of a material
due to mechanical deformation. When pressure is applied, the internal structure of

are relatively simple in design and have good spatial resolution and high sensitivity.
However, they may exhibit hysteresis and temperature sensitivity, which can affect

sensor [6], which is widely used in robotic applications.
• Lastly, visual­tactile sensors combine cameras, lighting systems and soft materi­
als to capture detailed contact information when an object is grasped. Instead of
relying on electrical signals (like capacitive or piezoresistive sensors), they use tac­
tile images to detect surface deformations and apply computer vision techniques to
extract tactile properties. These sensors provide high­resolution tactile feedback,
force estimation and surface texture analysis. However, they require advanced im­
age processing and high manufacturing costs.
2.1.2
Integration of tactile and vision sensors
Vision sensors, together with advanced image and data processing techniques, play a
key role in enabling robots to operate autonomously. Analogous to human vision, robots
rely on these sensors to interpret their environment, identify objects, estimate distances
and determine target properties such as size, shape and orientation. These capabilities
are critical for tasks such as object detection, classification and identification of grasping
points, which improve motion planning and interaction with the environment.
However, despite the versatility of vision systems, their effectiveness is limited by fac­
tors such as poor lighting, occlusions and the inability to obtain real­time interaction in­
formation. These limitations can make robotic grasping tasks based on visual sensing
considerably more difficult, especially in dynamic or messy environments. To address
these challenges, the integration of tactile and visual sensors has emerged as a robust
solution, inspired by human sensory modalities [7]. Humans combine visual data for con­
text and object recognition with tactile information for accurate interaction and adaptation.
Similarly, robots can also benefit from this integration. During grasping, vision provides
the initial data for object identification, localization and estimation of gripper positioning.
As the task progresses, tactile sensors refine this understanding by providing real­time
information on contact, sliding and force distribution [8].
The advantages of tactile sensing are most evident when vision encounters some of its
limits. They also stand out in the post­contact phases, where they allow the robot to
make fine adjustments based on real­time feedback. This capability is crucial for handling
objects with diverse properties, such as fragile or heavy items and irregularly shaped
objects [9].
A recent study by Qian Mao et al. [9] presents a robotic framework that combines visual
and tactile sensors to enhance grasping performance. The framework utilizes vision to
determine the optimal gripper positioning and relies on tactile feedback during execution
to detect slippage. This feedback enables the robot to react dynamically by adjusting
the grasping force or maintaining the current grasping state, significantly improving task
reliability.
In another example [8], visual­tactile fusion improves results by addressing the challenge
of correctly detecting and grasping transparent objects with vision sensors, even in dy­
namic and complex backgrounds. The framework combines a vision­based convolutional
neural network (CNN) for detecting grasping points, together with tactile modules for real­
time feedback, height sensing and position adjustment. In addition, the authors developed
a classification algorithm that combines visual and tactile data. As noted in the paper, the

2.2
Grasping Point
This section focuses on the initial stage of the robotic grasping process described in 1.1.
A brief overview of grasping generation is provided, along with a review of recent research
and innovations in this area.
Grasping generation involves two primary steps crucial for successful robotic interaction
with objects [1]:
• Object recognition: The first step is to identify the object and determine its geo­
metric properties along with other relevant features. This initial recognition is critical,
as it serves as the basis for the subsequent steps in the grasping process.
• Object pose estimation: The second step is to estimate the object’s pose and
subsequently determine the optimal grasping strategy, which includes the grasping
points and the gripper configuration. For example, for a two­finger parallel gripper,
this step determines the gripper opening width and the orientation of the gripper with
respect to the object or another coordinate frame. The grasping posture is key to
ensure stable and safe manipulation of the object and facilitates the execution of
more complex tasks.
One of the challenges researchers encounter in grasping generation includes model gen­
eralization, which involves developing a model that can accurately determine the optimal
grasping pose across a wide variety of objects [10], this is necessary if the ultimate goal
is to be effective in real­world applications. Another challenge is the gripper configura­
tion, as it significantly influences the grasping capabilities. For example, a robotic hand
with multiple fingers offers different functionalities and challenges compared to a simpler
two­finger parallel gripper. The complexity of the gripper can limit or enhance grasping
strategy and execution. On top of this, there is the challenge of environmental factors,
as visual sensors introduce additional challenges such as varying lighting conditions and
occlusions, which can obscure critical object details and complicate the detection of an
accurate grasping pose [11].
There are several studies that rely only on visual perception. For example, C. Wang et
al. [12] developed DenseFusion, a deep learning framework designed to estimate 6D
poses of known objects from RGB­D images. It uses a dense pixel­wise feature fusion
network to combine the features extracted from RGB and depth data and it also includes
an iterative pose refinement module to correct errors from initial predictions. Experimental
results show a better performance than previous methods that process RGB and depth
data separately.
The work of Dongeon Kim et al. [13] presents an algorithm for stable grasping of multiple
objects using deep neural networks (DNNs) and RGB­D data. The system first identi­
fies objects using a camera and classifies them according to their geometry and center
of mass. Next, optimal grasping points are obtained by applying probability­based cost
functions to evaluate the stability of the grasping. Finally, a DNN predicts the best grip
height and width, ensuring a stable grip adapted to the shape of the object.
Recently, tactile sensing has been explored as a primary or complementary approach
for object recognition and grasping pose estimation. The studies by Shoujie Li et al. [8]
and Qian Mao et al. [9], presented in the previous section (2.1.2), are good examples of
integrating visual and tactile sensors to improve grip accuracy and adaptability.

2.3
Grasp Control
Grasp control is essential in robotics, as it is present in all stages of robotic grasping,
enabling precise manipulation of objects in various applications. This section explores
two fundamental aspects of grasp control: force estimation and slip detection, focusing
on the integration of tactile sensors and advanced algorithms to improve robotic grasping
performance.
2.3.1
Force estimation
Grasping an object, whether by humans or robots, requires thoughtful consideration of the
object’s properties. This not only includes decisions about the number of fingers needed
for a secure grasp or the placement of those fingers but also the force needed to safely
grasp and manipulate the object. In robotic applications, in particular, this stage involves
the critical step of force estimation, which differs significantly from setting a default force
for a predefined task, a common practice in many industrial applications. While default
forces can be effective for routine tasks with known variables, dynamic force estimation is
essential for manipulating a wider range of objects and scenarios, where properties such
as weight, shape, size and material can vary significantly.
The importance of grip force estimation has long existed in the field of robotic grasping re­
search, but recent advances in tactile sensors have increased interest in this area. These
sensors enable new approaches to overcome existing challenges associated with grip
force estimation. Key challenges include operating in complex and unstructured environ­
ments, manipulating a wide range of known or unknown objects, and understanding the
dynamic interactions between grippers and different objects.
The most common methods for grasping force estimation with tactile perception presented
by Tong Li et al. [1] fall between two categories: physical model and machine learning.
The first focuses on the physical model of both the gripper and the object and the inter­
actions between them, therefore having a good understanding and accurate information
about both components is needed. In contrast, machine learning models shift attention
away from the physical behavior of the elements and prioritize data from tactile sensors
to map and predict applied grip forces [1].
Depending on the application, the previous knowledge of the object and gripper tool and
the available resources, there are numerous methods to estimate grasping forces.
For general­purpose tasks, Balakumar Sundaralingam et al. [14] present a robust map­
ping of tactile sensor signals to force using a neural network­based approach. This im­
plementation addresses some of the limitations of previous work, related to limited data
sources to validate performance and robustness in mapping tactile signal and force across
different tasks. The model proposed uses SynTouch BioTac sensors that detect contact
forces, micro­vibrations and temperature. Its implementation incorporates a neural net­
work with voxelized input features for spatial data representation and a regularization loss
function to ensure physically realistic force predictions. The training data comes from
diverse setups, including robotic interaction with a rigid ball and planar pushing tasks,
enhancing generalization across different scenarios. Moreover, the results obtained from
validating the model through real­world experiments demonstrate strong performance and
significant improvements over previous methods using the same SynTouch BioTac sen­
sors [14].
For specific tasks like safety handling, [15] introduces a framework for robotic grasping
that dynamically computes and regulates the optimal grasping force using a combination
of tactile sensing, force estimation and Control Barrier Functions (CBFs). The use of elec­
tromagnetic tactile sensors provides data on fingertip deformation, which enables contact

2.3.2
Slip detection
Once an object is securely grasped, the next challenge is to move it. In order to maintain
the stability of the grasp over time, it is essential to monitor the grip to detect slippage
of the object due to inadequate or excessive gripping force, incorrect positioning of the
fingers, or external disturbances [1]. Real­time slip detection is essential as it allows the
gripper to adjust its grip dynamically to avoid dropping or mishandling an object.
This step, along with the one described above, accounts for much of the increased in­
terest in tactile sensors as it has opened up a wider window of possibilities and greater
accuracy than the use of vision alone. Although visual data, such as the shape, type
and position of the object, are very valuable during the pre­grasp phases, they may fail
to detect slippage due to occlusions of the robot itself. On the other hand, tactile sensors
provide detailed data on the physical interaction between the robot and the object, such
as texture, hardness and force distribution, which are critical for effective slip detection.
[1].
Current methodologies for detecting slippage vary. As stated by Tong Li et al. [1], ap­
proaches based on mechanical analysis, while effective, require in­depth knowledge of
object dynamics and sophisticated modeling techniques. Feature extraction and pattern
recognition strategies offer versatility in different scenarios, but face challenges related
to data robustness and comprehensiveness. Machine learning techniques, especially ef­
fective, require extensive training data to accurately classify sliding events. And finally,
tactile­visual fusion methods address some of the individual shortcomings of sensors by
fusing features obtained with each type of sensor, improving the accuracy of slip detection
[1].
For instance, Zhen Deng et al. [16] developed a framework that allows automatic grasp­
ing force adjustment for unknown objects using a robotic arm and tactile information. This
system not only focuses on slip detection but also includes force grasp estimation and
grasping stabilization. Slip detection is handled within the online detection module, which
employs a DNN to classify contact events (non­contact, contact, or slip) while simulta­
neously predicting the object’s material (e.g., paper, foam, plastic, or metal). The DNN
processes multi­modal tactile data from BioTac sensors including force, pressure, and
micro­vibrations, capturing real­time interactions. The model is trained on a dataset of
grasping interactions, learning spatio­temporal patterns that distinguish slip from stable
grasps. Using convolutional layers for feature extraction and fully connected layers for
classification, the network accurately detects slip conditions, enabling the robotic system
to adjust its grasping force dynamically [16].
Another system presented by Moaed A Abd et al. [17] uses the same tactile sensors.
In this case, a novel slip detection method classifies the sliding direction of an object in
real­time using artificial neural networks (ANNs) and tactile sensors. The ANN is trained
to classify the interaction between a robotic arm and the objects into 6 states: touch, no
touch, and four sliding directions (North, South, East and West). Furthermore, based on
this classification, the robotic hand dynamically adjusts the grip force. Inspired by the
human grip response to the sliding direction of an object, if the sliding is detected down­
wards (towards the ground), the robotic hand increases the grip force to prevent the object

2.3.3
Human­inspired robotic grasping method
In object grasping research, there is a clear trend to explore human­inspired methods.
By studying human interactions with objects, researchers focus on understanding how
humans sense and adapt position, pressure and grasping movements to optimally ma­
nipulate different objects, as well as how they react to different external factors, such as
temperature or object material, or to collisions or slippage of the object. The goal is to
translate these complex and sophisticated human behaviors to robotic systems to improve
their functionality in complex environments or with diverse objects, thereby increasing both
the safety and efficiency of automated tasks [9].
A key study in this field is the one presented by Johansson and Flanagan [18], which pro­
vides an overview of tactile signaling in human­object manipulation. This study divides the
process of picking up and placing an object into distinct phases (e.g., reach, load, lift, hold,
replace, and unload) and associates specific tactile afferents (nerve receptors in the skin
that respond when the hand interacts with objects) with each phase. For example, during
the reach and unload phases, fast­adapting afferents (FA­I and FA­II), which are sensitive
to high­frequency dynamic skin deformations and transient vibrations, are predominantly
active. In contrast, during the load phase, slow­adapting afferents (SA­I), which respond
to low­frequency and sustained deformations, help determine the appropriate force for
grasping [18].
Building on this, J. M. Romano et al. [19] demonstrate how tactile sensing, combined with
position and force control, can be used to develop a human­inspired grasping controller.
This approach serves as the basis for the tactile sensor algorithm developed in this project.
Therefore, the key concepts from this work are outlined in the following section.
System and experimental setup
The experimental setup used in [19] is based on the Willow Garage PR2 robotic platform,
of which the two 7­degree­of­freedom (DoF) arms and the two 1­DoF parallel­jaw grippers
are used. The grippers are actuated by brushless DC motors (without direct force sensors,
relying instead on current sensing for motor effort estimation) and are equipped with a
digital accelerometer in the palm and pressure sensors in the fingertips, consisting of
22 individual sensing cells (with 15 located on the gripping surface) that measure the
perpendicular compressive force applied on them.
The system uses the Robot Operating System (ROS), an open­source robotic middleware
that uses software frameworks for robot software development, for the experiments with
the robot.

Experimental validation focuses on demonstrating that the implemented control strategy
reliably translates tactile feedback into efficient grasping performance. Key experiments
include tuning critical parameters such as KSLIP and KHARDNESS, which regulate the
dynamic response of the system during loading and slip correction. Other tests, such

needed and, in some cases, force estimation is not reliable. Although the application
demonstrates significant potential, it could benefit from the integration of additional sen­
sor modalities to provide richer information for more refined control.

Sensor operating principle
The capacitive sensors work based on variations in capacitance. When the gripper grasps
an object, pressure compresses the sensor surface, bringing the conductive sensing lay­
ers closer together and increasing capacitance. This change affects the rate at which a
taxel charges and discharges. Consequently:
• Fewer charge/discharge cycles indicate higher pressure, implying stronger contact
with the object.
• More charge/discharge cycles suggest either no pressure or minimal pressure, in­
dicating little to no contact with the object.
Therefore, the sensors give a contact pressure estimate.

and reliability.

4.3
Grasping Controller
Given the system limitations outlined in the previous section, the aim is to implement a
simple controller that can work without previous knowledge of the properties of the ob­
jects to grasp. Inspired by the approach explained in section 2.3.3, the implemented solu­
tion focuses on a tactile­based algorithm that relies on an incremental position controller
conditioned by the detection of significant events (e.g., sliding or contact), by means of
feedback from the tactile sensors.
There are several initial differences between the reference system of J. M. Romano et al.
and the one described in this project, which are listed below:
• This project only focuses on the first initial phases of grasping: Close, Load, and
Hold and Lift.
• The tactile sensors used in this project measure the number of charge/discharge cy­
cles of the taxels during a given period rather than the perpendicular compressive
force applied in each taxel. Therefore, the feedback from the tactile data in the cur­
rent system is contact (relative change from a reference state) and in the reference
system is force.
• The reference system also has a digital accelerometer in the palm of the gripper.
4.3.1
Signals and control
The high­level controller used for the end­to­end grasping process consists of several
lower­level building blocks, including tactile sensing, gripper motor actuation and robot
arm movement.
The tactile signals described in the paper are modeled after human touch receptors: SA­I,
FA­I, and FA­II. For this project, only the SA­I afferent is considered, as it responds to
low­frequency stimulation, whereas FA­I and FA­II receptors are sensitive to rapid, high­
frequency changes that the available tactile data is unable to measure effectively.
SA­I afferents, which are widely distributed in the fingertips, respond to low­frequency
skin deformations and maintained contact, providing crucial information about the over­
all force and pressure distribution. A comparable signal (although spatially considerably
less dense) is approximated by summing the contact values from all 24 taxels on each
finger, creating a composite signal intended to reflect the overall contact or pressure on
the sensor.

4.3.3
Constant values
This section reviews the constant values mentioned previously and how to obtain them.
Later, in Chapter 5.1, the results are analyzed and the chosen values are shown.
ICTHRESH (Initial contact threshold): The minimum contact value for both sensors
when they are in contact with the object.
The process of determination involves grasping the object while it rests on a surface and
recording tactile data. When analyzing the data, it is verified whether the contact values
from both sensors reach the selected threshold in the initial moments of interaction. If
they do not, the threshold is gradually decreased until an optimal value is determined for
all objects.
This procedure is repeated three times for each of the nine selected objects. During
grasping, a low speed of 12 mm/s and a force of 8N are applied. The tested objects
include: 3D cube, 3D box, remote control, cup, plastic bottle (full), apple, tomato, tennis
ball, and a LEGO wheel.
TSETTLE (Time to settle): The average time required for sensor data to stabilize after
initial contact with the object.
Tactile Sensor Algorithm for Robotic Object Grasping
27
==PAGE:41==
This is determined by running the Close and Load phases and stopping immediately after
the designated time (a high value to capture all the possibilities) has elapsed. During this
period, tactile data is recorded and analyzed using a window size and tolerance to estab­
lish stabilization time. The range between the maximum and minimum contact values in
a set of 12 readings (window size) must be smaller than 0.4 % (tolerance).
SCTHRESH (Stable contact threshold) : The maximum difference between Cg,min and
the 70% of Cg,des after TSETTLE, ensuring that contact with the object is considered
stable.
This value is obtained experimentally by identifying a threshold that triggers grip pres­
sure adjustments when necessary while avoiding excessive force that could damage the
object.
IDTHRESH (Initial distance threshold): The maximum allowable Euclidean distance
between the initial centroid and each subsequent centroid, which is used as a reference
to detect the initial slip.
The process consists of grasping an object resting on a surface (on which slippage is not
possible) and recording centroid data for 10 seconds after stable contact is established.
This test is performed six times for each of the 11 selected objects. The final threshold
is determined by calculating the average of the maximum centroid distances obtained in
the six iterations for each object. The tested objects include: 3D cube, 3D box, remote
control, cup, steel bottle, pen, plastic bottle (full), apple, tomato, tennis ball, and a LEGO
wheel.
DTHRESH (Distance threshold): The Euclidean distance between centroids, calculated
every four readings, to detect minor movements of objects.
This value is obtained experimentally by intentionally inducing sliding of objects of different
weights, materials and shapes to determine a suitable threshold for slip detection.
KSLIP (Slip factor): A scaling factor that increases Cg,des to optimize the controller’s
response to slip detection.
This value is determined experimentally to achieve a balance between preventing the
object from falling and avoiding excessive pressure application.
4.4
Tests
The testing process consists of two tests: a main test and a grip stability test, which is a
variation designed to assess grip stability under different conditions.
1. Main test: The gripper grasps an object resting on a surface, lifts it, and then rotates
it. This test evaluates the controller’s ability to maintain a secure grip throughout
different manipulation phases.
2. Grip stability test: This test follows the same flow as the main test but excludes the
rotation step. The objective is to determine whether the controller can hold heavy
objects without slipping, particularly under static conditions.
The objects are chosen based on shape, material, and weight to test the controller’s adapt­
ability. The main test uses a remote control, cup, full plastic bottle, apple, tomato, and
LEGO wheel to evaluate grip performance during lifting and rotation. The grip stability test
uses a steel bottle and a full can, selected for their higher weight to assess the gripper’s

ability to hold heavy objects without slipping. These objects, among others, can be seen
in Figure 3.5.
The following steps outline the implementation process for conducting and recording the
tests:
1. Task selection: The appropriate task is selected from the Desk, which commands
the robotic arm to move to reach the predefined grasping position. Some objects
require different gripping positions depending on their shape and size.
2. Grip initialization: The initial gripping width is adjusted based on the object’s size
before activating the controller.
3. Object manipulation: After 10 seconds, the next task is executed to either lift the
object (for up to 10­15 seconds) or lift and rotate it (for 5 seconds), depending on
the test type.
4. Data recording: At the end of the test, the controller stops, and all tactile and motor
data recorded during the process is exported.
Each test is performed ten times per object to ensure consistency. Additionally, the same
tests are conducted using the velocity controller from the GRASPIAN GUI, which does
not rely on tactile data. In this case, the grip width, speed, and force are manually set.
A speed of 30 mm/s is chosen, along with two force levels: 4N, the minimum allowed
by the API, and 12N, sufficient to grip the heaviest object in the set. This comparison
evaluates how applying a fixed force affects grasping performance across objects with
different properties.

Discussion
This chapter summarizes the key results of the previous chapters, discusses the limita­
tions encountered during the project, and outlines potential directions for future system
improvements and development.
6.1
Key Results
The grasping tests conducted after configuring the controller with the defined constant val­
ues provided valuable insights into both the strengths and limitations of the implemented
approach.
• The initial phases of the grasping process (Close and part of the Load phase) re­
vealed notable inconsistencies, with contact values varying significantly between
iterations for the same object. This variability resulted in inconsistent grasp pres­
sures, which reduced the system’s ability to establish a reliable initial grasp.
• The slip detection demonstrated the system’s ability to identify and respond to most
object drops, reflecting a certain degree of robustness. However, the detection was
occasionally delayed, and the grip adjustments were not always optimal. In several
cases, excessive force was applied instead of a balanced correction, while false
positives increased the risk of unnecessary grip tightening, potentially damaging
delicate objects. These findings highlight the importance of slip detection but also
expose the limitations of the current approach, suggesting the need for more adap­
tive methods to achieve a balanced grip without compromising object safety.
• The force drop adjustments effectively prevented sudden grip releases but intro­
duced new challenges. The system reacted by tightening the grip only after force
had already decreased, rather than maintaining a stable pressure. This reactive
behavior caused force fluctuations. While it was effective for rigid objects like the
can, it proved less reliable for softer or deformable objects, such as the tomato or
LEGO wheel, where repeated adjustments sometimes led to excessive pressure
and increased risk of damage.
• The comparison between the implemented controller and the GUI­based approach
highlighted the advantages of adaptive force control over fixed force settings. When
using a pre­set low gripping force (4N), some objects, such as the plastic bottle, re­
mote control or can, rotated significantly or even slipped. Conversely, applying a
fixed high force (12N) ensured stability but led to excessive compression of de­
formable objects like the tomato and the LEGO wheel. In contrast, the implemented
controller dynamically adjusted the grip based on real­time tactile feedback, allow­
ing a secure hold while minimizing excessive pressure. This adaptability allowed
for more effective handling of a range of objects compared to using a fixed force
setting.
This study confirmed that tactile sensing enables adaptive robotic grasping by providing
real­time information about contact, slippage and force variations. The results showed
that, even without prior knowledge of the object, the tactile data allowed the grasping
controller to dynamically adjust its grip, preventing slippage and ensuring stability.
Despite the limitations in the data and the instability of the tactile sensors, the results
confirmed that tactile sensing significantly improves robotic gripping and is essential for

adaptive strategies. More advanced sensors, capable of capturing force, pressure dis­
tribution or texture, would further enhance control, object recognition and overall grasp
effectiveness, reinforcing the value of tactile feedback in robotic manipulation.
6.2
Limitations
As mentioned in the Design and Implementation chapter (Section 4.2), the unexpected
failure of the desktop computer and the subsequent challenges in replicating the environ­
ment on another system led to the decision to abandon ROS­based control for the robotic
arm. This limitation prevented the integration of the grasping point, as the object position
and gripper orientation could not be communicated to the robotic arm through the GUI
(Desk).
Additionally, the instability of the tactile sensors and the gripper connection required the
use of three different grippers throughout the project together with several API updates,
resulting in numerous tests and inconsistencies. These hardware issues, combined with
the computer problems, consumed a significant portion of the project timeline, thus limiting
the time available for the development of the grip control. A more detailed description of
these problems is provided in Appendix C.
Another significant challenge was the variability in the readings of the tactile sensors,
which complicated both the data analysis and the implementation of the controller. While
Sensor 1 generally showed higher contact values than Sensor 2, there were cases in
which both sensors produced similar readings, apparently without a clear pattern. Daily
fluctuations in the sensor output were also observed, often accentuated by plugging and
unplugging the gripper.
On the control side, significant time was spent tunning the constant values and testing
various low­level configurations, including position control, position­velocity control, force
control, and eventually returning to position control with specific adjustments. These ad­
justments aimed to mitigate undesired behaviors, such as sudden drops in motor current
instead of maintaining stability once the position error was minimized. Although modifying
the motor controller parameters was beyond the scope of this project, it is believed that
such adjustments could have improved the robustness of the system by ensuring more
consistent motor performance.
6.3
Future work
Based on the current status project, several refinements can be made to increase the
performance, adaptability and robustness of the grasp control system.
• Enhancing sensor performance:
One of the main challenges encountered was
the disparity between the contact values of the two sensors, especially at lower grip
speeds. Further research is needed to understand this behavior and achieve bet­
ter alignment of the sensors. Additionally, enhancing sensor sensitivity to detect
soft contact, rather than requiring slight pressure for detection, would improve over­
all responsiveness. Since crosstalk between the taxels is unavoidable, tuning the
weight assigned to each taxel could improve the accuracy of the contact measure­
ments. Although Graspian developed an updated weight configuration at the end of
the project, time constraints prevented its application within the project.
Beyond improving sensor calibration, the system could benefit from enhanced use of
the object centroid and orientation data. While only centroid tracking was employed
in the current setup, the API also provides orientation feedback. This feature was ex­
cluded due to its instability but could be highly valuable once its reliability improves.

By combining centroid and orientation information, the controller could assess grip
quality more effectively, enabling adaptive responses such as regrasping.
• Motor control:
Another key area for improvement is the motor controller. During
testing, fluctuations in motor current led to inconsistent gripping force and occa­
sional object drops. Fine­tuning the motor controller to maintain a steady grip would
significantly improve system reliability.
Moreover, the current system estimates gripping force based on motor current,
which does not accurately reflect the actual force applied at the gripper fingertips.
Developing a force estimation model by correlating tactile sensor outputs with ap­
plied force would provide more accurate feedback for grasp control.
In addition, if the original system was fully integrated through ROS, allowing commu­
nication between the camera, robotic arm and gripper, further recommendations would
include:
• Adaptive grasp planning: To improve grasping performance, integration of cam­
era depth information could enable more versatile grasping strategies, such as lat­
eral or angled approaches, rather than relying solely on grasps where the gripper
grasps the object from above. In addition, using machine learning models to esti­
mate object properties could further optimize the grasping process. For example,
identifying whether an object is rigid, deformable or delicate provides the system
with extra information to use to adjust the grip force accordingly. Weight estimation
could further improve the lifting phase by ensuring that the applied force is sufficient
without being excessive.
• Exploring other sensors and control approaches: Exploring alternative tactile
sensors with higher resolution, lower crosstalk, or multi­modal feedback (e.g., pres­
sure, vibration, deformation) would provide richer contact information. Additionally,
employing neural networks to map tactile sensor outputs to actual gripping force
would enable more adaptive and precise control, improving the overall grasping
performance. However, these advancements would also increase the complexity
of sensor data processing and control, requiring more sophisticated algorithms and
computational resources.

7
Conclusion
In this master’s thesis project, a robotic grasping controller based on tactile sensory
feedback was developed to evaluate the capabilities of the Intelligent Tactile Gripper by
Graspian. A literature review was performed to highlight the fundamental stages, recent
advancements and challenges in robotic grasping where visual and tactile sensors have
been used.
Due to the challenges of integrating the robotic arm, camera and gripper into a fully unified
system, a simplified approach was taken. The final configuration excluded the camera
and focused on developing a grasping controller that operates without prior knowledge of
object properties like weight or type.
An initial analysis of the tactile sensors identified some limitations, leading to small adjust­
ments before designing the control algorithm. A high­level grasping controller, inspired by
a previous study, was then implemented. The core of this implementation relied on tran­
sitions between grasping phases and fine­tuned controller constants to detect key events
such as initial contact and slippage.
To evaluate the grasping controller’s consistency and robustness, two tests were per­
formed using eight different objects. These tests involved grasping and manipulating the
objects (lifting and rotating or just lifting). The data analysis focused on grip consistency
during initial contact and the robustness of slip detection and grip adjustments. The re­
sults indicated low consistency in establishing an initial grip but demonstrated reliable slip
detection and response, which could be further improved with faster detection and more
balanced force adjustments.
In conclusion, this work provides a basis for integrating tactile feedback into the Grapsian
gripper controller, emphasizing the importance of adaptive control for handling objects of
varying weights, materials and shapes. The study also highlights the fundamental role
of slip detection in ensuring a stable grip and identifies opportunities for refining both the
gripper and its tactile sensors. These findings lay the foundation for future improvements,
offering new directions for enhancing the system’s adaptability and overall performance.

A.1
Slip detection method 1
To determine an appropriate threshold for slip detection, the following step­by­step pro­
cess was followed using the centroid data recorded during object grasping:
1. Static grasping test:
• A low initial threshold value was set for the Euclidean distance between cen­
troid readings.
• The controller was run while grasping the object resting on a table, ensuring
there was no movement of the robot arm.
• During each grasp, the centroid data was collected and the Euclidean distance
between centroids was calculated with a separation of five readings between
each calculation.
• This test was repeated five times for each object. In a corresponding table, ”FP”
(False positive) was written if the calculated distance exceeded the threshold,
along with the maximum distance value observed during the tests.
2. Threshold adjustment:
• If multiple false positives were observed, the threshold was increased. Other­
wise, the same procedure was repeated under dynamic conditions (next step).
3. Dynamic grasping test (Lift and rotate the object):
• For objects with a higher likelihood of slipping, an additional test was performed
by lifting and rotating the object after a few seconds of stable gripping (This was
done to see if the threshold was too high and some slips were not detected).
• As before, the test was repeated five times per object. Successful slip detection
was noted as “Y”, while for failed detections it was “N” along with the maximum
Euclidean distance recorded during the test. If no slip detection occurred ”­”
was noted for that test.

A.2
Slip detection method 2
The initial slip detection used in the implementation. To determine an appropriate initial
threshold for slip detection, the following step­by­step process was followed using the
centroid data recorded during object grasping:
• The controller was run while grasping the object resting on a table, ensuring there
was no movement of the robot arm.
• During each grasp, the centroid data was collected and the Euclidean distance be­
tween the initial centroid and each subsequent centroid was calculated.
• This test was repeated six times for each object. In a corresponding table, the
maximum Euclidean distance value observed during the test was written.
• From these tables, the average and maximum values of each object are calculated
and used to obtain the final threshold value.

C
Technical Issues
During the course of this project, several technical problems occurred that affected the
timeline and required troubleshooting. The following is a chronological summary of these
problems:
• 01/11/24: One of the gripper sensors stopped working. A replacement sensor was
obtained on 13/11/24.
• 08/11/24: The desktop computer used to run the robot system stopped working
unexpectedly.
• 2nd week of November: Attempt to find a replacement computer. A computer was
obtained on Friday of that week.
• 3rd and 4th week of November: Extensive time was spent configuring the new com­
puter to replicate the previous system configuration and ensure compatibility with
the robotic arm and the robot operating system (ROS).
Problems appeared with the real­time kernel, NVIDIA drivers and various libraries.
• 1st week of December: Problems with the real­time kernel were not fully resolved,
which affected system performance.
The Franka Emika software communication_test example did not work correctly
when using ROS with the robotic arm.
TracIK, the inverse kinematics solver, produced unexpected results, failing to achieve
the desired positions accurately.
Additionally, three different grippers, along with several power supplies and cables
provided by Graspian, were used throughout the project due to stability issues and
data inconsistencies.
These technical difficulties required extensive debugging and troubleshooting, which im­
pacted the overall project workflow.
