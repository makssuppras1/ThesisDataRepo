Abstract 
Accurate fisheries data are vital for sustainable marine resource management, yet traditional 
Electronic Monitoring (EM) system face limitations from overwhelming video data and human 
review challenges. This thesis addresses these issues by developing a robust deep learning-
based re-identification (Re-ID) system to enhance automated fish documentation in EM 
systems with conveyor belts.  
The pipeline, developed and validated using the AutoFish dataset, integrates a fine-tuned 
YOLOv11-segmentation model, achieving exceptionally high accuracy in fish instance 
segmentation and species classification, (98.47% mAP50-95 in separated and 92.48% mAP50-
95 in touched configurations). This established a reliable foundation for individual level 
analysis. For Re-ID, Swin-T (Tiny) architecture consistently outperformed CNN based ResNet-
50 models, demonstrating superior capability in learning discriminative embeddings for 
individual fish identification. It achieved a peal of 41.65% mAP@R and 90.43% R1. Key 
optimization strategies included custom “resizing and padding to square” image 
transformation, which ensured the preservation of crucial fine-grained features, dataset specific 
normalization and strategic “Hard” triplet mining. Baseline experiments with off-the-shelf 
Swin-T and ResNet-50 showed significantly lower Re-ID performance, operating as a zero-
shot instance retrieval scenario. 
An in-depth analysis of Re-ID performance revealed that the primary challenge lies in 
distinguishing visually similar individuals from the same species (intra-species errors), rather 
than inter-species confusion. This was particularly evident in highly complex scenarios 
involving compound occlusions and changes in fish orientations, where performance was 
lowest. This critical insight, alongside the model’s performance under various challenging 
conditions, provides a direct roadmap for highly targeted future data collection strategies.  
This research significantly advances the automation of fish documentation. By enabling precise 
individual-level tracking, reducing the burden on human reviewers, and enhancing data 
integrity, the developed pipeline offers a powerful tool to improve compliance with fisheries 
regulations and inform more accurate stock assessments.  
Keywords: Electronic Monitoring (EM), Fish Re-Identification (Re-ID), Deep Learning, 
Instance segmentation, Swin Transformer

1. Introduction 
Our oceans are facing unprecedented challenges. Human activities, coupled with the escalating 
impacts of climate change, are exerting immense pressure on marine ecosystems. The 
consequences are far-reaching, threatening the balance of underwater life and the livelihoods 
and food security of billions of people worldwide.  
 
In 2021, the proportion of marine stocks harvested at unsustainable levels reached 37.7 percent 
and it increased from 10.0 percent in 1974 (Figure 1). This trend indicates a persistent and 
increasing challenge in managing global fisheries. The increasing percentage of current 
overfished stocks indicates that these stocks are being harvested at a rate faster than they can 
naturally replenish.  
The key to implementing informed and effective sustainable fisheries management lies in the 
availability of accurate, comprehensive, and timely fisheries data. This data serves as the 
foundation for understanding fish populations, monitoring fishing operations, and evaluating 
the effectiveness of management strategies. 
This thesis directly contributes to the need to get accurate data by developing and evaluating 
advanced electronic monitoring (EM) methods. Specifically, it explores how computer vision-
based re-identification techniques can provide unprecedented, individual-level insights for 
precise catch documentation and quantification. To provide this context, the subsequent 
sections will detail the main types of fisheries data, address current challenges in EM, elaborate 
on the significance of re-identification, and outline the core focus of this research.

1.1. What is Fisheries Data? 
 
 
Fisheries management depends on high-quality data [1, 2], which enables managers to develop 
evidence-based policies that balance ecological conservation with economic needs. Without 
robust data collection systems, fisheries managers operate uncertainly, risking both the health 
of marine ecosystems and the livelihoods that depend on them.  
The European Union’s Common Fisheries Policy (CFP) strongly emphasizes data-driven 
management. CFP explicitly requires Member States to collect and manage biological, 
environmental, technical and socio-economic data necessary for fisheries management [3]. 
These data feed into scientific analysis and advice, enabling assessments of fish stock status, 
fishing impacts on the ecosystem, and the economic performance of the fishing sector [3]. The 
EU’s Data Collection Framework (DCF), establishes detailed rules for collecting, managing 
and using these data categories. These data serve as the foundational input for stock 
assessments used by the fishery managers to set quotas that support sustainable harvest levels 
[4]. Key data types are needed to perform these assessments: catch data, abundance data, and 
biological data [5]. While these provide stock-specific information, they must be integrated 
with environmental data to account for habitat conditions, species interactions and climate 
variability, which influence stock dynamics [6].  
Furthermore, integrating socio-economic data ensures that management measures are not only 
ecologically sound but also socially equitable and economically viable, ultimately supporting 
long-term compliance and resilience of fishing communities [7]. The following subsections 
briefly discuss each of the key data types used in fisheries management to create an 
understanding of all the input data that impacts the decision of fishery managers. Figure 2 
visually synthesizes all these input data. Understanding the input data is important for 
understanding the potential applications of fish re-identification. 
1.1.1. Biological data 
Biological data provides crucial information regarding the life-history characteristics of target 
and non-target species, including growth rates, age structures, reproductive parameters (e.g., 
Fecundity), natural mortality rates, and migration patterns [8], [9]. Key biological variables 
commonly collected include individual fish size, age determination by examining annual 
growth increments in otoliths (fish ear bones), and assessments of reproductive maturity stages. 
These data are mainly obtained from fishery-independent scientific surveys, which provide 
unbiased baseline data, and from fishery-dependent sampling programmes such as onboard 
==PAGE:8==

observer programmes and landing site biological sampling, where significant quantities of 
commercially captured species are easily accessible [8]. When combined with cohort analysis, 
historical abundance data enable tracking of the survival and growth of specific age groups 
within a population, offering valuable insights into recruitment success and mortality rates. 
This is crucial for understanding population dynamics and ensuring long-term sustainability. 
1.1.2. Fish stock abundance data 
Abundance data provides quantitative measures or indices reflecting the relative number or 
biomass of fish present within a given stock or population. Ideally, this data originated from 
carefully designed, fishery-independent surveys [10] that systematically sample marine species 
across extensive geographic ranges and diverse habitats. Typically, these surveys utilize 
standardized methods and gear deployed annually by specialized research vessels or 
commercial fishing vessels contracted explicitly for scientific sampling purposes [8]. The 
systematic and standardized nature of these surveys, repeated consistently year after year, 
allows scientists to track relative changes (Catch-per-unit-effort – CPUE) in fish populations 
and ecosystem components over extended time series, free from the biases inherent in 
commercial catch data. In addition to these primary fishery-independent survey data, fishery-
dependent catch-per-unit-effort (CPUE) is also used. Derived from commercial fishing 
logbooks, can sometimes be calibrated and incorporated as supplementary indices of 
abundance [8]. 
1.1.3. Catch data 
Catch data refers to the total quantity of fish and other marine organisms that are removed from 
a stock through fishing activities and provides insights into the total fishing mortality. It 
encompasses both landed catch (the portion brought ashore) and discards, which include 
undersized or non-target individuals returned to the sea.  In the EU and globally, catch data is 
collected continuously through a coordinated network of fishery-dependent monitoring 
programmes. The principal sources of catch data are logbooks from fishers, dockside sampling 
(at ports), onboard scientific observer programmes, and electronic monitoring (EM) systems 
[8]. According to the CFP [11] and DCF [12] Regulations, and the Sea-Fisheries Protection 
Authority (SFPA)’s Fisheries Information Notice (FIN), commercial fishers and onboard 
observers must record detailed catch information for each fishing trip [13]. The core parameters 
in a paper or electronic logbook include species-level catch weights, fishing effort and gear 
specifications, location and time of fishing, discard quantities, and any interactions with 
protected species [14]. Catch data are incorporated with technical data such as fishing vessel

activity, including Vessel Monitoring System (VMS) and Automatic Identification System 
(AIS) data [9] to document the spatial and temporal aspects of fishing pressure. Accurate and 
comprehensive catch data are essential for evaluating pressure on stocks and are a cornerstone 
of fisheries science and sustainable management. 
1.1.4. Technical data 
Technical data encompasses information about fishing activities, fleets, and gear, essentially 
the “effort” and capacity side of fisheries. This includes the number of fishing trips or days at 
sea, fishing hours, gear type and mesh size, fishing areas, and other operational details [9]. It 
also includes measures of fishing capacity such as the number of vessels, vessel sizes (gross 
tonnage, length) and engine power, which are tracked in the EU fleet register [3, 15]. Technical 
data often describes the fisheries as métier (a combination of gear and species assemblage) 
[16]. In the DCF context, technical data might also cover vessel technology and efficiency, as 
well as spatial deployment of effort [12]. Almost all technical data are fishery-dependent 
because they originate from fishing operations and fleet records. Technical data serve to 
measure and manage the fishing pressure exerted on fish stocks. They are crucial for 
standardizing CPUE indices, which are a common proxy for stock abundance. By combining 
catch with effort, scientists derive CPUE series that, when properly standardized for gear and 
vessel differences, indicate how a stock’s abundance changes over time [17]. Accurate effort 
data (e.g. number of hooks, soak time, area fished) improves the reliability of these indices. 
Additionally, technical information on gear and vessel features allows researchers to model 
fishing power and catchability [18]. 
1.1.5. Environmental data 
Environmental data in CFP’s data collection context relates to information on the marine 
ecosystem that is relevant to fisheries. This includes data that assesses the impact of fishing on 
the environment, such as records of incidental catch of sensitive species (e.g. endangered 
seabirds, marine mammals, sea turtles, protected fish species) and the physical impact of 
fishing gear on habitats (e.g. trawl disturbance on the sea floor). These data indicate the 
ecological side effects of fishing beyond target stocks. Environmental data are collected during 
the scientific onboard observer sessions or by the fishers using logbooks or other recording 
methods. Furthermore, remote sensing and satellite data (e.g. sea surface temperature, 
chlorophyll concentration) provide additional independent environmental data that can be 
linked to fisheries. These data support the design of mitigation measures such as gear

modifications and time-area closures and help inform spatial management by identifying areas 
where fishing should be restructured (Marine Protected Areas – MPAs).  
1.1.6. Socio-economic data 
Socio-economic data covers the human and economic dimensions of fisheries. This includes a 
broad array of economic indicators, for example, operating costs (fuel, maintenance, crew 
wages), revenues from fish sales, capital investments and asset value of vessels, profits or gross 
value added, and financial viability metrics as well as social variables such as employment 
(number of fishers, full-time equivalents), age structure of workforce, education level, and 
community dependance on fisheries [9]. Socio-economic data is collected through a 
combination of industry surveys. Fleet records and official statistics are used to evaluate fleet 
profitability and community well-being. [9, 19, 20]. These data are especially valuable when 
assessing the impacts of management measures that may affect employment or economic 
viability in dependent regions [20]. 
1.2. Ensuring accurate data for fisheries management 
The effective management of fisheries and marine ecosystems, culminating in accurate stock 
assessments, fundamentally relies on the comprehensive array of data types previously 
outlined. However, the inherent challenges in acquiring consistently accurate and complete 
data across these crucial categories create significant data gaps. For fish stock assessments, 
catch data, biological data, and fish abundance data are essential [5]. For biological data and 
abundance data, a primary difficulty lies in accurately estimating growth rates, age structures, 
reproductive characteristics and population sizes from necessarily limited samples of entire, 
vast marine populations. Traditional data collection, including fishery-independent scientific 
surveys and fishery-dependent sampling via onboard observer programs, is often labor 
intensive, costly, and restricted by logistic constraints. These limitations also impact the 
resolution and consistency of catch data derived from logbooks and existing observation 
schemes. Such deficiencies invariably lead to misinformed decisions, potentially resulting in 
the implementation of incorrect quotas, ineffective regulations, and ultimately hindering the 
long-term sustainability and economic viability of fisheries.  
Recognizing these pervasive limitations and the increasing demand for high quality data, the 
fisheries sector is actively seeking innovative solutions. Electronic monitoring (EM) systems 
have emerged as an innovative alternative to conventional observer programmes in fisheries 
management [21].

1.3. Electronic Monitoring 
EM has emerged as a proven, efficient, and cost-effective alternative to traditional monitoring 
methods such as logbooks and onboard observer programmes. It involves the strategic 
installation of camera systems on fishing vessels to continuously record and monitor fishing 
activities throughout a trip. A review of the literature reveals various research objectives for 
implementing EM systems. These objectives include monitoring fisheries with a focus on 
reducing bycatch [22, 23], improving fish handling practices [24], minimizing discards [25], 
and assessing the effectiveness of EM systems as a monitoring tool in conjunction with 
feasibility studies related to Catch Quota Management (CQM) and Landing obligations [24]. 
EM systems present a powerful solution for collecting detailed, high-resolution data that is 
critical for today’s fisheries management. EM systems offer general benefits in collecting 
detailed video surveillance data and addressing some shortcomings of human observers [24], 
[26]. EM, with its 24/7 recording capabilities and sensor-triggered operations, fundamentally 
alters the volume and objectivity of the data collected. EM systems point towards the 
development of “smart fisheries”  
1.3.1. Components and operations of a typical EM system 
A typical EM system integrates several technological components: a GPS tracking device to 
record vessel positions, a network of strategically placed cameras to document fishing 
operations and catch composition, and various sensors that detect gear deployment and retrieval 
(hydraulic and drum rotation sensors) activities (Figure 3). These components work together, 
transmitting data to a central computing system that stores this comprehensive information for 
later analysis [24]. Trained analysts review EM footage to identify species and measure fish 
lengths, documenting both target catch and bycatch [27].  
1.3.2. Evolution and Regulatory context of EM 
EM was first introduced in 1999 in British Columbia’s crab fisheries, mainly to monitor and 
control catches and gear theft [24]. Since then, there has been much improvement in the EM 
systems applied to fisheries worldwide, with some notable increases in 2006 and 2015 [24]. In 
the EU context, with the implementation of the Landing Obligation, there are introductions of 
non-compliance for the catch reporting [28, 29, 30]. Due to these continuous problems with 
non-compliance, several amendments have been made to the EU regulations.

1.3.3. Limitations and challenges in current EM systems 
However, despite EM’s benefits, a significant challenge is emerging as these systems 
proliferate, the volume of video data has grown beyond what human reviewers can effectively 
process [32]. Other than the rapid increase of video volumes to review, several other challenges 
also prevail, affecting the efficiency of video reviewers. Vessel diversity complicates 
standardized camera installation, often resulting in suboptimal viewing angles [27]. The 
absence of resolution standards means some systems use low-quality cameras positioned too 
far from catch handling/ sorting areas to distinguish similar species. Environmental factors 
further compromise image quality, water and debris on lenses reduce clarity [33, 34, 35], 
inadequate lighting during night fishing or in shaded areas obscure details, and catch occlusion 
occurs when items overlap or are blocked by hands, gears or other fish [33]. This occlusion 
problem is particularly pronounced in large volume fisheries, conveyor belts, or deck sorting, 
where partial visibility of catch items makes accurate species identification, counting and 
length measurement difficult or impossible for human reviewers [33].

alternative analysis methods are developed to adapt to various situations without causing a 
considerable burden of replacing every EM system on fishing vessels. Figure 4 shows several 
of the most common image-quality issues. To address them effectively, researchers are turning

In the present study, the potential of fish re-identification was investigated to mitigate some of 
these challenges. This includes the potential to eliminate the need for extensive manual tagging 
for individual re-identification and to significantly expedite the collection of ground truth data 
for model training and validation. Integrating re-identification into EM systems offers a 
transformative pathway to overcome these remaining data gaps. This technology has the 
potential to improve precision of catch documentation and quantification. It achieves this by 
uniquely tracking individual items across different viewpoints, occlusions, and random 
appearances of catch items, enabling precise species classification and counting. Furthermore, 
it holds the potential to significantly improve the collection of biological and abundance data 
by automating tasks such as length measurements, thereby making data acquisition faster and 
more reliable across the board. These capabilities directly address the critical need for robust 
tracking and accurate data acquisition in dynamics fisheries environments, which form the core 
problems this thesis seeks to address. 
1.4. Problem definition 
The project will focus on extracting fine-grained visual features that distinguish individual fish and 
developing foundational methodologies and insights that contribute towards the future 
development of fully functional, open-world fish Re-ID systems suitable for deployment in real-
world commercial fisheries. The Re-ID approach is developed and tested on the Autofish dataset 
[36], which simulates a realistic catch environment for fishing vessels equipped with conveyor belts 
and provides clearly structured data simulating an open-world Re-ID scenario.  
 
This thesis aims to address the following key research questions: 
1. How accurately can instance segmentation models identify and delineate fish masks on 
conveyor belts when subjected to non-overlapping and overlapping configurations? 
2. What can be the suitable deep learning architectures for accomplishing the fish Re-ID task? 
3. What strategies can be helpful to improve the performance of the fish Re-ID task?

2. Related Work 
2.1. AI and Re-Identification in Fisheries 
2.1.1. Artificial Intelligence 
Artificial intelligence (AI) encompasses systems designed to carry out tasks that typically 
require human cognitive abilities, including perception, reasoning, and learning. Machine 
learning (ML) is a branch of AI that identifies patterns in data instead of adhering to 
predetermined rules. Deep Learning (DL) is a subfield of ML that uses multi-layer neural 
networks to learn complex feature hierarchies from substantial datasets, requiring significant 
computational resources. Computer Vision (CV), a specialized area of AI, focuses on 
understanding visual data such as images and video. Modern CV interestingly employs deep 
learning models, which automate the representation learning process, allowing DL-based CV 
systems to maintain robustness against variations in camera angles, lighting, or object 
positioning that challenged older methods. 
2.1.2. Computer vision-based Automated fisheries monitoring  
An effective automated computer vision system intends to assist experts by automating much 
of the tedious and costly manual analysis inherent in fisheries monitoring [21, 35]. The primary 
objectives of the computer vision component are: 
1. Automated catch quantification: To accurately and efficiently determine the catch 
composition and quantity. This is achieved by using deep learning algorithms to 
perform automatically: 
a. Species classification: Identifying the different species present in the catch  
b. Counting and size estimation: Counting the number of individuals of each 
species, crucially estimating their length. This detailed size information is vital 
for accurate stock assessments [21, 35]. 
2. Enhanced data generation for catch documentation: To provide reliable and 
accurate electronically logged data derived directly from the automated quantification 
process. This includes the species, count, length measurements and can be combined 
with data related to fishing operations (GPS locations, time stamps, etc.) [37]. 
2.1.3. Early work in the Automation of fisheries monitoring 
Automated catch monitoring systems leveraging computer vision have evolved significantly 
over the years. Early approaches to species identification and length estimation predominantly 
relied on handcrafted features. For instance, Zion et al. [38] developed an image processing 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
12 
 
algorithm based on moment-invariants to discriminate between three freshwater species and 
estimate their size, demonstrating insensitivity to fish size, orientation, and location. Strachan 
[39] described a machine for fish length measurement that could handle gutted and flexed fish 
with errors around 3% for randomly oriented fish. Building on these efforts, Strachan [40] also 
trialled a prototype computer vision-based machine learning at sea for sorting fish by species 
and size, achieving over 99% accuracy for flatfish and round fish at 40 fish/min using shape 
and colour descriptors. Similarly, White et al. [41] introduced the “CatchMeter”, a system 
capable of identifying and measuring seven species of fish on a conveyor belt with high 
accuracy (up to 99.8% sorting reliability and 1.2 mm length standard deviation) using image 
processing algorithms focused on orientation, flatfish/ roundfish identification, and length 
measurement based on color and shape. Furthermore, Antelo et al. [42] presented the Biomass 
Estimation Optical System (BEOS), an on-ship automated system for discard evaluation and 
species biomass estimation, integrating machine vision technologies and feature extraction via 
nonlinear modelling with artificial neural networks. This work represents an early, 
sophisticated use of neural networks before the modern deep learning revolution. 
While these traditional methods were employed for specific tasks, their inherent design posed 
limitations. They struggled to adapt to unseen objects and required substantial effort when 
introducing new species, often involving entirely new sets of features or extensive parameter 
tuning. Studies reveal that the performance of traditional CV techniques can degrade 
significantly when they encounter scenarios that are not represented in their training datasets, 
which is common in the fisheries sector [43]. Furthermore, distinguishing between visually 
similar individuals or species can be particularly challenging for those methods, impacting both 
classification and counting accuracy. 
2.1.4. Recent work - Deep learning-based Computer vision 
A transformative shift occurred with the advent of deep learning, introducing an era where 
feature extraction became largely automated and learned directly from data. This paradigm 
shift, propelled by developing highly effective Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for 
image classification, alleviated the need for manual feature engineering [44, 45]. Image 
classification alone was not suitable for identifying multiple fish scenarios because it does not 
account for the object’s spatial location of fish [36]. For scenarios involving multiple fish, 
determining each individual's object location (object detection) and species (species 
classification) is vital in monitoring fisheries.  
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
13 
 
Recent studies have employed YOLO (You Only Look Once) as a popular choice for object 
detection and classification using bounding box representations [46]. For example, van Essen 
et al. [47] successfully applied a YOLOv3 model for locating and classifying fish on conveyor 
belts. Similarly, Kuswantory et al. [48] proposed an optimized YOLOv4 algorithm, tested on 
videos of moving cultured fish on a conveyor, especially for automatic sorting purposes, 
achieving high accuracy. Another study by Sokolova et al. [49] employed a modified YOLOv5, 
enhanced with a regression output, to detect, classify, and even estimate the weight of fish on 
a conveyor belt. 
When dealing with soft-bodied organisms like fish, it’s essential to consider the body shape 
during object detection and species classification. Therefore, it produces misclassifications 
when the fish occlude, change the orientation by bending or are handled [36]. In such cases, 
representing fish (catch items) at the pixel level using segmentation masks is typically preferred 
[36]. French et al. [21] were early pioneers in proposing CNN-based methods for monitoring 
fish on conveyor belts using segmentation masks. Generally, Mask R-CNN (Mask Region 
Convolutional Neural Networks) has become a widely adopted model for instance 
segmentation of fish across various contexts, including EM images, within trawls, in processing 
boxes, and in specialized conveyor belt monitoring systems. Sokolova et al. [50] introduced an 
automated catch description method for commercial demersal trawl fisheries, leveraging Mask 
R-CNN and a novel in-trawl image acquisition system to classify and count catch items during 
fishing operations, specially optimized for Nephrops fisheries. Tseng and Juo [35] proposed an 
automatic approach using Mask R-CNN to detect and segment harvested fish in EM system 
videos at the pixel level. They also determined fish count using time and distance thresholding 
and identified fish types and body lengths based on Mask R-CNN outputs. Garcia et al. [51] 
introduced a novel image-based method for individual fish detection, localization, and 
segmentation using Mask R-CNN architecture on stereo images acquired directly within a 
trawl. This was designed to reduce catches of undersized fish, refine segmentation using local 
gradients to accurately estimate fish boundaries, even with overlapping fish. Ovalle et al. [52] 
utilized deep learning algorithms for fish species identification and length estimation on board 
fishing vessels, adapting the Mask R-CNN for instance segmentation and using MobileNet—
V1 for length estimation. Their work showed satisfactory results for both tasks in situations 
with moderate to low overlapping among individuals, aiming to assist in compliance with 
fisheries regulations.

All these studies have shown promising results, but they dealt with continuous frames of 
images from video footage or sequential image captures in the context of fisheries monitoring. 
But none of them have implemented the re-identification concept to identify the same 
individual fish (track) in different camera views or across discrete frames due to occlusion or 
fish handling. The collection and careful preparation of high-quality, ground truth (GT) datasets 
relevant to fisheries are essential for effectively training and evaluating advanced DL models, 
making this a continued priority in current research efforts. 
Achieving robust computer vision in fisheries operations is critical yet significantly hampered 
by unpredictable environmental factors such as inconsistent illumination, debris, and water 
droplets on camera lenses (Figure 4) [35]. Furthermore, these systems must effectively manage 
occlusions and the unstructured dynamic conditions inherent to fishing vessel sorting areas to 
be successfully deployed. 
2.1.5. Limitations and Challenges in Current Deep Learning-Based Automation 
Despite the significant advancements offered by deep learning, several limitations and 
challenges persist in its application to fully automated fisheries monitoring. 
1. Data dependency and computational demands: DL models are inherently data-
hungry, often requiring large volumes of correctly labelled data for optimal training, 
which can be resource-intensive to acquire in the fisheries domain. Furthermore, 
training and deploying these complex models for near real-time inference can produce 
high computational power demands, which may have limitations on some fishing 
vessels or for specific edge computing devices  [53]. 
2. Generalization capabilities: Although DL models are robust, their ability to generalize 
broadly depends on the quality, diversity, and representativeness of the training datasets 
used [54]. 
3. Distinguishing and tracking individuals: General challenges in visual object tracking 
includes target disappearance temporarily, appearance changes due to rotation, 
deformation or partial visibility, movements and occlusion and lack of specialized 
training dataset for domain specific tasks like fisheries [55]. 
4. Feature extraction in challenging conditions: Even with the general effectiveness of 
traditional CNN architectures [52, 56], feature extraction remains challenging in 
unpredictable fisheries conditions, such as severe light variations and complex cutter, 
where robust and domain-specific feature learning is still actively researched.

While the foundational successes have been achieved, automated systems require further 
refinement for greater precision. A key area for improvement is overcoming the difficulties in 
detecting and tracking individual items within complex catch scenarios. Advanced 
methodologies are essential to address this challenge effectively. The subsequent section delves 
into re-identification techniques as a key approach to enhancing current capabilities.

2.2.1.1.Open-world and closed-world Re-id scenarios 
Closed-world re-identification assumes that every object in the system needs to be identified 
as belonging to a known identity from a predetermined set [62]. In this scenario, the algorithm 
can be trained and optimized specially for those known classes, which means that at inference, 
any query object is assumed to be one of these known individuals [62], and the possibility of 
entirely new identities during deployment should not be considered. Here, supervised learning 
approaches are referred to, which rely on accurately labelled datasets [57]. These labelled 
datasets enable researchers to frame re-identification tasks such as classification, verification, 
or metric learning challenges using CNNs [63] to learn the discriminative features of the 
individual objects from the training dataset [64]. Close-world fish Re-ID can achieve high 
accuracy when sufficient training data for each identity is available, and test conditions match 
training [60].  
Open-world re-identification scenarios focus on more complex, real-world cases in which the 
system may encounter objects that were never seen in training or are not part of the known 
catalogue [65]. An effective open-world Re-ID system should correctly recognize individuals

2.2.2.  Re-identification for fisheries 
Re-identification in fisheries can be defined as the advanced and critical process of uniquely 
recognizing and consistently matching a specific individual fish or catch item across multiple 
observations that are potentially non-contiguous and diverse. These observations can occur at 
different points in time as the item moves along a conveyor belt (successive frames of a video 
sequence), or even across different camera views and sensor stations within a fish processing 
handling area or the discard locations. 
The primary goal of Re-ID in fisheries is to assign and maintain a stable, unique digital identity 
to each distinct fish individual from its first appearance until it exists the image acquisition 
area. This is crucial because basic multi-object tracking might lose an item during occlusions, 
high-speed movements, or transitions between camera fields of view (FOV), covering the 
camera view by the handling crew, and then assigns a new ID upon redetection [55]. Re-ID 
aims to overcome this by determining if a newly observed item in a different time step is, in 
fact, one that was detected before. Figure 5 shows a conceptual comparison between general 
multi-object tracking and re-identification (Re-ID). Re-ID typically relies on learning fine-
grained discriminative visual signatures for each fish that can even differentiate two individuals 
of the same fish species [60, 68]. In addition to differentiating remarkably similar individuals, 
the features must be robust enough to handle changes in pose and orientation, illumination and 
wetness, occlusion levels, scale and viewpoint changes (cameras), and other variations, such 
as the presence of debris with the catch.  
Non-deep learning methods have been applied to these Re-ID problems which focus on 
developing species-specific algorithms to extract features [69, 70, 71]. But with the 
introduction of deep learning, the feature extraction process became largely automated and 
learned directly from data. This removed the need for researchers to manually design these 
feature extraction algorithms to each species [68]. 
In the domain of fisheries, several studies have begun to explore fish re-identification using 
deep learning, primarily on controlled or semi-controlled environments. For instance, Haurum 
et al. [72] demonstrated that zebra fish (Danio rerio) can be reliably re-identified from a side-
view perspective using metric learning on RGB images, showing high accuracy with minimal 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
17 
 
annotated samples. Pedersen et al. [73] explored fish re-identification for giant sunfish (Mola 
alexandrini) using keypoint matching, evaluating both handcrafted and deep learning based 
(SuperPoint) keypoint descriptors. They demonstrated a robust identification without extensive 
training or fine-tuning and show generalization to other patterned species. Olsen et al.[74] 
developed a contrastive learning-based model using an Inception v3 network for individual re-
identification of wild corkwing wrass (Symphodus melops), specifically addressing the 
challenge of re-sighting individuals over intervals ranging from days to years. More recently, 
Fan et al. [61] constructed a multi-view synchronized underwater fish re-identification dataset 
(FS48) and proposed FSNet, a deep learning framework that fuses information from three 
camera views (front, top, side) to enhance re-identification performance, particularly 
addressing occluded or blurry fish. 
However, despite these advancements in fish re-identification, studies that specifically address 
implementation of re-identification for automated catch monitoring and quantification directly 
on-board commercial fishing vessels, particularly considering the complex, dynamic, and 
unconstrained conditions encountered during actual handling and discard operations, remain 
limited.

2.2.3.  n-Shot learning for data-efficient animal Re-ID 
In response to the data-intensive nature of conventional deep learning models which are trained 
in using extensive supervised learning, n-shot learning has emerged as promising paradigms 
for data-efficient animal Re-ID. At the core of the n-shot learning system is a robust feature 
extractor (Either a CNN or a Vision Transformer based model), often a deep neural network, 
which must first be trained. This initial training phase is crucial for the feature extractor to learn 
a discriminative embedding space where features from the same individual are clustered 
closely while those from different individuals are well separated. 
Once the foundational feature extractor is developed, the concepts of n-shot learning can be 
effectively applied. One-shot learning (OSL) empowers such a model to recognize or identify 
a new class from only a single enrolled example in the reference database or gallery [68]. Few-
shot learning (FSL) extends this by enabling learning from a very small number of examples 
per class [75]. Moving beyond scenarios where at least one visual example is available, Zero-
shot learning (ZSL) tackles the challenge of recognizing categories for which absolutely no 
visual samples were part of the feature extractor’s training dataset [76]. To make inferences 
about these entirely novel classes, ZSL systems typically bridge the gap by utilizing 
supplementary semantic information. This external knowledge, which might consist of textural 
attributes, detailed distributions, or relationships between classes, provides the necessary 
context for the model to reason about categories it hasn’t visually encountered [77]. Thus, 
ZSL’s generalization capabilities root from this connection between learned visual patterns and 
auxiliary descriptive data, rather than from direct exposure to visual instances of that target 
classes, which distinguishes it from direct, limited, visual learning in OSL and FSL. In the re-
identification, class refers to a single individual of a species. These approaches are inspired by 
human ability to learn and recognize new objects or individuals rapidly, often after just one or 
a few encounters [78]. The critical value of such data-efficient learning techniques lies in their 
ability to address problems where collecting and labelling extensive data for every individual 
or class is difficult. This is common in fisheries and wildlife monitoring, where new individuals 
may appear, or only limited observations of known individuals are available for creating a 
reference gallery. 
One of the most popular ways of solving n-shot learning problems is the similarity comparison 
networks [68].

2.2.4. Similarity comparison networks in Animal Re-ID 
Similarity comparison networks operate on the fundamental principle of contrastive learning 
[79], which aims to learn a discriminative embedding space where the proximity of image 
representations directly correlates with the identity of the individuals depicted. The core 
objective of contrastive learning is to pull representations of positive (similar) pairs closer 
together while simultaneously pushing representations of negative (dissimilar) pairs further 
apart [79]. These networks are trained to determine if two input images belong to the same 
individual or different individuals in the case of Re-identification. This is accomplished by 
transforming input images into low-dimensional feature vectors, or “embeddings”, within a 
latent space (Latent space embeddings) [68].  
The Euclidean distance (or other distance metrics such as cosine similarity) between these 
embeddings is then used as a measure of similarity: a small distance suggests the images are 
of the same individual, while a considerable distance suggests they are different individuals 
[68]. 
In the context of contrastive learning, assume that we have a data point called x (anchor), 
another data point x+ which makes the positive pair with x. And data point x- which makes a 
negative pair with x. Bishop et al. [79] mention three main contrastive learning approaches: (1) 
Instance discrimination approach where the positive pair is made up of anchor and an 
augmented (corrupted) version of the same instance (image). This is mostly used in 
unsupervised re-identification scenarios [80]. (2) Supervised contrastive learning approach

The core objective of training these networks is to teach the feature extractor how to produce 
these meaningful embeddings. This is achieved through an iterative optimization process 
driven by the chosen loss function (Contrastive or Triplet). During training, both loss functions 
guide the learning process to modify the weights of the neural network. The fundamental goal 
is to maximize the distance in the latent space for dissimilar images, while simultaneously 
minimizing the distance for similar images. This process relies on backpropagation. 
1. Forward pass: A batch of input pairs or triplets is fed into the network. Since these 
networks utilize shared weights, each image is processed by the same feature extractor, 
ensuring consistency. Each image is embedded in the form of a vector [68]. 
2. Distance and loss calculation: The distances between the relevant embedding are 
calculated. Commonly the squared distance is preferred over the standard Euclidean 
distance. This is primarily for computational efficiency and stability during training. 
Avoiding square root simplifies the calculation of derivatives (gradients) needed for 
backpropagation [81]. The calculated distances are then fed into the loss function to 
compute a single value representing how ‘wrong’ the network’s current embeddings are 
for that batch. 
3. Backpropagation: This loss value is then propagated backwards through the network. 
This backpropagation algorithm calculates the gradient, essentially how much each 
weight and bias in the network contributed to the final loss [76]. 
4. Weight update: An optimizer algorithm (like Adam or SGD) uses these gradients to 
make small adjustments to all the network’s weights. These adjustments are designed 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
23 
 
to reduce the loss, meaning the next time the network sees similar inputs, it will produce 
embeddings that are closer together, and embeddings for dissimilar inputs will be 
further apart, thus satisfying the loss function’s objective [68]. 
Through many iterations of this forward pass, loss calculation, and backward pass (weight 
update) across thousands of batches, the network progressively learns to map input images into 
an embedding space where geometric distance accurately reflects identity similarity. 
2.2.5. Public datasets for computer vision tasks for fish 
Training deep learning models for fisheries monitoring applications requires rich datasets 
representing a broad range of EM systems scenarios. For re-identification tasks these datasets 
should be highly organized to perform closed-world and open-world scenarios. Only a few 
public datasets are available now. Bengston et al. [36] provided a list of available computer 
vision compiled datasets related to fisheries. It includes FishNet [82], Fish Detection (FD) [47], 
Fish Detection and Weight Estimation (FDWE) [49], DeepFish [83] and newly added AutoFish 
[36].  
The FishNet dataset [82] comprises images from EM cameras on Pacific longline tuna vessels. 
These, typically showing one to a few fish, include bounding box annotations. Due to the wide 
field of view common in EM cameras, the object resolution is generally low. In contrast, the 
FD [47] and FDWE [49] datasets feature images captured by a specialized light and camera 
system positioned above the conveyor belt. They also provide bounding box annotations, with 
FDWE offering weight data alongside occlusion levels present in both. DeepFish dataset [83] 
contains iPhone-captured images of fish on a tray. It provides instance segmentation masks and 
fish lengths, determined by calibrating against the known dimensions of the tray. The latest 
AutoFish dataset provides comprehensive features which includes bounding boxes, instance 
segmentation masks, both sides of fish and strategically divided training, validation and test 
data designed for fine grained analysis of fish [36]. The data was collected in a controlled 
environment using an RGB camera, with fish placed in various configurations on a white 
conveyor belt.

3. Materials and Methods 
This project’s methodology is structured to address two tasks designed to systematically 
address the key research questions. 
1. Task 1: Instance segmentation and species classification: To answer research question 
1, a state-of-the-art YOLO model is fine-tuned and evaluated on the AutoFish dataset. 
This experiment directly assesses the model’s ability to accurately identify and 
delineate fish species in both non-overlapping and overlapping configurations. 
2. Task 2: Re-Identification. This task delves deeper to answer research questions 2 and 
3, focusing on distinguishing the same individual fish across varying viewpoints, poses, 
and occlusion conditions within a ‘Closed world’ setting. This approach involves a 
comparative analysis of feature extraction capabilities: exploring both established 
CNN-based architecture and Vision Transformer (ViT) based approaches. We evaluate 
their performance not only with their foundational pre-trained weights but also after 
optimizing their discriminative power through metric learning strategy. The objective 
is to learn robust embeddings where different images of the same fish are mapped 
closely in a feature space, while different fish are pushed far apart. This task concludes 
with an in-depth error analysis specifically dissecting the impact of visual ambiguities, 
such as occlusion and the inherent challenges posed by inter- and intra-species 
similarities, or re-identification performance.

The project was implemented in Python (version 3.9.21), and all deep learning experiments

In this project, task 1 (Instance segmentation with species classification) and task 2 (Re-

3.2.3. Transfer Learning 
Transfer learning was employed in this research project which is a powerful machine learning 
technique where a model developed for one task is reused as the starting point for a second, 
related task. Instead of training a neural network from scratch, which requires vast amounts of 
labelled data and computational power, transfer learning leverages the knowledge such as 
features, patterns, and weights that a model has already learned from a large, labelled dataset 
[54], [79]. In transfer learning it is possible to train only the last layer or several layers 
according to the amount of available data and if the entire network is retrained to adapt to a

3.2.4.1. Instance Segmentation - YOLO (You Only Look Once)  
For the instance segmentation and species classification task, the state-of-the-art YOLOv11-
seg architecture was chosen due to its high accuracy, real-time performance and its efficient, 
unified design, which makes it a good candidate for instance segmentation [46, 86]. As a single-
stage model, it performs object localization and mask prediction simultaneously in a single 
forward pass, making it significantly faster than two-stage alternatives like the Mask R-CNN 
family [87]. Instance segmentation is highly important in fisheries catch documentation to 
extract fine-grained features of the fish species precisely for advanced applications like Re-ID. 
The core YOLO architecture is comprised of three main parts: the backbone, the neck, and the 
head. The backbone is a CNN that acts as the central feature extractor. It takes an input image 
and generates feature maps at various scales. The neck then fuses and refines these feature 
maps, ensuring that the model can effectively detect objects of different sizes (multi-scale data). 
Finally, the head is responsible for the final detection, using the processed features to predict 
bounding boxes, masks and classify objects within them [87, 88]. In the segmentation model 
architecture (Figure 9), the head uses the processed features to predict bounding boxes, classify 
objects and predict a set of mask coefficients that are combined with learned prototype masks 
to generate the final, precise segmentation for each instance. YOLO provides model 
architectures designed to different tasks like object detection, instance segmentation, oriented 
object detection (OBB) and pose estimation. The segmentation architecture replaces the Detect 
module in the object detection models and replaces with a complex YOLOESegment module to 
predict mask coefficients for each detected object [86].

3.2.4.2. Re-Identification – CNNs as feature extractors 
CNNs are a specialized class of deep learning models that have revolutionized computer vision 
[79]. Their architecture is uniquely designed to process grid-like data, such as images, by 
automatically and adaptively learning spatial hierarchies of features. The core building block 
is the convolution layer, which applies a set of learnable filters (also called as Kernels) across 
the input (Figure 10). These filters detect local patterns like edges or textures, and the same 
filters are used across different parts of the image. Therefore, CNNs are efficient and can 
recognize patterns regardless of their precise spatial location. As data passes through successive 
convolutional layers, the network learns increasingly abstract and complex features, for 
example from lines in the early layers to more detailed components of the objects in the deeper 
layers. Often the pooling layers (max pooling or average pooling) are distributed to reduce the 
dimensionality and create more robust representations. Non-linear activation functions like the 
Rectified Linear Units (ReLU) enable learning complex features and relationships. This ability 
to learn hierarchical features directly from data, rather than relying on manually engineered 
ones, makes CNNs remarkably powerful for tasks like image classification, object detection 
and segmentation [79]. 
In the context of a Re-ID pipeline, these learned features become the cornerstone for identifying 
individuals. A neural network tunes its internal weights through extensive training with labelled 
data (or pairs/triplets in similarity learning) to capture distinct characteristics essential for the 
task at hand [68]. This process produces a learned combined effect between the network’s 
weights and activation functions [89]. This interconnection forms a versatile pattern-
recognition capability. However, for Re-ID using similarity comparison networks like Siamese 
or Triplet networks, CNN’s role is primarily that of a robust feature extractor backbone. Instead 
of directly classifying an input image to a specific ID, the CNN (highly improved CNN 
architectures such as ResNet (Residual Network) [63] and GoogleNet/ InceptionNet [90]) 
tasked with transforming the input image into a dense low-dimensional embedding vector.  
To achieve this, the final classification layer of a standard CNN is typically replaced or 
augmented with layers that project the learned features into this specific embedding space [68]. 
The CNN feature extractor is then trained end-to-end within the similarity learning framework. 
The contrastive or triplet loss functions provide the supervisory signal, guiding the CNN to 
adjust its weights such that the embedding produced for images of different individuals are 
pushed further apart. This target training allows the CNN to learn features that are not only 
descriptive but also highly discriminative for the subtle differences that distinguish individuals.

The common practice of initializing these CNN backbones with weights pre-trained on large 
scale datasets like ImageNet, followed by fine-tuning on the specific Re-ID dataset, further 
enhances their ability to extract relevant and robust features.

ResNet-50 (Residual Network) 
 
The ResNet-50 architecture is a seminal 50 layer deep convolutional neural network (Figure 
11). Its core innovation is the “residual block” which utilizes skip connections to allow the 
network to learn an identity function [63]. A common and counterintuitive problem in 
designing very deep CNNs is the accuracy saturation and degradation problem, where simply 
stacking more layers can paradoxically lead to higher training and testing errors [63]. Simply, 
deep CNN architecture stops increasing their performance or even get worse by increasing the 
layers, primarily due to optimization difficulties such as the vanishing gradient problem. This 
means that deeper models are not always easier to optimize.  
Figure 12: Schematic diagram of the skip connections in Residual network. The green block represents the input
(x), which passes through a stack of blue layers forming the residual function F(x). Simultaneously, an identity 
mapping of the input (x) is carried via a shortcut connection. The output of the residual block is F(x) + x, which 
then feeds into the rest of the network (grey) to produce the final output H(x) (red). 
Figure 11: ResNet-50 model architecture. The red colored stacked layers represent residual blocks, and the 
curved arrows represent the skip connections in the ResNet-50 architecture. Source: [102] 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
 
35 
 
Instead of forcing network layers to learn a desired underlying function directly, ResNet 
reformulates the layers to learn a residual function in reference to the layer’s input. For a desired 
mapping of H(x), the layers are tasked with learning the residual F(x) = H(x) – x, with the final 
output being F(x) + x. This is implemented via “shortcut connections” that bypass one or more 
layers and perform an identity mapping, adding the input x to the output of the stacked layers 
[63]. So, the data transfer happens through the block and around the block (Figure 12). 
The ResNet-50 model (Figure 11) specifically employs a more efficient “bottleneck” building 
block, which consists of a stack of three layers: a 1x1 convolution to reduce dimensions, a 3x3 
convolution for feature extraction, and a final 1x1 convolution to restore dimensions [91]. The 
key insight is that if an identity mapping is optimal for a given block, the solver can more easily 
push the weights of the residual function F(x) toward zero than attempt to fit an identity 
mapping with a stack of nonlinear layers. This framework makes the networks easier to 
optimize and allows them to gain accuracy from a considerable increase in depth. As a result, 
a ResNet-50 and its deeper variants demonstrate significant accuracy gains over their plain 
counterparts, effectively overcoming the degradation problem and enabling the successful 
training of substantially deeper networks [63]. Pre-trained ResNet-50 model was sourced from 
PyTorch (Torchvision) (https://docs.pytorch.org/vision/main/models/resnet.html).  
3.2.4.3. Vision Transformers as feature extractors 
Transformers, introduced by Vaswani et al. [92] and based on the attention mechanism, 
represent a significant advancement in deep learning. They transform an input set of vectors 
into an output set of the same size and dimensionality, creating a richer representation space 
for downstream tasks [79]. Initially developed for Natural Language Processing (NLP), their 
effectiveness in transfer learning, self-supervised learning, scalability, and efficient parallel 
processing on GPUs has led to widespread adoption across various applications [79]. 
The core innovation is the self-attention mechanism [92]. This allows each sequence element 
to dynamically weigh its importance relative to others, effectively capturing complex 
relationships and long-range dependencies, unlike traditional Recurrent Neural Networks 
(RNNs) [79]. Transformers typically comprise stacked encoder and/or decoder layers (Figure 
13a). Each layer includes Multi-Head Self Attention (MSA), which runs self-attention in 
parallel to focus on different relational aspects, and a feed-forward network, a simple neural 
network applied independently to each element. The encoder learns input representations, 
while the decoder decodes these and combines them with other input for prediction. 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
 
36 
 
Popular transformer variants include Encoder-only [93], Decoder only [94] and Encoder-
Decoder [92] models. Encoder-only models are commonly used for discriminative tasks, 
exemplified by the Vision Transformer (ViT) in computer vision [79]. ViT processes images 
by dividing them into fixed size 2D patches, linearly projecting them into patch embeddings 
(Figure 13b). A learnable [class] token and position embeddings are added to these, forming 
the input for a standard transformer encoder. This encoder, consisting of alternating MSA and 
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) blocks, processes the sequence. The final output state of the 
[class] token is then passed to an MLP head for image classification [95].  
 
Swin Transformer  
The Swin Transformer is a hierarchical vision transformer (ViT) designed to serve as a general-
purpose backbone for computer vision, addressing key challenges that arise when adapting 
standard transformers from language to vision. According to the original paper by Dosovitskiy 
et al. [96], standard ViTs (Figure 13b) architecture produce feature maps of a single, low 
resolution and are limited by a computational complexity that is quadratic to the input image 
size, making them unusable for tasks requiring dense prediction or high-resolution inputs [95]. 
This limitation is rooted from ViT’s use of a global self-attention mechanism, where every 
image patch must calculate its relationship with every other patch in the image [95]. 
Consequently, doubling the input resolution would quadruple the number of patches, leading

To overcome these issues, the Swin Transformer introduces hierarchical architecture that builds 
feature maps at multiple scales, like a typical convolutional network. It begins by splitting an 
input image into non-overlapping patches, treating each as a token, and then progressively 
merges groups of neighboring patches in deeper layers to produce feature maps with varying 
resolutions. This hierarchical design allows it to be effectively used in dense prediction tasks 
like object detection and semantic segmentation [95]. 
The second core innovation is Shifted Window based Self-Attention (SW-MSA). Instead of 
computing self-attention globally across all patches, Swin Transformer calculates it within non-
overlapping local windows, which reduces the computational complexity to be linear with 
respect to image size. To allow for information between these windows, the window 
partitioning is shifted in consecutive layers, providing cross-window connections that 
significantly enhance the model’s representational power while maintaining efficiency [96] 
(Figure 14). There are six different Swin Transformer architectures available in PyTorch and 
Swin-Tiny architecture with pre-trained weights will be used in this thesis project

3.3.2.3. Data transformation  
Resizing strategy 
All cropped images, for both training and validation, were transformed into a uniform 224 x 
224-pixel resolution. This standardization was necessary to ensure compatibility with the 
ResNet-50 and Swin-Tiny (Swin-T) transformer architectures. A specialized transformation 
method was implemented for this resizing process to prevent the loss of any identifying 
features, a common risk with standard preprocessing pipelines. 
Common Transformation Practices and Rationale 
In typical deep learning pipelines, particularly when leveraging models pre-trained on large 
datasets like ImageNet, distinct transformation strategies are often employed for the training 
and validation/ inference phases.  
• Training Transformations: For training, a common approach involves techniques that 
serve as powerful data augmentation, enhancing the model’s robustness and 
generalization capabilities. A widely used transformation is random sized cropping 
(Figure 18). This approach randomly crops a region of the image, varying both its area 
(e.g., between 80% and 100% of the original size) and its aspect ratio (e.g., a default 
range of 0.75 to 1.333), before resizing the cropped region to the target square 
dimensions (224 x 224 pixels). This process intentionally introduces spatial variations 
and distortions. Additional augmentations, such as random horizontal flips, color jitter, 
and random erasing, further enrich the diversity of training data. Images are then 
converted to tensors and normalized using statistics (mean and standard deviation) from 
pre-training dataset (e.g., ImageNet). 
• Validation and inference Transformations: For validation and final model inference, the 
goal shifts from data augmentation to consistent and deterministic evaluation. The 
standard practice typically involves resizing the image so that its shorter edge matches 
a specific dimension (e.g. 256 pixels), followed by a center cropping to extract a 224 x 
224-pixel region from the exact center of the resized image (Figure 17). This ensures 
that the model is evaluated on a fixed, representative view of the object, allowing for 
reproducible and comparable performance metrics. Like training data, these images are 
converted to tensors and normalized with the same ImageNet statistics. 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
 
43 
 
This seemingly mismatched application of transformations (random for training, deterministic 
for evaluations) is a deliberate strategy within PyTorch’s torchvision ecosystem. It enables the 
model to learn broadly from varied inputs during training while being assessed precisely on a 
consistent input format [97, 98]. 
Potential drawbacks of standard transformations for Re-Identification 
Despite their widespread use, the conventional random sized cropping for training and center 
cropping for validation might present significant drawbacks for certain tasks, particularly fine-
grained re-identification involving non-square objects like fish: 
• Loss of distinctive features: For fish, unique identifying marks (e.g., specific fish shapes 
or location, tail patterns, body textures or patterns) are often located at their extremities. 
Center cropping can consistently discard these crucial outer regions, even if the model 
learned to utilize them during training. This compromises the integrity of the 
evaluation, as the model is not assessed on all potentially discriminative features. 
• Reduced feature coverage during training: While random sized cropping is effective 
for generalization, its random nature means that during training, the model may 
frequently encounter only partial views of fish, potentially omitting important features 
necessary for precise individual discrimination. 
Custom transformation for enhanced feature presentation 
To overcome these limitations, a custom transformation (resizing and padding to a square) was 
implemented and applied uniformly across both training and validation datasets. This approach 
prioritizes the complete preservation of the fish’s body and its features, unlike cropping 
methods that may omit information. The process involves two steps: 
• The image is initially resized that its longer edge fits the target 224-pixel dimension, 
strictly maintaining the original aspect ratio. 
• This resized, non-square image is then centrally pasted onto a black 224 x 224 pixel 
canvas. The remaining empty space along the shorter dimension is filled with black 
pixels.

The primary advantage of this custom method is the visibility of the entire fish to the model, 
ensuring that all potential re-identification features are always accessible, regardless of their 
locations within the original image. This contrasts with standard cropping and resizing which 
carries an inherent risk of obscuring key patterns. While this method successfully maintains 
object integrity, the introduction of padded black areas subtly alters the image’s overall 
statistical properties. This necessitates the calculation of custom channel wise mean and 
standard deviation values for the dataset during the subsequent normalization step to ensure 
proper data scaling which will be discussed next. 
Normalization 
After resizing, the images are usually normalized using a channel-wise mean and standard 
deviation. This is a critical preprocessing step for two primary reasons. Firstly, it standardizes 
the input data by scaling the pixel values of each colour channel to a similar distribution (mean 
= 0, standard deviation = 1). This ensures that features with larger numerical values do not 
disproportionately influence the model’s weight updates, which leads to a more stable training 
process and faster convergence. Secondly, as this work leverages transfer learning, 
normalization is essential for aligning the statistical distribution of our custom dataset with that 
of the ImageNet dataset on which the backbone models were originally trained. This 
consistency is crucial for the effective utilization of the pre-trained features. To overcome the 
potential problems with the introduction of extra black pixels to the image, channel wise means 
and standard deviations were calculated for the cropped and resized dataset (mean = [0.0495, 
0.0503, 0.0535] and standard deviation = [0.1370, 0.1363, 0.1412]). This normalization was 
applied to the training and validation datasets during the model training. A comparison of the 
results from the custom normalization and pre-defined normalization will be compared in the 
results section.

3.3.2.4. Feature extractor modifications 
For this re-identification task, both the ResNet-50 and Swin-T Transformer architectures were 
adapted to function as specialized feature extractors rather than classifiers. This adaptation 
involved two key modifications to the standard pre-trained models. First, the final classification 
(FC) layer in ResNet-50 and “head” in the Swin Transformer was removed and replaced with 
an identity layer. This step is crucial as it exposes the high-dimensional feature vector generated 
by the backbone’s final stage, providing a rich raw summary of the image’s visual content. For 
the ResNet-50 backbone, this resulted in a 2048-dimensional feature vector, while the Swin-T 
Transformer backbone produced 768-dimensional feature vector. 
Second, to transform these high-dimensional vectors into the desired 512-dimensional 
embedding suitable for metric learning, a dedicated embedding head was appended to each 
backbone. This head consists of a single fully connected linear layer. This layer performs a 
linear transformation, effectively learning a projection matrix that maps the input features to 
the lower-dimensional space. The 512 output dimensions were chosen as a middle ground to 
prevent retaining too much irrelevant or noisy information and efficient memory utilization by 
having higher dimensional output like 2048 and to prevent creating an information bottleneck 
to lose subtle details of the objects by selecting small output dimension size as 64 or 128.  
Specifically, for the ResNet-50, this linear layer maps from 2048 dimensions to 512, and for 
Swin-T Transformer, it maps from 768 dimensions to 512. The weights of this layer are not 
fixed. They are learned and optimized alongside the rest of the network during the training 
process with the Triplet margin loss. This ensures that the dimensionality reduction is not just 
a simple compression, but an optimized projection learned specifically to create a 
discriminative embedding space for identifying individual fish (Figure 19).  
3.3.2.5. Model training 
The training process for the feature extractor was designed to optimize the model for metric 
learning using the Triplet Margin Loss. This process, illustrated in Figure 19. It involves a 
carefully configured set of components, including the PK sampler, feature extractor, optimiser, 
loss function, and the iterative training and validation loop. 
To prepare fish identities for metric learning, a label encoding step was performed. Although 
the original fish IDs were numeric, their sparse and non-continuous nature resulted from the 
fish ID being split between training, validation, and test sets. Therefore, it was required to 
convert them into a dense, sequential integer format (e.g., 0, 1, 2..). This is crucial for 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
 
48 
 
compatibility with metric learning libraries, which rely on contiguous integer labels for 
efficient batch sampling, loss computation, and evaluation. To ensure reproducibility and 
consistency across datasets, unique fish IDs were first sorted lexicologically before assigning 
their new integer labels based on this order. These encoded labels were then associated with 
each image instance for the training pipeline. 
• Optimizer and Scheduler:  
An AdamW optimizer was chosen to update the model’s weights [99]. AdamW is a robust 
optimization algorithm that incorporates weight decay directly into the update step, which can 
lead to better generalization than standard Adam. To guide the training process and enhance 
convergence, a dynamics learning rate adjustment mechanism was employed. This mechanism 
monitors a key performance metric, specifically the validation loss, after each training epoch. 
If the validation loss failed to show improvement for a predefined number of consecutive 
epochs (in this case, 10 epochs), the learning rate is automatically reduced by a certain factor 
(here 0.2, meaning it becomes 20% of its current value). This dynamics adjustment allows the 
model to make larger progress in the initial training phases ad then fine-tune its weights more 
carefully with smaller steps as it approaches convergence, preventing overshooting and aiding 
in finding an optimal solution. 
• Triplet Margin Loss:  
The core of the training was the Triplet Margin Loss (Triplet loss) function. This loss function 
is ideal for Re-ID as it explicitly trains the model to create a discriminative embedding space. 
It operates on a triplet of images (Figure 20). 
o Anchor – A baseline image of a specific fish 
o Positive – A different image of the same fish as the anchor 
o Negative – An image of a different fish. 
The objective of the loss function is to minimize the distance between the anchor and positive 
embeddings while maximizing the distance between the anchor and negative embeddings. This 
is enforced by a margin (set to 0.5 in this experiment), which dictates that the anchor-negative 
distance must exceed the anchor-positive distance by at least this value. During the training 
process, data is processed in batches, and from within each batch, a triplet miner selects the 
most informative triplets to compute the loss. The number of such triplets found can naturally 
vary from one batch to the next depending on the data within the batch (Eq. (8)).

because identifying individual fish with high inter- and intra-species similarity needs fine-grain 
information to discriminate these individuals. We hypothesized that when these fish are almost 
identical, ‘Semi-Hard’ triplets might not provide a strong enough push for the model to learn 
the incredibly subtle differences. Hard triplets, force the model to confront the most confusing 
examples (such as in occluded or flipped side scenarios), compelling it to find those minute, 
highly discriminative features that are essential for successful Re-ID. 
• Iterative Training and Validation: 
The model was trained for a set number of epochs. In each epoch, the model processed batches 
of training data constructed by the PK sampler, calculated the triplet loss on the hard-mined 
triples, and updated its weights via backpropagation (Figure 19). The model state (.pth file) 
was saved only if the current epoch’s validation performance (e.g. mean average precision) 
improved upon the best score recorded so far. This ensures that the final saved model is the one 
that generalized best to the validation data, not necessarily the one from the very last training 
epoch. 
3.3.2.6. Re-Identification Evaluation 
Following the training of the feature extractors, a comprehensive evaluation pipeline was 
executed on the unseen test dataset to quantitatively assess the model’s performance and 
qualitatively analyze the embedding space (Figure 21). The process, detailed in the evaluation 
script, can be broken down into several key stages. 
 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
53 
 
1. Model loading and test set preparation 
Evaluation begins by loading the weights of the best-performing model saved during the 
training phase. The model is set to evaluation mode, which disables non-deterministic layers 
like dropout to ensure consistent outputs. The test dataset is loaded, and its images underwent 
the same custom resize and pad to square transformation and channel-wise normalization as 
the validation set to ensure consistency. Unlike training phase, no data augmentation is applied. 
For compatibility with evaluation tools that require integer-based labels for efficient 
calculations (e.g., AccuracyCalculator from pytorch-metric-learning library), the existing fish 
IDs in the test set were also converted to unique, sequential integer labels through a 
lexicographical encoding process, as described in section 3.3.2.5. This ensures a consistent and 
reproducible mapping of original arbitrary IDs to the required dense format for evaluation. 
2. Embedding extraction 
With the model and data prepared, the core inference step is performed. Each image in the test 
set is passed through the trained feature extractor in a forward pass. This process is executed 
without gradient calculations because the model has already been learning from the training 
data and is no longer intended to improve it. The output for each image is its final 512-
dimensional embedding vector. These embeddings, along with their corresponding ground-
truth fish IDs, are collected and stored for subsequent analysis stages. 
Following the extraction, the high-dimensional embedding vectors for each fish instance are 
stored in direct association with their corresponding integer labels. This pairing of embeddings 
and labels is fundamental for several downstream, purposes in the Re-ID pipeline: It enables 
quantitative evaluation by allowing the calculation of metrics like Rank-1 accuracy and 
mAP@R based on ground truth identity, It facilitates qualitative visualization of the learned 
embedding space (through t-SNE plots), by grouping and coloring points based on their 
assigned labels, and it is essential for correctly setting up query and gallery sets during 
evaluation protocol, ensuring that true matches can be identified for each query. 
3. Query and Gallery protocol 
To simulate real-world search scenario, the test is partitioned into a query set and a gallery set 
according to standard Re-ID evaluation protocols. For each unique fish ID present in the test 
set, one image is randomly selected to be part of the query set. All other images, including the 
remaining instances of the query IDs, form the gallery (a Tensor). This creates a challenging 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
54 
 
single-query setup where the system must find matching individuals for each query within the 
larger gallery database. 
4. Quantitative performance Metrics 
The performance of the model is quantified by comparing each query embedding against all 
gallery embeddings using the Euclidean distance. The gallery images are then ranked based on 
their distance to the query. Two standard metrics are calculated. 
• Rank-1 Accuracy (R1): This metric measures the percentage of queries for which the 
top-ranked image in the gallery (smallest Euclidean distance) has the correct ID. A high 
R1 score indicates that the model is highly effective at finding the correct match as its 
first guess. 
• Mean Average Precision at R (mAP@R): This is a standard metric used in various 
applications, including information retrieval, object detection, and object re-
identification, based on the Average Precision (AP) [72]. In this case, AP works as 
follows: taking a list of ranked matches l for a query q, the precision at every rank li is 
calculated, which is then weighted by the change in recall from rank i-1 to i. Precision 
shows all the True Positives (TP) from all selected positive matches, and recall 
describes how well the method can choose all the relevant samples at k predictions. AP 
can be calculated using Eq. (9). Since the list is ordered from smallest Euclidean 
distance to the highest, then the top matches (predictions) ideally should all be True 
Positives. If several queries are being investigated simultaneously, then mean average 
precision (mAP) can be calculated, by averaging AP for each query as mentioned in Eq. 
(10). The mAP can be computed at each rank i (e.g., mAP@1, mAP@5 etc), but 
typically the mAP value when the entire gallery has been investigated is reported.

3.4.2.5. Data visualization 
Kernal Density Estimation (KDE) 
KDE is a non-parametric statistical technique used to estimate the probability density function 
of a random variable from a finite dataset. Being non-parametric, we don’t assume that the data 
comes from a Gaussian distribution. Unlike histograms, KDE produces a smooth, continuous 
curve that provides a more accurate representation of the underlying data distribution without 
relying on predefined bins. 
The core principle of KDE involves placing a kernel (a smooth, symmetrical weighting 
function, in this case Gaussian function) over each individual data point. The heights of these 
individual kernel functions are then summed across the entire range of data to form the final 
density curve. A crucial parameter in KDE is the bandwidth, which controls the width or spread 
of each kernel. A smaller bandwidth results in a spikier curve, preserving more local details but 
potentially overrepresenting noise. In this work, the bandwidth was automatically selected 
using a data-driven method (e.g., Scott’s or Silverman’s rule) for optimal smoothing. 
In this fish re-identification, KDE is employed to visualize the aggregated distribution of 
pairwise Euclidean distances between learned embeddings across the entire test set. The 
process systematically collects distances for every comparison made between query and gallery 
images: 
• The positive pair distribution represents distances between embeddings of images 
confirms to belong to the same individual. These distances are accumulated by 
comparing each query image to all its true matches within the gallery, with ground-truth 
labels ensuring accurate categorization of same-identity pairs. Ideally, this distribution 
should be tightly clustered around zero 
• The negative pair distribution illustrates distances between embeddings of images 
belonging to different individuals. These distances are aggregated by comparing each 
query image to all non-matching images in the gallery, again relying on ground-truth 
labels to definitively identify different identity pairs. Ideally this distribution should be 
centered at a significantly higher distance separated from the positive curve. 
To ensure the reproducibility of these specific distance distributions across multiple evaluation 
runs, a fixed random seed was employed throughout the pipeline. This fixed seed guarantees a 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
63 
 
consistent query-gallery split, ensuring the precise same set of positive and negative distances 
are generated for KDE construction each time. For KDE, seaborn library was used. 
t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbour Embedding (t-SNE) 
t-SNE is a powerful non-linear dimensionality reduction technique frequently employed for 
visualizing high-dimensional data in a lower-dimensional space, typically two or three 
dimensions. In re-identification research, t-SNE serves as an invaluable tool for qualitative 
assessing the quality of the learned embedding space, by projection each image’s high-
dimensional embedding vector (e.g., 512-D) into a visually interpretable 2D or 3D plot. The 
core mechanism of t-SNE is to preserve the local structure of the data. It aims to map similar 
data points in the high-dimensional space to nearby points in the low-dimensional 
representation, while ensuring dissimilar points are located far apart.  
Subcategory level species classification analysis 
This analysis evaluates the selected best performer model’s ability to accurately classify fish at 
the species level across distinct dataset subcategories, such as separated initial, separated 
flipped, touched initial and touched flipped conditions.  
For each image instance within a specific subcategory, its embedding is extracted using the 
trained Re-ID model. The species of the single closest retrieved neighbour in the embedding 
space is determined and assigned as the predicted species for the query image. A normalized 
confusion matrix is then constructed, comparing the true species of each query against the 
predicted species of its top-1 match, with results presented as values between 0 and 1.

In Figure 25, across both training and validation sets, all loss curves show a healthy trend. They

training and then plateau at high values. The mAP50 scores for both boxes and masks are

The column-normalized confusion matrix (Figure 26) provides a granular insight into the

4.2. Re-Identification  
4.2.1. Zero-shot Instance Retrieval using pre-trained models (Baseline) 
Both Swin-T and ResNet-50 models performed poorly as off-the-shelf solutions for this Re-ID 
task. Swin-T achieved a Rank-1 accuracy (R1) of 3.19% and a mAP@R of 0.27%, indicating 
it rarely identified the correct match at the top rank and its overall ranking was largely 
ineffective. While ResNet-50 showed a comparatively higher R1 of 23.40% and a mAP@R of 
2.26%, these figures still demonstrate that neither pre-trained model is suitable for robust Re-
ID without further fine-tuning (Table 10). 
4.2.2. Preliminary experiments 
Our preliminary experiments investigate the impact of model architecture, image 
transformations, normalizations, and triplet types on fish re-identification performance, 
measured by mAP@R and R1 accuracy. Swin-T outperformed ResNet-50 consistently by 
achieving higher mAP@R and R1 accuracy across all evaluated configurations (Table 9).  
Custom normalization and image transformations (preprocessing) showed the most dramatic 
improvement from the standard image transformations and pre-defined normalization values 
in Swin-T transformer. Swin-T (Exp. ID 3) achieved a significant improvement, reaching 
41.65% mAP@R and 90.43% R1. This is a phenomenal R1 accuracy, meaning that for over 
90% of our queries, the correct fish is the very first one returned. The mAP@R is also higher, 
indicating better overall retrieval.  
ResNet-50 (Exp. ID 4) showed a slight improvement in mAP@R (8.84% from 7.86%) from 
the standard transformations and default normalization, but a decrease in R1 (52.13% from 
62.77%). This is interesting and suggests that while custom image transformation and 
normalization might help ResNet find more correct matches across the ranked list (higher 
mAP@R), it might occasionally push the absolute best match down a few ranks (lower R1). 
However, the gains are marginal compared to Swin-T. 
Hard triplet mining consistently outperformed semi-hard triplet mining for both architectures 
and normalization types. Hard triplet mining yields substantially better results (e.g., Exp. ID 1 
vs. Exp. ID 5 for Swin T default norm, 23.54% mAP@R vs. 2.48% mAP@R). Semi-hard triplet 
mining results in very poor performance across the board (mAP@R in the single digits, R1 
below 40%). Therefore, for the main fine-tuning session, we selected hard triplet mining along 
with custom image transformations and normalization values.

4.2.3. Main model training results 
This series of experiments investigates explicitly the influence of varying batch sizes (16,32, 
64, 256) on the performance of both Swin-T and ResNet-50 architectures, while maintaining 
the optimal settings for image transforms (custom), normalization (calculated), and triplet 
mining (Hard) as decided in the preliminary tests (Table 11). 
The Swin-T architecture demonstrated remarkable stability and high performance across the 
entire range of tested batch sizes. All Swin-T experiments achieved mAP@R values exceeding 
39% and R1 accuracies above 87%. This indicates that the Swin-T model, with its inherent 
ability to capture both local and global features through self-attention, is highly effective at 
learning discriminative embeddings for fish re-identification, even with varying numbers of 
samples processed concurrently. The peak performance for Swin-T was observed at both the 
smallest (16) and largest (256) batch sizes, reaching 41.65% mAP@R and 90.43% R1. The 
strong performance at a smaller batch size suggests that Swin-T can efficiently learn from the 
limited number of Hard triplets available within smaller batches. Here, 16 was formed with 
four unique fish IDs (P) and four instances per fish ID (K). Its powerful architecture likely 
enables it to extract highly salient features that are effective for distinguishing identities. This 
can be advantageous in scenarios where GPU memory is limited. The equivalent performance 
at a larger batch size (256) indicates that increasing number of samples and thus the pool of 
potential Hard triplets for mining does not significantly degrade performance, nor does provide 
a substantial breakthrough beyond the performance by batch size 16 (Figure 27).  
In contrast to Swin-T, the ResNet-50 architecture exhibited a clear and substantial improvement 
in performance as the batch size increased. That batch size of 16, yielded only 8.84% mAP@R 
and 52.13% R1. However, increasing the batch size to 64 (P=8, K=8) improved mAP@R to 
13.75% and R1 to 61.70%. Increasing it further to 256 (P=32, K=8) boosted R1 to 70.21% but 
showed a slight reduction in mAP@R to 13.56%. This behavior suggests the critical interaction 
between batch size and hard triplet mining for ResNet-50. In metric learning with hard triplet 
mining, larger batch sizes significantly increase the probability of sampling truly hard positive 
and negative examples. A broader pool of samples allows the algorithm to find negatives that 
are very close to the anchor in the embedding space (hard negatives) and positives that are 
farthest from the anchor (hard positives). Training with these more challenging examples 
pushes the model to learn a more robust and discriminative embedding space.

4.2.3.1. KDE plot results 
The KDE plots in Figure 28 visually illustrate the learned embedding space by showing the 
distribution of Euclidean distances between positive (same ID, green) and negative (different 
ID, red) image pairs. A well-performing re-identification model is characterized by a clear 
separation between these two distributions, with positive distances concentrated at lower values 
and negative distances at higher values, and minimal overlap. 
The plots for the Swin-T architecture (a-d), corresponding to batch sizes 16, 32, 64, and 256 
respectively, consistently demonstrate a strong separation between positive and negative 
pairwise distances. Here, the green distribution (positive pairs) is sharply peaked at very low 
Euclidean distances (approximately 1.5-2.5), indicating that images belonging to the same fish 
ID are closely mapped together in the embedding space. Conversely, the red distribution 
(negative pairs) is concentrated at higher distances (extending beyond 5-6). Crucially, there is 
very limited overlap between green and red distributions across all Swin-T experiments. This 
visually confirms the model’s excellent discriminative power, as it rarely confuses images of 
different identities with those of the same identity.  Plots (a) and (d), representing batch sizes 
16 and 256, respectively, exhibit slightly better separation with less overall overlap, visually 
reinforcing their top quantitative performance (mAP@R 41.65%, R1 90.43%). Important 
observation is that in batch size 16, the positive distribution is centered around Euclidean

5. Discussion 
This thesis aimed to enhance the automated fish documentation process within Electronic 
Monitoring (EM) systems by developing an effective re-identification (Re-ID) system capable 
of extracting fine-grained individual fish features. The research specifically addressed three 
research questions: the accuracy of instance segmentation models for fish on conveyor belts in 
various configurations, the comparative performance and suitability of different deep learning 
architectures for the Re-ID task, and effective strategies to improve Re-ID performance. The 
findings demonstrate not only the feasibility but also a potential pathway towards developing 
next-generation EM systems capable of individual-level fish tracking, thereby promising a 
paradigm shift in the quality and granularity of fisheries data. 
5.1. Summary of key findings 
This study achieved significant progress in developing a robust fish Re-ID system for EM 
contexts. 
Firstly, for instance, segmentation and species classification, the fine-tuned YOLOv11-seg 
model demonstrated exceptionally high class-wise performance across all species in the 
AutoFish dataset. It consistently achieved significantly higher mAP50-95 values with notably 
lower variability compared to the original AutoFish benchmark models (ResNet-50 and Swin-
B). Specifically, the model reached 98.47% mAP50-95 in the separated configuration, 92.48% 
mAP50-95 in the touched configuration, and 94.39% mAP50-95 in the combination of touched 
and separated configurations. 
Secondly, for the Re-ID task, the Swin-Tiny (Swin-T) architecture emerged as the superior 
deep learning architecture. It consistently outperformed ResNet-50 across all tested 
configurations and batch sizes, demonstrating its strong capability of learning discriminative 
embeddings essential for fish Re-ID. The peak performance for Swin-T was observed at both 
the smallest (16) and largest (256) batch sizes, achieving 41.65% mAP@R and 90.43% R1 and 
41.25% mAP@R and 90.43% R1, respectively. In contrast, ResNet-50’s performance was 
notably lower and more volatile, though it showed improvement with larger batch sizes. 
Baseline performance (Table 10) of the Swin-T architecture showed significantly lower 
mAP@R of 0.27% and R1 of 3.19% while the ResNet-50 showed a high performance 
compared to Swin-T (2.26 %mAP@R and 23.40% R1). 
In-depth analysis of the best-performing Swin-T model revealed varying performance across 
dataset subcategories, with lower accuracy in scenarios involving viewpoint changes and 
==PAGE:92==
 
 
 
87 
 
combined touching/ flipping conditions. Crucially, almost all rank-1 errors were found to be 
intra-species errors (confusing individuals of the same species), with very few inter-species 
errors. 
Finally, the research identified three critical strategies for enhancing fish Re-ID performance. 
• Custom image transformation using the resize and pad to square technique, which was 
crucial for preserving all identifying features of the fish body, which often has the 
potential to be lost with standard transformation methods. 
• Dataset-specific normalization values, calculated from the padded images, were 
essential for proper data scaling and effective utilization of pre-trained features. 
• Hard triplet mining consistently outperformed semi-hard mining, providing 
indispensable for learning the subtle individual differences required for fine-grained 
Re-ID. 
5.2. Instance Segmentation  
The successful implementation of any advanced automated analysis in fisheries, particularly a 
complex task like Re-ID is fundamentally dependent on the system’s initial ability to perceive 
the scene accurately. The exceptional performance of the YOLOv11-seg model in this study 
serves as more than just a preliminary result, it establishes a robust and reliable foundation 
upon which the entire Re-ID pipeline is built. These high-accuracy results represent a 
significant leap in capability for practical EM applications. 
The choice to employ state-of-the-art YOLO architecture proved pivotal. Its ability to achieve 
significantly higher mAP50-95 compared to the Swin-B benchmark in the combined 
configuration [36], highlights the advantage of using architectures purpose-built for object 
detection and segmentation. While transformer-based models like Swin-B (Base) are powerful 
feature extractors [96], YOLO’s architecture is holistically designed for locating and 
delineating objects efficiently in a single pass, primarily for real-time object detection and 
segmentation [86], [88]. Its advanced neck and head designs (Figure 9), coupled with 
sophisticated loss functions, are optimized to handle the spatial localization task with the 
precision that generic backbones often struggle to match. The minimal variability (low standard 
error) across 10 training runs further underscores the model’s stability and reliability, which 
are crucial attributes for any system intended for scientific data collection. 
==PAGE:93==
 
 
 
88 
 
5.2.1. The Primacy of Precision: A favorable trade-off for catch documentation 
A deeper analysis of the performance metrics reveals a critical and highly favorable 
characteristic of the model. For most species, precision was consistently higher than recall 
(Figure 23). This statistical pattern has a direct practical interpretation: the model is highly 
trustworthy when it identifies a species, but prone to missing some individuals altogether. High 
precision signifies a low rate of false positives, meaning the system rarely misidentifies one 
species as another (e.g., incorrectly labelling a cod as a haddock). Its confidence in its species 
classification is high. Conversely, a comparatively lower recall indicates a higher rate of false 
negatives, meaning the system more frequently fails to detect a fish that is genuinely present, 
especially when it is heavily occluded or in an ambiguous context. This is further supported by 
the inference results in Figure 24 (e), which visually demonstrates the model’s inability to 
detect fish in an occluded scenario. For fisheries science and management, this is a vastly 
preferable trade-off. Inaccurate species identification (false positive) can directly contaminate 
catch composition data, leading to a flawed stock assessment. For example, consistently 
misidentifying one species as ‘quota-limited’ could create erroneous reporting of catch against 
quotas, giving the impression of overfishing or non-compliance where none exists. In contrast, 
a missed fish (false negative) results in an undercount, an error that is often more 
straightforward to model, estimate, and correct for.  
5.2.2. Background misclassification: A source of false positives 
While inter-species confusion was notably low, a primary source of false positives for the 
YOLOv11-seg model was the misclassification of background regions as fish (Figure 26). This 
tendency suggests that the model is highly comprehensive in identifying potential objects, 
resulting in a low false negative rate. However, it also indicates a propensity to occasionally 
over-detect objects, flagging “fish” where only background exists. From an operational 
standpoint in EM systems, this implies that while the system is less likely to miss actual fish, 
some human verification may still be required to filter out these background-derived false 
positives. 
5.2.3. The Saithe anomaly: The impact of data imbalance on model behavior 
However, the case of Saithe reveals a fascinating and equally important exception. As a 
significant minority class in the dataset (~3% of the total fish IDs) (Figure 7 and Table 3), saithe 
was the only species for which recall exceeded precision. This reversal demonstrates the 
model’s adaptive learning strategy when faced with data imbalance. To ensure it correctly 
identifies the rare class, the model develops a highly sensitive decision boundary for saithe, 
==PAGE:94==
 
 
 
89 
 
managing to find and correctly identify almost all the true saithe that were present (high recall). 
The trade-off is a decrease in precision, as the model may occasionally misclassify a visually 
similar fish to saithe. 
This sensitivity is further reflected in the model’s performance stability. The noticeably wider 
95% confidence intervals for saithe’s metrics compared to other classes (Figure 23) indicate 
greater performance variability across the multiple training runs. This instability arises because 
the model’s final state is disproportionately influenced by the very few Saithe examples it sees 
during training. Small variations in weight initialization or the specific instances in a training 
batch can lead the model down different optimization paths, resulting in a wider range of final 
performance outcomes.  
The saithe anomaly is therefore not a failure of the model, but rather a critical diagnostic 
insight. It demonstrates that data composition directly dictates model behavior and reliability. 
For fisheries scientists and managers, this underscores a critical operational consideration: if 
accurate monitoring of a rare but important species is required, simply deploying a model 
trained on naturally imbalanced data is insufficient.  It necessitates a targeted data collection 
strategy or the use of specialized loss functions to ensure the model learns a decision boundary 
that is not only sensitive but also robust and precise. This was out of the scope of this research 
project. 
These findings suggest that a YOLOv11-based system can be trusted to provide high-fidelity 
species data, which is a critical component for stock assessment. While recognizing the 
complexities of real-world fisheries, this significant progress indicates that the challenge of 
accurately identifying species is gradually diminishing, allowing efforts to increasingly focus 
on achieving more complete documentation. 
5.3. Re-Identification: The concept of the Individual level of fish tracking 
The re-identification component of this study yielded crucial insights into effectively 
distinguishing individual fish within EM contexts. It directly addresses the second and third 
research questions concerning suitable deep learning architectures and performance 
improvement strategies. The findings highlight the significant potential of advanced deep 
learning approaches to overcome the limitations of traditional methods in tracking individuals 
amidst complex real-world conditions. 
Initial baseline experiments using off-the-shelf Swin-T and ResNEt-50 architectures revealed 
significantly lower performance compared to the fine-tuned models. (Table 10). This outcome 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
90 
 
was expected, as these pre-trained models had not been exposed to any AutoFish images and 
were evaluated in a zero-shot instance retrieval setup. Their inherent capacity, while powerful 
for general vision tasks, was insufficient to learn the specific, fine-grained discriminative 
features required for individual fish Re-ID without domain-specific adaptation. This stark 
contrast highlights the crucial importance of domain-specific training. 
5.3.1. Architectural advantages for the Re-ID performance 
The superior performance of the Swin-T architecture for fish Re-ID consistently across all 
tested batch sizes (Table 11) underscores the effectiveness of attention-based models in 
capturing the fine-grained visual characteristics necessary to distinguish individual fish, even 
among visually similar conspecifics (individuals belonging to the same species). Unlike 
traditional CNNs, such as ResNet-50, which primarily rely on hierarchical local feature 
extraction through fixed-size receptive fields, Vision Transformers utilize self-attention 
mechanisms to establish long-range dependencies and integrate information globally across the 
entire image. This inherent capability of Swin-T, which processes broader contextual 
information and simultaneously builds multi-scale feature maps through the shifted window 
self-attention mechanism [96] is particularly advantageous for Re-ID. It enables the model to 
learn subtle, non-local distinguishing marks (e.g., unique patterns along the fish body or 
relationships between features located at two extremities of the body) that might be missed or 
less effectively integrated by purely convolutional architectures. The observed robustness of 
Swin-T to varying batch sizes, where optimal performance was achieved with both small (16) 
and large (256) batch sizes, further underscores its architectural efficiency and strong feature 
learning capabilities, requiring less batch diversity for effective learning compared to ResNet-
50. These results also support the higher accuracy results obtained by using Transformer 
architecture in various re-identification studies [60, 100]. 
5.3.2. Methodological advantages for Re-ID performance 
A key methodological contribution and critical strategy for improving Re-ID performance was 
the effectiveness of our custom ‘resize and pad to square’ image transformation and dataset-
specific channel-wise normalization. This approach directly addresses a common and often 
overlooked problem in fine-grained Re-ID, particularly for non-square or irregularly shaped 
objects, such as fish. Standard transformations, such as random-sized cropping and center 
cropping, carry an inherent risk of discarding crucial identifying marks located at extremities 
or specific parts of the fish body (Figure 17 & Figure 18). By ensuring the entire fish is 
presented to the model through padding rather than cropping, we maximize feature availability, 
==PAGE:96==
 
 
 
91 
 
leading to significantly more discriminative embeddings. The necessity of calculating the 
custom channel-wise mean and standard deviation values for normalization was also crucial, 
as the introduction of black padded areas subtly alters the image’s overall statistical properties. 
This requires tailored scaling for effective utilization of pre-trained weights. The dramatic 
improvement in Swin-T’s performance with these custom transformations underscores its vital 
role. As shown in Table 9, mAP@R increased from 23.54% (Exp. ID 1: standard 
transformation) to 41.65% (Exp. ID 3: custom transformation) This methodological 
contribution is highly relevant beyond fisheries, for any re-identification task involving non-
square or irregularly shaped objects where complete visual information is critical. 
5.3.3. Triplet mining strategy advantages for Re-ID performance 
Furthermore, the consistent advantage of hard triplet mining underscores its importance for 
training robust Re-ID models. As shown in Table 9, Exp. ID 1 (Swin-T with hard mining) 
achieved 23.54% mAP@R vs 2.48% mAP@R for Exp. ID 5 (semi-hard mining). This is 
because it forces the network to learn from the most challenging examples, where negative 
samples are initially very close to positive ones in the embedding space. The model is 
compelled to refine its feature extraction capabilities to an extreme degree. This is particularly 
fitting for fish Re-ID, where high intra-species similarity demands highly discriminative 
features. The visual confirmation from KDE plots (Figure 28), showing clear separation 
between positive (green) and negative (red) distributions with minimal overlap for Swin-T, 
strongly supports the quantitative performance metrics. This clear separation indicates that the 
model effectively maps instances of the same fish identity to a compact region in the 
embedding space while pushing different identities far apart. 
Ultimately, the demonstrated capability for robust individual-level fish Re-ID is paramount for 
EM systems, enabling a transition from mere species-level assessments to precise individual 
tracking that directly enhances the integrity and utility of catch data for sustainable fisheries 
management.

The in-depth analysis of the best-performing Swin-T model (16 batch size) across various 
dataset subcategories provides crucial and fine-grained insights into the model’s capabilities 
and limitations in distinguishing individual fish. These findings are exceptionally vital for 
understanding how automated Re-ID systems can function effectively in the diverse and

challenging conditions encountered in fisheries EM systems. This will give insights to future 
data collection as well. 
5.3.4.1. Baseline Performance 
The highest Re-ID performance was consistently observed in scenarios where both the query 
and gallery images were from identical conditions (green highlighted cells in Table 12). For 
instance, “Separated-Initial” vs “Separated-Initial” achieved an impressive 78.36% mAP@R 
and 96.81% R1. This demonstrates the model’s near-perfect capability for rank-1 identification 
when fish are distinct without occlusion and standard orientation without any significant 
bending or deformations. Similarly, “Separated-Flipped” vs “Separated-Flipped” of the same 
fish in the above separated-initial set also maintained a strong performance (75.18% mAP@R, 
94.68% R1). These baselines confirm the model’s fundamental ability to learn highly 
discriminative individual features when there is less occlusion. This signifies that in optimal 
viewing conditions (e.g., individual fish clearly separated on a conveyor belt), the system can 
be highly trusted for individual fish identification, laying a strong foundation for automated, 
precise counting and individual-level length measurements. 
However, a noticeable performance drop occurred even with identical conditions when fish 
were touching (occluding): “Touched-Initial” vs “Touched-Initial” yielded 55.43% mAP@R 
and 77.66% R1. This highlights that physical interaction and resulting partial occlusions or 
subtle deformations introduce an inherent challenge to discrimination, regardless of other 
factors. The “Touched-Flipped” vs “Touched-Flipped” also showed a lower performance of 
61.68% mAP@R and 86.17% R1, further emphasizing the cumulative difficulty when both 
touching and flipping challenges are present within the same query-gallery type. This directly 
informs EM system design, suggesting that minimizing fish overlaps on conveyor belts, where 
feasible, or developing more sophisticated algorithms for handling occlusion, remains crucial 
for maximizing accuracy. 
5.3.4.2. Viewpoint symmetry invariance 
Evaluating the model’s robustness to viewpoint changes (initial vs flipped) revealed a 
significant drop in performance. Querying “Separated-Initial” against a gallery of “Separated-
Flipped” of the same fish individuals resulted in 53.40% mAP@R and 71.99% R1 (Blue 
highlighted cells in Table 12). This considerable reduction from the separated-initial baseline 
indicates that while the model exhibits some learned invariance due to augmentations, perfectly 
matching a non-flipped fish to its flipped counterpart remains a considerable challenge.  
==PAGE:98==
 
 
 
93 
 
This challenge was dramatically compounded within “Touched” conditions. “Touched-Initial” 
vs “Touched-Flipped” showed the lowest performance across the entire analysis, at 36.31% 
mAP@R and 52.87% R1. This near symmetry in the lowest-performing scenarios (when 
querying flipped vs initial or vice versa) underscores that the combination of viewpoint change 
and occlusions due to touching (occlusion) makes the re-Id task significantly difficult for the 
model. For fisheries monitoring, this suggests that scenarios where fish are handled or moved 
in ways that significantly alter their presented orientation while simultaneously overlapping are 
most problematic for automated individual tracking. Future EM camera placements or 
conveyor belt designs could aim to reduce the likelihood of these specific compound 
challenges. 
5.3.4.3. Pose Invariances 
The model’s ability to maintain identity when fish transitioned between “Separated” and 
“Touched” configurations (pose invariance) also showed significant declines. For instance, 
“Separated-Initial” vs. “Touched-Initial” dropped to 41.34% mAP@R and 62.09% R1 (Pink 
highlighted cells in Table 12). A similar behaviour was also shown with “Separated-Flipped” 
vs “Touched-Flipped”. This steeper decline compared to the simple flipping indicates that the 
touching condition introduces more substantial challenges, likely due to the resulting 
occlusions or shape distortions inherent in such interactions. This reinforces the need for 
innovative systems that can either prevent or robustly compensate for physical contact between 
them. 
5.3.4.4. Compound Challenges 
Interestingly, specific compound challenge scenarios, involving both viewpoint changes and 
pose variations, exhibited surprisingly high performance. For example, “Separated-Initial” vs 
“Touched-Flipped” yielded 63.81% mAP@R and 91.69% R1, sometimes surpassing even 
single-challenge scenarios. This pronounced symmetry suggests that while individual factors 
(touching, flipping) can hinder performance, the model can adapt remarkably well to specific 
combinations. This observation is counterintuitive, as one might expect compound challenges 
to always yield lower performance. This unexpected robustness warrants further investigation 
to understand the underlying mechanisms within the learned embeddings that enable the model 
to maintain high accuracy under these specific mixed conditions. This might be due to the 
nature of the learned embeddings, which are then faced with multiple types of variation, might 
focus on a different set of invariant features.

5.3.5. Implications 
The findings of this thesis, encompassing both instance segmentation and individual re-
identification, have profound implications for the implementation and future development of 
EM systems. This research significantly advances automated fish documentation processes, 
directly contributing to addressing critical data gaps and operational challenges in current 
monitoring practices, which are paramount for effective and sustainable fisheries management. 
5.3.5.1.Enabling precise catch quantification and individual-level documentation 
The combined high accuracy of YOLOv11-seg for instance segmentation and the robust 
individual-level Re-ID capabilities of Swin-Tiny architecture provide a strong foundation for 
automated fish documentation. This enables a transformative shift from species-level 
assessments to precise, individual tracking, automating tasks like counting and length 
measurements, even in challenging, occluded conditions. This yields high-fidelity data on total 
==PAGE:100==

catch, discards, and individual characteristics, vital for reliable stock assessments and 
regulatory compliance (e.g., Landing Obligation). 
5.3.5.2. Overcoming human reviewer overload and improving data integrity 
Current EM systems are severely challenged by the overwhelming volume of video data that 
demands extensive manual review. The automated pipeline developed in this study tackles this 
problem to a greater extent by performing tasks traditionally done by human interpreters, from 
segmenting individual fish to re-identifying them across complex sequences. This significantly 
reduces manual effort, resulting in more scalable, cost-effective, and consistent data collection. 
While the YOLO segmentation model tended false positives (over-detecting background as 
fish), this is often more manageable than missing actual fish (false negatives), allowing for 
targeted human verification and improving overall data objectivity and integrity, thereby 
fostering greater stakeholder trust in reported statistics. 
5.3.5.3. Informing targeted data collection and driving advanced fisheries management 
The fine-grained insights from in-depth analysis are crucial for optimizing future data 
collection and refining management strategies. The finding that almost all Re-ID errors are 
intra-species pinpoints the primary challenge for fine-grained individual discrimination. This 
knowledge allows for a strategic shift in data acquisition. Instead of broad, resource-intensive 
sampling, efforts can now focus on acquiring “hard negatives”. These are visually similar 
individuals from the same species. Such targeted acquisition is particularly crucial for images 
in challenging poses, highly occluded scenarios, or during specific handling actions, like the 
“Touched-Flipped” condition, which showed the highest Re-ID error rates. 
5.3.5.4. Advancing regulatory compliance and stock assessment accuracy 
By enabling consistent and accurate individual-level tracking, this research offers a powerful 
tool for improving compliance with fisheries regulations. More reliable data on discards, catch 
composition, and individual fish characteristics can inform more accurate stock assessments, 
leading to scientifically sound and defensible quotas. This fosters greater transparency, 
promoting ecological preservation and supporting the socio-economic well-being of coastal 
communities. The ability to precisely quantify and track individuals directly contributes to the 
“completeness” of fisheries data, which becomes more tractable as detection and classification 
accuracy improve, providing a clear roadmap for refining automated Re-ID systems for data-
driven, sustainable fisheries management.

5.4. Limitations 
Despite the significant advancements demonstrated, this study acknowledges several 
limitations that offer avenues for future work. 
Firstly, while the AutoFish dataset is meticulously designed and simulates realistic conditions, 
it is a controlled dataset captured in a laboratory environment. Real-world EM conditions on 
commercial fishing vessels can be far more extreme and unpredictable, presenting challenges 
such as harsh and inconsistent lighting, motion blur, water droplets and debris on camera lenses, 
and a wider diversity of fish species and sizes from different geographical areas. The model’s 
generalization capabilities to such highly unconstrained environments remain to be fully 
validated. 
Secondly, although the Re-ID system can identify individual fish across multiple observations, 
the practical challenge of continuously assigning and maintaining a unique digital identity for 
every single fish throughout an entire fishing trip is considerable. This necessitates open-world 
Re-ID systems. These systems would require sophisticated, highly efficient indexing and 
retrieval mechanisms to manage and query extensive galleries of known individuals, which 
was beyond the scope of this work. 
5.5. Recommendations 
Based on the findings and identified limitations of this thesis, the following recommendations 
are proposed for advancing automated fish Re-ID in EM systems and supporting sustainable 
fisheries management. 
• Extended hyperparameter tuning and Augmentation strategies: It's essential to explore 
the depths of hyperparameter combinations to see how the model performance changes. 
Data augmentation is also a crucial factor in increasing the diversity of training images. 
Extended studies can be done to evaluate the model’s performance by artificially 
simulating real-world consequences in EM systems, such as blurring the camera lenses. 
• Prioritize real-world validation and domain adaptation: It is crucial to test and fine-
tune the developed Re-ID models on diverse datasets collected directly from 
operational commercial fishing vessels and various EM systems. This will involve 
developing robust domain adaptation techniques to account for shifts in lighting, 
background, camera types, and fish species distribution encountered between 
controlled laboratory data and heterogeneous real-world environments 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
 
97 
 
• Implement targeted data acquisition strategies: Future data collection efforts should 
strategically focus on acquiring more high-quality data, specifically for the “hard cases” 
identified by this study. This includes visually similar individuals within the same 
species, particularly in challenging poses or highly occluded scenarios. And also 
develop and integrate new annotation guidelines that prompt human annotators to 
explicitly highlight subtle, discriminative features (e.g., Unique patterns, scars, fin 
shapes) crucial for individual identification, thereby providing richer supervisory 
signals for model training. 
• Optimize EM system design for automation: EM systems designers should consider 
optimizing camera angles, improving lighting conditions, and exploring conveyor belt 
designs that minimize fish overlap and complex occlusions. This would directly 
mitigate the most challenging scenarios identified for automated Re-ID. This will lead 
to higher overall system reliability and reduced human verification needs. 
• Developed integrated End-to-End tracking and Re-ID systems: Future work should 
focus on integrating the re-ID feature extractors into a complete “track-by-ReID” 
framework. This would combine object detection, segmentation, and multi-object 
tracking with Re-ID capabilities to seamlessly assign and maintain unique identities to 
fish as they move, disappear, and reappear across different camera views or over time. 
This includes developing a robust open-world Re-ID system. 
• Explore Multi-Modal data integration: For extremely challenging scenarios where 
visual cues alone might be insufficient, future research could explore the integration of 
multi-modal data. This may involve combining visual data with 3D information from 
depth sensors to obtain precise morphological measurements. 
6. Conclusions 
This thesis successfully aimed to enhance automated fish documentation within Electronic 
Monitoring (EM) systems by developing a robust re-identification (Re-ID) system capable of 
extracting fine-grained individual fish features. The research addressed critical questions 
regarding the accuracy of instance segmentation for fish on conveyor belts, the suitability of 
deep learning architectures for fish Re-ID, and practical strategies to improve its performance. 
The study’s findings demonstrate significant advancements in automated fish monitoring. The 
fine-tuned YOLOv11-seg model achieved high accuracy in segmenting and classifying fish, 
even in challenging occluded scenarios, providing a reliable foundation for subsequent 
==PAGE:103==
 
 
 
98 
 
analysis. For individual Re-ID, the Swin-Tiny architecture proved superior, leveraging its 
attention-based mechanism to learn highly discriminative embeddings for individual fish 
identification. Key strategies, including custom image transformations that preserve full fish 
body details and the strategic use of hard triplet mining, were instrumental in achieving this 
high performance. 
Critically, the in-depth analysis revealed that the primary challenge for fine-grained individual 
Re-ID lies in distinguishing visually similar individuals from the same species (intra-species 
errors), rather than misidentifying species. This insight, coupled with the identification of 
specific challenging scenarios, such as “Touched-Flipped” conditions, offers a clear roadmap 
for highly targeted data collection. 
In conclusion, this thesis presents a robust and automated pipeline for individual-level fish 
tracking in EM systems. By enabling precise catch quantification and reliable individual-level 
documentation, this work directly addresses the limitations of manual review, mitigates 
environmental and operational challenges, and enhances data integrity. These advancements 
significantly contribute to strengthening compliance with fisheries regulations and informing 
more accurate stock assessments, ultimately supporting the global goal of data-driven and 
sustainable fisheries management.
