ABSTRACT
The premise of this work is to determine the eﬀects of reﬂow solder residue on water ﬁlm
formation and corrosion of PCBA components, under the inﬂuence a conductive water
layer formed as a result of exposure to diﬀerent levels of temperatures and humidity.
Out of all the constituents of solder paste, the ﬂux package contains chemicals that have
the most signiﬁcant ionic activity in water when left behind on the surface of the PCBA.
These substances, which include halides, halogen, carboxylic acids, etc. have the strongest
eﬀect on increasing surface conductivity and causing corrosion and failure by mechanisms
such as electrochemical migration (ECM) and high leakage current. The ﬂux additives
have diﬀerent levels of hygroscopicity and ionic activity in water, and thus have varying
eﬀects on PCBA surface corrosion depending on the ambient temperature and humidity
conditions.
This thesis explores some of these conditions in a systematic manner, and is divided into 7
chapters, to reﬂect the same:
Chapter 1 gives some insight into the evolution of Surface Mount technology, the advent
of reﬂow soldering and the use of the same. An introduction to corrosion of electronic
devices is also included.
Chapter 2 gives the background for the entire thesis, based on a literature review. This
chapter has contextually detailed insights into the nature of humidity, the interactions
of humidity with the PCBA surfaces and the formation of water layer, and the failure
mechanisms induced by the formation of this water layer. Also included are the nature
and constituents of reﬂow solder, along with some corrosion-related and performance-
related properties of these constituents, all of which are ﬁnally connected to PCBA failure
mechanisms. These mechanisms need to be characterized and measured, which is why a
section detailing the imaging methods of SEM and LOM has been included as the closing
of this chapter.
Chapter 3 gives an insight into the various materials used for testing, such as the Test
PCBs, solder pastes and various equipment. This chapter also details the methods of the 4
experiments that make up the research work- these experiments simulate diﬀerent humidity
and water layer conditions, and make measurements to determine the eﬀects of the various
additives under these simulated conditions.
Chapter 4 discusses the results of these experiments, along with connecting the observed
phenomena to theory. Rankings of corrosion performance have been provided for each test.
Chapter 5 is a summary discussion of all the observations made from the experiments,
along with reasoning to support these observations and build a narrative.
Chapter 6 includes the conclusions made from the experiments together.
Chapter 7 makes suggestions for future work, based on the results of the current work.

INTRODUCTION
The creation of the microchip ushered in a new era of electronics technology, which led
to advancements such as the development of smartphones, complex electronic safety and
control systems in automobiles and manufacturing equipment just to name a few. These
developments have led to product designers and engineers trying to ﬁt more functions in
smaller areas of hardware, and integrated devices getting progressively smaller by the rules
of Moore’s Law. This has been made possible by signiﬁcant and ongoing developments in
Surface Mount Technology.
Figure 1.1: Increasing density of components in devices[65]
1.1
Surface Mount Technology
Surface Mount technology is a branch of engineering that deals with, as the name suggests,
placement on, or “mounting” of electronic components on a circuit board.
1.1.1
History and development:
In the early days of electronics technology, electronic circuits were manufactured in a
“point-to-point” manner with all the components connected to each other by hand. A
very painstaking and time-consuming process, this was changed in the 1970s-1980s to a
new form factor called the Printed Circuit Board (PCB) which included pre-fabricated
Silicon boards, with conductive Copper tracks embedded into the substrate that eliminated
the need for wires. This was a massive step forward enabled by the rise of automation
technology, which greatly increased the quality and output of electronic components.
However, initial versions of the PCBs were designed such that components on the board
were connected through leads that passed through holes drilled through the thickness of
the PCB substrate. This created a setup that was structured such that the components
were placed on one side of the board, and the connections between the metallic conducting
track of the board and the metallic leads of the components were made by soldering on
the opposite side of the board.
1
==PAGE:11==
Though much faster and automated than hand manufacturing, this process required a
lot of logistical planning and rather diﬃcult designing, as the pitch and layout, and even
the dimensions, of both the component connector leads and the PCB holes had to match
within very close tolerances, in order to be connected properly and form a conductive and
mechanically stable connection. Due to the diminutive (and reducing) dimensional scale of
the PCB assemblies that were the deﬁning feature of a rapidly modernizing electronics
industry, this would logically entail tight tolerances. This directly leads to increase in
errors during manufacturing, requiring regular human intervention to correct. Additionally,
the multitude of holes drilled through the PCB would result in a potentially compromised
structural integrity of the circuit board.
These issues were pretty much eliminated with the creation of solder paste, that enabled
components to be placed on top of the board itself, with no holes or leads required. The
high viscosity of the paste would hold the components in place, and also contained the
solder alloy itself, which was melted by placing the entire PCB assembly in an oven to create
solder joints using the reﬂow process. This led to the name “surface mount technology” as
the samples were placed or “mounted” on the PCB.
Developments due to growth and improvement of SMT components:
The removal of the need for through-hole mounting of circuit components has naturally
created many advantages in every aspect of circuit assembly, such as:
1. Increased component density- This is a primary improvement over through-hole PCB,
which cannot have multiple holes drilled too close by, which will end up signiﬁcantly
weakening and even breaking the board. The ability to mount components without
removing substrate material enables more dense circuits.
2. Smaller, Thinner and lighter boards- The resultant increased density means boards
can be made smaller, and lack of material removal means boards can be made thinner
without compromising structural integrity. Lesser overall material means lighter
boards.
3. Components size can also be reduced. This in turn leads to:
a) Shorter leads for components (as compared to leads that require to be longer
than the thickness of the board, for through-hole mounting).
b) A direct eﬀect of smaller components and high packing density on boards is
shorter interconnections between components down to µm scale.
4. Modern SMT has also resulted in highly automated production lines, with an increased
output level and quality as compared to older through-hole boards.
As advantageous as modern SMT is, it has created new avenues for corrosion of electronics,
which severely aﬀect long-term reliability of these components.
2
==PAGE:12==
Figure 1.2: Conditions that contribute to corrosion. From [1]
The main factors responsible for corrosion in electronics (PCBAs in the case of this work),
based on electrochemical cell formation are:
• Presence of combinations of conductive metals and alloys.
• A water layer of suﬃcient thickness that is formed as a result of ambient humidity in
the surroundings of the assembly.
• Presence of a potential bias (diﬀerence in charge) between conductors separated
by (or covered by) a water layer. Potential bias acts as a driving force for ionic
movement.
The increase in capability of electronic devices has led to devices being used for diﬀerent
applications in diﬀerent locations worldwide, with diﬀerent climatic conditions. Due to
the increase in use of electronics combined with multitude of locations of use, electronic
device failures have become more frequent. The nature of these failures varies in frequency
of occurrence and severity, but all of them are problematic from the perspective of being
economical to use. A predominant cause for failure is the inﬂuence of humidity which
causes a build up of water on the surface of Printed Circuit Board Assemblies, that
provides the conductive medium for electrochemical reactions between biased points on
these PCBAs (as mentioned in Figure 1.2). The reactions cause current leakage and other
multi-material corrosion failures that reduce device functionality. As mentioned before, the
reduced distance of component interconnections is advantageous for packaging of devices,
but also makes formation of interconnections between biased points easier, due to the
increased electric ﬁeld strength between the biased points (E=V/d, where E – Electric
ﬁeld, V-voltage, d-inter component distance) [1][3]
These avenues for corrosion/failure are thus directly related to a requirement of increasingly
robust PCB assemblies, which can withstand the increased operating loads (electrical,
temperature, physical forces) that come with PCB use in more aggressive environments
and high-powered applications. The components must not fail under operating conditions,
as failures can potentially cause extensive damage to attached equipment, ﬁnances, and
3
==PAGE:13==
life due to accidents. The robustness and eventual operational reliability of the ﬁnished
PCBA products is heavily dependent on every stage of the production and operation of
the PCBA[1], which includes:
1. Design and Printing of the circuit board.
2. Materials used in the components.
3. Materials used for cleaning the boards at every stage of the production process.
4. The ambient environment maintained during the stages of production and operation
(temperature, humidity, chemical cleanliness).
5. The joining process used to connect the components to the printed board.
6. The materials used in the joining process.
The introduction of solder pastes to PCBA assembly brought with it an ampliﬁed propensity
towards humidity-related failures of electronic devices.
Solder paste contains ﬂuxing
chemicals like weak organic acid (WOA) activators, which are necessary for keeping the
soldering surfaces clean and oxide-free while soldering. This ﬂux would originally be
cleaned oﬀafter assembly, in an additional step. “No-clean” ﬂux was created to skip this
step by using ﬂuxing chemicals that burned oﬀduring soldering. This is not a perfect
technology, as some amounts of ionic residues are left behind, that are hygroscopic, and
cause an accelerated build-up of water on the PCBA surface[4][5].
Diﬀerent chemicals like amines and metal halides are added to solder pastes in order to
tailor the properties (solderability, surface cleanliness, printability) of the pastes to the
application. This comes with the caveat of diﬀerent residues of these additives left behind
on the PCBA surface, all of which combine to produce diﬀerent eﬀects on the failure and
corrosion reliability.
Devices that are used in non-regulated weather conditions (outdoor exposure, for example)
experience varying levels of temperature and humidity, which are often cyclic in nature
(day and night). This regular cycling further accelerates the accumulation of water layers
on the PCBA surface. The eventual combination of shorter conductor component distances,
increased presence of various ionic residues and thicker water layers leads to the formation
of highly conductive electrochemical cells that use the inﬂuence of applied operational
voltage on components to cause a reduction in a property of the PCBA surface material
called Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR) that occurs as a result of increased current
leakage. This is exacerbated by continued water exposure, until a point of breakdown
of the PCBA SIR, at which failure through Electro-Chemical Migration (ECM) occurs,
characterized by metal dendrites that bridge closely spaced conductors.
There are diﬀerent types of failures apart from Leakage Current and ECM, such as galvanic
corrosion, Conductive anodic ﬁlament formation, creep etc., which are based on diﬀerent
mechanisms (some of which involve impregnation of PCBA substrate by water and other
substances). However, the eﬀects of solder paste residues are largely limited to PCBA
surface failure mechanisms- Leakage current and ECM, as they do not penetrate the outer
surface of the PCBA.
4
==PAGE:14==
This Master’s Project is aimed at studying the eﬀects of the diﬀerent solder paste ﬂux
activators and other additives on corrosion reliability, under humid conditions. The focus
is on points 4, 5 and 6 of the previous list of PCBA corrosion contributors. The factors of
ambient environment conditions, joining process and materials used in the joining process
are the main contributors to PCBA surface corrosion according to ﬁgure 1.2, and thus
form the basis of the literature review.
On the experimental side, the eﬀects of constant and cyclic humidity on surface conductivity
were studied by using both non-destructive (AC) and destructive (DC) testing methods
with diﬀerent water layer amounts, in order to obtain a more practical picture of the
reliability issues resulting from potential operational conditions.
1.2
Motivation for the Thesis
The purpose of this MSc Thesis is to build on the current understanding of the eﬀects
of solder paste additives on the build-up of an adsorbed surface water layer, which is a
leading reason for corrosion in electronics. This is currently a leading cause for electronic
device failure, which necessitates comprehensive prevention measures. The studies carried
out in completion of this project are aimed at correlating the chemistries and properties of
the diﬀerent solder paste additives to the multitude of electrochemical reactions that occur
in an adsorbed water layer on the PCB surface. The results from this work can be used to
aid the selection of chemical additives with the viewpoint of creating a synergy of good
solderability, joint quality, corrosion reliability and subsequently device robustness.

1.3
Scope of the Present Work
Figure 1.3: Project Work-Flow
This MSc project is comprised of a systematic ﬂow of experiments that were aimed at
characterizing PCB surface conductivity as a function of the release and evolution of
solder ﬂux and additive residues, under the inﬂuence of temperature and humidity. A
detailed investigation into the release of residue laid the basis for the investigations, and
the evolution of said residues under diﬀerent conditions of water layer thickness was
studied. Non-destructive and destructive testing was combined with varying the ambient
temperature and humidity separately, in order to get a clearer picture of the complex
combined eﬀects of contaminant residue, ambient climatic conditions and applied potential
bias on surface corrosion reliability of Printed Circuit Board devices.

BACKGROUND-LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1
Corrosion in PCBAs
The shift from through-hole to SMT has allowed for an increased density of components
being packed onto PCBAs, and a signiﬁcant reduction in inter-component spacing. This,
however, increases the electric ﬁeld between the biased points (E=K∗q/r2) which in turn
increases the propensity of formation of a corrosion cell under the inﬂuence of moisture
encountered during daily operation. Keeping in line with studying the contributors to
corrosion, the ﬁrst step is the formation of water ﬁlm.
The focus of this thesis being the eﬀects of humidity on PCB reﬂow residue, it is of
paramount importance that suﬃcient elaboration be made on the nature of humidity
itself, given that one of the basic requirements for corrosion in general is the presence of a
moisture/water layer, that facilitates the transfer of charge between conductors to “create”
corrosion.
2.2
What is Moisture?
The Oxford Dictionary deﬁnes moisture as “water or other liquid diﬀused in a small
quantity as vapor, within a solid, or condensed on a surface” [6]. In the context of this work,
the focus will be solely on moisture consisting of water. A good strategy to understand the
nature of moisture is to start with the substance that makes up this context of moisture-
water, with a look at some of the properties of water that form the basis of the experiments
described henceforth.
Water
The most abundantly available compound on earth, water is a highly mobile dipole molecule
with a small volume, consisting of a H-O-H construction. The high mobility and small size
make it an eﬀective penetrant and ﬁller substance[7].
The H-O-H bonds formed are covalent on an intramolecular level, but interact with solid
matter through directional “Hydrogen bonds” Hydrogen bonds are attractive bonds formed
between positive and negative charges of interacting atoms, which are how water molecules
form bonds between each other, in a process called cohesion. In a similar vein, the formation
of hydrogen bonds between water molecules and other solids occur in a phenomenon called
adhesion[7].
2.2.1
Contextual Interactions of water
Water is omnipresent in the ambient environment, being present in both liquid and gaseous
forms. When in liquid form in a contained environment (constant temperature), there is
always an amount of water molecules that keeps changing states between liquid and solid.
7
==PAGE:17==
The rate of change of state of water is dependent on the temperature of the system. The
general properties of water molecules in gaseous state can be described mathematically by
the Ideal Gas Law equation, which is
pV=nRT, where p = pressure (N/m2 Pa), V = gas volume (m3), n = mole number of gas
molecules (mol), R= universal gas constant (8.3145 J/mol.K), T = absolute temperature
(K)[8].
Water vapor molecules in a contained system exhibit random motion within the conﬁnes
of the contained system. They additionally exert a pressure on the walls of the containing
system. However, this pressure is only a part of the total vapor pressure acting within the
system- there is a mixture of air and water molecules present, both of which contribute to
the total pressure of the system. these two gaseous components (dry air and water vapor)
exert partial pressures, which amount to the total vapor pressure.
The ideal gas equation for moist air, which is a common operational condition for most
components, is
pT.V = (nair + nW).R.T, where nair and nW are the number of molecules [mol] of air
and water respectively.
The vapor pressure of the two-component mixture increases with increased movement of
molecules inside the system. However, if the system is left undisturbed (temperature is
kept constant), there eventually comes a point where the net change in number of molecules
between states becomes zero. This an equilibrium state, that remains so until external
energy is added to the system, in the form of heat. This is in accordance with the 2nd law
of thermodynamics. The air is said to be “saturated” with water vapor, and the pressure
in the system becomes constant. Thus, this saturated vapor pressure is a function of
temperature.
The addition of a non-volatile solute to a solvent tends to reduce the vapor pressure of
water in the system. Putting it in simpler terms, the amount of water in gaseous form
reduces, due to water molecules in liquid forming bonds with the solute molecules and
remaining in the bulk liquid.
Solutes that dissociate into ions in water tend to have a greater eﬀect on reducing water’s
vapor pressure, as compared to solutes that do not undergo dissociation[8]. This can be
attributed to the polar nature of the water molecule, which allows is to be attracted to
both positively and negatively charged ions. These ions are the result of dissociation, and
thus succeed in retaining more water in the liquid form. This interaction will form the
basis of most of the henceforth discussed study.
Humidity
The amount of water vapor present in air is called humidity. The abundance of water all
around us, combined with diﬀerent temperatures that vary with location, to speak of one
factor, and other substances (natural and man-made) that are present in water sources all
over the world, makes for diﬀerent amounts of humidity in the ambient air.
8
==PAGE:18==
Quantiﬁcation of humidity is done in two ways:
1. The ﬁrst of these ways is Absolute Humidity, which is the term that is used
in meteorological descriptions and is also the quantity that the colloquial term
“humidity” represents.
Absolute humidity is the amount of water vapor present in the ambient air, represented
as a ratio
AH = mH2O/Vair (g/m3)
This amount varies with temperature, and every temperature point has a ﬁxed
saturation vapor content [8].
2. The second quantiﬁcation of humidity is Relative Humidity, which is a term more
widely used in scientiﬁc applications.
Relative humidity is described as a ratio between the real partial pressure of water
vapor in the system under consideration, to the saturation water vapor pressure
(the maximum amount of water vapor that can theoretically be held by air at that
temperature). It is expressed as a percentage value.
RH% = pw/po ∗100 [8].
This thesis will be centered around the interaction and eﬀects of varying relative humidity
with ionic solder ﬂux residues on the surface of PCBAs. It is thus necessary to not only
provide a background on humidity itself, which has been done, but also to describe the
nature of manifestation of humidity with respect to the surface of PCBAs, and the eﬀects of
this on the performance and reliability of said PCBAs, after interactions with contaminants
on the PCBA surface.
Humidity interactions with PCBA surface
Humidity (water vapor in air) has a rather multi-faceted mode of interactions with solid
materials; it is not as straight-forward as just condensation. The ingress of water on solid
surfaces is very dependent on the material, morphology, physical features, contaminant
presence, etc. of the solid. Each of these features of the solid lead to 5 main interaction
modes between water and surfaces[8], [9]:
1. Adsorption on to the surface of the solid
2. Capillary condensation
3. Deliquescence
4. Crystal hydrate formation
5. Vapor absorption into bulk of amorphous solids
Out of these methods, adsorption results in alteration of the surface properties of the solid,
deliquescence and capillary condensation result in build-up of condensed water on the
surface, absorption and formation of crystal hydrates result in high levels of ingress of
water into the solid bulk itself[8].
9
==PAGE:19==
To maintain brevity, focus will now be switched to creating a highly contextual explanation
of diﬀerent properties that directly aﬀect the scope of this work. Thus, the main focus will
be the condensation interactions of ambient humidity by surface adsorption the surface of
the PCB, that result in deliquescence of residues present on the surface of circuit boards.
Formation of water layer
The water from ambient atmosphere, under normal operating conditions, does not penetrate
the PCB substrate, but is condensed on the surface due to its hydrophilic nature. This
results in the formation of adsorbed water layers on the surface, which are the result of
interaction of water with the surface through hydrogen bonds, electrostatic attraction, etc.
In the case of PCBA material, the mechanism for formation of water layers is mainly
hydrogen bonding for the ﬁrst monolayer, where the water molecules are very tightly bound
to the surface and contaminant (if any) molecules, essentially rendering them immobile.
This “wets” the surface and increases surface polarity (surface energy too) which allows
more water monolayers to be deposited through condensation[10]. The layers have been
observed to form as islands initially, which eventually connect to form one continuous layer
of water[11].
It has been observed in previous research that for a metallized Alumina substrate, one
monolayer (single molecule thickness) of water is absorbed at about 35% RH. As RH is
increased to ca. 70% , 5 monolayers are adsorbed, and this number keeps increasing all
the way till 100% RH[11]. This illustrates that the closer to saturation relative humidity
(and higher vapor pressure, based on what has been explained in the previous section),
more water is adsorbed on the layer[12]. This conclusion also extends to modern PCBA
materials.
The amount of water that enters the PCBA system through condensation is rather small
in the context of bulk water scale, and the water layer, if consisting of pure water, does
not result in the formation of a signiﬁcant leak current between biased conductors, and
any related corrosion, due its relatively lower conductivity compared to bulk water[13].
The main issue of corrosion due to increased surface water layer conductivity arises in the
presence of ionic contaminants, which are added to the system due to the multitude of
manufacturing and handling processes that are entailed in production of PCBAs.
Contaminants on PCBAs
Manufacturing processes that constitute PCBA production are rather complex in the
current form, and include combinations of etching and plating to get a well-made substrate
with accurate conductive tracks and spacing of components. These processes are achieved
by using chemical baths with solder masks etc. all of which have a variety of diﬀerent
chemistries which can leave behind miniscule amounts of contamination around the PCB
surface[8], [14].
Additionally, processes such as mounting of components, handling, inspection etc. lead to
10
==PAGE:20==
the inclusion of various ionic residues (eg. acid decomposition byproducts that supply H+
ions) on the surface of the components [15], which can form highly conductive electrolytes
under conditions of deliquescence of the residues which happens in the case of residues
being hygroscopic in nature. The critical relative humidity level cRH required for formation
of the electrolytic cell is lowered[16][1][17].
Soldering is another major source of contamination of PCBs, with solder ﬂuxes being the
signiﬁcant contributor of contaminants. The nature of ﬂuxes and the subsequent eﬀects of
humidity on them vary with many factors, the most important of which being the nature
of soldering[5]. The scope of this work is concerned with the ionic residues of solder paste
additives left behind after reﬂow soldering, which combine with formed water layer to
cause corrosion by mechanisms such as Leakage current and electrochemical migration
(ECM).

2.3
PCB Failure mechanisms
The presence of ionic ﬂux residues on the surface of the humidity-exposed PCB leads to
two main failure mechanisms:
2.3.1
Leakage current due to reduction of Surface Insulation
Resistance (SIR)
Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR) is an extrinsic material property that determines
the conductivity of the surface of a dielectric material. The oﬃcial deﬁnition for SIR in
IPC-9201 is “a property of the material and electrode system. It represents the electrical
resistance between two electrical conductors separated by some dielectric material(s). This
property is loosely based on the concept of sheet resistance, but also contains elements
of bulk conductivity, leakage through electrolytic contaminants, multiple dielectric and
metallization materials and air” .
11
==PAGE:21==
This deﬁnition speciﬁes SIR as a combination of bulk and sheet resistance, but 99.9% of
the current leakage observed on FR-4 laminate (the material most commonly used to make
PC boards) is on the surface of the material, due to the surface-to- bulk volume resistivity
ratio being 1:1000 [10].
Exposing surfaces to humidity inevitably leads to adsorption of water on the surfaces.
Depending on the surface polarity, hydrophilic (high-polarity) surfaces will have more
presence of water, and hydrophobic (low-polarity) surfaces will have lesser presence of
water[18]. The water layer formed even at lower humidity levels of 60% RH increases
surface conductivity (lowers surface insulation resistance), but by itself is not enough to
cause failure.
This is in part due to the low conductivity of the thin water layer formed (by virtue of
hydrogen bonds formed between the PCB surface and water molecules of thin monolayers).
The presence of surface contaminations that arise from soldering, handling, operational
conditions etc. can cause large enough drops in SIR (due to high electrolytic conductivity
of surface water ﬁlm) to cause failure[19].
The drop in SIR is heavily dependent on the ambient RH and temperature, with higher levels
showing greatly decreased SIR[10]. An added presence of hygroscopic ionic contaminants
bolsters the drop in SIR to the point of causing leakage currents that hamper device
functionality- this is due to the fact that higher content of hygroscopic contaminants
attract more water from the humid surroundings, leading to a thicker water layer. As
mentioned before, the cRH for deliquescence of contaminants is also reduced, which causes
more dissociation and diﬀusion of conductive ions into the thick water layer, and a drop in
SIR[10], [16], [20].
The concept of SIR is not as straightforward as it may seem; being an extrinsic material
property, it is also inﬂuenced by other operational factors such as temperature and
humidity amounts and variation (cyclic), potential bias between conductors, spacing
between conductors, etc[10], [21].
The increased water ﬁlm conductivity due to the above-mentioned factors can cause
bridging between closely-spaced conductors and a leakage current, which if high enough,
can seriously hamper device functionality due to parasitic leakage circuits. If the SIR
drops low enough to the point of break-down of surface resistive nature, another failure
phenomenon called electrochemical migration can be caused.
2.3.2
DC Electrochemical Migration (ECM)
This is an interesting failure mechanism that is characterized by the formation of metal
ﬁlaments (dendrites) between closely-spaced conductors on a PCBA surface[8], [22]–[25].
These dendrites are the result of migration of metal ions between the biased conductors,
that arises from a diﬀerence in pH between anode and cathode (depends on polarity of
applied DC voltage).
The leakage of current due to lowered SIR and increased electrolyte ionic conduction results
12
==PAGE:22==
in a reduction process at the cathode and an oxidation process at the anode, based on the
following reactions[8]:
Cathode:
2H2O + 2e- →2OH- + H2
OR
½O2 + H2O + 2e- →2OH-
Anode:
H2O →½O2 + 2H+ + 2e-
This results in an acidic pH at the anode and a basic pH [pH is deﬁned by the concentration
of H+ (acidic) and OH- (alkaline)]at the cathode, creating a pH gradient that can either be
sharp at the center of the region between the conductors, or show an overlap, depending
on the space between conductors[22].
Additionally, the anode can involve oxidation reactions of metals too. In this case, the
anode will experience a dissolution of metal (through oxidation into metal ions):
Me →Mez+ + ze-
If the prevailing electrolyte conditions are suitably conducive, and a suﬃcient bias voltage
is present, these metal ions migrate to the cathode (due to pH gradient-the pH level for
corrosion can be determined from a pourbaix diagram of the metals in question) where
they undergo reduction into pure metal and are deposited, thus increasing the cathode size
at the point of deposit (which also increases reaction rate). The ions attach successively
to the deposited metal due to a reduced distance between the conductors at the point of
deposit (energetically more favorable) thus forming dendrites which eventually reach the
anode, forming a metal bridge, and causing a short circuit failure.
The switch over to use of Sn-based (Tin) solder alloys has brought about a change in
study of ECM. Traditional solder alloys used Sn-Pb (Tin-lead) eutectic, which had lower
resistance to ECM due to the high susceptibility of Pb to dissolution [26], [27]. The new
SAC alloys consisting of Sn, Ag, Cu (Tin, Silver, Copper) have been found to possess an
increased resistance to ECM, due to the lower propensity of Sn to dissolve in ionic form.
An alkalization reaction (increased concentration of hydroxyl ions) at the cathode leads
to precipitation of Sn(OH)4 which is a stable compound, and prevents ECM. Increased
alkalization (higher pH) can lead to formation of Sn(OH)6- which can be reduced to
metallic Sn at the cathode.[22]
Presence of Corrosion-promoting substances like halides is expected to increase Sn
dissolution and surface ionic conductivity, but decrease ECM due to insuﬃcient alkalinity
required to form Sn(OH)6- . It can however result in intense anodic degradation in lower
alkalinity, and intense ECM ﬁlament formation if other residues that raise alkalinity are
present on the PCB surface.[22], [28]
Just as in the case of SIR, the occurrence of ECM also depends on the thickness of water
13
==PAGE:23==
layer and its conductivity, which is a function of amount of hygroscopic ionic contamination,
and the prevailing humidity conditions[22]. The thickness of water layer required to cause
ECM varies between metals, with Ag and Cu needing only a “humid” condition (thin,
invisible) water layer, and Sn, Au, etc. requiring a thicker “condensed” condition [22]. This
thicker layer can be formed in case of cyclic variations of temperature and/or humidity,
which is often the case for electronic devices in outdoor conditions.
There are also other failure mechanisms observed in general, such as galvanic corrosion,
creep corrosion, Conductive Anodic Filament formation, polymer degradation, etc[2]. but
these are outside the scope of this work (the occurrence is not limited to the outer PCBA
surface) and have thus not been explored.
2.4
Soldering
As previously discussed, the method of joining PCBA components and the materials that
comprise the method are also contributors to corrosion- This section will thus look at Reﬂow
soldering and its components.
Soldering is currently the leading method of joining electronic components to Printed
Circuit board assemblies. the process of soldering is similar to welding (albeit on a smaller
scale), where a molten metal is used as a bonding agent to connect metallic parts.
The parts involved in the soldering process are always metallic and consist of the “connector”
part of the component, which are usually in the form of pins, connected to solder pads.
2.4.1
Solder
The metallic connection made between PCB components and the PCBA surface is made
of solder, which is a fusible metallic alloy with the following features[29]:
1. Low melting point compared to metals to be joined- this is required for easy
workability of the alloy, which simpliﬁes the joining process and minimizes damage to
the PCBA and component materials, which are usually polymers, with lower melting
points. Higher solder melting temperatures will damage the integrity of the PCBA
as a whole.
2. Short solidiﬁcation time- the molten solder is required to solidify rather quickly,
to avoid spreading of the material to other parts of the PCBA, and thus increase
the accuracy of the connection. Additionally, longer solidiﬁcation times can lead
to inclusion of greater amounts of impurities in the joint, which can compromise
the operational strength and even the conductivity of the ﬁnal formed joint. Short
solidiﬁcation time can avoid these issues.
3. The solder material should not react and form compounds with the connector
materials, or the PCBA substrate.
These properties of solder alloys are critical to successful process execution. However, the
nature of the joints formed are also inﬂuenced by the method of application of the solder,
of which there are a few[29]:
14
==PAGE:24==
1. Hand soldering- This method involves the use of a solder iron, which is heated
(usually electrically) to the melting point of the solder material. In order to prepare
the surface for soldering, solder ﬂux is often applied by hand, which functions to
increase surface wettability and achieve better coverage of solder material over the
required area. The solder metal is then applied by melting solder wire, or by using
solder paste. This method is much slower and less eﬃcient than its counterparts,
making it unviable for larger-scale applications.
2. Wave soldering- This method is signiﬁcantly quicker than hand soldering and is
thus used for bulk soldering of components. It involves passing PCBAs with glued-
on through-hole components over a “wave” of molten solder that is created by a
pump, and letting the solder solidify by the time the component exits the soldering
equipment. This allows for a conveyor-belt type of process ﬂow, making it incredibly
eﬃcient.
3. Reﬂow soldering- This is a method that ensures proper amounts of ﬂux, and solder
alloy used in the process of soldering. It consists of solder paste which is applied
on the point to be soldered, and the assembly is placed in an oven with a deﬁned
thermal heating proﬁle, which results in accurate soldering of the components to the
surface of the PCBA. Reﬂow soldering is the sole focus of this study.
2.4.2
Reﬂow Soldering:
This method, as previously described, is a soldering method that is centered around the
use of solder paste that is used to hold the components to the PCB, which is then heated
to melt the contents and form the solder joint.
Procedure
Modern SMT assemblies are produced using reﬂow soldering, which consists of the following
steps explained in suﬃcient detail to give an appropriate background to the rest of the
content in this document[30]:
1. Mixing of solder paste, and cleaning of PCB-
The solder paste is a mixture of diﬀerent chemicals, that will be explored in detail in
the next section.
As with any mixture of solids and liquids with diﬀerent solubilities, heavier
constituents tend to settle at the bottom of the container in storage, under the
inﬂuence of gravity. This can be rectiﬁed by proper mixing of the paste before
application.
Additionally, storage of most solder pastes is done at very low
temperatures to avoid solvent loss through evaporation. It is therefore important that
the solder paste be allowed to come to application temperature (room temperature)
before mixing and subsequent application.
This also ensures the best possible
rheological properties of the paste.
As PCB assemblies and components get progressively smaller and are packed more
densely, the relative size of contaminants such as Coarse (> 1 µm) and ﬁne particle
15
==PAGE:25==
(< 1µm), atmospheric moisture, and gaseous contaminants increases[1]. The PCB
surface must also be cleaned and made free of these contaminants, as particulates
can act as added insulation which increases heat in the system, thereby potentially
reducing performance and structural integrity of the components. Additionally, other
ﬁner chemical contaminants can combine with atmospheric condensate and form
conducting solutions, which can cause short circuits and other forms of corrosion
(will be explored in this thesis). The cleaning is done by using a combination of
solvents such as isopropyl alcohol and ethanol, along with clean air jets, all of which
are used in a hermetically sealed environment.
2. Application of solder paste-
This is carried out by methods such as screen printing that uses a screen/stencil
with apertures cut into it, to allow the viscous solder paste to ﬂow through it. High
production requirements usually employ a metal stencil instead of a traditional wire
mesh screen, as it provides less obstruction to ﬂow. These apertures are arranged in
a pattern that matches the required solder locations (connector pads of components)
on the substrate. The volume of solder paste deposited at each location is critical
to the ﬁnal solder quality, and it is thus metered by changing the thickness of the
stencil in accordance with the viscosity of the paste, and the force applied by the
apparatus used to spread the paste across the screen. This determines the height
component and thus the volume of the solder paste deposited. This thickness can be
varied at diﬀerent locations of a stencil, thus getting customized amounts of paste
commensurate with desired volumes[4], [30].
The highly viscous nature of the solder paste at application temperatures means
some work must be done in order to physically push the required amounts of paste
through the constricted apertures of the stencil. This force has to be in accordance
with the quantities of viscosity of paste, aperture size, stencil thickness, etc.. It
cannot be too less, which will lead to a phenomenon called under-printing, causing
defects such as voids (gaps in ﬁnal solder joint due to insuﬃcient material coverage)
that reduce solder joint strength and conductivity [30]. Too much force will lead
to over-printing, an excess of paste on the pads (usually in terms of height), that
can lead to a phenomenon called “slump” in which the solder paste spreads out
after removal of printing force. This causes bridging, which is the formation of
interconnections between leads of a PCBA- these cause short circuits[30].
The force control is achieved by using a Squeegee blade made of rubber, plastic or
metal[31], that is moved over the surface of the stencil. The tip of this blade makes
a line contact with the stencil and is in close contact with the paste material that is
dispensed on the stencil[30]. The squeegee is moved with a pre-determined downward
force, speed and vertical angle to the plane of the stencil. Changing these parameters
allows for varying the force exerted on the paste that moves along the stencil surface,
in front of the squeegee blade. The paste is pushed out of the stencil apertures and
onto the PCB during squeegee movement[4], [30].
3. Reﬂow soldering:
16
==PAGE:26==
In this step, the PCB assembly with components placed at the desired locations is heated
in an oven, either with or without the presence of a protective gas (Nitrogen) atmosphere.
The protective atmosphere is used depending on the solder alloy, and its tendency to
passivate.
The heating is not a straightforward process at a single temperature; the PCBA is subjected
to a combination of temperatures in order to ensure uniform temperature throughout the
assembly, to eﬀect the desired level of activation of ﬂux, and most importantly, to heat the
solder alloy till the point of optimum ﬂowability[30], [32], [33]. Non-uniform temperatures
will create defective joints[34]. This requires the formulation of a heating proﬁle that
ensures all required conditions are attained prior to, and during soldering.
The parts of the reﬂow proﬁle are[30], [32], [33][35]:
• Preheat and soak: The PCB with solder paste is heated (at a somewhat gradual rate
to avoid damage to the board material due to thermal shock) to a temperature below
the melting point of the solder metal, in order to activate the ﬂux part of the solder
paste, priming it for function during soldering. Additionally, the binder is liqueﬁed
and surface tension is reduced, enabling spreading of the paste across the metal path.
The PCB is held at this temperature for 60-120 seconds, in order to stabilize the
temperature across the assembly- this is the soak stage, which can be viewed as a
separate part of the reﬂow process.
• Reﬂow- At this stage, the temperature of the reﬂow oven is increased above the
melting point (liquidus temperature) of the solder alloy. The surface tension is
completely broken, and the solder metal is completely free to wet the surface. In
modern solder pastes, this temperature stage also sees the ﬂux dissolve metal oxides
that may be formed during the preheat stage and before it and burn oﬀ(vaporize).
This stage is kept running long enough for proper wetting to occur. The reﬂow
temperature point must be chosen carefully, as a lower temperature closer to liquidus
of the alloy may not achieve suﬃcient ﬂowability. A signiﬁcantly higher temperature
can cause formation of larger amounts of brittle intermetallics, along with causing
damage to the PCB assembly itself. An optimal temperature range has been observed
to be in the vicinity of 220-270 °C. Dwell time at this temperature must also be
selected with some care, to avoid thermal loading of the components. Dwell time of
20-60 seconds has been observed as optimal.
• Cooling- The oven heating is stopped, and the PCB is allowed to cool gradually in
the oven itself, as taking it out too soon can lead to breakage of thinner solder joints,
due to thermal shock.
The process of reﬂow soldering may have been explored, but any description is incomplete
without a detailed insight into solder paste, a concoction that revolutionized the electronics
industry, by fueling the shift from old-school through-hole construction, to surface mount
construction.
Solder paste, by one deﬁnition, is a “homogenous and kinetically stable mixture of solder
alloy powder, ﬂux and a vehicle, which is capable of forming stable metallurgical bonds
17
==PAGE:27==
at a set of soldering conditions that can be readily adapted to automated production in
making reliable and consistent solder joints” [36], [37].
Building on the above deﬁnition, the soldering process is physically the phenomenon of
conductive joint formation facilitated by the coalescence of the solder powder, which is
comprised of highly graded “balls” of solder alloy into a solder mass which wets the surface
and solidiﬁes, thus forming the joint. The coalescence process is critically dependent on
the surface of the solder balls themselves, and the soldering surfaces being chemically clean,
which is free from any metal oxides and miscellaneous residues[5]. The function of removal
of these contaminants is done by an important additive to solder paste- solder ﬂux. The
entire mixture is encapsulated in a “carrier” material. These will be explained in the next
section.
2.4.3
Solder paste
SOLDER paste is mainly composed of three components:
1. Solder alloy
The solder alloy is the functional heart of the solder paste, responsible for formation
of the conductive metallic joint. The most important properties of solder alloys that
must be considered are:
a) Alloy melting range,
b) Wettability on the substrate in question,
c) Application-speciﬁc mechanical properties of solder joints
d) Potential formation of intermetallic compounds
e) Corrosion properties of solder alloy
The ﬁrst generation of solder alloys were primarily Sn-Pb (Tin-Lead) eutectic (Sn-
37Pb was commonly used) and near-eutectic alloys, with small fractions of other
metals mixed in, to tailor the alloy to speciﬁc applications. This is due to[13]:
a) Eutectic composition (or near-eutectic) which ensures quick liquid-solid
transition upon cooling, and the lowest possible melting point for the alloy
during the application of solder alloy.
This results in increased ease and accuracy of production, and reduction of
overall costs.
b) The presence of Pb in the alloy reduces the propensity of Sn to form
intermetallics, which allows less hampered ﬂow of molten solder- this translates
as good wettability and coverage of the solder alloy.
However, in recent years, increased attention to health and safety of users, and
general environmental pollution concerns have highlighted the high toxicity of Pb,
which has triggered an industry-wide transition towards use of Lead-free (Pb-free)
solder alloys.
18
==PAGE:28==
This has been enforced in Europe by the Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment
Directive (WEEE) and the Restriction of Use of Hazardous Substances Directive
(RoHS), which have essentially banned the use of Lead, Mercury, Cadmium,
hexavalent Chromium and other chemicals in electrical and electronic equipment.[38]
[39][40]
The removal of Pb has resulted in a new prominent alloy type that is more commonly
used, that is high in Sn content, called SAC alloy. SAC alloy consists of Tin-Silver-
Copper (Sn-Ag-Cu).
The removal of toxic Pb from the Alloy system is a huge step forward for health and
safety, but it is one that comes with its own set of technical challenges[40], [41]:
a) The SAC alloys have higher liquidus temperatures than the traditional Sn-Pb
alloys, which changes the required soldering temperatures for these alloys to
higher temperatures. Higher temperatures can cause potential degradation of
the PCB and components, which necessitates changes in the material selection
for the same.
b) The removal of Pb increases the propensity of the Sn to form intermetallic
compounds and oxides, which means increased corrosion and potentially reduced
performance.
c) The removal of Pb also reduces the wettability of the solder alloy.
The shift to SAC alloy has necessitated creation of solder ﬂuxes with diﬀerent
properties[42].
2. Flux
In order to obtain the highest quality (mechanical properties and conductivity)
of solder joints, all surfaces involved in the connection must be free from any
contamination and oxides. This is necessary to ensure maximum wetting of the
solder surface by molten solder alloy, which gives the best joint quality in terms of
conductivity and structural integrity. The function of removal of these contaminants
is carried out by ﬂuxing agents. Fluxing agents are chemically reactive, and form
compounds with the metallic oxides and other non-metallic contaminants present on
the PCB surface, which can be cleaned oﬀby diﬀerent methods.
The main expected functions of solder ﬂux are the removal of any tarnish or protective
ﬁlm from the surfaces to be soldered, aiding optimum thermal transfer (rapid heating
of the base metal), and the reduction of surface tension between the base metal and
liquid solder for better wetting. [4], [43], [44]
There are two main types of ﬂux that have been used:
a) Water-washable ﬂux- These ﬂuxes are usually organic in nature, and are a
mixture of glycol carrier, water/alcohol solvent, a wetting agent, and organic
acids that carry out ﬂuxing. They have an intense ﬂuxing action, but the
by-products of the ﬂuxing reactions need to be washed oﬀwith water in an
additional post-soldering step[45], [46].
19
==PAGE:29==
b) No-clean ﬂux- In order to eliminate the need for an additional step of washing
oﬀﬂux residue and contamination after soldering, no-clean ﬂuxes have been
introduced. Rosin based ﬂuxes usually qualify as No-clean[46].
Flux is made up of 4 main components[44]:
a) Vehicle/Filmformer: This is the base of the ﬂuxing medium, that can be
a solid or non-volatile liquid. It functions to provide a base coating to the
connector surfaces, and in ideal cases, improves wettability of the surfaces, thus
making up for the reduced wettability of the molten SAC alloy. Additionally, it
contributes to dissolution of the products of reaction between the ﬂux activators
and the metal oxides on the surface of the connectors, which are metal salts.
Examples of materials used as ﬁlmformers are Rosin, Resin, and Glycols. Rosin
is a natural product obtained from the resinous extract of Portuguese pine (in
europe). Rosin is also hydrophobic in nature, which increases surface resistivity
and can reduce corrosion occurrences compared to some other additives[13].
Rosin has low corrosivity and is non-polar in nature. It thus cannot remove
heavy chemical tarnish, and requires additives like organic acids, amines or
amine hydrohalides to increase reactivity and ﬂuxing action[43].
b) Activator: This is the main ﬂuxing component of solder ﬂux. Activators are
usually Weak Organic Acids (WOAs), which contain hydroxyl groups that serve
to cause a reduction reaction of surface metal oxides, to form metal salts that
are either washed oﬀ(water-soluble ﬂux) or burned oﬀ(no-clean ﬂux). The
activators can either be used singularly, or as composite combinations of acids.
These substances are hygroscopic in nature.
c) Solvent: This serves to dissolve all the components of the ﬂux at temperatures
below operational temperatures, and must evaporate upon heating to soldering
temperatures, thus leaving behind the previously dissolved components to
complete their intended functions. Usually Alcohols, glycols, glycol esters/ethers
are used as solvents.
d) Other additives:
A host of other substances are added to solder paste
formulations, in order to bring out certain speciﬁc functions. These can consist
of:
i. Fluxing additives/modiﬁers: These are added to solder paste in order
to improve the eﬃcacy of the ﬂux. Include:
Amines- These are added to solder ﬂux in order to stabilize the acidity
of the organic acid activators, due to their basic nature. This results in a
lower acid value for ﬂuxes with organic amines added. One such amine used
is triethanolamine, which improves wettability of organic acid activators,
due in no small part to the added ﬂuxing activity provided by the amine
which, in combination with the organic acids, provides additional reactive
groups (-NH2) that can reduce surface metal oxides[47], [48].
Halides- These serve a very crucial ﬂuxing action in addition to organic
acids by virtue of their high reactivity. Chloride, Bromide, Iodide salts have
a high aﬃnity for Sn, Cu, Ag, etc. which are all the main constituents of
solder alloys, and thus constitute the majority of surface oxides. The high
20
==PAGE:30==
aﬃnity results in the formation of metal halide salts that can be removed
from the PCB surface. However, this can also be a double-edged sword,
as the high ionic activity of free halides can result in increased corrosion,
which will be explored in the course of this document. These substances
are extremely hygroscopic in nature too, which adds to the corrosion issue.
ii. Rheological additives: These are essentially gelling materials which are
used to increase the cohesion and adhesion of the solder paste. Cohesion in
this case relates to the attraction between components of the solder paste
such as the solder powder particles. Adhesion is the physical sticking of
the paste to tooling such as the squeegee blade and stencil. Materials that
have thixotropic properties are usually selected as rheological additives.
(Thixotropic materials are those materials which have higher viscosity at rest
and exhibit a decreased viscosity when work energy is applied to the paste
by physical shearing.[4], [30][29]) These materials will show a temporary
reduction in viscosity when the solder paste is mixed and applied using the
squeegee blade. This ensures better print quality[40].
3. Carrier/Vehicle system:
This is often the same as the ﬁlmformer that is used in solder ﬂux. The purpose of the
carrier is to hold the ﬂux and solder alloy, which are released upon heating. Additionally,
a solder paste vehicle is also responsible for the rheological functions of the solder paste,
which are comprised of providing adhesion/tack to the components that are placed on
the PCB before soldering, and deﬁning the ﬂow characteristics of the paste itself, which
inﬂuences the applicability of the paste using prevalent methods such as screen-printing.
[4], [30], [37]
To sum up the above section, modern no-clean solder paste used in industry is comprised
of the following components:
1. Rosin system: Responsible for determination of thermal stability, ﬂuxing agent
activation, tack, softening point of the solder paste upon application.
2. Fluxing activator: System responsible for carrying out the ﬂuxing action over the
range of operational temperatures.
3. Binder: The base of the solder paste, responsible for the viscosity of the paste (aﬀects
paste application and printing, post-printing slump)
4. Solvent: Component responsible for solubility of ingredients, which aﬀects rheology,
thermal and chemical interactions.
5. Fluxing and rheology modiﬁers: used to tailor the properties to the application.

2.5
PCBA Failure mechanisms due to solder paste:
The change in solder alloy composition form Sn-Pb eutectic to SAC (Sn-Ag-Cu) alloys has
brought with it altered conditions and features of operational failures. In the traditional
Sn-Pb alloy, Pb had a higher susceptibility to ECM, which could be controlled rather easily
with the addition of slightly higher Sn content in the alloy. SAC alloys by themselves have
a increased resistance to ECM than Sn-Pb alloy [23].
The formation of dendrites due to ECM is dependent on the solubility of the
Me(OH)x complex in the water layer. If solubility is low, ionic movement will also be
slower due to lesser availability of Mex+ ions for reduction and subsequent deposition as
dendrites. Out of Sn, Ag and Cu, pure Ag theoretically has a higher susceptibility for
ECM due to its higher solubility product Ksp in Ag(OH) form, as compared to hydroxides
of Sn and Cu[59]. However, a surprising observation upon studying SAC-alloy dendrites is
that the dendrites are rich in Sn[22].
This is due to higher aﬃnity of Ag and Cu for Sn, rather than towards metal hydroxide
formation. Ag and Cu form intermetallic compounds with Sn, which are deposited as a
milky precipitate on the PCBA surface[59]. The higher Sn content in the alloy means
that there is suﬃcient Sn left to form dendrites upon attaining suitable conditions. The
rate of dissolution (and diﬀusion) of Sn increases with higher potential bias and increased
conduction of the electrolyte solution.
The use of SAC-alloys is accompanied by new types of ﬂux such as no-clean ﬂux, which
employs the use of WOA (Weak organic Acid) activators in higher amounts, in order to
obtain a more aggressive ﬂuxing action that is very eﬃcient in removing surface oxides.
These acid residues are hygroscopic in nature, and the higher the hygroscopicity, the
lower is the critical RH (cRH) required to initiate surface water ﬁlm formation through
deliquescence[8]. Flux activator WOAs like adipic acid, glutaric acid, malonic acid, succinic
acid have a tendency to dissociate in water, and the strength of the acid solution formed is
given by their dissociation constants. Strength of acid solution is inversely proportional
to the logarithmic value of -pKa (the lower or more negative the pKa, higher is the
conductivity of the solution due to increased ionic diﬀusion)[64].
Fluxing modiﬁers like halides are also added to further the eﬃcacy of oxide removal in
combination with the WOA activators. This is eﬀective in achieving better solderability
and joint integrity, but has the issue of free halide ions increasing water layer conductivity
along with dissociated H+ ions from the WOAs. This increased conductivity is due to
reduction of Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR), that causes leak currents which hamper
device functionality, and cause other forms of corrosion and failures. The increased use
of these WOAs in particular has led to a higher susceptibility to ECM failures. Studies
have shown that the “no-clean” ﬂux leaves behind residue even at the higher soldering
temperatures required to melt SAC alloys ([68]), which can be an issue. The ester oils used
as the ﬂux base can trap dust and moisture from the surroundings, increasing the severity
of corrosion and failure. Rosin based ﬂuxes are expected to have better corrosion reliability
due to the hydrophobic nature of rosin, that is more adept at preventing a continuous
water layer formation[5]. Halide additives have a high acidity, which is signiﬁed by a high
magnitude of -pKa, as has been mentioned in the work of Piotrowska et. al [68], which
22
==PAGE:32==
showed a higher magnitude of -pKa value for a mixture of halide and 1 WOA activator,
than a binary mixture of WOAs. The value of -pKa magnitude for halide and 1 WOA
mixture was closer to the -pKa value of a 3-WOA mixture.[68]
It is the combination of these residue compounds and the ambient temperature and
humidity that lead to corrosion failures on PCBAs. The eﬀect of humidity and temperature
is also critical, as described earlier:
Increased solubility of residue compounds results in more deliquescent behavior at lower
RH. This behavior is only exacerbated in the presence of mixtures of highly hygroscopic,
deliquescent compounds such as halides and WOAs, which is the case for most modern
solder pastes with the above-mentioned chemical contents[8]. Halides have higher ionic
activity, which makes them the most aggressive of all residue substances, with respect to
causing corrosion[40]. Additionally, the number of WOAs in the ﬂux activator also has an
eﬀect on residue hygroscopicity and ECM. Research has shown that a binary mixture of
WOAs is more hygroscopic than a single WOA activator [67].
The dew point is an important factor in determining the amount of condensation on the
PCBA surface. Dew point is lower than the ambient temperature, and the diﬀerence
between the two is dependent on the relative humidity of the surroundings. A higher
humidity will result in a smaller diﬀerence, as the closer the air is to saturation pressure,
the tougher it is for the air to hold on to water vapor (increased partial pressure of
water vapor at higher RH, increased saturation temperature of water vapor(dew point))
The condensation of water vapor on the surface becomes very sensitive to decrease in
temperature. A thick water ﬁlm can form with just a small drop in ambient temperature
at higher RH, leading to increased occurrence of corrosion and failure[8]. Another eﬀect of
ambient conditions is the lowering of deliquescence RH for hygroscopic contaminants like
WOAs with an increase in temperature [67]. Hence, a combination of high humidity and
cyclic changes to temperatures, or vice-versa, can lead to a thick water layer formation
and high possibility for ECM and corrosion.
Diﬀerent testing methods have been formulated to assess the eﬀects of humidity, water
ﬁlm thickness, residue content and other contamination on the reliability of PCBA devices.
These methods can be either destructive or non-destructive in nature, and give diﬀerent
perspectives of the same property- the electrolyte conductivity of surface water ﬁlm.
2.6
Climatic Reliability Testing Methods
Given the multitude of electrochemical reactions that contribute to corrosion and failure
of electronic device PCBAs, it is imperative that appropriate methods be used to
qualitatively and quantitatively assess the impact of the diﬀerent failure mechanisms
on device functionality. The goal is to use the results from testing to arrive at a suitable
idea of reliability of the components (design, material, climatic, etc.) that go into PCBA
production, assembly and operation.
It has already been established that the major eﬀects of solder ﬂux residue in humid
conditions are on the surface conductivity of PCBAs. These failures are leakage current
23
==PAGE:33==
due to reduction of SIR, and electrochemical migration. Selection of testing methods for
this study has been done with the aim of gaining an insight into both bulk and surface
properties of the electrolyte, and the electrolyte-PCBA interface, that eﬀect the processes
of diﬀusion and charge transfer which are the basis for the failure mechanisms in question.
2.6.1
Conductivity measurement
A relatively simple test performed to measure the conductivity (speciﬁc conductance)
of water with dissolved solids that have been ionized due to the polar nature of water.
Instrumentation consists of a probe and a system to calculate and display conductivity
values. The probe consists of two electrodes separated by a small gap in the tip, which
is immersed in water. A voltage is applied across the electrodes, which drops due to the
resistivity of water. This diﬀerence between applied and measured voltages is used to
calculate the conductivity of the water.
Speciﬁc conductance is the reciprocal of resistivity and is measured in the unit called
Siemens (S). A commonly used unit for conductivity is µ S/cm.
2.6.2
Leakage current measurement (ECM)
This testing method, as the name suggests, is used to determine the development of a
leakage current on the surface of the test specimen as a result of formation of water layer
(deliquescence of hygroscopic residue) and ionic dissociation of said residue, which reduces
Surface Insulation Resistance.
The test involves applying a DC voltage bias across the electrodes, and recording the
in-phase current response continuously. A clean surface will show a constant, low leakage
current response from the conductive track only. The presence of ionic residue on the
surface will be marked by spikes in the current response due to current leaking through
the bridging electrolyte layer between two biased conductors[49][50]. In the case of locally
prevailing conditions (pH, contaminant amount, temperature, applied voltage etc) being
suitable, dissolution of the conductive metal and subsequent ECM (Electro Chemical
Migration) will lead to metallic connections between conductors, resulting a short circuit,
represented by a very sharp spike of high leakage current, that often goes in to the mA
region (compared to µ A level currents that are usually observed in PCB components).
This rise in current is a result of local electrolysis that depletes any electroactive species
near the electrode in a conductive solution. The magnitude of current is proportional to
the amount of present ionic species in the bulk solution[51].
2.6.3
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) measurement
EIS is a leading method for testing corrosion reliability of specimens. As has been previously
established, the in-ﬁeld failure of PCB devices is largely a consequence of reduction of the
Surface Insulation Resistance (SIR) due to formation of a highly conductive electrolyte
24
==PAGE:34==
layer (adsorbed/condensed water and ionic residue). This drop in SIR increases surface
conductivity, causing leakage currents and eventual ECM failure. Measurement of SIR
using DC is a commonly applied technique for the qualiﬁcation of ﬂux residues and the
eﬀects of surface contamination on SIR.
Straightforwardly ohmic as it may be, it is not possible for the DC measurement method
to characterize and make measurements of the conduction processes at the electrolyte-
metal track and other conduction processes that occur in the thin water ﬁlm between the
conductive tracks, under the presence of various contaminants such as ﬂux residue. The
use of AC at small amplitudes of mV level for measurement by EIS has been successful in
triggering and accessing diﬀerent reactions[52].
Principle of EIS
EIS measurements in laboratory settings are carried out using a potentiostat connected
to a computer. A sinusoidal “stimulus” voltage in the low mV range is applied on top of
a DC potential[53]. This is called a perturbation, and the reason for small voltage is to
avoid causing a disruption to the system under testing, thus keeping the system response
as linear as possible. The applied voltage perturbation elicits a current response that is
generally of a diﬀerent amplitude and phase from potential. The ratio of the amplitude
and phase of the perturbation and response at a particular frequency of the sine wave
is calculated as the impedance at that frequency. The perturbation is applied across a
range of frequencies of sine wave, and the impedance results are put together to create an
Impedance spectrum.
The basic principle is the variation in time constants (reciprocal of frequency) of the
diﬀerent electrochemical processes occurring in the system. At low frequencies, the process
time constants can keep up with the perturbation. As perturbation frequency is increased,
the process response time constants start to lag in phase and reduce in amplitude, which
is how diﬀerent processes can be identiﬁed using a plot of impedance vs frequency, also
called a bode plot[52]–[54].
This method is quite capable, and can measure very diﬀerent processes such as mass
transfer and electron transfer, just by virtue of testing using a range of applied voltage
frequencies[53].
Given the number of conduction processes that occur on a PCB surface, it is diﬃcult to
create a one-size-ﬁts-all equivalent circuit to model the electrochemical cell. In the case
of parallel conductors with thin water layer such as the samples used for this study, an
equivalent Randles circuit can be used, which is a combination of resistive, capacitive and
inductive components that are representative of the diﬀerent electrochemical reactions.

Faradaic reactions are those reactions that are deﬁned by movement of charge

They follow the description of faradays law, hence the name.
Non-faradaic reactions are those reactions that do not involve charge transfer, but are
usually dependent on mass transfer (adsorption, absorption) with changes in solution
composition and/or electrode potential and area (applied potential, deliquescence of
ﬂux residue, etc.). External Currents can still ﬂow in the absence of charge transfer,
due to the previously mentioned changes in conditions. The external current is a
charging current that ﬂows as a result of charge accumulation on one of the electrodes

2.7.1
Light-Optical Microscopy[63]
This is a method of studying material surfaces that uses the refraction of light through
lenses in order to create magniﬁed images of samples to be studied. Visible light can
be described as a plane electromagnetic wave (self-propagating transverse oscillating
electric and magnetic ﬁelds), with a deﬁned wavelength and velocity in a given media.
This wavelength and velocity vary in diﬀerent media. this gives rise to a method of
characterization of diﬀerent media in relation to vacuum, called the refractive index of
the medium. Refractive Index n=c/v, where c- speed of light in vacuum (3*108 m/s); v-
velocity of light in the medium.
The diﬀerences in velocity of light between diﬀerent media results in the changing in
direction of an incident beam of light, at the interface of the diﬀerent media. This change
in direction (bending) of the light ray is termed as refraction Two types of lenses are used
in the operation of light optical microscopes- convex lenses and concave lenses. Convex
lenses are converging, which means they refract a incident beam of light perpendicular
to the lens plane, to a single point on the lens axis (on the opposite side from the light
source), called the focus. Concave (diverging) lenses have their focus on the same side of
the lens, as the source. the distance from the focus to the centre of the lens is called the
focal length of a lens.
The placement of an object at diﬀerent distances from the focus point results in images
that vary in size from the object itself. If this distance is smaller than the focal length, a
magniﬁed (larger in size) image is obtained.
Another condition essential for image formation in a microscope is diﬀraction, which is the
splitting of a wavefront into smaller wavelets upon passing through a diﬀraction grating.
Light-optical microscopes use a combination of diﬀraction and refraction to produce images.
A parallel incident beam from the source is diﬀracted by the sample into RGB components,
which are then refracted and focussed onto the image plane by the microscope lenses, to
form a magniﬁed image of the part of the sample object under study.
There are diﬀerent imaging modes in an LOM, which are as follows:
Bright Field Illumination
This is the most encountered mode of usage in an LOM, which has the incident light
on the specimen perpendicular to the specimen plane. Light reﬂected by the specimen
28
==PAGE:38==
surface features are reﬂected back through the objective lens, which results in the parts of
the surface that are perpendicular to the incident beam being visualised as bright, and
non-perpendicular surface features visualized as dark.
Dark Field Illumination
In this mode, the light reﬂected by the specimen understudy is not incident perpendicularly
through the objective, but obliquely. This results in surface features parallel to the specimen
place visualized at dark, and surface features that are non-parallel being visualized as
bright.
Oblique Illumination
In this mode, the illumination source is de-centered at an oblique angle, thus producing
shadows on surface features, and providing surface contrast. Knowledge of the direction
of incident illumination is helpful in diﬀerentiating between raised and dimpled surface
features.
Diﬀerential Interference Contrast
In this mode, a beam-splitting prism, a polarizer and analyzer are placed in the light ray
path which can be used to produce shadows based on the diﬀerences in height between
surface features.
Polarized light
In case etchants are used to create a more deﬁned diﬀerence between orientations of
diﬀerent surface features, a polarizing ﬁlter is inserted into the light beam path in order to
create a color contrast that is obtained by the use of the etchants[63].
The microscope used for this work is a digital microscope, which uses a CMOS sensor
and image processing hardware as the image plane as opposed to a traditional eyepiece.
The imaging system is connected to a screen which enables easier and higher-quality
visualization of the obtained images.
2.7.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy[63]
This is the most widely used variant of the electron microscope, which can be used to
obtain very high resolution images of micro-meter and nano-meter size range of features in
samples.
SEM diﬀers from LOM in the kind of radiation used to obtain images. LOM uses visible
light radiation and glass lenses to obtain images, whereas SEM uses a focused electron
beam and electromagnetic lenses to obtain images.
The highly focused electron beam has a diameter of 1-20 nm, and surface images are
obtained by scanning over the entire surface in the ﬁeld of view. Images are formed by the
29
==PAGE:39==
excitation of atoms in the sample by the bombardment of a high-energy incident electron
beam. These excited atoms eject electrons which are incident on a detector connected to
an image processing system that includes a display screen.
The imaging is also dependent on the type of electrons that are emitted by scattering from
the sample atoms, as a result of collision and energy transfer with electrons in the incident
electron beam:
• Elastic scattering produces Back-Scattered Electrons (BSEs) which are high-energy
electrons deﬂected right back in the direction of the source.
• Inelastic scattering produces Secondary Electrons (SEs), which are low-energy
electrons deﬂected obliquely and randomly form the source direction.
BSEs and SEs have diﬀerent energies in the atom, with BSE>SE. They are emitted from
diﬀerent depths of the specimen- the region in the specimen that experiences interactions
from the incident electron beam is called interaction volume.
Figure 2.4: Interaction volume of an atom during SEM. [63]
The low energy of SEs means that they can only escape from small depths within the
specimen, close to the surface. SEs are thus used for topographical contrast images.
High-energy BSEs are emitted from larger depths in the interaction volume, and can be
used to obtain compositional and orientation contrast images.
The resolution of the image depends on the number of atoms emitted after scattering- Low
atomic number atoms emit more SEs than BSEs, and the number of BSEs emitted increases
with atomic number.This is the principle behind compositional contrast, as regions made
of elements with higher Z will appear brighter than low-Z regions[63].
This study involved use of SE imaging, as imaging of topographical features was desired.

on the stencil next to the pattern cutout, after alignment.

Reﬂow Pad Conductivity test:

were labelled to represent that for ease of operation, as follows:

residue in the water.

of this formed cavity was calculated to be ca. 218 µ L.

the testing equipment.

RESULTS AND INTERPRETATION
The binder, base and surfactant are the same for all samples, along with the conditions
of soldering and testing. This eliminates the eﬀect of these chemicals for the purposes of
comparison, in this study.
The eﬀects of ﬂux activator, amine additive, and no clean additives is considered the main
frame of comparison.
4.1
Reﬂow pad Conductivity
Figure 4.1: Graph showing the variation in surface conductivity with time
The results clearly show an easily discernable upward trend of conductivity on the PCB
surface, which is conﬁrmed by pictures (below) that show an increase in amount of residue
that is released as a result of extended exposure to high humidity conditions. In this case,
the eﬀect of the humidity exposure is highest on CABL5, WITH CABL2 AND CABL4
being comparable, and CABL3 showing the smallest increase in conductivity.
At 0 days (no exposure), CABL4 has the highest conductivity, followed by CABL2, CABL5
and CABL3, with CABL2 and CABL5 being quite similarly conductive, which seems to ﬁt
the chemical proﬁle of CABL5 being similar to CABL2, save for the addition of a halide
ﬂuxing modiﬁer.
50
==PAGE:60==
Under the testing conditions, the water layer formed will be a rather thin one as compared
to bulk. This brings out the dominant eﬀect of additives like Halide and halogen, which
also have a more ionic nature than carboxylic acids in a thin water layer.
This order of conductivity after 15 days exposure is primarily due to the presence of halogen
and halide in CABL5 and CABL4 respectively, which would result in a more aggressive
ﬂuxing action overall, but cause higher water layer conductivity due to ionic dissociation.
1. Additionally, the biggest change in conductivity for all samples is observed within
the ﬁrst 5 days of exposure, with CABL3 showing a very slow rise in conductivity
after. CABL3 HAS NO ADDITIVES, which could explain the eventual near stability
of the conductivity trend after the ﬁrst 5 days of exposure.
2. Conductivity of CABL2 increases by almost twofold within days 5-10, and then
becomes relatively less aggressive. This is due to the release and dissociation of the
2 WOA activators, which eventually slows down.
3. CABL4 shows a relatively small change from day 5-10, which could be due to the
neutralizing presence of the amine present, that reduces the acidity and eventual
ionic conductivity of the solution formed. The increase from day 10-15 is of almost
50% , which is due to the eﬀect of highly ionic (see: ionic activity/diﬀusion) halide
in the solution, under the eﬀect of moisture. The eﬀect of amine additive is not
suﬃcient to override the increase of conductivity due to halide.
4. The presence of halogen and 2 carboxylic acids in CABL5 points to an increased
hygroscopicity of residue[67], resulting in an increased conductivity with time due to
more ionic activity in the larger water layer.
Figure 4.2: Images showing the release of residue with continued exposure

The ﬁgure 4.2 shows the distribution of residue with continued exposure to humidity and
formation of a thin water layer. This spreading is due to the hydrolysis (absorption of

DISCUSSION
In light of the above tests and observations, it is safe to say that the combination of halogen
additive and high carboxylic acid content in CABL5, though advantageous in terms of
ﬂuxing action and heavy duty usage (halogen additives are also ﬂame retardant), made for
poor solderability and the poorest humidity reliability of all tested solder pastes.
The addition of an amine additive to CABL4 gave it the best solderability of the lot due
to a greatly improved ﬂuxing action when combined with the single carboxylic acid ﬂux
activator [47,48], but the addition of halide, which is responsible for an aggressive ﬂuxing
action in practical applications[40], revealed a higher surface water ﬁlm conductivity during
all tests performed. This is also due to the high spreading of residue for CABL4, which
increases leakage current due to bridging connections between conductive lines. However,
interestingly enough, the cyclic DC leakage current test showed very little corrosion or
damage to the sample, if any. This can be attributed to the amine additive, which acts as
a corrosion inhibitor.
CABL2 showed lower residue solution conductivity and higher surface impedance than
other pastes, CABL4 and CABL5 in particular, which could be attributed to the lack of
any additives other than 2 Carboxylic acids, and the low spreading of residue as compared
to CABL3 and CABL4. However, the ﬁnal leakage current test showed a sustained amount
of ECM that was corroborated through images of a signiﬁcant number of dendrites formed
(ﬁgure 4.21), which show the true practical eﬀect of the higher carboxylic acid content.
This would provide aggressive ﬂuxing action and help improve solderability, but as a
double-edged sword, resulted in ECM leakage current between solder lines[49], though
lower in leakage current value that was 2 orders of magnitude smaller than that of CABL5.
The acceptable limits of leakage current for any application, if they exist, might determine
the utility of this paste.
CABL3 remained the most stable of all pastes through all testing, maintaining the middle
trends for results. CABL3 samples showed appreciably low residue conductivity, middle-
high range of surface impedance for both humidity and bulk water EIS tests (due to
higher spreading of residue as compared to CABL2 and CABL5), and also remained stable
throughout the DC testing.

CONCLUSION
With the aid of the discussion, it is safe to conclude that:
1. The release of ionic substances onto the PCBA surface with continued exposure of the
ﬁlmformer to humidity occurs in any case. This has been the prevailing observation
throughout testing.
2. The inclusion of halides and amine residues (which increase wettability and residue
spreading) on the surface contributes to increasing surface conductivity in presence
of low bias (AC), which is explained by the higher surface conductivity (lower
impedance) values of CABL5 and CABL4 from experiment 1, 2 and 3 (refer Sections
4.1, 4.2, 4.3 respectively). The spreading of ionic residue to form interconnections
between closely-spaced conductors also has an eﬀect on surface conductivity, along
with the ionic activity of the residue substances themselves.
3. The eﬀect of increase in amount of carboxylic acid content (unary vs. binary mixture
vs long-chain unary) is seen most dominantly on ECM under DC bias (higher driving
force for corrosion) and cyclic temperature conditions, with the higher leakage current
values and ECM observed on CABL2 and CABL5, both of which have binary mixtures
of carboxylic acid activator.
4. The long chain carboxylic acid in CABL4 appears to be neutralized by the presence of
an Amine additive. However, any halide additive and amine additives would increase
surface conductivity upon dissociation, which partly explains the paradox of high
surface conductivity and no observed ECM (The other part of this explanation is the
corrosion inhibition eﬀect of amine additive, which prevents ECM.). This eﬀect of
carboxylic acid content on ECM is further conﬁrmed by the lowest leakage current
value for CABL3, which contains a single acid additive, and no other additives.
5. The best combination of additives on the basis of this project can be suggested as:
No halide or halogen additive. 2 carboxylic acid activator (better ﬂuxing action to
make up for the lack of halide), Amine additive (improved solderability and corrosion
inhibition).

7
SUGGESTIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
The tests performed as part of this project have yielded some interesting results which
conﬁrm the eﬀects of Carboxylic acids, Halides and halogen additives on the surface
conductivity and leakage current observed under diﬀerent water layer conditions.
The eﬀect of amine additive as a corrosion (ECM) inhibitor has been determined by the
leakage current test. However, the true eﬀect of the amine additive on surface conductivity
has not been determined by the testing- low impedance values for CABL4 have been
attributed mainly to the inﬂuence of the halide additive. Future work on testing the eﬀect
of amine additives in solder paste might be prudent, as current knowledge is rather scarce.
The immersion test, which is a new testing method, has shown promise, and can potentially
give accurate in-situ results with optimization of the water layer thickness. An avenue of
future work might be to determine the appropriate water layer thickness to simulate bulk
water ionic conductivity.
The use of halides and halogen additives must be reduced to make solder pastes
environmentally friendly, and similar testing of halide-free pastes can be suggested for
future work.
Finally, as this testing has explored the eﬀects of diﬀerent additives for similar PCBA
parameters such as spacing of components, a good future project might be to study the
variations in these eﬀects with diﬀerences in PCBA design and layouts.
