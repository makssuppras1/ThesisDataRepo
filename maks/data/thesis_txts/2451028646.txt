Abstract
The European Commission and Denmark are aiming to reduce the climate impact and in-
crease the cost eﬃciency within the construction sector by 80-95% and 90%, respectively. In
their aim to do so, they are encouraging innovation and digitalization within the industry.
This research has investigated how a system for tracking and visualization of materials in
construction logistics management can contribute to maintain a competitively, economically
and environmentally sustainable construction industry.
A literature study and a case study in collaboration with a Danish contractor have been con-
ducted to characterize the most prominent challenges and opportunities within construction
logistics management. Each challenge and opportunity have been analysed with respect to
performance indicators identiﬁed as central to construction. Finally, is the research consid-
ering how the system for tracking and visualization of materials can target the challenges,
opportunities and performance indicators.
The research has characterized an industry failing to perform on several areas central to
logistics. More so, essential to meet the goals developed by the European Commission and
Denmark. The research highlights the most critical challenges within construction logistics
management; poor or lack of time for planning, insuﬃcient communication and stakeholder
coordination, resistance to change and lack of knowledge and experience.
From the literature study and the case study, the research has concluded that the system
can target and improve some of the identiﬁed challenges. The most important target areas
are; (1) Planning, as a result of better sequencing of deliveries, better estimates of stresses
on loading and storage areas and better overview of what-if scenarios; (2) Stakeholder in-
teraction, as a result of better understanding of change orders and their consequences, for
the contractor and for the client; (3) Better coordination and scheduling of deliveries be-
tween actors. The system is further showing potential to work together with other current
and future functions within logistics. E.g. construction consolidation centres, construction
logistics plans or pre-fabricated elements.
A set of requirements have also been established for a successful implementation of the
TVS; (1) It requires a reliable and eﬃcient platform for communication where all involved
stakeholders have easy access; (2) It requires incentives for any involved stakeholder in order
to engage them in the implementation. (3) A long-term implementation strategy with close
follow-up;(4) The TVS responsible for each project must have a great understanding of CLM
and the technical aspect of the TVS.

1.1
Background and motivation
This research has been developed together with a Danish contractor, Company A. Company
A is currently developing a system for tracking and visualization of materials in construction
logistics(TVS). The system is still at an initial phase of development, but Company A
has initiated a test for the system. The test will target precast staircase elements in the
construction of a 80-meter tall building in Copenhagen, Denmark. If the test is successful,
Company A is looking to expand and implement the system on more elements, primarily
diﬀerent types of prefabricated elements and modules.
The TVS is working as follows: All elements are given a unique ID. Initially, it will only be
bigger pre-fabricated elements, but depending on the success and the attainable eﬃciency, it
may be applied to smaller, more general construction materials. Each element represented by

1.2.1
Construction logistics
Recent years, there has been a great increase in literature focusing on construction logistics,
but also several important sub-disciplines within the ﬁelds of construction logistics. Logistics
are one of the most important elements in construction projects.
It is inﬂuencing site
performance factors such as cost, speed of construction and plan reliability[14]. CLM can
be described as the design, planning, scheduling, storage and delivery of all materials and
equipment on- and oﬀsite at the right place and right time[15].
One of the most extensive literary works on CLM is the book "Managing Construction
Logistics", by Sullivan et al.[14]. The book addresses a wide range of diﬀerent aspects related
to CLM. Most importantly it discusses the current situation in relation to the challenges in
the industry. The topics are described in great detail, and the authors are showing a high
level of understanding and knowledge about the construction industry. It further discusses
the future of the ﬁeld, covering several exciting areas, e.g.
Construction Consolidation
Centres (CCC), which will be discussed later. Sullivan et al. are discussing technologies for
tracking, but does not address the use of BIM based technology, which will be central to
this research.
There are several other studies focusing directly on classical approaches to CLM[16, 1, 17].
Each of the articles are showing diﬀerent approaches to CLM. What is interesting with the
studies conducted by Chen et al.[1] and Harker et al.[17], is that they are connecting the
logistics practices with challenges like e.g. waste management and uncertainty. Like Sullivan
et al., none of these studies are connecting CLM with BIM based technology. Further are
Song et al.[18] discussing site layout and material logistics, while there are several studies
addressing relevant logistics concepts like Just in Time (JIT) and CCCs[13, 14, 17, 2].
1.2.2
BIM in logistics
The Centre for Digital Built Britain[19] captures the essence of BIM and its purpose as:
"At its core BIM uses 3D models and a common data environment to access and share
information eﬃciently across the supply chain and so boost the eﬃciency of activities around
asset delivery and operation.
By helping the entire supply chain to work from a single
source of information, BIM reduces the risk of error and maximizes the team ability to
innovate".
It is these beneﬁts that Company A wishes to utilize in their construction
logistics management. Adaptation and implementation of BIM in the logistics planning is
not an entirely new concept.
In terms of literature that connects BIM to CLM, one of the most discussed topics is 4D-
modelling[20, 21, 22, 23, 24]. The principles of TVS are closely related to the 4D-modelling
concept, that has made its entry in the construction industry the last years. 4D-modelling

1.2.3
Success criterion and performance indicators
Within construction literature, there are many ways of measuring success. Depending on
the subject, the literature are focusing on a variety of success criterion and performance
indicators. It is thus important to identify what is regularly considered when measuring
success, and hence deﬁne what indicators are most relevant in the context of CLM.
Firstly, throughout the literature, time, cost and quality, what is commonly know as the
"Iron triangle", is more or less consequently used as the main measurement of success. It is
thus impossible to not consider the three factors when discussing CLM. Studies like Larsen
et al.[27] and Abu et al.[28] are writing about the triangle, while other studies like Aljohani
et al.[29], Flyvbjerg et al. [9], Sweis et al.[30], Adam et al.[31] and Park et al.[32], are
focusing on each of them individually. The three factors are further mentioned repeatedly
in general logistics and construction literature [1, 14, 18].
Further, one of the most mentioned indicators is safety. Firstly, there are strict govern-
mental guidelines that have to be followed [33]. Safety is further addressed as central in
CLM by Song et al. [18], Zhang et al.[23] Whitlock et al.[13] and Bortolini et al. [20].
Naturally, as the core of CLM is the design, transport and storage of materials, waste and
environmental impact becomes central as performance indicators. Similar to safety, there
are also governmental guidelines on how to handle construction waste[34]. Furthermore,
there is a general consensus in the literature that waste and environment are closely related

2. Theoretical Background
2.1.1
Cost
All project participants and stakeholders have a fundamental interest in the costs of a
project. Public actors, private companies or each individual worker, all have an economic
stake in the project. This is why costs are always a central performance indicator in any
construction project.
All stakeholders strive to stay within budgets and limit any cost
overruns. Across literature the deﬁnition of costs and cost overruns are in general quite
uniform. Flyvbjerg et al.[9] deﬁnes cost in a simple and applicable way for general research
purposes. The costs consists of two elements. The actual costs, accounted for the entire
project course at the project completion. And, the estimated costs, that are the budgeted
costs at the time of decision to build. If the costs exceeds the budget, the diﬀerence divided
by the estimated cost this will be the total cost overrun in percentage.
According to Flyvbjerg [10] the construction industry have been suﬀering from cost overruns
for the last 70 years(At the time of the study in 2003). The study covering 258 infrastructure
projects on a global scale shows that 9 out of 10 projects have cost overruns. The average
cost escalation is at 28% of the estimated project costs. Furthermore, numbers from the
years 2012-2015 shows that 69% of projects are exceeding their budget by more then 10%[35].
Meaning, that there are still no signiﬁcant improvement in terms of cost overruns. Managing
the costs of a project is a complex discipline and is involving every system, process and action
within the project. Limiting the costs can hence not be accounted for in one single measure,
but must be made up by improved performance in all project areas. The costs are also
having an impact outside the scope of the project. It will usually aﬀect stakeholders in both
an organizational and industrial level as well, and is thus often recognized as one of the
most important factors in running a sustainable business.
It is estimated that the costs of materials can account for up to 70% of the costs of the con-
struction, meaning that there are possibly big gains of improving the logistics and material
supply chain in a cost perspective[18]. In the already existing BIM environment, cost esti-
mates are commonly generated through visual models and built-in cost estimates features.
The dynamic process of updating the model before the estimates are automatically updated
according to the changes are one of the great features of BIM application today[36]. By
visualizing the logistics the same technology is applicable to create better estimates also in
the logistics.
Studies that are approaching logistics with the aim to visualize the process, are discovering
improvements in costs as a result of several factors. These factors are directly or indirectly
aﬀecting the costs of a project, and will be presented and discussed in the following sections.

2. Theoretical Background
2.1.2
Time
Time constraints, schedules and deadlines are very important in a CLM perspective. The
timing of design, production and delivery in relation to how the construction site is ar-
ranged and managed can be essential for a project’s success. Delays are aﬀecting several
stakeholders, and the building owner has usually strict requirements for deadlines. Similar
to costs, the deﬁnition of time and delays are quite uniform across diﬀerent literature. For
the purpose of this research Sweis et al.[30] deﬁnes it in an applicable manner. The sched-
uled period of a project will be speciﬁed in the project contract or will be an agreed upon
date between the parties of the project. Delivery times also applies to other milestones or
deliverables that is agreed upon and is not limited to the overall project hand-over. If the
duration of the project exceeds these scheduled dates, it will be regarded as delays.
Research shows that only 25% of projects are within 10% of the original deadline. Showing
a global inability to meet scheduling requirements[35]. Larsen et al.[27] are pointing out
several factors to why projects are failing to meet their scheduled deadlines. According to
them the ﬁve biggest causes to delays are:
"...unsettled or lack of project funding, delay or long process times caused by other authori-
ties, unsettled or lack of project planning, errors or omissions in construction work and lack
of identiﬁcation of needs."
Siemiatycki[37] are discussing that people are in general over-optimistic in the estimates
of both time and costs, and are over-estimating their ability to control uncertain events
in the future. The eﬀect is enhanced by the tendency among project participants to give
also over-optimistic promises in an interdisciplinary manner[14]. Furthermore, according
to Adam et al.[31] factors like poor planning, late design information, long approval times
and inadequate site management as a result of inexperienced contractors are leading to
signiﬁcant delays. These are referred to as none-excusable delays, which is the contractor’s
responsibility and not the client’s.

2.2.1
Planning logistics
Generating detailed and high quality logistics plans in the preconstruction phase is crucial
for the CLM to run eﬀectively and eﬃciently[14]. The time span of a construction project,
its limited on-site storage space and large quantity of materials required, leads to a ﬁne
balance between ordering and storage costs. It is cheaper to buy in quantum, but does at
the same time increase the storage costs. This is why building materials and elements are
produced several times to balance the costs[18]. Park et al.[32] are arguing that due to the
inability to realize the dynamic nature of scheduling decisions, productivity and quality are
sacriﬁced for the purpose of meeting deadlines. Accelerated work as a reaction to keep up
with the initial plans are increasing the risk of error, and site managers tends to schedule
the delayed activities with over-optimism. Sullivan et al.[14] are establishing an inability to
realize the capacity of deliveries on-site: "...if you have 20 hours’ unloading time and each
delivery takes an hour don’t plan for 25 deliveries. This sounds obvious when it’s stated,
but time and time again logistics operations come unstuck because of precisely this kind of
mistake."
The construction industry is generally associated with high risks, and uncertainty due to its
complexity. Thus, making the planning of logistics equally diﬃcult to plan [14]. Rohani et
al.[24] are in their studies stressing the urgent need for eﬀective and eﬃcient on-site logistics
and, are arguing that it will require visualization of logistics. Azhar[36] is further presenting
several beneﬁts of applying BIM to improve planning in construction. In relation to logistics,
this is especially involving the planning of materials and delivery schedules. Larsen et al.[27]
are further arguing that there is also a need for increased pre-planning to reduce the factors
aﬀecting time, cost and quality, which is further supported by Yang et al.[45].

Communication
Across construction and logistics literature, communication is repeatedly referred to as one
of the most important factors of project success [1, 21, 16, 14, 20]. However, Gamil et al.[46]
are in their studies concluding that poor communication is a major issue on a global per-

in CLM, Sullivan et al.[14] are discussing typical characteristics of communication in CLM.
Firstly, they are pointing out that there is a tendency that the industry are under-delivering
as a result of giving over-optimistic promises. They are further arguing that this is a nat-
ural reaction to the inherent uncertainty in construction projects. They are also seeing an
aﬀect of interdisciplinary mis-understandings as a result of "diverse perceptions, attitudes
and values". Gamil et al. [46] are also discovering that the most reoccurring causes for
poor communication in construction projects are "Lack of eﬀective communication between
construction parties", followed by "Lack of eﬀective communication system and platform".
Sullivan et al. [14] and Bortolini and Fomoso [20] are emphasizing the importance of good
communication as a key factor in CLM. More so, how it is important that key personnel
in the logistics management should have a higher attendance in meetings with the project
administration. Essentially, improve interdisciplinary communication. One measure that
has proven to be a constructive contributor to collaboration and communication is BIM.
The studies [36, 13, 20] are showing that visualization and tracking of logistics operations
through 3D and 4D models are allowing logistic strategies to be communicated quickly and
clearly, and that it improves both the eﬃciency and the understanding of the information.
Showing and illustrating the communicated information makes is less open for individual
interpretation and mis-understandings.

Stakeholder coordination and interaction
In construction projects, there are usually a wide range of stakeholders. It ranges from build-

their peers can promote understanding of logistical requirements, and better foresee poten-
tial conﬂicts between the parties. It promotes better communication and enables solutions
to be discovered and discussed in the interaction between the stakeholders. This interactive
process is also described and supported by several other studies [20, 21, 49]. It addresses
several of the conﬂict factors described by Rauzana[48]; "Lack of communication among the
team members", "Poor management, control and coordination" and "Failure of plan and

2.3.1
Construction logistics plan
A Construction Logistics Plan (CLP) is most importantly targeting the impact which logis-
tics has on the road network. According to Transport for London (TfL) a well-developed
CLP can reduce environmental impact, road risk, congestion and costs. Through the de-
velopment of a CLP, there are several typical aspects that are referred to in the literature,
that makes up the content of the plans[13, 18, 14, 50, 17]. An outline of the features are
explained as follows:
• Project introduction: The project outline contains information about the overall
project. The introduction should describe the purpose of the CLP and contain in-
formation about the responsible for preparing and approving the CLP, general site
speciﬁcations and locations, the structure of the CLP and the diﬀerent phases of the
CLP.
• Site layout plan: The site layout plan are facilitating the material movement on-site.
Loading zones, tower crane locations and reach, site access, facilities like staﬀbarracks,
waste handling, equipment containers and on-site movement routes. In addition it will
handle pedestrians routes and other adjacent traﬃc regulations.
• Supply chain management/material logistics: The supply chain is an important
part of the CLM. It must consider all materials arriving and leaving the site. Ways
of transport, whether there should be used pre-fabricated elements or modules, appli-
cation of CCC’s and integration between other sites and waste management should
all be included. In addition, all procurement’s must be planned and detailed in the
supply chain.
• Planning of-site surroundings: This part includes neighbour considerations, access
routes for transport and an estimated traﬃc loads and congestion. Further, there must
be developed route plans and temporary vehicle holding areas for traﬃc waiting to
enter the site.
Across literature the division of the plan is diﬀerent, and the level of detail also varies.
The mentioned above are the most relevant aspects, repeatedly referred to in the literature.

Pre-fabricated elements
One of the most important measures in construction projects and especially those located

Pre-fabrication of materials

To fully utilize the beneﬁts of pre-fabrication and mitigate the inherent barriers, it is impor-

Data and materials tracking
Tracking of materials as a part of Information Communication Technology (ICT) are in-
creasingly used in construction and in general manufacturing and logistics. It holds the
possibilities for automating and streamlining many processes in the industry. Examples of
such technology are: Radio Frequency IDentiﬁcation (RFID), bar codes, QR-codes, laser

is the RFID technology that is most present in the latest literature. The RFID technology
can store more data than e.g. bar codes, and have the advantage that the data chip does
not have to be in line of sight to be registered. Meaning, that it is easier to retrieve the
information of the materials, e.g. when they leave the fabric or enters the site. The theory

in real time[21, 13, 24].
There are still several barriers in terms of making use of such technology. The technology is
costly and tagging the construction materials are considered challenging and time consum-

are not ﬁtted to utilize the RFID technology, nor a systematized use of bar codes. Even
though they are surprised that the technology has not made a bigger impact considering
the value it has created in the manufacturing industry. The fragmented supply chain, con-
tractual standards and the limited product speciﬁcation from architects limits the concept
to an ambition.
However, the literature is seeing great possibilities in the automatic data collection. If it is

2.3.5
Construction Consolidation Centre
CCCs are small or medium sized warehouses for handling and distributing materials and
goods between one or several construction sites, as illustrated in Figure 2.3. All materials
are stored in safe and dry environments, with all the appropriate equipment required for
storing, organizing and loading materials. Instead of having all loads delivered directly to
the site, possibly congesting it, there is an additional route between the site and CCC, which
handles only the currently needed loads, while the rest is stored at the CCC. Some loads will
still be transported directly to the site, but this is to a much smaller degree than without
the CCC. Waste will also be returned to the CCC to be sorted and handled there[2].
Figure 2.3: The principles of a Construction Consolidation Centre[2].
The personnel traditionally having to manage on-site deliveries of materials and goods
are known to be: invariably over-paid, over qualiﬁed, unmotivated, under-trained and ill-
equipped to undertake this role[14]. Using CCCs, will leave the on-site personnel free to focus
on their core tasks, rather than on supplies and goods leaving and arriving on site, and thus,
utilizing their skill sets to a much higher degree[17]. A study conducted by the Transport of
London[54] are showing a signiﬁcantly decrease in construction site traﬃc of up to 70% and
several other beneﬁts like increased delivery reliability, labour force productivity and waste
limitations. The studies are also pointing out that the implementation of CCC is requiring
a few years of establishment to realize its full potential[54]. In conditions where the site is
ﬁt for internal transport in a dry and safe environment, the consolidation can be handled
equally well on site. The size of the project however, is not necessarily a factor[17].

3 | Method
The purpose of this research is to discover how a system for tracking and visualization of
materials in logistics management can contribute to maintain a competitively, economically
and environmentally sustainable industry. The research will take a contractors point of
view. To understand how the TVS can target the current and future construction industry,
the research will consist of two parts: a literature study and a case study in the form of a
workshop.
The workshop is done in collaboration with Company A and key personnel from the com-
pany’s projects. All of the participants have experience from the industry and are referred
to as Subject Matter Experts (SME). The intention of the workshop is to bring together
the SMEs, collect their knowledge and experience, and review the process of logistics man-
agement within the company. The workshop is described in detail in the workshop guide.
The guide can be found in Appendix A.
The advantage of using workshop as a research method is that it creates a platform for
the exchange of knowledge and experience. It also encourages discussions and reﬂection
between the participants, which would not be possible if using e.g. interviews. The ﬁrst-
hand knowledge that the workshop provides is important as literature and reality are not
always comparable. Moreover, it opens for the participants to consider topics that they
otherwise would not come up with themselves. It also opens up for the researcher to take a
role as a passive listener, limiting the bias that would be present in an interview situation,
where the researcher might ask leading questions[55]. Company A is generally using this
type of workshop in their development of new systems. The participants are thus familiar
with the concept.
The drawback of using a workshops is, ﬁrst, the ability to go more into depth with each SME
is limited. Secondly, it may increase the bias of the participants, as there is a possibility
that they will be aﬀected by their co-workers’ opinions and also peer pressure. Finally, it is
harder to document the results of the workshops. This is solved by recording the workshops
and the presentations of the results.
To analyze the results from the workshops, it is important to establish a basis of knowledge.
Thus, literature studies are essential. Construction logistics management is a complex dis-
cipline and consists of a wide range of processes and sub-disciplines. As the purpose of this
study is to understand how a system can inﬂuence construction logistics, it is important
to gather as much information as possible about the current practice, the main challenges
of this practice, and eventually, how a system for tracking and visualization can address
these challenges. In combination with the case study, the literature will provide the basis
for determining these aspects.

This section discusses the identiﬁed challenges within CLM. It compares the challenges
identiﬁed through the literature study with the challenges from the case study. It further
addresses the potential role of TVS within each of the challenges.

5.1.1
Planning
One of the most prominent focus areas from the case study is planning. All of the teams
are in some form mentioning planning.
As stated in section 2.2.1, planning logistics is
essential for a project’s success. The inherent complexity and uncertainty of construction
projects is emphasizing the need for planning, but is also limiting the ability to plan for all
scenarios. Eventually, the inability to follow the established plans will lead to costly delays,
inadequate quality, more waste and lower on-site safety. The causal explanations for why
planning logistics is failing are many.
The teams are describing a general lack of time for planning logistics. This applies to both
pre-construction and the entire construction phase. Firstly, are the teams describing that
they as a contractor have a limited or none inﬂuence in the design phase. Secondly, are they
often only given a few months from the design phase to the initiation of the construction to
plan. In the activities matrix (Appendix B), activities like making a logistic plan/strategy
are placed in the pre-construction phase. The importance of planning in the pre-construction
phase is emphasized in section 2.2.1. As the contractor do not have enough time during
the pre-construction phase to create the plans and strategies, they will unlikely be able to:
Firstly, plan for all events, and secondly, follow the plan due to the increased uncertainty
as a result of poor planning.
The lack of time for planning in the pre-construction phase is creating a vicious circle,
where lack of planning is causing delays, thus also limiting the time to plan during the
construction, which eventually leads to even more delays. This may be an explanation to
the teams’ perception of not having enough time for planning also during construction, and
not only in the pre-construction phase. The literature is also addressing how intensifying
the construction process to make up for lost time is leading to decreased safety and quality
and increased costs, as described in section 2.2.1.
As of the current situation the literature and the case study is pointing at limited time as
of one of the main factors to inadequate planning. In addition to the inherent complexity,
it is likely that this has to do with the over-optimistic estimation of time and costs, as
described in sections 2.2.2 and 2.1.2. The over-optimism is further worsened by the lacking
material management. Having the right equipment and manpower available at the right
time is crucial. During the case study it is clear that providing this is a challenge. In
relation to resource management, it is mostly the delivery and loading of materials on-
site that is desribed as the most critical part. Loading space, crane capacity and loading
equipment are described to be the biggest bottlenecks. Combining the lack of resources
with the over-optimistic approach to delivery schedules, there will be conﬂicts. Planning
and distribution of equipment to diﬀerent site locations will be meaningless if the contractor
or sub-contractors are not following the delivery schedules. This is described as a big concern
in both the literature and in the case study. This is possibly also one of the reasons why

5.1.2
Communication
Construction projects consist of a wide range of diﬀerent stakeholders, companies and dis-
ciplines. These will be located on-site, like e.g. the project management and personnel
responsible for the construction, but also oﬀ-site, like e.g.
the architects, customers or
project owners. When the project increases in size, so will the project organization. Bigger
projects requires better communication and coordination. The teams are reporting that
when the projects get bigger, so does the distance between key actors like the design team,
deliveries, construction and logistics management. Both the teams from the workshops and
the literature are arguing that the communication in construction projects are not good
enough. One of the teams are considering poor communication as the absolute biggest chal-
lenge in construction projects, supported by the literature as described in sections 2.2.2 and
2.2.4.
What seems rather inconsistent in the literature, is how in most of the functions in the CLM,
like CCC, CLP, pre-fabrication and JIT, they are emphasizing the need of early planning
and correlation between pre-construction planning, design and construction. However, in
the literature there are few examples on how communication across the phases are neither
planned nor executed. This can also be seen in relation to how the literature are arguing
that late design information and long approval times are one of the main causes to delays in
construction projects. The challenge is very much similar to what they are experiencing in
terms of planning, where the contractor’s participation in the design phase is very limited.
The literature focus more on the interdisciplinary communication, while the case study is
clear that poor communication is also the case within the company. Both argue that one
of the main causes to poor communication is the lack of good systems and platforms for
communication. It is also a challenge that the communication between diﬀerent stakeholders
is aﬀected by a lot of "noise"; unnecessary information that each discipline will have to sort
to ﬁnd the relevant information. Furthermore, the diﬀerent actors have their own systems

and platforms for communication, without the ability to interact. Ineﬃcient channels for
communication will eventually aﬀect most of the other processes in a construction project
and thus, any success criterion as a consequence of that.
It has been established that communication can be improved by visualization. First and fore-
most, in terms of the understanding of information in an interdisciplinary manner. Mainly,
as practices and elements can be shown and described visually, rather than by a 2D CAD

commit to actually using the platform. This means, that there must also be incentives for
any aﬀected sub-contractors or stakeholders.
5.1.3
Resistance to change
In relation to many other industries and sectors, the development and adaptation of new
technology is signiﬁcantly slower in the construction industry. It is clear that the industry
is held back by a complexity and uncertainty that is unique for the industry. The relatively
short project duration is also highly relevant, which is supported by the fact that companies
with long term implementation strategies are meeting less resistance.
The literature is claiming that the conservative culture is playing a major role in the resis-
tance to change. This is also supported by the discussions between the teams during the
workshop. The most engaged discussion is coming when the teams are discussing the value
of investments. One of the teams are clearly arguing that they do not have the incentives to
invest in implementing new measures, mostly because they would not know if it would pay
out. The other two teams are arguing against it, and is blaming one of the team members
for acting "project manager like", which he agrees with. What seems to be the meaning
behind this, is the big focus on the project costs, which is ultimately the project manager’s
responsibility.
The inherent resistance to change is not necessarily something which will be improved by
applying the TVS, but is rather a requirement for the implementation to be successful.
The case study is discovering some resistance. However, considering that the Company

5.1.4
Stakeholder coordination and interaction
From the case study, it is clear that the interaction between the project stakeholders are
insuﬃcient. They are struggling to provide a transparency between the actors, and the
coordination between sub-contractors is inadequate, especially in the delivery and storage
interface. The consequences of poor stakeholder interaction according to the literature is
possible severe ﬁnancial harm. The main factors aﬀecting the coordination is desribed to
be poor communication and project management and failure to implement change requests
from customers. This is in alignment with the challenges the case study is experiencing in
relation to planning and communication.
In terms of logistics, the case study is highlighting the coordination of deliveries and storage
on-site as one of the biggest challenges in the coordination between sub-contractors. The
teams are arguing that this is as a result of planning, communication and transparency.
Moreover, the self-interest of the other companies is often coming before the project, and is
contributing to the diﬃculties.
Logistics as a discipline is met with diﬀerent perceptions in the literature and amongst the
professionals. Some have a very simplistic approach to logistics, and are merely considering
the logistics as the transportation and storage of goods. The more comprehensive approach,
that is becoming increasingly popular in more resent literature is considering more aspects
like design, consolidation and scheduling as well. The misalignment between the traditional
approach and the more developed one is also aﬀecting the attitudes of the participants in
construction projects, which is evident in the case study. The diﬀerent perceptions and
attitudes are causing troubles in terms of the coordination, because people do not work
from the same basis nor towards the same goal. The case study is identifying a need to
better deﬁne the Key Performance Indicators (KPI), to align the stakeholders’ motivations.
The teams are during the case study suggesting that deﬁning more clear goals and how to
approach them can counteract the diﬀerence in attitudes.

5.1.5
Knowledge and experience
From the workshop it is clear that the lack of competence and experience is a challenge.
This is supported by several studies, and the challenges are even bigger in relation to new
technology.
The lack of technological skills and understanding is most present between
on-site personnel. While the literature is mainly either focusing on the technological or the
overall knowledge, are the teams more focused on planning. The teams are mostly concerned
that the knowledge gap in terms of reactive measures is the biggest challenge. Meaning,
that the project teams are not suited to act when things are not going according to the
plan. They want to see more backup plans and standards for reacting to unforeseen events
as measure to cope with the lack of knowledge. As knowledge and experience is aﬀecting
many other processes within logistics, a lack of it, will eventually also aﬀect the performance
indicators.
Increased training of workers employed by both the contractor and the subcontractors is
described in the case study as a measure to better improve the logistics. They believe that
the training can improve especially how the diﬀerent actors coordinate e.g. storage on-site,
and can make it more eﬃcient.
It is unlikely that the TVS will serve as an educational tool. On the contrary, it is depending
on an already existing knowledge and understanding. As the TVS is mainly managed by a
handful of personnel, most likely the logistic management or the site management, poten-
tial knowledge gaps will be aﬀordable to cover. The information input that the suppliers,
manufacturers or sub-contractors will be responsible for is based on simple spreadsheets to
be ﬁlled out. The TVS is hence, not directly impacted by the general lack of technological

5.2.1
Construction logistics plan
A CLP represent many elements which is accounted for in general planning of construction
projects. However, it represents a more dedicated approach to the impact that logistics
has on the road network. The literature also identiﬁes a positive impact on environmental
aspects and costs from the application of a CLP. Even though the case study does not address
CLP in its pure form, they are considering several aspects of it, e.g. travel distance, material
consolidation and site layout. This do not necessarily mean that Company A systematically
use CLPs in their projects, but it enforces the correlation between traditional planning and
the CLP.
Based on the activity matrix, is Company A already planning most of the areas that a CLP
will target. It is thus unlikely that a CLP will present any drastic changes to what is already
being considered in the planning. The CLP will thus be eﬀected by the TVS similar to the
general planning area. However, the clear and consise framework could be used to better
strucure the planning within CLM.
5.2.2
Pre-fabricated elements
As discussed in section 2.3.2, oﬀ-site, pre-fabrication of elements has become an important
process to better adapt to small cites in urban, dense areas. It has been recognized to
facilitate higher safety, reduction of waste and increased quality. However, they are also
complex and hard to manage from a logistics perspective. Pre-construction of materials
have also been connected to JIT principles. The challenges that the case study is identifying
in terms of JIT, storage and deliveries on-site is emphasized when the size and complexity
is increased. They require more resources and space.

Just in time
In both the literature and case study, it has been identiﬁed that despite the eﬀort, they are

with the planning and execution of deliveries on site. In section 2.2.1 it is described how the

to follow. This is what the teams in the case study are considering not following "the rules
of logistics". The consequences are delays, more congestion around site leading to more
pollution and less safety and eventually extra costs.
The beneﬁts that are attainable if JIT is performed successfully are several, and it is thus
important to make the deliveries on-site smooth and eﬃcient. This is areas that have been
previously discussed in the planning and stakeholder section. The JIT are depending on the
same actions. More transparency and better planning and coordination.

are not considered during the case study. As desribed in section 2.3.4, this technology is
currently very costly and is not suited for the current way of practicing CLM. When the
case study do not mention it neither, it is unlikely that it will become a resource within

5.2.5
Construction consolidation centre
The increasing use of CCCs is a topic closely described in the literature. It is recognized
by the literature as an appropriate tool to limit waste, reduce traﬃc and congestion, make
on-site deliveries more accurate and eﬃcient. The use of oﬀ-site storage and consolidation
is also mentioned during the workshop. The teams are deﬁnitely seeing a value. However,
the teams are more critical to the concept than the literature. Mostly, because of the risk
with storing everything in one facility in case of e.g. ﬂoods or ﬁre. They are also worried
about the responsibility that they commit to. If the CCC is used for both them and their
subcontractors it also opens up for conﬂicts in terms of the storage and the delivers between
the CCC and site.
CCCs oﬀers opportunities that can address many of the challenges described in the literature
and during the workshop. Firstly, the challenge the teams are experiencing with organising
the deliveries on site. Consolidating goods before they are transported to site, limits the
stress on the loading zones and cranes. It also limits the need for on-site storage of materials.
Hence, limiting cases like the the carpenter mentioned in the case study, who were storing
loads of materials without needing them at the time. This way it keeps the site cleaner,
better organized and it limits the traﬃc loads around site.
The teams are also worried about the costs of a CCC. However, they have not done any
cost/beneﬁt analysis of the concept yet. If the company is able to implement the CCCs
according to the successful cases desribed in the literature, there are no reason why they
should not also gain the same beneﬁts as the literature are experiencing.
Making use of CCCs are further presenting opportunities as a supportive measure for the
TVS. Most importantly, it facilitates the tracking of the materials. Having materials stored
in an organized facility, improves the overview of the materials. Even though the use of
intelligent tracking like RFID, is currently considered too expensive to be applied to con-
struction projects, the CCCs are oﬀering a stable and organized environment more similar
to typical manufacturing, where automated tracking has become a core function. This can
facilitate any future intelligent tracking of materials in the TVS.

5.4.1
Opportunities
One of the advantages of the TVS is that it is building on already existing technology.
This technology is considered with a suﬃcient maturity to support the TVS. If a successful

element is delivered. If the element below is not designed or manufactured yet, the
team will easier detect this as it have been visualized in the model. They can thus
postpone the delivery instead of having to store it on-site while waiting for the other
element.
By targeting these areas the TVS shows potential to improve several areas that have been
identiﬁed as challenges within CLM. Consequently, it can also be a supportive measure

Barriers
In order for the TVS to be implemented successfully, there are some barriers that must be
considered. Below are the most crucial elements to support the implementation of the TVS.
• The TVS depends on a reliable and eﬃcient platform for communication and infor-
mation distribution. This platform will have to be easy to access for every involved
stakeholder.
• The TVS is depending on actors within every step of the value chain to commit to
the system. It have been identiﬁed a trend where actors are putting their self-interest

interest. Hence, implementing the TVS requires incentives for any involved actor to
be engaged in the implementation of the system.
• Company A are showing a willingness to adapt to new technology and develop new

It is reasonable to believe they will meet some internal resistance, like the case study
identiﬁes. It is thus important to have a close follow-up and a long-term implementa-
tion.
• The responsible for the TVS on each project need a suﬃcient knowledge and experience
within the ﬁeld of CLM, and must be able to understand the technical aspects of the
TVS.
The barriers that are described have a clear connection with the overall challenges that the
industry is facing. This emphasises the importance on addressing these barriers, because the
are aﬀecting much more then just the TVS. Both the literature study and the case study
are identifying areas like planning, communication, stakeholder coordination, experience
and knowledge as some of the biggest challenges within the construction industry. These
areas can at the same time be considered as some of the core functions within project
management. This means that even though Company A develops and implements systems
and methods with great outlooks, they will do so unsuccessfully if the core functions within
the projects are failing.

6 | Conclusion
The purpose of this research has been to establish how the TVS can address challenges the
construction industry are facing, to maintain a competitively, economically and environ-
mentally sustainable industry. Subsequently, the following questions have been deﬁned: (1)
"What performance indicators are most aﬀected by logistics?" (2) "What are the biggest
challenges in construction logistics management and its belonging processes?" Once these
questions have been answered it is possible to answer the ﬁnal question: (3) "How can the
TVS target and improve the established processes and challenges in construction logistics?"
To answer these questions, a literature study and a case study have been conducted.
Logistics has been found to be one of the core activities within construction projects. The
consequences of poorly executed logistics can aﬀect almost every single process on site.
Through the literature studies, the most important performance indicators within logistics
have been deﬁned as cost, time, quality, waste, safety and environment. In this case, the
research has identiﬁed a miss-alignment between the literature and construction practition-
ers in terms of how they are assessing performance indicators. The case study has identiﬁed
cost, time and quality as the most important indicators. However, they are not considering
the three remaining performance indicators waste, safety and environment. The research
have discovered that the construction industry is failing to deliver satisfactory results on
every single performance indicator. Furthermore, it has been identiﬁed a lack of attention
to the three indicators, waste, safety and environment among the construction practitioners.
These are factors characterized by the literature as with great signiﬁcance to logistics.
The identiﬁed challenges within construction logistics and its functions have been established
and evaluated in terms of their impact on performance indicators. Due to the complexity and
the inherent uncertainty that characterizes construction projects, the challenges are strongly
interrelated, and the challenge can thus not be seen in the light of just one aspect. The
most prominent challenges have found to be within the areas of planning, communication,
stakeholder coordination, resistance to change and lack of knowledge and experience. Each
of these are aﬀected by several parameters, also each other. They are together drastically
limiting the project performance and the established indicators. The mentioned challenges
are inevitably aﬀecting most functions in project management and not just logistics. Within
logistics it is clear that it is delivery and storage of materials that are the most critical areas
aﬀected by these challenges.
This research has identiﬁed a set methods and functions within logistics, central to the cur-
rent and future processes of CLM. Namely, CCC, pre-fabrication, JIT, tracking of materials
and CLP. Pre-fabrication and JIT are already established methods, but with potential of
improvement. The remaining are relatively new concepts that has found to be promising in

6. Conclusion
the aim to improve CLM. TVS are showing potential to support each of them, but this is a
mutual relation, and the functions can provide equal support for the TVS.
The TVS can address challenges within CLM in the following ways; (1) Planning, as a
result of better sequencing of deliveries, better estimates of stresses on loading and storage
areas and better overview of what-if scenarios; (2) Stakeholder interaction, as a result of
better understanding of change orders and their consequences, for the contractor and for
the client, and better coordination and scheduling of deliveries between actors. Together
these areas can have an impact on the established performance indicators. However, a set of
requirements have been established in order for the TVS to be successfully implemented; (1)
It requires a reliable and eﬃcient platform for communication where all involved stakeholders
have easy access; (2) It requires incentives for any involved stakeholder in order to engage
them in the implementation. (3) A long-term implementation strategy with close follow-up;
(4) The TVS responsible for each project must have a great understanding of CLM and the
technical aspect of the TVS.
To summarize, it is evident that the construction industry is facing challenges in terms
of logistics, but also in an interdisciplinary manner. These challenges are aﬀecting perfor-
mance in areas like cost, time, quality, waste, safety and environment. This research has
characterized functions and methods that can contribute to improving logistics and it has
evaluated how the TVS can interact with these. It has further established how the TVS can
improve areas within logistics management and its limitations. In conclusion, the TVS can
have a positive eﬀect on construction logistics performance. However, the challenges within
communication, planning, stakeholder coordination, knowledge and experience have to be
solved in order for the industry to maintain a competitively, economically and environmen-
tally sustainable industry. This requires more change and initiatives then what TVS can
oﬀer.
The method applied to this research has been a combination of a literature study and a
case study in the form of a workshop. Using a workshop as a research method is beneﬁ-
cial because it opens up for discussions between experienced practitioners of construction
logistics management. However, it also limits the researcher’s ability to follow up on spe-
ciﬁc subjects. In this case, it would be beneﬁcial to have follow-up interviews afterward
to get answers to elements that would be unclear or not discussed during the workshop.
Furthermore, even though there is a joint session in the end where all teams are presenting
their thoughts, it is also possible that viable input gets lost as the researcher can not be
present in all teams at the same time. Moreover, this study have not done any quantiﬁable
measurements, meaning that the research can address areas for improvement, but it do not
provide a basis for a cost/beneﬁt analysis of the TVS.
Construction logistics management is a comprehensive discipline and there are many exciting
opportunities that could be discussed in more detail. Especially in the interface between

logistics and diﬀerent forms of ICT and BIM technology. This research is only presenting
one form of BIM application, but there are many more to be addressed. This research has
established a set of challenges that is prominent in construction logistics management and
has set the basis to go even deeper into how each of them are aﬀecting the performance of
construction projects. This study has had a qualitative approach, and can thus not place
an actual value on improving the logistics. Creating a baseline to measure the impact the
identiﬁed challenges has on construction performance can also be a future approach.

The workshop consists of several parts.
Part 0: Introduction
The ﬁrst part is the introduction. The information part has a duration of approximately 30
minutes. All the participants are introducing themselves, before the leader of the workshop
are going through the purpose of the workshop, the activities and how everything will be
done. The participants are further divided into three teams. For each activity, the teams will
work individually, before a summary of the activities are done collectively. The participants
and their roles in the company can be found in Table A.1.

Part 1: Challenges and opportunities
The ﬁrst part of the workshop is identifying the biggest challenges and opportunities "as
is" based on the participants own experiences. The activity consists of discussions within
each of the teams. All the identiﬁed challenges and opportunities are written on post-its in
a "live board". The live board is a virtual wall, where the participants can create post-it
notes. All changes can be seen by all of the teams while it is being updated.
The activity is divided into several parts. The teams are ﬁrst considering the challenges.
Approximately 30-45 minutes are used for this. Each team are then presenting their chal-
lenges and they are discussed between the teams. The same procedure is repeated for the
opportunities.

Part 2: Activity matrix
The second part is based on the same live board, but is now considering the most important
activities making up the construction logistics. Each team are going to ﬁll out a matrix
consisting of the following areas:
• Main activity: What is the activity
• Deliverables: What does the activity consist of and what has to be delivered
• When: When is the activity executed
• Good ideas and tools: Suggested ideas and tools to improve the activity
• Subject matter expert: Who is the most experienced person on the subject
• Responsible: Who is responsible for the activity
The activity matrix is not presented in a joint section like the challenges and opportunities.
