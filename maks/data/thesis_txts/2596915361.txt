Executive Summary 
The European Union requires renewable energy support (RES) auctioneers to conduct 
“technology-neutral” auctions when dividing support payments for such projects. This is an elusive 
goal for auctioneers, but the first step in working toward neutrality, is to analyze the bias that exists 
in previous technology-neutral auctions so that they can find ways to avoid this bias. 
This study uses a discounted cash-flow analysis (DCF) to analyze bias in two case studies of multi-
technology auctions in Denmark and Germany that each include wind and solar photovoltaic (PV) 
projects. This is then used to determine general factors that affect bias in RES auctions and the 
way the impact the auction’s bias. 
The DCF model is performed from both the auctioneer and bidder’s perspectives. The auctioneer 
perspective proved quite useful in analyzing data through a levelized cost on society (LCOS) that 
is explained and used throughout this paper.  
The LCOS made it possible to quantify the bias that occurs when a bidder is shielded from the 
market by analyzing the German case, which uses a one-sided sliding feed-in-premium (FIP). In 
this auction there was a case when a wind project bid less than one of the awarded PV projects but 
after an undisclosed malus factor on the wind projects, was not awarded. At first glance the wind 
project seemed to be unfairly biased against. However, the LCOS calculations showed that though 
the methodology for why is happened is unclear, that this project has a significantly larger cost to 
society. 
Overall, this project used the LCOS to quantify bias when possible and give an example scenario 
of how an auctioneer could award bids instead of by simply choosing the lowest FIP. The project 
also provides an overview of auction factors.

1. Introduction 
As global climate change continues to become a larger and more urgent issue, many countries are 
providing financial support to renewable energy projects in order to reduce the carbon emissions 
from conventional energy sources. Renewable energy auctions have generally been used in order 
to award renewable energy projects for a specific technology, region, or location. A new push for 
so called “technology-neutral” auctions has been spreading across many countries with the first 
auctions having recently been performed in several different countries. As very few renewable 
energy sources have reached grid parity with conventional sources, auctions for renewable energy 
sources are still necessary in order to reach emission reduction goals [1]. In fact, the European 
Union requires that its member states to determine their member states to determine the support 
through “non-discriminatory” (technology-neutral) auctions unless a compelling reason for other 
auction types [1]. Although there are several examples of technology neutral auctions occurring 
throughout the recent years, there is a large overreaching question of what it actually means in 
order to be “technology neutral” and how an auctioneer should design auctions in order to get as 
close to “technology neutral” as possible since being completely neutral is an impossible thing to 
achieve [1]. That being said, there is still a need to evaluate and improve on the inevitable biases 
that exist in technology-neutral auctions of today. This is the focus of this paper, to quantitively 
analyze some of the auctions that have occurred on the world stage thus far and to create a basic 
model in order to be able to analyze future projects.  
1.1. Advantages of technology neutral auctions 
Technology neutral auctions have several advantages over technology-specific auctions, which is 
the main alternative to technology-neutral auctions. Technology-neutral auctions allow for the 
highest social benefit by increasing the static efficiency of the auction, meaning the bidders with 
the lowest costs are awarded more often than in technology-specific cases. This can increase social 
welfare. Additionally, there are often lower prices than in technology-specific auctions, though 
this is also dependent on the support payment type offered.  
1.2. Limitations of technology neutral auctions 
Though there are some large advantages to performing technology-neutral auctions, there are some 
potential problems that need to be considered and accounted for in order to create an auction that 
is economically efficient, maximizes social welfare, promotes technology innovations, etc. As 
more potential technologies are considered in auctions the auctions themselves become 
increasingly complicated. There are many factors that can vary greatly from one technology to 
another. Some of these factors that affect the auction-design and the bidder-strategies are 
integration costs, balancing costs, full load hours, lifetime, module pricing, capacity factors, etc. 
These different factors all have an impact of a project’s levelized cost of energy (LCOE). The 
LCOE gives the cost of producing one unit of energy levelized over the lifetime of the project and 
generally can be thought of as the producer’s cost to produce one unit of energy. Depending on 
the way the energy system and policies are designed in a given country, some factors, such as the 
integration costs or balancing costs may be covered by a different entity, such as the transmission 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
2 
 
system operator (TSO). This means that often the LCOE will not fully reflect the cost of one unit 
of energy, but the complexities of these other costs are not considered in the scope of this project.  
Generally, in auctions today, the awarded projects are based on the projects with the lowest costs 
(lowest LCOE). This is not a bad indicator in many ways, but it neglects one aspect that could be 
important for the auctioneer to consider, self-cannibalization. “Self-cannibalization” is when a 
large penetration of variable renewable energy lowers the energy prices on the market due to the 
drastic increase in variable renewable energy sources (VRES) being scheduled at a specific time. 
This can become so dramatic with higher VRES penetration that economic inefficiencies occur 
from the auctioneer’s point of view, since they must pay larger differences between the market 
value of the energy produced and the agreed FIT due to this “self-cannibalization”. This implies 
that auction-design should consider how to account for the potential changes in market value. 
These could be through a different support payment type that exposes the bidders to the market 
value of their produced energy, or creating quotas in the auction in order to ensure some diversity 
of projects, or by creating a different awarding criteria that is based on the “Net Marginal Cost”, 
which is the difference between the FIT and the market value that the energy actually is anticipated 
to be sold for. This illustrates how the goals of the auctioneer can be addressed by with several 
different techniques. This also makes it more difficult to compare auctions and to limit the bias in 
technology-neutral auctions.  
1.3. Alternatives to technology-neutral auctions 
Some alternatives to technology-neutral auctions include technology-specific auctions, quotas in 
technology-neutral auctions, and project specific-auctions. These types of auctions can help to 
encourage new technologies to be developed as technology-neutral auctions generally work best 
when the technologies that are considered are competitive with one another. This means that new 
and emerging technologies, which tend to be more expensive, will have a hard time being awarded 
in a technology-neutral auction. New technologies still should be encouraged in order to meet the 
climate goals so these types of technologies may benefit more from another auction type. 
Additionally, one way of fighting the aforementioned “self-cannibalization” and to encourage a 
more stable and diverse energy mix is to have diverse VRES types and locations. For this reason, 
there might be energy systems that have a high penetration of one type of VRES and want to 
encourage another type by hosting other auction types. This project will primarily focus on 
technology-neutral auctions that do not have quotas but analyzing the times when quotas or other 
auction types are more beneficial is something that should be considered and analyzed in the future.  
1.4. General auction background 
As mentioned earlier, RES auctions are performed in order to meet political emission reduction 
targets. At the same time these targets should be met while spending the minimum amount of 
money possible. The auctions themselves can be viewed from the bidder or the auctioneer’s 
perspective. Both perspectives will be discussed briefly but the primary focus of this project is 
from the auctioneer’s perspective, taking a more socio-economic view of things. 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
3 
 
1.4.1. Auctioneer 
The auctioneer designs an auction in order to meet a set of goals they define. These could be set 
based on several criteria including setting bid volume restrictions, a total monetary, capacity, or 
generation budget, different award criteria, etc. The type of financial support that is awarded can 
be a FIT, a FIP, or some other support scheme. The auctioneer’s goal is to meet their goals (award 
amount, etc.) while minimizing the amount of support payments. This means that for different 
types of support payments, the amount of aid the auctioneer awards will be calculated differently. 
In an FIP system the aid can be fixed, which means there is a flat amount of aid paid per unit 
produced. There are also one-sided and two-sided FIPs, which are discussed in more detail in 2.4. 
Since the FITs offer a fixed price to the bidder, the difference between the market price and the 
support level is the aid payment. The FIT support payment system does not expose the bidder to 
the market value of the energy produced (the actual amount the energy is being sold for). This 
means that in the FIT scenario there is a large difference in the bidder and the auctioneer 
perspective. The auctioneer should aim to minimize the necessary aid payments not just the LCOE 
of the projects in question (these are often but not always the same projects).  
1.4.2. Bidder 
The other perspective of an auction is the bidder. The bidder calculates their total LCOE for a 
project and wants to ensure this will be covered. For a FIT auction, the bidder will bid their total 
LCOE to be their FIT. In other auction types the bidder calculates the additional premium on top 
of the average market price they require in order to cover the project’s LCOE. This means that 
from a bidder perspective the FIT system is a simpler concept as they will know exactly how much 
they will receive for each unit of produced energy. However, this is the opposite for the auctioneer 
who will not know exactly how much they will have to pay in order to fulfill the awarded FIT. As 
for the FIPs, these tend to be simpler for the auctioneer to predict but the bidder must project the 
necessary amount of support necessary in order to cover their LCOE. The focus of this project is 
on the auctioneer and the socio-economic viewpoint of an auction but it is important to remember 
that gross underestimations of necessary support levels by bidders in auctions awarding FIPs may 
lead to an awarded project going bankrupt, which is not good for social welfare either.  
1.5. Auction strategy ideas 
There are some auction strategies exist for designing new auctions. One of the auction strategies 
is to determine the best way to expose a project to the market value of the energy it produces. As 
mentioned earlier, the projects with the lowest net marginal costs (aid payments) will be awarded 
a FIT even if their LCOE is higher than some projects not awarded in order to minimize the aid 
payments. Other ways to expose the projects to the market value are to change from a FIT to other 
aid payment types, such as an FIP.  
Within any auction type there are different ways to determine the received price of the awarded 
bidders. The pay-as-bid strategy gives the bidder the amount of support they requested if they are 
awarded. Alternately, there is uniform pricing, which pays all awarded bidders the same amount 
and is usually awarded based on the lowest bid that was not awarded. In general, the pay-as-bid

strategy leads to a lot more economic efficiency and is favored in the auctions in the AURES II 
project database. 
1.6. Scope of Thesis 
This thesis aims to investigate the biases of multi-technology auctions through quantitative and 
qualitative investigations. The analysis is performed by developing a discounted cash-flow 
analysis (DCF) model that uses archetype data to investigate biases in two case-studies of 
technology-neutral auctions. The model is created to analyze the data primarily from the 
auctioneer’s or society’s perspective with a small insight into the bidder’s perspective.

2. Methods 
The methods for this project include different calculations made after performing a discounted 
cash flow analysis (DCF) in Excel. DCF is a common technique that involves estimating the free 
cash flows over the lifetime of a project and discounting them in order to account for the time-
value of money. The model for this project uses the “free cash flow discounted at the weighted 
average cost of capital (WACC)” method as described in [2].  
2.1. Levelized Cost of Electricity 
The levelized cost of energy is traditionally a common metric to determine if the correct projects 
were awarded in a multi-technology RES auction. The logic behind this is that in an economically 
efficient auction the projects with the lowest realization costs should be the projects awarded and 
if this happens then the auction is efficient. [3] 
The LCOE is a representation of the total cost to produce one unit of electricity. There are different 
methods to calculate the LCOE of a project. Some of the simplified methods do not require a 
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) but since other analyses are performed in this project, there is no 
need to simplify the LCOE as there are limitations in the number of variables that can be included 
in these methods.

analysis is found by summing the discounted costs over the lifetime of the project and dividing it

is sold for on the day-head (spot) market. This received electricity price is important when 
performing DCF analysis on the data since it, along with the support payments make up the 
project’s revenue. The Market value for a specific electricity source changes hourly and is

The average market value of different energy sources is not inherently equal to the average 
electricity price for the entire market (i.e. country, region). This is especially relevant for RES 
because of the self-cannibalization effects of variable RES on the spot price when a specific RES 
is available. This is due to the low marginal cost of RES, which is the additional cost to produce a

bidders, who have a relatively high marginal cost due to the cost of the raw material that is the fuel 
for their generation. When one RES project comes online and produces electricity, it is likely that 
other RES projects of the same type also come online, which means that depending on the 
penetration of a specific RES in an electricity system, the market price when that RES is online 
could be greatly decreased. This depends a lot on the penetration of that specific RES.

in the average market value of solar, onshore wind, and offshore wind. Since the DEA only

average market values of each technology. More details about the assumptions and procedure are

2.2.2. Market Value Factor 
Once the average market value was found, the technology-specific average market values for 
Danish onshore wind needed to be determined, offshore wind, and PV projects and the German 
onshore wind and PV projects. In order to find these technology- and region-specific market 
values, a market value factor (MVF), can be used in order to adjust the average market value of 
the system to the technology- and region-specific average market value in question. The MVF is 
a simply the ration between the technology- and region-specific market value and the average 
market value of the same region. Since the MVF is a ratio, it is possible for a specific technology 
to have an MVF greater than one. In fact, in countries where a specific RES has a low penetration, 
the MVF is often greater than one. For example, in 2017, the MVF for Danish PV was 
approximately 108% [6]. 
If the average market value of the technology in question and of the entire system are known, the 
MVF is a simple calculation. However, when the MVF needs to be predicted in order to convert 
from the average market value or spot price to the average market value of a specific technology, 
there are different schools of thought in order to determine how to find the MVF.  
One common way to predict the MVF is by using simple linear regression on existing data to 
extrapolate beyond the bounds of the existing data. These linear regressions are generally found in 
terms of the share of electricity consumption (penetration). In Figure 2, the historical German MVF 
data is shown in two forms. On the left is the MVF data in terms of time. This illustrates very 
clearly how the MVF for PV was well above the average market value until around 2013. The

wind at lower penetrations in Germany. This is illustrated by the steeper slope and faster decline

2.3. Levelized Cost to Society 
Due to the nature of multi-technology renewable energy auctions, there are additional factors to 
consider when auctioning off support compared to technology-specific auctions and even more 
factors to consider when compared to project specific auctions. The addition of multiple 
technologies in an auction adds signification variation in project lifetime, capacity factor, average 
market value of electricity, land lease costs, and other balancing and integration costs. These 
additional factors make it more difficult to directly compare the auction bids from project to 
project. The LCOE only considers the costs of the project developer (bidder) but does not consider 
the costs to the auctioneer (government). Since there is a self-cannibalization effect from RES 
sources, which is not reflected in the LCOE, there is a distinct possibility that by supporting the 
projects with the lowest LCOE there might be a higher support payment requirement for some of 
these projects than if some of the projects with a higher LCOE are awarded. 
This is more likely to occur when multiple locations and technologies are included since there are 
more factors that can change the relative market value of electricity  The In order to compare the 
relative societal benefit of different projects that are different in most  it was important to find a 
metric that The marginal cost to society is an important concept for this project as it is a way of 
comparing the net cost to society of a project over its lifetime.  
The Marginal cost to society is illustrated in Figure 4. The spot price is the received electricity 
price that is determined during the day ahead auction. This is the market value of the RES 
electricity because it is the amount money that electricity is worth on the market. Then the support 
payment is paid out in the agreed upon amount from the government. This can be in the form of a 
fixed FIP that would always have the same support payment, a one-sided sliding FIP that makes 
up the difference between the spot price and an agreed upon received price only if the spot price 
is below the agreed upon received price, then finally there is the two-sided FIP, which makes up 
the difference between the spot price and the agreed upon price on a monthly basis, which makes 
it so that the received price is usually fixed. The different support payment types all lead to 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
12 
 
different amounts of exposure to the market and how the support payment amount is determined 
but ultimately make up the difference between the received price and the spot price.  
There are then some additional costs to society that are not reflected in the support payments. The 
first is the balancing costs from forecast errors. The quantity of the generation bid in the day-ahead 
markets for RES sources is largely dependent on forecasting. Naturally, there are errors to the 
forecasting, which leads to balancing costs to make up for missing electricity or to use excess 
electricity. Then there are the network costs of the RES projects, which may or may not be paid 
by the project developer. [11] 
 
Figure 4: Components of Marginal Cost to Society 
The graphic in Figure 4 illustrates how, especially in cases when the spot prices are lower due to 
the self-cannibalization effect, the cost to society of a specific RES project can become much 
larger. The specifics of how much different support schemes expose the projects they support to 
the market are explained in 2.4. 
The auctioneer perspective and cost to society (total aid paid) is often overlooked when using the 
LCOE to determine if an auction was economically efficient. Since auction design and the inherent 
biases of creating multi-technology auctions come from the auctioneer and not the bidder, the 
auctioneer perspective is important in order to analyze projects. Instead of using LCOE to 
determine if an auction was economically efficient using a net marginal cost to society is a more 
appropriate metric. [12] In the German case described in [12], the objective was to show the 
economic inefficiencies in certain German auction cases by calculating the sum of the additional 
costs to society from the awarded projects in a specific year over these projects’ lifetimes. This 
metric gives useful information when analyzing a specific auction design, but it is not easy to use 
when comparing different project bids to determine which should be awarded. This is because 
across different technologies and projects there is not a set generation level-i.e. one project can 
cost less to society and generate a smaller amount of electricity. Since the LCOE gives a cost to 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
13 
 
produce one unit of energy, it is easy to compare bids. This is one of the big benefits to using the 
LCOE to analyze the efficiency of a multi-technology auction. 
In order to easily compare the cost to society of bids in a multi-technology auction, it is desireable 
to use a metric like the LCOE but from the auctioneer’s (government’s) perspective. This led to 
the marginal cost to society being a levelized quantity, meaning, like the LCOE, it is discounted 
over the project’s lifetime and divided by the “discounted” production. In this case though the 
costs are not the CAPEX, OPEX, etc. but are instead the support payment, additional integration 
and balancing costs that are not covered by the bidder. In the case of a fixed-FIP the support 
payment is simpler to calculate than in the cases when the project is more shielded from the market 
value of the electricity being sold. The other benefit to levelizing the net marginal costs is that it 
can be done over the course of the entire lifespan of the project. The support payments do not 
always last the entire project’s lifetime. In these cases, the project will continue to generate clean 
electricity while receiving the market value for the energy without support payments. This still has 
a benefit to society since it aids in avoiding carbon emissions and so this time should be considered 
in the denominator of the metric.  
Some support schemes involve the auctioneer covering the balancing or grid connection costs, but 
in the case of all the case studies in this project, these costs are not covered by the auctioneer and 
therefore this part of the cost to society is assumed to be zero. However, there are potential cases 
when the auctioneer would need to include these in their levelized calculation of the marginal cost 
to society, also referred to in this paper as the levelized cost to society of LCOS.   
2.4. Support Schemes 
Different support schemes work in different ways from the auctioneer and bidder perspectives. 
Three of the most common support schemes are the fixed, one-sided sliding, and two-sided sliding 
FIPs. Each of these expose the projects they support to the market values in different amounts, 
which is very important to the strategy the bidders use to determine their bid amounts. It also 
changes the amount of risk involved in the bids themselves. The more exposed to the market, the 
more risk there is in a project as forecasting is not always very good.  
2.4.1. Fixed-FIP 
The fixed feed-in premium (fixed-FIP) is a support scheme that pays a fixed premium to the project 
developers for every unit of electricity generated. This form of an FIP exposes the developer to 
the market the most because they are given a smaller amount of money as a premium on top of the 
market value. This means that if the market values are very high the developer still gets the FIP 
and if the markets are very low, they only get the FIP as support and in fact may lose money. Using 
a fixed FIP puts much of the risk on the developer instead of on the auctioneer. In fact, this type 
of support payment has a much higher level of certainty for the auctioneer because they know 
exactly how much they will be paying per unit of energy during the support period. The auctioneer 
will not know the amount of electricity produced per year but can use forecasting to determine the 
amount of electricity produced by each project. 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
14 
 
2.4.2. One-Sided Sliding-FIP 
The one-sided sliding-FIP is a form of a price guarantee where the producer is only exposed to the 
market on a monthly basis if the average market value for the energy produced is greater than the 
one-sided sliding-FIP. This means that the producer is pretty shielded from the market if it gets 
low but can benefit from higher market prices. This means that there is a lower risk to the producer 
since they can set their bid to be equal to the minimum electricity price required in order for the 
project to cover its costs. Conversely, this scheme puts more of the risk and uncertainty on the 
auctioneer. By shielding the project developers from the market, it is more likely that the self-
cannibalization effect has a significant role in these aid payments if the awarded bids are not 
determined in a way that aims to maximize the benefit to society while aiming to do so at a 
minimum cost. This means that such a support scheme requires more calculations in order to 
determine the bids to be awarded. 
2.4.3. Two-Sided Sliding-FIP 
The two-sided sliding-FIP works similarly to the one-sided sliding FIP but in this case the project 
is not exposed to the market prices even if they are higher than the FIP. The auctioneer still pays 
the aid payments as the difference between the market value and the FIP but in this case, on a 
monthly basis this difference is calculated. The way the aid payments work aim to minimize them 
but there is still a large amount of shielding of the bidders that occurs in this process. This is 
therefore exposing the auctioneer to a larger amount of risk in order to make these payments. This 
is because the auctioneer does not know what the market values will be and if they are significantly 
different from expected then they could cost a lot more than expected. This is particularly true if 
the self-cannibalization effect is larger than expected or the spot prices become deflated for some 
reason. These are a few reasons why in a mature market the auction designer may aim to avoid 
two-sided sliding-FIPS. However, in newer markets (i.e. Offshore wind), the security of this 
support scheme may be necessary in order to get to the point where the project developers can be 
exposed to the market.

3.1.1. CAPEX 
The capital expenditure (CAPEX) includes the different components that make up the initial 
investment to implement a functioning RES project. For different wind projects the CAPEX 
primarily comprises of the cost of the turbines, site preparation costs, grid connection costs, 
planning and other project costs, and land costs (often land is leased instead of purchased). This 
list shows that the CAPEX is much more than simply the cost of turbines, which is part of the 
reason the policy and current grid infrastructure of a region or country can have a large impact on 
the CAPEX of a project. This is in addition to the economies of scale that benefit larger projects 
as their cost of a turbine is generally lower than smaller projects. For the CAPEX of solar projects, 
similar costs make up the CAPEX (but with solar modules instead of turbines). For the purposes 
of this project the CAPEX is assumed to be the total of all the costs to get the project to the first 
year of operation [14]. For the DCF, the CAPEX is assumed to be paid in the valuation year using 
the end of year convention.

3.1.2. OPEX 
The OPEX is the annual operational costs once the project is online. The OPEX includes the land 
lease costs (if land is not owned, in which case the land maintenance costs are included), 
maintenance costs, insurance, security and administration costs, and any other annual costs. For 
solar power the OPEX is generally quite low due to low amounts of required maintenance. The 
OPEX for PV projects is therefore generally given in as an 100% fixed-OPEX (€/MW/year). The 
OPEX for wind projects also contains a variable-OPEX, which is in terms of annual production 
due to the higher amount of maintenance required when more electricity is produced (due to the 
mechanical strain on the turbine). The OPEX for wind projects is then split into a fixed-OPEX, 
which are the fixed annual costs, such as land leases, administrative costs, etc. While the 
maintenance is often split out into variable-OPEX as it is largely effected by the amount of energy 
produced [14] [15]. The DEA and IRENA sources have comparable definitions of OPEX and thus 
are used in their respective archetypes. One thing to note is that the German wind OPEX is 
notoriously high due to their above average land lease costs [14].  
3.1.3. Capacity Factor 
The capacity factor and full-load hours are integral in determining the production and therefore 
the revenues of a given project. The capacity factor depends on many different factors including 
the turbine design, turbine placement with regards to other turbines, and the amount of wind at the 
project location. As modeling capacity factors is outside of the scope of this project, the capacity 
factors for each technology is estimated within each respective country. The DEA catalogue gives 
projected capacity factors and full-load hours for each of the three eligible RES technologies in 
the Danish auction. The IRENA data gives the estimated capacity factors for the German wind 
case. The German solar estimate is not explicitly given but the European capacity factor is provided 
in the IRENA costs database in one of the summary charts for solar PV projects. The P-50 capacity 
factor is given for the European case and adjusted to be slightly lower than the European average

German solar PV case [17].

3.1.4. Degradation Rate 
For PV projects, there is an additional technical factor that effects the annual production. Over the 
lifetime of the project, there is a small amount of wear on the PV module that degrades it 
production over the lifetime of a project. A solution for accounting for the decrease in production 
is to use an additional factor called a degradation rate. This is an annual production decrease for 
the project. This rate often also reflects the risk of failure of the project. Throughout the lifetime 
of a PV project there are different rates of failure, with the initial rate of failure being high and 
quickly dropping off once the project is operational. The failure rate generally is then low until the 
project approaches the end of its lifetime. There are many potential causes of failure such as 
soldering failure, cell cracks, junction box failure, weather damage, etc. It is very difficult to 
account for the myriad of potential failures and the small amount of decrease in production over 
the lifetime of the project. Estimating the degradation factor is outside of the scope of this project, 
but the estimates for annual degradation rate of PV projects, generally fall within 0.3-0.5% [15]. 
The effects of these values are not considered in the initial analysis due to their complexity but are 
considered in 6.5.

3.2. Auction Data 
The two case studies in question are multi-technology auctions that occurred in 2018 in Denmark 
and Germany. Even though Germany and Denmark are interconnected and share a boarder, their 
auction designs were quite different. This section gives a brief overview of the two auction designs 
and their outcomes.  
3.2.1. Danish Data 
The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) completed a multi-technology auction in November of 2018. 
Though there was also a 2019 auction performed by the DEA, at the time of this project, much of 
the data on the specifics of this 2019 auction was not yet available. For this reason, the 2018 auction 
is used instead of the 2019 auction for the Danish case study. The auction was open to PV, onshore 
and offshore wind projects. The auction was restricted by a budget of €34,000,000, a ceiling price 
of 13 ore/kWh, awarded based on the price, and awarded on a pay-as-bid strategy. Though open 
to offshore-wind projects, none of them participated in the auction. The auction involved a 
completion bond that was calculated using a different equation for PV, onshore wind, and offshore 
wind. The equation that was used to set the completion bonds was FLH*170 DKK/MWh, which 
gave a completion bond of 196.35 DKK/kW for solar, 578 DKK/kW for onshore wind, and 756.50 
DKK/kW for offshore wind [13]. The large differences in completion bonds are due to the 
differences in capacity factors and the fact that it takes a lot higher capacity of PV to produce the 
same amount of electricity as from wind due to PV’s lower capacity factor.  
The auction had material pre-qualifications that were strict for offshore wind but lax for onshore 
wind and PV projects. This auction did not have quotas, malus, or limits on size. The auction 
design was quite similar for all three technologies except for the completion bond prices (though 
those seem to be different to make things fairer) and offshore wind having stricter material 
requirements. The auction awarded both onshore wind and PV projects. Whether this is the 
economically efficient thing to do is something that will be investigated for this specific auction. 
3.2.2. German Data 
The German auction was completed in April of 2018 by Bundesnetzagentur. It was open to PV 
and onshore wind projects. The auction awarded 200,000 kW of capacity in this auction. The pre-
qualifications and auction restrictions varied significantly between the two technologies. Bid for 
both technologies needed to be at least 750 kW but PV projects were limited to 20,000 kW, while 
wind projects were not limited. The bid bond for PV was €5/kW but €30/kW for wind. There was 
a €45/kW completion bond for PV but none for wind. The material prequalification for PV was 
low, while it was high for wind. The auction awarded a one-sided sliding FIP with a ceiling price 
of 8.84 ct/kWh and the awards were given independent of other bids. The was a maximum quota 
on wind capacity in Netzausbaugebiet as well as an undisclosed region- and technology- specific 
malus [13]. This malus impacts the ranking, and therefore which bids are awarded, but it does not 
affect the awarded prices. 
There was a significant amount of participation from both solar PV projects (236010 kW submitted 
of qualified bids) and wind projects (129250 kW submitted of qualified bids). However, there were 
no awarded wind projects despite the lowest wind bid being lower than the highest awarded PV 
bid. This is something that is not intuitive, and will be analyzed using the marginal cost to society.

4. Model 
In order to analyze the data, a DCF model with several components is used, the net present value 
(NPV) calculation, the determination of the required support level, and the calculation of the 
LCOS. The DCF was performed in Excel using different functions and the aforementioned inputs 
and assumptions. 
4.1. Net Present Value Calculation 
The NPV is necessary to calculate in DCF models. The “NPV” function in Excel is an easy tool to 
calculate the NPV and was used to perform the analyses in this project. In order to use this Excel 
function, the annual sum of the cash flows (revenue, OPEX, CAPEX, and Payable Tax) and the 
discount rate (WACC) is required. This function defaults to end of year convention, which is used 
throughout this project and discounts to the year before the first entered value in the range of cash 
flows. For the cases in this project, the investment date is assumed to be the same year as the 
auction and paid in a lump, up front, sum. This means that when using the NPV function the 
investment is simply added on to the results of the NPV function that was found using all of the 
cashflows starting the year after the auction, through the lifetime of the project. 
4.2. Required FIPs Using Goal Seek 
Once the NPV was calculated for each of the cases, a section to the revenues for support payments 
was included. The way this section works, depends on the support type, but the basic idea is that 
it is a fixed price that is either added to the average market price (fixed FIP) or replaced the average 
market price if the support level is higher (one-sided sliding FIP). In order to model the behavior 
of the fixed FIP, a line that had a fixed price in it for the duration of the support time as dictated 
by the auction was used. The prices all reference a single cell, which can be changed to whatever 
FIP is desired.  
For the one-sided sliding FIP, the project developer is guaranteed at least this amount. It is assumed 
that in order to be safe, calculating the support required on an annual basis was a fair assumption 
since the project developers want to ensure they are covering their costs. Then a line that compared 
the average market value to a referenced cell that holds the FIP value, which can be changed using 
goal seek was created. If the average market value is greater than the FIP, this is the received rate 
and if not then the FIP is the received rate. 
In the case of either type of FIP there is a cell that can be changed using the goal seek function. 
The goal seek uses an iterative process to set one cell equal to a specified number by changing 
another cell. For calculating the required support level, the NPV is set equal to zero using goal 
seek by changing the cell containing the FIP value, which is referenced by any uses of the FIP.  
4.3.    Calculating the LCOS 
Once the FIP is determined, the LCOS needed to be calculated. The theory behind this value is 
given in 2.3. The practical way of implementing this was by summing up the support payments 
that are maid, either the fixed-FIP*the production, or if the sliding FIP is higher than the average 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
22 
 
market value, the sliding-FIP*the production. This is summed annually and then the NPV of this 
is calculated back to the auction year and then divided by the NPV of the production over the 
lifetime of the project (note that this is not a physical embodiment of anything, but a mathematical 
manipulation of levelizing the costs). This is then used to compare the LCOS of each archetype to 
one another in order to determine the cost to society to create one unit of electricity using a clean, 
renewable energy source.

5.3. German Break-Even Point 
The initial results indicated that the FIP is not always proportional to the LCOS and indicates that 
there is likely a point at which the LCOS of one technology is lower than another even when the 
FIP is higher than the other. In the German auction, the onshore wind case submitted a lower bid

the solar case the LCOS was 0.2273 cent/kWh but the onshore wind case was much higher at

society was the goal. However, this likely occurred due to the technology- and region-specific 
malus of the auction and not because the auctioneers performed an economic analysis of this type. 
In order to determine how low the bid for an onshore wind case would need to be in order for the 
LCOS to be below that of the solar case, it was useful to calculate the breakeven point when the 
LCOS for the wind case is equal to that of the solar case with the LCOS = 0.2273. Using the goal 
seek function to adjust the FIP until the LCOS = 0.2273. The resulting FIP was 4.86 cent/kWh. 
This is significantly lower than in the wind case but is still within the range of FIPs that were 
awarded to solar projects in the German Auction.  
This break-even point is highly dependent of technical lifetime and market value, which are the 
two factors that German solar has a big advantage over German wind projects. Though the

projected cost to the auctioneer is potentially a better way of determining the awarded projects if 
the price forecasting advanced. This may be something more difficult to do at present since the 
market penetrations being achieved now compared to the total energy mix are unprecedented. 
However, if reliable forecasts become available, it will be in the favor of the auctioneers to use 
LCOS or a similar metric to determine the best projects to award and to avoid the bias of

The support level or bid amount in terms of an FIP is a value that can greatly affect the LCOS.

That being said in cases, such as the Danish onshore wind cases, where it seems that there may not

Since the cost to the auctioneer is not always evident from the bids, especially if the support type

6. Sensitivity Analysis 
The nature of this project requires many assumptions about the financial data that is input into the 
DCF and then ultimately effects the analysis of which projects should have been awarded. There 
were many factors that are considered in the DCF with varying degrees of uncertainty. The 
CAPEX, OPEX, capacity factor, lifetime, WACC, project size, and the average market value are 
all factors of uncertainty. In order to analyze the amount that changes in each of these factors 
effects the cost to society, a sensitivity analysis is performed. There are several schools of thought 
when performing these, but first the plausible ranges for each of the cases that the inputs would 
vary over were determined. Then individually performed a sensitivity analysis on each of the input 
values in question by varying their values over the predetermined range. 
Though there are many factors that could be analyzed in detail in a sensitivity analysis, it was 
important to remember to focus of this project when determining the factors to include. This project 
focuses on the levelized cost to society in the form of support and other aid payments. In both the 
German and Danish case, balancing and grid connection costs are part of the project costs so the 
cost to society is simply for the aid payments. So, for the sensitivity analysis, the required support 
can be varied as a factor itself, as the levelized cost to society in both cases is made up of aid 
payments. The lifetime of the projects is also an important factor since the support payments are 
being levelized over the entire lifetime and not just over the support period. However, due to the 
nature of levelizing costs and the time value of money, the costs that are closer to the beginning of 
the project should have a stronger effect on the levelized costs than the ones later in the project 
and therefore the lifetime could hit a point when it becomes more insignificant that other factors. 
The societal discount rate is a value that should be analyzed through a sensitivity analysis big 
assumptions had to be made on what this would be. Finally, the average market value of each 
technology should be analyzed. This can not only greatly affect the amount of required support, 
but also the cost of aid payments (especially in sliding FIP cases). These will be the focus of the 
sensitivity analysis, though some other sensitivity analyses were performed on the archetypes 
themselves in order to try to determine some of the weird results when compared to the auction 
outcomes, especially in the Danish case. 
6.1. Lifetime 
The lifetime assumptions of the projects varied depending on the technology of the proposed 
project. In order to determine the influence of changing the lifetime of each of the eligible 
technology types in each of the countries, the LCOS is used as the indicator for each of the cases.

in 2018. Based on the information available in the DEA technology catalog, in 2020, the 
anticipated lifetime is 27 years but may be between 25 (the technical lifetime of turbines in 2015) 
and 35 years. This is a 10-year range, with the initial assumption being on the low end of this 
range. However, in order to determine the effects of a different technical lifetime on the onshore 
wind cases, it is varied between 25 and 35 years for the onshore wind cases [15]. For the Danish 
case, the bid is assumed to be .305 cent/kWh as this is the simple mean of the range of awarded 
onshore wind projects in the Danish auction. Though this is different than the approximated

The offshore wind case in Denmark also is analyzed over the 25-35 range, but in this case the

6.2. Discount Rate 
The discount rate for this case is the societal discount rate that is used to calculate the LCOS. This 
discount rate was assumed to be the same for all the cases within a specific country. For the cases 
in which a societal cost of capital was needed, as mentioned in 3.1.5, there is not a clear strategy 
for determining this value and as stated in [21], there is research that is needed in order to determine 
how to calculate the social cost of capital as this is a newer concept that aims to reflect the costs 
to society, which should include costs of carbon emissions. Since there are currently no methods 
or estimates for determining the societal discount rate, a sensitivity analysis by varying the 
discount rate by ±20% if conducted since the rates initially chosen were based off of the WACC 
and there is a chance that the societal cost of capital will be higher or lower than the standard 
WACC. 
When this sensitivity is performed the same values for the support level are used as in the 
sensitivity analysis on the technical lifetime. The relative change in the LCOS were calculated by 
taking the difference between the base case and the case with the changed discount rate and then 
divided this value by the base-case. This gives a percent change. Figure 10 shows that the German 
cases were generally more affected by the change in discount rate. This is likely due to their initial 
discount rate being lower and so any changes in the discount rate made a larger impact on the 
required rate of support. The changes in the discount rate had a disproportionate effect on the 
LCOS of both solar PV projects. These projects have longer lifetimes than the wind projects do. 
This is supported as the cause of the difference because the percent change for the onshore wind 
and offshore wind cases is almost identical even though their cases are different in the makeup of 
their LCOE. Additionally, the wind cases were tested when their lifetimes were extended the 
effects of the change in discount rates were more pronounced. 
These two factors indicate that the discount rate used in the LCOS calculation has a larger effect 
on the projects with a longer lifetime. However, it is not reasonable to assume all proposed projects 
have the same lifetime in a multi-technology auction because at current times solar PV projects

6.3. CAPEX 
For the Danish case the CAPEX assumption came from the DEA technology catalog. This catalog 
gives CAPEX values for 2015 and 2020 and, as mentioned in 3.1.1, linear interpolation was used

CAPEX prediction. The percent change was used from this CAPEX prediction to determine the 
amount to vary the CAPEX for the sensitivity analyses for each of the technologies. For the solar 
PV cases this range is from 92%-117% of the original CAPEX estimation, for the offshore wind 
the range is 89%-105% of the original CAPEX estimation, and the onshore wind ranges from 69%-

many turbine types, locations, and other factors that could explain the range of CAPEX being 
larger. The DEA explained that it confirmed the estimated cost decreases with several project 
developers and that there are some additional costs that are starting to increase as the amount of 
available land to build wind projects on decreases including the cost to decommission wind 
projects and payments to neighborhood compensations due to property value decreases [15]. Since 
these ranges are based off IRENA data and this is the original organization used for the German 
data, the same ranges were used for the CAPEX of the German data as well. 
The effect of varying the CAPEX depends on the technology and location that is being analyzed. 
For the PV cases, the CAPEX dominates the LCOE and therefore the required support. So, when

6.4. OPEX 
In order to determine how much the OPEX effects the different cases relative to their initial support 
estimates, a similar procedure to the CAPEX is repeated. The same set of coefficients, 0.95, 0.97, 
0.99, 1.00, 1.01, 1.03, and 1.05, is used to see how large of an impact changing the OPEX has on 
the required support payments for each case. The results of this analysis are shown in Figure 12. 
The onshore wind case in Denmark is not plotted as all the results were equal to zero as in the 
CAPEX analysis. The other four cases behave similarly. The German PV case is not as affected

by changes in the OPEX as it was with the CAPEX. This is to be expected with solar PV projects 
since they are generally low maintenance [15]. Since the solar case requires such a low level of 
support, it makes sense that the factor of change in the LCOS is still relatively large compared to 
the other cases. 
Across all of the cases in this and the CAPEX analysis, there was still a significant impact on the 
LCOS, which implies that there is a distinct possibility that some of the differences between the

6.5. PV Degradation 
In order to determine the effect that PV degradation, as described in 3.1.4, would have on the 
projects in question, a series of sensitivity analyses will be performed. Existing studies vary in 
what is included in these calculations, where some only include the decrease in amount of 
production and others attempt to levelized the risk of early component failure. The studies tend to 
result in a value between 0.15% and 0.5% [15] [9]. Therefore, the degradation rate is varied 
between 0.15% and 0.5% for this sensitivity analysis. This is performed for both the German and 
Danish PV cases, where all other assumptions are held to their baseline assumptions. 
The results are shown in Figure 13. The degradation rate increases the LCOS since more support 
is required and the projects produce less over their lifetime. The slopes are similar in both cases, 
but the intercept points are very far apart as shown in the equations representing the best-fit lines.

6.6. Market Value Factor 
The market value is one of the factors that is hard to estimate, especially as the electricity mix in 
a country changes and the self-cannibalization effect becomes stronger. The market value factor 
can be used to change the market value for a project and to see how it will affect the required level 
of support. For the Danish PV case, the DEA projections from [23] for MVF are much lower than 
the current case, where the MVF is currently 108% according to the DEA’s Energy outlook from 
2018 [6]. The DEA projections give a much lower MVF for the PV case. By 2030 the DEA 
projections have the MVF for solar energy at the same level as wind energy, which is 85%. The 
MVF for wind is already at 87%, so according to the DEA projections, it is essentially constant. 
Wind is a much more mature market in Denmark so there is more to base the MVF predictions on. 
The solar case on the other hand is very immature in Denmark and therefore there is not a lot to 
base the predictions on. 
Since the Danish archetype for onshore wind is giving that it does not require support in any of the 
sensitivity studies, the threshold MVF that makes it so that it would require support is calculated, 
assuming to be constant. The goal-seek function is used in order to find this threshold to be an 
MVF of 0.7306. This is much lower than the current Danish MVF, but is close to the wind MVF 
in Germany, which is .76 [6].  
For the Danish solar case, it does not make sense to calculate this type of threshold analysis since 
the solar cases in Denmark require support. Instead, the value will be varied between the baseline 
case where the MVF quickly becomes equal to that of Danish wind and the current scenario of 
108%. This is reasonable since there is only 2% wind in Denmark and so predicting how its MVF 
will behave in the market is very unclear. When the MVF is set to 1.08 in the Danish PV case, the 
LCOS is only 0.3764 cent/kWh, which a large decrease from the initial assumption. This shows 
that the forecasting is important in determining the cost to society of a specific project.

7. Discussion 
The results from the initial analysis and the sensitivity studies give some insight into the biases 
that occur in existing multi-technology auctions and give some insight into how these could be 
prevented. Additionally, this analysis gives an insight into the challenges faced by the bidders to 
win a bid without failing to complete their projects. 
7.1. LCOS 
The main quantitative indicator of bias throughout this project is the LCOS. It is a better way to 
determine the cost to society in the form of support payments for one unit of avoided carbon 
emissions. The LCOS represents the economic benefit to avoiding these emissions since aid 
payments are theoretically equal to the benefits of avoided emissions in the form of avoided costs 
for compensating for these emissions [11].  
7.1.1. Benefits and Uses 
The LCOS or a similar metric that considers the cost to the auctioneer to subsidize one unit of 
electricity is a better metric for the auctioneer to determine who to award. This is especially true 
in the case of sliding FIPs, which do not expose the bidder to the market value of the electricity 
they produce so the auctioneer must make up the difference between the agreed upon price and the 
market value. Since the market values from one technology to another are not the same, due to the 
self-cannibalization effect, in order to be as impartial as possible and to incur the lowest cost 
possible onto society, using the more traditional methods to determine which bids to award (lowest 
bid, lowest LCOE) can lead to projects with a higher LCOS getting awarded over others. It is clear 
how this could occur after analyzing the German case seeing how big the difference is between 
the LCOS for wind and solar for the same support level.  
For this reason, it makes sense for the auctioneer to forecast the market value for each eligible 
technology in their auction and determine the approximate LCOS they would have to pay based 
on this information and some additional information provided to the auctioneer by the bidder. If 
the project lifetime and capacity factor were provided by the bidder in the prequalification of the 
auction, then the LCOS would be easier to calculate. If the auctioneer cannot get this information 
from the bidders in the prequalification, then they could also create an estimate of their own in 
order to calculate the LCOS.  
The LCOS is also useful in comparing projects of different lifetimes, sizes, etc. By levelizing the 
costs, they can be more easily compared to one another and in turn lead to a less-biased decision 
when awarding bids. 
7.1.2. Future Improvements and Research 
In the future it would be useful if, when designing an auction, the auctioneer created an equation 
or relationship between the LCOS and a series of inputs such as the one comparing LCOS to the 
FIP in 5.4. This concept could be expanded to create a multi-variable equation for a specific auction 
that the auctioneer can use to determine their approximate LCOS, resulting in them awarding the 
projects that cost the least.  
==PAGE:45==
 
 
38 
 
For the auctioneers to do an LCOS, or similar calculation, that will help them to determine the 
appropriate bids to award, they need to have good data and assumptions. By studying the behavior 
of the MVF as renewable energy penetration increases, the market value can be more accurately 
predicted. 
As the total share of renewable energy sources in a specific country increases, how does this effect 
the MVF of the energy sources in the system? How does this effect the system-wide market value 
since renewable energy sources tend to bid zero on the day ahead market? Will the market values 
of solar and wind start to decrease over time if their penetration becomes too high? These are 
questions that should be considered in future research projects in order to improve the forecasting, 
which is vitally important to any DCF model.  
Additionally, as with any new idea, the LCOS concept should be thoroughly investigated in order 
to determine if it contains biases that would lead to unfair results if used in an auction. One case 
where this might be the case is when there are two technologies that have drastically different 
maturity in a market. If one of the technologies is mature and has especially low costs, while the 
other is still being integrated into the market, by strictly using the LCOS to award projects, there 
might not be a chance for the more immature technology to ever get established in that market. 
The effects of these types of factors should be investigated in an auction where more information 
about both the awarded and unawarded projects is available. 
The other big improvement to the LCOS if it were to be used for decision making in an actual 
auction, is that the discount rate for the society should be established as this is an area of research 
that is still lacking. The discount rate has a significant impact on the LCOS calculation, as 
discussed in 6.2. This especially effects the projects that have a longer lifetime, which would 
benefit more from a lower discount rate since the equation for the LCOS puts more weight on the 
benefits earlier in a project’s lifetime as it accounts for the time-value of money. 
7.1.3. Limitations  
There are some limitations to the LCOS. The main limitation to the LCOS is that it relies on 
forecasting. The LCOS is only as good as the forecasts and assumptions it is calculated with. This 
means that especially in a fixed-FIP case, such as the Danish one, if bad forecasts and assumptions 
are made when using the LCOS to make a decision, that the wrong decision could easily be made 
since the fixed-FIP cases have a smaller deviation between the bid and the LCOS, which was 
shown in 5.4. 
Additionally, the LCOS only gives a quantitative way to analyze and avoid biases in the part of 
the auction after the bidding occurs. The bidders may have to adjust their bidding strategy in order 
to account for other factors in the bid design that are biased such as the bid bonds, completion 
bonds, etc. If a bidder of a project type or location is disproportionately impacted by these other 
factors, then they will be forced to bid higher or risk losing money on the project. Since the bidder 
is likely to bid higher on a project if there is a biased factor in the auction design, the LCOS will 
be impacted by the increased bid, but the LCOS does not help to diagnose the increase in bid or 
the bias that occurred. 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
39 
 
7.2. Bias in Technology-Neutral Auctions 
The biases that occur in a multi-technology auction are often difficult to quantify. The LCOS is 
useful in quantifying bias, but as discussed in 7.1.3, the LCOS is only a tool and it is not able to 
diagnose the cause of bias in an auction on its own. There are some actions that this project showed 
can be taken in order to reduce bias and some causes of bias that still need further research. 
7.2.1. FIP-Type Bias 
The primary form of bias that was seen throughout this analysis was the bias that occurred when 
shielding the bidder from the market through different types of sliding FIP. When a fixed-FIP is 
used, it is less likely that a project with a larger cost to the auctioneer would be awarded since the 
FIP and LCOS are linearly related and, generally, the small differences in the LCOS for a specific 
FIP for projects of different types, is caused by the discount rate, the amount of annual generation, 
and the lifetime of the project. This means that if an auctioneer does not want to go through the 
process of forecasting and finding a relationship between the bid and the LCOS for their particular 
area and the eligible technologies, that using a fixed-FIP is a method that, though not unbiased, 
reduces the amount of bias of an auction. This is consistent with the findings of the German case 
study that analyzes economic inefficiencies in auctions [12]. This study uses a different metric 
than the LCOS, but came to a similar conclusion that, by shielding the bidder from the market, the 
more economic inefficiency (bias) occurs.  
7.2.2. Lifetime-Bias 
One of the other biases that occur when awarding projects based on their bid-values, is that the 
support payments only last for a portion of a project’s technical lifetime. This means that the bidder 
will only receive the market value for their electricity after the support ends. However, the benefit 
from avoiding more carbon emissions does not end when the support ends. The LCOS is a tool 
that attempts to solve this problem. As discussed in 6.1, as the lifetime of a project increases the 
LCOS decreases. This means that, though hard to generalize this bias to all cases since it is 
dependent on the annual production and discount rate, there is a bias towards shorter projects when 
discounting the production after the support ends. The exact magnitude of this bias must be 
calculated for a specific auction and is also dependent on the quality of the forecasting. 
Theoretically, if there is data available for past auctions and bidders, this calculation could be done 
in arrears in order to analyze the relationship, though few multi-technology auctions have been 
held and none of them have projects that have reached the end of their technical lifetime so this is 
not a feasible technique at this time. 
7.2.3. Dissimilarity-Bias 
One thing that became apparent throughout this project is that it is hard to prevent bias in an auction 
when the projects are too dissimilar in that region. For example, in the Danish case the solar 
penetration is very low, and it is new in the market, while the wind is long established in the Danish 
grid. The results even showed that the wind projects should not need support payments, though 
this could be an issue with the assumptions and data. Conversely, they indicated that Danish PV 
projects should not be able to succeed due to their high costs and low production. Yet, the Danish 
auction awarded 3 solar and 3 wind projects. Though the archetype results were unexpected, this 
enlightens an issue with technology-neutral auctions and their attempts to be biased. In some cases, 
==PAGE:47==
 
 
40 
 
when the technologies in a multi-technology auction differ too much, the auction simply won’t 
award one since the other dominates. This is what occurred in the German auction when only solar 
projects were awarded.  
In the Danish case, it is obvious that there is a lack of accurate data for the PV cases, which makes 
some sense since Denmark has such a low solar penetration. However, if the wind data is more 
accurate, this would imply that the Danish auctions are awarding FIPs when they should not require 
support. If this is the case, then there is bias in the way the auction prequalifies its bidders. The 
auctioneer should attempt to ensure not only that the bidder will be able to complete their 
obligation, but that there is an actual need for them to get support and that they are not just doing 
it in order to receive additional revenue streams. 
7.2.4. Other Biases 
There are some other factors that cause bias in a multi-technology auction. However, they are too 
difficult to quantify in the scope of this project and should be the subject of more studies.  
One of these biases is the completion-bond bias. There are some methods that attempt to quantify 
the cost to a bidder for a completion bond and translate these factors into an equation. This is still 
an immature area of study and therefore it is hard to fully understand the effects of including a 
completion bond in an auction. One equation that can be added to the archetypes in the project in

The bid bond is another cause of bias since it disproportionately affects smaller producers as it is 
a cost that generally occurs before the financing is done. There is little quantitative research on 
this subject and only a few qualitative analyses. It is possible that the bid bond affects the bid in a 
similar way that sunk costs do, which are often disregarded in economic analysis of a project but 
are considered by accountants for these types of projects. The sunk costs generally seem to have a 
small impact on the project [24], but should not be disregarded, especially since having a bid bond 
effects smaller producers more and the relationship between size and bid bond should be

7.2.5. Bias in Danish vs. German Case 
Based on the auction results the two auctions, the German case is more likely to have bias, due to 
it using a sliding-FIP. Additionally, it uses a completion-bond that is based on the capacity, which 
disproportionately affects the PV projects since they have a lower capacity factor. The German 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
41 
 
auction also uses a bid bond, which can keep smaller developers from being able to access the 
auction. The German malus for certain technologies might attempt to compensate for some of these 
factors, but since they are not explicit in how they work, it seems that they are still prone to these 
biases. 
The Danish case has eliminated much of its risk for bias by eliminating a bid bond, making the 
completion bond based on the production instead of capacity, and by using a fixed-FIP. However, 
it is unclear how they determine if the bidder actually needs any support at all and there is a chance 
they are supporting projects that don’t need it. 
7.3. Model Analysis 
The DCF model created for this project had its strengths and weaknesses when attempting to 
analyze such cases as the auctions in Germany and in Denmark, however there are also some areas 
that the model in this project can be improved. 
7.3.1. Strengths 
By creating a DCF model in Excel, the model became easy and quick to use. The model included 
CAPEX, OPEX, tax, depreciations, and revenues (both support and market revenues). The 
archetypes and model were used to evaluate bias and the DCF allowed the model to easily be 
considered from the auctioneer’s perspective. The model utilized the what-if analysis a lot in Excel 
and was able to perform basic optimizations. 
7.3.2. Weaknesses 
The DCF model in Excel utilized the what-if analysis in Excel to the point when it might make 
sense, if a more in-depth analysis is needed, then using a program such a GAMS might be useful 
in optimizing the required support while varying multiple inputs (goal-seek is limited to one 
variable). This is something that makes the model less accessible as GAMS can take a very long 
time to run. Another option would be to feed some of the data into a Python, MATLAB, or other 
program to perform certain analyses, such as a regression to find a function that determines the 
LCOS based on multiple variables instead of just one. 
This next step would give a lot of information, but it might not be right. The biggest weakness of 
the DCF model is most probably the reliance on so many forecasts. A model can only be as good 
as its forecasts and assumptions and, though it is tempting to want to continue adding complexity 
to this DCF model, this data might not be vary useful due to the lack of research surrounding some 
of the variables, such as economic influence of bonds and MVF behavior with changing 
penetration. The assumptions that can be made based on the best available data, do not seem to be 
very accurate. Continuing to try to fit a model on data that is lacking is not as useful as working 
on analyzing these relationships, though each could be its own project. 
A specific example of questionable data and results in this project is the Danish case study. The 
DEA provides ample data that can be used but the results to not match the auction results. As 
mentioned earlier, if the data is not very accurate, making the model more specific and detailed 
will not add value to the model.

7.4. Thesis Scope and Reflection 
Overall, the scope of this thesis was reasonable. However, due to the large number of potential 
factors that could be considered, the scope had to be limited to the LCOS and a basic archetype 
model. The archetype model did not include much financing data, which could negatively impact 
the archetypes of riskier projects due to an increase cost of debt, or lower debt-to-equity ratio. The 
project also excluded any type of forecasting, which meant that the project relied on existing data, 
which in many of the cases, such as the impaction of different auction bonds, on the required 
support. This is something that would require its own project, so it is still the correct scope, though 
it did not allow quantification of these factors on the bias in any way other than attempting to 
adjust the WACC based on the risk.

8. Conclusion 
When an auctioneer designs an auction with the goal of creating a “technology-neutral” auction, 
they are engaging in an act of futility. A technology-neutral auction is an impossible feat. However, 
there are some auction designs that lead to more bias that others.  
Some of these factors include: exposing the projects to the market through a support payment such 
as a fixed-FIP, awarding projects based on a levelized cost to the auctioneer (LCOS) instead of 
simply based on the bid price, eliminating or modifying bonds in order to allow small producers 
to participate and to prevent solar producers from having too large of a burden.  
Another key takeaway is that the auctions eligible for an auction need to be competitive with one 
another, otherwise, it is pointless to host a multi-technology auction because it incurs higher costs 
on the auctioneer due to the more complicated auction design and in the end will only award one 
project type.  
In the future for the LCOS and bias to be analyzed further, more data needs to be collected and 
more predictions need to be made in order to get results that are consistent with the auction results. 
The LCOS could be a useful tool in quantifying the effects of different auction designs on their 
biases.
