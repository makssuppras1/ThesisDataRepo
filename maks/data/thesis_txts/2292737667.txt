Summary (English)
This thesis explores the possibilities of using eye tracking data to estimate user
engagement. Based on existing literature diﬀerent eye tracking metrics are se-
lected to evaluate levels of emotional and mental states. Furthermore, based
on the "eye-mind" hypothesis, the inﬂuence of neuroaesthetics and saliency of
visual attention is examined. This thesis is based on ﬁve diﬀerent hypotheses,
each of which has been sought validated through ﬁve diﬀerent eye tracking ex-
periments. These examine diﬀerent eye tracking metrics and their relation to
emotional and mental states and to visual attention. The eye tracking metrics
that have been used in this thesis are scan paths, ﬁxation frequency, ﬁxation
length, blink rate, blink length and pupil size. The ﬁrst two experiments examine
the general degrees of attention and emotional attraction in relation to the four
primary colors and their contrast stimuli. The results indicate that green/red
contrast attracts great attention and that yellow and blue color spectres elicit a
higher degree of emotional engagement. The third experiment further explores
the inﬂuence of the four contrast colour combinations on emotional engagement.
Here, the results primarily indicate an overall declining commitment relative to
time-on-task. Furthermore, the results of the third iteration reveal considerable
inter-individual diﬀerences between the visual behaviour of the ﬁve test subjects,
which is linked to individual top-down factors, including aesthetic preferences.
Fourth and ﬁfth iteration seek to compare the assessed levels of engagement
and the subjects’ subjective ratings. The results however indicate no signiﬁcant
correlation. The results in this paper illustrate some general and individual vi-
sual patterns which are considered to possess the potential to indicate diﬀerent
emotional states if combined with additional parameters such as biometrics and
demographic metrics.

Preface
This thesis was prepared in the period from August 2014 to January 2015 at the
department of Informatics and Mathematical Modelling at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark under guidance of Michael Kai Petersen and Per Bækgaard.
This project accounts for a total of 30 ECTS points.
The thesis deals with the analysis of eye tracking data with the main objective
of investigating how eye tracking data can be used to estimate user engagement.
The thesis consists of the following chapters:
Chapter 1: Introduction to the context and motivation of the study.
Chapter 2: Research objectives.
Chapter 3: Related studies and research ﬁelds.
Chapter 4: Presentation of the tools which have been used.
Chapters 5-9: The ﬁve iterations with details about the ﬁve experiments and
presentation of the ﬁndings.
Chapters 10: Conclusions of the ﬁve iterations.
Chapters 11: Discussion of possible points of improvement and recommenda-
tions of further work.
To aid the readability of this thesis a list with brief explanations of eye tracking
speciﬁc terms is provided in Appendix A.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The technical revolution has resulted in a notable shift in consumer behaviour.
Many day-to-day products and activities are no longer restricted to the physical
world but have found their place on the various digital platforms.
2 billion
people worldwide are connected to the internet [25] and many of them are living
a great part of their lives online leaving behind them a very valuable trail of
information.
With each click, type, tap and scroll we generate an extensive
amount of information which can be linked to our interests and preferences.
As a result of this development many companies are constantly seeking new
and innovative ways to help them generate data about their customers and the
market in which they navigate.
1.1
You are more than what you tweet
Market assessments have moved beyond the reactive model where companies
gathered information only when they received it from their customers to a proac-
tive model where companies continuously scrape the internet for data generated
by their customer base. This proactive approach provides the opportunity to
conduct a sentiment analysis and thereby gain some level of insight into customer
needs. This Big Data phenomenon has been known to generate great value in
diﬀerent business applications but it still holds much unexploited potential.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
Data gathered from social media platforms and other types of internet data
are not always of high value as they can be heavily biased and thus can paint
an inaccurate picture of user behaviour. In general it applies that the level of
insight that can be gained from Big Data highly depends on the types of raw
data collected, the methods of data collection/ﬁltering/classiﬁcation applied and
the data analysis methods used [25].
1.2
Eye tracking opportunities
Passive eye tracking has been heavily used in research to provide raw sensory
data to help evaluate the user experience of products, interfaces, web pages, etc.
It is a technology that helps us to better understand human behaviour based
on information about our visual patterns. Bojko explains: "(...) since there
seems to be a strong (though not absolute) connection between what people are
looking at and what they’re paying attention to, eye tracking can provide another
set of useful clues about what they’re thinking (...)" [10, p. xii]. The eye-mind
hypothesis (originally formulated by Just and Carpenter, 1980) links our visual
focus to our attention, which essentially determines what we process mentally.
Thus, our visual behaviour holds information about our emotional and cognitive
state which can be used to generate great value.
Meanwhile, the recent emergence of low cost commercial eye trackers (e.g. the
Eye Tribe [1]) has created new opportunities for the use of eye tracking in an
active manner to conduct eye control of interfaces based on the point of gaze.
By combining passive and active eye tracking we would be able to continu-
ously record eye tracking data of users interacting with e.g.
games and use
it to dynamically modify adaptive interfaces in relation to assessed emotional
engagement and visual attention. We could even imagine online e-learning and
training applications being able to adapt the diﬃculty levels to the individual
student based on the registered cognitive load. The possibilities are numerous,
but it requires that we investigate how eye tracking data can help asses our
emotional and cognitive state.
1.3
LEGO 3D applications
In search of utilising the many digital data opportunities many companies are
moving their product portfolios onto digital platforms. Amongst them we ﬁnd
LEGO. With applications such as "Build with Chrome" and "LEGO 3D Cat-
alogue" LEGO has set the stage for their users to digitally interact with the

Chapter 2
Research Objectives
The main objective of this study is to explore the possibilities of using eye
tracking technologies to estimate the engagement of users interacting with vir-
tual 3D scenes. In order to achieve this objective, within the set time frame,
the study has been conducted with a lean approach [29]. In accordance with
the lean methodology the research process has consisted of a string of iterations
each aiming at validating or invalidating hypotheses with the help of various
eye tracking experiments.
2.1
Research question
The study aims at answering the following research question:
"Can eye tracking data help to estimate user engagement?"
This main research question will be answered with help of the following sub-
questions:

Chapter 3
Related Work
3.1
Visual behaviour and mental state
Several studies have investigated the correlation between our visual behaviour
and emotional engagement. Eye tracking parameters have been used in many
diﬀerent studies to help attain a better understanding of mental workload and
mental states. The following contains a descriptive walk-through of related work
within this ﬁeld.
3.1.1
Pupillary changes and emotional arousal
In a study from 2008 Bradley et al. investigated the correlation between pupil-
lary responses and emotional arousal [13]. Skin conductance, heart rate and
pupil diameter were measured on 27 test subjects viewing 96 pictures portray-
ing either pleasant, unpleasant or neutral content. Results showed that changes
in skin conductance and pupil diameter correlated, with the largest reactions
connected to emotional engaging stimuli of both unpleasant and pleasant con-
tent [13].
==PAGE:24==
8
Related Work
3.1.2
Pupillary changes and cognitive load
In his book "Thinking, Fast and Slow" [23] Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman
describes two diﬀerent ways in which the brain forms thoughts; System 1 which
we do not control, and system 2 which is controlled. System 1 accounts for the
fast thoughts guided by our intuition while system 2 is slow and analytical. The
two systems, naturally, have diﬀerent eﬀects on our mental work load. System 2
requires a higher level of cognitive processing and according to Kahneman it can
be measured based on pupillary changes. In an interview with Spiegel Online
Kahneman explains: "Slow, deliberate thinking is hard work. (...) It’s accom-
panied by physical arousal, increasing heart rate and blood pressure, activated
sweat glands and dilated pupils."[18].
Bojko’s measures of performance provide information about mental workload,
cognitive processing, target ﬁndability and target recognisability. According to
Bojko, the most common measure of mental workload is pupil dilation. However,
as previously mentioned, pupillary changes can also reﬂect levels of emotional
arousal [13]. Bojko explains: "Changes in the pupil size of a person leisurely
looking at pictures will indicate emotional arousal (..) when the same person
is asked to solve a complex mathematical problem, pupil diameter will suddenly
become a workload measure."[10, p. 134].
3.1.3
Scan patterns and emotional arousal
In 2011 Bradley et al. conducted a study where scan patterns were analysed
in relation to emotion, complexity and repetition [12].
The ﬁrst experiment
of the study was set up as a picture viewing test focusing on how emotional
content and complexity inﬂuenced the scan patterns of 24 participants. The
experiment showed that emotionally engaging pictures (both pleasant and un-
pleasant) elicited a larger number of ﬁxations compared to pictures portraying
neutral content. Furthermore the results showed that the emotionally engaging
content prompted signiﬁcantly shorter ﬁxation durations. The analysis of sac-
cade lengths also showed a clear pattern. The scan path proved to be overall
longer for emotional pictures compared to neutral pictures.
3.1.4
Scan patterns and complexity
In the same study [12] the number of ﬁxations, ﬁxation durations and saccade
lengths were also analysed in relation to the complexity of the portrayed pic-

tures. The simple ﬁgure-ground compositions were deﬁned as pictures with a
focal ﬁgure and a constant or uniform background. The complex scenes de-
picted multiple objects and a varied background. Results showed that complex
scenes evoked a larger number of ﬁxations compared to simple ﬁgure-ground
compositions, and with overall shorter ﬁxation durations. The saccade lengths
were, similarly to the emotional pictures, longer for complex scenes compared
to simple ﬁgure-ground compositions.
Mackworth and Morandi conducted a study investigating the eﬀects of details on
the visual attention of adults [28]. The analysis was based on a direct comparison
between subjective ratings of the importance of diﬀerent regions within a picture
and the visual choices revealed by gaze data. Results showed that regions rated
as being very recognizable, which contained unpredictable contours and unusual
details, received high concentrations of ﬁxations. Simple and predictable areas
on the other hand were seldom ﬁxated upon.
3.1.5
Blink and fatigue
Caﬃer et al. studied the link between spontaneous eye blinks and fatigue [15].
The study used infrared technology to continuously record eye lid movement on
60 test participants. From the results of the study Caﬃer et al. showed that eye
blink parameters can be used as an indicator of drowsiness based primarily on
the characteristics of the waveform of spontaneous blinks. Blink duration was
found to be the most informative parameter as increases were clearly associated
with higher levels of drowsiness.
3.1.6
Fixations and fatigue
Cazzoli et al. have conducted a study investigating how visual parameters such
as ﬁxations are inﬂuenced by; fatigue due to chronotypical factors and fatigue
due to time spent on task [16]. Results showed signiﬁcant increases in mean ﬁxa-
tion durations triggered by subjectively more pronounced fatigue and decreases
in saccadic speed relative to time-on-task.

3.2.1
Saliency
Several studies have investigated the factors which control our visual attention.
Some of the factors that inﬂuence our visual attention are: motion, contrast,
size, shape, colour, location, foreground/background, people and context [31].
These factors are often categorised depending on whether they are task inde-
pendent factors (bottom-up) or task and context driven factors (top-down).
Our bottom-up attention is stimulus driven and highly eﬀected by visual saliency.
In a review conducted by Itti and Koch it is explained that: "Saliency is indepen-
dent of the nature of the particular task, operates very rapidly and is primarily
driven in a bottom-up manner, although it can be inﬂuenced by contextual, ﬁg-
ure–ground eﬀects" [22, p.194]. Especially the investigation of colour saliency
has proven relevant in a wide range of diﬀerent studies eg. for improving no-
ticeability of warnings [33], analysing webpage saliency [32] and improving in-
structional materials [11]. The majority of these studies conclude that colours
of red hue have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on noticeability.
Knickerbocker et al. have taken a diﬀerent approach by studying the inﬂuence
of emotional saliency on eye movements and processing [27]. The study was
based on a reading test where participants eye movements were recorded while
reading a series of sentences containing either neutral target words or emotional
target words. The study showed that emotional saliency had a positive eﬀect on
the process time of the test participants. Sentences containing both positive and
negative words were processed faster than sentences containing neutral words.
3.2.2
Neuroaesthetics
The ﬁeld of neuroaesthetics is also relevant as it investigates the experience of
beauty at the level of brain function and mental states. A review conducted by
Chatterjee and Vartanian explores the biological bases of aesthetic encounters
[17]. The discipline of neuroaesthetics merges empirical aesthetics with cogni-
tive and aﬀective neuroscience in search of a deeper understanding of what con-
trols our individual aesthetic preferences. It is stated that aesthetic experiences
emerge from the interactions between a triad of neural systems: sensory-motor,
emotion-valuation and meaning-knowledge circuitry [17].
A related study by Kirk et al., investigating the eﬀects of expectations on neural

either enhance or diminish visual pleasure [26]. In general it can be said that
context and knowledge aﬀect our individual aesthetic experiences which thereby

laureate Eric Kandel explains: "Top-down signals rely on memory and compare

5.1
UX attraction measures
In order to evaluate whether or not it is possible to measure user engagement
based on eye tracking data it is initially important to set the framework for the
study by determining what attracts the human eye. What do we notice when
we look at still or dynamic scenes? What attracts our attention and what visual
properties are important in order to maintain our interest?
5.2
Theory
Bojko has written the book "Eye Tracking the User Experience" [10] as a guid-
ance to those seeking to beneﬁt from eye tracking in order to improve user
experience of both products and interfaces. According to Bojko [10], user expe-
rience research objectives are either attraction- or performance-related, hence
Bojko diﬀerentiates between attraction and performance related measures.
Attraction measures can be used to asses the ability of a speciﬁc area to draw
the attention of the observer.
There are three diﬀerent types of attraction
==PAGE:32==
16
First Iteration
measures: Area noticeability measures, area interest measures and emotional
arousal measures. This ﬁrst iteration focuses on evaluating area noticeability
measures and area interest measures. The pupil will be used as a measure of
emotional arousal in later iterations.
5.2.1
Area noticeability measures
Area noticeability can tell us about the visual prominence of certain areas. How
easy is something to notice? Diﬀerent stimuli can be used to direct our bottom-
up attention to a speciﬁc area of e.g. a product, website or label. Noticeability
can also be aﬀected by top-down attention, thus making objects stand out based
on the observers previous experiences and expectations. This makes it possible
for certain objects, areas or functions to stand out from their surroundings
despite a lack of visual prominence.
According to Bojko[10] the noticeability of a speciﬁc area of interest (AOI) can
be measured in eye tracking studies based on the percentage of test participants
who ﬁxated on the AOI, the number of ﬁxations they conduct prior to ﬁxating
on the AOI and the time they take to ﬁxate on the AOI.
5.2.2
Measures of area interest
In addition to analysing area noticeability it can be helpful to examine the levels
of interest received by diﬀerent AOIs. Some areas will be better at drawing and
holding on to the interest of the observer. The number of ﬁxations and the dwell
time on the AOI can help assess how much interest something receives once it
is noticed [10].
5.3
Hypothesis
This ﬁrst experiment will seek to test Bojko’s theories. The hypothesis is that it
should be feasible to indicate levels of area noticeability and area interest based
on Bojko’s eye tracking measures.

6.2
Theory
6.2.1
Colour saliency
The ability of a stimulus to draw the viewer’s attention depends partly on its
salience features.
Visual prominence of an area or a speciﬁc object can be
inﬂuenced by e.g. size, placement and colour. In a saliency study conducted by
Osberger et al. in 2001 Osberger explains: "A strong inﬂuence occurs when the
colour of a region is distinct from the colour of its background."[31, p. 362].
==PAGE:42==
26
Second Iteration
As previously mentioned in chapter 3, Related Work, the saliency of colours have
frequently been investigated with the aim of identifying how diﬀerent colours
inﬂuence our visual behaviour and attention. A study conducted by Bradford
[11] investigated noticeability as the extent to which a particular coloured item
was noticed and recalled, when exposed together with other items diﬀerently
coloured. The study was carried out with help from a series of stimulus cards in
six diﬀerent hues (red, orange, yellow, green, blue and violet) and results showed
that stimuli coloured in the orange-red spectrum had the highest noticeability
factor.
Similar results have been found in other studies investigating colour saliency.
Gelasca et al have conducted a colour experiment with the objective of deter-
mining which colours best catch our eyes [19]. The experiments included 12
diﬀerent colours uniformly spread in CIELab. In accordance with the study by
Bradford [11], results showed that red was the most salient colour. Furthermore,
yellow and green also proved to have high noticeability.
6.3
Hypothesis
The hypothesis to be validated in this second iteration takes basis in the ﬁndings
from the above mentioned studies. If some colours are truly more noticeable
than others and if contrast has a high inﬂuence on our visual attention, it is
assumed that diﬀerent contrast colour stimuli should generate diﬀerent levels of
visual attention. Scan patterns are thus expected to indicate a higher level of
focus when test subjects are presented to stimuli of e.g. red hue.

7.2.1
Visual behaviour and colour saliency
In order to further explore the correlations between user engagement and visual
behaviour this third iteration has been based upon a variety of studies cov-
ering diﬀerent eye tracking measures and their relation to levels of emotional
engagement.
Bradley et al. have studied the eﬀects of hedonic valence and emotional arousal
on pupillary responses [13]. The study showed greatest pupillary increases for
pleasant and unpleasant pictures compared to neutral pictures, which indicates

that pupil diameter increases when people observe stimuli, which they ﬁnd emo-
tionally engaging (regardless of hedonic valence).
In an other study conducted by Bradley et al., investigating the relation between
scan patterns and emotional arousal, results showed that emotionally engaging
content is linked to an increase in the amount of ﬁxations and a decrease in
ﬁxation length [12].
The link between blink patterns and drowsiness can also be useful as fatigue
and drowsiness are common indicators of poor or low engagement. According
to Caﬃer et al. drowsiness and sleepiness are associated with increases in both
blink duration and proportion of long duration blinks [15].
7.3
Hypothesis
On this basis the hypothesis for this third iteration is that it should be feasible
to measure levels of attraction and levels of emotional engagement based on
scan patterns and blink frequencies, blink lengths and pupillary reactions.

Materials
The test was based on the same contrast stimuli concept as the test from the

The case of test subject 5
The case of test subject 5 proved to be particularly interesting as it was reported
that this subject felt fatigued while conducting the experiment, which proved
to have notable eﬀect on the visual patterns.
Based on this speciﬁc ﬁnding, Per Bækgaard, Michael Kai Petersen and Jakob
Eg Larsen have conducted a study investigating the eﬀects of drowsiness on
visual patterns [14]. The experiment was set up as the test used in this third
iteration with the addition of white/black contrast trials. The objective was to
investigate to which degree eye tracking data reﬂects default states and levels
of engagement. To help achieve this objective eye tracking data was collected
from 2 test subjects twice a day over a week.
Spread and duration of ﬁxations proved to be inversely related to the reported
fatigue levels compared to the hypothesis stating that ﬁxations would be more
focused on the contrast stimuli in the morning when the subjects felt more alert.
Moreover, results showed inter-individual diﬀerences in reaction time, yet this
measure proved to be independent of both time of day and week and contrast
stimuli colour for both test subjects, thus exclusively reﬂecting personal visual
tendencies.
This last ﬁnding could suggest that inter-individual diﬀerences in the scan path
signatures might to some level be related to top-down signals making the indi-
vidual mental processing unique for each subjects. Information being processed
from the top-down relies heavily on our individual memory and tastes, which
can make individual colour preferences aﬀect the way in which we observe visual
stimuli presented to us.

7.6
Sub-conclusion
Just prior to the conduction of this third experiment the Eye Tribe had released
an update to their SDK (v.0.9.47) which had big inﬂuence on the outcome of
the data. As can been seen on the data visualisations, the data sets from this
third experiment seemed more accurate than those from the second iteration.
The results of the data analysis have shown potential for estimating levels of user
engagement based on the utilised eye tracking measures; ﬁxations frequencies,
ﬁxation durations, blink frequencies, blink durations and pupil sizes.
The ﬁrst test cycle with yellow baseline and blue stimuli proved to have gen-
erated the highest ﬁxation frequency, the lowest mean ﬁxation duration and
the shortest mean blink duration, which indicates the highest level of emotional
engagement among the four test cycles. The fourth and ﬁnal test cycle with
green baseline and red stimuli trials revealed the greatest proportion of long
duration blinks, indicating the lowest level of engagement. Thus, the ﬁxation
and blink patterns have revealed an engagement level decreasing as a function
of the time-on-task.
The pupillary patterns across all four test cycles showed the greatest mean
pupil size for the second test cycle with red baseline and green contrast stim-
uli trials. Based on the signiﬁcant diﬀerences in brightness between the four
baseline/background colours, this result is presumed to indicate reactions to
brightness rather than levels of engagement.
What also proved to be interesting were the inter-individual diﬀerences of the
visual patterns. The data visualisations in this third iteration hinted that the
individual signatures to some level reﬂect individual mental and physical states.

8.1
LEGO 3D Catalogue example
Previously the four primary colours and their eﬀects on the attention and emo-
tional engagement of observers have been investigated. In order to work towards
the main objective of estimating user engagement in 3D scenes this fourth it-
eration has sought to build a bridge between the simplistic contrast stimuli
and more detailed 3D scenes by utilising Adobe Color CC [8] palettes as visual
stimuli.
The LEGO 3D Catalogue application enables the users to, via smart devices,
view 3D models of the products seen in the physical catalogue. In order to
simplify these LEGO scenes, Adobe Color CC palettes have been used to reduce
complexity, thus making the images more suitable for this type of test.
In
order to ascertain the correlation between the colour palettes and the original
images this fourth iteration introduces user rated arousal levels to quantitatively
compare to the visual responses recorded by the eye tracker. This allows us to
stay within the framework of analysing emotional engagement based on colours
while making use of more complex scenes.

9.1
From 2D to 3D
The ﬁndings from the fourth iteration suggested that the simpliﬁed LEGO colour
palettes did not elicit engagement levels corresponding to the test subjects’
subjective ratings of the LEGO 3D Catalogue scenes. On this basis this ﬁfth
and ﬁnal iteration seeks to estimate levels of user engagement when observing
3D models. Introducing a third dimension to the visual stimuli enables us to
assess the impact of 3D details on the visual responses of the test subjects.
9.2
Theory
9.2.1
Complexity and mental processing
Because 3D scenes in their nature consist of a higher level of detail and complex-
ity, the processing of such scenes are connected to a higher degree of cognitive
workload. In this context it is relevant to look further into the factors, which
aﬀect our mental processing.
==PAGE:72==
56
Fifth Iteration
Mackworth and Morandi investigated the eﬀects of details on the visual attention
[28]. Results showed that regions containing unpredictable contours and unusual
details, received high concentrations of ﬁxations. Simple and predictable areas
on the other hand were associated with a lower ﬁxation frequency. In accordance
Bradley et al.
[12] suggested that complex scenes evoke a larger number of
discrete ﬁxations and longer saccade lengths compared to simple compositions.
Knickerbocker et al. [27] studied the eﬀects of emotional versus neutral words
on the processing time. The results showed that emotional words were processed
faster than neutral words, with shorter reaction time and shorter ﬁxation du-
rations. In the study it is stated that : "The shorter ﬁxation durations suggest
that participants can process emotional items in less time than neutral stimuli
(...)" [27, p. 17].
9.3
Hypothesis
Based on these studies it is assumed that areas and objects with high levels
of detailing and object which are perceived as informative should generate high
ﬁxation frequencies and that content rated as emotionally engaging should elicit
shorter ﬁxation durations. The hypothesis for this ﬁfth iteration is thus that
there should be a direct link between what is being ﬁxated upon, how long it is
being ﬁxated upon and what is perceived as informative or engaging areas.
9.4
Method
9.4.1
Materials
The visual stimuli for this ﬁfth iteration consisted of 4 videos displaying a simple
Unity 3D scene depicting a long narrow room with one object of interest situated
at the end. The 4 objects embodied diﬀerent saliency properties; one rotating
object (motion), one coloured object (colour), one complexly shaped object
(shape) and one large object (size).
All 4 Unity 3D scenes can be found in
Appendix G. The camera followed a ﬁrst person controller slowly moving closer
to the object with each video sequence having a total duration of 8 seconds.
Each of the 4 video sequences were presented twice with 2 seconds neutral grey
frames separating each sequence. The 8 video sequences and the 7 neutral frames
together accounted for 80 seconds of visual stimuli.
==PAGE:73==

9.4.2
Set-up
The visual stimuli was presented on a standard MacBook Pro Retina running
PsychoPy. After completing the test all test subjects were asked to rate the 4
Unity 3D objects according to their personal preferences. All data was extracted
through the Eye Tribe’s API console using PeyeTribe.
9.4.3
Data analysis
The data of this ﬁfth test was analysed with help of the following eye track-
ing measures: Fixation frequencies, ﬁxation durations, blink frequencies, blink
durations and pupil sizes.
The clustering algorithm used to identify ﬁxations in the two previous iterations,
generated erroneous data for this ﬁfth test, thus the ﬁxation analysis has been
based on the ﬁxations from the frame data speciﬁed by the Eye Tribe.

theory, Mackworth and Morandi state: "If adults have learned to pick out the
important details for analysis, they might be expected to agree to some extent
on which were the informative areas." [28, p. 547]. On this basis it is assumed
that the test subjects processed the diﬀerent objects in similar manner based
on their perceived informativeness.

9.6
Sub-conclusion
Based on the studies by Mackworth and Morandi [28] and Knickerbocker et al.
[27] it was initially hypothesised that ﬁxation frequencies and durations should
reﬂect levels of engagement and perceived informativeness.
Results revealed no relation between the assessed levels of engagement and the
subjective ratings of engagement. However, results showed that the complexly
shaped object prompted the longest mean ﬁxation duration for 4 of the 5 test
subjects, which could suggest that the unpredictable contours resulted in a
higher degree of focus.
The largest mean pupil sizes were prompted by the large object and the object
in motion, which suggest that these scenes were linked to the highest levels of
emotional engagement. However, these result did not in all cases correlate with
the subjective rating of engagement which thereby indicates that the pupillary
reactions were rather related to perceived informativeness of the diﬀerent objects
and thus the cognitive load.
The analysis of the blink patterns did not lead to any useful ﬁndings as the
blink data seemed to have been erroneous. Few blinks had been recorded for 2
of the 5 test subjects which prevented a comparative analysis.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This study has consisted of ﬁve iterations each aiming at exploring diﬀerent
aspects related to analysing levels of user engagement based on eye tracking
data.
The ﬁrst iteration explored the use of Bojko’s measures of area noticeability and
measures of area interest [10] to evaluate user engagement and user attraction of
4 colour spectres (yellow, red, blue and green) and their respective contrast stim-
uli. Results suggested that green contrast stimulus on red spectrum background
was the most noticeable and that red stimuli on green spectrum attracted the
most interest. The yellow and blue colour spectres (both including and exclud-
ing contrast stimuli) prompted longer total scan paths indicating a higher level
of emotional engagement.
The second iteration sought to look further into the aﬀects of contrast stimuli
on the visual focus of the test subjects. The scan patterns were analysed both
for baseline frames and contrast stimuli trials. Results showed inter-individual
diﬀerences on all 4 baseline colours and 4 contrast stimuli sequences, which sug-
gest that these individual visual signatures are related to the top-down factors
which rely on individual memory and thereby control the visual pleasure unique
to each observer.
The ﬁrst two iterations were constrained by the erroneous data recorded by the
==PAGE:80==
64

Eye Tribe eye tracker and did thereby not lead to any truly credible ﬁndings.
Nevertheless, the results from these ﬁrst iterations suggested some common
tendencies and colour related patterns which helped set the framework for the
subsequent iterations.
The eﬀects of colour saliency on visual attention were further investigated in
the third iteration. Furthermore, diﬀerent eye tracking measures were utilised
in this iteration to estimate levels of user engagement. Results of the analysis of
ﬁxations and blinks suggested that the levels of engagement decreased relative
to the time-on-task. The analysis of the pupillary reactions indicated that the
mean pupil sizes reﬂected a reaction to the brightness of the baseline colours
rather than levels of user engagement or cognitive load.
Gaze visualisations
revealed a strong inﬂuence of fatigue levels on attention. One very tired test
subject prompted a highly smoothed gaze reﬂecting a low level of attention to
the presented stimuli. This ﬁnding revealed that our visual behaviour is not only
controlled by bottom-up and top-down factors but are also highly inﬂuenced by
our individual default states.
The fourth iteration moved beyond analysing the eﬀects of contrast colour stim-
uli by introducing colour palettes based on 4 LEGO 3D Catalogue scenes. The
objective was to analyse how the diﬀerent palettes aﬀected the eye tracking re-
sponses of the test subjects and comparing these results to subjective ratings of
user engagement. Visualisations of the individual scan patterns revealed notable
inter-individual diﬀerences with no evident palette dependencies. These indi-
vidual scan patterns were thus thought to rather reﬂect individual gaze biases.
The ﬁxation frequency and mean ﬁxation duration of one of the 5 test subjects
showed reverse relation to the subjective engagement rating compared to the
ﬁndings of Bradley et el. [13]. The blink analysis revealed great inter-individual
diﬀerences which could neither be linked to palettes nor subjective engagement
ratings. As in the third iteration the pupillary reactions proved to be palette
dependent suggesting reactions to brightness of the displayed colours.
The ﬁfth and ﬁnal iteration sought to investigate the eﬀects of motion, colour,
complex shapes and size on user engagement and visual attention. The visual
stimuli utilised in the experiment consisted of 4 simplistic Unity 3D scenes each
portraying one object of interest embodying one of the four saliency properties
(i.e.
motion, colour, shape and size).
Results suggested that the complexly
shaped object elicited the longest duration ﬁxations and thus a high degree
of focus from the test subjects, which corresponds to the hypothesis [28] that
unpredictable contours and unusual details are perceived as informative areas
more so than simple predictable areas.
The pupillary reactions revealed to
be highly object dependent, which again, is thought to reﬂect the engagement
and cognitive load as a results of the perceived informativeness of the diﬀerent
objects. While the contrast stimuli trials and the Lego 3D Catalogue palettes
==PAGE:81==
65
revealed great inter-individual preferences the 4 Unity 3D scenes revealed a
higher degree of stimuli dependent tendencies. The visual stimuli utilised in the
ﬁfth test consisted of very simplistic and neutral scenes with only one object of
interest to inﬂuence the visual behaviour of the test subjects. This can, to some
degree, have resulted in the bottom-up factors having a stronger eﬀect on the
visual behaviour of the test subjects.
The ﬁrst three iterations primarily focused on analysing the general attention
levels and emotional engagement levels of the 5 test subjects with no comparison
to subjective ratings of engagement. This enabled the identiﬁcation of general
visual patterns prompted by the 4 diﬀerent baseline colours and their contrast
colour stimuli. While no absolute correlations were concluded, the results re-
vealed some colour dependent tendencies suggesting that, in the big picture,
some colours do seem to produce greater focus and broader scan patterns which
indicates an overall higher degree of engagement.
The ﬁnal two iterations investigated how visual patterns correlated with sub-
jective engagement ratings. In these two iterations the results from the 5 test
subjects were analysed separately and it proved diﬃcult to identify direct rela-
tion between assessed levels of engagement based on eye tracking measures and
subjective engagement ratings.
The eye tracking measures of emotional arousal and cognitive load, for the most
part, diﬀerentiated the test subjects from one another.
Results suggested a
strong inﬂuence of aesthetic preferences and default states on visual behaviour,
although these proved to be diﬃcult to link to the subjective ratings of emotional
engagement. However, contrast stimuli and complexity proved to have a positive
eﬀect on visual focus. The ﬁndings of these ﬁve iterations thus suggest that while
individual top-down systems heavily contribute to the way in which individuals
perceive and react to visual stimuli contrast colour saliency can, with beneﬁt,
be utilised to direct bottom-up attention.

Chapter 11
Discussion and further work
The assessment of user engagement based on eye tracking measures proved to
be a challenging task. As presented in chapter 3, Related Work, the diﬀerent
eye tracking measures can all be used to assess diﬀerent mental states and levels
of attention, thus restricting the possibilities to draw absolute conclusions based
on eye tracking data. The pupil proved to be a particularly diﬃcult measure to
work with as it is very sensitive to external factors and might therefore not be
a valid measure in passive eye tracking studies which are not set up in highly
controlled environments.
The data analysis in some of the iterations was further restricted by the inac-
curacy of the Eye Tribe eye tracker. Inconsistencies in the data sets and the
clustering data proved to have notable eﬀects on the results making it chal-
lenging to identify subject and stimuli speciﬁc visual patterns. However, we do
see a constant improvement of the data quality as a result of the continuous
updates of the Eye Tribe software and hardware, making it feasible in the near
future for eye tracking studies, in less controlled environments, to provide valid
information about visual behaviour.
The fourth and ﬁfth iterations investigated the correlation between assessed
levels of engagement and subjective engagement ratings, however, little corre-
spondence was identiﬁed. The possibilities remain however, for this to be caused
by falsely rated levels of engagement by the test subjects. Subjective statements
==PAGE:84==
68
Discussion and further work
of engagement might be heavily biased by expectations of what we "should" ﬁnd
engaging. In the fourth iteration the test subjects were asked to rate 4 LEGO
3D Catalogue scenes according to their personal preferences and what was no-
table about the outcome was the gender speciﬁc ratings of the LEGO Friends
scenes and the Star Wars and Technic scenes. It can thus not be excluded that
these ratings did not reﬂect the actual emotional engagement levels.
In general it applies that the way in which we individually perceive and process
visual stimuli is highly aﬀected by top-down systems which rely on our memory,
experiences and expectations. When analysing eye tracking data it can be diﬃ-
cult to assess the exact impact of these factors on our visual behaviour. The test
subjects who participated in this study varied a great deal in both age, gender,
occupation, etc. and showed notable inter-individual diﬀerences in the ways in
which they visually responded to the diﬀerent stimuli. This raises an intriguing
question as to whether these diﬀerent "user proﬁles" can help to foresee aﬀects
of top-down factors on visual responses.
Further exploring the intersection between visual behaviour, subjective ratings
of engagement and "user proﬁles" might make it possible to categorize the eﬀects
of top-down memory based systems and default states on visual responses. This
could in the long run allow for the design of computational models which can
automatically evaluate levels of engagement by combining demographic metrics,
biometrics and eye tracking measures.

Appendix A
List of Terms
AOI: Area of Interest. A region of the visual stimulus which is of speciﬁc rele-
vance to the research question.
Eye tracking: Process of recording where someone is looking while measuring
eye characteristics such as pupil sizes.
Eye tracker: Hardware which allows to record eye movements and character-
istics.
Fixation: When the foveal vision is focused on a speciﬁc area. Visual informa-
tion is extracted during these ﬁxations.
Saccade: Rapid eye movement in between ﬁxations.
Scan path: The path which the eye have followed during observation of the
visual stimulus. Total scan path is deﬁned as the total sum of saccade lenghts.
Scan pattern: Patterns identiﬁed based on how the test subjects scanned the
visual stimulus.
Time-on-task: Refers to the time spent actively involved in a given task.
Visual stimulus: Material observed by the test subjects during eye tracking
experiment.
