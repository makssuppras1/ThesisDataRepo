Abstract
High Temperature Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cells (HT-PEMFCs) are promising
electrochemical devices that convert chemical energy into electrical energy, offering potential as
renewable energy technologies.
One critical challenge is their limited lifetime, prompting
research into the mechanisms of degradation. This thesis investigates the long-term durability of
HT-PEMFCs utilizing phosphoric acid (PA) doped polybenzimidazole (PBI) membranes, with a
focus on acid loss as a primary cause of degradation.
Through experimental research, accelerated stress testing (AST) was employed to shorten testing
time, assessing 13 membrane electrode assemblies (MEAs). This thesis has industry cooperation
with Blue World Technologies providing the tested MEAs.
Three main experiments were
conducted, investigating the effects three parameters have on degradation and acid loss:
temperatures of 160, 180 and 200 °C, current densities of 200, 400, and 800 mAcm−2, and
lambda air (gas stoichiometries) of 5, 10 and 20.
Six protocols were developed to evaluate temperature effects on degradation. Findings indicate
that higher temperatures significantly increase degradation. EIS measurements show increased
ohmic loss, suggesting phosphoric acid loss due to evaporation and dehydration. Polarization
curve analysis indicates additional charge transfer and mass transport losses, linked to carbon
corrosion of the catalyst.
The effects of current density on degradation was evaluated for six cells, revealing trends similar to
temperature effects. Higher current densities accelerated degradation and increased ohmic losses,
likely due to acid mobility. At 200 °C, higher current densities also increased charge transfer
and mass transport losses, whereas these losses were minimal at 160 °C and 180 °C, indicating
temperature’s significant influence on these degradation modes.
Trends for lambda air effects were less clear. High lambda lead to degradation, but changes in
lambda air values did not significantly affect degradation rates or losses, suggesting that more
tests are needed to draw definitive conclusions.

Chapter 1:
Introduction
In this section, an introduction to fuel cells is given, and more in depth about the High Temperature
Polymer Electrolyte Fuel Cell (HT-PEMFC). The problem statement is formulated at the end of
the introduction.
1.1
Why Fuel Cells?
In 2012, as a part of the Danish energy settlement, Denmark targeted complete fossil fuel
independence per the year of 2050 [1]. This ambitious goal is contributing to an accelerating
focus in alternative energy sources, specially renewables.
Fuel cells (FCs) have emerged as a promising alternative technology,
functioning as
zero-emission energy conversion devices. Renewable energy sources like wind and solar are
intermittent, meaning their power generation can be inconsistent. To address this, energy can be
stored by producing hydrogen by a process called electrolysis, and converted back to electricity
by using FCs when renewable sources are not producing energy [2].
Among several FC technologies, High Temperature Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cells
(HT-PEMFC) based on polybenzimidazole (PBI) membranes doped with phosphoric acid (PA)
have received increasing attention in recent years. The advantages of HT-PEMFCs include higher
operating temperatures, strong carbon monoxide (CO) tolerance, and simplified water and heat
management [3]. However, several challenges must be addressed before this technology can be
widely implemented.
Key issues include high costs, durability concerns, and the need for
adequate infrastructure [4]. This work focus on understanding the degradation mechanism, and
how different parameters affects the degradation.
1.2
Working Principle of a Fuel Cell
FC is an electrochemical device, converting chemical energy from fuels into electricity. These
fuels can come from hydrogen, methanol, ethanol etc. [5]. There are several ways to categorize
FCs. One way of classifying them is by dividing them into low-temperature and high-temperature
FCs. Low-temperature FCs operate below 200 °C and include Alkaline Fuel cell (AFC), Proton
1
==PAGE:10==
Electrolyte Membrane Fuel cell (PEMFC), Direct Methanol Fuel Cell (DMFC), and Phosphoric
Acid Fuel Cell (PAFC). High-temperature FC range between temperatures of from 500 °C and
1000 °C, and include Molten Carbonate Fuel Cell (MCFC) and Solid Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC) [6].
Generally, a single FC consist of two electrodes (anode and cathode) separated by an electrolyte.
The anode (negative electrode) is the fuel electrode, and the cathode (positive electrode) is an
oxidant electrode. The electrolyte conducts ions between the anode and the cathode. Physically,
it separates the fuel (for example hydrogen) from the oxidant (air or oxygen), ensuring that the
fuel does not combust in oxygen. Since the electrolyte is a non-electric conductor, the electrons
moves through an external circuit generating electricity. The assembly of the anode, cathode and
the electrolyte is referred to as the membrane-electrode assembly (MEA) [5]. The construction of
a PEMFC can be seen in Figure 1.1.

1.3
High Temperature PEM Fuel Cells
HT-PEMFCs are in scientific reports usually compared to low temperature PEMFCs
(LT-PEMFCs) [4]. LT-PEMFC typically has a membrane made of perfluorosulfonic acid (PFSA),
e.q.
Nafion.
This membrane has proven a good performance (high conductivity and good
chemical stability). The drawback with this membrane is that to function, it need to be in a
hydrated state [7]. In this membrane, water is the charge carrier for the proton conductivity. This
means that water is required to keep the membrane hydrated. Water management is an important
aspect of the stack design, as too little water will dehydrate the membrane causing the proton
conductivity to decrease. On the other hand, too much water will cause the electro-catalyst on the
cathode side to flood, and restrict the transportation of oxygen through the porous gas diffusion
electrode [4]. Since LT-PEMFC operates at temperatures between 80 °C and 100 °C (close to
boiling point of water), it involves a two-phase system. Here, the water will vaporize at low
humidities, and condense at high humidities. Therefore, temperature control is crucial to operate
the fuel cell efficiently. When the LT-PEMFC operates at temperatures between 60 °C and 80 °C,
fuel impurities such as carbon monoxide (CO) is poisoning to the catalyst [7].
At higher
temperatures, the CO poisoning is expected to be less harmful.
HT-PEMFC is defined as a fuel cell that usually operates in a temperature range of 120 °C and 180
°C [3]. This allow for higher fuel-impurity tolerance, specifically CO. At temperatures between
150 °C and 200 °C, the HT-PEMFC can tolerate a CO-poisoning of 3 % (in the fuel stream).
Compared to LT-PEMFC which can tolerate 10-100 ppm at 80 °C [7]. The membrane is the
key component that enables PEM fuel cells to function at elevated temperatures. By using a
PBI membrane doped with PA as the proton conductor, it possible to operate the fuel cell at
temperatures up to 200 °C [4].
The target range for durability for fuel cells is between 5 000 h for automobile application and
40,000 h for stationary application [8]. PBI based HT-PEMFC have shown a stable performance
under steady-state conditions, reaching up to 17 000 h of operation time, but higher degradation
rates are observed under variable load operation [9]. Eberhardt et al. [10] conducted research on
HT-PEMFC by accelerated stress test (AST), by employing a temperature of 190 °C, hydrogen
and air stoichiometries of 13.2 and 11. This AST protocol lead to a failure of the cell after 2 830
h [10].

1.3.1
Polybenzimidazole (PBI) and Phosphoric Acid (PA)
PBI based membranes are the most studied membranes for HT-PEMFCs [11]. PBI has a high
thermal stability, chemical resistance and mechanical strength. PBI membranes are doped with
PA in order to achieve proton conductivity [12]. After doping, the backbones of the polymer are
separated by the free acid, which can lead to a decrease in the mechanical strength. Therefore, the
doping level should be chosen based on both the proton conductivity and the mechanical strength
of the PBI [13]. Meta-PBI (m-PBI) and AB-PBI are the two most prominant PBIs, and can be
seen in Figure 1.2 [12].
Figure 1.2: The two most common PBIs: m-PBI and AB-PBI [12].
PBI doped with PA has since it first got introduced in 1995 [14], been the most promising
electrolyte for HT-PEMFC. PA is used to dope the membrane to achieve a higher proton
conductivity for the fuel cell.
As mentioned, because of the electrolyte and operating
temperatures in the LT-PEMFC, balancing water management is a delicate task. HT-PEMFC
exhibit a greater degree of tolerance in relation to this aspect. PA-based PBI type membranes
have a high proton conductivity even in an anhydrous state. Since the HT-PEMFC operates above
water boiling point, there is only a single phase (water vapor) present in the catalyst layer making
transportation of water in the MEA easier. PA is thermally stable at temperatures above 100
°C [12].

Components in a HT-PEMFC
Figure 1.3 shows the components in a PEMFC [15]. The PEMFC consists of end plates with inlets

gas- diffusion layers (GDL), and the MEA. The MEA is inserted between the GDL, two gaskets

placed. All of these components are held together by endplates made of stainless steel, tightened
together using bolts [16].

1.4
Accelerating Stress Test
AST is a testing approach designed to reduce the testing period while providing valuable insights
into a product’s durability. It is a common tool used to understand the degradation mechanisms
in HT-PEMFCs. Stressors affect multiple mechanisms, and therefore it is necessary to develop
good AST protocols to understand the relationship between different stressors and the degradation
mechanisms. The product tested, in this case FCs, are employed to stress in order to study its
degradation mechanisms [17].

1.5
Problem Statement
The transition from a petroleum-based economy to one centered on renewable sources is crucial,
with fuel cells expected to play a significant role in this shift. Among the various types of fuel
cells, HT-PEMFC emerge as a promising conversion technology. However, due to the challenge
of their short lifespan, numerous studies and tests have been conducted to address and mitigate
this issue.
The research conducted in this report aims to understand the degradation of HT-PEMFC due to
phosphoric acid loss, by testing 21 cm2 Dapozol® From Blue World Technologies, in single
cells. The MEAs are tested under constant operational parameters at different AST protocols.
The parameters to be investigated are temperature, current density, and lambda air (gas
stoichiometries). The cells will be analyzed using electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS)
and polarization curves, in addition to constant recordings of the voltage throughout the
operation time (durability curves).
Additional objectives of this thesis include project planning, identify safety issues for fuel cell
research, learn lab skills and research disciplines, and developing AST protocols.

Chapter 2:
Durability Issues and Degradation
This section aims to review the durability issues and degradation mechanisms present in a HT-
PEMFC, based on existing literature. Acid loss as a degradation mechanism is the main focus in
this report, but membrane degradation and catalyst degradation will be briefly mentioned.
2.1
Introduction
HT-PEMFC is a promising technology for the implementation of hydrogen. Commercialization
of the fuel cell is hindered by lifetime and reliability. Therefore, research on the durability of
the fuel cell is crucial [7]. Different operational conditions can cause different cell components
to degrade [18]. The lifetime is strongly affected by operating conditions (temperature, pressure,
fuel impurities etc.). Higher temperatures, which is the case for HT-PEMFC, has shown to cause
material durability challenges [18].
There are several degradation mechanisms in a HT-PEMFC. These include degradation due to
polymer oxidation, catalyst instability and acid loss [7]. The membrane experiences stress during
operation, which may result in mechanical failure, polymer oxidation, and membrane thinning.
Thermal and wet swelling/shrinking during start/stop or load cycling can potentially cause tearing
or cracks in the membrane. Oxidative degradation can lead to thinning, pinhole, and hotspots [7].
Polymer oxidation can result in membrane thinning during operation of the FC, potentially leading
to an increase in hydrogen and air crossover and a lower open circuit voltage (OCV). Membrane
thinning can be illustrated in a cross-sectional Scanning Electrode Microscopy (SEM) image of
the MEA before and after operating for a significant amount of time [7]. Oono et al. [19] studied
the membrane thickness before/after 17 800 h of operation, based on SEM imaging. As seen in
Figure 2.1, the results obtained by Oono et al. [19] strongly indicates a reduction in membrane
thickness.
7
==PAGE:16==
Figure 2.1: Cross-sectional SEM image of MEA before (a) and after (b) 17 800h [19].
The state-of-the-art catalyst for HT-PEMFC are Platinum (Pt) and its alloy [7].
Catalyst
degradation is a primary contributor to voltage loss in a HT-PEMFC [18]. Pt particle growth
causes loss of electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) and hence a decrease in the
performance of the FC. There are different mechanism that can cause Pt particle growth [7].
These are illustrated in Figure 2.2 [7].
Figure 2.2: Three Pt particles growth mechanisms in a fuel cell [7].
Pt particle coalescence is a process where Pt particles in the catalyst undergo merging or
combination through agglomeration or sintering, resulting in larger particles. This process is
known as the Ostwald ripening [7]. For instance, the Pt particle size is approximately 3 nm at the
beginning of lifetime testing.
However, after accelerated aging testing, the particle size can
8
==PAGE:17==
increase to 10 nm or even 20 nm [20].
Higher operational temperatures compose more
challenges with catalyst degradation [21]. Shao et al. [22] have provided a review of catalyst
degradation mechanism in LT-PEMFCs, that to some extent are relevant for HT-PEMFCs. In this
review it is evident that increase operating temperature lead to catalyst degradation.
Performance degradation for PA doped PBI membrane relates to unfavorable reactions and
corrosive environment. Main problems are in literature found to be loss in the catalyst active area
due to catalyst agglomeration, and PA leaching out from the cell. A cause of degradation is due
to high potentials of the catalyst on supported structures. OCV contributes to the agglomeration
of Pt particles, as it promotes both Pt oxidation and carbon corrosion within the electrode [9].
Carbon corrosion as a cause of degradation in HT-PEMFC has received more attention in recent
years. This mechanism involve carbon reacting with water and further generating CO2 [23]. Oh
et al. [24] conducted research on carbon corrosion in LT-PEMFC compared to HT-PEMFC. Their
findings indicates that HT-PEMFC are more vulnerable to carbon corrosion due to the elevated
temperatures. Stevens et al. [25] demonstrated that carbon supports with various Pt loadings
experienced significantly increased carbon corrosion as the temperature rose from 125 °C to 195
°C. Carbon corrosion in HT-PEMFCs is a catalyst-related issue that can reduce the
electrochemically active catalytic surface area. [21].
2.2
Acid Loss
In order to achieve proton conductivity, PBI cells are doped with PA. Therefore, acid loss is a
critical aspect in relation to the fuel cell performance [7]. There are several mechanisms that
relates to acid loss, including acid doping level, acid evaporation and dehydration, and acid
mobility.
Søndergaard et al. [26] conducted a parametric stress study that employed stressors of temperature,
current density, and air flow. In their work, they found a general trend where the MEA lifetime
decreased with increasing value of the three stressors. From post-test acid-base titration, they
found that the average voltage rate followed the same general trend as for the average acid-loss
rate.

2.2.1
Acid doping
The ohmic resistance depends on the specific conductivity and the thickness of the membrane.
When comparing membranes for LT-PEMFC (typically PFSA) with membranes for HT-PEMFC
(typically PBI), the PBI generally has a higher ohmic resistance. The thickness of PFSA is below
25 µm, and the area specific resistance is 20-40 mΩcm−2. The proton conductivity of PBI depends
on the acid doping level (ADL). Normal ADL is 11-12 H3PO4 per polymer repeat unit (PRU)
at high molecular weight polymer. At this ADL, the proton conductivity is about 0.1 S cm−1
at temperatures around 160 °C. The typical thickness of PBI membrane is 40-100 µm, with an
area specific resistance of around 100 mΩcm−2. When comparing the resistance to the PFSA
membrane, it is significantly higher [7].
The acid doping level of a PBI membrane is depended on several aspects. Pure PBI absorbs up to
around 20 wt% water, corresponding to about 3 molecules per PRU. This happens because water
molecules form bonds with certain parts of the PBI called benzimidazole moieties. When exposing
the PBI for PA, the benzimidazole units in the polymer get fully charged with protons, forming a
kind of polymeric salt with phosphate as the counter anions. To make the polymer a good proton
conductor, more PA than the amount needed to balance out the benzimidazole units are needed.
After soaking the membrane in a solution containing 15 M (85 wt%) of PA, it reaches an average
doping level of 11 molecules of H3PO4 per unit of the polymer chain (PRU), which is the typical
ADL. As summarized in Figure 2.3a, ADL of 11 is reached after soaking the membrane in 15 M
(85 wt%) H3PO4 at room temperature (red plot). As seen in Figure 2.3b, the ADL is related to the
conductivity, and emphazise the importance of a correct ADL. As mentioned, the normal ADL is
around 11, and can also be observed on the plot [7].

2.2.2
Acid Evaporation and Dehydration
The vapor over the PA solution are mostly consisting of water. The estimated acid content in the
vapor phase is approximately 0.7 µbar at 160 °C and 3.7 µbar at 190 °C. Over an extended period
of fuel cell operation with high airflow, the loss of acid due to PA evaporation becomes significant.
When considering a current density of 0.2 A cm−2 and stoichiometries of λH2/air = 1.2/2.0 these
saturated acid vapor pressure values can be converted into an acid loss rate of 3.6 and 19 µg m−2
s−1 at 160 and 190 °C, respectively [7].
Søndergaard et al. [27] conducted research on the degradation of a HT-PEMFC due to acid loss
caused by the evaporation mechanism. In their work, they found a strong correlation between the
gas flow rate (H2+air) and temperature, indicating that the acid evaporation mechanism
significantly contributes to the degradation.
An article by Oono et al. [28] proclaims that
temperature affects the PA evaporation of a phosphoric acid fuel cell (PAFC). The resulting
evaporation rates rates are estimated by them to be 0.6, 1.8 and 9 µg m−2 s−1 for cell
temperatures of 150, 170 and 190 °C, respectively. Elevated temperatures are found to be one of
the main causes of acid evaporation.
The effect of evaporation is decrease in proton
11
==PAGE:20==
conductivity [29].
Acid loss due to evaporation can be measured by collecting trace acid evaporated with anodic
and cathodic off-gases. Not all acids in the gas phase will be carried out by the off-gases, which
can lead to an underestimation of acid loss by evaporation [11].
Evaluating acid loss by
analysing off-gases might not provide a realistic picture of this degradation. This is because the
mechanism considered are the ones that occur in gas phase, and the acid in the gas phase can
re-condense before collection. Another way of analysing acid loss is acid-base titration. This
technique evaluate the remaining acid in the MEAs, and not what is removed out of the cell by
off-gases [26].
Even though PA has a good thermal stability, it can undergo dehydration at elevated temperatures.
It is desirable to avoid dehydration because it can dramatically change the conductivity of the
membrane, and therefore also the performance of the FC [7]. At higher temperatures, the water
molecules can evaporate, and therefore the acid molecules will not receive the hydration they
need in order to transfer protons which may decrease their mobility and conductivity [30]. The
behavior of acid hydration and dehydration are important because they lead to water generation in
FC, influencing the conductivity through changes in the acid-water compositions [11].
The main driving forces for acid dehydration is found to be temperature, current density and gas
stoichiometries [31] [32]. When the FC is fed with methanol reformate there will be some water
in the fuel stream. Acid hydration and dehydration behaviour is of concern because the acid-water
composition can lead to changes in the membrane conductivity [7]. Dehydration of the fuel cell
can potentially lead to increased resistance in the membrane [33].
2.2.3
Acid Mobility
The acid transfer within MEAs, though with no measurable loss out of the fuel cell, is another
critical issue for the performance degradation. Since PA is mobile within membrane, it can move
into the catalyst layers, and this transfer is another critical issue for performance degradation. This
is happening during the break-in period for the FC, which can last for a few hundred hours. By
capillary forces, PA is driven from the membrane into the electrodes. When a FC is operating,
acid migrates from the cathode to the anode and balanced out by the back diffusion in the opposite
direction. In literature it is found that at higher current densities (800 mA cm−2), PA droplets were
observed at the anode. This means that there is a redistribution of the PA and that the migration
12
==PAGE:21==
is faster than the back diffusion [7]. PAs mobility to move lead to increase in acid loss in the
membrane [34].
The migration of PA was studied by Eberhardt et al. [35]. Their study indicates that current
density is the most influential factor for the acid migration rate, and the amount of free acid in
the membrane [35]. This is supported by another research made by Yu et al. [36], where it was
indicated that increasing the current density increased the acid losses. The consequence of PA
loss is a decrease in the membrane conductivity and an increase in the resistance of the FC [7].
In another research article, Wannek et al. [37] found that increasing the current density also lead
to an increase phosphoric acid, furthermore decreasing the conductivity in the membrane and
increasing resistance in the cell.

Diagnostics for Fuel Cell Performance
In this section, tools and methods used for the characterization of the MEA during operation are
presented. This include the polarization curves, durability curves and EIS.
3.1
Polarization Curves
Polarization curve is a common electrochemical technique to evaluate FC performance. The
polarization curve shows how the cell voltage changes with current density, as seen in Figure 3.1.
These curves provides information about the fuel cells overall performance under specific
operating conditions [38]. In the figure, the OCV is the starting point of the polarization curve
the voltage axis when the current density is zero, meaning no load applied. This represents the
maximum voltage the FC can produce.
As seen in the figure, the OCV is lower than the
theoretical voltage. This may be caused by hydrogen crossover in the membrane, meaning that
fuel is transferred from the anode to the cathode reacting with oxygen without any current
generation [39]. There are three main polarization losses; activation losses, ohmic losses, and
mass transport losses. As seen in Figure 3.1, activation losses are dominant in the low current
density region. Increasing to intermediate current density, the voltage losses are related to ohmic
losses. At higher current densities, the mass transport losses are dominant, due to transportation
limitation of the reactant gas [40].
Figure 3.1: Theoretical polarization curve illustrating the regions of a FC [38].

In Equation 3.1 the relationship between current density (mA cm−2) and cell potential (V) is
presented. This equation was derived in 1988 by Ticianelli et al. [41].
E = C −b log(i)−Ri−ηMT
(3.1)
Where E is the cell voltage, C is a constant, b is the Tafel slope, i is the current density, R is the
area specific resistance within the cell, and ηMT is the mass transport overpotential.
3.2
Durability Curves
Durability curves illustrate how the output voltage of a FC evolves over time. Typically, the FC
have a stable voltage output in the initial period (early operation). Over time, the voltage output
may gradually decline due to various factors. The rate of degradation is critical in assessing the
overall durability of the FC. Eventually the durability curve reaches a point where the voltage
output falls below an acceptable performance, indicating the end of life (EoL) for the FC.
Søndergaard et al. [26] tested HT-PEMFCs resulting in the graph seen in Figure 3.2. The output
voltage decreases over time, and at constant temperature, higher current density leads to faster
degradation.
In this work, durability curves are used to evaluate how different operational
protocols affect the degradation.

3.3
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS)
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) is a non-destructive technique commonly used
to study electrochemical devices. It provides information on several electrochemical phenomena
by applying a low-level alternating current waveform to the FC and measure the respones of the
cell. This technique provides a detailed real-time approach to diagnose changes in processes
and loss mechanisms [38]. In fuel cells, voltage loss originates from three fundamental sources:
charge transfer activation, or ”kinetic” losses, ion and electron transport, or ”ohmic” losses, and
concentration-related losses, or ”mass transfer” losses.
The data obtained from an EIS test typically represent the components of the total impedance of
the cell evaluated across a wide frequency range. These data, when plotted on a graph, illustrate
the impedance components as shown in Figure 3.3. Interpreting the Nyquist diagram allows for
the determination of each internal resistance. In Figure 3.3, the different zones of ohmic, charge
transfer, and mass transport resistance are presented. [42]. In a Nyquist plot the imaginary part
of the impedance (Im) is plotted against the real part at each frequency (Re Z) [43]. For this

is assumed to be related to acid loss.

EIS analysis
This section aims to give an understanding of the methods used to analyse the EIS measurements

A typical measurement from EIS is shown in Figure 5.1. One critical point for data analysis are

is related, but not limited to the acid loss. The ohmic resistance is a combination of membrane
resistance, contact resistance and electronic resistance. [3].

6.2.4
Temperature Effects: Discussion
As a general trend, cells operating at 200 °C degrades faster compared to cells operating at
180°C and 160 °C. Voltage extracted from polarization curves supports this trend. From EIS
measurements it is clear that ohmic loss is increasing with increasing temperature. Increase in
temperature can cause the membrane to dehydrate, and through the evaporation mechanism acid
loss is present. Aili et. al [7] determined the acid in the vapor phase to around 0.7 µbar and 3.7
µbar for cells operating at 160°C and 190°C, respectively. This further supports that more acid is
lost at higher temperatures.
Oono et al. [28] estimated acid evaporation rates where it was
strongly indicated that increasing temperature also increased the acid evaporation rates, leading
to a decrease in conductivity. Furthermore, Eberhardt et al. [10] found that an acid concentration
41
==PAGE:50==
of 36 mg H3PO4 cm−2 was sufficient for the targeted lifespan (50 000 h) when the fuel cell
operates at 160 °C and 600 mA cm−2. When increasing temperature to 190 °C, 90 % of the acid
had evaporated after only 2 830 h.
Observations of increasing charge transfer and mass transport losses are supported by Eberhardt
et al. research [10], where they found that the FC declining performance not only was caused by
acid loss, but also growth of Pt particles and changes in the catalyst layer’s hydrophobicity. From
the results obtained in this thesis, increasing ohmic loss is only present at 200 °C and 180 °C. At
160 °C, there is no significant increase in ohmic loss.
The temperature effects demonstrate that both ohmic losses and charge transfer and mass transport
losses are temperature dependent. Charge transfer and mass transport losses are greater than
ohmic losses, suggesting that degradation is not solely due to acid loss. At higher temperatures,
degradation may also result from carbon corrosion and catalyst deterioration [24]. Current density
effects are also evident in this section. C2 and C6 in comparison 2, operating at 800 mA cm−2,
exhibit more rapid degradation compared to C1 in comparison 1, which operates at 200 mA cm−2.
In summary, increasing temperature leads to higher degradation and ohmic loss, likely due to acid
evaporation and membrane dehydration. Additionally, there significant increase in charge transfer
and mass transport losses, indicating carbon corrosion affecting the catalyst.

6.3.4
Current Density Effect: Discussion
Comparison 1 (at 200°C) and comparison 2 (at 180°C) both shows the same trend: higher current
densities lead to faster degradation. Specifically cells operating at 800 mAcm−2 exhibit rapid
degradation from start. In contrast, cells operating at lower current densities maintained a more
stable performance for a longer period, though they eventually began to degrade (except C9).
The increase in ohmic resistance loss, similarly increased with higher current densities. The
consistent increase in ohmic losses across all cells experiencing degradation indicates that acid
loss is contributing to the performance loss. This effect can be related to the acid mobility. As
mentioned in Section 2.2.3, the PA is mobile and moves from the membrane into the electrodes.
47
==PAGE:56==
As PA migrates out of the membrane, the membrane’s resistance increases. The cells operating at
800 mA cm−2 (C2 and C6) showed a significant increase in ohmic resistance early in their
operation, indicating PA loss. In contrast, cells operating at lower current densities (200 mA
cm−2), such as C1 and C9) showed more stable ohmic losses, suggesting slower PA migration
and more balanced back diffusion [7]. Again, it is important to emphasize that flow rates are
different, and the flow rate is higher at higher current densities. Higher airflow will potentially
carry more acid out of the membrane through evaporation mechanism, further indicating acid
loss.
Results showing that increasing current densities lead to increased charge transfer and mass
transport loss is supported by Eberhardt et al. [10]. They found that increasing mass transport
overpotential was a major contributor to performance loss at high current densities, most likely
due to changes in hydrophobicity of catalyst layer. The trends are more clear for comparison 1
than comparison 2. For comparison 1 (200°C) the charge transfer and mass transport loss are
higher than for comparison 2 (180°C). Between these comparisons the only change is the
temperature, indicating that this parameter should also be the main influence for degradation
related to catalyst.
To summarize the findings for the current density dependence: applying
higher current density to the cells lead to faster degradation and increase in ohmic, charge
transfer and mass transport loss. The higher temperature applied, the clearer the trend is.

Chapter 7:
Conclusion and Future Work
The work in this thesis focused on understanding the influence different parameters have on High
Temperature Polymer Electrolyte Membrane Fuel Cells (HT-PEMFCs) degradation and
phosphoric acid loss. Three main experiments were conducted focusing on:
1. Effects of temperature: 160, 180 and 200 °C
2. Effects of current density: 200, 400 and 800 mA cm−2
3. Effects of lambda air (gas stoichiometry): 5, 10 and 20
The MEAs evaluated, Dapozol®, are provided by Blue World Technologies. Analysis are based on
data from durability curves, polarization curves, ohmic loss and charge transfer and mass transport
loss. The ohmic loss are measured using a tool called electrochemical impedance spectroscopy
(EIS), while charge transfer and mass transport loss are estimated from polarization curves. Acid
loss are assumed to be related mostly to ohmic loss, while other degradation mechanisms causing
catalyst degradation is assumed to be related to charge transfer and mass transport loss. BoL
(Beginning of Life) curves shows the importance of testing MEAs from the same batch when
comparing them under different conditions.
Evaluation of temperature effects are based on six protocols, increasing the temperature from
160 °C to 180 °C and to 200 °C. In order to get a good set of data to base the result on, the
tests are conducted at a current density of 200 mA cm−2 and 800 mA cm−2. The general trend
is: increasing the temperature lead to faster degradation, increasing ohmic loss, and increasing
charge transfer and mass transport loss. Ohmic loss are related to phosphoric acid loss, most
likely because of acid evaporation and dehydration. Since the charge transfer and mass transport
loss are increasing as well, the degradation can not be isolated to be caused by acid loss, but
catalyst degradation may also be present.
Evaluation of current density effects are based on six protocols increasing the current density
from 200 to 400 and to 800 mAcm−2. These effects are tested at 200 °C, and again at 180 °C to
further support the trends. The general trend observed: Higher magnitude of current density lead
to faster degradation of the cells. EIS measurements of ohmic loss indicates that acid loss is a
mechanism contributing to the degradation, probably because of acid moving from the membrane
54
==PAGE:63==
to the electrodes, and hence increase the resistance. Specially for the test conducted at 200 °C,
the charge transfer and mass transport loss are higher than the ohmic loss, indicating degradation
related to the catalyst. From the results in this work, the trends are more ”extreme” at higher
temperature, indicating that this parameter has influence on the results.
Evaluation of lambda effects are based on only three protocols, increasing the lambda value from
5, to 10, and to 20. The degradation, ohmic loss, and charge transfer and mass transport loss where
highest for lambda 20, as expected, then for lambda 5 and 10. The increasing ohmic loss indicates
that acid loss is a cause of degradation, most likely due to acid evaporation. Since the degradation
increased more for lambda 5 than for lambda 10, it is hard to come to a conclusive trend for this
experiment.
First of all, future work should include repeating the experiments to verify results. In order to
make the comparisons more reliable, the cells should be tested simultaneously and measurements
conducted at exact same days for all cells. Specifically for lambda effects more testing protocols
needs to be tested to find a clear trend. Post test analysis such as Scanning Electron Microscopy
(SEM), Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM), X-Ray diffraction analysis (XRD) and titration
were not conducted in this work due to time limitations, but should be done to further investigate
the state of the MEAs and what aspects causing the degradation.
A method that was not utilized in this work was quantifying PA from anode and cathode exhaust
gases. Yu. S et al. [36] connected two bottles of distilled water to the anode and cathode outlets of
HT-PEMFC in order to quantify the PA leaching from the FC. By analyzing the PA in water they
found that during steady-state long-term operation, the PA loss rate was a few µg cm−2. Future
work should include this to further quantify the amount of acid loss during operation.
