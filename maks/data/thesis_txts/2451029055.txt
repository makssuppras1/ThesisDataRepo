Abstract
This Master thesis investigates the relation between inventory optimization and complexity
management on component inventory. The research is case-based, investigating a leading
supplier of audio medical devises, Demant A/S, more specifically the brand in focus is
Oticon. The case study is focusing on packaging product families due to its high product
variety. The large product portfolio within packaging has resulted in an extended amount
of SKUs with low or no profitability.
Moreover, a minimal focus within the packaging
category has further increased the number of low-profitability products. The large variety
of products increase the amount of stock needed due to an order-to-stock policy. By reducing
Demant’s packaging portfolio it is assumed that cost cutting can be achieved and complexity
decreased. This thesis is focusing on analyzing packaging complexity shipped from Demant’s
Polish production site. Unexpectedly high levels of lacking data or low data quality were
found, adding emphasis to the need for applying complexity management tools.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter provides an introduction to the project and the problem faced by Demant.
Additionally, the company Demant and its subsidy, Oticon, is introduced, and the scope of
the project is stated.
1.1
The project
In this section, the project is presented alongside an in-depth description of the scoping
processes. The various product families included in the scope are showcased. Lastly, some
of the current processes are established.
1.1.1
Introduction to Problem
Within the last decade, Demant has experienced growth both in terms of FTEs and products
offered. In the meanwhile, the packaging category has primarily been left unattended. Con-
sequently, in the last couple of years, Demant and its sub-brand, Oticon, have experienced
losing control regarding the packaging of their equipment. Demant’s digital management
systems have not been updated comprehensively when new SKUs have been introduced.
Opposing, SKUs have generally not been removed when they have become obsolete. Oticon
is experiencing a decreasing profit margin increase, as profit is not increasing at the same
rate as previously. [1] Therefore, extra emphasis is put on containing unnecessary costs.
Moreover, Oticon has a decentralized power structure. This results in Oticon having almost
no control of packaging in several countries. Some countries repack the shipments before
supplying them to the customers. This can be a compromising situation as items are being
branded as Oticon. However, the local packaging does not necessarily fulfill corporate stan-
dards, as well as legal requirements. [2] Oticon’s primary goal is to regain complete control
over their complexity and product portfolio. Obsolete products are not removed from the
various systems, which add great complexity. At the moment, Oticon has low transparency

and limited control over their data. This issue is occurring because data is either not cor-
rectly migrated or consolidated. Additionally, many data-sets have missing information due
to lacking governance within the organization.
1.1.2
Introduction to the project
The goal of this project is to assist in creating an overview of Demant’s product portfolio
within packaging solutions for Oticon’s brand and no-brand packaging. Complexity man-
agement within the packaging is an area, which has not been investigated extensively. One
of the goals in this report is to identify and possibly modify already established methods and
techniques to cope with this type of complexity. Hence it should investigate how packaging
complexity costs can be identified, quantified, and mitigated. Oticon’s packaging category
has more than 14.000 SKUs, thereby being prone to arising complexity. For this project,
limited product families have been chosen, due to resource constraints; when the initial
analysis of the data is conducted scenarios are created, which should increase profit and
reduce complexity. Oticon has issues with demand and stock management, as they for some
SKUs have several hundred percents more in stock than what is needed. In this project, it
will be of great importance, to look into stock management, and give an estimate on how
the stock can be reduced to free up financial resources to be reinvested elsewhere. The area
of Mass Customization will also be investigated as a possibility to solve some of the issues
Oticon has presented. The project aims to reduce overall complexity, and a configurator
could force Oticon to regularly update their product portfolio, as their customers would have
direct contact with it, thus making it vital that their customers do not have the possibility
to chose obsolete products.
1.1.3
Research Question & Hypothesis
Due to increasing cost and an enhanced complexity awareness in Demant, the packaging
area is experiencing an increasing focus. Among others, focus comes from the operations
team. The packaging category has previously been loosely regulated. Therefore, from initial
analyses, the operations team has the hypothesis that,
H1: complexity can be cut within the packaging category.
Moreover, the team believes that,
H2: complexity cutting can lead to cost-saving initiatives.
The hypotheses are used to formulate the following research question:
==PAGE:16==
1.1 The project
3
What is the effect of applying complexity management initiatives within inventory for
Oticon packaging products?
The reason for basing this thesis on a broad research area is the lack of existing knowledge
and data transparency within the packaging category. The positive influence of research in
the project in a more exploratory way is the adaptability gained. The negative effect is the
extended fluffy period in the early phases. Moreover, it is essential that the process is still
guided, even though the research question is loosely formulated. This is to ensure research
integrity of the project. This project is very data-driven, and the amount and quality of
data will define the path to success. Non-the less it will be investigated if overall complexity
can be reduced, if it is not possible to quantify the cost savings potential.
1.1.4
Scoping of the Project
The scoping process is done in collaboration with the project’s supervisor and two key
personnel from Demant, namely Paul Wiberg and Heidi Gade. Paul represents the interests
of operations and production, while Heidi represents the interests of marketing and branding.
By including all three stakeholders in the scoping process, the best outcome in terms of
feasibility and applicability is ensured.
The scope of this project is narrowed down to
contain Oticon and no-brand SKUs in the area of packaging.
As a result of a limited
number of SKUs, between 450-500, will be considered for the analysis. For a thesis around
400 SKUs are considered a reasonable amount, due to the potential complexity that occurs
while trying to locate necessary data for immature business areas. It is also a fair number
of SKUs to investigate within the project period, as more SKUs would demand more time.
Even though Demant is a mature global corporation, certain business areas have been left
mainly unattended for more extended periods. The unattentiveness is, among other reasons,
due to high-profit margins, combined with limited resources. The scope is limited to direct
packaging. In other words, immediate packaging is all Oticon parts being used, to distribute
a product to a customer and further to the end user. Furthermore, in terms of markets
considered the global scope is Europe and Asia. The range is further narrowed down to
focus only on shipments from one production site. The site included is the polish production
site. The polish site is handling the vast majority of all packaging material associated with
Oticon products. Lastly, any labeling or medical SKUs are not considered part of this scope.
Working with medical devices requires greater caution than regular consumer goods. As a
result, anything that is regarded as a medical device or pharmaceutical product requires a
legislative procedure, like a label. Consequently, these are considered out of this thesis’s

will also include areas of mass customization, as mass customization provides useful tools

2.1.1
What is Complexity
Complexity overall, comes in three different forms, Product Complexity, Process Com-
plexity and Organizational Complexity.
Complexity management is the structured
method for managing increasing complexity as a result of some degree of business growth.
Due to the nature of complexity, the increase in complexity cost is not linearly following
non-complexity costs. This is due to the three-faced nature of complexity. Often, complex-
ity creeps over time as a result of variants added, new departments created, sourcing from
multiple suppliers, etc. This results in large, if not all, profits being swallowed by complex-
ity costs. The task of reducing complexity can be long and tedious. However, it is often a
necessary means to create a strong and healthy company, with room for good complexity
growth. There are six principles when considering complexity.
• Principle 1: There is good complexity and bad complexity (reduce the bad and make
the good less expensive to deliver)
• Principle 2: Complexity is a multi-dimensional issue (and must be viewed as such to
be understood in its fullness)
• Principle 3: Piecemeal approaches will not move the needle on cost reduction (don’t
nibble at the edges of the issue)
• Principle 4: Unlocking the benefits requires “concurrent actions”
• Principle 5: Complexity costs “creep in” incrementally, but you need to remove them
in chunks
• Principle 6: This need not be a long, academic exercise
[5]
2.1.1.1
Product Complexity
Product complexity is the complexity that arises from an increasing variety and numbers
of products within, e.g., a company. This increase in complexity often occurs as a result of
the efforts done to meet customer demands and needs.

2.1.1.2
Process Complexity
Process complexity is the complexity that origins from increasing operational elements. This,
for example, is all the non-value-adding activities that are conducted to create products.
Bad process complexity includes duplicate work, coordination, rework, etc.
2.1.1.3
Organizational Complexity
Organizational complexity develops from the way a company or organization is structured;
this can, for example, be an increasing amount of departments or teams. It can also arise
from increasing decision levels or elements such as staffing and policies.

• ABC analysis of products

unhealthy variety. The initiatives can be divided into to categories, being short-term, mid-
term, and long-term scenarios. Short-term initiatives are usually referred to as fast gains

3.1
Five Step Procedure of Complexity Reduction
The methodological framework used for this thesis is a five-step procedure for complexity
reduction based on quantification of complexity costs. Iterations are used to quantify the
complexity costs and finding suitable initiatives to reduce complexity. The five steps are as
followed:
1. Define the scope of the products and processes to include in the analysis
2. ABC analysis of products
3. Identification and quantification of the most significant complexity cost factors
4. Identification and quantification of possible initiatives for the reduction of complexity
costs
5. Evaluation and prioritization of initiatives and establishment of the complexity cost
reduction program
The first step is to scope, or delimit, the analysis. It has to be established what products
and product families it will make sense to work with, as well as, what level of finished
products that should be considered, markets, etc. It is in this phase that the availability of
the resources is evaluated in relation to time and data. Additionally, a suitable scope for
the project is established.
In the second step, the products are divided into categories following the Pareto principle.
The products are classified in A, B and C products where A products account for 80% of
==PAGE:45==
3.1 Five Step Procedure of Complexity Reduction
32
the revenue and contribution margin, B products account for 15% and C products account
for the last 5%. If a product falls out of one of these groups, it will be listed to the strongest
clustering.
In the third step, the most significant complexity factors are identified. There are some
rules to be obliged by in order for a cost to be considered a complexity cost. First of all,
it has to be of significant size, e.g., 1% of the total cost. The cost should also be unevenly
distributed. Otherwise, it will not be possible to tell the impact of removing one product
compared to another.
Examples of such can be inventory, freight, component sourcing,
packaging, etc.
[6] In this step, the complexity costs are also analyzed, quantified, and
allocated.
In the fourth step, the purpose is to identify and quantify possible initiatives, which
should reduce the complexity costs. From the knowledge gained in step 2-3, possible so-
lutions should be created.This is to reduce costs. Following, the solutions are categorized
as either short-, mid, or long term solutions. Short term solutions, and to some extend
mid-term solutions or initiatives, are more low-hanging gains. To exemplify, this can be
done by trimming the product portfolio and finding suitable substitutions for the products
being cut out. It is crucial to quantify savings separately for each of the initiatives, as well
as the costs of pursuing such a project.
The fifth and last step in the framework is characterized by prioritization and evaluation
of initiatives. A plan on how to implement the initiatives, identify complexity cost drivers,
and how to reduce complexity costs based on these is to be created. It is also vital to note
reflections on the effect caused by implementing these initiatives. How is forward seeing the
given initiative? Is it challenging to implement? How much uncertainty is accompanied by
the implementation? Finally, to what extent is it possible to actually eliminates or mitigates
complexity and the quantified complexity costs derived from step two?

PVM - Product Variant Master
It can be challenging to manage and keep an overview of products and their variety for many
companies. In order to manage all these varieties comprehensively and create an overview,
a tool called product variant master is used. Every single part used in the product portfolio
is a part of the PVM. A PVM is a structural approach to identify the parts used to create
the finished products and thereby creating a better overview [6]. The PVM is divided into
different types and consists of different attributes, which explains the nature of the parts.
The three types are:
• Customer View
All necessary information needed from the customer
• Part View
An overview of every single part
• Engineering view
How each part is constructed in details
For the customer view, the PVM is created with information that is relevant for a
customer, which could be a list of features and options or applications [6]. The engineering
view focuses more on the functional structure, and the functional variety [6]. The part view
can be described as The physical realization of the product [6].” which is a physical structure
(e.g., BOM) and a set of combination and rules; if item part a is chosen, parts B and C
cannot be used, or specific types can only be used. The picture below illustrates the PVM
of a bike to show a simple example.

5.2
Limitations
There are several limitations in the process of reducing complexity in Oticon packaging. One
of the major ones is the lack of data, or at least the structure and transparency of it. The
data regarding cost, resource consumption, storage time, etc. is minimal, and if it exists, it is
difficult to extract as Oticons internal system, QAD, is not frequently nor comprehensively
updated. This hurdle makes it challenging to create initiatives for improvements, as the
decision making is not backed up by comprehensively and accurate data, and inherently
is accommodated with a lot of risks.
The other major limitation is the time constraint
regarding the period of this thesis project. Another apparent weakness is resource allocation
limitation. Demant does naturally not have unlimited resources both concerning FTEs and
money to put into cleaning up and managing to package. This does, of course, affects the
potential solution space. Moreover, this analysis only considers part of Demant’s packaging;
the only warehouse site viewed is site Poland, only Oticon products are included, and only
a hand full of all packaging product variants are considered. This limit the comprehensive
assessment of Demant’s collected packaging optimization potential. Additionally, the lack
of value categorization on individual packaging level hinders the creation of traditional ABC
analyses to be conducted. The limited knowledge of product lead times and costs associated
with the products reduce the accuracy of the investigation. Lastly, the lack of proper product
descriptions, such as sizing, color, etc. removed the potential for quantifying, i.e., product
substitution.
5.3
Future Recommendations
It is recommended that Demant conducts a complexity management analysis on all its sub-
brands to get a collected understanding of where complexity arises. Moreover, creating clear
policies for how, when, and where SKUs are added and removed can help mitigate future
complexity increase [6]. Furthermore, looking into changing the customer order specification
decoupling point could be beneficial. At the moment, Oticon uses assemble-to-order, as
==PAGE:98==
5.3 Future Recommendations
85
they have stock, and whenever they receive an order, they will assemble the necessary parts
needed, and ship it. It could be of relevance to change this decoupling point in a given
scenario, and determine if it could either make a job more comfortable or save money or
capacity. For now, Oticon has a lot of waste when they fulfill an order, and one solution
could be having complete packages ready. Moving the decoupling point is also constrained
by the complexity of the products. As Oticon does not have elaborate packaging, and many
of the parts are considered fairly commodities, moving the decoupling point is doable; if the
products were more complex, like a flight engine, using make-to-stock could be an expensive
solution. From a more practical approach, it is also recommended that Demant encompass
structure problem-solving tools into the common work-ways. A method that with benefit
can be utilized going forward by the operations and marketing team in union are A3. A3
is a method known from LEAN principles. The problem-solving approach originates from
Toyota. The tale of the name is that back in the days, one department would solve a specific
problem. To make sure other departments wouldn’t conduct the same issues or would know
how to solve them in case they would occur, the problem solution would be shared in between
departments. The problem solution would be shared using fax, which maximum size was an
A3, hence the name. As written, A3 is a structured problem-solving method consisting of
eight steps. The first step is to write down an initial problem description. After that, in step
two, the problem is made more specific. Preferable data is used when possible. Step three
is establishing the point of Cause. After that, a temporary containment of the problem is
done. This is done to ensure that the problem does not grow while the problem is being
further dissected. Next, a fishbone diagram is being used to map out the cause and effect.
The most likely causes are looked into further in step 6. The method 5xwhy is used to get
to the origin of the problem. Once the underlying cause is established, step seven is begun.
Step seven is permanent solutions. Lastly, but equally important sharing of knowledge and
solutions are done.
From an academic point of view, it could be recommended to look at the inventory
packaging area at Oticon as well as other companies through the lens of project management
and change management. By doing a multi-case retrospective analysis, areas of concern can
be identified. The area of packaging is currently not an area that has been explored greatly.
Therefore, creating a broader case study could help companies gain a better understanding
and provide a tool for managing their packaging. This is both a cost and environmental
benefit.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
From the original 14.000 SKUs within the entire packaging category, the scoping decreased
the total SKUs considered to be 449.
This project was scoped to only work with non-
MD Oticon packaging within a particular set of product families. The main issue was the
increasing complexity, as the category has not been updated, and Oticon has lost control of
what SKUs they have in their catalog, and how many of them that have gone obsolete. A five-
step analysis, proposed by [17] was used to scope, identify, and mitigate the problem. From
the analysis conducted complexity reduction areas were established. The initial hypothesis
regarding the possibility for cutting complexity within the packaging category was found
to be true.
Several initiatives were explored.
Using the principles of cutting the tail a
total of 449 SKUs were narrowed down to between 67 and 125 active SKUs. Moreover,
the initiates yielded cost reductions, which was quantified in complexity cost.
The two
most significant initiatives were utilising a stock management policy and reducing double
packaging by implementing a configurator linking orders to shipments. Currently, two sets of
packaging are sent even if the same end-user is receiving binaural hearing aids. Through the
utilization of a configurator Oticon can reduce costs with about 18.2-36.4 mDKK depending
on the percentage of users ordering binaural. It is estimated that more than 80% of the
world’s hearing aid users requires a dual hearing aid set. The stock reduction scenario proved
more challenging to work with, as there is a massive lack of information and data. One of the
main parameters to re-calculate the stock is the re-calculation of the safety stock. The issue
is that the lead time for orders s unknown, which makes it impossible to calculate the safety
factor. From literature it is found that a rough estimate of 20% SF can be used, resulting
in a one-off realistic saving of 3.8 mDKK. It has to be emphasized that this estimate is very
rough, and the consequences of outcome is highly uncertain. What it did show is that there
is a cost saving potential. The precise magnitude is unknown. It can be concluded that there
is a savings potential in improving the stock management. To conclude, H1 and H2 were
found to be be valid and the effect of applying complexity management initiatives within
for packaging products at Oticon was quantified. Not only does the initiatives impose a
==PAGE:100==

financial stick, the data transparency is likely to increase if the initiatives are implemented.
It is of the highest importance that the data transparency is regulated on a regular basis.
The fact that the SKUs have not been updated comprehensively, and the data transparency
has been low, has been a key factor of the rising complexity.
