Summary
In many countries around the world, ready to adopt the electronic-based ticket system in their
public transportation and some countries already introduced that. Because of this, the paper-
based ticketing may be phased out in future. More speciﬁcally, the Oyster card in England,
Ez-link in Singapore and some other ticketing systems are examples of how has been satisﬁed
both the people and the public transport companies by the electronic ticketing (e-ticketing) usage.
The e-ticketing system provides many advantages to the public transport authority and their
customers, e.g. fast and compatible veriﬁcation, replacement facilities for the lost ticket, etc.
and at the same time, is increasing forgeries activities by using cryptographic mechanisms. In
practice, most of the e-ticketing systems are based on RFID technologies. In this technology can
establish an authentication process by publishing temporal data that shows customer-related
sensitive data e.g.customer location.
Furthermore, obtainable written works for RFID technology-based on privacy enhancement pro-
tocols have insuﬃcient practicability for the current world scenarios. In this paper, we discussed
relevant privacy and security requirements for the e-ticketing system and noticed the weakness
of current systems. After that, we suggested solutions for actual privacy-enhancement e-tickets
based on studied cryptographic techniques

Introduction
In the current world, public transport ticketing attends to become electronic ticketing (e-ticket).
Many countries are ready to execute such system. E-tickets provide many advantages to users
as well as involve certain risks.
Beneﬁts of e-tickets can be seen from public transport authority’s perspective and user’s per-
spective. In the ﬁrst case, e-tickets can help to reduce the maintenance cost and they are more
diﬃcult to fake because of cryptographic techniques used. From a user’s perspective, e-ticket
utilization is normally automated and one e-ticket can be used multiple times. Being digital also
makes a ticketing system have plenty of settings. In this way, users can save time and choose
a fare according to their preferences. Additionally, a user can reclaim an e-ticket if he lost or
broke it, and his balance will be saved.
There are also risks involved, especially regarding the privacy of users. When a user buys an e-
ticket, he might be requested to submit personal information such as User’s name, address, date
of birth, etc. Based on the functions of an e-ticketing system, the sensitive user’s information
might be approachable by di↵erent parties, including those which are not un-authorized, if the
information is not correctly secured.The latter can abuse the user’s information in such a way
that it will do harm to both the ticketing system and authorized users (for example, by cloning
an authorized user’s e-ticket). Furthermore, some users would like to protect against informa-
tion about them (such as users travelling patterns) being available to any party. In addition, the
issues related to protocols, that used protocols may be face to various attacks in the e-ticketing
system, for example, replay attack, man-in-middle attack. In this report, we will study relations
between security and user privacy and discuss how these aspects can be combined in one digital
system.
1.1
Motivation
Nowadays, most of the ticketing system for public transport is becoming as smart transport sys-
tems by a↵ecting the ubiquitous computing. The front -end of the e-ticketing system for public
transport is basically based on RFID/NEC technologies (more details see section 3.5). Such
these technologies based on e-ticketing systems have already presented in around the world, es-
pecially big cities, for example, oyster system (London )[7], EZ-link system (Singapore)[3], etc.
Moreover, the recent advance functionality of mobile technology accepting a valid e-ticket veri-
ﬁcation through the smart-phones (NFC technology-based phone). This progress has opened a
new platform for e-ticketing for the public transport system and at the same time creating like
e-ticketing systems even more ubiquitous computing.
The growing smart e-ticketing systems for public transport provide several beneﬁts and same
time concerns rise up about the user’s privacy. Especially, using e-ticket based on NFC tech-
nology smart card or smart-phone is going to deﬁnitely increase the digital tracks (by the users
leaving sensitive information on smart devices). In case, if not involved in privacy protection
techniques or mechanisms, it will become big issues. Until now, this issue has not been enough
protection mechanisms. The suggest educational solutions proposed still are either based on ex-
tra suggestions or are too diﬃcult to be uniﬁed into the real system. Furthermore, the underlying
trade-o↵s between privacy protection and privacy eﬃciency playing a big challenge for building
the actual system. Moreover, most of the academic solutions have a negative result rejection of
ignoring some main features of the e-ticketing for the public transport system. However, it also
provides several advantages, these are ﬁne-grained billing, easy to use, for ﬂexible price scheme,
etc. Thus, to fully protect the e-ticketing for public transport a privacy solution is extremely
required.

1.2
Main goal of the work
The main goal of this work is to build up an infrastructure for an e-ticketing system based
on loosely coupled privacy-preserving which supports for local validation of e-tickets and ﬁne-
grained billing process. Loosely-coupled building design describes that terminal can carry out
check-in/out process in the front-end without contact to the back-end. This is an important
need because of it to a wide-reaching deﬁnes the real-life applicability of an e-ticketing system.
According to our main goal of this thesis, the particular some research questions have been ob-
tained for this project, these are summarized as follows-
Q1. How to establish the terminal side of privacy-preserving local validation
1. Valid e-tickets stay on anonymous to the terminal
2. Fake /invalid e-tickets are rejected.
Q2.How to accept for privacy preserving travel records procedure in the back-end
1. The ﬁne-grained billing process is possible for the registered e-tickets.
2. The customer identiﬁcation is protected.
Main challenges
The above research questions arise the following challenges for system developers- Cha 1. In the
public transport system, terminals are very largely placed and thus it can not be fully trusted
to handle sensitive information to e-tickets and users (for avoiding privacy issues). Therefore, it
should be feasible for terminals to authenticate e-tickets and to verify their legality entry without
able to identify them. Cha 2. In order to allow ﬁne-grained billing process for genuine cus-
tomers, the customer’s various rides patterns have to be correlated in a proper way. However, this
has privacy suggestion, because of an e-ticket could be included in illegal tracking (accordingly
e-ticket owners) [6]
1.3
Main contribution
The main contribution of this thesis is to establish an e-ticketing based privacy enhancement
framework against the privacy-related issues. This system builds based on ﬁne-grained billing
and ﬂexible pricing scheme. Therefore, the public transport authority (PTA) would be able to
completely use the entire potential of such systems. And, our suggested solution covers privacy
protection from the particular system design. Moreover, in compare to some other solutions,
ﬁnally the suggested framework is based on a loosely-coupled architecture suggest that terminal
does not carry out check-in/out process in the front-end without contact to the back-end. The
main some of contribution of the thesis are listed below -
1. Design and evaluation of a framework agree to develop privacy-preserving e-ticketing sys-
tems for public transport.
• The advanced techniques make able for the implementation of ﬁne-grained billing and
transparent price schemes in a common way.
• At the same time, privacy properties can be maintained.
2. Categorization of the ﬁeld with aspect to privacy enhancement in the build of public transport
systems based on e-ticketing was provided.
In this thesis we learning the levels of privacy that could be reached in an e-ticket system.
We mention the weakness of the solutions and take a look at how familiar cryptographic tools
can be applied to understand anonymization of RIFD technology based on e-tickets. At same
time, retain the main goal is to secure a usable system that make sure no sensitive information
disclosure on the user (e.g. user location, etc.)

1.4
Deﬁne the context
With the e-tickets we have the increase of security, against fraud and cloning of the card, but
we have to pay an expensive prize for privacy. For example, the electronic card can record the
passengers’ travel history and this can reduce the fraud at the expensive of privacy.
The e-ticket systems are supported by the ISO 14443 standard for NFC (Near Field Com-
munication). For these chip-cards, the semi-conductor industry o↵ers so-called SAM modules,
which can be installed in public transport terminals, as a contact device. These SAMs (Secure
Access Module) are cryptography devices, which store keys in a non-readable form and perform
cryptographic operations. The cryptographic processes are not restricted to single transactions:
• Securing data exchange between partners in an e-ticket system association
• Ticket purchase using a card reader at the PC
• Personalisation of chip-cards during creation
• Creation of printable paper tickets, with 2D Barcodes (e.g. train tickets)
All these processes use cryptographic methods, whether signing exchanged data, or protecting
transport keys during personalisation.

2.1
Rejsekort system
Being the information collecting stage of the project work, a meeting with the Rejsekort represen-
tative was arranged [8] As claimed by the representative, the full documentation is not available
to the public as it is mostly made up of private enterprise documents. However, the main idea of
functions was presented and general questions (regarding history, business, decision reasoning)
were answered. This subsection will summarize the information obtained in the meeting.
The Rejsekort system can be visualized as three communicating elements: a user’s card (e-ticket),
a reader (terminals check-in or check-out) and back-end node. A user’s starts to communicate
with the system by introducing his card to a reader. Being granted with valid read-key and
write-key the reader is able to write data from a user’s card and read data to it actually. The
card itself can be considered a memory-card, which stores unencrypted data about user’s jour-
neys followed by a digital signature as well as additional parameters (such as the detail of being
checked by an inspector, for instance) for communicating with the rest of the system. The cal-
culations are carried out by the reader. There are two reasons for this. The ﬁrst one is reduced
card expenses, as a card with a more advanced chip would have a higher cost. The second reason
is that Rejsekort sets a time required for operation. For example, check-in should take around
200-300 ms [27].
In a particular interval, a reader sends which data was read from the di↵erent cards to the
back-end system. In short, the back-end node is responsible for organizing a journey out of
various transactions, storing the data and checking it for thickness and possible frauds [27].
As one can observe, the Rejsekort solution is focused on eﬃciency and security, while privacy-
enhancement is missed out. As it was shown by the representative, the back-end node stores
detailed data regarding journeys/transactions, which can be linked to each other as well as with
the card holder’s identity. This enables fraud-detecting techniques to work with the so-called
overall picture, rather than standalone journeys. Once fraud is detected, the related card will be
blocked and its holder - persecuted. The representative stated that in case of card cloning one
can at most cheat the system for the maximum allowed card balance money before the fraud
is detected and measures are undertaken. Since security in real life is approached as a business
solution, the cost of defending the aforementioned asset appears to be larger than the value of the
asset, so the company’s strategy is to tolerate that kind of attack and then undertake measures
based on the back-end analysis.[27]
It is possible to conclude, that the security aspect relies heavily on the non-anonymous con-
struct of the system (i.e. processing plain detailed data). The other argument against privacy-
preserving mentioned in the meeting is problems with implementing an anonymous customer
service.
Moreover, the travel history can be regarded as less sensitive data, some people may still be
against third parties having knowledge about their location. The short description of the Re-
jsekort system was given. Clearly, there exist privacy issues like in any non-anonymous system.

EZ-link system
The EZ-link card is a contactless card, used for the payment of public transportation fares in
Singapore, since 2001. The card can be also used for Electronic Road Pricing (ERP), Electronic
Parking System (EPS), printing and photocopying services, payment at selected food & beverage

and has a lifespan of 5 years.
Until September 2017, the card allows the EZ-Online service. This service allowed passengers to
see their card details, including the most recent 30 transactions (travel records) download dis-

service. For this purpose, they consider the implementations in Figure 1. They used a CEPAS-

The authors examine if a CEPAS-compliant EZ-Link card leaks any sensitive or personal in-
formation related to the card owner by performing real top-up.
For this, the authors used

to intercept messages between the PC and the EZ-Link server.
Based on this observation, the information of travellers is transmitted between the reader and
the PC, in clearly. Thus, it is able to trace someone’s records. For example, carrying an EZ-Link
reader and walk close enough to an EZ-Link card, it is then possible to retrieve the target’s

3.5.1
Radio Frequency Identiﬁcation (RFID)
The term RFID includes the whole of contactless identiﬁcation system. This small electronic de-
vices that be composed of a small chip and an antenna. The chip basically is capable of managing
2,000 bytes of data or less. The RFID device work for the same purpose as a magnetic stripe or
a bar code on the credit card, it provides a unique identiﬁer for that object. But, di↵erent with
these two objects functions are, a bar code or magnetic strip basically used to get information,
the RFID device normally used to retrieve the identifying information. The RFID has main
three parts, an antenna (scanning device), a transceiver (with a decoder to clarify the data)
and a transponder (the RFID tag or chip - that has been programmed with information). The
antenna is scanned devices places out radio frequency signals in a comparably short range. The
radio frequency does two main things, it organizes a means of interaction with the transponder
(The RFID tag or chip) and It also provides the RFID tag with the strength to communicate (
passive RFID tags). This is a perfectly key pattern of technology. The RFID tags do not need
to contain batteries and can usable for very long time periods.
When an RFID tag passes through that in the radio frequency scanning antenna, it discover
and activation the signal from the antenna. That RFID chip transmits the information on its
chip to be collected by the antenna. Nowadays, RFID technologies are using many places, for
example, public transport system, troll system, ﬁtness centre, etc.
Globally, most of the RFID technologies based smart card are used in e-ticketing systems, namely
• MIFARE family - used this technology in the following e-ticketing systems: London’s Oyster
Card, the Puget Sound ORCA Card in the US, Dutch OV-chipcard. In the world more
than 80 % of contactless smart cards are based on MIFARE technology [45].
• Calypso standard - Used this technology in Calypso e-ticketing system (China, France,
Belgium, Portugal, Canada, Israel, Italy, etc.)[2].
3.5.2
Near Field Communication(NFC)
The one of the method of wireless data communication called NFC that detects and then per-
mits technology close vicinity to communicate without an internet connection. It’s very eﬃcient,
easy and works automatically. This technology is now widely using many places, especially in
smart phones functions. e.g. contactless payments via mobile phones. NFC is a technology
closely related to RFID technology. This technology contains a protocol and an interface which
is developed on top of RFID and this is the reason that NFC device is agreeable with existing
RFID technology.
NFC is a short range based wireless technology which works in globally accessible unlicensed
of 13.56 MHz radio frequency. The reason of this short range, because it is designed for the

3.6
Preliminary threat description
Clearly, the main goal of the project is to prevent unauthorized users from using the e-ticketing
system. Therefore, some notable attacks aim to violate this goal. In this section, we are going
to analyse that kind of subtle attacks as follows-
Impersonation - this attack against e-ticket systems is activated by unauthorized parties. The
adversary must get or imitate an e-ticket that is recognized by a trusted user. To reach this,
the adversary may carry out several attacks like replay attacks or man-in-middle attacks against
the mutual authentication protocols. Otherwise, they may try to create a fake e-ticket or try to
copy e-ticket of the right users.
Tracing -this subtle attack main aim is gathering users information and observing users ac-
tivities/movements in the public transport system. For example, the public transport companies
may be interested to observe their user activities in the public transport network. That reason
is, an e-ticket can be easily identiﬁed that’s right user by using the authentication protocol ver-
iﬁcation. Moreover, it enables to ﬁnd e-tickets inside the network. Furthermore, if a user used
his credit card to buy an e-ticket or top-up money his control (identifying payment method), the
issuer or employer can combine the e-ticket to the identity of its owner. In this case, more espe-
cially, the issuer and payment veriﬁers are in the same network, therefore, the complete system
is under the control of the same individual, this e↵ect is an entire loss of the user’s privacy.
Moreover, e-tickets basically are based on RFID technology, thus wireless devices are using in the
network for data transfer and thus entire data communication can be eavesdropped by unknown
adversaries. Furthermore, the adversary may imperceptibly communicate with e-tickets. As a
result, the user’s e-ticket may be traced by external entities (e.g. di↵erent issuer or veriﬁer).
In short, a basic goal is that e-ticket system prohibits disclosure of users information and their
activities or movements to none trusted external entities.
Denial-of-service - in this attack, an adversary may need to harm the network by stopping
trusted users from using the network. As above mentioned, the RFID based wireless devices
that can be attacked imperceptibly. This means that an adversary may try to ﬁnd the weakness
of authentication protocol such that kind of e-ticket is no longer validated by the veriﬁer. Based
on the fundamental business model, the e-tickets protocol must be design to prohibit this kind
of attacks.
In the context of the current project, it makes sense to categories all attacks into two large
groups.
1. Attacks on the system. These attacks are carried out in order to abuse the system. The
following can work for as examples: replay attacks, exploiting the poor design of a system,
cloning users’ cards, etc.
2. Attacks on the information. The motivation of these attacks is not an advantageous
use of the utilized system itself. These attacks aim at data spread in the system during
==PAGE:19==
12
its normal utilization. This data can be both user personal information and information
related to the system.
With this data in possession, an attacker can attempt a more
powerful attack.
As our project main aim is to design a solution that prevents operators from assigning detailed
usage proﬁles to user identities while at the same time allows for convenient billing and attractive
fares. For this reason, we will mainly focus on the privacy-enhancement aspect of the project.
This means that privacy against authorized parties is the point of interest, even though an au-
thorized party getting access to user personal data is not an attack in its classic understanding.
Moreover, attacks from the second group above will also be prohibited against. Based on the
ﬁrst group, we can say that the system will be approached from a privacy-enhancement point
in such a way that it still secure and protected from typical attacks on the system [27]. For the
attacker model please see section 4.5.
In this way, we will not be re-ﬁnding cryptographic techniques against attacks, for example,
man-in-the-middle attack. We will instead depend on already developed mechanisms which have

are not that straightforward for privacy-preserving. After studying several existing e-ticketing
systems in di↵erent countries, we came to a conclusion that security is what most solutions
mainly take care of, while privacy-preserving has a lower priority and, in general, tends to be

• Pseudonymity (integrity) - is useful to enable the communicating entities to perform the

(e.g. the authorised to use the respective message decryption key)
• Unlinkability (availability)- is protect a malicious third party from acting interconnection
of di↵erent pieces of information which apply to a certain entity and are shared in time or
space and therefore from illegally obtaining the entity’s personally identiﬁable information.
Above the set of three concepts determine the basic technical aspects of the user’s privacy and
can be used. Therefore, it can be used as the required basis for the privacy properties of the

4.4.2
Privacy Against External Observers
Most of the attacks targeted at privacy violation are conducted by external entities not directly
involved in the information system. The protection of privacy against such kind of attackers is
especially intense in the front-end system due to the terminals and contactless communication
between e-tickets and terminals. Thus, the external entity should be not able to derive any
user-identifying or tracking user information by observing communication between terminals
and e-tickets. This implies retaining a front-end secure against privacy attacks on any possible
communication between users of the system and its front-end, e.g., the terminals used for starting
and ending a journey, to avoid interception of any kind of identifying information from that
interaction.
4.4.3
Privacy against front-end terminals
In the public transport network, the terminals are widely distributed in the entire network,
thus, they can be not always under control with the PTA. Furthermore, the wireless interface
used for e-ticket validation can be misused by unauthorized external third parties. To void that
unauthorized parties involvements in the network, the terminals are storing as little information
about for the user starting and ending a journey.
In this point, we would like to mention
concerning the user privacy, nobody should not able to track or identify any user based on their
tickets. The PTA is responsible to store as a minimum sensitive date at the terminal side as it
is possible. The previous provokes to really reduce the amount of private information disclosed
from the e-ticket to a terminal during the check- in/out sessions. Moreover, terminals must be
prevented from tracking valid e-tickets, determine between them as well as from identifying the
users combine with e-tickets.
4.4.4
Privacy against the back-end terminals
The back-end should also be restricted from tracking and identifying individual communication
between e-tickets and terminals. It is highly sensible, however, that ﬁne-grained billing enabled
at the back-end. Thus, an acceptable agreement is allowed in this case, especially the back-end
is restricted from identifying the users combined with e-tickets but it can correspond di↵erent
registered as travel records to a single e-ticket pseudonym for billing purposes.

4.4.5
Billing support
One of the main advantages of e-ticketing for the public transport system is to o↵er ﬂexible fare
prices to customers. This advantage is very useful to the public transport service to attract more
customers and to be competitive against other companies. The ﬁne-grained billing can able to
o↵er a great fair price plan according to the customer’s travel pattern (e.g.one day travel ticket -
a customer can take unlimited travel in a day with a certain amount.). In this way, the customer
can save some travel money. The ﬁne-grained billing system also provides to the customer some
additional services and accuracy fare rate. According to our research, currently, most of the e-
ticketing public transport systems are used to ﬁne-grained billing approach [4],[5]. Therefore, the
support for ﬁne-grained billing manages an important part in the process of perfectly leveraging
the possible of e-ticketing systems.
4.4.6
Fare Calculation
Another one of the important processes with regards to ﬁne-grained billing is the fare calcula-
tion. In this analyse, two main approaches can be conducted 1). fare calculation centralized
in the back-end and 2). fare calculation decentralized at the client-side [6]. The decentralized
model is considered fare calculation to the user side, which requires additional computational
elevated for customers and more demanding requirements to the reliability and complication of
the user-side equipment. The second approach is considered proﬁtable for user privacy. The
user’s pieces of sensitive information (location and time, user ID, etc.) are handled at the user
side. This information is partly disclosed to the PTA for administration purpose (e.g., location
check,etc)[37]. However, the decentralized approach main defects are the more additional de-
manding requirements for the user device and the user is responsible for maintaining the process
to fare calculation and submission result to the back-end. e.g. the user response is to connect the
card reader to the home computer, handle system malfunction, etc. the decentralized approach
suggests additional costs for users expected to gather extra pieces of hardware (RFID reader,
etc). Therefore, the centralized fare calculation model is mostly recommended for the privacy
protection in e-ticketing system by customers (easy to handle) and public transport companies
( able to make some correctness).
4.4.7
Loose-coupling
Nowadays, public transport e-ticketing systems are largely distributed and have to manage a huge
check-in and check-out process at the same time. Thus, the back-end should manage all processes
come from terminals in the entire network at a time. Practically, it can be highly unrealistic
process and maybe a very costly entire architecture. In the current world scenario, the terminals
do not maintain a continual real-time connection to the back-end but fairly receive regular
updates which do not have to be performed in real-time. Moreover, the privacy-enhancement
solutions should be initially considered for public transport e-ticketing system and must be
adaptable with loosely-coupled architecture.
4.4.8
Eﬃciency
In the current world scenario, to successfully establish a privacy-enhancement system must satisfy
with eﬃciency requirements. In the e-ticket system, it’s a very critical process to ﬁnd where
passenger check-in/check-out in the system. This event must be handled in a timely pattern
to avoid long passenger queues. In practice, the terminals must take a decision of whether to
accept or reject an e-ticket within certain time frame, for example, the London oyster card and
Singapore EZ-link system’s maximum accepted time frame are from 0.2 sec to 0.2 sec [30],[3].
4.4.9
Multilateral Security
The PTA’s main security goals are each user must be correctly used each e-ticket in the public
transport system. For example, that user’s cannot cheat and use the transport service with-
out paying the proper payment. Moreover, the functions of forging e-ticketing must to block

In the mutual authentication process, on every check-in/out should not leak any additional

Currently, there are many state-of-the-art cryptographic techniques tools available to protect the

information on each special e-ticket. The previous challenge of bootstrapping the authentication
process, it can be solved by basically checking the special certiﬁcate securely. Di↵erent from the
usual certiﬁcate-based authentication, the e-ticket cannot be traced and uniquely identiﬁed its
group signature.
5.1.2
Local Revocation
The public transport system must be granted permission to access for the valid user travel e-
ticket, at the same time the terminals must be blocked for the invalid e-tickets (which has been
in blacklisted). Thus, the terminals initially must be to check if the e-ticket has been valid or
not. In this situation, an important condition is that a valid ticket must stay on anonymous and
untraceable. One of the possible solutions would be to make use of cryptographic techniques [14].
In this situation, rather of checking if the current e-ticket has been blacklisted, it is conﬁrmed
(zero-knowledge) that a particular value stored at the e-ticket side, it has been admitted into the
terminals side white-list. The process of revocation then would basically be removing the par-
ticular value from the white-list and recalculating it as new. After that, the new white-list must
be distributed to all participated terminals in the system. An important condition is that the
terminal side white-list must be updated on each revocation. In the public transport scenario,
this technique provides it unsuitable to apply where e-tickets are for the most time o✏ine. In case
the updated white-list were dynamically delivered to a user device during check-in/check-out,
recalculating the credentials would propose additional expenses and therefore add a costly time
delay to the handling of time-critical check-in/-out.

5.1.3
Path reconstruction
Finally, in order to provide a bill, di↵erent rides have interacted to each other in such a way
that the fundamental identity of each user remains unknown to the transport authority.
In
this case following challenges should be considered, one hand, the billing fare schemes need to
be extensible and ﬂexible.
That is, a fare collection method should not approach very hard
method to the privacy-enhancement mechanism in use (for example [24]). It should choose a
very simple mechanism. Moreover, it should be able to adoptable the discount of rides. On
another hand, the billing process must be carried out a privacy-enhancement way. That is, it
should avoid directly identifying the user information and his/her behaviours.
The previous
issues propose a diﬃcult conﬂict between fare scheme ﬂexibility and user privacy. The price of
fare calculation is based on where the user check-in/check-out can be represented in the form of
a fare matrix [18]. However, the using matrix-based method leads to appreciable disadvantages,
namely billing ineﬃciency (involve a huge number of travel records) and billing inﬂexibility (to
issue a discount ticket). Therefore, we are chosen our framework based on pseudonymization
approach. Moreover, this method considered reasonable trade-o↵, namely check-in/check-out
functions are entirely unlikable and the front-end is untraceable, the back-end is able to agree
with various rides to a single pseudonym and to later apply the fare scheme for billing.

5.2.5
Billing process
As can be understood, the billing process is much di↵erent from described existing solutions. To
summarize, there is no check-in/check-out validation as such. In short, it is always billing-wise
possible to check-in or check-out, since there is no balance one-ticket to take money from, and
the bill is calculated at the end of some period [27].
This type of billing scheme might be beneﬁcial for public transport agency because calcula-
tions can be carried out o✏ine (heavy ones delegated to back-end node) and communication is
not done in real-time, so computational demands are lower for front-end. However, this unlimited
check-in/check-out policy can bear both inconveniences for users and potential dangers.

From a user perspective, one will ﬁnd it inconvenient and stressing not to be in control of how
much money is currently spent on public transport usage. Even if we assume that a user keeps
track of his journeys and corresponding expenses, there is still a chance for the system to have a
bug and compute the cost of a journey wrongly [27]. When the user gets an overestimated bill at
the end of a period, there will occur a problematic situation, which public transport agency must
be responsible for solving. Complications appear also because there is no direct communication
channel between a user and PTA, so TTP will have to be involved in the manual handling of
problematic scenarios.
Another point regards risks. In case of a balance billing solution (Rejsekort, for instance), if
a user has his e-ticket lost or stolen, he will at maximum lose the amount of money equal to
the current balance of the ticket. With the suggested solution, if a fraudster steals or somehow
forges a legitimate user’s e-ticket, he can use it as much as he wants, and the user will be charged
for it. Since e-ticket by itself does not carry identiﬁcation information of its holder (only TTP
does), it will be hard to di↵erentiate a fraudster from the owner of the e-ticket.
These two concerns substantiate the necessity of customer service. One of its features is demon-
strating the current bill to a user on his request. Bill calculation is done on the back end by
PTA and it stores a database where bills are linked to pseudonyms P A

7.1
Inspection
Inspection of a traveler’s e-ticket can be performed in the following way. Even though the pro-
posed scheme does not violate the design of the system we consider in incomplete (because of
potential dangers arising), so it was to decide to consider it a further step rather than an estab-
lished design part.
When a user checks in, a session pseudonym SPj is created on e-ticket’s side. It will be stored
on check-in terminal and on the traveler’s e-ticket. When the traveler checks out SPj will be
removed. Please note, that check-out terminal will not have access to the stored check-in SPj.
On a contact with e-ticket, it will simply send a command to e-ticket to clear a speciﬁc memory.
Check-in session pseudonym storage is cleared once a particular mean of transport reaches its
destination.
When an inspection has to be done, a responsible person makes the inspecting device com-
municate to check-in terminal in order to obtain the list of session pseudonyms created in the
current iteration of a transport route. Mutual authentication is necessary here, especially for the
terminal to be sure about the legitimacy of the inspecting device. If a certiﬁcate-based challenge-
response scheme is used, then the certiﬁcate of the inspecting device must be signed by PTA
just like the one of a terminal and an e-ticket. The inspecting device then scans an e-ticket and
compares its SPj with all entries in the obtained list. If there is a match, then the traveler was
successfully checked in. If there is no match, then a fraud is likely being carried out.
There is a problem with this approach. Let us say that a route is composed of points A, B,
C,D and E. A legitimate user checks in at point A and checks out at point B. SPj corresponding
to his check-in is however carried over to points C,D and E. Now if a fraudster can somehow
extract SPj from a terminal and on his e-ticket forge a SPj of a checked out user, then he can
use the system for free. This, however, requires a fraudster to guess which SPj corresponds to a
user who left the vehicle, so as far as we can judge this attack is not guaranteed to always work
out.
==PAGE:52==
45
This approach requires proper management of session pseudonyms as well as their uniqueness.
Another requirement of this approach is that check-in terminals are located in a vehicle and not
on stations so that all check-ins performed at all points of a route are collected at one place for
an inspector to read them.
