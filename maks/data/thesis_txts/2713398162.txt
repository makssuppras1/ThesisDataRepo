Abstract  
There is abundant observational evidence that individuals with low levels of social trust 
and/or high cynical hostility have an increased risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD) and an 
overall higher mortality rate. Causality in the association has not been declared, but research 
suggests that a chronically active stress response triggered by such attitudes could lead to 
systemic inflammation, atherosclerosis and eventually CVD. In this work we explore the 
hypothesis that our genes could exert a pleiotropic effect on both our personality and 
cardiovascular health. We present a genetic study of self-reported social trust and cynical 
hostility in a Northern European sample from the Danish Blood Donor Study (n=25,819 and 
n=20,573, respectively). Through genome-wide association studies, we identify a genomic 
locus in chromosome 10 that associates with social trust and maps to a gene, PLPP4, that is 
highly expressed in brain tissues. We observe simultaneous association of variants in and 
around this locus with the psychological, psychiatric and neurological traits of neuroticism, 
depressive symptoms and the volume of the isthmus of the cingulate, a part of the brain 
involved in memory, pain processing and mood regulation. Also in chromosome 10, we 
discover a locus that associates with cynical hostility and maps to PRKG1, a gene known to 
regulate both neuronal and cardiovascular functions. The locus further associates with the 
need for treatment with simvastatin, a lipid lowering medication commonly used to reduce 
the risk of CVD. At the cross-sectional level, we find associations between social trust, cynical 
hostility, sex, age, level of education, and the CVD risk factors of BMI and smoking, but not 
alcohol consumption, hypertension or diabetes. This study is the first to explore the genetic 
underpinnings of social trust and cynical hostility and provides novel evidence on their 
potential to impact cardiovascular health.

INTRODUCTION 
The connection between the mind and the body has been debated since ancient times. 
From the Greek philosophers to the first cardiologists, the idea that strong emotions affected 
one's physical health, particularly that of the heart, was popular (Siegman & Smith, 1994). But 
it was not until the second half of the 20th century that those claims, purely based on 
experience, were put to the test empirically. A number of longitudinal studies were launched 
and the results were surprising: a coronary-prone behavior type seemed to not only exist, 
but to carry a risk equivalent to that of smoking, obesity or any of the other traditional 
cardiovascular risk factors (Rosenman et al., 1975). Negative findings challenged what was 
quickly becoming accepted in the medical community, but also helped boil down this 
behavior type to its main component: cynical hostility or the “negative attitude toward 
others, based on enmity, denigration, and ill will” (Siegman & Dembroski, 1989; Smith, 1994). 
Simultaneously, health psychologists studied the effects of a trusting personality on physical 
health. They followed a long tradition of sociological research that argued that trust, as a 
form of social capital, improved the health of communities. However, a shift in perspective 
brought it closer to the studies on hostility: it was not only a good social context that 
benefited health but also a positive personal attitude towards it or trust as “the belief in the 
honesty, integrity and reliability of others” (Kawachi, 2018; Pew Research Center, 2007). 
These associations were observed time and again, but causality was never declared. A 
number of hypotheses were formulated and many pointed at how highly hostile and 
distrusting individuals integrated poorly in society, struggled to adhere to medical 
recommendations and even experienced negative events more frequently than the 
average. However, these factors were not enough to fully explain the phenomenon (Kawachi 
et al., 1999; Siegman & Smith, 1994). For that, biology was proposed to be necessary and the 
fight or flight response became the main contestant. Could these negative attitudes be 
chronically activating a stress response known to have the potential to cause tissue damage, 
systemic inflammation and eventually heart disease? (Fernandez & Smith, 2015; Siegman & 
Smith, 1994). 
We study this question from a novel perspective that may help us better understand how 
personality connects with disease and if it does so via our genes. The literature shows that 
personality has a heritable component of around 30% (Bouchard, 1994) and that many of its 
facets have specific genetic patterns associated with them (Baselmans et al., 2019). Recent 
findings also reveal that some of those patterns simultaneously associate with physical risk 
factors of disease (Nagel et al., 2018a), meaning some of the genes that influence our 
==PAGE:12==
 
2 
 
personality might also be predisposing us to better or worse physical health. If this extends 
to hostility and trust, then we might be closer to validating their association with disease.  
The main goals for this work were thus: (1) to identify genetic variants associated with social 
trust and cynical hostility and (2) to evaluate whether they further confer an increased risk 
for cardiovascular disease. For this purpose, we used data from a large prospective cohort: 
the Danish Blood Donor Study. 26,217 blood donors completed a validated questionnaire 
that provided a measure of their degree of social trust and cynical hostility. These where then 
combined with additional self-reported lifestyle assessments, National Health Registry data 
and readily available genotyping data to investigate the association between genetic 
variants, social trust, cynical hostility and their joint impact on cardiovascular health.

LITERATURE REVIEW 
With the goal of putting the reader in context, the most relevant literature to date is 
reviewed here. The order is, for the most part, chronological, which hopes to provide a sense 
how interest in the topic at hand is anything but new. 
 
On the millennia-old connection between the mind and the body 
First mentions of the influence of the mind on the body can be traced back to antiquity. As 
Siegman & Smith describe in their 1994 book (Siegman & Smith, 1994), both the Bible and 
the Talmud contain references to how strong negative emotions can have a detrimental 
effect on one’s physical health. Similarly, weight is given to the health benefits of positive 
emotions and experiences, as well as of strong social support. Of a similar time in history is 
Socrates’ appreciation for a healthy mind. In Plato’s Charmides dialogue, he refers to the 
comment that the Thracian king Zamolxis made to him: 
“Let no one persuade you to cure his headache until he has first given you his soul 
to be cured, for this is the great error of our day in the treatment of the human body, 
that physicians separate the soul from the body.” (Jowett, 1892) 
Although anecdotal and rather divergent from the modern scientific method, these claims 
set the scene for a topic that would continue to be discussed over the centuries and up to 
today. They show that the holistic approach to explaining and treating disease, which 
acknowledges the role of emotions in health, is not a recent one. 
Even with the rise of modern scientific medicine from the 18th century and the focus strongly 
set on physical factors as only cause of disease, a number of physicians continued to bring 
up the importance of psychological factors. Of particular interest to the work presented here 
are those who talked about cardiovascular disease, which were many. William Harvey, 
known as the “father of cardiovascular physiology”, observed: 
“A mental disturbance provoking pain, excessive joy, hope or anxiety extends to the 
heart, where it affects its temper, and rate, impairing general nutrition and vigor” 
(Harvey & Leake, 1928) 
Heberden, one of the first to clearly describe the symptoms of the angina pectoris, also 
emphasized the role of strong emotions, especially anger, in this disease (Heberden, 1772). 
Similar views were shared by other physicians (Fothergill, 1923; Trousseau, 1982; Wardrop, 
1851) and also by psychanalysts of the school of Sigmund Freud (Alexander, 1930).  
==PAGE:14==
 
4 
 
Empirical evidence for a coronary-prone behavior type 
Claims presented up to this point were purely observational and, despite coming from 
bright minds certainly worth some attention, do not fit modern scientific standards. This 
changed in the second half of the 20th century, when two cardiologists, Friedman and 
Rosenman, set out to empirically test the effect of personality on cardiovascular disease 
(CVD). Their efforts were partly inspired by the fact that traditional risk factors for coronary 
heart disease (CHD) such as smoking, hypertension, dyslipidemia, obesity and parental 
history only accounted for less than 50% of cases (Keys, 1970; Rosenman, 1983). Could the 
negative emotions repeatedly referred to over the years be one of the missing pieces? 
Friedman and Rosenman thus proposed what they named the Type A behavior pattern 
(TABP) as risk factor for CHD. They described the Type A individual as: 
"a person who is engaged in a relatively chronic and excessive struggle to obtain a 
usually unlimited number of things from his environment in the shortest period of 
time or against the opposing efforts of other things or persons" (Friedman & 
Rosenman, 1971) 
A structured interview was developed by the investigators themselves as a method of 
systematically assessing the pattern and, soon after, the Western Collaborative Group Study 
(WCGS) was launched. It was a large prospective epidemiological study that followed 3,154 
men, ages 39-59, and recorded their behavior, lifestyle and cardiovascular events over 8.5 
years. The results of the study disclosed that, relative to other individuals, the Type As were 
around twice as likely to show clinical manifestations of CHD, even after multivariate 
statistical adjustment for the traditional risk factors. The amount of risk associated with the 
TABP was therefore about the same as that conferred by all other risk factors (Rosenman et 
al., 1975; The Review Panel on Coronary-Prone Behavior and Coronary Heart Disease, 1981). 
Other longitudinal and cross-sectional studies provided evidence that supported the same 
idea, even when using slightly different tactics in measuring the participant’s behavior 
pattern. Some examples are the Framingham Heart Study (Haynes et al., 1978; Haynes & 
Feinleib, 1982), and those carried out by Jenkins (Jenkins et al., 1974) and Brozek (Brozek et 
al., 1966). However, plenty of negative results also followed (Arrowood et al., 1982; Dembroski 
et al., 1985; Shekelle et al., 1985) and many questioned the validity of previous findings. A 
number of explanations were given for these inconsistencies, mostly focusing on: (1) the 
differences in study designs and target populations and (2) the inherent multidimensionality 
of the TABP construct. With diverse components like competitiveness, ambition, hyper 
alertness, hostility and aggressive behavior embedded in the trait, consistently measuring 
and drawing the association with disease was a difficult endeavor (Siegman & Smith, 1994).  
==PAGE:15==
 
5 
 
As a result, Dembroski proposed a change in the direction of coronary-prone behavior 
research (Siegman & Dembroski, 1989) and suggested what he considered to be the most 
likely "toxic" component of the TABP: cynical hostility. When measured with the MMPI-
derived Cook-Medley Hostility (Ho) Scale, the trait correlated significantly with the severity 
of CHD (Williams et al., 1980, 1985). Furthermore, re-analyses of previous studies had found 
that ratings for hostility derived from the original TABP measurements significantly 
associated with the incidence of CHD, even when global TABP scores did not (Dembroski et 
al., 1989; Shekelle et al., 1985). This made cynical hostility the new protagonist when it came 
to coronary-prone behavior and, as such, is one of the traits we choose to explore in this work. 
 
Social trust as predictor of physical health 
Simultaneously, the study of trust as predictor of health was picking up speed. The focus 
here was slightly different, with mostly social and political scientists within the public health 
field driving the research. Trust was seen as a matter of social context; it was a direct result 
of the social cohesion around the individual and of their perceived trustworthiness of the 
community (Kawachi, 2018; Macinko & Starfield, 2001).  
To capture such a construct requires that one measures trust at the individual level and later 
aggregates over groups (e.g. workplaces, neighborhoods or nations). This way, populations 
with different levels of social trust can be compared and variations in their health and life 
expectancies can be investigated. A number of studies from the late 20th century showed 
that these variations did in fact exist and more trusting communities enjoyed better health 
than their less trusting counterparts, particularly when it came to the heart (Hamano et al., 
2010; Subramanian et al., 2002). Trust was described as a “moral lubricator” in society, 
facilitating collective action, reinforcing social norms, strengthening social support networks 
and creating an environment where positive health messages were well received and 
complied with (Berkman & Glass, 2000; Kawachi et al., 1999).  
However, an alternative approach to trust was taken within health psychology and 
psychosomatic medicine. They argued that, regardless of the environment, every individual 
had their own character and predisposition towards trusting the people around them. Trust 
was seen as a part of personality and so was its absence (de Raad, 2000), opening up the 
possibility that it was not only a good social context that brought health but also a positive 
attitude towards it. On the other side of the coin hid the tendency to distrust other people, 
what in psychology was referred to as cynical mistrust and coincidentally was tightly 
connected with cynical hostility and the TABP construct (Contrada & Jussim, 1992; Cook & 
Medley, 1954). 
==PAGE:16==
 
6 
 
It is this last conceptualization of trust that we focus on in this work, both because of its 
relationship to cynical hostility and because it concerns the individual, giving sense to the 
use of a genetic approach like ours.  
 
From personality to disease  
With all of this in mind, one surely wonders how a cynically hostile or mistrusting personality 
sets an individual up for disease. The working hypotheses are as follow. 
Starting with the biology, the fight or flight response is to date the main protagonist. First 
described by Cannon (Cannon, 1932), this automatic response takes place in the body when 
a situation is perceived as dangerous, stressful or frightening. It is driven by the sympathetic 
nervous system and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, and triggers a number 
of acute physiological responses such as the release of stress hormones, the acceleration of 
the heart rate and the rise in blood pressure. Although adaptive when normally regulated, a 
chronically altered stress reaction represents a disruption of homeostasis that can lead to a 
wide range of psychological and physical pathological conditions. Of particular interest to us 
is the potential this imbalance has for tissue damage, atherosclerosis and eventually CVD 
(Chrousos, 2009). As Kawachi underlines, individuals with cynical and distrusting attitudes 
could have a tendency to be more easily provoked to anger by other people’s behaviors, and 
those persistent experiences of negative emotions could be setting off their stress response 
so often as to cause coronary damage in the long term (Kawachi, 2018).  
Parallel to this theory stand others that point out the effect of a poor integration in society. 
Individuals with low trust for their surroundings or high in hostility prove to have weaker 
support systems (Chida & Steptoe, 2009; Steptoe & Kivimaki, 2013) and adhere poorly to 
medical regimens and healthy lifestyle recommendations (Kawachi et al., 1999; Lee et al., 
1992). In addition, their negative outlook on life could increase their chances of experiencing 
intrapersonal distress and stressful life events such as divorces or trouble at work (Siegman 
& Smith, 1994). Although these factors likely contribute to their predisposition to poor health, 
studies that adjust for them tend to maintain a strong association and thus reveal they are 
not playing solo (Miller et al., 1996).  
 
Personality and behavior: nature or nurture 
The last piece of background information that ties the motivation for this work together is 
the notion that personality and behavior are, at least partly, genetically determined.  
==PAGE:17==
 
7 
 
Plenty of empirical evidence supports this notion and is mostly the result of a history of twin 
studies that investigate virtually all psychological traits. These studies do not look at DNA 
directly, but aim to separate the effect of the environment from that of the genes by 
comparing identical (monozygotic) to fraternal (dizygotic) twins. Having grown up in 
presumably almost identical environments and sharing different proportions of their 
genetic material, differences in the frequency with which the trait of interest co-occurs in 
twins provide an estimation of the trait’s degree of heritability. In their review, Bouchard & 
Loehlin (Bouchard & Loehlin, 2001) provide estimates for the main five domains of 
personality as defined in the Revised NEO Personality Inventory (NEO-PI-R): extraversion, 
agreeableness, conscientiousness, neuroticism and openness. They all range from 0.3 to 0.6, 
suggesting a moderate contribution of genetics to personality. The NEO-PI-R is the most 
commonly used model of personality, and it classifies hostility and trust as facets of 
neuroticism and agreeableness, respectively. The two are orthogonal in the model, which 
fits with the previously mentioned idea that cynical hostility and mistrust are closely related.  
Although studies that investigate the genetic variation directly are not currently available on 
our two traits of interest, they do exist for many related psychological and psychiatric traits. 
They show complex, non-Mendelian, polygenic architectures often similar to one another 
(Bulik-Sullivan et al., 2015). Of special interest to our work are those on neuroticism, given its 
connection to cynical hostility and mistrust, and also the abundance of genetic research that 
has been conducted around it. This trait captures the disposition to experience negative 
affect and is highly correlated, both phenotypically and genetically, with mental disorders 
like major depressive disorder and anxiety disorders (Jeronimus et al., 2016; Lo et al., 2017a; 
Nagel et al., 2018b). Interestingly, shared genetic signals have also been found between 
neuroticism, CHD and cardiovascular risk factors like body mass index (BMI) or high-density 
lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol levels (Torgersen et al., 2021). These could partly explain the 
connection between personality and disease and we hypothesize could extend to other 
related phenotypes like social distrust or cynical hostility.  
Altogether, the literature reviewed motivates our search for the genetic basis of trust and 
hostility, and how it might interact with disease. 
AIMS 
The aims of this work are thus: 
1. 
To investigate which genetic variants are associated with social trust and cynical hostility 
in the Danish population. 
2. To explore whether those variants could have a simultaneous effect on physical health, 
particularly cardiovascular health and/or its major risk factors.

The cohort

prospective cohort of around 145,000 healthy blood donors that was initiated in 2010 and 
aims to identify predictors of health and disease in this population. Various types of 
information is collected for these individuals, both self-reported and registry-based. In 
addition, genetic data is available for around 110,000 participants who constitute the 
Genomic Cohort within DBDS. A more detailed description of the cohort is provided by 
Hansen et al 2019 (Hansen et al., 2019).

Data foundation 
The data used in this work is of three main types: genetic, self-reported and registry-based. 
The genetic data are imputed whole genome genotype data. As is described in Hansen et al 
2019 (Hansen et al., 2019), DNA was extracted from whole blood and genotyped by deCODE 
genetics using the Infinium Global Screening Array by Illumina, which has a content of ≈ 
650,000 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). These data underwent an initial quality 
control, where samples and SNPs with more than 10% missing data were excluded, as were 
samples deviating more than three standard deviations from the population heterozygosity. 
Genotype imputation was then performed using an in-house reference backbone of North-
Western European whole genome sequences including approximately 8,000 Danish 
samples. After imputation, more than 10 million genetic variants were accessible for genetic 
analysis. 
Various self-reported measures of the participating donors’ health, lifestyle and 
demographics are collected regularly through questionnaires. To this date, multiple rounds 
of questionnaires have been circulated, with questions on self-experienced physical and 
mental health, smoking habits, alcohol intake, exercise, food intake, height, weight, as well 
as on a variety of disorders and conditions of interest for research (e.g. allergies, migraine, 
stress, skin diseases…) and demographics. The last round was distributed in May of 2021 as 
part of a COVID-19 symptom survey and included thirteen questions designed to elicit 
individual levels of social trust and cynical hostility. To this date, 36,405 have replied to the 
questionnaire and more are expected to do so throughout 2022. 26,217 are within the 
Genomic Cohort and constitute our study population. 
These thirteen questions are central to our work. Ten are a selected subset of the 50-item 
Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)-based Cook-Medley Hostility Scale, 
designed to capture cynically hostile personalities. As is described in the Literature Review 
section, this scale has been widely used in research dealing with psychological influences on 
health. The questions are to be answered with either “Yes” (suggesting a hostile tendency), 
“No” (suggesting a non-hostile tendency) or “I do not know”. The remaining three questions 
aim to elicit individual levels of social trust and were taken from the European Social Survey. 
They are to be responded to within scale from 1 to 10, with higher scores representing higher 
levels of trust. All thirteen questions were translated into Danish from English and have been 
previously validated in both languages (Brummett et al., 1998; Mortensen et al., 2012). The list 
of questions used in this study is displayed in Table 2. 
Lastly, other kinds of data like hospital-registered medical diagnoses, drug prescriptions or 
various socioeconomic variables are available through different Danish national registries

(Schmidt M et al., 2015). Our use of these data is very reduced, but we mention their 
availability given their relevance for future analyses and refer to them in the Discussion.

Exploration of questionnaire data and phenotype definition 
We started our analyses by investigating the structure of the questionnaire responses 
provided by our study participants, asking questions like: How often do participants relate to 
the different statements? Are questions that target the same trait highly correlated? Do 
some capture more information than others? With this, we aimed to better understand the 
nature of our measures of social trust and cynical hostility, and to assess the best way of 
aggregating the questionnaire responses into simple overall scores of the two phenotypes.  
==PAGE:22==
 
12 
 
To answer the first question, we generated a simple visualization of each question’s response 
pattern and show it in Figure 1. This step informed our choice of methodology for exploring 
the correlations between questions. 
Next, we computed all pairwise correlations among questions targeting the same trait. For 
cynical hostility, we used the adjusted phi coefficient. This method is commonly used for 
computing correlations between dichotomous variables that, like ours, have different 
proportions in the response categories. For social trust, we instead used the Spearman’s rho 
coefficient, a rank-based alternative to the Pearson’s coefficient that is better suited for 
ordinal and non-normally distributed data.  
In addition, we explored the dimensionality of the captured constructs by applying principal 
component analysis (PCA) to the questionnaire data. This multivariate statistical technique 
allowed us to understand if our measures of cynical hostility and social trust had one or more 
main dimensions and whether some of the questions contained more information than 
others. Responses to the questions targeting cynical hostility were binary and thus an 
adaptation to the traditional PCA method was required. We used the exponential family PCA 
proposed by Collins (Collins et al., 2001) and implemented in the logisticPCA R package 
(Landgraf & Lee, 2015) through de Leew’s algorithm (J. de Leeuw, 2004). For social trust, we 
applied a classical PCA for continuous data using the prcomp function in the R stats 
package (R Core Team, 2022). We acknowledge the fact that responses to the trust questions 
are not strictly continuous - they are instead ordinal - but accept the method as a fair 
approximation that serves its intended purpose of simple exploration.  
As is presented in the Results section, these exploratory analyses revealed considerable 
multidimensionality of the measured phenotypes, particularly of cynical hostility. As a 
consequence, we decided to generate the previously mentioned aggregated scores of 
hostility and trust while giving all questions equal weights. Answers to the questions 
targeting cynical hostility were coded as 0/1, where 1 indicated hostile tendency, and then 
added them together to produce an overall score ranging from 0 to 10. In the case of social 
trust, the score was calculated as the average of the three questions targeting it, ranging 
from 1 to 10 and with higher scores reflecting higher levels of trust. 
Lastly, it was necessary to handle missing responses. Around half of the participants had left 
one or more of the questions targeting hostility blank (see Figure 2). Omitting these 
individuals completely from our analyses would have reduced the power considerably, so 
we decided to include those with two or fewer missing answers and impute them with the 
most common answer. This happened to a much lesser extent in the questions targeting 
social trust, so individuals with missing answers were simply removed. The final sample sizes 
were of n=25,819 for social trust and n=20,573 for cynical hostility.

Genome-wide association analyses 
For the discovery of genetic variants associated with the traits of interest, we performed 
genome-wide association studies (GWAS). This method screens the genome for genetic 
variants that occur more frequently in individuals with a certain trait compared to those 
without it. Although this association does not imply a direct causal relationship between the 
variant and the trait, it suggests a special relevance of that region of the genome that can 
be further investigated with other methodologies. We used the implementation provided 
by REGENIE, which is based on a whole-genome regression that predicts traits as linear 
combinations of marker effects while accounting for population structure and sample 
relatedness (Mbatchou et al., 2021).  
It is an assumption of our GWAS model that the phenotype evaluated, if quantitative, will be 
continuous and normally distributed. Violation of this assumption can impact both Type I 
error rate control and statistical power (Goh & Yap, 2009). As described before, our 
aggregated scores of social trust and cynical hostility were ordinal in nature and largely 
skewed, so we instead fitted ordinal regressions to them and used their normally distributed 
surrogate residuals for the analyses (Liu & Zhang, 2018). In these models, we included as 
covariates the variables of age, sex, geographic region within Denmark, questionnaire 
submission date and an indicator of the presence of COVID-19 symptoms at the time of 
submission. The last three were included in an effort to remove COVID-19 related effects, as 
the questionnaires were circulated in May of 2021, parallel to the lifting of restrictions after 
the second national lockdown in Denmark. With these covariates corrected for in advance, 
the GWAS model only included as covariate the genetic structure of the population. Through 
a PCA performed on 30,730 genetic variants in approximate linkage equilibrium and with 
minor allele frequencies (MAFs) larger than 5% in our two study populations (flashpca in R, 
(Abraham & Inouye, 2014)), we observed that the proportion of variance explained plateaued 
at the fifth principal component (PC) (see Supplementary Data 1). Therefore, we concluded 
that the coordinates for the first five PCs were enough to capture the structure of the 
population while limiting the complexity of the model.  
The genomic inflation of the GWAS results was evaluated and is reported through λGC and 
quantile-quantile plots of the association p-values (Figure 6). The genome-wide 
significance threshold was set at the Bonferroni corrected 5e-8, to account for the large 
number of simultaneous tests and limit the number of false positives. Variants 
surpassing this threshold were filtered for imputation quality > 0.9 and genotype and 
sample missingness < 0.1. MAFs and tests of Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (HWE) were 
also calculated with PLINK (Purcell et al., 2007).

Functional annotation and gene mapping 
We used the FUMA GWAS platform (Watanabe et al., 2017) to annotate, prioritize, visualize 
and interpret our GWAS results. 
FUMA defined independent significant SNPs as those having genome-wide significant p-
values (5 × 10−8) and being independent at r2 < 0.6. Independent significant SNPs that were 
further independent from each other at r2 < 0.1 were defined as lead SNPs. Finally, lead SNPs 
whose linkage disequilibrium (LD) blocks were closer than 250kb apart were merged into a 
single genomic risk locus, which also included any SNP in LD (r2 > 0.6) with one of the 
independent significant SNPs. All SNPs in genomic risk loci were functionally annotated with 
ANNOVAR (K. Wang et al., 2010), combined annotation-dependent depletion (CADD) 
(Kircher et al., 2014) and RegulomeDB scores (Boyle et al., 2012).  
Variants within the identified genomic risk loci were also mapped to genes using three 
strategies. With positional mapping, FUMA mapped SNPs to protein coding genes based on 
their physical distance in the human reference assembly (GRCh37/hg19). With expression 
quantitative trait loci (eQTL) mapping, SNPs were linked to genes with which they show a 
significant eQTL association (i.e., allelic variation at the SNP affects the expression of the 
gene). Data for this mapping strategy was taken from all eQTL repositories available in FUMA 
at the time of analysis: EyeGEx (Ratnapriya et al., 2019), eQTL catalogue (Kerimov et al., 2021), 
PsychENCODE (Akbarian et al., 2015), van der Wijst et al. scRNA eQTLs (van der Wijst et al., 
2018), DICE (Schmiedel et al., 2018), eQTLGen (Võsa et al., 2021), Blood eQTLs (Rekola et al., 
1985), MuTHER (Nica et al., 2011), xQTLServer (Ng et al., 2017), CommonMind Consortium 
(Fromer et al., 2016), BRAINEAC (Ramasamy et al., 2014) and GTEx v8 (The GTEx Consortium, 
2015). With chromatin interaction mapping, SNPs were mapped to genes based on 
significant chromatin interactions with promoter regions of genes (up to 250 base pairs 
upstream and 500 base pairs downstream of the transcription start site). Hi-C chromatin 
interaction data of 23 tissue and cell types from GSE87112, along with PsychENCODE (D. 
Wang et al., 2018) and FANTOM (Kawaji et al., 2009) datasets were used. A false discovery rate 
of 10−5 was applied to deﬁne signiﬁcant interactions. Given the high LD and gene density in 
the extended major histocompatibility locus on chromosome 6 (25–33 Mb), this region was 
excluded from the FUMA annotations. 
 
Gene- and gene set-based analyses 
Using the implementation of MAGMA (C. A. de Leeuw et al., 2015) in FUMA, the joint signal of 
all variants within each gene was also tested for association with social trust and cynical 
hostility. This type of analysis can uncover gene-level signals that might go unnoticed in 
variant-based analyses. In total, 19,067 protein-coding genes were covered by at least one 
==PAGE:25==
 
15 
 
variant and used in the genome-wide gene association analyses. We applied a stringent 
Bonferroni correction for the number of tested genes, resulting in a genome-wide 
significant p-value threshold of 2.622 × 10-6.  
Similarly, we tested the association between our phenotypes, 10,678 gene ontology (GO) 
gene-sets (Ashburner et al., 2000; Carbon et al., 2021) and 54 GTEx v8 tissue expression 
profiles. For all gene-sets, competitive p-values were computed, which resulted from testing 
whether the combined effect of genes in a gene-set was significantly larger than the 
combined effect of a same number of randomly selected genes. Competitive p-values were 
Bonferroni corrected, with a significant p-value threshold of 4.65 × 10−6. Finding that 
overrepresentation of certain gene-sets or tissues is predictive of the association with the 
phenotypes could point to a special relevance of a pathway or tissue in their etiology. 
 
Functional analysis of prioritized genes 
With the GENE2FUNC tool in FUMA, we explored the expression pattern of the prioritized 
genes and tested for their enrichment in pre-defined pathways. Average gene expression of 
the prioritized genes (in Transcripts Per Million) in 54 different tissues was obtained from 
GTEx v8 and visualized in a heatmap (Figure 9). Overrepresentation in sets of precomputed 
differentially expressed genes (DEG; sets of genes which are more (or less) expressed in a 
specific tissue compared to other tissue types) for the 54 GTEx v8 tissue types was also tested 
as a way to identify tissue specificity of prioritized genes. Finally, enrichment of the prioritized 
genes in biological pathways and functional categories was tested against gene-sets 
obtained from MsigDB (Liberzon et al., 2011) and WikiPathways (Kutmon et al., 2016). 
 
Genetic associations with other traits 
As far as we are aware, social trust and cynical hostility have never been systematically 
measured in a genetic cohort before, so traditional replication of our GWAS results was not 
possible. Instead, we evaluated the credibility of our signals by exploring if they were similar 
to those of closely related phenotypes. We also investigated parallelisms with seemingly 
more distant phenotypes, as they could reveal potential pleiotropic effects and point to 
pathways connecting trust and hostility with physical health. 
With cross-trait LD Score regression (Bulik-Sullivan et al., 2015), we computed genetic 
correlations at the genome-wide level between our traits of interest and a non-exhaustive 
list of related psychological and psychiatric phenotypes. This method compares variant 
effect sizes between traits and throughout the entire genome, while taking into account LD 
structure. We used the publicly available summary statistics for the phenotypes of attention 
==PAGE:26==
 
16 
 
deficit and hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), Alzheimer’s disease (AD), anorexia nervosa (AN), 
anxiety disorders (ANX), autism spectrum disorder (ASD), bipolar disorder (BIP), depressive 
symptoms (DEP), major depressive disorder (MDD), neuroticism (NEU), obsessive-
compulsive disorder (OCD), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and schizophrenia (SCZ), 
available on the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium portal (https://www.med.unc.edu/pgc/). 
References and sample sizes can be found in Supplementary Data 5. Correlation p-values 
were Bonferroni-corrected to account for the 105 statistical tests, which is conservative given 
that the tests are not strictly independent. 
GWAS signals around the discovered risk loci were also explored graphically through 
regional Manhattan plots. Overlapping the signals of social trust, cynical hostility and the 
listed psychiatric and psychological conditions allowed us to detect similarities in the pattern 
of genetic association. Regional plots were generated with the topr R package (Juliusdottir 
& Stefansson, 2022). 
We performed Phenome-Wide Association Studies (PheWAS) to test the association of the 
variants reaching genome-wide significance in our GWAS (Table 6, Supplementary Data 6) 
with a broader set of phenotypes. This included all phenotypes available within the 
Copenhagen Hospital Biobank Cardiovascular Disease Cohort (CHB CVD, n=1,241 
phenotypes) (Laursen et al., 2021), along with studies of European populations (or meta-
analyses including them) stored in the GWAS ATLAS resource (n=3,223 phenotypes) 
(Watanabe et al., 2019). CHB CVD phenotypes were evaluated with an in-house PheWAS tool 
based on REGENIE that performs association testing between the variants of interest and 
the cohort phenotypes, defined through International Classification of Diseases (ICD) -based 
PheCodes. Statistical significance was evaluated after Bonferroni multiple testing correction, 
where the number of independent tests was taken as the product of the number of principal 
components that captured 99.5% of the phenotypic variance and the number of tested 
variants after pruning for LD. Summary statistics available in the GWAS ATLAS were analyzed 
directly on the platform. 
 
Cross-sectional phenotypic associations 
In the last analysis, we tested the cross-sectional association of our measures of social trust 
and cynical hostility with the demographic variables of sex, age and highest education, as 
well as with some of the best well-known risk factors for cardiovascular disease: BMI, 
smoking habits, alcohol consumption, diabetes and high blood pressure. Our cohort being 
a notably healthy one, we chose to investigate CVD risk factors instead of the actual disease 
outcomes, which were too rare to yield statistical significance. 
==PAGE:27==
 
17 
 
For this, we fit a multinomial logistic regression to each phenotype, with the first outcome 
category (i.e. hostility score of zero and trust score of one) as reference. Despite the 
complexity of this model, we found it to be the most appropriate given the nature of the 
data. In a first attempt to treat both phenotypes as pseudo-continuous and employ the more 
easily interpretable linear regression models, it quickly became apparent that the normality 
assumption was not met by either. A Box-Cox transformation considerably improved the 
normality of the social trust measures, but not of cynical hostility. Variables ought to be 
treated as ordinal, with a total of 11 categories in the case of hostility and 10 in the case of 
trust (trust scores were rounded to the nearest integer to simplify the model). An ordered 
logistic regression would have been the most parsimonious model for this kind of outcome, 
but the proportional odds assumption which it relies on did not hold. Therefore, the more 
complex multinomial logistic regression was required.  
Covariates included in the model, their categories and distribution or prevalence in the study 
population are described in Table 1. The model summaries, including odds ratios, confidence 
intervals and p-values, are reported in the Supplementary Data 8. The large number of 
categories in the models make these summaries rather difficult to interpret, so we also 
provide the more easily interpretable results of the likelihood ratio tests (LRTs) for the 
increasingly complex nested models. These describe the overall significance of each 
covariate in relation to social trust and cynical hostility outcomes. All p-values were adjusted 
for multiple testing using the Benjamini-Hochberg correction.

Genome-wide association analysis 
We discovered genome-wide significant associations (p < 5 × 10−8) with cynical hostility at

Genetic associations with physical health-related phenotypes 
Finally, through PheWAS, we explored any genetic associations between the variants in the 
two genomic risk loci and a broad set of phenotypes that could reveal potential pleiotropic 
effects connecting personality and physical health. From the phenotypes in CHB CVD, acute 
upper respiratory infections of multiple or unspecified sites and amblyopia showed to 
significantly associate with two risk variants for social trust. Neither of the two variants 
associated with cynical hostility were available for CHB CVD participants, so no tests could 
be performed. From the GWAS ATLAS, the levels of monocyte specific chemokine 3 showed 
significant correlation with two of the top social trust variants. Height, childhood sunburn 
occasions and the need for treatment with simvastatin associated with two risk variants for 
cynical hostility. Details on these significant associations, including references to the original 
studies are provided in Table 6. The full set of PheWAS results, including all non-significant 
associations, and references to the GWAS ATLAS studies are provided in the Supplementary 
Data 6.

DISCUSSION 
Our measures of cynical hostility, and to a lesser extent social trust, on 20,573 and 26,217 
Danish blood donors proved to hide strong multidimensionality. The Cook-Medley Hostility 
scale has received criticism for its unaccounted substructure (Strong et al., 2005) and 
evidence suggests that related traits like neuroticism are also highly heterogeneous (Nagel 
et al., 2018a), so this finding was not a surprise. It also likely reflects the reduced selection of 
questions that were used to measure hostility, an inevitable consequence of the large scale 
of our cohort. With such complex phenotypes, studying their genetic architecture 
represented a challenge. 
In our GWAS, we found one genomic locus associated with social trust and one associated 
with cynical hostility at the genome-wide significance level. The variants investigated did not 
display clear evidence of functionality and/or deleteriousness, but lie within intronic regions 
of protein coding genes. This suggests that, even though they may not be  directly linked to 
the phenotypes, the genes to which they map could be.  
The risk locus associated with social trust lies within PLPP4. This gene codes for the 
phospholipid phosphatase 4, an enzyme that catalyzes the conversion of phosphatidic acid 
to diacylglycerol (Safran et al., 2021). It is mainly expressed in the brain, kidneys and testis 
(Keen & Moore, 2015; Lonsdale et al., 2013). SNPs within the identified risk locus have been 
shown to strongly affect the expression of PLPP4 in the cerebellum (Sng et al., 2019), and 
also of two other nearby genes, WDR11 and C10orf85. PLPP4 has been associated through 
GWAS with the cognitive decline in Alzheimer’s disease (Sherva et al., 2014) and Parkinson’s 
disease (Brolin et al., 2022). There is also evidence that abnormal expression of PLPP4 is 
associated with the development and progression of glioma and pancreatic cancer, possibly 
affecting the immune status in these cancer tissues (Tian et al., 2021).  
The locus associated with cynical hostility falls on PRKG1, a gene that codes for two isoforms 
of the cyclic GMP-dependent protein kinase. It is most strongly expressed in all types of 
smooth muscle, including cardiac tissue, but also in platelets, cerebellar Purkinje cells, 
hippocampal neurons and the lateral amygdala (Safran et al., 2021). PRKG1 has been shown 
to regulate cardiovascular functions such as platelet activation-adhesion and smooth 
muscle contraction, but also neuronal processes like axon guidance, hippocampal and 
cerebellar learning, circadian rhythms, nociception and serotonin regulation (Bateman et al., 
2021; Langmesser et al., 2009; Zhang & Rudnick, 2011). Genetic studies have also identified 
PRKG1 as a risk locus for post-traumatic stress disorder (Ashley-Koch et al., 2015) and 
potential point of interaction between trauma exposure and alcohol misuse (Polimanti et al., 
2018). 
 
==PAGE:44==
 
34 
 
Because the phenotypes of social trust and cynical hostility have not been measured in a 
genetic cohort before, traditional replication of our GWAS results was not possible. Instead, 
we looked for genetic similarities between our phenotypes and other psychological and 
psychiatric traits evaluated in independent cohorts. Given that most are phenotypically and 
genetically correlated (Lo et al., 2017b), they could help validate our results.  
We found overall genetic correlations between social trust, neuroticism and depressive 
symptoms and observed suggestive signals of local association between these phenotypes 
and variants around PLPP4. Such findings demonstrate that the association is not specific 
to our study population and could reflect a shared etiology among the three traits. 
Alternatively, they could suggest that social trust is a component or symptom of 
neuroticism, depression, or both.  
We mention in the Literature Review the relevance that neuroticism has to our work. It 
captures the disposition to experience negative affect and is closely connected to cynical 
hostility and mistrust. Much like we observe with social trust, neuroticism is highly 
genetically correlated with major depressive disorder, depressive symptoms and anxiety 
disorders (Lo et al., 2017a; Nagel et al., 2018a). Worth mentioning is neuroticism's genetic 
heterogeneity. The items that measure it correlate to different extents and sometimes even 
in opposite directions with other psychological, psychiatric and physical conditions. For 
example, some items that measure neuroticism significantly correlate with anorexia nervosa 
or ADHD, but not others, and some positively correlate with BMI or waist-hip ratio while 
others do so negatively (Nagel et al., 2018a). This is consistent with the phenotypic 
multidimensionality that we observe in our measure of cynical hostility and suggests that 
we might benefit from exploring it further.  
We find another intriguing connection between social trust and the volume of the isthmus 
of the cingulate, a part of the brain that is involved in memory, pain processing (Nielsen et 
al., 2005) and mood regulation (Whitford et al., 2014). Alterations in the structure and 
connectivity of the isthmus have been reported in neuroimaging studies of both major and 
subthreshold depression (McLaren et al., 2016), so finding a shared genetic signal between 
social trust and the size of this structure supports the available evidence regarding its 
function. 
In addition to identifying and validating the genetic variants associated with social trust and 
cynical hostility, we aimed to explore the possibility that some were exerting a pleiotropic 
effect on cardiovascular health. Such a phenomenon has previously been observed in 
neuroticism research, where a number of genetic signals simultaneously associate with the 
personality domain, CHD and cardiovascular risk factors like BMI and HDL levels (Torgersen 
==PAGE:45==
 
35 
 
et al., 2021). We hypothesized that this pleiotropy could extend to social distrust and cynical 
hostility and help explain their phenotypic correlation with CVD. 
We tested for phenotypic correlations between social trust, cynical hostility and a selection 
of cardiovascular risk factors (BMI, smoking, alcohol consumption, hypertension and 
diabetes) and found that, after accounting for sex, age and educational level, higher BMI and 
smoking were significantly associated with higher scores of cynical hostility and lower social 
trust. We also found genetic evidence that variants within the discovered risk locus for 
cynical hostility also associated with the need for treatment with simvastatin, a lipid-
lowering medication typically prescribed after a cardiovascular event or to patients at risk of 
CVD, as evaluated in the UK Biobank. With PLPP4 being mostly expressed in the 
hypothalamus and the kidneys, two of the central structures in the HPA axis, and PRKG1 
known to be involved in both neuronal & cardiovascular functions, we argue that the 
potential for pleiotropy between the two systems exists and should be further investigated.  
Other genetic associations were found between variants in the highlighted risk loci and the 
levels of monocyte-specific chemokine 3, height, childhood sunburn occasions, amblyopia 
and acute upper respiratory infections of multiple or unspecified sites. Higher chemokine 
levels could reflect inflammation and amblyopia resonates with our phenotypes in that it 
involves the brain and sensory system. We believe that the association with respiratory 
infections could reflect a bias in the sample, as the questionnaire was distributed during the 
global COVID-19 pandemic and many of its items targeted the severity of symptoms 
experienced by those who became infected. It points at the need to better correct for 
COVID-19 outcomes in future analyses. We cannot provide clear explanations for the genetic 
associations with childhood sunburn occasions and height, and recognize that they could 
be spurious. 
The limitations of this study have been outlined throughout the report but can be 
summarized in four main points. The first one is the size of our sample, which is relatively 
small for GWAS and an immediate bottleneck for detecting associations under the strict 
correction for multiple testing. At the time of writing, a new round of questionnaires has 
been sent out to all DBDS participants, so we are expecting an increase in sample size within 
the next months that should improve the power of the study considerably. The second 
limitation is the lack of a replication cohort. Our temporary alternative has been to look for 
genetic equivalences in related psychological and psychiatric traits, but we hope that in the 
future other independent cohorts will measure social trust and cynical hostility for a more 
direct replication to be possible. Along the same lines, the fact that our cohort is of such a 
restricted ancestry greatly limits the applicability of our conclusions. It is possible that the 
associations we observe are population-specific, so similar studies in other ancestries would 
be highly desirable. Thirdly, we must take care to avoid potential biases within our cohort, 
==PAGE:46==
 
36 
 
which is highly homogeneous and susceptible to raising spurious associations. An example 
is the correlation we find between a variant in the risk locus for social trust and respiratory 
infections, that likely reflects a bias caused by the main theme of the questionnaire being 
the COVID-19 pandemic. Lastly, and probably most challenging, is the limitation that comes 
with measuring such complex and multidimensional phenotypes like cynical hostility and, 
to a lesser extent, social trust. There is a trade-off between measuring a complex trait 
accurately (e.g. through a longer questionnaire) and acquiring a large enough sample size 
to reach statistical significance in a GWAS. Combined with the fact that the Cook-Medley 
Hostility scale has been repeatedly criticized for its unaccounted substructure and the 
evidence that related traits like neuroticism also are highly heterogeneous, this represents a 
possibly unavoidable challenge. However, we are inspired by the work of Nagel et al., 2018a, 
to take the opportunity to question the established definitions of these psychological traits 
and contribute to the growing evidence that they might be more complex than they seem. 
This project has only just begun. For the next steps, we plan to repeat the genome-wide 
association analyses with the larger sample size, which we hope will highlight other genetic 
variants associated with our traits of interest. We will make some small adjustments to the 
study design, such as accounting for COVID-19 factors that may have influenced the 
participants responses and biased the results. We will also investigate the raised connection 
between social trust and depression, possibly using medical diagnoses of the disorder or 
self-reports of its symptoms. We will assess whether PLPP4 associates with social trust even 
when accounting for these factors. We will further explore the dimensionality of our 
constructs, particularly on the genetic level, in order to understand if we face a similar 
genetic heterogeneity as is found in neuroticism. If that is the case, then we might benefit 
from evaluating the main dimensions separately instead of through an aggregated score. 
Once the genetic signals have been established, we will interrogate their causality on social 
trust and cynical hostility through Mendelian Randomization and determine if some are 
simultaneously causally associated with cardiovascular outcomes and/or risk factors. Finally, 
we will use the discovered risk variants to build a polygenic risk score that we can use to test 
the association between personality and disease in a larger, independent and less healthy 
cohort like the UK Biobank.
