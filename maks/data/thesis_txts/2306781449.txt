Abstract
This thesis investigates the non-linear dynamic behaviour of self-supporting lattice towers.
Rambøll suspects, that the design codes for towers are too conservative. They have per-
formed measurements on existing towers during severe storms that showed sections forces
signicantly less, than what was found using the design codes. The purpose of this report is
to investigate this, by performing non-linear dynamic analyses using numerical modelling.
The majority of the analyses are made by utilising the commercial nite element program
Abaqus.
The focus of this thesis has been the analysis of the dynamic behaviour relative to the
static behaviour. A set of simplications were made, e.g. for damping of the tower and the
load magnitudes originating from the wind. These phenomena also have a large impact on
the response.
The analyses of the tower were performed on two levels:
• A single member in a lattice tower.
• Full scale lattice tower.
For the single member, it was found, that load periods would have to be signicantly
smaller than typical realistic load periods, in order to gain extra dynamic capacity, when
not accounting for increased yield strength. A large increase in yield strength was found
for a typical short duration buckling problem. However, buckling is controlled by stiness,
and the increase in buckling capacity, because of the increased yield strength, was found
to be 3%.
The investigation of the tower situation showed, that the typical natural frequency of a
tower coincides with typical wind load frequencies. This means, that there is a risk of
the phenomenon dynamic amplication, which will induce amplied vibrations depending
on the amount of damping. It was found, that for a realistic wind history, the dynamic
capacity was signicantly less than the static capacity. A 30 m tower with V-type bracing
was investigated, and here it was found, that the dynamic capacity was less than half
of the static capacity. In order to investigate the eect of adding plastic capacity to the
dynamic analyses, a second tower was constructed. This was identical to the original tower
except for a replacement of the bracing to a cross-type. Here it was found, that the cross-
type bracing setup yielded considerably increased dynamic capacity. The two towers had
similar static capacities, but the cross-type tower had an increased dynamic capacity of
approximately 60% relative to the V-type tower.
Investigating the eects of damping and the application of the wind load more in-depth
could prove very interesting because it is suspected, that the solutions for these eects are
performed too conservatively.

Resumè
Dette projekt undersøger den dynamiske ikke-lineære opførsel for selvbærende gittertårne.
Rambøll mistænker, at de benyttede designkoder for tårne er for konservative. De har
foretaget målinger på eksisterende tårne under kraftige storme, disse målinger viste, at de
målte snitkræfter var meget mindre end de fundne ved design. Formålet med denne rapport
er at undersøge dette, ved ikke-lineær dynamisk analyse udført ved hjælp af numerisk
modellering. Størstedelen af analysen er udført ved at benytte det kommercielle FEM
program Abaqus.
Der fokuseres i denne rapport på undersøgelsen af den dynamiske opførsel sammenlignet
med den dynamiske opførsel. En række simplikationer er lavet for f.eks. dæmpning af
tårnet og last størrelserne der udspringer fra vindbelastningen. Disse fænomener har også
en stor betydning for responset.
Analysen af tårnet er udført på to niveauer.
• Et enkelt konstruktionselement fra et gitter tårn.
• Gitter tårn i fuld skala.
For det enkelte konstruktionselement, blev det fundet, at lastperioder skulle være meget
mindre end typiske realistiske lastperioder for at opnå ekstra dynamisk kapacitet, når forø-
get ydestyrke ikke er medregnet. En stor forøgelse i ydestyrken blev fundet for et typisk
korttids stabilitetsproblem. Stabilitetsproblemer er styret af stivheden for det betragtede
element hvorved den forøgede bulingskapacitet blev fundet til 3%.
Undersøgelsen af tårnsituationen viste, at typiske egenfrekvenser for tårnet var sammenfal-
dende med typiske lastfrekvenser fra vindbelastning. Dette betyder at der er en risiko for
fænomenet dynamisk amplikation, dette fænomen vil medføre at vibrationer bliver forøget
afhængigt af niveauet af dæmpning. Det blev fundet for en realistisk vindtidsserie, at den
dynamiske bæreevne blev formindsket i forhold til den statiske bæreevne. Et 30 meter højt
tårn med V-type gitter blev undersøgt. Her blev det fundet at den dynamiske bæreevne
var mindre en halvdelen af den statiske bæreevne. For at undersøge eekten af forøget
plastisk bæreevne i den dynamiske analyse, blev et tårn med en krydsgitter-konguration
undersøgt. De to tårne havde tilsvarende statisk bæreevne, men tårnet med krydsgitter-
kongurationen havde en forøget dynamisk bæreevne påomkring 60% sammenlignet med
tårnet med V-type gitter.
Ved at undersøge dæmpning og anvendelsen af vindbelastningen mere dybdegående kunne
interessante resultater forekomme, da det er mistænkt at de benyttede antagelser vedrø-
rende dæmning og vindbelastning er for konservative.

Introduction
This thesis investigates the dynamic behaviour of self-supporting lattice towers. When
dealing with towers and masts, the two are often distinguished from each other. Towers
are self-supporting tall and slim structures.
Masts are dened as tall towers that are
supported by cables. The cables are used to further strengthen the structure by containing
some of the horizontal load, and thereby decrease the base moment. The base moment
increases by the square of the height. This technique is therefore used, when the structure
has to be very tall, whereby the more complicated design and construction is justied by
the reduced material usage.
Lattice towers are widely used across the globe. The usage includes telecommunication
towers which will be the main focus of this thesis. The truss conguration of the structure
is used because of the optimal performance to wind loading. A typical lattice tower used
for telecommunication is shown on the following gure.
Figure 1.1: Three legged self-supporting tower with V-type bracing.
Reference: Martin Jespersen - Rambøll Telecom.
For towers, wind loading is typically the most critical type of load. The increasing mag-
nitude of the wind load for large heights and the large moment created at the base of the
structure, demands a lot from the structure. Because of the wide usage of lattice towers,
optimization of the design calculations can possibly save a lot of material, provided that
it is found, that the current design calculations proves unnecessarily conservative.

Problem formulation
A possible area of optimization is to investigate the dynamic response of these towers when
subjected to wind loading. Eurocode suggests that the wind loading is modelled as a static
load, where the dynamics of the wind load are handled by increasing the magnitude of the
wind load, whereby the wind load might become more conservative than needed.
Examples have been given from Rambøll where stresses have been monitored on existing
lattice towers, where it was found that the stress magnitudes in the tower were much lower
than expected. This indicates that the design method is too conservative. This thesis will
investigate the dynamic behaviour of lattice towers subjected to wind load.
It will be investigated if the towers have increased capacity when analysed dynamically.
Two situations are investigated to analyse this problem.
• Buckling capacity of a single structural member subjected to short duration loading.
• Global capacity of a full scale lattice tower subjected to short duration loading.
Both situations will be investigated using analytical and numerical methods in order to as-
sess the performance to short duration loads. It was discussed whether the single structural
member should be tested empirically. However, the preliminary analyses indicated, that
there was no signicant capacity gain for the structural member. This test was therefore
omitted in order to investigate the tower situation more in-depth.
The commercial nite element program Abaqus will be utilised to perform non-linear dy-
namic analyses. The focus of this thesis will be to investigate the dynamic response for
dierent situations. The nature of the wind load will not be investigated in-depth, instead
given loads or wind speeds will be used to describe the load. Another important aspect
when dealing with dynamic analyses is the damping of the structure. To model this, a typ-
ical degree of damping from Eurocode will be applied. This value is a very rough estimate,
and will cause some uncertainties compared to the real response of the towers.
Besides the dynamic response analyses, the yield strength of steel is investigated when
subjected to short term loading. Previous tests have shown, that for relatively large strain
rates, the yield strength is increased signicantly.
The theory behind the analyses performed using numerical modelling will be briey pre-
sented, after which the analysis of the single structural member (SMA) is performed and
discussed. Two dierent towers are then investigated. The original tower is created as
a copy of a "30m North Light" tower designed by Rambøll, which is a three legged self-
supporting tower with V-type bracing as shown on Figure 1.1. For the second tower that
was investigated, the bracing was altered in order to make the tower further statically
indeterminate to obtain additional plastic capacity. An additional diagonal was placed for
each existing diagonal to form a cross for all diagonals.
It was discussed whether to investigate a third tower with a lower natural frequency. A
lower natural frequency would reduce the eect of dynamic amplication. However, it
was assessed that, it would be very dicult to create a realistic tower with a natural fre-
quency reduced enough to remove dynamic amplication eectively. This investigation
was therefore omitted in order to make space for the cross-type tower.

2.1
Non-linear Modelling
In this thesis two types of non-linearities are considered; geometric and material non-
linearities. The numerical analyses are carried out by use of the commercial FEM pro-
gramme Abaqus. In this section, the geometric and material non-linearities will be pre-
sented shortly. Furthermore, the solvers used by Abaqus to implement the non-linearities
are presented.
Geometric Non-linearities
When using numerical tools to perform structural analysis, it can be done linearly or non-
linearly. The linear analysis is performed when the deformations of the considered body are
small. The original geometry is used in the equations of equilibrium throughout the anal-
ysis, whereby change of geometry during the analysis is not accounted for. This generates
signicant dierences when large deformations occur. However, when the deformations are
small, the deviation of the actual geometry from the original geometry is not decisive.
When considering the non-linear analysis the equilibrium equations are updated with the
deformed geometry in every step, whereby this analysis method is applicable for structural
problems where large deformations occur. Lattice towers often experience large deection
at the top, before any yielding starts to occur, whereby a non-linear analysis is important.
[1]
Elements
Dierent methods can be used in order to model beams.
The usual way is based on
translations and rotations of the points located along the beam axis and the associated
cross section. When considering large deection problems, dierent beam models can be
used to describe the strains and curvature of the beam. These models include elements
based on a Lagrangian formulation or an Eulerian formulation.
The Lagrangian formulation has a xed global frame of reference, from which the total
displacements and rotations are formulated.
The Eulerian formulation uses a frame of reference corresponding to the current position
in space. The motion is then split into two dierent measures; the motion of the local
frame of reference and deformation of the beam element within this frame of reference.
This means, that the rigid body movement and the deformation are split up. This yields
more precise results for large displacements. The Lagrangian formulation falls short for
large displacements, where large rigid body movement and rotation often will produce a
small strain. [1]

The Arc-length method uses the same kind of procedure. But instead of extending the

tangents are extended, can nd points on the internal forces curve after a maximum. The
drawback of the arc length method, is that it is more calculation expensive. Abaqus uses
a variation of the Newton Raphson method in the step type called "Static, general" and
in the step type called "Static, Riks" an arc length method called Modied Riks is used.

When dealing with structural dynamics dierent numerical techniques can be used in
order to describe the structural behaviour when a system is subjected to a time dependent
load. In Abaqus the numerical solver for dynamic problems uses Hilber-Hughes-Taylor
time integration (HHT) which is an extension of the Newmark time integration. On the
following gure, a time history of the displacement x(t) for a single degree of freedom

The goal of the Newmark procedure is to compute the state vector (xn+1, ˙xn+1). This is
done for the time tn+1 = t+∆t by utilizing the known state vector (xn, ˙xn) at the previous
time tn. The Newmark procedure is established by use of two steps:
1. The increments of x(t) and ˙x(t) during the time increment [tn; tn+1] are expressed
by integrals of the acceleration ¨x. The acceleration will not be known throughout
the time interval. The integral will be evaluated by values of the acceleration at the
end points of the time interval tn and tn+1. These values can be weighted dierently
to determine the acceleration.
2. The ordinary equation of motion of the state vector x(t) is rewritten in terms of the
acceleration and solved for the acceleration at the new step time tn+1.
When the acceleration is found, the equations formed in step 1 are used to determine the
state vector at tn+1. The Newmark method sets up the equation of motion at forward
time tn+1, assuming it is satised at this step. The Newmark method is an implicit time
integration solver because it used information from the following step and the current step
in order to calculate the following step. [4]
The HHT procedure uses a weighted mean between the force terms in the equation of
motion at tn and tn+1, while the acceleration term is retained at tn+1. This procedure
introduces improved numerical damping properties. When considering dynamic problems,
the low modes are generally the modes of interest. Numerical damping is included in the
Newmark approach to dampen the higher modes, however, the low modes are aected as
well. The eect on the lower modes are minimized using the HHT approach [5].
The Newmark time integration family and the Hilber-Hughes-Taylor time integration are
described in detail in [4] and [5].

For typical members used in a lattice tower, the duration of a natural oscillation is much

The analyses performed in this chapter

Summary
In this chapter, the results from chapter 3 are rened by including some of the eects that
were neglected and the single member has been investigated further.
It was found, that a 3-node beam element is able to solve the non-linear dynamic problem
with a relative dierence of Rd ≈0.5% compared to the much more calculation costly
element types; shell and solid. Therefore, these beam elements will be used to create the
tower model.
It was found, that in order to get signicant capacity increase for a given yield strength,
the load frequencies has to be in the same order of magnitude as the natural frequency.
This mounts up to a maximum allowable period of a half trigonometric load wave between
13 ms and 23 ms, for capacity increases between 5% and 20%. For these ndings, the static
part of the load is 80% of the static load capacity.
The eect of the strain rate dependant yield strength was investigated. The strain rate
was set to the rate that occured around the time of initial yielding for a S355 member. A
relatively large increase from fy = 355 MPa to fy,dyn = 443 MPa was found. However,
when considering buckling problems, the yield strength is not as decisive, compared to the
bending stiness, which explains why only a 3% increase of capacity was found.

Sensitivity Analysis
In order to determine the seeding of the model and the size of the time increment for the
dynamic analysis, a sensitivity analysis is performed. The sensitivity analyses were made
without introducing imperfections. The sine type load for the dynamic part is used with a
half wave period of T1/2 = 0.1 s. This is chosen, in order to get a situation with large load
dierences in the model during short periods of time, whereby the requirements for the time
increment will be high. Yielding of the structure will further complicate the calculations,
and in order to make sure, that the mesh and time increment sizes are adequate, the load
is scaled to ensure yielding will appear. Imperfections are not introduced in the sensitivity
analyses for simplicity.
The wind load applied is equal to PRT scaled by a factor. First o, the load is scaled by
a factor which ensures initial yielding in the non-linear static case. This factor is found
by running a "Static Riks" analysis with Abaqus, whereby a factor of approximately 1.75
is found. On top of this, a factor of 1.5 is applied in order for yielding to appear when
the dynamic loading with a high frequency is applied. This ends up in a factor of 2.625
applied to the wind load. Furthermore, the load is divided into static and dynamic. The
static load is equal to 60% of the full load, and the dynamic load has an amplitude of 40%
of the full load.

5.7. SUMMARY
5.7
Summary
In this section, a 30 m lattice tower with V-Type bracing was analysed with non-linear
and dynamic eects.
From the static non-linear analysis performed in section 5.4 it has been determined that,
the characteristic capacity of the tower is 1.60 times larger than the load outlined in the
RAMTOWER report. By applying the material partial factor the design capacity of the
tower was found to 1.37.
To determine the dynamic behaviour of the tower, dierent analyses have been made. The
rst analysis that was performed was to test the capacity when subjected to single wind
gust on top of a constant static load. This single wind gust was modelled as half a sine
wave. The magnitude of the static load was set to 80% of the non-linear static capacity.
It was found, that in order to gain 5% - 20% extra capacity, the single gust should have a
maximum period between 0.08 s and 0.13 s. This is relatively low, compared to a typical
wind gust. From the wind data received by Rambøll (Figure 2.10), the duration of the
absolute peaks of the wind was typically around 0.5 s. The capacity gain for this load
period is therefore signicantly less than 5%.
In order to simulate a situation, where dynamic amplication was included, the response
was investigated for varying load frequencies and linearly increasing load magnitudes
through time.
Here it was found that for linearly increasing trigonometric loads with
a frequency, near the natural frequency of the tower, dynamic amplication will kick in
which will decrease the capacity of the tower below the static capacity.
It was found,
that for load periods in the range 0.37 s < T1/2 < 2.50s an amplication of the stress
on the structure occurred. This means, that the tower is at risk of experiencing dynamic
amplication for typical wind gusts.
The behaviour of the tower was investigated for a realistic wind speed history, by applying
the wind speed history obtained from Rambøll. It was found, that the tower could endure
around 47% of the static capacity. This dynamic capacity seems unreasonable, and it was
indicated, that it is caused by a too conservative damping ratio. The fact that the existing
towers designed using the current versions of the practical codes could indicate that the
applied load is too large.
Furthermore, the plastic deformation throughout the time periods investigated was anal-
ysed. It was found, that plastic deformations occurred around the maximum magnitude
of applied wind load. The tower was able to endure this load, but failed at a later point in
time at a lower load magnitude, indicating that, the failure happens because of the plastic
deformation that was introduced previously.

6.7. SUMMARY
This type of behaviour is also indicated by the non-linear buckling analyses (Figure 5.15 and
Figure 6.10). From these it was found, that tower 2 had more post buckling capacity. This
means, that more strain of the tower is needed, to create the same absolute deformations.
The fact that more strain in the tower is needed to create further absolute deformations
of the tower, and that the magnitude of the plastic deformations are smaller for similar
absolute deection situations, means that, it is harder to obtain total failure of the tower.
For tower 1, total failure occured during the load scale increase of around 5% from 0.95,
where no yielding occured, to 1.0. For tower 2, no yielding occured up until a load scale
factor of 1.3, with increments of 0.1. Total failure occured for a load scale factor of 1.6.
This corresponds to a total load factor increase of around 23% from initial yielding to total
failure. This shows, that there is a much larger relative region in load scale during which
the cross tower can sustain plastic deformations.
6.7
Summary
In this section, a tower similar to tower 1 was analysed, where the bracing was changed
to a cross type. This alteration was analysed, because the introduction of the cross-type
bracing results in a structure that is more structurally indeterminate. Which gives more
plastic capacity.
From the static non-linear analysis performed in section 6.4 - Static Capacity it was de-
termined that, the characteristic capacity of the tower was 1.71 times larger than the load
outlined in the RAMTOWER report. This capacity corresponds to an increase of 7% in
the static capacity compared to tower 1.
The same dynamic analyses as for tower 1 were performed. The magnitude of the applied
loads were scaled according to the static capacity of tower 2. For the single gust analysis,
it was found, that tower 2 had a signicant increase in the allowable gust periods T1/2,max.
It was found, that in order to gain 5% - 20% extra capacity, the load period should be in
the range 0.180 s < T1/2,max < 0.335 s. These values are, however, still not larger than the
periods of typical wind gusts. The increase in allowable periods compared to tower 1, was
a result of the increased plastic capacity. The tower experienced initial yielding similar to
tower 1.
The analysis with a linearly increasing trigonometric load for varying load frequencies
yielded a smaller region of load periods in which the dynamic capacity was smaller than
the static capacity. This region was found to be 0.77 s < T1/2 < 2.50 s. Many wind gusts
will still be in this region and therefore cause dynamic amplication to some degree.
The tower was analysed when subjected to the load corresponding to the wind speed
history. By applying this load, a maximum magnitude of the applied load history corre-
sponded to approximately 70% of the static capacity. This capacity is lower than expected,
but it is signicantly higher than for tower 1. This can be caused by the smaller region in
which dynamic amplication occured found for the previous analysis.
It was found that, for similar maximum deection above the deection that causes initial
yielding, the amount of plastic strain created was signicantly less for tower 2. The in-
creased plastic capacity found in the static analysis means that, additional external load
on the structure is needed to cause the same absolute deection. These two ndings mean,
that it takes a lot more external load on the structure before failure occurs.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, non-linear dynamic behaviour of lattice towers has been investigated when
subjected to short term loading. The majority of the analyses were performed using nu-
merical modelling with the commercial program Abaqus. The analyses were performed on
two levels of the structure:
• A single member in a lattice tower.
• Full scale lattice tower.
The duration of the short term loading experienced by a typical member is dependent on
the natural period of the tower. It was assessed, that the natural periods of typical towers
are in the order of magnitude of 1 s.
By performing a series of analytical calculations for dierent members sizes, it was found,
that the member had no signicant capacity increase for load periods corresponding to the
natural period of the tower. In order for extra capacity to be obtained for a single gust,
the load periods would have to be in the order of magnitude equal to the natural period
of the members themselves.
A specic member that has been used in the towers designed by Rambøll was investigated
in-depth.
When the wind load was simulated as a single half wave of a trigonometric
function, the maximum periods of the loading was found to be in the range from 13 ms to
23 ms for capacity increases from 5% to 20%. This means, that there is only insignicant
capacity increase for load periods in the magnitude of 1 s.
The eect of increased yield strength when subjected to high strain rates was investigated.
When the strain rate was chosen to the value around the time of initial yielding in a
dynamic buckling analysis, a signicant increase in yield strength was found. An increase
from 355 MPa to 443 MPa. However, buckling is mainly controlled by stiness. This
meant, that the capacity, when subjected to a compressive force, was only increased by
3%.
Two towers were investigated for short term loading from wind. The duration of a typical
wind gust was assumed be between 0.5 s and 3 s. The original tower (tower 1) that was
investigated is a 30 m lattice tower with V-type bracing, where the design was received by
Rambøll.
The ultimate characteristic non-linear static capacity was found to be 1.6 times the loads
outlined by Rambøll. This capacity was then used in order to nd the dynamic capacity
relative to the static capacity.
Similarly to the single member analysis, maximum allowable load periods of a single simu-
lated wind gust were found for given capacity increases. To gain capacity from 5% to 20%,
the maximum load periods should be from 0.08 s to 0.13 s. These periods are signicantly
lower than the wind load periods, indicating, that there is no extra capacity.
In order to include eects from dynamic amplication properly, a wind load history was
simulated by a combination of a trigonometric function and a linearly increasing function.
It was found that, the half periods of the trigonometric function in the region 0.37 s to 2.50
s would yield decreased dynamic capacity relative to the static capacity. Many wind gust
93
==PAGE:103==
periods have a duration inside this region, whereby it was suspected, that the dynamic
capacity would be reduced for a real wind situation.
A simulation of a real wind situation was performed by utilizing a set of wind speeds
over a 600 s period received by Rambøll. This was transformed to load on the structure.
It was found, that the tower had a dramatically reduced capacity relative to the static
capacity.
By using a maximum wind load in the real wind history, with a magnitude
relative to the static capacity of 47%, failure occurred. A lower capacity was expected,
because of the coincidence of the values found for the natural frequency of the tower and
the wind load frequency. However, this relative capacity seems very low. It was discussed
if some of the assumptions made for the model were too conservative. The amount of
damping introduced to the tower was set to the value indicated in Eurocode. However,
in other literature signicantly higher damping values are proposed, which would increase
the dynamic capacity by reducing the eect of dynamic amplication. Furthermore, it was
discussed, that the load originating from the wind speed is too conservative. This would
mean, that the actual loads in a dynamic analysis should be lower, than in a static analysis,
where the loads are scaled for e.g. the natural frequency.
For tower 1 the failure happens shortly after initial yielding. It was therefore sought to
create a tower with additional plastic capacity. This was done replacing the bracing in
tower 1 with cross-type bracing (tower 2). The proles of the bracing were decreased in
order for the static capacity to be similar for the two towers. With the new setup, the static
capacity was found to be 7% higher than for tower 1. The same kind of analyses for tower 1
were performed, and it was found, that due to the increased plastic capacity, the dynamic
capacities were increased signicantly. The durations that the tower could sustain for a
single wind gust were found to be more than 2 times that of tower 1. However, the periods
were still in the region of the typical wind gust periods, whereby dynamic amplication
could still occur. Furthermore, the dynamic capacity when subjected to the real wind
history was found to be approximately 1.6 times that of tower 1.
The original hypothesis of additional capacity of the tower when analysed dynamically
was not found for the analyses that were focused on, in this thesis. This mainly happens,
because of the coincidence of the the wind load frequency and the natural frequency of the
tower. On the contrary, a reduced capacity was found. This reduced capacity is suspected
to be caused by the application of the wind load and the assumption on the amount of
damping.
Future Work
The authors suspect, that additional capacity can be found by investigating the amount
of damping and the load originating from the wind more in detail. In this thesis, these
phenomenons were treated by applying typical values and solutions.
The eect of damping can be investigated further by investigating some of the dierent
proposals made in the literature about amount of damping. Furthermore, full scale exper-
imental testing of a tower could prove very informative, because the existing literature on
the amount of damping of towers is scarce.
The load originating from the wind can be tested experimentally by performing wind tun-
nel tests. Furthermore, a much more detailed approach to modelling the wind load can be
done compared to the approach made in this thesis.
