Abstract
The purpose of the project was to investigate the solid and liquid fractions generated
by processing BSG with the Dacoﬁ® ﬁlter press. The compositional diﬀerences between
Pilsner malt and un-malted oat in both total and soluble protein, FAN, sugar proﬁle and
density was determined by using appropriate analytical methods. The Dacoﬁ® ﬁlter press
gave a consistent pressing proﬁle when processing both Pilsner malt and un-malted oat,
respectively.
The overall mass balance for the Pilsner malt and oat closed within 5 %, however
approximately 20 % protein was left unaccounted for in pressing of both cereal. For un-
malted oat, the ash balance did not conform and, unlike the Pilsner malt, 16 % was not
accounted for in the 04APR17 press.
The total protein analysis showed that the ﬁlter press was suitable for enriching both
the 300 µm and 100 µm PSG fractions, with little distinction between ﬁlter pore size,
while increasing the dry matter content by removing water during the pressing.
The LSG fractions were analysed for FAN, soluble protein, glucose and maltose in re-
gards to potential application for fermentation or as a supplement to sparge water in the
brewing process. The analysis showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the 300 µm and 100
µm ﬁlters in the FAN concentration, with noticeable higher content in the 300 µm fraction.
The diﬀerence between ﬁlter pore size for glucose and maltose was indistinguishable
for Pilsner malt. The LSG 300 µm fractions of the enzyme-aided mashing of un-malted
oat produced signiﬁcantly higher maltose concentration than the Pilsner malt, contrary
to the glucose concentration that was noticeably lower. Furthermore, there was a size-
able diﬀerence between the 300 µm and 100 µm ﬁlter in the maltose concentration for oat.
The result of the Bradford soluble protein assay showed that there was a noticeable
diﬀerence between Pilsner malt and un-malted oat, and showed oat having approximately
50 % higher concentration. There was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the 300 µm and
100 µm ﬁlters.

Introduction
Brewing is by many considered the oldest biotechnological process and dates back to 3000
BC [4, 5]. In brewing a microorganism, usually a derivative of the genus Saccharomyces,
is utilized for the bioconversion of a substrate (an organic carbon source such as glucose,
maltose, etc.) to produce beer. Historically, brewing has been instrumental in both a va-
riety of scientiﬁc discoveries, for instance with the isolation of a pure yeast strain by Emil
Christian Hansen for Carlsberg Laboratories in the early 1880’s [4], as well as a vital aid
in early civilization by supplying a source of uncontaminated beverages in a time where
clean drinking water was scarce. The brewing industry remains a frontier in industrial
innovation and aims to increase performance through optimization and discoveries in both
microbial and process technological areas. Beer is among the most abundantly consumed
beverages worldwide, alongside tea, coﬀee, milk and carbonated beverages [6], with an
estimated production of 1.93 billion hectoliter in 2015 [7]. In terms of biproducts, the
vast production of beer results in approximately 39 million tonnes brewer’s spent grains
(BSG) annualy [8].
As a result of the high demand for beer world wide, BSG is produced all over the globe,
and is generally considered a waste product. One of the major drawbacks of BSG is the
high water content (∼80 % [9, 8]), which makes transportation and storage problematic
due to spoilage. In many cases, BSG is used as animal feed, or is discarded in landﬁlls.
In order to use BSG, certain pretreatment is required; typically removal of water. Since
drying BSG by heating requires a substantial amount of energy, it is beneﬁcial to me-
chanically remove a fraction of water from the grains. In the brewing industry the batch
operated Meura 2001 and Meura 2001 Hybrid mash ﬁlters are used to press BSG. The
Meura presses have a large capacity of 15.5 tonnes per press and up to 14 presses per day
[10]. The pressed BSG contains less than 65% water [11], a vast improvement compared
to the unprocessed BSG. An alternative to the Meura mash ﬁlters is the Dacoﬁ© ﬁlter
press, which can be operated continuously or in batch mode with a capacity of 0.5 tonnes
per hour, and yield pressed BSG with a water content of 60-65%. The Meura 2001 Hy-
brid mash press has up to 160 ﬁlter plates, each designed for a nominal capacity of 90 kg
malt [10], and take up a substantial amount of space, whereas the Dacoﬁ© is signiﬁcantly
smaller.
The use of any mash ﬁlter can increase brewery performance by extracting residual
nutrients from the BSG, increasing extract yield and can be used to produce high gravity
wort [10]. Industrial application of the pressed grains could be advantageous not only
from an economical stance, but also from a sustainability point-of-view.

3
Scope
The scope of this Master’s thesis was to investigate the diﬀerence between certain cereals
typically used in brewing, more speciﬁcally the composition of key parameters such as
total nitrogen, FAN, sugars, etc., in fractions of BSG. The fractions were generated by
using the mashing system and Dacoﬁ© ﬁlter press at the DTU Brewery. BSG from Pil-
sner malt (malted barley) and un-malted oats were investigated in duplicate experiments.
Further experiments on un-malted rye and wheat was planned, but proved unavailable
due to lack of suitable raw materials and equipment inadequacies, namely greatly reduced
steam pressure, which made heating during mashing impractical. The experiments with
rye and wheat were not conducted due to insuﬃcient raw materials from same batches,
and were excluded due to the large batch variation in cereals.
4
Aim
The aim of this Master’s thesis was to compare the total protein, FAN, soluble protein
and sugars content in 300 µm and 100 µm BSG fractions of Pilsner malt and un-malted
oat kernels. Furthermore, potential applications of the LSG fractions were considered in
regards to increasing sustainability of the brewing process by optimizing use of utilities.

5.1
General overview of the brewing process
The brewing process can roughly be divided in to mashing, wort boiling, fermentation and
maturation, although certain steps precede and succeed the aforementioned. These are
intentionally left out in greater detail in this report due to their lack of direct signiﬁcance
to the scope of the project. In Figure 1Schematic overview of the brewing process. The
shaded areas are currently not performed at DTU Brewery.ﬁgure.caption.6 an overview
of the brewing process can be seen.
Figure 1: Schematic overview of the brewing process. The shaded areas are currently not
performed at DTU Brewery.
Before the mashing step can commence, a variety of raw materials of a speciﬁc quality
are required, thus malting is the ﬁrst step of brewing. The main purpose of malting is to
produce enzymes through careful germination of e.g. barley, which is initiated by absorp-
tion of water (steeping) and is subsequently interrupted by a drying procedure known as
kilning. A variety of enzymes are produced during malting, among the more signiﬁcant
are α-amylase and β-amylase, which are essential for starch degradation, while proteinases
and peptidases, as well as lipases, are needed for protein and fat degradation, respectively
[12]. Optimal malting requires suﬃcient water, oxygen and temperature control to yield
a high quality product, which increases performance in the subsequent brewing steps.
The mashing and lautering processes are the most important parts of wort produc-
tion.
In mashing, malted cereal and water are mixed, activating the enzymes in the
3
==PAGE:12==
malted cereal, degrading starch to sugars [12].
Un-malted grains can be used in the
mashing process with the addition of a brewing enzyme package, such as Ondea® Pro
produced by Novozymes, which supply a variety of enzymes that are normally produced
during malting. In order to ensure optimal enzymatic activity, temperature and pH are
critical factors, which must be maintained within certain parameters depending on raw
materials and beer style.
When the mashing process has ﬁnished, the wort containing both fermentable and
non-fermentable sugars, as well as proteins and free amino acids [12] is transferred to
a kettle through a process known as lautering. Lautering and sparging are essential in
separating sugars, amino acids, proteins, etc., into the wort, and aims to leave as little nu-
trients in the mashed cereal as possible. The wort is thus separated from residual grains,
husks and hulls, insoluble starches and other compounds, which as a whole is known as
brewer’s spent grains (BSG).
The boiling process is a critical step that releases undesirable aroma compounds from
the wort, as well as the bitterness compounds in hops, and sterilizes the wort prior to
fermentation. Depending on the beer style, hops are introduced to provide the character-
istic aroma and bitterness. Hops with diﬀerent qualities are added to the process during
wort boiling and supply bitterness and aroma by releasing α-acids into solution, which
are converted into iso-α-acids during boiling. Furthermore, the bitter substances in hops
contribute with antimicrobial properties [12]. After wort boiling, whirl-pooling separates
larger particles, consisting of e.g. denatured proteins, known as trub (”hot trub”) from
the wort. Wort cooling ensures that the wort reaches an appropriate temperature and is
ready for inoculation/yeast pitching.
Fermentation is often carried out in stainless steel cylindroconical vessels [5]. Depend-
ing on the beer style a suitable yeast is pitched, and a lag phase follows where the yeast
adapts to the nutrient rich environment. The lag phase is followed by an exponential
growth phase, where the yeast propagates while using up dissolved oxygen and nutrients
in the wort to produce biomass, and later anaerobically metabolize sugars into ethanol
and CO2, as well as other volatile compounds such as esters and higher alcohols. As the
nutrients are used up, fermentation comes to a halt and yeast is removed, followed by
a maturation period. The diﬀerence between lager and ale yeasts, conventionally known
as bottom- and top-fermenting yeasts, respectively, can be found in the microorganism
responsible for the conversion of sugars to ethanol and carbon dioxide. Top-fermenting
strains, named so due to the yeast ﬂocculating and rising to the top, Saccharomyces
cerevisiae, are fermented at 18–25°C, which results in a faster fermentation, while S. pas-
torianus (formerly known as S. carlsbergensis) are generally bottom-fermenting strains, a
hybrid of S. cerevisiae and S. eubayanus, and are fermented colder at 8–15°C [4, 5]. The
bottom fermenting strains ﬂocculate and fall to the bottom. Lager beer is subjected to
a low-temperature maturation period (lagering) to reduce certain unwanted metabolites
such as vicinal diketones (diacetyl in particular), however, addition of enzyme packages,
such as Maturex® by Novozymes, can shorten the maturation period by preventing the
formation of diacetyl and 2,3-pentanedione.
4
==PAGE:13==
The brewing process uses a range of ingredients; water, malted or un-malted grains,
hops, adjuncts and yeast in the production of the popular beverage. The process requires
a sizable amount of energy distributed on diﬀerent utilities (heating, cooling, etc.), and
produces a substantial amount of waste such as BSG, trup, surplus yeast and slurry from
ﬁltration [13].
The term trub (”hot trub”) covers the fraction that is left after whirl-pooling separates
coagulated and insoluble proteins [14] and is composed of protein (40–70 %), bitterness
compounds from hops (7–15 %), and other organic compounds and mineral substances
(20–30 %) [15]. The nutritional value of trub makes it interesting in regards to production
of foodstuﬀor as an addition to fermentation [15].
5.2
Brewer’s spent grains
Brewer’s spent grains is a major by-product and accounts for approximately 85 % of total
byproducts generated (14 - 20 kg/hL [14]), and is thus readily available as a high volume,
low cost commodity [6]. BSG has been used for animal feed, microorganism cultivation,
bioethanol production, additives in brick production, or simply discarded as waste in
landﬁlls [6, 16].

The composition of BSG varies according to the cereal variety, malting and mashing
conditions, and adjuncts. The composition is approximately 28 - 30 % protein [12, 17],
28 % lignin, 25 % hemicellulose and 17 % cellulose [17], and to a certain extent other
carbohydrates, ﬁbers and lipid (8.2 % according to Kunze [12]). This makes BSG inter-
esting from a nutrition perspective [6, 18]. Due to the nutritional value, BSG has to be
processed or stored cold within a short time of production to avoid spoilage.
Figure 3: The BSG generated from Pilsner malt with the mashing system at the DTU
Brewery. The batch 15MAR17 seen through the sight-glass on the mashing vessel.
The generation of BSG at the DTU Brewery, like any other brewery, is unavoidable,
and thus the Dacoﬁ© ﬁlter press was used to reﬁne the by-product by separating it
into 4 fractions; 2 solid fractions and 2 liquid fractions, which depending on ﬁlter size
contains more or less nutrients, and is explained in further detail in section 6Materials &
methodssection.6.
5.3
Enzymatic activity in mashing
The enzymatic starch degradation is carried out in steps known as gelatinisation, liquefac-
tion and sacchariﬁcation. Gelatinisation is the swelling and bursting of starch granules in
solution, which renders starch chains available for degradation by amylases, and increases
viscosity. Once the starch molecules are exposed, liquefaction takes place where the starch
molecules are broken down by amylases; α-amylase splits amylose and amylopectin chains
to form dextrins of 7-12 glucose units. These are subsequently degraded by β-amylases,
speciﬁcally at the non-reducing ends, by splitting oﬀmaltose, thus producing glucose and
6
==PAGE:15==
maltotriose, as well as other sugars [12]. Due to the speciﬁcity, β-amylase is signiﬁcantly
slower than α-amylase. The viscosity of the gelatinised starch is reduced during liquefaca-
tion. Sacchariﬁcation is the complete degradation of starch to maltose and dextrins by α-
and β-amylases. The optimal temperature for β-amylases is 62-65 °C, while α-amylases
work optimal at 72-75 °C [12].
Amino acids are essential for yeast growth and wort must contain at least 200 mg/L
Free Amino Nitrogen (FAN) for succesful fermentation [12]. FAN is formed at 45-50 °C by
peptidases, these low molecular weight proteins however have a negative eﬀect on foam
stability in the ﬁnal beer. The production or release of proteins such as lipid transfer
protein (LTP1) and protein Z, which have a positive eﬀect on foam stability, happens by
activation of proteinases at 60-70 °C. [12]

6.3
Description of system
The set-up used for the mashing procedure at the DTU Brewery for the generation of wort
and BSG, was designed by food technologist Preben Bøje Hansen. The process aims at
keeping a continuous ﬂow of liquid through the mash, while maintaining the temperature
within the desired intervals to activate enzymes.
The unique mashing system at DTU Brewery consists of a ∼550 L ﬂat-bottomed stain-
less steel vessel with a built-in mixing system. The mixing system ensures a continuous,
temperature controlled, liquid ﬂow throughout the mashing process, by allowing an alter-
nating ﬂow direction through the mash. The alternating ﬂow is made possible by the two
sets of ﬁlters. One set of ﬁlters mounted on the impeller and another at the mid-section
of the tank, allowing ﬂow from top to bottom and vice versa. The ﬂow direction is man-
ually operated by switching pump direction during mashing, which also serves to prevent
equipment failure or damage by preventing pressure build-up.

6.5.1
Dumas combustion method
The Dumas combustion method was used to determine the total nitrogen content in all
samples. The equipment used was the rapid MAX N exceed® by Elementar. The method
is based on the combustion of samples in an induction furnace at 950-1050°C in the pres-
ence of oxygen to form water, CO2, SO2, NOx, and N2. The CO2 and SO2 are then
removed, and NOx is reduced to N2. Total N2 is measured using a thermal conductivity
detector, and a nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor (N/p-factor) can then be used to
calculate the total amount of protein in the sample [22].
In the total nitrogen determination by the Dumas combustion method, the N/p-factor
of 6.25, known as the Kjeldahl factor, was used. There is evidence suggesting that the
Kjeldahl factor is not the most accurate N/p-factor to use, and Jones, D Breese et al
(1941) [23] and the rapid MAX N exceed® technical notes [24] suggest a N/p-factor of
5.83 for barley and other cereal. The Kjeldahl factor is however widely accepted and used
in peer reviewed studies, and thus the 6.25 N/p-factor is also used in this thesis.

6.5.2
Free Amino Nitrogen (FAN) analysis
The FAN analysis was performed as described in section 8.10 of the Analytica-EBC by
the European Brewery Convention, however the protocol was adapted to the available
equipment and a 90°C water bath was used.
The principle of the method relies on the reaction of ninhydrin with all primary amines.
Most free amino acids are oxidatively deaminated to form ammonia in the presence of
ninhydrin, which is reduced to hydrindantin. It is the reaction between ammonia and hy-
drindantin that forms the purple diketohydrindylidene diketohydrindamine (Ruhemann’s
purple) that can be quantiﬁed at 570 nm wavelength [22].
Some known limitations to the method are amino acids such as cysteine and proline,
which does not give the Ruhemann’s purple color due to the formation of other products
with ninhydrin [22].
The LSG samples were centrifuged to remove sludge from the samples and were di-
luted 1:100, before they were treated according to the FAN analysis in the Analytica-EBC,
and were then measured on a spectrophotometer along three glycine standards for every

6.5.3
Bradford (Coomassie) soluble protein assay
The Bradford soluble protein assay is a colorimetric assay based on the formation of
a color complex between soluble proteins in solution and the dye Coomassie Brilliant
Blue G-250, as shown in Figure 9Reaction schematic for the Bradford protein assay
[3].ﬁgure.caption.19.
The protein-dye complex causes a shift in the absorption maxi-
mum of the dye from 465 to 595 nm. The assay detects peptides containing three or more
amino acid residues and the linear concentration range is 0.1-1.5 mg/mL of protein with a
bovine serum albumin (BSA) standard. The assay was used in the 96-well microtiter plate
format and performed in accordance with the Sigma-Aldrich technical bulletin (B6916).
Figure 9: Reaction schematic for the Bradford protein assay [3].
Similar to the FAN analysis, the samples were centrifuged to remove sludge, and an
initial analysis was performed. A dilution gradient was set up to investigate if the pro-
tein concentration of the LSG samples were within the linear concentration range prior
to further analysis. The initial analysis also assisted in determining whether a diﬀerence
between LSG samples taken at the start, middle and end of the experiments was seen.
The assay was performed with 5 µL sample and 250 µL bradford reagent, and the protocol
used called for 30 seconds mixing and a 10 min rest at room temperature prior to reading
absorbance. The dilution gradient showed that the undiluted samples were within the
linear concentration range and the diﬀerence in concentration between samples were rela-
tively close (approx. 10 % variation), so the middle set of samples was chosen for further
analysis. A limitation of this method is that it is unable to detect peptides or proteins
<3000 Da [22].
Due to the nature of the assay, speciﬁcally the small amount of sample used, all
samples (including standards) were analysed in duplicates to reduce the risk of human
error inﬂuencing results.

6.5.4
Brix, Speciﬁc Gravity & Plato
In general Brix-% (°Bx) and °Plato (°P) are two diﬀerent scales that measure the weight
percentage of sucrose solution. °Bx is commonly used in the wine industry and °P is widely
used in the brewing industry, and can be used interchangeably [25]. °Bx was measured
with the refractrometer Mettler Toledo Refracto 30GS, which works by determining the
angle of total reﬂection of the D-line of sodium (589.3 nm). The measurement range is
0-85 °Bx with an accuracy of ±0.2 °Bx in the temperature range of 10-40 °C [26]. The
method does not diﬀerentiate between contributions to the refractive index (RI), and thus
compounds that alters the RI will have an eﬀect on the measurement.
Mettler Toledo Densito 30PX was used to measure speciﬁc gravity (SG), which is de-
termined by the oscillating body method, where a U-shaped sample chamber is ﬁlled and
induced to vibrate [27]. The eigenfrequency of the oscillation is inﬂuenced by the mass,
and thus the density can be determined [28]. The Densito 30PX has a working range of
0.0000-2.0000 g/cm3 and an accuracy of ±0.0001 g/cm3 in the temperature range of 5-35
°C [27].
The °Bx was measured on LSG supernatant after centrifugation, at room temperature
by the MT Refracto 30GS, where the SG was measured on LSG samples containing sludge,
at 20 °C with the MT Densito 30PX. The °Bx and SG were measured in triplicates and
the average value of the middle fractions were converted to °P by equation 2Brix, Spe-

conversion to °P was done in order to compare the two methods and respective conversions.

8
Discussion
In this section some of the more general choices and tendencies of the results presented
in section 7Results & discussionsection.7 are discussed.
8.1
Selection of sugars for HPLC
The selection of sugars, and mainly omission of maltotriose, in the analysis by HPLC was
done based on literature showing low amounts in BSG. Furthermore, not all brewer’s yeast
utilize maltotriose well, and since glucose and maltose are highly utilized in fermentation,
these were selected. Another contribution to the decision of omitting maltotriose in the
experiments was advice given by Ph.D students at DTU National Food Institute, who
indicated that the current state of the available HPLC unit was unsuited for maltotriose.
The Analytica-EBC suggested that maltotriose would be detectable on the column, but
was omitted due to the reasons above.
The variance seen in the samples for glucose in Table 5Data from initial experiments
done on the 300 µm LSG fraction from the press 28MAR17 by Dumas combustion method,
FAN analysis, Bradford soluble protein assay, refractometer, densitometer and HPLC.
The data portrayed here is a segment of the full data set, which can be found in Ap-
pendix AAppendix Aappendix.A, BAppendix Bappendix.B, CAppendix Cappendix.C,
DAppendix Dappendix.D andEAppendix Eappendix.E.table.caption.18 was likely due to
the samples being analysed on two diﬀerent instruments. The equipment was changed to
another HPLC unit with same type column, but with newer software, after maintenance.
8.2
Variance in results between samples
During the analysis of the samples from start, middle and end of the presses diﬀer-
ence in concentration of total nitrogen, FAN, °Bx and SG, as well as for glucose and
maltose, was observed. An example of these diﬀerences between sampling can be seen
in Table 5Data from initial experiments done on the 300 µm LSG fraction from the
press 28MAR17 by Dumas combustion method, FAN analysis, Bradford soluble protein
assay, refractometer, densitometer and HPLC. The data portrayed here is a segment
of the full data set, which can be found in Appendix AAppendix Aappendix.A, BAp-
pendix Bappendix.B, CAppendix Cappendix.C, DAppendix Dappendix.D andEAppendix
Eappendix.E.table.caption.18.
The variance between start, middle and end samples could be due to the the build-up
of material (BSG ”ﬁlter cake”) on the rotating ﬁlters over time. Initially, the clean ﬁlters
had no layer of material, and thus the build-up of material over time on the ﬁlters decrease
the distance between the rubber rollers and ﬁlters, and as a result increase the pressure
between the rollers.
Another possible cause of the variance in the samples was the physical appearance
of the mashing vessel, more speciﬁcally the sight-glass on the vessel. The structure of
39
==PAGE:48==
the vessel, being a ﬂat-bottomed tank with a sight-glass, allows for stagnation in cer-
tain sections around the bottom. As a result of this, some cereal is subject to a lesser
degree of circulation than other, depending on the placement in the vessel. Therefore,
cereal with higher circulation would have more nutrients extracted. This could have a
potential inﬂuence on the results.
However, the unloading of the mashing vessel and
subsequent feeding of the Dacoﬁ© ﬁlter press was done without speciﬁc sequencing, and
was more or less done at random, thus making this an unlikely contributor to the variance.
8.2.1
β-glucan investigation
A potential contributor to the diﬀerences in the pressing of cereals could be the content
of β-glucan in the cereal. Oat having a higher level of β-glucan than barley [29]. The
β-glucan level in wort relates to the viscosity [12], and could merit further investigation.
8.3
Wet vs. dry samples for analysis
The BSG, PSG and LSG samples were analysed wet and not dry (except for ash anal-
ysis), whereas the literature generally represent the data on dry weight basis, meaning
that the samples were dried prior to analysing. The rationale for analysing the fractions
without pretreatment was to portray the composition of the fractions immediately as
they were produced in the mashing process. This was done in order to investigate the
potential application of the fractions in an industrial setting, where the use of product
streams should be as feasible as possible (require little pretreatment), in order to be viable.
8.4
Filter pore size
Based on the comparison of the 300 µm and 100 µm fractions from the Dacoﬁ© ﬁlter
press, showing little variation between chemical composition, it is recommended to inves-
tigate ﬁlters with a larger pore size.
If a larger pore size was to produce a LSG fraction with similar composition, it could
be advantageous to run with a larger pore size to obtain a larger LSG volume for sparging
purposes.

9
Conclusion
The analysis of the PSG and LSG fractions generated with the Dacoﬁ® ﬁlter press showed
that, with the exception of FAN and glucose, the oat fractions contained higher levels of
nutrients compared to Pilsner malt. Without further investigation it is however diﬃcult
to predict, whether Pilsner malt or oat fractions posses the more favourable characteris-
tics, as these depend on the application.
The diﬀerences between the concentrations in the full spectrum of analysis was too
subtle between experiments, to conclude if the mashing conditions mentioned in section
6.4Description of experimentssubsection.6.4 had direct impact on the chemical composi-
tion of BSG, and thus the PSG and LSG fractions.
The LSG fractions display similarities for most analysis, with the exception of FAN
concentration and volume produced. The intent for the application of LSG is to replace
sparge water. Despite the speciﬁc qualities required for LSG in order to extract nutrient
from BSG have not yet been investigated, the results generated in this thesis suggest that
the LSG 300 µm fraction is preferable, due to the larger amount produced and similar
chemical composition as the 100 µm fraction. The LSG 300 µm fraction is thus suggested
for future potential brewing applications.

10
Future work
10.1
Substitution of LSG for sparge water
Since sustainability has been a hot topic in the recent years, and brewing rely on substan-
tial amounts of various utilities; electricity, process steam, chemicals and water, it could
be beneﬁcial to investigate the viability of LSG as a replacement or substitute to sparge
water. The chemical composition of supernatant from LSG 300 µm could prove valuable
in reducing the utilities.
The investigation should include a baseline experiment using conventional sparge wa-
ter to establish a frame of reference. All experiments should include following samples
where relevant; unprocessed BSG, wort, LSG 300 µm fraction prior to sparging, BSG
after sparging. The wort samples of both reference experiment and the following inves-
tigational experiments could provide data on the potential enrichment of the wort. A
controlled fermentation study could follow up on the improvements or disadvantages, and
aid in detecting possible brewing speciﬁc diﬃculties. Increased protein levels can lead to
protein (”beer”) haze, generally an undesirable trait in beer from a marketing, as well as
consumer point-of-view.
Potential problems with using LSG supernatant as sparge water could be that the
nutrients from LSG are retained in the BSG, rather than extracting additional nutrients.
Furthermore, there might be complications utilizing the LSG in already established sys-
tems due to the sugar content, as well as the subsequent regulatory approvals from the
Danish Veterinary and Food Administration, however this needs investigation.
An experimental setup could initially use the lab-scale mashing unit at DTU Brewery
to investigate whether LSG actually functions as a replacement for sparge water, and then
follow up with a scale up to the DTU Brewery mashing system (pilot plant scale).
The pros and cons taken into consideration, the potential upside of increasing sustain-
ability by lowering water utility consumption, as well as utilizing a waste stream for the
production of a bi-product, merits consideration.
10.2
Trub fractions for increased fermentation performance
The pressing of trub from the batches 15MAR17 and 28MAR17 yielded two quite interest-
ing process streams, both with a pleasant organoleptic characteristic due to the aromatic
hops used in the boiling process. The use of the liquid fractions from pressed trub as
an addition to the wort could increase yeast vitality and fermentation performance of S.
cerevisiae [15], while adding an interesting ﬂavour compound. Similar to the aforemen-
tioned suggestion for utilization of LSG supernatant for sparge water, the potential issue
of protein haze in the ﬁnished product could be a drawback.
