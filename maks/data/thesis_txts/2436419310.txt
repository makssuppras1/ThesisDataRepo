Summary
Controlling the motions of a marine vessel in the changing environment of the sea is by far
no trivial task. The unknown characteristics of the sea makes it even more difﬁcult. The wish
for control can be due to positioning like in dynamic positioning, safety related linked to the
strength of the vessel or the energy consumption used to progress the ship through the environ-
ment. The control strategies differs depending on the purpose, where safety related and energy
efﬁcient control can be exercised through change of course, weather-routing or weather-vaning
in order to protect the vessel from excessive motions and related forces. Common for any con-
trol strategy related to motion control of marine vessels whether it is automated or relying on
human operators to take appropriate actions is that it requires accurate and frequently updated
information about the sea state.
This thesis deals with sea state estimation using the motions of the marine vessel. The ma-
rine vessel can be considered as one completely rigid body and its motions modeled with 6
degrees of freedom. The vessel motions due to the impact of waves can be used to estimate
the sea state, that caused the motions. The assumption is that the motion of the vessel is linear
to the incident wave. This is also known as the wave buoy analogy and can be used for ship-
board sea state estimation. The approach is to measure global motion components and vertical
acceleration at speciﬁc positions relative to the centre of gravity of the vessel. The transfer func-
tions of the vessel expresses how the impulse of the wave is transferred to a motion of the vessel.
In this thesis, the sea state estimation algorithm suggested by Brodtkorb et al., (2018b) is
implemented into the Matlab/Simulink environment. This algorithm ﬁnds the wave spectrum
estimate from the vessel response measurements in the frequency-domain by solving a set of
linear equations through iteration. The algorithm rely on the introduction of a residual deﬁned
as the difference between measured and theoretically calculated responses. The algorithm is
based on heave, roll and pitch responses of the vessel corresponding to the 3 degrees of free-
dom, which is not normally under inﬂuence of applied actuators.
The objective of this thesis is to implement the sea state estimation algorithm and reach an
appropriate level of accuracy of the estimated sea state in a near real-time scale.
The experimental methods includes simulation in the Matlab/Simulink Marine Cybernetic
Simulator (MCSim) developed by master students and PhD candidates over more than a decade
now. It also includes model test conducted at the marine cybernetics laboratory (MCLab) fa-
cilities at Norwegian University of Science and Technology, (NTNU), Norway, with the model
ship Cybership 3, which is a model of a platform supply vessel.
The sea state estimation algorithm was successfully run real-time in a simulation environ-
ment. The peak period and wave direction were mostly estimated sufﬁciently accurate, however
the signiﬁcant wave height was challenging to estimate correctly.

Chapter1
Introduction
In this chapter the motivation and background for the thesis, research questions and methodol-
ogy are introduced. The layout of the thesis structure is given at the end of the chapter.
1.1
Motivation and Background
Controlling the motions of a marine vessel in the changing environment of the sea is by far
no trivial task. The unknown characteristics of the sea makes it even more difﬁcult. The wish
for control can be due to positioning like in dynamic positioning, safety related linked to the
strength of the vessel or the energy consumption used to progress the ship through the environ-
ment. The control strategies differs depending on the purpose, where safety related control can
be exercised through change of course, weather-routing or weather-vaning in order to protect
the vessel from excessive motions and related forces. Common for any control strategy related
to motion control of marine vessels whether it is automated or relying on human operators to
take appropriate actions is that it requires accurate and frequently updated information about
the sea state.
Information about the sea state can be obtained in many different ways like e.g. visual
estimates by the captain and his mates or satellite imaging providing weather data are both
frequently used method by seafarers. In some areas wave rider buoys are present at ﬁxed loca-
tions providing very accurate estimates for a local area. This is most common near coastlines,
harbours or offshore construction sites or offshore production areas, where accurate sea state
estimates are needed on a more frequently update rate than in the open space of the oceans.
The motions of the marine vessel can also be used to estimate the sea state. A marine vessel
can be considered as one completely rigid body and its motions modeled with 6 degrees of free-
dom (DOF). Depending on the shape of the vessel, its operating proﬁle in the sea column with
trim, depth and heeling as deﬁning parameters and the shape of the free surface of the sea, the
vessels responses will be more or less coupled in the 6 DOF when subjected to impulses from
either the environment or its actuators (Fossen, 2011).
The mass distribution is part of the operational proﬁle of the vessel and it changes depending
on the task, that the vessel has to solve and the technology set up of the vessel. Since operational
proﬁles changes and the environment at sea is a constantly changing variable, no one control

strategy is optimal.
Hybrid control deals with this. In Hybrid control, multiple control strategies can be imple-
mented in the governance system of the vessel and depending on the operational proﬁle and
sea state, priorities can be set for switching between the different control strategies for the best
match for the given task at hand (Brodtkorb, 2017). Within the hybrid control setup the estima-
tion of the sea state is needed on a near real-time scale for ensuring switching between control
parameters at the proper time.
Nguyen et al., (2007) proposes a hybrid switching framework for DP controller parameters
for a changing sea environment from calm to extreme sea. Brodtkorb et al., (2014 further in-
vestigated this concept and in Brodtkorb et al., (2018b) and Nielsen et al., (2018), two other
methods of estimating the changing sea environment based on the actual energy distribution of
the waves, the wave spectrum, from which the signiﬁcant wave height, Hs and the relative wave
direction, β as well as the peak frequency, ωp can be calculated.
Shipboard sea state estimation, (SSE) has over the years been solved in different ways. Not
only for the purpose of implementation in control strategies, but also for performance evalua-
tion like improved energy efﬁciency, see e.g. Papanikolaou et al., (2016) or evaluation of safety
levels in for instance decision support systems, see e.g. Nielsen, (2010). One approach is based
on the analogy of a vessel to a wave buoy. Nielsen, (2017) gives a comprehensive account of the
different ways of using the wave buoy analogy for shipboard SSE. The approach is to measure
global motion components and vertical acceleration at speciﬁc positions relative to the centre of
gravity of the vessel. The assumption is that the 6 DOF motion of the vessel due to the impact
of waves is linear to the incident wave. The transfer functions (or response amplitude operators,
RAOs) of the vessel expresses how the wave elevation is transferred to a motion of the vessel.
RAOs can be calculated on the basis of the hull shape using strip theory, panel codes or CFD.
A large number of commercial software exits for this purpose. It can also be measured and is
part of standard model testing programs recommended by ITTC, (2011). There are two main
approaches for using the wave buoy analogy, namely solving the problem in the time-domain
or in the frequency-domain.
A frequency-domain approach is suggested by Brodtkorb et al., (2018b) with high compu-
tational efﬁciency, which makes this SSE look very promising for implementation into control
models or for on site decision support. This algorithm ﬁnds the wave spectrum estimate from
the vessel response measurements by solving a set of linear equations through iteration. The al-
gorithm relies on the introduction of a residual deﬁned as the difference between measured and
theoretically calculated responses. The algorithm is based on heave, roll and pitch responses
of the vessel corresponding to the 3 DOF, which is not normally under inﬂuence of applied
actuators.
1.2
Research Questions
In this thesis, the sea state estimation algorithm derived by Brodtkorb et al. (2018b) is imple-
mented into the Matlab/Simulink environment, which enables applications for control purposes.
The algorithm will in this thesis be refereed to as the SSE algorithm. The objective of this thesis

1.3 Methodology
is to reach an appropriate level of accuracy of the estimated sea state in a near real-time scale.
The framework of the thesis can therefore be formulated as:
"Implementation of Sea State Estimation in a simulation environment for Marine Vessels."
Within this framework, the following questions are addressed in this thesis:
1. How can the SSE algorithm be implemented in Matlab/Simulink to allow near real-time
estimates of relevant sea state parameters?
2. What is the relationship between the computational time and the accuracy of the algo-
rithm??
3. What are suitable key point indicators for the estimation accuracy for veriﬁcation of the
successful implementation of the SSE algorithm?
1.3
Methodology
This thesis is based on empirical evidence obtained through tests of the algorithm performance
based on simulated response data, and response data obtained through model-scale experiments.
The methods for obtaining empirical evidence is based on previous research work and accepted
theoretical concepts within the ﬁeld of maritime engineering. The experimental methods in-
cludes simulation in the Matlab/Simulink Marine Cybernetic Simulator (MCSim) developed by
master students and PhD candidates over more than a decade now, which has its origin in the
work published by Sørensen et al., (2003). It also includes model test conducted at the marine
cybernetics laboratory (MCLab) facilities at the Norwegian University of Science and Tech-
nology, (NTNU), Norway. The experiments were conducted with the model ship Cybership 3,
which is a model of a platform supply vessel. The empirical evidence is compared to previ-
ous obtained results within the research ﬁeld of sea state estimation and the SSE algorithm is
assessed for use in online control applications by the use of deﬁned key point indicators (KPI’s).
The applied methodology and scope of work follows the step-wise implementation strategy
deﬁned by the following steps:
• Implement the SSE into the framework of Matlab/Simulink and deﬁne KPI’s.
• Simulate the performance of the SSE utilizing the tools of MCSim and tune the SSE for
reaching the stated KPI’s.
• Experiments in various sea conditions in the MCLab for veriﬁcation of the SSE against
the stated KPI’s.
Due to time constraints, only station keeping will be considered, that means that no treat-
ment of Doppler shift due to forward motion will be included. The applied simulation models
and experimental setups will be simpliﬁed to the outmost extend in order to limit sources of
interference with the SSE algorithm. For the simulations, this means, that any functions not
related to the vessel dynamics due to the wave excitation, like e.g. wind and current forces, will
be turned off and control functions will be simpliﬁed as much as possible. For the model test-
ing, this means that it will be done in a moored setup to avoid issues with the control algorithms
and the vessel monitoring and control system. The sea states considered are fully developed
wind-generated and long-crested. Only wave forces is considered.

1.4
Thesis Structure
The thesis is organized as follows:
• Chapter 2: contains background theory and deﬁnitions used in this thesis.
• Chapter 3: describes the SSE algorithm and its implementation in Matlab/Simulink.
• Chapter 4: describes the setup for the conducted simulations in MCSim and the lab-
oratory setup in MCLab for the conducted experiments. Estimation results based on

Chapter3
Sea state estimation algorithm
This chapter describes the sea state estimation algorithm and its implementation in Matlab/Simulink.
3.1
The Sea state estimation algorithm
In this section the work of Brodtkorb et al., (2018b) is described. It is based on the wave buoy
analogy (see e.g. Nielsen, (2017)) and uses the measured positions in heave, pitch and roll as
input as well as pre-calculated transfer functions for the applied vessel. Results are focused
entirely on ships without forward speed, such as a station-kept vessel. For zero forward speed
the incident wave frequency is equal to the encounter frequency. In the published paper, closed
form expressions for the wave-to-motion transfer functions (see Jensen et al., (2004)) are used,
but for the vessel considered in this thesis, ShipX VERES by SINTEF has been used to ob-
tain the transfer functions. The SSE algorithm does not assume any shape or orientation of the
estimated wave spectrum. Instead it solves a set of linear equations relating the wave spec-
trum to the measured responses through the vessels transfer functions. The sea state estimation
algorithm has two main steps; ﬁrst ﬁnding an estimate of the wave spectrum, and secondly
considering the energy contained in the wave spectra estimates to ﬁnd the wave direction.

3.2
Implementation in Matlab/Simulink
Some work had already been done on implementing the SSE algorithm in the Matlab/Simulink
environment by Brodtkorb et al., (2018a) prior to the start of this thesis. The overall imple-
mentation strategy has been to create a Simulink module, that will take measured position in
a 6 DOF vector η as an input and give Hs, ωp and β as output as shown in Figure 3.1. The
input vector is ﬁrst sent through a selector for sorting out heave, roll and pitch. These three
positions parameters is processed through a Cross-Spectrum Estimator, which is a standard
Simulink block for calculation of cross-power spectrum density on the basis of time series data.
In order to obtain time series, the position parameters is sampled by the use of a zero-order
hold and a buffer for collection of the sampling. The control of sample time and buffer-size
gives the offset together with the iteration time for which the sea state parameters is estimated
in time. The Buffer-size is also used as the length for the FFT calculation in the Cross-Spectrum
Estimator. The position parameters is coupled in 6 different ways; heave-heave, roll-roll, pitch-
pitch, heave-roll, heave-pitch and roll-pitch. The 6 response spectra is assembled as a matrix
and given as input to a Matlab function, FastSSE.m (Appendix A), which follows the procedure

Deﬁnitions of Key Point Indicators
Based on Table 2.3 the following key point indicators (KPIs) is proposed for a sea state estima-

• KPI for Hs:±10%
• KPI for Tp: ±10%
No KPI is deﬁned for the relative wave direction, β. The accuracy of the two other parame-

Hs and Tp. For some control applications an accurate estimate of β can be very relevant like for
instance in station keeping. If no desired global heading is speciﬁed, keeping a speciﬁed relative
heading to the wave ﬁeld is often the most energy optimal control strategy. This is termed as
weather-vaning and some DP control strategies has been developed for this, see e.g. Kim and

weather-vaning has also become relevant for adverse weather conditions as a survival strategy
for under-powered ships see e.g. Papanikolaou et al., (2016).

Cybership 3 was used in a moored setup as seen on Figure 4.17. The moorings for head and
following sea was made in a breast-like pattern with 4 lines in order to minimize the inﬂuence
of the mooring lines to the movement of the vessel in heave and pitch. During the experiments
no restriction effects was observed in heave nor pitch, but some were observed for roll. This
was mostly when pitching bow down and roll occurred at the same time, introducing a yawing
moment due to hydrodynamic lift forces on the hull. The restriction from the mooring lines on
yawing, then introduced a damping on the roll motion as well. It was tried to reduce this effect
by only having one point attachment in each end of the hull, but it could not be completely
removed with the available equipment in the MCLab. It was also tried to use weights attached
to the mooring lines to act as dampers, but these seemed to introduce damping of the pitching
movement, so this was discarded. The lines were tied up to clamps attached to the sides of the
tank using a clove hitch for ﬁrm attachment. The heading was adjusted by adjusting the lengths
of the individual lines and using the yaw reading from the Qualisys positioning system.

Chapter5
Conclusions and Perspectives for Future
Work
This chapter concludes the thesis and gives perspectives and suggestions for future work.
5.1
Conclusions
The sea state estimation algorithm was successfully run real-time in a simulation environment.
The peak period and wave direction were mostly estimated sufﬁciently accurate, however the
signiﬁcant wave height was challenging to estimate correctly. The proposed key point indica-
tors showed good correlation with the accurate estimates indicating, that the SSE algorithm can
be implemented and used for control models, decision support or other purposes, which needs
accurate estimates of the current sea state.
However, ﬁnding the right wave direction is crucial for the SSE algorithm to perform. When
the direction estimate is wrong, so are the other estimated parameters.
The computational efﬁciency was found to depend mostly on the number of iterations for the
residuals to converge below the speciﬁed threshold. The parameters that have an inﬂuence on
the number of iterations is the number of frequencies, the number of potential wave directions,
the step size h and the threshold size ε. For the step size h and the threshold size ε, ways of
predetermining their values was utilized with good results. The time-delay of the SSE algo-
rithm was found to depend on the number of iterations and the buffer-size. The buffer-size also
inﬂuences the accuracy and steadiness of the estimates.
The SSE algorithm has only been tested using JONSWAP spectra and only for some sea
states and vessels, so some care should be taken when using the module in cases not similar to
those examined in this work.
5.2
Future Work
Several proposals arise for future work based on the work presented in this thesis.
• Determine cause of energy deviation: The energy of the wave spectrum estimates (Hs)
from the SSE algorithm was found to consistently deviate strongly from the speciﬁed
35
==PAGE:54==
Chapter 5. Conclusions and Perspectives for Future Work
wave spectrum Hs.
• Perform a Quality Assurance Work on MCSim: During the work with this thesis, some
uncertainties was discovered with MCSim related to the environment module and the
vessel dynamics module, where some differences where found between speciﬁed wave
spectra, realized wave spectra and vessel motion series. This could also be related to the
deviations found in energy of wave spectrum.
• Include Closed Form Expression: In this thesis, pre-calculated transfer functions is used
for the SSE algorithm, but these are essentially only valid for one velocity and one hydro
dynamical proﬁle which means that they change with changes in the operational proﬁle
of the vessel. One way of dealing with this is to have a large data base of pre-calculated
transfer functions that the SSE algorithm can use as look-up table for the actually case
at hand. Another way is the closed-form expressions. These two methods should be
compared for accuracy and computational efﬁciency.
• Test on more Sea Types: In this thesis only a few JONSWAP wave systems has been
used for testing of the SSE algorithm. The SSE algorithm should be tested on more sea
types in order to validate its full potential.
