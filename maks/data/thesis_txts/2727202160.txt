Abstract
Long waiting times during transit not only cause inconvenience for daily passengers using public
transport, but also inflate economical and environmental costs to the society. One approach to
mitigate these issues is by synchronizing arrival and departure times through minor timetable
adjustments, while considering the impacts on the feasibility of vehicle schedules. Addressing
these concerns by finding an optimal solution for large-scale real-world instances is impractical
within a reasonable computation time.
Additionally, purchasing a commercial optimization
solver for these issues is prohibitively costly for public transport agencies. An ideal solution
should be fast, able to handle large-scale problems, and have low implementation costs.
This thesis addresses above issues by minimizing the transfer waiting time cost as the ob-
jective to the integrated timetabling and vehicle scheduling problem (IT-VSP). Given an initial
timetable and passenger transfer demands, a weighted sum of the transfer time cost is minimized
by adjusting timetables within a set of headway and vehicle schedule constraints. A heuristic
approach of adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) was proposed to solve the IT-VSP by
selecting a subset of timetabled trips (via destroy operators) and modifying their departure time
(via repair operators), with an aim of reducing the passenger transfer time cost. The vehicle
scheduling constraints was fulfilled separately by simply solving the model of multiple depot
vehicle scheduling in a general free solver HiGHS.
The proposed ALNS was applied to a real-life case study of the Greater Copenhagen area.
Results indicate that for the small-sized instances the ALNS is capable to reduce the transfer
costs (in 5-hr) that are comparable to those obtained by a standard commercial solver Gurobi
in 24 hrs, with the largest gap of 1.5%. The ALNS consistently performs well even in cases of
larger instances where the Gurobi solver fails to find a feasible solution within the given 24-
hr. Furthermore, the ALNS is able to improve the transfer cost, in comparison to the original
timetable, by 15.5-34% in 5 hrs, depending on the size of instances. Lastly, for the largest
instance, the ALNS can reduce the average passenger transfer time from 5.9 to 5 mins and
increase the number of passengers with desired transfer time by approximately 45% in 5-hr.
These results suggest that the proposed ALNS can potentially reduce passenger transfer
costs by solving IT-VSP in a reasonable computation time and with a low implementation cost.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Public transport affects the quality of life of billions of people around the world.
It has a
profound impact on personal opportunities as well as the global economy.
Its detrimental
influence on our living environment is also significant. According to the International Energy
Agency (IEA), the transport sector accounted for 24% of global CO2 emissions in 2018 —
passenger vehicles being the largest contributor at 45.1% (IEA, 2018). Denmark has set a goal
to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions by 70% from 1990 levels by 2030. Agencies like Movia
play a crucial role in achieving this target by offering low-fare, attractive public transport.
However, public transit transfers can sometimes cause serious inconvenience with delays and
long wait times, and they are often inevitable in large transport systems. Long wait time at
stations or stops is widely considered as one of the main reasons for making public transport an
unfavorable mobility option among passengers. Reducing transfer waiting times can improve
the appeal of public transit and potentially diminish congestion and CO2 emissions.
In recent years, many studies have been dedicated to reducing transfer times to enhance
ridership in public transport (e.g., Abdolmaleki et al., 2020; Barrena et al., 2014; Z. Liu and
Shen, 2007; H. Shang et al., 2019; Wong et al., 2008). Addressing timetabling and vehicle
scheduling problems together has been regarded as an effective strategy to reduce passenger
transfer waiting times while maintaining similar operating costs (Cao and Ceder, 2019; T. Liu
and Ceder, 2017; Petersen et al., 2013). For example, Fonseca et al. (2018) aims to minimize
both transfer waiting time and operational cost by addressing the integrated timetabling and
vehicle scheduling problem (IT-VSP) as a bi-objective mixed integer programming problem. A
matheuristic is created to solve the mathematical model of the IT-VSP. The effectiveness of
the matheuristic is demonstrated on the express-bus line in the Greater Copenhagen area with
data provided by Movia. Results have indicated that the matheuristic can seek better feasible
solutions faster than a commercial solver that is used for solving the IT-VSP model directly.
However, there are several practical challenges that make it difficult to implement the pro-
posed method (Fonseca et al., 2018) in practice. The first practical challenge is the high cost of
commercial solvers, such as Gurobi. In order to effectively and efficiently implement the pro-
posed matheuristic algorithm, Movia is required to purchase the expensive solver. Nonetheless,

due to the shoestring budget, acquiring these expensive solvers is limited. Hence, there is a
desperate need for cost-effective solutions. One option is utilizing free, open-source optimiza-
tion solvers like HiGHS., but such solvers perform significantly slower than their commercial
counterparts. A promising alternative is designing a heuristic algorithm, which can often find
a good solution with much less computational effort and without the need for costly solvers.
The second challenge relates to the incomplete picture of actual operational costs. These
costs are indeed primarily driven by timetabling, vehicle scheduling, and crew scheduling. The
interplay between vehicle and crew scheduling can result in unexpected costs. For instance,
changes to the timetable may improve service quality and vehicle scheduling but could lead to
poor crew scheduling with more unintended operational costs, or even render the current prac-
tice unfeasible. Addressing all these issues simultaneously, however, is too complex to handle.
Therefore, a practical approach for Movia to reduce passenger transfer waiting times would be
fixing vehicle scheduling or making small changes that are not disruptive to its subsequent op-
erations, such as keeping the number of vehicles and the number of trips for each line constant.
Doing so ensures the new timetable is not too expensive to carry out.
Third, resistance to change must be considered when seeking for solutions to reduce passen-
ger transfer waiting times. Although the case study in Fonseca et al. (2018) dictates the transfer
time can be potentially reduced, the change in existing vehicle scheduling is substantial. For
example, the number of schedules has been reduced from 205 to 195, and 42.8% of all trips
are required to be assigned to different depots from their initial locations. Such changes might
demotivate Movia from adopting the proposed framework due to the potential change resistance
from various stakeholders, including the public transport operators, community, and bus depot
providers. To minimize the impact on current practices and reduce resistance, the study should
also strive to minimize changes to the existing system while improving the service quality. It
can therefore increase the likelihood of successful implementation of the proposed timetable.
This thesis aims to resolve the above three practical issues faced by Movia when implement-
ing the proposed framework in Fonseca et al. (2018). Thus, this project focuses on answering
the following research question:
Given an initial transit network timetable,
can a heuristic optimization approach be a low-cost solution to improve
passenger transfer time in reasonable computation time?

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Researching practical solutions for public transport is inherently challenging. Challenges typ-
ically arise from the naturally complicated interface between service operation, customer sat-
isfaction, and financial limitation (Currie, 2021). Addressing them as a whole is particularly
difficult. Traditionally, optimization problem in public transport is divided into a subset of
smaller problems and solved in a sequence at different stages of the planning process - strategic,
tactical, and operational, as described in the reviews by Desaulniers and Hickman (2007) and
Guihaire and Hao (2008). For instance, the process commences with strategic planning, where
it considers long-term decisions such as bus station location and transit routes. Afterwards, the
service offered to passengers (i.e., tactical planning) is assigned, such as bus service frequencies
and timetables. Last is the operational planning phase, focusing on how to offer the service at
the lowest cost, and in which the vehicle scheduling problem is solved according to the timetable,
then followed by crew scheduling and rostering.
One drawback of sequential planning is that it ignores interdependence between different
problems in the planning process.
This lack of consideration can lead to unforeseen conse-
quences, such as a minor alteration to the timetable could render the initial vehicle schedules
infeasible or create more costly vehicle schedules. Moreover, solving problems separately often
results in sub-optimal solutions in the bigger picture. In the past, in order to get a more holistic
view of solutions, researchers have begun drawing to the integrated approach to problem-solving
in the late 1990s and early 2000s. For example, Ceder (2001) investigated the integration of
timetabling and the single-depot vehicle scheduling problems (SDVSP). Similar studies from
Freling et al. (1999) and Freling et al. (2001) have provided a comprehensive look at models
and techniques for integrating vehicle and crew scheduling in 1999, and later a practical applica-
tion of the integrated approach in 2001. Bornd¨orfer et al. (2004) also examined the integration
of multi-depot vehicles and duty scheduling. Since then, this integrated approach has gained
growing attention in the public transportation research community (e.g., Ibarra-Rojas et al.,
2014; Laporte et al., 2017; Petersen et al., 2013; Sch¨obel, 2017; Van den Heuvel et al., 2008).
Studies on this issue have already proposed innovative models and methodologies that might
be used in practical settings in the near future.

Public transport optimization problems are generally formulated as a mathematical pro-
gramming (MP) model. To solve large-scale real-world public transport problems, most studies
have been devoted to heuristic or metaheuristic approaches for solving MP models. This is
mainly because these approaches can often generate good solutions with much less computa-
tional effort, when compared to using optimization solvers like Gurobi. T. Liu et al. (2021) and
Guihaire and Hao (2008) have presented a comprehensive review of the commonly used classes
of meta-heuristic algorithms, including:
• Neighborhood searches, such as simulated annealing (e.g., Domschke, 1989; T. Liu and
Ceder, 2017; Poorjafari et al., 2014; Sch¨ule et al., 2009; J. Wang et al., 2021) and tabu
search (e.g., Adamski and Bryniarska, 1997; Daduna and Voß, 1995; Jansen et al., 2002;
Voss, 1992);
• Evolutionary search, such as genetic algorithm (e.g., Cao and Ceder, 2019; Nachtigall
and Voget, 1996; P. Shang et al., 2018; Shrivastava and Dhingra, 2002; Tuzun Aksu and
Yılmaz, 2014; Y. Wang et al., 2020 )
In this thesis, the solution method for the MIP model is based on an extension of the large neigh-
borhood search (LNS) metaheuristic, namely the adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS).
In LNS, a neighborhood solution is generated by first destroying current solutions and then
repairing the destroyed solution. Because a large part of the solution can be destroyed, the
neighborhood holds a large amount of solution, which allows the LNS to explore the solution
space easily and thus traverses a more promising search path (Gendreau and Potvin, 2019).
This is in contrast to small neighborhoods of other metaheuristics and hence is what makes
LNS distinctive.
ALNS simply extends LNS by using multiple destroy and repair methods
within the same search process. Each destroy and repair method is given a weight to con-
trol the probability of being selected for use during the search and the weights are continually
adapted as the search progresses.
Apart from the study by Fonseca et al. (2018), the study from Petersen et al. (2013) also
shares some similarities with this thesis in terms of its problem and solution method. Petersen et
al. (2013) aims to reduce passenger transfer waiting time by considering an integrated timetable
and vehicle scheduling problem, which is modeled as an integer programming problem with two
objectives (i.e., passenger service and operating cost) and is solved using an LNS. The proposed
LNS is tested on the express-bus network in the Greater Copenhagen area. The results indicate
that modifying the timetable can potentially decrease passenger transfer waiting times by up
to 20%. There are two destroy methods used in LNS. The first one is simply removing trips
at random and the second is randomly removing a trip that is highly related to another trip
based on the relatedness principle. During the search process, the fixed weights of 0.15 and
0.85 are assigned to the first and second destroy methods, respectively. The repair method used
==PAGE:15==
5
to reinsert the removed trips is a randomized greedy heuristic, which inserts the trip with the
lowest insertion cost.
To solve large-scale problems with ALNS more efficiently, there are some interesting per-
spectives on analyzing the mathematical properties of the problem before designing the destroy
and repair methods. For example, Barrena et al. (2014) developed a fast ALNS metaheuristic
to solve the mathematical programming model, which formulates the train timetable problem
on a single rail rapid transit line under the dynamic passenger demand, with the objective
of minimizing the passenger average waiting time (AWT). They found that when a train is
inserted into (removed from) the timetable, the passenger AWT decreases (increases). This in-
sight allows designing several useful destroy and repair operators, such as inserting (removing)
trains only in the largest (smallest) departure interval, as it guarantees to provide minimum
services even if there are a few passengers waiting for the next train. The results show that the
proposed ALNS can reduce the average passenger waiting time by 26%, using only less than
1% of the computation time required by the solver-based algorithm. A similar idea to develop
ALNS destroy and repair methods is also employed in Yin et al. (2021), where they showed that
the proposed ALNS algorithm outperforms the optimization solver CPLEX evidently in terms
of computational speed when seeking for better solutions.

3.1
Problem Description
This thesis focuses on the integrated timetabling and vehicle scheduling problem (IT-VSP),
which is essentially the same problem as presented by Fonseca et al., 2018.
In this thesis,
IT-VSP involves determining the most efficient timetable for passengers’ transfer waiting time,
taking into account factors such as vehicle capacities, time constraints of timetable modification,
various types of transfer request. It is described in the following sections.
3.1.1
Transfer Opportunity and Transfer Waiting Time
Transfer waiting time becomes relevant when a passenger takes a transfer opportunity, i.e.,
transferring from a particular trip to another line at a specific stop. The transfer takes place at
the intersection between two bus lines or interconnection between bus and train lines.
For instance, Figure 3.1 (a) illustrates a transfer opportunity at a Lyngby station between
two bus lines, 300S and 400S. Passengers are expected to transfer from an arriving trip of a
feeder line (e.g, 300S) to the earliest available trip of their desired line (e.g.,400S) at a specific
stop (e.g, Lyngby station). The number of passengers using a transfer opportunity is known
and fixed.
To understand what transfer waiting time means, we consider an example in Figure 3.1 (b),
which depicts a situation where passengers experience a transfer waiting period when disem-
barking from a trip of 300S, walking to the stop of the 400S line, and then embarking on 400S
at the Lyngby Station. Given a minimum transfer time of 4 minutes in the current timetable,
trip i of the 300S line arrives at 9:30 AM, providing a 10-minute transfer time before the next
departing trip j3 of the 400S line at 9:40 AM. This results in a 6-minute excess transfer time.
The extra transfer time is thus defined as the passenger transfer waiting time.

3.1.2
The Objective of the IT-VSP
The aim of the IT-VSP is to minimize the transfer cost, which is defined as a sum of transfer
waiting time per passenger. The transfer waiting time per transfer opportunity is weighted by a
value of time cost per passenger CTR. Given an initial timetable and a fixed number of transfer
passengers, the weighted sum of transfer time cost is minimized by allowing shift changes in
departure time of trips from their first stops, when subject to a set of headway and vehicle
constraints.
3.1.3
Timetable Modifications
The minimization of the transfer cost is achieved by modifying the provided timetable. For
example, a modification to the departure time of the trip j2 from 9:30 AM to 9:34 AM would
reduce the transfer waiting time from 6 to 0 minutes for passengers wishing to transfer from
trip i of 300S to line 400S.

3.1.4
The Integrated Vehicle Scheduling
The modifications in trips’ timetables would change the departure (arrival) time of a vehicle
at the start (end) terminal, which in turn affect vehicle scheduling. In this thesis, the vehicle
scheduling problem is integrated as a part of constraints in the IT-VSP model. In particular, it
is related to the multiple depot vehicle scheduling problem (MDVSP). It consists of assigning a
set of timetabled trips to vehicles from a set of depots within a certain budget (e.g., a limited
number of available vehicles). The inputs to the MDVSP include timetabled trips to be serviced,
a fleet of vehicles available for scheduling, and distances between all terminal stops and depots.
Vehicles must commence and conclude their routes at the same depot.

3.2
Mathematical Formulation for the IT-VSP
The mathematical formulation for the integrated passenger-oriented timetabling and vehicle
scheduling problem (IT-VSP) is mainly adapted from the work in Fonseca et al., 2018. The
main differences between the IT-VSP model addressed in this thesis and the original IT-VSP
model in Fonseca et al., 2018 are:
• First, the objective function in this thesis considers only the minimization of passengers’
transfer waiting time cost.
• Second, the vehicle scheduling component of the IT-VSP is considered only in the model’s
constraints. Moreover, an extra constraint on the total number of available vehicles is
introduced. This additional constraint was established in collaboration with Movia based
on its current practice to maintain its operational costs within a specific budget. Thus,
this model aims to leverage all available vehicles to enhance passengers’ transfer time.
• Third, all components regarding the dwell time added to a trip are removed from the
model’s objective function and constraints. According to Movia’s practice, not adding
dwell time to a trip in timetable modification facilitates the implementation of the new
improved timetable, as it is only a shift change of the departure time of a trip from its
first stop. The elimination of dwell time elements also makes the model less intricate, thus
reducing the computational load.

Chapter 4
A Heuristic Approach
This chapter proposes a heuristic approach of adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) to
solve the IT-VSP discussed in Chapter 3. As the number of trips, transfer requests and con-
straints grows, IT-VSP becomes exponentially more complex and computationally demanding
to solve. Due to its complexity, finding an exact optimal solution for the large-scale problem
instance is infeasible within a reasonable time frame. In fact, for an instance considered in this
project, an feasible solution of the IT-VSP cannot be obtained using a standard commercial
solver even after running for 4 days. In addition, it is too expensive to buy a commercial opti-
mization solver to solve IT-VSP from Movia’s perspective. A developed method should ideally
be reasonably fast, able to handle large-scale problems, and with low-implementation cost.
This chapter proposes a heuristic ALNS approach to the IT-VSP. Instead of achieving an
optimal solution, the ALNS method focuses on quickly finding good-quality solutions that are
often sufficient for practical purposes, by using a set of destroy and repair operators. Since the
ALNS method is a heuristic algorithm, it does not rely on complex mathematical formulations
or specialized solvers. It is generally implemented using standard programming languages and
can be developed and deployed at a relatively low cost. Its speed and low-implementation cost
make the ALNS a valuable tool for practical applications, such as solving IT-VSP for Movia in
this thesis.
The remaining part of this chapter is structured as follows. Section 4.1 provides an overview
of the general ALNS and highlights its key distinctions from the large neighborhood search
(LNS) approach. Section 4.2 elucidates the adaptation of the ALNS heuristic specifically de-
signed to address the IT-VSP problem, focusing on the main components of the algorithm.
4.1
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search Metaheuristic
To solve the IT-VSP using a heuristic approach, it requires a heuristic to select certain trips, then
modify the timetables of those selected trips while reducing the transfer cost within constraints
defined in Chapter 3. One of such heuristics is the adapted large neighborhood search (ALNS)
metaheuristic, first proposed by Ropke and Pisinger, 2006, which has been commonly used to

repair operators.

VSP in this thesis. A destroy operator could randomly select 20% of trips that are allowed for
timetable modifications, while a repair operator could repair the trips at random by making a
shift change in the departure time of the chosen trip from its first stop.

4.2.6
Stopping Criteria
The ALNS algorithm runs continuously until a termination condition is met. Commonly used
termination criteria include a maximum number of search iterations, a maximum runtime, a
predefined number of iterations when there is no improvement in the best-found solution, or
a maximum number of reheating (Barrena et al., 2014). For our implementation, the ALNS
algorithm stops if it exceeds a predefined time limit and returns the best solution found within
the given time frame.

4.2.8
Feasibility Check
IT-VSP is subject to a list of constraints about timetable modification and vehicle schedules.
It is necessary to check if these constraints are fulfilled in the new solution to make sure it is a
==PAGE:37==
27
4.2. Applying ALNS to Solve IT-VSP
feasible solution.
The ALNS heuristic proposed so far is not able to check the feasibility of vehicle schedules
in the new solution. However, this matter can be resolved by separately checking timetable
modification and vehicle schedules in two simple stages. At the first stage, the feasibility of
timetable modification is checked in the proposed ALNS, while the feasibility check of the
vehicle schedules is completed at a later stage by solving MDVSP using the new improved
timetable generated by ALNS.
In ALNS, given a trip i to repair and the current solution S, a list of departure time T d
S,i
for selection is created based on the allowed timetable modifications defined in the IT-VSP
model’s constraints. Moreover, to ensure feasibility of a newly repaired trip, all other trips were
considered to check, including both selected and non-selected trips. Furthermore, the current
departure time of the repaired trip is included as one of the time instants to be selected to
guarantee that there exists at least one feasible departure times.
Although it may be inconvenient to include an additional stage of solving MDVSP in a solver
to complete a full solution feasibility check, ALNS might investigate more solutions, allowing
the search to visit previously infeasible solutions, by relaxing some constraints (i.e., vehicle
schedules constraints). In addition, it is observed that the required computational resources
associated with solving MDVSP are not significant.
The second-stage feasibility check can
therefore be executed with a free solver like HiGHS.

Case Study
This chapter describes the real-world data that was used to demonstrate the performance of
the proposed ALNS algorithm for IT-VSP. The data employed in this thesis is derived from the
express-bus (S-Bus) network in the Greater Copenhagen area in Denmark. The data includes
information such as bus stops and bus lines, bus original schedules, passenger transfer demand,
and distances between depots and each bus trip’s terminal stops. This information is necessary
for modeling and evaluating the algorithm’s performance.
Two distinct real-life data sets (with a total of 6 instances) are considered in this thesis. One
is a smaller data set, constructed and used by Fonseca et al. (2018). This data set provides an
additional benchmark of the performance of ALNS algorithm, compared to the results obtained
directly from standard optimization solvers, such as Gurobi. The other is a recent data set from
November 2022. This data set was developed during the thesis project in collaboration with
other two master students (Baptiste Victorin Redon and Jean Vincent Joseph Delair) using
data provided by the Danish public transport agency Movia. The most recent data set reflects
current situation of passenger transfer waiting time in the Greater Copenhagen area in 2022,
and has a much larger size in terms of the number of transfer opportunities. By anaylzing the
most recent data set, the project can provide Movia valuable insights and recommendations for
improving the current transportation efficiency and addressing the evolving needs of passengers.
The rest of this chapter is organized as follows. Section 5.1 describes the data in detail,
including the network of S-Bus and local S-Train in the Greater Copenhagen area. In Section 5.2,

values of parameters in the case study are presented.

Chapter 6
Parameter Tuning
This chapter focuses on the parameters that require tuning, the process of parameter tuning,
and the outcomes obtained through tuning experiments. The goal of parameter tuning is to find
the most suitable set of parameter values that maximizes the ALNS algorithm’s performance.
Section 6.1 presents a total 6 tuning parameters. While developing the algorithm in Sec-
tion 4.2 in the early adhoc trial-and-error phase, these six parameters are found to have a
relatively significant impact on the ALNS algorithm’s effectiveness. After choosing these pa-
rameters, tuning experiments were implemented following the process outlined in Section 6.2.
Various experiments were conducted using different parameter settings. The performance
metrics expressed by the average gap of the objective function value were recorded, and eval-
uated for each parameter configuration. Finally, the tuning results and analyses of the impact
of changing specific parameters on the behavior of ALNS algorithm are shown in Section 6.3.
6.1
Parameters
This section presents the parameters that were identified for fine-tuning in the ALNS imple-
mentation. Table 6.1 summarizes a total of 6 key parameters that were tuned in this study.
The first column shows the tuning parameters. A description of each parameter is provided
in the second column. Tuning involves systematically adjusting the parameter values within a
given range, defined in the last column.
Regarding the tuning parameters, first there are parameters relating to destroy operators:
(1) ξmin, ξmax, which control the minimum and maximum number of trips that are selected
to repair at each iteration; (2) λ, which determines the number of lines to be selected in the
function of CostProbabilityDestroy in Algorithm 2; (3) κ, which influences the number of
trips that are considered as expensive trips in the function of MostExpensiveTripsDestroy
in Algorithm 3. As for repair operators, there are no parameters to be tuned for the random
repair operator and the greedy repair operator. The randomised greedy repair operator contains
a threshold parameter α that controls the randomness of the operator.

6.2
Tuning Process
To tune the above parameters, this study adopts a similar strategy as outlined in Ropke and
Pisinger, 2006.
The tuning process can be divided into two phases.
First, an initial guess
parameter setting is generated in the trial-and-error phase during the development of the ALNS
algorithm.
In the second phase, an improved parameter setting is found by changing one
parameter with a number of predefined values, while keeping the remaining parameters fixed.
Each parameter setting is applied to a small and representative instance1 over five times. The
setting with the best average performance (in terms of the average percentage gap to the best-
seen solution) is selected. Then, it proceeds to the next tuning parameter, and uses the best
performance values found so far, as well as initial tuning values for the other parameters that
have not been considered. This process continues until all parameters have been tuned. While
the process can be repeated by tuning parameters in different orders or using a new initial guess
as a starting point, for this study it was terminated after one pass due to limited time of the
1The tuning instance should be small as there are numerous tuning experiments and it should be related to
the problem at which the ALNS is targeted.

Computational Experiments
This chapter evaluates the performance of the proposed ALNS through a series of computa-
tional experiments on real-world instances introduced in Chapter 5. It includes the definitions
of two performance metrics of the ALNS (i.e., computational performance and solution quality)
in Section 8.1, analyses of computational performance and objective value convergence in Sec-
tion 8.2, as well as analyses of solution quality based on transfer cost and passengers transfer
quality in relation to the transfer waiting time in Section 8.3.
The computational performance is defined in relation to solving the IT-VSP model directly
with a commercial solver Gurobi in 24 hours, without giving it an initial feasible solution. The
ALNS algorithm and IT-VSP model were implemented in the Julia programming language. The
mathematical model was solved using Gurobi (Version 9.0.3). All experiments were conducted
on a computer using Intel(R) Xeon(R) CPU E3-1230 v5 3.40 GHz processor. The parameter
settings resulting from tuning experiments, in Table 6.4, were applied to all experiments in this
chapter. All improved timetable solutions obtained by both ALNS and Gurobi have passed the
feasibility check by solving MDVSP in a solver with less than 10 minutes of computation time.
In the course of this thesis, an attempt was made to use the free solver HiGHS for directly
solving the IT-VSP model in order to compare the ALNS computational performance. However,
it is observed that even on the smallest instance, HiGHS failed to get a feasible solution. In
particular, the computation process of HiGHS appeared to be stuck after running for approx-
imately 12 hours and did not make any further progress even after 24 hours of computation.
In contrast to the results obtained from the free solver HiGHS, the proposed ALNS demon-
strates additional advantages for public transport agencies like Movia. Some agencies might be
interested in knowing whether the free HiGHS solver can potentially solve the IT-VSP model,
thereby circumventing the need to invest in an expensive commercial solver. This study found
that utilizing the free HiGHS solver for computing the IT-VSP model is impractical and not
feasible.

Chapter 9
Conclusions and Future Work
This thesis proposed and implemented an adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) algorithm
for an integrated timetabling and vehicle scheduling problem (IT-VSP). The ALNS method is
a heuristic algorithm which excels at performing quick searches for good-quality solutions to
optimization problems, by using a set of destroy and repair operators. It is implemented with
Julia programming language and deployed at a relatively low cost, making it an attractive
alternative to expensive commercial solvers, in solving practical optimization problems.
In this thesis, it focuses on addressing a practical question of whether a heuristic optimiza-
tion approach like ALNS, could be a low-cost solution to improve passenger transfer time in
reasonable computation time, given an initial transit network timetable?
To answer this question, we applied the ALNS algorithm to optimize the public express-bus
timetable in the Greater Copenhagen area, in an attempt to reduce passenger transfer waiting
times, with data provided by the public transport agency Movia.
Specifically, given an initial timetable, the passenger transfer waiting time is minimized by
allowing modifications to the departure time of a trip (a shift change) from its first stop, within
a set of timetable and vehicle schedules constraints. The proposed ALNS algorithm improves
the initial timetable by first selecting a subset of all timetabled trips, and then making timetable
modification to these selected trips. To implement ALNS, several methods for selecting trips
(i.e., destroy operators) and modifying timetables (i.e., repair operators) were proposed and
compared.
To optimize the performance of ALNS, six parameters in the algorithm were first fine-
tuned. With an optimal choice of these tuning parameters, a series of ALNS analyses were
subsequently conducted, to gauge the performance of each destroy and repair operator using the
large neighborhood search (LNS) heuristic, as well as the impacts of dynamic weight adjustment
and reheating settings. Results indicate that all destroy operator perform more or less the same,
while the randomised greedy repair operator performs best. The proposed ALNS outperforms
the best results obtained via LNS in terms of the solution quality. In addition, it is found that
ALNS using dynamic weight adjustment as a means to control the selection probability of an
61
==PAGE:72==
Chapter 9. Conclusions and Future Work
62
operator, is more likely to surpass ALNS which uses only fixed equal probability. Furthermore,
ALNS performs slightly better when reheating.
To evaluate the effectiveness of ALNS, a set of computational experiments were carried out.
Results indicate that for small-sized instances, ALNS can generate solutions with objective
values comparable to those obtained using the standard commercial Gurobi solver, within 24
hours of run time. However, for larger instances, ALNS consistently reduces transfer costs,
even in cases where the Gurobi solver fails to find a feasible solution within the given 24-hour
time frame. Overall, the transfer cost of the original timetable has been reduced by about
15.5% ´ 35%, depending on size of the instance. Remarkably, when applied to the largest and
most complicated recent (2022) data set, ALNS successfully improved the original timetable,
increasing the number of passengers with desired transfer times by approximately 45%, and
reducing the average transfer waiting time from 5.9 to 5 minutes.
These results suggest that the proposed ALNS can potentially reduce passenger transfer
costs by solving IT-VSP in reasonable computation time.
9.1
Outlook
There are several areas of improvement on the ALNS algorithm to improve solution quality.
First, the addition of dwell time in the intermediate stops could be considered in addition to
the shift change in the departure time of trips at the first stops.
This has been shown in
Fonseca et al., 2018 for reducing both transfer and operating cost. Second, one could also aim
to incorporate the vehicle scheduling constraints into the proposed ALNS, thereby eliminating
the necessity of using an external solver for solution feasibility check.
Finally, it would be
interesting to consider tuning how much percentage of a solution worse than the current one to
be assigned as the new solution when reheating. It was observed that resetting current solution
to a solution that is 5-10% worse than the current one can somehow improve the solution quality
found. Changing the percentage may lead to a better performance of the ALNS.
