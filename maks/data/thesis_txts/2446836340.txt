ABSTRACT
In the context of wind power forecasting, the availability of large amounts of data at
diﬀerent levels of aggregation makes it convenient to arrange them in hierarchies. However,
while the measurements of the bottom levels would obviously sum up to the ones of the
aggregate levels, their forecasts, if generated independently, will not be aggregate consistent
across levels, i.e. the sum of the forecasts will not be equal to the forecast of the sum. This
creates inconsistencies in decision-making problems across the hierarchy. In this framework,
reconciliation of Hierarchical Time Series (HTS) gained increasing attention, as it allows
for more consistent decisions across the hierarchy. Additionally, reconciliation of non
consistent forecasts would often, but not necessarily, increase the accuracy of the forecasts
it applies to. In the direction of increasing the performance of the forecasts while ensuring
their aggregate consistency, in this document a new approach to reconciliation is proposed
that is more data-driven than any other reconciliation method. Having as objective the
minimization of the forecast error, the core of the methodology is built around a simple
Least Square approach. Reconciliation is then ensured constraining the minimization
problem. Two versions are proposed, a batch version and an online version. While the
batch version trains the parameters on a batch of data, the online version updates its
parameters at each time step, depending on the most recent error. The proposed methods
are tested on simulated and real data, and compared with existing reconciliation methods.
The results show a very good performance of the online method, which is able to outperform
the existing methods in both 2-level and multi-level settings, and for two diﬀerent sets of
base forecasts. The batch method shows in general a lower performance, but in speciﬁc
settings, it results to be comparable to the most accurate existing methods.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Context
The share of renewable power generation in the power systems is increasing worldwide.
The inherently unpredictable nature of power produced by stochastic generators as wind
power plants and photovoltaic (PV) units makes it challenging to integrate them in the
system, both from the operational and the economic perspectives. Power system operation
management, electricity markets and regulatory frameworks need to adapt in order to
accomodate high shares of Renewable Energy Sources (RES), especially in view of reaching
100% RES penetration by 2050. The transition towards such a system must be guided
while ensuring adequacy1, security2 and maximum welfare.
In this context, forecasts of renewable power generation gained increasing importance as a
tool to be used in related decision-making problems, such as characterization of resource
availability, day-ahead scheduling of power generation, operation and maintenance planning
of generating units [1]. Especially in electricity markets, renewable energy forecasting
is widely recognized as essential for the reliable, safe and cost-eﬀective integration of
renewable energy. System operators, traders, and in general all those who participate in
the electricity markets, use forecasts as input for their decision-making problems, such as
bidding strategies, risk hedging, power reserve scheduling [2].
Forecasts do not possess any value by themselves, unless they are able to inﬂuence the
decisions made by their users [3]. Their value can often (but not necessarily) be increased
if they are more accurate (i.e. their prediction error is lower), and that is why most of the
research has been focused on improving forecast accuracy. However, as elegantly described
in [3], as much as three types of goodness can be identiﬁed when talking about goodness
of forecasts. Increasing goodness in this senses, increases the value of the forecast in
decision-making. Type 1 goodness is achieved when there is correspondence between the
forecast and the knowledge base of the forecaster. That means, all the information available
to the forecaster should contribute to the formulation of the forecaster best judgment
and eventually be used to produce the forecast. The forecaster best judgment means a
judgment that is consistent with the current state of art of forecasting while reﬂecting
any uncertainty. Type 2 goodness reﬂects the correspondence between the forecast and
the observation, which can be referred to as accuracy. Type 3 goodness refers to the
incremental beneﬁt given to the forecast user. Such beneﬁt can be economical (quantiﬁable)
or perceived, therefore not quantiﬁable. For instance, for the decision maker the very
fact of having a forecast at his disposal represents something valuable already, as the

inherent risk linked to the decision is perceived as lower. Especially if the decision maker
is accountable for that decision, forecasts oﬀer a support, a justiﬁcation.
Following the needs of the forecast users, in Denmark a big part of the research is centered
around forecasting wind power, as it alone covers half of the total annual national power
generation. What has been done so far to improve the forecast value in the framework of
wind power forecasting? The main direction has been towards improving the quality of the
forecasts, tailoring models to speciﬁc spatial and temporal resolutions, ﬁnding new relevant
information to include as input to the models. Lot has been proposed in a probabilistic
setting as well, in line with [3], according to which a forecast to be “type 1 good” must be
expressed in probabilistic terms. The interested reader can refer to [4] and [5] for a review
of wind power forecasting models.
In recent times, large collections of wind power production data have become available,
at diﬀerent spatial and temporal resolutions. Using such information to improve wind
power forecasting models appears as a must-do, according to what has been told about
goodness of forecasts. Inputing oﬀ-site information [6], taking into account spatio-temporal
correlations among wind-farms [7] [8] are just a few examples of how to make use of the
mentioned large collections of data to develop new forecasting models.
Indeed, more can be done to increase forecast value in decision-making (speciﬁcally type
1 goodness) by realizing that forecasts in many applications can be naturally arranged
in hierarchies. Such hierarchies can be spatial (also called contemporaneous or cross-
sectional hierarchies) or temporal. As an example, a cross-sectional hierarchy can be the one
consisting of forecasts of energy consumption: individual consumers, substations, regions,
countries, represent diﬀerent nodes on diﬀerent levels of such hierarchy. On the production
side, single generation units, power generation farms, portfolios, regions and countries can
be seen as nodes of the cross-sectional hierarchy of power production. In the same way,
forecasts at diﬀerent time resolutions (15-minutes, hourly, daily, monthly), can be seen as
levels of a temporal hierarchy.
Given the availability of large amount of data at diﬀerent levels in the hierarchies, it seems
sound to actually look at the forecasting problem from a hierarchical point of view. The
measurements of such hierarchy are such that each node of the hierarchy is equal to the
sum of its sub-nodes, e.g. the energy consumption of a country is equal to the sum of the
energy consumption of the consumers in that country. However, if forecasts are generated
independently at each node, the intrinsic property of aggregate consistency, often referred
to as coherency, will not hold. In simple words, the sum of the forecasts will not be equal
to the forecast of the sum, and that is an issue for several reasons.
From a perception perspective, decision makers would rather receive one forecast as input
for their problem than two possible forecasts. Let us take as an example the Transmission
System Operator. Which input should he be using for the problem of scheduling power
reserve capacities for the next day, the sum of the wind farm forecasts he receives as bids,
or its own forecast? Such possibility adds uncertainty and it reﬂects the impossibility of
making the best use of the available information. Being able to demonstrate to have followed
the best forecasting practice is also the best defence against any claim of negligence. In this
sense, reconciled forecasts, i.e. forecasts satisfying the aggregate consistency constraint,

Moreover, as a consequence of inconsistency of forecasts at diﬀerent hierarchy levels,
misaligned decisions can stem, bringing to sub-optimal decisions.
For some speciﬁc
problems, such as the Security Constrained Optimal Power Flow (SCOPF) and the

motivating examples are given in Appendix A, the ﬁrst one inspired by [10], related to
the SCUC problem, and the second one related to the portfolio manager problem,
relevant in the electricity market framework.
Let us now see how the problem of reconciliation of forecasts has been handled in the
literature.
1.2
Reconciliation in the literature
A wide literature is available around the topic of reconciliation of Hierarchical Time Series
(HTS) forecasts, which can be sorted by: type of hierarchy involved (cross-sectional or
temporal); application (inventory management, macroeconomic studies, domestic tourism
forecasts, electricity consumption, wind power); statistic framework (deterministic or
probabilistic); reconciliation method applied. Furthermore, the reconciliation methods can
be categorized depending on which data input they make use of.
The ﬁrst reconciliation methods ever applied to HTS forecasts, were the so-called Bottom-
Up (BU), Top-Down (TD) and Middle-Out (MO) methods. BU derives higher level
forecasts simply aggregating the bottom level forecasts. It does not use any series at the
aggregate levels, which are often smoother and easier to predict [9] and it is unlikely to give
good results when the aggregation involves a large number of series [11] [12]. TD method
will use the top-level forecast and derive the lower level forecasts from its disaggregation
using proportions derived from historical data. In [12] the authors showed that such
forecast will always be biased even if the base forecasts are not. MO simply combines
the two methods just described and uses the forecasts from a speciﬁc middle level, to
derive from its aggregation and disaggregation higher and lower level forecasts, respectively.
Many studies are available that compare these methods but no clear-cut answer was given
about which one is the best.
BU, TD and MO, though, do not use information from all the hierarchy, which goes against
the principle behind type 1 goodness described in Section 1.1. Simply aggregating or
disaggregating information from a speciﬁc level to predict the remaining levels does not
make the best use of the hierarchy information and peculiarities (diﬀerent patterns at
each level), and disregards potential useful information one could get from an independent
forecast. In fact, independently forecasting each level would allow the forecaster to use a
speciﬁc model for each level, beneﬁting from diﬀerent levels of accuracy [9]. This is why
forecasts at diﬀerent levels exist in the ﬁrst place. For this reason, one can expect that
forecast quality at the generic node can be improved by using the information from the

remaining nodes of the hierarchy. The possibility of achieving higher forecast accuracy
while reconciling forecasts is obviously attractive and oﬀers yet another motivation to
reconciliation. Based on this, 2-steps approaches have been proposed, in which the ﬁrst
step produces independent incoherent base forecasts for all the nodes in the hierarchy, and
the second step reconciles them. [12] proposed a combination approach which starting
from the base forecasts would estimate the reconciled forecasts based on an Ordinary
Least Square (OLS) estimator. This kind of estimation would combine the base forecasts
with weights that depend on the structure, thus constant over time. Such method has been
applied to the Australian tourism demand dataset [12], showing improvements compared
to BU and TD. However, OLS estimator, seen as special case of Generalized Least
Square (GLS) estimator, assumes homoscedasticity of the hierarchy, i.e. it assumes
that the forecast errors at all nodes have the same variance. Such assumption has been
considered too simplistic [13], [14]. Indeed, it completely ignores possible correlation and
diﬀerences among nodes, which looks quite a limitation especially in relation to wind
power forecasts. Hierarchical Least Square (HLS) estimation, implemented by [10],
relaxes the assumption a little, assuming that the variances depend on the position in the
hierarchy. However, this also can be argued as being simplistic. Changing perspective,
[14] proposed a Game-Theoretically OPtimum (GTOP) reconciliation method that
guarantees reconciled forecasts at least as good as the base ones, which represented a
breakthrough, since no reconciliation method so far guaranteed such a property.
To be noted, the solutions described so far do not take as input wind power measurements
whatsoever. A slightly more data-driven approach, is introduced by [15] and [16], who
estimate variance and covariance of the forecast error from historical data. [15] suggests
a Weighted Least Square (WLS) estimator, which uses an estimation of the variance
of the forecast error at each node as input to combine the base forecasts. Following up,
[16] formalizes the Minimum Trace (MinT) reconciliation method, which estimates the
whole covariance matrix of the forecast error based on historical data and uses it as an
input for the recombination of base forecasts. Depending on the application, WLS and
minT would perform on average better than OLS and other methods. It is clear, though,
that the variance and covariance estimation, besides making the reconciliation heavier
computationally, will represent a good estimation for a speciﬁc subset of data but can
potentially perform poorly when applied to a diﬀerent subset, if the underlying processes
change in time.
Reconciliation has been performed on probabilistic forecasts as well, which conﬁrms the
importance of expressing forecasts in probabilistic terms. To this end, [11], [17] and [18]
propose approaches that build on MinT, WLS and other methods, to reconcile probabilistic
forecasts.
Interestingly, among all the studies mentioned so far about reconciliation and to the
knowledge of the author, [18] is the only one applying reconciliation of hierarchical forecasts
to a wind power hierarchical dataset. This shows the increasing importance that the topic
is gaining outside of the traditional research areas, while underlining the gap existing
between reconciliation and wind power forecasts. As this appears to be a new research area,
it is not surprising that existing reconciliation methods are not quite suited for wind power
data applications. Indeed, applying reconciliation to wind power hierarchical forecasts

requires some important considerations regarding the peculiarity of wind power time series
and the spatio-temporal correlation structure existing within the hierarchy, in order to
make the forecast and reconciliation processes more eﬀective and reliable. Indeed, the
spatio-temporal correlation between neighbouring wind farms would aﬀect the distribution
of the forecast errors in the hierarchy [7]. If such information was accounted for, it could be
useful for identifying a speciﬁc covariance matrix to be used as input for methods such as
WLS and minT. However, changes in wind farms layouts, wind condition variations (due
e.g. to changing in atmospheric pressure) and seasonal patterns aﬀect the dynamic of the
wind and the underlying forecast error over time, and would therefore make it necessary
to recalculate the model parameters (re-estimate the covariance). A model that is able
to follow such changes is referred to as an adaptive model. In general terms, a simple
approach to achieve adaptivity is ﬁtting the model on sliding window training data, but
this implies reﬁtting of the model with a certain frequency. A diﬀerent approach, more
advanced, uses the exponential forgetting of past data, which is proven to be more eﬃcient
and less memory demanding [19]. In [19], such adaptivity is achieved using recursion, a
method that does not require the recalculation of the parameters from scratch, but updates
them every time new information becomes available, based on the error at the previous
time step. Such adaptivity has not been applied to the reconciliation problem whatsoever.
1.3
Objectives and contribution
In the light of what has been discussed, it is clear that a completely data-driven and
adaptive approach to reconciliation of hierarchical forecasts is missing. It is intuitive
that a data-driven adaptive method could be suited for the reconciliation problem as
well. In fact, keeping in mind that reconciliation can also be seen as the combination of
the base forecasts, each one with its own weight, a data-driven approach would be able
to calculate the weights based on the error, and adaptivity would allow to adjust those
weights depending on the error in a continuous online way.
The aim of the present work is then to propose a completely data driven approach to
reconciliation of HTS point forecasts. The proposed models will be based on least square
estimation and will aim at minimizing the forecast error. The correlation structure of the
hierarchy will be taken into account, while the reconciliation will be ensured by constraining
the regression. Two versions will be proposed, a batch and an online version. The ﬁrst
one will learn parameters once for all over a subset of the data. The latter will implement
adaptivity by the means of recursion, thus it will be able to track system changes over
time. The proposed methods are then to be tested on both simulated and real data.
Even recognizing the importance of extending the proposed methods to the probabilistic
setting, for time-related reasons said extension has not been implemented, but kept for
future work. The same can be said about a distributed version of the methods. The
assumption of the all-knowing reconciler is indeed a strong assumption, that eventually
needs to be relaxed. Acknowledging that in that case privacy needs to be ensured while
exchanging information, further work could be focused on a distributed version of the
method.

1.4
Structure of the report
This report is structured in ﬁve chapters. Chapter 1 is the introductory chapter, and this
is the end of it.
Chapter 2 contains the mathematical formulation of all the reconciliation methods presented
in this study, including the proposed novel methods. It is divided in four sections. Section
2.1 includes preliminaries about concepts that will be used through the chapter, such
as time series, least square estimation, etc. Along with preliminaries, some notation is
introduced, to clear up any possible confusion. Section 2.2 gives an overview of the existing
reconciliation methods and presents the implementation algorithm for each one of them.
In Section 2.3, ﬁrstly the theoretical framework of multivariate least square estimation
is introduced, and extended to the constrained case. Then, the theory is adapted to
the reconciliation framework, the novel batch reconciliation method is proposed and its
algorithm presented. In Section 2.4, ﬁrstly the theory of recursive least square is introduced
and its properties are tested on toy datasets. Afterwards, recursion is applied to the batch
reconciliation method and the novel online algorithm is presented. Finally, the properties
of the proposed methods are presented and discussed.
Chapter 3 contains all the information about the simulated testing framework and related
results. Section 3.1 describes how the synthetic hierarchical dataset was generated. Section
3.2 deﬁnes the veriﬁcation framework, i.e. the evaluating measures that will be used to
compare the tested methods. Sections 3.3 and 3.4 contains the performance results of
the methods tested on the simulated data, in a 2-level setting and a multi-level setting,
respectively.
Chapter 4 includes all the information about the real-data testing framework and related
results. Section 4.1 describes the danish wind power dataset provided by Energinet.dk.
Section 4.2 gives information about the selection and preprocessing stages prior to the
utilization of the dataset. Section 4.3 shows the performance results of the methods tested
on the real dataset, both in a 2-level setting and a multi-level setting.
Chapter 5 draws conclusions and suggests extensions for future work.
All code implementations are done using R-Project software [20] and available libraries.

2.1
Preliminaries
Throughout the report, the reader will encounter time series, regression analysis, hierarchical
forecasts, autoregressive processes, Least Square analysis. Even if the reader is acquainted
with such topics, a short but clear introduction is necessary, along with some speciﬁc
notation, to avoid misunderstandings. Let us start by introducing time series.
2.1.1
Stochastic Processes and Time Series
A time series is a sequence of values ordered in time. In our wind power framework,
it is one possible realization of the stochastic process of wind power production. If the
stochastic process is indicated by {Y (t, ω)}t∈T,ω∈Ω, where T is the index set and Ωis
the sample space Ωof all the possible outcomes, then {Y (·, ω = ω0)} is a time series and
{Y (t = t0, ·)} is a random variable.
Given the purpose of this project, the hierarchical structure the time series belong to has

{Y (·, ω = ω0)(l,nl)}, where (l, nl) unambiguously identiﬁes the node in the hierarchy; the
meaning of the tuple will be discussed better in Section 2.1.3. In the following, to lighten
the notation, ω is dropped so that the time series is indicated by Y (l,nl) and the random
variable by Y (l,nl)

2.1.2
Autoregressive processes
In the framework of time series, an autoregressive (AR) process is a process for which each
measurement depends on past measurements. How back in time this dependency goes will
deﬁne the order of the AR process. As an example, an autoregressive process of second
order can be written as
Yt = ϕ0 + ϕ1Yt−1 + ϕ2Yt−2,
(2.1)
where ϕ1 and ϕ2 are the ﬁrst and second order AR coeﬃcients. Usually, a constant term
ϕ0 is included to account for the mean of the time series.
2.1.3
Hierarchy
Let us now introduce a generic multi-level hierarchy in a more formal way. The number of
levels in the hierarchy will be indicated by L ∈Z+, while N ∈Z+ will be the total number
of nodes. S is the set of all the nodes. For all l ∈{1, . . . , L}, Nl ∈Z+ is the number of
nodes in level l, which constitute a subset Sl, such that N = PL
l=1 Nl and S = SL
l=1 Sl.
The tuple (l, nl) then uniquely identiﬁes node nl in level l. For all (l, nl) non-bottom series,
its child nodes form a subset Cl,nl.
2.1.4
Regression analysis
Regression analysis needs to be properly introduced, as it will be constantly used throughout
the chapter. Regression analysis is the set of statistical techniques which purpose is to
describe and estimate the relation among variables, speciﬁcally between one or more
dependent variables, also called response variables and one or more independent variables,
also called explanatory variables or regressors. Their relation is described by parameters or
regression coeﬃcients. We distinguish between univariate and multivariate regression
whether the response variables are one or more than one, respectively.
To clear up
any confusion, multiple regression identiﬁes instead the inclusion of more than one
explanatory variable. Additionally, regression can be categorized in linear, quadratic,
et cetera, depending on the nature of the relation among the variables. Let us introduce a
linear multiple univariate regression model by the means of
yt = x⊤
t θ + ϵt,
(2.2)
where t ∈{1, . . . , n} is the generic time step, yt is the realization at time t; xt is the vector
of independent variables at time t; θ is the regression coeﬃcients vector; ϵt is the error term
such that ϵ = [ϵ1, . . . , ϵn] has zero mean and variance σ2. It has to be noted that usually a
constant term is included among the regressors, even if theoretically it is expected to be

2.2.5
A game-theoretical approach to reconciliation
The authors of [14], propose a completely diﬀerent approach to the reconciliation problem,
i.e. a game-theoretic approach. Similarly to other studies, they perform a post-processing
of base forecasts apt to reconcile them, rather than issuing already reconciled forecasts.
However, they do not perform regression, nor they propose a closed-form solution estimator.
Rather, they solve a minmax problem to compute the reconciled forecasts
V = min
˜yt∈A max
yt∈A∩B {l(yt, ˜yt) −l(yt, ˆyt)} ,
(2.38)
where l is any loss function based on a Bregman divergence, A and B are, respectively,
the set of vectors that are aggregate consistent, and an additional set allowing for the
inclusion of any other information about the data. The reconciled forecasts are thus chosen
to minimize the maximum loss brought by the reconciliation process itself (in terms of
forecast error). The result is a miminum adjustment of the base forecasts. The advantages
of this approach are several. Any loss function can be used, so that the prior information
about diﬀerent loss function per node can be included. Fewer assumptions are required
about the forecasts (no need for them to be unbiased) and the forecast error. V is always
lower than or equal to zero, which makes the reconciled forecast always at least as good as
the base forecasts. However, there is no closed-form solution available for this problem,
unless the hierarchy is a simple 2-level hierarchy; it does not scale very well for large
collections of time series [13]. Also to be stressed, the reconciled forecasts are at least as
good as the base ones from an overall perspective, not from the perspective of each node.
Before entering into the details of the GTOP solution, let us dig deeper in the choice of
the loss function. The authors of [14] choose a squared loss as loss function deﬁning it for
each and every node as

2.3
A proposal batch learning reconciliation method
A common striking characteristic of the existing methods analyzed in the past section,
is that they do not take as input true realizations when reconciling the base forecasts.
The closer existing methods got to a data-driven approach is represented by WLS and
minT methods, as they take as input a variance-covariance estimation run over historical
samples of base forecast error. However, none of the existing methods directly aimed at
reducing the forecast error itself. In fact, the error of (2.22) should not be confused with
the forecast error, or residual, as deﬁned in (2.31). Diﬀerently from existing methods,
instead of projecting the available base forecasts onto the aggregate consistency plan to
oﬀset the reconciliation error, the data-driven methods that are going to be presented
perform a regression to minimize the forecast error.
At this stage of the project, the focus is on batch learning, which refers to the practice of
learning the set of parameters over a batch of historical data, the training dataset. The
parameters will then be used for predicting (and reconcile) future observations. Learning
the relation between historical base forecasts and true realizations, the proposed method
will reconcile out-of-sample base forecasts available at the whole hierarchy. However,
a simple regression cannot ensure that the out-of-sample predictions will be aggregate
consistent. How to force the learned parameters to do so? This can be ensured constraining
the regression problem, as it will be exposed later in this chapter. Building up on the LS
estimation introduced in 2.1.5, its Multivariate extension will be formalized in Section
2.3.1, to acquire the tools to be able to handle the whole hierarchy. In Section 2.3.2, the
constrained version of the Multivariate Least Square (MLS) regression problem will be
introduced. Once all the tools have been collected, the batch reconciliation method, called
Multivariate Least Square Equality constrained (MLSE) reconciliation method, can be
formalized and its algorithm presented. This will be done in Section 2.3.3.

2.4
From batch to online learning reconciliation
As opposed to batch learning, in this section the focus will be on online learning. This term
refers to the practice of learning new parameters as new data become available, updating
the old parameters. The rationale behind this concept is going to be exposed in Section
2.4.1, where the limitations of batch learning are discussed and supported by simulation
results over a toy dataset. Then, the online approach of Recursive Least Square (RLS) is
introduced in Section 2.4.2, extended to the Multivariate case in Section 2.4.3, and to the
Equality-constrained case in Section 2.4.4.

2.4.1
Limitations of batch learning
As mentioned, if LS estimation is used, a unique set of parameters is calculated over the
batch data. The limitation of this approach are thus clear when the underlying system is
dynamic, since the estimates do not capture the changes in the system. Let us visualize the
mentioned limitations emerging when LS estimation is performed over a dynamic dataset.
Let us refer to the univariate linear dynamic dataset D1, whose simulation details are
described in Appendix B.0.1. The true and estimated parameters are shown in Figure 2.2.
(a) θ1
(b) θ2
Figure 2.2: True values and LS estimates for the model parameters θ1 and θ2.
It is visible that the so-called oﬄine estimates, calculated once for all over the batch
dataset, do not follow the dynamic changes of the true parameters. This can be improved
using a sliding window training dataset, which requires the periodical re-estimation of
the parameters through the re-run of the LS estimation. However, such an approach can
become computationally heavy for large size problems, especially if parameters need to be
recalculated with high frequency. Online approaches have been implemented to overcome
said limitations. This kind of approach updates the parameters every time new information
becomes available, without solving the LS problem from scratch. A particular online
approach for LS estimation is the Recursive Least Square (RLS) estimation, widely used in
the literature in many ﬁelds such as control analysis, time series, etc. In the following, the
RLS approach will be described step by step, and the improved performance on dataset
D1 will be shown.

2.5
Properties of the proposed methods
After presenting the novel MLSE and MRLSE algorithms for reconciliation of hierarchical
forecasts, it is necessary to stress their properties. These are:
• reconciliation of out-of-sample forecasts (MLSE and MRLSE);

3.1.5
Study cases
It is acknowledged that some of the made choices will aﬀect the value brought by
reconciliation.
As an example, AR(1) were chosen instead of AR(2) to produce the
base forecasts, so that the base forecasts would not be “too good”, in terms of accuracy.
In fact, whenever they are (unrealistically) too accurate, then there is a higher chance
for methods as BU and TD to outperform more advanced reconciliation methods. Even
with the given choice, the fact itself that data are simulated and forecasted through AR
models will make it highly possible to have “too good” base forecasts, as it will be clear in
the result sections. It was out of the scope of this project to test all the possible ways to
simulate or forecast the data, so that the choices related to this stage have been kept as
simple as described.
As a further example, the choice of simulating a drift in the underlying generated processes
will also obviously have an impact on the results. Both base and reconciled forecasts will
be aﬀected, and possible diﬀerences among batch and online methods will be emphasized.
If this drift was not simulated, likely said diﬀerence would not be that relevant. The choice
made directly relates to the nature of wind processes, which led us to the drift choice and
motivates this entire project. In real applications, as already explained, the adaptivity
of the forecasting models, to follow that drift, can be achieved through the re-estimation
of the model parameters. Thus, it has been deemed as relevant to test the reconciliation
methods over 2 diﬀerent sets of base forecasts, as summarized in the following.
1.
Batch base forecasts. In this case, the base forecasts will be obtained training

A choice that will also inﬂuence the results is the choice of not normalizing the data prior
to the application of the reconciliation methods. For this reason some of the methods will

3. Not-normalized data. This will be the main simulation setting, obtained as described

3.2.2
Review
A number of studies regarding deterministic forecasting in general and reconciliation of
hierarchical point forecasts, both temporal and cross-sectional, were reviewed.
In [19], short-term forecasts obtained with diﬀerent forecasting models are normalized
and their BIAS, RMSE, MAE computed. However only MAE and RMSE are used for
comparison of the forecasting methods. Speciﬁcally, the forecasts are compared by skill
scores, i.e. a measure of the improvement of MAE or RMSE over a deﬁned benchmark
method (in this case the persistance method).
In [10], the authors reviewing existing methods for reconciliation, the evaluation is adapted
to a hierarchical framework. After computing the NRMSE for each series in the hierarchy,
the reconciliation methods are compared for each of the aggregated series in terms of
normalized RMSE. As the bottom-level is quite large (96 bottom-level series), only the
average performance is used for comparison. The bottom-level is then further analyzed
by pairwise comparison of the ﬁve reconciliation methods. This comparison is carried out
by plotting one method’s NRMSE against another method’s NRMSE for each one of the
bottom series, through scatter plots. The said plots allow to instantly visualize for how
many nodes a method results to perform better than a diﬀerent one in term of NRMSE.
Additionally, the authors of [10] assess robustness from a statistical signiﬁcance perspective
by performing pairwise Diebold-Mariano tests for each series over the veriﬁcation dataset.
Then, counting the instances for which a particular method is signiﬁcantly better than
40
==PAGE:51==
3. Application to synthetic data
another particular method (in terms of NRMSE), they show the results in percentage of
instances.
In [14], the authors use a quite diﬀerent approach, both regarding the proposed optimal
reconciliation method and the evaluation setup. The reconciliation methods are still
compared using a percentage of improvement over a benchmark (in this case the base
forecasts), but now the utilized score is not the RMSE. The authors calculate a total
cumulative loss for each method, summing up the losses of the diﬀerent nodes of the
hierarchy, where the loss for each series is equal to the sum of squared errors weighted by
a factor speciﬁc for that node. This is to account for the operational cost of the prediction
error that can vary depending on the node. In this way, the nodes will be weighted more
if their error is considered to be more costly. The analysis then focuses on testing some
speciﬁc simulation parameters such as the mentioned weights and parameters related to
the shape of the noise of the time series. For each of the combination to be tested, 100
replications are simulated for statistical signiﬁcance.
Other studies were revised, such as [13], [12] and [9], where similar measures are used
for evaluation.
RMSE, MAE, MSFE (Mean Square Forecast Error), percentage of
improvements and skill scores were used to evaluate accuracy of the forecasts. t-tests and
p-values were used to test the statistical signiﬁcance of the results, along with multiple
replications. Worth to note is the complex simulation setup created by [13], where the time
series are simulated so to reproduce some speciﬁc characteristics, as correlation among
nodes, geographical smoothing eﬀect for the aggregate time series, seasonality. Diﬀerent
sizes of the hierarchy and diﬀerent base forecasts models and diﬀerent time horizons were
tested as well. Similarly to the other studies, the score used for evaluation was the relative
ratio of reconciled and base forecasts RMSEs, averaged by level.

chosen, mostly inspired from [10].
Accuracy assessment. In order to assess the accuracy of the forecasts, the NRMSE is

and (3.16), are computed but will not be shown in the results unless they provide
important information. The normalized version of the scores is chosen as it allows for easy
interpretation of the results. Indeed, a percentage value allows for comparison between
diﬀerent studies as well, regardless the diﬀerent scales used during simulations. The
normalization will be carried out over the error, meaning that RMSE will be calculated
over the normalized error, obtained dividing the error by the capacity. The capacity for
each bottom node is calculated as the diﬀerence between the maximum and the minimum
of the time series of that node. The capacity of the aggregate nodes is calculated as the
sum of the capacities of their child nodes.
Conﬁdence intervals. Since the generation of the AR coeﬃcients and of the errors is
random, the simulations per se are subject to sampling variance, i.e. if the simulation is
repeated many times, a particular statistic calculated in sample is subject to variation.
Thus, R replicates will be simulated for each simulation setup, to build a conﬁdence
41
==PAGE:52==
3. Application to synthetic data
interval for each of the statistics that will be shown. In [27], [28], [29] and [30] additional
information is provided regarding how to choose R. For this project, as a compromise
between resources deployment (time consumption) and accuracy, the number of replicates
is set to R = 50. The resulting NRMSE scores will thus be given with a conﬁdence interval
(50% and 90%).
Statistical signiﬁcance. To give information about to which extent the diﬀerences in
scores between forecasts series are statistically signiﬁcant, it is necessary to test said
diﬀerences using speciﬁc tools, available in the literature. However, when talking about
statistical signiﬁcance much eﬀort has to be posed on avoiding misinterpretation of the
results and of the power of the statistical tools employed. In the literature, signiﬁcance
tests applied in research studies are a common practice to test whether the related results
are statistically signiﬁcant or not. However, the subject is wide and complex and the
correct interpretation requires deep knowledge in statistics and good understanding of the
problem, as fast and standardized techniques lead to misuse of statistical concepts and
often to wrong conclusions and interpretation of the results. The actual relevance of widely
used tools as Null-Hypothesis Signiﬁcance Tests (NHST) is now questioned [31], while a
literature emerged about common misinterpretations [32]. The authors itself are seldom
forced to clarify their own work years later, as in the case of Diebold - Mariano test [33].
The main motivation behind the application of signiﬁcance testing in this study is that
the results are subject to sampling variation. In fact, as mentioned, the simulation of
data requires the sampling of errors and parameters, under some distribution assumptions.
Additionally, the results are out-of-sample, obtained over a speciﬁc dataset (the veriﬁcation
dataset). Thus, when presenting the results one must account for this randomness and
test whether the obtained results are due to chance or statistically signiﬁcant. To this
end, at each replication, pairwise Diebold-Mariano (DM) tests will be performed to assess
whether the loss diﬀerential between 2 sets of forecasts is random or statistically signiﬁcant.
The DM test is described in details in Appendix C, which the reader can refer to, if
interested. The advantages of using this test are several. It is model-free, that means
one does not need to access the underlying forecasting model to perform the test: the
2 sets of forecasts are enough. The loss function does not need to be quadratic. The
forecast errors do not necessarily have to be gaussian and serially uncorrelated, ergo
can be contemporaneously correlated. Additionally, this test can handle multi-period
forecasts. Only disadvantage, the results of the DM test strongly depend on the length
of the veriﬁcation dataset, which at this stage of the project can be prevented by simply
generating a longer dataset. The signiﬁcance level, to determine whether a diﬀerence
between two sets of forecasts is statistically signiﬁcant or not, is set to α = 0.05 (see
Appendix C for additional information).

APPLICATION TO REAL DATA
4.1
Danish wind power production data - Energinet.dk
The dataset provided by the Danish Transmission System Operator, Energinet.dk, includes
wind power data for 349 wind farms in western Denmark, for the period between January
2006 and March 2012. The measurements have a 15-min time resolution and represent
the average kW injected into the grid in that time period.
In particular, the values
provided are measurements coming from the closest transformation stations, but it will
be assumed they reﬂect the wind power production of wind farms. This dataset has been
used previously in [7], [8] and [19]. All of them focus on the spatio-temporal correlation
properties of the dataset and on ways of exploiting them to improve forecasting. In [7],
the spatio-temporal stochastic structure of wind power prediction error is investigated,
to be used in various tasks related to wind power management as forecasting, predictive
scenarios, outliers detection, resource assessment. In [8], forecasting models are proposed
to generate probabilistic forecasts at both individual and aggregate levels that take into
account the mentioned spatio-temporal correlation structure. In [19], an online Vector
AutoRegressive model is applied to the data to forecast simultaneously multiple wind
farms, accounting for their spatio-temporal correlation structure and their dynamics. In
this project, the dataset has been used to test the state-of-art and the novel reconciliation
methods described in Chapter 2.

4.2
Data selection and pre-processing
Only a subset of the available dataset was actually used. Firstly, the sites presenting more
than 100 Not Available entries were discarded. Out of the 250 sites left, only 100 sites
were randomly selected. They are shown in Figure 4.1.
These 100 sites were then further cleaned, by removing missing observations. Afterwords,
the dataset was further shrunken, by selecting only 2010 - 2011 data (around 70000
timestamps). For each site, observations exceeding 1.5 times the 99% quantile of the
distribution were removed, i.e. those lying outside [0, 1.5q0.99
i
], where q0.99
i
indicates the
99% quantile of the population at site i. The data were then normalized by dividing each
site measurement by the capacity of that site. However, as the capacity of the wind farms
changed over time, a function computing rolling maximums was used for its estimation
(package zoo [34]). For the bottom nodes, the normalization leads to adimensional values
in the range [0,1]. Once the ﬁnal wind farm time series were obtained, the aggregate node
series was calculated as their sum. Obviously, the values of this series are not between 0
and 1, and it does not make sense to normalize them to [0, 1], as the aggregate consistency
constraint needs to hold. The values related to the aggregate level are then adimensional, in
the range [0, 100]. In this way, a 2-level hierarchy of wind power production measurements
was obtained, to be used for testing the reconciliation methods in the 2-level setting.
To test reconciliation on a multi-level setting, a middle level of aggregation was built in a
very similar way. The 100 wind farms were grouped in 4 quadrants, or regions, as shown
in Figure 4.2. A time series for each region was build summing up its 25 wind farm time
series, so that each regional time series is in the range [0, 25]. The aggregate, regional and
bottom nodes obtained with the described procedure, constitute the multi-level hierarchy
of wind power measurements used for testing the reconciliation methods in the multi-level
setting.
Base forecasts generation
Prior to testing the reconciliation methods, the base forecasts needed to be produced. In
order to do that, two diﬀerent models were used. Both of them assume AR(2) processes can
model the time series at all levels. The ﬁrst model consisted in training the AR parameters
over a speciﬁc subset of data, namely January - February 2010. Then, the forecasts were
issued on the remaining dataset (March 2010 - December 2011) using the same parameters.
The base forecasts obtained in such a way will be called batch base forecasts, since the
training stage was performed on a batch of data. The second forecasting model makes use
of a sliding training dataset, i.e. the parameters used to issue the forecasts of a certain
pair of months, were learned on the previous pair. The base forecasts produced in this
way will be called sliding window base forecasts.
Details about subsets handling
For the sake of clarity, some details need to be given about how the observation subsets
were handled. As mentioned, out of the 2 years of data selected, 2010 - 2011, January
and February 2010 were kept as training dataset for the batch base forecasts (and as ﬁrst
training window for the sliding window base forecasts). Additionally, March and April

CONCLUSIONS
The work presented in this document focused on the development of a new method
for reconciliation of point hierarchical wind power forecasts.
The motivation behind
reconciliation has been discussed in details in Chapter 1, along with the reasons behind
the implementation of a new method. Shortly, the new reconciliation method was aiming
at increasing the value of the forecasts in three ways: improving consistency between the
base knowledge and the forecaster judgment; increasing accuracy; providing beneﬁt to the
forecast user.
After reviewing the existing reconciliation methodology and the machinery for its
implementation, it was recognized that the space for its improvement was signiﬁcant,
especially in relation to the wind power application. For this reason, a new data-driven
methodology for reconciliation was built, that led to the development of two novel
reconciliation methods, namely MLSE and MRLSE. All the methodology of reconciliation,
was presented in Chapter 2. The proposed methods have several advantages. Both of them
oﬀer a closed-form solution to the problem of reconciliation and can be easily adapted
to any hierarchical structure.
They oﬀer reconciliation with the speciﬁc objective of
minimization of the forecast error, which represents a new approach to the reconciliation
problem. Additionally, MRLSE is adaptive, meaning that is able to track changes of the
forecast error and update its parameters accordingly. While MLSE requires historical data,
MRLSE only needs the last realization, which makes it computationally light and less
memory demanding.
Finally, MLSE ans MRLSE were tested on both simulated and real data (Chapters 3 and
4). It can be concluded that MLSE has a good performance, comparable to advanced
methods as minT and WLS, only when reconciling forecasts produced with the same model
as the forecasts it was trained on. This means that if the base forecasts are updated over
time, MLSE needs to be updated too. On the other hand, being adaptive, MRLSE shows
high performance regardless from the base forecasts. On both real and simulated data,
MRLSE was the best performing of the reconciliation methods, reaching high accuracy
in both 2-level and multi-level settings. Worth to mention is that MLSE and MRLSE
performance was found to be dependent on the scale of the data, which calls for further
analysis. It is recognized here that this project represents only the ﬁrst step in the direction
of data-driven adaptive methods for reconciliation of hierarchical forecasts and as such
further analysis needs to be carried out. In the following section, some possible extensions
and future work are suggested, as they could not be conducted in the period of time
dedicated to this project.

5. Conclusions
5.0.1
Limitations and future work
As the scope of this project was limited by the ﬁniteness of time, many analyses were not
formally carried out. To better understand how each of the reconciliation methods would
perform depending on the shape of the hierarchy, on the model used to issue the base
forecasts and on the scale of the data, a more comprehensive analysis could have been
done, varying the mentioned parameters.
Diﬀerent correlation structure scenarios were tried, but did not oﬀer that much of insight,
due to the high degree of randomness within the simulation.
For instance, it was
thought that the diﬀerent predictive accuracy at diﬀerent nodes could be inﬂuenced
by the correlation structure, so that depending on the degree of correlation, one could
expect a higher or lower accuracy. However, the results were diﬃcult to replicate and to
understand.
A strong limitation of this project is represented by the deterministic framework. As
mentioned in Chapter 1, expressing forecasts in probabilistic terms has become a must-
do for forecasters and forecasts users. Indeed, extending the proposed methods to the
probabilistic setting would higher the value of the forecasts produced and widen the
applicability area of the methods.
As mentioned in the document, one of the main assumptions on which all the methodology
is built, is that the reconciler has access to information at all levels of the hierarchy. This is
sometimes the case, if for example the reconciler is the TSO and the hierarchy consists of
the individual bids and their sum. If not, the assumption of the all-knowing reconciler is not
realistic. In such a case, the information would need to be shared among nodes and privacy
would need to be ensured, which calls for a distributed extension of the proposed methods.
Besides protecting the privacy of the nodes and lowering further the computational burden,
a distributed version of MLSE and MRLSE would increase their feasibility in real-world
applications.

E PROJECT PLAN
Context and motivation
General context.
As renewable unpredictable power generation increases its share,
challenges arise in power system operation and electricity markets.
In this context,
forecasts gained increasing importance as a tool to be used in related decision-making
processes. System operators, traders, and in general all those who participate/play in
the electricity markets, use forecasts as input for their decision-making problems, such as
bidding strategies, risk hedging, power reserve scheduling.
Forecast value - the aggregation constraint. Forecasts do not possess any value
unless they are able to inﬂuence the decisions made by their users [3]. Their value can
often (but not necessarily) be increased if more accuracy is provided, and that is why most
of the research has been focused on improving forecast accuracy.
Nevertheless, more can be done to increase their value in decision-making by realizing that
forecasts in many applications can be arranged in hierarchies, spatial or temporal - they are
called contemporaneous or cross-sectional hierarchies, and temporal hierarchies, respectively.
On the consumption side, individual consumers, substations, regions, cities, countries,
represent diﬀerent nodes on diﬀerent levels of the cross-sectional hierarchy. Similarly on
the production side, single generation units, power generation farms, portfolios, regions
and countries can be seen as part of a cross-sectional hierarchy. In the same way, forecasts
at diﬀerent time resolutions (15-minutes, hourly, daily, monthly), can be seen as levels
(sometimes overlapping) of a temporal hierarchy. However, if forecasts are generated
independently at each level, the intrinsic property of aggregate consistency, often referred
to as coherency will not hold. In simple words, the sum of the forecasts at level k will not be
equal to the forecast at their aggregated level. As a consequence, misaligned decisions could
stem, diminishing the value of the forecasts in decision-making problems. Reconciliation of
forecasts in this sense is thus required, to allow for aligned decisions across the hierarchy
and support consistent decisions [9].
State-of-the-art of hierarchical forecasts reconciliation.
A wide literature is
available around the topic of reconciliation of Hierarchical Time Series (HTS) forecasts,
which can be sorted: by type of hierarchy involved (cross-sectional or temporal); by the
application (inventory management, macroeconomic studies, domestic tourism forecasts,
electricity consumption, wind power); by the statistic framework (deterministic or
probabilistic); by reconciliation method applied (GLS, OLS, WLS, game-theoretical
approach, and others).
The ﬁrst reconciliation methods applied, now used as benchmark for more advanced
strategies, were the so-called bottom-up, top-down and middle-out methods. Bottom-up

would derive higher level forecasts simply aggregating the bottom level forecasts. It does
not use any series at the aggregated levels, which are often smoother and easier to predict
([9]) and it is unlikely to give good results when the aggregation involves a large number of
series ([11], [12]). Top-down method will use the top-level forecast and derive the lower
level forecasts from its disaggregation using proportions derived from historical data. In
[12] the authors showed that such forecast will always be biased even if the base forecasts
are not. The middle-out simply combines the two methods and uses the forecasts from a
speciﬁc middle level, to derive from its aggregation and disaggregation higher and lower
level forecasts, respectively. Many studies are available that compare these methods but
no clear-cut answer was given about which one is the best.
However, it is clear and intuitive that simply aggregating or disaggregating information
from a speciﬁc level k to predict the levels i ̸= k does not make the best use of the
hierarchy information and peculiarities (diﬀerent patterns at each level), and disregards
potential useful information one could get from an independent forecast at levels i. In
fact, independently forecasting each level would allow us to use a speciﬁc model for each
level beneﬁting from diﬀerent models’ levels of accuracy ([9]). For this reason, 2-steps
approaches have been proposed, in which the ﬁrst step produces independent incoherent
base forecasts for all the nodes in the hierarchy, and the second step reconciles them. [12]
proposed a combination approach which starting from the base forecasts would estimate the
reconciled forecasts based on an OLS estimator. [15] suggested a WLS estimator instead.
These combination approaches would work well on average, but would not guarantee to be
more accurate than the base forecasts at all levels. Changing perspective, [14] proposed a
game-theoretically approach to reconciliation that guarantees reconciled forecasts at least
as good as the base ones, providing a breakthrough. [16] recognizing some theoretical
ﬂaws in [12] formalizes the MinT (minimum trace) reconciliation method, wrapping it
up in a rigorous theoretical framework and comparing it with the GLS, OLS and WLS
methods. [9] builds on these method to compute the adjustments in a sparse fashion, in
order to minimize the number of adjustments needed to reconcile the forecasts. Recently,
in [13], a newer version of [16], the authors prove that the MinT method also guarantees
better revised forecasts compared to the base ones. It is clear from the mentioned studies,
that more accurate forecasts can be generated when taking into account information at all
levels, as one could intuitively expect. Even acknowledging that the decision-maker and
the forecaster have diﬀerent loss-functions [3], we assume in this document that higher
accuracy always brings beneﬁt to the decision-maker in the reconciliation framework. One
could therefore agree that higher accuracy gives an additional motivation to the process of
reconciliation.
Probabilistic extension. It must be said that all the methods described so far focus
on the reconciliation of the expectation of the forecasts, i.e. they generate reconciled
point forecasts. However, as stated in [3], a forecast to be consistent must be expressed in
probabilistic terms. To this end, [11], [17] and [18] propose probabilistic approaches, that
build on the MinT, Copula-based and other methods. The probabilistic setting will also
be investigated in the present work. Among the mentioned articles, [18] is the only work
applying reconciliation of hierarchical forecasts to wind power, which shows the increasing
importance that the topic is gaining outside of the traditional research areas.

An online approach proposal. In the framework of wind power forecasting, many other
considerations would have to be made to make the forecast and reconciliation processes more
eﬀective and reliable. Firstly, there is a spatio-temporal correlation between neighbouring
wind farms that has to be accounted for ([7]). This would aﬀect the distribution of the
forecasting errors in the hierarchy, an information that could be useful in identifying a
speciﬁc variance-covariance matrix in the case of an LS regression reconciliation method.
Secondly, high dimension multivariate large datasets require special care to avoid overﬁtting
and infeasibility due to computational issues. This problem is commonly overcome using
recursion ([19]). Furthermore, wind condition changing (due e.g. to changing in atmospheric
pressure) and seasonal patterns aﬀect the dynamic of the wind and the underlying forecast
error. Model parameters in this case necessitate a recalculation, a concept known as
adaptivity of the model. A simple approach to achieve adaptivity is ﬁtting the model on
sliding windows training data, but this implies reﬁtting of the model every time new data
is available. Another approach, more advanced, uses the exponential forgetting of past
data, which is proven to be more eﬃcient and less memory demanding ([19]). In [19] an
adaptive and recursive lasso VAR is implemented to meet all the needs mentioned above.
The VAR forecast method exploits spatio-temporal correlation present in a given set of
wind farms; lasso regularization allows for adaptivity; recursion reduces the computational
burden.
It is intuitive that an adaptive method could be suited to reconciliation methods as well.
In fact, keeping in mind that the reconciliation can also be seen as the projection of the
base forecasts onto the space of aggregation consistency, or as the combination of the base
forecasts weighted diﬀerently, adaptivity would allow to adjust those weights depending on
the error from the previous step, which in turn depends on diﬀerent conditions (season,
wind condition, distance and reciprocal position of the wind farms).
This work then proposes the implementation of an adaptive reconciliation method and
its evaluation on simulated and real data. The probabilistic setting and the idea of a
distributed online constrained optimization will also be investigated, to improve consistency,
and to deal with computational and privacy issues, respectively.
Objectives
The project’s objectives are the following:
• Implementation of the reconciliation algorithms presented in [12], [14], and [10] for
benchmarking purposes;
• Development of a novel customized algorithm for online reconciliation of hierarchical
forecasts;
• Application of the customized method on synthetic and real wind farm data.
Investigate forecasting skill and time to ﬁt the diﬀerent models;
• Make the algorithm computationally cheap so that it is applicable to a large set of
wind farms;
• Discuss objectively the real-life application of the developed tool.

Learning objectives
A student who has met the objectives of this project will be able to:

Methodology
The ﬁrst step of the project will consist on the creation of a synthetic dataset. Assuming
that AR processes can describe wind power time series [24], time series for a 2-level
cross-sectional hierarchy will be generated accordingly. The error sampled during the
generation of the ts is assumed to be correlated over the bottom level nodes (representing
the individual wind farms). More focus and possibly more speciﬁc assumptions on the
error term will be given at a later stage based on [7]. Training AR parameters on the
produced time series will then give the corresponding forecasted time series. At ﬁrst, the
data will be assumed to be hourly wind generation data, issued for 1-ahead time step only.
The time series are centered, to avoid an intercept. These characteristics will be changed
at a later stage if necessary. The size of the hierarchy is to be chosen.
As a second step, some of the existing reconciliation methods, including the bottom-up, the
top-down, the OLS regression method from [12], the game-theoretical approach from [14]
and other methods from [10] will be implemented. These methods will be then used as
benchmark in the future analysis.
To verify the accuracy of the forecasts and measure the performance of the methods, a
loss function will be chosen or designed for the purpose, investigating the possibility of
capturing the overall beneﬁt from both the forecaster and the decision-maker point of
view. Otherwise, a standard Sum of Squared Errors measure will be chosen, confusing
decision-maker and forecasters beneﬁts.
Adaptive, recursive, online algorithms will be investigated, starting from [19]. Online
constrained optimization will be also studied, taking as reference [36] and other methods
from literature. A choice will be made about how to incorporate the adaptivity in the
reconciliation framework.
The adaptive (and possibly distributed and probabilistic) algorithm of reconciliation is to
be implemented at this stage of the project. Afterward the novel approach will be tested
on simulated and real data. Prior to the testing, the real dataset, which can be that from
Horns Rev or from the whole Denmark (Energinet), or both, has to be investigated, as
previous studies could provide useful insights.
