Abstract 
Myeloproliferative neoplasms are unique hematopoietic stem-cell disorders, characterized by 
the clonal expansions of myeloid linage cells, due to gene mutations that activate the 
signaling pathways responsible for hematopoiesis. The somatic mutations in the calreticulin 
(CRT) gene are identified in some PMF and ET patients. CTR is a 46 kDa calcium-buffering 
chaperone, located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), and consists of three main structural 
and functional domains. CRT is important in cellular processes, and its dysfunction leads to 
relocation of the protein and disruption of normal cellular activity. 
In this thesis wildtype CRT has been visualized in the ER using immunocytochemistry. Also, 
the theory stating that an increased amount of platelet production is due to fs CRT’s 
translocation to the cell surface, where it binds to thrombopoietin receptor is tested to some 
extent, but further testing and development is needed to reach a conclusion. Furthermore, 
extracted CRT from different starting materials, is analysed and detected via immunoblotting. 
The results showed that only wildtype CRT was identified, while the fs CRT seemed to be 
degraded. Several adjustments are still needed to optimize the protocols developed so far.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPN) and their history 
Myeloproliferative disorders were initially described by the haematologist William 
Dameshek in 1951, but have since been renamed by the World Health Organization as 
Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs). MPNs are a heterogenous group of clonal diseases of 
hematopoietic stem cells caused by genetic alterations leading to an increased number of 
mature cells produced by a single or multiple blood cell lineages [1]. The four main 
myeloproliferative diseases, listed in table 1, are categorized by either the presence or 
absence of the Philadelphia chromosome: 
Table 1 – The 4 main myeloproliferative neoplasms: MPNs categorized by either the 
presence or absence of the Philadelphia chromosome [1] 
Philadelphia chromosome positive 
Philadelphia chromosome negative 
 
Chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML) 
Essential thrombocytosis (ET) 
Polycythemia vera (PV) 
Primary myelofibrosis (PMF) 
The first myeloproliferative disease was published in October 1845 by the English 
pathologist John Hughes Bennett, who observed and described CML in a report titled ‘Case 
of hypertrophy of the spleen and liver in which death took place from suppuration of the 
blood’ [2]. Almost a century passed to get a better molecular understanding of CML, and it is 
now known for its unique somatic abnormality; the translocation of the Philadelphia 
chromosome leading to the BCR/ABL gene fusion [2][3]. PMF was first described in 1879 
by a German surgeon Gustav Heuck, and he was also able to distinguish between the 
morphological features of PMF and CML, due to the presence of marrow fibrosis and 
extensive extramedullary hematopoiesis [2], which still is the predominant feature of this 
disease. In 1892 PV was described by a French physician Louis Henri Vaquez after he had 
examined a patient with increased red blood cells (RBCs), and in the beginning of the 19th 
century William Osler’s and other scientists’ observations contributed to further elaborate PV 
from relative polycythemia and secondary polycythemia, and recognized 
leukoerythroblastosis and increased granulocytic and megakaryocytic activity characteristic 
for PV [2]. Essential thrombocytosis, the last of the myeloproliferative diseases listed in table 
1 was described in 1934 by the Austrian pathologists Emil Epstein and Alfred Goedel after 
they among other things noticed the presence of high platelet counts in their patients. Later in

1.2 MPN mutations  
Myeloproliferative neoplasms are caused by various mutations that lead to a dysregulation of 
cell proliferation. As mentioned in the previous section, CML has a gene translocation, where 
part of chromosome 9 is transferred to chromosome 22 (and vice versa) in the Philadelphia 
chromosome, resulting in the production of an oncoprotein that gives an increased tyrosine 
kinase activity leading to a deregulation in the proliferation of leukaemic myeloid cells [4]. 
The most common MPN patients are ET, PV and PMF, who are often associated with 
mutations in either the Janus-associated kinase 2 (JAK2), myeloproliferative leukemia 
protein (MPL) and calreticulin (CALR) genes [5][6]. Normally JAK2 is a signalling protein 
that leads to cell proliferation following the binding of thrombopoietin (TPO) and 
erythropoietin (EPO) to their respective receptors. The V617F mutation in JAK2 is an exon 
14 point mutation leading to a permanent proliferation and over-production of specific cell 
types [4][5]. Another mutation, where either an insertion or deletion occurs in JAK2 exon 12, 
results in erythrocytosis alone. Among PMF and ET patients, MPL mutation can occur, 
especially somatic mutation in exon 10 in the juxtamembrane region at codon 515 of MPL 
(W515L, W515K, S505N), which forces a change in the receptor conformation, that activates 
the JAK2 without TPO binding to its receptor. This leads to a defective signalling pathway 
giving a gain in function and proliferative advantage to megakaryocytes [5][7]. See figure 1.

1.3 Calreticulin   
1.3.1 Structural domains of Calreticulin 
Calreticulin (CRT) was first identified in 1974 by Ostwald and MacLennan as a Ca2+-binding 
protein of the muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum, and the encoding DNA was isolated in 1989. 
CRT has a very conserved genomic DNA and amino acid sequence throughout evolution and 
across species, indicating that it has an important cellular function. It is present in all 
nucleated cell types and has various biological functions in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). 
The human gene for CRT is 5891 base pairs (bp) long with 9 exons and 8 introns, the 
encoded protein CRT has 417 amino acids, and is an acidic protein with a molecular mass of

and consists of three structural and functional domains; the N-terminal which is encoded by 
exons 1-4, the P-terminal encoded by exons 5-7 and the last two exons encode the C-terminal 
[11][14]. The linear and three-dimensional representation of CRT domains is illustrated in

1.3.1.1 The N-terminal domain 
The protein CRT is synthesized with a cleavable 17 residues N-terminal amino acid signal 
sequence that directs the protein to the ER. Following the signal sequence, from residue 1-
170 the N-terminal is formed as a folded globular structure with eight anti-parallel β-strands 
connected by loops. The conserved N-domain, which includes the polypeptide- and 
carbohydrate-binding sites, contributes to the chaperone activity together with the P-domain, 
a Zn2+-binding site and a disulfide bridge formed by two of the three cysteine residues (Cys88 
and Cys120) located in the N-domain contributing to its globular structure. It is the N-domain 
that stabilizes CRT to avoid proteolysis in the presence of calcium ions [8][9][11][15][16].

1.3.1.2 The P-terminal domain 
The middle part of the protein, from residue 171-285, is named the P-domain due to the 
proline-rich sequence. The P-domain forms a flexible arm that is stabilized by three 
antiparallel β-sheets formed by three repeats of two amino acid sequences 
IXDPXADXKPEDWDX (figure 2A, repeat A) and GXWXPPXIXNPXYX (figure 2B, 
repeat B) that interact with an oxidoreductase folding enzyme, the ER protein 57 (ERp57), 
and thus contributes to the chaperone activity. The binding-site for ERp57 to CRT is shown 
in figure 2A and 2B [9][11][16]. 
1.3.1.3 The C-terminal domain 
Residues 286-400 form the C-domain that is negatively charged due to the acidic amino acid 
sequences that mostly consist of aspartic and glutamic acid with some basic residues of lysine 
and arginine. Due to the negatively charged residues, the C-domain functions as a calcium-
buffering region that plays a vital role for controlling and regulating cellular processes, 
including exocytosis, metabolism, transcription and proliferation. The calcium-buffering 
domain can bind over 50% of ER Ca2+ with high capacity (25 mol of Ca2+ per mol of protein) 
and low affinity. Furthermore, the chaperone activity and formation of folding complexes is 
also dependent on the calcium concentration. The C-domain terminates with KDEL, the ER 
retrieval sequence [9][11][16]. 
Mutational analysis has shown that specific amino acid residues are necessary for the 
chaperone function of calreticulin; a Trp from the N-domain and another from the P-domain 
are both critical. Furthermore, a His from the N-domain was not only important for the 
chaperone function but also for the structure of calreticulin, whereas the disulfide bridge if 
disrupted would only partially affect the chaperone function of calreticulin. In general, either 
conformational changes due to mutations or variation of the calcium concentration can affect 
the chaperone function of CRT [16]. 
1.3.2 The function of Calreticulin, a calcium-buffering chaperone  
As mentioned earlier, CRT has various functions, including antigen presentation via the 
major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I molecule by assembling the class I heavy 
chain and β2-microglobulin with a stabilizing peptide. Furthermore, CRT outside the ER can 
exist in the cytoplasm; research indicates that CRT regulates cell adhesion, translation, gene 
expression and nuclear export, at the cell surface; enhancement of the immunogenic response 
to tumour antigens and stimulation of immunogenic cell death and in the extracellular

functions include modulation of calcium homeostasis and chaperone activity, where it

1.3.2.1 Calreticulin/calnexin chaperone cycle   
Extracellular and cellular proteins are folded in the ER by not just the proteins CRT, CNX 
and ERp57 but by a specific set of chaperones that contributes to the CRT/calnexin cycle. 
Calnexin (CNX) is a 90 kDa ER integral membrane protein that also has a cleavable N-
terminal amino acid signal sequence directing it to the ER, and a C-terminal ER-retention 
signal. Calreticulin and calnexin share structural and functional similarities, including 
domain-like structure and together with ERp57 form the functional complex for the 
CRT/CNX cycle that controls the folding and correct conformation of newly synthesized 
glycoproteins before secreting them to their locations [8][9].  
The CRT/CNX cycle is initiated by an enzyme, oligosaccharyltransferase, that recognizes a 
specific sequence in the newly synthesized protein, the nascent chain NXS/T (Asn-Xaa-
Ser/Thr), and binds oligosaccharide composed of Glc1Man9GlcNAc2 to the asparagine 
residue via an amide linkage. The glucose moieties are removed by two ER luminal enzymes, 
glucosidase I and II, by cleaving the terminal glucose residues, but before the last 
glycoprotein is cleaved, a quality-control by CRT and CNX is executed. If the glycoprotein is 
folded correctly, it exits the CRT/CNX cycle after glucosidase II cleaves the last glucose, but 
if the glycoprotein is not folded correctly, the terminal glucose is attached again by an 
enzyme, the UDP-glucose:glycoprotein glucosyltransferase (UGGT), which can distinguish 
between folded and unfolded proteins. Unfolded glycoproteins undergo cycles of interaction 
with CRT and CNX, that form complexes with ER chaperones, including protein disulfide 
isomerase (PDI) and ERp57. Unsuccessful folding and prolonged interaction in the 
CRT/CNX cycle sends a protein for degradation by an interaction with ER degradation-
enhancing 1,2-mannosidase-like protein (EDEM), where it is recognized by α1,2-
mannosidase I, that removes a mannose residue, targeting it for the ER-associated 
degradation (ERAD) machinery [9][11][13]. See also figure 3 for an illustration showing the 
CRT/CNX cycle.

1.3.2.2 
The role of Calreticulin in Ca2+ homoeostasis   
Calcium ions are mostly located in the lumen of the ER and are essential for a cell, due to the 
fluctuations that effect the cellular processes and development. Ca2+ homoeostasis and 
signalling are maintained by the InsP3R (inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate receptor) and ryanodine 
receptor, that control Ca2+ release from the ER, whereas SERCA refills the ER stores. The 
Ca2+ present in the ER stores helps regulate various enzymes and proteins in the ER. Any 
interference of the Ca2+ homoeostasis can potentially activate transcriptional and translational

~ 14 ~ 
 
cascades, that regulate chaperones responsible for protein folding within the ER, proteins 
responsible for ER stress and ERAD, as well as proteins involved in the apoptotic pathway. 
These functions are modulated by Ca2+ binding to the C-domain, which as mentioned earlier 
is an acidic region with high capacity for binding Ca2+ ions to 43 acidic amino acid residues 
out of the last 82 amino acids of the protein. Studies conducted for analysing chaperone and 
Ca2+-buffering properties of CRT using animal models and cells overexpressing or deficient 
in CRT have demonstrated that functionally, CRT is dependent on the buffering and 
regulation of calcium ions. CRT-deficient cells have impaired Ca2+ homoeostasis, but 
modestly decreased protein folding activity. Increased expression of CRT does not affect 
protein folding activity, but significantly affects Ca2+ homoeostasis. Further studies with 
calreticulin and calnexin gene-knockout mice have shown that these proteins are unable to 
replace each other, especially CRT’s role in modulation of Ca2+ homoeostasis cannot be 
undertaken by CNX. The most important observation noted with CRT gene deletion is that it 
is an embryonic lethal mutation, due to defects in the heart and body wall that lead to cardiac 
failure. This demonstrates that CRT is important during cardiac development. Experiments 
have demonstrated that CRT is upregulated in the heart during the middle stages of 
embryogenesis, while at later stages and after birth CRT is expressed at low levels. This 
indicates the importance of the protein in the development of the heart, and data indicates that 
the most likely function to modulate this development is the cellular Ca2+ signalling. It can 
therefore be noted that non-normal functions of CRT, due to mutations, can lead to a 
deficiency of Ca2+ homeostasis and thereby affect the Ca2+ signalling pathways 
[8][9][13][18]. 
1.3.3 Mutated calreticulin 
In 2013, Klampfl et al. in Vienna performed whole-exome sequencing in PMF patients, who 
lacked JAK2 and MPL mutations, and detected 36 types of somatic mutations (insertions and 
deletions) in exon 9 of the CRT gene. Approximately 30-45% ET and PMF patients lacking 
JAK2 and MPL gene mutations were discovered to have somatic mutations in their CRT 
gene. Since then, more than 50 different indels in CRT exon 9 have been identified, but the 
most common variations were p.L367fs*46, a type 1 mutation with 52-bp deletion, and 
p.K385fs*47, a type 2 mutation with 5-bp insertion. Studies have shown that of all CRT-
mutated patients, these two types of mutations account for over 80%, and other mutation 
types were observed at much lower rates including type 3, 4, 5, and 11. CRT mutations cause 
frameshift in the reading frame and are observed in hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells.

Most of the CRT mutations are heterozygous mutations, whereas homozygous mutations are 
extremely rare and are 5-bp insertions. All mutated CRTs share a novel C-terminus with 
negatively charged amino acids replaced by neutral and positively charged amino acids 
causing a loss of Ca2+-buffering function and the KDEL signal, thus delocalizing the protein 
[1][18][19]. 
1.4 Calreticulin: autoimmune diseases, cancer and apoptosis 
CRT can be detected in a number of diseases, and not only MPN patients. During disease, 
cells are often exposed to stress that potentially can cause cell death, triggering the release of 
intracellular proteins like CRT. Studies have demonstrated that ER chaperones that are 
exposed to the extracellular surroundings can activate the immune system, where the innate 
immune system responds by producing autoantibodies towards the “threat”. In various 
autoimmune disorders, CRT can associate with other complexes and function as a surface 
autoantigen that autoantibodies target, such as Sjögren’s syndrome and SLE disorders. 
Autoantibodies against CRT have also been identified in systemic lupus erythematosus, 
coeliac disease, polychondritis, rheumatic disease, etc. indicating that the immune system 
recognizes CRT as an epitope. When CRT is delocalized to the cell surface, it interacts with 
C1q, which is a part of the innate immune system that activates the classical complement 
pathway resulting in apoptosis of the cell and engulfment by macrophages [9][20]. A growing 
focus for a novel treatment against cancer involves CRT for removing cancer cells. 
Immunogenic cell death is mediated by the co-translocation of CRT with ERp57 to the cell 
surface, activating the T-cells after presentation resulting in apoptosis of the tumour cell. For 
cancer therapy, medical research is still ongoing for using CRT as an adjuvant to target a 
response towards tumour cells for enhanced immunogenicity [20].

2 Purpose of project  
Statens Serum Institut (SSI) is a governmental institute under the Danish Ministry of Health 
and has various departments that contribute with disease surveillance, specialized diagnostics 
and research. In collaboration with the department of Autoimmunology & Biomarkers this 
master project, Calreticulin in Myeloproliferative Neoplasms, was completed. The research 
and experiments conducted for this thesis were based on the following points:  
1) Location of CRT:  Optimize the immunocytochemistry (ICC) procedure and visualize 
the location of CRT using specific antibodies. 
2) Binding ability of the thrombopoietin receptor: Investigating the mutated CRT’s 
binding properties towards the thrombopoietin receptor (TPO-R) with a commercial 
enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kit 
3) Translocation of CRT to the cell surface: Use of fluorescence-activated cell sorting 
(FACS) to examine if specific cells translocate wildtype CRT to the cell surface. 
4) Detecting wildtype (WT) frame-shifted (fs) CRT: Sodium dodecyl sulfate-
polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) was used on patient samples for 
separating proteins and visualizing CRT/mutated CRT by western blotting.

5 Methods 
5.1 ICC: Localisation of CRT 
Slides containing HEp-2 cells with 13 wells and ANCA slides with 10 wells containing 
Granulocytes and HEp-2 cells, with their respective substrates are applied to visualize CRT 
using anti-CRT antibodies. The supernatant from a blocking mixture, that is mixed for 3 
hours, containing TTN, 0.1% OVA and 0.1% animal serum and centrifuged at 3113g for 10 
min is prepared, 25µL is pipetted to each well and incubated overnight (O/N). Next day the 
slides are washed with TTN, followed by dipping in TTN for 10 mins and then gently dried 
around the wells. Antibodies are diluted 1:100 with the blocking mixture and added to each 
well for incubation in a humidity-chamber for 2 hours. The washing process is repeated and 
then the fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) conjugated anti-rabbit or anti-mouse antibodies 
diluted 1:300 in TTN are added and incubated in a humidity-chamber for 2 hours, and again 
followed by the washing process. The slides are dried after TTN dipping and 1 drop of 
mounting buffer is added to each well followed by placing a microscope cover glass on top. 
The slides are looked at through a fluorescence microscope with a 20x objective. 
5.2 FACS: Translocation of CRT to the cell surface 
5.2.1 Isolation of white blood cells (WBC) from peripheral blood 
Add to an Eppendorf tube 50µg HEPARIN, 200-300µL blood from a finger-stich and fill 
with PBS to 1mL. Mix well, centrifuge the contents of the tube and remove the supernatant 
consisting of plasma. To the precipitate with the blood cells, add 500µL lysis buffer and mix 
thoroughly using a Vortex mixer for 5 min, then centrifuge the cells at 49g at 5°C for 10 min, 
remove the supernatant containing lysed red blood cells (RBC) from the white blood cells 
(WBC). Wash the WBC with 2·100µL PBS, centrifuge as previously. Resuspend the WBC in 
PBS. See figure 5 for an illustrative description of the experimental setup for section 5.2.1. 
and 5.2.2.  
5.2.2 WBC treatment with/without CRT translocating reagent

6.1 Visualizing CRT in cells by fluorescence imaging 
It is known that Calreticulin is predominantly located in the ER due to its KDEL signal, but 
when the protein is mutated it loses the KDEL signal and the position of the protein is 
probably shifted. Therefore, immunocytochemistry was performed, using various anti-
calreticulin monoclonal- (mAb) and polyclonal-antibodies (pAb) on slides containing Hep-2 
cells and granulocytes, for optimizing the procedure to visualize WT CRT, so that when 
patient samples with mutated CRT are obtained, the ICC protocol is followed to detect and 
identify the fs CRT’s location. In the final protocol, blocking reagents OVA and animal 
serum, either rabbit or goat depending on the animal the primary antibody was produced in, 
were added, so unspecific binding of the secondary antibody conjugated with a fluorochrome 
is prevented.

6.2 Translocation of fs CRT to the cell surface and binding ability to TPO-R 
Mutated CRT has been reported in patients with ET, that lead to an increased number of 
circulating platelets, and PMF, where excessive scar tissue is formed in the bone marrow 
damaging its ability to produce normal blood cells. However, the mechanism by which CRT 
mutations promote cancer is not fully uncovered, but one theory states that mutated CRT is 
involved in activating TPO-R and the JAK2 signalling pathway that regulates megakaryocyte 
and platelet production [28][29]. Several studies have demonstrated that CRT is able to 
translocate to the cell surface when the cell is exposed to a certain environment by specific 
factors, like stress, or chemicals [30][31], while other studies, such as Marito Araki et al. and 
Shannon Elf et al., indicate activity when mutated CRT binds to TPO-R [28][32]. A certain 
factor before initiating FACS experiments is the presence of mutated CRT in granulocytes, 
but not lymphocytes, in MPN patients [19][33]. Therefore, WBCs are isolated for examining 
the above mentioned concepts on the cells in the blood. A FACS protocol was developed and 
optimized, on a beginner level, for testing WT CRT translocation to the cell surface.  
The WBC where isolated and either fixated and/or permeabilized before using SPA-601-F 
conjugated FITC, to analyse the cells using the FACS instrument after 0 h and 24 h. The 
FACS analyses showed best results with a staining concentration at 1:1000 using primary ab. 
conjugated to FITC, fixation of the cells with 0.4% formaldehyde was necessary, and when 
the WBC were permeabilized the FACS instrument could identify FITC-labelled 
granulocytes. No big difference was observed when analysing the samples, the same day or

7 Conclusion and future perspective 
The thesis is composed of results from four parts; locating CRT in cells, promoting CRT to 
translocate to the cell surface, test the binding ability of CRT towards the TPO-R and to 
detect WT and fs CRT.  
To locate CRT an ICC protocol was developed, and observations from HEp-2 cells and 
granulocytes identified the location of CRT in the ER. The ICC protocol can be applied when 
paraffin-embedded bone marrow samples from MPN patients with mutated CRT are 
obtained, to identify the location of the fs CRT, inside or on the cell surface. If it is on the cell 
surface, the theory of an increased amount of platelet production, because of fs CRT’s 
translocation to the cell surface and binding to the TPO-R, can be tested more thoroughly, but 
after optimizing the FACS protocol. During the FACS experiments that are preliminary and 
can be improved, two translocator reagents were used to promote the translocation of WT 
CRT, 0.2 mM MTX and 1mM H2O2. MTX did not show such activity, whereas the examined 
cells when treated with H2O2 had a FITC intensity indicating SPA-601-F conjugated FITC 
was bound to WT CRT on the cell surface. Unfortunately, too few cells were analysed and 
the isotype controls were not negative, requiring additional testing and development of the 
protocol. Furthermore, to test fs CRT’s binding ability to TPO-R, a commercial ELISA kit 
was purchased, where the bottom was coated with TPO-R, and tested, but no activity was 
measured when adding recombinant fs CRT. Last but not least, CRT was extracted from 
thrombocytes, cells in a buffy coat and from dried blood on paper membrane, and detected by 
SDS-PAGE and Western blot showing bands when developed in the non-mutated part of the 
protein, indicating that the fs CRT from MPN patients probably is degraded and therefore not 
detectable.
