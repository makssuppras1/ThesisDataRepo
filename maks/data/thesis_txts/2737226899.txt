Abstract
As the adoption of electric vehicles continues to rise, the need for understanding user
behavior is increasing. As, the literature review revealed a lack of studies utilizing exten-
sive real charging data, this thesis aims to address this gap by analyzing the charging
behavior of home charger users in Denmark, utilizing both historical data and user sur-
vey responses.
First, the delay in charging sessions is modeled using XGBoost machine learning al-
gorithm. This modeling is approached as a dual task: modeling the decision to delay
charging (classification) and the duration of the delay (regression). Two approaches are
explored: a single model for delay behavior across all users and personalized models
for each user.
For the classification task, the evaluation of the machine learning models’ performance
indicates that while the rule-based benchmark - which is based on previous delay use
and plug-in hour of the day - outperforms the model. Incorporating price-related features
significantly enhances the single model’s accuracy and F1 score, both reaching 0.94 and
outperforming benchmark. Results show that delays are more likely to occur when the
hour of plug-in is from 16:00 to 19:00, while prices lower than 2DKK/kWh at the time
the user plugs in their vehicle decrease the likelihood of delay, and prices higher than
2DKK/kWh increase it. Despite the inclusion of price predictors, personalized models
still perform worse than the benchmark for the majority of the cases.
For delay duration modeling, the single model shows mediocre performance, tending to
overestimate short delays and underestimate long ones, with significant dependence on
the cosine transformation of the session start time. Personalized models, despite being
tailored to individual behaviors, are outperformed by the rule-based benchmark in 80%
of cases, which calculates delay duration by targeting a power start at 1:00. Predictions
are generally accurate from 16:00 to 19:00, but less reliable for late-night and early
afternoon sessions. From the Distribution System Operator’s (DSO) perspective, when
predicting delay duration with the benchmark there are deviations from the actual power
start ranging from 1-2 hours, depending on the seasonality and the hour of the plug-in.
Survey data analysis reveals that the majority of users check prices before charging, with
less willingness to postpone charging due to high prices. Additionally, 37.3% of users
prefer third-party managed charging services, primarily motivated by cost minimization,
with reducing CO2 emissions being the least important factor. The survey showed that
users managing their charging manually are more proactive about price checking and
delaying charging when prices are high. For users who provided their charger ID it
was able to match their survey responses with the charging delay modeling results,
identifying correlations.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The European Union is striving to achieve its goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions
by 55% by 2030. Given that cars and vans produce 15% of the EU’s total CO2 emissions,
actively transitioning to sustainable transportation is essential to meet this target [1]. As
a result, electric vehicles (EVs) have emerged as a significant contributor to reducing
greenhouse gas emissions. Since 2010, the adoption of EVs has increased, according
to data from the Statistics Denmark, as shown in Figure 1.1 [2].
Figure 1.1: New registrations of electric cars, Statistics Denmark [2].
In Denmark, according to the 2023 Climate Status and Outlook Report, emissions from
the transport sector represent 36% of total Danish emissions, with road transport being
responsible for the majority of these emissions (95% in 2019). Consequently, the inte-
gration of electric vehicles in Denmark is increasing and expected to amplify by 2035,
as a result of the Revised Regulations on CO2 Emissions [3].
The growth of EVs presents significant challenges for Distribution System Operators
(DSOs), who are tasked with maintaining a stable electricity supply, as an increase in
coincidental factor may lead to voltage instability and grid congestion [4]. Alleviating this
concentrated demand requires a deeper understanding of user preferences regarding
the initiation of users’ charging sessions. Upon connecting their vehicles to a charger,
users can either start charging immediately or delay the session to a later hour (i.e.
through Spirii’s (e-Mobility Platform Provider) app or directly from the vehicle if it sup-
ports this functionality). This decision to delay, defined as the difference between the

time of arrival (hour of the plug-in) and the power start (the time charging starts), reflects
user comfort by highlighting preferred charging times. Understanding the intentions be-
hind this decision to delay the session, identifying patterns, and modeling their behavior
allows for:
• Adapting to User Behavior: By modeling user behavior, the development or ad-
justment of smart charging solutions and pricing schemes to align with current user

• Predicting Behavior: Accurate behavior modeling enables the prediction of user

Furthermore, the rise in electric vehicle adoption, as illustrated in Figure 1.2 makes it
urgent to gain deeper insights into EV charging behavior. To address this, a survey
was conducted targeting owners of Spirii (e-Mobility Platform Provider) home chargers.
The survey aims to understand the configurations that may influence user behavior,
by exploring charging habits, flexibility, and user preferences regarding manual versus
third-party managed charging and their underlying motivations. Participants are option-
ally asked for their charger ID to integrate their responses with historical data, providing
a comprehensive view of their EV charging behavior and preferences.
1.2
Literature review
A review is conducted, synthesizing recent research that explores electric vehicle (EV)
user behavior and preferences through various methodologies and data sources (sur-
2
==PAGE:11==
vey, historical).
The research by Plenter et al. (2018) utilized choice-based conjoint analysis on surveys
from European consumers to investigate economic and technological factors influencing
EV adoption. They emphasized the strategic placement and characteristics of charging
points as crucial factors affecting users’ willingness to pay [5]. Additionally, Dorcec et
al. (2019) employed a gamified survey approach to delve into EV owners’ willingness
to pay for charging services, highlighting the impact of charging speed, state of charge,
and reference pricing [6]. As it comes to Denmark, Visaria et al. (2022) researched
Danish EV users’ charging preferences through literature review, interviews, and two
experiments, finding a preference for all-inclusive pricing and interoperability between
charging networks [7]. These insights are crucial for improving user satisfaction and
reducing the barriers to EV adoption.
Focusing more on home charging, Klein et al. (2020) analyzed the influence of home
charging on the spread of EVs in Germany through agent-based simulations and con-
joint analysis, noting the importance of government incentives and technological ad-
vancements [8]. This study highlights how supportive policies and technological ad-
vancements can accelerate the adoption of electric vehicles by making home charging
more accessible and cost-effective. Finally, in their study, Delmonte et al. (2020) investi-
gated UK EV users’ preferences for user-controlled versus supplier-managed charging,
showing a preference for the former due to its cost efficiency and user autonomy, though
they also recognized the societal benefits of the latter [9]. This study underline the bal-
ancing act between individual preferences and broader societal advantages, suggesting
a dual focus for future policies.
Collectively, these findings provide a robust understanding of the factors shaping EV
user behavior, which is crucial for crafting effective support strategies for the transition
to electric mobility. Despite these insights, the field could benefit from more nuanced,
user-specific data to better understand residential charging practices, preferences for
manual versus third-party managed charging, and to correlate user responses with their
historical data for deeper behavioral insights.
When it comes to modeling the behavior of users, a review of methods for modeling EV
usage highlighted various approaches, noting the advantages and drawbacks of each,
and stressed the need for models that more accurately represent charging behaviors,
with activity-based modeling (ABM) seen as particularly promising despite its limitations
s [10].
A study analyzing data from 176 EV models in Australia developed a customer classifi-
cation using machine learning to understand plug-in behaviors, identifying driving range
and battery capacity as key variables [11]. Pelka et al. Pelka and colleagues presented
an advanced model for optimizing electricity costs based on Prospect Theory, demon-
strating that less user control over charging could yield significant cost savings. This was
supported by data from a scenario-based survey and a field experiment with German
prosumers, underscoring the importance of behavioral considerations in EV charging

==PAGE:12==
strategies [12].
Overall, there is a significant deficiency in studies that utilize extensive real charging
data, underscoring the necessity for more comprehensive representation of user be-
havior in EV usage models. Ideally, these models should integrate historical data with
survey data to enhance their accuracy and relevance.
1.3
Research Aim
The present study seeks to model the delay of user’s charging sessions to a more con-
venient time, utilizing historical data. Specifically, this user behavior, indicating their
preference and sensitivity when it comes to charging their electric vehicle, can be seen
as two actions:
• The decision to delay charging to another hour.
• The decision on the length of the delay duration.
Considering these actions, the following questions are explored:
• What motivates users to postpone their charging to a later time?
• How can the duration of the delay period be understood and predicted?
Additionally, this research seeks to gain insights into the behavior of home charging
users through surveys, with a particular focus on the question: What are the key factors
influencing charging behavior, and what preferences do home charger users in Denmark
have?
1.4
Thesis Outline
The thesis consists of six chapters. The first chapter introduces the motivation for the
investigation, the literature review, and the research aim. The second chapter provides
an overview of the dataset used for the study and outlines the framework for modeling
users’ charging delay. Continuing, the third chapter presents the results of modeling
users’ decisions to delay charging, while the fourth chapter contains the results of mod-

ducted, detailing its structure and results. Lastly, the sixth chapter offers conclusions
and suggestions for future work.

2.2
Dataset Overview
The dataset used in this research originates from meters of home chargers recorded
sessions that occurred between September 1, 2021, and January 7, 2024, provided by
SPiRii an e-Mobility Platform Provider (eMPP). It contains information for each session,
including the user ID, session start time (plug-in), power start time, power stop time,
plug-out time, and session maximum power [13]. The dataset variables are illustrated
in Figure 2.1. Users were filtered to be under the Radius Distribution System Operator.
To investigate the impact of pricing, the dataset employed includes information on hourly
spot price alongside the corresponding Time-of-Use [14].
2.2.1
Dataset Filtering
Observing the delay use over the dataset’s timeframe reveals a significant change, after
2022. According to the Danish Energy Agency report of energy statistics for 2022, the
electricity price in Denmark in 2022 increased by 98.1% compared to the year before
[15]. This event caused a notable change in user behavior regarding delay usage as
6
==PAGE:15==
shown in Figure 2.3. Therefore, data from before September 2022 is disregarded as it
would skew the model’s performance.
Figure 2.3: Average monthly delay usage across all users.
The dataset was further refined to include users with at least 100 charging sessions over
the course of a year, indicating that these users charge their vehicles approximately
twice a week. This filtering ensures sufficient data to accurately model user behavior.
Additionally, the focus is on battery electric vehicles (BEVs). Chargers where the 90th
percentile was less than 30 kWh were filtered out, aiming to keep the Battery Electric
Vehicles (BEVs).

Pearson and Spearman Correlation
The Pearson correlation coefficient measures the linear relationship between two con-
tinuous variables, with values ranging from -1 to 1. A value of 1 indicates a perfect
positive linear relationship, -1 indicates a perfect negative linear relationship, and 0 in-
dicates no linear relationship. The Spearman rank correlation coefficient, on the other
hand, measures the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between two
variables, which can be non-linear. It also ranges from -1 to 1, with similar interpretations
to Pearson’s coefficient [17] [18].
Both Pearson and Spearman correlations indicate that previous delays (Delay-1 and
Delay-2) and the rolling mean are positively correlated with the current delay.

2.5
Methodology
As mentioned in Chapter 1 the delay of the chagring session is treated as a double task:
• The decision of users to delay their session to a later hour, approached as an
inference classification task: a session is classified as True (delayed) if the delay
duration exceeds 0.13 hours, and False (not delayed) if it is 0.13 hours or less, as
defined in Section 2.1.
• Modeling the duration of the delay, approached as a regression task. Once a
session is delayed, accurately modeling the duration of the delay before the power
start can help identify user behavior patterns and predict potential peak load hours.
After identifying the most important features for the model, two modeling techniques are
assessed and benchmark for each of the tasks:
• A single model for all users
• Personalized models for each user
A detailed depiction of the process flow is provided in Figure 2.11. A more detailed
explanation of the algorithm and the process is provided in the next sections (Section
2.5.1, 2.5.2, 2.5.3).

2.5.1
Algorithm Employed
To model delay of the power start in the charging session, XGBoost (Extreme Gradient
Boosting) is utilized. XGBoost is a highly efficient machine learning algorithm renowned
16
==PAGE:25==
for its robust performance in both classification and regression tasks. This algorithm
constructs an ensemble of decision trees sequentially, each new tree addressing the
errors of its predecessors and optimizing the loss function through gradient descent.
Key hyperparameters, including the number of trees, learning rate, and tree depth, were
fine-tuned to prevent overfitting [16, 19].
The advantages of XGBoost include:
• Minimizing the loss function by training each new tree to correct residual errors.
• Incorporating regularization parameters to prevent overfitting.
• Effectively handling sparse data.
• Providing valuable insights into model interpretability.
Hyperparameter tuning was performed using Optuna, a framework that employs ad-
vanced Bayesian optimization techniques to efficiently navigate the parameter range
[20]. Various combinations of parameters, including the number of estimators, learn-
ing rate, tree depth, subsample ratio, were iteratively tested. Each iteration involved a
five-fold cross-validation to ensure robust performance assessment [16].
The parameter search was strategically guided by the outcomes of previous iterations to
avoid overfitting and enhance the model’s generalizability. This adaptive tuning process
aimed to maximize performance.
2.5.2
Modeling Users Decision to Delay Charging (Classification)
As aforementioned, to model the decision to delay charging, two primary modeling ap-
proaches are considered:
• A single model that predicts delay behavior for all users.
• Personalized models tailored to each user’s specific behavior. As there is not
a strong correlation across the aggregated user data and the decision to delay
charging (Section 2.4), an individual modelling approach is employed and explored
to ascertain if stronger dependencies exist that could better predict delay decisions
within specific user subsets.
The impact of price predictors on the decision to delay charging sessions is also evalu-

For modeling the decision to delay charging, XGBoost Classifier algorithm is used as

analysis (Section 2.4), are employed. Given the non-linear relationships between the
features and the target variable, these features are further assessed through the feature
importance metrics provided by XGBoost. More specifically, during training, XGBoost
constructs multiple trees. At each split, it selects the feature that maximizes the gain,

which represents the improvement in accuracy. Once all trees are built, XGBoost ag-
gregates the gain values for each feature to determine the overall feature importance

algorithm’s performance.
For individual modeling the features assessed to be selected for modeling each of the
users decision to delay are the ones described in Section 2.3. A systematic exploration
of the average F1 and Accuracy scores across models was conducted to demonstrate
the impact of incrementally employing different numbers of features. This approach
assesses feature importance and and the optimal number of features based on the
models’ performance.
The process of selecting features comprises of the following steps:
• Initial Setup: The dataset is partitioned into training and validation sets (Section
2.5.1. The training set is utilized to train the model, while the validation set is
instrumental in evaluating the model’s performance to prevent overfitting.
• Incremental Addition: The selection begins with the most significant feature for
each user, identified using XGBoost’s feature importance metrics. The model is
then trained and evaluated on this feature. Subsequently, features are added one
at a time, in order of their importance, to the model configuration. After each addi-
tion, F1 scores and accuracy are recalculated to observe changes in performance.
• Performance Evaluation: Each incremental feature set undergoes re-training
and validation. Performance metrics for each configuration are derived through
cross-validation within the training set, ensuring that the model’s effectiveness is
reliably measured.

A rule-based benchmark was developed based on insights derived from the correlation

The model’s performance is quantitatively assessed using two key metrics: accuracy
and the F1 score. These metrics are chosen to provide a comprehensive overview of

• Definition of F1 Score: The F1 score is a harmonic mean of precision and recall,

classes are imbalanced. The F1 score is defined as:
F1 Score = 2 × Precision × Recall
Precision + Recall
Precision is the ratio of true positive predictions to the total predicted positives,
while recall is the ratio of true positives to the actual positives. This metric is crucial
for understanding the model’s accuracy concerning each class independently [24].

6.1
Conclusions
This study aims to investigate the behavior of home charging users in Denmark. First
the delay of the charging session is modeled, based on historical data of home charg-
ers provided by Spirii, focusing on the occurrence and duration of these delays using
XGBoost—a robust machine learning algorithm for both classification and regression
tasks. The modeling employs two approaches: a single model that predicts delay be-
havior across all users and personalized models that are tailored to the unique behav-
iors of individual users, thus addressing the variations in delay usage observed among
different user groups more effectively.
The classification of delayed sessions is based on whether users utilized the delay func-
tionality for the charging session. The single model achieves an accuracy and F1 score
of 0.83, identifying key predictors such as the rolling mean delay from previous ses-
sions and the sine transformation of the plug-in hour. Specifically, delays are more
likely to occur when the rolling delay mean exceeds 5 hours and during late afternoon
to evening hours (14:00 to 21:00). Comparatively, the benchmark outperforms the ma-
chine learning model in 48% of cases, showing zero difference in accuracy for another
10% of cases. When individual machine learning models are evaluated against the
benchmark, 68% are outperformed by the benchmark. Those models that outperform
the benchmark often exhibit more variation in features concerning past delays and the
specific times users charge their vehicles.
When price-related features are included, the model demonstrates an overall accuracy
and F1 score of 0.94, with individual user test scores consistently exceeding 0.9. As-
sessing the model’s interpretability, the probability of the session to be delay is higher
the late afternoon to evening hours (13:00-21:00), and when the energy used during
a session exceeds 20 kWh. Furthermore, pricing dynamics play a crucial role; prices
lower than 2DKK/kWh at the start of a session generally decrease the likelihood of a de-
lay, while prices higher than 2DKK/kWh increase it. Comparative evaluations with the
rule-based benchmark-based its prediction on previous delay decisions and the hour of
plug-in- show that the machine learning model outperforms the benchmark in 69% of
cases.
For modeling delay duration, initially a single-model approach is evaluated. However,
delay duration does not exhibit a collectively strong correlation with the features when
all users are considered. To capture the individual behavior of users, a personalized
modeling approach is applied. The model exhibits a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of
Investigating User Behavior and Preferences in Residential Electric Vehicle Charging

==PAGE:70==
1.3 hours and a coefficient of determination (R2) of 0.58, indicating rather poor perfor-
mance. The residual plot shows that the model tends to overestimate shorter delays
and underestimate longer ones, pointing to areas where model performance could be
improved. Analysis of the SHAP values reveals a significant dependence on the cosine
transformation of the session start time, with other features having minimal impact on
the predictions. Considering these, an individualized modeling approach was assessed
to better capture unique user patterns and enhance model performance.
Despite this tailored approach, the rule-based benchmark-which predicts delay duration
by subtracting the hour of the plug-in from the target of 01:00- performed better for 80%
of the users, suggesting its effectiveness in certain scenarios. Analysis of individual pat-
terns showed that models outperformed by benchmark follow a predictable delay pattern
based on the time of arrival, while models better captured by the machine learning algo-
rithm exhibit more variable behaviors. For predicting delay duration, individual models
are used when they outperform the benchmark, while the benchmark is preferred for
other users. These models generally provide accurate predictions when plug-in hour is
between 16:00 and 19:00, but they are less reliable for late night and early afternoon
sessions. From the Distribution System Operator’s (DSO) perspective, benchmark test-
ing for predicting delay duration across a winter and a summer month showed deviations
from the power start ranging from 1-2 hours. The errors were larger in the summer, with
predictions becoming more accurate in the late afternoon and evening hours.
To further investigate the behavior of home charging users in Denmark, this study an-
alyzes survey data to identify key factors that influence user charging habits and pref-
erences. The findings reveal that while 62.2% of users regularly check prices before
charging their vehicles, the willingness to postpone charging to another day due to high
prices varies. Most indicated that they would often (33%) or sometimes (26.3%) post-
pone charging, with 45% of those who always check prices willing to delay the session
for a day. Overall, there is a trend that indicates that although users frequently check
prices, they are less inclined to postpone charging by a day due to high prices, suggest-
ing a reluctance to delay charging too extensively.
Furthermore, 37.3% of users prefer third-party managed charging services, with 55.9%
opting to use them every time they charge. Cost minimization emerges as the main
motivation for choosing third-party managed charging, while reducing CO2 emissions is
less of a concern. Interestingly, users who prefer manual charging are more proactive
about checking prices and more willing to delay charging when prices are high. In con-
trast, those opting for third-party managed services, despite being less active in price
checking, prioritize cost efficiency, indicating a preference for a managed approach that
simplifies their decision-making process.
For users providing their charger ID, their responses were analyzed to study the correla-
tion with model performance. A comparative analysis of a model using price predictors
to classify delays revealed distinct user behaviors. Users modeled by machine learn-
ing often checked prices and delayed charging but rarely postponed charging beyond
62
==PAGE:71==
midnight. In contrast, those predicted by the rule-based model showed more varied
behaviors. The rule-based model, which emphasizes the plug-in hour, generally out-
performed the machine learning approach. Interestingly, users aligned with the bench-
mark model did not have a consistent after-midnight target to delay charging, displaying
varied behaviors regarding price checking and session timing.

Future Work
For future work, the following are suggested:
• Using the methodology presented in this study, the analysis could be repeated
with more recent data.
• Different machine learning algorithms could be evaluated.
• Focusing on the DSO side and exploring how the presented approach can be used

• Matching the EV charging data with the responses to create user behavioral mod-

4. Third-party managed charging preferences and motivations  
Third-party charging optimizes your charging process, by making sure that you charge in the cheapest 
or most environmental way possible. For example, when you plug in your electric car after work, it 
will not start charging until electricity prices are at their lowest, while still guaranteeing that your car 
is fully charged by the time you need it the next morning.
