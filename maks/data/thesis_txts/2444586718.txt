1 ABSTRACT 
Much focus has been on motorist and the use of mobile phones. But how about cyclists, how is their 
behavior with mobile phones in the traffic. The purpose of this study is to determine how often cyclists 
are using a mobile phone, and what they are using it for. Furthermore, a risk perception analysis has 
been applied to data sampled from a survey, containing both cyclist and non-cyclist participants. The 
analyses were done by Chi-Square Test, Latent Class Analysis, and Logistic Regression. The Chi-
Square test revealed some association between age, gender and distance when looking at using the 
mobile phone. The Latent Class Analysis compiled 4 clusters, one including cyclists involved in 
accidents. The Logistic Regression indicated that if you use the mobile phone in a specific method, 
the risk perception for that method is affected.

3 INTRODUCTION 
3.1 PURPOSE STATEMENT 
A frequently recurring campaign from the Danish Council of Safe Traffic, advices car drivers to stop 
using a mobile phone while driving cars, even with their latest campaign, they focus on car drivers 
and mobile phones. But is it only car drivers that uses a mobile phone, or does other road users use a 
mobile phone as well? The purpose of this study is to determine how much cyclists uses a mobile 
phone while riding a bike. Along with the use of mobile phones, risk perception will also be a focus 
point. 
When categorizing the possible method to use a mobile phone, there will be distinguished between 
two different methods. There is an active way of using a mobile phone, and a passive way to use a 
mobile phone. Both methods will be highlighted throughout the report and will be explained more 
detailed in section 4. 
This study has three major components to it. The first is the general use of mobile phones, how is the 
mobile phone usage among Danish cyclists, and is there any significant difference in the passive and 
active use amongst the different groups. The second part is self-reported risk perception. As well as 
the first part, this will be divided into passive and active mobile phones use. Both cyclists and non-
cyclists must report how much of a risk they perceive using a mobile phone may cause to the cyclists 
in general. Cyclists will be questioned to their own risk perception in different situation, such as bad 
weather and foggy days etc. as well as different infrastructural changes. The third and last part is 
regarding accidents. Have the participants been involved in an accident while riding a bike within the 
last two year, with the participants noting and reporting how much they felt the mobile phone had of 
effect on the accident. 
3.2 TRANSPORT HABITS 
Compared to other countries, in Denmark, bikes play a much bigger role as a transport mode. The 
average Dane between the age of 10-84 makes 0.46 bike trips per day, with an average distance of 
3.46km. The most common reasons for trips are for commuting (work and education) at 44% and 
leisure at 33%. Young adult between the age of 20-30 are the age groups most frequently biking, and 
the most frequent distance among all ages are trips below 5 kilometers. (Center for Transport 
Analytics, 2018). 
3.3 MOBILE PHONE USAGE 
It is a well-known fact, that doing other things while driving a car, such as using a mobile phones or 
texting etc., increases the risks of being involved in an accident, as a direct result of the distraction 
(J. M. Owens, 2018). It has been concluded that the prevalence of using mobile phones while driving 
a car impairs the drivers’ performances, and even using the hands-free option for phones, whether it’s 
through a cars Bluetooth system, or a headset, does not entirely adverse the effect of using the mobile 
phone while driving (K. Lipovac, 2017). 
By mid-2017, 88.7% of Danes owed a smart phone (DR (Danish Broadcasting Corporation), 2017). 
Within the ages of 16 to 44, 90-93% have access to a smart phone (Statistics Denmark, 2017). With

in 2010. Older users noted to use the phone for a better availability for their job or family. Whereas 
younger users did not have the same obligation to be available from either part. 
The two reports from the Netherlands, from 2010 and 2012, seems to have a quite lower number of 
cyclists using a mobile phone, whereas the Danish report from 2017 seems higher. Whether this is a 
cultural thing, or a general increase over the years, is unknown. 
3.4 RISK PERCEPTION AND ACCIDENTS 
The use of mobile phones or just listening to music through a mobile phone while riding a bike can 
be a potential risk factor. The 2.2% observed talking on the phone and the 0.6% overserved using the 
mobile phone to text in Groningen were using the mobile phone in two different way. Talking on the 
phone, whether it being handheld or handsfree, or listening to music may not require as much attention 
from the cyclists, because the cyclists are not required to look at the mobile phone constantly, whereas 
sending a text message may require much more attention. (A. Stelling-Konczak, 2017).  
The study concluded that risk perception was negatively related to cyclists listening to music, 
suggesting that cyclists with a higher risk perception were less prone to listening to music while 
biking. However, there was a positive relation between risk perceptions and risky cycling behavior. 
The survey also questioned how much the cyclists could hear while listening to music, and how much 
they felt were necessary, to be able to safely ride a bike. They concluded that roughly 90% of all age 
groups, indicated that a cyclist is required to hear all sounds in order to cycle safely. But only 66%-
81% reported that they were able to hear all the required sounds while they bike.  
With the increase in electrical vehicles, this might even be a much bigger problem. Electrical and 
hybrid cars are much quieter than regular cars, and this cause a  decrease in the possibility of cyclists 
detecting the vehicles, especially if they are distracted by music or using the mobile phones. The 
cyclists did however report that they almost never encountered the quieter electrical or hybrid cars. 
The study reported that the cyclists may be compensating for the loss of audio from the cars, when

cyclist think about the infrastructure, when evaluating how safe they feel, therefore the infrastructure 
will have an impact in the cyclists’ mobile phone usage. An accident may make cyclists to reevaluate 
their habits after the accident, and may therefore use a mobile phone less

Latent Class Clustering is known as a finite-mixture clustering and is used to identify any potential 
patterns on the cyclist’s behavior and their use of mobile phones. The advantages of using Latent 
Class Clustering for pattern identification is the ability to represent overlap across clusters and the 
ability to allow calculating cluster probabilities, and lastly, it provides goodness-of-fit criteria, making

impact a chosen dependent variable. The chosen regression model is Logistic regression, and it’s used

As mentioned, using a mobile phone are chosen to be separated into two parts, active and passive use. 
Active use is when the user is directly using the mobile phone. Active use can include sending texts, 
or maybe checking social media etc., so a use that require a direct use of hands. Passive use, on the 
other hand, does not require hand usage. This can include listening to music through a headset, or 
some might track their distance by using a fitness app, with the phone either in a pocket, or at least 
not with a direct hand usage of the phone.

4.3 ANALYSIS 
The data analysis is separated into three major subjects. The cyclists’ mobile phone usage, risk 
perception, and accident involvement. For all subjects, an overview is made, to present the data. For 
analyzing how the cyclists are using mobile phones, a descriptive analyze, using Chi-Square Test is 
performed, and a Latent Class Clustering is performed to identify pattern of those using a mobile 
phone. For risk perception analysis, Chi-Square Test is used to evaluate any associations between 
variables, and Logistic Regression is performed to examine which variable may affect the risk 
perception. Accident involvement will not have any analyzing method applied, but will be presented 
by an overview, to show if those involved in an accident felt phone may have been the cause, and 
how bad the injuries were. 
4.3.1 Descriptive statistic 
The independence between variables will be tested by a 2-test test for independence. The test is used 
to determine if there is a significant association between two categorical variables. This method is 
appropriate for this survey sample, because the data are random sampling through the questionnaire, 
and with an expected frequency count of at least five for each possible answer. 
The test of independence will be conducted on two mayor topics. Mobile phone usage of cyclists, and 
risk perception for both cyclists and non-cyclists. For the mobile phone usage, the variables will be 
divided into four different answer possibilities. 1 for those who aren’t using a phone, 2 for those only 
using a mobile phone passively, 3 for those only using a mobile phone actively and 4 for those using 
a mobile phone both actively and passively. Each of the test for risk perception will be conducted 
twice, one test for active use and one test for passive use. The categories will be the risk of doing a 
misdemeanor while using a mobile phone, the risk of getting in an accident while using a mobile 
phone, and lastly the risk of getting into different types of accident while using a mobile phone.

− Bike distance and mobile phone usage 
• Risk perception:

4.3.3 Regression model 
To evaluate if more than one variable affects the dependent variable, Logistic Regression is used. The 
Regression models are based upon the Clusters found, based on the data containing cyclists.  
Logistic Regression is applied when the dependent variable is binary, thus 0 or 1. The goal of the 
logistic regression is to determine the best fitting model, describing the relationship between the 
dependent variable (outcome) and the independent variable (explanatory).  
The Logistic Regression model is performed upon 6 dependent variables, 3 variable each with the 
option of active or passive use. “Risk of being involved in specific accident types”, “Risk of doing 
different misdemeanors”, and “Does the use of mobile phone increase the risk of being involved in 
an accident”. The two first variable have answer ranges from 1 to 5, depending on the “range” of risk 
perception. It is not possible to use logistic modelling for non-binary variables, and the variable will 
be adjusted accordingly. The variables will be converted to only consists of two variables, one for 
low risk, and one for medium to high risk. “Medium”, “High risk”, and “Very high risk” is marked 
as 1, and “Very low risk” and “Low risk” will be marked as 2.

5 RESULTS 
At first the survey respondent will be presented and verified, to evaluate accuracy. There will be 
shown different groups, such as age groups and genders, to see how the use of mobile phone is 
between the groups. After the presentation of the data, the results of the analysis will be present. 
Analyzing the data consists of standard descriptive statistic, and the methods presented in section 4.3. 
The three different method used to analyze the data, are used on different aspects of the data. For 
analyzing the use of mobile phones, the Chi-Square Test is used to find relationships between age, 
gender and distance with the use of mobile phones. For analyzing the risk perception, the Chi-Square 
Test are performed on age groups, and different risk perceptions. Latent Cluster Analysis is used to 
identify different patterns of cyclists. This include both using a mobile phone, actively or passively, 
accident involvement, and different types of routes along with other factors. Chi-Square test is used 
to analyze the association between age and risk perceptions, gender and risk perceptions, and using a 
bike or not and risk perceptions. The Logistic Regression Analysis is used to evaluate which variables 
have an impact the risk perception of the participants. Whether using a mobile phone, the distance 
driven, or other variables affect the risk perception will be evaluated.

6 CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION 
With 94% of Danish citizens being online daily (Statistics Denmark, 2017) an online survey was the 
best method to distribute the survey. The survey was not representative enough, due to the low number 
of participants. Comments about this can be seen in section 6.1. 
The Chi-Square Test showed a relationship between the use of mobile phones and age, gender and 
distance, the test does however not indicate what kind of relationship. This is confirmed by Figure 
5.5, showing a large percentage using a mobile phone under the age of 25, and then decreasing for 
each older age group. Gender and distance also showed an association, as well with age, it shows that 
the more weekly kilometer the cyclists are driving, the less likely they are to use a mobile phone, 
seeing how gradually the use decreased, the longer the distance became. This may indicate that for 
shorter runs, cyclist is using the phone, because it is a short trip. Almost half of those biking below 
40 km on a normal week, reported to use the mobile phone actively, which is quite a lot, and it fell to 
almost every third person between 41 and 80 km. It may be that the majority of these cyclists only 
bike below 5 km, which the most common distance reported in TU-data, therefore with more data, it 
should be possible to distinguish between the different distance further, and see if there is one specific 
distance, that makes the different between using a mobile phone or not.

Cluster group C1 consisted of those cyclists that had been involved in an accident. The group uses 
the mobile phone passively, and not actively, and were the only group containing those using the 
phone as habit. They also used the mobile phone for fun. They do not stop or slow down if they are 
required to use the mobile phone while on the trip, some did however note to pull the bike, which 
could indicate that they were in a hurry, since stopping is not an option. The group consists of both 
male and female between 25 and 35, driving below 40 kilometers, and were of those rarely driving a 
car. Whether stress could be the cause of the accidents were not examined. 
14 reported to use a mobile phone, and 3 of those reported they felt the phone was a direct cause of 
it. However, those feeling the phone was the impact, did not receive any severe injury, which could 
help to indicate that if cyclists are using a mobile phone, they do in fact change they behavior on the 
roads, as mention by (C. Goldenbeld, 2012). There is not enough data regarding accidents from this 
survey, to make accurate conclusions about mobile phone use and accident. 
The logistic regressions models showed mixed results. Sometimes using a mobile phone had a 
positive affect on the risk perception, other times it had a negative affect on the risk perception. Even 
accident involvement had mixed relationship. Accident involvement resulted in a lower risk 
perception in 3 models. This may be caused because most participants had not been involved in an 
accident. This may require a separate analysis, only containing cyclists involved in an accident, to 
not have any affect by other cyclists. 
Cyclists’ risk perception was not affected by if they use car of public transport, however, this were a 
survey marked at cyclists. There were some car users in the survey. The analysis was based on the 
cyclists, and therefore the car drivers risk perception was not included in the logistic regression, 
because a lot of question from the survey, used in the models, were skipped by non-cyclists. 
The Chi-Square and the overviews indicated some association between using a mobile phone and 
age, gender and distance. Percent wise, the older the participants, the less they used a phone, likewise, 
the longer the weekly average trip were, the less they used a mobile phone. 
Only 3 out of all cyclists participating in the survey reported that the mobile phone was the cause of 
the accident, out of the 14 using a mobile phone before the accident. The report by (D. De Waard, 
2010) indicated that cyclists adjust their cycle behavior dependent if they use the mobile phone or 
not, may prove current. The group from the cluster groups, containing cyclists involved in accident, 
also noted not to use the mobile phone active, indicating that they may have been talking on the 
phone, and listening to music before the accident, and may have overheard the car or just fell. This 
can give an indication that when cyclists use the mobile phone actively, they account for the 
surroundings, i.e. driving slower.
