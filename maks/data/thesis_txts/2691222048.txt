Abstract
Within medical applications magnetic resonance imaging is at the forefront of technological
development providing high resolution pictures of the human anatomy with a non-invasive pro-
cedure. Unfortunately, these procedures can take upwards of an hour to perform and due to
the minute signals being detected, equipment must be properly calibrated. Therefore, it is of
interest to combine detection coils for several isotopes decreasing procedure time and calibration
error. This project aims to develop and test a broadband low noise amplifier, which covers 13C,
23Na and 129Xe frequencies. The core amplifier is based on an existing narrowband design from
Technical University of Denmark and was updated with a novel five-element broadband noise
matching circuit. A balun was built to stop common mode current in the coaxial transmission
line and metallic covers were designed to reduce interfering radiation between the components
and scanner such that signal integrity was not compromised. Key performance improvements
including noise figure, decoupling and signal-to-noise ratio were compared against the existing
narrowband design, serving as a baseline measurement. The results showed that the broadband
amplifier core simultaneously had consistent signal-to-noise ratio at the frequency span of inter-
est and better decoupling, demonstrating that the unit could be implemented for detection of
13C, 23Na and 129Xe isotopes.

Introduction
1.1
Historical Context
The development of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in a clinical setting has been the result
of scientific achievements throughout several decades. The first related discovery was made by
Wolfgang Pauli in 1924, where he suggested the possibility of an intrinsic nuclear spin. This
concept of nuclear spin was studied for decades, mainly in Europe and the USA, which eventually
led to the discovery of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR).
Transistor technology was rapidly developing during the 1970s, yielding enough computation
power to process the data of NMR experiments and creating images of the results. The first
successful NMR images of in vivo human anatomy were published in 1977 and clinical trails
were evaluated in the late 1980s [1].
The clinical adaptation of NMR is in form of MRI where atomic nuclei is scanned in adjacent
sections to form a 2D image. The combination of increased computational power and lower cost
has resulted in OECD countries greatly adopting MRI examinations [2].
1.2
MRI Basics
While this project focuses on the design and implementation of select radio frequency (RF)
hardware, the basic theoretical concepts of NMR will be presented. This will allow the reader
to become familiar with the motivation for this the project.
Every nucleus, and thereby every element, has an intrinsic nuclear spin. This is a quantum
phenomenon where the nucleus rotates around its own axis.
From elementary physics it is
known that a moving electrically charged object induces a magnetic field according to the right
hand rule [3]. As an example one can look at 1H, that is a hydrogen atom with a single proton
located in its nucleus. Due to the positive electric charge, a polarity is associated with the atom
similar to a magnetic bar with a north and south pole as shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Polarity and intrinsic spin of 1H
The spin of said atom creates a small magnetic field with a magnetic momentum µ that is
strongly correlated with the amount of protons and neutrons inside the nucleus. As the atom is
spinning around its own axis, there is also an angular momentum ρ associated with the motion.
The magnetic momentum and angular momentum are linked through the gyromagnetic ratio
γ, described by (1.1). This is a constant characteristic that depends on the composition of the
nucleus [4].

Generally atoms in the human body are randomly oriented causing the magnetic momentum to
cancel on a macroscopic scale. If an electrically charged nucleus is subjected to an external static
magnetic field with intensity (B0), it will align itself with said field. In modern MRI scanners,
this magnetic field is induced through metal conductors that are cooled down to approximately
4 K, a temperature near absolute zero. This reduces the resistance within the conductors and
the assembly becomes a superconducting magnet when high currents are used [4]. The majority
of atoms will align themselves with B0 in a low energy state, while few atoms will be rotated
180◦relative to B0 into a high energy state as shown in Figure 1.2. Overall the majority of low
energy atoms creates a net magnetization M which can be observed.
Figure 1.2: External magnetic field and aligned atoms
It is of paramount importance that a uniform magnetic field is created throughout the measure-
ment area. To achieve this uniformity gradient coils are used to create a magnetic field that can
be varied across three orthogonal directions. This allows the MRI scanner to calibrate the static
magnetic field spatially, creating a uniform magnetic field that only varies a few mT within 20
cm. The scanner to be used in this project operates at 3 T. This is equivalent to approximately
60000 times the earth’s average magnetic field [5].
When a group of atoms are subjected to a static magnetic field, they will begin to spin in a
manner known as precession. This movement is similar to that of a spinning top that is offset
relative to the static magnetic field. The rate of precession motion is referred to as the Larmor
frequency fL and can be calculated through Larmor’s Law as shown in (1.2). This law describes
the relationship between the magnetic and mechanical properties of the isotope [4].
fL = γB0
(1.2)
As all charged nuclei will be aligned with B0, it is not possible to measure the voluminous density
of individual isotopes such as 1H. Therefore, the aligned nuclei are excited with a second magnetic
pulse B1 that is perpendicular to B0 where the frequency of B1 is determined through Larmor’s
Law.
Some of the RF radiation will be absorbed by the low power level nuclei, essentially
disturbing the protons, leading them to flip to the high power state or changing phase of the
precession. This change in phase or switch of power state can be seen as a resonance, due to the
disturbance at a single frequency and hence the naming NMR and MRI [5]. The main interest
of this project lies in detecting 13C, 23Na and 129Xe isotopes. The Larmor frequency for said
isotopes are given in Table 1.1 when using a uniform magnetic field strength of 3 T as B0 [6].

A large portion of the human body consists of water and fat, key binding
agents for 1H [4]. Radiation from this isotope will thus be present during imaging and require
special attention to mitigate its detrimental effects toward signal integrity from the three former
isotopes.
Select elements can be pushed perpendicularly using B1 and will become aligned at a so-called
flip angle α relative to the static magnetic field B0, shown in Figure 1.3. Note that in order
for the isotope to physically shift from the uniform magnetic field, it must be exposed to its
defined Larmor frequency. Thus isotopes that are subjected to RF energy outside their respective
resonance frequency will not be impacted, allowing one to target specific substances in the body.
Once the magnetic field B1 is switched off, the process is reversed and the shifted isotopes return
to the parallel position while emitting RF energy at their respective Larmor frequency.
Figure 1.3: Nuclei at flip angle α relative to B0
The time it takes for the flip-angle magnetic moments to realign with B0 is also known as the
spin-lattice relaxation, commonly defined as T1. However, as the nuclei are rotating, the flip-
angle magnetic moments will also begin to rotate around the nuclei creating a phase deviation
relative to the initial alignment when B1 was initiated. This loss of phase coherence is known as
the spin-spin relaxation and defined as T2. A overview depicting the visual differences is shown
in Figure 1.4.

There are many parameters influencing the measurements of these magnetic moments, and
thereby the creation of MRI images. The effects of T1 are much easier to measure than T2,
as the spin-lattice relaxation is a direct consequence of applying the perpendicular field B1,
causing the protons to exchange energy with their surroundings. Therefore T1 moments are
most commonly measured [4].
1.3
Objectives
The motivation of this project stems from optimizing the MRI process, both for the patient and
hospital staff. A broadband detector array will be designed and implemented, such that the
isotopes 13C, 23Na and 129Xe can be measured with a single device. This will increase comfort
for the user, as a single detection device can be used to scan for several isotopes simultaneously
reducing the procedure time. From a healthcare perspective, procedural equipment and clutter
is reduced and the chance of applying the incorrect detective device is completely abolished.
The design and implementation will be executed in several stages, with the first stage of this
project focusing on developing a stable low noise amplifier (LNA), based on previous research
conducted at DTU. This includes developing a proper output matching network, a noise match-

familiar with the tools used for the project, including software, hardware and laboratory dex-

constructed, serving as a magnetic dipole antenna for sensing the desired isotopes.
A trap

Upon arrival of the second PCB, the board will be assembled, tested and debugged. Detailed
measurements of the units noise, S-parameters and spectrum analysis will be conducted to
compare the practical and simulated results. The antenna, enclosure and balun assemblies will
also be attached and tested as a final product.

Low Noise Amplifier
The RF signals being detected in an MRI scanner are extremely weak, typically in the order of
a few mV [7], and therefore prone to degradation if used with a standard amplifier that is power
matched to a detector coil. Therefore, this project will utilize a LNA that is noise matched to
the detector coil. While this design does not only provide maximum amplification of the source
signal, it also limits the amount of noise introduced in the amplification stage as a whole.

2.4
Active Decoupling
When multiple coils are present in the vicinity of each other they will mutually couple through
overlapping magnetic fields.
This can essentially be seen as crosstalk between the devices,
changing the impedance of the individual coil. This becomes a major issue, as the Q-factor and
thus signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) will decrease when the detection coil is no longer matched to
the LNA [15].
This effect can be mitigated by connecting the coil to a high impedance, absorbing most current
induced through mutual coupling. By shutting down all but one coil, the array temporarily
behaves as a single device with its original coil impedance Zcoil [16]. Spatial data can then be
collected by sequentially measuring the RF signals of each coil in quick succession whilst the
remaining coils are presented with high impedance.
One way to decouple the coils is by designing a resonance circuit that can actively be switched
on and off. By using the existing MRI interface cable as a DC switch and implementing the
resonance circuit on the LNA PCB, spatial constraints are kept to a minimum. An additional
DC trace was routed from the output port to the matching circuit, so that a diode could be used
to switch the resonance circuit on and off as required. The diode of type MA4P11250NM from
MACOM has previously been used and was therefore also implemented for this design. This is
a diode that can handle up to 1 kW of continuous wave power while having very low resistance
of 0.8 Ωwhen being forward biased [17].
Several circuit topologies were explored to form the active decoupling network, where having
a minimum number of components were of high importance to reduce noise. An overarching
element in this design phase was the DC trace from the LNA output. 18 µH inductors were used
to block the RF signal from feeding back into the amplifier input. A low pass filter consisting
of two parallel capacitors (470 pF and 220 nF) and the aforementioned inductor were used to
remove the remaining RF signal from the feedback loop.
The matching network was first modified by creating a resonance circuit for the two capacitors.
The two capacitors are in parallel and thus their values can be simplified by summing their
capacitance requiring an inductor of 296.6 nH. Furthermore, a 10 nF capacitor was used as
a DC block when activating the resonance circuit ensuring that the supplied voltage forward
biases the diode. A second resonance circuit was also created for the matching circuit inductor,
by placing a 8.29 pF capacitor in parallel forming the layout shown in Figure 2.6.

Balun
Interfacing the LNA and MRI scanner will happen through a coaxial cable, which has an inner
and outer conductor, separated by a concentric insulator. This is an inherently unbalanced line
which is designed to have its outer conductor connected to ground. In this instance, the coaxial
cable will be used for bidirectional communication and therefore the outer conductor cannot be
guaranteed to continuously act as perfect ground connection.
The two coaxial conductors are not coupled to the LNA in the same manner, resulting in an
unbalanced current flow. The issue lies with the outer conductor, where the current on the
inside and outside part of this conductor are different. If the conductor was perfectly balanced
the currents I1 and I2 would be of equal magnitude and opposite direction as there is a single
load ZL. However, part of I2 also makes its way on the outermost conductor leading to a net
current I3 flowing to ground. By inspecting the electrical diagram in Figure 3.1 it is evident
that I3 can be reduced by increasing the impedance of Zg thus creating a balanced line where
the net current is zero [23]. A coaxial line is optimized for signal transmission making Zg very
small when the cable is used in its original form.
Figure 3.1: Diagram of a coaxial conductor and corresponding electrical diagram
The coaxial cable is also subject to the 3 T magnetic field when the MRI scanner is in operation.
A common-mode current will then be injected into the coaxial cable essentially creating an
additional radiating element. This new coaxial antenna will mutually couple with the existing
measuring coils modifying their impedance. As a result the antenna will no longer be noise
matched with the LNA potentially lowering the SNR to immeasurable levels. Furthermore, the
common-mode currents also generate heat in the coaxial cable, creating a safety hazard that
could burn the patient [24]. Fortunately, such common-mode currents can also be mitigated by
increasing Zg.
These two sources of unbalanced current can be mitigated by using a device called a balun,
derived from the name balance to unbalance, which essentially creates a high impedance Zg on
the outer conductor of the coaxial cable. As the induced current is a product of the Larmor
frequency within the MRI scanner, this impedance can be maximized by designing a resonator
for said frequency. Bazooka baluns are commonly used on transmission lines where a quarter
wavelength hollow cylinder is attached to the conductor, creating a large impedance for the
selected wavelength [23]. This becomes an issue for this particular design, as the Larmor fre-
quency for the targeted isotopes are centered around 33.7 MHz, yielding a quarter wavelength
of 2.3 m. It is simply not feasible to attach such a long tube in a clinical setting, thus requiring
another approach. Furthermore, the balun will also be required to attenuate 1H frequency at
127.7 MHz, requiring a second resonance circuit.

Broadband Low Noise Amplifier
Once the parasitic impedance of the first LNA PCB design was modelled in detail, attention was
focused on designing a broadband filter to substitute the three-element filter. The fundamental
intent of this project is to design a filter that simultaneously can encompass the Larmor frequency
of 1H, 23Na and 129Xe i.e. the amplifier must be noise matched to all three substances without
sacrificing decoupling eﬀiciency nor NF.
5.1
Systematic Approach
At first a standard set of filters were explored using the ADS filter design tool, to get acquainted
with the concept of broadband filtering, with potential candidates being based on a Maximally
Flat, Chebyshev or Bessel-Thomson designs. The major setback was the ADS only supports
filter design using 50 Ωterminations, requiring separate matching networks. This initial de-
sign attempt required 14 lumped components and the procedure was abandoned due to sizing
constraints and deficient performance in terms of NF and forward gain.
The main problem of the ADS filter design tool was discovered to be its prioritization of gain
in the overall design having the design power matched to each termination. This is contrast
to a noise matched LNA, where minimum NF and maximum output power can be achieved at
the expense of input and output matching, respectively [33]. The notion of noise broadband
matching an LNA had been attempted previously, albeit with a purely resistive source [33].

5.2
Expanding The Three Element Network
Other scientific works were studied to gain a better understanding of how a broadband response
can be achieved. One work successfully implemented a broadband noise matching filter using a
circuit that approximately imitated a negative capacitor [34]. This was required to match the
varying Zopt of the transistor across the frequency range of interest. As a negative capacitor
does not exist, the equivalent response was obtained by creating a passive resonance network.
The lossy resonant circuit can thus imitate the behavior of a negative capacitor around the
resonance frequency [34].
The notion of applying an additional resonance circuit to the three-element network was explored
by adding an additional pole in the filter, essentially expanding the noise matched spectrum. A
capacitor and inductor was connected in series, and the original values from the three element
circuit was used as a reference point. Initial values for the new components C3 and L2 were
defined as 68 pF and 2.2 µH, respectively, as seen in Figure 5.5.
Figure 5.5: Initial five-element broadband filter
The circuit was first calibrated using the tuning utility and then the optimization tool was used
to further refine the component values. The primary goal was again to achieve low NF, high
decoupling and low output noise voltage across the desired frequency range and this is what the
filter was tuned for. Goals akin to Figure 5.3 were setup and the below matching filter could be
approximated using real components. From here on, this filter will be referred to as Broadband
I.

5.5
Broadband PCB Design
During the testing and ADS Momentum simulation of the previous generation PCB, much
experience and knowledge was gained for the production of the second generation unit. It was
evident that the previous unit was unstable and several improvements were made to stabilize
the new broadband LNA PCB.
Before the actual design began, various sources, how-to guides and articles were studied and it
was clear that several improvements could be made. Firstly, the RF signal path was placed in
a straight line, such that the distance from the input to the output was significantly reduced.
This reduces the signal loss due to the short distance and simultaneously avoids any potential
reflections when making sharp turns. The input and output connections were also placed on
separate ends of the PCB to minimize crosstalk between the connections [35].
Secondly, traces on the PCB were updated. The width of the trace was increased from 0.2
mm to 0.3 mm. Having a wider trace simultaneously reduces the chance of error in production
due to higher tolerances and provides a better signal path. When making turns with a trace,
several corner types were also defined. A curved type with radius of 1 mm was chosen to avoid
reflections reflections within the trace [36]. This was in some cases challenging to fit on the PCB
and a mitered corner type was therefore used in tight areas. The via terminations were also
modified with a teardrop shaped trace also providing a less resistive signal path. An overview
of these modifications are shown in Figure 5.12.
Figure 5.12: Trace modifications

Discussion and Future Work
A novel implementation of a broadband LNA has been simulated and manufactured yielding bet-
ter decoupling, NF and NV than its narrowband counterpart at 13C, 23Na and 129Xe frequencies.
Previous works in this project evolution had also called for cable traps and enclosures to protect
the amplifier from excess RF emissions. These devices were also simulated, manufactured and
tested producing a device that is one step closer to clinical implementation.
The narrowband LNA PCB was primarily used to get the author acquainted with the laboratory
tools and equipment. This amplifier iteration consistently failed stability tests, due to inductor
coupling on the input matching network, but it was a great exercise in debugging circuits and
gaining laboratory dexterity. This work resulted in numerous updates to the PCB, including
a straight line path for the RF signal, compartmentalizing circuitry into sections and layers,
having nearby inductors at 90◦angles and updating all trace corners. ADS Momentum was
also used to simulate the PCB traces with parasitic coupling, such that a more realistic model
was created. Unfortunately this aspect took much longer than expected and was worsened by a
shipment delay of more than a month.
Measurements on the second LNA involving noise, including SNR, NF and NV, were in disagree-
ment and also the main cause for circuit tuning, hence the many PCB iterations. The culprit
of these deviations was the noise parameters supplied by Skyworks as they were defined in a
frequency range of 400 MHz to 2300 MHz, requiring ADS to extrapolate down to the region of
33.7 MHz, creating a high degree of uncertainty in the simulation results as noise parameters
are otherwise defined in 50 MHz increments. The final iteration of broadband LNA, namely V4,
was tuned such that a compromise was made between the noise parameters. NV was exception-
ally low at 3.55 µV in the region of interest. NF had its minimum of 2.73 dB at 31.75 MHz
and gradually increased up to 4.92 dB at 35.3 dB. SNR was measured and compared against a
reference coil. While being significantly lower at 10 dB, the response is uniform between 32.1
MHz and 35.3 MHz and consolidated measurements can thus be made for 13C, 23Na and 129Xe
isotopes.
Common mode currents had been an issue in former projects and the concept of a floating balun
was explored to mitigate this effect. It is a flexible design that can be mounted on several cables
while being easy to manufacture. Modelling the device as a series resonator provided excellent
attenuation of 14 dB at 33.7 MHz, but in practice the device was unable to perform at these
low frequencies. The two axial copper plates were too sensitive for proper positioning on the
coaxial cable and the 1.04 dB of practical attenuation could be cancelled by simply rotating the
balun. The results from this work has shown that a floating balun is of no practical use at 33.7
MHz, and the authors recommendation is to use another device for common mode suppression.
A more traditional twin pole air solenoid balun was therefore constructed and provided excellent
practical attenuation of 20.0 dB and 23.2 dB at 33.7 MHz and 127.8 MHz, respectively. A
copper shield was also printed for the balun and showed no difference in performance in this
enclosure relative to open air performance. With enclosure dimensions of 22 × 33 × 18 mm it
is both small enough for clinical use but also sturdy enough to be handled without sacrificing
performance. Improvement can be made to the assembly process with a future edition having
a refined anchoring mechanism for the coaxial cable. Currently, the cable is glued to the PCB
and its shape is held in place by a plastic shroud. While the current design functions well, it
had numerous technological steps that could be reduced in a future revision.

Conclusion
In this project, a novel implementation of a broadband low noise amplifiers was constructed for
magnetic resonance imaging purposes. The unconditional stable device was was tuned for the
Larmor frequencies of the isotopes 13C, 23Na and 129Xe being in the frequency range of 32.125
MHz to 35.331 MHz. For proper signal processing within the scanner, the frequency response
was tuned to be approximately equal for all three isotopes at the expense of overall gain. As
a result the device yields 11.33 dB of gain at 33.7 MHz, but this is accomplished with a noise
factor of 1.25 dB, decoupling of 41.62 dB and a signal-to-noise ratio of 44.74 dB. All of the
performance indicators have been improved relative to a narrowband design, which served as a
baseline of performance. Furthermore, the optimal spacing between an array of antennas was
determined to 12.5 % overlap of the antenna diameter, in this case equating to 1 cm.
The measuring coil itself was a flexible loop with diameter of 8 cm. A trap circuit was constructed
to attenuate 1H at 127.8 MHz. Due to the abundance of 1H in the body and thereby spectral
contents for this frequency, a two metallic covers was designed for the trap circuit. A 3D plastic
cover dipped in copper paint provided excellent radio frequency blocking capability, with no
signal being detectable. A copper infused plastic was also tested with attenuation of 7.49 dB,
whereas a pure plastic shield attenuated the signal by 3.23 dB. The choice of cover is up to the
end user, but both solutions provide an anatomically fit design for patient comfort due to their
small size.
Common mode currents in the coaxial transmission cable essentially act as unwanted antennas,
changing the impedance of the measuring coils. There were suppressed by baluns, essentially a
type of trap circuit. A floating balun was explored due to its flexible installation, but proved
to be ineﬀicient at the required frequency range. A twin pole air solenoid balun was therefore
implemented, yielding attenuation of 20.0 dB and 23.2 dB at 33.7 MHz and 127.8 MHz, re-
spectively. A copper infused plastic cover was also constructed for the balun, with test results
showing no change in performance.
It can be concluded that the primary goal of designing a broadband low noise amplifier has been
achieved, providing uniform low noise amplification across the three isotopes of interest. Excess
noise sources have been mitigated in form of baluns and metallic covers, while conforming to
patient anatomy. Unfortunately, the array implementation could not be fully explored both in
the lab and at Aarhus University Hospital, due to stability issues when the whole amplifier was
assembled i.e. including the active decoupling network and voltage regulation circuit. Future
development would focus on improving amplifier layout for stability such MRI for several isotopes
can be consolidated into a single procedure. This would simultaneously reduce scan time for the
patient and provide healthcare professionals with more precise equipment.

Design and implementation of a broadband detector array for MRI
==PAGE:69==
MRI imaging of low abundance isotopes, such as carbon 13C and xenon 129Xe requires novel RF coil
detectors for eﬀicient signal utilization. Flip angle calibration of MRI, which is diﬀicult to achieve
at the 13C and 129Xe frequencies, can be accurately performed at the sodium 23Na frequency using
the natural abundance signal that exists in any living tissue. The purpose of this project is that
to design, construct and test a broadband MRI detector array, which covers 13C, 129Xe and 23Na
frequencies.
