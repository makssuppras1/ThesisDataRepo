Abstract 
 
This study researches the understanding and implementation of corporate sustainability (CS) practices 
within engineering consultancies in the architecture, engineering and construction (AEC) industry. 
While sustainability has become a focus point within industries today, particular challenges face the 
AEC industry as it is largely project-based. Intricate stakeholder relationships and traditional project 
outcomes, such as time and budgets, dictate most projects, complicating sustainability integration. 
Through semi-structured interviews with professionals working in engineering consultancies and in the 
sustainability field, the thesis adopts a qualitative research approach. In addition to the empirical data, 
three theoretical frameworks support the research and analysis, namely stakeholder theory, principal-
agent theory and creating shared value. The analysis reveals challenging sustainability integration gaps 
between strategy and projects with its implementation relying heavily on clients and personal 
motivation. Key findings highlight diverging incentives, misaligned coordination efforts, information 
asymmetries and differing stakeholder priorities. The study underscores the importance of clearer 
coordination efforts, improved accountability strategies and increased stakeholder engagement to 
promote corporate sustainability across organisations. It therefore provides theoretical and empirical 
insights to sustainability strategies in engineering consultancies.

1 Introduction 
 
With increased pressures in the AEC industry to improve practices towards environmental, social and 
economic goals, sustainability has become a significant challenge for engineering consulting companies 
operating in the industry. Although notions of sustainable construction are recognised and practiced, 
limited understanding remains around the implementation of corporate sustainability practices in 
engineering consultancies operating in the industry. This chapter presents the underlying drivers of the 
AEC industry and the importance of sustainability for the industry, outlining the theoretical frameworks 
and introducing the research aims and questions that guide this thesis. 
 
1.1 The AEC industry  
 
The architecture, engineering and construction (AEC) industry plays an important role in shaping 
today’s built environment. It is currently one of the largest industrial sectors globally, and encompasses 
the design, planning, and construction of buildings, infrastructure and transportation facilities (Jiang & 
Wong, 2016). The industry is also integral to countries’ economic development and society’s wellbeing, 
supporting habitation and social activities (Jiang & Wong, 2016). Projects range in scale and nature and 
include residential and commercial buildings and large-scale infrastructure such as bridges, highways 
and technical facilities, playing to the industry’s significant economic and social influence. 
 
The AEC industry is largely project-based, operating in a multidisciplinary environment with various 
stakeholders and activities (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). Projects are driven by budgets, set of objectives, 
such as work schedules and scopes, differing from the stakeholders involved. They also require careful 
navigation between economic landscapes, client expectations and organisational structures, further 
enabling risk and uncertainty. Large project-based organisations within the industry, such as 
engineering consulting companies, play a significant role by offering their expertise and mediating 
between the owners of projects and suppliers who deliver projects (Sergeeva & Kapetanaki, 2022a). In 
this case, consulting companies are largely concerned with meeting company objectives and mission, 
maximising profits, meeting stakeholder and client needs, maintaining market position or entering new 
markets (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017).  
 
1.2 Sustainability in the AEC industry 
 
As defined by Brundtland’s report, sustainable development is described as “development that meets 
the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs” 
(Montiel & Delgado-Ceballos, 2014). Within the AEC industry, sustainability has become an important 
consideration, largely driven by the increasing awareness of environmental, social and economic 
impacts associated with the industry. A shift in expectations in the industry has changed how 
construction projects are evaluated. Traditional measures of success such as cost, time and quality are 
increasingly being outweighed by the need for projects to be delivered with minimal negative impact 
on the current and future environment (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). The notion of sustainable construction 
has become an important topic, emphasising the efficient use of resources throughout the whole project 
lifecycle, including planning and design, construction, operation and maintenance, and demolition. 
Sustainable construction aims to utilise construction methods in ways that minimise environmental 
impact, enhance human wellbeing and reinforce economic stability (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017).  
 
==PAGE:8==
 
8 
Environmental considerations in sustainable construction seek to optimise resource consumption, 
minimise waste and pollution and protect ecosystems. These considerations include carefully choosing 
project sites and construction materials, utilising energy and water efficiently and minimising pollution. 
Social considerations focus on enhancing quality of life, promoting accessibility and ensuring health, 
safety and wellbeing. In sustainable construction, this includes aspects such as, community engagement, 
employee health and safety and cultural preservation (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). Economically, 
sustainable construction involves addressing construction and operational costs, maintaining 
competitiveness and ensuring value for money. Economic considerations also encompass investments 
into innovation and research, supporting local economies and improving profitability sustainably (Oke 
& Aigbavboa, 2017). As such, the growing significance of sustainable construction echoes the wider 
industry, recognising that the built environment has an essential role in promoting sustainable 
development. As a result, construction projects need to increasingly be designed and delivered with 
wider sustainability considerations in mind. 
 
1.3 Defining corporate sustainability 
 
The definition of corporate sustainability (CS) is closely linked to Brundtland Report’s definition of 
sustainable development, defining it as development that meets present needs without compromising 
the capacity of future generations to meet their own (Montiel & Delgado-Ceballos, 2014). As outlined 
previously, sustainability has shifted from strictly environmental considerations to also include social 
and economic dimensions. This shift in perspectives has broadened the responsibilities of corporations 
in modern society. Consequently, corporate sustainability has developed into a management approach 
that requires corporations to consider economic growth while balancing short and long term social and 
environmental impacts (Dyllick & Hockerts, 2002).  
 
CS aims to incorporate sustainability into core business models, often leading to shifts in corporate 
governance, stakeholder relations and performance (Lozano et al., 2015). Various theories such as 
stakeholder theory and institutional theory aid in explaining why firms embed CS strategies. These 
theories underline the importance of stakeholder demands, regulatory changes and societal pushes in 
defining sustainability for companies. Although CS definitions vary, the common scope underlines the 
need for businesses to consider their economic, social and environmental capital. As the AEC industry 
is largely project-based and resource-intensive, CS becomes a crucial challenge to address. Therefore, 
this thesis aims to explore how corporate sustainability is addressed and implemented in engineering 
consultancies and how companies attempt to meet sustainability goals while balancing project and 
stakeholder requirements.  
 
1.4 Thesis introduction and problem statement 
 
As sustainable development becomes an increasingly important topic in current discourse, the AEC 
industry faces pressure to adapt and change. Characterised by short-term project goals and resource 
intensive methods, concepts such as green building materials and circular construction have gained 
interest. Although sustainability efforts within projects continues to be addressed, corporate 
sustainability perceives less attention in how it is understood, implemented and structured within 
organisations delivering these projects, particularly in engineering consultancies. 
 
In the current context, sustainability in the AEC industry is narrowly focused on measurable 
environmental outcomes (Lu & Zhang, 2016). However, corporate sustainability navigates larger 
transformation within an organisation, requiring internal shifts in governance, operations and 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
stakeholder management. Thus, engineering consultancies face a complex challenge in structuring 
sustainability at both corporate and project level which is often client-driven.  
 
Therefore, this thesis aims to identify and respond to this research gap. While previous literature has 
researched sustainability outcomes in construction projects (Lu & Zhang, 2016), a limited number of 
studies have focused on how dynamics between project operations and strategy influence sustainability 
implementation within engineering consultancies. Additionally, the stakeholder dynamics in the 
industry dictate power dynamics and drive incentives which remains relatively underexplored. 
 
Consequently, there is a need for qualitative research embedded in theory to explore how engineering 
consultancies address sustainability. This thesis aims to research how sustainability is understood in 
organisational strategies and projects and how stakeholder relations are navigated. In order to address 
this, the thesis utilises three theoretical frameworks, namely stakeholder theory, principal-agent theory 
and creating shared value. Stakeholder theory is useful to understand how consultancies manage and 
meet varying stakeholder needs when meeting sustainability requirements. In turn, principal-agent 
theory is used to study information asymmetries, incentives and motivation that surround corporate 
leadership, project teams and clients. Finally, concepts from creating shared value are adopted to analyse 
the value of sustainability for the firm.  
 
1.5 Research aim and questions 
 
As such, this study intends to explore the topic of corporate sustainability, its implementation and 
structuring within engineering consultancies. The analysis is concentrated on how sustainability 
strategies are adapted and acted upon throughout the organisation and how firms meet stakeholder 
demands within projects. The thesis is therefore guided by the following research questions: 
 
• 
How is corporate sustainability understood and implemented within engineering consultancies 
in the AEC industry? 
• 
How do stakeholder relationships influence sustainability implementation within engineering 
consultancies? 
• 
How do principal-agent dynamics influence sustainability outcomes in engineering 
consultancies?  
 
In addressing these research questions, the study presents further understanding into how sustainability 
is addressed in project-based organisations where short-term project goals conflict with long-term 
strategy. By providing theories around organisations and sustainability implementation, practical 
implications for consultancies and clients arise.

2.1 Sustainable business 
 
Since the evolution of the United Nation’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the notion of 
sustainable business has driven companies to reassess their ambitions beyond traditional business 
practices. Through the wider sustainable development agenda, companies face increasing opportunities 
to deploy new technologies, adapt their business models and embrace sustainability in innovation 
(Rosati & Faria, 2019). Integration of sustainability into core business operations has increasingly 
become a strategic aspect, moving away from philanthropic activities and obligations and rather shaped 
by internal and external drivers. Research shows that businesses which deploy sustainability early on 
often do this as a strategic option, responding to various opportunities and risks. Rosati & Faria (2019) 
highlight the importance of sustainability reporting, serving not only to improve stakeholder relations 
but to also reduce information asymmetry, further strengthening legitimacy. This further supports the 
significance of sustainability as tool for transparency and competitiveness, reinforcing long-term value 
creation and corporate decision-making (Lowitt, 2013).  
 
Further literature explores how effective business strategies are integral to developing a company’s 
sustainability approach. Mahmood et al. (2023) utilise Porter’s framework on strategic typologies to 
explore sustainability, categorising firms into those deploying cost leadership and differentiation 
strategies. Research suggests that sustainability in cost leadership is motivated by operational efficiency 
with the aim of decreasing costs. Although cost leaders might see sustainability as an initial financial 
obstacle, sustainability can, in the long term, reduce business costs (Mahmood et al., 2023). In 
differentiation strategies, sustainability is seen as a competitive advantage. Therefore, companies 
employing a differentiation strategy often leverage sustainability to increase reputation and charge an 
increased price. 
 
The adoption of sustainability into business models is further explored by Sanford (2011), who 
comments on the approach of distancing sustainability into separate company departments or agendas. 
Rather, he suggests the notion of “responsible business” which aims to ingrain sustainability across the 
entire company, from business departments into strategies. To attain this, stakeholder relationships need 
to be redefined. Stakeholders need to become a holistic entity, not seeing them as separate individuals 
but rather as a system which incorporates the environment, community, customers and investors 
(Sanford, 2011). Similarly, Lowitt (2013) describes sustainability as a tool to drive competitive 
advantage and long-term value creation. Through incorporating sustainability into the whole value 
chain, firms can improve innovation and reduce risks.  
 
These perspectives echo key developments in sustainability and business strategy, showcasing the shift 
from traditional risk management and corporate social responsibility to a holistic approach to 
sustainability. Firms increasingly engage with sustainability topics to respond to external pressures but 
to also take advantage of it to strengthen innovation, business performance and stakeholder relations. 
This important repositioning underscores the concept of corporate sustainability and how firms can 
eventually embed sustainability thinking into core business strategies and company identity.  
 
2.2 Corporate sustainability 
 
Moving beyond sustainable development, corporate sustainability (CS) has materialised into a 
framework utilised in the corporate environment. With its origins in Brundtland report’s definition, CS 
incorporates triple bottom line thinking, namely environmental protection, and social and economic 
viability. Although this triple bottom line approach is widely utilised in CS literature, its practical 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
implementation, measurement and consolidation into business remain widely contested (Montiel & 
Delgado-Ceballos, 2014). A key barrier in the literature remains around the universal definition of CS. 
However, Wilson (2003) provides a thorough understanding of CS, underpinning notions of sustainable 
development, stakeholder theory, corporate social responsibility (CSR) and corporate accountability. 
Therefore, corporate sustainability can be understood as a leadership approach rather than a compliance 
one, integrating environmental, social and economic aspects into business models. This is further 
supported by Ashrafi et al. (2020), who explain that sustainability within firms requires new governance 
structures, a shift in corporate culture and continuous stakeholder engagement across regions.  
 
While interest in CS has risen in academia and industry, measuring CS remains a challenge for firms. 
Despite tools such as the Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) and the Dow Jones Sustainability Index 
(DJSI), standardised mechanisms for measuring social and economic impacts remain underdeveloped 
(Montiel & Delgado-Ceballos, 2014). As a result, defining and recognising successful corporate 
sustainability stands unclear within industries. Theories around CS have also evolved, increasingly 
incorporating research from stakeholder theory, institutional theory and resource-based view to examine 
organisations and their strategies. Stakeholder theory supports the notion that corporate sustainability 
is not entirely internal but is shaped by institutional and reputational pressures (Montiel & Delgado-
Ceballos, 2014). Institutional theory further underpins this as it underscores how various pressures can 
drive firms to adopt sustainable business practices. Finally, resource-based view draws a connection 
between environmental and financial performance (Montiel & Delgado-Ceballos, 2014).  
 
Langer et al. (2003) explore a CS framework approach, dividing CS integration into three aspects: 
system properties, capacities, and process. System properties focus on how sustainability embeds itself 
into corporate systems, placing an importance on the triple bottom line, long-term view, geographies 
and risk management. Capacities include the organisational foundations needed for sustainability. 
Lastly, process aspects look into how decision-making is performed and monitored. This includes 
stakeholder participation, cooperation and tracking sustainability progress (Langer et al., 2003). 
Although definitions on CS differ, it can still be seen as an organisational approach that focuses on 
firms’ environmental, social and economic capital. Ultimately, it requires both short- and long-term 
planning and stakeholder engagement. As a result, the challenges faced in implementing CS practices 
in project-based environments creates the foundation for this thesis’ research into how engineering 
consultancies understand and implement corporate sustainability.  
 
2.3 Critiques and limitations of corporate sustainability 
 
Concepts of corporate sustainability (CS) and corporate social responsibility (CSR) are increasingly 
distinguished in literature. CSR has long been described as a corporation’s ethical and philanthropic 
obligations in society, often translating into strategies such as donations, volunteering or various 
company policies (Sergeeva & Kapetanaki, 2022b). On the other hand, corporate sustainability attempts 
a more consolidated approach within business models, integrating long-term value creation with the 
triple bottom line (Kantabutra & Ketprapakorn, 2020). Despite theories on CS, its measuring and 
tracking continues to bring challenges. Furthermore, even if environmental performance utilises 
multitude of frameworks and tools, social dimensions of CS remain underdeveloped. Additionally, CS 
implementation remains unclear due to lack of clear frameworks to integrate sustainability into 
operations and value chains (Langer et al., 2003). This is further complicated as corporations operate in 
multilevel environments, balancing short- and long-term goals, local and global impacts and multiple 
stakeholders. 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
This complexity is particularly prevalent in project-based industries such as engineering and 
construction where sustainability is often considered mainly at project level. As different construction 
projects face varying stakeholders and differing community needs, the applicability of both CS and 
CSR can be questioned (Loosemore & Lim, 2018). Sergeeva (2022a) explores CSR in project-based 
organisations, stating that it is often deployed in two ways: strategically and practically. The strategic 
approach involves marketing and branding, whereas the practical approach relies on operational 
activities which can often lead to increased financial performance, such as risk mitigation and employee 
commitment. 
 
However, criticism has developed around the Western approach to both CSR and CS. Arguments around 
the applicability of transferring CSR practices developed in Western societies to other contexts have 
emerged as they are often developed at management and executive levels (Khan & Lund-Thomsen, 
2011). In turn, corporate practices in developing regions are more often implemented to respond to local 
needs and informal structures, frequently dictated and defined by multinational corporations 
(Loosemore & Lim, 2018). Corporate management approaches also heavily support power imbalances 
as large corporations exert influence over employees, creating dependencies (Drebes, 2016). These 
approaches can often simplify challenging socio-economic issues, categorising them into management 
issues that can be easily addressed through policy. 
 
In the AEC industry, similar issues are seen where global supply chains, short-term project goals and 
the project value chain challenge wider corporate sustainability goals. Often, project-level performance 
is prioritised over corporate-level sustainability, further increasing the gap between strategy and 
execution (Loosemore & Lim, 2018). While both CS and CSR aim to address environmental and 
societal concerns, CS places importance on a strategic and long-term vision. Nevertheless, its 
applicability in project-based and Western-driven context remains disputed. The focus on environmental 
performance and changing local needs highlights the need for additional research into how 
sustainability is embedded and practiced in the AEC industry (Lu & Zhang, 2016).  
 
2.4 Sustainability and the AEC industry  
 
Although the constuction industy contributes significantly to economic development and society’s 
wellbeing, it contributes significantly to climate change. The industry contributes to nearly 50% of all 
resource consumption and one-third of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions through mainly 
building operations such as heating and ventilation (Sijakovic & Peric, 2020). The European 
Environment Agency (2024) reports that the industry generates a significant amount of waste, consumes 
half of all raw materials and contributes to 5-12% of total GHG emissions in the European Union (EU). 
This highlights the immediate need for more sustainable approaches across the entire industry life cycle.  
2.4.1 Environmental, social and economic impacts 
 
Sustainablity in construction can also be examined through the triple bottom line, considering 
environmental, social and economic performance. Environmental impacts include changes to land use, 
biodiversity disruption on construction stes, high energy usage and pollution (European Environment 
Agency, 2024). High carbon footpints of construction materials, such as concrete and steel, also 
contribute to the negative impacts the industry has on people and the environment. Social concerns 
include health and safety, employment, accessability, human comfort, and cultural preservation (Oke 
& Aigbavboa, 2017). Research also shows that social considerations are often not prioritised with 
environmental factors taking precedence (Lu & Zhang, 2016). There are also several economic impacts 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
involved within the industry. Opportunities, such as employment, make the industry an important driver 
of the economy. In the EU alone, the industry employs over 25 million individuals across a range of 
companies (European Environment Agency, 2024). Generating an annual turnover of over EUR 2 
billion in the EU, the industry is also a key contributor to the economy. Additionally, building practices 
within construction can promote innovation, further supporting local economies (Oke & Aigbavboa, 
2017). 
2.4.2 Sustainable construction 
 
Sustainable construction involves projects that not only meet traditional performance metrics such as 
cost, time and quality but also minimises negative environmental impacts and increases economic 
benefits. This should be considered across the entire project life cycle, including design, construction, 
maintenance, demolition and potential reuse (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). Key elements incorporate social 
sustainability, namely considering health and safety, wellbeing, cultural preservation and inclusivity. In 
sustainable construction, environmental sustainability is concerned with minimising resource 
consumtpion and emissions, and placing an importance on indoor environmental quality. Lastly, 
economic sustainability focuses on cost-effective projects while supporting local communities, and 
innovation (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). Despite the increased consideration of sustainable construction, 
barriers still exist. This includes hesitation towards change, lack of stakeholder engagement and 
awareness, regulations, access to technologies and economic barriers. Moreover, the traditional nature 
of the industry with building codes, bureaucracy and legislations slow progress towards sustainable 
practices across the industry. 
 
2.5 Theoretical frameworks 
 
To understand how corporate sustainability can be implemented within engineering consultancies, it is 
vital to explore the relationships between the different actors involved in the AEC industry. Two 
theoretical frameworks present themselves as particularly relevant, namely stakeholder theory and 
principal-agent theory. Both theories provide understanding into the various incentives that exist, power 
relations and information asymmetries which all influence corporate strategies in project-based 
environments. Lastly, creating shared value provides an important lens to explore how sustainability is 
framed within engineering consultancies, consolidating the findings from stakeholder theory and 
principal-agent theory. 
2.5.1 Stakeholder theory  
 
Stakeholder theory aids in explaining how value creation for organisations must address others than just 
shareholders but to also include clients, employees, communities, investors, suppliers and the 
environment (Sanford, 2011). This further supports corporate sustainability approaches when 
addressing business’ responsibilities in engaging diverse needs and delivering positive outcomes. 
Sanford (2011) explores stakeholder relations as systems of value exchange, where each stakeholder 
holds responsibility in the organisation’s capability to convey value. Therefore, organisations begin to 
view stakeholders not only as separate stakes but rather as co-responsible to creating value (Sanford, 
2011).  
 
The theory largely views the firm as a system of relationships between groups and individuals who 
influence or are influenced by the firm (Freudenreich et al., 2020). Stakeholders can be further divided 
into internal and external ones. Internal stakeholders include groups such as employees and 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
management whereas groups such as customers, suppliers, NGOs are seen as external stakeholders. In 
some instances, a stakeholder, such as shareholders, can be both internal and external (Lozano et al., 
2015). Lozano et al. (2015) also differentiates primary – with direct influence or more directly impacted 
- and secondary stakeholders who are less influenced. Within corporate sustainability, stakeholder 
theory implies that firms need to move beyond profitability to also address the needs of both primary 
and secondary stakeholders.  
 
In construction, where stakeholders range from public and private sectors, responsible management of 
different interests and needs is essential in order to respond to sustainability ambitions. Project-based 
environments face challenges in navigating various influence levels and temporary requirements, 
ranging from client needs, contractors to end-users. Therefore, corporate sustainability within 
construction not only requires recognising stakeholders but also controlling conflicts. This closely 
relates to critique surrounding stakeholder theory which highlights the lack of clarity in defining and 
prioritising stakeholders (Mainardes et al., 2011). Furthermore, it does not take into account real-world 
power dynamics within stakeholder groups nor the emerging stakeholder interests over time. Arguments 
have also been raised about the theory’s oversimplification of firms’ roles in society, underplaying the 
complex socio-political environments they operate in (Mainardes et al., 2011).  
 
Freudenreich et al. (2020) develop stakeholder theory further by exploring how different stakeholders 
engage in value creation within businesses. In the case of customers, engagement with value takes place 
within the offered products or services while employees participate with their knowledge in exchange 
for salaries and professional development. Other external stakeholders such as communities, 
governments and NGOs anticipate wider social and environmental value exchange, while financial 
stakeholders contribute through their capital with financial returns in mind (Freudenreich et al., 2020). 
Schaltegger (2019) further expands on this, implying that sustainability is a business case in which 
value creation for all stakeholders becomes the firm’s purpose. This challenges the notion of prioritising 
either profits or sustainability, and instead demonstrates how value can be environmental, social and 
economic in nature. These ideas closely echo with later discussion on creating shared value, providing 
a link between stakeholder theory and value creation within sustainability.  
 
2.5.2 Principal-agent theory 
 
While stakeholder theory aids in mapping out the complex relationships faced in construction, principal-
agent theory provides insights into organisational structures and behaviours between stakeholders. 
Principal-agent theory explores the relationships of when one party (principal) gives out tasks to another 
(agent) who completes them on behalf of the principal. This frequently leads to information 
asymmetries where the principal and agent have conflicting interests, with the agent often holding more 
information than the principal (Pratt & Zeckhauser, 1985). Within the AEC industry this phenomenon 
is common and frequent. In engineering consultancies, the clients (principals) depend on consultants 
(agents) to deliver projects and meet their requirements. The same structure can be applied internally 
where corporate leadership (principals) delegates tasks to project teams and project managers (agents) 
(Sha, 2019). While not covering all stakeholders involved, Figure 1 illustrates the intricate relationships 
between principals and agents and their related tasks in the context of client-consultants.

Adapted from (Ceric, 2012) 
 
With diverging interests, information asymmetry creates two agency problems, namely moral hazard 
and adverse selection (Gailmard, 2014). Moral hazards describe situations where agents operate in ways 
that makes it challenging for the principal to observe, especially in cases with diverging interests. In 
turn, adverse selection illustrates situations where the agent holds information that the principal does 
not have access to. In engineering consultancies, project managers may disregard sustainability-related 
matters, arguing for cost or short-term goals (Ceric, 2012). This challenge is further complicated by 
issues of monitoring and the costs associated with. Gailmard (2014) explains that agency relationships 
come with varying levels of monitoring, often dictated by costs. When monitoring is expensive or 
cheaper alternatives exists, less monitoring is put into place for the activities performed by agents. 
 
Additionally, common agency presents a significant challenge in engineering companies as multiple 
principals are involved, all independently attempting to influence the agent (Sha, 2019). Both the 
company and client often deploy their own project managers, further complicating the principal-agent 
relationship. In this case, multiple principals – clients and the company – try to persuade project 
managers with differing project goals and aspirations (Sha, 2019). With diverging goals, sustainability 
integration becomes increasingly challenging as project managers are tasked with balancing financial 
performance, client needs, and broader corporate sustainability strategies. As a result, principal-agent 
theory emerges as a useful theoretical framework to explain the difficulties in implementing corporate 
sustainability strategies within organisations. The theory provides insights into the organisational 
challenges engineering consultancies face, such as gaps in monitoring, lack of incentives and 
communication.

2.5.3 Creating shared value 
 
The concept of creating shared value (CSV) can be utilised to underpin corporate sustainability 
strategies. Introduced by Porter and Kramer (2011), CSV shifts business away from corporate social 
responsibility (CSR) and places value at the centre, with capabilities of creating positive economic and 
societal impact (Wieland, 2017). Ultimately, CSV can be described as various policies and company 
operations that can expand competition while addressing economic and social concerns of communities. 
In contrast to capitalism and traditional CSR, CSV seeks to unlock opportunities for market expansion, 
increased productivity and innovation (Porter & Kramer, 2011). The theory argues that social and 
environmental factors can act as drivers of competitive advantage. Porter and Kramer outline CSV as 
activities to innovate products and align markets to meet social needs, targeting productivity in the value 
chain and creating industry clusters to promote collaboration (Yang & Yan, 2020). 
 
This approach to sustainability ingrains sustainable development as a driver for CSV, further framing 
sustainability as a long-term investment into environmental, social and economic assets (Vargova, 
2013). Furthermore, CSV requires companies to redefine business strategies and how they collaborate 
with stakeholders and supply chains in order to drive growth. It places importance on an inclusive 
approach to value creation, encouraging all actors to participate (Vargova, 2013). Crane et al. (2014) 
highlight strengths of the CSV framework. Particular attention has been paid to how it attempts to 
engrain social goals into core business operations. The framework also promotes the role of 
governments in accommodating regulation that enhance value creation. Ultimately, CSV offers a unified 
approach to CSR, stakeholder management, social entrepreneurship and innovation, promoting the 
intersection between businesses and society.  
 
However, critique has emerged about their thinking and strategies. At the same time, Crane et al. (2014) 
imply that the concept remains unoriginal, as it loans CSR thinking and stakeholder theory without 
addressing their theoretical frameworks. Although Porter and Kramer (2011) raise arguments against 
CSR for being disparate from business profits, they disregard long standing research that reinforces the 
business case for CSR. Additional criticism includes the framework ignoring the complex relationship 
between social and economic goals. By implying that shared value can be achieved without conflict and 
trade-off, the concept of CSV may ignore larger systemic injustices (Crane et al., 2014). This approach 
may prioritise initiatives and strategies that provide quicker profits, often disregarding less influential 
stakeholders or marginalised communities (Wieland, 2017). CSV can therefore further promote social 
inequalities, reinforcing aspects of greenwashing under the assumption of doing good. Despite the 
criticism, CSV remains as a powerful tool to consider corporate sustainability by incorporating social 
issues into business planning and enhancing stakeholder collaboration. Its theoretical framework has 
led to conversations about the role of businesses and their role in society.

research focusing on project environmental performance in the AEC industry, there is limited 
understanding into how corporate sustainability is applied in organisations within engineering

3.1 Research design 
 
This study utilises a qualitative research approach to explore how corporate sustainability is practiced, 
understood and driven within engineering consultancies in the AEC industry. Qualitative research 
presents itself as a useful research approach for this study due to the nature of the study area. Qualitative 
research is understood as collecting data through the form of words rather than statistical numbers and 
data. It is further defined by its flexible nature and ability to respond to context (Busetto et al., 2020). 
As the study explores corporate sustainability, uncovering organisational structures and stakeholder 
dynamics, a qualitative approach is deemed appropriate. The use of qualitative methods outlined in the 
next sections further aid in understanding how sustainability is implemented within the industry. 
Through insights from participants with different roles and scope of knowledge, the study allows for an 
understanding of sustainability practices rooted in a rich context. Furthermore, to guide the analysis, an 
abductive approach is utilised to allow for fluidity between theory and empirical data (Awuzie & 
McDermott, 2017).

3.4 Data analysis 
 
The analysis of the data collected through interviews followed an abductive approach in combination 
with thematic analysis. This approach aimed to create a comprehensive connection between the theory 
presented and empirical insights. An abductive approach allows for flexibility between the data 
collected and theories which guide the thesis (Awuzie & McDermott, 2017). To organise and analyse 
the data, a digital coding system was created in Airtable. The process followed the thematic analysis 
steps proposed by Braun & Clarke (2006). 
 
Firstly, a familiarisation phase was adopted after transcribing the interview recordings by reading each 
transcript (Braun & Clarke, 2006). Relevant elements of each transcript were then copied into the 
Airtable database. This process guaranteed that important quotes were utilised for further analysis. The 
copied lines were then written again into simplified descriptions, rephrasing the naturalised transcripts 
in order to summarise the key meanings of each transcript. The rewritten parts were used to not only 
capture the intent of the lines, but to also allow for direct comparison between the different interviews. 
 
Each cell within Airtable included the simplified description and was assigned an initial concept (Braun 
& Clarke, 2006). The concepts acted as early indicators and categories emerging from the data and were 
short in description while also reflecting the literature. Example concepts included aspects from the 
literature such as role of sustainability managers, size and complexity challenges and challenges in 
project implementation. In total, 35 concepts were identified. At this stage, concepts were assigned 
codes which reflected the simplified descriptions and allowed room for re-emerging ideas. For example, 
phrases referencing “corporate leadership” and “organisational strategies” were grouped into the same 
concept of “corporate structure”. Where possible, theoretical frameworks were used to develop the 
concepts. 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
After all the phrases and concepts were completed, the initial codes were reviewed again and then 
grouped into the relevant literature. This phase included both inductive and deductive approaches, 
recognising relevant themes from literature (Thompson, 2022). From this, concepts were categorised 
into sub-themes that also related to the literature. In this case, concepts such as “challenges in project 
implementation” and “consultancies educating clients” were combined under the sub-theme of “client-
consultant dynamics” which relates to broader stakeholder theory literature. Codes that were too generic 
were combined to another concept or edited again. 
4 Empirical background 
 
The following chapter provides the necessary empirical background for comprehending the research 
aim of this thesis. Providing an overview of the architecture, engineering and construction (AEC) 
industry, it focuses on the industry’s structure and current challenges. It then moves to provide insights 
into the role of sustainability within the industry before discussing engineering consultancies and their 
role in the construction process. With the help of this background, the subsequent analysis will have 
context into the challenges and complexities faced in the industry. 
 
The AEC industry includes a variety of activities, spanning from the planning, design, engineering, and 
construction of the built environment. Appendix II illustrates the complex timeline and delivery of 
construction projects, involving architects, engineers, contractors and consultants who deliver 
infrastructure and building projects. It is currently one of the largest industries in the world, contributing 
to over $13 trillion in global output in 2023 (McKinsey & Company, 2024). Although the industry is 
globally economically relevant, it faces multiple challenges, including low profit margins, slow 
digitalisation processes and increased demand for sustainability. Tight labour markets remain an issue, 
with shortages in roles such as engineering, craftsmanship and project management. Competitive 
bidding for projects is also key to the nature of the industry, reinforcing low-profit contracts and 
reducing financial flexibility (McKinsey & Company, 2024).  
 
Furthermore, the sector relies heavily on subcontracting and is often dictated by its supply chains. 
Research shows that principal construction firms only contribute to a small part of the project, with 
approximately 75% of the project being delivered by suppliers and other subcontractors (Segerstedt & 
Olofsson, n.d.). Consequently, this calls for larger coordination efforts which further complicates efforts 
for unified practices and innovation. This is additionally echoed by the industry’s slow adoption to 
change due to varying stakeholder interests (I. E. Wilson & Rezgui, 2013).  
 
Sustainability has increasingly become a topic of concern for the AEC industry due to its significant 
environmental, social, and economic impacts (Sijakovic & Peric, 2020). Consuming large quantities of 
resources and contributing to greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, the industry plays a crucial role in 
mitigating environmental concerns. Environmental impacts have shifted discourse to sustainable 
construction methods, largely driven by regulatory requirements and shifting market landscapes (Ernst 
& Young, 2024). New regulations require firms to disclose emissions, consider climate-related risks, 
and consider climate resilience in their practices. Shifting trends include a move towards circular 
construction, prioritising the reuse of materials, waste reduction and life cycle integration (Ernst & 
Young, 2024).  
 
Additional industry trends include digital transformation, with technologies being increasingly 
deployed to drive change in sustainability. Digital technologies, such as 3D printing, smart building 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
technologies and focus on data, are shifting construction practices towards more efficient and 
environmentally friendly methods (Ernst & Young, 2024). Green construction materials are increasingly 
being utilised to replace traditional materials. Moreover, climate resilience is becoming central to design 
and construction as the industry faces rising insurance costs and impacts from extreme weather events 
(Ernst & Young, 2024). Although development in sustainability is taking place, implementation remains 
inconsistent (De Paula et al., 2017). Research has revealed misalignments between awareness around 
sustainability and practical implementation. Although technical innovation is essential, organisational 
structures are also important with training, collaboration and leadership further driving integration (De 
Paula et al., 2017). Many professionals quote lack of time, internal capacities and unclear knowledge 
into best practices as barriers to sustainability implementation. 
 
In this evolving and challenging environment, engineering consultancies hold a notable role. Operating 
in the wider professional services industry, engineering consulting companies specialise in providing 
technical expertise, strategy advice and design solutions rather than partaking in physical construction 
(Kreitl & Oberndorfer, 2004). They influence across the project life cycle and can operate in investment 
planning during feasibility studies, design work, or cost and quality control during project execution. 
Engineering consultancies are distinguished by their breadth of knowledge and multidisciplinary nature 
(Huang & Hsueh, 2007). These firms also rely heavily on human capital, with large teams working 
across architecture, environmental services, and engineering disciplines (Kreitl & Oberndorfer, 2004). 
Due to the project-based environment, the firms’ organisational structures are developed into temporary 
project teams that work directly with clients. Therefore, project success is not only dictated by the 
team’s skills and knowledge but also by the capacity to retain strong client relationships. Reputation is 
also central to the sector, often impacting the firm’s ability to win bids and secure profitable contracts 
(Kreitl & Oberndorfer, 2004). 
 
Competition remains high in the industry, requiring engineering consultancies to adapt to changing 
regulations and respond to sustainability concerns while operating within tight budgets and timelines. 
Although their position within the sector allows them to influence sustainability practices, its often 
limited by client requirements, internal capacity and contracts (Kreitl & Oberndorfer, 2004). To further 
support the empirical background of the study, key players in the engineering consultancy sector were 
also identified. The market remains fragmented with larger firms operating globally, delivering projects 
such as infrastructure, water systems, and urban planning (Kreitl & Oberndorfer, 2004). Within the 
sector, larger firms engage in sustainability through their sustainability strategies and in-house 
sustainability expertise. Despite maintaining anonymity of participants’ organisations, the general 
characteristics of large firms in the sector are utilised to provide context in the analysis of participants’ 
insights.  
 
The chapter has evidenced the organisational and sustainability concerns prevalent in the AEC industry, 
further examining the role of engineering consultancies. Subsequently, it situates the thesis and its 
exploration of how corporate sustainability is understood and implemented within project-based 
organisations. The following analysis chapter will examine how these challenges relate to the insights 
shared by professionals from the field.

5.2 Stakeholder theory 
 
The following section of the analysis employs stakeholder theory to understand the complexities 
engineering consultancies face when navigating stakeholder relations within the construction sector. 
Lozano (2015) expands on the definition of stakeholder theory, explaining how organisations exist with 
differing stakeholders and their varying demands, expectations and levels of influence. By exploring 
the theory through interview data, this section of the analysis attempts to reveal the challenges 
consultancies face when aligning sustainability efforts with stakeholder requirements. 
 
5.2.1 Stakeholder engagement and mapping 
 
Stakeholder engagement plays a critical role in the AEC industry as projects are large and complex in 
nature and this engagement dictates project and sustainability goals. As Lozano (2015) describes, 
stakeholder theory describes how companies operate and are influenced by individuals and networks of 
groups. In the AEC industry, stakeholders are identified as those who partake or are impacted by 
construction activities throughout the project life cycle. Within the industry, stakeholders cover a large 
network of groups and individuals. These include clients and investors, building users such as tenants 
or employees, as well as supply chain actors including contractors, material suppliers, architects and 
engineers, surveyors and developers. Additionally, public stakeholders such as local and national 
governments, policymakers and local communities influence projects (Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). 
 
Across the interviews, participants underscored the need to engage stakeholders, particularly internal 
stakeholders in the early stages of a project. Participant 1 (P1), a senior project manager, described the 
nature of construction projects where technical experts tend to be involved only at later design stages: 
 
“Many engineers are not used to working in the early stages […] these [stages] are crucial for 
the implementation of sustainability” 
 
Involving engineers only in later design stages highlights the wider limitation of stakeholder 
involvement, where knowledge from different actors is often not considered. Participant 2 (P2), another 
senior project manager, also critiques the traditional way of working of engineers who often follow 
what has been done before: 
 
“engineers have to be able to think differently, to think out of the box, to take [into] 
consideration a lot of parameters that maybe they weren’t looking for […] the engineers 
replicate existing systems […] there is not much innovation drive.”  
 
Co-creation is particularly crucial in projects with multiple stakeholders involved (De Paula et al., 
2017). Participant 1 (P1) describes this, underscoring the need to include all internal stakeholders: 
 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
“Stakeholder analysis […] it’s important to include fire, to include health and safety, to include 
sustainability, to include everyone and then still make the organisation work. It ends up being 
really complex.”  
 
Engaging with internal stakeholders was also described by Participant 3 (P3), emphasising the need to 
adapt ways of communication according to the different departments: 
 
“I need to try to translate into their terms. If they work in finance, how can I simplify their work 
through these KPIs? Or how can I make the legal department’s work easier”. 
 
This raises important considerations towards also considering internal stakeholders and how 
stakeholder engagement moves beyond external actors (Sanford, 2011). The dynamics between internal 
and external stakeholders was also raised by Participant 5 (P5) who outlined the difficulties in 
coordinating multiple parties along the project value chain with “numerous stakeholders involved like 
vendors, engineers, contractors”.  
 
Participant 4 (P4), a senior sustainability manager, recounted examples of how external stakeholder 
engagement takes shape in their organisation through user workshops and involving municipalities: 
“The municipality is always there, they are the one giving us the building permits […] and you always 
have a lot of user workshops. It’s a must-do”. Although stakeholder engagement is mandated by 
regulations, involving various individuals and groups also drives sustainability as explained by 
Participant 4 (P4): “The more people who can use a building, the better […] it’s definitely a huge 
sustainable aspect in getting all the stakeholders involved”. Participant 4 (P4) also extended external 
stakeholders to also include biodiversity, describing occasions where projects were moved from sites 
due to discovering endangered species, illustrating the breadth of stakeholders involved. 
 
Additionally, sustainability is largely dictated by the stakeholders involved in the project. According to 
stakeholder theory, stakeholders are defined by key attributes, namely urgency, legitimacy and power 
(Oke & Aigbavboa, 2017). These attributes are prevalent in engineering consultancies where clients 
often hold the most power over decisions whereas engineers and consultants bring legitimacy with their 
expertise. Relating to this, Participant 2 (P2) highlights “clients are aware they must commit but it really 
depends on how the project is structured, who the stakeholders are”. Coordinating varying stakeholder 
demands and balancing stakeholder attributes echoes critique of the literature where stakeholder theory 
often lacks limited understanding in how these conflicting priorities should be resolved or predicted 
(Mainardes et al., 2011).  
 
It is therefore evident that stakeholder engagement in engineering consultancies is crucial but remains 
a complex matter. It often involves negotiating around traditional engineering thinking, coordinating 
project environments and adapting sustainability-related language into each internal business 
department. As demonstrated through the interviews, stakeholder mapping and early engagement are 
necessary in order to drive sustainability goals in AEC projects. 
5.2.2 Client-consultant dynamics 
 
Within stakeholder theory, clients are considered primary stakeholders, largely influencing project 
outcomes through their interests and level of power (Freudenreich et al., 2020). Particularly in the AEC 
industry, the relationship between engineering consultancies and their clients becomes important in 
guiding sustainability at project level. Interview findings show how consultants often hold two roles – 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
meeting client expectations while guiding and educating clients at the same time in order to challenge 
sustainability.  
 
One major theme that re-occurred in interviews was the role consultants play in educating clients on 
sustainability. This reflects Sanford’s (2011) view on value creation as a shared process, where 
stakeholders share responsibility. Participant 1 (P1) expanded on the effects of co-creation by describing 
a project: 
 
“it was actually to help a client understand what sustainability means to them […] they actually 
didn’t know anything about sustainability in the built environment […] and they were extremely 
happy that we could give them this input.” 
 
These efforts by consultants further align with the goals of stakeholder theory in increasing the firm’s 
ability to “deliver value for investors, customers, and employees” (Schaltegger et al., 2019), as reaching 
sustainability ambitions is closely linked with client satisfaction and long-term performance. The role 
of consultants is expanded on by Participant 3 (P3), who stated: “I think a lot of the work within 
sustainability there, is kind of showing to the clients why sustainability is useful and worth their money 
and time.” This is further supported by Participant 4 (P4), who describes: “A lot of our clients are also 
asking for sustainability but they don’t know how. So this is where our expertise definitely comes in.” 
 
These examples showcase how consultants hold responsibility in communicating the benefits of 
sustainability to clients. Sanford (2011) explains how values for each stakeholder need to be understood 
from their perspective and communicated effectively. As Participant 2 (P2) explained: “it’s the way it’s 
communicated to the client that will gain interest”. Guiding clients towards sustainable solutions 
instead of directly giving solutions also appeared as an important strategy for consultants. Participant 1 
(P1) describes: “if we take the consultant first, you have to be really good at asking questions.” 
 
Interview findings also showed how client-consultant dynamics and sustainability integration also 
depends on the type of project and sector. Participant 6 (P6) states: “the integration of sustainability 
probably depends on the “readiness” of the specific market to buy sustainable services”. Participant 1 
(P1) further explains how highly regulated industries are slower to adapt sustainable innovation due to 
the traditional nature of the industry: 
 
“because they [highly regulated industries] have so many regulations and rely so much on what 
we used to do […] it’s [sustainability integration] still kind of limited.”  
 
Additionally, client openness also emerged as an important factor to implementing sustainability. 
Participant 4 (P4) explains: 
 
“it’s definitely easier to persuade a client when it’s a client who owns a building and will own 
the building afterwards […] our main issues is when we have developers who just want to build 
and then they want to sell it […] they just want to make the minimum requirements”. 
 
This demonstrates a common challenge in client-consultant dynamics when addressing stakeholder 
demands as not all stakeholders value sustainability in similar ways. Shaltegger et al. (2019) outline 
how power struggles are a frequent occurrence in stakeholder engagement when addressing 
sustainability-related matters. In this case, certain clients prioritise short-term financial goals while 
consultants or sustainability managers focus on long-term sustainability goals. Balancing these different 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
priorities reflects the nature of projects within engineering consultancies where each stakeholder guides 
projects with their own experiences, expertise and interests. Although consultants aim to guide and 
educate clients on sustainability, the integration of sustainability heavily depends on the clients’ 
openness and priorities as Participant 1 (P1) notes: “of course the client has to be open […]. They have 
to realise themselves”. 
 
Similar findings can be seen in literature where Freudenreich (2020) describes how stakeholders only 
engage when they see value corresponding to their needs. In the case of engineering consultancies, 
sustainability is only pursued if its value is seen as beneficial for especially the clients. However, 
findings show that sustainability is often treated as a negotiable factor rather than a common need and 
want. Therefore, consultants approach sustainability differently depending on the stakeholder whether 
it is to reduce operational costs, manage risks or improve the quality of life for end-users. 
5.2.3 Social dimensions of sustainability  
 
Although environmental factors surrounding sustainability are often prioritised in the AEC industry, 
social factors remain crucial to address. Stakeholder theory further reflects this as it considers creating 
value beyond shareholders, reinforcing the need to take into consideration all stakeholders involved 
(Freudenreich et al., 2020). Freudenreich et al. (2020) considers a wide scope of stakeholders in relation 
to society, such as communities, the government, other external agencies and media who all play a key 
role in shaping project success and creating long-term impact. Yet, findings from interviews uncover 
continuing challenges in addressing social sustainability due to its measurement and definition.  
 
Participants described social sustainability as being qualitative in nature. Participant 3 (P3) underlined 
misalignments between measuring climate factors and social factors:  
 
“There’s been a lot of KPIs existing, for instance, for climate and also because more has been 
done in the market on climate than it has been, for instance, on social impact […] So it’s very 
complex but it’s very, very important.” 
 
The lack of data for measuring social impacts reflects industry trends, where social considerations are 
often less prioritised due to their unclear metrics (Hale et al., 2019). Environmental factors, such as 
carbon emissions, can be measured through tools like Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) but social 
sustainability continues to depend on non-quantifiable data (Lu & Zhang, 2016). This is additionally 
described by Participant 3 (P3):  
 
“It’s not solely numbers, it’s a lot qualitative research that needs to be done. We’re talking, you 
know, groups of people that have lived in a place a long time […]. And it’s a lot of historical 
data, and it’s also future progressions and looking into what do we believe will happen.” 
 
Participant 1 (P1) stated that an area within social sustainability which is more defined and prioritised 
is health and safety, particularly employee wellbeing: “We also have health and safety which is 
important in the built environment for, yeah, the wellbeing of employees”. This reflects wider 
discussions in literature which highlights the need to consider social sustainability when arguing for the 
business case for sustainability within stakeholder theory (Sanford, 2011). Additionally, Participant 5 
(P5), working in ESG reporting, explained how social issues are included in wider corporate 
sustainability reporting: “ESG data points: employee wellbeing, ethics, wages”, which further directs 
to an increased push to include social sustainability within reporting (Ernst & Young, 2024).  
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
 
Participant 4 (P4) provided arguments for social sustainability considerations, and how it can maximise 
accessibility, further promoting environmental performance:  
 
“You need to make a building as accessible to as many as possible because the more people 
who can use a building, the better, the less buildings we should have.” 
 
Insights provided by Participant 4 (P4) align with literature surrounding stakeholder engagement to 
increase sustainable development. As Sev (2009) highlights, designing inclusive and affordable spaces 
contributes to wider sustainable development goals, ensuring value for all users. Furthermore, 
challenges surrounding social sustainability were also evident when faced with ethical considerations 
in engineering consultancies. Participant 3 (P3) explains: 
 
“When something is flagged in the Ethics Committee as not being a sustainable project, a lot 
of the time that’s related to social sustainability […]. For instance, when you have projects in 
[anonymised country], you know, there’s a whole conversation around if you would want to do 
projects there and if you want to do projects there, they should be projects that increase social 
sustainability.” 
 
This emphasises the regional differences when considering social sustainability, where stakeholder 
needs, expectations and local contexts differ across regions and continents. Such complexities underpin 
critique found in stakeholder theory literature which underlines how the theory often reduces complex 
societal relationships (Mainardes et al., 2011). 
 
The interview findings reveal that while engineering consultancies are increasingly considering social 
sustainability, it still remains a challenge. The lack of qualitative data, ethical considerations and 
knowledge gaps, leaves environmental performance to be more considered over social sustainability. 
Ultimately, stakeholder theory suggests that not considering social dimensions can undermine long-
term value creation and project legitimacy. This highlights the crucial need to integrate social factors 
not only for moral obligations, but also strategic ones.  
5.2.4 Navigating between internal and external stakeholders 
 
An important finding from the data collected was the often conflicting and challenging relationship 
between internal and external stakeholders when implementing sustainability. Stakeholder theory 
underscores the need for organisations to meet demands of multiple stakeholders, who directly or 
indirectly, affect or are affected by the company’s activities (Lozano et al., 2015). For engineering 
consultancies, this becomes important as the project-based nature, organisational structures and external 
factors influence business outcomes. Participants highlighted the role of internal disarrays in hindering 
sustainability integration. Participant 3 (P3) illustrated the mismatch between strategic goals and 
practical project operations: 
 
“Sometimes you could have the Board that find it difficult to understand, you know, how much 
viability there is in sustainability. […] and sometimes you have the people that work closely 
with the clients that see that the clients don’t want to pay for it.” 
 
This also echoes additional topics relating to principal-agency, discussed later, where misalignments 
between leadership and practice happen. Participant 3 (P3)’s insights additionally reveal how the 
==PAGE:28==
 
28 
economics of sustainability, where cost is considered over long-term value, can cause resistance 
internally. Furthermore, internal and external stakeholder conflicts appear in the insights shared by 
Participant 6 (P6):  
 
“the experience is that during the development of a building project, the environmental and 
social values of the project is prioritised […]. The economic values is prioritised over the 
environmental and social values in the last phases where the project is actually built. So the 
question is how it is possible to maintain the initial ideas of the project all the way to the end.” 
 
In contrast, external demands also contribute to misaligned stakeholder priorities. Participant 4 (P4) 
noted to the perception of stakeholder engagement: “Yeah, you choose to listen to more and which 
weighs a little more than others”. This statement reflects literature by Schaltegger et al. (2019) and 
Lozano et al. (2015) who describe that power and influence of each stakeholder determines which 
stakeholders are prioritised. Certain external stakeholders may apply more influence in public projects 
as explained by Participant 4 (P4): 
 
“it’s a subject where we end up using quite a lot of money because someone [external] higher 
up says this is something we need […] which is just a waste of money and time if you ask me.” 
 
Insights shared by Participant 4 (P4) exemplify how external stakeholder agendas can oftentimes take 
priority over sustainability considerations, resulting in inefficient resource allocation and internal 
frustration. Participant 5 (P5) shared experience working in a different region, where internal and 
stakeholder priorities had to be balanced: 
 
“External stakeholders want quick results […]. Internal stakeholders [have] lack of 
knowledge” 
 
This further underlines how stakeholder influence is affected by different stakeholder expectations. 
External participants often prioritise short-term project goals, including profits and time, while 
disregarding wider long-term sustainability considerations. The interview findings illustrate the 
relationships between internal and external stakeholders and how varying interests and levels of 
knowledge, all influence sustainability outcomes. As a result, engineering consultancies act as 
intermediaries in a challenging environment, balancing corporate strategies, client needs and external 
agendas. While it is evident that stakeholder theory provides an understanding into these relationships, 
challenges remain around the different power imbalances between stakeholders, particularly between 
consultants and clients and how strongly influence contributes to sustainability implementation.  
 
5.3 Principal-agency theory 
 
The principal-agent relationship becomes important when trying to understand the complexities in 
implementing sustainability within engineering consultancies. The theory explains how an agent (such 
as a project manager or the consultancy in some cases) acts on behalf of a principal (such as corporate 
leadership or a client). In this case, engineering consultancies often operate in a multi-principal 
environment, where project managers are situated between two principals, the clients and corporate 
leadership. As a result, the project managers become accountable for these two principals with often 
conflicting expectations and interests on sustainability (Ceric, 2012). The following section of the 
analysis utilises principal-agent theory to explore the internal dynamics of engineering consultancies 
==PAGE:29==
 
29 
and their interactions with clients and how lack of communication, different priorities and levels of 
influence impact sustainability outcomes of projects in the AEC industry.  
5.3.1 Information asymmetry in sustainability integration 
 
Principal-agent theory is largely driven by information asymmetry, in which only one participant, either 
the agent or principal, holds crucial information, leading to misalignments (Greiner & Sun, 2021). 
Within engineering consultancies, information asymmetry is present both, between corporate leadership 
and project teams, and between consultants and clients. When faced with corporate-project level 
dynamics, participants explained how corporate sustainability strategies are rarely clear and easily 
implementable in projects. This is described by Participant 4 (P4): 
 
“You have a strategy, very high level, a good strategy […] but if you’re not really into what is 
sustainability and what is meant by the strategy, then it’s impossible to incorporate into a 
project.” 
 
They further add: 
 
“I wouldn’t say that it’s the top that are the culprits who’ve done anything wrong because they 
have communicated it […] but then moving further down the line, you suddenly get to the 
projects.” 
 
The quotes shared by Participant 4 (P4) showcase how organisational-level sustainability strategies 
exist, but project-level strategies remain at large. Participant 2 (P2) reflected on the gap between 
implementation, stating that “nobody tells you that you have to” engage with sustainability, 
exemplifying inadequate knowledge or rules. This gap between corporate strategies and practical 
implementation illustrates how staff work in isolation or feel misaligned from strategic initiatives. 
Participant 3 (P3) mentions how it is “difficult translating between these two worlds”, suggesting the 
limited connection within the company. The increasing gap directs towards the principal-agency 
problem: the agents (project teams) do not have the same level of access or clarity to the expectations 
set by the principals (corporate leaders).  
 
On the client side, the empirical data revealed how clients often lack knowledge on sustainability. 
Participant 1 (P1) explains: 
 
“Most clients are on the level of carbon emission […] it’s still this very narrow understanding 
of sustainability that they have.” 
 
This reflects the nature of professional services and consulting where the created asymmetry leads 
consultants to not only deliver solutions but also educate clients. Additionally, bigger clients will also 
have their own corporate strategies, where they face similar challenges around information asymmetry 
and relationships between their internal agents (client’s project manager) and principal (client’s 
corporate leaders). This was highlighted by Participant 1 (P1): 
 
“Our biggest clients, they obviously have a sustainability strategy but what we see is often that 
people that we meet, the clients, project managers, they don't really know how to translate 
strategy into the projects, so we often help them do that. […] High level and difficult to translate 
into actual initiatives in the projects.” 
==PAGE:30==
 
30 
 
Similar experiences were shared by Participant 4 (P4) who revealed that clients often want to make 
“their projects more sustainable but they don’t know how”. Between clients and consultants delivering 
projects, information asymmetry also arises, limiting the integration and consideration of sustainability. 
As Participant 1 (P1) stated: 
  
“Some project managers with little knowledge on sustainability. They sometimes tend to like to 
emit sustainability.” 
 
This critical information asymmetry reveals the important role sustainability managers hold within the 
complex principal-agent relationship between engineering consultancies and clients. Participant 1 (1) 
reflects on the role of sustainability managers saying: 
 
“implementation of the initiatives will stay with the sustainability manager [who] keeps the 
overview of the project and sees that we reach our goals.” 
 
Furthermore, Participant 4 (P4), a sustainability manager, describes the role and its position within the 
organisation: 
 
“I'm actually sitting at the top with the project management so that sustainability gets 
incorporated into all the teams.” 
 
The role sustainability managers play within engineering consultancies is particularly substantial in 
alleviating information asymmetries. Poor sustainability integration or misaligned goals can be slightly 
mitigated with the guidance of sustainability managers. Without their expertise, engineering 
consultancies risk sustainability implementation gaps, particularly in projects. 
5.3.2 Misaligned interests and commitments to sustainability  
 
Central issues found in principal-agent theory are the divided interests between principals and agents 
(Greiner & Sun, 2021). The empirical data showed misalignments emerging both internally between 
corporate leadership and project teams and between consultants and clients. Internally, there is a strong 
drive and communication around sustainability through corporate strategies but projects are inclined to 
prioritise timelines and budgets. Participant 1 (P1) depicted the decoupling between initiatives and 
implementation: 
 
“the fact we have a sustainability strategy that states that we should, that we should be 
proactive and implement sustainability in all our projects.” 
 
However, Participant 1 (P1) added: 
 
“[project managers] they would say we don’t have time for this, we don’t have budget for it.” 
 
This disarray highlights the principal-agent relationships, where corporate leadership (principal) are 
attempting to drive corporate sustainability efforts while project managers (agents) tend to concentrate 
on project constraints such as time and budget. Participant 2 (P2) further adds to this: 
 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
“And again, a project manager usually is not very keen because they see their general 
commitments, which is, again, time and money.” 
 
These insights reveal behaviours that project managers are inclined towards, where they act in their own 
project interests. Their interests lie in delivering projects for clients instead of driving sustainability, 
possibly seeing sustainability as an option rather than a requirement. This sentiment was also shared by 
Participant 4 (P4): 
 
“do you actually use the time to figure out what is actually meant by the strategy? […] No, you 
just do as you always do” 
 
Externally, project managers can also find themselves in-between two principals (corporate leadership 
and clients) who are, ultimately, after the same thing as shared by Participant 3 (P3): 
 
“they [engineering consultancy and client] of course, they care about the same thing which is 
money because you are a business” 
 
This was reflected by Participant 4 (P4), sharing similar insights: 
 
“of course is very focused on economy and when something gets too expensive, it’ll always 
come down to, well, what is the cheaper solution?” 
 
Additionally, time and budget constraints appear when faced with client relationships and balancing 
project goals with budget as explained by Participant 3 (P3): 
 
“client pays for a few consultants to do some work for a few months, it’s very hard there for the 
project manager to also have a lot of extra time to assess sustainability of the project” 
 
Within the wider industry, interests are also misaligned, further complicating sustainability initiatives 
and implementation. Participant 6 (P6) shared: 
 
“The value chain in the building industry is divided. All actors will work to serve their own 
interests as best as possible.” 
 
These insights reveal the complexities in multi-principal relationships and exemplify the need for 
aligned interests and initiatives to drive sustainability goals. Without, sustainability implementation 
becomes fractured and inconsistent throughout all projects and departments. 
 
5.3.3 Monitoring and accountability systems 
 
A major barrier to productive sustainability implementation reflected findings in literature surrounding 
principal-agent theory. The theory suggests that without effective monitoring systems in place, agents 
either fail to act completely, or behave opportunistically (Pratt & Zeckhauser, 1985). This phenomenon, 
also known as moral hazard, emerged as a significant obstacle when faced with integrating sustainability 
into projects. In the empirical data, moral hazard appeared in projects where sustainability is 
deprioritised due to lack of accountability or consequence. 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
Participant 1 (P1) explained the lack of accountability within the organisation: 
 
“I’m not employed in any sustainability department. So whatever I do on behalf of 
implementing sustainability, I don’t think that will be seen. That would not be registered 
anywhere.” 
 
“the way it’s measured is on the resources. You know, the people and the hours of sustainability 
people that’s put into a project.” 
 
Participant 4 (P4) echoes this, linking lack of accountability as a barrier to sustainability, saying: 
 
“and if no one is checking upon it, then it’s difficult.” 
 
The lack of performance metrics related to sustainability ultimately restricts the scope of its 
implementation. If performance metrics are solely focused on time and budget, no incentives incline 
project teams to prioritise non-financial factors. Participant 2 (P2) emphasises: 
 
“So if you are not able to optimise or touch upon these two aspects [time and money], they 
[client and project manager] won’t listen.” 
 
As a result, the lack of incentive leads to personal motivation of employees becoming an important 
driver for sustainability. Participant 2 (P2) describes personal motivation leading the work in integrating 
sustainability: 
 
“I used it [sustainability managers] because I was interested in developing this work. But 
nobody tells you that you have to” 
 
“Even though the sustainability agenda is quite strong at [anonymised company], this shows 
you that that doesn’t matter. It’s more a personal mindset. People are driving this.” 
 
Furthermore, the job role at an engineering consultancy highly influences the level of interest around 
sustainability. Participant 3 (P3), a sustainability manager, explains how “sustainability really drives 
my work […] and it’s really part of what I do”. Consequently, sustainability managers within 
engineering consultancies play a role in driving interest around the topic amongst employees and project 
teams. Participant 3 (P3) adds: 
 
“[we] make sure that we highlight the projects that are advanced within sustainability, so that 
people get inspired to work on those types of projects […] so that they’re passionate about it 
personally as well” 
 
Findings reveal how the presence of sustainability managers on the client side also incentives clients to 
adopt sustainability measures in projects. As Participant 1 (P1) highlights:  
 
“But on the client side, I think what is really important there is if they have a sustainability 
manager, to get this person on board and if this person has a voice then that's really, really, 
helpful […] else you'll be met by a lot of stakeholders on the client side who doesn't know 
anything and who doesn't want to risk anything” 
 
==PAGE:33==
 
33 
Although Greiner (2021) explains how intrinsic motivation can be an important driver, overly relying 
on it can create diverging project sustainability outcomes. Certain agents can become figureheads in 
sustainability, without it being their task. Inconsistent operations across sustainability highlights the 
importance of accountability to promote sustainability goals within organisations.  
5.3.4 Strategies for influence and escalation  
 
Although structural barriers exist within the complex ecosystem of engineering consultancies, 
sustainability managers and project managers utilise strategies to promote sustainability initiatives. 
Strategies often include addressing hierarchies, escalation of issues and underlining the importance of 
regulations. These efforts reveal the initiatives agents employ to reduce information asymmetries and 
adverse selection. Participant 1 (P1) explained how lack of engagement faced from the client project 
manager is often addressed through hierarchies: 
 
“if the client says: “No, I don’t want to talk about sustainability”. Then we have a problem […] 
and I will mention to my managers and say: “We have a problem here, could you please take 
that with the client’s project manager”. And then they get a good talk about it […] So you’ll 
just take it a level above.”  
 
Insights shared by Participant 1 (P1) reveal the intricacies that sustainability advocates within the 
organisation face and how interests are often aligned through chain of command. On the other hand, 
sustainability professionals directly decline working on projects that lack sustainability aspirations. 
Participant 4 (P4) states: 
 
“I’m luckily at that position where I try to say “no” to that project because it’s no fun for me 
to be on, to be honest” 
 
“sometimes I choose not do the projects because it’s not in line with my values” 
 
The practice of using professional discretion in organisations suggest a degree of individual agency, 
particularly for senior staff members who possess more leverage. Moreover, external regulations 
imposed on the industry also play a role in ensuring a minimum level of sustainability. As Participant 4 
(P4) explains: 
 
“So for those who are not ambitious, there are at least some minimum requirements which they 
have to meet. And it can sometimes be a battle but there’s not anything they can do about it.”  
 
Therefore, certain sustainability certifications and regulations set by governments act as reinforcement 
in multi-principal relationships. Through principal-agent theory, the findings reveal crucial barriers that 
impede sustainability implementation within engineering consultancies, namely exploring information 
asymmetries, conflicting interests and lack of monitoring systems and incentives. When considered 
together with stakeholder theory, a fuller picture emerges. On the one hand, principal-agent theory 
supports findings of how misalignments between corporate leadership (principals), clients (principals) 
and project managers (agents) lead to implementation gaps. On the other hand, stakeholder theory 
explains the wider ecosystem of groups and individuals involved who often compete against diverging 
interests. The findings evidence that agents often work for multiple principals and are required to 
navigate corporate strategies with stakeholder requirements and project goals. Therefore, the two 
theories support the notion that sustainability implementation within engineering consultancies is not 
==PAGE:34==
 
34 
driven by one single actor but by several stakeholders with varying levels of power and knowledge and 
differing priorities. 
 
5.4 Creating shared value 
 
This section of the analysis utilises concepts from creating shared value (CSV) introduced by Porter 
and Kramer to explore how engineering consultancies are addressing sustainability in their business 
models, projects and with their clients. The creating shared value framework seeks to integrate social 
and environmental measures into businesses’ role in value creation. While interview participants did 
not directly reference the framework, their insights highlight the efforts in embedding sustainability as 
a strategic, reputational and economic imperative. The following section investigates how sustainability 
is considered as a value driver, embedded into operations and approached with clients, echoing concepts 
from the CSV framework. 
5.4.1 Sustainability as value driver 
 
The interview data revealed how sustainability is increasingly considered not only as an immediate 
concern, but as a form of strategy for the engineering consultancy. Porter and Kramer (2011) describe 
CSV as an approach to reconnecting businesses with society, addressing societal needs through business 
strategies. In the interviews, participants interpret the benefits of sustainability, citing market 
differentiation, mitigating risks and long-term business operations which all align with CSV’s 
definitions around value creation. 
 
Participants underscored the need to integrate and implement sustainable strategies early into project 
design phases in order to increase impact and reduce costs. Participant 1 (P1) explained: 
 
“what I always tell our clients is that if you want to implement sustainability, the best idea is to 
address it as early as possible. Because there it has the highest impact and lowest cost” 
 
Participant 2 (P2) adds: “In my approach is to integrate it [sustainability] right away.” 
 
The CSV model proposed by Porter and Kramer also uses risk mitigation as a positive benefit to shared 
value through business strategies. In line with this, Participant 3 (P3) describes how discourse around 
sustainability is framed as risk mitigation: 
 
“and risk is very related to sustainability, so if you show to them how acting unsustainable is a 
huge business risk […]. Risk is a big one, showing how we can decrease risk by implementing 
sustainability” 
 
Across the interviews, it was also noted how sustainability is utilised as a competitive advantage, linking 
it to strategy introduced in value creation. As Participant 3 (P3) highlighted: 
 
“a lot of it is through the tender process of showing that ok in the tender, we can implement 
more sustainability and already there we have kind of pitched why we are a good choice for the 
company [client]” 
 
==PAGE:35==
 
35 
This reinforces how sustainability can not only benefit the client and society but also strengthen the 
firm’s value proposition in the market. Participant 2 (P2) similarly described sustainability as a value 
proposition: 
 
“I believe big companies like [anonymised company] can use the sustainability services a 
driver for more projects or work, so as a driver or as a sales pitch.” 
 
Vargova (2013) explores how Porter and Kramer’s notion of value creation relates to strategies around 
long-term investing for the firm, aligning with integrating sustainability early to achieve the highest 
impact. Additionally, it sees sustainability as a means for value creation for both the firm and the society 
in which it operates (Vargova, 2013). This notion highlights how firms can utilise sustainability to their 
own “corporate advantage” (Yang & Yan, 2020). Furthermore, participants noted that sustainability 
highly influences brand reputation and stakeholder engagement. Investor engagement was illustrated 
by Participant 5 (P5): 
 
“if the company is sustainable, they get more investments and easier loans from banks, then 
credit approval rating goes up” 
 
Participant 3 (P3) depicted brand reputation by describing the benefits of sustainability as a mean for 
employee retention and future recruitment: 
 
“when you are a company that has people that are actively caring about sustainability, you will 
have an easier time getting more people that care about sustainability, so kind of attracting 
those people […] especially the young people. They really care about sustainability and they 
want to work in [anonymised company].” 
 
These insights reflect CSV’s approach to creating economic value by integrating sustainability into 
businesses and addressing societal needs (Porter & Kramer, 2011). Another key insight that reflects 
CSV’s approach to integrating value creation into businesses is related to how organisations implement 
sustainability internally. Both Participant 1 (P1) and Participant 3 (P3) describe the organisational 
structure as being divided into “sustainability consulting” and “strategic sustainability”. Participant 1 
(P1) elaborated: 
 
“There’s one area called sustainability consultancy, that’s mostly related to buildings. And then 
we also have strategic sustainability which is more on a strategic level.” 
 
Participant 3 (P3) expanded on the role of the sustainability department within the organisation: 
 
“We [sustainability department] are making sure that we are seeing the sustainable effects 
across the company and then also making sure that for the future we will also have a 
sustainability strategy. The strategy gets developed by the strategy team […] and then the work 
of [the] sustainability team is to make sure that then gets translated throughout the company 
and to also the people that work on projects” 
 
This highlights how sustainability is integrated at multiple levels in an organisation. As described by 
Porter and Kramer (2011), CSV drives firms to re-structure internal systems to create value over time, 
moving beyond traditional CSR teams to embedding sustainability in all departments. However, risks 
were also pointed out with integrating sustainability and using it as a strategic advantage, reflecting 
==PAGE:36==
 
36 
criticism around CSV as well. Crane et al. (2014) perpetuate that while CSV promotes positive business 
strategies, it can often disregard systemic issues and encourage greenwashing practices as highlighted 
by Participant 5 (P5): 
 
“greenwashing is also a major problem nowadays […] they say some stuff but in reality that 
doesn’t reflect in their work. So this is a major problem” 
 
As the empirical data demonstrates, sustainability is increasingly being used as a value driver in 
engineering consultancies. Early integration into projects, promoting innovation and stakeholder 
engagements show how sustainability is moving economic and societal goals. Although participants did 
not directly reference the CSV concept, their insights suggest that firms are integrating elements of CSV 
into their organisational structures and business strategies. However, challenges remain around practical 
approaches to projects and incorporating shared value to achieve long-term, sustainable, success.   
5.4.2 Tools to drive sustainability innovation 
 
Participants also described a large range of tools and workshops utilised to embed sustainability into 
project team strategies and client interactions. Sustainability assessments play a key role in the bidding 
process, where projects are filtered for their sustainability impacts. Participant 3 (P3) noted the 
increasing use of sustainability assessments within the organisation: 
 
“we’re expanding the way that we assess projects that we do on sustainability, so making sure 
we have more data about their sustainability impact and how fit they are for being sustainable” 
 
Participant 4 (P4) echoed similar insights: 
 
“also ruling out some projects which are not having any sustainable approach […] we have 
some minimum requirements which we, as a company, have chosen. So when we send out a 
proposal for a new project, we screen it for sustainability” 
 
This suggests aspects of CSV in which sustainability assessments can lead companies to discover new 
opportunities and recognise new markets (Porter & Kramer, 2011). The concept of CSV also explores 
how business practices interact with their value chain, shifting towards more localised approaches 
(Vargova, 2013). These approaches include transforming the ways in which organisations collaborate 
with customers, local suppliers and communities when addressing environmental, social and 
governance (ESG issues). Similar approaches can be seen from the empirical data where participants 
described the various tools and workshops utilised with clients and employees to inform sustainable 
strategies. Workshops with employees were used to inform sustainability strategies as depicted by 
Participant 4 (P4): 
 
“the entire aim of this [workshop] was we wanted everyone in the building sector to have the 
same knowledge, they had to know what is your minimum requirements like what do we need 
to highlight when we are on a project” 
 
This was further expanded on, describing how additional workshops focused on developing new ways 
of developing sustainability strategies and methodologies. New insights from the workshop reflect CSV 
literature which describes how businesses transform internally to respond to new economic and social 
value creation (Vargova, 2013). As Participant 4 (P4) states:

37 
 
“of course we had a workshop saying “so what could we do? What do we need to develop? 
Where do we need new competences?” 
 
Sustainability integration into projects was crucial within the organisation to create shared value and 
shift value chains as described by Participant 4 (P4): “we wanted to do with that workshop is give the 
power to the people actually doing the project”. Workshops are additionally used throughout the project 
with clients. Participant 2 (P2) outlined a workshop held for a client: 
 
“We had a workshop […] they were very impressed by our presentation, how you can implement 
sustainability from the start”  
 
Additionally, various sustainability-related tools are implemented when interacting with clients. 
Participant 1 (P1) explained how two different tools are utilised within projects to broaden sustainability

“[anonymised company] has developed something that we call the [anonymised] and the

The use of tools was used to broaden clients’ views on sustainability topics as explained by Participant

“So these are the two steps that I use because you never know with your client, you know, at

The concept of CSV can also be seen in the way engineering consultancies adopt design thinking and 
innovation to project approaches. This is highlighted in CSV literature which explains the role of 
creating shared value to explore new market opportunities and differentiation (Porter & Kramer, 2011). 
Participant 1 (P1) revealed the way of working in the team: 
 
“So I use this approach, you know, with design thinking, strategic design. This is very important

Participant 2 (P2) echoed these insights, illustrating the importance of innovation in driving project

“These new approaches provide them new ways of thinking, so they will have to contact new 
suppliers, look into new materials, new instruments. So it’s a way that engineers also use to 
improve and update themselves” 
 
These insights further suggest CSV and how innovation can shift value chains within the industry, 
requiring new approaches to address sustainability issues within projects.

5.4.3 Client partnerships  
 
Participants also revealed how aspects of CSV are not solely led by the firms themselves but also in co-
creation with clients. This aligns closely with CSV which suggests transforming value chains to include 
more stakeholders (Porter & Kramer, 2011). Participant 3 (P3) shared: 
 
“we need to justify to the people, to the client, that that [sustainability] will also give them a 
benefit because they will have a pilot project that they can be proud of, that they can highlight, 
that they can also put in their annual reports etc” 
 
This exemplifies the role of sustainability as not only a company benefit but also reputational value for 
the client, strengthening the arguments of CSV in creating shared value. Participant 1 (P1) additionally 
reflected on this and how sustainability can create shared benefits: 
 
“But if they [clients] come with an open mind, we train them and give them this knowledge, so 
that they can make better decisions” 
 
Participant 4 (P4) described how advising clients on sustainability transforms value chains, benefiting 
the client as well: 
 
“they [client] wanted to use it [solution] to improve how they operate the buildings and how 
they maintain them […] there is actually a big interest about actually using tools available to 
drive [sustainability]” 
 
Additionally, Participant 4 (P4) outlined how co-creation has led successful client outcomes: 
 
“the entire project is very driven by some of these [sustainability] requirements which are set 
in collaboration with us” 
 
This was also seen in another client project where sustainable approaches were integrated into client’s 
strategies: 
 
“They [client] really use it as a way of driving the projects but also looking back at older 
projects, seeing how they could do better” 
 
Therefore, the interview data shows shifts towards consultancies viewing clients not just as project 
outcomes but as co-creators in creating long-term value. This ultimately helps redefine how 
sustainability success is measured in projects in the AEC industry.  
 
5.5 Integrating theories  
 
The previous sections explored corporate sustainability implementation through distinct theoretical 
frameworks. However, interview data also underlined themes that illustrate the relationships between 
all three theories, providing an integrated view on how sustainability is addressed within the AEC 
industry. Thus, the final section of the analysis introduces the role of regulatory frameworks and the 
impact of organisational sizes. Together, they both influence sustainability implementation. For 
example, regulatory bodies act not only as stakeholders but also as drivers of creating shared value. 
==PAGE:39==
 
39 
Likewise, the scale of firms determines internal and external stakeholder relations and how innovation 
is driven within organisations.  
5.5.1 Role of regulations and policies 
 
The role of regulatory frameworks in corporate sustainability implementation is crucial to address as it 
has strong implication in shaping the AEC industry. Across interviews, participants mentioned 
regulations, local policies and European Union (EU) legislation as driving forces behind sustainability 
across the industry. These insights reflect wider literature on the importance of external monitoring and 
organisational structures in shifting business practices towards sustainable outcomes  (Crane et al., 
2014; Porter & Kramer, 2011). EU-level legislation, such as the EU Taxonomy and Corporate 
Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and building regulations were frequently mentioned as 
drivers of sustainability for firms and clients. These were used with clients with especially low 
sustainability ambitions as cited by Participant 4:  
 
“So even when you have a client who does not have high ambitions, the building regulations in 
Denmark does set standards […]. So for those who are not ambitious, there are at least some 
minimum requirements which they have to meet.” 
 
This insight reflects principal-agent theory which describes the importance of external monitoring in 
reducing moral hazard (Sha, 2019). In this example, various regulations act as reinforcers to ensure 
principals and agents meet sustainability ambitions, even when internal motivation or capacity is 
lacking. When examining these insights from a stakeholder perspective, regulatory institutions are 
important stakeholders to consider, often influencing sustainability mandates and incentives. Participant 
4 (P4) noted: 
 
“In the building industry, I would say we are really lucky because we are getting more and more 
requirements […]. They [municipalities] are requiring to see documentation if they are aligned 
with EU Taxonomy etc. […] they [clients] are not able to rent out anything because it’s not 
certified” 
 
In these cases, regulatory stakeholders drive clients towards more sustainability strategies, aiding 
engineering consultancies to drive the sustainability agenda. Moreover, the empirical data supports 
literature by Porter and Kramer (2011) who highlight the crucial role governments play in reinforcing 
regulations that promote shared value and drive innovation. Other reporting mechanisms such as 
emissions reporting aid in mapping social and environmental impacts across value chains. Participant 
6 (P6) discussed how emissions are widely tracked, while Scope 3 emissions remain a challenging task: 
 
“[Scope 3 emissions] the most complex and challenging data point […] companies are not 
directly involved in their emissions but it’s still part of the overall supply chain which they have 
to map and show the authorities.” 
 
Interviews also revealed the regional differences in regulatory frameworks and adherence. Participant 
2 (P2) observed: 
 
“Europe is stricter now […] and Asia is non-existent. I would say it’s a Western thing at the 
moment” 
 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
Variation in regulatory strictness illustrates how sustainability is context-specific and highly dependent 
on markets. It also echoes critique by Wieland (2017) that CSV fails to address the influence of politics 
and policymaking in shaping societal norms. This suggests that sustainability implementation and 
integration is not a universal approach and is often market-driven, determining what is required and 
possible. Furthermore, current market conditions dictate the scope of sustainability and how 
policymakers drive sustainability ambitions. As described by Participant 3 (P3): 
 
“currently, the built environment market is very struggling […] and spending money, from 
municipalities and government, on huge schools and buildings and parks and whatnot, is not 
the top priority” 
 
5.5.2 Organisational size and agility 
 
The scale and structure of firms within the AEC industry influence how sustainability is considered, 
prioritised and implemented. Interview insights evidenced a clear difference between large 
multinational engineering consultancies and smaller firms operating in the industry, particularly in how 
they understand and integrate sustainability into their operations. The difference within firms further 
influences the ability to drive sustainable development within the industry.  
 
When considering principal-agency theory, larger organisations often face information asymmetries and 
misalignment between corporate leadership and project practices. In larger firms, sustainability 
strategies are frequently managed at corporate level but may not be practically implementable for 
project teams and project managers (Lu & Zhang, 2016). Participant 1 (P1) explains the complex nature 
of large engineering consultancies and their sustainability limitations: 
 
“big consulting companies, they don’t stand a change of moving forward with sustainability. 
They will not be the frontrunners because it’s such a big machinery and we are so dependant 
on clients” 
 
The bureaucracy and client dependency faced in large firms often restricts the scope and frequency of 
sustainability ambitions. This complexity mirrors moral hazard faced in engineering consultancies with 
challenging principal-agent relationships where individuals can deprioritise sustainability goals when 
faced with client demands or the nature of the project. In contrast, the interviews revealed the agility of 
smaller firms compared to large consultancies. Exhibiting more entrepreneurial behaviour, smaller 
firms align more closely with ideas related to CSV (reference). As Participant 2 (P2) notes: 
 
“Smaller companies can actually use it [sustainability] to drive innovation. That’s what I have 
seen. It’s more like, let’s find a different solution.” 
 
However, smaller firms face resource constraints due to their sizes. As Participant 5 (P5) describes: 
 
“smaller companies […] only hire one or two people for sustainability […] required to do 
everything” […]. Bigger companies enjoy the luxury of bigger teams” 
 
This evidences that larger firms enjoy larger influence and capacity to implement sustainability. 
However, multinational engineering firms face inertia as Participant 3 (P3) highlights: 
 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
“we’re a consultancy and, you know, we want to do sustainable work but at the end of the day, 
the consultants need to get paid for their work because we are not running an NGO.” 
 
This dilemma, often faced in professional services, where billable hours and client-centred projects take 
priority, underlines the trade-offs between business and sustainability. Stakeholder theory can therefore 
be used to explain the complex environment large engineering consultancies face. Large firms have 
more stakeholder groups which need to be managed and often with conflicting interests (Oke & 
Aigbavboa, 2017). Ultimately, as Participant 1 (P1) observes, this challenging environment has pushed 
professionals out of larger firms altogether: 
 
“I know several people who have left these big consulting companies to simply make their own 
small company because that makes more sense and they are more agile.” 
 
This increasing trend can suggest that if large firms are unable to respond to sustainability concerns of 
employees, they risk losing talent to smaller, more agile, organisations. Consequently, this can have 
long-term consequences on value creation, impacting knowledge sharing, innovation and 
competitiveness. Therefore, it can be said that organisational size and agility impacts how firms can 
implement sustainability strategies and respond to stakeholder needs. Large consultancies profit from 
more resources and access to larger projects and clients but often lack practical sustainability 
implementation. Although faced with resource constraints, smaller firms can drive innovation and 
promote entrepreneurial thinking, further supporting sustainability ambitions. As a result, both, large 
and smaller firms, have diverse advantages, suggesting the need for collaboration.  
6 Discussion 
 
6.1 
Theory findings 
6.1.1 Stakeholder theory 
Employing stakeholder theory to examine how engineering consultancies address various actors 
provides itself as a useful framework. At the same time, the empirical data suggests that stakeholder 
relations are imbalanced, suggesting gaps in how sustainability is achieved in organisations. Findings 
reveal how clients appear as the powerful stakeholders, dictating project requirements through budgets 
and intentions. Project managers are then driven to prioritise certain stakeholder engagements, 
emphasising those with the most influence.  
 
The data highlighted that prioritising stakeholders is highly dependent on context. In instances where 
clients exhibited low sustainability ambitions, project managers count on external stakeholder such as 
regulatory bodies or internal mechanisms such as corporate escalation to implement sustainability goals. 
In other cases where clients showcased enthusiasm, engaging stakeholders went beyond to also actively 
include end users and wider societal interests. This illustrates the power dynamics in the AEC industry 
while also challenging the ideas behind stakeholder theory that suggests that value alignment is 
attainable. 
 
6.1.2 Principal agent theory 
The use of principal-agent theory in engineering consultancies informs the complexities in integrating 
sustainability goals. Compared to traditional principal-agent relations, project teams often employ a 
==PAGE:42==
 
42 
dual agency role, functioning as agents to both principals, corporate leadership and clients. Findings 
revealed how this relationship leads to misalignments with project managers balancing diverging 
expectations. Furthermore, the data showed how project managers are often required to not only operate 
within billable hours but also meet client requirements, frequently sacrificing internal corporate 
sustainability aims.  
 
Another substantial theme was the navigation of misaligned incentives. Although project managers 
were aware of corporate sustainability strategies, these are frequently set aside due to time constraints 
and lack of accountability. This reveals a wider organisational agency issue where short-term incentives 
such as client deliverables, time and cost are put in place for projects managers while corporate 
sustainability remains at large. As a result, corporate leadership may involuntarily hold up sustainability 
integration by not implementing cohesive incentive structures. 
 
Consequently, behavioural agency theory provides a useful perspective as proposed by Greiner (2021). 
The theory acknowledges that agents are also driven by other motivations such as personal values and 
purpose. This supports data that highlighted how sustainability in projects was often driven by personal 
motivation and individual interest rather than corporate requirements. This suggests that corporate 
sustainability, if purposefully driven in organisational culture, can promote better agency relationships. 
In contrast, overly relying on personal motivation can lead to implementation gaps throughout the 
organisation and projects. 
 
The data revealed that sustainability managers play a key role in addressing these challenges. Often 
placed between corporate leadership and project teams, these managers often employ roles that 
coordinate agency alignments. Through supporting project teams and aiding in strategy creation, they 
mediate between agents and principals. Playing an important role in reducing information asymmetries, 
sustainability managers place themselves as possible actors in mitigating further agency problems. 
Together, the findings show how sustainability within engineering consultancies is largely dictated by 
complex agency relations, incentives and culture.  
 
6.1.3 Creating shared value 
While the concept of creating shared value (CSV) was not explicitly referenced in the data, the findings 
reveal that some aspects of the framework are integrated in practice. As such, some participants framed 
sustainability as a competitive advantage, risk mitigating and a source of innovation. These closely align 
with the CSV framework introduced by Porter and Kramer (2011). This raises questions around the 
theoretical value of the framework and whether the framework requires formal implementation in 
organisations if it is already being integrated.  
 
The data also questioned the principles of CSV within organisations. Participants frequently referred to 
projects being the most powerful point of impact, pointing out stakeholder collaboration, design choices 
and measurable sustainability outcomes. This is particularly prominent in engineering consultancies 
and the broader AEC industry where projects become the grounds for shared value creation. 
Consequently, this raises important considerations for corporate sustainability in the industry and 
whether it should be formulated around projects. 
 
Traditional corporate strategies often employ top-down implementation whereas project-based CSV 
could offer opportunities to consolidate sustainability efforts into project value chains. This shift could 
affect how engineering consultancies measure performance and impact and organisational incentives. 
==PAGE:43==
 
43 
Additionally, project-based CSV could allow for differences in client requirements, regulatory bodies 
and project types. However, this approach adds risk as the lack of comprehensive organisational 
structures could lead to dispersed implementation and over-reliance on project teams to deliver on 
sustainability. Therefore, strong internal systems are required to build strong relations with 
sustainability managers across departments and develop appropriate incentive structures. The findings 
propose that the concepts around CSV are relevant and current, providing a possible framework for 
implementing sustainability in engineering consultancies.   
 
6.1.4 Integrating theories 
Although the findings indicate that stakeholder theory, principal-agent theory and creating shared value 
offer valuable insights in the analysis, their shared context provides ground when exploring how wider 
factors influence sustainability implementation in engineering consultancies. The role of regulations 
proved itself as a crucial mechanism to responding to agency challenges, especially when faced with 
clients with low ambitions towards sustainability. However, overly relying on regulations can also lead 
to a check listing activity where innovation remains unapproachable. This echoes limitations in CSV 
theory where value creation and innovation are highlighted but approaching highly regulated 
environments, where the minimum is often preferred, is given minimal consideration.  
 
Additionally, macroeconomic conditions appeared as an important enabler and barrier for sustainability 
implementation. Participants described instances where less mature markets face more challenging 
sustainability integration despite organisational drive. This presents a significant implementation gap 
in the theories as external economic conditions are not considered when examining stakeholder 
engagements and sustainability. Furthermore, findings revealed notable differences between larger and 
smaller firms within the AEC industry when addressing sustainability. Where large engineering 
consultancies get access to major projects and clients, they also lack the drive for innovation due to 
client reliance and bureaucracy. On the other hand, smaller firms in the industry were highlighted as 
more entrepreneurial and responsive, fostering innovation and sustainability. However, they face 
constraints with resources and influence, limiting their impact scale. The empirical data therefore 
suggests a possible point of collaboration between large and small firms. Through CSV theory, this 
collaboration proposes avenues for partnerships within the industry and knowledge and resource sharing 
amongst stakeholders.  
 
6.2 Limitations 
 
While this thesis provides qualitative insights grounded in theory, it does present itself with several 
limitations. The study is based on a small number of professionals working in large engineering 
consultancies or in sustainability-related professions in Denmark. The research is highly context-
specific, shaped by the experiences of Denmark’s market and regulatory scope. Therefore, it does not 
cover the broader implications multinational engineering consultancies have when engaging with 
various stakeholders across continents where approaches to sustainability may largely vary.  
 
The participants also solely include mid-level and senior professionals and does not include 
professionals working on boards or perspectives of clients, regulatory bodies and other stakeholders 
across the value chain. The structure of interviews also limits additional insights into how sustainability 
is practiced at a larger scale in project teams. Although stakeholder theory, principal-agent theory and 
creating shared value supports the analytical framework of the study, it does not take into consideration 
==PAGE:44==
 
44 
other theoretical concepts. This results in possible missed opportunities to explore complex institutional 
factors affecting sustainability. Nonetheless, the findings of this study provide valuable insights into the 
complexities surrounding sustainability implementation in project-based organisation in the AEC 
industry. 
 
 
6.3 Future research  
 
The findings also reveal several areas for future research. Firstly, while environmental sustainability 
was largely covered by participants, social sustainability remained less considered within organisations. 
Thus, additional research could examine how social sustainability is considered within engineering 
consultancies. Exploring social indicators such as community impact and ethical considerations could 
further explain how sustainability is embedded in organisations. This study was also conducted within 
the Danish AEC industry, however, the empirical data showed how sustainability implementation is 
largely dictated by project typologies and sector. Additional research into different sectors in the 
industry, particularly studying the difference between highly regulated and less regulated sectors, can 
provide more thorough insights into the challenges surrounding sustainability. 
 
While the study involved professionals working in Denmark, future research into multinational 
engineering consultancies in different regions can be beneficial to understand the full context in the 
global industry. Lastly, the interview data highlighted the significant differences between smaller and 
larger firms. Additional research into the partnerships between different sizes of firms within the 
industry could better inform sustainability strategies.  
 
7 Conclusion  
 
This thesis aimed to comprehend how corporate sustainability is addressed and acted upon in 
engineering consultancies within the AEC industry. Utilising qualitative research approaches tied in 
stakeholder theory, principal-agent theory and concepts from creating shared value, the study uncovered 
multiple challenges and insights. Initial insights describe sustainability as a largely strategic objective 
but its implementation into projects remains at large. Challenges such as balancing project requirements 
and client priorities, internal misalignments and time constraints were highlighted as significant 
barriers. Stakeholder relations, especially between clients and consultancies, contribute significantly to 
sustainability implementation with project managers navigating various actors. In addition, principal-
agent dynamics evidence how information asymmetries and lack of accountability shape sustainability 
from strategies into projects. The findings unveil the need for clearer frameworks and mechanisms for 
corporate sustainability, with a focus on creating long-term value for all stakeholders. This requires 
alignment between project teams and corporate leadership and further promoting client collaboration 
with shared sustainability goals. Coordinating corporate strategies with project practices is therefore 
essential to drive sustainable development within the AEC industry.
