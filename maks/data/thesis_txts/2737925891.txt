Abstract
Robotics is a promising direction for Search and Rescue missions that helps reduce re-
sponse time and risks to humans. Legged robots are especially fitted, as they offer the
ability to traverse challenging terrain. However, classic control often fails with slippery or
uneven surfaces, thus novel methods use Reinforcement Learning for more performant
control. This thesis aims to develop a Reinforcement Learning method for robust control
of the quadruped robot Unitree Go1, which will be used at Virtual Vehicle company to
deploy in Search and Rescue missions.
I present a solution for learning a locomotion controller in simulation that can to traverse
many challenging terrains. The agent is first taught with knowledge about surrounding
terrain, and later its knowledge is transferred to a blind agent, that can be easily deployed
on the real robot. The method is then compared to another implementation from the
literature.
The results of the evaluation in simulation show improvements in robustness compared
to the baseline, achieving 0.48 falls/h on rough terrain and 1.26 falls/min on rail tracks.
However, the model could not transfer correctly to the real robot and was prone to falls.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
One of the main purposes of robots is to replace humans in risky environments to protect
their health and save lives. Search and Rescue (SAR) missions are an excellent example
of hazardous conditions where there is a big potential for robots’ help [1]. The usage of
robots removes people from direct contact with danger. It can also improve search efforts,
as it has the potential to be easier and cheaper to train a robot operator than to train a
human in rescue.
SAR missions are also often characterized by difficult terrains [2]. Environments can
range from urban buildings to underground tunnels to bodies of water. In this project,
I will focus on relatively flat urban areas and railway tunnels. Even in those relatively
simple scenarios, the terrain can be challenging as there might be obstacles or holes in
the ground, especially in the SAR context. Legged robots are known for their ability to
traverse uneven terrain and are usually more suited for this kind of mission. Contrary to
the wheeled robots, they can simply step over holes or climb onto obstacles. They are
also more natural for human-centered environments [3].
However, control for legged robots is significantly more difficult than for wheeled robots.
Wheeled robots are usually statically stable, meaning they can be stopped at any point
in their motion and they will not tip over. It is also easy to model their kinematics and
thus implement trajectory planning and control [4]. Legged robots, on the other hand, are
much more complex. It is not obvious which gait should be used in which conditions and it
might be difficult to maintain stability during movement. Modeling is also difficult, as robot
dynamics can become a significant factor during rapid movement [5].
In recent years, Reinforcement Learning (RL) has gained popularity as a method to create
controls for systems through learning. Instead of explicitly crafting a control system, RL
algorithms learn to control the system by interacting with it and trying to maximize the re-
ward they receive [6]. They must be able to perceive long time horizons to choose actions
that will pay off only after a long time. Their main advantage is that there is no need to
model the system and they can handle very nonlinear cases with complex dynamics, e.g.
Atari games [7], Go [8], etc., often achieving results better than humans. One of the main
disadvantages is unstable learning behavior, where the algorithm often fails to converge
to optimal control and a lot of hyper-parameter tuning is required. Another drawback is
poor sample efficiency, meaning that algorithms typically require a lot of interactions with
the environment to properly learn control.
1.2
Problem statement and research questions
Most of the recent studies focus on achieving decent performance at high speeds or in
difficult tasks but not many focus on high robustness in simpler conditions. In this project,
the expected environments are moderately challenging, i.e. small slope <15◦, gravel, and
railway tracks. However, SAR missions require very high reliability of the system so the
robot does not fall unexpectedly. Therefore, this thesis will focus on measuring metrics
related to the locomotion robustness of the baseline built-in controller and on trying to
develop a better RL-based one.

1.3
Project requirements
In consultation with the Virtual Vehicle company, the following list of requirements was
developed:
• The controller loop must operate at 50 Hz.
• The controller shall be able to work in conditions where vision might be impaired,
e.g. due to smoke or dust. If the policy is using exteroception (e.g. cameras), it
must be able to handle noisy data. For example, Miki et al. used a “belief encoder”
that fuses proprioceptive and exteroceptive data, and when depth measurements
are unreliable, it uses proprioception for correction [9].
• The controller shall be able to work in an unstructured environment, e.g. it should
not model it with boxes or pallets. For example, Hoeller et al. assumed only boxes
are in the environment and tried to reconstruct them [10].
• The robot shall avoid damage to itself and the environment.
• The robot shall be able to maintain stability in simulation in the presence of external
disturbances. The goal is to improve robustness during training. For example, Lee
et al. applied up to 50 N external force on the robot [11].
• The robot shall be able to navigate and walk in the following environments: flat
terrain withfriction coefficients from wet to normal road and slope < 15◦), and uneven
terrain like sand, gravel, and rails with slope < 15◦.

Theory background
This section describes the general theory behind Reinforcement Learning, which is the
main method used in this thesis.
2.1
Markov Decision Process
Robot walking can be thought of as an agent acting in the environment. A useful descrip-
tion of this problem is Markov Decision Process (MDP). In this setting, an agent (also
often called actor) is interacting with the environment (also often called the world) [6].
The interactions are happening at discrete time steps t = 0, 1, 2, ..., T. The agent ob-
serves a state st ∈S (S - state space) and a reward rt ∈R and executes an action at ∈A
(A - action space). Then, it perceives a new state st+1 and a new reward rt+1, which are
partially influenced by its action. For example, in a video game, a state could be the dis-
played pixels, the reward could be the gained score and actions are inputs to the game.
Figure 2.1 shows the diagram of the interactions. A series of interactions ending at a time
step T is called an episode.
For the process to be Markovian, the next state st+1 must depend only on the previous
state st and action at, not on any older ones. This means, that the state st must completely
describe the state of the environment. From this, the state-transition probabilities (also
known as environment dynamics) can be defined as p(s′|s, a).
However, sometimes the whole state cannot be seen, only some part of this. This makes
the problem a Partially Observed Markov Decision Process (POMDP). Instead of states st,
observations ot are perceived, which may not give the full picture and older observations
might be needed to get more information. For example, if object A starts obscuring object
B in front of the camera, it can be deduced that object B still exists from older frames.
In literature, observations are often called and denoted as states as a practical shortcut.
Most of the time it does not cause any problems, as in practice MDPs and POMDPs
behave in a very similar way.
The policy that the agent uses to choose actions is denoted as πθ(at|st). It is a probability
distribution over actions a ∈A, conditioned on the current state st, parameterized by θ,
which can be for example weights in the neural network. Making the policy stochastic
allows the use of probabilities in mathematical proofs. If the policy is deterministic and
will always choose a single action ast in the state st, it can be written as

Two big classes of methods in RL are model-free and model-based, which differ whether
or not the explicit model of the world is used. Model-free techniques try to directly compute
actions from the observed state and they learn optimal actions through trial and error.
Model-based methods try to estimate the environment’s dynamics and use them to plan
optimal actions, similar to Model Predictive Control (MPC).
The biggest advantage of the model-based methods is good sample efficiency. Because
they create the approximation of the environment, they can use it to simulate experience.
However, if the model is not accurate enough, the performance will be poor. They also re-
quire more computation cost in run-time for planning. Model-free methods typically have
opposite characteristics. They learn through trial and error, which means they require
more samples. However, they do not need to model the world and have less computa-
tional overhead in run-time.
Dyna-style methods attempt to merge those two techniques [12]. The algorithm interacts
with the environment and uses this information to update the world and reward models.
It also uses this real experience to update model-free policy. Next, the agent generates
simulated samples from the world model and uses them to update the policy. This method
allows better sample efficiency and better exploration, as it can generate a lot of simulated
experience. It also means it is computationally expensive to train but for inference, it can

2.3
Proximal Policy Optimization
In this project, I am using the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm [13], which is
an on-policy, policy gradient method for solving RL tasks “On-policy” means, it needs to
collect samples using the current policy πθk, with the parameters θk from the current k-th
iteration. It cannot re-use experience from other policies, including from older iterations,
e.g. πθk−1. “Policy gradient method” means, it estimates the gradient of the objective
function ∇θJ(θ) and uses it to update the parameters θ. The crucial part of the PPO is
the clipping of the update so that the new policy is not too different from the previous one,
which improves the stability of the algorithm. The clipping threshold is controlled by the
hyperparameter ϵ and it usually works well with the default value ϵ = 0.2.
Furthermore, PPO uses an actor-critic framework. It has two main components: “actor”,
which represents the policy πθ(a|s), and “critic”, which estimates the value function V πθ
ϕ (s),
parameterized by ϕ. The critic is used by the algorithm to reduce variance in policy up-
dates and stabilize the algorithm. The value function is another model that needs to be
updated to match the observed rewards from the environment.
2.4
Transfer learning
Transfer learning, also known as knowledge distillation, is a process of transferring knowl-
edge from one model to the other. Typically it is used to decrease the model size and
increase speed during inference. However, in reinforcement learning it is often used to
change the modality of the inputs [14]. For example, it is typically difficult to train reinforce-
ment learning directly on depth camera data, as it has very high dimensionality and it is
difficult to infer correlations through trial and error. Training on a simpler representation,
for example, a height map scan, is usually easier. After this “teacher” network is trained,
transfer learning can be used to train the “student” network using supervised learning to
mimic the actions of the “teacher” using different inputs. Typically, the cross-entropy loss
function is used to calculate the loss of the student [14]:

3
Literature review
This section reviews the available literature on quadrupedal locomotion and reinforcement
learning.
3.1
Reinforcement learning in robot control
RL methods suffer from poor sample efficiency, meaning they need many interactions with
the environment to learn. This poses a major obstacle when training control of a real-life
robot because it would mean many days of training. For example, Kalashnikov et al. used
7 real robotic manipulators working in total for 800 hours to achieve object grasping using
an RGB camera [15]. For more complex tasks, the number of training hours required can
be much higher. Furthermore, at the beginning of training, the policy actions are random
which may cause harm to the robot and the environment.
For those reasons, simulation is often used for training the RL model. However, this in-
troduces a sim-to-real gap because not every aspect of the real world can be modeled in
the simulation. Domain randomization is one of the most popular ways to deal with this
discrepancy. It randomly changes some attributes in the simulation, like friction coeffi-
cients or colors, which forces the RL agent to learn to generalize its behavior for different
environments. Andrychowicz et al. trained in simulation a robotic hand to manipulate ob-
jects and were able to directly transfer the policy to the real world [16]. For comparison
with the previous number, they collected about 100 years of simulated experience in 50
real-world hours.
3.2
Legged robot control
Classic methods for legged robot control often use trajectory optimization, which neces-
sitates the use of simplified models, so that the optimization can perform in real time.
Furthermore, those models need to be manually designed and tuned [5]. However, due
to the simplifications, those methods typically do not perform very well on slippery or ir-
regular surfaces [17].
As an alternative, RL has been tried for controlling legged robots. The first successes
in simulation were achieved around 2017 in 3D humanoid control [13]. One of the first
successes in transferring policy learned in simulation to the real quadruped robot was
achieved by Tan et al. in 2018 [18]. Hwangbo et al. compared learned control to the
classical approach and RL achieved better velocity tracking and less power usage [5]. It
was also successfully applied to bipedal robots traversing stairs [19].
Since simulation is needed to train an agent, it is necessary to be able to run it fast and with
a few instances in parallel. Rudin et al. were one of the first that used the NVIDIA Isaac
Gym simulator to collect experience from a few thousand robots running in parallel on a
Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) [20]. This massively parallel approach allowed them to
train a policy capable of walking on medium-difficulty terrain in a matter of minutes using
a single GPU.
However, training the agent in simulation can make it harder to transfer to the real robot
later. Simulation cannot perfectly reflect the real world and each separate robot can have
slightly different dynamics. To circumvent this difficulty, many methods use domain ran-
domization, which varies the parameters of the simulations slightly, so the agent needs
Learning Agile Robotic Locomotion Skills for Search and Rescue Missions
7
==PAGE:20==
to generalize to different circumstances [20] [21]. Margolis et al. used another approach,
where they first train a teacher agent in simulation which observes the dynamic properties
of the robot and the terrain and encodes them to latent space using a neural network [22].
Then, using transfer learning, they teach a “student” to reconstruct this latent space from
the past few robot states and actions. This allows the robot to “learn” the dynamics during
the run.
An alternative to RL is Imitation Learning (IL), which also tries to learn the policy to achieve
a particular task, but instead of interacting with the environment, it is provided with “expert
knowledge” which it is trying to imitate. For example, Peng et al. extracted expert data
from samples of walking dogs using motion capture and from human animators [23]. The
policies were then successfully transferred to a simulated and real robot, which could
perform multiple gaits, dynamic hops, and turns. IL was also used for teaching to perform
difficult tasks like backflip [24] or as a gait “style transfer” [25].
While some of the publications use “blind” systems, i.e. relying only on proprioception
[19] [26], recent works introduced exteroception, usually using depth cameras or lidars.
This allowed controllers to perceive the terrain around them and adapt their gait to obsta-
cles in the environment. Some works managed to train robots to do very agile parkour
moves, like running, jumping, climbing, and crawling [27] [28] [10]. One recent work com-
bined RL with trajectory optimization to achieve impressive robustness in an extremely
challenging terrain [29]. Miki et al. used both proprioception and exteroception to create
a “belief” of the environment [9]. This allows it to “trust” either one or the other source,
depending on the conditions. For example, transparent obstacles are invisible to lidar but
can be “felt” by proprioception. On the other hand, deformable objects like grass would
be present in the lidar data, but proprioception can detect that they deform. Miki et al. this
further, by generating velocity commands based on the voxel occupancy grid in confined
3D spaces [30].
Most publications typically make the robot follow a given velocity command. It usually fits
well with existing frameworks for planners, which send such commands. However, this
may be problematic in cases, where temporary violation of the command is necessary,
e.g. jumping over a gap. Rudin et al. and Zhang et al. presented an alternative approach,
where they define only an endpoint and the time left for the robot to reach it [31] [32]. This
allows the robot to decide about the speed and path it is going to take. Thanks to that,
the robot could execute very dynamic motions, for example walking on thin beams, which
would be nearly impossible with the standard approach.
Schneider et al. introduced another method, guided by the risk sensitivity parameter [33].
It allowed them to change online whether the robot is risk-seeking or risk-avoiding. Avoid-
ing risk might prevent a robot from falling but also stop it from overcoming an obstacle.
This approach is especially interesting in the context of SAR missions, where the opera-
tors generally want the robot to be safe but may sometimes allow some risk if it depends
on the mission’s success.

5.1

5.1.1
Robot locomotion as a reinforcement learning task
The task of the locomotion controller is to receive high-level commands and output low-
level controls to the joints of the robot so that the high-level objective is achieved.
There are several different ways in which high-level commands can expressed. For ex-
ample, an external planner may provide footsteps and the locomotion controller needs to
control the joints to reach those points. Another way is to provide only the endpoint that
should be reached and have the controller fully plan how to achieve it in terms of low-
level controls. In this project however, I will borrow from a popular approach used also in
wheeled robots, in which the commands are the target linear and angular velocity of the
robot’s base. This allows easy integration with other components of the robotic system.
It is also widely used in the Robot Operating System (ROS), see Figure 5.1.
The commands used in training are uniformly re-sampled after a time interval, so the
agent can learn to walk with different velocities.
Table 5.1 shows the parameters of
the sampling. Furthermore, 10 % of the environments were commanded zero velocity
(vx = vy = ωz = 0), and another 10 % were commanded to rotate in place (vx = vy = 0),
so the agent can learn to execute those actions more effectively as they are often en-
countered in real deployments.
It is also worth noting that the locomotion controller is only expected to follow the velocity
command to the best of its abilities. Still, there can be circumstances in which it is not
perfect. In those cases, open-loop control of the robot may drift, and the robot will not
be where it is expected to be. It is a normal phenomenon present in all types of robots.
If that happens, it is the task of the higher-level planners to detect it and send correcting
commands, not the task of the locomotion controller.
To summarize, the RL task of the project is to design a controller, that follows the velocity
commands by executing low-level actions on joints, see Figure 5.2.

5.1.4
Algorithm
For this RL task, I used γ = 0.99, as used in [20]. PPO has only one hyperparameter for
the clipping threshold, but it usually does not require tuning and I used the default value
ϵ = 0.2.
I used the Adam [40] optimizer with the starting learning rate α = 0.001. The learn-
ing rate was adapted during the training to keep the policy changes as big as possible
while keeping them below the threshold. The changes to the policy were calculated using
Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence, and the threshold was set to ϵKL = 0.01. If the KL di-
vergence was below half the threshold, the learning rate was increased. If the divergence
was above twice the threshold, the learning rate was decreased.
The training algorithm’s pseudo-code is shown in Algorithm 1. The strategy for initializing
the policy is described in subsection 5.1.10. The Storage stores the samples from a
given policy πk and returns them in a random permutation for the learning algorithm. The
samples are divided equally into mini-batches, which the PPO processes simultaneously
and updates the policy. In this thesis, I am using nmini_batches = 4. The process repeats
nepochs = 5 times. In a real implementation, the policies do not get replaced at the end, this
distinction was made to emphasize that at each k-th iteration effectively different policies
are used, and during a single iteration only a single policy is used to interact with the
environment.
Using Isaac Sim allows me to run many parallel environments in parallel. During each
interaction, the algorithm interacts with all the environments at the same time and collects
samples from all of them. Therefore, the number of samples collected per environment
nnum_samples should be closely tied to the number of all environments nenvs. If only one
is decreased, then the total number of samples nsamples_total = nenvs · nnum_samples will
decrease. I tested, how that affects the learning progress and tried two combinations:
• nenvs = 1024 and nnum_samples = 24
• nenvs = 1024 and nnum_samples = 96

5.1.5
Reward function
Designing a reward function for legged robots may be a tedious task and there are many
ways to specify it, as described in Chapter 3. Typically, the function is expressed as
a weighted sum of a few component terms. In this project, I am using a function from
Rudin et al., which is available in the ORBIT framework, as a baseline. The function’s
terms and original weights are described in Table 5.2. Except for the terms for tracking
the command velocity track_lin_vel_xy_exp and track_ang_vel_z_exp, it also has a
few “styling” terms, whose purpose is to make the generated actions smoother and more
natural. However, the original terms were optimized for different terrain and I needed to
fine-tune them for this project.
First, I tested the baseline performance of only velocity tracking terms. Then, I tested the
performance of the function from the original paper with all terms. Finally, I fine-tuned
the weights of the terms and implemented a new one discouraging the robot from feet
colliding with each other.
5.1.6
Episode terminations
The general locomotion task has an infinite horizon and does not have a clear end. How-
ever, it is often practical to implement time limits for a single episode. If the agent reaches
a timeout, then it is performing well enough and can be reset to start in a new, randomized
state and learn more. In this project, the time limit for a single episode is 20 s of simulation
time.
Furthermore, I also terminate the episode when the trunk of the robot makes contact with
the terrain. It is detected in the Isaac Sim with contact sensors measuring the contact
force on each body. This has the goal of discouraging the agent from colliding with the
environment and the robot falling.

5.1.8
Terrains
As the robot is planned to be used in SAR missions, it needs to be able to walk on uneven
terrain. To train robust agents, I used several types of simulated terrains, presented in
Table 5.3, most of which are implemented in the ORBIT framework. One of the require-
ments for the robot is to be able to walk near and on the railway tracks. I implemented
tracks with randomized width and sleepers (beams perpendicular to the rails) distance
and with a parameterized height of the sleepers and rails, which can be seen in Table 5.3.
A common technique used in RL is curriculum training, which presents an agent with
environments of increasing difficulty. The main idea is to first teach the agent on an easy
terrain, so it can learn basic movements and gaits, and then increase the difficulty so it
can improve robustness and learn more specific skills. In this project, I used 6 levels of
difficulty. Ranges of the parameters of the terrains are listed in Table 5.3. The concrete
values for each terrain are a linear interpolation between the minimum and maximum
values. After the agents reach maximum difficulty, they are respawned at random levels,
to prevent forgetting old experiences.
To improve the simulation performance, all the terrains are generated at the beginning of
the training and placed according to their difficulty [20]. The agents are then initialized at
different points, according to their performance level.
The railway tracks proved to be a difficult terrain for the robot to traverse. The rails can
reach a height of up to 25 cm, over half of the robot’s height when standing, which is 40 cm.
In one of the experiments, I tested how the relative ratio of the rails environment to the rest
affects the performance of the agent. The hypothesis is that the difficult environments will
improve the performance, as the terrain will be more representative in the training sets. I
tested with the environments where the rail tracks were 20% and 50% of all terrains.
5.1.9
Domain randomization
Domain randomization is a popular training technique meant to make the policy general-
ize for different conditions by randomizing some elements of the environment [21]. For
example, one can randomize the color of the robot, mass, friction coefficients, etc. By
experiencing different conditions during the training, the policy cannot overfit to some
specific values but needs to be able to handle many of them. This is especially useful for
sim-to-real transfer, where the simulation does not fully model the reality and each real
robot can have slightly different parameters.
In this project, I applied some of the domain randomization components, that are listed in
detail in Table 5.4. Most of them were meant to improve the transfer to the real robot.
In Isaac Sim, robots can be enabled to collide with each other, but in this case, they
should only interact with the terrain. However, the collisions are still being detected by the
simulation, which reduces its speed when there are a lot of robots at the same place [20].
Therefore, they are initialized at some distance from each other to improve the simulation
performance.
The “push” component was meant to increase the stability and robustness of the policy.
By simulating the pushes of the robot, the policy has to learn how to recover from them.

5.1.10
Neural networks architecture
In this project, I use three different models, which are instantiated by neural networks.
First, the PPO algorithm uses actor and critic models for solving the RL task. The actor
is also a teacher for the transfer learning task, and the third model is the student. Using
recommendations from [43], I initialized the last layer of the actor network with 100 times
smaller weights. I also used wider layers for the critic than for the actor networks, without
any shared layers. I used three hidden fully connected feed-forward layers for all the
models.
Recurrent layers can act as a memory for the agent of a single episode. Using it, it
can potentially adapt to changing friction, external obstacles, etc., which could improve
performance. As an experiment, I added a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) layer with
256 units at the beginning of the teacher’s actor and critic, to compare it to the fully feed-
forward network. For the student in the transfer learning, I always used a single LSTM
layer with 256 units. The student is using only proprioception for the locomotion and needs
“memory” e.g. to remember that it hit a step and needs to raise a leg higher.
I conducted a quick experiment to test, how the number of neurons affects the training
and the final performance of an agent. I tested two configurations of the actor’s network:
[512, 128, 128] and [512, 256, 256]. For the student network, I always used [256, 128, 128]
architecture.
Activation functions add non-linearity to the neural networks. There are many different
ones to choose from and each can lead to different behaviors. In this thesis, I tested
how Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), Exponential Linear Unit (ELU) and Scaled Exponential
Linear Unit (SELU) functions impact the actor and the critic networks.
The final layer is an output of the agent and does not have any activation function. Dur-
ing the training however, instead of using the output µt directly as action at, the policy
samples the actions from a normal distribution around the output: at ∼N(µt, σ2
t ). This

5.1.11
Transfer learning
To facilitate the training of the blind agent, transfer learning was used. Figure 5.5 shows
the diagram of the training architecture. First, a teacher policy πteacher was trained using
reinforcement learning, with access to privileged observations about the terrain and the
robot’s linear velocity. Then, a student policy πstudent was trained to match the teacher’s
actions.
During the student’s training, the actions are taken from the student’s model, instead of
the teacher’s. This improves exploration of possible states, as the teacher would stay
close to the good trajectories, while the student at the beginning makes mistakes and
can learn how to correct them [14]. The student also samples the actions, similar to the
teacher during training, to increase exploration: at ∼N(µstudent,t, σ2
student). However, in
transfer learning, the student’s output µstudent,t should match the teacher’s µteacher,t, and
putting standard deviation σstudent into the optimization task is not obvious. Therefore, I
set it to constant σstudent = 0.5 for the whole transfer learning process.
5.2
Deployment on the real robot
Deployment on the real robot is based on the code from [44]. It uses the robot’s low-level
interface to get proprioception data, remote controller commands, and send target joint
angles.
The policy needs to be first exported to the TorchScript format.
It generates a self-
contained file, with the definition of the neural network architecture and its weights. It
makes it easy to change the parameters during the training and then copy only a single
file to the robot. Furthermore, the TorchScript format is better optimized than the Python
code, which improves the inference time on the real robot. The model is then read on the
robot and it is receiving real observations and outputting actions to the robot. One of the
issues I encountered was PyTorch version incompatibility between the training environ-
ment (2.0) and the robot (1.9), which caused the generated .jit file to not be readable.
To fix this issue, I had to create a separate Docker environment with the older PyTorch

version, which was reading the raw weights stored in a .pt file and then exporting it to
TorchScript.
After generating a .jit file, it needs to be copied onto the robot to the appropriate direc-

on the remote and one must run the Python code. The connection to the robot is through

5.3
Testing methodology
5.3.1
Training metrics
During the training, a few metrics were tracked to assess the training progress and the
final performance of an agent. First, the mean terrain level of the robots was measured. In
curriculum learning, the agents are promoted and demoted based on their ability to follow
the commanded velocity. Higher levels correspond to higher terrain difficulty, therefore
this metric measures the ability to traverse those terrains. It also indirectly measures ro-
bustness, as poor agents would fail before reaching the target distance and would be
demoted. As a more direct metric for robustness, I measured failure rate, which is the
frequency of robot falls during a given period, e.g. falls per hour. The falls are detected
as the trunk of the robot touching the terrain, as described in subsection 5.1.6 Next, I mea-
sured the velocity tracking error. This metric directly measures the utility of the controller:
the agent is expected to follow the velocity command. Linear and angular (yaw) velocities
are measured separately. Additionally, I measured the mean episode reward and mean
episode length. Those metrics heavily depend on terrain difficulty and reward function
terms and can be difficult to interpret directly. However, they can be used to compare two
policies that have minimal changes, e.g. different numbers of units in the neural network.
5.3.2
Evaluation
As a baseline, the method from Rudin et al., which is included in the ORBIT, was used
[20]. The environment was modified to not use height scan and the robot’s linear velocity,
as in my method. The method uses the original reward function (Table 5.2) and does not
use “Rail tracks” terrain for training. The implementation in ORBIT offers feed-forward
and recurrent versions for the agent. I chose the recurrent model, as I am also using such
in my method.
I compared the baseline to my method, which included all the decisions described in this
section and Chapter 6. The goal was to compare that baseline to the system trained with
all the modifications and how they impact the performance. For the evaluation, domain
randomization was disabled. The agents were tested separately on “Random rough” and
“Rail tracks” terrains at difficulties 3, 4.5, and 6. The measured metrics were velocity
tracking error and failure rate. The command velocity was the same as in training.
Testing the policy deployed on the real robot was agreed to be optional due to the limited
time. The tests would measure the speed achieved by the robot and its robustness in
different environments.

6.1
Number of samples per environment
Figure 6.1 shows the results of the experiment, both runs were running with nenvs = 1024.
The one with nnum_samples = 24 showed much slower progress with respect to the training
steps and seems to plateau at a lower performance. The run with a higher number of
samples showed much faster progress and better performance, but it came at the cost
of the real-time collection. A single iteration with nnum_samples = 24 takes around 2.5 s, but
nnum_samples = 96 takes approximately four times as long, 10 s. This is expected because
the agent needs to collect 4 times as many samples from the simulation. The optimal
total number of samples seems to be around nenvs × nnum_samples = 1024 × 96, which is
big enough to achieve good performance, but a bigger number might increase the training
time needlessly. One way to reduce the computation time is to simply increase the number
of environments. However, more environments require more VRAM memory on the GPU,
which was one of the bottlenecks for this project. RTX 4060 Ti with 16 GB VRAM was
able to easily handle up to 4096 environments, in which case I set nnum_samples = 24 to
keep the product constant. RTX 3070 had only 8 GB of VRAM and often struggled to
run 4096 environments. In those cases, I set the parameters to either nenvs = 2048 and
nnum_samples = 48, or if it still was not enough nenvs = 1024 and nnum_samples = 96. In
the following experiments, the training performance seemed comparable as long as the
product of the two numbers was kept constant.

6.2
Reward function
First, I compared the performance of the purely tracking reward function to the reward
function with the “styling” terms. Results are shown in Figure 6.2. Even though the pure
tracking function had no penalties and potentially could exploit more aggressive policies,
it performed worse than the function with the “styling” components. The agent is only
able to reasonably track linear velocity and has very poor performance in tracking angular
velocity. With the “styling” terms, the agents learn to properly track both velocities. Those
components not only help to obtain gaits that are nice to the human eye but also help guide
the learning process in finding better policies. Furthermore, by looking at visualizations
of both policies it could be seen that the “styling” terms smoothed out the overall motion
of the robot, while the policy with only tracking terms produced very jerky motions, which
could potentially harm the real robot.
Next, I added the term feet_close_together, penalizing the agent for having feet close
to each other. Self-collisions are disabled in Isaac Sim and without this term, the policies
often generated trajectories where feet touched each other, e.g. when rotating in place
(see Figure 6.3), which would most likely lead to failure on the real robot. Adding this term
encouraged the policies to learn to move without putting their feet too close to each other.
Finally, I fine-tuned the rest of the terms manually by observing the behavior and how
each term affects it. One significant observation from the process is that the robot would
often stand on only three legs when commanded to stay in place, which can be seen in
Figure 6.4. To remedy that, the weight of the dof_torques_l2 term was increased to guide

6.3
Mirrored observations
First, I tested the method by augmenting the data with mirrored observations and actions.
The results were mixed.
This method caused numerical instability in the RL algorithm. PPO is an on-policy algo-
rithm but the mirrored samples are off-policy. Internally, the algorithm calculates the log
probability of the policy π taking an action a at state s: log(π(a|s)). However, the off-policy
mirrored samples are not tied to the policy π, which caused the probability to be very low,
which in turn caused the log probability to be a big negative number. After a few itera-
tions, the accumulating errors caused float number overflow and crashed the program.
I implemented a clipping mechanism to limit the log probability from the overflow. With

6.5
Neural networks architecture
Neural networks with a recurrent layer proved to be difficult to train through RL. Figure 6.8
shows the results of the experiment. While the agent with a recurrent layer was able
to achieve the same performance as the agent with a fully feed-forward neural network,
it took around four times as many learning steps. Furthermore, recurrent layers are a
little more computationally expensive and as a result, a single iteration was also longer. I
decided to not use any recurrent layers for the teacher’s models.
The results of the experiment with different numbers of units are shown in Figure 6.9.
The actor with more units in the layers managed to train only slightly faster, while the final
performance of both models was the same. I chose to use [512, 128, 128] architecture to
keep the parameter count low, as simpler models are typically preferred in Deep Learning.
Figure 6.10 shows the comparison of the different activation functions. ReLU function
showed very poor performance and only started progressing near the end of the train-
ing. ELU and SELU both showed good progress in learning with very comparable per-
formances, with ELU being only slightly faster. For the rest of the experiments, ELU
activation function was chosen for the teacher and SELU for the student.

The result of the transfer learning is shown in Figure 6.11. The plots show the error in

This is because, during the training, actions are sampled from the distribution and have
some noise, and the student has less noise (σstudent = 0.5) than the teacher (usually
σteacher,t > 0.7).

7
Discussion
In this chapter, I will discuss the results of my experiments.
7.1
Reward function
The reward function proved to be one of the most important design components of the RL
task. Simply coding the direct task as a reward is often not enough and one must spend
time “shaping” the function to guide agents towards the expected behavior.
7.2
Activation functions
I expected that the choice of activation function would have minimal impact on the per-
formance and would only be necessary for fine-tuning to get a few more percent of the
performance. However, their impact turned out to be crucial to the speed of learning. I
was especially surprised by the poor performance of the ReLU function, as it is one of the
most popular ones in DL. Choice of the activation function is still one of the key design
decisions and there is not one that is good for all the tasks, one has to experiment with
which works the best in the specific application.
7.3
Terrains
The choice of the terrains used in the training is important for the final performance of the
controller. As with most DL methods, the agent is trained to perform well on the samples
it experiences. Therefore, the terrains used in the training should be representative of the
terrains that the agent will experience during the deployment. For example, the baseline
method was not trained on the rail tracks and it performed quite poorly in the evaluations
with 3.87 falls/min.
7.4
Transfer learning
Transfer learning proved to be working very well and allowed to quickly transfer knowledge
from the teacher using exteroception to a blind student. It requires training only a single
teacher, which can later be distilled to another neural network with other sensor modalities.
It could potentially be used not only to train the student without any exteroception but also
with other types of sensor inputs, like a lidar point cloud or an RGB camera image.
7.5
Simulation evaluation
My controller design was shown to be more robust than the baseline controller on difficult
terrains. The robustness was assessed as the failure ratio, meaning how often the robot
falls in a given amount of time. On a random rough terrain, the failure rate was around
0.48 falls/h, which could probably be used for deployment in real applications. However,
while outperforming the baseline, there is still a lot of room for improvements on the rail
tracks. With the failure rate of 1.26 falls/min, it would be difficult to rely on it in a SAR
mission and it could potentially damage itself.
That being said, the rail track’s terrain is not very representative of the real-life conditions.
In the generated terrain, the rails are crossing each other at 90◦angle and they are located
very close to each other. It is good for training, as the difficult terrain forces the agent
to learn more robust policy, but in real life, there are usually one or two tracks running in
parallel. Furthermore, during the evaluation, the maximum velocity command could reach
Learning Agile Robotic Locomotion Skills for Search and Rescue Missions
33
==PAGE:46==
the value of 1.4 m/s. In the real application, the higher-level planner could detect the rails
and reduce the speed of the robot to overcome the obstacle.
In terms of velocity command tracking, both policies had similar performances. It can be
seen that the error on the rail tracks is much higher than on random rough terrain. This
is because the rails are a much more challenging obstacle and it is difficult for agents to
follow the command precisely while stepping over the rails.
7.6
Limitations
The biggest limitation of this project is sim-to-real transfer. The agent was not able to
perform well on the real robot, even on flat terrain, despite working reasonably well in the
simulation. One of the reasons could be the gains of the robot’s internal PD controller.
The simulation and the real robot had matching values, but small differences in the real
robot could lead to needing different gains.
Another reason could be the deformable feet on the real robot. In the simulation, every
part of the robot is modeled as a rigid body, but the real robot has rubber feet for better
grip. This is a problem because I observed that the agent learned to raise legs only a little
above the ground. This can work in simulation, but in real life when the agent “thinks” it
raised a leg, while the rubber feet only returned to their original shape. This caused the
robot to trip on its legs, while it tried to move them while they were still in contact with the
ground.
Another limitation is a lack of comparison to the robot’s built-in controller. Unitree unfor-
tunately does not provide their controller to use in simulation, so it can be only evaluated
on the real robot. It would be interesting to compare it in simulation to my controller or
to improve my sim-to-real transfer and compare them on the real robot. However, it can
still be concluded that the built-in controller works much better on the real robot than my
controller.
7.7
Future work
7.7.1
Sim-to-real transfer
The most focus in future work should be put on improving sim-to-real transfer, as it proved
to be one of the most difficult things during deployment on the real robot.
One direction could be in further tuning the reward function or applying different shaping
components. One of the problems observed on the real robot was that the agent did not
raise its legs high enough. A component rewarding the agent for raising their legs higher
could potentially solve this problem.
Another problem with the deployment was the tuning of the robot’s PD controller. One
solution would be to apply different PD gains in simulation as part of the domain random-
ization and potentially have those gains as inputs to the teacher. This would force the
agent to learn to walk with different values of gains. Then, the knowledge would be trans-
ferred to the student without the knowledge of gains, which would force it to adapt the
actions based on the robot’s behavior.
7.7.2
Using RGB camera
One of the reasons why the RGB camera was not used in the project, was the high compu-
tational cost of its simulation, which set a practical limit of around 20 parallel environments.
Typically, the RL training in this project was performed with 2048 or 4096 environments,
so adding a camera would increase the training time around 100 times. However, transfer
learning is much faster and requires less iterations. One idea for future research could

be to train a teacher policy without a camera, which could run with thousands of envi-
ronments, and then transfer it to a student policy with a camera, which should learn in a
tractable time even with a limited number of environments.
However, this project requires the locomotion policy to be able to work in dusty environ-
ments, which means that additional effort would be necessary to handle noisy data.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, I developed an RL model for quadrupedal locomotion in challenging terrains.
The model was tested in simulation and compared to the other model from the literature.
The robustness of my controller was much better compared to the baseline, however, it
still leaves room for improvement in the hardest scenarios.
Thanks to the development of Isaac Sim running thousands of parallel simulations on a
GPU, the training of an RL controller is possible in only hours on a mid-range GPU. This
massively speeds up experiments and reduces the cost of entry into this research.
Tuning the reward function is tedious and requires a lot of experience to identify important
components and how their weights should be tuned. Even then, the changes might not
have the expected effect.
The architecture of the neural network, especially the activation functions and recurrent
layers, is an important factor in the performance of an agent. Recurrent layers were
difficult to train by an RL method, but they behaved well in a supervised learning setting
through transfer learning. The choice of activation functions is important and can decide
on the agent’s ability to learn.
Sim-to-real transfer proved to be still one of the most difficult tasks in robotics. Despite
applying numerous techniques to facilitate the transfer, the final policy could not work
safely on a real robot. The built-in controller was therefore working much better in the real
world.
In summary, the experiments in simulation show the potential for RL methods to enable
quadrupedal robots to walk in challenging terrains. Through the shaping of the reward
function and choice of terrains used during the training, the robustness of the agent can
be greatly improved compared to the baseline. However, more work is necessary for the
successful sim-to-real transfer.
