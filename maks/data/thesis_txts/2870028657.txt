Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-based AI assistants, are increasingly present
in higher education, yet their impact on advanced, conceptually demanding subjects re-
mains underexplored. This study investigates how integrating a GPT-based assistant
into a Master’s-level course influences student performance, confidence, and engage-
ment. Employing a mixed-methods design, students completed two homework assign-
ments incorporating both individual problem-solving and AI solutions, followed by reflec-
tions and surveys on their experience. Quantitative analysis showed that while students
reported higher confidence and appreciated the AI’s step-by-step clarifications, particu-
larly in conceptual definition tasks, concerns arose over the assistant’s inaccuracies in
complex mathematical reasoning. A comparison with a group of students that did not use
AI revealed minimal differences in final grades, suggesting that although LLMs enhance
day-to-day learning, they do not necessarily alter mastery outcomes. Qualitative data
highlighted the importance of verifying AI outputs, with many students identifying the tool
as a catalyst for deeper engagement and self-reflection rather than a substitute for critical
thinking. Overall, these findings demonstrate that LLM-based assistants can serve as
valuable, supportive tools in advanced coursework if carefully integrated, combined with
rigorous instruction, and accompanied by clear guidelines on responsible use.
Keywords: Artificial Intelligence (AI), Large Language Models (LLMs), AI in Education,
AI-Assisted Learning, Machine Learning (ML), Higher Education, Student Confidence and
Engagement, Critical Thinking, Educational Technology

Introduction
1.1
Background of AI in Education
Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly Large Language Models (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s
GPT series, is increasingly shaping numerous aspects of our society, including education.
These advanced models are capable of understanding and generating human-like text,
enabling a range of applications in information retrieval, content creation, and interactive
learning experiences [1]. As students discover how to leverage these capabilities, LLMs
offer the promise of more personalised, accessible, and adaptive educational environ-
ments.
However, the rapid adoption of LLMs by students often meets resistance from educa-
tional institutions and instructors. Concerns about academic integrity, overreliance on AI-
generated solutions, and the potential erosion of critical thinking skills have led educators
and institutions to discourage or even ban the use of such tools. While some students use
AI responsibly to enrich their understanding of course concepts, others may be tempted to
use it as a shortcut, bypassing key steps in the learning process. This discrepancy raises
questions about academic misconduct and whether students truly master the material or
merely appear to do so. This tension can be seen as a form of educational clash between
students, who may adopt AI tools for various reasons such as enhancing their learning
or handling time constraints, and educators who fear the unintended consequences of
unregulated AI usage. Yet, as AI becomes ubiquitous, it is neither feasible nor desirable
to ignore its role in learning environments. Instead, it is essential to investigate how these
technologies can be integrated in a manner that upholds academic standards and fosters
genuine intellectual growth.
Growing evidence suggests that LLMs can do more than just produce quick answers.
When carefully integrated, they have the potential to support higher-order thinking, guide
complex learning tasks, and enhance feedback mechanisms. The challenge now is to
move beyond a defensive stance against AI and toward a constructive engagement that
helps educators harness its transformative potential. Doing so involves understanding
not only the accuracy and trustworthiness of AI-generated content, but also how these
tools impact student confidence, motivation, and engagement, particularly in advanced
and complex subjects.
1.2
Problem Statement
While students frequently use LLM-based tools for learning, educators lack robust, data-
driven insights about their effective integration into teaching practices. Much of the debate
around AI in education focuses on simple fact retrieval or language-based exercises. Far
less is known about how LLMs perform and influence learning in specialised, conceptually
demanding domains, areas where the accuracy, reasoning ability, and consistency of AI-
generated content are crucial. In such contexts, reliance on an AI tool that occasionally
produces imprecise or contextually misleading information could misinform students or
undermine the development of critical analytical skills [2].

This gap is especially pressing in higher education, where advanced courses demand
rigorous conceptual thinking.
To date, limited research has assessed how effectively
LLMs can support students in deeply analytical fields. Educators worry that suboptimal
or unchecked use of LLMs might encourage superficial engagement or promote shortcut
solutions at the expense of genuine understanding.
1.3
Purpose and Objectives of the Study
This study aims to address these concerns by examining the integration of an LLM-based
AI assistant into a Master’s-level course on Logical Theories for Uncertainty and Learning
at the Technical University of Denmark (Danmarks Tekniske Universitet, DTU) during the
winter semester of the 2024-2025 academic year. This particular course, which deals with
probabilistic reasoning, logical frameworks, and complex analytical problems, provides
an ideal testing ground to explore LLM integration since it requires both mathematical
rigor and conceptual depth, making it significantly more challenging than many standard
subject areas where LLMs have been studied.
To investigate the practical implications of LLM integration, students in the course were
asked to use a custom GPT-based assistant developed as part of this study for two Home-
work Assignments. In these assignments, students first attempted solutions indepen-
dently, then consulted the AI assistant for guidance, and finally compared their reasoning
against the model’s suggestions. Additionally, they provided feedback on their confidence
in both their own solutions and the AI’s, as well as their sense of engagement and per-
ceived shifts in their understanding and problem-solving capabilities after interacting with
the assistant.
Through this practical approach, the study seeks to close the gap between theoretical
potential and actual practice. By examining student outcomes, experiences, and percep-
tions, this research can shed light on how LLMs influence complex learning processes
and whether they can be effectively aligned with rigorous academic standards.
1.4
Research Questions
The research is guided by the following questions, framed in the context of the specialised,
conceptually demanding course environment:
• Accuracy and Trustworthiness: How accurate and contextually appropriate are
the AI assistant’s outputs in the specialised domain of an advanced course?
• Learning Outcomes: Does the integration of an AI tutoring assistant improve stu-
dent understanding and problem-solving outcomes in complex tasks?
• Student Confidence: Does the use of AI assistance influence students’ confidence
in their own reasoning and solutions?
• Student Engagement: Does interacting with an AI assistant affect student motiva-
tion, curiosity, and overall engagement with challenging course material?
By examining accuracy, learning outcomes, and confidence alongside motivational fac-
tors, this study aims to guide the ethical and effective integration of AI tutoring in higher
education, informing educators on best practices and future research.

1.5
Significance of the Study
By focusing on a specialised, advanced course at a Master’s level, this research con-
tributes to a deeper understanding of how LLMs function beyond general informational
tasks. Academically, it fills a gap in the literature by providing empirical evidence of LLM
effectiveness and limitations in a complex educational setting. Methodologically, the study
models a structured approach to integrating LLMs into coursework and evaluating their
impact on learning processes and outcomes.
Practically, the results can guide educators in making informed decisions about introduc-
ing AI-based tools into their curricula. Understanding when and how LLMs add value or
create pitfalls enables institutions to develop guidelines and best practices. Such knowl-
edge is particularly relevant in higher education, where cultivating advanced reasoning
and critical thinking skills is crucial. Finally, insights from this study may inform future
research and tool development, contributing to the refinement of educational AI systems
that enhance learning while preserving academic integrity and rigor.

2
Literature Review
The following literature review provides an examination of existing research related to
the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly Large Language Models (LLMs),
in educational contexts. It begins by exploring foundational concepts in AI and Machine
Learning (ML), then moves on to discuss deep learning architectures and the Transformer-
based models that underpin modern LLMs. The review then considers how AI has been
leveraged for personalised and adaptive learning, examining both the opportunities and
the reported challenges. Finally, it addresses the implementation and effectiveness of
LLM-based educational tools, their ethical considerations, and the emerging gaps in cur-
rent research, particularly in specialised, conceptually demanding domains where empir-
ical evidence remains sparse.
2.1
Introduction to Key Concepts
Artificial Intelligence is defined as the capability of a machine to imitate intelligent human
behavior, including learning, reasoning, and problem solving [3]. Machine Learning, a
subset of AI, enables systems to identify patterns and make decisions with minimal hu-
man intervention [4]. Early applications of AI in education typically focused on rule-based
systems and rudimentary question-answering tools [5] , laying the groundwork for more
advanced AI-based educational solutions.
Deep Learning, which relies on artificial neural networks with multiple hidden layers, has
substantially advanced the ability of computers to recognise patterns in data [6]. Initially,
deep architectures made significant contributions in areas such as computer vision [7] and
speech recognition [8]. Eventually, these techniques were adapted to Natural Language
Processing (NLP), driving progress in language understanding and generation.
The development of the Transformer architecture, by Vaswani et al. [9], revolutionised
NLP by enabling more efficient handling of long-range dependencies and context in tex-
tual data. Subsequent expansions of Transformer-based models gave rise to LLMs, which
scaled up parameters and training data, culminating in models capable of producing
human-like prose, reasoning through complex queries, and generating domain-specific
explanations.
LLMs, such as OpenAI’s GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformers) series, leverage
massive datasets and billions of parameters to understand and generate coherent, context-
sensitive language [10, 11]. They have demonstrated capabilities across a range of tasks
such as summarising content, assisting with code generation, offering writing feedback,
and explaining complex concepts [12, 13]. Despite these advances, concerns remain
about the accuracy of AI-generated information, as LLMs can produce plausible-sounding
yet incorrect or misleading content. This phenomenon, often referred to as hallucination,
highlights the need for careful scrutiny and verification of AI-generated outputs [2].

2.2
AI and Personalised Learning
2.2.1
The Role of AI in Creating Adaptive Learning Pathways
AI’s potential in education lies largely in its ability to deliver personalised learning experi-
ences. By analysing student performance data, AI-based systems can dynamically adjust
content difficulty, suggest supplementary materials, and offer targeted feedback [14]. One
application of this approach is Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS), which have been shown
to enhance learning outcomes by aligning instructional strategies with individual student
profiles [15]. These systems emulate one-on-one interaction. They monitor a student’s
progress, adapt to their learning pace, and provide immediate, tailored feedback. For in-
stance, when a student struggles with a specific concept, the ITS can present additional
explanations or alternative methods to foster deeper understanding.
Several studies also indicate that adaptive AI tools can increase student motivation and
engagement by meeting students where they are. Personalised systems are better able
to recognise individual learning styles, preferences, and even emotional states, creating
a more engaging environment that can sustain student interest and encourage active par-
ticipation. However, this body of research has predominantly focused on well-structured
domains, such as mathematics, language learning, and coding, where concepts are rel-
atively straightforward to quantify [16]. The question of how effectively these adaptive
technologies extend to more nuanced, conceptually complex courses remains largely un-
explored. Future research thus needs to investigate both the pedagogical implications of
deploying AI in advanced domains and how the emotional intelligence of these systems
might further personalise and improve learning experiences.
2.2.2
Previous Studies on AI Tutors and Assistants
Empirical findings highlight the potential and the drawbacks of integrating AI tutors into
educational settings. Studies have shown that AI-generated feedback in writing tasks can
enhance students’ revision strategies, motivation, and emotional engagement [17]. For
instance, students who received feedback from a Large Language Model (LLM) demon-
strated improved writing skills, alongside higher levels of task motivation and positive emo-
tions, compared to those without AI-generated feedback. In coding education, research
suggests that hybrid approaches, combining AI code generators with manual coding, can
yield better learning outcomes. However, there is also evidence that overreliance on AI-
generated solutions can hinder the development of independent problem-solving skills,
emphasising the importance of fostering critical engagement with these tools [16].
An emerging approach known as robosourcing tasks AI models with producing base-
line educational content for students to evaluate and refine [18]. This interactive process
encourages critical thinking and active learning, positioning students not merely as recip-
ients of information but as active participants in assessing and improving AI-generated
materials. While these findings are promising, most existing research has focused on
introductory-level tasks or procedural skills. Consequently, there is still a scarcity of knowl-
edge regarding how students interact with LLM-based assistants in domains requiring ad-
vanced conceptual reasoning and logical analysis. Understanding these interactions is
crucial, especially as AI’s pedagogical scope expands into more complex cognitive areas.

2.3
Large Language Models in Education
2.3.1
Capabilities and Limitations of LLMs like GPT-Based Models
Large Language Models, including GPT-based models such as GPT-4, have demon-
strated remarkable capabilities across various educational contexts. Studies have docu-
mented their ability to provide instant explanations, generate examples, and break down
complex ideas, assisting students in tasks such as drafting essays, explaining intricate
concepts, generating code, and even offering step-by-step feedback in mathematics or
science problems [19]. Their interactive, conversational nature allows student to pose
questions and receive immediate, contextually relevant responses, simulating the style of
expert tutors [20].
Despite these advantages, LLMs show limitations that raise concerns about their relia-
bility. Because they generate text by predicting statistically likely sequences of tokens
rather than through genuine comprehension, they may supply confidently stated but in-
correct or contextually irrelevant information [21] . This risk is particularly troublesome in
specialised domains such as advanced logic or engineering, where conceptual rigor is cru-
cial. Overreliance on LLMs can also hinder the development of students’ critical thinking
and problem-solving skills [22]. Moreover, existing AI detection tools have proven inade-
quate, making it impractical to ban LLM usage outright [23]. Instead, scholars increasingly
recommend guiding students to use LLMs critically and responsibly, emphasising the cul-
tivation of original thought and careful evaluation of machine-generated outputs. While
current research highlights the effectiveness of LLMs in generic writing tasks or factual
Q&A, there is a pressing need for further investigation into their domain-specific reliability
and into how students interpret and respond to potentially flawed outputs.
2.3.2
Case Studies of LLM Implementation in Educational Contexts
A growing body of case studies illustrates the ways in which Large Language Models are
being integrated into educational contexts. In medical education, for example, ChatGPT-
based systems have been used as virtual tutors, guiding students through clinical reason-
ing exercises and sometimes yielding promising outcomes [20]. Meanwhile, in creative
fields, AI tools such as ChatGPT and DALL∙E, OpenAI’s image-generation model that
can produce visuals from text descriptions, have been incorporated into project-based
assignments, stimulating creativity, broadening students’ perspectives, and enhancing
motivation [24].
Despite these advances, a number of limitations remain. Research on novice program-
mers shows that although LLMs can offer correct coding suggestions, they can also intro-
duce subtle errors that inexperienced students may struggle to identify [16]. Beyond this
domain-specific challenge, there is a notable gap in studies exploring how LLM-assisted
learning functions in courses emphasising abstract reasoning or conceptual depth. To ad-
dress this gap, this study examines how LLMs can be integrated into an advanced course
like Logical Theories for Uncertainty and Learning and explores both the opportunities
and risks of using AI-driven tools in demanding educational settings.

2.4
Ethical Considerations in AI Education Tools
2.4.1
Data Privacy Concerns
As AI systems become more prevalent in education, data privacy and security remain
fundamental concerns. Personalised learning platforms often rely on potentially sensi-
tive data, requiring rigorous compliance with regulations such as the GDPR to protect
individual privacy.
Achieving transparency in data collection and handling involves clearly communicating
what information is gathered, how it is stored, who has access to it, and for what purposes
it is used. This can be done by publishing straightforward data-handling policies, offering
orientation sessions on AI usage, and integrating user-facing notifications or dashboards
that outline the platform’s data practices. Such measures not only maintain student trust
but also mitigate potential ethical risks by enabling students to see, correct, or request
deletion of their personal data.
By integrating privacy-preserving techniques, such as data minimisation and encryption,
and incorporating bias screening into AI tool development, educational institutions can
help safeguard students’ personal information while delivering the benefits of adaptive,
AI-driven instruction [25].
2.4.2
Bias and Fairness in AI Responses
Bias in AI responses is another critical ethical concern, given that Large Language Models
learn patterns from extensive datasets that can contain biases. Because these models
are built on real-world text, they can inadvertently reflect stereotypes, prejudices, or im-
balances found in their training data, ultimately undermining the fairness and inclusivity
of educational environments [21]. In practice, this can perpetuate harmful stereotypes
and create unequal support for students from different backgrounds. To address these
risks, educators and institutions must implement bias detection and mitigation strategies,
such as incorporating robust screening mechanisms, so that AI-assisted learning remains
equitable for all students.
2.4.3
Student Dependency on AI Tools
One of the most pressing concerns surrounding AI integration in education is the risk of
fostering passive learning behaviors and diminished critical thinking skills [16, 22]. When
students rely too heavily on AI-generated answers they may fail to develop the under-
standing needed for robust analytical competencies. Moreover, self-confidence plays a
crucial role in shaping how students interact with AI. Rather than being guided by their
trust in the AI system itself, students are more likely to accept or reject AI outputs based
on their own sense of competence [26]. This dynamic can become problematic if poor
AI performance undermines student confidence, leading to a cycle of overreliance on AI-
generated content.
To mitigate these risks, educators must proactively cultivate students’ abilities to use AI
tools both effectively and responsibly. This can be done by incorporating lessons on digi-
tal literacy and critical thinking, showing students common pitfalls or ”hallucinations” in AI
outputs, and encouraging them to verify AI suggestions through additional sources, such
as textbooks or peer discussions [27]. In-class demonstrations of how to scrutinise AI re-
sponses with follow-up questions or alternative prompts can help students learn to assess
the reliability of an AI-generated solution. Structured reflection exercises, such as com-
paring and contrasting AI outputs with the student’s own work, can strengthen students’

capacity to discern errors and question assumptions.
By emphasising AI as a complement to, rather than a substitute for, students’ own ef-
forts, instructors can promote an approach that leverages AI’s benefits while preserving

2.5
Summary
The literature emphasises AI’s potential to personalise education, improve feedback mech-
anisms, and increase student motivation. LLMs, built on Transformer architectures, repre-
sent a significant leap in AI’s capacity to engage in nuanced language tasks and offer ad-
vanced tutoring experiences. Yet, critical gaps persist. Much of the existing work focuses
on well-defined tasks or introductory levels of education, leaving unanswered questions
about how LLMs can be effectively integrated into advanced, conceptually demanding
learning domains.
Ethical concerns, including data privacy, bias, and the risk of student overreliance on AI
tools, further complicate the picture. While some research has examined LLMs in fields
like medical education, few studies have investigated how these tools function in courses
that demand complex logical reasoning and deep conceptual engagement.
This study aims to address these gaps by empirically examining the use of an LLM-based
AI assistant in a Master’s-level course like Logical Theories for Uncertainty and Learn-
ing. By evaluating the accuracy and trustworthiness of the AI’s outputs, its impact on
student engagement, confidence, and the overall learning process, this study contributes
to the emerging body of evidence on LLM integration in higher education. It offers insights
that may guide future instructional design, helping educators navigate the challenges and
realise the transformative potential of AI-driven learning in specialised domains.

3
Theoretical Framework
This study is at the intersection of educational practices and state of the art AI tools. As
AI assistants enter academic contexts that demand complex reasoning and conceptual
depth, it becomes critical to interpret their influence through robust educational theories.
By drawing on frameworks such as Constructive Alignment, the distinction between Deep
and Surface Learning, the Technology Acceptance Model, and Self-Determination Theory,
we can better understand how students interact with and learn from LLM-based tools in
challenging domains.
3.1
Introduction to Theoretical Framework
Unlike factual retrieval or procedural tasks, the Master’s-level course examined in this
study, Logical Theories for Uncertainty and Learning, engages students in abstract rea-
soning, probabilistic modeling, and the application of logical frameworks to complex sce-
narios. In such a setting, an AI assistant’s role cannot be evaluated solely by correctness
of output or speed of response. Rather, it must be understood in relation to how stu-
dents learn, what motivates them to engage with AI, how they adopt new technology, and
whether their interactions with AI deepen their cognitive engagement.
The theories discussed here provide lenses to interpret these interactions:
• Constructive Alignment ensures the pedagogical design involving the AI tool sup-
ports intended learning outcomes of the course and fosters meaningful understand-
ing.
• Deep vs.
Surface Learning helps differentiate whether the presence of an AI
tool encourages deeper conceptual engagement or inadvertently reinforces shal-
low, shortcut-driven approaches.
• Technology Acceptance Model frames how perceived usefulness and ease of use
influence students’ willingness to integrate the AI tool into their study routines.
• Self-Determination Theory highlights how the AI tool can affect students’ intrinsic
motivation and psychological needs, including autonomy, competence, and related-
ness, especially when facing advanced tasks.
Taken together, these perspectives help us evaluate not just whether students can com-
plete assignments more efficiently with AI, but whether they also develop the deeper com-
petencies that define high-level learning such as critical thinking, independent problem-
solving, and reflective judgment.
3.2
Relevant Educational Theories
3.2.1
Constructive Alignment
Constructive Alignment, as developed by Biggs and Tang, [28], argues that effective
teaching and learning occur when intended learning outcomes, instructional activities,

==PAGE:20==
and assessment tasks are coherently aligned. Students in the Logical Theories for Un-
certainty and Learning course must develop both conceptual understanding and the ability
to apply learned concepts to unfamiliar problems.
The introduction of an AI assistant into coursework must therefore be purposeful. If the
intended learning outcome is to enhance students’ ability to reason about uncertainty and
evaluate logical models, the AI assistant should facilitate tasks that require analysis and
comparison rather than simply providing solutions. For example, assignments can prompt
students to generate their own reasoning first, then consult the LLM, and finally critique
both sets of reasoning. In this way, the assistant’s role is aligned with learning objectives
and assessed outcomes, encouraging students to reflect critically on both human and
AI-generated solutions.
Constructive Alignment ensures that the LLM is not a random addition but an integrated
component designed to reinforce intended competencies. For instance, instructors can
spell out which learning outcomes are targeted by each LLM-aided activity, such as analysing
solution steps, evaluating logical consistency, or contrasting approaches to a proof.
By structuring how and when students interact with the LLM (e.g., generating a prelim-
inary solution first, then consulting the LLM, then critically comparing both), educators
create conditions that encourage deeper engagement with the subject matter rather than
passive answer adoption. This coherence between learning outcomes, activities, and
assessments is what helps the LLM integration genuinely support the development of
intended competencies.
3.2.2
Deep vs. Surface Learning
Rooted in the work of Marton and Säljö [29], the distinction between deep and surface
learning captures how students approach complex academic content. Deep learning in-
volves building meaningful understandings, integrating new concepts with existing knowl-
edge, and engaging critically with material. Surface learning, conversely, is characterised
by memorisation, minimal engagement, and a focus on completing tasks with minimal
cognitive effort. Students’ learning strategies may shift between deep and surface modes
depending on factors such as time pressure, motivation, and the perceived difficulty of
the subject matter.
This duality has implications for motivation and self-regulation. Students who embrace a
deep approach typically demonstrate higher intrinsic motivation, devoting attention to the
underlying structure and rationale of ideas rather than simply recalling facts. Research
suggests that deep learners are more likely to connect course content to broader con-
texts or personal experiences, which fosters greater persistence and adaptability. Sur-
face learners, by contrast, often concentrate on rote memorisation or shortcuts that al-
low them to ”get by” with minimal conceptual engagement. This surface orientation can
become self-reinforcing if students perceive that short-term success, such as passing
exams, does not require deeper comprehension.
In the context of an AI assistant, there is a dual potential. On the one hand, the LLM
can guide students toward deeper understanding by offering hints, clarifying complex
terms, or illustrating reasoning steps in logical frameworks. On the other hand, it can
also tempt students into surface strategies by simply accepting AI-generated solutions
without verification or reflection. This dichotomy is especially relevant in a domain that
requires conceptual rigor. By analysing students’ reflections, confidence ratings, and en-
gagement levels, the study can determine whether the presence of AI nudges students
toward deeper engagement or reinforces superficial coping strategies.

3.2.3
Technology Acceptance Model
The Technology Acceptance Model, introduced by Davis [30], provides insight into how
users come to adopt new technologies. It identifies perceived usefulness and perceived
ease of use as key factors of a user’s intention to use and, ultimately, their actual usage of
a technological tool. Particularly, in an educational context, these factors can influence not
only whether students initially try a novel system, but also how thoroughly and consistently
they integrate it into their study routines over time.
Applying the Technology Acceptance Model to an LLM AI tool in a Master’s-level course
offers a way to understand students’ behavioral responses to the tool. Students are more
likely to embrace the AI tool if they find it genuinely useful for tackling complex prob-
lems and if its interface is intuitive and accessible. If the LLM reduces cognitive load by
explaining difficult proofs or guiding probabilistic reasoning steps, it may heighten its per-
ceived usefulness. Conversely, if students encounter confusing instructions or unhelpful
answers, their perceived ease of use and overall acceptance may diminish.
In addition to usefulness and ease of use, user attitudes and trust can shape technology
acceptance. For instance, if the LLM displays accurate and coherent answers across
multiple interactions, students may develop a positive attitude that reinforces adoption.
However, sporadic errors or ”hallucinations” in AI outputs could undermine users’ confi-
dence in the tool’s reliability. This interplay between perceived system quality and user
trust emphasises the importance of clear guidelines on verifying AI-generated outputs,
mitigating potential pitfalls that might discourage sustained use.
By examining students’ reported ease of interaction and their reflections on how the LLM
supported their learning goals, the Technology Acceptance Model provides a framework to
interpret the adoption patterns observed in the study. It also connects to other constructs
like trust and confidence where students who perceive the LLM as consistently helpful may
be more inclined to rely on it, influencing their approach to problem-solving. Ultimately,
understanding these dynamics can guide educators in designing course activities that
leverage the LLM’s strengths and minimise students’ negative perceptions, ensuring that
advanced AI tools are integrated in a way that aligns with students’ educational needs.
3.2.4
Self-Determination Theory
Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Deci and Ryan [31], emphasises the im-
portance of intrinsic motivation and the fulfillment of three basic psychological needs,
namely autonomy, competence, and relatedness, for effective learning. Autonomy refers
to an individual’s sense of control over their actions and decisions. Competence involves
feeling capable and confident in achieving desired outcomes. Relatedness reflects the
need to feel connected to others and develop a sense of belonging. For students in ad-
vanced courses, the increased complexity of the material can either reinforce these needs,
or leave them feeling overwhelmed if sufficient support is not provided.
In such challenging academic contexts, the perception of autonomy often plays a crucial
role in sustaining motivation. If students feel they have meaningful choices about how
to approach complex problems they are more likely to take ownership of their learning.
Equally important, feeling an increasing sense of competence when they make progress
in rigorous tasks can reinforce their belief in their own capabilities, fostering continued
effort and curiosity rather than disengagement.
The integration of an LLM can influence these motivational factors. For autonomy, the LLM
can offer multiple solution paths or alternative explanations, allowing students to choose

how they engage with material. For competence, the LLM’s immediate feedback can help
students gauge their understanding, refine their reasoning processes, and catch errors
early. Relatedness, though less direct, could be fostered if the LLM prompts collaborative
discussions, with students comparing notes on how they used the AI’s suggestions.
However, there is a caveat. Overreliance on AI-generated solutions may undermine au-
tonomy and competence if students feel that their ability to solve problems independently
diminishes. Students who routinely rely on the LLM’s suggestions may begin to doubt
their own capabilities or sense of control over the learning process. Monitoring students’
reported confidence levels and their subjective feelings about problem-solving after using
the LLM can reveal whether the tool supports or hinders the psychological factors that
support intrinsic motivation.
3.3
Summary
This theoretical framework merges well-established educational theories with the emerg-
ing reality of advanced AI tools in higher education. By applying Constructive Alignment,
we ensure that AI integration is purposeful and goal-oriented. Through the perspective
of Deep vs. Surface Learning, we assess the depth of student engagement with com-
plex material. Technology Acceptance Model explains how perceptions of usefulness
and ease of use shape technology adoption, and Self-Determination Theory sheds light
on how AI interactions influence autonomy, competence and relatedness.
Together, these theories guide the research design, data collection, and analysis meth-
ods of this study. They provide a structured conceptual map for interpreting findings and
deriving insights into how LLM-based AI assistants can, when effectively aligned, enrich
learning in domains that demand conceptual rigor and critical thinking.

4
Methodology
This chapter outlines the methodological framework and processes used to explore the
integration of a AI-based AI assistant into a Master’s-level course on Logical Theories for
Uncertainty and Learning. The study employed a mixed-methods approach, combining
both quantitative and qualitative data to gain a solid understanding of how students in-
teracted with the AI assistant, how it influenced their confidence and engagement, and
whether it facilitated deeper learning.
4.1
Research Design and Approach
4.1.1
Mixed-Methods Design
A mixed-methods design was adopted to capture the ways in which an AI assistant could
influence students’ learning processes and outcomes. The quantitative component in-
volved collecting numerical data on student confidence, the perceived quality of the AI
assistant’s solutions, and post-hoc questionnaire responses. The qualitative component
involved gathering open-ended textual feedback, reflective comparisons between human-
and AI-generated solutions, and responses to open-ended questionnaire questions. By
integrating these data sources, the study aimed to produce an understanding of the im-
pact of the AI assistant on student learning in a complex and conceptually demanding
subject.
4.2
Context and Participants
4.2.1
Course Setting
The study took place in a Master’s-level course titled Logical Theories for Uncertainty
and Learning at the Technical University of Denmark (Danmarks Tekniske Universitet,
DTU). The course, open to Bachelor’s degree holding students with an engineering back-
ground, aimed to equip students with an understanding of various logic and probability-
based frameworks for modeling knowledge, uncertainty, and learning. Specific learning
objectives included:
• Describing prevalent techniques for modeling uncertainty and learning,
• Evaluating the respective roles and limitations of logic and probability in modeling
knowledge,
• Comparing different techniques for solving given knowledge-modeling problems,
• Exploring and writing about relevant literature in a style akin to a conference article.
4.2.2
Participant Demographics
A total of 85 students from diverse backgrounds and ages, all holding at least a Bachelor’s
degree in some engineering discipline, enrolled in the course. Their prior experience
with AI tools varied widely, from no familiarity to extensive familiarity using AI-powered
assistants. While all 85 students engaged with the AI assistant for the two homework

assignments, participation in the final questionnaire was voluntary. In total, 38 students
chose to respond to the questionnaire, providing both quantitative ratings and open-ended
comments.
4.3
Integration Description
4.3.1
AI Assistant Integration
The AI assistant used in this study was created within OpenAI’s GPT ecosystem. Custom
instructions and relevant course materials were embedded into the GPT model, func-
tioning as a form of fine-tuning of the model to the course. This GPT model was then
shared with students via a unique link. Students were instructed to treat the AI assistant
as a supplementary learning resource, seeking clarifications, explanations, and alterna-
tive perspectives on course material. Guidelines emphasised the importance of verifying
AI-generated content and not relying solely on the assistant for correct answers.
Students received a set of best practices for engaging with the AI assistant. These in-
cluded verifying outputs through their own knowledge and course notes, iteratively refining
prompts if responses were unclear, and maintaining awareness of the assistant’s poten-
tial for mistakes or biases. Importantly, they were informed that the AI assistant should
not replace collaboration with peers, teaching assistants, or instructors but instead serve
as an additional resource for exploring course concepts.
4.3.2
Homework Assignments
Two separate Homework Assignments were designed to integrate AI usage in a structured
manner. Each assignment consisted of two parts:
• Part A: Did not require the use of the AI assistant. The students solved the problems
in this part independently, without the use of AI.
• Part B: Explicitly incorporated the AI assistant.
– Exercise B1: A more mathematical or advanced problem where the AI assis-
tant was expected to perform less well given the complexity.
– Exercise B2: A definition-based exercise requiring students to explain the dif-
ference between conceptual topics covered in the course. The AI assistant
was expected to perform more accurately on this less reason-intensive, more
descriptive task.
Students were asked to initially solve Exercises B1 and B2 without any AI assistance,
noting down their solutions. They then recorded their confidence levels (from 1 to 5).
Next, they consulted the AI assistant using the same exercises, recorded their confidence
in the AI’s solutions (also on a 1–5 scale), and wrote brief reflections comparing their own
answers to the AI’s. Finally, they reported whether their confidence in solving similar
problems increased, decreased, or remained unchanged after reading the AI’s solution,
and whether their understanding of the problem and engagement with the exercise were
influenced.

4.4
Data Sources and Instruments
4.4.1
Quantitative Measures
Confidence Ratings (Exercises B1 and B2)
In Exercises B1 and B2, students were asked to assess their confidence in their own solu-
tions and in the solutions provided by the AI assistant. For both assessments, they used
a 1–5 scale, where 1 represented Not confident at all and 5 represented Very confident.
The responses were then recorded and organised in an Excel sheet for further analysis.
Categorical Responses (Confidence Change, Understanding, Engagement)
Following a comparison of their answers with those provided by the AI, students re-
ported changes in their confidence when approaching similar problems. They reported
whether their confidence had increased, decreased, or remained unchanged. Addition-
ally, they evaluated the impact of the AI’s solution on their understanding of the problem
and whether the exercise felt more engaging, using the same system.
Google Forms Questionnaire
Before the final exam, and after completing both homework assignments, students were
invited to participate in an anonymous Google Forms questionnaire. This questionnaire
collected additional quantitative data through Likert scale questions, addressing topics
such as the usability and perceived quality of the AI assistant, as well as their confidence
levels. It also gathered demographic information, including their familiarity with AI tools.
A total of 38 students completed the questionnaire, with multiple-choice and Likert scale
responses being coded and organised for descriptive and inferential statistical analysis.
4.4.2
Qualitative Measures
Reflective Comparisons
Following their interaction with the AI for Exercises B1 and B2, students wrote short anal-
yses highlighting similarities and differences between their solutions and the AI’s. These
textual reflections offered insight into students’ critical thinking and perceived value of AI
feedback.
Open-Ended Questionnaire Responses
The Google Forms survey included free-text questions about how the AI assistant influ-
enced learning, challenges encountered, and suggestions for improvement. Responses
provided rich qualitative data on students’ subjective experiences and attitudes toward
the AI tool.
4.5
Data Collection Procedures
4.5.1
Homework Submissions
Students had one and a half weeks to complete each homework assignment, with ap-
proximately one month’s interval between the two assignments. They submitted their

solutions, along with their confidence ratings and reflections, through the course’s estab-
lished submission system. The resulting data, which included students’ original answers,
the AI outputs they received, confidence ratings, and short comparisons, were compiled
and anonymised.
4.5.2
Online Questionnaire
After both homework assignments were submitted, students were given a link to a Google
Forms questionnaire. The questionnaire was voluntary and anonymous, collecting both
quantitative ratings (on Likert or multiple choice scales) and qualitative reflections (in
open-ended fields). The survey explicitly stated that their grades would not be affected
by participation and included a disclaimer indicating the data’s intended use for research
purposes only.
4.5.3
Data Storage and Anonymisation
All data collected, including student submissions and questionnaire responses, were stored
on secure, password-protected platforms in accordance with institutional data protection
guidelines. Personal identifiers were replaced with codes to preserve anonymity. Only
aggregate results and anonymised quotes were used in the subsequent analysis and re-
porting.
4.6
Data Analysis Methods
4.6.1
Quantitative Analysis
Descriptive Statistics
Descriptive statistics were used to analyse the data, with mean, standard deviation, me-
dian and mode for confidence ratings, both self-assessed and student-evaluated con-
fidence in the AI, having been calculated and represented visually through bar charts.
Additionally, the proportions of students reporting increases, decreases, or no change
in confidence, understanding, and engagement were summarised using bar charts and
frequency tables for a clear overview of the results.
Correlation Analysis
To explore the relationships between key variables, Spearman’s rank correlation analysis
was conducted, given the ordinal nature of the data. This analysis examined connections
like the students’ self-confidence and their confidence in the AI assistant’s solutions.
4.6.2
Qualitative Analysis
Manual Thematic Analysis
A manual thematic analysis was conducted to examine open-ended responses from the
reflective comparisons and the questionnaire. The goal was to identify recurring themes
related to student engagement, perceived learning, and challenges encountered. A cod-
ing scheme was developed to categorise students’ perceptions of how the AI assistant
influenced their problem-solving process, with categories such as clarified concepts, and
encouraged self-checking having been identified.

Interpretation of Feedback
Student reflections on comparing their own solutions to the AI’s were reviewed for ev-

4.7
Grade Comparison Between 2023 and 2024
In addition to the primary quantitative and qualitative data sources described above, anonymised
final grade data for the course in both 2023 and 2024 were provided by the course in-
structor. The 2023 group of students did not use any AI tools, whereas the 2024 group
integrated an AI assistant into homework assignments and other activities throughout the
semester. Grades were reported on the standard 7-point scale (A = 12, B = 10, C = 7, D
= 4, E = 2, Fx = 0, and F = -3), and were available for five parameters, namely Homework
Assignment 1, Homework Assignment 2, Exam, Scientific Paper Report, and the Final
Grade.
4.7.1
Statistical Analysis
To investigate whether incorporating AI corresponded with significant shifts in perfor-
mance, two statistical tests were applied to each assessment:
• A Chi-square test of independence was used to examine whether the distribution of
letter grades (A, B, C, etc.) differed significantly between 2023 and 2024.
• A Welch’s t-test was used to compare the mean numerical grades (12, 10, 7, etc.)
between the two groups. Welch’s t-test was chosen over a standard t-test to account
for potential differences in variance and slightly different sample sizes (85 students
in 2023 vs. 91 in 2024).
All grade data were fully anonymised prior to analysis, and no additional demographic
information was linked to individual grade outcomes. The significance level for both the
Chi-square and Welch’s t-test was set at α = 0.05. Detailed results from these analyses
are presented in Section 5.5 of the Results chapter.
4.8
Ethical Considerations
4.8.1
Informed Consent
All students were informed at the start of the course about the AI assistant’s role and the
study’s purpose. For the optional questionnaire, students were presented with a clear
consent disclaimer, reminding them that their participation was voluntary and their grades
would not be affected by their responses.
4.8.2
Privacy and Anonymity
Homework data and questionnaire responses were collected in anonymised form. Iden-
tifying details were either omitted or replaced with numeric codes. In compliance with
institutional guidelines, only the principal investigator and authorised research personnel
had access to the raw data.

4.9
Limitations of the Methodology
Despite the thorough data collection, several methodological constraints should be noted.
First, participation in the questionnaire was voluntary, with only 38 out of 85 students
choosing to respond, potentially affecting the representativeness of the results. Second,
measures of confidence and engagement were based on subjective self-reports, which
are susceptible to biases such social desirability. Third, the results may have limited gen-
eralisability due to the specific and unique complexity of the course, which may not reflect
other domains or educational settings. Finally, potential influencing variables, such as
differences in prior experience with AI tools or individual learning styles, may have influ-
enced how students engaged with the assistant, but these factors were not fully controlled
in the study.
4.10
Summary
This chapter outlines the systematic approach taken to investigate the efficacy and impact
of integrating a GPT-based AI assistant into a challenging Master’s-level course. The
study’s mixed-methods design makes use of both quantitative and qualitative insights
to provide a holistic view of how students engaged with and perceived the AI assistant.
By detailing participant demographics, integration methods, data collection methods, and
ethical considerations, this chapter lays the groundwork for the subsequent discussion of
the results and their implications for using AI in higher education contexts.

dence changed, we conducted a chi-square test of independence with these two groups
(high vs. low self-confidence) and the three-category outcome for change in confidence
(increased, decreased, unchanged).

which is students’ initial self-confidence (high vs. low), and the dependent variable, which
is reported change in confidence (increased, decreased, unchanged), are categorical.

pendent variable differs systematically between the categories of the independent vari-
able, thus allowing us to determine if there is an association between initial self-confidence
levels and how students’ confidence changed after using the AI assistant. Thus we have
the following hypotheses:

5.3
Qualitative Results from Homework Assignments
5.3.1
Thematic Analysis for Homework Assignment Reflections
Confidence in Problem-Solving
In complex reasoning tasks, many students experienced a confidence boost when they
saw that the AI’s solution aligned with their own. This agreement served as validation,
especially when students recognised the same train of thought and realised that their logic
was sound. Others, however, felt less confident upon comparing their explanations to the
AI’s polished, step-by-step approach. They sometimes perceived their own solutions as
”incomplete” or lacking the AI’s formal structure. This sense of being outdone linguistically
also surfaced for simpler, definition-based questions. While some students reported no
change in their fundamental understanding of the concept, they grew less confident in
their ability to articulate explanations with similar clarity.
Notably, students tended to differentiate confidence in correctness (i.e., believing they
had reached the right answer) from confidence in presentation (i.e., believing they could
present the answer as well or as thoroughly as the AI). In the simpler tasks, where the AI
often provided neatly structured definitions, students realised that their own descriptions
lacked the AI’s ”textbook-like” style. This created slight decreases in confidence for some,
even when they were sure their underlying ideas were accurate.
Influence on Understanding
Across both complex and simpler exercises, most students remarked that the AI helped
clarify or deepen their understanding of core concepts. Particularly in definition-heavy
contexts, such as explaining course concepts like probability measures or belief vs. plau-
sibility, the AI’s ability to switch between formal mathematical language and intuitive ex-
planations proved beneficial. Students appreciated getting alternative perspectives or
rephrasings, which not only reinforced their existing knowledge but also exposed them to
details they might have missed.
Still, a few students noted caution when the AI employed unfamiliar syntax or provided
novel reformulations. While they might appreciate the new viewpoint, they wanted to
verify its reliability against course materials or standard references before fully embracing
it. Hence, the AI was most helpful when it complemented students’ existing knowledge,
allowing them to interrogate or explore nuances rather than when students felt compelled
to rely on the AI for entire solutions from scratch.
Engagement and Interaction
Reflections on engagement were more polarised. A substantial group found the AI’s in-
teractive, conversational nature to be motivating and, at times, even entertaining. They
enjoyed the dialogue aspect, asking the AI for clarifications, cross, checking differing an-
swers, or ”debating” its reasoning. Some compared the AI to a ”study companion”, which
made the process less lonely and encouraged them to spend more time thinking about the
problem. Others described it as a helpful ”second opinion” that made them more attentive
to their own arguments, especially if the AI’s solution conflicted with theirs.

On the other hand, a notable subset found the AI to be less engaging or even added
”extra work” if they had to verify its correctness after already solving the problem inde-
pendently. Repetitive or verbose explanations, potential errors, and the need to interpret
the AI’s formal approach sometimes felt tedious, especially if the exercise at hand was
straightforward. In such cases, students preferred traditional resources like textbooks,
lecture notes, or peer discussions and felt that checking the AI’s output was unnecessary
labor.
Perceptions of AI Correctness and Utility
When asked explicitly about their confidence in the AI’s correctness, most students indi-
cated they believed the AI to be factually accurate in simpler tasks, though occasionally
”incomplete” regarding assignment-specific requirements (e.g., using personal language
instead of textbook jargon). For more sophisticated problems, students generally recog-
nised that the AI could be a powerful resource for step-by-step logic, but it also risked
introducing errors. This led to an overall attitude of ”cautious trust”. The AI’s solutions
were often correct enough to validate key steps or confirm final answers, but students felt
responsible for critically reviewing the AI’s output rather than accepting it at face value.
The utility of the AI was tied to how well it addressed the learning objectives of each ex-
ercise. Students found it most beneficial when they could examine and adapt the AI’s
explanation to their own style. In fact, many recognised the AI as a valuable clarifica-
tion tool rather than a full-solution provider, particularly for clarifying definitions, exploring
alternate viewpoints, and finding new lines of reasoning.
5.4
Qualitative Results from Questionnaire
5.4.1
Thematic Analysis for Questionnaire Open-Ended Responses
Simplification of Complex Concepts
A recurring theme across the responses is that students gravitated toward the AI assistant
for its ability to break down difficult or abstract ideas into more digestible explanations.
Many students commented that definitions in textbooks or slides could feel too dense or
not always easy to relate to, whereas the AI offered alternative rephrasings and tangible
examples. In particular, students praised how the tool gave clearer distinctions between
similar but nuanced ideas. For students struggling with a given topic, the AI’s capacity to
simplify jargon and provide illustrative scenarios proved invaluable, serving as a reference
point that complemented, rather than replaced, the official course materials.
Personalised and Contextual Support
In addition to the AI’s skill at clarifying concepts, students highlighted its usefulness in of-
fering personalised, repeated explanations. Several students with specific learning needs,
including ADHD, described the AI as a ”miracle solution” when it came to obtaining the
extensive context they required. Because the AI can dynamically generate multiple ex-
amples, it effectively tailored responses to each individual’s needs. Rather than feeling
pressed for time or left behind in fast-paced lectures, these students were able to request
more iterations or further elaboration, thus deepening their comprehension. This interac-
tive, ”ask anything” dynamic allowed them to explore nuances that might be disregarded
in traditional course formats.

Facilitating Problem-Solving and Exploration
Another mentioned benefit was how students used the AI as a sort of ”study partner”
or ”sparring partner”. When confronted with a problem, they would compare their own
reasoning with the AI’s suggestions. Even when the AI presented flawed or incomplete
solutions, this mismatch often led to deeper reflection. Students noted that pointing out
the AI’s mistakes or requesting a more thorough justification helped them refine their own
understanding. In this sense, the AI served not just as a source of information but also as
a catalyst for active learning, leading to iterative questions and back-and-forth dialogue
similar to that of collaborative study sessions.
Deepening Theoretical Understanding
Beyond simple clarifications, the AI also helped students in seeking more intuitive expla-
nations of complex frameworks. From belief functions to game-theory concepts, students
reported that the AI excelled at integrating and distilling key insights behind these theo-
ries, offering a high-level why rather than just the what. This guidance helped them see
how new concepts linked back to the broader structure of the course material.
Confidence and Need for Verification
Responses on how the AI impacted confidence were mixed but highlight the value of ver-
ification. On one hand, matching answers with the AI or receiving detailed clarifications
could confirm a student’s grasp of the material boosting their confidence. On the other
hand, students quickly recognised that the AI is prone to errors, especially on logic-heavy
or domain-specific tasks, and learned that blind trust could be detrimental. Many reported
adopting a ”cautious trust” attitude since they found the AI’s explanations reassuring, but
only after cross-checking them against course textbooks, lecture notes, or their own in-
tuition. Thus, the AI’s role in reinforcing confidence was closely tied to each student’s
willingness to double-check its accuracy.
Overreliance, Misinformation, and Erosion of Self-Confidence
Several students described instances where the AI hindered their understanding or caused
them to doubt correct solutions they initially had. Due to the AI’s tendency to present incor-
rect answers with certainty, students occasionally found themselves questioning whether
they truly understood a concept. This erosion of self-confidence was most pronounced
when students lacked sufficient background knowledge to spot the AI’s mistakes. They
were susceptible to adopting incorrect definitions or solutions at face value.
Conse-
quently, students highlighted the need for approaching AI responses with critical thinking
to avoid being led astray.
Suggestions for Improvement and Integration
Students emphasised the importance of clarifying how, when, and to what extent the AI
should be used within the coursework. Many also suggested adopting a ”teaching assis-
tant” style, in which the AI gives strategic hints or references to relevant course materials
in an attempt to nudge students towards solutions rather than giving them outright. An-
other recommendation was to fine-tune or better train the AI on specific domain content
so that it is more aligned with the complexities of the course. Finally, students consistently
advocated for fostering ”AI literacy”, instructing users on how to evaluate, verify, and, if
necessary, challenge the AI’s outputs to ensure they develop robust, independent critical
thinking skills.

The study’s quantitative analyses indicate that self-confidence (SC) and confidence in

exercises, implying that students with higher self-confidence also tended to trust the AI
more for those exercises. A key outcome was the change in confidence observed among
different groups of students after comparing their work to the AI’s, though changes were
not uniform. Chi-square tests indicated that the AI’s impact not only depended on initial
self-confidence but also depended on both the nature and content of the exercise.
The questionnaire results reinforced these patterns. Students rated the AI as easy to use
and appreciated its clarifications, but expressed some caution about trusting it outright
highlighting a need for critical verification. Qualitative reflections from the homework and
open-ended questionnaire questions reinforced these results. Many students valued the
AI as a ”study partner”, yet its errors occasionally eroded trust or self-confidence. Overall,
the AI assistant often enhanced engagement and deepened understanding, especially for
students seeking conceptual clarifications. However, critical thinking and clear guidelines
remain essential to maximise benefits and minimise the risks of overreliance or confusion.
Beyond these individual-level insights, a comparison of overall course grades suggested
relatively minor shifts in mean performance across most assessments, with one notable
exception. The scientific paper report grade distribution and mean both declined in 2024
when AI was used. This finding points to possible changes in how students approached
deeper analytical or writing tasks after AI integration. The final grade, however, showed
no statistically significant difference in either distribution or mean, indicating that general
mastery of course content appeared to remain consistent year over year. Taken together,
these results imply that while AI can be beneficial for conceptual clarity and student en-
gagement, its impact on final performance may depend strongly on the nature of the as-
signment and the approach of students to using the AI.

This chapter interprets the results presented in the previous chapter and situates them
within the context of existing literature and the theoretical framework supporting this study.
First, the major quantitative and qualitative results are briefly revisited. Next, these re-
sults are connected to relevant educational theories, namely Constructive Alignment,

6.1
Overview of Key Results
6.1.1
Confidence Patterns and Task Type
A clear distinction emerged between the mathematically demanding exercises (B1) and
the definition-based exercises (B2). Students often reported higher self-confidence than
their confidence in the AI for the more complex tasks, where the AI tended to underper-
form. Conversely, in simpler or more conceptual tasks, where the AI excelled, students’
confidence in the AI frequently surpassed their own.
Quantitative data revealed this pattern clearly. Descriptive statistics showed that, on av-
erage, self-confidence (SC) was higher than confidence in the AI (AIC) for B1, while the
reverse was true for B2, where AIC exceeded SC.
Qualitative reflections supported these results, with many students describing the AI’s
conceptual explanations as thorough and ”textbook-like.” However, they also noted that
the AI’s mathematical solutions were occasionally faulty, limiting its reliability for complex
problem-solving tasks.
6.1.2
Association Between Self-Confidence and Confidence in the AI
Spearman’s rank correlations showed moderate positive relationships between SC and
AIC especially in the definition-based exercises (B2), suggesting that the higher a stu-
dent’s confidence was the higher was their confidence in the AI in simpler contexts. How-
ever, in B1, these correlations were weaker or nearly nonexistent, highlighting that trust
in AI was weaker when the problem was complex and the AI’s accuracy less reliable.
6.1.3
Changes in Confidence and Initial Self-Confidence
Across both homework assignments, the majority of students reported either an increase
or no change in confidence, with only a smaller fraction experiencing decreases. How-
ever, chi-square tests suggested that the confidence change was not only influenced by
students’ initial self-confidence levels but also by the nature and content of the exercise.
For instance, in specific cases such as HW1-B1 for students with low initial self-confidence,
there was a significant correlation between their starting confidence and the changes they
experienced after interacting with the AI. Similarly, in the case of HW2-B2, students with
high initial self-confidence were significantly more likely to exhibit distinct patterns of con-
fidence change or stability, compared to their less confident peers. These results suggest

that initial confidence levels played a nuanced role in shaping students’ responses to the
AI assistant’s outputs.
6.1.4
Questionnaire Insights
The questionnaire data revealed that students generally found the AI assistant both easy
to use and useful, with mean ratings ranging between 3.5 and 4.0 on a 1–5 scale. Despite
these positive evaluations, students expressed reservations about the AI’s trustworthiness
in specific areas, emphasising the importance of critically verifying its outputs.
Open-ended responses further shed light on several recurring themes. Many students ap-

helpful for understanding complex topics. At the same time, there was notable skepticism,
with students adopting a cautious approach to reliance on the tool. Some highlighted the
potential for deeper engagement facilitated by the AI, while others reported occasional
erosion of self-confidence when the AI presented conflicting or incorrect solutions.
Overall, students expressed a preference for integrating the AI assistant into future course-

change final academic achievement.

6.2
Interpretation Through Educational Theories
6.2.1
Constructive Alignment
Constructive Alignment, as proposed by Biggs and Tang (1999) [28], emphasises the
importance of coherently structuring learning activities, outcomes, and assessments to
foster meaningful learning. In this study, the AI assistant was intentionally integrated into
the homework design to align with this framework.
The course prioritises both mathematical rigor and conceptual understanding. To ad-
dress these objectives, we assigned two exercises to each homework assignment, B1,
which were complex, mathematical tasks, and B2, which were conceptual, definition-
based tasks. The AI assistant was employed for each type of exercise and students
were guided to compare its solutions with their own and reflect on the differences. This
approach aligns with Biggs’s framework by explicitly tying the AI-supported activities to
the course’s intended learning outcomes.
The integration of reflective practices enhanced alignment with constructive learning prin-
ciples. Students were asked to articulate whether and how their confidence changed after
interacting with the AI, fostering metacognition through the analysis of their own cognitive
processes. By critically evaluating the AI’s outputs in relation to their own reasoning,
students engaged in activities that encouraged deeper learning and self-awareness.
Some misalignment may have emerged in tasks where the AI underperformed. Students
who relied heavily on the AI for these tasks might have encountered confusion or misin-
formation. Ensuring alignment thus requires explicit instruction on how to critically assess
AI outputs for advanced exercises.
Interestingly, the 2024 group of students’ lower report grades may indicate misalignment
between intended deeper analytical work and the manner in which AI was utilised, thereby
highlighting the need to guide students in critically evaluating AI-generated content in such
written tasks.
6.2.2
Deep vs. Surface Learning
The dichotomy between deep and surface learning, as described by Marton and Säljö
(1976) [29], provides a valuable perspective for interpreting students’ interactions with
the AI assistant in this study.
Several students reported using the AI to explore alternative solution paths, critically eval-
uate its logic, or reinterpret their own approaches. These behaviors suggest a deeper,
more engaged learning approach, particularly in simpler tasks where the AI’s explana-
tions were accurate. In such cases, students leveraged the AI to reinforce or expand their
understanding, demonstrating characteristics of deep learning. The sense of autonomy
and curiosity present in these interactions also reflected increased intrinsic motivation,
indicating that students’ active engagement with the AI contributed to a more meaningful
and personally driven learning experience.
Conversely, some students admitted to accepting the AI’s solutions without fully verify-
ing them or simply memorising its phrasings. These practices reflect a surface learning
approach, where the primary focus is on achieving correctness or task completion rather
than on developing a genuine understanding of the material. Task complexity appeared
to play a key role in whether the AI led to deep or surface approaches. In definition-based
AI’s Transformation of Educational Paradigms

==PAGE:48==
tasks, the AI’s clarity often reinforced comprehension, while in more advanced problems
students sometimes defaulted to a superficial acceptance of AI-generated solutions.
In light of the 2023 vs. 2024 grade comparison, there is some concern that surface learn-
ing may have increased among 2024 students for longer, report-style assessments, where
more passive reliance on AI-generated text could diminish critical thinking. Nevertheless,
the stable final exam scores imply that fundamental mastery was largely maintained. This
tension highlights how AI tools can foster deeper exploration for certain tasks while si-
multaneously risking shallow engagement in others, suggesting a nuanced interaction
between AI use and overall course outcomes.
6.2.3
Technology Acceptance Model
Davis’s Technology Acceptance Model (1989) [30] proposes that user acceptance of new
technologies depends on two key factors which are perceived usefulness (PU) and per-
ceived ease of use (PEOU). The results from this study provide insights into both dimen-
sions.
Most students who completed the questionnaire reported that the AI assistant was help-
ful, particularly for clarifying definitions and offering step-by-step logic. These responses
reflect a high level of perceived usefulness, as the AI effectively supported learning in
areas where it performed well.
The AI assistant also received strong ratings for ease of use, with a mean score of approx-
imately 4.4 on a 5-point scale. This suggests that students found the tool straightforward
and accessible, supporting a high level of perceived ease of use.
High PU and high PEOU typically lead to positive attitudes and behavioral intention to use
the technology, which is evident in the finding that many students intend to keep using AI
tools in future courses. However, trust concerns also emerged, suggesting that accep-
tance is moderated by the AI’s perceived reliability in domain-specific contexts, aligning
with research indicating that trustworthiness can affect long-term adoption.
6.2.4
Self-Determination Theory
Self-Determination Theory by Deci and Ryan (1985) [31] identifies competence, auton-
omy, and relatedness as essential elements of motivation. The use of the AI assistant in
this study impacted each of these dimensions in distinct ways.
Students reported varied effects on their sense of competence when comparing their so-
lutions to the AI’s outputs. For some, observing alignment between their work and the AI’s
or learning new insights from the tool enhanced their sense of competence. However, for
others, encountering superior or contradictory solutions from the AI eroded their confi-
dence, highlighting the influence of AI feedback on a student’s perceived competence.
The AI assistant offered students an additional resource to explore the material inde-
pendently and at their own pace, thereby supporting their sense of autonomy. Students
could choose which questions to pose and how deeply to engage with the AI’s explana-
tions, allowing them to tailor their learning experience to personal needs or preferences.
However, there is a potential downside if students grow overly reliant on AI-based so-
lutions, effectively outsourcing their problem-solving processes. Such dependence risks
undermining true autonomy and may inhibit the development of core analytical and critical
thinking skills, particularly when facing more complex, open-ended tasks.

6.3
Implications
6.3.1
Pedagogical Integration
To maximise the educational benefits of AI tools, instructors should adopt a guided ap-
proach to their usage and ensure alignment with the learning context.
The results suggest that instructors should provide clear and explicit guidelines on how
and when to use the AI effectively. For instance, students can be encouraged to engage
in iterative questioning to refine their understanding, cross-check the AI’s outputs against
lecture materials or trusted sources, and critically reflect on any discrepancies they en-
counter. Additionally, educators should clearly communicate where in the coursework stu-
dents are incentivised or disincentivised to use the AI. This can include specifying tasks
where AI use is encouraged for exploration and learning, as well as those where inde-
pendent problem-solving or original reasoning is required. Establishing these boundaries
ensures that students use the AI purposefully and appropriately, fostering a balanced and
thoughtful engagement with the tool.
Additionally, incorporating a Socratic approach into these guidelines as well as into the AI
solutions can further strengthen students’ critical engagement. Rather than providing di-
rect solutions, the AI can be designed or instructed to respond with exploratory questions,
for example, asking students to justify a step, compare alternative definitions, or asking
how they arrived at a given result. This mirrors the essence of Socratic questioning,
where students are gently ”nudged” to articulate their reasoning, challenge assumptions,
and discover gaps in understanding on their own. By prompting students to supply the
reasoning for each step, the AI would foster active, deep learning and help them develop
a more robust understanding of the course material.
6.3.2
Encouraging Balanced Confidence
Introducing AI tools into the classroom can have mixed effects on student confidence.
While some students may gain valuable reassurance from the AI, others may feel over-
shadowed if the AI’s outputs appear more polished than their own solutions. To address
these varied outcomes, it is essential to foster a balanced sense of confidence that recog-
nises the AI’s potential value but preserves individual problem-solving autonomy.
To this end, instructors can encourage students to maintain a healthy level of skepticism
when the AI’s responses conflict with their own reasoning. Rather than accepting the
AI’s output at face value or automatically doubting themselves, students benefit most
by examining discrepancies, verifying assumptions, and refining their conclusions. This
reflective process supports both self-reliance and the productive use of AI, ensuring that
the tool complements rather than displaces critical thinking skills.

6.3.3
Enhancing Trust and Reliability
The data reveals that trust in the AI’s correctness is not consistent among students, high-
lighting the need for strategies to enhance its reliability and effectiveness. However, this
issue is likely to improve over time as AI models continue to evolve, becoming increasingly
adept at reasoning and handling more advanced tasks. With ongoing advancements in
AI development, future iterations of models are expected to exhibit greater accuracy and
reliability in tackling domain-specific challenges.
Future AI tools could be fine-tuned on the specific content of advanced courses. By tailor-
ing the AI’s training to include the precise mathematical and logical frameworks covered
in the curriculum, the likelihood of factual errors in solutions or explanations could be sig-
nificantly reduced. This refinement, combined with the natural progression of AI models
toward more sophisticated reasoning capabilities, will enhance AI tools’ effectiveness in
educational contexts.
Encouraging students to ”flag” AI mistakes for discussion with peers or educators can fos-
ter a collaborative learning environment. This practice not only helps identify and address
errors but also normalises the process of critically evaluating both human and AI outputs.
Creating a safe space for such explorations reinforces the notion that mistakes, whether
from the AI or the student, are valuable opportunities for growth and deeper understand-
ing.
6.4
Limitations of the Study
While this research provides insights into the integration of AI in educational settings,
several limitations should be acknowledged.
6.4.1
Sample Size and Diversity
Although 85 students engaged with the AI assistant, only 38 completed the questionnaire.
It is possible that those who responded had stronger opinions, whether positive or nega-
tive, compared to non-respondents, potentially introducing response bias and limiting the
representativeness of the results.
Moreover, the sample was relatively homogeneous in terms of academic background and
level of study, as all participants were enrolled in a Master’s-level engineering course at
the same university. This limits the applicability of the findings to other student popula-
tions. Future research could benefit from broader participant pools that include students
from various institutions, and geographic locations to capture a more diverse range of
experiences and attitudes toward AI tools.
6.4.2
Self-Reported Measures
The study relied on self-reported measures of parameters like confidence levels and
changes in confidence. However, subjective impressions may not always align with ob-
jective performance outcomes, such as actual exam scores.
Additionally, factors such as recall bias and social desirability may have influenced re-
sponses. Students might inadvertently overestimate or underestimate their confidence
when filling out questionnaires, especially if they perceived a positive or negative stance
toward AI use as more acceptable. This issue highlights the challenge of drawing defini-
tive conclusions about the AI’s impact solely from self-reported data.

6.4.3
Contextual Specificity
The course content focused on logical and probabilistic reasoning, which may limit the
generalisability of the findings to other disciplines. When applying these insights to broader
contexts some caution should be had as the unique nature of the material may have in-
fluenced both the AI’s effectiveness and the students’ interactions with it. The specific
challenges of this course may create learning patterns that differ significantly from those
in fields where assignments center on creative production or rote memorisation, mean-
ing the extent to which AI-driven tools foster deeper engagement or, conversely, prompt
surface-level strategies may vary across domains.
6.4.4
Temporal Factors
Students’ familiarity with the AI tools likely evolved over the semester. As they became
more adept at using the AI and verifying its outputs, their experiences and confidence
levels may have shifted, potentially skewing the results of later assignments compared to
earlier ones. Such improvements in problem-solving or changes in perceived usefulness
could reflect both learning gains and growing proficiency in interacting with the AI rather
than the course content alone.
6.4.5
Lack of a True Control Group
To ensure fairness, all students had access to the AI tool and were given the same as-
signment questions. However, this approach meant that the lack of a control group made
it challenging to isolate the AI’s effects from other factors, such as individual learning pro-
gression or external influences. Designing a more controlled experimental study could
provide a clearer understanding of the AI’s specific impact. Such a future experiment,
however, would need to be conducted outside the context of regular assignments to main-
tain fairness. This would ensure that students are not disadvantaged in their coursework
due to differential access to the AI tool, while still allowing researchers to rigorously ex-
amine the tool’s influence.
6.4.6
Student Group Differences
Although this study compared the 2023 (no AI) and 2024 (with AI) groups, differences
in student composition or external factors may also have influenced performance. The
observed grade shifts could arise partly from variations in group characteristics rather than
AI integration alone. Nevertheless, as noted previously, the 2023 group was used only
as an approximate benchmark rather than a strict control, making any direct comparisons
interpretive rather than definitive. Future controlled designs, or random assignment within
a single semester, would help isolate the AI’s specific impact.
These limitations highlight the need for careful interpretation of the results and suggest
areas for improvement in future studies. Addressing these constraints could enhance the
robustness and applicability of future research on AI integration in education.

6.5.1
Longitudinal Studies
Future research could investigate how students’ confidence, engagement, and perfor-
mance evolve over multiple courses or semesters of AI usage. Tracking students over
time would reveal patterns in how familiarity with AI tools influences the development of
deeper analytical skills versus potential overreliance. Longitudinal studies could also help
identify critical points in a student’s journey where AI tools are most beneficial or where
intervention may be needed to correct overdependence.
6.5.2
Domain-Specific AI Tools
The development and evaluation of more specialised or fine-tuned AI assistants, trained
on specific course content, could help reduce inaccuracies and improve student trust. By
tailoring the model’s knowledge base to reflect the unique terminology and complexities of
a specific domain, educators can ensure that AI outputs are more accurate and context-
aware. Future research might explore how domain-specific tuning influences both error
rates and user acceptance.
6.5.3
AI Literacy Training
Another important direction involves investigating the efficacy of explicit ”AI literacy” mod-
ules, which would teach students to spot errors, critically question AI outputs, and employ
the tool effectively. By incorporating critical thinking strategies for verifying the correct-
ness of AI solutions, these modules could help students become more discerning users
of AI-generated content. Future research might explore how such guidance influences
both immediate performance outcomes and the long-term development of critical thinking
skills, determining whether it fosters deeper learning rather than passive acceptance of
AI answers. Ultimately, enhancing AI literacy could reduce overreliance and encourage
a more confident, reflective approach to integrating AI in advanced coursework.
6.5.4
Collaboration vs. Individual Use
Another area for exploration is how group-based interactions with AI tools compare to indi-
vidual use. Research could investigate whether collective engagement with AI-driven sug-
gestions enhances peer learning and fosters deeper critical examination, such as when
classmates discuss an AI-generated solution, challenge its validity, and collaboratively
refine their understanding. Conversely, it is possible that working in groups might rein-
force existing biases or encourage consensus-based errors, particularly if students place
too much confidence in AI outputs without sufficient skepticism. Gaining insight into these
collaborative dynamics would help educators design more effective group tasks and out-
line best practices for team-based AI usage, ensuring that group work with AI promotes
meaningful learning and robust problem-solving.
6.5.5
AI as a Guiding TA Rather Than a Solving Tool
Students have suggested using the AI in a teaching assistant (TA) style, where it offers
incremental hints or references to relevant course materials rather than full solutions.
By withholding final answers, the AI would more closely mimic a human TA who poses
questions that foster reflection or deeper analysis. This approach can be further enhanced
by adopting a Socratic style of dialogue, leading students to defend their reasoning step by
step. For example, instead of handing over a complete solution, the AI might ask ”What
does this definition imply about your next step?” or ”Why do you think that assumption
holds?”. Such Socratic nudges encourage metacognitive processes, requiring students
to articulate and evaluate their own thinking.

Investigating how this partial-disclosure model affects mastery of complex concepts would
be valuable. By measuring whether students gain deeper insights, and how that compares
to scenarios in which the AI provides direct solutions, future research could clarify whether
guided, question-based AI interactions lead to more sustained conceptual understanding.
If successful, this structure could mitigate surface learning by ensuring students wrestle
with problem-solving, while still benefiting from real-time feedback and clarification.

Summary
This study highlights both the potential and challenges of integrating an AI tool into ad-
vanced coursework. The AI’s strengths lie in clarifying conceptual material, providing
immediate feedback, and encouraging reflective comparisons between student and AI-
generated solutions. However, its limitations become evident in rigorous mathematical
tasks, where incorrect or faulty responses require critical engagement and structured
guidance to foster deep learning.
Frameworks such as Constructive Alignment, Deep vs. Surface Learning, the Technol-
ogy Acceptance Model, and Self-Determination Theory suggest that a well-integrated AI
tool can foster active, motivated students, provided it aligns with curriculum goals and
includes support to evaluate AI outputs. The study also reveals that factors like initial
self-confidence, task complexity, and perceived AI reliability significantly influence stu-
dent engagement.
Future research could explore refining the AI to act more like a guiding teaching assistant,
offering incremental hints and posing questions rather than providing final answers. This
approach could strengthen problem-solving skills, deepen understanding, and mitigate
overreliance, balancing AI support with student autonomy and critical thinking.
Ultimately, successful integration of AI in education requires more than advanced technol-
ogy. It demands thoughtful design, clear guidelines, and a culture of critical engagement
to empower students to use AI as a tool for deeper learning rather than a shortcut to
completion. By refining these practices, educators can ensure AI serves as a tool for
meaningful learning in complex domains.

Ethical Considerations
As AI tools become more prevalent in academic environments, their ethical implications
must be examined in parallel with their pedagogical value. This chapter addresses the
main ethical issues associated with deploying AI, particularly Large Language Models, in
higher education, with a focus on data privacy and security, bias and fairness, academic
integrity, and the broader social and psychological impact on students.
7.1
Data Privacy and Security
7.1.1
Collection and Use of Student Data
As educational institutions integrate LLM-based AI assistants, they may collect student
data in various forms, including usage logs, query text, and generated responses [25].
These data can be beneficial, enabling personalised feedback, adaptive content, and
data-driven insights, but also introduce risks if not properly safeguarded. Students may
be unaware that information entered into the AI assistant could be stored indefinitely,
potentially shared with third parties, or used to improve commercial models.
Mitigating these concerns requires a transparent and well-communicated data policy. In-
stitutions should provide clear documentation on what student data are collected, how
they are stored, and for what purposes they may be used. Anonymisation strategies,
such as removing personal identifiers and aggregating usage data, also help minimise
the risk of exposing sensitive information. As many regions enforce strict privacy regu-
lations (e.g., GDPR in the European Union), adhering to these legal frameworks helps
ensure that AI usage in education remains both lawful and respectful of individual rights.
7.1.2
Security Measures
Beyond privacy concerns, data security is crucial for maintaining trust in AI-assisted learn-
ing. Without strong technical safeguards, educational data can become vulnerable to
breaches and cyberattacks. Ensuring that LLM platforms incorporate robust encryption
and access controls is essential [25]. Institutions should assess potential vulnerabilities,
especially when partnering with third-party vendors. Moreover, periodic security audits
and compliance checks encourage accountability, reducing the likelihood of unintentional
data leaks or misuse of student information.
7.2
Bias and Fairness
7.2.1
Sources of Bias in LLMs
LLMs, including GPT-based models, learn linguistic and conceptual patterns from large
and sometimes uncurated datasets. These corpora frequently contain historical and so-
cietal biases related to race, gender, or socioeconomic status which can be reproduced
in the model’s responses or reasoning [21]. If unaddressed, the AI could unintentionally
perpetuate stereotypes, marginalise certain groups of students, or overlook diverse per-
spectives. Although these biases may be subtle, their potential impact on learning may
be significant, especially in courses aimed at developing critical and inclusive thinking.

7.2.2
Mitigating Bias in AI-Assisted Learning
Addressing algorithmic bias involves both technical and pedagogical strategies. On the
technical side, developers can employ bias detection and mitigation methods, such as
refining training data, adjusting model parameters, or post-processing outputs, to reduce
biased language and assumptions. Pedagogically, instructors can encourage students to
approach AI-generated content with a critical lens, prompting them to question potential
blind spots or to compare the AI’s viewpoints with alternative sources [27].
Educators can also create structured activities that examine how AI may privilege or ex-
clude certain voices. By teaching AI literacy, which includes the skill of detecting and
responding to model bias, institutions can prepare students to use AI tools responsibly
while upholding academic values of inclusivity and fairness.
7.3
Academic Integrity
7.3.1
Risk of Overreliance
One of the most cited concerns about AI in education is its potential to undermine aca-
demic integrity. When students become overly dependent on AI-generated answers, they
may bypass essential learning processes, such as critical thinking, problem-solving, and
knowledge synthesis. This reliance can result in the submission of solutions that they
neither fully understand nor have devised themselves, raising concerns about genuine
comprehension and intellectual development [16].
Yet it is important to recognise that students approach AI with varying intentions. While
some leverage the technology responsibly, using it to refine their ideas, explore alternative
reasoning, or overcome conceptual barriers, others may view AI-generated content pri-
marily as a shortcut for completing assignments with minimal effort. In the latter case, the
tool facilitates cheating, as students rely on AI outputs without engaging in the underlying
intellectual work.
7.3.2
Promoting Ethical Use of AI
Establishing and communicating clear guidelines on AI usage can mitigate these risks
[23]. Such guidelines might include:
• Instructor-Led Demonstrations: Illustrating responsible engagement with AI tools
during lectures or tutorials, showing how to refine prompts, evaluate outputs, and
integrate AI insights ethically.
• Integrity Checks: Encouraging or requiring students to submit outlines, intermedi-
ate drafts, or reflective commentaries that demonstrate personal understanding and
independent reasoning, especially for longer written assignments such as scientific
reports.
A balanced approach, recognising AI’s legitimate support role while limiting uncritical
adoption of AI outputs, upholds the rigor and authenticity of academic work. By emphasis-
ing that AI should complement rather than replace a student’s own efforts, instructors help
cultivate an environment where the technology supports genuine learning while reducing
incentives for misconduct.

7.4
Student Autonomy, Well-Being, and Responsibility
7.4.1
Maintaining Autonomy and Critical Thinking
As discussed in Chapter 6, students often oscillate between making use of the AI’s bene-
fits for deeper learning and risking overreliance when facing challenging tasks. Excessive
dependence on AI for solutions can erode the development of problem-solving skills, re-
ducing students to passive consumers of generated text [22]. To preserve autonomy,
educators must convey that AI is a supplementary resource rather than a substitute for
analytical effort. Structured activities that demand iterative reasoning, peer-review dis-
cussions, or manual verification of AI outputs can ensure students remain active agents
in their learning processes.
7.4.2
Psychological and Social Impacts
Beyond academic integrity concerns, the shift toward AI-assisted learning can affect stu-
dents’ mental health and sense of identity as students. Some report diminished confi-
dence when the AI’s polished solutions appear more sophisticated than their own, poten-
tially leading to self-doubt [26]. Others describe the AI as a motivational ”study partner”,
reducing stress by offering immediate clarifications.
Recognising these varied emotional responses is crucial for creating supportive learning
environments. Institutions could integrate discussions on balancing AI use with sense
of competence, perhaps by encouraging reflective journals that document the student’s
thought process before and after consulting AI tools. By addressing these social and
psychological impacts, educators can better foster resilience, motivation, and sustained
curiosity in their students.
7.5
Transparency and Accountability
7.5.1
Explainability of AI Outputs
While LLMs excel at producing natural language content, they often operate as black
boxes, making it difficult for students or instructors to trace the model’s exact reasoning
steps [2]. Encouraging or requiring models that provide transparency features, such as
highlighting relevant training data, summarising logical connections, or describing step-
by-step reasoning, can help users evaluate the accuracy of the AI’s outputs. However,
true explainability remains an open challenge, and not all LLMs can reliably reveal how
they arrive at specific outputs.
7.5.2
Institutional Responsibilities
If educational institutions adopt AI tools, they must be prepared to address model inac-
curacies, biases, and privacy breaches. Administrators should develop policies for audit-
ing AI tools, and investigating reported errors. Such measures highlight a commitment
to accountability, emphasising that AI providers and institutions share responsibility for
maintaining ethical and pedagogical standards in AI-assisted learning [25].

As this study demonstrates, the introduction of LLM-based AI assistants offers promising

Conclusion
This study investigated how an LLM-based AI assistant could be integrated into an ad-
vanced Master’s-level course like Logical Theories for Uncertainty and Learning. Using
qualitative and quantitative methods, the study explored how students interact with and
perceive AI-generated solutions, examining shifts in their confidence, understanding, and
engagement. The findings offer a picture of both the potential and limitations of deploying
AI in complex, Master’s-level coursework.
8.1
Summary of Results
This study revealed important insights into the role of the AI assistant in advanced course-
work, showcasing both its strengths and limitations. The AI assistant performed well in
concept-definition exercises, where its explanatory clarity gave it an edge. However, it
underperformed in mathematically rigorous tasks, leading students to often rate their own
confidence higher than their confidence in the AI for complex solutions. In contrast, stu-
dents trusted the AI more for definition-based problems, reflecting its strengths in simpler,
concept-oriented contexts.
The AI also positively influenced student confidence, with most students reporting either
increased or unchanged confidence after using the tool. This impact was especially sig-
nificant for students with initially lower self-confidence, suggesting that the AI can serve
as a confidence booster for less assured students. Furthermore, many students found the
AI engaging, which might have contributed to their motivation throughout the coursework.
Another key finding was the importance of critically validating the AI’s outputs. While
the AI’s assistance proved beneficial, errors in complex tasks highlighted the need for
scrutiny. Students who approached the AI with a critical mindset, actively comparing its
reasoning to their own, were more likely to enhance their understanding. These findings
highlight the potential of AI to support learning when paired with thoughtful engagement
and reflective practices.
8.2
Contributions to Research
This study contributes to the growing body of literature on AI integration in higher edu-
cation by providing insights into its contextual efficacy, theoretical alignment, and prac-
tical applications. The findings highlight the complex role of AI tools in a Master’s-level
course. While the AI excelled in tasks centered on concepts and definitions, it struggled
with advanced proofs and intricate solutions. This highlights the importance of aligning AI
capabilities with specific task demands to ensure its effective integration into coursework.
Through the application of frameworks such as Constructive Alignment, Deep vs. Sur-
face Learning, the Technology Acceptance Model, and Self-Determination Theory, the
study explores how well-designed tasks can maximise the educational value of AI. These
frameworks reveal that AI tools, when paired with intentional pedagogical strategies, can
promote active learning, intrinsic motivation, and deeper engagement with course mate-
rial.

By using AI to support conceptual understanding, educators can help students clarify

rather than passive reliance. These contributions provide a valuable foundation for further

8.3
Limitations of the Study
This study has several limitations. Although 85 students used the AI, only 38 completed
the post-assignment questionnaire, potentially introducing response bias by overrepre-
senting motivated or opinionated students. The course’s focus on logical and probabilistic
modeling is highly specialised, limiting the generalisability of the results to other disciplines
or educational levels. Finally, the lack of a true control group complicates efforts to isolate
the AI’s specific impact, as all students had access to the tool. These factors highlight the
need for further research to refine methods and broaden the scope of future studies.
8.4
Future Directions
Building on the results, this study suggests several promising directions for research and
pedagogical innovation. One direction involves refining AI tools to adopt a nudge-based
approach, where they provide incremental hints, withhold direct solutions, and pose reflec-
tive questions. This design could foster deeper engagement, critical thinking, and reduced
overreliance on the AI. Another area for exploration is longitudinal analysis, tracking how
students’ trust, confidence, and proficiency evolve with repeated AI usage across multiple
semesters of advanced coursework.
Another interesting direction for research is the involvement of teaching assistants (TAs)
in future AI integration. Studies could explore how TAs can collaborate with AI tools to
give richer feedback or moderate AI usage, potentially reducing overreliance and steering
students toward deeper learning.
Expanding the study to other disciplines, such as engineering design, humanities, or data
science, could reveal how domain-specific content influences the AI’s perceived useful-
ness and reliability. Additionally, investigating collaborative AI use, where small groups
of students share prompts, discuss AI outputs, and critique responses, could provide in-
sights into whether such interactions enhance learning outcomes and satisfaction.
Additionally, participant characteristics such as gender, nationality, or cultural background
were not examined in this study. Future work could explore how these factors shape stu-
dents’ attitudes toward AI, their propensity to trust AI-generated solutions, and their over-
all learning experiences. Studying these dimensions may uncover important variations in
how AI tools are adopted or resisted, offering further guidance for educators seeking to
create inclusive and effective learning environments.
Finally, future research might investigate the feasibility of using AI tools for automated
grading of assignments. While the accuracy and fairness of AI-based evaluation would
require careful scrutiny and alignment with institutional standards, such an approach could
streamline assessment processes and free educators to focus on providing targeted, high-
