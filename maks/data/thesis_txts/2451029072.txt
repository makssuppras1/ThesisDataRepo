Summary
In this thesis, a study was conducted to determine the potential ancillary services
that can be provided by a fleet of drives with passive rectifiers. To perform this, an
RMS model of the AC drive was created based on mathematical equations that de-
scribe the drive components and functions. A detailed dynamic model was also built
to validate the results of the RMS model. The RMS model needed minimal compu-
tational time for the simulations without compromising the accuracy of the results
in comparison with the detailed dynamic model. Having the RMS model as a base,
a drive fleet was modelled. The drives of this fleet were controlled with a developed
drive fleet controller. Using these models in a realistic low voltage distribution grid
of the standard IEEE 13 node feeder, different grid disturbances like undervoltage,
overvoltage, underfrequency and overfrequency were simulated. By utilizing demand
response of the drive fleets it was observed that these disturbances can be mitigated
up to a point. In the end, there is a discussion of the reasons why it is beneficial for
the owner of a fleet of drives to participate in demand response.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Frequency converters (FC) are electronic devices that convert alternating current
(AC) from one frequency level to AC of another frequency. One of their various uses
is the automated control of the speed or torque of three-phase motors. Nowadays
FC’s are an increasingly important component for optimising motor operation, and
the system attached to the motor. The speed or frequency adjustment has plenty of
advantages on the process. One of their main advantages is the energy efficiency opti-
mization of the motors. By being able to adjust the speed of the motors automatically
depending on the varying needs of the loads, a noticeable increase in energy savings
compared to the motors that run on the same speed continuously can be achieved.
Up to 30% of savings can be achieved by variable speed drives. The speed and the
torque of the drive are matched with the process requirements. As the demands of
the production in factories increase, the FC’s also help in establishing a higher degree
of automation that leads to improved process control. The use of FC’s for speed or
frequency control of the motor has reduced the mechanical stress of the loads and the
machines, as well as reduced the noise levels that were caused by previous hydraulic
and mechanical technologies like friction drives and variable speed gears [4]. Another
important advantage of the use of VSDs is that it addresses power factor issues com-
pared to having a motor connected directly on the main grid. This happens because
the FC only draws active power from the grid which means near unity displacement
power factor as its internal capacitor is used to supply the motor with reactive power.
However, the effect of harmonics that are created by the diode rectifier have to be
mitigated by appropriate measures or by using active front end rectifiers [35]. An an-
other significant advantage of VSD is that it reduces the risk of motor damage during
start up and stop hence it increases the motor longetivity. This happens because of
the motor soft start capability that reduces the motor starting current utilizing the
speed ramp rate of the drive. Although, the FC’s were expensive in the beginning,
the massive production of them in the later years made them an standard component
in all automated process plants today [4]. A FC is a device connected to the electric
grid and has an effect on it. The FC is a consumer of the power system that needs to
draw power from it in order to operate. By controlling its active power consumption
and the operational speed of the AC drive the motor’s safety and the power system
stability can be potentially improved during disturbances. Their grid support actions

however always have an effect on the process of the drive. Some drives can perform
support actions but there can be others that have to operate according to their pro-

1.2
Problem Statement
The power system is frequently subject to disturbances and these can cause minor
or major problems to the power system participants like under/overvoltage that can
cause problems to the connected equipment or a complete system collapse if they are
left unrestrained. Ancillary services can be provided by connected generators, control-
lable loads and network devices. These services are required by the Grid Codes and
needed by DSO and TSO to keep the frequency and the voltage of the power system
within operational limits or to recover the system back to the operational state after
a disturbance[19]. Furthermore, because of the increased integration of distributed
generation like renewable energy sources, the grid operators face challenges due to
their varying production during the day for solar PV and wind farms. To address this
issue, Demand Response needs to be adopted so the balance of supply and demand
can be maintained by changing the loads of the consumers [7]. A fleet of a drives in a
commercial or a industrial building can be considered as a variable load hence it can
participate in demand response and help the power system to mitigate any occurring
disturbances.
1.3
Research Questions
In this project, the following research questions are attempted to be answered:
• What are the realistic ancillary services that can be provided by the control of
AC drives?
• How can a fleet of drives be modelled in a less computational demanding but
accurate way?
• To what extent can the grid disturbances, that cause voltage and frequency
variations, be mitigated by controlling a drive fleet in a realistic distribution
grid?

1.4 Aim and Scope

1.4
Aim and Scope
The aim of this thesis was to explore the grid support actions of an AC drive done
by the FC. To achieve that goal, the first objective was to develop an RMS model of
the AC drive. Its results were tested and compared with a detailed dynamic model
of an AC drive to validate that the RMS model can be used as an substitute for the
further analysis of grid support actions. Based on that model, the next objective was
to model and control a fleet of drives that can participate in demand response. After
that, the next objective was to simulate and evaluate the grid support actions of the
drive fleet for different grid disturbances in a realistic grid. Their grid supporting
actions have to comply with the established grid codes and with realistic demands of
the owner of the drives fleet . This survey was limited to drives that have frequency
converters with passive rectifier (diodes). The reason for that is that these types
of rectifiers are the most common topology in industrial and commercial es as they
are the least expensive option. Only induction motors were investigated as they are
the most common type of motor. Finally, this project was limited to low voltage
distribution grid, as most drives that are supplied by low AC mains voltage.
For the purposes of this thesis, Matlab and Simulink which is a graphical exten-
sion of Matlab, developed by MathWorks were used for modelling the RMS drive
model and subsequently a fleet of them. MATLAB combines a desktop environment
tuned for iterative analysis and design processes with a programming language that
expresses matrix and array mathematics directly.
Simulink is widely used in au-
tomation control because it is a graphical programming environment for model-based
design, simulating and analyzing multidomain dynamical systems. Afterwards, the
results for the drives that were obtained from the Matlab code for the fleet of drives,
were used for the simulations in DigSILENT PowerFactory. The latter is a power
system analysis software for analyzing generation,transmission, distribution and in-
dustrial systems.

CHAPTER 2
State of the art
In this chapter the state of the art regarding ancillary services, the roles of the power
system operators in providing them and more detailed description about demand re-
sponse of VSDs are presented.
2.1
State of the art for ancillary services and Demand
Response
The ancillary services nowadays include voltage control, frequency control and black
start for system restoration. The frequency control includes frequency containment
reserve ( <5, 10 or 30 seconds), frequency restoration reserve (<15 minutes), replace-
ment reserve (15 minuted to hours), fast frequency response or synthetic inertia (<2
seconds) and ramping margin (1,3,8 hours ahead). The voltage control includes in
normal operation the control of power factor and reactive power for voltage restora-
tion and fast reactive current injection during voltage dips and post fault voltage
control. For system restoration the black start and islanding services are included.
The costs of the ancillary services include the investment cost for the capability, the
cost for readiness which consists of the cost for capacity reserved and opportunity cost
for loosing energy that cannot be sold, and the utilization cost which is the actual
provision of the services [19]. After the appearance of smart grids, extra ancillary
services in distribution network have emerged that include the advanced metering,
demand response, virtual power plants and current congestion mitigation [17]. To de-
ploy ancillary services, there should be a clear distinction of roles and responsibilities
between the grid operators, the utilities and the other grid participants.
The transmission system operator (TSO) is responsible for system security and
coordination of ancillary services provision on transmission grid. The distribution
system operator (DSO) is responsible for the continuity of ancillary services in distri-
bution grid. The TSO is responsible for the balancing, frequency control and system
recovery while the DSO is responsible mostly on congestion and voltage control. In
order to achieve efficient coordination between them, the observability and the share
of information between operators and participants need to be improved. Furthermore,
the active power management of distribution energy resources (DER) and demand
response (DR) are also vital. This can solve the problems of congestion at both the

transmission and distribution level, but also to maintain the frequency within the
operational values to maintain the system stability. The active power regulation of
the TSO and DSO impacts all network levels. The frequency control is typically done
by generators with increasing the active power injected to the grid with a certain ac-
tive power-frequency droop control during overfrequency and decreasing their active
power during underfrequency in the power system. The voltage control is performed
by generators with increasing the reactive power injected to the grid with a certain
reactive power-voltage droop control during overvoltage and decreasing the reactive
power during undervoltage in the power system. It can also be done with power factor
control [29].
The AC drives belong to the consumers of the power system as they are control-
lable loads. Because of that they can take part in the ancillary services provision
in the form of demand side management (DSM) and demand response (DR). The
definition of demand response according to U.S Department of Energy is ”changes
in electric usage by end-use customers from their normal consumption patterns in
response to changes in the price of electricity over time, or to incentive payments de-
signed to induce lower electricity use at times of high wholesale market prices or when
system reliability is jeopardized”. The DSM includes all activities that can change
the consumer’s demand profile to correspond to the supply profile. DSM leads to eco-
nomic and technical benefits for utilities and consumers by decreasing their electricity
bill, peak load of the power system shaving, electricity price reduction, decrease of
the power system investment cost and maintain the stability, reliability and power
losses.
Its drawback is its deployment cost of new electrical meters, controls and
communication systems. Incentive-based programs pay customers that reduce their
consumption during times that the system is under stress in fixed or time-varying
price. Incentive-based programs can be divided into classical methods and market-
based methods. Customers receive payments in the form of bill credits or discount
rates in classical methods and in market based methods they are getting paid depend-
ing on their contribution.
For the classical method of direct load control (DLC) the utility can remotely
change each customer’s consumption. To achieve that, a real time communication
system is needed that can reduce the power consumption in critical times. The utility
can only control the scheduling of not critical loads to reduce the peak demand in
expense of the human comfort. Another classical method is the interruptible load
method, in which the customers get an incentive payment to decrease their load up
to a certain value buy they get a penalty if they do not comply with it. In market
based programs a customer gets paid by biding on load reduction in the corresponding
electricity ancillary services market. If the customer enters the market, he/she is
required to do the load curtailment that is specified in the bid or else he/she gets
penalty. In emergency binding program it is allowed for customers to reduce more
than their bidding load and they get additional payment for that proportional to their
load reduction during the emergency. In the capacity market program, the customers
take part in day ahead market and reduce their power when an disturbance occurs. In
the ancillary service program the customers are allowed to take part in spot markets

2.2
State of the art use of drives for ancillary services
The control of the drives to participate in DR can be achieved with centralized dis-
patch or with a decentralized approach for each customer individually. In paper [26]
a two layer communication based control for the management of individual consumers
by the aggregators is presented. In the lower layer an energy management system
is installed in each consumer’s building to optimize the operation of the controllable
devices. This system sends information to the aggregator several minutes before so
the aggregator can allocate power setpoints related to the size of the flexible demand
for each consumer. The upper layer manages the point of common coupling and not
the electrical devices seperately. The aggregator’s central manager has direct con-
trol on consumers and can control the aggregated power consumption by reallocating
consumer flexibilty from the the consumer with no acceptable delivery output to an-
other with acceptable. The load curtailment is allocated proportionally to customer’s
volume availability.
In paper [9] it is noted that the flexible loads e.g. the thermostatically controlled

3.1.1
Limitations and Assumptions
One limitation of the RMS model is that it can not describe transients and dynamic
effects that occur in the the start-up, acceleration or deceleration of the motor. Fur-
thermore, the dc link capacitor is ignored which means that the current that comes
out of the rectifier is equal to the current that goes in the inverter. This assumption
has practically no effect on the results. Another limitation of the model is that it
refers only in induction motors as they are the most commonly used motors. Lastly,
the harmonic distortion of the input current and voltage is not considered.

3.1.3
Intermediate DC circuit
The role of intermediate dc circuit is to decouple the rectifier and the inverter. It also
reduces the interference of the main grid and behaves as an energy buffer that the
motor can draw from or return energy to the grid by the inverter.
An intermediate DC circuit consists of a filter that includes a capacitor and an
inductor. The capacitor is used for smoothing the pulsating DC voltage. It can also
store energy that can be used by the motor in voltage drops of the mains. This stored
energy can be gained by the generative operation of the motor. The inductor (choker)
can smooth the current ripple. It protects the system against mains transients.
Figure 3.6: Intermediate DC circuit [4].
There can be other types of intermediate circuits based on the type of rectifier
and inverter. In the case of a AFE rectifier the pure DC voltage can have variable
amplitude but in the passive rectifier the DC voltage has an constant amplitude and
is related to the mains DC voltage [4].
The intermediate dc circuit capacitor is neglected in the created RMS model which
means that the current that goes out of the rectifier is equal to the the current that
goes in the inverter.

3.1.6
U/f control
In VSI drives the speed can be controlled without or with a speed feedback loop

is controlled by the input speed reference signal wref.
The reference vector that

the motor constant, the ratio of U/f must remain constant This ratio is determined
by the nominal values of voltage and frequency which are on the nameplate data of
the motor. These values are the maximum limits of the U/f control. By keeping the
U/f ratio constant the output torque is the optimal [4].

A closed loop U/f controls an encoder feedback which can be added to control the
actual motor’s speed. An error signal is generated based on the difference between
actual speed and the external speed command, and the controller produces a new
frequency to compensate for the error. While it improves speed regulation, closed-
loop U/f control is not common due to the added cost and complexity of the encoder
and feedback hardware.
U/f control is the most common control because it is a simple and low cost
method to control the VSD. It is suitable for both constant torque and variable
torque applications and can provide up to 150 % of the rated torque at zero speed
for startup and peak loads. One important benefit of U/f control is that it allows
the control of multiple motors by a single VFD. [11]

3.3
Control methods of a drive fleet
In this section the different control methods of a drive fleet will be presented. Based
on the created RMS model, a drive fleet can be modelled. When there is a disturbance
at the power system and the supply can not meet the demand, a fleet of drives can
participate in Demand Response as a variable load and support the power system.
==PAGE:46==
32
3 Methodology
After prior communication of TSO and DSO, the DSM aggregator is responsible for
sending the signal to the customer that owns the drives to change his/her consumption
to a new power setpoint so the balance of power can be restored in the system. This
can happen ahead of time or real time. After receiving the information the customer
can control the power output of the drives using a drive fleet controller which can be
modelled in two different ways.
3.3.1
Drive Fleet controller of same load type
In this category, all drives have the same type of load. This means that they have the
same type of mechanical power to reference speed characteristic but they can differ in
power ratings. There can be loads that they have power proportional to the cube of
speed or directly proportional to speed or they can have constant power. From their
new power set point which is commanded by the DSM aggregator and the owner, a
new reference speed is extracted so the disturbance can be mitigated. Their control
is exactly the same thus their new reference speed is the same for every drive. This
means that all motors can be controlled by one single drive.
Figure 3.26: Drive Fleet controller of same load type.
3.3.2
Drive Fleet controller of different load types
In this category the drive fleet has different types of loads and ratings. This means
that the previous type of control can not be used in this case. Depending of the new
commanded power setpoint, each drive type should have its own new reference speed.
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Control methods of a drive fleet
33
For the centrifugal loads (fans,pumps) their new reference speed is calculated as the
cube root of their new mechanical speed. For constant torque loads their reference
speed is directly proportional to their new mechanical speed and for the constant
power loads their reference speed is unaltered. However, all the drives of the same
type of load will have the same reference speed.
Figure 3.27: Drive Fleet controller of different load types.
Below is the flow chart of the algorithm of Drive fleet controller. If the allowed
power reduction/increase threshold that is set by the owner of the drive fleet is higher
that the power reduction/increase that is required by the aggregator then the load
change can take place according to the command of the utilities. On the contrary,
the load change will be limited to the maximum allowed power reduction/increase
setpoint by the owner. By using the new power setpoint, the new reference rotational
speed can be calculated based on their mathematical relation with the new mechanical
power that depends on the type of the load (centrifugal, constant torque or constant
power). The new reference speed is the same for every drive with the same load
profile. However, there can be drives with centrifugal load profile for example that
must operate with constant power all the time. These drives belong to the constant
power load category. Then considering the efficiency of the motor and the converter
and the number of drives of each load profile, their new accumulated power input is
calculated. Finally, if there are drives with different type of load, the total required
power input of the drive fleet is calculated by adding the individual power of every
drive with different load type.

3.3.3
Centralized versus decentralized Drive Fleet controller
The proposed drive fleet controller is based on a centralized approach of controlling
the load in demand response and it has as prerequisite a solid communication infras-
tructure of the power system. Through reliable and fast communication between TSO,
DSO, Aggregator, generators and consumers the system stability can be maintained.
==PAGE:49==
3.3 Control methods of a drive fleet
35
This can be achieved utilizing different technologies of communication by transferring
data and control signals between the power grid participants that include Ethernet,
Internet and fiber optic cables. The monitoring of the power system can be performed
by a control center with the use of supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA)
systems that makes control decisions for the balancing authorities that are respon-
sible for maintenance of the stability of the region [32]. These technologies can be
expensive to implement to a power system and they are also prone to cyber-attacks.
Another possible drive fleet controller can use a decentralized approach that in-
cludes an voltage-current (VI) droop control for every drive fleet that can regulate
the voltage of the power system. The drive can realize the change in voltage by the
deviation of voltage in DC intermediate circuit. Based on that deviation they can
change their mechanical power output hence their required input current accordingly.
This can be considered a ”smarter” controller, however it has some weaknesses. One
major weakness is that the drives can not realize the deviations in the frequency if
no additional appropriate equipment is installed. These drives can participate in fre-
quency regulation if there are smart meters installed in the same point of connection,
that can measure the grid frequency. Based on that a frequency-active power droop
controller can be used that can adjust the output power of the drives according to
frequency deviation. Another problem is the human factor. These controllers should
be installed having a limit depending of the power that each owner is willing to cur-
tail. Some owners of drive fleets can decide to deactivate these controllers if they
do not want to participate in demand response at a certain period thus jeopardizing
the system stability. To compensate that, the other fleet owners that participate in
DR have to increase their load reduction to compensate for that change. To do that,
there should be available communication infrastructure between the participants so
the power system stability can be achieved. This means extra costs. For these rea-
sons, the centralized approach Drive fleet controller was preferred to be used because
of its simplicity and the lower overall cost.
3.3.4
Rating of drives
The model of the drive should also take into consideration the rating of the drive.
By operating under the following conditions, the protection of the motor and the
frequency converter can be ensured. If these conditions are violated the drive must
be tripped off. [43]
• The VSD shall be rated to operate from 3-phase power at 208 VAC to 240 VAC
+10/-10% / 380 VAC to 500 VAC +10/-10% / 525 to 690 VAC (600 VAC, UL
and CSA) +10/-10% . The overvoltage trip level shall be a minimum of 30%
over nominal, and the undervoltage trip level shall be a minimum 35% under
the nominal voltage.
• The VSD shall be rated to operate from input power from 48 Hz to 63 Hz.
==PAGE:50==
36
3 Methodology
• The output voltage and current ratings shall match the adjustable frequency
operating requirements of standard NEMA design A or NEMA design B motors.
• The normal duty overload current capacity shall be 110% of rated current for
one (1) minute out of five (5) minutes.
• The heavy duty overload current capacity shall be 150% of rated current for
one (1) minute out of five (5) minutes.
• The VSD efficiency shall be 97% or better of the full rated capability of the
VSD at full speed and load.

CHAPTER 4
Results
In this chapter the capability of a drive fleet to provide ancillary services to a power
system after a disturbance was investigated. Using the RMS drive model and the drive
fleet controller in a realistic distribution grid, different simulation tests were conducted
to analyze and evaluate different possibilities for grid support actions. The examined
disturbances were under/over voltages and under/over frequencies. This chapter also
includes a sensitivity analysis about the different capacity limit with which the owner
is willing to participate in DR. Lastly, the effect of drives in short circuit faults is
examined.
4.1
Distribution Grid
The selected distribution grid is a modified version of the standard IEEE 13 node
test feeder for the requirements of this project. The grid is a part of low voltage
distribution grid. The external grid (transmission grid) is replaced in simulations
with a synchronous generator for simulation purposes. The reason is that in Power-
factory the power loss of generation can be only simulated in generator component
and not in external grid component. The grid includes 13 loads (customers), 11 lines
and 23 busbars. The distribution grid is supplied by an external grid of 5 MVA at
115 kV and a 5 MVA step down transformer. The voltage at the secondary of this
transformer is 4.16 kV. There are also eight 0.5 MVA , a 0.63 MVA and one 1 MVA
4.16/0.4 kV distribution transformers at the positions of the loads. The loads of a
low voltage distribution grid are mainly households, commercial buildings, industrial
and agricultural consumers. Based on the data of Danish Energy Agency the annual
electricity consumption is almost equal for residential, commercial and industrial sec-
tor thus the electrical consumption is allocated equally [41]. In commercial buildings,
the main use of the drives is in the HVAC system. It is assumed that these drives
do not participate in demand response as the decrease or increase of their reference
speed can cause discomfort to the building occupants in winter and summer. Also,
in critical loads like hospitals the drives have to operate consistently and not to trip
in voltage sags as failures of them can cost lives. The drives are used predominately
in the industrial sector thus the industrial loads are considered to be the drive fleets
that can participate in the demand response. There are 4 drive fleets, one for each
industrial customer. Each drive fleet consists of three different categories of drives re-
garding their power ratings. Also, each industrial customer’s drive fleet has different

4.2
Simulation scenarios
In the investigated distribution grid, there are no other means to support the grid
by restoring the voltage or the frequency to the nominal level like automatic tap
changers, capacitor banks or automatic voltage/frequency regulators in conventional
or renewable generators. Because of that the only thing that can be done is demand
response. From the loads of the distribution grid, it is assumed that only the industrial
loads (drive fleets) can participate in demand response.
Demand response scheme for the drives were investigated for various disturbances
of the power system including undervoltage, overvoltage, underfrequency and overfre-
quency. All these disturbances occur from changes of the supply (external grid) of
the distribution grid. The aim is to change the load of the drives accordingly so the
voltage and frequency can restore back to their nominal values. Firstly, the change of
generation power is calculated as the difference between the generation power before
and after the disturbance in the external grid. This difference of power is allocated to
the industrial loads depending on their nominal power and their power capacity with
which they participate in DR. The simulations run for 120 seconds and the generator
event (decrease/increase of power) occurs at 1.2 seconds.
4.2.1
Undervoltage
The undervoltage is the most common disturbance and can cause overheating in
motors and damage to sensitive equipment like PLCs and VSDs [6]. One effect of
a reduction of power supply is the undervoltage in the power grid. In the case of
undervoltage the applied demand response scheme of the drive fleets is to decrease
their mechanical power to a level that the voltage of the grid will return to 1 p.u. As
the load decreases, the current decreases as well thus the voltage can be increased
back to its normal value.
Most grid codes including UK grid code CC.6.1.4 state that at 400 kV the voltage
limits are normally maintained within ±5% of nominal value unless abnormal condi-
tions prevail [42]. A voltage drop of 5% is examined first. In order to compensate
for this supply power loss, the total power consumption of each industrial customer
has to be reduced by 17,5%. In the table below, the new reference speed and the

Underfrequency
Underfrequency can occur when there is unbalance of active power in the power sys-
tem that can be caused e.g. by loss of generation power or increase of the loads. It
can cause higher magnetizing currents, overheating and reduction in speed for electric
machines [39]. The grid codes and the british code CC.6.1.2 specifies that the fre-
quency range of the power system shall be controlled within the limits of 49.5-50.5Hz,
unless exceptional circumstances prevail [42]. The drives can not realize the change in
the frequency without extra equipment like metering systems, as they are decoupled
from the mains AC.

flashing effects [12]. The types of short circuit faults are:

contact with the ground.
• Phase to phase fault: Two conductors come into contact with each other.
• Double Phase to Earth fault:
Two conductors come into contact with the
ground.

three conductors come into contact with the ground

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter includes a small discussion about the advantages of using drives in DR,
the conclusions and the future work of this project.
5.1
Advantages of using drives in demand response
Using drive fleet in demand response yields benefits both for the owners of the drives
and the utilities. Commercial and industrial drive fleet owners can save money and
contribute to the power system stability. In one month of 2012, the USA grid operator
PJM paid out 5$ m to DR providers. Schneider Electric stated that DR schemes can
earn back 5-20% of the companies annual electricity costs. DR earning values vary
based on speed, location, load profile and willingness to participate. [14]. In USA
the commercial customers can expect payments in range of 20,000-45,000 $ per MW
curtailed annually. The owner can make money both from participating to the DR
program and the level of energy reduction, which is based on his/her average peak
demand during high demand periods. The owner is paid extra money for every DR
event that he/she participates which are most likely to occur in summer. For a 25
kW reduction level 5 year DR program, the annual participation payment is 1,125$
and in total 1,575 $ for participation in 10 events. This means that the total earnings
over 5 years will be 7,200 $. For a 1 MW reduction level the same program yields
288,000$. The curtailments are typically at least 15% of the owner’s power capacity.
Participants are given typically notice 24 hours before the event[28]. In the area of
national grid Everett, Massachusetts an industrial consumer who sheds at least 50 kW
of its load can receive 3 $ per kW per month in summer and additional 0.5 $ kW per
kWh for energy reduced during an event [44]. Because of that, it is be more beneficial
for the larger customers to participate in DR, compared to the smaller customers as
the latter do not possess a considerable amount of capacity to turn off or on. Helping
the system maintain the nominal voltage is also beneficial for the equipment of the
drives. If there are voltage sags, then the drives have to to trip frequently which can
cause re-start delays and production losses. Although, proper design can mitigate
the damage to the equipment during voltage sags, it can cause disturbance in the
continuous production process. It is lengthy and costly task to replace the damaged
equipment that can occur because of the continuous trip offs. A mitigation method is
the ride through of the VSD with reduced speed that means lower power output and
then acceleration of the VSD back to nominal speed, after the voltage sag is cleared.
==PAGE:72==
58
5 Discussion
This avoids turning off the drive and a complicated, costly restart. This issue can
be addressed by having larger capacitors, boost converters, Active front ends or from
the kinetic energy of the motor shaft. All these solutions mean additional costs to
the design of the drive. However, if the drives fleet participate in DR by reducing
their power output then the voltage drop can be mitigated faster and the owner will
get paid for that.
Utilities also benefit by DR because by being able to control the demand they
can avoid turning on or building new peak power plants that have to be used only a
few times in a year to meet the high demand. By achieving this, the electricity price
can be kept down which benefits every power market customer including the owner
of the drives [14]. DR also is more convenient for the utilities especially in power
systems where the energy storage is limited and it is easier to address high surges
or low generated power from the increasing integration of renewable generators in
the power systems. Demand response is preferred by the utilities as it is faster and
cleaner than ramping power stations up and down [1].
5.2
Conclusions
This thesis performed a study of the possible ancillary services that a drive fleet
with diode rectifier can provide. A simplified RMS model of VSD was created that
provided accurate results in a dramatically less computational time compared to in
built models of the used software. This model was the foundation of modelling a
drive fleet with minimal computational time. An efficient drive fleet controller was
developed that has a prerequisite an adequate communication system between the
power market participants. Using the above models, the drive fleets can participate
in DR programs in a realistic distributed network and can provide undervoltage,
overvoltage, underfrequency and overfrequency mitigation when there is a drop or
increase of the supply power of the external grid. It can be concluded that the drive
fleets perform better in mitigating undervoltage and underfrequency because there
is the availability of decreasing their power output in a high range, if the owner
provides this flexibility. However, because of the motor nature, it is not possible to
increase their power output above their rated value which means that the drives can
mitigate overvoltage and overfrequency at a lesser level and only when they do not
work in full speed and have enough capacity. Realistic conditions were considered as
the drive fleet owners often do not allow full flexibility in DR and the criticality of
specific drives that need to run at a constant speed was also considered. Different
load profiles including centrifugal and constant torque loads were also considered for
a wider use of the proposed the fleet controller. It was observed that the drive fleet
controller can not mitigate short circuit faults. Lastly, it can be concluded that there
are considerable benefits for the drive fleet owners to participate in DR not only for
them but for the preservation of the power system stability.

5.3
Future work
To have a complete overview of the ancillary services that can be provided by the
FCs, the active front end (AFE) FCs can also be investigated. The difference of this
rectifier with the passive rectifier is that the diodes are replaced by IGBTs. IGBTs
are devices that their switching is controlled electronically. The AFE reduces the
harmonic distortion of the input current waveform. Because of that they can provide
almost a unity power factor. A suitable control of AFE can ensure a constant DC
voltage in DC intermediate circuit even at unbalanced voltage supply. The switch-
ing functions of AFE can be determined by space vector PWM or sinusoidal PWM
method. Another advantage is the bidirectional power exchange between AC mains
and DC circuit. The AFE rectifier allows four quadrant operation which means that
the regenerative energy that is produced by the motor during the breaking can be
fed back to the grid instead of being dissipated in a resistor [10]. These drives can
send back to the grid the regenerative active and reactive regenerative power and can
potentially help the voltage and frequency regulation in addition of participating to
demand response. When the motor breaks it behaves like a generator because it de-
velops negative torque and slip when the speed of the shaft is greater of the reference
speed. This means that in this brief time period until the speed of the shaft (rotor)
can become greater than the speed of the stator, there will be a surge of active and
reactive power back to the grid. These drives can possibly help the grid in a superior
way. However these type of drives have higher cost and are not that commonly used.
Lastly the ancillary services of FCs can also be examined in a medium voltage grid
as there are also drives that can operate in medium voltage range.
