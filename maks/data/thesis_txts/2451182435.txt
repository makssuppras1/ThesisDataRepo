Abstract
Using unmanned aircraft systems (UASs) to monitor hydrometric properties allows for
high spatial resolution and great mobility. A six motor and rotary wings unmanned aerial
vehicle (UAV) platfrom with a payload consisting of an action camera, a radar ranging
sensor and a global positioning system (GPS) device was utilized to obtain recordings of the
water surface, as well as measurements on the orthometric water height. The image quality
was enhanced in two ways to improve the performance of particle image velocimetry (PIV)
methods.
A stabilization tool was applied on the obtained imagery to correct for movements and
vibrations of the platform while the camera was recording. The videos shot with a ﬁsheye
lens were corrected for visual distortion. To achieve this the camera intrinsic parameters and
coeﬃcients were determined with an experimental setup and camera calibration software.
Two PIV applications were used to calculate surface velocities from the imagery. PIVlab
and DischargeKeeper are Matlab and Python-based toolboxes, respectively. The perfor-
mance of the PIV-tracking algorithms was evaluated against OTT MF Pro water ﬂow meter
measurements at 0.2 times the local stream depth. When suﬃcient traceable features are
present PIVlab captures the peak velocities with an accuracy of 8%. DischargeKeeper over-
estimates the surface water velocity. Additionally, a fully UAS-based discharge estimation
methodology is proposed.

Introduction
1.1
Overview of hydrometric measurement techniques
Surface waters shape and inﬂuence the landscape and environment (Tucker et al., 1998).
Extreme hydrological events, such as ﬂoods and droughts can heavily impact societies and
ecosystems (Schultz et al., 2013). Therefore, it is of importance to obtain accurate measure-
ments of hydrologic and hydrometric properties. Water surface levels, ﬂow velocity and river
discharge measurements are important to water resources management and are required to
calibrate and validate hydraulic and hydrological models. So far, multiple monitoring meth-
ods have been applied to assess and quantify these surface water processes. Traditionally,
hydrometric properties are measured using invasive in-situ methods. Current meters, trac-
ing systems (Tazioli, 2011) and Acoustic Doppler current proﬁlers (ADCPs) (Yorke et al.,
2002) are used to measure ﬂow velocity and spillways or weirs to estimate discharge.
In small streams immersed current meters can be used to measure the velocity at multiple
points in a cross-section. An alternative are tracers, such as dye or salt that can be used
to measure the mean ﬂow velocity (Planchon et al., 2005). When the cross-sectional area
of the stream is known, the discharge can be derived from these measurements using the
area-velocity method.
In larger rivers the estimation of discharge often relies on the applicability and validity of
rating curves, which can be constructed from ﬂow velocity and water depth measurements.
These measurements can be taken by equipping a boat with a current meter or an ADCP for
ﬂow velocity measurements and a sonic sounder to obtain the geometry of the cross section,
this is known as the moving boat method (Smoot et al., 1969). However, this method is
hindered during peak discharges, which causes the constructed rating curves to be prone
to high uncertainty during such high discharges.
Furthermore, the relationship between
stage and discharge can be complex in tidal rivers, which limits the accuracy of discharges
obtained from water surface levels.
The use of these invasive techniques is also limited to easily accessible environments.
Other issues are that ground-based methods are labor intensive and suﬀer from insuﬃcient
spatial and temporal coverage. Therefore, the density of gauging and monitoring stations is
in decline (V¨or¨osmarty et al., 2001).
To mitigate these issues, several remote sensing methods have been developed. With
satellite altimetry, the water surface levels of large water bodies can be determined (Birkett
et al., 2002). However, reliable surface ﬂow velocity estimates have not been obtained via
satellite so far. Attempts have been made by applying interferometric processing on along-
track synthetic aperture radar data from the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM)
(Romeiser et al., 2009). This method showed low sensitivity to small velocity variations and
high sensitivity to topographic features, causing a low signal-to-noise ratio. Furthermore,
the use of satellite based methods is limited by their low spatial resolution.
Another common method is to deploy an ADCP horizontally onto a structure to retrieve
surface velocity proﬁles (Marmorino et al., 1999), this method is known as Horizontal ADCP
(HADCP). If the bathymetry and local bed roughness of the stream are known the depth-
mean velocity can be derived from these surface velocity proﬁles using the law of the wall
and subsequently the discharge can be obtained (Hoitink et al., 2009). This method allows
for continuous monitoring, but is spatially stationary.
1.2
Hydrometric measurement techniques using Unmanned
Aircraft Systems
Unmanned Aircraft Systems (UASs) can be used to take non-invasive measurements from
ﬂuvial systems. The operational cost can be reduced when using a UAS to measure hydro-
metric properties. Moreover, UASs can reach environments that are inaccessible for most
conventional techniques and the mobility granted by UASs enables a high spatial coverage.
1
==PAGE:7==
Another advantage is that the use of UASs is not limited during ﬂood events. A UAS can
include a variety of measurement devices and therefore a wide range of hydrological and
physical properties can be monitored, such as water surface elevation (Bandini et al., 2017),
bathymetry (Bandini et al., 2018) and surface ﬂow velocity (Detert et al., 2015; Tauro et al.,
2016b).
In the water surface elevation monitoring workﬂow, the onboard Global Navigation Satel-
lite System (GNSS) receiver measures the vertical position of the drone. Subtracting the
distance from the drone to the water surface level measured by an onboard radar the ortho-
metric height of the water surface level can be calculated (Bandini et al., 2017). This way
water surface elevation can be retrieved with an overall accuracy of 3 cm.
There are several methods to measure the bathymetry using a UAS. With an onboard
camera the bathymetry can be obtained by applying signature-depth correlation on retrieved
optical imagery (Flener et al., 2013).
Another method is based on the Structure-from-
Motion (SfM) technique, which creates a digital elevation model from through-water pictures
(Bagheri et al., 2015; Dietrich, 2017). However, the use of these two methods is restricted to
clear and shallow streams, because of the limited penetration of natural light in turbid water
bodies. Single-beam sonars allow for measurements in deep and turbid water bodies. A UAS
platform has been used to drag a ﬂoating tethered single-beam sonar over the water surface
to measure the bathymetry (Bandini et al., 2018). However, this method is quasi-invasive
and has only little advantages over methods that use a vessel instead of a UAS platform.
Non-invasive airborne methods using a Ground-Penetrating Radar (GPR) have been used
to obtain bathymetry data (Melcher et al., 2002; Costa et al., 2006). A GPR can potentially
be incorporated in a UAS payload, however measuring bathymetry with this method has
not been tested.
1.3
Particle Image Velocimetry
Recently, image based methods have become available to determine ﬂow velocities in streams.
Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is such an image based method. The displacement of fea-
tures on recorded videos is determined by applying cross correlation on consecutive images.
From this a velocity ﬁeld at the water surface can be obtained.
PIV methods have been tested and applied with several diﬀerent methodologies and
setups. Multiple studies have been conducted in small scale laboratory setups (Fujita et al.,
1998; Weitbrecht et al., 2002; Kantoush et al., 2009; Kantoush et al., 2011). The transition
of PIV from small to large scale systems brings additional challenges. For instance, it is
not possible to illuminate the site with laser systems to enhance the PIV performance. The
method applied in the ﬁeld is Large Scale Particle Image Velocimetry (LSPIV) and often
relies on artiﬁcial seeding when natural tracers are lacking (Fujita et al., 2003). In the ﬁeld
a static approach has been applied by ﬁxing a camera onto a structure, such as a pole or
bridge (Muste et al., 2008; Hauet et al., 2008). However, from the captured imagery the
ﬂow velocities can only be obtained at one location and is restricted by the ﬁeld of view
(FOV) of the ﬁxed camera.
Applying PIV on imagery retrieved with a UAS allows monitoring of surface water dy-
namics with great mobility. The potential of UASs to collect surface velocity measurements
has been tested in various studies (Detert et al., 2015; Tauro et al., 2015; Tauro et al., 2016a;
Tauro et al., 2016b). Having a camera with its axis oriented perpendicular to the stream the
UAS platform hovers above the river while the camera records a video of the water surface.
The retrieved imagery has to be corrected for the vibrations and movement of the platform
(Tauro et al., 2015; Fujita et al., 2011). This can be done with stabilization algorithms.
In multiple studies GPS referenced Ground Control Points (GCPs) were placed within the
FOV of the camera (Kim et al., 2008; Tauro et al., 2015; Detert et al., 2015). These GCPs
are used for ortho-rectiﬁcation and to convert image units (pixels) to metric units (meters).
After these steps a surface velocity ﬁeld can be obtained from the imagery using PIV. Dis-

charge has been estimated using PIV combined with bathymetry data (Hauet et al., 2008).
This was done using a numerical simulation and videos were obtained with a ﬁxed camera.
So far, UAS derived imagery has not been used to create discharge estimation. This thesis
describes a method to obtain discharge estimates by applying the law of the wall on surface
velocity proﬁles, which are obtained by performing PIV on imagery retrieved with a UAS.
1.4
Research Objectives and Questions
This thesis aims to assess the capabilities of UASs to measure surface water velocities and
eventually stream discharge. At the moment, discharge estimations are mainly obtained by
applying rating curves, current meters or in-situ structures. With the use of UASs and PIV
there is the potential to monitor hydrometric properties with high spatial resolution and a
wide coverage, including diﬃcult to access environments. Two PIV image processing appli-
cations are explored and evaluated; PIVlab and DischargeKeeper. Afterwards, the discharge
is estimated by using the PIV results. To validate the results, the surface water velocity
proﬁles and discharge estimations are compared with measurements from electromagnetic
current meters. An additional objective is to propose a discharge monitoring workﬂow that
functions without GCPs by including a radar ranging sensor into the UAS payload.
The main research questions are:
• How can stream surface velocity be derived from time-lapse digital images obtained
with Unmanned Airborne Systems?
• Can accurate discharge estimates be obtained based on PIV-derived surface velocity
proﬁles?

2.1
UAV and payload
The Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) platform used in this research is the DJI Matrice 600
Pro (Figure 1). This is a platform with six motors and rotary wings, a maximum take-oﬀ
weight of 15.5 kg, a maximum payload weight of about 2 kg and a wing span of 1 meter.
This rotary wing UAV has hovering capability and a vertical take-oﬀand landing.
Figure 1: The DJI Matrice 600 Pro in action.
Two diﬀerent action cameras were used to record the water surface. In the Vejle and
˚Amose campaigns the UAV was equipped with a Hawkeye FireFly 8SE and in the Usserød
campaign a GoPro HERO6 Black was used. With both the FireFly 8SE and the GoPro
HERO6 the videos were shot in a 4 K resolution and at 30 and 24 frames per second
(fps), respectively (Table 1). Previously, issues occurred when higher frame rates were used
and therefore frame rates of 30 and 24 were chosen.
This lowers the storage space and
computation time, as well.
The position of the platform was determined by using carrier-based Diﬀerential GNSS
(DGNSS). With DGNSS the relative position of the platform to a base-station is determined.
This method allows for cm-level accurate platform position observations. The onboard GNSS
consists of a GNSS receiver and a dual frequency (GPS and GLONASS) ﬂight antenna. The
used base-station is comprised of a NovAtel ﬂexpack6 receiver and a GPS-703-GGG pinwheel
triple frequency (GPS and GLONASS) antenna.
A radar ranging sensor was used to determine the distance between the UAS platform
and the water surface.
By subtracting this distance from the altitude derived from the
GNSS receiver, the orthometric water height has been obtained. The local water surface
slope S was derived from water level elevation observations at a stretch of ca. 100 m along
the stream.

2.2
Field sites and data acquisition
During several ﬁeld campaigns three small streams have been monitored; Vejle, Usserød
and ˚Amose. The Vejle stream is located in the Jutland peninsula and has a catchment
of 190 km2. It ﬂows mainly through agricultural areas until it reaches the town of Vejle
where it ﬂows into the Vejle fjord. Flights were performed at six diﬀerent sites along the
Vejle stream. Usserød and ˚Amose are both located at Sjælland. The ˚Amose catchment
covers 286 km2 of rural area. Four sites have been monitored at the ˚Amose stream. The
Usserød ﬂows from lake Sjælsø into the Øresund and has a catchment of 74.7 km2. Most
of the catchment is in urbanized areas.
At all sites ﬂights, video recordings and in-situ
current meter measurements were conducted. Local water surface elevation observations
were obtained at most sites, however are lacking at some. Therefore the water surface slope
has not been derived for those sites.
The ﬂow velocity was measured with an OTT MF Pro water ﬂow meter at a cross-
section of the stream. Measurements were taken at 0.2, 0.6 and 0.8 times the local depth
at multiple distances from the bank. From these observations the reference discharge values
were obtained using the velocity-area method. The surface water velocity proﬁles obtained
with the UAS were compared to the velocities measured at 0.2 depth.
Video recordings started before take-oﬀand ended after the landing of the UAS platform.
Hover mode was enabled when the platform was directly above the stream. Woodchips were
chosen as seeding particles because of the density relative to surface water. The seeding
particles were manually added upstream of the ﬁeld sites. Sections of the video with opti-
mal seeding conditions and relatively constant platform altitude were subtracted from the
full recordings. These subsets have a length of between 3 and 8 seconds, which roughly
corresponds to 80 and 260 consecutive frames.

2.3
Pre-processing
Below is a workﬂow chart (Figure 3) showing the complete methodology that has been
applied to obtain surface velocity proﬁles from the imagery. To get reliable PIV results
the image quality has been improved in two ways.
Videos have been corrected for the
vibrations and movement of the UAS platform and the imagery obtained with the GoPro
action camera has been corrected for lens distortion. After the tracking surface velocity
proﬁles are obtained from the resulting velocity vector ﬁelds. In Section 4.3.2 a method to
obtain discharge estimates with the sole use of a UAS is described and proposed.
Figure 3: Workﬂow chart of the image processing.
2.3.1
Video stabilization
A Matlab toolbox developed by Photrack AG was used to stabilize the imagery. This Matlab
toolbox is a GUI-based application that requires AVI, mp4 or MOV ﬁles as input and uses
a multi-point tracking method. Regions of interests (ROIs) were manually selected and the
application uses these ROIs as anchor points for the stabilization. Then for each frame the
features within the ROIs are allocated. This way the displacement was determined and
corrected for.
2.3.2
Lens distortion correction
The original recordings obtained with the GoPro action camera were shot with a ﬁsheye
lens which is an ultra wide-angle lens causing visual distortion. Light rays bend more on the
edges of the lens than in the center, when a ﬁsheye lens is used. This results in non-uniform
representation of real-world dimensions by pixels, which if uncorrected for would cause errors
when the PIV tracking is performed. A lens distortion correction is therefore applied on the
obtained imagery. Using the radial (k1, k2 and k3) and tangential distortion coeﬃcients (p1
and p2) of the lens as well as the camera intrinsic parameters (focal length, optical center and
skew coeﬃcient) undistorted frames have been constructed. These camera coeﬃcients and
parameters are not provided by the manufacturer and therefore a camera intrinsic parameter
estimation was performed. Imagery of a checkerboard pattern was obtained from multiple
angles and distances. Then the Matlab Camera Calibrator toolbox and the OpenCV package
were used on the imagery to estimate the camera coeﬃcients and parameters and the value
can be found in Table 2. Subsequently, a Matlab script has been used to perform the lens
distortion correction and an example of the results is shown in Figure 4.

2.4
Tracking
The two PIV applications that were used to calculate surface velocities are PIVlab versoin
2.02 (Thielicke et al., 2014) and DischargeKeeper version 3.5.0.
2.4.1
PIVlab
PIVlab is a freely available GUI-based toolbox for Matlab. It requires decomposed frames
as input material. There are several pre-processing options available within PIVlab that can
inﬂuence the resulting surface velocity ﬁeld. These options are enabling histogram equal-
ization, enabling highpass ﬁlter, instensity capping and applying a Wiener2 denoise ﬁlter.
The eﬀect of those parameters are described in existing literature (Thielicke et al., 2014).
Traceable features, seeding density and image quality have a relatively greater inﬂuence
than these pre-processing options when PIV is used to calculate surface water velocities
(Kantoush et al., 2011; Lewis et al., 2015).
The cross-correlation algorithm option used is the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT).
This method uses small sub images as interrogation areas. For each interrogation area the
most probable displacement between consecutive frames is calculated by cross-correlation.
This is done by performing pattern recognition between the frame pairs.
This pattern
recognition is limited to a certain pixel distance from the interrogation area and this distance
7
==PAGE:13==
is speciﬁed as the step. PIVlab allows for up to four passes on an image pair of consecutive
frames. With each pass smaller interrogation area sizes can be selected and the resolution of
the resulting vectors and thus the vector ﬁeld improves (Thielicke et al., 2014). The sizes of
the interrogation areas used in the four passes are 256, 128, 64 and 32 pixels. The steps for
each pass were half of the interrogation size. This way both the high and low velocity ﬂow
information can be captured. PIVlab calculates the displacement in pixels per frame using
this method. This is converted to velocity in meters per second by inserting a reference
distance and a time step per frame. The time per frame has been obtained from the frame
rate of the original recordings. For the recordings obtained with the GoPro action camera
(24 fps) the time per frame is equal to 41.167 ms.
PIVlab calculates a velocity ﬁeld for each frame couple. Outliers have been removed
by manually selecting the boundaries of valid vector magnitudes. A velocity ﬁeld with the
average vectors is calculated and used to create the surface velocity proﬁles (see Section 2.5).
2.4.2
DischargeKeeper
DischargeKeeper is a GUI-based Python toolbox developed by Photrack AG. It requires AVI,
mp4 or MOV ﬁles as input, as well as two text ﬁles describing the position of the GCPs
and the bathymetry at the cross section. FFmpeg was used to mount videos (.mp4) from
the stabilized and distortion corrected frames. DischargeKeeper is designed to apply PIV
on imagery and supplement the resulting velocity proﬁle with a Manning number estimate
and bathymetry data to obtain discharge estimates. Pre-processing options allow for the
calibration of the position and orientation of the cross section.
DischargeKeeper uses a special variant of the PIV cross-correlation technique, which
aims to reduce the inﬂuence of glare and shadows on the water surface and is less reliable
on traceable features, such as seeding particles (L¨uthi et al., 2014; L¨uthi et al., 2018). This
variant of PIV is called Surface Structure Image Velocimetry (SSIV). The cross-correlation
algorithm uses window size and window oﬀset, which are the equivalent of PIVlab’s interro-
gation areas and step, respectively. The interrogation area and step are described in section
2.4.1. A window size of 64 pixels was used with an oﬀset of 32 pixels.
During the velocity ﬁeld calculation a local and global ﬁlter were applied. This way, sub-
windows with a displacement that deviates from their neighboring sub-windows or the global
ﬁeld were rejected. Besides that, a signal-to-noise ratio of 0.3 was used to omit displacement
measurements derived from a sub-window with a relatively low signal.
With DischargeKeeper the displacement in pixel per frame and a velocity vector ﬁeld
(Figure 5a and 5b, respectively) are calculated. Besides that, the velocity magnitudes are
plotted against the distance along the river width (Figure 5c) and a velocity proﬁle is ﬁtted
onto the scatter plot. The ﬁtted velocity proﬁle can be adjusted by and is very sensitive
to available ﬁtting parameters, such as bulkiness, symmetry and skewness. The ﬁtted ve-
locity proﬁle was omitted and the velocity magnitude observation points have been used to
calculate velocity proﬁles with an alternative method described in Section 2.5.

2.5
Velocity proﬁle construction
From the velocity ﬁelds created with PIVlab and DischargeKeeper velocity proﬁles are con-
structed. For PIVlab the resulting velocity vector ﬁeld is given as u and v vector components.
The u and v components are oriented horizontally and vertically to the frame axes, respec-
tively. Since the cross section is (usually) not aligned with either of the two frame axes the
correct velocity magnitude has to be calculated. To obtain the velocity magnitude perpen-
dicular to the cross section a rotation and vector decomposition has been applied. These
computations have been performed within R (version 3.3.0).
For both the PIVlab and DischargeKeeper results the stream width was divided in n
intervals. And the velocity vectors were placed in their corresponding interval. This way,
the velocity vectors were grouped by distance along the river width. Then the mean velocity
and standard deviations were calculated for all groups. The velocity proﬁle was constructed
from these mean velocities. The PIVlab and DischargeKeeper velocity proﬁles are evaluated
against the current meter in-situ measurements at 0.2 depth and several error metrics have
been calculated.
The ﬂow velocity near the banks is usually relatively low and seeding often converges to
the central part of the stream, which lowers the traceable features at the edges. Lower veloc-
ities also imply a lower signal and in the shallower edges of the stream emerging vegetation
increases the noise. This lowers the overall signal-to-noise ratio, which limits PIV-tracking
in those areas and velocity vectors are lacking. Therefore, the velocity proﬁles created do not
cover the full width of the stream. A linear interpolation has been applied to estimate the
surface water velocity at those small regions. The velocity at the riverbanks was assumed
to be equal to zero.

True distance estimation (pixel to meter)
Previous studies have used GCPs to obtain the real-world size of a pixel (Kim et al., 2008;
Tauro et al., 2015; Detert et al., 2015). This is done by dividing the exact distance in real-
world dimensions between two GCPs by the amount of pixels between these two GCPs in
the image. Applying this method requires the banks of the stream to be accessible in order
to place the GCPs in the FOV of the camera. This part describes how the usage of GCPs
can be avoided by including a radar ranging sensor into the UAS.
This method uses the fact that the meters per pixel (mpp) equals the full width of the
FOV in meters (FOVw) divided by the total amount of pixels over the width of the frame
pxw. The videos were shot in 4 K (3840x2160) so the amount of pixels over the width of the
frame is equal to 3840.

pxw
FOVw is dependent on the object distance OD and several camera settings and characteris-
tics, most importantly the focal length. The object distance is equal to the distance between
the water surface and the UAS platform and is measured with the radar ranging sensor. The
camera settings and characteristics have been combined into one empirical variable a.
FOVw = OD a
Combining the two equations shows that mmp is a function of the object distance, a and
pxw.
mpp = OD a
pxw
With a simple experimental setup a has been determined for the GoPro action camera and a
value of 2.18 has been derived. This experiment is described in Appendix A. The mpp value

calculated, which translates directly to the velocity vectors. However, this error is relatively
small compared to the errors introduced by the tracking and radar ranging sensors can thus

4
Discussion
4.1
In-situ OTT MF Pro water ﬂow meter measurements
The in-situ velocity measurements are taken at 0.2 depth whilst the PIV methodology cal-
culates a velocity at the water surface. Since ﬂow velocities are not uniform over the depth
of the channel (Figure 9) there is a diﬀerence in actual velocities which causes an error in
the evaluation of the velocity proﬁles. A sub-optimal solution could be to estimate the ﬂow
velocity at the water surface, using a Manning number estimate and extrapolate the 0.2
depth measured velocity to the water surface. In future ﬁeld campaigns the current meter
measurements should be taken as close to the water surface as possible to limit the error in
the evaluation.
Figure 9: The ﬂow velocity usually decreases by depth,
due to bed friction. However, the ﬂow velocity at the
water surface could possibly be lower than the velocity
at 0.2 depth, due to wind drag.
4.2
Velocity proﬁle construction
To obtain the velocity proﬁles the velocity magnitudes in the velocity ﬁeld are divided in
groups depending on their distance along the river width. Then for each group the mean
is calculated and plotted against its corresponding distance (see Section 2.5). However, the
velocity magnitudes that the mean is calculated from show a left-skewed (negative skewness)
distribution (Figure 10). So perhaps the median or even the mode (value that occurs most
often) is more representative for the actual surface ﬂow velocity.
4.3
Full UAS-based discharge estimation methodology
In this part the opportunities and possibilities to obtain discharge estimates with a fully
UAS-based methodology is described. The goal is to monitor streams without GCPs, which
is currently the conventional method to obtain the reference distance used in the PIV-
tracking. And without invasive current meters or quasi-invasive tethered single-beam sonars
to obtain bathymetry measurements. In Section 2.6 a method is described that uses a radar
ranging sonar to obtain the reference distance. This way the mpp value can be calibrated
without GCPs being placed on the stream banks.
4.3.1
Bathymetry observations with GPR and LiDAR
Non-invasive airborne methods using a Ground-Penetrating Radar (GPR) have been used to
obtain bathymetry data at submerged parts of the stream. A helicopter-mounted (Melcher
et al., 2002) and a ﬁxed GPR (Costa et al., 2006) have been tested and used for bathymetry
measurements. There are lightweight GPRs on the marked that can potentially be incor-
porated in a UAS payload, however measuring bathymetry with this method has not been
tested so far. For the dry parts of the river channel a Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR)
system can be used (Mandlburger et al., 2015; Mandlburger et al., 2016).

5
Conclusion
The main objective of this research was to derive the stream surface velocity from time-lapse
digital images obtained with Unmanned Airborne Systems. Flights were conducted at three
diﬀerent small streams in Denmark to obtain recordings of ﬂowing surface water. After
stabilization and lens distortion correction of the imagery, PIV-tracking was performed with
PIVlab and DischargeKeeper. The resulting velocity proﬁles were evaluated against current
meter measurements.
The PIV-tracking performance diﬀered strongly between the monitored ﬁeld sites. Fac-
tors that improved the PIV performance are a high density and uniform distribution of
traceable features, uniform channel properties and static objects within the ﬁeld of view
of the camera to. Limiting factors are high wind velocities, overhanging tree branches and
emerging vegetation.
PIVlab’s tracking algorithm relies on added seeding particles. When seeding particles
are present PIVlab captures the highest ﬂow velocities with an accuracy of 8%. Areas where
seeding particles are absent cause large errors in the ﬂow velocity calculation.
DischargeKeeper’s tracking algorithm relies less on seeding particles as traceable features.
A systematic overestimation by DischargeKeeper can be observed at the Usserød stream,
where the videos were obtained with the GoPro HERO6 Black action camera in contrast to
the Hawkeye FireFly 8SE used at the Vejle and ˚Amose streams. At the Vejle and ˚Amose
stream the sites were less suitable for PIV-tracking and the performance was therefore
unsatisfactory.
When the circumstances were right, high quality imagery has been obtained, which
contains a lot of information. Further research has to be performed to improve the extraction
of the right data from this imagery.
