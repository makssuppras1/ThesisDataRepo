Abstract 
 
Noble metal colloidal nano-structures recently emerged as outstanding candidates for a vast 
amount of applications. Their ability to confine and enhance light at the nanoscale results in a 
remarkable improvement of photocatalytic processes that are widely studied nowadays. The 
features of these novel nanoparticles strongly rely on their size and subsequently on their 
aggregation into larger networks. Their aggregation, at the same time, depends on their colloidal 
stability. Nanoparticles stabilized via three different capping agents were studied, entailing both 
electrostatic and steric stability. Their response to both addition of electrolyte and irradiation at 
different wavelengths was characterized by UV-Visible spectrometry and corroborated by TEM 
images. The extinction spectra showed a large influence of the capping agents on the behavior of 
AuNP colloids and their eventual structures. For electrostatically stabilized nanoparticles, the 
addition of several electrolytes led to the conclusion of a large effect of the ionic strength in the 
final aggregated networks. For sterically stabilized nanoparticles, instead, irradiation with light 
seemed to have a larger effect than the ionic strength of the solution. However, irradiation at 
specific wavelengths might also give rise to either light-stabilized events or the formation of well-
defined larger structures. This dependence brings the potential of directing AuNP assembly and 
tailoring the properties of ultimate nanostructures by controlling the excitation and electrostatic 
environment.

Motivation 
 
While a majority of the world’s current energy supply is generated from fossil fuels, these 
traditional sources face a number of challenges such as rising prices, concerns over dependence 
on imports, and growing environmental involvement over the climate change risks associated with 
power generation. Among all the new emerged renewable sources, the incoming solar radiation 
depicts a truly clean energy source, thereby making the utilization of this electro-magnetic wave a 
potentially powerful source of energy to drive a vast amount of processes. 
Nowadays, it is widely recognized that it is necessary to develop advanced inorganic materials and 
new molecular devises –preferably from earth abundant elements- as efficient photocatalysts in 
order to accomplish the process of water splitting and carbon dioxide reduction with solar energy 
as a driving force. It has also been documented that assemblies of nano-sized particles are useful 
in a variety of optical applications owing to their resonant interaction with the electromagnetic 
radiation. Hence, nanotechnology plays a crucial role in enabling the design of structures, surfaces 
and interfaces with controllable properties at the nanoscale. Successful utilization of nanoscale 
processes often entails the comprehension of colloidal nano-structures and their stability. Among 
all the metal nanoparticles, Gold nanoparticles are presented as an outstanding candidate due to 
their unique optical and physical properties. These properties are conferred by the interaction of 
light with electrons on the nanoparticle surface, which are dependent upon their shape, size, surface 
structure and agglomeration state. Their size is strongly determined by their agglomeration state, 
which in turn depends, among other factors, in their surface structure. Therefore, understanding 
how stability depends on the coatings of those nanoparticles –and the forces between them- under 
certain conditions will represent one step beyond synthesis in the development of structures for 
greener technology.  
Many materials aspects of photocatalysts influence the photocatalytic performance, such as 
electronic, structural and morphological features. Even though it is well known that molecular 
catalyst have the advantage of design flexibility and structural tunability, larger structures have 
not been that widely studied to date. Studying such structures means comprehending the unique 
interactions between those nanostructures and light, which leads to the assembly into larger 
structures. Assemblies of nanoparticles for photocatalytic purposes therefore brings unique and 
exciting features that are not inherent in the individual building blocks.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Over the past 50 years, the development of colloidal nanoparticle syntheses has been extensively 
tackled due to their enormous technological applications and fundamental scientific interest1. 
There are very different types of colloids according to the physical phase of dispersed compounds 
and the environment of dispersion2, but the properties of inorganic colloids -and more precisely 
metal nanoparticles (NPs)- have particularly aroused interest over the last 2 decades3. Therefore, 
it is not rare that noble NPs have been extensively studied in the frame of nanotechnology because 
they possess properties –mainly optical- that are unique and markedly different from those of their 
corresponding bulk forms4,5. These properties can be preserved if the general tendency towards 
aggregation can be overcome, as when a suitable stabilization can be developed6. Taking 
advantage of their outstanding properties, noble metal nanostructures have nowadays found use in 
a vast amount of fields, ranging from medicine to electronics7,8. 
In parallel to the development of colloidal nanoparticle research, our fundamental understanding 
of catalytic processes has also advanced rapidly by developing solid catalysts, especially those 
involving highly dispersed metal nanoparticles, often supported on metal oxides9. Nonetheless, 
many industrial catalysts currently in use consist of small metal particles with large variations in 
size and shape10. While particle size and shape are an important consideration, many other factors 
such as geometry, composition, oxidation state, and chemical environment can play a role in 
determining NP reactivity. However, the exact relationship between these parameters and NP 
catalytic performance might be system dependent11. As a result of the versatility in nanoparticles 
reactivity, there have been numerous types of reactions that have been catalyzed using colloidal 
and supported metal nanocatalysts such as oxidations, cross-coupling reactions, electron transfer 
reactions, hydrogenations, fuel cell reactions, and many others12. 
Among all the range of features that noble metal nanoparticles exhibit, their optical properties have 
garnered immense scientific concern even though they depict one of the most recent exploited 
characteristics13. Metallic nanostructures with diameters in the order of few nano-meters are 
capable of squeezing light beyond the diffraction limit for a wide range of energies when photons 
interact with the sea of electrons in the metal14. The term plasmon refers to the quantization of 
coherent oscillations of conduction electrons in metals in response to the electric field of the 
incoming light15. Since electrons are forced to oscillate within the nanoparticle physical 
boundaries, they accumulate at the edge of the structure inducing large electric field intensities. 
The excitation of these localized surface plasmons (LSP), can be further enhanced if the frequency 
matches the LSP resonance (LSPR) frequency16. Therefore, this phenomena in which light can be 
manipulated by structures significantly smaller than its wavelength results in an empowerment of 
NPs with huge potential in a variety of applications17. These present-day applications are found in 
a vast range of fields, including photo-thermal cancer therapy, bio-imaging, drug delivery, optical 
microscopy and especially photovoltaics4,18,19. 
Besides the above-mentioned optical properties of noble metal nanoparticles, gold is well-known 
as one of the unique metals to form stable nanoparticles20. Apart from chemical stability, they also 
possess attractive properties, such as high thermal and electrical conductivity, photothermal 
effects, tunable size and shape dependent optical properties, and facile functionalization, which 
==PAGE:11==

make them suitable for a wide range of applications21. Even though gold nanoparticles (AuNPs 
henceforth) can be found in many different shapes and morphologies, nanometer-sized spherical 
gold nanoparticles present the smallest surface-to-volume ratio of NPs, which is the main factor 
accounting for their wide-spread use as catalysts22. This high surface energy, though, makes them 
hugely reactive resulting in aggregation and subsequently in modification of their optical 
properties unless their surface is protected or passivated. Hence, once prepared, it is important to 
stabilize and protect a given gold nanoparticle, certainly if the desired optical properties are to be 
preserved20.  
The challenges involved in AuNPs synthesis has grown hand in hand with the increasing 
requirements for well-defined shape and monodispersity. It is well-known that there are two 
methods for preparation of NPs: top-down and bottom-up methods. Physical methods often rely 
on a top-down approach by subdividing some bulk material into smaller units, reaching a product 
with nanoscale dimensions. Chemical procedures, in contrast, are mainly bottom-up approaches 
that start with a chemical reaction giving the metal atoms, followed by controlled aggregation of 
atoms into particles22,23. When compared to physical methods, chemical have better control over 
size distribution, surface defects and shape. Therefore, the method chosen to carry out the NPs 
synthesis affects their size distribution, morphology and surface deffects1,22. Commonly, for 
chemical syntheses, AuNPs are often prepared in aqueous or non-aqueous solutions by the 
reduction of a dissolved metal precursor, together with a reducing agent and carried out in the 
presence of a stabilizing agent1. Developed in 1951, the well-known Turkevich method is the 
simplest way to produce aqueous suspensions of monodispersed AuNPs with good stability21. 
Despite its clear advantage of using three starting materials –auric acid, sodium citrate and water-
, the AuNPs obtained by the Turkevich method have a broader distribution of size and shape when 
compared with those prepared in organic media24. Nonetheless, a versatile synthesis method 
developed in 2009 allows not only the use of mild reaction conditions, but the use of sustainable 
and renewable reducing and capping agents for the synthesis of metal nanocatalysts with rather 
high monodispersity. The umbrella term saccharide-based approach to metallic nano-structure 
synthesis (SAMENS) was used to embrace all these syntheses25. 
As the research in the optical and physical properties of AuNPs increased, the number of devices 
in which they can be applied also increased steeply. Their high photothermal conversion 
efficiencies and large extinction cross section have made them ideal candidates for bio-
technological applications, such as photothermal therapy and drug delivery26. Biomedical 
applications made use of their absorption and scattering power to use near infrared light to achieve 
deep penetration into soft tissues27, while their sensitivity and selectivity response to biological 
environments have been use for bio-sensing28. Characterization techniques have also been 
improved using the plasmon enhancement of AuNPs. The enormous enhancement of Raman 
intensity for absorbed molecules on their surfaces allowed the characterization down to the single-
molecule level29. Their plasmon-enhanced fluorescence, instead, allowed their integration as 
optical nanoantennas30. AuNPs are also used to increase the power conversion efficiency of 
photovoltaic cells because of their strong light confinement, thereby reducing the physical 
thickness of the absorber layers31. Finally, their incorporation to metal and metal oxide supports 
(often into ZnO or TiO2) made them an excellent photocatalysts for a wide range of

environmentally friendly reactions, being water splitting, CO oxidation and H2O2 synthesis the 
most acclaimed photocatalyzed reactions32–35. 
 
Project aim  
The aim of this project is to delve into how different surface functionalization of AuNPs has a 
bearing on their response to excitation with broad band visible light, as well as how different parts 
of the visible spectrum affects the kinetics of aggregation and ultimately the morphology of the 
aggregates. In order to observe how excitation of surface plasmons leads to growth and 
aggregation, different concentrations of divalent salts, e.g. CaSO4, were used to partially 
destabilize the particles during the light-induced reaction. The experimental part entails (1) the 
design of a setup that couples UV-Vis spectroscopy with optical excitation of AuNP suspensions, 
(2) the synthesis of AuNPs with different capping agents and/or types of stabilization, (3) time-
dependent UV-vis spectroscopy of AuNP colloids during continuous excitation, and (4) 
characterization of AuNP suspensions by measurement of ionic conductivity and zeta potential to 
relate their electrostatic environment –i.e. stability- with their response to counter-ion 
concentration and light excitation.

2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
2.1. Concepts 
2.1.1. 
Colloidal Nanoparticles: Solution Synthesis and Formation 
Modern scientific evaluation of colloidal gold did not begin until Faraday’s ground-breaking work 
in 185736. Since then, a great deal of experimental methods for the synthesis of metal, metal oxide 
and semiconductor NPs have been developed37. 100 years later, during the 1950s, LaMer and co-
workers explained the processes of colloidal formation, which is still today seen as a general basic 
model. Nonetheless, the lack of reliable experimental information about the particle growth 
process –in particular of the particle size and concentration during the growth process- prompted 
several studies that demonstrated that the classical nucleation theory fails to describe the growth 
process1. The formation mechanisms of NPs are still debated even after 150 years of research in 
the metal colloids field. 
Even though the details of the processes still remains unclear, the basic principle of NP formation 
is the combination of two major processes: nucleation and growth. These can be interpreted as a 
transition from a homogeneous to a heterogeneous phase38. The term “burst nucleation” is a crucial 
concept in the synthesis of monodisperse nanocrystals. This concept was first used by LaMer and 
his colleges back in the 1950s to describe how nuclei are generated at the same time due to 
homogeneous nucleation –without any seed- and subsequently grow without additional 
nucleation37. In the nucleation process, a high energy barrier exists due to the spontaneous 
formation of a solid phase and soli-liquid interfaces39. The LaMer plot (Figure 1) is very handy for 
visualizing how the energy barrier works to induce the burst nucleation, while Gibbs free energy 
is used to interpret the nucleation thermodynamically. The process of nucleation and growth can 
be divided into three elemental steps. First step entails the increase in monomer concentration due 
to reduction of metal precursor and surpasses the solubility limit (Cs). Precipitation, does not occur 
due to the extremely high energy barrier for spontaneous nucleation (I). In the second step, the 
supersaturation increases and reaches the critical nucleation limit (Cmin) where it is high enough to 
overcome the energy barrier for nucleation (II), leading to monomers undergoing the above-
mentioned “burst nucleation”, which significantly reduces the concentration of free monomers in 
solution.  The concentration eventually drops below Cmin, after which no nucleation occurs. The 
systems enters the third and last step (III), in which nucleation is effectively stopped and the 
particles undergo a growth process controlled by the diffusion of monomers through the solution. 
The remaining monomers are transferred from the solvation sphere to the surface of existing solid 
particles during the growth phase until the monomer concentration stabilizes at the solubility 
limit1,37,40.

In principle, the model rationalizes the conventional strategies for controlling mean particle size 
and dispersity of colloidal NPs through timing of both the nucleation and growth phases following 
LaMer’s plot, a fast reduction would induce a rapid buildup of supersaturation and particle 
formation dominated by the nucleation event that will lead to many small NPs. Since the 
diffusional growth only depends upon the monomer flux, smaller particles will grow faster than 
larger particles, leading to a size focusing and narrowing the size distribution. Broadly, the particle 
concentration is determined in the nucleation phase while the size is determined in the growth 
phase where concentration of NPs is constant but the size increases. Even though the LaMer model 
is commonly used to describe the NP size distribution, it is not able to characterize the evolution 
of the mentioned distribution. Therefore, it only describes the process of nucleation followed by 
growth of the stable nuclei, but the characteristics of the growth remain more or less 
unspecific1,11,41. 
The monomer concentration is not a boundary for the advancement of the NP size distribution, 
even under the solubility limit. The final stage of the colloidal NPs synthesis is better explained 
by a spontaneous and thermodynamically driven process called Ostwald ripening. This process is 
based on how the surface tension depends on the particle size. Since atoms on the surface of a 
particle are energetically less stable than the ones in the interior, which are bonded to more atoms, 
larger particles are more energetically favored than smaller particles. As the system tries to lower 
its overall energy, atoms on the surface of a small particles may detach from the particle and diffuse 
into the solution before depositing on another (or the same) particle. Overall, smaller particles 
shrink, while larger particles grow and the average size will increase1,42,43.

2.1.2. 
Colloidal Nanoparticles: Stability 
For a broad range of NP syntheses, the colloidal stability and not the thermodynamic stability 
determine the minimum particle size, which demands the description of the colloidal stability. 
Colloidal dispersions have a high interfacial area and are therefore unstable, so they have to be 
stabilized. This stability can be understood by considering the various types of forces between 
particles. The most important ones are the van der Waals forces, which are usually attractive and 
are the cause of instability by leading to irreversible aggregation of unprotected particles. These 
mentioned attractive forces can be counteracted by coating the colloidal particles, which can lead 
to stable colloids through either steric or electrostatic stabilization effects. Therefore, stabilization 
can be either steric (polymer chains) or electrostatic (Columbic interactions). Steric stabilization 
is often achieved with polymers, creating a short-range repulsive force between colloidal particles 
when they are compressed1,43. Nonetheless, there are other repulsive forces, such as the elastic 
forces when particles get in contact to each other, and especially those repulsive forces due to the 
steric interactions with surfactants, polymers or other molecules adsorbed on the particles, thereby 
causing ionization of the particles and resulting in columbic repulsion screened by counterions 
released into the solution (electrostatic forces). These columbic repulsive forces are responsible 
for stabilizing the dispersion if they are larger than the van der Waals forces –and the kinetic energy 
of the particles-, even though steric forces may also play a crucial role for stability. In broad terms, 
stabilization is accomplished when repulsive forces are larger than attractive forces and kinetic 
energy of the colloidal particles. All these forces play at distances of the order of nanometers, even 
so they have a direct effect on macroscopic properties of dispersions and agglomerates43–45. 
Before delving into colloidal nanoparticles stability it is important to specify some of the concepts 
related to unstable colloidal systems. There are no widely standardized terminologies but the term 
“aggregation” is often used for all the phenomena shown in Figure 2, although they do represent 
different situations. “Aggregates” is the term used to describe the structure formed by unstable 
colloidal particles. Coagulation implies the formation of a compact aggregate, while flocculation 
refers to the formation of an “open network” which may or may not separate out macroscopically, 
i.e. by agitation. On the other hand, coalescence (liquid or gas particles) or sintering (solid 
particles) refers to when particles merge to form a larger continuous structure. The rate of 
aggregation is in general determined by the frequency of collisions and the probability of cohesion 
during collision. If the collisions are caused by Brownian motion, the process is called perikinetic 
aggregation; if by hydrodynamic motions (e.g. convection or sedimentation) one may speak 
of orthokinetic aggregation43,46.

The theory of the stability of charged colloids in suspension was developed by Derjaguin, Landau, 
Verwey and Overbeek (DLVO) and takes into account repulsive and attractive forces into a single 
intermolecular potential. To track the colloidal stability this theory only considers the Van der 
Waals attractive forces which lead to instability (negative potential), and the repulsive electro-
static forces (positive potential), Figure 3. The shape of the curve is therefore the sum of the 
exponential and steep decay of the repulsive and attractive terms, respectively. The resulting 
maximum of the curve represents an aggregation barrier that two particles have to overcome when 
they collide, and therefore determines the colloidal stability1,47,48. Nonetheless, if this potential 
barrier is much higher than the kinetic energy of the particles, the dispersion will be kinetically 
stable. The balance between Van der Waals attraction electrostatic repulsion may give rise to a 
secondary minimum, Figure 3. Particles can aggregate within this minimum at large nanometer 
range. Such reversible aggregation is called flocculation and the particles can be separated by a 
small energy input, i.e. gentle sonication. For small particles (radius less than 10 nm), the 
secondary minimum is never deep enough for flocculation to occur in those cases where the 
potential energy maximum is high enough to prevent coagulation into the primary minimum. If 
the particles are larger, flocculation in the secondary minimum may cause observable effects such 
as macroscopically visual aggregation2,43,49. 
==PAGE:17==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES 
 
Page      8 
 
 
Figure 3: Colloidal stability is typically represented by a representative DLVO diagram. The plot shows potential 
energy as function of interparticle distance48. 
Manipulating colloidal stability implies knowing how to influence the various interparticle forces, 
especially Van der Waals and electrostatic forces. Owing to the fact that interfaces are nearly 
always charged electrically, usually due to adsorbed ions from the surroundings, they may have 
an either positive or negative charge, although the latter is the most common. This charge at the 
interface is compensated by mobile counter-ions. These are attracted electrostatically but can 
freely diffuse away creating an electrical double layer that exists in almost all colloidal 
dispersions1,44,48. The electrostatic double-layer force dominates at relatively medium-large 
separations. However, very distant from the surfaces (very large separations) and when the 
surfaces are brought very close to each other, the attractive van der Waals forces (may) overcome 
the repulsive forces and dominate the interactions. If Van der Waals forces dominate the surfaces, 
they will be pulled into a strong adhesive contact, whereas stability is obtained in the region where 
the repulsion forces dominate. There are other types of stabilization –e.g. steric forces due to 
polymers or surfactants- which are not accounted for the DLVO theory43. Nevertheless, steric 
repulsion may also play an important role for colloidal stability – especially for non-charged 
particles - and it will be dominant at high surface coverage. Thus, it has been suggested that the 
DLVO theory is an adequate explanation of effects of the adsorbed macromolecules if the surface 
coverage is low43,45. 
As a conclusion, several parameters ultimately affect the stability of the system: the particle size, 
the value of the surface (zeta) potential, and the ion type and concentration.

2.1.3. 
The Surface Zeta Potential and the Electrical Double Layer 
The major contribution in the colloidal kinetic stability relies on the electric charge on the surface 
of the particles –i.e. electrostatic repulsion-. Because of this charge, counterions tend to gather 
around the charged particles, forming an ionic atmosphere surrounding them. There are two 
differentiated charged region. First, a static layer of counterions that adhere rather strongly to the 
surface of the colloidal particle, known as the Stern layer. Second, the charged particles in this 
layer attract again an oppositely charged atmosphere of mobile ions coming from the bulk solution 
and forms a less well-defined layer called the slipping plane43. The charged inner shell and the 
outer ionic atmosphere is called the electrical double layer (Figure 4), and its thickness is measured

2.2. Properties of Colloidal Noble Metal Nanoparticles  
2.2.1. 
Optical Properties 
Until the apparition of plasmonic science, colloidal metal nanoparticles have attracted much 
attention for a great deal of years due to their colorful appearance. Nevertheless, it has not been 
until the research in their optical properties thrived that has been understood the association 
between those bright colors and their unique capability to support the collective oscillations of 
their conduction-band electrons54. These optical properties of metal nanoparticles –as well as those 
of quantum dots- are a good example of size-dependent properties. In such NPs, the conduction 
electron cloud is brought to oscillate by an incident electromagnetic field thus producing a resonant 
==PAGE:20==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES 
Page      11 
 
surface plasmon response (Figure 5), known as surface plasmon resonance (SPR). These coupled 
oscillations have lower energy than those in the bulk form and can also propagate along an 
extended interface, surface plasmon polaritons (fs), or they can exist as localized surface plasmon 
resonances (LSPR) on finite nanoscale structures55,56. For certain metals such as gold, the surface 
plasmon resonance appears in the visible range, leading to rich color changes of colloidal solutions 
of NPs of different sizes. Hence, the spectral properties of this resonance upon illumination depend 
on the constitutive material, the geometry of the NP and its environment, i.e. the dielectric 
constant4,56. 
 
Figure 5: Illustration of surface plasmon resonance (SPR), which arises from the coupling of incident 
electromagnetic waves and conduction band electrons5. 
In photochemical processes, excitation results in the generation of a molecular excited state, 
empowering both the oxidizing and the reducing role5. In plasmon-mediated reactions, though, the 
excitation of the LSPRs is followed by several subsequent effects. The most alluring feature of 
LSPRs is the confinement and concentration of electromagnetic energy at the nanoparticle surface. 
This energy can afterwards be dissipated through the formation of charge carriers within the 
nanoparticle accompanied by heating or by scattering of photons into the far field4,57. Therefore, 
plasmonic NPs can behave as nanosources of heat, light and/or energetic electrons4. 
 
a) 
Plasmonic enhancement of absorbed light 
The electron cloud of a metal nanoparticle oscillates at the same frequency as the incident 
electromagnetic wave. When this frequency matches the LSP resonance of the nanoparticle, the 
oscillations give rise to very strong electromagnetic fields near the particle surface, which further 
focuses the light into the particle and increases the photon flux for molecules located at this surface. 
This oscillating electron cloud is confined to only a few nanometers above its surface and 
subsequently produces electromagnetic field strengths 100 to 10 000 times that of the incident 
light. The enhancement of the electromagnetic field does not always occur uniformly along the 
particle surface. The shape of the particle or particle assemblies confines the enhancement into so-
called “hot spots”4,5,58. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES

b) 
Heat generation 
When a nanoparticle is irradiated with a wavelength of light that overlaps its SPR, the resonant 
oscillation of its electron cloud is also responsible for energy dissipation into the metal. The 
oscillating electrons lose energy by electron-electron scattering, eventually increasing the NP 
temperature59. The name “photothermal effect” has been given to this phenomena. The associated 
temperature increase is not only dependent on the total heat power absorbed, but on the NP 
morphology and the thermal conductivity of the surrounding medium. The mentioned heated 
region is therefore remarkably small (considering the size of the NP) and this provokes a very fast 
heating and cooling dynamics, which is used –uniquely up to now- in bio-related applications4,5,60. 
 
c) 
Hot electron injection 
Hertz observed in the end of the 19th century that when a material is illuminated with ultraviolet 
radiation –i.e. highly energetic photons-, hot electrons are generated and emitted from the material 
via the photoelectric effect. Even though it was not until 1905 that Albert Einstein provided the 
key explanation of this effect, this is the most well studied phenomenon involving hot electrons. 
The excitation of an electron in a solid results in two types of carriers: an electron and a hole. 
These carriers are named “hot” if their energies are larger than those from thermal excitations at 
ambient temperatures. The rising of nanoplasmonics has shown that plasmonic nanostructures are 
able to directly convert the absorbed light into electrical energy by generating hot carriers. When 
the surface plasmon decays energetically, electron-hole pairs can be generated, leading to a 
broadening of the linewidth of the plasmon resonance4. Even though this process is considered 
counterproductive for plasmonic properties, it might be useful for driving chemical reactions and 
for rearrangement of plasmonic junctions. Since light is mainly absorbed through interband 
electronic transitions (d-sp transitions), excited electrons can be injected into molecular orbitals of 
adsorbed molecules, thereby accelerating the reactions taking place on the surface61. In this 
electronic process, energized electrons or holes are jumped into a higher unpopulated electronic 
state of the adsorbed molecule, causing the adsorbate to move to a different potential energy 
surface (PES). The forces induced to the atoms of the adsorbate in the new PES lead to a nuclear 
motion of the atoms, therefore resulting in chemical 
transformations (Figure 6)62. A number of cross-

The net charge of a molecule is affected by the pH of its surrounding environment and can become 
more positively or negatively charged due to the gain or loss of protons. The isoelectric point (pI) 
is therefore defined as the pH at which a molecule has no net electrical charge (known as 
zwitterion). Isoelectric point is mostly used when dealing with large molecules that have a high

groups, instead, pKa values are sufficient to describe their state as function of pH. Zwitterion is

isoelectric point can be finally calculated from the mean of the pKa values of the compound.   
Since surfaces charge themselves to form a double layer and the common charges are determined

environment. Hence, at low pH, the surface can be charged positively by the adsorption of protons; 
whereas it will be negatively charged at high pH due to exhaustion of protons. Thereby, the surface 
charge –and hence the electrostatic stability- reaches a minimum when the isoelectric point moves 
closer to the pH66.

2.3.1. 
Ultra Violet-Visible Light Spectrophotometry 
 
When radiation interacts with matter, a number of phenomena can take place, including reflection, 
absorbance, scattering, fluorescence and phosphorescence. Because light is a form of energy, 
absorption of light by molecules provokes the energy content of those molecules (or atoms) to 
increase. In several molecules and atoms, photons of ultraviolet (UV) and visible light have enough 
energy to cause transitions, such as charge transfers or collective electron oscillations. The 
wavelength of light absorbed corresponds to the energy required to make an electronic transition 
to a higher energy level. Therefore, the more easily excited the electrons are, the longer the 
wavelength it can absorb. As a result of the energy difference in energy levels of the absorbing 
species, these transitions give rise to very narrow absorbance bands. Nevertheless, since many 
different transitions can occur –corresponding to many different energies-, those bands are 
broadened (Figure 7). The shape of the absorption band is therefore determined by the excited state 
lifetime, i.e. by the relaxation mechanism68.

transitions took place.

2.3.2. 
Transmission Electron Microscopy 
In light microscopes, light from a light source is made into a parallel beam by a condenser lens, 
this passes through the object and is later focused as an enlarged image onto a screen by an 
objective lens. In electron microscopy, photons are substituted by electrons giving rise to a higher 
resolution. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is a microscopy technique where a beam of 
electrons is transmitted through an ultra-thin specimen, interacting with the sample as it passes 
through. Hence, in this setup the light source is replaced by an electron source (tungsten filament), 
the glass lenses are replaced by magnetic lenses and the projection screen is replaced by a 
fluorescent screen which emits light when hit by electrons (Figure 9). In order to avoid scattering 
from unwanted atoms, the whole trajectory from source to screen is under vacuum and the sample 
has to be very thin since it has to allow electrons to penetrate it71. 
 
Figure 9: Basics of transmission electron microscopy (TEM): (left) Schematic setup of TEM, and (right) signals 
generated when high-energy electrons interact with a specimen72. 
The high resolution of electron microscopy comes from the short wavelength of the electrons used 
for illumination. In TEM, a monochromatic beam of electrons is accelerated through a potential of 
40 to 300 kilovolts (kV) and passed through a strong magnetic field that acts as a lens. The 
wavelength of electrons is about 10,000 times shorter than that of visible light and, if lens oddities 
can be minimized, the resolution of modern electron microscopes may reach less than 0.5Å (less 
than the typical separation between two atoms in a solid). This resolution is 1,000 times greater 
than a light microscope and about 500,000 times greater than that of a human eye71,73. 
The TEM images presented in this thesis are recorded -by trained personal- using a Tecnai G2 T20 
microscope from FEI Company at the Center for Electron Nanoscopy at DTU. Images are recorded 
at 200 kV accelerating voltage corresponding to a wavelength of about 2.5 pm.

Zeta Potential and Particle Size Measurement 
Even though there are several equations to calculate zeta potential numerically (Hückel, 
Smoluchowski…)43, the surface potential is measured via electrophoresis experiment. The 
instrument measures velocity of a charged colloid particle (u) influenced by an electric field with 
strength E in a stationary liquid. Charged particles that have a non-zero zeta potential will migrate 
towards one of the two electrodes under the electric field (Figure 10). As particles are irradiated in 
migration, scattering light causes Doppler shift depending on the electrophoresis mobility. 
Therefore, the speed is simply measured by a laser Doppler electrophoresis and theoretical 
approaches are eventually applied to calculate the surface zeta potential74,75.

3.3. Synthesis of Gold Nanoparticles 
Gold Nanoparticles (AuNPs) have been prepared via relatively similar procedures employing 
different capping agents. All the procedures carried out in this work are derived from the Turkevich 
synthesis method77 and/or the SAMENS method. While the Turkevich method was carried out in 
order to synthesize gold nanoparticles by reduction with citrate, a method developed in the group 
(Department of Chemistry, NanoDTU, 2009) was used for both the MES-optimized synthesis 
(MOS) and the only buffer synthesis (OBS)78. 
All the AuNPs have been prepared in volumes of 20 mL for those nanoparticles synthesized by 
reduction of citrate and in volumes of 12.5 mL for those prepared by MOS or OBS. In each 
synthesis, buffers, capping and reducing agents were stirred and heated prior to the addition of the 
precursor (HAuCl4). All the mixtures were heated at 95 ◦C in an oil bath with constant and generous 
stirring. To avoid evaporation of reagents, a condenser was added to the 3-necks round bottomed 
flask.  
The size variation of each synthesis can be realized by changing the concentration of capping and 
reducing agents, i.e. mixing ratio of components. The size and its distribution of the produced 
nanocrystals are thus controlled by the LaMer model explained in Section 2.1.1. The specific 
conditions of each synthesis are founded in the following sections. 
 
3.3.1. 
Citrate-stabilized Gold Nanoparticles (AuCit) 
Synthesis of AuCit is a well-studied and straight-forward process, first reported by Turkevich in 
195177.  The reducing and capping agent (trisodium citrate) is added to the aqueous medium (Milli-
Q water) and pre-heated for several minutes. The precursor (tetrachloroauric acid) is then added 
to the solution as fast as possible (so-called hot injection), leading to a very dark reddish solution. 
The resulting AuNPs are heated and stirred for 15 minutes, and removed from the oil bath to cool 
down for another 15 minutes.

3.5. Techniques Preparation 
Most of the measurements employed in the present work require additional preparation of the 
samples, as well as a previous cleaning-up of the set-up and/or the samples themselves. 
 
3.5.1. 
UV-Vis Spectrometry 
The entire set of measurements employing UV-Vis spectrometry were performed using a 4-side 
polished quartz cuvette. Since quartz does not absorb light in the UV-Visible range, this cuvette 
was optimal for irradiating the sample with light while at the same time UV-Vis measurements 
were taken. This cuvette was cleaned with Milli-Q water and subsequently ethanol after every 
measurement, as well as occasional cleaning with aqua regia to eliminate deposited gold.  
Owing to the distortion of the spectrum –due to too strong absorption-, some samples required 
dilution beforehand, which was carried out using Milli-Q water. A blank was recorded before 
every measurement employing Milli-Q water as well.  
In order to keep track of the power provided by the light source throughout the whole set of 
measurements, calibrations of the power output of the manipulated beam were also carried out 
from time to time employing a spectrometer flame and OceanView software from Ocean Optics79. 
Calibration of the spectrometer itself was also done. 
 
3.5.2. 
pH and Ionic Conductivity Measurements 
Both pH and conductivity measurements were carried out using a glass beaker, previously 
immersed in nitric acid and rinsed with Milli-Q water. For pH measurement, the pH-meter was 
calibrated beforehand employing 3 standard solutions with well-known pH (with pH equal to 9, 4 
and 7). Before any measurement, the sensor was rinsed with abundant Milli-Q water. The 
conductivity meter, in contrast, required only one solution with known conductivity (KCl). The 
measurements were taken without any temperature correction and with constant stirring of the 
solution. 
 
3.5.3. 
TEM 
In order to make sure any structure obtained after any treatment or measurement –e.g. light 
irradiation- would remain unchanged until the revelation of its morphology, TEM samples were 
prepared right after any experiment. The TEM grids were held at the very edge with a pair of sharp 
tweezers and a solution drop of approximately 10 µL was placed on the grid. After its evaporation, 
the resulting sample was kept on a filter paper in a Petri dish until its imaging. The imaging of the 
sample was done by PhD student Frederick Stappen and Postdoc Christian Engelbrekt of the group.

3.5.4. 
Zeta Potential and Size  
Even though zeta potential and size measurement took place in the same instrument, their sample 
preparation differ a bit. For size measurement, the solutions are simply put in a glass cuvette and 
introduced into the instrument. Since the particle size is measured by light scattering, there is a 
boundary of minimum and maximum concentration. The concentration should be selected such 
that the sample gets slightly turbid75. For zeta potential measurement, a disposable cell with two 
built-in electrodes is used. Since the instrument employs electrophoresis to carry out the 
measurement, it is mandatory that the solution covers both electrodes, and that air bubbles are 
absent in the cell. The zeta potential cell is cleaned using Milli-Q water, followed by the addition 
of the next measured sample solution.

4.1. Constructing the in-situ setup and data analysis 
As a basis for the discussion of the results an introduction to the set-up utilized to carry out the

4.1.1. 
Coupled excitation and extinction spectroscopy 
A newly build set-up designed by the group was used in this work to accomplish the recording of 
extinction spectra as function of time while exciting with broadband white light. A mounting base 
was crafted by the DTU Chemistry workshop exclusively for the Agilent 8453 UV-Vis 
spectrometer, on which the different facilities were fixed. The mounting base was placed between 
the light source and the detector of the spectrometer (Scheme 1). The plate was made as an optical 
breadboard with an array of tapped holes to allow for the placement of different optical posts and 
clamps making the arrangement very versatile. The quartz cuvette was placed in the middle of the 
spectrometer beam, while insuring that the beam irradiated the center of the cuvette. The solid 
state plasma light source (HPLS245, ThorLabs) was placed next to the spectrometer and the light 
coupled to a liquid light guide optical fiber.

The fiber tip was mounted in a lens assembly tube and held by a fiber mount (Figure 12). In order 
to focus the white light into a narrower beam, two different lenses were placed in front of the

4.1.2. 
Adjustments for Measurements 
Different conditions and settings were tested in order to insure the reliability of the results and 
optimize the excitation of the particles in the cuvette. The light beam was placed at different 
distances from the cuvette (2-15 mm) and 3 mm was found to be the distance guarantying the 
maximum power density. Different volumes of solution were also tested, and 2 mL were found to 
be the optimal volume. Convection in larger volumes would cause a lack of excitation of the 
nanoparticles since particles do not spend enough time within the irradiated volume. On the other 
hand, the UV-Visible beam would not fit completely inside the solution at lower volumes. Since 
heating was powerful and experiments long enough to induce significant evaporation, a lid was 
placed at the top of the cuvette. In order to test if the thermally induced convection of the solution 
was enough to mix the solution, bubbling with air was added during the measurements. Even 
though oxygen has no effect on the stability of the particles that are stable under ambient conditions 
(it does not interact with the capping agent), the bubbling was cooling down the particles, as well 
as interfering in the absorption measurement, so it was dismissed. Finally, it was demonstrated 
that the broadband light was not interfering with the beam from the spectrometer by covering the 
light source during the measurements. Therefore, both beams were placed perpendicular to each 
other in the same plane (facing the bottom of the cuvette).

4.1.4. 
Interpretation of Spectra 
It is frequent to represent the UV-Visible spectra with absorption as function of the wavelength of 
the incident light. Nevertheless, noble metal nanoparticles exhibit scattering of light besides 
absorption of the incident beam. Since the detector of the spectrometer captures the light that has 
gone through the sample, i.e. the transmitted light, the measurement no longer corresponds to 
absorption but extinction. This unit is therefore used to account for the light scattered together with 
the light absorbed. 
For most of the compounds, a peak in the UV-visible spectra is obtained when an electron from a 
HOMO level absorbs appropriate energy and jumps into a higher unfilled orbitals. For noble metal 
nanoparticles, however, a LSPR band is obtained due to oscillations of free electrons, which have 
a natural frequency of oscillation (see Section 2.2.1). When this oscillation matches the frequency 
of incident radiation, absorption of light occurs leading to a LSPR band. Therefore, the peak 
wavelength, the peak width, and the effect of secondary resonances yield a unique spectral shape 
for a plasmonic nanoparticle with a specific size and shape. When nanoparticles aggregate, the 
metal particles become electronically coupled and this new system has a different SPR than the 
individual particles. For spherical particles, the LSPR peak shifts to longer wavelengths and 
broadens with increasing particle diameter. Gold nanoparticles with diameters below 2 nm do not 
present LSPR. Uneven shaped particles –e.g. nanorods-, display two distinct features as a result of 
the presence of both transversal and longitudinal LSPR modes, and the latter shifts significantly 
into the far-red region of the spectrum.  
 
Figure 16: Diagram showing the different parts of a spectrum according to the origin of the extinction. 
Aggregation results in dramatic changes to the extinction spectrum with intensity increase in the 
red/infrared region of the spectrum (Figure 16), and a (clear) change in color. When excited AuNPs 
approach each other, the electron oscillations start to influence each other through dipole-dipole 
interactions. Electrons are able to tunnel between the particles at even shorter distances. Finally, 
==PAGE:42==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES 
Page      33 
 
when particles touch and connect, they start to behave like rod-shaped particles with a longitudinal 
plasmon mode. 
As depicted in Figure 16, the extinction spectrum can be divided into three parts according to the 
origin of the bands. The region entailing d-d transitions of the metal corresponds to the section at 
wavelengths below (approximately) 450 nm, i.e. the highest energies. During the irradiation of a 
sample containing transition metals, the electron is excited from an initial lower energy d orbital 
to a higher energy state by absorbing photon energy from the spectrometer beam. Hence, as long 
as the wavelength of the incident beam has enough energy to promote an electron to a higher 
energy d orbital, this transition will be detectable in the spectrum. The shape of the spectrum in 
this energetic region will give information about the actual concentration of metal in the sample, 
decreasing in intensity as precipitation occurs. The second region at roughly 500-600 nm 
corresponds to the plasmon resonance of (in this case) spherical gold nanoparticles. The plasmon 
peak at 520 nm is a fingerprint of spherical AuNPs, so any modification of the shape or position 
of this peak will indicate a change in the structure of the nanoparticles. Increasing the AuNPs 
diameter will result in a red-shifting of the spectra (shifting toward higher wavelengths), as well 
as when the presence of ligands increases. Aggregation will give rise to a decrease in intensity, 
indicating the decay in the amount of spherical AuNPs. Finally, the third region involves the 
presence of bigger and aspherical plasmonic networks. When particles aggregate, their conduction 
electrons near each particle surface become delocalized and shared among particles nearby, which 
results in a change in their optical properties. When this takes place, the SPR shifts to lower 
energies (longer wavelengths), causing the extinction peak to red-shift to longer wavelengths. 
Therefore, an extinction increase in this region will suggest the sintering and/or approach of the 
particles to each other. The absorption from the glass cuvette results in a noisy spectrum below 
300 nm wavelengths, which is not observable when using quartz cuvettes. Overall, as the particles 
destabilize, the original extinction peak will decrease in intensity and (often) the peak itself will 
broaden or a secondary peak will appear at longer wavelengths owing to aggregates forming.

4.2. Synthesized Structures and Role of their Components 
Besides the gold itself, the different capping agents employed in the synthesis of the different 
nanoparticles play a crucial role in the characterization of the processes taking place when those 
particles undergo irradiation with white light and/or addition of electrolytes. Therefore, it is 
important to highlight the role of each component in the synthesis and explain the interactions 
leading to either stability or instability.  
 
4.2.1. 
Citrate-capped Gold Nanoparticles (AuCit) 
AuCit nanoparticles are synthesized using only three reagents. Excluding the precursor providing 
the noble metal nanoparticles (HAuCl4) and the solvent (Milli-Q water), the capping and reducing 
agent (sodium citrate) remains as the only contributor to stability of the synthesized AuNPs. 
Citric acid is a weak organic tricarboxylic acid, having three different pKa values at 25 ◦C: 5.21, 
4.28 and 2.9280. Since the pH of the solution is determined by the ratio of citric acid/citrate, the 
citrate will not only act as a reducing agent and protection ligand, it also performs a pH 
mediation81. Even though it is well known that citrate brings stabilization by electrostatic 
(repulsive) forces, it is not clear how the binding with the gold surface takes place. In any case it 
involves the carboxylates (Figure 17c) and pH will have a large influence on the stabilization. 
Higher pH values will shift the equilibrium towards a more-charged compound and therefore 
provide more electrostatic stabilization to the AuNPs.  
 
Figure 17: Size distribution (a), TEM images (b) and the capping agent (c) of AuCit nanoparticles. 
Size and size distribution of AuCit nanoparticles were obtained via both TEM and light scattering 
experiments (Zetasizer). Measurements of particle diameter in TEM showed an average particle 
diameter of 14 ± 3 nm (Figure 17a), while size measurements in the Zetasizer gave an average 
particle diameter of 25.2 ± 0.4 nm with a polydispersity index (PdI) of 0.41. The surface zeta 
potential was measured as -49 ± 28 mV.

4.2.2. 
MES-stabilized Gold Nanoparticles (OBS) 
AuNPs synthesized by the procedure referenced as the “only buffer synthesis” are highly similar 
to AuCit nanoparticles, but with the difference that 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid (MES) 
was used instead of citrate, acting as both buffer (pH adjusted to 8) and reducing agent. The overall 
pKa has been reported to be 6.1682. MES contains a morpholine ring and an ethane-sulfonic acid 
moiety (Figure 18c). The buffering capacity comes from the amine which will get protonated at 
acidic pH values, while the sulfonate will be deprotonated except for very acidic conditions (pKa 
of the sulfonate in taurine, a similar compound with a terminal amine instead of the morpholine 
moiety, is below 0). Therefore, as long as the pH is not above the pKa of MES, MES will also 
contribute to stability by electrostatic repulsive forces.

4.2.3. 
Starch-stabilized Gold Nanoparticles (AuMOS) 
In contrast to the two above-mentioned syntheses, the MES-optimized synthesis entails three 
different organic reagents, each of them presenting different roles. MES at pH= 7 will provide the 
buffer solution, while starch will act as a capping agent and glucose as reducing agent. 
Starch is a polysaccharide consisting of a mixture of two glucose polymers forming branched 
structures. Even though there a great number of starch compounds, the Zulkowsky starch has been 
employed in this synthesis due to its solubility on account of its short chains consisting of roughly 
12 glucose units83. Glucose (pKa= 12.3) is the monosaccharide unit of starch and its role as a 
reducing agent is owing to the opening of the ring, thereby proving a reactive aldehyde group84. 
The aldehyde of the glucose reacts with the Au precursor and forms gluconic acid65: 
 
 
In reality, MES competes with glucose for the reduction of the gold precursor. The synthesis of 
AuMOS nanoparticles without glucose does not present significant differences to the AuNPs but 
it affects the formation of currently unidentified side products. Synthesis in the absence of MES 
would entail the reduction by glucose and a large pH change65. It is therefore evident that glucose 
present a very subtle role in the synthesis, whereas MES and starch are essential components for 
the synthesis. Unlike the AuCit and AuOBS, AuMOS nanoparticles are stabilized sterically by 
starch due to its bulky structure and zero net charge.

4.3. Destabilizing Gold Nanoparticles 
The original aim of the project was to follow the destabilization of gold nanoparticles with different 
coatings by irradiation with intense white light, and force the growth of extended networks of 
sintered AuNPs. Initial experiments unexpectedly showed that the colloidal AuNPs were very 
stable even after prolonged excitation. Different methods and strategies were examined to disrupt 
the stabilization and achieve the successful light-induced morphological changes. The different 
experiments carried out led to the identification of conditions used for the central part of the present 
work: Study the effect of electrostatic and steric destabilization of gold nanoparticles on light-
induced changes to morphology, and guiding the evolution by controlled excitation.  
 
4.3.1. 
Initiating Destabilization 
The irradiation of gold nanoparticles with a broadband white light unexpectedly did not give rise 
to destabilization and subsequent aggregation, as can be seen by the lack of change to the extinction 
spectra even after 80 minutes of excitation. All particle solutions are stable under ambient light 
conditions and thus not very sensitive to light. To examine if the setup was not providing sufficient 
excitation to induce changes, several experiments with modifications of the setup were carried out 
to optimize the excitation of the AuNPs (briefly explained in Section 4.1.2): 
 Arrangement of the set-up (Table A1 - Exp. 2/5/6/8/35).  
The results of the experiments depends mainly on what is inside the cuvette and the power of 
the irradiating light, but how the different pieces of the setup are arranged also played an 
important role. The position of the light source –relative to the cuvette-, as well as the height 
of the cuvette compared to the UV-Vis beam, determine the amount of light absorbed by the 
nanoparticles and the recording of the spectra. The experiments manifested that the irradiating 
beam must collide in the middle of the cuvette, thereby heating the particles uniformly. If the 
solution was irradiated on the surface, a tiny spot originating from bubbling along the surface

 Bubbling (Table A1 - Exp. 1-3).  
In order to insure the proper mixing of the solution and uniform heating of the particles, 
bubbling with air was tested. The experiments showed that not only the thermal convection 
was enough for the mixing, but bubbling with air was cooling down the particles, thereby 
causing an opposite effect. Additionally, the bubbling had to be stopped during measure-ments 
to avoid interference of the air bubbles with the UV-Vis beam. Bubbling was therefore 
avoided during the rest of the measurements. 
 Intensity of the light beam (Table A1 - Exp. 36-40).  
It was firstly believed that very strong excitation might end up having a stabilizing effect. 
Since the focal point of the light source is a very narrow spot, the power provided by volume 
could occasion the speed up of the Brownian motion of the particles. The nanoparticles would 
therefore not spent enough time in the beam and the irradiation would not be successful. Also, 
extreme heating of the AuNPs might lead to formation of a water vapor shell around the 
particles acting as an additional stabilizing barrier85. The experiments suggested that the 
excitation of the particles increased (roughly) proportionally to the intensity of the light beam 
by showing tiny changes in their extinction spectra as the intensity was increasing. The rest of 
the experiments were therefore performed at 100% intensity. 
 Scattering (Table A1 - Exp. 3/18).  
In contrast to most of the compounds analyzed via UV-Vis spectrometry, colloidal metal 
nanoparticles scatter light. Since light is scattered in all directions, it was also a concern that 
irradiating the particles during the measurements could interfere the extinction measurement. 
In order to test this, the light source was covered during the recording of the spectrum, which 
showed no difference compared to when the light hit the particles. These experiments showed 
that the light beam did not interfere with the spectral measurement, even when they were in 
the same plane-. 
 Volume of solution (Table A1 - Exp. 1-10).  
While large volume (e.g. 3 mL) would lead to a lower overall heating (too many molecules 
for the same power), the spectrometer beam would not irradiate the solution at too low 
volumes –e.g. 1 mL-. 2 mL was found to be the optimal volume. 
 Less stable particles (Table A1 - Exp. 19).  
It was also proposed that particles were too stable to induce agglomeration by photons only. 
A batch of AuNPs with double the amount of gold precursor (AuCit-2) was synthetized in 
order to see if less available capping agent would affect the behavior. The gold precursor 
/capping agent ratio was not found to have an effect. 
 Artifact (Table A1 - Exp. 24/33).  
All instruments have an associated error in the representation or perception of the result. These 
experiments attempted to find out if some changes showed in the spectra were due to artifacts 
of the instrument or due to the actual change in morphology. It was shown that the instrument 
itself was not influencing the results by taking systematically fast measure-ments of the 
sample.

4.4. Chemically-induced Destabilization 
Owing to the fact that light-induced destabilization was not achieved, addition of different 
electrolytes at different concentrations was conducted as a chemical trigger for destabilization. 
While the addition of salts provided an electrostatic destabilization by increasing the ionic strength, 
the addition of acid induced changes in the structure of the capping agents leading to 
destabilization by the contribution of protons as well as ions. Different amounts of different 
electrolytes have therefore been added to solutions of citrate-stabilized nanoparticles in order to 
study how those additional ions affect the electrostatic environment. 
 
4.4.1. 
 Addition of CuCl2 
Since HAuCl4 is used as a precursor, chloride is already present in solution. However, it has been 
shown that these ions are responsible for destabilizing AuOBS at low concentrations65.  CuCl2 was 
therefore chosen because the already known effect of chloride and the divalent cation. Different 
volumes of a 20 mM solution of CuCl2 were added to a solution of AuCit diluted 1:4 and the 
correspondent extinction spectra was recorded during 80 minutes under irradiation with white 
light. Even though there is clearly an observable change proportional to the volume of electrolyte 
added, their extinction spectra eventually showed a transition when adding 40 µL of CuCl2. The 
delay in which this decrease at 520 nm was evidenced depended on the amount of salt; i.e. the 
larger the addition of CuCl2, the longer it took for the extinction to increase again at 520 nm.

in order to eventually achieve destabilization under white light irradiation. At this point, the effect 
of the different coatings is already visible. Observing the evolution of each extinction spectra is 
easily deducible that the same electrolyte gives rise to three totally different aggregate 
structures/interactions, confirming the existence of three different networks.

Figure 27 shows the proportional increase of the ionic conductivity with the amount of electrolyte 
added. Addition of CaSO4 results in an increase of the slipping plane overall charge caused by the 
increase of divalent ions, thereby increasing the charge density carried by each nanoparticle. 
Nonetheless, addition of electrolyte to the AuCit solution results in an exponential increase of the 
ionic conductivity despite the three volumes contained the same amount of calcium sulfate. The 
reason of this non-linear proportionality is most likely due to coordination between citrate anions 
and calcium cations. There have been reported two different ways in which calcium binds to 
citrate. Since citrate has 3 deprotonable oxygen atoms, calcium can either bind to two oxygen 
atoms of the same citrate molecule, or bind to two oxygen atoms belonging to two different 
molecules, thereby causing the bending of the molecule into a ring. The ratio is 1.5 calcium atoms 
per molecule of citrate (3 calcium atoms/2 citrate molecules)87. In this way, the change in the ionic 
conductivity would be explained by the initial coordination of calcium into the citrate. After larger 
amounts are added, the citrate is saturated with Ca2+ and the following cations contribute directly 
to the ionic conductivity of the bulk solution. The linearity observed for AuOBS and AuMOS 
solutions show the lack of bend Ca2+ with their capping agents.

The differences in pH in the different samples before and after the addition of electrolyte are related 
to fluctuations in the measurement and changes in the ionic strength rather than in the H+ 
concentrations. When determining the pH value, the pH meter uses the activity of protons, not 
their concentration, which depends on the activity coefficient (Equation 2.5). This coefficient, at 
the same time, depends on the ionic strength of the solution (Equation 2.6). Hence, temperature, 
ionic strength, ion charge and the density of the medium are all factors influencing the activity of 
the solution. When adding CaSO4 into the solution, the ionic strength of the solution increases 
exponentially with the charge of each ion (Equation 2.8), thereby increasing a factor of 16 for 
calcium sulfate. The addition of calcium sulfate will therefore result in a remarkable increase of 
the ionic strength, which in turn will cause the decrease of the activity coefficient and finally a 
decrease in the protons activity. Hence, the pH decrease a bit in solutions with high ionic strength 
(AuCit and AuMOS solutions)88. Since the ionic strength of the AuOBS solution is the lowest one 
among them (small addition of electrolyte), the tiny increase in the pH value is associated to 
fluctuations of the instrument.

4.5. Combined Destabilization 
Once the minimum amount of electrolyte required for destabilization has been found and tested 
for each particle, similar measurements with both the addition of the selected volume and with 
light irradiation were performed. For each AuNP type, spectra under irradiation with white light, 
as well as utilizing a 550 ± 20 nm and a 650 ± 20 nm band-pass filter were recorded over a duration 
of 120 min. 
Samples were irradiated during the addition of different volumes of electrolyte in order to probe 
the influence of light on the partially-destabilized particles. Broadband white light is provided by 
a high-power plasma light source, covering wavelengths from 350 nm to 800 nm. This range of 
frequencies are pretended to simulate the range of visible light from sunlight, thereby reproducing 
its effect on gold nanoparticles. The light source output spectrum will therefore supply this range 
of frequencies and the resulting spectra will be evaluated. Band-pass filters 550 nm and 650 nm 
will also be employed. These filters will transmit wavelengths of 550 and 650 ± 20 nm 
respectively, while rejecting energies higher and lower than this range. The narrowing down the 
wavelengths will supply energy in a very specific range (Figure 28), thereby allowing the study of 
the gold nanoparticles behavior under very specific conditions. The 550 nm band-pass filter will 
provide frequencies matching the spherical gold nanoparticles plasmon resonance and the 
subsequent effect on the morphology will be evaluated. The 650 nm, instead, will irradiate at 
wavelengths matching the characteristic plasmon resonance of larger gold nanoparticles networks. 
The three different irradiations (white light, 550 nm and 650 nm) will be tackled for each gold 
nanoparticle in order to study the presence of any possible dependence between the wavelengths 
of the illuminating light and the resulting network structure.

broadening of the peak at 520 nm. The images also show the presence of numerous isolated 
spherical AuCit particles, which matches the still present peak at the original LSPR wavelength.  
Figure X shows the extinction spectra of AuCit nanoparticles illuminated selectively at the LSPR 
wavelength of the isolated nanoparticles with a 550 ± 20 nm band-pass filter. The LSPR peak 
corresponding to the isolated AuCit nanoparticles decreases in intensity. In contrast to white light 
illumination, a peak appearance in the spectral region 600-900 nm can hardly be observed (Figure

length (Figure 29d and Figure C1, Appendix C). Although they are usually bi-dimensional, dark 
spots show the growth of this chained structures in three dimensions. The presence of an intuitive 
peak at the far-red region indicates the presence of well-shaped networks, while the still present 
peak at 520 nm implies the presence of AuCit spherical nanoparticles. The tiny red-shifting 
observed for this peak might relate to changes in the environment which affects the position of

spectra reveals the existence of several structures in solution, and/or chains of various lengths.

from the LSPR wavelengths of spatially isolated nanocrystals. The extinction spectra shows almost 
no change in the LSPR of AuCit and a smooth increase in the spectral region of 600-900 nm, most 
likely due to the coalescence of the particles into bigger aspherical particles (Figure 29e). Pictures 
from TEM corroborate this hypothesis (Figure 29f). The most significant result is obtained when 
comparing the spectra (e) with the one seen in the dark (Figure 26a). While the dark spectrum 
clearly shows the formation of aggregated networks, the illumination at 650 nm seems to block 
completely the aggregation of the particles.

different wavelengths, giving rise to three different morphologies. Irregular shaped structures and 
inter-particle coupling are found when irradiating with white light, whereas chain-like structures 
appear when illuminating at the plasmon resonance. Illuminating at higher wavelengths does not 
lead to a change in morphology.

indicate the anisotropic growth of the isolated AuOBS nanoparticles and high crystallinity.

of the AuOBS particles into a cluster-like aggregates. Irradiation at specifically 550 nm brings the 
particles together, giving rise to a red-shifted broad peak at longer wavelengths. Illuminating at 
further longer wavelengths (650 nm), in contrast, causes ordered aggregation of the particles highly 
faceted nanocrystals.

4.6. Nanoparticles behavior depending on the capping agent 
Throughout the previous sections, effect of both the addition of a divalent electrolyte and the 
irradiation with different wavelength ranges have been shown for each synthesized AuNP, as well 
as the effect of the electrolyte addition itself and the structure of the isolated nanoparticles. This 
sections deals with the comparison of the different capping agents employed and how those drive 
the corresponding effects under the presence of light and/or electrolyte. 
The pH values affect the binding energy between the functional groups and gold nanoparticles, as 
well as the electrostatic repulsion between the gold nanoparticles –i.e. electrostatic stability. 
Decreasing the pH value (increasing the H3O+ ion concentration) will lead to protonation of crucial 
functional groups on the capping agents, i.e. carboxylic acid and amine groups, and loss of 
stability. This is what is observed during addition of HCl to AuCit solution. However, the need for 
more protons for charge compensation in these samples have been reported.  
Addition of the displayed electrolytes (CaCl2, CuCl2 and CaSO4) also corroborates the dependence 
between ionic strength and the stability presented in the theoretical background. Addition of ions 
give rise to an increase in the ionic strength, thereby leading to a decrease in the Debye length and 
consequently in zeta potential (Equations 2.1 and 2.2). Copper (II) and calcium chloride led to 
overall similar observations when added to AuCit solution (Figure 22 and Figure 23 respectively). 
However, for the addition of similar amounts of electrolyte, the addition of copper (II) results in a 
formation of an intuitive peak at the far-red region, while Ca2+ addition leads to a levelling of the 
extinction spectrum and at significantly higher concentrations. The different spectral shapes 
suggest a different behavior in the colloidal environment between calcium and copper. When 
looking at the effect of the addition of both CuCl2 and CaCl2, a different spectral shape is observed 
for the same concentration of electrolyte added. This difference most likely relies on the stronger 
binding of copper to citrate. If the copper was binding more strongly than calcium, the Cu2+ cations 
with stick to the slipping plane of the particles, thereby screening the potential created by the Stern 
layer and reducing the electrostatic repulsion, i.e. instability is reached quicker. 
Addition of CaSO4, with both the anion and the cation being divalent, increases exponentially the 
ionic strength (Eq. 2.8). Nonetheless, addition of a salt containing a weakly coordinated inorganic 
anion (i.e. sulfate) also causes a decrease in the thickness of the electrical double layer, thus 
favoring aggregation of the nanoparticles86. The electrolyte volume was chosen according to 
observations of spectral changes during the systematic addition of this salt (Figure 25). The 
minimum amount of electrolyte added in order to induce destabilization was proportional to the 
electrostatic stability of the particles of the electrostatically stabilized AuNPs. Thus, AuCit 
required 3.5x larger amounts of CaSO4 compared to AuOBS that have a smaller zeta potential. 
The pH measurements were taken before and after the CaSO4 addition, showing a smooth decrease 
from 5.27 to 5.19 for AuCit nanoparticles. This decay in pH is mainly attributed to the repulsion 
between OH- present in solution and the sulfate anions added89. However, both effects are 
supported by the zeta potential values displayed in Table X: the surface zeta potential decreases 
almost 20 mV, arising from the addition of electrolyte that results in both a decrease in pH and an 
increase in ionic strength. AuMOS, which is not relying on electrostatic repulsion for stability, was 
==PAGE:66==

Page      57 
 
stable at much higher CaSO4 concentrations than both of the electrostatically stabilized AuNPs. 
The largest decrease in the total zeta potential value is found for citrate-stabilized nanoparticles 
owing to their three deprotonated functional groups leading to an importantly electrostatic 
stabilization. They are therefore more sensitive to fluctuations or changes in the ionic environment. 
AuOBS nanoparticles presented the smallest amount of salt needed to induce instability. Although 
they are stabilized by the same repulsion as AuCit nanoparticles, the amount of CaSO4 needed was 
minor than the one needed with citrate. The differences in stability between these two 
electrostatically stabilized particles relies on the net charge of the capping agent: Citrate has more 
charged groups than MES at the same pH, which therefore needs more ions in the slipping plane 
to screen the repulsion forces. AuMOS present the largest volume needed for destabilization, while 
their zeta potential values show a decrease of just 5 mV after the addition of salt. This slight 
decrease in the total potential despite the large volume added is attributed to the type of 
stabilization found in these particles. Their stability is based on the bulky starch ligand, leading to 
steric stabilization barely affected by their ionic environment. Therefore, addition of salt in 
AuMOS will induce an increase in the ionic strength of the solution but will scarcely affect the 
stability of the nanoparticles in it. Thus, the use of charged species in a solution produces changes 
in the charged NPs, and the strength of this effect depends on the capping agent involved. 
As depicted throughout the last section, illumination of electrolytic solutions with light at different 
spectral regions give rise to different networks. While the addition of salt without light irradiation 
induced a decrease in the SPR at 520 nm in all the samples, a broad peak at longer wavelengths 
only emerges for electrostatically stabilized nanoparticles, i.e. AuCit and AuOBS nanoparticles.  
When irradiating at the entire visible spectrum (white light), the three samples present different 
aggregation states, all of them different than when only the salt is present. To begin with, AuCit 
presents a pronounced broad peak at the 700 nm region when only CaSO4 is added, whereas with 
illumination the peak shows a blue-shifting as well as a decrease in its intensity as a result of the 
anisotropic aggregation of the particles. AuOBS with white illumination, in contrast, presented a 
very similar response compared to when only CaSO4 was added, presenting a less distinctive 
shoulder at 660 nm. Among the three samples, AuMOS presented the largest change when the 
particles where destabilized by light (besides electrolyte). The spectra in Figure 31c showed an 
intensity depletion in the plasmon resonance peak and a smooth extinction increase at longer 
wavelengths, suggesting an aggregated network corroborated by the TEM images. When these 
particles were destabilized only by electrolyte addition (Figure 26c), the variation at the far-red 
region was not observed. Thus, the aggregation into different shapes is fully attributed to the 
induced sintering of the particles by the electromagnetic radiation. Figure 32 displays the overall 
change in the extinction spectra of the three samples. Corroborating what has been stated earlier 
in this paragraph, AuMOS presents the largest difference between the dark and the light spectrum, 
showing a much larger increase at the far-red region under white light irradiation. AuMOS and 
AuCit present very similar behavior: the dark spectra has a larger influence on both the LSPR peak 
and on longer wavelengths, both showing a peak at 700 nm. Thus, it is confirmed again the 
similarities between the two electrostatically stabilized NPs as well as the difference between those 
and the sterically stabilized NP.

5. CONCLUSIONS 
5.1. Nanoparticles response to destabilization 
This project provides an overview of the differently stabilization types in three different 
synthesized AuNPs and their response to both electrostatic and electromagnetic destabilization. 
The mechanisms of aggregation of plasmonic networks is an area of intense interest due to their 
optical properties at the nanoscale, leading to a vast amount of groundbreaking applications in 
which they might be applied.   
The theory presented provides a basic outline of how particles form within solution and the 
different mechanisms leading to stability. Likewise, factors and phenomena affecting their 
instability have also been described and quantified. Parameters describing the basic properties of 
colloidal noble metal NPs have been explained, as well the most outstanding features of these 
studied nanoparticles: their optical properties. Instruments employed have been described in terms 
of the basic principles on which they are relying and by the method they are utilizing to carry out 
the measurements of the several parameters described beforehand. 
Aggregation strongly depends on the mechanisms by which NPs are stabilized. Three different 
nanoparticles have been synthesized; two of them stabilized by electrostatic repulsions (AuCit and 
AuOBS) and one of them sterically stabilized (AuMOS). Citrate and OBS syntheses provided 
charged capping agents (i.e. citrate and MES) to contribute to repulsion by Columbic forces, 
whereas MOS synthesis provided steric repulsion by the presence of a long and branched ligand, 
i.e. starch. The addition of two 1:2 electrolytes (CuCl2 and CaCl2) to an AuCit sample provided 
information about the response of citrate-stabilized NPs to the addition of electrolyte. Addition of 
a strong acid (HCl), instead, supplied data regarding the effect of protons increase to the 
nanoparticles aggregation. The effect of the addition of both electrolyte and acid were 
characterized by UV-Vis, showing a change in the extinction spectra as particles were aggregating. 
Since aggregation also depends on the particle size, particle diameter and size distribution were 
determined by both TEM and Zetasizer. Nevertheless, the actual particle diameter was taken from 
the TEM images owing to the poor reliability of the Zetasizer measurements, shown by the large 
standard deviations. TEM images showed an average particle diameter of 14 ± 3 nm for AuCit, 10 
± 2 nm for AuOBS and 9 ± 2 nm for AuMOS. Aggregation was further studied with the addition 
of a 2:2 electrolyte, i.e. CaSO4. Different amounts of a 5 mM CaSO4 solutions were added to three 
AuNPs as a result of the need for different amounts of chemical destabilization agent depending 
on the stabilization type. Therefore, AuMOS evidently required the biggest amount of calcium 
sulfate, followed by AuCit and lastly AuOBS. Zeta potential measurements were taking before 
and after the addition of CaSO4, as well as pH measurements. Surface zeta potential agreed with 
the stabilization type for each nanoparticle: AuCit presented the largest total zeta value indicating 
the strongly electrostatic stabilization of these nanoparticles, followed by a rather large negative 
value for AuOBS nanoparticles. AuMOS presented a small negative surface zeta potential, but 
with a standard deviation, which was actually larger than the value itself. The pH measurements 
==PAGE:71==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES 
 
Page      62 
 
showed a very slight variation after the addition of electrolyte, which was fully attributed to the 
increase in ionic strength. 
Once the electrostatic destabilization was studied, light-induced destabilization was examined in 
detail. The three NPs where irradiated under white, 550 nm and 650 nm light, and the 
corresponding spectral changes were supported by TEM images. By comparing the dark and the 
white spectra, AuMOS corroborated the small influence of the electrolyte addition by showing a 
very subtle variation in the dark and a large variation in the white spectrum. On the other hand, 
AuOBS presented an opposite effect by showing a larger response to electrolyte addition in the 
dark than under any light irradiation. This highlights the strong dependence of its stability on the 
ionic strength of the surrounding medium, but also indicates an unexpected effect of light. AuCit 
also corroborated the larger influence of the electrolyte, but presented the biggest change when 
irradiating at the LSPR peak wavelength (550 nm). Illumination at 550 nm resulted in an annulling 
effect of the electrolyte addition for AuMOS, whose 550 nm spectrum showed no change at all at 
the far-red region. This counterbalancing effect suggested a stabilizing secondary effect of light 
irradiation at this wavelength. Illuminating at longer wavelengths (i.e. 650 nm) resulted in an 
unwanted stabilizing effect for AuCit nanoparticles, while for AuOBS and AuMOS formation of 
well-defined polyhedral AuNPs was detected. Those triangular and hexagonal shapes suggested 
the focused effect of light towards those structures. However, while TEM images of AuOBS 
showed a clear aggregation of all the nanoparticles into those networks, AuMOS presented two 
sets of structures: these polyhedrons and the original isolated nanoparticles. 
Further analysis of these mechanisms will open the possibility to predict the effect of light in the 
aggregation networks, and to utilize electromagnetic waves to direct the aggregation of NPs 
towards specific structures. Nevertheless, the physics behind the behavior of nano-sized plasmonic 
structures and the chemistry linked to the formation mechanisms of these structures still has to be 
further developed in order to provide a fully controlled synthesis and utilization of this 
continuously investigated particles.

5.2. The next step 
Due to time restrictions, some experiments that would have contributed to a better understanding 
of the results showed throughout this project have not been carried out. This section presents 
several experiments that will be useful for the complementation of the results presented and/or 
new measurements that would lead to a new perspective of the different phenomena that are taking 
place. 
 It has been shown that AuOBS, and especially AuCit NPs, present a very different spectra 
when only electrolyte is added and when electrolyte is added and the solution is irradiated 
under 650 nm. It has been suggested that 650 nm wavelengths might have had an opposite 
effect, i.e. stabilization of the particles. Hence, it would be mandatory to explore different 
irradiation times and the subsequent recording of TEM samples to better study the 
supposed stabilizing effect and eventually corroborate if it is actually stabilizing the 
particles. It would also be necessary to record the spectrum during a certain time with both 
electrolyte and 650 nm irradiation, and then afterwards stopping the illumination to see if 
the particles will aggregate again or they will remain isolated. This trial will ultimately 
provide information about the hypothetical stabilizing effect of those wavelengths. 
 
 As showed in Figure 31f, AuMOS under 650 nm irradiation presented two different size 
of particles, i.e. large agglomerates with triangular and hexagonal shapes and the original 
isolated particles. No networks with a particle diameter in between those structures were 
observed. Therefore, the matter comes with the absence of intermediate structures. 
Prolonged irradiation at 650 nm and extraction of solution for TEM imaging at regular time 
intervals would help to determining if those structures result from the minimization of the 
surface energy or only as a result of irradiation during specifically 120 minutes. Longer 
irradiation times would contribute to figure out whether the remaining nanoparticles will 
eventually aggregate giving rise to the same networks or not. 
 
 Coating AuNPs with different compounds (e.g. Pt shells) could also be tried. Looking at 
how particles would give rise to different networks –or not- depending on the amount of 
electrolyte and the features of the illuminating light would also provide interesting data. 
Sintering of nanoparticles would be limited by the capping agent, even though an enough 
strong excitation could also cause the sintering of the coating compounds. When AuNPs 
are being coated, their optical properties change, so light stimulated coating might allow 
for selective shell growth on uncoated or coated particles. In any case, this experiment 
would provide a better understanding of the behavior of AuNPs under light irradiation. 
 
 Irradiation with 550 ± 20 nm and 650 ± 20 nm band-pass filters has been tested, but there 
are a great deal of different band-pass filters covering the range of interest for AuNPs 
(Figure 13). Irradiation of the same samples at different wavelengths would also generate 
valuable results. Irradiation at wavelengths shorter than 550 nm would entail the d-d 
transition region (Figure 15) and could cause the occurrence of different spectral 
==PAGE:73==
LIGHT-DRIVEN EVOLUTION OF GOLD NANOPARTICLE MORPHOLOGY AND PROPERTIES 
 
Page      64 
 
modification. Irradiation at 600 nm (i.e. the point in between) would demonstrate whether 
the showed effect corresponds to a combination of both wavelengths or not. Longer 
wavelengths would correspond to illuminating away from the LSPR peak, also providing 
a new set of evaluable data. All in all, irradiation at different and several parts of the 
spectrum will surely complement the results extracted from this work. 
 
 Stabilization by both electrostatic and steric repulsion have been tested in this project. 
Nevertheless, it is still unclear the effect of the structural difference among the different 
electrostatic capping agents, i.e. citrate and MES. Therefore, the same experiments with 
several capping agents will provide information about the correlation between the chemical 
structure and the destabilization achieved. Capping with polymers of difference lengths 
may also give rise to a more defined relationship between the particle diameter and the 
strength of the stabilization.
