Abstract
Floating oﬀshore wind energy is one of the most attractive and eﬃcient method of energy production
available for the near future. Therefore, the need for ﬂoating wind turbines to extract as much energy
as the site’s potential can produce is crucial. One of the most challenging issues in ﬂoating platforms is
the rotor’s misalignment to the wind direction due to ﬂoater being subject to wave and current loads.
This thesis presents the implementation of an individual pitch controller (IPC) on the X1Wind X140
Platform, which aims to reduce the yaw misalignment of the platform with the wind direction. The
designed controller is evaluated through HAWC2 simulations in terms of yaw misalignment correction.
The platform is subject to various conditions. The examined cases include aligned wind and wave condi-
tions, normal and extreme turbulent wind with irregular waves and lastly the eﬀect of current loads on
the platform. Furthermore, an analysis on the equivalent loads of the out-of-plane blade-root bending
moment is performed and the results are compared to the baseline. Finally, the blade pitch activity is
investigated, as well as the annual energy production.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The wind energy sector is one of the the key contributors for several countries to meet the Paris Agreement
[1], in order to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions. The wind energy industry has shown a tremendous
increase in installed capacity in Europe, reaching a cumulative capacity of 15.4 GW in 2019, which covered
15% of the total energy demand of the EU countries on that year, according to Wind Europe [2]. Figure
1.1-1 illustrates the European share of electricity generation by source for 2019.
Figure 1.1-1
|
European share of electricity generation by source in 2019 (Source: EnAppSys.com)
According to the same source, the oﬀshore installations ratio over the total wind energy annual instal-
lations is slightly increased from 2018 to 2019, due to oﬀshore wind market being more attractive than
onshore. This fact indicates that the trend in the wind industry is going to turn to installing more oﬀ-
shore wind farms, in which implications related to visual impact and permissions are far less important.
Furthermore, in 2019, a record of 3.6 GW of oﬀshore installed capacity has been achieved in Europe.
However, the most developed oﬀshore foundation type are the bottom ﬁxed wind turbines, which can
only be installed on shores with a maximum depth of 50 meters due to structural limitations and thus the
possible areas of wind turbine installations are limited. It is worth mentioning that the most exploited
oﬀshore area is the North Sea which has the ideal conditions (water depth, distance form shore, etc.).
1
==PAGE:10==
In 2019, the only oﬀshore installations happened in the North Sea for the previously mentioned reasons.
As a result, lately, the wind industry has started focusing on developing ﬂoating platforms which will
allow the installation of wind farms in deeper waters than bottom ﬁxed foundations and therefore relieve
the limit of how far from shore a wind farm can be installed (USA, China, Mediterranean Sea, etc.).
Furthermore, the wind conditions far from shore are stronger and more stable since there is no roughness
on the surrounding area and no disturbances on the wind ﬂow occur due to nearby land.
The ﬂoating oﬀshore structures are categorized based on how the stability of the whole structure is
achieved. Figure 1.1-2 presents the four main ﬂoating platforms.
Figure 1.1-2
|
Floating Oﬀshore Wind design concepts (Adopted by [3])
The ﬁrst type illustrated is called the Barge and consists of a platform anchored to the seabed by catenary
lines. The platform stability is based on the inertia of buoyancy and its main advantage is the low draft.
However, it is more likely to be exposed to higher wave loading and thus increased motion [4].
The next type of platform shown is called the semi-submersible (or Semi-Sub) which is, like the Barge,
also stabilized based on the inertia of buoyancy. Moreover, it is also anchored to the seabed via catenary
mooring lines. The advantages of this platform is the ﬂexibility to operate in shallow water depth, as
well as the beneﬁts regarding assembly and repairing process[5].
The third type is called the SPAR and consists of an elongated buoy, which is anchored via catenary
lines to the seabed with a ballast at its lower part, which lowers the center of gravity and thus makes
it more stable. The main advantage of the ﬂoater is the simple structure and that it can be fabricated
relatively easily compared to the other ﬂoater. However, the large draft requirement can create logistical
challenges and can constrain deployment to water depths of higher than 100 meters[5].
The last type is called the tension leg platform (or TLP) and consists of a platform anchored to the
seabed via tensioned mooring lines which stabilize the whole structure. This fact allows the structure to
have shallow draft and be lighter. In contrast, the use of this design increases the stresses on the tendon
and anchor system and thus increases the risk of failure on the moorings [4].
1.2
Motivation
One of the most challenging issues regarding the ﬂoating wind turbines in contrast to bottom ﬁxed oﬀshore
wind turbines or onshore wind turbines, is that the base of the structure is constantly in motion and its
positioning is mostly inﬂuenced by the wave forces and the currents. According to Barth [6], the wind
and wave directions appear to have a larger misalignment with higher wind speeds. The same study notes
that a misalignment of 30o is considered common, while 60o of misalignment has a very low probability
of occurrence and concludes that the yaw misalignment error results not only in a decrease in energy
2
==PAGE:11==
production, but also in an increased power ﬂuctuation. For a wind wave misalignment of 30o, which
is common as mentioned above, another study by Liew et al. [7] has concluded that in such condition
the power production deﬁcit can reach a value of up to more than 20%. Given the fact that oﬀshore
wind turbines are installed with generator capacity much greater than onshore, this deﬁcit becomes even
greater in absolute terms. Furthermore, the structural loads are aﬀected heavily when the wind wave
misalignment exists, in terms of increased roll and sway moments due to the change of the structure’s
motion.
The most common mitigation for this challenge is the implementation of an active yaw system, which
is installed on the connection between the tower and the nacelle. There are two main types of bearing
systems that support and connect the nacelle to the tower. First, there is the roller yaw bearing, upon
which the nacelle is mounted and the the azimuth rotation is achieved either via a plurality of powerful
electric drives or via a system of hydraulic drives, which are also responsible for the braking of the system.
The other type of bearing used for active yaw system is called the gliding yaw bearing. The azimuth
rotation is achieved once more via the use of either electric or hydraulic motors, however the nacelle is
mounted on a friction based gliding bearing, which neglects the need for a braking system, leading to
signiﬁcant cost savings.
In order for the oﬀshore ﬂoating wind turbines to be more attractive, the cost needs to be decreased
further, compared to the onshore and the ﬁxed foundation oﬀshore wind turbines. Therefore, this paper
suggests the implementation of individual pitch control (IPC) for yaw misalignment correction with no
further active yaw system installed. Since the blade pitch controller is already installed in every wind
turbine produced nowadays, an additional individual blade signal can be included to the collective blade
pitch so that, if designed correctly, the rotor could align itself perpendicular to the wind direction during
the operation of the wind turbine, thus extracting the optimal energy out of it. The IPC implementa-
tion has been studied mostly for blade load reduction and there are only a few studies regarding yaw
misalignment correction. One is the few studies in which the IPC has been used for yaw misalignment
correction, was carried out by Navalkar, van Wingerden and van Kuik [9]. In the aforementioned study,
the IPC strategy has successfully reduced the yaw misalignment, while still reduced the blade loads.
1.3
Report Outline
The goal of this project is to study the implementation of the individual pitch controller on the X1Wind
oﬀshore ﬂoating platform for yaw misalignment purposes. The report is structured as follows:
Chapter 2: Simulation Setup
This chapter gives an insight on how the high accuracy simulations are performed and how the IPC is
implemented via the HAWC2-Python interface. It further deﬁnes the wind turbine and ﬂoating platform.
Finally, the key parameters of the simulations are presented.
Chapter 3: Theoretical background
This chapter provides the theoretical background needed for the sake of the project. First of all, a relation
between wind speed and yawing moment is outlined. Furthermore, the PID controller theory is presented,
followed by the IPC principles that are necessary for this project. Finally, the controller properties are
presented.
Chapter 4: Yaw Misalignment Correction
This section outlines the design process of the IPC with the objective of the wind turbine passively

3.1
PID control theory
Feedback control is a control mechanism that uses information from measurements. In a feedback con-
trol system, the output is sensed. There are two main types of feedback control systems: 1) positive
feedback 2) negative feedback. The negative feedback control systems are used for error rejection and
such a system will be designed for this project. PID controllers are commonly used in feedback control
of industrial processes and still remain widely used for its simplicity. The PID controller can be un-
derstood as a controller that takes the present, the past, and the future of the error into account[22].
A proportional–integral–derivative controller (PID controller) is a method of the control loop feedback.
This method is composed of three controllers:
• Proportional Controller
• Integral Controller
• Derivative Controller
The role of a proportional depends on the present error, while the integral depends on the accumulation
of past error and the derivative on the prediction of future error. The error (e(t)) of a signal is deﬁned
as the comparison between a desired set-point and the measurement of the signal. The weighted sum of
these three actions is used to adjust the output of the controller. The respective controller components
are explained thoroughly below.
3.1.1
Proportional controller
Proportional control is a simple and widely used method of control and it depends on the present error.
In a proportional controller, steady state error tends to depend inversely upon the proportional gain. The
proportional response can be adjusted by multiplying the error by a constant kp, called the proportional
gain. The proportional term is given by the following expression:
P = kpe(t)
(3.1)
By observing this equation, one can note that the higher the proportional gain, the larger the change of
the proportional term P will be for a given change of error. The proportional gain needs to be chosen
carefully. More speciﬁcally, if the proportional gain is very high, some systems might become unstable,
while if the proportional gain is too low, the control action will be too small when responding to system
11
==PAGE:20==
disturbances. Consequently, a proportional controller (kp) will have the eﬀect of reducing the rise time
and will reduce, but never eliminate, the steady-state error.
3.1.2
Integral controller
The Integral controller is proportional to both the magnitude of the error and the duration of the error.
The integral in a PID controller is the sum of the instantaneous error over time and gives the accumulated
oﬀset that should have been corrected previously. Consequently, an integral control will have the eﬀect
of eliminating the steady-state error, but it may make the transient response worse. The integral term is
given by the following expression:

reducing the overshoot, and improving the transient response. However, the derivative gain (kd) should

The scope of the IPC implementation for correction the yaw misalignment of a wind turbine is to adjust
each blade pitch angle to a certain position based on the azimuth angle of each blade in order to create
a moment around the vertical axis of the tower. By doing so, if the nacelle is allowed to move freely
on the assembly point with the tower top, a rotational motion can be performed and the yaw error can
be reduced. Therefore, a relationship between the wind direction yaw angle and the yawing moment is
established.

investigated through experimental tests and it is assumed as the translation of the thrust force applied

Controller algorithm
This section presents the individual pitch control algorithm. The algorithm is executed on each time step

Collect measurements
In each of the simulation time steps, the algorithm collects measurements such as the blade azimuth

PID Controller tuning process
The tuning process of the controller is an iterative process that needs testing in order to get the optimal
set of controller gains. There are a few heuristic tuning methods in literature such as the Ziegler-Nichols
method, the Chien–Hrones–Reswick method and the Cohen–Coon method [26]. However, these methods
do not apply in such systems with such massive inertia, therefore the tuning needs to be performed
manually.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Recommendations
In this study, the implementation of the IPC for yaw alignment correction on the X1Wind X140 platform
was investigated. The conclusions derived from the results of this study are summarized and future work
is suggested.
6.1
Conclusion
The IEA 15-MW Reference Wind turbine model was mounted on top of the X1Wind X140 platform and
the IPC was implemented on it. The motivation of doing so was for the platform to align with the wind
direction, so that the electrical power output was maximized.
The IPC takes as input the horizontal wind speed components, calculates the yaw angle oﬀset and is
constantly pitching the blades in a way that a yawing moment around the PivotBuoy is produced so that
the platform aligns with the wind direction. The IPC comprises of a PI controller, of which the design
process was thoroughly described. Two sets of controller gains are selected, one conservatively designed
with lower proportional gain and one more aggressively designed with increased proportional gain, for
further analysis in order to derive the advantages and disadvantages of each strategy.
First, the ﬂoater normal operation is analyzed under steady wind and wave conditions. The yaw oﬀset
of the platform is recorded and a relation between the yaw oﬀset and the thrust force is derived, along
with the eﬀect of the generator torque on the platform motion.
The controller performance is analyzed through HAWC2 simulations under various metocean conditions.
These conditions include, aligned steady wind and regular waves, steady conditions with wind direction
change, aligned turbulent wind and irregular waves, aligned turbulent wind and irregular waves with 90°
misaligned currents and lastly, aligned extreme turbulent wind and irregular waves. The metocean data
are taken from measurements in Begur, Spain.
The performance analysis comprised of comparison between platform yaw angle oﬀset in terms of mean
and standard deviation, blade pitch activity, fatigue loads in terms of short term damage equivalent
ﬂapwise blade-root bending moments and the respective lifetime fatigue load and lastly electrical power
output and annual energy production.
The steady state analysis has provided results about the electrical power output increase in the region
below rated wind speed when the IPC is implemented in both aligned wind and wave case and with a
direction change during simulation. Furthermore, it has shown that the platform alignment is a faster
process when the IPC is implemented.
The aligned turbulent wind and irregular wave analysis resulted in platform yaw angle oﬀset correction
when the IPC is implemented and shown that the aggressively designed controller reduces the standard
deviation of the yaw angle, thus making the platform more stable. It is also shown that the lifetime
fatigue loads are reduced when using the conservative design by 6.2%, while there is an increase by 4.27%
for the aggressive design. However, since the platform position is not the same for both simulations, the
mean ﬂapwise blade-root bending moments were analyzed, providing similar loads for the majority of
the wind spectrum besides the low wind speed region in which the IPC cases provide lower mean loads.
The blade pitch rate is also investigated, showing that it is well within the acceptable range. Finally,
the electrical power output is slightly improved when the IPC is implemented, resulting in an increase of
41

0.61% for the conservative controller and 0.82% for the aggressive one.
The aligned turbulent wind and irregular wave with misaligned currents analysis has made the importance
of the thrust force, for aligning purposes when either the waves or the currents are misaligned with the
wind, observable. The need for alignment correction is higher on wind speed ranges that produces less
thrust force either due to the wind being too low or due to the collective pitching of the blades. The
platform yaw angle oﬀset is corrected signiﬁcantly compared to the case with no IPC. The blade pitch
activity is increased when the thrust force is low, due to the need for the blades to pitch more in order
to produce a larger yawing moment to counteract for the moment produced by the current loads. This
increase in pitch activity leads to increased fatigue loads indicated by the lifetime equivalent fatigue load
increase of 7.69% for controller 1 and 11.22$ for controller 2. Finally, the electrical power output shows
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence. The AEP is decreased slightly (0.52%) for the conservative controller, while it
merely increases by 0.06% for the aggressive one.
Finally, the IPC is tested under extreme turbulent wind with aligned irregular waves. The yaw oﬀset
is corrected once again, at the cost of higher pitch activity, since the extreme turbulent wind results in
a noisier signal for the wind speed. As a consequence, the fatigue loads are massively increased. The
lifetime equivalent load is increased by 11.67% for the conservative controller and by 25.83% for the
aggressive controller.
6.2
Future work
Platform fatigue analysis
This project does not cover the eﬀect of the IPC implementation on fatigue on the structural components
of the platform. The fatigue on the point of connection between the nacelle and the main mast would be
of big interest, as well as an analysis on the fatigue on the bearing of the PivotBuoy.
Pitch bearing fatigue analysis
The IPC implementation has shown to increase the blade pitch activity.
Therefore, a more detailed
investigation is needed to quantify exactly how big of an impact the IPC implementation has on the
pitch bearing fatigue.
Gain scheduling
The yaw oﬀset is not fully diminished in a single set of controller gains in this analysis, thus indicating
potential need of gain scheduling.
Diﬀerent controller approach
There are various controller approaches used in IPC implementation other than the PID approach. The
single-input single-output approach could be also investigated for this implementation, in order to examine
whether that approach is more eﬀective.
