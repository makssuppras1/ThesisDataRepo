Abstract
Classical methods of parameter estimation usually imply the minimization of
an objective function, that measures the error between some observations and
the results obtained by a numerical model. In the presence of uncertainties, the
minimization is not straightforward, as the output of the model depends on those
uncontrolled inputs and on the control parameter as well. That is why we will
aim at minimizing the objective function, to get an estimation of the control
parameter that is robust to the uncertainties.
In this thesis, a toy model of a coastal area has been modelled and imple-
mented. The control parameter in this study is the bottom friction, upon which
classical methods of estimation will be applied in a simulation-reestimation exper-
iment. The model will then be modified to include uncertainties on the boundary
conditions in order to apply robust control methods. After a sensitivity analysis
of the objective function to assess the influence of each considered variable, we
will define different concepts of robustness, and introduce some algorithms that
rely on surrogate models and black-box optimization techniques to solve this
estimation problem.

Context
Nowadays, numerical simulations are necessary in order to have a better under-
standing of what is happening and to predict natural events. However, these
models are often incomplete, as there exist complex and small scale phenomena
that cannot be represented fully. To bypass this issue, parametrized correction
terms are usually added but they need to be properly estimated. A case in point
is the estimation of the bottom friction of the ocean bed. Indeed, bottom friction
affects the general circulation, especially in shallow waters. Direct observation is
usually not possible because of the spatial disparity of this parameter, so the only
observations available come from the movement of the water surface. In addition
to that, uncertainties are present on some other characteristics that influence
the circulation as well. Several techniques, such as data assimilation, perform
reasonably well without uncertainties, as in Das and Lardner, 1991, but they can
introduce large errors in the estimate when uncertainties are present.
There is then a need to find robust estimates of the wanted parameters, which
will perform well under uncertainties.
Scope of the project
In this project, we are interested in the modelling of a coastal area. More precisely,
we wish to estimate the friction parameter induced by the ocean bed, based
on observations of the height of the water. Some techniques, derived from the
optimal control theory, allows for determining the parameter by minimizing an
objective function. However, in this study, we will introduce some uncertainties
in the boundary conditions, and extend this approach to take them into account
using what is called robust control. This concept of robustness can take different
forms which will raise different formulations of the problem. We will review some
of the methods that lead to “robust” estimates of the bottom friction.
This master thesis will first consist in implementing a toy model of the shal-
low water equations with variable bottom friction, and apply classical estimation
techniques on it in chapter 2. Afterwards, we will define and add some uncertain-
ties to the model: the environmental variables. In order to measure the impact of
each of the variables, we will perform a sensitivity analysis, that will lead to the

2.1
The shallow water equations
2.1.1
General form
The shallow water equations, also known as Saint-Venant equations, are a set
of hyperbolic partial differential equations. These equations result from depth-
averaging the Navier-Stokes equations, when we consider the horizontal carac-
teristic length to be way greater than the vertical caracteristic length.
More
precisions about the mathematical derivation can be found in Szydlarski and
Simeoni, 2007. These equations are easier to implement and permit the study of
rivers, lakes, or ocean with great accuracy. We are interested in the 1-dimensional
version of these equations. In its conservative form, the equations describe the
evolution of the following conservative variables:
• h(x, t): the height of the water above the ground at the point x and at the
time t
• q(x, t) = u(x, t) · h(x, t): the discharge at the point x and at the time t.
u(x, t) is thus the horizontal water velocity.
The gravitation constant will be written g = 9.81 m/s2, and the bottom topogra-
phy Z(x). The height of the free surface is then H(x, t) = h(x, t) + Z(x). These
notations are depicted on Figure 2.1.

2.4
Adjoint method
In the subsection 2.3.2, the brute optimization procedure showed its limits. In-
deed, the program has to minimize the sum of squares over Nx parameters, as
the bottom friction is discretized on the spatial domain. Furthermore, the com-
putation of the sum of squares itself is usually time-expensive in computational
fluid dynamics (CFD). A way to speed up the optimization is to use the deriva-
tive (gradient in the multidimensional case) with respect to K, and use specific
methods involving this gradient, such as gradient-descent. The general principle
is the following. At the step l, we dispose of Kl. The next value Kl+1 is selected
in the direction of the negative gradient of j at Kl:
Kl+1 = Kl −βl∇j(Kl)
(2.17)
and the choice of βl depends on the method of optimization. One can consult
Nocedal and Wright, 2006 for more precision on these methods.
Usually, computing the gradient in a direct way is very expensive: the easiest
way is to proceed by finite differences but this raises some issues. For a number
p of variables, computing an approximation of the gradient will at least require p
simulations of the model. Another issue is the choice of the step-size of the finite
difference. A too large one will cause a low accuracy, and a too small one will
lead to numerical errors, due to the limited precision of the machines.
An alternative way to compute the gradient of the cost function is to use the
adjoint method. The principal advantage is that this method is independent of
the number of input parameters. The general framework is the following: the
tangent linear model is written, by taking the appropriate directional derivatives,
and then, this linear model is transposed in the sense of a well-chosen inner
product. To do so, there are three different starting points:
• Derive the adjoint model from the continuous equations, discretize the
model, and implement it
• Derive the adjoint model from the discretized equations, and implement it
• Derive the adjoint model directly from the computer code
As suggested in Blayo and Nodet, 2012, it makes more sense to consider to
work from the discretized equations, or from the computer code. Indeed, the
accuracy of the first approach is dependent on the discretization performed af-
ter the derivation of the adjoint model. Moreover, in the second and third ap-
proaches, the gradient computed will be effectively the gradient from the output
of the computer code. The third approach can be performed using an Automatic

3.1
Design of experiments
Design of experiments (often abbreviated as DoE) is the method of choosing the
inputs to be passed to a given model. These methods are often used in order to
have good space-filling properties, see for example Gilquin, 2016, and to have a
better rate of convergence of the estimators based on these designs. In this thesis,
experimental designs are used to ensure better estimates than the ones given by
pure random methods.

CHAPTER 4
Metamodeling
As seen in chapter 3, a sensitivity analysis can be used to reduce the number of
inputs in the model, by discarding those which are not relevant to the modeling.
That means also that the input space to explore for another analysis is reduced,
and in a context where a single run of the model can be very costly, this can be
a great improvement.
Another way to reduce the number of simulations is to create a metamodel.
This framework leads to the introduction of functions, cheap to evaluate, that
mimic as close as possible the behaviour of the original model. The construction
of such metamodels requires however the evaluation of an initial design of experi-
ment by the original function. This design (or training set) can have a particular
“rigid” structure, such as sparse grids, or be more random, such as LHS.
Reducing the computation time is not the only objective of this part. Indeed,
black-box optimization techniques make an extensive use of surrogate models, as
they allow to define potentially interesting regions of the input space, where the
objective function may be close to a minimum. This will be further discussed in
chapter 5.
In this chapter, we will focus more closely on two techniques: polynomial
chaos, and kriging. The first method consists in the decomposition of the output
of the function on a basis of orthogonal polynomials, and the other one is based
on Gaussian process regression.

4.2
Kriging and its extensions
Kriging has been named after Krige, who has introduced this method in Krige,
1951. A more recent formulation has been introduced in Matheron, 1962, 1969.
Kriging is a statistical method of construction of a response surface, that permits
to estimate the value of the function at unknown points with a relative precision.
Historically, it has first been applied on geostatistical problems. The core of krig-
ing is the assumption that the function to model is the realization of a gaussian
process (GP), whose covariance values depends only on the distance between two
points of evaluation. Such a probabilistic model have the advantage to provide
confidence intervals on the predicted values.

Robust Optimization
In a fully deterministic case, we have seen that methods using adjoint-based
gradient are really effective, even in high dimensional cases. However when some
uncertainties are included, the procedure is way less precise. We saw then in
chapter 3 how to deal with uncertainties by constructing a surrogate model, that
provide different informations about the original function.
In this part, we will present a non-exhaustive list of different strategies to
get a robust estimation of the control parameter K ∈K, after having defined the
concept of robustness in an optimization problem. A simple example is presented
next.

We call xc the control variable, and Xe a random variable,
modelling an environmental variable with a given probability distribution. The
function we aim at minimizing is (xe, xc) 7→f(xe, xc) = ˜f(xe + xc), in a “robust”
sense.

the environmental variable Xe has a support of [−3, 3], and follows a truncated

Instead of trying to minimize a single quantity, one can aim at minimizing a pair
of objectives in the Pareto sense. In this thesis, we will focus on the minimization
of the objective vector consisting in the mean of the response, and its variance.
These quantities have to be estimated, but as it is often the case, an exhaustive
computation of all the combinations of control variable K and environmental
variables xe is impossible.
These methods are not applications of adaptative
sampling, as they do not aim at finding a single value of K, but a whole range
of Pareto-optimal K. The algorithms in this section are presented in Dellino,

The methods presented above do not use the information brought by the adjoint
code, to say the gradient of the response with respect to K.

cost function, and the ones of its gradient.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Discussion
In this thesis, we went through all the process of the modelling of a coastal area.
First, we discussed the implementation of the shallow water equations, and
then used the adjoint method to get the gradient with respect to the control
variable K. This method, used along steepest descent algorithms, is efficient but
seems limited when uncertainties are included. Aiming at obtaining an estimate
which is valid in a lot of scenarii is part of the framework of robust estimation.
But as there are several definitions for robustness, that are usually based on
statistical moments of the random objective function, there are also different
methods that will yield different estimates.
One of the biggest limitations in these sorts of problems is the cost of a
simulation (time, computer resources). Because of that, the number of model
runs is usually restricted. This issue can be circumvented by the use of surrogate
models, whose precision is directly linked to the number of initial runs needed
to construct them. There is then a balance to be found between the number of
computations and the expected precision of the model.
Due to the limited number of runs, all prior knowledge such as a quadratic
trend or the gradient can be considered as a valuable input in the modelling.
Perspectives and future work
This work raises some issues that are to be further studied. First of all, the model
used is a one-dimensional toy-model, that does not represent a real physical
situation as the values of the parameters were set arbitrarily.
Moreover, for
an easier derivation of the adjoint code, a simple scheme has been used which
suffers from low accuracy. In the future, this model can be modified to have two
dimensions, and to be able to take care of dry bottoms (allow zero value for the
water height).
==PAGE:82==
74
6 Conclusion
Also, in most of this thesis, the bottom friction has been assumed constant
on all the spatial domain.
This is in practice not valid and extensions to a
multidimensional case of the presented algorithms are usually not trivial.
Finally, to move on to a more realistic case, the nature of the observations Y
have to be discussed. In this work, the observation operator H provides all the
values of the sea-surface height. We can then restrict the availability of those
observations, for instance by observing only the water height of a few points, at
specific time steps, considering that in real-life, sea-surface height is usually not
measured everywhere. Once this issue is addressed, a future work should then be
able to estimate parameters in a robust way using real datasets instead of staying
in the simulation-reestimation framework.
