Abstract
Rod seal contact is the single largest contributor to mechanical losses in reciproc-
ating hydraulic applications. This work focuses on spring-energized seals made of
Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) and presents a state of the art numerical model
that predicts frictional loss, pressure distribution, deflections, and film thickness
developed in the contact. The entire model was developed in a commercial finite
element software where the presented solutions were obtained using a direct iteration
scheme where the aforementioned elements are solved simultaneously.
Surface topography was analysed, in order to reveal important surface charac-
teristics that were used as inputs to the Greenwood and Williamson model for the
contact mechanics analysis. Due to the non-linear material properties of PTFE,
tensile tests were conducted to derive a stress-strain curve to accurately simulate
the seal deformation in the numerical model. The friction force caused by seals in
reciprocating hydraulic applications was then experimentally investigated at various
rod velocities and pressures. Though the thesis focused on one specific compound
of PTFE, more compounds were tested.
The main findings in this work showed through comparative analysis, that ad-
ditional measures are needed to improve the prediction of the frictional loss. An
improvement to the numerical model is thereafter proposed that consist of dynam-
ically modelling the friction coeﬀicient, based on running conditions, in the asperity
contact. Though promising effects of this improvement is presented in this thesis,
additional experimental work is, however, required to fully construct it.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The topic of this thesis is spring-energized u-cup seals for hydraulic cylinders. This
chapter introduces reciprocating rod seals and a brief description of their purpose.
Next follows the motivation behind this research and thereafter the objectives and
the overall goals are outlined.
Lastly, the chapter concludes with the review of
relevant literature for the topic of seals.
1.1
Reciprocating Rod Seals
Piston rod seals come in different sizes and shapes, all depending on the application.
This thesis focuses on seals used in reciprocating hydraulic cylinders and in particu-
lar, the spring-energized u-cup from the company A/S Gunnar Haagensen (GH). A
schematically represented section view of a typically constructed hydraulic cylinder
is illustrated in Figure 1.1. The same figure also illustrates close-up of the seal and
it’s location in the cylinder. The seal is located in a groove in the cylinder housing
and as the name indicates, the seal’s primary function is to retain the fluid inside
the cylinder and prevent leakage [19]. They considered as one of the most critical
elements in hydraulic systems [44].
A u-cup seal has two lips, an inner- and outer lip, also referred to as static-
and dynamic lip, respectively. The contact between the housing and outer lip is
considered as static, since the seal is sitting more or less confined in the cylinder
groove. The contact between the inner lip and rod is, on the other hand, dynamic as
the naming suggests. The pressure groove on the u-cup is what makes the seal a great
design. With increasing pressure the lips are pressed further against the contacting
surfaces and thereby increase the contact area and provide a better sealing. The
main function of the metal spring is to generate initial contact pressure. This ensures
the seal is functional if the pressure in the cylinder is low.

sophisticated and detailed methods available in the literature, such as numerical
analysis. Therefore this thesis explores the possibilities of application of numerical

1.2
Motivation
This thesis is based on a project developed by the author in collaboration with GH.
One of the leading producers in customized sealing solutions in Denmark, GH has
for some time been interested in optimising their seals and developing new methods
of testing and designing their products. Last year a MSc thesis was written and
defended by Tim Hantusch [17]. The thesis consisted of developing a numerical
model that was based on both inverse hydrodynamic lubrication theory and mixed-
lubrication. Additionally, an experimental test rig was constructed to measure the
friction force generated from rod seals to validate the numerical model. There was,
however, a mismatch between the experimental- and numerical results and according
to Hantusch it might be due to possible errors in both the numerical model and the
experimental evaluation.
After initial consultation with the technical department of GH, it became evident
that the company was interested in closing the gap between the experimental- and
numerical results. As a result, this thesis proposes another numerical model, based
on the direct-method.
1.3
Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to develop a numerical model that can predict
the friction force generated by the seal by taking into account pressures, external
loads, seal material, fluid properties, and surface topography in a high-fidelity model
framework for spring-energized u-cup seals. Furthermore, the results of the simula-
tions based on the designed model will be validated by experimental investigations.
The work presented here can be further broken down into separate sub-objectives:
1. Setup a numerical model with the use of a Finite Element Analysis (FEA) for
spring-energiced u-cup seals.
(a) Utilize a direct iteration scheme to solve the Reynolds equation and seal
deformation simultaneously.
(b) Conduct tensile tests of the seal material to derive a stress-strain curve
and implement it in the FEA model.
(c) Implement a contact model based on surface topology.
2. Conduct experimental investigations of spring-energiced u-cup seals to evalu-
ate the friction force.
3. Validate the numerical model with the experimental investigations.

1.4
State of the Art
Tribology is not a new research field. The term tribology, originally derived from
the Greek word tribos which means rubbing [3], was first used by Peter Jost [8] in
a report (often called the Just report) in 1966. Even though the terminology was
first introduced 55 years ago, the subject matter of tribology has been an interest
for scientists for a long time. Frictional awareness dates back at least as far as the
ancient Egypt period, where they used lubricated sledges to transport colossi [14].
By the time the Just report was released, two different types of lubrication re-
gimes were already identified, namely hydrodynamic lubrication (HL) and boundary
lubrication (BL). The discovery of HL started with experiments conducted by Tower
[41] with brass journal bearings in 1883. In 1886, Reynolds [30] puplished an ana-
lytical paper that followed up on Tower’s work and provided an explanation for the
HL. Reynolds used a reduced form of the Navier-Stokes equation along with the
continuity equation to develop a second-order differential equation for the pressure
in a narrow converging gap between two surfaces [16]. This equation is now com-
monly known as the Reynolds equation. Though Reynolds equation showed a good
agreement with Tower’s experiments, later research suggested that there was more
to it. As Jacobson describes [22], a publication by H.M. Martin predicted a film
thickness between gear teeth that was smaller than the surface roughness and would
not work without wear.
Elastohydrodynamic lubrication (EHL) is a rather recent development and was
only introduced a few decades ago [33]. The work of Ertel [9] introduced a break-
through by taking into account elastic deformation of the contacting solids and
adjusting the viscosity variation of the lubricant with the pressure increase. This
was analyzed for the inlet zone of lubricated non-conformal contacts. EHL is quite
dependant on the running conditions. Too high loads and and/or too slow run-
ning speed causes the surface asperities to penetrate the lubricant, causing a mixed
lubrication conditions [31].
Before the introduction of personal computers, existing EHL methods consisted
of using analytical calculations for simple shapes. Dowson and Higginson [7] de-
veloped a numerical solution to the elastohydrodynamic problem in 1959, called the
inverse method. This solution consisted of computing the fluid film from the in-
verse solution of the Reynolds equation by assuming that the pressure distribution

As Finite Element Methods (FEM) emerged, work of Ruskell [32] applied the dir-

computes the hydrodynamic pressure distribution and film thickness simultaneously
while still taking elastic deformation of the seal into account. This generated a huge
impact within the field of tribology as more complex structures could be analyzed.
When simulating rough surface contact mechanics, the most used model is a
one developed by Greenwood and Williams [13]. They considered an elastic rough
surface to be nominally flat and in contact with a perfectly smooth surface. The
rough surface is statistically constructed by assuming that all asperities have the
same radii and the asperity height varies with a statistically defined distribution.
With these simplifications, it is possible to use Hertzian theory to calculate the
elastic displacement.
The complexity of tribological analysis for seals has been increasingly growing
where modern models require relatively large computational power. One of the more
advanced models is by Yand and Salant [44] that uses a coupled fluid mechanics,
contact mechanics, deformation, and thermal analyses. In their paper, a hyperelastic
material model is used for the seal and the performance of both o-ring and u-cup
seals are compared with each other. The u-cup is, however, not spring energized.

2.1
Lubrication
While in some cases, for example in interference fit, friction is desirable, in assem-
blies that require dynamic movement, such as in hydraulic actuators, it is rather
undersirable. It leads to low eﬀiciency and excessive wear of components. Under the
right circumstances, lubrication can act as a medium between two surfaces in con-
tact and thereby decreasing friction. Full separation between a seal and the piston
rod is, however, an undesirable situation since it would result in leakage.
A big factor in the operating conditions is the geometry of the surfaces in contact.
Conformal contacts have a load distributed over a larger surface which thereby leads
to low contact pressure. Non-conformal surfaces have, however, the opposite effect
due to the small contact area [16]. Figure 2.1 shows a schematic of lubricated contact
between a rod and seal in a hydraulic cylinder where the rod is travelling with a
velocity ur in longitudinal direction. The figure clearly illustrates a non-conformal
contact between the surfaces. For the sake of simplicity, o-ring seal was used for this
illustration, but the same deductions can be made for a u-cup seal.
As the rod, in Figure 2.1, travels in the longitudinal direction, a thin layer of fluid
is dragged towards the convergent shape of the seal due to fluid friction and is then
compressed between the two surfaces, creating a suﬀicient pressure to carry load
without external pumping agency [3]. This phenomenon is called hydrodynamic
lubrication (HL). The hydrodynamic pressure occurs only in the contact and is
illustrated in Figure 2.1 where the colour change of the lubricant, indicates pressure
increase.
Stribeck curve is a very useful way to classify different types of lubrication regime.

2.3
Contact Mechanics
Two types of contact models were used for the simulations in this thesis. The first
one was used for acquiring the static contact pressure between rod and seal when
assembled. In this contact model, the contact is treated on a macro scale where it
is the nominal dimensions that generate the pressure. The second model was used
for the fluid dynamic part to compute the film thickness in a ML regime. Here the
contact is treated on the micro scale where it is the surface topology that plays a
vital role.

There are two methods of solving contact problems in COMSOL Multiphys-
ics®, namely augmented Lagrangian method and the penalty method. The penalty
method is simple and robust which makes it easy to implement. Practically, this
method inserts a stiff compression spring between the contacting boundaries. As
a result, minor sacrifice is made in accuracy as the contact objects can penetrate
each other. Augmented Lagrangian, on the other hand, ensures no penetration in a
well converged solution. It provides better accuracy, but requires additional degrees
of freedom and consequentially higher computational cost and is also less stable
in terms of convergence. In terms of the gap between the contacting objects, the
film thickness is expected to be h(x) ≤10 µm which does not leave much space for
inaccuracy. On this basis, the augmented Lagrangian method was opted for.

4.1.4
Experimental Design
Determining the experimental factors can be challenging task. The initial intention
of this experiment was to test multiple seal materials, making it the easiest factor
to determine. The remaining experimental factors are, however, dependant on the
control parameters of the test rig, which are the following:
• Frequency converter for the hydraulic pump;
• PRV regulating the pressure for the driving cylinder ( 3
in Figure 4.2);
• PRV regulating the load pressure for the driven cylinder ( 7
in Figure 4.2);
• and Stroke length.
The main concern of this experiment was the variation of seal material, loading
condition, and rod velocity. The stroke length was kept at the maximum stroke
length for all observations and thereby omitted as an experimental factor. The PRV
for the driving cylinder has also been omitted based on the assumption that the
valve stays closed for the entire experiment. This assumption is valid as long it is
set to a value that is substantially greater than the load pressure pl. This left the
material, load pressure and frequency converter as experimental factors.
The treatment values for the load pressure and rod velocity were determined
by establishing maximum and minimum values and thereafter adding a few treat-
ments in between. The limiting factor for the hydraulic system was the pressure
transducer that had a maximum value of 250 bar, but due to the severe pressure
spikes mentioned in subsection 4.1.2 when the directional valve switches position,
the maximum load pressure was determined to be at 150 bar to not destroy the
pressure transducer.
The resulting treatment values are presented in Table 4.2 which ended up being
five treatments for load pressure, six for velocity, and three for the material type.
For each treatment level, ten observation were recorded, where one observations was
considered as single in- and single out stroke.

4.1.5
Test Protocol
By following a test protocol for each experiment, many negative influencing factors
can be omitted, thereby increasing the repeatability and making the experiments
more comparable with one another.
The test protocol made for this thesis had two different stages, the first one was
for the insertion of a new seal and the second for general testing.
Protocol for inserting a new seal
• Check for seal damage It was easy to damage the seal when mounting it to
the rod and in some cases a part of the inner lip could fold inward during this
procedure. Therefore, utter care was taken when mounting the seal to the rod
and the inner lip was visually inspected after each assembly.
• Remove air from cylinder A bit of air gets usually trapped in the cylinder
when switching seals and had to be removed. In case of air staying in the
system, it was both visually and audibly detected as the directional valve
switched directions, but only for a loading pressure of 50 bar or above.
• Run-in perioid After air removal a run-in period of 5 min at 0 bar and a
run-in period of 5 min at 50 bar was performed before each experiment.
• Leakage check During the run-in period, a visual check for leakage was
performed for the components that were disassembled and assembled.
Protocol for testing
• Ambient temperature It was made sure that the ambient temperature in
the room was set to 20 °C before starting the experiment.
• Double check back pressure settings When changing the back pressure,
both the manometer by the tank, and the I/O values on the PLC were checked.
• 10 obesrvations 10 full cycles were caught for each treatment level.

5.3
Model Improvement
The following section proposes an addition to the numerical model that will improve
the accuracy significantly. This addition requires further experimental work that is
necessary to finish the model. To prove the concept, rather rough assumptions were
made to temporarily construct the model and should not be considered as final
solution.
In most cases within mechanical engineering, the friction coeﬀicient is considered
to be a static or kinetic, but in both cases it is a constant. This is also true for
most contemporary numerical models designed for analyzing rod seals. Previous
experimental research by Stanton and Taylor [35], and Tzanakis et al. [42], has
shown that when dealing with PTFE/steel contact, the friction coeﬀicient is far
from being a constant and is highly dependant on the running conditions. The list
below withholds a few of the many variables that influence the friction coeﬀicient,
according to Stanton and Taylor [35].
• Contact pressure
• Speed of travel
• Roughness of the mating surface
• Load and travel history
• Temperature
• Creep
• Load and travel history
• Specimen size

Both models used for the verification in Figure 5.10 used a constant kinetic
friction coeﬀicient from the technical data sheet in Appendix C and therefore does
not take any of these factors into account. The work of Stanton and Taylor [35]
addressed a few of these factors and showed that for a PTFE/steel contact, the
friction coeﬀicient is linearly positively correlated with the velocity and linearly
negatively correlated with the pressure. This means that the friction coeﬀicient can

f(v) = avv + bv

and g(p) a function of contact pressure, where
g(p) = app + bp
(5.3)

feasible to conduct these experiments within the given time frame.

5.4
Partial Conclusion and Discussion
In the course of this chapter, results from the numerical model have been presen-
ted that showed a successfully working contact model where no penetration occurs
between the contacting surfaces. Minor non-physical oscillations where, however,
observed in the contact pressure that could not be improved.
A comparative analysis was thereafter presented, where results from the nu-
merical model, experimental investigations, and bench-marking results from Tim
Hantusch from the technical department at GH. The results obtained in this thesis
yielded very similar results to what Hantusch arrived to showing that no major
difference is between the inverse method, and the direct method. Both numerical
models showed, however, a significant deviance from the results of the experimental
investigations.
From chapter 4, it was assumed that the friction loss measured by the test rig was
only contributed by the rod seal. There is, however, a slight chance that frictional
losses from other elements, such as guide rings, are influencing the results. This,

friction coeﬀicient, while preliminary research has shown that the friction coeﬀicient
in a PTFE/steel contact is extremely dependent on the running conditions.
In the last section of this chapter an improvement to the numerical model was

conditions. A roughly derived model was used to illustrate the impact on the results,
that in return yielded far better results. Further experimental work is, however

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Future Work
This Chapter is devoted to summarize the results of the research presented in this
thesis, followed by the future work needed to further develop the numerical model,
that allows the friction force to be accurately predicted.
6.1
Conclusion
The work presented here consisted of three main objectives, setting up a coupled mul-
tiphysics numerical model, experimentally investigate spring-energiced u-cup seals,
and lastly validate the numerical model with the experimental investigations.
The tribological contact between a rod and u-cup seal is extremely complex with
a great many physical phenomenon interwoven. The presented numerical model
combined fluid, and contact mechanics in a commercial finite element software us-
ing a direct iteration scheme.
Both macro- and micro scale physics where used
to evaluate the pressure distribution, film thickness, and consequently predict the
friction force generated in the contact between rod and u-cup seal. The model con-
sisted of utilizing state of the art EHL theory, surface topography, and a non-linear
material model to simulate the seal deformation. The non-linear material model
was constructed from tensile tests to get an accurate representation of the material
behaviour.
Through the experimental investigations of spring-energiced u-cup seal, it was
found that friction force is highly positively correlated with velocity. The predicted
friction force from the numerical model failed to yield the same correlation and
consequently greatly under evaluate the friction force.
Based on preliminary research, outside the field of rod seals, an additional model
was proposed to take into account the dynamic effects of the friction coeﬀicient based
on the running conditions. This dynamically modeled friction coeﬀicient (DMFC)
can, however, only be constructed through further experimental work. To test the ef-
67
==PAGE:76==
Chapter 6. Conclusion and Future Work
fects the including the DMFC in the numerical model, rough estimations were taken
to construct this model. The results were thereafter compared to the experimentally
evaluated friction force which yielded very promising results.
6.2
Future Work
Though the model presented in this thesis is quite advanced, there is always room for
improvement, such as adding flow factors (as in Hantusch [17]) or thermal analysis
(as in Yanada and Salant [44]). This research has, however, shown that a DMFC
model is needed to have a numerical model that can properly predict the friction
force developed by the spring-energized rod seal. Therefore, it is essential that the
friction coeﬀicient in a dry contact will be measured.
Measuring the friction coeﬀicient requires measuring the friction force and normal
force.
The test could be performed on the already acclaimed ‘pin on disk’ that
measures both friction and normal force. It is, however, expected that the friction
coeﬀicient is dependant on contact pressure, entrainment velocity, and seal and rod
surface roughness. Since the seals are milled on a turning lathe, it is rather diﬀicult
to acquire nominally flat surfaced samples with the same surface roughness. Perhaps
a simplfied version of the test rig, utilized in this thesis, without hydraulics would
have to be developed where an equally polished rod and identically milled PTFE
samples would be used.
