Abstract
This thesis evaluates the performance of the Athena X-ray telescope and its
silicon pore optics. To achieve this, a simulation program by the name of Pallas
was made. Pallas reconstructs Athena’s mirror geometry down to the pore level,
then simulates it with the help of McXTrace, the Monte Carlo X-ray Tracing
program. Two possible cases are simulated: one where the sidewalls of the mir-
ror’s pores absorb incident light perfectly, and another where they reﬂect light
according to reﬂectivity curves matching smooth SiO2.
Athena’s eﬀective area is shown to match theoretical predictions when con-
sidering on-axis light. The eﬀective area is greatest at low photon energy and
low grazing angles, falling oﬀdramatically as these parameters rise.
The mirror focuses on-axis light very tightly, contained in a radius several or-
ders of magnitude smaller than the pixels of the imaging detector aboard the
spacecraft. Oﬀ-axis light suﬀers some aberration, which grows to the size of
several pixels as the source approaches an oﬀ-axis angle of 20’, the edge of the
detector’s ﬁeld of view.
In the case of the SiO2 sidewalls, signiﬁcant quantities of stray light are ob-
served for oﬀ-axis sources, most notably due to striking the left and right walls
of the pores. For sources at 20’, there is more stray light than focused light.
For sources at 30’, some stray light lands on the detector, even when the source
itself is out of the instrument’s ﬁeld of view.
Removing the outer 5 rings of Athena’s mirror reduces the aberration of the
focused light when viewing oﬀ-axis sources, but increases the fraction of stray
light at all oﬀ-axis angles between 1’ and 20’.

Vocabulary
This section lists and explains a number of terms that are used throughout the
paper.
Aberration A distortion in an image. In the context of telescopes, often caused
by the focusing optics.
Angstrom [˚
A] unit of measurement, 1/10 of a nanometer
Arcminute ([am]/[’]) 1/60 of a degree
Arcsecond ([as]/[”]) 1/60 of an arcminute
Azimuth / azimuthal The direction that curves around the optical axis.
Collimated light source A collimated light source emits light rays that are
exactly parallel.
Grazing angle The angle between the propagation vector of light hitting a
surface and the surface itself. Equal to the oﬀ-axis angle, in the context
of Athena’s mirror.
Incidence angle The angle between the propagation vector of light hitting a
surface and the normal vector of that surface.
L2 Lagrangian point One of 5 ﬁxed points in the Earth-Sun system where
the sum of forces cancels out, and an orbit can be maintained with little
to no corrections.
Oﬀ-axis light Light that enters the telescope at an angle.
Oﬀ-axis angle The angle between light entering the telescope and the optical
axis.
On-axis light Light that enters the telescope head-on, parallel to the optical
axis.
Optical axis The central axis of an optical instrument. Light that enters a
telescope ’head-on’ runs parallel to the optical axis.
Propagation vector The direction in which a photon / light wave travels.
Radial The direction that runs directly away from the optical axis.
Relativistic Moving close to the speed of light, so the eﬀects of relativity are
no longer negligible

Introduction
The universe is a diverse place, containing objects of vastly diﬀerent sizes and
characters. Some of the most exciting ones are also the hottest and most ener-
getic: pulsars, neutron stars, black holes and their accretion disks, the list goes
on. All of them fascinating, all of them highly energetic.
One of the most reliable and accurate ways to observe these hot objects is
through X-rays, which they often emit in large quantities. But focusing and
measuring X-rays is no small feat, and highly specialized telescopes have had to
be designed to achieve this goal. From the humble Uhuru probe to juggernauts
like Chandra and NuSTAR, orbital X-ray observatories have become ever more
advanced over the last half century. [1]
The Athena mission is the European Space Agency’s next generation X-ray
observatory. It will use its advanced instruments, and an optical array bigger
than that of any X-ray observatory before it, to take a detailed look at some of
the most energetic objects in the universe. [2]
This thesis presents a simulation program, designed expressly to test the per-
formance of Athena’s mirror design. Using raytracing techniques, and realistic
reﬂectivities for the mirror surfaces, the optics have been evaluated at a range
of photon energies and grazing angles.
1.1
The Athena Mission
The Athena X-ray telescope is a future orbital X-ray observatory, slated to
be launched in 2028.
Its design pioneers the use of Silicon Pore Optics, an
adaptation of the Wolter type 1 X-ray mirror used in several previous X-ray
obseratories such as NuSTAR and the Chandra X-ray observatory. Athena will
be larger and more powerful than its predecessors, boasting a primary mirror
array nearly 3 meters in diameter, and covering a wide range of energies, between
0.1 and 12 keV. It will be placed in a wide halo orbit around the L2 Lagrangian
point, where it will fulﬁl a 5 year primary mission. Figure 1 shows a schematic
of Athena. [3]
1.1.1
Mission objectives
Athena was proposed in conjunction with ESA’s Cosmic Vision program, which
covers the long-term planning of missions between 2015 and 2025. Cosmic Vi-
sion posits several themes around which scientiﬁc research can be organised.
Athena aims to address the theme of The Hot and Energetic Universe.
As the name suggests, this theme covers the observation and study of high-
energy objects, of which there are a great variety in space. On the smaller end
of the scale, there are discrete objects such as pulsars and neutron stars, emit-
ting X-rays due to their hot, dense nature. Similarly, the accretion disks around
active galactic nuclei, or AGN ’s, and in X-ray binary systems, have X-ray bright
central regions, where the maelstrom of stellar matter reaches extreme temper-
atures and pressures.
1
==PAGE:11==
Figure 1: Schematic of the Athena X-ray telescope. The mirror is the circular
feature at the top end of the satellite. Source: [14]
These hot, dense objects create some of the most extreme environments known
to science, and X-rays allow us to observe their properties in detail, revealing
clues to the fundamental workings of the universe, such as General Relativity
and the behaviour of matter under very high pressure and gravitational strain.
On the large end, there is the Warm-Hot Intergalactic Medium, vast clouds
of hot baryons that permeate the space around and between galaxies.
This
medium is bright in the X-ray regime because of its temperature, and studying
it may oﬀer insight into the large-scale structure of the universe and how it
evolves over time, as well as the quantity and properties of dark matter and
dark energy. [4]
1.1.2
Athena’s instruments
Athena contains two scientiﬁc instruments: an imager and a spectrometer. The
former is designed around a ﬁne spatial resolution and a large ﬁeld of view,
while the latter sacriﬁces those aspects to provide better energy resolution.
The Wide-Field Imager, or WFI, provides spatial resolution of extended X-ray
sources. Its development is led by the Max Planck institute for extraterrestrial
Physics, but includes partnerships with instances in 10 countries around the
world.
The WFI has a ﬁeld of view of 40’ by 40’, and will capture images in the 0.2
keV to 15 keV energy band.
To achieve this, the instrument houses a 1024
by 1024 pixel DEPFET detector with a pixel size of 130 µm by 130 µm. The
WFI therefore has a spatial resolution of 2.2” by 2.2”. Its energy resolution
has a full width at half maximum, FWHM, of ≤170 eV at 7 keV. To minimize
background noise and radiation damage, the detector is surrounded by radiation
and particle shielding. A ﬁlter wheel provides a way to select particular energy
bands to image. [5]
The X-ray Integral Field Unit, or X-IFU, is a cryogenic spectrometer with an
2
==PAGE:12==
energy resolution of 2.5 eV, a spatial resolution of 5” and a ﬁeld of view of 5’
by 5’. The instrument’s intended goals include resolving weak spectral lines
through its ﬁne energy resolution, and 3-dimensional spectroscopic mapping to
reveal the composition of the WHIM, not only as a whole, but spatially resolved
as well.
The X-IFU, like the WFI, is being developed by an international consortium,
encompassing members from 9 EU member states, as well as Japan and the
United States. [6]

2
X-rays
X-rays are photons whose energy lies between roughly 100 eV and 100 keV,
corresponding to wavelengths of 10 pm to 10 nm. They make up part of the
high-energy electromagnetic spectrum, and are invisible to the naked eye.
2.1
X-ray emission
X-rays are generated when charged particles of a suﬃcient energy interact with
matter in various ways. Due to the energy requirement, this generally happens
when strong electric ﬁelds are present. The ﬁrst man-made X-ray sources were
early cathode ray tubes, in which voltages up to dozens of kilovolts propel elec-
trons through a vacuum tube.
On Earth, the only observed natural X-ray radiation comes from radioactive
decay of unstable atomic nuclei, such as naturally occurring radon. In space,
however, there are numerous natural sources of X-rays that work with a variety
of emission mechanisms.
Despite the relative abundance of X-ray sources in space, no such radiation
reaches the Earth’s surface; the atmosphere is very absorbent at X-ray wave-
lengths. X-ray observatories must therefore be placed beyond the Earth’s at-
mosphere to be eﬀective.
Figure 2 shows three typical spectra of X-ray emission mechanisms. The ob-
served spectrum of actual objects in space is usually some combination of the
three.
Figure 2: Typical spectra for X-ray emission mechanisms.
The following sections give a quick explanation of some of most signiﬁcant
X-ray-emitting processes in astrophysics.
2.1.1
Blackbody radiation
Any object with an observable temperature will emit radiation according to its
blackbody spectrum, which will inevitably include some measure of photons in
the X-ray band. This is true for the aforementioned distant, energetic objects,
4
==PAGE:14==
but also includes more familiar stellar objects such as the Sun. The surface of
the sun is too cold to emit any signiﬁcant amount of X-rays, however; most X-
rays from the Sun are emitted from the solar corona, which has a temperature
on the order of 106 K. In fact, the observation of X-rays from the Sun was a key
factor in discovering this anomalously high temperature.
2.1.2
Bremsstrahlung
Bremsstrahlung (from German, lit. ”braking radiation”) occurs when a charged
particle is decelerated. Kinetic energy from the charged particle is converted to
radiant energy, and a photon is emitted. Often, the name ’bremsstrahlung’ is
used speciﬁcally when talking about electrons decelerating, as other terms are
used when diﬀerent kinds of charged particles are involved.
Bremsstrahlung is the most prominent source of radiation in galaxy clusters,
where it mainly occurs in the hot (≈107 K) intracluster medium; as ions in
the plasma collide with each other, they accelerate and decelerate, emitting
bremsstrahlung. Because of the thermal nature of this process, i.e. particles
colliding with one another, it is also called thermal bremsstrahlung.
2.1.3
Synchrotron radiation
Synchrotron radiation is a kind of bremsstrahlung, where the charged particles
move in circles at relativistic speeds. In astronomy, synchrotron radiation is
often emitted in regions with strong magnetic ﬁelds. Charged particles, usually
electrons, become trapped by the Lorentz force, spiralling around the magnetic
ﬁeld lines, and emit radiation.
For trapped electrons to emit X-rays, they must have a kinetic energy on the
order of 10 TeV. This requires a constant inﬂux of energy to keep the electrons
at such high energy levels, which occurs, for example, around neutron stars
and pulsars. Synchrotron radiation has a distinct power law spectrum, and is
polarized if the magnetic ﬁeld is aligned.
2.1.4
Inverse Compton scattering
Inverse Compton scattering occurs when photons interact with relativistic elec-
trons, and energy is transferred from the electrons to the photons. This process
is assumed to be common in the accretion disks around black holes, contributing
to their power law emission spectrum. [7]

2.2.1
X-ray refractive index
The ﬁnal puzzle piece, then, is the refractive index for X-rays, which can be
expressed as:
n = 1 −δ + iβ
(5)

the reﬂecting material, and β ≪δ.
This expression reveals an important aspect of X-ray physics: the refractive
index of X-ray light is strictly less than 1, which stands in stark contrast to
almost all other observed refractive indices.
2.2.2
Total external reﬂection
The vacuum of space has a refractive index of 1. For X-rays travelling through
space and striking any given surface, Snell’s law predicts that there is a partic-
ular incidence angle at which the transmitted angle is 90◦. This incidence angle
is known as the critical angle, αc.
At incidence angles beyond αc, Snell’s law no longer has a purely real solution,
but the Fresnel equations do shed some light on the matter. Figure 3 shows
the reﬂectivity for polarized light of a smooth interface between the vacuum of
space and some hypothetical material, as predicted by equation 4. Refractive
indices of n1 = 1 and n2 = 0.999 + 10−8i are assumed, and the incidence angle
spans a range between 85 and 90 degrees.
The reﬂectivity is seen to start close to 0, then rapidly rises and plateaus close
to 1 beyond a certain angle, correlating with the aforementioned αc. Beyond
this angle, all incident light is reﬂected, a phenomenon known as total external
reﬂection.

2.2.3
Bragg’s law
X-ray reﬂectivity often experiences peaks at regular angle intervals. An example
of this can be seen later in the thesis in ﬁgure 8.
This phenomenon is described by Bragg’s law, which relates the reﬂectivity of
a crystal lattice to the wavelength and incidence angle of the reﬂected light, as
well as the structure of the lattice itself. This is particularly useful in X-ray
optics, since coatings are most often crystal lattices.
Bragg’s law speciﬁes the angles at which peaks in reﬂectivity occur as:
2d sin θ = nλ
(6)
where d is the distance between two layers of the crystal lattice, θ is the grazing
angle of the light, λ is its wavelength and n is a positive integer. At the angles
that fulﬁl this condition, photons constructively interfere with one another re-
gardless of how many layers they travel into the lattice before being reﬂected,
resulting in higher reﬂectivity. Straying from these angles causes destructive
interference, reducing the reﬂectivity.
2.2.4
Non-specular reﬂection
In reality, no interface between two media is perfectly smooth; undulations of
some scale or another will always be present. This roughness means that par-
allel rays of light will experience diﬀerent incidence angles depending on where
they strike the surface, and will thereby become divergent.
According to Elements of Modern X-ray Physics [8], the deviation of the re-
ﬂected rays from the expected, specular reﬂection vector, can be described using
a probability distribution. The rougher the surface, the broader the distribution
becomes.

2.2.5
Coating design
When designing a coating for X-ray optics, two factors are key: electron density
and smoothness. The materials with the highest reﬂectivity in the X-ray regime
are usually those with the highest electron density. Common materials used in
X-ray coatings are gold, iridium, platinum, tungsten, and similar metals with
high atomic weights.
Single layer coatings have the advantage of being simple to manufacture, but
are often limited in their eﬀective energy range. A way to broaden the energy
range of a mirror is to create a coating with multiple layers, each made of a
distinct material. Furthermore, this set of layers can be repeated many times
to create a multilayer coating. The repeated interfaces between materials each
reﬂect a fraction of X-rays, thereby increasing the total reﬂectivity. Multilayers
are most eﬀective if the diﬀerence in refractive index between them is great,
which correlates with a large diﬀerence in electron density, and therefore atomic
weight. [8]

X-ray optics
Due to the low grazing angles necessary to reliably reﬂect X-rays, traditional
parabolic mirrors are of little use in X-ray observatories. Specialized mirrors
have been designed to focus X-rays, with their own distinct features and re-
quirements.
3.1
Wolter mirrors
In 1952, German physicist Hans Wolter presented three mirror designs specif-
ically meant to focus X-rays, as illustrated in ﬁgure 4. Each design consists
of two sets of mirrors, one paraboloid, the other hyperboloid, and rotationally
symmetric around the optical axis. Wolter’s idea was to use the sections of
these shapes that are nearly parallel with the optical axis. This minimizes the
grazing angle of incident light, and exploits total external reﬂection. In practice,
manufacturing limitations mean that the type 1 mirror is the only one that is
practical to use in astrophysics.
Figure 4: The three types of Wolter mirror.
The combination of parabolic and hyperbolic mirrors grants the designs a
comparatively large ﬁeld of view. If only a parabolic mirror were included, the
focused light would be subject to signiﬁcant aberration at all but the slightest
oﬀ-axis angles.
At such low grazing angles, however, the cross-sectional area of a single parabolic
or hyperbolic mirror surface is very small. To make Wolter mirrors practical,
multiple mirrors are nested inside one another, each with a diﬀerent radius and
appropriate curvature, but all with the same focal point. This ’nested shell’
design results in a composite mirror, and has been the predominant design of
X-ray reﬂecting optics for many years. An example of such a mirror, ﬂown on
the NuSTAR observatory, is shown in ﬁgure 5. [7]

tures of the paraboloid and hyperboloid mirror surfaces, that is, the best bal-

used to do this are the focal length of the optics f, the angle α, and the ratio

3.1.1
Stray light
Light rays passing through a Wolter mirror are focused by reﬂecting twice: ﬁrst
oﬀthe parabolic mirror, then oﬀthe hyperbolic mirror. If a light ray deviates
from this pattern, it is not focused properly. This kind of light is known as
stray light. It is most common when light from an oﬀ-axis light source enters
the optics.
At best, stray light lands outside the detector and is absorbed by the tele-
scope’s structure. In this case, it detracts from the light the telescope is able
to focus, making the image dimmer. If stray light does land on the detector, it
creates ’ghost’ images of light sources, or causes blurring.

3.2
Silicon Pore Optics
In Athena, the traditional nested shell design of mirror is expanded upon by
dividing the shells azimuthally into so-called pores, a design known as Silicon
Pore Optics, or SPO. This is achieved by digging grooves in the backside of ap-
propriately curved silicon plates, leaving behind a thin plate with ribs at regular
intervals. When another plate is placed on top of the ﬁrst, the ribs make contact
with the bottom of the second plate, thereby creating a set of channels, or pores.
Each pore is made of two halves, the parabolic and hyperbolic parts of the
mirror, and both halves consist of four walls; the top wall, which is part of the
mirror and is coated in a reﬂective material, and the three side walls, which
are left uncoated and are thus assumed to absorb any photons that collide with
them. It should be noted that, for the purposes of this thesis, ’side walls’ refers
to any pore wall that is not intended to reﬂect and focus light, meaning the
bottom, left and right walls.
The aim of SPO is twofold: reduce the amount of deformation in the mir-
ror by providing structural integrity, and reduce the amount of stray light that
passes the optics, by absorbing photons that enter the mirror at high oﬀ-axis
angles when they collide with the ribs. [10]

5
Pallas, the Athena simulation program
Pallas is a simulation program I created for this thesis to assess the performance
of Athena’s optics under various conditions. In greek mythology, the goddess
Athena was often given the epithet Pallas, hence the program’s name.
Pallas reconstructs Athena’s mirror out of numerous small components, carefully
placed to represent the mirror’s geometry. It then uses McXTrace, the Monte
Carlo X-ray Tracing program, and part of its newly developed AstroX toolbox,
to simulate the mirror’s underlying physics.
5.1
McXTrace
McXTrace, the Monte Carlo X-ray Tracing program, is software in which optical
instruments can be deﬁned, illuminated with various light sources, and the rays
traced through the instrument according to the laws of optics. Instruments are
deﬁned as a set of components, through which a ray of light propagates. To
simulate interactions with matter, rays of light are weighted according to the
processes that govern the interaction.
A ray that reﬂects oﬀa surface with
reﬂectivity 0.5 will be weighted to 50%, a ray that hits a beamstop will be
absorbed, etc. Special components that act as detectors can be placed in the
beamline to analyze its cross section.
Ray-tracing is a mathematical technique commonly used in simulations of phe-
nomena with ray-like characteristics, such as beams of light. In ray-tracing, rays
are propagated through some mathematical space until they interact with their
environment. In the case of photons, this interaction can be any number of elec-
tromagnetic interactions with matter, such as reﬂection, refraction, absorption,
etc. When an interaction occurs, the ray is altered to reﬂect the impact of the
interaction; a beam of light may change direction, be absorbed by the material
it interacted with, etc.
If an interaction is continuous, as for example propagating a photon through a
nonhomogeneous medium with varying refractive index, the ray can be propa-
gated in small steps, and the calculation performed or approximated for each
step. Raytracing is suited to complex systems that would be hard to solve an-
alytically.
The Monte Carlo in McXTrace’s name refers to a type of simulation of the
same name, where the outcome of the simulation is determined stochastically;
each time a process occurs whose outcomes can be described with a probabil-
ity distribution, an outcome is sampled according to this distribution, and the
simulation continues. With enough samples, the true nature of the process is
reﬂected in the results of the simulation, allowing Monte Carlo to model com-
plex systems that would be hard or impossible to solve analytically.
In McXTrace, Monte Carlo manifests in several ways, such as the initial loca-
tion or propagation vector of a ray emitted by a lightsource. Diﬀerent sources in
McXTrace provide diﬀerent probability distributions for these quantities, allow-
ing for isotropic or non-isotropic point sources, beams with uniform or gaussian
proﬁles, etc.
17
==PAGE:27==
However, not everything is performed according to MC principles. For example,
when a ray reﬂects oﬀa surface with a given reﬂectivity, the ray is weighted
accordingly. In a pure MC simulation, the ray would have a probability of being
absorbed or reﬂected, in line with the reﬂectivity, but this requires a far greater
amount of statistical events to be representative of the truth. Weighting the
rays instead is more eﬃcient, and achieves the same level of accuracy.
5.1.1
Instrument and component deﬁnition
At its core, Pallas is an instrument for use with McXTrace, albeit a heavily
customized and modiﬁed one. Writing an instrument in McXTrace is done in
its domain-speciﬁc instrument language. It generally consists of stringing to-
gether a number of components, deﬁnining their position and orientation, and
setting their parameters if necessary. Examples of components include mirrors,
beamstops, slits and so on.
Lightsources provide the rays to trace through the instrument. They can be
point sources or extended, and can have diﬀerent emission density distributions.
Monitors are a third type of component, and are used to register certain prop-
erties of the ray as it passes through the instrument. Built-in monitors measure
the total intensity as a function of some variable, such as wavelength or posi-
tion. Specialized monitors can be made that measure user-deﬁned parameters,
though this is a more involved process.
5.1.2
Expanding McXTrace
While creating Pallas, I also modiﬁed several of the McXTrace components used
in the program to better ﬁt its requirements. These modiﬁcations are described
in section 5.3.4.
Expanding the existing functionality of McXTrace can be done in two ways.
The ﬁrst is by the use of the EXTEND block; a section in each component which
can be given a block of native C-code to run after a ray has interacted with
it. This EXTEND block has access to variables used by the component itself (or
other components in some cases), as well as variables and functions deﬁned by
the user for the instrument as a whole. In Pallas, part of the data collection
is done in the EXTEND blocks of various components. It is also used to handle
photons that do not pass through any pores on a plate.
A more advanced method of extending McXTrace is by modifying its com-
ponents, or writing entirely new ones. The syntax for McXTrace components
is similar to its Instrument language, but relies more heavily on native C-code,
as this deﬁnes the behaviour of the component. For Pallas, several components
were customized in this way; they are described in section 5.3.4.
5.2
The AstroX toolbox
The AstroX toolbox is a set of components to be bundled with McXTrace,
speciﬁcally designed to simulate optics for use in astrophysical x-ray observa-
tions. It has been developed with Athena in mind, but has been made generic
18
==PAGE:28==
enough that it can be applied to a variety of Wolter type 1-style optics. It builds
on two core components: the Pore and the Module, modelling the structures
of the same name in the SPO design as described earlier. Pallas is based around
the Pore component, modiﬁed to best ﬁt the program.
The Pore component actually consists of two components: the Pore P and
Pore H components, corresponding to the parabolic and hyperbolic halves of
the pore, respectively. Both operate in a very similar way, but use their respec-
tive reﬂective surfaces.
When using one of the Pore components, key input parameters are the radii
of the reﬂecting surface at the entry, middle and exit plane, the focal length of
the telescope, and the width and height of the pore.
Pore components have a top and bottom surface, curved according to the
speciﬁcations in VanSpeybroeck and Chase [9]. The curvature and length of
the pore halves are determined based on the input parameters described above.
The Pores also have left and right deﬁned as planes. Two auxiliary planes,
the entry and exit plane of the pore half, control when a passing ray enters and
leaves the pore, but do not reﬂect incident rays.
5.3
Pallas program design
Pallas is designed to reconstruct and simulate the geometry of Athena one plate
at a time. Given a module and plate number, Pallas will calculate the dimensions
and position of the plate, then place the pores present on the plate accordingly.
A light source then emits a set number of rays to be passed through the plate,
and a monitor at the focal plane registers where the rays land. Various data
collection functions gather information and store it in binary ﬁles for further
analysis.
5.3.1
Available geometry information
The Athena Reference Design document [12] speciﬁes Athena’s mirror geometry
with a number of key pieces of information.
On the module level, it gives the dimensions of each module present on the
mirror, as well as the position of the central pore of each module (and thereby
the position of the module as a whole). On the pore level, it lists the dimensions
of the pores, and the radial and azimuthal spacing between them.

5.3.5
Data collection and output format
Throughout various stages of the simulation, diﬀerent data points are collected.
At the end of a simulation, they are written as bytes to a data ﬁle, ready to
be read by an analysis program. The data of individual plates is collected per
module, so one data ﬁle exists for each simulated module.
The gathered data includes the following data points:
For the plate in general:
• Amount of emitted rays
• Amount of rays that passed through the plate
• The collective weight of the rays that passed through the plate
• The source ﬂux, given in rays/m2
For each ray:
• Where on the source it was emitted
• The location where it entered the parabolic pore
• The locations of each reﬂection inside the pore, as well as the type of the
reﬂection
• The location where the ray exits the optics
• The location where the ray was absorbed
• The ﬁnal weight of the ray before being absorbed
To keep track of which bytes belong to which data point, they are preﬁxed with
a tag byte. These tag bytes can be used by analysis programs to identify the
data that follows them. A detailed description of the tag byte format can be
found in appendix C.
5.3.6
Workﬂow and compilation chain
Since the simulation of each plate is run as a diﬀerent process, a number of aux-
iliary scripts were made to group simulations together on an increasing scale.
Examples include running all plates in a particular module, or running a full
mirror using a particular photon energy and incidence angle. These scripts are
written in a combination of bash shell script and Python, and build on one
another. A description of the auxiliary programs is given in appendix C.
Compilation times for McXTrace instruments grow signiﬁcantly as the number
of components grows. To reduce the time needed to run a simulation, Pallas
assumes that the required instrument ﬁles are precompiled. The dimensions and
position of pores in a plate can be adjusted in real time, but the amount of pores
cannot. It is therefore necessary to precompile an instrument for each unique
pore count, but plates that share a pore count can use the same instrument ﬁle
regardless of their position on the mirror.

6
Simulation setup
When performing simulations, three primary parameters were considered:
Photon energy: The energy of the photons emitted by the collimated plane
source. Can be any multiple of 1 keV between 1 and 10 keV. This set of
energies represents a majority of the energy range that Athena will cover.
Oﬀ-axis angle: The angle at which the source illuminates the plate, measured
from the optical axis to the normal of the source plane. Varies in multiples
of 1’ between 0’ and 10’, but also includes 20’ and 30’, and rotates the
source’s position and orientation around the X-axis. This set of angles
covers the ﬁeld of view of the WFI’s detector, and goes beyond for the
purpose of detecting stray light.
Sidewall absorption: two cases were considered: total absorption and reﬂec-
tivity according to SiO2 reﬂectivity curves. These cases will be referred
to as the ’ideal case’ and the ’SiO2 case’.
To cover each possible combination of these three parameters, 260 distinct sim-
ulations are run, given that each simulation assumes a single parameter combi-
nation.
For each simulation, 5000 photons were emitted per mirror plate.
Because
plates diﬀer in size, the source emitting these photons must grow and shrink in
accordance with the plates, resulting in a change in incident photon ﬂux. To
this end, all ray weights have been normalized to correspond to a source with a
photon ﬂux of 1/s/m2.
In addition, a smaller design for Athena’s mirror, which removes the outer 5
rings of mirror modules, was also assessed. This conﬁguration uses the same
photon energies and oﬀ-axis angles, and the SiO2 reﬂectivity curves for its side-
walls. Given this, no new data was generated for the smaller mirror. Instead,
the data for the inner 15 rings of the SiO2 set of simulations was used.
6.1
Detector asymmetry
Since Athena’s mirror is assumed to be rotationally symmetric, the grazing an-
gle of the incoming light only varies in one direction. That is, only a single
azimuthal angle was considered for the source. If this assumption is not made,
the azimuthal angle of the source must also be varied to make an accurate as-
sessment of the mirror. This would multiply the amount of required simulations
by a signiﬁcant factor.
The detector on the WFI, however, does not share this rotational symmetry.
This means that some light, focused or stray, may or may not land on the de-
tector depending on which azimuthal angle it entered the optics at.
Speciﬁcally, rays that land on the focal plane at a distance from the optical axis
between the 1 and

2 times the half width of the detector may or may not land
on the detector, depending on the azimuthal angle of the source. An illustration
of this issue is illustrated in ﬁgure 13.

7.2.5
Summary
Athena’s oﬀ-axis performance showed both expected and unexpected proper-
ties. Its eﬀective area was shown to fall for rising oﬀ-axis angles, and the exit
plane ﬂux was shown to stretch out along the axis of rotation of the source.
The focal pattern, however, is seen to exhibit strong aberration, suspected to
originate from the outermost rings given that the aberration is weakest for high
energy levels. For low photon energies and high oﬀ-axis angles, the aberration
is a serious detriment to the focusing capability of the telescope, spreading the
light over an area that measures up to almost 8 pixels in length.
Vertical-only stray light is seen at angles beyond 9’ at worst, and beyond 20’
at best. At 30’, all light reaching the detector is stray light, as the focused
light is now out of the ﬁeld of view. This stray light therefore still interferes
with observations even when its source is outside the instrument’s ﬁeld of view,
although this interference is considerably weaker than the focused light.

Summary
The eﬀect of removing the outermost rings of Athena’s mirror is twofold. On
the one hand, it reduces the abberation on the focused light signiﬁcantly. On
the other, it results in an overall smaller fraction of focused light, and therefore
a larger fraction of stray light.
Both eﬀects are most impactful at low photon energies, which is to be expected
given the strong dependency on energy of the performance of the outer rings.

8
Discussion
8.1
Design of Pallas
The following are comments on the technical aspects of the simulation, as well
as the development process of Pallas.
8.1.1
Iterations on the design
In earlier designs, Pallas would not simulate a whole plate at once, instead
evaluating a single pore per process. This decision was a holdover from the
predecessor of the program, made for an earlier project. It proved untenable,
however, as the individual processes were very brief, and caused a lot of perfor-
mance overhead in switching between pores.
The decision was made to group pores together to some capacity. The ﬁrst
attempt was a simulation of all pores in a full module at once. This proved to
require more elements than McXTrace is designed to handle, so the grouping
was reduced to simulating the pores on a single plate at once.
This group-
ing proved to strike a good balance between complexity and speed, reducing
overhead performance costs and improving runtimes while remaining straight-
forward in design.
8.1.2
The AstroX Module component
At the time of writing, the AstroX toolbox is fast becoming a fully-ﬂedged suite
of components for McXTrace. Among these components is the Module, anal-
ogous to one of Athena’s mirror modules.
It works by calculating, for each
ray, which of the module’s pores the ray would enter (if any), then moving a
single ’virtual’ Pore to the corresponding location, performing the ray-tracing
through said Pore, and repeating. It accomplishes the job of Pallas’ Plate
instrument while using only a single pore, and wastes a far smaller fraction of
photons doing so as the full mirror module conforms much better to the shape
of the source. The reason, then, why this component was not used in the design
of Pallas, is simply that the Module component did not yet exist when devel-
opment on Pallas began.
The plate-based approach is eﬀectively a parallel evolution of the Pore com-
ponent, accomplishing the same goals as the Module component, but through
diﬀerent means. The two approaches can therefore be cross-validated, reducing
the risk of errors in both.
8.2
Opportunities for expansion
On a technical level, there are a few directions in which Pallas could be ex-
panded.
The various surfaces in the Pore component are made to do one of two things:
specularly reﬂect an incident ray and weight it according to the appropriate
reﬂectivity, or absorb the ray entirely. Non-specular reﬂection has not been ac-
counted for in the program, since it is highly dependent on surface roughness,
51
==PAGE:61==
a quality which is not yet determined for certain for Athena.
Transmission of the photons through the pore walls has also not been imple-
mented. In practice, some fraction of photons would penetrate the pore walls
instead of reﬂecting or absorbing, and enter into a neighboring pore. Beyond
more sophisticated reﬂection code, this implementation would require either the
possibility to move and reshape a pore on the ﬂy, as the photon passed from
one pore to the other, or it would require that all neighboring pores are present,
and some advanced control ﬂow procedures to pass control from one pore to the
other.
8.3
Assumptions and simpliﬁcations
Pallas makes several assumptions and simpliﬁcations with regards to Athena’s
true speciﬁcation, to limit the scope of the project or when concrete information
is not yet available.
8.3.1
Mirror surfaces
The AstroX Pore component uses mirror surfaces that are curved exactly along
the theoretical paraboloid and hyperboloid surfaces speciﬁed in VanSpeybroeck
and Chase [9]. In reality, it is not feasible to manufacture such mirror surfaces,
so conical approximations of the theoretical surfaces are used instead. These
approximate surfaces would induce some small aberrations in the optics that
are not included in the simulation.
8.3.2
Curved focal plane
VanSpeybroeck and Chase [9] indicate the use of a curved focal plane to mini-
mize the point-spread function of a Wolter mirror; the optimal focal plane would
rise above the original focal plane as a function of distance from the optical axis.
Since the focal plane is, under most circumstances, ﬁxed at a detector on board
the telescope, this can be achieved by oﬀsetting segments of the mirror itself.
In Athena, this would mean moving the mirror modules along the optical axis
to match the curvature of the focal plane.
The eﬀects of a curved focal plane were assessed with a number of smaller
simulation runs, and were observed to be minimal for Athena’s geometry. Fur-
thermore, it is much more technically feasible to produce a Wolter mirror in
which all shells share the same center plane, so there is a large probability that
this feature will not be included in the design of Athena. For these reasons,
Pallas assumes a ﬂat focal plane.
8.3.3
Sidewall surface roughness
Since an unrealistically smooth surface was chosen to represent the pores’ side-
walls, it is almost certain that the results shown in this paper overestimate the
amount of stray light present in the real telescope. As previously mentioned,
the smoothness of the simulated sidewalls was decided upon since their true
52
==PAGE:62==
roughness is yet to be determined. A worst-case scenario was thus deemed the
best approach for this analysis.
8.4
Discussion of results
8.4.1
Stray light presentation
In all, some 255 distinct types of stray light have been observed, assuming that
reﬂections on the left and right walls of the same pore half are interchangeable.
This great variety of stray light types poses a challenge when it comes to pre-
sentation, and a trade-oﬀhad to be made between detail and readability. It
was decided to favor readability, as the studies in this paper remain relatively
surface-level, and a large portion of stray light types only register a handful of
counts.
8.4.2
Missing eﬀective area
The on-axis eﬀective area observed from simulation data is less than the the-
oretical values predicted by equation 10. While it was initially proposed that
this diﬀerence was due to on-axis stray light. However, this type of stray light
accounts for only 0.2% of the total ﬂux at the focal plane, far too little to match
the missing eﬀective area.
Further investigation may be required, but it is perhaps not unlikely that part
of the approximations made in equation 10 is incorrect.

9
Summary
This thesis has presented a simulation program for Athena’s mirror geometry
by the name of Pallas. The program was made speciﬁcally for the thesis, and
builds on McXTrace, the Monte Carlo X-ray Tracing program, and its AstroX
toolbox.
Pallas simulates Athena’s mirror one plate at a time by recreating a number of
pores, with positions and orientations that match the plate to be simulated. A
collimated source then illuminates the pores, and the focused and stray light
that passes through is collected at the focal plane.
The assessment covered
a range of energies and oﬀ-axis angles representative of Athena’s real require-
ments.
Pallas also modiﬁes some of the components already present in McXTrace and
AstroX, to accommodate them for the simulations’ particular needs, or to facil-
itate data collection.
A grand total of 260 simulations were run, consisting of 2 large simulation
sets. Each set comprises 130 combinations of incidence angle and photon en-
ergy, achieved by adjusting the source in the simulation.
The ﬁrst run assumes that the sidewalls absorb photons perfectly, eliminating
as much stray light as possible.
The second, in contrast, simulates the sidewalls using reﬂectivity curves that
match a smooth SiO2 surface.
This represents a worst-case scenario for the
sidewall’s absorbing capabilities, resulting in a high amount of stray light.

10
Conclusion
From the 80+ GB of data produced by Pallas has emerged an outline of the
character of Athena’s optics. The analysis focused on a few key points: eﬀective
area, stray light and its categorization and the spread of focused light.
Athena’s mirror design was proven to focus on-axis light extremely well, down
to an area on the order of picometers. A small amount of stray light, 0.2%,
is shown to detract from the focused light, but since it does not land on any
detector, its interference with observations is somewhat limited. The photon
ﬂux at the exit plane of the optics is highest around the center of the optics,
and drops oﬀtowards the edges, more dramatically so for high photon energies.
As the source moves further oﬀ-axis, the focused light becomes signiﬁcantly
more spread out, forming a cone-shaped abberation. The spread is more pro-
nounced at low energies, indicating that it is mainly the outer rings which cause
the abberation. This is further proven by the assessment of the smaller mirror
design; similar incidence angles and energies produce much smaller patterns on
the focal plane when the outer rings are removed.
For oﬀ-axis light sources, increasing amounts of various types of stray light
are seen. Most notably is the strong presence of side-only stray light, which is
observed between 1’ and 20’. At the focal plane, this stray light is seen forming
a dense ring around the optical axis.
Vertical-only and mixed-stray light are one to two orders of magnitude weaker
than the side-only light, but persists beyond 20’, outside the ﬁeld of view of the
WFI.
The eﬀective area observed from the simulations closely matches theoretical
predictions for on-axis sources, and drops oﬀas a function of both photon en-
ergy and incidence angle. Comparing ideal to SiO2 sidewalls, the latter shows
greater eﬀective area for oﬀ-axis sources, especially at high incidence angles.
This correlates to the relative abundance of stray light at these angles.
The smaller mirror presents a trade-oﬀbetween focal patter spread and stray
light. As mentioned before, the small mirror design causes less spreading in
oﬀ-axis light, thereby increasing resolution. The mirror’s eﬀective area is also
reduced, but only signiﬁcantly at low energies. Most importantly, however, the
small mirror experiences a larger fraction of stray light, hence the trade-oﬀ.

==PAGE:65==
11
Further work
This thesis paves the way for several avenues of further research.
By increasing the amount of simulated conﬁgurations, a more in-depth char-
acterization of Athena’s behaviour under various circumstances.
This could
include simulating more incidence angles or photon energies. In addition, sim-
ulations involving realistic, extended sources (such as accretion disks or clouds
of plasma) could give further insight in Athena’s performance during real ob-
servations.
An obvious way to improve Pallas’ simulation of Athena is to replace the per-
fectly curved mirror surfaces with conic approximations, as these are more likely
to be used in the real observatory. Furthermore, various mirror geometries could
be assessed, and the mirror design optimized. Variations on the geometry might
include changing the dimensions of the pores or the mirror modules.
Pallas could be further reﬁned by including the simulation of imperfections in
the mirror surfaces, ﬂaws in the various curvatures, or other possible production
errors in the mirror modules. Non-specular scattering could be implemented as
described before. When more precise ﬁgures for the roughness of the sidewalls
become available, these could be implemented to produce more accurate mea-
surements of stray light, as opposed to the worst-case ﬁgures presented here.

A.1
Auxiliary scripts
The easiest way to run Pallas is to invoke one of the helper scripts described
earlier. The scripts created for Pallas as of writing are as follows:
A.1.1
module.sh
Simulates a single module on Athena’s mirror, and saves the data in a sin-
gle module data ﬁle. Run directly from a bash terminal using the command
./instruments/module.sh. Takes the following input arguments as a space-
separated list:
Ray count: (1 or more) How many rays should be simulated per plate.
Module index: (between 0 and 1061) Index specifying which module to run.
Counts from inner to outer ring.
Pore count on plate: (should ﬁt with one of the included instruments) Spec-
iﬁes which instrument ﬁle to use.
Source angle: (in arcminutes, 0 or higher) The incidence angle of the source.
Photon energy: (in keV, between 1 and 10, in multiples of 1 keV) The energy
of the photons emitted by the source.
Output path: The path where the output ﬁle should be saved, including the
name of the ﬁle (but excluding the.dat extension). If a ﬁle of the same
name exists, data is appended to that ﬁle.
A.1.2
fullMirror.sh
Simulates the entire mirror for a single photon energy and incidence angle, via
repeated use of module.sh. Run with python using the command python
./runs/fullMirror.sh. Takes the following input arguments:
-n # (1 or higher) How many rays should be simulated per plate.
-o <path> (string) path where the output ﬁles should be saved. The same
ﬁle appending behaviour as in module.sh applies.
-a # (arcminutes, 0 or higher) Incidence angle of the source.
-e # (keV, between 1 and 10, in multiples of 1 keV) Energy of the photons
emitted by the source.
-t # (1 up to the amount of available processing threads) Speciﬁes the amount
of threads to divide the simulation across. The simulation is only split
down to the level of modules; one module is guaranteed to be run on a
single thread.
59
==PAGE:69==
-start # (between 0 and 1061) Index of the module to start the simulation
on. Modules before this index are not included. Useful for testing only
certain rings of the mirror.
-end # (between 0 and 1061) Index of the module to end the simulation on.
Modules after this index are not included.
A.1.3
effectiveArea.sh
Simulates the entire mirror for a predeﬁned range of photon energies and inci-
dence angles, via repeated use of fullMirror.py. Uses the input arguments
-n, -o and -t in a fashion similar to fullMirror.py
