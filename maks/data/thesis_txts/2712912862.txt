Life cycle assessment for food waste in the Danish food processing industry
Abstract
This project focuses on the quantification of the environmental benefits associated with the
implementation of new approaches for the prevention of food losses in the food industry. In
collaboration with Danish Technological Institute and two companies, Simple Feast and Cater
Food, information were provided in order to create a real life scenario of food waste handling
and treatment.
The project assesses a new technology (DNAPROKON), developed by DTI
and Copenhagen University, for on-site monitoring of bacteria growth in food production and
food products. Through better control of bacteria growth, the intentions with DNAPROKON
is to reduce food waste and improve the selflife products. More specifically, a range of scenarios
were created for the purpose of comparing this technology with different food waste management
treatments and evaluating which scenario is causing fewer environmental impacts. This is a com-
parison between the different approaches in the same company and not between the companies.
Anaerobic digestion is how food waste is currently handled and by implementing the DNAPROKON
technology, the aim is to reduce food waste by 10%. Hydrolysis for the production of biodiesel
and dehydration for animal feed are also alternatives that are taken into account. For the mod-
elling, the EASETECH software is used.
From the results, it can be seen that anaerobic digestion has a higher environmental impact
compare to the production of biodiesel and animal feed. When the DNAPROKON technology
is implemented the savings are clear for both of the companies.
Even though this is not a
comparison between the companies, the avoided production of food and packaging for Simple
Feast through the re-distribution has distinct savings for all of the impact categories, something
that is not seen to Cater Food.

Introduction
1.1
Background
With the constant growth of the population, food demand keeps increasing. Even though the
world seems to be in need of edible materials, one third of the world’s edible food, about 1.3
billion tonnes, is being wasted every year. On average, developed countries generate 100-170 kg
of food waste per capita annually, and this number keeps rising creating major environmental,
ethical and economic problems. [29], [46] Food waste is one of the largest sources that, until now,
cannot be efficiently recovered. The production of food, even from the very beginning, is based
on natural resources such as land use and water, but also energy, fertilizers and transportation
means. By the unnecessarily wasting of food, all of these resources are being depleted, leading to
an increase of environmental impacts mostly during production, processing, and transportation.
Food losses that happen during the production stage also result in wasting of resources. When
food has not been properly sorted and handled, it may be deposited in a landfill. This makes
the food decompose creating methane and carbon dioxide. Methane is a greenhouse gas that
contributes to climate change. Along the food supply chain, food waste contributes with 8-10%
of total global greenhouse gas emissions. [4], [14]
In 2018, the US EPA published that the total municipal solid waste generated by the material
was 292.4 million tonnes, showing that the second largest category was food with 22%, while the
first one was paper and cardboard with 23.%, showing that the difference is not significant.[6]
This puts the US in the first place as the country that discards the most food compared to the
rest of the world. [37] The same year, the global waste composition showed that 44% of the
total waste, belongs to the food/green category, once again indicating the importance of food
waste. The same report also showed that this percentage can be easily influenced by the differ-
ent incomes across countries, with the high income ones having less food waste compared to the
middle-low. [32]
In the European Union, up to 20% (about 88 million tonnes) of all food produced ends up as
food waste every year, including both edible and non edible parts. In Europe, many countries
have already started implementing new rules and legislation, with the EU mostly being focused
on adopting the food loss and waste reduction targets created by the United Nations General
Assembly as part of the 2030 Sustainable Development Agenda. [28] This regulation includes a
target to globally decrease the food waste by 50% per capita, at the retail and consumer levels
and reduce food losses along production and supply chains, including post-harvest losses, by
2030. To help this action, SDG 12.3 was implemented. A few further obligations were intro-
duced on the 30th of May 2018, where the European member states agreed on improving the
reduction of food waste on every level of the food supply chain and to keep track of the food
waste levels. This would be succeeded with waste prevention programs and re-distribution of the
food (donations). [14], [13]
Food recovery hierarchy is an action, created by the EPA, to help organizations prevent and
redirect the food waste. [3] The food hierarchy has been divided into seven categories. Starting
from the top of the pyramid is the most preferred option, prevention, where the goal is avoidance
of surplus food generation throughout the supply chain, food production and consumption. If

Denmark is one of the countries in Europe that has very high standards for food safety in the
production stage. In an agreement with the farmers, food industries and the authorities, many
strategies have been implemented about the safety of the products. [16] As an example, Coop
Denmark started to sell all its bananas individually, rather than in bunches. This change was
associated with 6,000 fewer bananas being wasted each day. [15] Furthermore, Denmark has de-
veloped a smartphone application called Too Good To Go, where retailers have the opportunity
to sell products that otherwise should have been thrown away, at a lower price. This allows
consumers to reduce food waste and save money. With this app, 10 million meals have already
been saved in Denmark so far. [42]
Denmark is also considered as one of the countries, in the EU, with the largest amount of waste
per capita with 1,214,000 tonnes of food waste in total per year, of which 814,000 tonnes are
edible fractions. Their main national target is to reduce the amount of food waste across the food
supply chain. Incineration is the most common treatment, with almost 80% of the household

Food losses and waste are definitions that through out the years have been difficult to distinguish.
Food waste and loss are both referring to the decrease in the quantity or quality of food, but with
food waste resulting from the actions and decisions taken by the retailers, food service providers
and consumers, while food loss results from decisions and actions by food suppliers in the chain,
food service providers and consumers, but not retailers. [21] Both, food loss and waste, are
part of the food supply chain, which is the whole process from the extraction of raw materials,
for food production, until the consumption. Food supply chain has five steps: i) production ii)
processing iii)distribution iv) retailer v) consumer. Although, in 2021 it was agreed that all the
materials that are not edible from human beings, will be considered as food waste and also in
this report, food waste is also referring to non edible food. [45]
As mentioned above, food losses during the production stage contribute to a vast environmental
and economical problems. Water, energy, and land use are resources that are being mistreated
which leads to environmental impacts like climate and an increase in methane production. The
problem that occurs to the production chain causes also economic losses and this highlights that

1.2
New technology: DNAPROKON
Danish Technological Institute and Copenhagen University, considering the reduction of food
waste, came up with the idea to develop a new DNA-based process control technology. The
project is called DNAPROKON and is a new process management tool that helps reducing food
waste in the value chain from processing to the consumer. The DNAPROKON consists of three
modules: i) Analysis of microorganisms in the production environment and food, ii) data pro-
cessing and iii) process control [25]
This project combines existing DNA-based analysis methods with new techniques for sample
preparation as well as a thorough knowledge of process hygiene and durability. This will happen
with the implementation of a small measuring device that can easily be included in the food
companie’s process control and provide an overview of the DNA content of bacteria in the food.
This offers a more accurate knowledge of which microorganisms dominate the food. This process
makes the microbiological analyzing faster than it used to be, since it needs only one day for the
results. With DNAPROKON, the food companies can detect not just the number of bacteria
but also the species, which makes it easy to answer to identify situations or conditions leading
to degradation of food products.
The DNAPROKON project aims to reduce approximately 10% out of the 19% of the food waste
lost in the processing stage in Denmark and save the edible products from being wasted. [26]
As mentioned above, in this project there are companies that participate in order to test the
new device. In this report the companies that have been selected as cases are Simple Feast and
Cater Food.
Simple Feast is a company that have been established since 2017. They focus on making meal-
kits, where the food products and the menu changes weekly with plant based and organic ma-
terials. The food that cannot be used is disposed in the company’s bio-bins, which are emptied
once a week.They will test this method on its meal boxes with vegetarian and vegan dishes.

1.3
Alternative uses of food waste
Food waste, in most of the countries, gets disposed at landfills and is considered to be the
largest category of municipal solid waste. Landfills create air pollution, leaching and bad odor,
and generally are known to generate carbon dioxide and methane.
[2] Considering these, a
sustainable management of food waste is needed.
Food waste collected from the production
stage it is considered to be simpler and easier to be managed.
Its quality is higher and its
composition is simpler, thus is easier to be redirected to produce animal feed, and biodiesel. So,
in order to ease the environmental impact associated with of food waste, alternative strategies
are needed. [46]
1.3.1
Anaerobic digestion
Anaerobic digestion is a process where different microbial species decompose organic materials
in the absence of oxygen. Anaerobic microbes convert biomass and organic waste into biogas.
[1] More specifically, food waste enters an enclosed tank with no oxygen where microorganisms
are digesting the organic matter. As the food waste breaks down it creates fertilizer and gives
of biogas, where methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2), are formed. The biogas is collected
and used to generate heat and electricity. AD is suitable for all types of food waste, however, it
is preferred to be used as a final treatment method for impure and low-quality food waste. [46]
Figure 3: AD for food waste [43]
1.3.2
Biodiesel
A conversion of food waste to potential liquid biofuels is important, since fossil fuels are depleting
and causing a severe environmental damage. Biofuels, like bioethanol and biodiesel, are intended
to be used as a replacement for diesel fuel and gasoline in many countries.
Biofuels can be
produced from both edible and non edible feedstocks, but considering the cost and the shortage
of land use due to the growing of inedible crops, not edible feedstocks are a preferable solution.
[31] Food waste is considered a not edible resource that can be used for the production of biofuels.
In this regard, food wastes can be adjusted to produce biofuels without using land intended for

which converts oils and fats of natural origin into fatty acid methyl esters. [30]
1.3.3
Animal feed
Due to the environmental impacts that food waste has, there is a need for redirection to a more

1.4
Aim
Materials and substances, most of the time, have an end-of-life date and they need to be dis-
carded. Many times, though, this period comes sooner, than might be expected, if the consumer
decides to. These materials that they no longer need, are considered residential waste. Waste
generation has always been an important topic for discussion, with governments making new
legislation but also individual people trying to reuse or recycle instead of throwing these items
away. The most common material fractions, from households, are paper, glass and plastic and
throughout the years those materials were the dominant ones for trying to find ways of handling
or sorting them.
Recent studies have shown that food waste was always an important topic but not mentioned
compared to the other material fractions. For the last couple of years, it is considered that one
third of the world’s food is being wasted every year creating the need for new measurements. [21]
The importance of food losses is rising considering its relatively high share in the global waste
composition, i.e. 44% of total waste. Therefore, there is a need of addressing the environmental
impacts associated with food losses considering the supply chain.
A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) report would help with quantifying the current problem, through
the environmental impacts that the losses cause, and would also create new alternatives which
might lead to better scenarios.
This thesis aims to quantify the environmental benefits associated with the implementation of
new approaches for the prevention of food losses in the food industry, as well as subsequent use
of the food products by consumers. The main purpose is to implement scenarios in EASETECH
software (a baseline scenario- or current situation - once selected and selected new scenarios) and
interpret the results. This, in a collaboration with the DTI, which provides the new technology
that will be implemented in the supply chain of two selected companies, Simple Feast and Cater
Food, where quantities and current management, as well as the types of food waste, are identified
based on information that will be provided by the companies.

2.1.1
LCA Methodology
Life cycle assessment is a methodology used for measuring the environmental impact that is
related to a given specific project, over its lifetime. It assesses the environmental impacts of
a product's life cycle. LCA has been standardized by ISO 14040 and gives the opportunity to
measure how the life cycle of a product contributes to an extensive set of environmental indicators.
[27] These are grouped with respect to, damage to human health, damage to ecosystem quality,
and resource depletion.
This way products and systems can be compared by using specific
environmental impacts. LCA provides information as to which product/system might have the
lowest or highest environmental impact on specific categories. LCA also shows in which life cycle
stage environmental impacts are highest.
Figure 4: LCA stages [39]

2.2.5
Animal feed
Animal feed can be considered as another alternative use for food waste, since it still has nu-
tritional value, especially for animals.
It is estimated that 1 tonne of dry food waste could
meet the protein requirements of a given animal as the same amount of maize grain. Maize, a
primary feed source containing 8%-10% of protein, can be replaced by food waste (19.2% protein).
Dehydration is the process of removing water, which prevents microbial growth and preserves
food quality. Food waste contains a high fraction of water in its total biomass, which is estimated
to be around 80%-90%. When the moister content is that high, it helps the microbial growth
which makes the food waste to a toxic substance. The direct drying process not only preserves the
food waste, but also offers reduction of weight, which enables easier packing and transportation
at low cost. After the dehydration, in the model, there is 10% water content left. [33]

EASETECH Model

consequential, long-term model was used. This system model uses substitution to substitute

4.2
Future Perspectives
Even though, a lot of assumptions where made during this study for the LCA modelling, the
scenarios with the DNAPROKON technology, showed that the 10% reduction of food waste
could provide considerable benefits to the environment. With more specific and accurate data
from the companies, this implementation could, probably, have more accurate results. A future
perspective could be that the operation of this technology will be implemented in a different part
of the supply chain. In this study, it was placed right after the storage but in a different case
Christina Martzakli
35
==PAGE:46==

it could be placed right after the purchase of the ingredients or before the preparation of the meal.
The raw materials might be purchased a while before the meals are produced, which means
that some of the ingredients might go bad, if they are not handled properly. Implementing this
solution before the food production, could make a difference, since it should affect the energy
used for the preparation and maintenance of the meal and the non edible food would be handled
properly and placed to be collected for the pre-treatment.
As another perspective that DNAPROKON could be tested, is that instead of just being imple-
mented in anaerobic digestion, could be done in combination with other end-of-life of food waste
processes like hydrolysis (for biodiesel) and dehydration (for animal feed). Composting, could
also be included as a new approach.
4.3
Recommendations
Based on the results of this study, a recommendation would be that the Canteen and Monday
shop from Simple Feast is actually a good solution also for Cater Food, since they don’t have
anything similar in their food supply chain.
Approximately 19% of the total food waste generated in the EU comes from to the processing
sector and 5% is generated in the wholesale and retail sector (around 5 million tonnes). [29] The
prevention of food waste has to be a top priority for food and beverage manufacturers. Many
companies have already started to adapt, trying to include sustainability strategies in their man-
agement. Since, the surplus of food cannot be avoided, the first consideration should be that
it can be redirected to people, as the food prevention hierarchy mentions.
There are many
opportunities in this sector to prevent and reduce food waste, including innovative packaging
solutions, offering a diversity of portion sizes, raising consumer awareness about date marking,
re-distributing surplus food and promoting circularity through food, feed and non-food uses.
[36], [20]
• Food re-distribution within the food sector and donations, especially for Cater Food. An
option could be to create a social media account informing when and where the food will
be distributed.
• As it has already been stated, animal feed is an option when the products are not possible
to be consumed by humans, so the companies could evaluate their options and find a way
to provide that surplus food to the proper treatment facilities.
• Packaging solutions that enhance food quality, freshness and safety. Find a balance between
reducing packaging material and ensuring food safety and waste prevention.
• Offer a range of portion sizes as different packaging sizes. There could be different ranges
between a single person and a family portion.
• Raise awareness about food waste prevention throughout the company’s organization. De-
velop training programs could reduce technical errors during processing.
• A digital tool to help monitor the supplies and create a better planning in order to have a
better overview of the balance between the demand and purchase of the products.
• Implementation of a system regarding the expiration dates, providing better instructions
to the costumers,in order for them to know when a product is still good to be consumed.

In this study, due to the fact that the companies producing the food are also the ones who sell
it, the recommendations will be applicable for both manufacturing and retail stage, but if in a
different case those two stages are not in coordination, a few recommendations would be: [36], [22]
• Support suppliers by improving the communication and their monitoring.
• Coordination between the manufactures and the retailers for better communication.
• Build consumer awareness concerning the food waste (i.e. advertisements).
• Create a database, for the store, that monitors the products, which could show when they
are close to their expiration date, and sell them in a lower price (i.e. Too Good To Go
[42]).
• Provide recipe ideas for food that it expires soon.
Hence, it is recommended that more research should be conducted to improve such technologies
and make data available for sustainability assessments.
4.4
Alternative Technologies
Composting could be considered as an alternative technology. Composting is when the organic
waste is exposed to oxygen through the use of living organisms. During aerobic decomposition of
organic waste, methane is greatly decreased (or even prevented), increasing crop productivity by
15-25%, while reducing the need for chemical fertilizers. Composting reduces or even prevents the
emission of methane during the breakdown of organic matter, allowing nutrients to be returned to
depleted soils and moisture to be retained, and it even results in lower greenhouse gas emissions.
[24]

5
Conclusions
On the total of 814,000 tonnes of food waste per year that are edible fractions, the DNAPROKON
technology showed that with the 10% of food and packaging savings, the contribution to the en-
vironmental impacts is beneficial. Considering that the implementation is already taking place,
food losses are expected to decrease since an extension of the life expectancy of the products will
be expected.
The variations in the flow are significant since they show potential fluctuation. The fluctuation
might occur from the seasonal sales and the ingredients that are available.
Prevention of avoidable food and packaging production is always preferable, as it may contribute
to larger savings in all of the environmental impacts. For Simple Feast, that has included re-
distribution into their supply chain and from the results it can be seen that with donations,
Monday shop and the canteen, their benefits in climate change are up to -127 kg CO2 for 1000
kg of food waste. Given that, Cater Food might need to consider adding re-distribution processes
into their supply chain.
Changing the management strategy for the food losses from anaerobic digestion to either of
the two other alternatives, biodiesel or animal feed, may in itself contribute with considerable
environmental benefits.
