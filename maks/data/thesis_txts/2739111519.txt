Abstract 
This thesis explores the design, construction, implementation, and testing of a pico 
hydropower plant at the historical Stubberup Mølle site in Denmark. The primary 
research objectives are twofold: first, to assess the feasibility of implementing a pico 
hydropower system at a low-power site, and second, to experimentally validate the 
performance of the Cink mechanism – a proposed flow regulation device aimed at 
enhancing the efficiency of crossflow turbines under partial-flow conditions. The study 
adopts a comprehensive approach, incorporating computational fluid dynamics 
simulations, experimental testing and validation, mechanical design, sensor integration, 
and field testing. 
The findings demonstrate that a fully functional pico hydropower system can be 
successfully implemented at the site, making a modest yet meaningful contribution to 
annual energy production. The ability of the Cink mechanism to maintain stable high 
efficiency at partial flows was observed to some extent, though not as pronounced as 
indicated by current research. While the mechanism did improve overall system 
efficiency at partial flows, the experimental setup revealed certain limitations that 
complicated the precise characterization of the Cink mechanism’s performance. These 
findings suggest that further optimization and research are necessary for a more 
accurate and nuanced understanding of its effects. 
The conclusions of this research underscore the potential of pico hydropower systems 
as a viable, sustainable energy solution for small-scale applications. This study provides 
valuable empirical data, marking an initial step toward bridging the gap between 
theoretical models and real-world performance of the Cink mechanism, while laying a 
foundation for future advancements and further research in this area.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The global transition to sustainable energy sources is one of the most pressing 
challenges facing the world today. Due to the rising uncertainty and environmental and 
economic costs associated with fossil fuels, there is a growing interest in exploring and 
developing alternative and renewable energy sources. Among the various sources of 
sustainable energy, hydropower remains one of the most widely utilized, having played 
a significant role throughout human history. 
While large-scale hydropower plants are well-established and widely used worldwide, 
there is increasing interest in small-scale alternatives, such as micro and pico 
hydropower systems. These systems utilize the power of flowing water in smaller creeks 
and streams, presenting a potential solution for decentralized, sustainable energy 
production, particularly in remote or rural areas without access to stable grid power. 
One specific technology used to convert the energy in moving water into usable energy 
is the crossflow turbine, also known as the Ossberger or Michell-Banki turbine. 
Compared to other turbine types, the crossflow turbine offers several advantages, 
including a simple design, ease of manufacture, low cost, and self-cleaning properties 
that help it resist debris. However, it suffers from lower efficiency compared to other 
turbine types. Addressing this efficiency issue could significantly enhance the viability 
and attractiveness of crossflow turbines in small-scale, decentralized sustainable energy 
systems. 
One method to improve efficiency, especially at partial flow rates, is the innovative flow 
regulation device proposed by Miroslav Cink. However, the performance of the Cink 
mechanism has only been studied theoretically through computational fluid dynamics 
simulations, particularly by Adhikari (insert reference), which have shown promising 
results in maintaining higher turbine efficiency at lower flow rates. 
In an effort to contribute valuable research to this field and evaluate the performance of 
the Cink mechanism in a practical setting, this thesis involves the design and 
construction of a small-scale or pico hydropower system in the field, serving as a test 
bench for the Cink mechanism. The selected test site for the hydropower system is 
Stubberup Mølle, Denmark, a location with a rich history of utilizing hydropower for 
centuries. Stubberup Mølle features a mill pond fed by an underground spring that 
provides a steady flow of water throughout the year. Throughout this thesis, the specific 
hydropower system developed and tested will be referred to as the “Stubberup System,” 
while the project itself will be denoted the “Stubberup Hydropower Project” for clarity.

2 Theoretical Framework 
This chapter establishes the theoretical foundation for the design, implementation, and 
evaluation of the hydropower system in the Stubberup Hydropower Project. It covers 
fundamental principles, key components, and essential terminology related to 
hydropower. This provides the understanding necessary for evaluating hydropower 
potential and feasibility, as well as for designing the system and its components, and 
experimentally testing the Stubberup System performance. 
2.1 Hydropower Fundamentals 
This section covers the fundamental principles and terminology of hydro power, creating 
the necessary base of understanding and context for the rest of this thesis. It will cover 
the general structure and components of hydro power plants and specify how hydraulic 
power and its primary equation constituents are defined and quantified. It is important 
knowledge affecting the entirety of the work, acting as framework and design guideline 
for the system designed and implemented in this thesis. In addition, it serves as an initial 
base for evaluating hydropower potential and feasibility at a given site.  
2.1.1 Hydropower System Overview  
This subsection briefly introduces the essential components of a hydropower system, 
and the related terminology used throughout this thesis. It forms the necessary 
understanding of the basic functions of each component and their interplay and outlines 
the components to be designed, fabricated and implemented in the Stubberup 
Hydropower Project.  
Hydropower plants are often classified according to their output capacity, where the 
smallest capacity category is the “pico” hydropower plant with an output capability up to 
5 kW [3]. Initial measurements and estimations have indicated that the hydraulic power 
available at Stubberup Mølle is well below 5 kW, hence making the Stubberup System 
fall into the pico hydropower plant category. 
A simplified overview of the Stubberup System and its components are illustrated in 
Figure 2.1. Although the sketch specifically outlines the Stubberup System, it 
corresponds closely to the general composition of most other larger scale hydropower 
systems.

Below is a numbered list of the elements shown in Figure 2.1. 
1. Reservoir and headwater 
The reservoir contains an elevated body of water known as the headwater, which stores 
potential energy critical for the hydropower system. The elevation, or head, of the 
headwater relative to a reference plane, typically the tailwater elevation, is a key factor 
in determining the power potential of the hydropower system. This elevation difference 
maximizes the available potential energy for conversion into mechanical and electrical 
energy. The reservoir will be referred to as the "millpond" throughout this thesis, 
acknowledging its historical purpose. 
 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
 
Figure 2.2: View of the mill pond, looking towards the outlet. 
 
2. Dam 
The dam is the physical structure that enables the crucial elevation differential between 
the headwater and tailwater, increasing the height from which water falls and thereby 
enhancing the potential energy available for conversion. Since Stubberup Mølle was 
once a mill, the dam is already constructed, providing a historical infrastructure that can 
be adapted for the hydropower project. 
3. Penstock 
The penstock is a pipe or series of pipes that transport water from the millpond to the 
turbine, capable of handling the hydrostatic pressure developed by the overlying water. 
In the Stubberup System, a siphon penstock is used instead of a direct pipe from the 
millpond to the turbine. This siphon configuration offers several benefits, including simple 
aboveground installation, which eliminates the need for extensive excavation, easier 
maintenance access to the intake and penstock, and increased flexibility in positioning, 
adjustments, and alterations. 
4. Trash rack 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
A trash rack is a filter or mesh that prevents larger debris and wildlife from entering the 
system, potentially clogging or damaging the turbine and harming the animals. The 
aperture size of the filter determines the maximum debris size that can pass through, 
ensuring only particles of acceptable size enter the system. The pre-existing trash racks 
of the two main spillways, as shown in Figure 2.3, have a coarse grid that allows most 
leaves and other plant matter to pass through. The Stubberup System will require a finer 
mesh for effective animal and component protection, ensuring reliable long-term 
operation. 
 
 
Figure 2.3: Existing trash racks at the two main Ø150 mm spillway inlets. 
 
5. Gates 
The gate in a hydropower system is a large valve that controls the amount of water 
entering the penstock. It allows for safe and complete shutdown of water flow in 
emergency situations or when the system needs maintenance. In the case of a siphon 
penstock, as used in the Stubberup System, the siphon needs to be primed because it 
is not self-starting. Priming involves creating the necessary conditions for the siphon to 
function. This is achieved by using two gate valves at either end of the penstock to carry 
out the following steps: shutting off both gates, filling the penstock completely with water 
through an access port, closing the access port, and finally opening both gates to start 
the siphon effect. 
6. Spillway 
A spillway serves as a safety measure against high flow, preventing overflow of the 
millpond by redirecting excess water through a duct or pipe situated at the maximum 
allowable elevation of the headwater. In the Stubberup System, this function is achieved 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
by two primary Ø150 mm pipes that determine the headwater elevation, along with two 
additional Ø75 mm pipes positioned higher to provide further overflow protection.    
 
 
Figure 2.4: View of the two existing spillway exits, as seen from the tailwater. 
 
7. Turbine and nozzle 
A turbine is the mechanical device that converts hydraulic power into rotational shaft 
power. The nozzle directs water to the turbine at an optimal angle and converts pressure 
into velocity, ensuring efficient energy transfer and maximizing the turbine's 
performance. 
8. Draft tube 
The draft tube is a specially shaped tube located directly after the turbine, designed to 
extract residual energy from the discharge water. This process allows for greater power 
output by utilizing more of the available energy and makes use of the elevation drop from 
the turbine to the tailwater. 
9. Generator and gearing 
The generator converts mechanical shaft power into electrical power, making it more 
suitable for use by modern appliances. The gearing between the turbine shaft and the 
generator ensures that both devices can operate at their optimal performance levels. 
10. Tailwater 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
The tailwater, or discharge water, is the water exiting the system through the draft tube 
outlet after completing its energy conversion process. The vertical distance between the 
tailwater elevation and headwater elevation directly influences the available hydraulic 
power. In the Stubberup System, the tailwater forms the origin of a small stream, 
transporting the water away from the system. 
2.1.2 Definitions and energy equations 
When examining the mechanical energy exchange and conversion of a fluid moving from 
one point in space to another, for instance from reservoir to tailwater in a hydropower 
plant, the Bernoulli equation in equation 2.1 is an obvious tool for such analysis [4, p. 
199]. This fundamental equation relates pressure, P, velocity, V, and elevation, z, also 
termed head, H, in an incompressible steady flow fluid under the assumption that viscous 
forces are small compared to inertial, pressure and gravitational effects. It basically 
assumes the mechanical energy of a fluid particle moving along a streamline is constant 
in position 1 and position 2, if effects of friction, compressibility and heat transfer are 
neglected. In this research Bernoulli’s equation is a useful tool providing reasonable 
approximations of initial and rough estimates of system performance and for derivations 
of velocities, pressures and elevations throughout the system.

As the Bernoulli equation is derived from a force balance of a fluid particle moving along 
a streamline, it is not applicable for sections of fluid flow containing a device adding or 
extracting mechanical shaft power such as a pump or turbine. It is however applicable 
for the flow section prior to and past the device, noting that the constant will be different 
in the two sections due to power being either added or extracted from the fluid. 
By measuring, simulating, or otherwise approximating two terms of the equation, the 
remaining term can be derived, which reduces the need for additional sensors to 
measure its value. In the initial stages of the hydropower system design, the Bernoulli 
equation allows for the calculation of rough estimates, such as water velocity at the 
turbine nozzle and static pressure at various points along the pipe system. 
A useful way of visualizing the energy of a fluid described mathematically by the Bernoulli 
equation, is through an energy grade line (EGL), expressing each term as its equivalent 
elevation energy. The EGL is defined as the line that represents total head of the fluid, 
P/(ρ g) + V2/(2 g) + z, comprised of elevation head, z, pressure head, P/(ρ g), and velocity 
head, V2/(2 g) [4, p. 205].

water's energy at this point is entirely attributed to gravitational potential, corresponding 
to the headwater's elevation.  Following the fluid path through the system, the ideal EGL 
– assuming no losses – remains horizontal and constant. This reflects energy 
conservation, although in varying constituents of velocity, pressure and elevation as the 
initial elevation is converted to pressure and velocity. When the water reaches the

2.2 Theory of Turbines and Hydropower System Components 
The turbine, penstock, and draft tube have a vital influence on the performance of the 
hydropower system. Understanding their functioning and working principles is crucial for 
optimizing system performance. This chapter delves into the theoretical aspects of these 
components, providing insights into their roles, design considerations, and impacts on 
overall efficiency. 
2.2.1 Turbine Types and Selection  
This section provides a brief overview of the different types of hydro turbines and the 
criteria for selecting the most appropriate type for a specific site and application. 
Selecting the appropriate turbine is crucial for ensuring efficient and optimal energy 
conversion as well as reliable operation. The choice of turbine significantly impacts 
various other components of the hydropower system, including generator selection, 
gearing, and the mechanical design of the turbine casing and draft tube. Proper turbine 
selection enhances the overall performance and integration of the hydropower system. 
Hydro turbines can be divided into two main categories: impulse turbines and reaction 
turbines.  
In an impulse turbine, pressure energy in the water is converted to kinetic energy in a 
nozzle, creating a high-velocity water jet. This jet impinges on the turbine blades, creating 
an impulse force as it is deflected and its momentum changes. The impulse force imparts 
torque on the shaft, causing it to rotate. The most common impulse turbines, illustrated 
in Figure 2.7, include the Pelton turbine, Turgo turbine, and crossflow turbine, such as 
the one implemented in the Stubberup system. All three turbines rotate in an air-filled 
cavity, rather than being submerged in water, as that would otherwise cause 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
unnecessary drag forces on the rotor and power dissipation of the jet, decreasing power 
output and efficiency. 
 
 
Figure 2.7: Simplified illustrations of the most common impulse turbines: crossflow 
turbine (left), Pelton turbine (center) and Turgo turbine (right). 
 
Reaction turbines differ from impulse turbines in that the turbine is fully submerged in 
water, and the pressure drop occurs as the water passes through the turbine rather than 
in a nozzle. Reaction turbines rely on both hydrodynamic lift forces and momentum 
exchange with the water to generate shaft torque. The two main types of reaction 
turbines are the Francis turbine and the Kaplan turbine, as illustrated in Figure 2.8. Since 
it has already been established that a crossflow turbine is implemented in the Stubberup 
system, reaction turbines will not be elaborated further. 
 
 
Figure 2.8: Illustrations of the two main types of reaction turbines: Francis turbine (left) 
and Kaplan turbine (right).

Flow variability is a common characteristic of hydropower sites due to seasonal 
variations in rainfall and meltwater from snow and ice. However, the consistent flow 
provided by the spring feeding the millpond significantly reduces these variations, with 
increases occurring only during rainy periods. Since the flow through the penstock is 
limited to less than the total flow, the turbines will only experience variations due to minor 
head fluctuations. Therefore, high efficiency at partial flows – flows below the design or 
maximum flow rate – is not critical for the Stubberup system. In the event of system 
expansion to utilize the full flow, flow variability would become more critical. Thus, 
selecting a turbine with favorable part-flow efficiency can enhance the system's

2.2.2 Crossflow Turbine  
This section describes the basic principles, advantages, and disadvantages of the 
crossflow turbine (CFT) in the Stubberup System, also known as the Banki, Mitchell, or 
Ossberger turbine. Detailed design principles and parameters for creating an efficient 
CFT and nozzle are covered in Section 3.1. This overview provides an understanding of 
the fundamental aspects of CFTs in the context of their application in the Stubberup 
System, setting the stage for more in-depth discussions on their design and application. 
As previously noted, the CFT is of the impulse turbine type, meaning it exchanges 
momentum with a jet of water to generate power. A side view of a typical CFT showing 
the flow path of water and its components is illustrated in Figure 2.14. The construction 
of the turbine rotor is remarkably similar to that of a radial impeller, with two flat discs as 
sides, a shaft passing through the center axis, and semicircular blades spanning each 
side disc placed around the outer perimeter. 
 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
 
Figure 2.14: Schematic illustration of a CFT, showing the path of water and the main 
components of its construction. 
 
Governing the flow to the turbine is conventionally done using an adjustable guide vane 
inside the nozzle, which can be rotated to provide more or less constriction to the flow 
path. A more efficient flow regulation device, the Cink mechanism, is introduced in 
Section 3.1. The specially shaped nozzle with a rectangular cross-section converts 
pressure head to velocity head, directing the water jet radially and evenly at the correct 
angle to the turbine blades. The high tangential velocity water passes through the blades 
of the first stage, traverses the airspace inside the rotor, until it encounters the backside 
of the blades and passes through the second stage. The first stage accounts for roughly 
70 percent of the total power extraction, while the second stage extracts the remaining 
30 percent [9, p. 179]. When the water leaves the second stage, ideally all its initial 
tangential velocity is extracted. The water then falls to the free water surface inside the 
draft tube, simultaneously acting as a casing for the turbine. An adjustable air inlet valve 
ensures that the height of the free surface does not back up and flood the rotor, which 
would cause power loss due to drag between the rotor and the surrounding water.  
The advantages of CFTs, some of which are covered in Section 2.2.1, are listed below 
[1, p. 43][10]: 
• 
Versatility because it works with a wide range of heads and flow rates. 
• 
Simple design 
• 
Low-cost manufacturing 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
• 
Self-cleaning 
• 
Minimal maintenance requirements 
• 
High part-flow efficiency enables power production even in low-flow periods. 
• 
Zero axial thrust on bearings allows use of simple low maintenance bearings. 
In regards to the site characteristics set forth in Section 2.2.1, CFTs meet the 
requirements for low maintenance and have exceptional debris resistance. The low 
environmental impact is indirectly achieved because the turbine is well-suited for both 
low head and low flow, meaning fewer requirements for the construction of large dams 
and diverting large masses of water to the turbine. 
The limitations of CFTs, already addressed in Section 2.2.1, are restated below. Note 
that they are either irrelevant to the conditions at Stubberup Mølle or will be addressed 
later: 
• 
Lower maximum efficiency compared to other common turbine types such as 
Pelton and Francis. 
• 
Limited maximum capacity and operating head compared to other turbine types.  
Generally, impulse turbine performance can be estimated analytically with reasonable 
accuracy using the Euler turbomachine equation and velocity triangles. This method 
works well for Pelton and Turgo turbines, where the jet behavior is predictable and simple 
in terms of geometry and flow characteristics. However, for crossflow turbines, with their 
complex nozzle geometry and flow field inside the turbine, these analytical methods 
become less accurate. The simplifications of the Euler equation and velocity triangles 
break down, necessitating the use of alternative approaches such as computational fluid 
dynamics, empirical data from past experimental studies, practical experience with 
turbine operation, and an iterative design process, as described further in this thesis. 
Therefore, this section will not delve into these analytical methods.  
The efficiency curve of a CFT follows a parabolic shape, similar to that of other impulse 
turbines, as illustrated in Figure 2.15. Although not depicted in the figure, the torque-
speed characteristics of a CFT are linear, with maximum torque occurring at zero 
rotational speed and decreasing linearly to zero torque as the speed reaches its 
maximum. 
 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
 
Figure 2.15: Example experimental efficiency curve as a function of revolutions per 
minute [13, p. 21]. 
 
The maximum efficiency point on this curve corresponds to a rotational speed close to 
half the tangential inlet velocity of the water jet. At zero rotational speed and zero 
efficiency, the torque is at its maximum. Conversely, when efficiency drops to zero again, 
the torque is zero and the speed reaches its maximum, typically at 1.8 times the design 
speed [1, p. 74]. If the head changes, the resulting nozzle velocity increases, leading to 
a different optimum operating speed. Identifying and maintaining the optimum operating 
speed is essential for maximizing power extraction, especially at low-power sites like 
Stubberup Mølle. Fortunately, from observations, the water level and thus the head of 
the millpond is very stable throughout the year, thanks to a constant spring fed water 
supply and high-capacity spillways. This translates to a very stable optimum operating 
speed, simplifying the design process and ensuring consistent performance.

true for a siphon penstock, where the pipes are not supported and held in place by a

2.2.4 Draft Tube  
Most turbines need to be installed at least 2 meters above the tailwater for economical 
and practical reasons [12, p. 77], and in the case of impulse turbines, to allow for an 
airspace for the rotor to operate. In most high-head impulse turbines, the turbine simply 
rotates in atmospheric pressure. In a low-head setting, such as Stubberup Mølle, this 
would waste significant power potential due to the unrealized head from the turbine 
elevation to the tailwater. The draft tube allows recovery of most of this power potential, 
increasing the project feasibility of low-power sites. Draft tubes are not used with high-
head impulse turbines, as the relative power increase of a 2-meter gain in net head is 
negligible compared to, for example, a 500-meter gross head. 
The draft tube works by inducing a low pressure directly after the turbine, corresponding 
to a greater pressure differential over the nozzle, which facilitates a greater flow rate and 
jet velocity. This low pressure is caused by the standing water column inside the draft 
tube, whose height is controlled by the air inlet valve. If the air inlet valve is fully closed, 
the suction head is maximized and equal to the turbine elevation. However, in this case, 
the column would rise to fill the entire draft tube, flooding the turbine and resulting in drag 
and lowered efficiency. If the valve opens too much, the column height decreases 
unnecessarily along with the pressure differential, reducing power production. The 
optimum setting of the air inlet valve is a compromise where the height of the column is 
just below the turbine, avoiding drag losses while maximizing the pressure differential. It 
==PAGE:31==
23 
 
is important to note that for the column to develop, the outlet of the draft tube must always 
be submerged under the tailwater elevation. 
The draft tube is an integral part of a reaction turbine, and those cases have a 
sophisticated design, with attention paid to the geometry and rate of expansion to 
maximize the efficiency of energy recovery. For a CFT, the draft tube shape and 
construction are less complex, usually represented by a simple rectangular or circular 
constant cross-section tube. A large cross-sectional area decreases the average flow 
velocity, converting kinetic energy to useful pressure energy. Due to the simpler 
geometry, the flow interruption posed by the intermediate airspace, and the water column 
reduction caused by the air inlet valve, the efficiency of a CFT draft tube is not as high 
as for a reaction turbine.

This chapter discusses the electrical aspect of a hydropower system, which is a critical

2.3.1 Electrical System Overview 
The electrical domain of a hydropower system consists of components critical for 
converting mechanical power to useful electrical power; ensuring adaptable and

2.3.2 Generator 
The generator is a critical component in the electrical system, as it converts mechanical 
shaft power from the turbine into electrical power that can be utilized by everyday 
appliances. Understanding the different generator types and their relevant performance 
characteristics is essential for designing an efficient energy conversion system and 
ensuring compatibility with other electrical and mechanical components.  
Mechanically, a generator is comprised of a rotating part, the rotor, and a stationary part, 
the stator, rotating relative to each other. One of these parts generates a magnetic field, 
while the other contains wire or armature windings. As the rotor is driven by an external 
power source, the armature windings experience a changing magnetic field, inducing an 
electric current therein.  
Knowledge covered in the following subsections is crucial for: 
• 
Proper selection of generator type and specifications 
• 
Design of generator test bench and relevant experiments 
• 
Matching of turbine-generator performance characteristics for maximum 
efficiency and power extraction  
• 
Ensuring compatibility with power electronics 
• 
Achieving long-term, reliable operation 
Generator Types 
Generator variations are numerous, and the specific setup in which it is integrated 
determines the most appropriate type for the application. The generator criteria and 
circumstances for the Stubberup System are as follows: 
• 
Low power output  
• 
Low-speed turbine 
• 
Variable speed compatibility, necessary due to unknown turbine characteristics 
in early design process and minor head fluctuations. 
• 
DC power demanded by power electronics including the battery and MPPT 
charge controller.  
• 
Capable of operating during power outages. 
• 
High efficiency prioritization 
• 
Economic constraints 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
The overarching generator types are the asynchronous (induction) generator, the 
brushed DC generator, and the synchronous generator. Both induction and synchronous 
generators are AC machines that require external circuits to generate DC power. 
An asynchronous generator, also known as an induction generator, outputs AC power 
when its rotor is mechanically rotated above its synchronous speed. The synchronous 
speed is determined by the grid frequency and the number of generator poles. 
Advantages of asynchronous generators include their simple and robust design, making 
them cost-effective for certain applications. However, their disadvantages include 
reduced efficiency at variable speeds and the need for reactive power support from the 
grid or capacitors. Based on these characteristics, the asynchronous generator is 
assessed to be unsuitable for the Stubberup system. 
The brushed DC generator produces DC power without relying on grid connection by 
implementing a built-in mechanical switching device that rectifies AC power from the 
armature windings into DC power. This switching device periodically reverses the 
armature conductor polarity as the shaft rotates, using a commutator attached to the 
shaft and brushes attached to the stator. The advantage of the brushed DC generator is 
its simplicity of operation, as it requires no external components to transform AC to DC 
power. In addition, it can be designed to operate over a wide range of speeds [2, p. 780]. 
However, the commutator and brushes come with drawbacks, including added 
mechanical complexity, frequent maintenance requirements, shorter service life, and 
reduced efficiency due to mechanical friction losses between the commutator and 
brushes [2, p. 776, 855]. These downsides render the brushed DC generator 
incompatible with the Stubberup system requirements, especially due to the continuous 
operation of the system and low maintenance requirements. 
Synchronous generators are suitable for lower-speed applications compared to induction 
generators and are used almost exclusively in large-scale electric power generation [2, 
p. 839]. They are characterized by the rotor speed being equal to or synchronous with 
the speed of the rotating magnetic field. Because of the composition and functioning of 
the electrical system in the Stubberup system, grid-connected synchronous machines 
are not applicable and thus not covered in this section. Synchronous machines display 
several characteristics required by the Stubberup system, particularly the permanent 
magnet synchronous generator (PMSG). 
A PMSG has a magnetic field supplied by permanent magnets (PM) and a behavior 
much like the brushed DC generator [2, p. 856]. As opposed to the magnetic field coming 
from a field winding excited by an external a power source, increasing losses and 
operational complexity. Therefore, one of the advantages of a PMSG is its relatively high 
efficiency. They are primarily used in lower-power applications, are compatible with 
harsh environments, and feature a long service life with minimal maintenance due to 
their simple construction [2, p. 856] [7, p. 191]. Unlike induction generators and grid-tied 
synchronous machines, a PMSG is not dependent on a grid or limited to a single 
rotational speed but can generate power efficiently over a wide range of speeds. As the 
PMSG is virtually identical in construction to a brushless DC motor (BLDC), a BLDC can 
be operated as a PMSG. The commercial market for three-phase BLDCs is much greater 
and more diverse in its selection than PMSGs, both in specifications and cost, 
significantly expanding the available selection of suitable generators. 
For generating DC power, using the three-phase PMSG in conjunction with a rectifier 
circuit is generally favored over the use of DC generators with commutators [2, p. 802, 
==PAGE:35==
27 
 
806]. This approach, along with the consideration of the advantages and disadvantages 
of different generator types, is why the Stubberup system employs a three-phase BLDC 
as a generator paired with a three-phase full-bridge rectifier.

Turbine-Generator Maximum Efficiency Matching 
This section discusses the importance of matching the maximum efficiency points of the 
turbine and generator to optimize the overall performance of the hydropower system.  
As seen in Figure 2.15 and Figure 2.19, both the CFT and PMSG display nonlinear 
efficiency curves as a function of rotational speed. Assuming constant head, the speed 
at maximum efficiency of a CFT coincides with its maximum power point. For a specific 
load case or power input, the PMSG will also have a certain speed where its efficiency 
is maximized. Unless the exact operating conditions and performance of the turbine are 
known early in the design phase, and a commercial or custom-designed generator 
matching the exact operating speed at the exact power input can be obtained, the turbine 
and generator optimums will most likely not coincide.  
Therefore, gearing between the turbine shaft and generator shaft will be implemented in 
the Stubberup System, allowing flexibility in generator selection and accommodating the 
performance characteristics of the turbine. Designing this gearing requires detailed 
knowledge of the specific generator and turbine performance, obtained through 
experiments and simulations. When optimizing a single operating point, the ideal gear 
ratio is simply the optimum rotational speed of the turbine divided by the optimum 
rotational speed of the generator – or vice versa – for the given load-case. From this 
gear ratio, appropriate gears can be selected with teeth numbers matching or 
approaching the value.   
2.3.3 Power Electronics 
Power electronics are crucial in the Stubberup System, affecting overall system 
efficiency, component selection, specifications, and requirements. Understanding the 
functions and requirements of each component, such as voltage and current limits, is 
vital in the design and selection process. This process is iterative and lengthy due to the 
interconnected nature of the components and their dependence on turbine 
characteristics, while also being constrained by regulations and limitations of system 
complexity and financial resources. 
Full Bridge Three Phase Rectifier  
As described in Subsection 2.3.2, an AC machine with a rectifier is favored for DC power 
production. The three-phase full bridge rectifier converts the three-phase AC input to DC 
output using six diodes in a bridge configuration, where each diode only allows current 
to flow in one direction. In this configuration, pairs of diodes conduct alternately to rectify 
each phase of the AC input, ensuring continuous conduction. The resultant DC waveform 
is reasonably smooth but contains some voltage ripple, dependent on the speed and 
number of poles in the generator. The ripple in the DC signal must be within acceptable 
limits dictated by the MPPT charge controller. The ripple can be reduced and the DC 
signal smoothed by adding capacitance, but often the inductances and inertia of the 
generator help smooth the ripple [2, p. 802]. 
 
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
 
Figure 2.22: Schematic illustration of a three phase AC waveform compared to its 
resultant DC waveform [41]. 
 
The diodes used in the rectifier have an inherent forward voltage drop of around 1.2V 
total, causing a concurrent power loss. The magnitude of the power loss is more 
pronounced at higher current levels, and the forward voltage drop is dependent on the 
temperature of the diodes. Sufficient cooling of the rectifier to dissipate heat energy and 
ensure efficient operation is important. Running the generator at a higher speed with 
associated higher voltages will reduce the current for a given power, thus reducing 
rectifier losses.  
The unavoidable power loss associated with using a full bridge rectifier in the electrical 
system is accepted due to the added benefits of high flexibility, reliability, robustness, 
and efficiency of a PMSG compared to a DC generator with a commutator. 
MPPT Charge Controller 
The MPPT charge controller, or simply MPPT, converts rectified DC power from the 
generator into a suitable DC voltage determined by the battery type, nominal voltage, 
and state of charge. Simultaneously, it ensures maximum power is being extracted by 
the turbine-generator complex. Compared to pulse-width modulated (PWM) charge 
controllers, an MPPT can achieve a 30 percent increase in efficiency [25].  
The maximum power point of the turbine-generator is realized through impedance 
matching, which involves matching the electrical load impedance to the power source to 
maximize power transfer. In the context of the turbine-generator complex in the 
Stubberup System, this means adjusting the effective resistance presented by the load,

The effective resistance is obtained in practice by adjusting the current flow through the 
MPPT, which to the generator appears as varying resistance. As described in Subsection 
2.3.2, changing the load through current will change the torque, which in turn will change 
the speed of the turbine-generator. The algorithm in the MPPT constantly monitors the 
incoming electric power, and depending on the specific algorithm, regularly perturbs the 
effective resistance. Measured power at the perturbed load is then compared to the 
previous power value to determine whether the perturbance was applied in the correct 
direction. This iterative tracking method happens continuously through the use of a highly 
efficient DC to DC voltage converter.  
Readily available MPPT charge controllers are almost exclusively designed for solar 
systems, but they have been reported to work well with hydro turbines as well [15] [16]. 
Battery  
The battery is the electrochemical component of the power electronics, storing electrical 
energy as chemical energy for later conversion back to electrical energy. To illustrate 
why this function is necessary, the following circumstances must be considered: 
The continuous output of the Stubberup System is greater than the base load of the 
household with most appliances shut off at night, but a fraction of the peak power 
demands during activities like cooking, while it might be equal to or slightly below 
average power consumption. Without a battery, excess power would be exported to the 
grid at night and sold at the given price, while power would need to be imported from the 
grid during peak use hours at a higher price. This means excess energy is sold at a less 
favorable price, and power is bought at a more expensive rate [17] [18]. 
Using an energy storage device like a battery offsets this disadvantage by storing energy 
during low demand periods and allowing large power draws during peak hours without 
grid dependence. This cycle repeats daily.  
The cyclic nature of battery operation in such a system requires consideration to ensure 
long-term and efficient operation. Battery life is related to the number of cycles it has 
==PAGE:42==
34 
 
undergone, as the internal structure degrades with aging and the natural electrochemical 
process of charging and discharging, reducing battery capacity over time. This effect is 
more pronounced for deeper discharges [19]. 
Battery chemistry significantly impacts the battery's rate of capacity decline. The deep-
cycle lead-acid battery, specifically optimized for deeper discharge, starts declining after 
100 to 200 cycles, while a lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery can have a cycle life of 
more than 2000 full cycles [20] [21]. MPPT charge controllers and inverters are most 
often designed around lead-acid battery characteristics. However, LFP batteries can be 
used interchangeably with lead-acid batteries, as they come in the same nominal voltage 
and voltage range [22]. In addition to high cycle life, LFP batteries offer greater specific 
energy, meaning they have less volume and mass than equivalent lead-acid batteries 
[21]. Although these advantages come at a higher cost, they are outweighed by the many 
benefits of LFP batteries, especially over time [22]. 
Another factor to consider is the coulombic and energy efficiency of the battery. 
Coulombic efficiency (CE) is the ratio of extracted discharge energy to input charging 
energy over a full cycle. Due to internal resistance and the electrochemical process, 
some energy is lost during a cycle. For a lead-acid battery, the CE is typically 90%, while 
for a lithium battery, it can be upwards of 99.9%, meaning that an LFP battery will waste 
significantly less energy. Energy efficiency accounts for the rate of discharge relative to 
battery capacity over the cycle. It is typically lower than CE by a few percent due to higher 
internal losses at higher discharge rates. This effect can be mitigated by choosing a 
higher capacity battery [23]. 
Taking these characteristics into consideration justifies the implementation of an LFP 
battery in the Stubberup System, maximizing energy utilization and improving the 
financial feasibility of a small but continuous power source such as the turbine in the 
Stubberup system. 
Hybrid Grid-Tied Inverter 
The term "hybrid" implies additional functionality compared to a standard grid-tied 
inverter, as it allows integration of a battery and associated battery management 
features, such as charging, discharging, regulation, and safety functions. It converts the 
DC power from the battery to AC power matching the grid's frequency and voltage, while 
also being able to convert AC grid power to DC for charging the battery.  
This component is critical as it connects the electrical system to the grid while also 
affecting battery charging and discharge. The inverter contains important safety 
functions and must ensure quality power is fed to the grid, meeting regulations and 
technical requirements defined by authorities such as Green Power Denmark. Using an 
approved inverter means the integrated battery and energy source, typically a solar panel 
but in this case a turbine, are not subject to the positive list requirements [24]. 
The range of suitable hybrid inverters complying with the rest of the electrical system 
was narrow. The chosen inverter is compatible with the generator and battery voltage, 
efficient in its power conversion, and provides useful functionality such as providing 
power during outages, powering critical loads, and allowing battery charging during low-
cost energy periods for use during high-demand hours.

3.1 High Efficiency Crossflow Turbine, Nozzle and Flow

The design principles for the CFT employed in the Stubberup Hydropower Project are 
primarily derived from the extensive research conducted by Ram Adhikari in his doctoral 
thesis, "Design Improvements of Crossflow Hydro Turbine", submitted to the University 
of Calgary [9]. Adhikari’s thesis offers a comprehensive analysis and a detailed 
methodology for optimizing CFT design, which has been instrumental in guiding the 
development of the Stubberup System’s turbine. 
Adhikari’s research focuses on optimizing the efficiency of CFTs through the application 
of advanced computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations coupled with extensive 
experimental validation. His work has resulted in significant performance improvements 
for small-scale, low-head hydropower systems, particularly under site conditions similar 
to those at Stubberup Mølle. Additionally, Adhikari introduces the Cink mechanism, a 
flow regulation device initially proposed by Miroslav Cink, which is specifically designed 
to optimize CFT efficiency under part-flow conditions by enabling low-loss flow 
regulation.

from Adhikari’s research that have been applied in this thesis. The following areas are of 
particular significance for this study:

• 
Optimized analytical nozzle geometry model 
• 
CFD study structure and methodology 
• 
Deeper understanding of the governing phenomena in a CFT and what 
characterizes an efficient design, especially the importance of the nozzle in 
converting pressure head to velocity head, the complexity of the flow field inside 
a CFT, and the impact of the Cink mechanism.

phases repeated iteratively: 
1. Preliminary design phase: Establishing turbine configuration and calculating

4.1 Initial measurements and feasibility  
The initial measurements of the hydropower equation terms, flow rate, and gross head, 
determine the project's feasibility. These fundamental measurements are crucial 
throughout the Stubberup Hydropower Project, influencing most calculations and design 
decisions. 
4.1.1 Flow Rate Measurement 
Flow rate is a fundamental term in the power equation, extensively used in the Stubberup 
hydropower project for power and energy calculations and the turbine design process. It 
is determined at multiple stages throughout the project, generally with increasingly 
accurate methods as the project progresses. 
The initial flow estimate was a historical value available online. However, the accuracy 
or measurement method of this value is unknown, necessitating additional 
measurements for validation. 
Bucket-Timer Method 
For a rough estimate of the flow rate, the bucket-timer method was utilized. It relies on 
the definition of volume flow rate: fluid volume per time. The fluid volume was measured 
using a large bucket of known capacity, and the time interval to fill the bucket was 
measured using a phone timer. For increased accuracy, the measurement was repeated 
several times, and the values were averaged. 
Figure 4.1 illustrates the basic layout of the bucket-timer experiment.

Two different bucket sizes were used for Spillway 1 and 2 due to significantly different 
flow rates. For Spillway 1, with the greatest flow rate, a 90 L bucket was chosen, while

The experiment required two people: one person to position the bucket under the jet and

4.1.3 Feasibility and Installation Permission 
This section describes the methodology used to determine the feasibility of the project 
during the early stages. Feasibility depends on obtaining permission from local 
authorities to install the system in protected nature, permission from the local utility 
company to connect the turbine to the grid, and determining if the estimated power 
generation justifies the project's efforts and costs. 
The millpond, stream, and surrounding nature are protected by Natura 2000 and 
paragraph 3, requiring detailed justification and protective measures for wildlife and 
ecosystems. Correspondence with the municipality's nature group and inspection by a 
biologist were necessary steps. Safety features such as fine intake mesh and minimal 
construction size were implemented, documented in the project description in Appendix 
9.6. Due to these protective measures and biologist approval, installation permission was 
granted. 
Permission for grid connection was granted by the local utility company due to the 
system's small predicted power capacity and the use of an approved grid-tie inverter.

the hydropower equation, and estimated efficiencies of the turbine, generator, and power 
electronics. Initial calculations can be found in Appendix 9.7. Feasibility was determined 
by comparing the household's yearly average energy consumption to the estimated 
yearly energy production of the Stubberup System.

4.2 Generator Selection and Characterization 
The generator is a crucial component whose required characteristics are based on initial 
hydropower and turbine calculations. Characterizing the PMSG is important for 
designing the gearing to ensure optimal turbine-generator matching and for selecting the 
appropriate power electronic components. This process involves simulations for early 
estimates and experimental characterization for later stages where more accurate data 
is required. 
4.2.1 Generator Selection 
A BLDC motor can be operated as a PMSG, as described in Subsection 2.3.2. Therefore, 
the BLDC motor market was examined to find a suitable generator. Various fields of 
BLDC use were considered, such as hobby and drone motors, and electric transportation 
devices like scooters, segboards, and electric bikes. Initial power and mechanical 
performance estimates from turbine calculations served as approximate BLDC 
specification guidelines. 
The first BLDC motors explored were those intended for hobby and drone use, valued 
for their high power density, compact size, and relatively high efficiency. However, they 
were ultimately excluded due to their limited robustness, short runtime capabilities, 
inadequate protection against environmental conditions, and design for high-speed 
operation, which would require high gear ratios. As a result, the search shifted to other 
BLDC applications. 
Next, BLDC motors designed for electric scooters and segboards were considered. 
These motors are inexpensive, designed for lower speeds, more robust in construction, 
and better protected against the elements. However, their typical power ratings of 200 to 
300 watts meant they would need to operate continuously at the upper limits of their 
capacity. Additionally, the lower cost of these motors raised concerns about their 
durability and service life in long-term operation. 
A BLDC motor intended as a front drive for an electric bike was chosen for the Stubberup 
system. This motor is direct drive, meaning it has no gearing that could reduce efficiency 
or compromise service life. Its internals are sealed with a dynamic oil seal, protecting it 
from harsh outdoor environments with water, salt, and particles. It is designed to 
withstand high loads, impact forces, and vibrations seen by the front wheel of a bike, 
along with the generally greater power requirements of electric bikes compared to 
scooters and segboards. Furthermore, its design for low speeds means a higher voltage 
output at lower operating speeds, requiring less gearing to achieve appropriate voltages 
for the power electronics and better matching with the low-speed turbine. The rugged 
mechanical construction and oversized power capabilities ensure safe continuous 
operation and long service life, albeit with slightly increased mechanical losses. 
The chosen BLDC motor is the Nine Continent Front Hub Motor of the standard wind 
FH212 model, supplied by GRIN Technologies, as shown in Figure 4.5.

Functions and design considerations of the illustrated mechanical components and 
assemblies are summarized below. 
• 
Intermediate shafts, ball bearings, and bearing seats 
The torque sensor is sensitive and expensive, requiring that vibrations and angular and 
radial shaft misalignments be limited. Intermediate shafts on either side of the transducer 
are fully supported by two ball bearings mounted in bearing seats, isolating the torque 
transducer from potential misalignments and vibrations, as well as unintended failures. 
• 
Couplings 
A star coupling connects the motor shaft and intermediate shaft 1, while a rigid aluminum 
coupling provides a shaft connection point on the generator. For all other connections, 
double flexible bellows couplings were utilized. 
• 
Mounting plates 
The mounting plates served as attachment points for the mechanical components, 
facilitating easy modifications and the addition of mounting holes. Plates 3 and 4 are 
designed for adjustability along the axial direction, accommodating different sizes of 
torque transducers. 
• 
Stands  
The stands align the components at the correct height and plane. This is achieved by 
machining the stands to the correct height and utilizing keyways in the fixed and 
adjustable tables. To avoid over-constraining the torque transducer, the stand allows 
more movement and is not excessively tightened. 
Reusing and modifying existing components were priorities in designing the mechanical 
setup, meaning some compromises were necessary. 
• 
A star coupling was used on the motor shaft instead of a bellows coupling, which 
is more sensitive to shaft misalignments, but was chosen because no adequately

4.2.4 Experiment Setup, Procedure and Data Processing 
The procedure for the experimental generator characterization followed the general 
structure outlined below. 
1. Mechanical installation 
This involved securely mounting the mechanical components in the correct positions and 
aligning the generator shaft coupler and output shaft by adjusting the height of the 
adjustable generator base. The mechanical couplings were thoroughly tightened to avoid 
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
slippage, and adjustments were made to minimize shaft misalignments throughout the 
drive chain. 
2. Electrical connections and settings 
The three phases of the generator were connected to a connector that split the signal to 
the rectifier and power analyzer. The range of the current and voltage differential probes 
was manually adjusted to match the expected range of the generator output as closely 
as possible to minimize measurement error. 
3. Performance and setup verification 
To verify the correct installation and functionality of the experimental setup, the drive 
motor was run briefly at different speeds. The mechanical system was inspected visually 
and audibly for any faults or indications of improper installation. Sensor outputs were 
compared to expected values to ensure they were within reasonable ranges. 
4. No-load test 
A no-load test was conducted by recording generator output and mechanical input 
parameters at different constant rotational speeds, ranging from 100 to 600 rpm in 
increments of 100 rpm. Data was collected for approximately 2 minutes at each speed, 
and the software was set up to save data files automatically. 
5. Load test 
The load test followed the same procedure as the no-load test, but for each speed, the 
load was increased incrementally. Starting from the no-load condition, the DC load 
voltage was decreased in increments of around 0.5 to 1 V, corresponding to an 
increased current flow and load on the generator. This process continued until the 
output current stalled, indicating a decrease in efficiency, at which point the driven 
speed was increased to the next value. Data was recorded continuously from 100 to 
600 rpm. 
Data processing was performed using Python, which included smoothing oscillating 
and noisy data, deriving speed from smoothed AC phase voltage, calculating electrical 
and mechanical power, and determining efficiency. The measured and calculated 
parameters were plotted in various graphs as a function of time to provide a clear 
overview. The primary focus was on the relationship between rotational speed, 
efficiency, and load. The Python script can be found in Appendix 9.8.

4.3 Design Methods 
This section describes the design methods applied throughout the Stubberup 
hydropower project to develop the final Stubberup system. These methods are 
characterized by their iterative nature, involving prototypes and exploring various 
approaches to optimize and simplify the final product and its components based on 
findings and experiences from previous iterations. This iterative approach inherently 
involves a significant amount of unused work in the form of physical prototypes, 
simulations, CAD designs, experiments, and calculations. 
4.3.1 Turbine Direct Design Method 
The turbine design followed a direct design methodology, as described in Section 3.1. 
The preliminary design was based on flow and gross head measurements, design 
guidelines, and parameters defined in Section 3.1, and the available manufacturing 
techniques. The detailed design phase involved improvements guided by CFD 
simulations and prototyping. This two-step design process was iteratively repeated until 
an acceptable turbine design was achieved. 
The preliminary design calculations, found in Appendix 9.9, provided the necessary 
geometric parameters to create a CAD model of the turbine and nozzle. An estimate of 
the rotational speed at maximum power output was also calculated. The intended 
manufacturing methods influenced important geometric features, such as the outer 
turbine diameter and blade thickness. 
With the turbine and nozzle geometry and the estimated optimum rotational speed, the 
detailed design phase commenced. This involved conducting CFD simulations to 
analyze the simulated shaft power at the given rotational speed and evaluate the flow 
characteristics within the turbine and around the blades. Key metrics evaluated from the 
simulation results included: 
• 
Correspondence between hydraulic input power and mechanical shaft power, 
indicating efficiency and the absence of unphysical values or low efficiencies. 
• 
Flow behavior, particularly flow separation around the blades, indicated by a 
sharp transition to low-velocity regions along the blade perimeter. 
• 
Conversion of pressure head to velocity head in the nozzle, suggested by 
maximum velocity in the nozzle throat and comparison to experimental values. 
The feasibility of the detailed design was also assessed through prototyping and 
research into the required manufacturing methods. If the proposed design was deemed 
unsuitable on any evaluated criteria, modifications based on the gained experience and 
research were made to the preliminary design, and the process was repeated. In this 
case, over 30 design iterations of varying degrees of modification were produced and 
evaluated. 
CFD Simulation Settings and Methodology 
CFD simulation of a CFT is complex due to the multiphase flow and non-linear nature of 
fluid dynamics. Developing a simulation model that reasonably approximates the real 
system involved trial and error and extensive consultation of COMSOL documentation, 
building from simple studies with limited experience to more complex and accurate 
simulations. 
==PAGE:63==
55 
 
Several approximations and simplifications were necessary to achieve a functioning 
simulation model compared to the initial goal. Ideally, a transient 3D simulation with 
surface tracking of the water-air fluid interface and a comprehensive mesh convergence 
study would be used. However, due to time constraints and the non-linear challenges of 
the model, this ideal standard was not feasible. 
Some of the approximations and simplifications made are listed below: 
• 
2D analysis 
The flow problem was initially modeled in 3D, but as model complexity increased, so did 
computational load and convergence issues. The decision was made to simplify the 
model to a 2D approximation to reduce these issues. This is a reasonable assumption, 
as noted in Section 3.2. 
• 
Steady state  
The flow problem and rotor were modeled under the assumption that the simulated 
parameters were relatively constant over time. This was implemented using the "Frozen 
Rotor" feature in COMSOL 6.2, where the turbine is fixed, and rotational forces are 
imposed on the rotating domain surrounding the turbine geometry. This is a reasonable 
approximation, especially in the design process, as stated in Section 3.1 and 3.2. 
• 
Mixture model physics interface 
To model the multiphase flow in a CFT, the "Mixture Model" physics interface in 
COMSOL 6.2 was used. This model simulates the multiphase flow as a continuous 
phase, air, and a dispersed phase, water particles. An important assumption is that the 
flow is a homogeneous mixture, with the dispersed phase uniformly distributed within the 
continuous phase, but allowing the volume fraction of each phase to vary throughout the 
flow domain. In reality, the phases are not homogeneous but separated as bubbles or 
droplets with distinct boundaries. This model is chosen for its computational efficiency, 
traded for accuracy compared to other models. 
• 
Limited mesh convergence study  
A mesh convergence study was conducted with four different mesh densities using the 
"Physics-generated mesh" feature in COMSOL 6.2, with settings "Extremely coarse," 
"Extra coarse," "Coarser," and "Coarse." Finer mesh resolutions caused high 
computational load and convergence issues. 
• 
Limited mesh element quality analysis 
Ideally, mesh quality indicators like y+, orthogonality, aspect ratio, and skewness should 
be analyzed to ensure accuracy. Due to time limitations, these were only visually 
inspected. The "Physics-generated mesh" feature, considering model physics, 
dynamically controlled mesh resolution around rotating regions and complex geometrical 
features, adding boundary layers on wall boundaries. The mesh configuration yielding 
the best quality is illustrated in Figure 4.10. 
 
==PAGE:64==
56 
 
 
Figure 4.10: Screenshots of the “Coarse” physics-generated mesh on the final 
geometry.

•

•

•

To improve convergence, an iterative solver was used, which extended computational 
times. Once the simulation was complete, parameters such as average inlet pressure, 
average nozzle velocity, and torque on the turbine were derived from the calculated flow 
fields. 
Total torque on the turbine was obtained by integrating the traction force over all blade 
perimeters and multiplying by the turbine's axial length. The mechanical output power 
was calculated by multiplying the resulting torque with the specified rotational speed, 
while hydraulic input power was derived from average inlet pressure relative to the draft 
tube pressure, as detailed in Subsection 2.1.2.  
Case Study: Design Iteration 12 
Examining a specific design iteration provides insight into the iterative process and the 
range of explored designs. Design Iteration 12 was a revised version aimed at simplifying 
turbine manufacturing by moving from a lasercut and welded stainless steel design to a 
resin 3D-printed turbine, printed as a single part. 
3D printing the turbine significantly reduced manufacturing effort and time, as the 
process is highly automated. Resin printers offer exceptional print quality and detail for 
complex geometries and a wide range of tough engineering resins. However, the 
available printer, the Formlabs Form 3+, had a build volume of 145 x 145 x 193 mm, 
limiting the turbine diameter to 145 mm, significantly less than the previous designs with 
diameters around 250 mm, based on design guidelines in Section 3.1. 
In addition to potential impacts on flow characteristics and turbine performance, the 
reduced blade thickness compromised the turbine's structural integrity. To ensure the 
new design could handle worst-case loads, static FEA analysis was performed in both 
SOLIDWORKS 2023 and COMSOL 6.2. The worst-case load scenario was 
approximated as the turbine in a static position with maximum flow rate, with impulse 
forces calculated analytically. Forces were distributed over the inner blade surface, with 
one side of the turbine fixed and the other free to rotate and slide axially, modeling the 
loading arrangement. The design is depicted in Figure 4.11.

To collect and process sensor data, an Arduino Mega 2560 microcontroller platform was 
utilized. The Arduino can interface with various sensor types, supporting up to 16 analog 
sensors, 54 digital sensors, and multiple I2C and SPI sensors, which is more than 
sufficient for the performance experiment. The Arduino also enables real-time data 
logging when connected to a laptop and initial data processing to simplify the 
performance analysis. 
The Arduino provides 10-bit resolution for analog inputs. To increase measurement 
accuracy and precision, a higher resolution 16-bit external ADC was originally intended 
to be integrated using the Adafruit ADS1115 ADC. However, due to last-minute issues, 
this was not possible, limiting the measurement resolution to 10-bit. 
The analog inputs of the Arduino can only accept voltage signals from 0 to 5V, meaning 
the 0 to ±10V output of the T20WN and the 4-20 mA output from the TUF-2000B cannot 
be directly connected. To convert these outputs to analog voltages within the acceptable 
range, simple circuits using high-accuracy resistors were used. The T20WN output was

scaled down to 0 to ±5V through a voltage divider consisting of two 10kΩ , 0.6W resistors 
with a tolerance of ±0.005%. For the TUF-2000B, a 250Ω resistor rated for 0.6W and a 
0.01% tolerance was connected in parallel to the analog output to convert the 4-20 mA 
current signal to a 1 to 5V voltage signal. The Arduino code can be found in Appendix 
9.10.

The procedure of the experiment is outlined below. 
1. Set the electronic DC load current to zero. 
2. Stop the flow by slowly shutting off both gate valves. 
3. Disconnect the penstock and install the relevant nozzle in the casing. 
4. Reconnect the penstock and start the flow by slowly opening the gate valves. 
5. Observe the water column height and adjust the air-inlet valve to stabilize just 
below the turbine. 
6. Read the flow rate on the flow sensor and set the new max flow scaling factor in 
the Arduino code. 
7. Create a new Excel document to record load current, voltage, and power 
readings from the DC load. 
8. Start recording Arduino sensor data in the specified folder using Putty and note 
electrical parameters in the Excel document. 
9. Stop data recording after approximately 1.5 minutes. 
10. Increase the DC load current by 0.5A. 
11. Adjust the air-inlet valve to stabilize the water column height just under the 
turbine. 
12. Log data for approximately 1.5 minutes, noting electrical parameters in the Excel 
sheet. 
13. Repeat the process of load increase, air-inlet adjustment, and recording for 1.5 
minutes until the power output has clearly passed its maximum power point. 
14. Repeat these steps for the next nozzle. 
The resulting data was processed in Python, with averages calculated over the 1.5-
minute intervals and plotted for all load values against the measurement point index. 
Additional calculations were performed to correct inaccurately measured parameters and 
derive unmeasured ones. The resulting performance plots, particularly the power and 
efficiency curves, were then compared to Adhikari's results, as well as to the simulated 
and experimental turbine and generator characteristics. The Python scripts can be found 
in Appendix 9.11 and 9.12.

6 Discussion 
The primary objectives of this thesis were to determine whether a fully functional pico 
hydropower system could be successfully designed, constructed, implemented, and 
tested at Stubberup Mølle. Additionally, the study aimed to evaluate the performance of 
the Cink flow regulator mechanism in a practical setting and to validate the system's 
overall performance through experimental measurements, comparing the results to the 
CFD analysis conducted by Adhikari. 
This chapter critically examines the findings in relation to the research objectives. It 
assesses the various stages of the project, including site evaluations at Stubberup Mølle, 
generator characterization, turbine simulations, and field measurements of the final 
Stubberup System. The performance measurements are compared to Adhikari’s 
findings, which serve as a guiding reference. 
Furthermore, this chapter discusses the implications and limitations of the research 
findings, addressing unavoidable constraints and their impact on the overall conclusions. 
Suggestions for future work and potential improvements are also detailed. 
The structure of this chapter follows a logical progression, by first discussing the primary 
tasks necessary to achieve the research objectives, thereby establishing the relevant 
context for evaluating the final system's performance. 
6.1 Flow and Head Measurements  
This section details the methods and results of flow and head measurements conducted 
at Stubberup Mølle, which were critical for determining the available hydraulic power and 
guiding the turbine design process. The measurements were performed using both direct 
and indirect methods, with results validated through numerical and computational 
simulations. These findings provided a foundational understanding of the site conditions, 
which directly influenced the subsequent design and optimization of the hydropower 
system. 
The initial total flow rate measurement using the bucket-timer method yielded 35.16 L/s, 
closely aligning with the historical value of 33.33 L/s, considering expected measurement 
uncertainties. 
Subsequent measurements using an ultrasonic flow sensor indicated a maximum flow 
rate through the penstock of 23.41 L/s, as seen in Figure 5.1. Although this value is 
significantly lower than the total measured flow, it aligns well with expected outcomes. 
The variation in measured flow rates, demonstrates the impact of altering the penstock 
configuration—specifically, shortening the pipe sections—which clearly shows that pipe 
length significantly affects the flow rate. An increased flow of over 25 L/s was observed 
by installing a 3D-printed, large, rounded flare at the penstock inlet; unfortunately, this 
data was not logged. 
Subsequent numerical flow estimations using the Colebrook equation and Darcy-
Weisbach friction factor, as described in Appendix 9.2, substantiated the maximum 
measured flow rate with a relatively close estimated flow of 25.88 L/s. Similarly, CFD 
simulations of the penstock in COMSOL 6.2 estimated a flow rate of 24.653 L/s as shown 
in Table 5.1, suggesting that a higher mesh resolution – impossible due to memory errors 
==PAGE:117==
109 
 
and convergence issues – might have brought the simulated flow even closer to the 
measured values. 
To obtain a more accurate flow estimate, the flow was re-measured using the ultrasonic 
flow sensor, this time with different 3D-printed nozzles and a draft tube installed at the 
siphon outlet. The resulting values were slightly reduced, likely due to the constriction 
and head loss introduced by the nozzle. The measured and averaged flow rates for 
different slider settings of the Cink Mechanism, expressed as nozzle throat height h_0, 
are presented in Figure 5.2. The fully open nozzle, with an h0 of 62.7 mm, yielded the 
highest flow rate an average of 21.01 L/s. 
This experiment led to the derivation of the maximum average nozzle velocity, calculated 
from the average flow values and nozzle throat geometry, at 4.19 m/s. This nozzle 
velocity was used in preliminary CFD design and energy calculations, as well as in the 
detailed design phase for boundary conditions in the final turbine simulations. 
Gross head measurements, taken multiple times throughout the project, varied slightly 
due to the natural fluctuations of the mill pond. These variations, however, were within a 
few centimeters, ranging from 2.125 m to 2.144 m over 15 to 20 minutes, and were also 
influenced by the flow rate through the penstock, as illustrated in Table 5.2. The gross 
head value of 2.13 m was selected for the CFT design process and energy calculations. 
The total hydraulic power available, neglecting penstock head losses, was calculated to 
be 735 W, with 439 W available at the maximum measured penstock flow rate. 
To improve the accuracy and reliability of flow and head measurements at the Stubberup 
Mølle, future work should focus on continuous monitoring of gross head and flow to 
capture seasonal variations. The current project focused on a single measurement point, 
so long-term data collection is essential for gaining a deeper understanding of site 
conditions and optimizing system efficiency over a broader operating range. Additionally, 
improving net head by optimizing the penstock design – such as minimizing head loss 
with efficient fittings and streamlined components – will further enhance overall system 
performance. Validating the ultrasonic flow sensor against a known reference or 
independent method is also crucial to ensure consistent accuracy across various flow 
conditions. 
6.2 Generator Characterization 
This section discusses the characterization of the selected generator through both 
simulation and experimental testing. The initial simulations provided valuable insights 
into the generator's performance characteristics, while the subsequent experimental 
results were used to validate these findings.  
Initial constant power simulations using the Trip Simulator indicated achievable 
efficiencies approaching 90% with the chosen generator, offering useful information 
about the performance characteristics of the machine for testing and design purposes. 
These characteristics included the working voltage and speed range, stall torque and 
current, and limitations related to thermal behavior. 
Experimental results obtained on the generator test bench under different rotational 
speeds and load conditions are presented in Figure 5.4 and Appendix 9.13 to 9.17. The 
measured data shows good agreement with the expected characteristics of the PMSG 
discussed in Subsection 2.3.2. This includes a directly proportinal relationship between 
==PAGE:118==
110 
 
voltage and speed, a linear relationship between torque and current, an optimum 
efficiency point at a specific speed, and friction losses represented by a no-load torque 
of approximately 1 Nm. Maximum efficiencies approached 80%, with the lower-than-
simulated values likely caused by rectifier losses. As previously stated, the experimental 
generator data could not be used directly in the derivation of the gearing due to delayed 
measurements. However, it did confirm the kv-ratio of 8.2 RPM/V specified in the 
manufacturer’s documentation, a value that was instrumental in determining the 
rotational speed of the generator and turbine during performance testing. 
The constant power simulation of the generator presented in Figure 5.5, used to design 
the gearing, also showed good agreement with the covered theory. With a mechanical 
power input corresponding to the CFD-simulated maximum power output of the turbine, 
the optimum operating point was identified at 208.86 RPM, with an efficiency of 86.10%, 
an 8.10 A phase current, 24.00 V output, and a stabilized core temperature of 35.5°C, 
assuming an ambient and initial temperature of 20°C. 
For future improvements, the optimum gear ratio should be reassessed and optimized 
based on the experimental generator characterization. Additional experimental data 
should be collected to create a comprehensive efficiency map of the generator, providing 
a more detailed representation of its performance across a broader range of operating 
conditions. This would enable system performance optimization beyond the single 
simulated operating point utilized in this project. Furthermore, efforts should be made to 
investigate and reduce rectifier losses through sufficient cooling, which contributed to the 
lower-than-expected generator efficiency. Long-term testing is also recommended to 
evaluate the generator's thermal behavior and efficiency stability during continuous 
operation. 
6.3 Turbine Simulations 
This section presents the results of the final COMSOL 6.2 multiphase CFD simulations 
conducted to evaluate the turbine's performance across a range of operating speeds. 
These simulations provided key insights into the turbine's efficiency and torque-speed 
characteristics, which were crucial for optimizing the turbine-generator matching and 
estimating the overall system performance. Additionally, the implications of the 
simulation settings and the limitations encountered are discussed.  
The final multiphase CFD simulations of the turbine, carried out for 36 different operating 
speeds at a constant flow rate of 25.903 L/s, resulted in the parabolic performance curve 
shown in Figure 5.6, which closely resembles the theoretical curve discussed in 
Subsection 2.2.2. Additionally, the simulations confirmed a linear torque-speed 
relationship, displaying a linear decline in torque as rotational speed increased. 
Visualizations of the simulated velocity field and the volume fraction of water within the 
model domains are presented in Figures 6.1 and 6.2. Figure 6.3 shows flow separation 
occurring around the blades of both stages 1 and 2, along with heavy interaction between 
the crossflow and shaft at a sub-optimal operating speed.

By fitting a second-order parabolic curve to the power data and a linear fit to the torque 
data, the optimum operating point was estimated. This point occurred at a rotational 
speed of 265.91 RPM, with a mechanical power output of 226.38 W and a torque of 8.29 
Nm. It is worth noting that the mechanical power input used in the final generator 
simulation was slightly lower, at 225.38 W, due to an oversight. However, relative to other 
sources of error, this discrepancy is not deemed significant to the final performance of 
the Stubberup System. 
This rotational speed, along with the optimum generator speed of 208.86 RPM, was used 
to calculate the necessary gear ratio for matching the turbine and generator. The 
resulting gear ratio was determined to be 208.86/265.91 = 0.78545 or approximately 0.8, 
achieved using a 52-tooth pulley on the turbine shaft and a 65-tooth pulley on the 
generator. These tooth-numbers were selected from standard timing belt pulley tooth-
numbers, allowing future implementation of standard metal pulleys. 
The final simulations were conducted using an "Extra Coarse" physics generated mesh 
resolution, as this density yielded faster and more reliable convergence compared to 
finer mesh settings. To validate the accuracy of the "Extra Coarse" mesh, a crude mesh 
convergence study was performed, testing three different mesh resolutions Table 5.4. 
The results obtained from finer meshes did not significantly differ from those of the "Extra 
Coarse" mesh, thus not justifying the increased computation times required for higher 
resolutions. 
==PAGE:121==
113 
 
The calculated turbine efficiency of approximately 76% was determined by multiplying 
the volume flow rate by the simulated average nozzle inlet pressure relative to the draft 
tube outlet pressure. This efficiency is at least 10% lower than those obtained by 
Adhikari, likely due to the cumulative effects of the 2D approximation, the coarse mesh 
resolution, the limited mesh convergence study, and the multiphase flow model 
implemented. 
Contrary to initial intentions and due to time constraints, the simulations performed by 
Adhikari were not duplicated or verified, and the turbine performance for different slider 
settings of the Cink mechanism was not simulated. Such simulations would have 
provided valuable insight into the accuracy of the simulations and the expected 
performance of the Cink mechanism. 
Future work should focus on enhancing the accuracy and reliability of the turbine 
simulations during the detailed design phase. Conducting a more thorough mesh 
convergence study, investigating finer mesh resolutions until parameter convergence is 
achieved, would improve the accuracy of the CFD simulations, likely yielding more 
precise performance parameters and facilitating more informed turbine optimization. 
Additionally, implementing full 3D model simulations would better capture the complex 
flow dynamics and reduce the inherent errors associated with 2D approximations. A 
sensitivity analysis on the effects of different turbine parameters – such as blade number, 
outer diameter, blade angle, and shape – would also be an important step in optimizing 
the turbine for the given site conditions. 
This thesis primarily focused on a single flow rate, corresponding to a mostly constant 
head, during the turbine design process. Future simulations should explore a broader 
range of operating conditions, specifically head and flow rates, to develop a 
comprehensive performance map. This would allow for the optimization of the turbine 
and generator matching across various scenarios, ensuring maximum efficiency under 
different operating conditions. 
6.4 System and Cink Mechanism Performance 
The performance of the Stubberup hydropower system and the impact of the Cink 
mechanism were evaluated through field testing at the intended installation site, 
Stubberup Mølle. This section discusses the results of these tests, including the overall 
system performance and the specific influence of different settings of the Cink 
mechanism. Testing involved various configurations, such as a fully open nozzle setting 
and different gear ratios, to assess the system's efficiency and power output under 
varying conditions. 
The system's performance was measured across several key metrics, as illustrated in 
Figure 5.7. These metrics include rotational speed, mechanical turbine power output, 
turbine torque output, load current and voltage, and electrical power output under 
different operating conditions. The operating conditions were controlled by adjusting the 
flow rate, determined by the specific slider setting of the nozzle, and the turbine and 
generator load, determined by load current. A vertical line indicating the operating point 
at maximum electrical power output is added to the plots, along with the corresponding 
performance parameters, to provide an overview of the metrics around the point of 
optimum performance. 
==PAGE:122==
114 
 
The Cink mechanism was tested with three different slider settings to evaluate its 
effectiveness in regulating flow and maintaining turbine efficiency. The fully open nozzle, 
which was simulated and optimized for continuous operation, served as a baseline for 
comparison. The chosen gear ratio, derived from the generator characterization and 
turbine simulation, was implemented and tested; however, other gear ratios were also 
explored. Due to adjustment limitations, only a lower gear ratio of 52/72 ≈ 0.72 could be 
practically tested. This ratio resulted in slightly lower power output, suggesting that 
maximum efficiency might occur at a higher gear ratio, which should be tested in future 
iterations. 
The behavior of the performance metrics generally aligned with expectations. For 
example, the linear relationships between voltage and speed, current and torque, voltage 
and current, and torque and speed are evident in Figures 5.8, 5.9, 5.10 and 5.11. The 
electrical and mechanical power curves also approximate the expected parabolic 
shapes, while the no-load torque remains mostly constant and close to the value 
obtained during the experimental generator characterization. A noteworthy effect, more 
pronounced at larger entry arc angles, is the inverse proportionality of flow rate to turbine 
speed. For the fully open nozzle, the flow rate at maximum speed is 17.10 L/s, while at 
minimum speed, the flow rate increases to 20.85 L/s. 
One parameter that deviates from expected values is the rotational speed of the turbine 
and the mechanical output power derived from this value, both being significantly greater 
than anticipated. This discrepancy was observed during testing, but the rotational speed 
could not be validated through other measurements at the time, and is likely due to signal 
noise or errors in the code processing the speed signal pulses. The inaccurate speed 
measurements were confirmed through multiple methods: 
• 
The maximum theoretical turbine speed was calculated as 452 RPM, even under 
conservative assumptions that applied assumptions of 100% conversion of head 
to velocity, zero frictional torque on the rotor, and the turbine’s outer diameter 
rotating at the same tangential velocity as the inlet flow. This theoretical maximum 
is still significantly below the maximum measured speed of over 550 RPM. 
• 
The gross head required to achieve the measured speeds was calculated as 3.2 
m, far exceeding what is measured or even possible at Stubberup Mølle. 
• 
The measured generator no-load voltage during performance testing was 
compared to the experimental generator characterization, indicating that the 
measured rotational speeds should have resulted in greater voltages. 
• 
The calculated generator efficiencies were much lower than those observed 
during experimental generator characterization and simulations. 
• 
The mechanical output power of the turbine was much greater than simulated 
values. 
To address these discrepancies and provide a more accurate conclusion to the research 
objective, the rotational speed was derived indirectly using measured electrical 
parameters, which had a higher confidence level and better corresponded with expected 
values. This was done using the theory covered in Subsection 2.3.2, applying the 
experimentally validated kv-ratio and specified rectifier voltage drop. The resulting 
rotational speeds aligned much more closely with expected values. 
Despite resolving the rotational speed measurement issue, the differential pressure 
measurement required to determine net head – and thus calculate hydraulic input power 
and turbine efficiency – remained unaddressed, making it challenging to conclusively 
==PAGE:123==
115 
 
answer the research objectives. To derive the net head, head loss was iteratively 
calculated as described in Subsection 2.2.3 and detailed in Appendix 9.18 for each 
measurement point, based on flow rate and geometrical parameters of the penstock. The 
resulting net head values seemed reasonable, given the derived turbine and generator 
efficiencies generally aligned with expected values. 
With reasonably derived estimates of rotational speed and net head, more informed 
conclusions regarding the research objectives were now feasible. The specific derived 
parameters – turbine rotational speed, turbine output power, hydraulic input power, head 
loss, net head, generator efficiency, turbine efficiency, and overall system efficiency – 
provided more nuanced insights into the system’s performance and the effects of the 
Cink mechanism. 
Comparing the corrected voltage-speed relationship in Figure 5.12 with the uncorrected 
relationship in Figure 5.8, it is evident that the uncorrected voltage is shifted upwards, 
not coinciding with the origin, while the corrected relationship is now directly proportional 
to speed, reaching zero voltage at zero speed as expected. 
Generator efficiencies in Figure 5.13, derived from the corrected turbine rotational 
speeds and electrical power output in Figure 5.14, reveal more realistic values that 
correspond well with the experimental generator characterization data, peaking around 
75% with performance relatively consistent across the tested nozzles. 
Figures 5.15 and 5.16 illustrate mechanical output power and turbine efficiency as 
functions of rotational speed, showing that the power decreases for the fully open nozzle 
compared to the nozzle with the smaller entry arc angle θ’s = 78.4°. At first, this result 
seemed counterintuitive, as a greater flow rate typically results in greater power output. 
However, upon further analysis, the cause became clearer: lower flow rates with smaller 
entry arc nozzles result in lower average penstock flow speeds, corresponding to lower 
penstock head losses, as illustrated in Figures 5.17 and 5.18. Consequently, the 
resulting net head is greatly reduced for the larger nozzles and varies more significantly 
with load as seen in Figures 5.19 and 5.20. The resulting hydraulic power as a function 
of flow rate varies parabolically, indicating that there is an optimal point where hydraulic 
input power is maximized, striking a balance between increased flow rate and head loss 
as illustrated in Figure 5.21. For the current system configuration, the slider setting of θ’s 
= 78.4° reaches this sweet spot for power production. In a properly dimensioned 
penstock, this behavior would likely differ, and the hydraulic power would not decline as 
drastically as observed in the current system. This head loss effect also explains why 
turbine efficiency is not directly proportional to mechanical power output, as the θ’s = 
78.4° nozzle achieves the greatest power output of 156.99 W but a lower efficiency of 
78.20%, compared to the fully open nozzle, which has a lower power output of 140.57 
W but the highest efficiency of 85.86%. 
When comparing the simulated and measured turbine maximum operating points for the 
fully open θ’s  = 90° nozzle, the simulation predicted a maximum power point at 265.91 
RPM with an output of 226.38 W and an efficiency of 76.07%. In contrast, the measured 
optimum point occurred at 161.13 RPM with an output of 140.57 W and an efficiency of 
85.86%. This substantial difference in speed is most likely attributed to the variation in 
flow rates between the two cases: the simulation used a flow rate of 25.903 L/s, whereas 
the measured flow rate was lower at 20.45 L/s. This reduction in flow rate leads to a 
decrease in nozzle velocity, resulting in a lower rotational speed required to achieve 
maximum angular momentum extraction and power output. For the nozzle with a smaller 
==PAGE:124==
116 
 
entry arc angle of θ’s = 39.1° and the highest net head, the nozzle velocity is 
correspondingly higher, which is reflected in the measurements as an optimal operating 
speed of approximately 220 RPM – much closer to the expected value. The simulated 
efficiency, which is nearly 10% lower than the measured value, can be attributed to the 
limitations and assumptions inherent in the simulation, as well as differences in operating 
conditions. When compared to the maximum efficiency of 89.45% achieved by Adhikari, 
the measured maximum efficiency is within 3.59%, indicating a reasonably close 
correlation. An additional source of uncertainty in measurements is the unquantified 
dimensional inaccuracies introduced by the 3D printing process, affecting both the nozzle 
and turbine geometries. 
Figures 5.22 and 5.23 further illustrate the effects of increasing head loss on total system 
efficiency and hydraulic input power as a function of speed. System efficiencies are 
highest for smaller entry arc angles due to the reduced head loss. Interestingly, the 
turbine efficiency of the fully open nozzle is higher than that of the nozzle with greater 
system efficiency. As seen in Figure 5.16, the rotational speed at the maximum efficiency 
point of the turbine shifts lower as head loss increases. This is logical, as greater head 
loss results in lower nozzle flow velocity, translating to a lower rotational speed required 
for maximum power extraction. 
Figure 5.24 show turbine and generator efficiency as functions of turbine rotational 
speed, indicating that the optimum operating points for the turbine and generator are 
acceptably matched for smaller entry arc angles. However, for larger entry arc angles, 
the turbine's optimum operating point appears lower than that of the generator, implying 
that a larger gear ratio could further optimize power output and efficiency for larger 
nozzles. 
Figures 5.14 and 5.25 illustrate how a given electrical power output can be obtained at 
two distinct operating conditions: a high-current, low-voltage operating point and a low-
current, high-voltage operating point. The latter is preferred for continuous operation, as 
it results in lower generator temperatures, better matching with power electronics 
specifications, and reduced armature and power line losses. 
The maximum turbine efficiencies in Figure 5.16 and the corresponding flow rates are 
69.85% 8.36 L/s, 70.65% 11.52 L/s and 78.20% 17.47 L/s for the three slider settings – 
arranged in order from smallest to largest entry arc angles – show a maximum difference 
of 8.35% at 47.8% flow rate. Comparing this value to the maximum difference of 2.7% at 
50% flow reported by Adhikari reveals a discrepancy of 5.65%. This difference is within 
a reasonable range, especially when accounting for measurement error. The 
implementation of the Cink mechanism does therefore indicate some degree of 
maintaining efficiency at part-flows, although the results do not fully meet the anticipated 
advantage or expected values. However, when comparing system efficiencies, the trend 
reverses, with the highest efficiencies observed at smaller entry arc angles of the slider. 
As previously discussed, this effect is primarily attributed to head loss in the penstock. 
When comparing θ’s = 39.1° to the fully open θ’s = 90° nozzle with a maximum turbine 
efficiency and corresponding flow rate of 85.86% 20.85 L/s, the difference in efficiency 
amounts to a 16.01% reduction at 40% flow rate.  
The added complexity of the penstock behavior, along with the derived rotational speed 
and net head, increases uncertainty and makes it difficult to draw definitive conclusions 
about the performance effects of the Cink mechanism. Several factors need to be 
==PAGE:125==
117 
 
improved to provide more confident and nuanced performance characteristics and 
assessments of the Cink flow regulator. 
The evaluation of the system's performance in achieving the first research objective – 
developing a functional pico hydropower system – focuses on its power production 
capability and operational stability. Annual energy production potential at its maximum 
measured electrical power output of 118.5 W, with the θ’s = 78.4° slider setting, yields an 
annual production of approximately 1040 kWh. This figure constitutes a valuable addition 
to the annual energy consumption, and can likely be significantly increased through 
system optimizations, such as refining the gearing and penstock configuration. The 
system's operation appeared very stable and smooth, with performance meeting 
expectations: the drivetrain and turbine operated quietly with low noise emissions, no 
leakage flow around the turbine occurred at the maximum operating point, and the 
mechanical strength and stability of the turbine, nozzle, and housing showed seemed 
sufficient with no vibrations or signs of strain. Additionally, the air-inlet valve effectively 
regulated the draft tube column height. However, to draw proper conclusions about the 
long-term operational stability of the system, extended operation and observation, along 
with detailed measurements, are required. 
6.5 Challenges, Limitations and Future Work 
This section summarizes the limitations encountered during the performance 
experiments and the challenges faced throughout the project. It also outlines potential 
future work to address these issues and improve the overall system. 
One of the primary challenges experienced during the experimental system performance 
tests was the inability to implement the differential pressure sensor for net head 
measurement and the inaccurate measurements of rotational speed. These challenges 
negatively impacted the accuracy and introduced uncertainty in the measurements, as 
key values had to be derived from other measured parameters using simplified formulas 
and system assumptions. These assumptions included linear generator performance 
equations, assuming constant generator phase resistance, a fixed kv-ratio, and a 
constant rectifier voltage drop. Another source of error and uncertainty was the assumed 
value of gross head. As previously explained, the gross head can vary by a few 
centimeters over relatively short time intervals. Therefore, assuming a constant gross 
head is not ideal or entirely accurate. However, if a properly implemented net head 
measurement is conducted, gross head measurement becomes less critical but would 
still provide additional, useful data on the system and penstock performance. Future work 
must address these measurement issues by implementing and calibrating accurate 
sensors. 
Another issue related to system performance was that the measured load voltages at 
maximum power were below the minimum requirements of the MPPT charge controller 
across all tested nozzles. This implies that the generated power would be impossible to 
utilize under the given operating conditions, especially when considering voltage drop 
across power cables from the turbine to the charge controller. The voltage drop caused 
by load current and the resulting load voltage were therefore not adequately considered 
in the initial gear ratio design, necessitating future testing with higher gear ratios. The 
torque capabilities exhibited by the turbine indicate that it can handle higher gearings 
needed to achieve greater speeds and load voltages. Additionally, some performance 
measurements suggest that, for larger entry arc angles, there may be an even higher 
==PAGE:126==
118 
 
gear ratio that better matches the turbine and generator, enhancing overall efficiency 
and power output. 
Due to system measurement uncertainty and the confounding effects introduced by the 
highly variable head loss from the improperly dimensioned penstock, improved 
measurement accuracy and a properly dimensioned penstock are essential for ensuring 
consistent nozzle velocity. These improvements are critical for accurately evaluating the 
performance effects of the Cink mechanism. Another future point of investigation is the 
development of a functional Cink mechanism using a movable and controllable slider to 
test and evaluate its robustness, performance, and feasibility in practice. 
Due to time constraints, the Stubberup System was not integrated with the selected 
power electronics, leaving the performance characteristics of the final Stubberup System 
under continuous operation unknown. Future testing and integration of the power 
electronics are necessary to fully assess the system's actual performance and evaluate 
its annual energy production potential. This includes evaluating the MPPT charge 
controller and other components under actual operating conditions to identify any further 
optimizations needed.

7 Conclusion 
The primary research objectives of this thesis were to evaluate the feasibility of 
designing, constructing, and testing a fully functional pico hydropower system at 
Stubberup Mølle, and to experimentally validate the performance of the Cink mechanism 
as a flow regulator within this context. These objectives were largely met through a 
combination of field measurements, CFD simulations, and experimental testing. The 
design and implementation of the pico hydropower system were successful, and the 
experimental validation of the Cink mechanism yielded valuable insights, while also 
identifying areas for further refinement. 
Key findings include the successful design and deployment of the Stubberup System, 
which operated smoothly and demonstrated the viability of pico hydropower at the 
selected site, generating a maximum measured electrical power of 118.5 W and an 
estimated annual energy production of 1040 kWh. Flow and head measurements, along 
with experimental and simulation-based turbine and generator characterizations, 
provided crucial insights into the system's performance under varying operating 
conditions.  
The experimental results indicated that the Cink mechanism effectively regulated flow, 
maintaining relatively consistent turbine efficiency at reduced flow rates, and even 
enhancing overall system efficiency in the Stubberup system. The maximum turbine 
efficiency observed in practice dropped 8.35% at a part-flow of 47.8% between the tested 
slider settings, compared to a drop of 2.7% at 50% part-flow achieved by Adhikari. The 
maximum measured turbine efficiency reached 85.86% compared to 89.45% simulated 
by Adhikari. Lower system efficiencies observed for larger entry arc angles were primarily 
attributed to the suboptimal design of the penstock, which caused significant head losses 
at higher flow rates, and added a confounding effect to the conclusive performance of 
the Cink mechanism. These findings along with measurement limitations and uncertainty 
suggest that, while the Cink mechanism shows promise as an efficient flow regulation 
device for crossflow turbines, further experimental optimization and research is required. 
The implications of this research are significant, particularly in contributing to the 
empirical validation of the Cink mechanism. This study represents an initial effort to 
bridge the gap between theoretical analysis and practical application by experimentally 
testing the Cink mechanism in a real-world setting. The findings provide valuable 
empirical data on the mechanism's performance under various operating conditions, 
offering insights that extend beyond theoretical predictions. This research not only 
advances the understanding of the Cink mechanism’s practical efficacy but also lays a 
foundation for further experimental studies aimed at optimizing its design and 
implementation in small-scale hydropower systems. Additionally, the research 
underscores the importance of optimizing system components, such as the penstock 
and generator gearing, to maximize efficiency and output. Future work should focus on 
refining these components, enhancing measurement accuracy, and expanding 
operational testing to capture seasonal variations and long-term performance data. 
In conclusion, the Stubberup Hydropower Project has yielded valuable insights into the 
practical challenges and opportunities associated with small-scale hydropower systems, 
particularly concerning the Cink mechanism. While the research demonstrated the 
mechanism’s potential to effectively regulate flow, it also highlighted areas requiring 
further refinement and optimization. This study provides a basis for future research that 
==PAGE:128==

can build on these findings, with the aim of enhancing the efficiency and reliability of 
decentralized energy solutions in real-world applications.

from datetime import datetime

def read_and_process_file(filepath): 
    with open(filepath, 'r') as file: 
        lines = file.readlines() 
 
    timestamps = [] 
    flows = [] 
    velocities = [] 
    i = 0 
    while i < len(lines): 
        line = lines[i].strip() 
        if line == '' or not line[0].isdigit(): 
            i += 1 
            continue 
 
        try: 
            timestamp = datetime.strptime(line, '%y-%m-%d %H:%M:%S') 
        except ValueError as e: 
            print(f"Error Parsing Line '{line}': {e}") 
            i += 1 
            continue 
 
        flow_value = None 
        velocity_value = None 
        # Process the block to find both flow and velocity 
        j = i + 1 
        while j < len(lines) and lines[j].strip() and not lines[j].strip()[0].isdigit(): 
            line_content = lines[j].strip().split() 
            if line_content[0] == 'FLOW:': 
                flow_value = float(line_content[1]) 
            elif line_content[0] == 'VEL:': 
                velocity_value = float(line_content[1]) 
            j += 1 
 
==PAGE:148==
140 
 
        # Only append data if both flow and velocity values are found 
        if flow_value is not None and velocity_value is not None: 
            timestamps.append(timestamp) 
            flows.append(flow_value) 
            velocities.append(velocity_value) 
         
        i = j  # Move to the start of the next data block. 
 
    start_time = timestamps[0] 
    time_differences = [(timestamp - start_time).total_seconds() for timestamp in timestamps] 
 
    return time_differences, flows, velocities

def read_and_process_file(filepath): 
    with open(filepath, 'r') as file: 
        lines = file.readlines() 
 
    flows = [] 
    velocities = [] 
    i = 0 
    while i < len(lines): 
        line = lines[i].strip() 
        if "FLOW:" in line: 
            flow_value = float(line.split()[1]) 
            i += 1 
            if i < len(lines): 
                line = lines[i].strip() 
                if "VEL:" in line: 
                    velocity_value = float(line.split()[1]) 
                    flows.append(flow_value) 
                    velocities.append(velocity_value) 
        i += 1 
 
    time_differences = list(range(len(flows))) 
 
    return time_differences, flows, velocities

Projektbeskrivelse 
 
Introduktion 
Der anmodes om tilladelse til at installere en 425 watt vandturbine 
ved udløbet fra en gammel mølledam, med henblik på at generere elektricitet til 
elnettet, og dermed udnytte lokale vedvarende energikilder som det er gjort før i 
tiden. Design og fremstilling af turbinen vil udgøre undertegnedes 
kandidatprojekt hos Danmarks Teknisk Universitet på studieretningen 
Konstruktion og mekanik, og er planlagt at forløbe fra juni 2023 til og med 
januar 2024. 
 
Formål 
Formålet med projektet er at designe, fremstille og implementere et 
system der kan udnytte energien i det rindende vand på grunden. Det rindende 
vand blev for mange år tilbage brugt i en vandmølle til at male korn.  
Baseret på indledende målinger af faldhøjde og volumen flow er 
den tilgængelige energi i vandet på omkring 700 watt. Når der tages højde for 
energitab i systemet, er den estimerede elektriske effekt på cirka 425 watt 
kontinuert, svarende til lidt over 3700 kWh genereret per år. Denne 
energimænge er svarende til husstandens årlige forbrug af elektricitet, så der er 
potentiale for at dække energibehovet gennem en lokal grøn energikilde. Selvom 
systemet bliver designet til at være nemt at afmontere, skal det stadig opfattes 
som en fast installation med virke over flere år eller til systemet har udtjent sig.   
 
Det har været et krav og en prioritet fra begyndelsen, at turbinen 
ikke kommer til at have nogen form for negativ indflydelse på det naturlige 
dyre- og planteliv i området. Dette krav har således været med i projektets 
overvejelser og designproces op til nu og omfatter følgende punkter:  
• Sikring mod store svingninger i mølledammens vandstand (både for høj 
og for lav vandstand) 
• Afskærmning af indtaget og afløbet fra turbinen så dyr ikke kan suges 
ind og skades 
• Mulighed for at lade blade og andet flydende materiale passere videre fra 
mølledammen 
• Minimere turbinens fodaftryk for at optage så lidt naturareal som muligt  
• Ingen lækage af smørelse eller lignende til omgivelserne

• Samme frie faldhøjde af vandet og dermed iltning af vandet der føres til 
bækken 
• Afskærmning af elledninger for at undgå at dyr kan få elektrisk stød 
Måden hvorpå disse punkter bliver adresseret er uddybet i afsnittet ”Teknisk 
design”. 
 
Stedvurdering 
Som førnævnt har der tidligere eksisteret en mindre kornmølle, 
Stubberup mølle, som har været ude af drift i mange år. Møllen lå placeret ved

til med jord. I stedet for det gamle afløb er der nu installeret to Ø150mm rør, 
som leder vandet fra mølledammen og ned til en bæk, der udspringer netop her. 
Vandet kommer fra flere kildevæld, hvilket giver en meget ensartet vandføring 
hen over året. Dengang Stubberup mølle malede korn, brugte man løs af vandet 
indtil mølledammen var tømt godt ned, hvorefter blev den fyldt op igen om 
natten. Denne metode er dog ikke mulig i dag, da den ville forringe 
mølledammens vandstand og i øvrigt ville tilføje unødig kompleksitet til 
systemet. I stedet bliver turbinen designet til kun at kunne tage imod den 
naturlige konstante vandtilførsel således at vandstanden kan holdes ved sit 
nuværende niveau.

• Trykrør 
Vandet løber gennem dette rør til turbinen og opbygger tryk på grund af 
faldhøjden. Afløb 1 forventes at fungere som trykrør. 
 
• Dyse og ventil 
Dysen omdanner vandtrykket til hastighed, som den pågældende turbinetype

tillader delvist eller helt afbrudt flow. 
==PAGE:158==

• Turbinehus og turbine 
Turbinen sidder i et lukket turbinehus, som kan åbnes op for at inspicere 
turbinen. Vandet roterer turbinen, som omdanner vandets energi til mekanisk 
effekt, i form af en vinkelhastighed og et moment. 
 
==PAGE:159==
151 
 
• Generator  
Den roterende aksel fra turbinen er forbundet til generatoren, som omdanner 
den mekaniske effekt til elektrisk effekt.  
• Udløbsrør 
Udløbsrøret fra turbinehuset til bækken tillader turbinen at udnytte hele 
faldhøjden, og danner et lukket system mellem mølledammens og bækkens 
vandspejl. Ved at lade udløbsrøret gå under vandoverfladen kan mængden af 
den producerede energi forøges med omkring en tredjedel, sammenlignet med 
at lade udløbsrøret stoppe over vandspejlet.  
• Inverter 
Inverteren er en elektrisk komponent der omdanner jævnstrømmen fra 
generatoren til 230V vekselspænding, således at energien kan sendes til 
elnettet. 
 
Sikring mod store svingninger i mølledammens vandstand 
håndteres på forskellige måder: 
• Turbinens dyse designes således at den kun vil tillade det målte volumen 
flow at passere, baseret på Bernoullis lov og CFD-simuleringer. På denne 
måde kan der altså ikke passere mere vand igennem turbinen end der 
bliver tilført fra kildevældene.  
• Flowet gennem turbinen kan yderligere justeres eller helt afskæres via en 
justerbar ventil eller ved at lukke for flowet i indløbet. 
• Den laveste vandstand i mølleåen bestemmes af en vandbarriere, der 
sidder nederst i indløbet. Højden på denne vandbarriere kan let justeres, 
idet den blot består af en række brædder, der glides ned i en slidse i 
indløbet. Med denne løsning, som allerede er implementeres, er det ikke 
muligt for turbinen at ”tømme” mølledammen.  
• Den maksimale vandstand i mølleåen begrænses af de to eksisterende 
overløb, og i det endelige system tiltænkes afløb 2 ligeledes at fungere 
som overløb. Afløb 2 konverteres til overløbsrør ved blot at skifte det 
eksisterende gitter ud med en lukket plade, der har samme højde som 
den maksimalt tilladelige vandstand. Denne plade vil således fungere 
som en vandbarriere. Diameteren på de to eksisterende afløb er 
betydeligt overdimensioneret i forhold til det eksisterende volumen flow.  
Ovenstående punkter resulterer i at mølleåens vandstand maksimalt kan variere 
med omkring 200mm, hvilket kan reduceres yderligere om nødvendigt. 
 
For at undgå at dyr kan suges ind og skades i turbinen, vil et 
finmasket gitter blive installeret i indløbet. Størrelsen på hullerne i gitteret kan 
laves i lige netop den størrelse der ønskes, og hvis overfladearealet på gitteret 
øges, kan vandhastigheden gennem gitteret reduceres således at tendensen til at 
dyr og vegetation suges ind ligeledes vil reduceres. Den eneste vej ind i turbinen 
==PAGE:160==
152 
 
kommer til at være gennem gitteret, så der ikke er nogle alternative veje for 
hverken vand-, land- og luftlevende dyr at komme ind ad.  
De mindste passager inde i turbinen kan herefter designes således at de er på 
størrelse med – eller større – end gitterets huller. På denne måde undgår man, 
at helt små dyr der bliver suget ind i turbinen, undgår at blive klemt mellem 
eksempelvis et turbineblad og turbinehuset.  
For at forhindre dyr i at komme ind gennem turbinens udløb, og dermed 
risikere at blive beskadiget af turbinen, kan der også her blive installeret et 
gitter med den ønskede hulstørrelse.  
Den valgte turbine, en såkaldt ”Cross flow turbine”, er af en type der roterer 
langsomt sammenlignet med andre almindelige turbinetyper.  Hvis dette 
faktum kombineres med afrundede kanter på turbinens blade, vil 
sandsynligheden minimeres for at et dyr bliver skadet hvis det passerer gennem 
turbinen.  
 
I løbet af efteråret falder der betydelige mængder blade ned i 
mølledammen fra de omkringstående træer. For at undgå at disse blade hober 
sig op i mølledammen, er den nuværende metode, at fjerne gitteret i et af 
indtagene, således at de omkringliggende blade på overfladen bliver suget ned 
gennem afløbet og føres videre ud med bækken. Dette kan gøres med jævne 
mellemrum efter behov i den periode hvor der er flest blade.  
Med det implementerede system kan samme metode stadig anvendes: her vil 
afløb 2 være dedikeret til dette formål, ud over dets funktion som overløbsrør.  
 
Turbinens fodaftryk – inklusive turbinehus, generator, dyse og 
udløbsrør – forventes ikke at overstige et område på 0.5m x 0.5m. Dette område 
vil befinde sig ved udmundingen af afløb 1, og forventes at have en højde fra 
bækkens vandspejl på omkring 0.6m.  
Herudover bliver turbinen ikke en fast installation med betonfundament eller 
lignende: den bliver designet således, at den spændes fast på mundingen af 
afløb 1 med et par bolte. Dette betyder derfor også, at turbinen nemt kan 
afmonteres og flyttes igen, såfremt dette skulle blive nødvendigt. Der er altså 
tale om en meget lille tilføjelse til den allerede eksisterende infrastruktur, 
svarende til at forlænge afløb 1 med maksimalt 1 meter.  
 
Den mekaniske del af et vandkraftværk kræver anvendelse af lejer 
til turbineakslen. Lejer smøres oftest med olie eller fedt, og for at undgå at 
denne smørelse lækker ud i det gennemstrømmende vand, vil der blive anvendt 
forseglede kuglelejer designet til fødevare- og drikkevareindustrien. Dette 
betyder at de ikke frigiver nogen form for forurening til det miljø de er i kontakt 
med. Se følgende link til SKF’s serie af denne type kuglelejer: 
https://www.skf.com/id/industries/food-and-beverage/products-and-
solutions/food-line-corrosion-resistant-deep-groove-ball-bearings-range.

For at bevare den eksisterende frie faldhøjde af vandet, når det 
strømmer ud af afløbet og falder ned i bækken, kan udløbets tværsnitsareal 
øges. Ved at øge tværsnitsarealet reduceres vandspejlets højde inde i udløbet og 
vandet kan strømme ud og falde uhindret af udløbets vægge. Dette princip er 
illustreret nedenfor:  
 
På den måde bliver vandet stadig iltet når det bevæger sig fra turbinen og til 
bækken, samtidig med at den producerede energi stadig kan øges med en 
tredjedel. Denne effekt kan synliggøres i det færdige system ved at installere en 
transparent plade på en eller flere af udløbets sider. Dette vil også gøre det 
muligt at inspicere turbinen løbende, og sikre sig at der ikke er kommet dyr op 
eller opstået andre problemer inde i turbinehuset.  
Elledningen, der fører strømmen fra turbinen til ejendommen, skal 
afskærmes for at undgå, at dyr kan få elektrisk stød hvis de f.eks. gnaver i det. 
Her tages forskellige metoder i brug for at minimere risikoen for skade på dyr: 
• Spændingen i kablet holdes lav, dvs. at den først omdannes til 230V AC 
inde i huset hvor inverteren skal installeres. Der forventes en spænding 
på mellem 25 og 60V, alt efter hvilken inverter der vælges. 
• Kablet beskyttes af en fleksibel, men hårdfør plastkappe lavet til 
udendørsinstallationer. Ideelt vil kablet blive gravet ned for at beskytte 
det bedst muligt.  
• Hvis kablet oplever en kortslutning, f.eks. fordi det bliver gnavet i, vil 
turbinen blive bremset til meget lav rotationshastighed. Ved lave 
rotationshastigheder sker der to ting: spænding falder til meget lave 
niveauer, og den producerede effekt bliver meget lille. Det vil sige at der 
hverken vil være tilstrækkelig spænding eller strømstyrke til at gøre 
nogen skade. 
• Inverteren har adskillige sikkerhedsfunktioner indbygget, som vil 
frakoble turbinen, hvis den registrerer uønskede forekomster.

# Calculate frequency using zero-crossing method 
def zero_crossing_frequency(signal, time_increment): 
    zero_crossings = np.where(np.diff(np.signbit(signal)))[0] 
    if len(zero_crossings) < 2: 
        return np.array([]), np.array([]) 
     
    periods = np.diff(zero_crossings) * time_increment 
    frequency = 1 / (2 * periods) 
    return zero_crossings[1:] * time_increment, frequency
