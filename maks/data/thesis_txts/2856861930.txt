Abstract
This thesis investigates the investment potential and challenges of regenerative startups within
the context of impact investing. Regenerative startups aim to create a net-positive impact by
restoring and enhancing socio-ecological systems, going beyond the harm-reduction focus of
sustainability-oriented and circular economy models. Despite their transformative potential,
these startups face significant barriers in attracting impact investment, primarily due to their
long-term, multidimensional objectives and their reduced growth orientation.
The research adopts a comparative approach to evaluate the alignment between impact investors
and three categories of startups: sustainability-oriented, circular, and regenerative. Through
thematic analysis of interviews with impact investors and founders, the study identifies gaps in
existing investment frameworks that prioritize measurable, short-term outcomes and scalability
over the complex, adaptive strategies of regenerative models. To address these challenges, the
thesis develops an adapted investment framework customized to the needs of regenerative
startups. It emphasizes systemic thinking, net-positive goals, and the integration of long-term
value creation into investment processes. Additionally, the framework employs novel investment
mechanisms, including blended finance and Variable Capital Companies (VCCs), which reduce
investment risk and support mission-aligned funding for ventures with systemic objectives.
The findings highlight the need for an evolution in impact investment practices to foster the
growth of regenerative startups. By bridging the gap between investor expectations and the
transformative ambitions of regenerative ventures, the proposed framework aims to drive
systemic change and accelerate progress toward a sustainable global economy.

1. Introduction
1.1 Research Background and Context
Climate change, driven by anthropogenic activities, has led to a significant rise in global
temperatures, resulting in strong disruptions to the planet’s ecosystems and human societies. This
warming has brought extreme weather events, such as intensified heatwaves, hurricanes and
floods, contributing to biodiversity loss and ecosystem collapse (Abbass et al., 2022; Gabric,
2023). These environmental disruptions have significant societal and economic consequences.
Particularly in low-income regions, climate variability affects agricultural output, resulting in
food insecurity and increased vulnerability (Abbass et al., 2022; Kotz et al., 2024). Rising global
population and economic growth have further intensified natural resource depletion and fossil
fuel consumption, driving greenhouse gas emissions and intensifying climate change impacts
(Wang & Azam, 2024).
The growing urgency to address climate change arises from the unprecedented pace of its
impacts and the clear inadequacy of current mitigation efforts. Global greenhouse gas emissions
continue to rise, with average annual emissions from 2010 to 2019 being higher than in any prior
decade (Calvin et al., 2023)​.
Addressing this crisis requires transformative systemic changes. Incremental approaches are
insufficient and the IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) emphasizes the
necessity for ambitious adaptation and mitigation strategies to limit global warming to 1.5°C, as
exceeding this threshold will result in irreversible ecological and societal damage (Calvin et al.,
2023). Furthermore, research highlights the economic and environmental benefits of immediate
actions, emphasizing the cost-effectiveness of mitigation compared to the escalating costs of
climate-related damages (Kotz et al., 2024).
Sustainable business models play an essential role to address these systemic changes and
immediate actions for tackling global challenges by integrating environmental, social and
economic value creation. While large corporations leverage established resources to contribute,
they often face structural barriers such as the focus on short-term profits (Bocken & Geradts,
1
==PAGE:8==
2020). In contrast, startups strive through flexibility and innovation, making them key drivers of
sustainable entrepreneurship. By creating and scaling business models that align sustainability
with market opportunities, they address climate change accelerators and catalyze systemic
change (Lüdeke‐Freund, 2020). Thus, startups can play a pivotal role in fostering transformative
solutions to improve the global transition towards a more sustainable economy (Kurek et al.,
2023).
Established forms of business models are sustainability-oriented startups, aiming to align
economic, social and environmental goals by integrating sustainable development in their value
creation in order to create less harmful solutions (S. Evans et al., 2017; Geissdoerfer,
Vladimirova, et al., 2018) and circular startups, which prioritize resource efficiency by
implementing
closed-loop
systems
such as recycling, remanufacturing and product-life
extensions to decouple growth from resource consumption (Bocken et al., 2016; Geissdoerfer,
Morioka, et al., 2018).
These two types of startups can make significant strides in mitigating harm and optimizing
resource use, but they often fall short of addressing the systemic, interconnected crises of climate
change, biodiversity loss, and socio-economic inequality. The IPCC (2023) emphasizes that
incremental solutions are insufficient for limiting global warming to 1.5°C or reversing
ecological degradation. Instead, transformative approaches which actively restore and regenerate
ecosystems and communities are necessary (Calvin et al., 2023).
An emerging concept to address these requirements suggested by the IPCC is the regenerative
economy. Business models following this concept are creating net-positive impacts by restoring
ecosystems, enhancing biodiversity and fostering community resilience by embedding systemic
thinking and co-evolving with socio-ecological systems through strategies like restoration,
preservation and enhancement, prioritizing long-term environmental and social regeneration
(Hahn & Tampe, 2021). However, to successfully drive climate change mitigation, startups in the
regenerative economy are dependent on sufficient funding to survive (Bauer et al., 2024; Garg &
Shivam, 2017).
2
==PAGE:9==
A major mechanism for funding climate change mitigation solutions is impact investing. This
category of investors prioritizes measurable social and environmental outcomes in addition to
financial determinants (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2021; GIIN, 2023). The industry, which managed
$490 billion USD in total Assets under Management (AUM) in 2023 with a Compound Annual
Growth Rate (CAGR) of 14% over the past five years, is already channeling significant
investments into sustainability and circular economy sectors (Hand et al., 2024).
While, according to the Global Impact Investment Network (GIIN) (Hand et al., 2024),
sustainability-oriented and circular startups seem to already receive a significant amount of
impact investment, regenerative ventures are not directly mentioned. Further, the profound
differentiators of regenerative business models, such as striving for net-positive and systemic
long-term impact, compared to sustainability-oriented and circular models might hinder the
funding process and scare off impact investors.
1.2 Research Objectives and Research Questions
This thesis investigates the intersection of impact investing and regenerative startups, aiming to
address the potential misalignment between the transformative objectives of regenerative startups
and the conventional frameworks employed by impact investors. While impact investing has an
important role in addressing global sustainability challenges, its adaptability to the emerging
concept of regenerative startups remains underexplored. This research seeks to fill this gap by
achieving the following objectives:
Research Objective 1: Assessing the alignment between impact investors and regenerative
startups compared to other impact startups
This objective involves evaluating the extent to which the objectives and priorities of impact
investors align with the unique characteristics and ambitions of regenerative startups.
3
==PAGE:10==
By comparing these findings with the alignment observed in other impact startups, such as
sustainability-oriented and circular startups, this study aims to identify specific alignment gaps
and areas of discrepancy. To address this objective, the research is guided by the following
questions:
●
RQ 1: What factors drive the evaluation and decision-making processes of impact
investors when assessing investment opportunities?
●
RQ 2: What kind of unique challenges do regenerative startups face in attracting impact
investment compared to sustainability-oriented and circular startups?
Research Objective 2: Adapting the current impact investment structures into a
framework optimized for regenerative startups
Building on insights from the first objective, this research aims to modify existing impact
investment models to better accommodate the systemic, long-term goals of regenerative startups.
The proposed framework will integrate principles and practices tailored to the regenerative
economy, fostering increased investment flows into this transformative sector. This objective is
addressed through the following research question:
●
RQ 3: How do current impact investment models have to be adapted to align with the
attributes of regenerative startups?
By addressing these objectives and questions, this thesis not only highlights the challenges and
opportunities for regenerative startups in the impact investment landscape but also offers
actionable solutions to bridge the gap, thereby contributing to both academic literature and
practical investment strategies to accelerate regenerative startups.
1.3 Scope and Methodological Significance
This thesis focuses on the relationship between regenerative startups and impact investors,
examining the alignment of the systemic regenerative ambitions and priorities of impact
investors. It aims to investigate the research objectives through an exploratory design and
qualitative data from impact investors and founders of regenerative, sustainability-oriented and
circular startups.

While all three founder categories share a common goal of addressing environmental and
societal challenges, this thesis emphasizes the distinct attributes and systemic ambitions of
regenerative startups. Comparative insights from sustainability-oriented and circular startups are
used to review findings and highlight unique challenges faced by regenerative ventures.
Due to the significant amount of capital employed into tackling climate change through impact
investing, the research delves into the priorities, evaluation criteria and decision-making
processes of these investors. It examines how traditional impact investment models align (or fail

The concept of regenerative startups is still in its early stages. Therefore, consistent with the

distinct potential for impact or impact investment, the findings are broadly applicable across
industries and geographies.

Building on the emerging academic discourse on regenerative business models, the thesis
positions regenerative startups as a distinct category within the broader impact startup
ecosystem. Through a comparative analysis with sustainability-oriented and circular startups, it
identifies unique challenges regenerative startups face in attracting investment. Additionally, the
research develops a founder-centric regenerative investment framework, offering a novel
methodological approach to align current impact investment structures with the systemic,
long-term goals of regenerative businesses.

2. Literature Review
This literature review lays the groundwork for understanding the intersection of startups, impact
investing, and the emerging concept of regenerative businesses, providing a structured basis for
the subsequent thematic analysis. First, the review defines the core characteristics a business
must fulfill to qualify as a startup, emphasizing its innovative, growth-oriented, and scalable
nature. In the context of impact investing, impact startups are positioned as ventures that scale
solutions delivering a sustainable net benefit and actively advance sustainable development by
introducing alternatives superior to the socio-technical status quo (Horne & Fichter, 2022). These
startups are often categorized into three distinct models: Sustainability-oriented, circular and
recently emerging, regenerative. Each type is reflecting unique approaches to addressing global
environmental and societal challenges (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017; Hahn & Tampe, 2021). For
comparative and analytical purposes, the categories sustainability-oriented (in 2.1.1), circular (in
2.1.2), and regenerative (in 2.1.3) are analysed individually due to their distinct approaches and
attributes.
This categorization highlights significant differences, particularly regarding regenerative
startups'
systemic,
long-term
focus
and
their
challenges
within
traditional investment
frameworks. The review also explores key aspects of impact investing, such as investor
priorities, evaluation methodologies, and processes, to identify perspectives of existing literature
on decision-making and impact evaluation. Finally, novel and evolving financial approaches,
including blended finance and Variable Capital Company (VCC) organizational structures, are
examined as potential solutions to align impact investment models with the unique attributes and
needs of regenerative businesses. This comprehensive approach ensures that the literature review
aligns with the thematic analysis, establishing a robust theoretical foundation to contextualize the
challenges and opportunities faced by regenerative startups in attracting and sustaining impact
investment.

2.1 Categorization of Impact Startups
To conceptualize the different types of startups in this thesis, it is important to first have a look at
the definition of a startup itself. While there is no standardized and universal outline, some key
characteristics have been established to describe a startup organization.
The idea of a startup is typically grounded in an innovation meeting an unmet need or solving a
specific problem within the market. This identified market gap is then addressed through the
creation and application of innovative technology, products, services, or business models
designed to offer a unique solution. Such innovation allows startups to differentiate themselves
by effectively responding to consumer demands that are either underserved or unaddressed by
existing offerings. An essential criterion is the absence of a pre-existing business model,
necessitating the creation of a novel business model (Geissdoerfer, Vladimirova, et al., 2018;
Sevilla-Bernardo et al., 2022).
Opposite to conventional small and medium enterprises, which are typically founded to serve a
local market with linear growth, innovation-driven startups aim for using their technology,
process, product or other innovation in order to rapidly expand and achieve exponential growth
in a scalable way (Aulet, 2013). Influenced by various determinants such as team, leadership,
business structure, culture and the access to technology, scalability remains a crucial role in
enabling growth and facilitating the survival of the startup (Hanifzadeh et al., 2024).
Startups, characterized by high levels of innovation and untested market environments, are
inherently exposed to significant risks. These risks span financial, market, and operational
domains, which create uncertainties in securing a customer base and obtaining necessary funding
(OECD, 2018a). The previously described prioritization of scalability and rapid growth
compounds these risks while enabling the potential for significant returns if managed
successfully (OECD, 2018a; Todeschini et al., 2017).
The substantial initial costs required to develop and implement new technologies, products, or
services typically result in negative cash flow for startups (Bauer et al., 2024). Consequently,
they rely heavily on external capital to support early operations.
9
==PAGE:16==
The amount and type of external funding required can vary widely, and this dependency often
influences strategic choices. To meet investor expectations, startups may need to make
compromises, which can affect both operational priorities and the business’s long-term direction
(Bauer et al., 2024; Garg & Shivam, 2017).
In academic literature, startups are generally defined as young, small-scale businesses
established to innovate or scale rapidly. While definitions vary, most studies typically classify
startups as companies which are maximum between 3 and 15 years old, with employee counts up
to a maximum of 50 to 250, depending on industry and region. These variations reflect the
diverse nature of startups and the focus on flexibility and high growth potential during their early
years (Baskoro et al., 2022; Cockayne, 2019).
In conclusion, for this thesis a startup will be conceptualized as a young venture no older than
15 years, with a workforce of up to 250 employees. Startups are characterized by their focus on
innovative solutions, typically through technological, product, service, or business model
developments which aim to fill specific gaps in the market. They prioritize rapid scalability and
high growth potential, distinguishing them from traditional small businesses with linear growth.
Startups are also characterized by their reliance on external funding to support their early-stage
growth and innovation.
2.1.1 Sustainability-Oriented
Sustainability-oriented startups are pivotal in addressing global environmental and social
challenges while maintaining economic viability. These businesses prioritize scalable solutions
that provide a sustainability net benefit, aligning with Environmental, Social, and Governance
(ESG) criteria (S. Evans et al., 2017; Horne & Fichter, 2022). Understanding their characteristics
offers a foundational perspective for comparing more complex models, such as regenerative
startups, and their appeal to impact investors.
10
==PAGE:17==
In response to the growing global concern of environmental health, concepts of implementing
ecological sustainability guidelines and management systems in organizations have already been
introduced throughout the 1990s (Daily & Huang, 2001). A major early concept of sustainability
in the organizational context was introduced by Elkington with the Triple Bottom Line (1998).
The three “bottom lines” social, economic and environmental are interdependent and constantly
shift for any organization. Designed as a framework to audit current environmental performance
and future targets in the ecosystem, the triple bottom line helps organizations to assess and
improve their sustainability performance (Elkington, 1998). Major subsequent scientific and
academic approaches build on the foundation of the triple bottom line (S. Evans et al., 2017).
While this concept was revised to understand organizational sustainability more thoroughly
(Dyllick & Hockerts, 2002), business model innovation was used to create sustainability-oriented
businesses (Lüdeke-Freund, 2010). Lüdeke-Freud defines a sustainable business model or
organizational eco-innovation as a business model which generates a competitive advantage
through greater customer value and simultaneously contributing to the sustainable development
of the company and the society (2010). Further, a process of business adaptation or creation
qualifies as sustainability innovation, if it promotes sustainable development through positive
contributions or by mitigating adverse impacts on the environment, society, and the enduring
economic prosperity of the organization and its stakeholders. Additionally, it has to integrate
sustainability-oriented solutions or features within the elements of its value proposition, value
creation, and value capture, as well as throughout its value network (Geissdoerfer, Vladimirova,
et al., 2018).
A sustainable start-up is defined as a newly created organization with a sustainable business
model
(Geissdoerfer,
Vladimirova,
et
al.,
2018).
However,
businesses
pursuing
sustainability-oriented models face multiple challenges, particularly in balancing profitability
with social and environmental objectives (S. Evans et al., 2017). As Evans (2017) notes, such
companies must manage complex trade-offs between financial goals, social impact, and
ecological responsibility.
11
==PAGE:18==
The scope and application of sustainability-oriented innovations are highly diverse, including
various types of innovations and strategic approaches to sustainable development (Bocken et al.,
2013; Klewitz & Hansen, 2014).
As
sustainability
has
become
a
recognized
megatrend
(Mittelstaedt
et
al.,
2014),
sustainability-oriented innovations have progressed alongside macro trends in areas such as
agricultural
innovation,
circular
economy,
clean
technology,
energy
harvesting, ethical
consumption, and zero waste. Most startups in this space primarily focus on product and service
innovations, with technical innovations following closely (Gaudig et al., 2021).
In recent years, scholars have conceptualized sustainability-oriented startups as their own domain
rather than a subdomain of sustainability-oriented innovations (Martins De Souza et al., 2024).
They are described as “[...] innovative new ventures that diffuse solutions at scale that have a
sustainability net benefit [...]” (Horne & Fichter, 2022, p. 1), further designating them as “impact
startups” (Horne & Fichter, 2022). De Souza (2024) outlined that sustainability-oriented startups
possess significant competitive advantages over those not focused on sustainability. This
advantage arises from their ability to align economic and environmental benefits, reducing the
need to compromise profitability for sustainability goals. Additionally, their frequent compliance
with ESG regulations further enhances their appeal to investors, making them particularly
attractive investment options (Martins De Souza et al., 2024).
For this thesis, a sustainability-oriented startup is a startup (as defined in 2.1), focused on
creating novel products, technologies, or business models specifically designed to address unmet
environmental and social needs. These startups integrate sustainability into their core business
operations, aligning economic, social, and environmental impacts. Characterized by their
scalability potential and reliance on external funding, they frequently comply with ESG criteria,
prioritizing growth that supports sustainable and societal goals.
The literature review revealed that sustainability-oriented startups have a broad spectrum,
allowing flexibility in how these businesses approach sustainability objectives. Thus these
startups can range from offering products, services, or technologies that are marginally less
harmful than conventional alternatives to implementing comprehensive sustainability strategies.
12
==PAGE:19==
While this flexibility may support scalability and investor alignment, it also highlights a potential
limitation of prioritizing incremental harm reduction while maintaining a highly lucrative
business model over transformative impact.
2.1.2 Circular Economy
Unlike the broad spectrum of sustainability-oriented startups, circular economy startups operate
within stricter boundaries as they aim to minimize waste and optimize resource use by creating
closed-loop systems through recycling, remanufacturing, or models like product-as-a-service
(PaaS) (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017).
The circular economy concept originated with Boulding (1966) as a closed-loop system
contrasting the linear industrial economy. Pearce and Turner (1990) further developed it by
incorporating
resource
provision, waste assimilation, and utility supply, solidifying its
foundational framework.
In the upcoming decades, the concept of a Circular Economy was increasingly researched and
described by academics and corporations. The modern conceptualization of the Circular
Economy, along with its implementation in economic systems and industrial processes,
integrates a diverse range of elements and insights from various frameworks that emphasize the
principle of closed-loop systems.
Some of the most popular and academically developed theories in the circular economy include
cradle-to-cradle (McDonough & Braungart, 2002), performance economy (M. Evans, 2013) and
blue economy (Pauli, 2010). The Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2013) designed a concept around
designing out waste, keeping products and materials in use, and regenerating natural systems. It
makes a distinction between technical cycles - materials like metals and plastics that are recycled
or reused - and biological cycles, which are things that break down and return to nature. With
this strategy, the economy is transformed from a linear "take-make-dispose" model to a
regenerative one that is advantageous to society and the environment (MacArthur, 2013).
13
==PAGE:20==
Another significant tool in circular economy development is the 9R Framework. Building on the
original 3Rs (Reduce, Reuse, and Recycle) the framework expands to include Refuse, Rethink,
Repair, Refurbish, Remanufacture, Repurpose, and Recover. In this hierarchy, Recycle or
Recover is ideally a last resort (De Melo et al., 2022). While the framework promotes
closed-loop practices, it offers limited specific guidance on individual-level actions and
behaviors (Hunger et al., 2024).
These concepts of the circular economy provide the foundation for specific business models and
business model innovation to transition from a linear to circular economy. These business
models, such as PaaS, closed-loop supply chains and resource recovery offer companies
pragmatic guidelines for the transition to implementing circular systems into their business
models (Bocken et al., 2016; MacArthur, 2013). These models offer unique approaches to
increasing resource efficiency, decreasing environmental impact, and prolonging product life
(Geissdoerfer et al., 2017; Urbinati et al., 2017). As a result, they are essential in promoting
creative methods of economic resilience and sustainable value generation (Lüdeke‐Freund et al.,
2019).
Although circular economy frameworks, such as the cradle-to-cradle model (McDonough &
Braungart, 2002), advocate for a comprehensive redesign across all stages of the business value
chain, most circular economy-oriented business model innovations concentrate on specific stages
of the innovation process. This focus frequently overlooks the need for continuous organizational
adjustments that enable the company to adapt its capabilities in response to both internal and
external dynamic changes (Pieroni et al., 2019). Building on the works of McDonough &
Braungart (2002) and Stahel (2010), the closed-loop system was expanded by Bocken (2016)
into a foundational circular business model framework with three strategies: slowing resource
loops
by
designing
durable products and extending their lifespans through repair or
remanufacturing, closing loops through recycling to maintain a circular flow of resources
between production and post-use and achieving resource efficiency by using fewer resources per
product through narrowed resource flows (Bocken et al., 2016).
14
==PAGE:21==
Another much discussed business model innovation approach within the circular economy is
PaaS (Tukker, 2015), which focuses on selling services or performance rather than products
(Kjaer et al., 2019). By retaining product ownership, companies can optimize resource use,
reduce waste, and ensure products are reused, remanufactured, or recycled. This creates a more
sustainable
and
resource-efficient
value
chain while generating recurring revenue and
strengthening customer relationships (Kjaer et al., 2019; Tukker, 2015).
Sustainability oriented and circular business models can be thought of as a subclass of business
models and are closely connected streams of literature (Antikainen & Valkokari, 2016).
However, the concepts of sustainability and circular economy should be evaluated as distinct
frameworks. While circular economy innovations are often regarded as mechanisms to promote
sustainability, they primarily emphasize the economic and environmental dimensions within a
more limited scope (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017).
Nonetheless, not all systems (e.g., businesses, value chains) which adopt circular principles are
necessarily more sustainable, as they may still overlook broader social and ecological impacts
(Geissdoerfer, Vladimirova, et al., 2018). When comparing sustainability-oriented and circular
economy oriented business model innovation, Pieroni et al. (2019) described, that there are both
shared objectives, such as resource efficiency and economic growth, but also distinct attributes
like social relevance as a key driver for value generation in sustainability-oriented innovations
(Pieroni et al., 2019).
For this thesis, we define a circular economy startup (as defined in 2.1) as one that follows
circular economy principles to reduce waste and optimize resource use. These startups create
closed-loop systems such as PaaS models, remanufacturing, or recycling to extend product life
and minimize resource consumption, aiming to decouple growth from raw material use. They
typically seek external funding and prioritize scalability to implement circular business models
that address both environmental and economic challenges.
Compared to sustainability-oriented innovations, circular economy startups also focus on
resource optimization but emphasize closed-loop systems, making them distinct within the
broader sustainability category.
15
==PAGE:22==
Circular economy startups have clearer, more focused objectives on resource efficiency and
waste reduction, which attracts impact investors seeking measurable environmental outcomes.
However, their reliance on specific infrastructure may limit scalability, appealing more to
investors prioritizing environmental impact over rapid growth or broader transformative change.

The Concept of Regeneration and Regenerative Economy
The concept itself and terms such as “Regeneration” or “regenerative” have been mentioned
increasingly in several domains over the past decades. These include conversational biology
(Young, 2000), medicine (C. Mason & Dunnill, 2008), agriculture (Newton et al., 2020) or
landscape (Gibbons et al., 2018) and industrial development (Rahimifard et al., 2018).
In sustainability, regeneration is linked to a net positive approach, shifting from merely
minimizing harm to actively restoring and enhancing socio-ecological systems (Rahimifard et
al., 2018; Reed, 2007; J. Robinson & Cole, 2015). Unlike sustainability, which focuses on
maintenance, regenerative thinking aims to restore and improve social and ecological systems,
creating thriving, adaptive networks in harmony with natural principles (Drupsteen & Wakkee,
2024; Du Plessis, 2012).
Regeneration is often viewed as a key component of the circular economy (Geissdoerfer et al.,
2017; Morseletto, 2020). However, over the past decade, regenerative economy has emerged as a
distinct concept.
16
==PAGE:23==
While definitions of the circular economy stress the integration of a restorative and regenerative
design, the primary objective of such design within the circular economy is to establish
closed-loop systems that optimize resource use while promoting the maintenance, regeneration,
and renewal of natural capital, resources, and materials to prevent value loss from the
environment (Drupsteen & Wakkee, 2024; Howard et al., 2019; Kirchherr et al., 2017;
Lewandowski, 2016; Morseletto, 2020).
In contrast, the regenerative economy encompasses environmental, social, and economic
regeneration within an integrated framework (Fullerton, 2015). Particularly relevant to sectors
like agriculture, It focuses on renewing natural capital - such as soil fertility, biodiversity, and
carbon storage - and developing resilient social infrastructures in areas like energy, water,
healthcare, education, and transportation to ensure long-term economic and social well-being
(Lehmann et al., 2022; Morseletto, 2020). By shifting from natural capital extraction to
reinvesting in ecological systems and community resilience, the regenerative economy goes
beyond resource efficiency to actively enhance environmental value (Drupsteen & Wakkee,
2024).
Fullerton (2015) describes the regenerative economy as a system modeled after natural
processes, emphasizing principles such as holistic wealth, community integration, adaptability,
and fostering innovation at system intersections. These principles support systemic health by
ensuring resource flows, balancing demands, and promoting resilient, long-lasting economic
structures that mirror healthy ecosystems. Strategies for sustainable co-evolution of human and
natural systems must recognize the interdependence of social, ecological, and economic health
(Fullerton, 2015; J. Robinson & Cole, 2015).
Regenerative Business
Narrowing down on a regenerative business, aspects of the regenerative economy are combined
with the intrinsic creativity and innovation of human beings to generate an advancement for
customers and employees, as described by Sandford (2017).
17
==PAGE:24==
Rather than being externalized in charitable giving or other forms of outside remuneration, the
regenerative principals are anchored in the company's financial income model (Drupsteen &
Wakkee, 2024). A primary objective of regenerative businesses is to support the health and
well-being of both people and the environment by initially slowing, halting, and reversing their
negative environmental impacts, followed by actively fostering positive effects on the
surrounding socio-ecological systems (Caldera et al., 2022; Konietzko et al., 2023).
The concepts of regenerative business models and strategies remain relatively novel in academic
literature. One of the most cited and extensively discussed approaches was developed by Hahn
and Tampe (2021). They define regenerative businesses as “[...] businesses that enhance, and
thrive through, the health of SES [Socio-Ecological-System] in a co-evolutionary process”
(Hahn & Tampe, 2021, p. 459). Moreover, two principles are described as the foundation for
regenerative business activities. First, systems-based level of aspiration, referring to setting
business objectives that are directly informed by the needs, health, and constraints of the
surrounding SES rather than solely by business interests, focusing on ensuring the resilience and
integrity of the SES in which the business operates. Second, an adaptive management approach
bearing a responsive, reflexive management style that continuously adjusts to the complexities
and changes within the SES, focusing on place-specific and cyclical adaptation rather than
uniform solutions and engaging in iterative learning with stakeholder participation (Hahn &
Tampe, 2021).
In order to implement the approach into practice, three specific regenerative strategies are
suggested:
1. Restore: activities strive to return the ecosystem to a functional or previous state after
exploitation, focusing on minimizing harm rather than driving fundamental change.
Frequently economically driven and centered around compensatory restoration efforts,
this approach incorporates some regenerative elements.
2. Preserve: focused on maintaining ecosystems in their natural state, minimizing impact
and supporting biodiversity. This strategy considers ecological boundaries and strives for
a balanced, codependent relationship between nature and business.
18
==PAGE:25==
3. Enhance: Create a net positive impact on SES by aiming to improve the adaptive and
life-supporting capacity of ecosystems. Through participatory, context-sensitive, and
iterative methods, businesses serve as catalysts for positive change, benefiting both
human communities and the environment. This strategy places an emphasis on symbiotic
connections with SES (Hahn & Tampe, 2021).
Thus while Hahn and Tampe suggest strategies for regenerative businesses which are not yet
net-positive (“Restore” and “Preserve”), other scholars see the aim for net-positive impact as a
key character of regenerative business along with a circular flow and the mutual participation of
relevant stakeholders (Konietzko et al., 2023; Ryan et al., 2023).
Das and Bocken (2024) conducted a study on regenerative businesses, finding that most operate
within the food, consumer goods, and fashion industries. Common strategies among these
businesses include regenerating biodiversity, revitalizing communities, and restoring ecosystems.
Notably, these ventures are predominantly small-scale (40.3%) and typically privately owned,
often by their founders (Das & Bocken, 2024).
For the purpose of this thesis, we conceptualize a regenerative economy startup as a small,
innovative business (in line with startup definition in section 2.1), which aims for actively
restoring and enhancing environmental, social and economic systems. While a precise definition
is still emerging and varies across sources, common attributes include net-positive impact,
systemic thinking and proactive regeneration of the ecology and community. Moreover, most
scholars are in unison that regenerative businesses transcend traditional sustainability-oriented
businesses by aiming to positively impact their socio-ecological surroundings. Major strategies
include Restore, Preserve and Enhance. Financial models are typically designed to reinvest in
both ecological and social capital, reflecting a multidimensional approach to capital.
The ambitious regenerative objectives may lead to challenges in attracting traditional impact
investment due to longer time horizons, complex systemic goals and the lack of standardized
metrics to capture their qualitative multidimensional impact.

2.2.1 Conceptualizing the Term “Impact”
The term “impact” has been utilized in several contexts and applications over the past decades,
such as psychology (Tesser & Campbell, 1980), healthcare (Biederman, 2000) or communication
studies (Schalkwijk, 1982). However, already in the last century, a predominant use of the term
“impact” began to be applied in the field of environmental science (T. Dietz & Rosa, 1994;
Leopold, 1971). Meadows (1974) described impact as the influence evolving technologies have
on our environment. In the years after, it has been increasingly used to describe the influence we
humans and businesses have on the environment and how it should be reduced (DeSimone &
Popoff, 1997). While the utilization of the term “impact” seemed to steer in the same direction, a
clear definition of the specific characteristics and scope was lacking.
Impact in the context of business models usually refers to the systemic changes that occur at
levels beyond individual stakeholders, affecting larger systems such as communities, ecosystems,
or global environments. Unlike value, which is the perceived benefit to stakeholders from a
business activity, impact involves long-term changes that can be either direct or indirect,
intended or unintended, and can contribute to both positive and negative outcomes at the system
level (Dembek et al., 2023). In this context, impacts are broader effects that emerge from
business model activities and often require a systems theory perspective to fully understand their
interconnectedness and scale. This perspective highlights the interrelations between system
elements, feedback loops, and delays, showing how individual stakeholder actions collectively
influence broader system behavior (Meadows & Wright, 2011).
Within the concept of “impact,” three subdivisions can be distinguished. First, environmental or
ecological impact describing the consequences human demands have on natural resources
(Glasson et al., 2012; Wackernagel & Rees, 1996).
23
==PAGE:30==
Second, social impact describes the broader, often complex effects on human well-being,
communities or equity and can range from improvements in health, education, and livelihoods to
unintended consequences like displacement or inequality (Bacq & Eddleston, 2018; Rawhouser
et al., 2019). Lastly, economic impact defines how environmental or resource-related business
activities and public policies affect the economic system in the long-term (Daly, 1996; S. Dietz et
al., 2021).
2.2.2 Measuring and Evaluating Impact
To investigate the impact outcome and assess what difference a certain intervention makes to the
outcome, different forms of measurement and evaluating methods are used (Gertler et al., 2010).
Commonly used frameworks like the SDGs, offer standardized indicators for measuring impact
and promote sectoral uniformity (Fukuda‐Parr & McNeill, 2019; Sachs et al., 2016). Researchers
support systems thinking frameworks that evaluate consequences across interrelated social and
ecological systems rather than only at the stakeholder level in order to capture complex,
system-wide changes (Geissdoerfer et al., 2017; Meadows & Wright, 2011). However, tools and
frameworks on the individual systems can be helpful and are frequently applied. For example,
Life Cycle Assessment can be used to calculate the environmental impact a product or service
has throughout its lifecycle (Hauschild et al., 2018). Regarding social systems, the Social Return
on Investment (SROI) is a tool to quantify social value by calculating the ratio of social
outcomes to investment costs (Yates & Marra, 2017). Despite a wealth of tools and frameworks
for impact assessment, scholars and industry professionals often differ on optimal approaches,
with choices largely dependent on the specific scope, context, and unique characteristics of the
ecosystem under consideration.
2.2.3 Defining Impact Investing
While impact itself can be positive or negative, intended or unintended (2.2.1), impact
investments are: “investments made with the intention to generate positive, measurable social
and / or environmental impact alongside a financial return” (GIIN, 2023). Although the phrase
itself is commonly used to refer to any investment that integrates ESG considerations, the
definition's central focus is on a systemic transformation (Busch et al., 2021). While impact
24
==PAGE:31==
investments aim to generate positive financial returns, they are also designed to deliver
measurable social or environmental benefits, positioning them as strategic tools to advance
societal goals aligned with the SDGs (Fernández Fernández et al., 2021; Kölbel et al., 2020).
Though especially common in certain sectors, there are no indications that impact investments
are limited to certain sectors (Höchstädter & Scheck, 2015).
The key differences and similarities between traditional and impact investors center on
objectives, returns, and investment structures. Both types of investors often use similar financial
vehicles, such as debt, equity and venture capital to structure investments (Fernández Fernández
et al., 2021; Höchstädter & Scheck, 2015). However, impact investors distinguish themselves
through a dual focus on financial returns and social or environmental benefits, an aspect that
is usually not considered in traditional investment approaches prioritizing market-rate financial
returns alone (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2021; Brest & Born, 2013). They intentionally focus on
generating impact, rather than merely viewing social or environmental benefits as a byproduct of
investment activities (Rangan et al., 2012).
While some impact funds aim for market-rate returns, they may also accept lower returns if they
lead to significant positive societal or environmental outcomes (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2021).
Studies suggest that impact funds may generate slightly lower internal rates of return (IRRs)
compared to traditional funds, though their structure often mirrors that of traditional private
equity funds (Barber et al., 2021). Both traditional and impact investors require financial stability
and sustainable returns from startups, but impact investors may be more flexible on financial
returns to prioritize positive societal outcomes (Heinz & Velamuri, 2024). Another notable
difference is the approach to evaluating startups: impact investors may place unique emphasis on
a company’s potential for societal impact, while traditional investors typically focus more on
financial metrics and team backgrounds (Block et al., 2021).
While the literature suggests the presence of impact-first investors, it has to be analysed how
they actually assess investment attractiveness and whether they are willing to compromise
financial returns to fund for example regenerative impact.

2.2.3 Types of Impact Investors
Impact investment types vary significantly, ranging from the socially conscious and sustainable
strategies of large institutional investors to fund managers' targeted investments in social
enterprises on behalf of high-net-worth individuals (Reisman et al., 2018).
The academic and business literature offers various frameworks for categorizing impact investors
and investment types. These frameworks often differentiate the types by investment institution
(Heinz & Velamuri, 2024). Synthesizing these perspectives with factors such as return
objectives, institutional influence, and sector focus, this paper categorizes impact investors based
on their profit orientation and their distinction as institutional or private actors.
Financial-First Impact Investors
Financial-first impact investors prioritize competitive, market-rate returns while integrating
impact objectives into their investment strategies. These investors aim to achieve financial
performance comparable to conventional investments but with an added emphasis on
sustainability or social impact. Examples include impact-focused venture capital firms, private
equity funds, and certain family offices. Venture capital firms in this category seek to deliver
high financial returns while funding enterprises in sectors like renewable energy, health-tech, and
education, employing private equity as their primary instrument (Fernández Fernández et al.,
2021; Findlay & Moran, 2019). Business angels can be considered financial-first investors when
they prioritize profit maximization in high-growth startups while occasionally integrating
secondary social or environmental objectives (Viglialoro et al., 2024).
Impact-First Investors
In contrast, impact-first investors accept below-market (concessionary) financial returns to
achieve deeper or more widespread social and environmental outcomes. Their willingness to
prioritize impact over financial gains enables the funding of projects and enterprises that might
not
attract
purely
financial-first investors. This group often includes foundations and
philanthropic organizations expanding their focus to hybrid or mission-related investing
(Fernández Fernández et al., 2021; Findlay & Moran, 2019). Philanthropists can be considered

==PAGE:33==
impact-first investors when they provide catalytic capital or program-related investments (PRIs)
to fund ventures that prioritize social or environmental outcomes over financial returns, often
de-risking projects to attract private capital (Brest & Born, 2013). Business angels operate as
impact-first investors when they intentionally prioritize social or environmental outcomes over
financial returns, often targeting underserved sectors like healthcare and clean technology
(Viglialoro et al., 2024).
Nonprofit and Public Sector Investors
Nonprofit organizations, including foundations and venture philanthropists, play an important
part in the early funding of impact investing. These investors provide grants, seed funding, and
first-loss capital to social enterprises, successfully filling financial gaps in high-risk industries or
projects without immediate economic viability (Moody, 2008; Tekula & Andersen, 2019). As
these enterprises develop and achieve financial sustainability, they often attract larger, traditional
impact investments while continuing to pursue their social missions (Höchstädter & Scheck,
2015). Philanthropists similarly fall within the nonprofit and public sector investor category if
they provide grants or other non-returnable capital to drive systemic change in industries such as
education and public health, without expecting financial returns (Moody, 2008). Public funding,
including governmental grants, serves as an additional funding source, particularly for
early-stage startups in the pre-seed and seed phases, helping them validate business models and
transition from conceptualization to commercialization (Islam et al., 2018).
2.2.4 Process of Impact Investing / Evaluation Criteria
The investment process for traditional and impact investors shares a foundational reliance on
thorough due diligence, yet diverges in priorities and methods due to the integration of social and
environmental impact considerations in impact investors.
For traditional investors, the due diligence process is linear, typically emphasizing profitability,
scalability, market acceptance, and exit strategies (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2019). It involves
assessing financial health, business models, and market conditions using established methods to
ensure risk-adjusted financial returns (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2019; Ormiston et al., 2015).
27
==PAGE:34==
Traditional tools effectively capture the operational and financial characteristics of startups,
focusing primarily on maximizing financial performance (Eckerle & Terzidis, 2024).
On the contrary, impact investors conduct the business due diligence of traditional investors and
incorporate additional layers of assessment, including the social mission, alignment with SDGs,
and the startup’s capacity for measurable impact (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2019; Ormiston et al.,
2015). Due diligence includes analyzing the entrepreneur’s ability and empathy to ensure
alignment between the startup’s impact mission and the investor’s goals, which increases
post-investment alignment and sustainability (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2021).
Impact investors also emphasize long-term engagement, using metrics like IRIS+ to monitor
social and environmental objectives mid-investment and beyond, integrating performance
measurement with the broader goals of the fund (Bouri et al., 2019; O’Flynn & Barnett, 2017).
The IRIS+ program, which was initiated by the GIIN, created the Impact Management Project
(IMP) guideline with the aim to build a global consensus on how to measure and manage impact
(Bouri et al., 2019). The framework requires the systematic collection of data across five key
impact dimensions. These include:
1. What: examines the outcomes generated by the enterprise.
2. Who: identifies the stakeholders experiencing these effects and assesses their level of
disadvantage prior to the intervention.
3. How Much: measures the scale, intensity, and duration of the outcomes.
4. Contribution: evaluates whether the enterprise’s or investor’s efforts have achieved
results superior to what might have occurred otherwise.
5. Risk: gauges the likelihood that the impact achieved may deviate from expectations.
Specific metrics have been developed for each dimension, enabling impact investors to assess
potential outcomes prior to making investment decisions (Bouri et al., 2019). These differences
highlight the dual focus of impact investing on both financial viability and societal value
creation.

Conclusion Impact Investing
The literature review highlights that impact investing involves a dual focus on both financial and
measurable impact returns, with a due diligence evaluating ventures on these two dimensions. It
introduces the concept of impact-first investors, who prioritize social or environmental
outcomes, and finance-first investors, who emphasize competitive financial returns while
integrating impact objectives. Notably, traditional impact investors seem to retain financial
requirements similar to non-impact investors, adding the expectation of positive environmental
or social outcomes.
This dual expectation raises questions about how regenerative startups, with their increased
complexity compared to sustainability-oriented and circular startups, can meet these criteria.
Their systemic, long-term goals challenge the traditional frameworks of impact investing,
necessitating a closer examination of how these ventures can attract support while addressing
their unique funding and operational needs. These insights suggest potential adaptations within
the impact investment landscape to better align with regenerative startups' transformative
objectives.
2.3 Emerging Investment Mechanisms
While sustainability-oriented and circular startups seem to be compatible with impact investing
for the most part, the literature review suggests a potential misalignment between the concept of
regenerative startups and the objectives of impact investors. To address this gap, existing impact
investment structures may require adaptation, for which novel approaches could play a critical
role.

==PAGE:37==
The following section examines two promising tools: blended finance (in 2.3.1) and Variable
Capital Companies (VCCs) (in 2.3.2). Blended finance combines public or philanthropic
capital with private investment, offering a way to de-risk high-impact projects and support
ventures with long-term, systemic goals. VCCs, on the other hand, provide flexible fund
structures that can accommodate diverse investor needs and long-term strategies. By exploring
these innovative mechanisms, this section provides insights into how they might enhance
funding opportunities for regenerative startups while aligning with their transformative

2.3.1 Blended Finance
Blended finance, defined as the strategic use of public or philanthropic funds to attract additional
private investment, has emerged as a powerful tool for addressing financing gaps in areas such as
climate action, poverty alleviation, and sustainable development. The concept, while relatively
new, combines concessional capital with commercial funding to de-risk investments and make
high-impact projects viable in otherwise challenging markets (Flammer et al., 2024; OECD,
2018a).
Key Principles and Mechanisms of Blended Finance
Blended finance operates on several core principles aimed at aligning diverse stakeholder
objectives. Public funds, often provided by Development Finance Institutions (DFIs) or
philanthropic entities, serve as catalytic capital, improving the risk-return profile for private
investors. Common instruments include concessional loans, guarantees, first-loss capital, and
performance-based grants. These mechanisms are designed to mobilize private capital,
particularly in sectors with high development impact but limited commercial appeal, such as
renewable energy, health, and education (Attridge & Engen, 2019; Habbel et al., 2021).
Structured funds, syndicated loans, and development impact bonds are some of the advanced
mechanisms that further refine blended finance operations. Each approach offers unique
advantages in targeting specific investment challenges. For example, development impact bonds
provide outcome-based payments that link funding directly to measurable social results,
incentivizing performance and innovation (Habbel et al., 2021).
31
==PAGE:38==
The Role of Philanthropy
Philanthropic organizations play a crucial role in blended finance by providing flexible,
risk-tolerant capital. This capital often bridges gaps in high-risk sectors, enabling projects that
otherwise would not attract private investment. For instance, philanthropy has been instrumental
in financing initiatives such as nature-based solutions for climate adaptation, which require
long-term commitments and yield benefits that are often non-monetary (Brears, 2022).
Philanthropic capital does not only reduce risk for private investors but also allows for
experimentation with innovative financing models (Flammer et al., 2024).
Current Applications and Sectoral Focus
Blended finance has been extensively applied in addressing global challenges aligned with the
SDGs. Key sectors include:
●
Climate Finance: Blended finance supports projects in renewable energy, sustainable
agriculture, and biodiversity conservation. Initiatives such as debt-for-nature swaps and
carbon offset projects exemplify its utility in tackling climate crises (Bracking & Leffel,
2021; Brears, 2022; Flammer et al., 2024).
●
Social Inclusion: Education, healthcare, and gender equity projects have benefited from
blended finance mechanisms, leveraging public funds to scale private investment in
underserved regions (Attridge & Engen, 2019).
●
Startups and Innovation: Blended finance is increasingly seen as a vehicle for
supporting entrepreneurial ecosystems, particularly in low- and middle-income countries.
By providing seed funding or concessional loans, blended finance can de-risk early-stage
ventures, making them attractive to private equity and venture capital (OECD, 2018b).
Advantages and Challenges
The advantages of blended finance include its ability to mobilize large volumes of private
capital, diversify funding sources, and address market failures by de-risking investments. Its
flexible structure accommodates a range of stakeholder goals, making it a versatile tool for
development finance (Flammer et al., 2024).
32
==PAGE:39==
However, challenges persist. A lack of standardized evaluation frameworks complicates the
assessment of novelty and impact. Moreover, critics argue that the heavy reliance on private
capital risks diverting attention from systemic changes required to address global challenges. For
example, the growing dominance of private actors in climate finance governance has raised
concerns about equity and accountability (Bracking & Leffel, 2021).
Future Potential in Startup Financing
Blended finance has significant potential to foster innovation and entrepreneurship, particularly
in developing economies. By combining public or philanthropic capital with venture capital,
startups addressing social or environmental challenges can secure funding while achieving
scalable impact. This approach not only mitigates early-stage risks but also builds investor
confidence in unproven markets (Habbel et al., 2021).
2.3.2 Variable Capital Company
Variable Capital Companies (VCCs) represent a transformative fund structure operating only in a
few financial hubs till date, including Singapore (Variable Capital Companies Act, 2018),
Luxembourg (Chen & Gordon, 2024), and Mauritius (Calcutteea, Subadar, & Sahai, 2022).
These structures are designed to offer flexibility in fund management while catering to both
domestic and international investors. For example, Singapore introduced the VCC framework
under the Variable Capital Companies Act (2018), aiming to strengthen its position as a global
financial hub.
Key Characteristics
A distinctive feature of VCCs is their ability to incorporate sub-funds, each operating with
segregated assets and liabilities (Neo, 2024). For instance, Singapore mandates that sub-funds
must be registered with the Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority (ACRA), ensuring
transparency and legal compliance (Variable Capital Companies Act, 2018). Sub-funds offer
fund managers the ability to segregate investment strategies under a single entity, allowing
investors to choose specific sub-funds aligned with their risk appetites. Mauritius has adopted a
33
==PAGE:40==
similar structure, emphasizing that sub-funds under a VCC cannot act independently but must
operate as part of the umbrella entity (Calcutteea et al., 2022).
In addition to structural flexibility, VCCs facilitate the pooling of diverse capital sources,
including investments from high-net-worth individuals, institutional investors, and venture
capitalists (Neo, 2024). Managers can issue multiple share classes with distinct rights, including
voting privileges, redemption rights, and dividend preferences. This allows for the creation of
tailored return profiles to meet varying investor demands. For example, the ability to pay
dividends out of capital provides income-focused investors with attractive payout options, a
feature highlighted in both the Singaporean and Mauritian frameworks (Calcutteea et al., 2022;
Variable Capital Companies, 2018).
Advantages and Disadvantages
There are several advantages for utilizing a VCC structure. The flexible handling of shares
allows both open-ended and closed-ended fund strategies, making VCCs adaptable to a variety of
investment approaches (Neo, 2024; Variable Capital Companies Act, 2018). Additionally, VCCs
offer a cost-efficient structure by allowing sub-funds under an umbrella entity to share service
providers, such as fund managers, custodians, auditors, and administrative agents, enabling
economies of scale. While maintaining a private shareholder register, VCCs further enhance
flexibility by accommodating sub-funds with distinct investment objectives, investors, and asset
classes within the same corporate framework (Neo, 2024).
Despite these benefits, challenges remain. As a relatively new concept, VCCs have limited
empirical data and academic analysis, leading to uncertainty among fund managers. This can
lead to challenges in establishing and maintaining such structures.
2.4 Conclusion Literature Review
To assess the investment potential of startups within the regenerative economy ecosystem
through the lens of impact investing, a thorough literature review was conducted to explore the
interconnected roles of impact investors and impact-related startups.
34
==PAGE:41==
Startups were categorized into three distinct groups: sustainability-oriented, circular, and
regenerative startups, each of which was analyzed to understand its unique characteristics,
business models, and investment potential.
The interplay between impact investing and regenerative startups raises several considerations
essential for understanding their conceptual alignment. Impact investing primarily aims to
generate measurable social or environmental impacts alongside financial returns (GIIN, 2023).
Sustainability-oriented startups align well with this objective, as they encompass diverse
business models integrating sustainability at their core, combined with high growth potential
(Section 2.1.1). This compatibility enables sustainability-oriented startups to fit seamlessly
within the broader scope of impact investing. Similarly, while circular economy startups share
certain objectives with sustainability-oriented ventures, they represent a distinct category
(Pieroni et al., 2019). Circular startups emphasize resource efficiency and product longevity,
focusing less on broader social and societal impacts (Section 2.1.2). Despite this narrower scope,
circular startups have demonstrated significant investment potential and are prominently
mentioned in GIIN reports as a growing sector within impact investing (Hand et al., 2024).
In contrast, regenerative startups show distinct characteristics. These ventures are conceptualized
as
integrating
systemic environmental, social, and economic regeneration, striving for
net-positive impacts that create thriving, holistic systems aligned with ecological and societal
needs. However, regenerative startups are notably absent from impact investment reports and
lack substantial records of investment activity (Section 2.1.3). The literature reveals critical
gaps in understanding their investment challenges and potential (1), their alignment with
the objectives of impact investing (2), and distinct funding requirements (3).
This study seeks to address these gaps by systematically answering the research questions
outlined in the introduction. First, analyzing the factors that drive the evaluation and
decision-making processes of impact investors will address uncertainties identified in the
literature, specifically regarding the criteria that startups must meet to be considered attractive
investment opportunities. While the theoretical frameworks and objectives of impact investing
are well-documented, there is a lack of clarity on how individual funds evaluate and prioritize
startups.

==PAGE:42==
Additionally, regenerative startups may present unique challenges for impact investors, such as
the difficulty in quantifying their holistic impacts using traditional investment metrics. Research
question one aims to understand these chances and challenges to fill the research gap (1).
Secondly, the distinct features of regenerative startups are likely to pose challenges when
attracting impact investment compared to sustainability-oriented and circular startups. These
challenges, such as systemic complexity, longer timeframes, and limited scalability, remain
underexplored in the literature. This study aims to generate insights that will carve out these

Finally, the current literature suggests a potential misalignment between the impact investment

these issues, the study seeks to provide actionable insights that will bridge the gap between the
new field of regenerative startups and the established frameworks of impact investing.

3. Methodology
The following methodology section provides an overview of the research design (in 3.1),
research question and methodology alignment (in 3.2), data collection (in 3.3) and analysis
processes (in 3.4) applied in this study. The chosen approach is aligned with the reviewed
literature and research objectives to explore the investment potential of regenerative economy
startups and their attractiveness to impact investors when evaluating those ventures.
The study utilizes a qualitative and explorative research design to allow in-depth discovery of the
perspectives, experiences and outlook of impact investors and founders of regenerative economy
startups in comparison with sustainability-oriented and circular startups.
Following this, a framework will be developed to help regenerative economy startups during
their funding process (in 3.5). This will involve adapting existing theories and models from the
literature, refining them through the interview results and conclusions from the identified
patterns. The methodology part will be concluded with a discussion of reliability and validation
in (3.6) as well as ethical considerations (in 3.7).
3.1 Research Design and Approach
This thesis employs an exploratory research design. The literature review indicates that the field
of sustainability-oriented, circular economy, and particularly regenerative economy startups in
relation to impact investing is an emerging area with several unresolved correlations and research
gaps. Exploratory research is typically employed when a phenomenon lacks sufficient existing
theory or empirical evidence, making it well-suited to address the objectives of this study (Elman
et al., 2020; Stebbins, 2001). According to Stebbins (2001), exploratory research seeks to
generate new insights and concepts, often through qualitative methods such as interviews or case
studies. It is particularly valuable for identifying patterns or variables that remain underexplored.
Given the limited theoretical foundation of funding potential and challenges for regenerative
ventures in the literature, no clear assumptions can be made regarding the relationship between
impact investors and regenerative economy startups in comparison to sustainability-oriented or
circular economy startups.
37
==PAGE:44==
Instead, this thesis aims to generate new knowledge and conceptual frameworks, for which
an exploratory design is most appropriate (Elman et al., 2020). As noted by Elman, Herring,
and Mahoney (2020), one of the key advantages of an exploratory approach is its flexibility,
enabling the researcher to adapt the focus as new insights emerge - particularly useful in areas
where theoretical frameworks are still in development, such as the field targeted by this study.
To achieve the research objectives, qualitative data will be collected. This data method is
particularly suited for exploratory studies, as it enables in-depth understanding of complex and
underexplored phenomena through the collection of rich, contextual data (Creswell & Poth,
2018; Stebbins, 2001).
To gather primary data, semi-structured interviews were selected as the most suitable method.
A key advantage of this format lies in its ability to maintain a focused discussion while granting
the researcher flexibility to investigate relevant ideas that emerge during the conversation,
thereby enriching the understanding of the context (Adeoye‐Olatunde & Olenik, 2021). The
structure allows the study to grasp the structures and standards of impact investors and founders
during the investment, while it also enables the opportunity to gain in-depth knowledge into the
subjective evaluations of impact investors and regenerative founders with regards to the potential
of regenerative startups in the current impact investment landscape.
Based on the identified gaps in the literature review and the research questions, an interview
guide was developed (see Appendix D). In order to ensure that the main themes of the research
goal were covered, open-ended questions were created to encourage participants to consider their
reasons, experiences, and challenges. In keeping with the flexible character of exploratory
research, the semi-structured approach allows iterative modifications to the questions in response
to new information (Adams, 2015; Adeoye‐Olatunde & Olenik, 2021). Two key participant
groups were chosen for the interviews: (1) founders or early key employees of regenerative
economy startups, sustainability-oriented and circular economy startups, in order to compare
the results of the different kinds of startups and (2) impact investors. An interview guide was
created for each participant to ensure capturing perspectives on the process and potential of these
investment processes.

==PAGE:45==
Based on the qualitative and exploratory nature of the study, an iterative research process is
applied. This allows to refine the interview questions, investigate emerging themes and
conceptualize the new insights which are uncovered during the interviews (Srivastava &
Hopwood, 2009). As unexpected patterns and relations in the investment process of impact
investors and regenerative founders may arise, the iterative approach enables the process of
investigating those.
The iterative process begins with initial interviews with the two participant groups. After each
interview, the transcripts are processed and preliminarily analyzed for emerging patterns and key
insights. While the basic interview guide will remain the same, the emerging insights will refine
the questions in subsequent interviews and potential new hypotheses will be tested with other

for or against viewing regenerative startups as an interesting investment opportunity, these
reasons will be tested with the upcoming interviewed impact investors.
The data analysis aligns with the exploratory, qualitative research design, investigating the
dynamics between regenerative startups and impact investors. To ensure rigor and transparency
in the inductive process, Braun & Clarke’s Thematic Analysis (2006) and the Gioia Method

generating actionable insights and contributing to theory-building (described further in section
3.4).
To address the third research question, a conceptual framework has been developed based on the

impact investment models with the unique characteristics and challenges of regenerative startups
( further explained in 3.5).
Due to the early stage and uncertainty in the definition of regenerative economy startups and

analysis of the qualitative data.

3.3.2 Sample Size
The sample size for this study was determined based on both theoretical and practical
considerations. In total, twelve semi-structured interviews were conducted. Five of those were
impact investors, four founders of regenerative startups, two of sustainability-oriented startups
and one circular startup. This range was chosen to ensure diversity of perspective while
remaining manageable for in-depth qualitative analysis.
While conducting the interviews with the regenerative founders and impact investors, responses
began to converge, and no new significant insights were identified, hence thematic saturation
was reached after the determined sample size. Saturation is academically recognized for
determining the adequacy of a sample size, which is typically reached after 9-17 interviews
(Hennink & Kaiser, 2022; Lowe et al., 2018). Regarding sustainability-oriented and circular
startups, the feedback was in line with the literature review and hence considered sufficient for
the explorative and comparative purpose. However, the sample size for those two startup
categories was limited and thus a potential bias due to their answers has to be recognized.
3.3.3 Sample Strategy
The purpose of this study is to capture diverse and significant insights from both founders and
investors, ensuring that major determinants are not missed. Participants were selected based on
their extensive experience in the fields of regenerative startups and impact investing, with a
proven track record in either investing in “impact” startups as investors or securing investment as
founders. This allows the research to build an ample understanding of the investment process and
challenges in the emerging field of regenerative economy startups.
To achieve this, purposive sampling was chosen as the sampling strategy. This approach allows
specific categories of individuals, who are presumed to have different or important perspectives
on the topic, to be adequately represented in the sample (Andrade, 2021; J. Mason, 2002). To
further refine the selection process, stratified sampling was applied to distinguish between two
key groups: impact investors and founders (O. C. Robinson, 2014).
47
==PAGE:54==
Within each stratified group, the study incorporates critical case sampling to prioritize
participants whose insights and experiences are likely to yield valuable conclusions applicable to
others within the group (Etikan, 2016). This method is combined with typical case sampling,
which, according to Etikan (2016), allows to draw precise conclusions from common
experiences and perspectives representative of the broader population. By combining these two
strategies, the study aims to first interview participants identified as likely possessing critical
knowledge - such as founders of regenerative economy startups that have secured significant
funding - while also including typical cases to capture insights representative of the broader
context. This aligns with the Gioia Method, which underscores the importance of selecting
knowledgeable agents thus participants with firsthand experience and deep expertise directly
relevant to the research questions (Gioia et al., 2013).

3.4 Data Analysis
The data analysis process combines Braun and Clarke’s (2006) six-step thematic analysis with
the Gioia Method (Gioia et al., 2013) to ensure both systematic coding and theoretical
development. This dual approach facilitates a comprehensive understanding of the data,
balancing participant perspectives with theoretical abstraction.
Thematic analysis, as outlined by Braun & Clarke (2006), provides the foundational framework
for systematically coding, analyzing, and interpreting the data. Building on this, the Gioia
Method (Gioia, Corley, & Hamilton, 2013) structures the findings by linking participant-driven
first-order concepts to researcher-led second-order themes and aggregate dimensions, facilitating
theory-building grounded in empirical evidence.
The analysis follows Braun & Clarke’s six-step thematic approach, with the integration of
Gioia’s structured theory-building framework. Applied to the purpose of this study, the steps are
as follows:
1. Familiarization with the Data:
After recording the interviews, a transcript (see Appendix E) was created using Microsoft
Word Online. The transcripts were reviewed by reading through them and listening to the
recordings again. During this step, first impressions and recurring ideas were marked and
color-coded. This process helped to establish a foundational understanding of the
perspectives offered by both investors and founders, while also discovering adjustments
to follow-up questions for subsequent interviews.
2. Generating Initial Codes:
The transcripts were systematically coded to identify meaningful data segments relevant
to the research questions. Codes were assigned manually using descriptive labels, such as
“impact due diligence process,” “regenerative startup definition,” or “evaluating
attractiveness for investment.” This step ensured all significant data was organized for
further analysis. Consistent with the Gioia Method, first-order concepts were identified
based on participant language, preserving their perspectives.

==PAGE:58==
Open coding was utilized during this stage, allowing a bottom-up approach that reflected
the key patterns and conceptualizations discovered in the data (Blair, 2015).
3. Searching for Themes:
The codes were grouped into broader themes based on patterns and relationships. For
example, codes like “strong financial returns necessary” and “financial benchmark like
traditional VC” were clustered under the theme “financial viability and returns.” This
thematic grouping allowed for the identification of distinct perspectives from the two

second-order themes were developed during this step.
4. Reviewing Themes:

ability to provide insights into the process and challenges within regenerative economy

5. Defining and Naming Themes:

3.6 Reliability and Validity
To achieve reliability, a researcher must employ systematic methods, maintain a clear decision

4.2 Discussion and Reflection on Findings
This study aims to explore the investment potential of regenerative startups through the lens of
impact investing, addressing gaps in understanding their compatibility and comparison to the
potential of sustainability-oriented and circular startups. Semi-structured interviews with key
stakeholders, including founders and investors, were conducted to address the identified research
gaps. The findings offer valuable industry insights into the challenges and opportunities that
regenerative startups present for impact investors, highlighting the necessary adaptations to
current impact investment models to accommodate the unique characteristics of regenerative
startups.
The Dual Imperative: Financial Viability and Impact in Investing
The first research question: “What factors drive the evaluation and decision-making processes
of impact investors when assessing investment opportunities” aims to address the identified
research gap, how individual impact funds prioritize the characteristics of impact-related startups
to evaluate their investment attractiveness. The literature describes the procedure of assessing
potential impact investments as combining a traditional venture capital linear due diligence
process, emphasizing profitability, scalability, market acceptance and exit strategies with
additional layers of assessment including social mission, alignment with the SDGs and the
startup’s capacity for measurable impact (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2019; Ormiston et al., 2015).
This approach was confirmed in the semi-structured interviews. All interviewed investors
highlighted the critical importance of conducting rigorous business due diligence. Depending on
the specific fund, this process was carried out either before (I2), after (I1), or simultaneously (I4)
with the impact due diligence and was performed by either the same investment team or a
dedicated “business” team. Several investors emphasized that financial considerations are the
cornerstone of the due diligence process: “[...] the most important part when covering impact
investments is to look at the financials” (I4).
61
==PAGE:68==
Although many investors underscored the necessity for achieving a significant impact (“[...]
when there is no significant impact, then we drop the case right away” (I2); “[...] if it’s a no, you
stop looking at it” (I1)), the analysis of interview responses suggests that a robust and financially
viable business model is an indispensable requirement for impact startups to be deemed
attractive investment opportunities.
Are impact investors willing to compromise?
One investor acknowledged the possibility of trade-offs when the impact is exceptionally high:
“[...] For highly impactful companies, there can be trade-offs. They might not be as scalable,
which can lead to potentially lower returns” (I1). However, the same investor seeks strong
financial returns, comparable to traditional Venture Capital (VC) benchmarks, and the majority
of investors rejected the notion of trade-offs between financial and impact performance,
emphasizing the non-negotiable requirement for financial viability: “So we don’t, we’re not
willing to trade off financial for impact [...]” (I4). These findings suggest that, within the current
impact investment landscape, regenerative startups must demonstrate a strong financial business
model to attract investment successfully.
No clear standard to evaluate impact
The methodologies used for impact assessment vary significantly among investors. While some
rely on established public frameworks, such as the IRIS+ IMP Framework (I1) or the planetary
boundary framework (I2), others employ proprietary assessment methodologies tailored to their
fund's specific strategy (I2, I3, I4). Although proprietary methodologies are not fully disclosed,
evidence suggests that widely used public frameworks may inadequately capture regenerative
practices (Bouri et al., 2019). While a major impact within a specific sector can be particularly
appealing to investors - e.g., “Unemployment in Spain and Europe is a significant challenge, so
solutions aimed at reducing employment gaps across sectors are particularly attractive” (I1) -
the distinctive opportunities presented by regenerative startups may be underrepresented or
overlooked during the impact assessment process. Consequently, these startups may not stand out
as attractive investment opportunities under the current evaluation criteria employed by impact
investors.
62
==PAGE:69==
Scalability as the crucial attractiveness factor
Scalability is highlighted in the literature as a critical component of the investment due diligence
process (Agrawal & Hockerts, 2019). The interview analysis further confirms its significance as
one of the primary factors that impact investors consider when evaluating the potential of impact
startups. Investor 2 emphasizes the necessity of scalability: “[...] of course, we don’t just want
things to be sustainable - we also want them to be scalable” (I2). Investor 4 takes this a step
further, identifying scalability as the main focus during startup assessments: “So, high scalability
is the first priority” (I4). This emphasis on scalability is echoed by founders, with one noting,
“The investors are used to scaling products that can grow 500% in three months” (F5).
Achieving a systemic impact that addresses both environmental and social factors is regarded as
a "nice-to-have" rather than a necessity by impact investors (I1, I2): “[...] having both isn’t
necessarily a prerequisite.” Consequently, one of the defining characteristics of regenerative
startups (Section 2.1), their integrated focus on environmental and social regeneration, is not
particularly prioritized as a key factor in determining their attractiveness to impact investors.
While financial aspects and scalability appear to be primary determinants of a startup's
attractiveness to impact investors, the specific strategy and methodologies of each investment
fund must also be carefully considered. The interviews revealed significant variation in focus
among investors when evaluating startups. Even within the same impact investment firm, the
criteria for assessing ventures can differ between funds: “The criteria for evaluating investments
often depend on the thesis of each fund” (I1). For instance, one fund may prioritize entering at a
low valuation (I4), while another might target specific industries (I1) or follow market leaders
and customers as co-investors (I4).
Despite these differences, all interviewed impact investors employ some form of categorization
for their investments. For example, one investor highlighted key areas of focus: “We invest
across the key areas: (1) Education and future of work, (2) Caring - this includes patient care,
elderly care, and mental health - and (3) Environment, which encompasses climate change
mitigation and adaptation, agriculture, food tech, and related fields” (I1).

Similarly, another investor outlined priorities in four key areas: climate mitigation, resource
efficiency, waste prevention, and biodiversity (I2).
Although the semi-structured interviews did not identify explicit examples of investors actively
operating within a regenerative investment category, it cannot be ruled out that some impact
investors might value systemic regeneration as an appealing factor in their evaluation processes.
The semi-structured interviews and subsequent analysis confirmed the literature's perspective of
impact investors employing a dual due diligence process that combines traditional venture capital
priorities, such as financial viability, with evaluations of a venture's environmental and social
impact. The findings revealed considerable variation in the specific methodologies investors use
to assess impact and determine a venture’s attractiveness. Despite these differences, the analysis
underscored that a financially robust and highly scalable business model remains the top priority
and key factor in attracting impact investors. In contrast to the literature, defining the existence
of impact-first investors, the data analysis discovered exclusively finance-first impact investors.
Regenerative startups, which prioritize systemic and multidimensional impacts, may face
challenges in meeting these traditional criteria, particularly when their impact is not adequately
captured by existing public or proprietary assessment frameworks and metrics.
Delving further into these challenges for regenerative startups, research question two (“What
kind of unique challenges do regenerative startups face in attracting impact investment compared
to sustainability-oriented and circular startups? “) aimed to fill the research gap, what kind of
unique challenges arise in the impact investment landscape due to the unique features of a
regenerative startup. To determine these features, it was first analyzed how the perception of
regenerative startup characteristics differ between founders and impact investors.

sustainability frameworks.

Are regenerative startups and impact investors not compatible?
These observations highlight the systemic barriers faced by regenerative startups within the
traditional impact investment landscape. As discussed in Section 2.2, impact investors typically
seek ventures that deliver social or environmental benefits alongside financial returns, with
traditional investment processes continuing to prioritize market-rate financial gains (Agrawal &
Hockerts, 2021; Brest & Born, 2013). Regulatory frameworks, such as Article 9 (Eurosif, 2022)
(see 2.2.5), incentivize and govern such investments. However, the underlying investment
mechanisms and vehicles remain largely unchanged - a reality that some founders perceive as
misaligned with the principles of regeneration. As Founder 1 explained, the current financial
system “in its current form, does not correspond to nature [...] the pace of finance [...] the way
that it’s measured” (F1).
The semi-structured interviews revealed a shared belief that a major challenge for regenerative
ventures is systemic in nature. As Founder 4 articulated, “We’re dealing with systemic problems,
not linear ones. So, even the idea of solving a single pain point or focusing on one job to be done
doesn’t work. These new startups that should be focused on regeneration shouldn’t just address
one job - they should focus on one system” (F4). Founder 3 echoed this perspective, emphasizing
that “the system we currently live in is so fundamentally different from the model we’re trying to
create.” These insights suggest that the difficulty regenerative startups face in achieving
acceptance and securing funding is rooted in systemic misalignment with existing investment
frameworks.
Founders of regenerative startups frequently express a strong sense that the existing investment
system is poorly suited to their ventures, leading to significant challenges in finding suitable
investors. As one founder noted, their approach involves “[...] a complex model that is difficult to
pitch to investors who tend to think in more traditional terms” (F3).

==PAGE:74==
Another highlighted the misalignment between typical investment goals and their regenerative
mission: “Many investors are focused on achieving returns through dividends or exits.
Meanwhile, our goal is to focus on regenerating nature, which doesn’t fit the typical investment

This perception of incompatibility is not limited to founders; investors themselves have
expressed similar reservations. As one investor stated, “If they can come up with another
business model that works, then maybe it could work. But as it stands, I don’t think it’s an
obvious case for VC” (I2). These mutual concerns raise an important question: what specific
factors emphasize this apparent misalignment between regenerative startups and the traditional
investment system?
The first identified determinant is the handling of proprietary technology and intellectual
property. According to the literature review, the creation of a novel, innovative technology,
product, service or business model is an important success factor for a startup (Geissdoerfer,
Vladimirova, et al., 2018). The aim for regenerative startups, however, is to share and
collaborate: “[...] the vision behind a regenerative startup is one of collaboration.” (F4). For
example, the venture of Founder 2 has shared their core technology with competitors. An
approach which lacks understanding amongst investors: “It’s not the typical approach, right?
When we talk to classic (impact) investors, they ask, “What’s your intellectual property? Why
can only you do this, and why are you going to cut everybody else out of the market?” The
answer is, we don’t. Instead, [...] we want other people to participate in this market” (F2). A
novel approach which often comes with a lack of profit orientation: “I’m not motivated by
becoming wealthy, but by the impact we can create” (F3). This is in sharp contrast with the
business model orientation of sustainability-oriented startups. Founder 5 described their vision as
creating a product which is at least as good as their competitors, plus being more sustainable
while fitting into existing systems: “We can’t ask [...] manufacturing companies [...] to invest in
new equipment or change their processes. It has to fit seamlessly into their existing operations”
(F5). Thus, while sustainability-oriented startups seem to find sustainable mitigation solutions
within the current systems, regenerative ventures might not fit into the current investment
landscape and thus find it challenging to secure impact investing.

Building on this, another significant challenge related to the financial viability of regenerative
startups lies in the short-term focus of investors and the emphasis on rapid growth and
scalability. Regenerative founders highlight the inherent mismatch between the pace of their
practices and the expectations of the current impact investment landscape: “It’s not the same
pace of nature and the way nature works” (F1). One founder described the tension with venture
capital expectations, stating: “VC’s are a nightmare in this area; they essentially drive extraction.
They put money into sustainable businesses and say, ‘You’ve got to scale really quickly,’ because

regeneration” (F1). This perspective is shared by another founder: “All the other VCs and
accelerators focus on short-term goals like six- or eight-month break-even points, which doesn’t
account for the cost of natural capital or externalities” (F4). Investors also confirm this
short-term focus and its implications for ventures requiring more time: “Startups that need more
time to be validated are not our first choice” (I4).
In contrast, both interviewed sustainability-oriented startups reported no significant issues in

decision rather than a systemic incompatibility (F7). This divergence underscores the unique
systemic barriers faced by regenerative ventures in the traditional investment ecosystem.
These challenges are particularly difficult to address, as they conflict with the natural pace and
principles of regeneration (F1), and regenerative founders appear unwilling to alter their business
models to fit conventional frameworks. One founder explained this resistance, stating: “[...]

Simultaneously, impact investors show no willingness to adjust their expectations: “[...] of
course, we don’t just want things to be sustainable - we also want them to be scalable” (I2). This
mutual reluctance creates a fundamental disconnect, making it difficult for regenerative ventures
to secure support within the existing impact investment paradigm.

sustainable is crucial for the health of the organization” (F2) and funders take a role of rather
enabling instead of directing. On the other hand, some founders highlight the need for flexibility
through mechanisms like unrestricted funding, which would provide “[...] flexibility and support
for ventures to adapt and evolve as necessary” (F2). This tension underscores the complexity of
defining optimal stakeholder roles in regenerative investment models.
Opinions are more unified regarding the level of collaboration between regenerative ventures and
their investors. Founder 1 observed that communities of founders are already collaborating to
develop regenerative investment models (F1). This aligns with the overarching mission of
regenerative impact startups: “If 19 other companies are striving towards the same impact goal
as you, you want all of them to succeed. You do not want them to fail or lose resources; instead,
you want those resources distributed collaboratively so everyone can reach their goals together”
(F3). These insights highlight the necessity for a regenerative investment model to foster
collaboration rather than competition. Achieving this requires the establishment of appropriate
infrastructure, alongside a reimagined organizational structure and investment business model
that prioritizes collective success.
Bridging the Gap: Aligning Investors with Regenerative Principles
A regenerative investment framework must fundamentally rethink traditional approaches to align
with the principles of regenerative ventures. Founder 1 emphasizes the need to pioneer “a
different model, a different way of doing things,” one that demonstrates to both investors and
companies that alternative, regenerative pathways are viable (F1). Central to this approach is a
"smart investment committee" capable of discerning and deploying the most appropriate type of
capital at any given stage of a venture’s development (F1).
74
==PAGE:81==
Such a framework must address structural complexities, as Founder 3 highlights: “Companies
are organized with ownership structures and legal frameworks that don’t allow capital to just
extract value. The value should remain within the system,” which poses unique challenges for
capital deployment in a regenerative context (F3). Additionally, Founder 4 critiques the
limitations of existing frameworks, noting that even with blended finance, ventures often remain
tied to traditional systems that fail to prioritize long-term regenerative goals (F4).
To address these constraints, a regenerative investment framework must incorporate not only
innovative financial strategies, such as the previously discussed blended finance, but also novel
operational approaches. Specific structural models proposed include cooperatives (F4), where all
stakeholders collaborate for mutual benefit; foundations paired with for-profit entities (F3) or
variable capital companies (F1). The latter can utilize a unified investment committee governed
by regenerative principles, enabling the deployment of multiple types of capital within the same
fund or across funds with differing return profiles (F1)(2.3.2).
Such flexibility is essential for accommodating diverse investor expectations. Interviews with
impact investors confirm the literature’s findings that their typical focus includes achieving both
measurable social or environmental benefits and strong financial returns (Agrawal & Hockerts,
2021; Brest & Born, 2013) (I1, I2, I3, I4). In contrast, philanthropists often prioritize long-term
impact and may seek tax benefits through charitable donations (F4). Their contributions,
frequently in the form of grants or concessional funding, are aimed at enabling ventures that lack
immediate profitability (F1).
Capture and evaluate Regenerative Impact remains a key challenge
Another key theme emerging from the semi-structured interviews is the role of impact
measurement and evaluation, a critical component of both existing impact investment models
and proposed regenerative investment frameworks. As previously discussed, a significant
misalignment exists between the business models and impact priorities of regenerative ventures
and traditional impact investment approaches.
75
==PAGE:82==
Conventional metrics, such as revenue streams and monetization models, often fail to capture the
complexity and interconnectedness that define regenerative enterprises. To address this,
evaluation frameworks must evolve to include deeper, systemic inquiries that challenge the
ventures’ contributions to holistic impact.
For instance, instead of focusing primarily on the speed of profit returns, evaluators should ask
questions like: “How are you creating systemic impact that integrates community, water, and
biodiversity?” (F4) or “What’s the expected systemic benefit over time?” (F6). These questions
represent a broader and more nuanced understanding of regenerative impact, contrasting sharply
with the narrow focus of current impact due diligence practices. However, a critical challenge
remains: determining who establishes these metrics and how the evaluation process can be
effectively monitored. Addressing this issue is essential to ensuring that impact measurement
aligns with the long-term, systemic goals of regenerative investment models.
Additionally, the identified challenge with current growth and timeframe expectations requires
adaptation towards a longer period. Evergreen funds, which are designed for perpetual operation
without exit, exemplify the commitment to long-term relationships between investors and the
communities or ecosystems they support (F1). These structures reject the transactional mindset
often seen in conventional finance, instead prioritizing reciprocal commitments and enduring
partnerships (F1). The focus moves away from short-term results and aligns with a vision of
sustainable, systemic benefits over decades or even centuries (F4, F6).
Closely related to the timeframe, impact evaluation in this model necessitates a reduced
emphasis on growth according to the nature of regeneration. Recognizing the finite nature of
resources, regenerative investment embraces slower, more intentional growth that aligns with
ecological limits and sustainable practices (F3, F4). This requires a fundamental behavioral and
philosophical shift from profit-driven objectives to values of stewardship and reciprocity (F1,
F4).
While such an approach may align with the objectives of philanthropists, who prioritize
long-term impact over financial returns, it stands in contrast to the primary goals of many current
impact investors, who often seek high scalability and substantial financial gains (I1,I2,I3,I4).
76
==PAGE:83==
However, the previously discussed blended finance (2.3.1) and innovative operational structures
(2.3.2) could potentially be designed to balance these differing priorities. By channeling both
financial and non-financial returns in ways that align with the diverse expectations of various
investor types, such frameworks may satisfy a broad range of stakeholders while simultaneously
allowing for a reduced growth expectation for regenerative startups.
Policymakers must incentivize Regenerative Investments
Lastly, regenerative founders have highlighted the need for external factors to be implemented
and adapted to foster regenerative investments. According to Founders 1 and 3, a fundamental
shift in the current economic paradigm is necessary to establish something “entirely new” (F3)
(F1). Achieving this transformation requires active involvement from the government, which is
corroborated by the insights of impact investors: “[...] policies definitely play a big role in
shaping these industries. From my perspective, it’s crucial to have the right regulations in place,
especially for impact finance” (F3). As the regenerative sector scales, the industry must drive this
transition, creating a broader movement towards regenerative ventures. This shift could apply
pressure on the government to recognize the emergence of an alternative economic system,
prompting the creation of new policies or even new ministries focused on integrating
regenerative aspects (F4). Such systemic changes are essential to align regulatory frameworks
with the unique goals and needs of regenerative practices, ensuring their long-term sustainability
and success. Analyzed in the literature review, governmental policies and regulations have
previously shown success in steering investments towards more impact-related solutions (2.2.5).
Unique characteristics require unique Investment Structures
Regenerative startups face unique systemic challenges in securing investment due to a
fundamental misalignment between their principles and traditional investment expectations.
Regenerative practices emphasize a long-term, systemic approach that aligns with natural
processes and prioritizes community, ecosystem, and biodiversity impacts. This contrasts sharply
with the short-term focus on scalability and financial returns characteristic of traditional impact
investing. Founders describe conventional venture capital models as “a nightmare,” driving
extraction and rapid growth that contradicts regenerative principles.
77
==PAGE:84==
They advocate for creating models that prioritize systemic change over expansion, ensuring
impactful practices are adopted widely without compromising their integrity.
In contrast, sustainability-oriented and circular startups report fewer challenges, finding greater
compatibility
with
conventional
investment frameworks. These ventures demonstrate a
willingness to adapt their models to investor demands, often accepting compromises to achieve
rapid scaling. This divergence highlights the systemic barriers faced by regenerative startups,
including skepticism from investors about their financial viability and the added complexity of
securing their impact through mechanisms like “golden shares” and profit allocations to
communities.
The findings underscore the need for a regenerative investment framework that accommodates
these unique characteristics. Suitable investors must adopt a systemic mindset, prioritize
long-term impacts over short-term returns, and embrace diverse funding mechanisms, such as
blended finance and philanthropy. A regenerative framework would also reimagine metrics of
success, emphasizing systemic impact over growth and revenue, and foster long-term
partnerships through models like evergreen funds.
This framework seeks to bridge the gap between regenerative ventures and investors, creating an
ecosystem where collaboration and shared stewardship replace competition and extraction. By
aligning financial structures, evaluation methods, and regulatory frameworks with regenerative
principles, such a model could unlock the potential for these ventures to thrive while driving
systemic economic and ecological transformation.

4.4.1 Investment Framework Process from Startup Perspective
Startups can enter the framework at two distinctive entry points: before having an established
business model for value creation or after a business model has been developed.
For early-stage startups who seek value creation, the separate entity allows for philanthropic
funding, fostering regenerative outcomes without immediate financial pressures. During this
initial stage, the focus is on defining core regenerative value propositions, such as ecological
restoration and community engagement. Additionally, possible business models around the
created value are explored and developed. Once these determinants are established, the startup
can transition into a for-profit VCC entity.
They enter the for-profit structure at the same place, where startups with established business
models can directly access the fund. Startups with an established business model engage with the
framework by initiating contact with the Management and Advisory Network. The due diligence
process is tailored to each startup’s specific boundaries and ecosystems, ensuring individualized
evaluations that reflect the unique potential of the venture. Qualitative assessments, conducted in
collaboration with the Impact Steward, capture the intangible impacts crucial to the startup’s
mission.
After successful evaluation, the startups are allocated to sub-funds corresponding to their risk
profiles - low, medium, or high risk - aligning investor expectations with the risk and potential of
each venture. This risk-based allocation organizes the diverse appetites of investor types while
maintaining a cohesive environment within each sub-fund. Investors receive advice from the
Management and Advisory Framework to align their investments with both financial goals and
regenerative aspirations.
91
==PAGE:98==
Integrated into the fund structure, startups join a collaborative, ecosystem specific environment
under the stewardship of both Impact and Business Stewards. This dual stewardship fosters
ongoing support and promotes sustainable growth within an evergreen structure, prioritizing
long-term participation in dividends and reinvestments over rapid exits. Remaining within the
model, startups can move from high-risk to lower-risk categories as they become more
established and their regenerative impacts solidify.
4.4.2 Challenges and Limitations of Framework
The initial framework was tested through a second round of interviews with regenerative
founders and subsequently revised to better address the founders' experience and their needs.
Thus, the presented final proposition for a regenerative investment framework provides a solid
reflection of the desired funding ecosystem. However, certain challenges have to be considered.
Based on the founders’ feedback, a primary challenge is finding investors willing to provide
substantial capital. While they agreed on the general existence of individuals interested in
investing in regenerative ventures, they also expressed serious doubts about the amount that is
possible to accumulate. Regenerative projects often require significant financial resources,
particularly in their early stages, to support long-term ecological and social goals. F1 noted,
“Investors might want to learn from each other but keep their investments separate,” indicating a
reluctance to commit larger sums without clear, aligned incentives. This hesitancy is grounded in
the requirement for investors to be patient and tolerate extended time horizons typical for
regenerative outcomes. All three founders noted that VC is especially difficult to integrate into
the blended finance pool for regenerative ventures, as the fundamental objectives are very
different (F1, F2, F3). Without attracting investors willing to provide higher amounts of capital,
the framework may struggle to fund ambitious projects that require substantial upfront
investment.
Another significant hurdle is the legal structuring and creation of separate entities. While the
structuring of a VCC Company, a novel and regionally limited organizational approach in itself,
comes with potential legal complexity, the main uncertainty lies in the separate nonprofit entity.
92
==PAGE:99==
Founder 2 emphasized the necessity of “using a foundation for philanthropic investments and a
fund for non-philanthropic investments” to prevent conflicts and preserve tax benefits for donors.
Establishing and maintaining these separate entities within a VCC structure can be legally and
administratively complex, especially when ensuring compliance across different jurisdictions.
Furthermore, Founder 3 highlighted that “fencing in the monetary economy” is crucial to protect
early-stage value creation from profit-driven pressures, necessitating robust legal frameworks to
enforce this separation.
Metrics for evaluating regenerative impact present another challenge. According to the
first-round interviews with impact investors, even within the current frameworks, the process and
components of impact evaluation can vary greatly and there are no clear standards. Additionally,
traditional financial metrics are insufficient for capturing the holistic, qualitative outcomes
central to regenerative projects, such as biodiversity restoration and community well-being.
Founder 3 stated, “Value creation is not happening in the traditional economy; it’s in nature,
human hearts, and relationships,” underscoring the difficulty in quantifying these intangible
benefits. Developing comprehensive, context-specific metrics that integrate both qualitative and
quantitative assessments is essential but remains an area requiring significant innovation,
academic studies and consensus among stakeholders.
Finally, achieving the proposed return profiles and aligning them with the investors’ preferences
may be challenging. Investors typically seek clear, predictable returns, while regenerative
ventures prioritize long-term ecological and social impacts over immediate financial gains.
Founder 2 confirmed the challenge by stating, “It’s not about a quick profit […] more like a
merged philanthropy and investment model.” Even regenerative interested funders likely have
return expectations which exceed the possibilities of the presented framework structure and
capabilities of participating startups. Balancing the need for financial sustainability with the
mission-driven objectives of regenerative ventures requires innovative financial instruments and
flexible return mechanisms that can accommodate diverse investor expectations without
compromising the framework’s core values.

5. Conclusion
The growing urgency of addressing environmental, societal and economic crises has created the
need for transformative business models that can drive systemic change. This research explored
the intersection of impact investing and regenerative startups - an emerging business model that
strives for net-positive, systemic regeneration. By examining their unique characteristics and the
challenges they face in attracting investment, the study has identified critical misalignment with
traditional impact investment frameworks and proposes transformative solutions.
5.1 Summary of Key Findings
This thesis findings can be synthesized into three parts. First, the conceptualization of
regenerative startups as a separate and unique category within impact startups. They transcend
sustainability-oriented and circular economy models by prioritizing systemic, net-positive
impact. These ventures aim not merely to reduce harm or increase resource efficiency but to
actively restore and enhance socio-ecological systems. The perspective of regenerative founders
has further revealed that the terminology of regenerative businesses is significantly different to
those of other impact startups.
Second, while comprising significant potential to tackle global challenges, the unique
characteristics and objectives of regenerative ventures result in additional difficulties in securing
funding. Traditional impact investment structures favor short-term, financially strong outcomes
and a high level of scalability, which does not align with the concept of regeneration. In contrast
to sustainability-oriented and circular startups, where investment structures and funding streams
have been established, regenerative startups seem to attract a lot of attention and interest to
invest, but the traditional impact investment models do not seem to provide the infrastructure to
align the investors with regenerative ventures.
94
==PAGE:101==
Third, based on the challenges for regenerative founders, a framework was developed to provide
an infrastructure which applies blended finance and a VCC structure. This framework was
empirically validated and revised to integrate the experiences of regenerative founders and create
a strong founder-centric bridge between the expectations of impact investors and the objectives
of regenerative startups. It comprises a foundation for facilitating increased funding for
regenerative startups.
Based on these findings, the research questions can be answered profoundly:
RQ1: What factors drive the evaluation and decision-making processes of impact
investors?
Impact investors evaluate opportunities based on a dual focus on measurable
social/environmental impact and financial returns. Their investment-attractiveness evaluation is
guided by standardized and proprietary frameworks, emphasizing scalability, financial
sustainability, and alignment with their mission. Investors consider startups as particularly
attractive opportunities, if they can demonstrate a clear impact while maintaining a strong
financial business model, high scalability and the potential for quick multiples in return.
However, this focus on short-term and measurable metrics tends to favor incremental, easily
monitored impacts over systemic and long-term transformations.
RQ2: What unique challenges do regenerative startups face in attracting impact
investment compared to sustainability-oriented and circular startups?
Regenerative startups encounter unique barriers due to their systemic ambitions and long-term
goals. Unlike sustainability-oriented startups, which focus on incremental harm reduction, or
circular startups, which emphasize resource efficiency, regenerative ventures aim to create
net-positive impacts by restoring ecosystems and fostering socio-economic resilience. This
fundamentally different and more complex scope often exceeds the capabilities of current impact
evaluation frameworks, making it challenging to quantify their value. Additionally, regenerative
startups require longer time horizons to achieve results and show a lower potential for scalability,
which clashes with the typical investment priorities of impact investors.
95
==PAGE:102==
The lack of suitable evaluation standards and the intentionally missing objective to utilize
competitive advantages and becoming wealthy reinforces the “unattractiveness” of regenerative
startups for traditional impact investment structures.
RQ3: How do current impact investment models need to adapt to align with the attributes
of regenerative startups?
Impact investment models must evolve to fully support the transformative potential of
regenerative startups. This requires adopting systemic frameworks that capture interconnected
social, environmental, and economic impacts, alongside qualitative evaluation metrics for
measuring multidimensional outcomes like ecosystem restoration and community resilience on
an individual level. Aggregated funding mechanisms, such as blended finance, can de-risk
investments and allow institutional finance to be activated. Additionally, organizational
structures need to evolve to incorporate diverse stakeholders, evergreen timelines as well as
diversified return and reinvestment profiles. These changes are crucial for enabling regenerative
startups to harness their full potential and drive systemic, net-positive change while reshaping the
impact investment landscape for a more resilient and equitable future.
5.2 Implications and Limitations
Fostering the funding of regenerative startups offers immense potential to address global
challenges, but it requires alignment between startups, investors and policymakers. This research
has identified the barriers regenerative startups face today and provided actionable insights to
enable regenerative impact investments. The implications extend beyond individual stakeholders,
emphasizing collaborative efforts to realize systemic, long-term impacts.
For regenerative startups, a collective and systemic effort is necessary to bridge the “valley of
death” when securing funding. While the founders are rightfully not willing to compromise their
impact to satisfy the investors expectations, they need to continue building structures for aligning
closer with impact investors. Beyond articulating their goals, startups should develop robust,
context-specific metrics that make their impact tangible and comprehensible to investors.
96
==PAGE:103==
Collaboration with mission-aligned investors and other stakeholders is critical, allowing
regenerative ventures to navigate early-stage funding challenges and prove their potential to
other impact investors. The developed framework provides a foundation to collaborate with
diverse stakeholders and put the systems into practice.
While there is evidently interest from impact investors in funding regenerative startups, the
research has discovered a significant misunderstanding in the values of such ventures and a
misalignment with their current evaluation and financing structures. The results of this thesis and
the developed framework provides a base for impact investors to better understand the value of
regenerative startups and how they need to adapt their structures to align expectations and foster
regenerative investments.
A crucial role in the alignment process is dedicated to policymakers. They are obliged to create
a supportive environment for regenerative ventures by offering incentives, such as tax benefits or
grants, that encourage long-term, systemic investments. Establishing standards for impact
measurement and fostering public-private partnerships has shown success for other impact
startups and can further strengthen the regenerative ecosystem. Governments also play a key role
in reducing administrative and legal barriers, enabling innovative financial structures like VCCs
to thrive.
Limitations
This research has several limitations that must be acknowledged. The early and explorative stage
of the regenerative startup ecosystem limited the scope of empirical data and obligated an
exploratory approach. While a high consensus was observed with the interviewed regenerative
founders, the number of interviewed sustainability-oriented and circular founders was limited
and the comparative analysis might be biased on their answers. Moreover, there are many more
types of impact investors who might have different opinions about investing in regenerative
startups. Finding the suitable investors who are willing to participate in the developed
regenerative investment framework will be a major challenge.
97
==PAGE:104==
Additionally, the lack of standardized methods for evaluating systemic, multidimensional and
regenerative impacts can hinder drawing comparisons and proving the significant positive
outcomes of regenerative startups comprehensively. These challenges underscore the need for
continued research and collaboration between academics, regenerative entrepreneurs and
investors to refine investment frameworks and strengthen the regenerative economy.
5.3 Reflections and Recommendations
This thesis underscores the immense potential of regenerative startups to drive systemic,
net-positive change but also discovers the significant funding difficulties due to the misalignment
with impact investors. Compared to sustainability-oriented and circular startups, regenerative
ventures follow a different and complex approach which requires a different thinking, lingo and
funding structure to succeed in the long-term. However, there is a profound reason for hope.
Regenerative founders experience interest from some types of funders and are working
collectively on building structures to align their regenerative objectives with the expectations of
impact investors. The research has further revealed that while impact investing is seemingly the
most logical and capital-rich funding instrument, it might not be the ideal funding vehicle for
regenerative startups. Instead, they require a fundamentally new structure which combines
several types of capital, relying heavily on philanthropy.
The designed investment framework provides a strong gateway to build on and work towards
investments which include regenerative values. For it to succeed and improve, all affected
stakeholders are responsible for contributing and working towards an aligned model. Developed
primarily based on the perspective of regenerative founders, they need to continue investigating
through conversations with investors to validate and revise the framework. At the same time,
investors who consider themselves impact-first are called out to reconsider the true value of
regenerative innovations and take the risk to participate in such novel investment structures.
98
==PAGE:105==
This research has discovered the unique potential and challenges that regenerative startups face
at this moment. To unlock their transformative power, investors, policymakers and other key
stakeholders must collaborate with regenerative startups to evolve together by redefining the
value of nature and regeneration, investment success and growth itself. If they align their
objectives and thinking, regenerative startups can become a driving force in the action against
our most substantial global challenges.
