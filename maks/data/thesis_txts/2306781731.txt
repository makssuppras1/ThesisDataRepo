Abstract
With the advances of next-generation sequencing technologies a huge amount of
metagenomics data is generated and stored in publicly available databases. Au-
tomated methods are needed to process those datasets and extract information
that can differentiate one cohort from another by means of different phenotype
traits.
This study introduces an automated pipeline called OMEGA which downloads
raw sequencing data from the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA), performs a
taxonomic annotation and stores taxonomies, abundances and meta-data  in a
MySQL database. A comparative analysis of two cohorts allows for identification
of differentially abundant species.
Two type-2 diabetes (T2D) studies that have previously been published, utilized
information at gene level which is a different approach compared to the OMEGA
method where species taxonomies are used. The two studies are composed of
fastq files from  43 healthy and 53 T2D individuals for the first study and 171
healthy and 174 T2D for the second study. They are used here as proof of con-
cept to identify species with importance to that metabolic disease. Using an ad-
justed p-value cutoff at 0.05, OMEGA identified 62% and 82% of the species that
were stated as significantly different between normal glucose tolerance and T2D
cohorts. In total processing the fastq files from one individual took an average of
8-10 hours using 24 CPUs and jobs from all individuals were started simultane-
ously by submitting them in parallel. A majority of the species identified as differ-
entially abundant were verified in literature search as important in relation to
T2D.

1. Introduction
Metagenomics is a relatively young discipline in the field of Environmental Ge-
nomics which analyses the genetic material of microbial communities sampled
directly from their natural habitat. It grants access to in-depth knowledge of phy-
logenetic and evolutionary traits, metabolic pathways, novel enzymatic discover-
ies or even in vitro design of desirable species for the weal of biotechnology in-
dustry. The benefits are numerous, as are the complications in regards to the ex-
ploration of those wild communities. More than ninety percent of the microbial
species live in specific habitat and can not survive out of it. Since most of them
are not able to be cultured in sterile laboratory environment, scientists have
faced many issues in cultivating trials [1,2]. The development of the PCR technol-
ogy and the 16S rRNA sequencing breakthrough [3,4]  have given a rise to ampli-
con sequencing and provided a solution to the enduring problem of unculterable
organisms. Moreover – a step forward to the world of high-throughput sequenc-
ing and metagenomic data analysis has been made. Nowadays, with the ad-
vances  of  next-generation  sequencing  platforms  and  the  constantly  dropping
prices per sequenced genome, a tremendous amount of data is being generated
on daily basis  [5]. Together with that, new issues emerge, such as demand for
high computational power and laggard computational analysis of large metage-
nomic datasets. This study is focusing on the latter.
A primer metagenomic analysis begins when a given microbial community of in-
terest  is  sampled,  passed  over  library  preparation,  amplified  and  sequenced
through either whole genome or whole transcriptome shotgun sequencing. Be-
sides the selection of sequencer and protocols for metagenomic DNA extraction,
bioinformatics  tasks  such as  quality  control,  alignment, taxonomic  detection,
functional annotation and comparative analyses play substantial role in the work-
flow (Figure  1). Each of the steps might take significant amount of time, espe-
cially if large collection of samples have to be processed at once. Additionally, all
this is done by a variety of computational tools whose number tends to increase
1
==PAGE:9==
as the metagenomic field progresses. The choice of software for each step of the
analysis is a difficult task and depends on multiple factors (i.e. computational
power requirements, result accuracy or type of analysis to be performed)  [6].
Moreover, each tool is accompanied by a user's manual and sometimes the infor-
mation is too technical and out of the scope for people with purely biological
background and prevalent “wet” lab experience. In this regard, we developed an
automated pipeline for metagenomic analysis (OMEGA). 
OMEGA is a set of modules that automates the taxonomy assignment from multi-
ple metagenomic samples and identify species or genes that significantly differ-
entiate two cohorts from each other. This is done by:
•
Implementing computational tools with predefined commands.
•
Establishing  MySQL  database  of  taxonomic  annotations  using  publicly
available fastq files accessible through the European Nucleotide Archive
(ENA).
•
Fast retrieval of MySQL data and analysis (i.e. normalization, Wald test) to
identify significant clade features between two cohorts.
OMEGA  is reference-based  in a sense that it uses  reference databases  with
known annotations to align filtered high-quality reads. It handles computational
tools with optimized settings to filter, align, annotate, store and analyze data in
one go. OMEGA is capable of downloading ENA raw sequencing data from pub-
licly available databases. To verify the accuracy of the analysis we used actual
datasets with published results from two independent T2D studies  [8,9]. It is
worth mentioning that in terms of reproducibility, it is very difficult, sometimes
nearly impossible to reach the same results for a particular study for number of
reasons [10]. A large fraction of the species found as significant in the verifica-
tion studies matches the findings achieved with OMEGA. Nonetheless, using the
pipeline  provides  a  substantially  greater  number  of  differentially  abundant
species between the healthy and T2D cohorts. Furthermore, it is also important
to show whether OMEGA is able to accomplish its whole workflow revealing a

2.1. Studies and datasets
Two independent studies, their datasets and meta-data were used for verification
purposes.
The first study done by Karlsson et. all [8] , referred in this paper as study 1, in-
vestigates the composition of the gut microbiota between stool samples of Euro-
pean women (n=145), diagnosed with T2D (n=53), impaired glucose tolerance
(IGT, n=49) and normal glucose tolerance (NGT, n=43). Fastq files with raw data
are available under accession number  ERP002469 in ENA  [11] database. IGT
samples have been used for training and prediction approaches by Karlsson et.
all and thus they are not included in the current analysis.
The second study, referred here as study 2, is carried out by Qin et. all [9] and
observes the differences in the gut microbiota amongst stool samples of Chinese
individuals (n=345). The research is implemented in 2 stages, where the first
stage examines 145 individuals diagnosed with T2D (n=71) and control samples
(n=74) and a second stage with a total of 200 individuals with T2D (n=100) and
control samples (n=100). Additional samples (n=27) have been used for classifi-
cation of T2D and thus are not included in the analysis of this study. Raw data is
available under accession number SRA050230 and SRA045646 in the ENA data-
base. The analyses are performed in 2 stages where each of the stages can be
treated individually as the data in stage 2 is used for enrichment of the data in
stage 1 and validation of the results already achieved. Nonetheless, samples
probed in both stages were used for analysis with the OMEGA pipeline.
Here it is important to indicate that both of the studies slightly differ in regards
to the workflows and statistical approaches as well as the conclusions based on
the taxonomies identified as differentially abundant between control and T2D pa-
tients. In addition, gut metagenomes diverge between populations of various ge-
ographical origins and for this reason de novo assembly is usually performed on
samples and gene catalog is created, merged with non-redundant reference gene
5
==PAGE:13==
catalog (i.e. Metagenomics of the Human Intestinal Tract - MetaHIT  [12]) and
then used for definitive annotation of unique genes present in a particular popu-
lation. One of the goals for this study is whether a common pattern of taxonomic
clades significantly different between two conditional datasets can be established
in global aspect, skipping the de novo assembly step.

2.2.1 European Nucleotide Archive.
The European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) is part of the International Nucleotide
Sequence Data Collaboration (INSDC) and harbors vast amount of nucleotide se-
quence data related to published genomic studies. The policy of INSDC allows
free data access where updated information regarding new entries is provided
on daily basis. INSDC is in partnership together with the Sequence Read Archive
(SRA) – part of NCBI with servers based in Bethesda, MD, USA and the DNA
Data  Bank  of  Japan  (DDBJ)  located  at  the  National  Institute  for  Genetics,
Mishima, Japan. All three archives synchronize their sequence data on daily basis
[11]. OMEGA is accessing data for bulk download of fastq files from ENA as it is
the closest server-wise location (EMBL – EBI, Hixton, UK). In this regard, a
“fastqFileReport” meta data file with various information about ENA submissions
can be downloaded through the ENA's ftp1. The list is also updated on daily basis
so it has to be downloaded regularly and check-ups for new studies is able to be
done through OMEGA by simply skipping the download of already existing fastq
files. The list includes not only metagenomic studies, so filtering of the data for
further download has to be done manually (Table  1). A total of 4660223 fastq

2.2.2 Annotation databases.
The following databases are used as references for annotation of the prepro-
cessed  high-quality  reads:  Bacteria,  Bacteria_draft,  Archaea,  Plasmid,  Virus,
Fungi, Invertebrates and Protozoa, accessible through the GenBank ftp [14]–[16],
MetaHitAssembly  [12,17],  HumanMicrobiome  [18],  Human  (ref.  Genome
GRCh38.p3),  nt  (GenBank+EMBL+DDBJ+RefSeq),  ResFinder  [19],  Virulence2
[20] (Table 2). Prior to the alignment of the reads these databases are selected
by default in two different modes: best mode and full mode [13].
In full mode the order of the databases does not matter and all filtered high-qual-
ity reads are mapped to each reference database selected. If the read does not
pass a certain threshold it is counted as unmapped and added to the total of un-
mapped reads unless a better alignment to another reference sequence is ob-
served. If the read is aligned and passes the threshold to more than one data-
base, each of the alignment is taken into account.

2.3.1 Mgmapper
As the name implies, MGmapper is software for reference-based mapping of
metagenomic reads, developed by Petersen et al., Sci. Rep., 2015 [13]. MGmap-
per works with Illumina fastq files and pipelines a variety of tools and custom
made scripts delivering taxonomic annotations and summarized statistical files.
All of the annotation databases are part of MGmapper.
2.3.2 Cutadapt
Prior to the alignment, a quality control of the reads has to be done in order to
get rid of the adapter sequences ligated to the reads during sequencing phase.
For this purpose Cutadapt was used [26]. Depending on the settings, Cutadapt
can remove adapters at the beginning, the end or within the read (Figure 3). The
algorithm used is called semi-global (or glocal) alignment and it allows sliding
the positions of two reads and penalizing the differences only in the overlapping
region between them, but not the initial and the trailing gaps (end-gaps), if any.
An example of semi-global alignment:
             ACCTAGG_TC_____
             | | |     | |
             _____ AGGCTCAAGC
In this case the only mismatch penalty will be the missing nucleotide before TC
on the first read. This allows for optimal scoring of the matching intervals. In
comparison, the same set of sequences would score less or it will not be even
aligned if global alignment is used:
ACCTAGG_TC
                             |                        |                            
 AGGCTCAAGC
Cutadapt utilize the term unit cost instead of score which counts all mismatches,
insertions and deletions as errors. An option for maximum error rate can be set
to specify how many errors are allowed. After all possible semi-global alignment
combinations of the two reads being aligned, the lowest error rate alignment is
calculated and if it is more than one – the alignment that starts from the leftmost

2.3.3 BWA
BWA (from “Burrows-Wheeler Aligner”) is software package for short read align-
ment against reference databases or large genomes [28]. It can deal with single-
and paired-end reads and is based on Burrows-Wheeler Transform algorithm [29]
which can briefly be explained as follows (Figure 4):
If  Σ  represents the alphabet,  $  is lexicographically smaller than every letter,
string X = a0,a1...an-1 where an-1 = $ as a last character of X. Suppose that X[i] = ai
and i = 0,1...n-1then i is the symbol of X so X[i,j] = ai...aj is a substring of X where
ai  is the first position (letter) of the string and aj  is the last. Then X[i, n-1]= string
and  X[n-1] is the suffix of  X. S[i] is the suffix array where i is the position of the
smallest permutation before lexicographically ordering. When B[i] = $, S(i) = 0
(the original string with added suffix) and  B[i]=X[S(i) – 1] in any other case.
Defining the length of X by |X| will also define |B| and so |X|=|B|=n. This is
needed to estimate the working space usage (in bits) n[log2n]. 
An example with alignment can be given. If Y is a substring of X then looking into
the sorted permutations, one can take into account Y as prefix of X. Knowing the
concepts above, suffix array interval can be constructed:
If Y is empty string then R(Y)min = 1 and R(Y)max = n-1. [R(Y)min , R(Y)max] is
called suffix array interval of Y and the positions of all occurrences of Y in X is: 
An example from figure 4 – the suffix array interval of 'na' is [5,6] looked up in
the sorted permutations. The suffix array value for that corresponding substring
is [4,2] and those are all occurrences of 'na' in the string X. Therefore, the align-
ment is based on lookup in the suffix array. If the alignment is restricted to exact
matching then only one interval can be found. Else, the intervals might vary.

Since BWA is short read aligner, developed back in the days for reads below 100
bp [30],  additional algorithms have been added allowing for alignment of longer
reads. One such algorithm is BWA-MEM, which is also the one set in OMEGA
[31]. BWA-MEM is able to deal with longer (100+ bp) as well as short reads,
thanks to the addition the seed-and-extend paradigm [31]. This algorithm is im-
plemented in several steps during the alignment. First, the query is set to a posi-
tion  in  the  reference  and  the  longest  exact  match  that  covers  the  query  is
recorded as seed. Seeds that are close to each other form a chain. Short chains
that occur many times in a long chain and represent less than 50% or 38 bp less
length than the long chain are removed with the perspective of reducing unsuc-
cessful seed extension later on. Seeds are then sorted by seed length and the
length of the chain they belong. During extension if a seed forms an alignment
already observed then this seed is removed. Else, the seed is extended and re-
fined by dynamic programing (Smith-Waterman [32]). BWA-MEM is very efficient
memory-wise, compared to hash-based indexing [33].

2.3.4 SAMtools
The Sequence Alignment/Map (SAM) format is widely used set of utilities for
storage and manipulation of sequencing data for reads aligned against reference
genome [34]. It allows operations such as indexing, sorting, merging, SNP call-
ing from and to SAM formatted files, which can be converted to binary (BAM)
format of reduced size. A fast conversion back to fastq files from BAM can be
done easily with BEDtools  [35] which is included in MGmapper. The SAM file
consist of headers (denoted by '@' at the beginning) holding meta-data about the
alignment (length, name) followed by  the alignments comprised by 11 manda-
tory columns (Table 3) with various information content. Optional fields in format
TAG:TYPE:VALUE with information about the alignment tool (BWA here) might
also be part of the SAM file.

2.3.5 MySQL
MySQL is a relational database management system, developed by the Swedish
company MySQL AB (“SQL” stands for “Structured Query Language”) [36]. It is
freely available (in most cases5) and extremely fast for adding and retrieving in-
formation content out of relational databases. The term “relational database”
means that the information stored in a database is related to each other. This re-
lation is done by defining a primary key which is unique and related to two or
more tables in the database. Doing that, MySQL will easily navigate through the
pool of information by looking up for definite primary key and then retrieve sec-
ondary information from the tables containing that key. In principle the more key
are defined, the faster search will be performed, but this also means that the
lookup will be more limited to tables containing those keys, and for that reason
primary keys should be chosen by some criteria which will optimize the search
speed but not limit the search itself. 
OMEGA takes the advantages of MySQL to create databases which are then used
to insert sorted information from MGmapper annotated files in tables of various
content. The primary keys for each database are chosen based on their overall
occurrence in the tables. Several MySQL commands are utilized in the OMEGA
pipeline in order to keep the automated pattern. All tables created by OMEGA
via MySQL are named with a prefix “omega_” to avoid conflicts with already ex-
isting tables in user's database, assuming that the combination of “prefix_table”
is non-existent. If, by any chance, the latter is not true then complications may
appear, except if the table in question is already existent from previous runs of
OMEGA. Before entering any kind of information to a table, tab-separated file
named after the table to be inserted in is created and can be found in user-de-
fined directory, where each column of that file represent the corresponding col-
umn of the table. As mentioned above, information retrieval is extremely fast. Ad-
ditional information for each table, column contents and primary keys chosen is
available in the supplementary information (Appendix III, Tables S1-7).

2.3.6.2 DESeq2 
DESeq2 is an R package developed primarily for differential gene expression
analysis, but it can be used successfully to find differentially abundant taxonomic
clades [36,37]. Here, for the intention of this study, the word “gene” is replaced
by “clade” from the information interpreted out of the technical manual of DE-
Seq2 to avoid confusions, but the concept of the analysis is the same.
DESeq2 assumes that a n x m dimensional matrix Xij with non-normalized read
counts is loaded as input, where each row i represents a clade, each column j is a
sample  accession  number  and  Xi,j  denotes  the  number  of  occurrences  (read
counts) of clade i in sample j. To be able to differentiate between two datasets
with diverge traits, a conditional matrix Mab is also required, where each row a is
a sample accession  corresponding to its relevant sample accession j in matrix X,
each column b is a conditional factor and Ma,b  is condition b for sample a in the
matrix. A generalized linear model (GLM) is fitted for each clade, with a logarith-
mic link:
log2qij = Σrxjrᵦir

study condition A and condition B), ᵦir is the log2-fold change (L2FC) coefficients
showing the overall abundance strength, and qij is the logarithmic quantity of the

DESeq2 handles the problem of dispersion variability by clade-wise dispersion
estimation. The concept is that by sharing information between clades can be as-
sumed that the ones with similar abundance have similar dispersion. Clade-wise
treats each clade's dispersion estimation individually. Low count data might be
an issue, because it makes the analysis noisy as clades with low read counts of-
ten tend to be highly abundant. For this purpose an empirical Bayes shrinkage
for log2-fold change estimation is performed. First, a binomial GLM is fitted to
the clade's count data and maximum likelihood estimates (MLE) for the log-fold
changes are obtained. Then a Cox-Reid adjusted maximum likelihood [42] is per-
formed for the fitted parameters for correction of biases. A logarithmic disper-
sion mean prior is estimated and prior variance is obtained. Finally, a maximum
posterior (MAP)  for the  log2-fold  change is  formulated. The  log2-fold  change
shrinkage is stronger with low read counts, but the latter is not the only determi-
nant – additional information such as high dispersion in read counts of similar
abundance will also show high log2-fold change shrinkage.
Having the log2-fold change shrunken estimate for each clade, a hypothesis test
is performed for significance differentiation. For this study Wald test [43]  was
used, dividing the  log2-fold change shrunken estimate by its standard error. A
correction for multiple testing is performed, adjusting the Wald p-values (Ben-
jamini and Hochberg [44]) and minimizing the false discovery rate (FDR). Clades
with little to no abundance are filtered out from the test statistic. DESeq2 allows
detection of outliers – clades with low counts in all but few to one sample that
might have impact on the log2-fold change estimate, but should not be included
because do not indicate for differential abundance. DESeq2 solve that issue by
using  Cook's  distance  [45] where each clade read  count for each sample is
flagged as an outlier if it passes a given Cook's distance threshold (0.99) and
then  remove  that  count  from  further  analysis  and  re-calculates  the  log2-fold
change, dispersion and adjusted p-value on the clade. For datasets with six or
less samples the whole clade is excluded from further analysis.

2.4 Methods, software settings and workflow
The OMEGA pipeline is partitioned into five modules that can be executed either
together or individually, depending on the user's needs. This section describes
the whole workflow, starting from module one as well as explanation of the set-
tings chosen for each module to obtain the results for this study (Table  4 shows
the settings only for processing of accession  ERP002469 because they are the
same as for  SRA045646  and  SRA050230).  More about each OMEGA module's
commands can be found in the supplementary documentation (Appendix II).
2.4.1 OMEGA_module1.pl
This module downloads fastq files from ENA and prepares them as input to
MGmapper throughout the following steps:
•
A text file with all fastq files' downloading locations for the particular
study is retrieved from ENA. By default, only paired-end fastq/fastq.gz
files are being downloaded, but that option can be changed to either sin-
gle-end or both modes. A check up is performed beforehand and if the file
already exists its download is skipped.
•
After all fastq files for the study of interest are downloaded, OMEGA_mod-
ule1.pl creates two lists (if paired-end mode) – one with the location of all
19
==PAGE:27==
forward reads files and one with the location of the reverse reads files.
These two lists are then taken as input to MGmapper.
2.4.2 OMEGA_module2.pl
After all raw reads files have been processed through MGmapper, a number of
files  generated  in  directory  “MGmapper”  are  used  as  input  for  the  second
OMEGA module. The module itself creates tab-separated files and inserts their
content into their respective MySQL table (Appendix III). Each file's name match
the name of its corresponding MySQL table.
2.4.3 OMEGA_module3.pl
After the information content of all files is being inserted, OMEGA_module3.pl is
used to retrieve inquiries in regards to  particular studies, samples or database
IDs. This is done through a set of MySQL commands embedded into the module's
script.
2.4.4 OMEGA_module4.pl
The fourth module from the OMEGA pipeline does clade classification and nor-
malization of each clade's relative abundance conducted by user defined set-
tings. OMEGA_module4.pl does normalization only on the abundances deliber-
ately, as they are meant for input to machine learning approaches on later basis
(see  Discussion).  Normalization  of  the  read  counts  is  done  by
OMEGA_module5.pl as part of the statistical analysis with the DESeq2 package.
In principle, OMEGA_module4.pl is a modified version of Mgmapper_classify [13]
with added normalization factor (10000000 by default) and a “study_sample” ad-
ditional column for study accession recognition.
2.4.5 OMEGA_module5.pl
After  clade  classification  and  data  redundancy  based  on  the  settings  from
OMEGA_module4.pl, the last module of the pipeline does comparative analysis of
the data throughout an embedded R script (Appendix I). This is done in few

4. Discussion
This study showed that OMEGA successfully facilitated the analysis of metage-
nomic  datasets  avoiding  the  waiting  time  between  each  analytical  step  and
greatly decreasing the time needed for downstream analysis. Furthermore, to
test the accuracy of the analysis with OMEGA, two T2D studies were used for
verification and comparative analysis was performed on each of them. Overall,
62% and 82% of the species found as differentially abundant in study 1 (p-adj. <
0.05)  and  study  2  (p-adj.  <  0.01)  were  correctly  identified  by  the  OMEGA
method. A literature check was done for the top 20 species collapsed at genus
level and approximately 85% of the genera were correctly identified with respect
to their relative abundance in T2D and NGT individuals.
4.1 Interpretation of the results
Interestingly, all genera found as control-enriched by OMEGA, except Prevotella,
were butyrate producers. Butyrate induces mucin synthesis which is glycoprotein
that maintains the epithelium in the gut [61]. On the other hand, the genus Pre-
votella is responsible for mucin degradation [62] and as such was also enriched
in controls compared to T2D subjects.
Lactic acid producing bacteria are normally associated with healthy human gut,
but in T2D cases the increased relative abundance of these bacteria, especially
the genus Lactobacillus, might be a cause  to chronic inflammations [63].
Other short-chain fatty acids (SCFA) producers, such as Bacteroides associated
with propionate production, are highly abundant in T2D individuals. A theory be-
hind  this  finding  suggests  that  the  increased  abundance of  SCFA  producers
might be caused by the decrease in the abundance of butyrate producing bacte-
ria as all of them are competing for utilizing of lactate as a carbon source [59].
However, the genus Alistipes, which is also associated with SCFA (propionate, ac-
35
==PAGE:43==
etate) production was incorrectly identified by OMEGA and closer look at species
level might be needed.
The results from OMEGA included an additional of 150 and 313 differentially
abundant species for study 1 and 2, respectively. This might be caused by the
different methods used in the analyses. While OMEGA relies solely on reference
annotation databases for the mapping step, the analyses in both of the verifica-
tion studies are done at gene level, where:
•
de novo  assembly on all samples is performed at first and the resulting
gene catalog is then merged with the gene catalog from MetaHIT.
•
Functional  annotation  is  done  against  the  KEGG  [64] (study  1)  and
KEGG+eggNOG  [65] databases (study 2) and taxonomies are identified
based on metagenomic clusters (MGCs)
Another reason for the differences in the findings between OMEGA and the stud-
ies might be the use of Willcoxon rank sum test (study 1 and 2) instead of Wald
test.
4.2 Future perspectives
By far, a few important topics in regards to the functionality of OMEGA were con-
sidered and the following changes and additions will be done in the upcoming re-
lease:
•
Custom databases - In its current state, OMEGA does the mapping against
14 databases, prioritizing bacterial genomes. Thus adding an option for a
custom created database will be valuable for users who want to do their
analysis on different organisms or just using databases that are comfort-
able for them.
•
MySQL on Computerome - Another crucial work to be done is the installa-
tion of MySQL on Computerome. The current release of OMEGA is not
completely automated, mainly because only the first module is available
there. The rest of the modules are on server with already installed MySQL
36
==PAGE:44==
and the file transfer between the servers is still a tedious task.
•
Parallel download - Changes with respect to the download of fastq files
from ENA will be done as well as currently the OMEGA downloads one file
at a time.
An already existent option in OMEGA is set to create abundance matrix for differ-
entially abundant species in format ready to be used as input to artificial neural
networks. A future milestone is to use these networks to determine more pre-
cisely the impact of each species on the development of gastrointestinal compli-
cations. Therefore, analyzing more datasets related to that problem would bring
more clarification in our attempts to create a pattern for disease diagnosis.
