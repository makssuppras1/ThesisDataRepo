Abstract
From the early 1960s, robotic arms have been used in industry to manufacture, sort and
assemble a wide range of everyday items such as cars and electronic components. How-
ever, without vision systems and sensors providing feedback from the environment, their
functionality is limited to performing only predetermined motions. Even with a simple cam-
era, robotic arms can adjust and plan their actions based on input from the environment,
almost like a human would.
In this thesis, a robotic system was designed, consisting of an industrial robotic arm, a
basic webcam for guiding the robot, and a conveyor belt that supplied various parts for
the robotic arm to handle. The objective of this project was to use the robotic arm to
pick up different objects from the conveyor belt using only input from the camera. Vision
and object detection algorithms provided the robotic arm with the real-time position of the
detected part. The robot used this position to move to the part, grasp it from the conveyor
belt, and place it in a designated slot.
In the testing phase of the project, the system had a pick and place success rate of more
than 95% for lower conveyor belt speeds, and over 85% at higher speeds. The project
focused on using simple and effective methods as well as adaptability, allowing for easy
changes or modifications to vision and control algorithms.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As modern industrial automation has developed, more and more tasks have been taken
over by robotic arms. Robotic arms have taken tasks that were once performed by peo-
ple, especially repetitive, exhausting, or dangerous tasks. This has led not only to more
efficient and faster production rates, with a reduction in human errors, but also expanded
what can be achieved in manufacturing. robotic arms have made it possible to manu-
facture more advanced products with greater efficiency and intelligence. Key jobs per-
formed by robotic arms involve object tracking, handling and manipulation. Today indus-
trial robotic arms are ubiquitous in sectors such as electronic assembly or car manufac-
turing.[1]
A big breakthrough in industrial automation was the implementation of computer vision.
Humans depend on their sight to locate objects and navigate in the surroundings, and in
the same way, robotic arms can benefit from integration of visual systems to detect and
localize objects in their workspace. Adding vision to robotic arms enabled them to adapt
to their operating area, and not only perform preprogrammed movements, but also adjust
them based on the real-time input from the environment. The same way as humans react
when something falls to the ground, robotic arms can be programmed to notice it and
react accordingly using their visual input. In a more complex scenario, when an object is
moving, humans can follow and catch it using their sight. Similarly, robotic arms can rely
on visual systems or other sensors to do the same. [2]
This advancement is part of a wider transformation known as Industry 4.0. It‚Äôs often called
the 4th industrial revolution and revolves around introducing smart factories where all the
machines and sensors communicate with each other to optimize the process of the tasks
they do. Vision in this context plays a crucial role by adding to the system the ability
to make decisions based on visual input. By giving robotic arms the ability to ‚Äôsee‚Äô and
make decisions based on the data from their surroundings, manufacturers have gained
enormous flexibility and can automate a wider range of tasks. [3]
This project is inspired by the development of industrial automation and computer vision.
It‚Äôs meant to explore what is possible within a professional setup using an industrial robotic
arm, conveyor belt and a gripper, while using a simple web-camera and a personal PC

The goal of this project was to simulate an industrial assembly environment by developing

modules, allowing for simple modification or even replacement if needed, without affecting
other parts of the code. This approach also presents best practices in the design of the
automation software.
Before the work started on the project, several objectives were established to clearly de-
fine the work that had to be done.
1.2
Objectives
The main objectives of this thesis, listed by the priority are:
1. Part - Choosing, designing and 3D printing a product.
2. Supporting structures - Constructing supporting structures such as storage unit
and mount for the camera.
3. Image processing - Create an image processing algorithm for detecting and cate-
gorising the components as well as pinpointing the key points for guiding the robotic
arm.
4. robotic arm control - Create a code for controlling the robotic arm movements
based on the information and data from the image processing algorithm. It should be
robust and precise, ensuring that the parts are reliably picked up from the conveyor
belt and moved to a designated slot in the storage unit.
5. System integration - Create a code that will integrate parts of the systems together:
robotic arm, conveyor belt, camera and gripper.
6. Testing - Test the complete system and improve its performance if possible.
1.3

constraints or as a way to simplify the project to avoid unnecessary complexity. By setting

‚ÄĘ Simplified product design - The product designed and handled by the robotic arm
in this project is only a representation of an actual component. It is intended to
simulate the dimensions and size of an actual product that could be found in a real-
world setting. Its width in the widest section had to also be smaller than the width of
the conveyor belt to prevent the product from extending beyond its boundaries. It‚Äôs
important to note that the product is only a simplification and/or representation and
was inspired by the shape of a real-world component. The aim of this project was to

Problem Analysis
2.1
State of the Art
Vision-guided robotic systems are used in many manufacturing settings, where efficiency
and reliability are important. Unlike traditional robotic systems, relying on fixed taught
positions, the integration of a vision system provides additional flexibility. Fixed positions
can be adjusted, and the robotic arm can use visual guidance to move to completely new
positions based on the visual localization.
2.1.1
Example Applications of Robotics and Vision in Industry
Automatic systems for assembly are widely used to manufacture everyday products. For
example, in the paper [5] an author presents a solution for assembling mobile phones
using an industrial robotic arm with 6 degrees of freedom. This system uses a stationary
camera mounted above the conveyor belt to detect screen and shell components for cell
phones and estimate their position for the robotic arm to grab them. The system demon-
strated that it is possible to achieve a 100% pickup rate and a 98% assembly success
rate for mobile phone components in a real industrial environment.
Robotic assembly systems can also be applied to large industrial components as demon-
strated in the paper [6]. In this system, the robotic arm is used for a novel idea of assem-
bling wind turbine blades using sensor feedback. The panels of the blades are picked up
by the robotic arm using a vision system mounted to the arm and then precisely guided to
their placement using force control combined with position-based visual servoing (PBVS).
Visual servoing refers to a technique where feedback from the camera is used to dynami-
cally adjust the robotic arm‚Äôs position in real time to ensure accuracy and good alignment
during the assembly process.
An interesting usage of automation and robotics can also be found in solving a bin pick-
ing problem. The authors of [7] use a camera mounted on a linear frame with two laser
sensors providing depth data to localise parts from a container (bin). Researchers de-
cided to avoid using 3D vision, which would involve complex data processing and higher
computational costs compared to using a 2D vision system.
2.1.2
Object Localization in Robotics Vision
To localise objects in the camera‚Äôs field of view, various approaches could be used includ-
ing masking, template matching or deep learning algorithms such as YOLO.

Masking is a technique that takes advantage of colour differences between objects and
their background. It is a simple and resource efficient algorithm, however it is only ef-
fective in environments with stable lighting conditions. Additionally, it may struggle with
detecting objects whose colour is similar to the background, so it can only be used in
specific circumstances [8].
Another method used in the vision system to distinguish objects of interest from the back-
ground is template matching. This technique compares sections of an image to a ref-
erence template to locate objects. It is effective when the object‚Äôs appearance remains
consistent, this means lighting conditions must be stable, and the object should not be
rotated or tilted, as template matching struggles with detecting rotated or tileted objects
[9].
A very common method used in automation for reliable object detection and localization is
using YOLOv8 deep learning model [10]. Researchers from China Agricultural University
[11] used it in combination with Kalman filtering and low-pass filter to detect, localise and
grasp oysters from a conveyor belt. They achieved a high success rate of oyster pickup
in static and dynamic settings. The limiting factor was conveyor belt speed which when
increased, decreased the success rate of pickups. To improve detection and grasping
accuracy, researchers proposed:
1. Stabilizing the robotic arm to reduce vibrations,
2. Mounting the camera higher above the conveyor belt to increase the field of view
and allow for faster detection,
3. Moving the camera away from the robotic arm to give it more time to adjust its
position before grasping the oyster, reducing the number of unsuccessful grasps.
2.1.3
Visual Servoing and Feedback Control
Visual servoing has advanced in recent years. A review by Hashmoto [12] highlights the
development of vision based control algorithms. He explains the two main methods used
for visual servoing: image-based visual servoing (IBVS) and position-based visual ser-
voing (PBVS). IBVS uses the image from the camera directly, which provides resilience
against camera calibration errors, but may be problematic due to camera-object interac-
tions. On the other hand, 3D pose of the target object estimated using PBVS is more
intuitive, because it uses real-world position, but is highly dependent on accurate 3D
modelling and camera calibration. Overall, visual servoing is especially useful for sys-
tems where the robotic arm needs to adaptively change its position based on the target
object.
This project was also inspired by researchers from the Automation and Control group at
the Technical University of Denmark. They developed a system for picking and placing
loins of meat from a conveyor belt onto designated racks [13]. Their system achieved
a success rate of 85% and the failures were mostly caused by low friction between the
gripper and the loin which was slipping. Their robotic process is divided into four stages:
cruising, tracking, grasping, and hanging, representing the actual robotic arm state divi-
sion used in this project.
2.1.4
Prediction and Filtering Techniques for Object Tracking
The authors of [14] propose a visual servo control algorithm to estimate the position of a
moving object. Instead of using position-based visual servoing (PBVS), which relies on
precise models of the camera and the robotic arm to calculate the actual coordinates of
the object in relation to the robotic arm‚Äôs base, the authors propose an image-based visual
6

==PAGE:17==
servoing (IBVS) which controls the robotic arm based solely on image plane information,
avoiding the need to reconstruct 3D coordinates. This approach eliminates the errors
from robotic arm and camera models, which could come from camera calibration and
hand-eye calibration (see Section 5.3 for more details). To further improve the precision
of their system, the authors used a Kalman filter to improve pose estimation. The results
demonstrated that the incorrect data from a camera can be drastically reduced when using
a Kalman filter to correct it.
In another work [15], the authors presented a novel approach of using an adaptive Kalman
filter to estimate the position of a moving object. In contrast to a traditional Kalman filter,
which relies on fixed noise covariance matrices, the adaptive Kalman filter estimates the
measurement noise in real-time, making it ideal for environments with changing and un-
predictable conditions. For example, where the conveyor speed changes or the lighting
conditions vary in time. To achieve it, authors used fuzzy logic controllers to tune the
Kalman filter in real-time. Those essentially work by measuring the discrepancy between
camera output and predicted output and if the prediction quality degrades fuzzy rules ad-
just the noise covariance matrices to improve the prediction. The result authors obtained
from implementing this method demonstrated significant improvement in tracking quality
compared to a traditional Kalman filtering.
2.2
Decisions
This project focuses on the development of a robotic system that tracks and accurately
grasps items from a conveyor belt.
‚ÄĘ Product design - simplified power supply used as a product handled by the robotic
arm. More about this topic can be found in Chapter 3.
‚ÄĘ Camera mounting point - When it comes to the vision system, it was decided that
the camera will be mounted on the robotic arm itself. This decision was guided by
the fact that it would be very difficult to set up just one camera to completely cover
the relevant workspace while having enough resolution to accurately detect and
pinpoint objects in the camera‚Äôs field of view. Mounting the camera on the robotic
arm allows the system to move the camera during the performance of each task,
providing flexibility during part detection. This approach simplifies the hardware
setup and eliminates the need for several fixed cameras.
‚ÄĘ Camera mounting tool - the camera on the robotic arm could be mounted in many
configurations. Figure 2.1a presents example variations in configurations with dashed
lines that indicate alternative optical axes. The solid line represents the optical axis
of a camera in a configuration used in this project. However, as described in the pa-
per [16], if the optical axis of the camera is not aligned with the Z-axis, as shown in
Figure 2.1a, even a small image error can cause millimetre-scale position misalign-
ments. This results from geometric transformations in the hand-eye transformation
procedure, (see Section 5.3) and to avoid adding additional sources of error it was
decided that the camera will be mounted with its optical axis parallel to the Z-axis,
as shown in Figure 2.1b.

Product design: power supply
3.1
Product design choice
After evaluating several options, at first a battery pack was chosen, because of its struc-
tured layout and relatively low complexity. This choice was inspired by the work presented
in a paper [17], where researchers designed a robotic system for assembling batches of
CR2023 cells. However, CR2023 cells were considered too small for this project‚Äôs re-
quirements, so a larger 26650 cell was selected instead. The prototype design, based on
the 26650 cell, is shown in Figure 3.1b.
However, it turned out to be a poor choice, since the batteries were rolling off the conveyor
belt. Moreover, the conveyor belt used had a width of only 8cm, which made it impractical
to design a small tray or fixture to hold batteries safely during the movement of the con-
veyor belt. Because of those limitations, this prototype was discarded and other product
ideas were considered.
The second idea was to design a simplified smoke detector (see Figure 3.1a). The concept
was inspired by a real-world solution developed by Lyric Robotics1. The smoke detector
prototype was less prone to shifting or rolling during the robotic arm handling process,
because of its flat shape. However, it was discarded, because the round design turned
out to be troublesome. Its circular shape meant that the gripper‚Äôs fingers would have to
be redesigned to securely handle it. This added complexity led to the decision to choose
a more well-shaped object for the robotic arm to manipulate.
The third design idea was a power supply. The design choice was proposed collabo-
ratively by the thesis promoter and the author. Several prototypes were designed and
tested. They were easy to detect by the vision system, because of their uniform rectangu-
lar shape. Rectangles have well-defined edges and corners making them straightforward
for computer vision algorithms to identify and localise. Additionally, two out of three parts
of the designed power supply did not cast any shadows, which is very helpful for the vision
system. It is well known that shadows in vision systems and robotics often cause errors
in object detection. Even though various methods to eliminate them were developed to
address this issue, shadows still remain a challenge in vision systems [18]. Each part of

3.2
Design
This power supply consists of three parts: the top casing, bottom casing and integrated
circuit board. The top casing includes grills for simulating ventilation slots similar to those
found in real power supplies. The bottom casing contains a press-fit slot and includes
internal pins for mounting the dummy integrated circuit board. The integrated circuit is
not based on a real design, it is simplified and only includes two vertical walls meant to
represent heat sinks. Those heat sink structures also help the robotic arm to grab and
manipulate the integrated circuit board. All of the parts were designed in Fusion 360 and
were 3D printed using different PLA colours to enhance visual distinction by the camera.

Robotic Arm

4.1.1
Robotic Arm Motion Control Driver
The first driver manages the movement of the robotic arm by sending commands in accor-

for robotic arm control. More details about the UR communication interfaces can be found
on the Universal Robots website: Overview of Client Interfaces2.
Communication with the robotic arm in Python is set up using the socket library, through
which the commands are sent encoded in UTF-8. An example command is shown bellow:

This instruction commands the robotic arm to move to a specific position p[0.15, 0.766,
0.081, 3.1415, 0, 0] with an acceleration of 0.05 m/s2 and maximum velocity of 0.1 m/s
within a 3-second time-frame.
The driver only includes the most crucial commands for the robotic arm to operate within
the scope of this project. Additionally, all errors and information are displayed in the ter-
minal with the help of the logging library.

Vision: camera and algorithms
Vision systems are crucial in the robotic industry. They are often used in collaboration
with robotic arms to guide their movement and interact with moving elements. Beyond
positioning, vision systems are also used to identify and categorize objects. In some other
industrial applications, they are also used to detect flaws, imperfections, or damaged parts.
Each section related to coding and algorithms represent separate modules in the system.
These modules can be exchanged or altered without impacting other modules.
The camera used for image acquisition in this project was a standard webcam with a
resolution of 1920x1080 pixels and a frame rate of 30 FPS.
5.1
Related work
A substantial part of the vision algorithms and methodology applied in this project was
presented in the DTU course 34759 Introduction to robotic arm Vision [20].
The approach used in the work [21] forms the foundation of this project. The setup in this
paper resembles the setup used in this project, the camera is mounted near the robotic
arm‚Äôs grippers and is oriented straight down. This paper also describes the method used
for calibrating robotic arm with the camera and how the coordinates of an object in the

system, as shown in the work [22].

5.3
Hand eye calibration
In robotic systems, calibrating a robotic arm with a camera is known as hand-eye calibra-
tion. This process establishes a spatial relationship between the robotic gripper (hand)
and a camera (eye). When the calibration is done correctly, it allows for the robotic arm
to effectively cooperate with the camera. In this project, the camera is mounted to the
robotic end effector with a custom 3D printed bracket.
The optical centre of the camera is offset 8 cm from the centre of the gripper and is taken
into account in the calibration process.
There are two primary methods of setting the system for vision-based robotic arm control
as described in a paper [26].
‚ÄĘ Hand-eye calibration - is used when the camera is mounted to the robotic arm
and moves with the robot. It involves finding a transformation from the robotic arm
end-effector frame to the camera frame.
‚ÄĘ Robot-world-hand-eye calibration - is used when the camera is set stationary in
the robotic arm‚Äôs work space. It involves finding a transformation from the robotic
arm base or robotic arm end-effector frame to the camera.
Those two approaches differ in a mathematical description and the number of transforma-
tions. In this project, a hand-eye calibration was chosen, because the camera is mounted
to the robotic arm‚Äôs end effector. As proposed by the method used by Y.C. Shiu, S. Ahmad

After completing the camera calibration and hand-eye calibration, the image processing

parameters tailored to it.

black rectangles on the sides of the frame. This method avoids shifting the image
coordinates, which would happen if the image was cropped as a whole.

5.6
Part classification and angle estimation
Before guiding the robotic arm to grab an object moving on the conveyor belt, it is crucial to
determine which part of the power supply is currently under the robotic arm‚Äôs end effector.
The identified part number determines which JSON configuration file is loaded for the
masking algorithm. If an incorrect file is selected, the image processing pipeline will fail
to correctly identify the object‚Äôs contour.
Moreover, the part number is used during the sorting phase, where each part is placed in
a specific location within a 3D-printed part sorter. The part numbering used in this project
is shown in Figure 5.5.
3 - Top Cover
2 - Integrated Circuit
1 - Bottom Casing
Figure 5.5: Power supply part numbering
The classification algorithm works based on the area of the contour of the part in the
camera field of view. The method used cycles rapidly between available part numbers
(in this case from 1 to 3). For each part number an appropriate JSON configuration file
is loaded, which contains HSV thresholds and morphological parameters for the masking
algorithm (see Section 5.5). If the area of the detected contour is larger than the fixed
threshold of 20000 pixels that part number is considered a match. However, this part
number is preliminary and is further checked to ensure that the algorithm is robust.
Once the preliminary part number is found, it is further checked by capturing three valid
areas and angles of the part. If it fails to do so within a 15-second timeout the part number
is considered invalid and the algorithm terminates and displays an appropriate message
in the terminal.
If the algorithm manages to collect three valid (non-None and non-empty) area and angle
values, the data is then filtered. Afterwards, the average of both is calculated and the final
part number is selected based on the averaged area.

5.7.1
Center pose tracking overview
The pinhole camera model describes the projection of a 3D point onto a 2D image plane
(see Figure 5.7). This model shows how a 3D point (X, Y, Z) is projected at coordinates
(u, v) onto the image plane, using intrinsic camera parameters and known camera geom-
etry. This subsequent algorithm for position estimation is based on this model.
The algorithm in this section uses contours provided by the function from Section 5.5
to estimate object coordinates relative to the robotics arm end-effector. This function
provides multiple contours, so this algorithm only chooses the contour with the maximal
area. This assumption is valid, because if the masking is done correctly contour with the
maximal area is the part right under the camera on the conveyor belt.
After selecting the contour with the maximal area a minimum-area bounding rectangle
is fitted around it. This step provides pixel coordinates u, v of the part‚Äôs centre as well
as its angle of rotation. Additionally, after this step four corner points of the rectangle
are calculated and sorted to ensure their stability between frames. This is necessary,
because the raw order of the points can vary significantly between frames, which would
lead to instability in visualisation or potential problems in the later calculations. The sorting
is done by computing the angle between each corner point and the centre of the rectangle.
And arrange them in the next step in an anti-clockwise order.
However, in this algorithm, the corner points are used only for visualization and not for
any calculations relevant to the pose estimation. Since the distance to the object in the

This chapter overviews the motion and velocity control of the robotic arm. Efficient control
of the robotic arm‚Äôs movement is important to quickly and accurately catch parts moving on

The information about the rotation of the part is provided by the vision system designed

6.2
Standby
In standby mode, the robotic arm hovers stationary above the conveyor belt. The goal
in this mode is to detect a part on the conveyor and calculate its angle of rotation (see
Section 5.6) as well as the initial velocity of the moving part. Estimating the initial velocity
is crucial for the subsequent algorithms to work correctly, it will be further discussed in
section: 6.3.
6.2.1
Initial calculation of the part‚Äôs velocity
The average velocity vx, avg of the moving part is found by capturing several frames with a
camera and figuring out the part‚Äôs position in each frame. This velocity is measured along
the robotic arm X-axis (see Figure 6.3). The average velocity measured by this method
is negative, because the conveyor belt moves in the opposite direction to the robotic arm
X-axis.
Only the third and third-from the last positions are used for the average velocity calcula-
tions. In this way potentially noisy measurements are avoided, which often occur at the
beginning or end of the sequence. This usually happens, when the object is just entering
or leaving the camera‚Äôs field of view. The measured position can be then unstable due to
noise or because the part is not yet fully visible.
To check the visibility of the part, the pixel area of the object is calculated and compared
to a fixed threshold. If this area is smaller than this threshold, the object is considered not

fully visible, and the corresponding reading is discarded. However, this method does not
entirely prevent noisy measurements. Therefore, some of the measurements from the
start and the end of the sequence are still discarded.
This method is relatively simple and could be improved, but it proved to be reliable and
good enough for this project. An alternative method would be to check the aspect ratio
of the detected part and comparing it to a predefined reference value - big deviations
from a predefined reference aspect ratio would indicate that the part is not fully visible.
This approach also handles changes in the camera height better than the area method
used in this project. However, the camera is always at the same height, so this change is
unnecessary.

6.3
Pursuit
The goal in the pursuit mode is to follow the part until the robotic arm gripper is directly
above it to later grasp the part during the descend mode 6.4. During the pursuit mode, the
robotic arm tracks the part detected by the vision system using either real-time feedback
from the camera or a predicted position.
6.3.1
Robust coordinates
To track a moving part on the conveyor belt, the robotic arm is controlled using velocity
vectors that are sent continuously to the robot. Their calculation relies on the part‚Äôs coor-
dinates provided by the vision system (xrel, yrel). To maintain stable control over the robot,
these coordinates must be delivered in real time consistently and without delays.

object, when the algorithm detects invalid coordinates from the vision system, it switches
to using predicted coordinates. These are calculated from the last valid position of its
timestamp, as shown in equations (6.3) and (6.4).

The data gathered during the testing gave some insights into the behaviour of the system
at different conveyor belt velocities. Table 7.1 overviews the number of successful and
unsuccessful pickup attempts for each part at different conveyor belt velocities. Table 7.2
shows the timing and distance data during the conducted tests.
The data from the Table 7.1 was analysed based on the inverter frequency:
‚ÄĘ 25 Hz - When the conveyor moved at the highest considered velocity, the robotic arm
grabbed almost all parts number 1 and all the parts with number 2. However, part
number 3 proved problematic. Its velocity could often not be estimated, resulting
in errors. This is most likely, because the vision algorithms could not get sufficient
data to correctly estimate velocity of the moving part. Part number 3 appeared the
biggest from the perspective of the camera, therefore the vision algorithms had the
least amount of time to assess the velocity out of all the parts.
‚ÄĘ 20 Hz - At this frequency there was a significant improvement in performance in the
pick up of part number 3. At lower velocity, the vision algorithms had sufficient time
to estimate velocity of the moving part reliably. Overall, the system performed the
best at this velocity, with only 4 unsuccessful grasps out of 150.
‚ÄĘ 15 Hz - At the slowest conveyor belt velocity, the performance of the system was
similar to that observed at 20 Hz. Although, it was noted that the robotic arm at the
beginning of its movement moved relatively quickly and often overshoot its target.
The robotic arm usually corrected its trajectory and slowed down, however this be-
haviour sometimes caused the robotic arm to fail to pick up a part from the conveyor
belt.
The results from Table 7.2 show that when the conveyor belt moves slowly, the robotic
arm doesn‚Äôt travel as far, but it takes longer to estimate the velocity of the moving part
and catch it. This behaviour is expected, because the vision system uses as much time
as it can to estimate the velocity of the part, and at slower speeds of the conveyor belt,
the part remains longer in the camera‚Äôs field of view.
When the conveyor belt moves at its fastest speed, the times for identifying the part, esti-
mating velocity, and catch time remain relatively consistent. However for lower conveyor
belt speeds, the catch time becomes more variable, increasing the slower the conveyor
belt moves. This is likely, because of the overshooting behaviour described in List 7.1 for
frequency of 15Hz.
Overall, the testing showed that the system is generally reliable, but needs improvements
when it comes to catching part number 3. At the highest speed of the conveyor belt, the
system often failed to estimate its velocity, which is crucial for successful pick ups.

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to design a system for real-time object tracking and handling,
demonstrated through a case study using a simplified 3D-printed power supply meant to
mimic a product that could be found in industrial manufacturing. The project successfully
showed that even with relatively simple hardware - a webcam and a PC - combined with
an industrial robotic arm, conveyor belt, and gripper, it is possible to develop a functional
robotic system.
The vision system was fundamental to this project. It was used to determine the real-world
position of objects on the conveyor from image data. It provided accurate information
about the position of the object, but the image processing algorithms introduced delays.
This limited the update rate of data sent to the robotic arm, which reduced the system‚Äôs
ability to handle faster-moving objects. The key algorithm used to detect objects, colour
masking, although simple and mostly reliable, proved to be dependent on consistent light-
ing. While brightly coloured parts (blue and green) were used to improve visibility in this
experiment, such conditions are rare in real industrial settings. Shadows, especially for
the top cover of the power supply, caused inaccurate speed estimations and sometimes
detection failures. More advanced techniques such as a machine learning algorithm could
greatly improve the robustness of the system.
The control system for the robotic arm was mostly reliable, and effectively managed the
robotic arm‚Äôs velocities. However, it could be improved. In the current implementation
the robotic arm waits for a full velocity estimation before moving, which is not optimal and
could limit the performance of the system. If the robotic arm began its motion immediately
after detecting an object it could significantly improve the performance. Additional sensors
such as depth cameras could further enhance flexibility, since in this project the robotic
arm operated at a constant height when pursuing the part for simplicity.
At lower conveyor speeds, the system achieved a success rate above 95%, demonstrating
high reliability. Even at higher speeds, performance remained reasonable, although the
problematic top cover still posed some challenges and the system often failed to estimate
its velocity quickly enough, resulting in missed or failed grasps.
Overall, this project shows that an effective robotic system can be designed with the use
of low-cost hardware alongside industrial class hardware.
