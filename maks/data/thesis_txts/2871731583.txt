Abstract
With the continuous scaling of data center processors and AI accelerators, modern power
delivery systems face increasingly stringent requirements for high current, low output
voltage, and compact form factor. Traditional two-stage solutions are gradually becom-
ing inadequate in meeting the demands of power density and conversion efficiency. In
response, this thesis presents the design, implementation, and validation of an isolated
full-bridge current doubler rectifier (CDR) module, optimized for 48 V to 1 V single-stage
conversion, high output current, and vertical modular stacking.
The proposed topology employs a current doubler rectifier for its inherent magnetic
integration advantages and structural compactness. To further improve performance, a
novel magnetically integrated four-phase coupled inductor is developed. Drawing on the
interleaving principles of multiphase buck converters, the current doubler is reinterpreted
as a 180◦two-phase interleaved system. Negative coupling is introduced to achieve flux
cancellation and enable inductor downsizing.
A comprehensive magnetic reluctance
model is constructed to derive the post-coupling equivalent inductance formula, which
is validated through finite element simulations. Design trade-offs among core shapes,
leg heights, and magnetic materials are explored, resulting in the selection of a compact
inner-leg structure and DMR96A ferrite for optimal loss-power density balance.
To address mechanical integration and thermal management challenges, a vertically
stacked module architecture is implemented. Primary PCBs are positioned on both sides
of a central secondary PCB, enabling vertical power flow, reduced conduction losses, and
improved layout compactness. All primary and secondary windings are placed on the
secondary board, significantly minimizing AC resistance and leakage inductance while
enhancing design flexibility.
A prototype converter was built with two vertically stacked 8 V to 1 V modules. The
converter successfully delivers a continuous output current of 200 A at 1 V, achieving a
full-load efficiency of 85.71 % and a peak efficiency of 89.12 %. The power density reaches
473 W/in3 under 1 V output conditions. While absolute optimality cannot be claimed,
the proposed design demonstrates excellent performance in both efficiency and power
density, and can be expected to rank among the most competitive single-stage isolated
converters for high-current 1 V applications.
Keywords: Point of load (PoL), single-stage, current doubler, vertical integration

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
High-performance microprocessors are the driving force behind today’s digital transfor-
mation, powering applications from cloud computing to real-time analytics. According to
Allied Market Research, the global market for high-performance computing (HPC) chips
is projected to reach USD 13.68 billion by 2027 [4].
In 2024, the global data center market was valued at USD 242.72 billion and is projected to
grow to USD 584.86 billion by 2032, exhibiting a compound annual growth rate of 11. 7%
during 2025-2032 [5]. In 2022, data centers worldwide consumed approximately 460 TWh
of electricity, a figure that could exceed 1000 TWh by 2026 [6]. Looking further ahead, AI-
driven workloads are expected to drive data center electricity demand to roughly 945 TWh
by 2030, more than double current levels [7]. In addition, industry surveys indicate that
energy costs account for up to 80% of a data center’s operating expenditure [8]. These de-
velopments highlight the critical need to optimize power conversion topologies, especially
point-of-load converters, to minimize energy losses and reduce operating expenditures in
next-generation data center infrastructures.
Modern xPUs thermal design powers (TDPs), summarized in Figure 1.1, span from
roughly 200 W for older CPUs such as Skylake, to up to 1000 W for the latest AI-optimized
GPUs (e.g. NVIDIA B200, H200, and H100) launched since 2023 [1]. These TDPs are
translated into instantaneous core currents on the order of 1000 A when stepped down
to sub-1 V rail voltages. To deliver such extreme currents with minimal voltage ripple,
motherboard designers employ voltage regulator modules (VRMs), also known as volt-
age regulators (VRs), implemented as point-of-load (PoL) converters located directly at
the load. These PoL converters must supply precise voltages to all xPUs, including both
CPUs and GPUs, and react within microseconds to sudden workload changes. Tradi-

1.2
Challenges and Current Solutions
Today data centers are shifting from legacy 12 V distribution networks to higher voltage
schemes - most commonly 48 V - that can reduce I2R losses in upstream cabling by more
than 50 % [10]. Stepping down from a 48 V distribution bus to sub-1 V while supply-
ing currents of at least 500 A imposes stringent requirements on three key performance
parameters:
• Transient Response: Data center workloads exhibit abrupt load steps. PoL regu-
lators must achieve sub-1 µs load-step regulation (e.g., <1% overshoot/undershoot)
to avoid xPU instability, which requires advanced control algorithms and very low-
impedance output networks [9, 11].
• Power Density: Delivering hundreds of amperes within the tight PCB area adjacent
to xPUs demands compact converter designs (e.g., >0.24 A/mm2) and drives the need

for innovative magnetic integration and optimized component layouts.
• Efficiency: Minimizing both conduction and switching losses in semiconductors
and magnetics is vital. Each percentage point of improvement in converter efficiency
translates into megawatts of power saved on scale.
These considerations highlight the critical need for rigorous design frameworks that yield
PoL converters with small footprint, high efficiency, and fast transient response under
high-current, low-voltage operating conditions.
To improve overall power delivery efficiency, many data centers are beginning to supply
servers directly from a high-voltage bus, such as 48 V. PoL converters capable of handling
kilowatts deliver extremely low output voltages (≤1 V) at currents of several hundred
amperes, as illustrated on the Figure 1.2, these PoL stages have been supplied from a 48 V
bus.
In modern power distribution systems, a two-stage topology is typically employed: the
first DC - DC converter steps 48 V to an intermediate bus voltage, and the PoL converter
then provides closed-loop regulation to the final output of up to 1 V, ensuring fast transient
response. The early implementations of the front end typically used a fixed ratio 48 V to
12 V intermediate bus , leveraging the well-established technology for 12 V to 1 V PoL
(Figure 1.2b). More recently, to further improve the utilization and efficiency of switches,
researchers have explored lower intermediate bus voltages, such as 8 V, 6 V, or 4 V between
the 48 V source and the PoL stage.
Traditional first stage DC-DC converter topologies include LLC and DCX switch capacitor
[12, 13, 14], among others. An LLC converter with a matrix transformer was proposed in
[12] to achieve large voltage conversion ratios; however, its complexity and the multitude
of magnetic components result in increased cost and reduced power density. Switched
capacitor converters have attracted attention by leveraging the higher energy density of
capacitors as primary storage elements instead of inductors [14, 15], despite their high
theoretical power density, suffer from significant capacitor value derating under DC bias,
high charge redistribution losses, and poor transient response at high current levels.
These drawbacks, combined with increased control complexity, reliability concerns, and
lower efficiency at large voltage-conversion ratios, make them less favorable compared
to mature inductive PoL topologies. These designs typically establish intermediate bus
voltages between 6 V and 8 V, achieving conversion ratios of up to 8: 1, while higher ratios
introduce challenges in switch utilization and control complexity.
Although two-stage architectures can be easily adapted from established designs 12 V,
their additional DCX stage and decoupling capacitors increase the board area and losses,
limiting both the power density and overall efficiency, and these unfavorable factors
should not be ignored in high-current PoL applications. In the long term, the two-stage
architecture serves merely as a temporary bridge to single-stage 48 V to 1 V converters,
although single-stage designs continue to face substantial technical challenges.

For the PoL converter, considering that the maximum current of a module is greater than

2.1. Multiphase Current Doubler and Primary Side Topology
The multiphase current doubler converter can simply be viewed as a multiphase inter-
leaved buck converter [25]. Therefore, each current doubler rectifier can be viewed as an
equivalent two-phase interleaving buck converter. Many studies on multiphase interleav-
ing buck converters [28][29] have shown the following:
• By integrating multiple synchronous buck converters and compensating for their
timing signals, these converters significantly minimize input and output current
ripples while improving transient response capabilities.
• The inherent phase distribution of multiphase buck converters uniformly disperses
heat, minimizes hotspots, lowers junction temperatures, and reduces conduction
losses, thereby enhancing overall system efficiency.
• In a multiphase buck setup, the inductor is divided into smaller units, allowing for
a more compact magnetics. This allows the power density of the converter to be
further increased.
The study in [30] shows that for multiphase buck converters, the efficiency increases as
the phase inductance increases. In contrast, the transient performance of the converter
improves when the phase inductance is reduced. This creates a design dilemma for the
phase inductors in multiphase interleaving buck converters, as there is a trade-off between
optimizing for efficiency and enhancing the transient response.

Having completed the basic current waveform analysis and rms value calculation, the
next thing to focus on is the winding optimization. In PoL applications, conduction losses
in the windings are the dominant loss mechanism. Since winding optimization is critical,
this work is carried out from the following aspects:
1. DC loss and AC loss: Copper thickness and eddy current effect.
2. Minimum transformer leakage inductance 𝐿lk: Interleaving structure.
3. Winding layout: Shaping, window filling factor.
4. Manufacturability: Ease of winding, repeatability, and cost.
The loss of the integrated magnetics winding is divided into two parts, DC loss and AC
loss, and the winding design can be optimized by comparing the DC resistance value,
𝑅DC, with the AC resistance value, 𝑅AC. At high switching frequencies, the eddy current
effect, namely the skin, proximity, and fringing effects, dramatically increases winding
losses and undermines the performance of magnetic components in power converters.
Therefore, 𝑅AC can be thought of as consisting of the following parts.
𝑅AC = 𝑅skin + 𝑅pro + 𝑅fring
(3.18)
Since the integrated magnetization of the CDR contains DC bias, fringing loss caused by
the air gap is unavoidable. Many studies have shown that fringing loss is much greater
than skin loss and proximity loss, so the fringing effect must be carefully considered when
designing magnetic components with air gap [35]. The fringing loss cannot be calculated
using the 1-D Dowell equation, and finite element analysis is widely used, with the Ansys
Maxwell eddy current mode chosen as the tool for winding loss estimation [36].
Some studies have shown that for inductors with a single air gap distribution, arranging
the winding away from the air gap can effectively limit the fringing effect [37]. In this

leakage inductance 𝐿lk of the primary and secondary windings in each phase. Parasitic
energy stored in 𝐿lk can discharge during switching, producing voltage overshoot and
high-frequency ringing, which in turn generate electromagnetic interference and incur
extra losses. To mitigate these effects, the windings are interleaved: The primary and
secondary turns are alternated layer by layer, significantly reducing leakage inductance
and improving the switching performance [35].
As demonstrated in [39], the fully interleaved windings can minimize the leakage induc-
tance in planar transformers at medium and high frequencies. However, for coupled
inductors, where the secondary windings carry a DC bias and the excitation inductance
also serves as the output inductor, fully interleaving forces at least one primary layer
and two insulation layers between adjacent secondary turns. This increased spacing re-
duces tight magnetic coupling, leads to imbalance current distribution, and increases
AC winding resistance 𝑅AC [40]. Consequently, full interleaving may not yield optimal
performance for integrated coupled inductors.
The issue of current distribution imbalance is itself highly complex, as it depends on
the thickness of the insulation layer, the manufacturing tolerances, and the geometry of
the winding. A comprehensive investigation would require extensive prototyping and
multi-physics simulation of inter-turn coupling, efforts beyond the scope of this work.
Separately, when multiple modules are connected in series (as in a 12-module ISOP
configuration), their individual leakage inductances stack, exacerbating switching-ringing
issues. Therefore, to prioritize leakage-inductance reduction within a manageable design
envelope, this project adopts a fully interleaved winding structure as shown below.
The final interleaved winding and MMF distribution are shown in Figure 3.12. The 12-
layer winding is arranged symmetrically, with secondary turns on the top and bottom
PCB layers. This layout shortens the length of the winding, reducing 𝑅DC, and makes the
converter more compact by placing the output capacitors and secondary MOSFETs close
to the windings.

4.1
DSP Control Implementation
Digital Signal Processors (DSPs) are favored for PWM control because they excel in pro-
cessing complex algorithms and high-speed data, essential for precise power electronics
applications. Their ability to handle multitasking and integrated features such as high-
resolution PWM outputs and analog inputs also streamline hardware design and enhance
system efficiency.
The Delfino F28379D controlCARD (TMDSCNCD28379D) , part of TI’s C2000 Real-Time
Control Series from Texas Instruments (TI) was chosen. Because it is particularly popular
due to its dual-core capability, extensive peripheral support, and high-resolution PWM
outputs. These features make it ideal for demanding applications in motor control and
renewable energy systems, supported by robust software tools for accelerated develop-
ment. This chip can provide a substantial number of PWM outputs, specifically up to 16
PWM channels. The PWM channels are generated through multiple independent timers,
allowing for flexible configuration of period and phase in this 4-phase full bridge CDR.
Regarding clock control, the PWM channels can be based on the same or different clock
sources. This means that they can be configured to operate synchronously using a sin-
gle clock source or independently with different clock sources, accommodating specific
application requirements.

4.3
Vertical Assembly and PCB layout
As xPUs power in data centers continues to increase, traditional two-stage or multi-phase
power boards routed only in the x-y plane are struggling to maintain small size and high
efficiency at high ratios and currents: dense power/ground pins and crossover alignments
lead to bulk, high-current loops, increased parasitics, and crosstalk issues. Using the z-
axis for the vertical power supply, the alignment can be simplified, the loops can be
shortened, and parasitic resistance and inductance can be significantly reduced in three
dimensions. In this paper, we propose a solution to verticalize critical power paths and
keep the auxiliary circuits horizontally wired on the same PCB, taking advantage of both
layouts to achieve high power density and high efficiency.

Experiment Results
Before experimental testing, test benches for validation in low-voltage, high-current sit-
uations are mandatory. In this setup, a controllable bidirectional DC supply (Itech IT-
M3910D-10-1020) acts as the load and demands a minimum input voltage to draw the
specified current. In practice, it was found that current limiting is automatically triggered
when the voltage of the electronic load is about 0.6 V to 0.7 V. Considering the unavoidable
resistance of the motherboard to the electronic load, measures must be taken to ensure
that the voltage at the end of the load is maintained at the minimum limit.
In general, there are two common solutions; one is to increase the input voltage, which will
result in a corresponding increase in the output voltage, thus keeping the voltage drop
on the cable/contact within an acceptable range. However, this will cause the operation
condition of the converter to deviate from the design. The second method is to reduce
contact/cable resistance by using thick conductors or bus bars and multiple low-resistance
connection points. In addition to this, a third solution is mentioned in [43], by using a
controlled DC electronic load, one can implement a purpose-built, programmable resistor
network.
For the validation of the two prototypes, prototype 1 chooses the first solution described
above because the maximum output current of 200 A results in a limited cable/contact
voltage drop. For prototype 2, involves thick cable and/or bus bars is unavoidable because
the total output current increases to 400 A.
It should be noted here that the voltage across the two primary switches of the same
bridge leg was found to be unbalanced in tests in which the primary was not conducting,
a phenomenon that can lead to unbalanced switching stresses on the primary side. This
may be due to unequal capacitance values 𝐶𝑜𝑠𝑠in the primary GaNFETs. To solve this
problem, the conduction periods of the two GanFETs on the same bridge leg on the primary
side are set to be complementary, which does not affect the duty cycle of the CONVERTER,
and only requires an adjustment of the control. Since the TI 28379 controlCARD is not

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
This paper presents an isolated full-bridge CDR module with flexible adaptation, high
current, and vertical stack. The current doubler circuit is chosen for its compactness, ease
of magnetic integration, and suitability for single stage 48 V to 1 V. To obtain high power
density and high efficiency converter, a new magnetically integrated coupled inductors
are designed.
Firstly, with reference to the multiphase buck converter coupling method, one current
doubler rectifier is analogized as a 180◦two-phase interleaved buck converter and solved
for the appropriate number of phases, and a four-phase current doubler achieves flux
cancelation by negative coupling. At the same time, the size of the core can be further
reduced because the presence of mutual inductance for magnetic integration makes the
single phase less demanding on self-inductance.
The second part gives the detailed design process of the 4 - phase coupled inductors. The
equivalent inductance formula after coupling is derived by building a magnetic reluctance
model, and the simulation proves that the results of the inductance formula derived from
this model are valid. Secondly, by comparing different geometries, the appropriate inner
leg structure and the height of the core leg are selected to minimize the fringing loss.
Finally, powder material is used for comparison and the DMR96A ferrite material is
selected by weighing the loss and the power density.
The third section describes the realization of vertically stacked modules. This vertical
structure allows the power to be transferred from module to module vertically, thus
reducing conduction losses and external space. The primary PCB is placed vertically
on the left and right sides of the secondary PCB to achieve a more compact design. It
60
==PAGE:72==
6.2. Future Work
also avoids the thermal management problems associated with too many power devices
on a single PCB. The solution also places all primary and secondary windings on the
secondary board, minimizing AC resistance and leakage inductance between the primary
and secondary windings while achieving flexibility.
The final step involved the construction of a prototype of the proposed design. A prototype
of the 8 V to 1 V converter was built, consisting of two vertically stacked modules, and it was
demonstrated that it delivers a continuous output current of 200 A. The converter achieves
an efficiency of 85.71 % at the output of 1 V. Although absolute optimality is difficult to
claim, the proposed solution delivers top-tier performance in both power density and
efficiency, placing it firmly among the most competitive isolated 1 V converters reported
to date.
6.2
Future Work
First, a detailed loss breakdown analysis will be carried out to identify the dominant
sources of power loss in the current prototype.
This will guide further optimization
efforts in both circuit design and control strategy.
Second, work on the four-module full-bridge CDR is currently in progress. Given the
previously mentioned issues with cable/strip resistance and the voltage drop observed
in the live masters, the use of thicker conductors is under consideration. As shown in
Figure 6.1, the DC resistance of the motherboard can be effectively reduced through CNC
processing, which allows for precision shaping of thick copper layers. This enhancement
is expected to be completed within one month.
Third, once prototype 2 tests are complete, we will summarize the results and compare
them to current state of the art PoL solutions. This comparison will highlight the advan-
tages of our design and identify areas for further improvement.
Finally, soft-switching techniques can be introduced to solve the ringing problem and im-
prove efficiency, and a common approach is to use a phase-shifted full-bridge topology[44].
Related work is scheduled to be done after the prototype 2 test mentioned above.
