Preface
This thesis was conducted at the department of Civil Engineering at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark in fulﬁlment of the requirement for acquiring an M.Sc. in Civil En-
gineering. The aim of the work was to study the strength and fracture of mortar under
complex stress states and the eﬀect on varying aggregate size in a mortar mixture. A wide
range of experimental work was executed and the method and main results obtained from

Abstract
Mortar is widely used in the building industry as a ﬁnish material on various parts of
buildings.
When using prefabricated concrete elements mortar becomes an important
structural material when assembling joints of the elements.
Especially in shear joints
of wall elements where the key shear joint is the weak link of the shear wall. In modern
buildings the demand on open spaces is increasing which leads to higher capacity demands
on the shear walls in the buildings. Because of the weakness in the shear joints between
concrete elements the solution in modern structure is to use site-cast concrete walls for
these shear walls.
Due to research on a new type of site-cast joints between prefabricated concrete elements
the knowledge on the strength and fracture of mortar is important when investigating and
modelling the capacity of these joints. The work in this thesis focuses on investigating the
strength and fracture of mortar under complex stress state assuming the plastic theory of
modiﬁed Coulomb material applies.
The variation of aggregate size in mortar mixtures are studied under triaxial stress state
where the internal friction angle is measured and the failure envelope is studied for the
diﬀerent aggregate sizes. The mechanical behaviour under complex stress state is further
analysed with a microscopic analysis where the behaviour between diﬀerent stress states
are studied. The friction angle of the diﬀerent test series is found mechanical behaviour
observed and crack pattern suggested.
For comparison the internal friction angle is also measured with failure patterns of pris-
matic bodies of the same mortar mixtures as tested in the triaxial experiment. The failure
pattern is measured with Digital Image Correlation. The measured friction angle from the
prisms was close to the friction angle from the triaxial test and supports those results.
A single key shear joint is tested in shear where the test and a model of the bearing capacity
for the joint is presented. Digital Image Correlation is performed in Aramis to observe the
failure mechanism in the key during testing and measuring the crack displacement of the
yield plain. The analytical model with the tested material parameter are compared to the
testing and the comparison is evaluated. The analytical model was found to be a good ﬁt
for the specimens of longer key lengths but overestimated the capacity of the specimens
of shorter key lengths. About half of the angles measured from the crack displacements
was as predicted and deviation is discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
This thesis focuses on mortar as a construction material. Mortar is a concrete material
containing cement, water and aggregate. In building and civil structures mortar is widely
used. The utilization of mortar has primarily been in facade ﬁnishes, concrete repairs,
grouting and local supports. For this scope of mortar utilization, the only needed material
parameter for the failure criteria of mortar has been the compressive strength.
In modern buildings the ever increasing area of open spaces increases the demand on shear
walls in the buildings, used for horizontal stability. When using prefabricated element
walls for such shear walls the key joint is a site-cast with mortar. Due to the narrow
joint, mortar is a ﬁtting material because of the small aggregate size. These key joints are
the weak link of the shear wall and due to this weakness the shear wall in prefabricated
element buildings are made by site-casting walls.
The failure criteria of concrete are well known, where the material properties of concrete
have been established by researches over the last century. The failure criteria for concrete
can be described by the means of plastic theory with Mohr-Coulomb criterion. The failure
criteria for mortar or the eﬀects of variation in aggregate size in concrete is however not
well known. In relation to a research on shear joint in pre-cast concrete elements the
knowledge of mortar is important when investigating and modelling these joints. In this
thesis the strength and fracture of mortar is studied under simple and complex stress state
assuming the plastic theory of modiﬁed Coulomb material.
1.2
Objective
In relation to site-cast joint between pre-cast concrete panels, the fracture and strength
of mortar is investigated. This is to increase the accuracy of the bearing capacity model
being developed for these kind of joints.
Furthermore, there is a complex stress state locally with in the joint of this type of a
connection and for that reason it is highly relevant to have knowledge of the behaviour for
mortar under triaxial stress condition. Under triaxial stress state the material parameter
of the internal friction angle can be established.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
In this chapter the basic assumptions and theory is described that forms the basis of the
thesis. The deﬁnition of the main material used in the thesis will be introduced. The
theory of plasticity regarding this project will be presented, where the basic parts of the
theory are based on the work of M.P. Nielsen and L.C. Hoang [11] will be elaborated. The
microscopic mechanical behaviour of concrete materials is introduced for the following
analysis in this thesis.
2.1
Concrete material
In general concrete is deﬁned as being consisting of the main materials cement, water and
aggregates of gravel and stones. Moreover, can the cement be replaced by micro-silica
and ﬂy-ash.
A number of chemicals can be added to alter the behavioural properties
but still being deﬁned as concrete. In this thesis mortar is to be analysed. Mortar is a
concrete material in the sense that it consists of cement, water and aggregate. In general,
the diﬀerence between concrete and mortar is deﬁned by the deﬁnition that mortar has
smaller aggregate sizes than concrete. This is a wide concept and therefore is the deﬁnition
of mortar material used in this thesis when the aggregate size is smaller than a concrete
mixture that will be used as a reference.
2.2
Plasticity
Plasticity is when material experiences a non-reversible change in shape as a response to an
applied force. The main material in this project is mortar which is considered to be a rigid-
plastic material. A rigid-plastic material is deﬁned as a material where no deformation
will occur before stresses reach a yield point and then arbitrarily large deformation is
possible without any change in the stresses. This behaviour does not exist in real material
but this assumption can be used when the plastic strains are much larger than the elastic
strains. The behaviour of a rigid-plastic material is illustrated in Figure 2.2.1 showing the
stress-stain relationship for such a rigid-plastic material.

2.4
Extremum principle
The load carrying capacity of rigid-plastic materials can be determined by the extremum
principle described in the following sections.
2.4.1
The Lower Bound Theorem
The load carrying capacity determined by the lower bond theorem has a stress ﬁeld within
the yield surface f ≤0, which is a safe and admissible stress distribution. The solution
will therefore be smaller or equal to the actual carrying capacity of the material. Several
stress ﬁelds can satisfy these conditions but the largest solution is closest to the carrying
capacity of the structure.
2.4.2
The Upper Bound Theorem
The load carrying capacity determined by the upper bound theorem is found by the work
that has to be performed in a geometrical possible displacement ﬁeld, corresponding to
strains that are possible according to the normality condition. Several collapse mechanisms
can satisfy the conditions but the smallest solution will be closest to the carrying capacity
of the structure. The solution will therefore be bigger or equal to the actual carrying
capacity of the material.
2.4.3
The Uniqueness Theorem
A uniquely determined load carrying capacity is when the load determined by the lower
bound theorem and upper mound theorem is equal. This load will therefore be the exact
collapse load of the structure.
2.5
Failure criteria
The mortar recipes in this project are treated as modiﬁed coulomb materials. The modiﬁed
Coulomb criterion is one of the most common failure criteria to describe concrete and
mortar materials.
2.5.1
Modiﬁed Coulomb material
The failure criteria of a modiﬁed coulomb material is a combination of two failure modes,
sliding failure and separation failure. Sliding failure occur when the shear stress in the
section |τ| exceeds the sliding resistance which is expressed as:
|τ| = c −µσ
(2.5.1)
where c is the cohesion of the material, µ is the friction coeﬃcient and σ the normal stress
which is positive as tensile stress. A compressive normal stress will therefore give positive

• heterogeneous structure

The zone between the aggregate grains and the bulk cement paste is called an interfacial
bond zone. This zone cannot be seen in an untested and untreated thin section at Meso

a complex stress state. The interfacial bond zone is considered the weakest link in normal
strength concrete. The density of this zone is higher then in the bulk cement paste. When

Aggregate interlock is a phenomenon that can be seen in macroscopic level and is introduce
here for further use in the thesis. This is a cracking behaviour of a sliding failure in concrete
materials. These cracks start in the bulked cement paste with an inclination, forming a
yield line. In a normal strength concrete the strength of most aggregates is higher than the
cement paste. When the yield line runs into an aggregate grain it will be forced around
the aggregate trough the weak interfacial bond zone. This behaviour changes the yield
line of the sliding failure in concrete.

3.3.4
Epoxy impregnation
In this section the epoxy impregnation is presented. The process is described here and im-
age documentation can be found in Appendix A.4. This was a developing process through
the project because the triaxial tested specimens were too compact for a normal epoxy
impregnation. The section is therefore divided into the 2 methods that were executed. An
epoxy impregnation of the plane section of the specimens and then thin section that were
produced.
Plane section
The normal procedure of epoxy impregnating concrete cylinders that has been tested and
failed is to impregnate from the outside of the cylinder, so that the epoxy goes by pressure
from the cylindrical surface into the cracks and voids that is in the failed specimen. This
was tried with the specimen that had failed in the triaxial test but did not give satisfying
results. When cutting the impregnated cylinders the epoxy had only reached few mm inside
from the cylinder surface. This conclusion resulted in a change in the impregnation process
by cutting the cylinder in half along the height of the cylinder before the impregnation.
The procedure of this part of the experiment is fully documented in Appendix A.4 and
short description is given here below.
The cylinders were cut in half along the height and then dried in an oven at 40◦C over
night. The cylinders were then packed in 2 layers of thick plastic bags with a diameter
about 10mm more than the diameter of the tested specimen. This is done to insure that
the epoxy ﬂows around the surface of the cylinder. The bags are kept open on the top so
the epoxy can enter the specimen.
The epoxy mixture is of 3 components, a resin called Conpox Harpix BY158 a ﬂuorescent
color powder and a hardener called HY 2996. The ﬂuorescent color is mixed with the resin
and then the hardener is mixed in with the coloured resin by a ratio 1:0.3 (resin:hardener).
20
==PAGE:45==

The cylinder packed in the bags were put in a vacuum chamber with a rubber hose from
the top of the chamber to insert the epoxy mixture. When full vacuum was reached the
epoxy mixture was slowly inserted by the rubber hose. When the epoxy mixture had
covered all of the cylinder the cylinder was kept in vacuum for a couple of minutes or until
it was evaluated that the epoxy had ﬁlled the gaps that it could reach.
When the impregnation was done the specimen was moved to a bucket of water to ensure
that the plastic bags didn’t melt due to the heat produced by the hardening of the epoxy
mixture. The impregnated specimen was left to harden in the water for 24 hours.
When the epoxy had hardened the cylinders were cut again in the same section as they
were cut before the impregnation. The cutting had to be precise to ensure that the section
was the same as for the cutting section before impregnation. That was to ensure the same
distance from a surface that was contacted of the epoxy mixture.
To increase the quality of the impregnated specimens the plan of the cut section was water
polished with an angle grinder by a set of 8 pads. The set of pads had a grit level ranging
from course to ﬁne by a grit number 50 to 3000 and then ending with a buﬀer pad.

It is clear in Figure 3.4.2 and 3.4.3, that the failure curve of the mortar mixtures C8, C4
and C2 are more curved than the concrete reference mixture C16. There is a tendency of a
smaller failure envelope with lower aggregate size, despite that test series C2 has a slightly
higher failure envelope than test series C4. A possible reason for a higher failure envelope
of test series C2 than test series C4 is that the aggregate in C2 was very dry and could
therefore result in a higher aggregate volume than in the other series. Another factor is
the compression strength, which is lower for test series C2. It has been shown that the
lower compression strength tends to raise the principal stress in the vertical direction in
relation to the principal stresses in the horizontal direction [3].
The failure envelopes of the mortar series (C8, C4, C2) is quite linear and similar for a
conﬁnement pressure up to 0.5fc. It has been shown by Kaare B. Dahl [10] that concrete
follows the Mohr-Coulomb failure criteria up to 0.5fc and then starts to curve below the
criteria. The falling of the failure envelope at 0.5fc is also seen in all of the mortar series
which is also clear in Figure 3.4.3.
It can therefore be seen that a reduction of aggregate size has an inﬂuence of the failure
envelope of the material. Another factor for this behaviour could be the aggregate/cement
ratio which also reduces with the reduction of aggregate size in these test series. The fact
that the C2 test series lies higher then test series C4 could be an indicator of that. This is

In this chapter a failure pattern of a prismatic body under a simple stress state is studied.
The prisms were tested under uniaxial compression test. The same prisms were used to

Experimental program

that not all yield planes will be captured on the observed side of the prism. The test series
are the same as for the triaxial tests because they were produced together. For each test
series 14 specimens where produced and tested which result in 56 tested specimens.

4.3.1
Digital Image Correlation
The Digital Image Correlation (DIC) is a way to detect the fracture of the prisms during
the compression test. This is a non-contact measuring technique that is able to determine
the deformations at the surface of the specimen by cross correlating the images taken along
the test. During the testing several pictures are taken to track the whole experiment. The
image series is then run through a software used for the analysis, called ARAMIS from
gom. The software divides the image up into facets by the diﬀerence that can be detected
between the pixels. The facets are tracked and cross correlated through the image series.
To ensure that the ARAMIS software can detect the diﬀerence between the pixels the
surface has to be painted with a stochastic speckle pattern. A good pattern provides gray
scale contrast between every pixel in the image. This is achieved by spray painting the
surface area that is to be analysed with a white background and when the background has
dried, the surface is covered with random ﬁne black dots of spray paint.
The DIC analysis was done by taking pictures of the specimens during the compression
test. The images where taken on a Nikon D3300 with a 60 mm lens. The images had
the resolution of 5056x4032 and the light sensitivity was set to ISO 100. The camera was
mounted on a tripod in front of the specimen, so that the lens was parallel to the surface
that was to be analysed. Additional lighting was used to avoid shadows and to increase
the quality of the images, as can be seen in Figure 4.3.1 of the setup. Each test data was
calibrated by taking the ﬁrst image with measurement stick at the face of the surface.
The camera was connected to a computer and the images taken with a program called
NKRemote, which made it possible to take a time laps of the test with a time laps rate
of 15 images per minute. Only 1 side of the prism was analysed. Because only 1 side was
analysed not every test-analysis captured the fracture of the prisms.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis was to study strength and fracture of mortar under complex
stress states.
That has been done by investigated mortar in 4 diﬀerent experiments.
Designed mortar mixtures with varying aggregate sizes of 3 types and 1 commercial type
was examined. All the 4 mortar mixtures were tested in triaxial cell where the failure
envelope was investigated and the internal friction angle determined.
These 4 mortar
types were also tested in uniaxial compression where the failure pattern was observed and
measured by Digital Image Correlation in Aramis. The friction angles were measured from
the observed failure patterns to compare the measured friction angles from the triaxial
tests.
One of the mortar test series was analysed at a microscopic level to study the
mechanical behaviour of mortar subjected to triaxial stress. The fracture of the commercial
mortar was studied in a single key shear joint. An analytical model for 2 failure modes was
presented using the friction angle found in the triaxial test and compared to the push-oﬀ
test of the single key shear joint. One face of the single key joint was observed by Digital
Image Correlation and analysed in Aramis to measure the displacement and observe the
failure pattern in the mortar key.
The results from the triaxial tests of the mortar test series with varying aggregate size
revealed a diﬀerence in the failure envelope between the test series. Reduction in the failure
envelope was seen with the test series of smaller aggregate sizes. Despite the diﬀerence in
the failure envelope between the test series, it was found common for all the test series that
in the higher conﬁnement region the slope of the principal stress curve reduced towards
one (1). The friction angle was measured for the diﬀerent test series. Furthermore, it
was found that the aggregate size aﬀected the friction angle in a way that reduction in
aggregate size reduces the friction angle.
One test series was impregnated with ﬂuorescent coloured epoxy. Even though the spec-
imen had failed in the triaxial test, the specimen was to compact to reveal any crack
pattern or failure characteristics. Instead some thin section samples from the test series
was produced and with microscopic analysis the mechanical behaviour from a triaxially
tested mortar was analysed. The diﬀerence in mechanical behaviour between conﬁnement
regions was observed and a connection between the reduction in the slope of the principal
stress curve was made. With the information from the local crack pattern in the thin
section samples a suggestion of the crack pattern on a triaxially loaded mortar was drawn.
79
==PAGE:104==
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
The failure pattern on the prisms was measured and the same eﬀect from aggregate size
on the friction angle was found. The friction angle measured from the failure pattern was
found to be quite close to the one found in the triaxial tests.
The load bearing capacity of the mortar key in the push-oﬀtest was found to be close
to the analytical model. The results were more in line with the analytical model for the
test series with larger key lengths than for the test series with smaller key lengths. The
crack displacement of the specimens was measured with Digital Image Correlation and
the angle of displacement was compared to the predicted angle from the analytical model.
Some measurements were close to the predicted angle and some not. The possible reasons
for this diﬀerence in the predicted and measured angles were discussed and some argued
to be because a ﬂaw in the test setup and others to a diﬀerence in failure mode that was
predicted.
7.0.5
Future work
Although a value for the friction angle of the mortar mixtures was found the data points
used to measure the values were few. More data points at the low conﬁnement region
could be investigated to establish a more accurate value for the friction angle of mortar.
In this thesis the aggregate/cement ration was reduced with smaller aggregate sizes. The
diﬀerence of the eﬀects from aggregate sizes and respectively aggregate/cement ratio could
be investigated to ﬁnd the eﬀect that these 2 variables have on the failure envelope and
the friction angle of mortar.
The macroscopic analysis of the triaxially loaded mortar showed diﬀerent mechanical be-
haviour in diﬀerent conﬁnement regions. Further analysis could be done, for more con-
ﬁnement regions and specimens of diﬀerent aggregate sizes to gain further understanding
of the mechanical behaviour of mortar and the eﬀect of aggregate size.
