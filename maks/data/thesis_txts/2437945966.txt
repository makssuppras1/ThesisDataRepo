Abstract
In recent years structures of increasing complexity have become more predominant, due
to the higher availability of numerical analysis tools. In structural analysis the develop-
ment of the Finite Element Method in combination with the emergence of the personal
computer stands as a starting point for a new age, where constructing any complex shape
became possible. In this thesis one of the major extensions to this method, the Finite Ele-
ment Limit Analysis, will be investigated. This extension enables analysis of the ultimate
limit load for structures. The analysis is performed by solving a mathematical optimiza-
tion problem typically in the form of a second-order cone or semideﬁnite program.
The purpose of this thesis is to determine the ultimate limit load of reinforced con-
crete and steel structures subjected to triaxial stress states. The two differs by their yield
criteria; reinforced concrete failure is speciﬁed by the Mohr-Coulomb criterion and steel
failure by the von Mises criterion. Two elements with a mixed formulation are proposed;
a triangle in 2D and a tetrahedron in 3D. They are both formulated by the same principle
of constant stress assumed over the local element domain.
Through analysis of several theoretical and experimental examples, the elements have
been proven capable of modelling structures subjected to triaxial stress states.
Two
obstacles occurred in the 3D formulation, ﬁrst regarding the von Mises yield criteria,
and second in the speciﬁcation of the concrete tensile strength. The ﬁrst was overcome
by capping the von Mises yield criterion and the second can be overcome through a pre-
liminary tensile strength analysis.
Overall the proposed elements showed promising behaviour in modelling complex
geometries with both discrete and smeared reinforcement, due to the simple element for-
mulation, where higher order elements would fail, due to requirements for computational
power.

Resumé
Komplekse konstruktioner har set en stor fremgang i moderne byggeri, grundet den øgede
brug af numeriske analyse værktøjer. I konstruktionsanalyse har elementmetoden i kom-
bination med computeren startet en ny tidsalder, hvor ingen konstruktion er for kompleks.
I denne afhandling vil numerisk brudstadieanalyse benyttes, der er en af de nyeste udvi-
delser til den allerede veletablerede elementmetode. Udvidelsen muliggør en mere præcis
analyse af konstruktioner i brudgrænsetilstanden. Analysen udføres ved at løse et mate-
matisk optimeringsproblem, der typisk tager form som et andenordens kegleprogram eller
et semideﬁnit program.
Formålet med denne afhandling er at bestemme den ultimative brudgrænse for arme-
ret beton- og stålkonstruktioner udsat for en triaksial spændingstilstand. De to materiale
har forskellige brudkriterier; brud i armeret beton beskrives ved Mohr-Coulomb kriteri-
et, hvorimod brud i stål beskrives ved von Mises kriteriet. To elementer bliver opstillet
med en såkaldt "mixed"formulering, et trekantet element i 2D og et tetraeder i 3D. Begge
elementer formuleres ved brug af samme fremgangsmetode, hvor en konstant spændings-
tilstand antages over elementets lokale domæne.
Gennem analyse af ﬂere teoretiske og eksperimentelle eksempler, har elementerne
vist deres evne til at modellere konstruktioner udsat for triaksiale spændingstilstande. To
problemer opstod ved den benyttede 3D formulering; mere speciﬁkt i benyttelsen af von
Mises kriteriet og valget af betons trækstyrke. Det første problem blev løst ved at indføre
en begrænsning på de hydrostatiske spændinger i von Mises kriteriet og det andet kan
løses ved at udføre forudgående analyse af betons trækstyrke før modellering.
Generelt udviser de opstillede elementer god evne til at modellere komplekse geome-
trier med både udsmurt og diskret armering, grundet den simple element formulering. I
disse tilfælde ville højere ordens elementer fejle grundet store krav til computerkraft.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In modern construction the two most widely used structural materials are concrete and
steel, due to availability and beneﬁcial material properties.
Concrete is a compound material of sand, gravel, cement and water. Cement is a
mixture of different materials, which hydrate when mixed with water, thus hardening
and binding the aggregates together. Cement-like materials consisting of quicklime and
pozzolana have been used since the Egyptians (approx. 3000 B.C.) and Romans (from
300 B.C.). Until the 19th century several developments in the form of lightweight aggreg-
ates and embedded reinforcement have been done, expanding the knowledge in concrete
mix designs. In 1824, Joseph Aspdin obtained a patent for Portland cement, which is a
basic mixture of clay and limestone that have been crushed and ﬁred in a kiln and is a
common ingredient in modern concrete. Several supplementary materials have since then
been invented, which can be added mixture to give the concrete the wished properties
(Shaeffer, 1992).
Steel is an alloy of iron with a low carbon content. Iron has been used since the Iron
Age, where it replaced bronze as the primary material for weapons and tools. From the
17th century and onward, an increasing urbanization of Europe required a more effective
metal, due to the bad material behaviour of iron as a construction material. Steel was a
known invention, however only used in minor quantities, due to production difﬁculties. In
1856, Henry Bessemer developed a new method for introducing oxygen into molten iron
to reduce the carbon content, today known as the Bessemer Process. This development
was a large contributor to the beginning of the Industrialization Age, enabling steel to
be used as one of the primary construction materials. Developments in the production
methods has since then made it possible to create e.g. lightweight and high strength steel
(Gyldendal, 2016).
In modern construction the trend is highly inﬂuenced by architects, whose designs
increase in complexity. Both steel and concrete are highly applicable for creating these
structures, however verifying the capacity of the structures becomes more difﬁcult with
the increasing complexity of the designs. While the numerical simulation tools have
developed signiﬁcantly over the last decades, especially through the development of ﬁnite

1.1
Material Models
Several theoretical models can be utilized to describe material behaviour.
The most
widely used is the linear-elastic material model, where a linear relation between stresses
and strains is assumed. The linear-elastic model works efﬁciently in the elastic regime,
where most materials show linear behaviour. The linear-elastic material model, however,
fall short, when trying to describe the relationship between stresses and strains outside
the elastic region, where the typical behaviour is highly non-linear. The non-linearity
between stresses and strains in materials can be approximated by different approaches,
typically based on iterative procedures or by establishing plastic material models. The
focus will remain on the plastic material models in this report.
General plastic theory deals with materials capable of plastic deformations, while
subjected to a sufﬁciently large constant load. A plastic deformation is deﬁned as an
irreversible deformation meaning that the deformation will remain permanent, even after
the element is unloaded. A special case of the plastic material models are the rigid-plastic
material model, where the initial elastic deformation is excluded, thereby assuming zero
deformation before the plastic limit load is reached (Nielsen & Hoang, 2011).
1.1.1
Rigid Plasticity
The rigid-plastic material model describes a material, which experience zero deformation
at stress levels lower than the materials plastic yield stress, but will experience arbitrarily
large deformations at the yield point. This is a simpliﬁcation, but the approximation
works for materials such as mild steel. Mild steel shows nearly linear-elastic behaviour at
low stress levels and exhibit large deformations, with only small changes in stress level
due to strain hardening, when exposed to stresses near the yield capacity. This enables

1.2
Yield Criteria
In civil engineering the Mohr-Coulomb yield criterion generally performs well in de-
scribing brittle material behaviour and the von Mises yield criterion in describing ductile
material behaviour. The yield criteria are widely used as steel acts ductile under stresses
close to the yield stress, whereas concrete shows no ductile behaviour at these stress levels
and instead experience brittle crack formation.
1.2.1
The Mohr-Coulomb Criterion
The Mohr-Coulomb yield criterion contains a condition for sliding failure, which occurs
when the shear stress |τ| exceeds the sliding resistance. The sliding resistance is determ-
ined as a combination of the cohesion c and internal friction of the material, whereas the
internal friction normally is described by a certain fraction µ = tan(ϕ) of the normal
stress. In which ϕ deﬁnes the internal angle of friction. This can be expressed in the
following form:
|τ| ≤c −µσ
(1.2)
A material complying with this failure criterion is normally denoted a Coulomb material.
For certain materials, a tension cut-off is included in the Mohr-Coulomb criterion limiting
the separation resistance fA, which is expressed as:
σ ≤fA
(1.3)
A material complying with both failure criteria is denoted a modiﬁed Coulomb material.
It is clear, that three material parameters c, µ and fA should be known to utilize the Mohr-
Coulomb criterion (Nielsen & Hoang, 2011). The criterion is visualized in Figure 1.2
together with Mohr’s circles of principal stresses.

1.4.3
Uniqueness Theorem
The uniqueness theorem states that only one limit load can be determined, if the two
following criteria is fulﬁlled:
• The stress distribution is statically admissible meaning stresses within or on the
yield surface.
• The corresponding strains can be derived from a geometrically possible displace-
ment ﬁeld.
The two criteria together means that both the lower and upper bound theorem are valid,
which result in a unique limit load.
1.5
Analytical Limit Analysis
Throughout the 20th century several analytical methods have been developed based on
the extremum principles, which can be utilized to determine the limit load of rigid-plastic
structures. The most mentionable being the Strut-and-tie modelling method (Schlaich,
Schafer & Jennewein, 1987), the yield-line method (Ingerslev, 1923), the stringer method
(Kaern, 1979) and the method of homogeneous stress zones (Nielsen & Hoang, 2011). It
is worth mentioning that all of these methods were created for analysing two dimensional
structures as no simple manual limit state methods exist for three dimensional structures.
Some of the methods can be expanded into three dimensional structures, but quickly
becomes too complicated. In the following subsections the strut-and-tie method, stringer
method and method of homogeneous stress zones will be brieﬂy presented, which are all
method based on the lower bound theorem.
1.5.1

In the strut-and-tie method, the load is carried by struts in compression and ties in tension.
The struts and ties are normally located corresponding to ﬁelds with pure concrete or
reinforcement as in Figure 1.5, where the struts are grey and the ties are black.
It is assumed that the stresses, at the location of the load P and reactions RA and
RB are equal to the compressive strength of the concrete in a fully utilized disk. The
necessary size of the struts and ties can be determined, if the thickness of the disk is
known, by stress equilibrium at the load and support points. The collapse load can be
optimized by using more complex strut-and-tie layouts, which makes the solution very
sensitive to the chosen layout.

Finite Element Limit Analysis
In this chapter the fundamental principles behind Finite Element Limit Analysis (FELA)
will be presented, which is the state of the art method for solving limit analysis prob-
lems numerically. Initially the traditional stiffness based ﬁnite element method will be
presented, and compared to the equilibrium based ﬁnite element limit analysis suitable
for optimization. Afterwards mathematical optimization in the context of limit analysis

2.1
Numerical Methods
As discussed in the previous chapter, several analytical methods have been developed
and utilised to determine the lower and/or upper bound values for the limit load. For
simple structures all of the presented methods will often result in a decent approximation
of the correct limit load. However, all of the methods becomes inadequate, when trying to
analyse more complex structures with arbitrary shapes. This is due to general limitations
in how the methods are applied or that the problem becomes so large, that they can not be
solved efﬁciently. Thereby the necessity for numerical methods emerged.
The most preferred numerical method in the ﬁeld of civil engineering is the Finite
Element Method (FEM), which originates back to the early 1940s, where it developed
simultaneously with the computer. The method is based on establishing a global stiffness
matrix for the modelled structure through element assembly, which is also one of the
most valuable assets of FEM as this enables the method to model and analyse arbitrarily
shaped structures. To this date several modiﬁcation and extensions to the ﬁnite element
method have been introduced under different names for different purposes, however the

2.1.1
Finite Element Method
Generally the FEM is a numerical method for solving differential equations, whose ana-
lytical solution would require the solution to a boundary value problem for a partial differ-
ential equation, which could be difﬁcult or impossible to determine analytically. The ﬁnite
element method works by separating the domain of the model into sub-domains called
ﬁnite elements. Each ﬁnite element will have associated linear equations, which can
be assembled into an equation system describing the entire domain, whereby the global
solution to the full system can be approximated. An increased amount of sub-domains
increase the precision of the global solution, however increasing the sub-domains also
increase the computational requirements, which pose a balancing problem for an optimal
discretization.
The ﬁnite element method for problems in mechanics utilize the assumption of linear
elasticity, and can be written on the form:
Ku = r
(2.1)
Here K is the so called stiffness matrix based on geometrical and material properties
of the individual elements. This matrix together with the deformations in the vector u
and the load vector r describes a system of linear equations. The stiffness matrix K is
assembled from local stiffness matrices deﬁned for each element. Each linear equation
in the system correspond to the load-displacement in a degree of freedom (dof). The
element can share dofs in shared nodes, which is how the elements interact with each
other. Usually the load vector r will be known, and the deformations in vector u will be
the ones solved for.
2.1.2

given load, a plastic limit load is to be determined, which corresponds to a plastic lower
or upper bound solution dependent on the utilized elements. This changes the problem

equilibrium equation.

2.5.1
Problem Discretization
As with any implementation using ﬁnite elements, the problem domain Ωis discretized,
into sub-domains or elements. The discretization can be done with elements of different
shapes, a typical choice in a plane 2D problem is to use triangular elements, and in 3D to
use tetrahedrons, as these elements can model any complex geometry.
The mesh consisting of nodes and elements describe the geometry of the problem,
however, to perfectly describe the geometry is not enough. The discretization also deter-
mines in which points the optimization variables are found. So not only do the geometry
have to be deﬁned well, but equally if not more important, is that the discretization is ﬁne
enough, that the underlying distribution of the optimization variables is well described.
Another part of the problem that should be considered when creating a mesh is loads
and supports. Since the mixed formulation of FELA ensures equilibrium in the nodes,

2.5.3
The Optimization Problem
The equilibrium matrix establishes how the problem is connected, and how forces can be
distributed in the domain, by ensuring equilibrium between elements. The forces which
needs to be in equilibrium are calculated from the stress states in the elements. What
stress states are permissible is determined using yield criteria utilizing SOCP and/or SDP.
Earlier in this chapter the von Mises and Mohr-Coulomb yield criteria has been presen-
ted for a single material point, in both plane stress and triaxial stress. Each element in
the discrete domain has one or more material points, from which the stress state in the
element is determined. Each of these material points are required to either be inside or
on the boundary of the yield envelope, otherwise the constraints are not satisﬁed. In the
global constraints matrix the yield criteria for each material point and element are com-
bined into one problem. The assembly can be done arbitrarily, however, it is convenient
to have the stress variables together, since these interacts with the equilibrium matrix. As
the different parts of the optimization problem has been introduced in the previous sec-
tions, the problem can now be established in standard form, which makes it possible to

3.3
Final Remarks on 2D Modelling
The formulated constant mixed 2D element has proven capable of modelling abstract
structures consisting of both reinforced concrete and steel. The simple element formula-
tion makes is possible to analysed large structures with a ﬁne mesh, due to the relatively
small element equilibrium matrix and a single stress state in each element. In all the
presented examples the solutions converge from above, meaning that the mixed formula-
tion takes the form of an upper bound solution. This is not ideal from a design perspective,
since safe solutions are sought, however, the found solution should be closer than a true
upper bound formulation. Another limitation of the formulation is the observed checker
board pattern of the stress states, this solution is possible because the mixed formulation
ensures equilibrium in the corner nodes, which is shared by multiple elements. However,
this does not seem to give any problems regarding the convergence of solutions.

To remedy this problem the von Mises yield criterion is modiﬁed by a cut off, as it
is of large interest to be able to model pure steel stresses in 3D realistically. This modi-
ﬁcation limit hydrostatic stress states. The possibility of huge hydrostatic stress states is
a limitation of the von Mises yield criterion, as a case with extremely large hydrostatic
stress conditions would lead to failure in a real situation even though the yield criterion
states otherwise. The cut off is implemented by limiting the allowable values of the stress
optimization variables [σxx, σyy, σzz, σxy, σxz, σyz]. Previously these have only been
limited by the conic von Mises constraints, which has no inﬂuence on hydrostatic stress
states. By introducing additional linear constraints to the stress variables, the yield cri-
terion is cut off. For each of the stress components, a linear constraint on the following
form is implemented:
−χfY ≤σ ≤χfY
(4.12)
This produces yield surfaces as shown in Figure 4.10 corresponding to the von Mises
triaxial stress cylinder with cube like cut off. Dependent on the choice of the factor χ the

4.3
Final Remarks on 3D Modelling
The formulated constant mixed 3D element has proven capable of modelling structures
consisting of both reinforced concrete and steel. However some obstacles were present,
the formulation with constant stress elements lead to a checker-board effect, where the
stresses in adjacent elements counterbalance leading to huge ampliﬁcation of stresses.
This effect was signiﬁcant in the elements governed by the von Mises yield criterion,
where no limits are given for both positive and negative hydrostatic stress states. The
obstacle was overcome by implementing a stress factor limiting the hydrostatic stress
states. This made it possible to use the element with the von Mises yield criterion, how-
ever it is not optimal.
Another obstacle were the determination of the concrete tensile strength, which highly

inﬂuence the calculated capacities. This inﬂuence was clearly visible in the pull-out ex-
ample, where the capacity converged towards zero, when the tensile strength was lowered.
A calibration of the ft value has been conducted in the last example, where an in-situ cast
loop connection is analysed. This procedure is recommended in all cases, where concrete
structures subjected to complex stress states are analysed as there otherwise could be a
risk of ampliﬁed capacities.
As were the case for the 2D element, it is possible to analyse rather large meshes,
due to a small element equilibrium matrix. The element yield upper bound solutions like
the 2D model, which is due to the formulation of the equilibrium matrix. The elements
has been proven effective in modelling 3D structures of concrete and steel subjected to
triaxial stress states, where the clear advantage is the possibility of modelling ﬁne mesh
densities, which could be useful for complex concrete or steel connections.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
It can be concluded that a framework capable of determining the ultimate limit load of re-
inforced concrete and steel structures in both 2D and 3D has been successfully developed.
Both formulations consist of elements with constant stress states. In 2D the element is
triangular and in 3D it is tetrahedral.
Equilibrium between elements are ensured with an equilibrium matrix based on the
mixed formulation. The mixed formulation ensures equilibrium in corner nodes of the
elements, and thus yields neither true lower or upper bound solutions, but converge more
rapidly. From convergence tests, it is concluded that the element yields solutions, which
converge from above.
The formulation is capable of modelling the reinforcement in structures as either
smeared or discrete. Smeared meaning the reinforcement is incorporated into the yield
function of the concrete simulating a composite material and discrete meaning the rein-
forcement is modelled as pure steel elements with their own yield function.
For modelling concrete the Modiﬁed Mohr-Coulomb yield criterion with a tensile
cut-off is implemented, and for steel the von Mises yield criterion is utilized. Both for-
mulations are implemented for full triaxial stress states and for plane stress states. For
plane Modiﬁed Mohr-Coulomb and for both von Mises’ criteria, the yield surfaces are
implemented using second-order cone programming, whereas semideﬁnite programming
is used for the triaxial Modiﬁed Mohr-Coulomb.
The developed frameworks have been used in examples involving both pure concrete,
steel and reinforced concrete structures. The 3D framework was used to model four differ-
ent examples; a concrete cylinder in compression, a HE200B simply supported beam, pull
out of a reinforcement bar from a concrete cube and an in-situ cast loop connection sub-
jected to tension. The pure steel example of the HE200B simply supported beam showed
a problem, where the elements were capable of ensuring equilibrium by a checker-board
pattern of adjacent elements in hydrostatic stress states of tension and compression. This
problem was remedied by modifying the von Mises yield criterion by capping the yield
surface. The examples involving concrete showed that the choice of the concrete tensile
stress ft is very important for the capacity. Great care should be employed when choosing

5.1
Further Work
The developed framework shows great promise in modelling reinforced concrete struc-
tures with discrete or smeared reinforcement for large meshes. The framework makes it
possible to extrapolate from known experimental data. The in-situ cast loop connection in
tension example in this thesis could be further analysed, either by testing the other para-
meters from the study by Jørgensen and Hoang (2013) or by expanding the study with
new tests.
To improve the utility of the element several addition to the implementation could be
considered, some of which are summarised in the following:
• A framework capable of modelling with different elements could be beneﬁcial as
critical zones could be modelled with optimal element types or higher order ele-
ments.
• Modiﬁed constant stress element, which secures equilibrium normal to sides could
be established to mitigate the aforementioned checker-board effect, while still main-
taining a state of constant stress in the elements.
• Creation of transformation shell elements, correctly modelling the interaction between
concrete and steel in a discrete model.
• Implementation of multiple load cases.
• Implementation of subsequent material optimization for low utility elements.
