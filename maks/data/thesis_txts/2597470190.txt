Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to experimentally test the T-pylon transmission mast made
of ﬁbreglass to predict the quasi static and transient respond. The experimental results
are compared to FEM models, and the FEM models are updated to increase the accuracy.
The forces that are tested for on the T-pylon beam are from a numerical model that
predict the forces exerted from a single conductor wire model under the condition of the
galloping phenomena. The method used is to conduct structural experimental testing of a
full-scale horizontal T-pylon beam by mounting nine actuators on the beam to represent
the forces from the conductor wires. The data collected and compared with the FEM
models are strain gauges, DIC measurements, displacements and forces exerted by the
actuators itself. The most important ﬁndings of this thesis is that the transients eﬀects
has very little inﬂuence on the structural response on the T-pylon beam.
This means
that the experiments are easier to conduct since the time span of the experiment can be
increased and the forces decrease with representative analysis of the galloping forces. The
modiﬁed FEM model of this project predict the structural response very well. The main
limitation is that the experiments does not reﬂect the real galloping phenomena since there
might be a cross linking of forces throughout the beam, which it not represented in a single
conductor model.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The architect company Bystrup Architects have designed a new ﬁbreglass T-pylon
transmission mast for 400 kV transmission wire to replace the common steel truss pylon
tower. Because the T-pylon is made of ﬁbreglass, it works as an insulator which means
that the conductor wires are mounted diﬀerently compared to the steel truss tower with
ceramic insulators. This means that the forces from the conductors acting on the arm also
are diﬀerent. Figure 1.1 shows the forces from the conductor wires.
Figure 1.1: Forces in the horizontal, vertical and cable direction shown, from [11]
The T-pylon designed by Bystrup Architects can be seen in ﬁgure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: T-pylon transmission mast by Bystrup Architects
Previous projects from DTU have examined which loads act on the arm of the T-pylon
by creating a numerical FEM model. However, the model is simpliﬁed to only simulate a
single conductor wire. The three DTU projects which create the background knowledge
for this thesis are:
2
==PAGE:9==
3
• A thesis about the galloping single conductor model in ABAQUS by Nicolai J. Krog.
• A thesis of dampings in the galloping phenomena the ABAQUS model by Daniel
Johansen.
This thesis describe the dynamics of galloping and gives the displacements of the
conductor wires. The force ﬁles are used to analyse the time dependant forces acting
on the T-pylon beam in an experimental manner.
• A thesis about creating an ANSYS FEM model by Mads Bjerrum.
This model is modiﬁed so it matches the experimental setup. The model is essential
in creating the input ﬁles to the T-pylon actuators and to compare them to how well
it predicts the response of the T-pylon.
A specially important load is the galloping wire, which is a dynamic instability character-
ized by large amplitude and low frequency oscillations [6].
The purpose of this thesis is to perform experimental structural testing of the T-Pylon
beam of the quasi static and time dependent forces from the galloping phenomenon by
answering the main research question:
• How can the T-pylon beam be experimentally tested to predict the quasi static and
transient response with increased accuracy through FEM models?
By doing this the thesis will look at:
- Algebraic veriﬁcation of the FEM model
- Quasi static experiment and analysis
- Transient and time dependent experiment and analysis

deﬁnes that the rotations has to fulﬁll w2 << 1 and the cross section remains perpendic-
ular to the neutral axis of the beam. The elastic beam equation is:

To solve the elastic beam equation, four initial conditions need to be deﬁned since it is a
4. order diﬀerential equation. These equations require I to be constant of can be written
as a function of the axis along the beam.
In this thesis, the I is varying along the beam, with a discrete transition, which makes it

why the FEM models are created. The FEM model makes calculations more ﬂexible due

2.2
Basic FEM theory
The following section is knowledge from the book Finite Element Modeling of Stress
Analysis [5]. The ﬁnite element method aims to calculate a ﬁeld quantity such as a dis-
placement ﬁeld or a stress ﬁeld ect. It is a numerical method to solve a speciﬁc problem
and in this case a structural problem. Roughly speaking the methods of the FEM is to
cut a structure into elements connected by nodes. Each elements’ behaviour to loads can
be described mathematically. Usually, the system is described with a stiﬀness matrix K,
a known force vector R and a vector D which contain the unknown ﬁeld quantity for each
node such as the displacement. Therefore, the system can be described very simple with:
KD = R
(2.3)
5

6
It leads to several algebraic equations which can be solved, and it describes how the sys-
tem behaves. For more complex systems where transient eﬀects are considered, a mass
matrix and damping matrix can be established and nonlinear systems can be solved with
for example Newton-Raphson iterations.
The beneﬁt of the FEM model is that forces and geometry easily can be changed and
results can still be obtained. Since it solves many algebraic equations and sometimes non-
linear equation, computational time is the cost of a FEM model.
In this thesis, the FEM models are used to calculate the quasi static & transient response,
the time dependent force and the mode shapes and natural frequencies of the T-Pylon
beam.

CHAPTER3
Method
3.1
The fundamental experimental setup
The experimental setup consists of a 11.22 meters long ﬁbreglass beam which is ﬁxed
perpendicular to a wall. The beam has a hollow round cross section with a diameter that
decrease along the x-axis. On the beam, 9 actuators are attached which represent the force

To ensure that the actuators follow a desired command either in force or displacement
the PID controllers needs to be calibrated and tuned. When the actuators are dismounted,
they are calibrated in force and displacement with a oﬀset function in MTS. The tuning
of the actuators are with a sinuous command which is sent to the actuators. It has a
frequency of 2 HZ and in displacement control. The PID is adjusted until the actuators
follow the command with an acceptable accuracy. Usually, the gain P is 10 times larger
than the integration part I.
For safety reasons there are limits on the forces that the actuators can exert, so they do
not damage the structure. These limits are usually around 5 kN for the start up, but while
testing, the actuator exerts larger forces and the limits are increased.
The actuators can be in a force control or displacement control. It is best to be in dis-
placement control to avoid any coupling in forces between the actuators and there is a risk
that the system gets unstable.

CHAPTER5
Discussion
5.1
Comparison of algebraic solution and simple FEM calcu-
lation
The second area of moment varies with discrete transitions along the beam in the x-
direction E.C.S in the FEM model and it is diﬃcult to make a simple algebraic model. To
make sure that the algebraic solution is correct to compare it with the FEM model, two
solutions were made.
One solution was to estimate an average Iy,avg. It turned out that this approach was very
sensible to how the Iy,avg was calculated.
After all it is just an estimate of the beam
deﬂection to verify and therefore the result is concluded acceptable. The other method is
better since it is taking the variation of Iy along the beam into account. This result agrees
more concisely with the FEM model by a few percentages.
5.2
Comparison of QS and FEM
5.2.1
Comparison of strain and strain gauges
The strain obtained in the FEM model along the x-direction E.C.S is not a smooth
curve, but is very much dependant of the discrete transitions of Iy, and therefore in which
node the strain is collected from.
The comparison of strain gauges vary a lot. In general, the strain is approximately the
same until it reaches the end of the beam where the load is acting. Also, the strain gauges
must be checked for whether or not they work properly, but in this experiment they seem
to be mounted correctly and working as intended. It is worth noticing that the strain
gauges have an initial value and therefore only show the relative strain. It can easily be
corrected to subtract their initial strain from the signal.
5.2.2
Comparison of DIC system and rotation
When working with the DIC system it is critical that the spectral pattern is correct to
gain a suﬃcient resolution. Also, the calibration, bright light sources and well maintained
40

equipment is essential to gain good results.
In this experiment the same experiment was performed three times, to record six meters
of the T-pylon beam behavior. It is important that the experiments are performed con-
sistently, so that the results can be compared to each other. In the data processing, it is
important to merge the data in a correct data then.
Apart from a technical setup, the results can be very accurate. The beam deﬂection was
described with a 2.
order polynomial since it matched well to the curve, but another
mathematical regression could also be used.
5.3
Transient eﬀect and non-transient eﬀects
The assessments of the transient eﬀect on the T-pylon beam is based on two FEM
calculations. It is shown in sections 4.3 & 4.4 that the transient eﬀect can be neglected.
This is essential to be able to perform the experiments, since the actuators cannot be
controlled that fast. With no transient eﬀect, the time span of the experiment can be
increased while the forces can be decreased and still be representative for the galloping
forces. Also, another beneﬁt is the computational time for the TD FEM analysis which is
signiﬁcantly decreased.
5.4
Controlling the actuators
5.4.1
Coordinate transformation
During the experiments there have been obstacles with creating the input ﬁles to the
actuators in the right systems. If this is not done properly, the actuators can work against
each other and an interlock can be activated.
5.4.2
Increasing power of the forces
A parameter that has inﬂuence on how well the actuators can be controlled, is the
displacement cmd signal which they aim to follow. If the displacements are too large and
alternating too fast, it reaches the limits of what the actuators can perform. Because there
also is a limit to how high the PID setting can be set, before the controlling of the actuators
gets unstable and the system activates the interlock.
In general, when the force is increased from 10 % to 20 %, 30 % and 40 % the displacement
fdbk is more precise with the displacement cmd curve. The reason for this could be the
noise to signal ratio getting better. However, the results do not change signiﬁcantly and 10
% would still be representative for the 100 % of the input force. Most of the experiments
are at 10 % of the forces.
Especially in the beginning of the experiment, the actuators sometime overshoot the signal,
and it takes little time before they adjust to the curve. The initial displacement cmd also
inﬂuences how the actuators are controlled. For example, the actuators in the y-direction
O.C.S are always negative values. If the forces are set to 100 % of the forces, it will often
overshoot the initial displacement cmd and the interlock can be activated. A solution for
this is to make a displacement ramp of e.g. 10 seconds so that all the actuators can ﬁnd
their initial displacement cmd.

5.4.3
Increasing time span of the experiment
The original force ﬁles are from a long time analysis, but this thesis uses a sequence of
only 5 seconds which is representative for the galloping phenomena.
As described, the forces are oscillating with approximately 1-2 Hz. In order to control the
actuator to the desired command, the time span is increased by a factor of 10 and 20, so
that the time span is 50 s and 100 s, respectively. It is unlikely that the actuators can
osculate with 1 Hz and with 100 % magnitude of the force input ﬁles, it varies from -1.3
kN to 17 kN, and what decide it is the PID settings. But if the PID is set too high, the
system gets unstable and the actuators cannot follow the command. With a time span
of 50 s, it can follow the command curve suﬃciently. It is possible because the transient
eﬀects can be neglected. But increasing it to 100 s does not provide signiﬁcantly better
results.
5.5
Comparison of actuators’ response to time dependant
displacement input
With the current PID displacement settings, the actuators follow the displacement cmd
very well in all load cases. At loc. 3 the displacements are largest and the actuators follows
the cmd curve very precisely.
As shown in section 4.5, a common pattern is that at loc. 1, the actuators follow the
displacement cmd signal very poorly. There are several reason for this; First, the PID for
the actuators in loc. 1 are not as good as in the other locations. Usually, the PID is lower
at loc. 1 compared to loc.2 and loc. 3. Second, since the displacements are the smallest
at loc.1, the noise to input ratio is the worst.
Third, the EI could be wrong since the model has discrete transitions in Iy and the stiﬀ-
ness was corrected for act. y3 QS test, and might not be representative for the loc. 1.
Fourth, sometimes the actuators’ displacements cmd is not calibrated to the initial posi-
tion of the actuator. It can be seen that the actuator follow the cmd curves, but with an
constant error.
Fifth, the actuators sometimes also overshoot in the beginning, and it takes some time
before the fdbk adjusts to the cmd curve.
5.6
Comparison of input force ﬁle to FEM & force fdbk from
actuators
In general, the TD FEM force input match with the force fdbk from the TD experi-
ments. The accuracy is dependant on various factors such as the tuning of the actuators,
the locations of the beam and therefore how much the beam deﬂects. But also the time
span of the experiment and the force factor of the TD FEM force input ﬁles.
It seems like the stiﬀness of the beam is calibrated so it matches the forces while the co-
ordinate transformation is changing the coordinates of input ﬁles and results correctly.
The force comparison is a good indication that the FEM model in fact can predict the
structural force very well, since the force input ﬁles are ﬁrstly transformed into displace-
ments with the TD FEM model and the ﬁles then are used to displacement control the
actuators while they track their force fdbk. At loc. 1 the response is not very good and
only in the same order of magnitude. However, at loc. 2 and loc. 3, the results become
trustworthy since the force fdbk follows the predicted force curve from the TD FEM force
input ﬁles.

Summing up
The results of this thesis are all contributing to answering the main research question.
During the experimental work a lot of experiences are gained in order to control the ac-
tuators. MatLab/ANSYS script are written to pre- and post-process the forces and the

5.8
Limitation of the input forces
It is important to understand the limitation of this project. Neither the experiments
or the numerical FEM analysis reﬂect the reality of how a T-pylon behave in real life when
conductors are exposed to the galloping phenomena. The reason for this is that the force
ﬁles are generated based on a single conductor model, which gives one set of forces in the
three directions x,y,z in O.C.S. In this analysis the model ABAQUS and ANSYS model are
separated and to compensate for the forces’ cross linking, a phase shifting is introduced.
In reality, three wires are attached to the T-pylon beam and it is very unlikely that they
all will oscillate in a same way in or out of phase.
A more realistic model would be to merge the ABAQUS model with three conductors
with the ANSYS model which calculate the cross link between forces due to the deﬂection
of the beam. The disadvantage of this is that the computational cost of the model and the
complexity will increase a lot.
To assess how much the inﬂuence of the cross linking aﬀects the forces’ input, without
using a complex and costly computation numerical model, an experiment can be per-
formed. One actuator can be loaded, for example act. y3, and track the force output at y2
and inserted it in the ABAQUS model. If the force output of the ABAQUS model is the
same as the initial force input, then the cross link has very little eﬀect and if not, then the
cross link has some eﬀect.
5.9
Further work
This thesis has focused heavily on experiments and how to perform them. Since nine
actuators are acting on one system, complications easily can occur and therefore it was a
choice to displacement control all the actuators to avoid the actuators interferes with each
others. In future work, it would be interesting to make a force control experiment with
perhaps only low forces to investigate how well they can be controlled. To force control
the actuators to reﬂect the galloping phenomenon more precisely instead of, as in this
thesis, converting the forces to displacements with a FEM program and then compare the

experimental forces with the input forces.
Also, it would be interesting to perform DIC experiments where the beam is captured with
stickers instead of the spectral pattern to see if it is possible to capture the beam in one
take. However, this requires another calibration, and setup.
Another experiment which could be very interesting is discussed in section 5.8, and is the
cross linking eﬀect through the beam.
If these experiments show that there are a signiﬁcant cross linking eﬀects, it could be
interesting to create integrated FEM models with three conductor wires. The FEM model
could couple the eﬀect of the structural response of the T-pylon beam and the forces, even
though this would increase the complexity of the FEM model.

6.2.3
QS extremum analysis for TD forces
The purpose of the QS extremum analysis is to give an estimate of the maximum
quantities that appear under the TD force. When the actuators reach a maximum of 18
kN and the y actuators 1.87 kN. The T-pylon deﬂect around 10 cm in the z-direction
in E.C.S at the tip. The maximum strain are at the actuators locations, but in general
it is numerical largest at the beam clamping. Under the extremum conditions it reach
ϵx = ±9.53 · 10−4.
The z-rotation in E.C.S is also interesting since it can be compared with the DIC system.
It reach the values of 1.6 mm/m at the beam clamping to 7.7 mm/m towards the tip of the
T-pylon beam.
The principal stress are numerical largest at the beam clamping with 28.8 MPa.
6.3
TD experiments and TD FEM model
It turns out the lowest natural frequency for the T-pylon beam is 7.3820 Hz, which
is way higher than the than osculating of the galloping forces which is around 1 Hz.
For that reason the transient eﬀect has little inﬂuence. The transient and non-transient
displacement curves were compared and it showed as expected there were no signiﬁcant
diﬀerence.
This means that the experimental time span can be increased, with a factor 10 to 20. Also
the forces can be decreased to 10 % of the forces ﬁles. These corrections makes it easier to
control the actuators.
The TD FEM model still predict the structural responds well. It seems like the phase
shifting does change the responds a little bit, but with no increase in the displacement
amplitude.
6.4
Limitations and further work
The conducted experiments are limited by the force inputs, since it comes from a single
conductor wire model. The forces on the T-pylon beam might be cross linked throughout it.
For further work, the FEM models can be merged to one model. This model contains three
conductor wires and how the galloping phenomenon aﬀect them. Also how the conductor
wires aﬀect the T-pylon beam and how the beam deﬂection aﬀect the osculations of the
conductor wires. This model might be complex to create and the computational time can
