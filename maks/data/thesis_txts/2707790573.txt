ABSTRACT
To limit ongoing global climate change, a drastic reduction in the emission of greenhouse
gasses is required. As the transport sector is a major contributor to the emission of
greenhouse gasses the widespread adoption of Electric Vehicles (EV) is required alongside
the development of charging infrastructure supplied by renewable generation.
The work presented in this report focuses on the design of an advanced energy management
system for a DC microgrid on the Danish island of Bornholm. The DC microgrid combines
local Photo Voltaic (PV) generation, battery energy storage, DC fast chargers and an
inverter connection to the distribution grid to facilitate the fast charging of EVs using locally
generated renewable energy. It operates using three battery strings with a reconﬁgurable
topology that can adjust their operating voltage, allowing them to connect directly to any
of the components of the DC microgrid.
The designed energy management system utilises PV generation forecasts and EV demand
predictions to enact a dynamic control system that balances the often conﬂicting core
objectives of the DC microgrid using a objective priority weighting system. These core
objectives are to charge EVs, harvest PV generation and generate revenue. The energy
management system operates on two levels, the system level consists of three algorithms
for assigning the connections between battery strings and system components, along with
a process that selects the preferred assignment algorithm based on the forecast conditions
and objective priorities. The inverter level consists of a dynamic droop control system,
whereby the energy imported or exported between the DC microgrid and distribution grid
is dictated by a droop controller based on the state of charge of the connected battery.
The reference value, upper and lower dead bands of the droop control are continually and
dynamically adjusted based on the forecast conditions and objective priorities. Methods
for forecasting of PV generation and predicting EV demand are also presented.
This energy management system is then tested using a simulated model of the DC microgrid
in a number of scenarios, including: i) scenarios designed to test the energy management
systems adherence to the given priority weightings when not all objectives can be fulﬁlled
completely, ii) operating the DC microgrid at a reduced grid connection capacity, iii)
a comparison to the existing energy management system. These tests show the energy
management system completely fulﬁlls its core objectives in all scenarios based on recorded
conditions and in created scenarios where this is not possible its operation is adjusted
to best fulﬁll the prioritised objective. The DC microgrid was shown to be capable of
operating at a reduced grid capacity with minimal reduction in the potential to charge
EVs, however the reduced capacity did impact the capability to harvest PV generated
energy. When compared to the existing energy management system, the one proposed in
this report had minor improvements in scenarios where objectives could be easily achieved,
but the dynamic capabilities performed comparatively better in more stressful situations.
This enables the possibility of future work focusing to optimise of the size of components
in a DC microgrid designed to operate at a reduced grid connection.

INTRODUCTION
This chapter gives a brief overview of ongoing global climate change and the factors that
are causing it. It outlines how addressing this global challenge requires the transition to
an electrical energy system focused on renewable energy generation and the electriﬁcation
of sectors that traditionally directly consume fossil fuel. It presents an introduction to the
INSULAE project, an initiative that is being undertaken to aid this transition which the
DC microgrid this report is based on is apart of. Finally, it presents the problems this
report aims to address and an overview the reports structure.
1.1
Motivation
In 2021 the UN Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) released its sixth
assessment report [6]. This report states that it is unequivocal that human inﬂuence
has caused an increase in global temperatures and widespread changes in climate at an
unprecedented rate. The emission of green house gases, particularly carbon dioxide (CO2),
are the major driving force behind this change, causing an estimated global increase in
temperatures of around 2◦C. This has already had a severe eﬀect in all global regions,
and it is expected that the frequency of climate extremes such as heatwaves and tropical
storms is to increase, along with a rise in global sea levels, as this warming continues.
The IPCC report states that the limiting of global warming requires a drastic reduction
of CO2 emissions, to at least a net zero emissions, and a reduction in other greenhouse
gas emissions. Within the EU, the European Environment Agency (EEA) targeted a 20%
reduction in CO2 emissions from 1990 levels by 2020 [7], and while this target was met the
decarbonisation eﬀorts need to continue to meet the now reduced EEA targets of a 55%
reduction in CO2 emissions by 2030, and to be completely carbon neutral by 2050 [1]. The
EU greenhouse gas emissions in recent years, and targeted future emissions out to this
2050 target, is shown in ﬁgure 1.1.
Energy production and transport are the two sectors with the largest contribution to the
emission of greenhouse gasses in the EU, being responsible for 28% and 21% of all EU
greenhouse gas emissions in 2017 respectively [8]. Of this 21% of emissions due to transport,
14.5% are due to passenger cars and small commercial vehicles [9]. To reduce these emissions
due to light vehicles the EU aims to encourage the electriﬁcation of transport through
widespread adoption of electric vehicles (EV), while de-carbonising the energy production
sector by increasing the electricity generated from renewable sources. To facilitate and
encourage this adoption of EVs improvements in the EV charging infrastructure and the
availability of EV chargers are required. This can include encouraging the self-generation,
storage and consumption of renewable electricity, improving individuals ability to use
electricity generated from their own solar panels to charge their EVs [10].

The INSULAE project is an EU backed initiative that aims to encourage innovative
solutions for the de-carbonising of islands around the EU, transition them to locally
produced, sustainable and low-cost sources of energy [11]. This is done by developing
and demonstrating such solutions on three pilot islands, including the Danish island of
Bornholm, which aims to be completely carbon neutral by 2025. Like the rest of the EU,
the transport sector is one of the largest contributors to CO2 emissions on the island and
its electriﬁcation, particularly of the uptake of EVs, is one of the main transitions that
need to occur to achieve this goal [12].
This integration of EVs into an electricity system increases the overall load and can,
particularly a small island system like Bornholm, lead to congestion and unbalance the
distribution grid, with fast-charging of EVs having a notable impact on grid balance due
to high peak power demand. DC microgrids could oﬀer a solution to this issue, integrating
PV generation and battery storage to facilitate EV fast charging, encouraging the adoption
of EVs while lessening the impact of such charging on the distribution grid. As part of
the INSULAE project a prototype DC microgrid has been deployed on Bornholm, at the
education center Campus Bornholm, consisting of Photo Voltaic (PV) generation, battery
energy storage, two EV fast chargers and an inverter connection to the distribution grid
[5]. This gives the opportunity to test the potential for a DC microgrid to use locally
generated renewable energy to supply the fast charging of EVs at a power level greater
than what could be supplied by the local distribution grid.

This thesis aims to design and test an advanced Energy Management System (EMS)
consisting of dynamic control systems and decision-making algorithms for use within the

This chapter provides an overview of the DC microgrid that forms the focus of this study,
and compare it to other DC microgrid designs. It examines the reconﬁgurable nature of
the battery strings and the existing Energy Management Systems currently in use. It also

2.1
DC microgrid system
A microgrid is a decentralised, self-suﬃcient group of distributed, often renewable, power
generation sources and loads capable of functioning both connected to and independent
from the larger power network. Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are also often
integrated into their design. The use of DC microgrid oﬀers a number of advantages
over AC, including removing the need to control reactive power and AC frequency, along
with removing the requirement for AC/DC converters to connect many BESS and PV
generation [13].
2.1.1
Campus Bornholm DC Microgrid
The DC microgrid that forms the focus of this report is located at Campus Bornholm, and
is integrated into the electrical power infrastructure on the campus. The DC Microgrid
and its connection to the campus is represented in the single line diagram in ﬁgure 2.1.

the AC auxiliary load required to operate the control systems and thermal management

The Bornholm DC microgrid has a somewhat novel architecture. The general approach to
DC microgrids sees components connected to a central DC bus via inverters, to facilitate
connecting components with diﬀerent operating voltages [14]. The Bornholm system
however operates using three reconﬁgurable dual-string batteries designed by Nerve Smart
Systems which will be described in more detail in section 2.2.2. These dual-string batteries
are capable of dynamically reconﬁguring their topology to achieve a range of voltages
upon requested by the associated String Control Module (SCM) to enable the dual-string
battery to be connected to components with a diﬀerent operating voltage without the need
for voltage converters. Hence, each of the DC microgrid components can all be connected
directly to one of the three battery dual-strings despite their diﬀerent operating voltages.
This removes the need for inverters, reducing costs and power losses within the system.
However, as components can only be connected to the battery dual-strings, said batteries
act as the central hub of the micro gird, taking the place of the bus in more conventional DC
microgrids. To allow power to ﬂow between components in the system, a battery dual-string
has to charge, change connection and discharge. This is done while also performing their
energy storage functions. As such, a control system that manages the state of charge of
the batteries and the energy within the system eﬀectively is of increased importance. Such
a system is called an Energy Managment System (EMS) and acts as the central controller,
dictating the operation of each of the microgrid components and the connections between
these components.

The capability to charge at up to 350 kW means these chargers are more than capable of
supplying the peak charging demands of modern commercially available EVs during DC

from the batteries, and the voltage at which it is drawn, vary. The reconﬁgurable Nerve

2.2.1
Reconﬁgurable Battery Systems
Typically battery modules and systems consist of cells connected in either series or parallel.
According to Kirchoﬀ’s laws on voltage and current, connecting cells in a series connection,
i.e. connecting the positive Cathode of one cell to the negative Anode of the next, results in
a system with a voltage equal to the sum of the voltages of the cells, but a current capacity
limited to that of one cell. Conversely, connecting the cells in parallel, i.e. connecting the
positive cathodes of all the cells, and all the negative anodes, the current capacity of the
system is equal to the sum of the connected cells, but the voltage remains that of a single
cell. From this, a battery system is created by connecting a number of cells together in
both series and parallel to reach the desired rated voltage and current capacities.
Reconﬁgurable battery technologies aim to provide a more ﬂexible BESS by facilitating
the changing of the connections of the cells, allowing the number of cells connected in
series or parallel to be changed during battery operation. Such a system carries a number
of beneﬁts. It allows the rated voltage and current capacity of the BESS to be changed
allowing for the cells to adapt to diﬀerent functions [18]. By ensuring the even discharge at
a cell level it can improve battery lifespan [19], and it can improve the safety and thermal
management of a system [4].
There are a number of topologies for these reconﬁgurable systems to take. One of the
earliest, proposed by C. Song et. al. [19] allows for cells to be fully reconﬁgured into any
combination of series, parallel or a combined series-parallel. Other such fully reconﬁgurable
designs look to create a dynamic ’genetic’ framework for reconﬁgurable cells to increase the
scalability and allow for multiple terminals [20], or creating an adaptive reconﬁguration
algorithm based on graphical extractions of a simpler, but still fully reconﬁgurable, topology
[21]. Not all topologies are fully reconﬁgurable, as series-connected reconﬁgurable systems
have been proposed [22]. In this conﬁguration, all cells are connected in series, with two
switches present allowing the bypassing of individual cells. Hence the voltage of the system
can be varied with a large degree of freedom and the lifetime of the BESS can be improved
by isolating cells with low state of health, while simplifying the system and reducing the
number of switches but at the cost of not having the capability to adjust current capacity
of the system. A selection of these topologies are shown in Figure 2.3

2.3
Energy Management Systems
An Energy Management System (EMS) for a microgrid is the control center, a computer
system that performs a variety of functions, including the management of energy storage
systems, the microgrids connection to larger grids, and the monitoring, forecasting and, if
possible, scheduling of connected generation and loads [13]. Such processes are informed
by decision making strategies, of which there are a wide variety to suit the wide variety
of microgrids [24]. In this section an overview of existing EMS designs and strategies
are presented including the objectives they aim to achieve through their control of the
microgrids and the methods used to achieve these objectives. Following this, the most
recently designed EMS for the Campus Bornholm DC Microgrid, developed by J. Lage [2],
will be presented within this context.
2.3.1
Microgrid EMS Overview
Microgrid EMSs can be classiﬁed in a variety of ways, however for the purpose of this
report the main distinction that will be followed is between the EMS strategies that
follow rule-based energy management and those that follow optimisation-based energy
management [25]. The former uses a deﬁned set of rules to dictate how the EMS operates
the system given the conditions of operation of the microgrid, whereas the latter formulates
an objective function and operating constraints to create an optimisation problem, which
is then solved and dictates the operation of the microgrid. While these are two distinct
methods of operation, an understanding of both is useful in the design of an advanced
EMS, and hence both will be examined.
Various optimisation solving algorithms have been found eﬀective at solving optimisation
problems for the development of a microgrid EMS, including Inernal Point Algorithm,
Genertic algorithm and the Patern Search Method [26], or as presented by L. Vásquez et.

learning to ﬁnd a control system to optimally schedule a microgrid system [28]. Despite
these large varieties in method of optimisation, the diﬀerences between the optimisation-
based EMS strategies that are most of interest lie in the objective function that are
optimised. They deﬁne the priority of the EMS, and what it will aim to optimise while
operating the microgrid.
Often, the main priority of the EMS is to reduce operating costs [29] [30], especially in
a system consisting of residential loads, PV generation and BESS. In this situation the
objective function is often formulated as a cost function, where the local loads, generations,
battery SOC and cost of buying/selling power are considered and optimised to minimise
cost of power [31]. This concept of optimisation for cost saving can be developed upon,

Where the optimisation-based management strategies discussed all focus on the formulation
of an objective function and the associated optimisation, rules-based energy management
systems have a wider variety of potential approaches to energy management.
Rules-based energy management often have a number of predetermined modes of operation
and based on the operating conditions switch between these modes of operation. In
microgrids that contain BESS, the battery SOC is often the microgrid condition that
dictates the mode of operation of the system [35]. In systems with increased complexity
such as a larger number of operating states, a series of binary decisions formed into a
decision tree are utilised to choose a mode of operation [36]. As an alternative to complex
decision trees control systems will sometimes operate using fuzzy logic, or fuzzy control,
whereby analog inputs can be given more than two values, as in a binary system. This
allows fuzzy logic to make decisions based on more detailed information, rather than if
a statement is simply true or false. For example A. Kamel et. al. [37] present a fuzzy
control system that deﬁnes operating states based on a fuzzy control system with three
states for system conditions, Low, OK and High. The implementation of decision trees or
fuzzy control allows for more system conditions to be taken into account when deciding
state of operation.
A controller consisting of a ﬁnite number of control states can be described and represented
as a Finite State Machine (FSM), giving mathematical representation of the states and the
transition between them. This allows for Finite State Control, where a ﬁnite set of inputs
dictate the switching between the control states. In a DC microgrid, this can facilitate
a two leveled control system, where ﬁnite state control is used for grid-wide modes of
operation, but each component within the system has its own, lower, level of control,
reducing communication requirements while maintaining a reliable control system [38].
Such a two-level approach is utilised by Y. Han et. al. [39] to create a DC microgrid control
system that follows the control objectives of both maintaining system operation conditions
and minimising equivalent hydrogen consumption of a connected hydrogen fuel cell. The
separating of device level control and system level control allows for the rules-based control
methodology to be applied on a system level the system, with components operation
dictated by more speciﬁc control methods.
Such component level control in a rules-based system can take many forms. This including
PI control [37], which provides a reliable and continuous form of control but are diﬃcult

2.3.2
Existing EMS
Within the Campus Bornholm DC Microgrid, the EMS is responsible for deciding which
battery string is connected to which of the four grid components. It also dictates the power
setpoint of the inverter, controlling the ﬂow of power into or out of the DC microgrid.
Due to the somewhat novel architecture of this DC microgrid as described earlier in this
chapter, the operation of each of the connected components is depended on the SOC of
the battery strings and the monitoring, panning and controlling the SOC of the batteries
is the primary responsibility of the EMS [5]. This responsibility entails ensuring that the
SOC conditions across the three battery strings are suitable for the completion of the
operational objectives of each of the components.
As the objective of the PV system is to maximize the energy generated, the EMS must
ensure that when energy is being generated there is a battery string with suﬃcient available
capacity to collect the generated energy, i.e. a battery with a low enough SOC. However,
the objective for the EV charging is to fulﬁll the charging requests of any EV that connects,
hence the EMS must ensure there are battery strings available with suﬃcient stored energy
to meet these charging requirements. The objectives of the grid connection and inverter
can vary. They can be used to generate revenue by trading energy on the electricity market,
providing grid services or supplying local demand within Campus Bornholm, but may also
have speciﬁc objectives such as to minimize power import to maximise self consumption
within the DC microgrid. These objectives often have contradictory expectations for the
battery SOC, and its the responsibility of the EMS to balance the objectives, and hence
the batteries SOC, so that all can be fulﬁlled as completely as possible [5].
The most recent EMS designed for the Bornholm system was created by J. Lage [2], and
was tested on a simulated model of the system. It is a multi leveled approach, whereby
the system level control, the assignment of the connections between battery dual-strings
and the system components, is approached based on a rules-based binary decision tree,
and component level inverter setpoint control is performed by speciﬁc control system.
The existing decision system for the assignment of the battery dual-strings to components
within the system can be seen in Figure 2.5. This string assignment algorithm runs every
15 minutes of operation and each time an EV connects to or disconnects from one of the
two chargers.
The highest priority of this EMS is fulﬁlling the charging requests of connected EVs, and as
such the ﬁrst stage is to assign a newly connected EV to the available battery dual-string
with the highest SOC. This dual-string will then remain connected to that EV until the
EV stops charging, where the string will be disconnected. The decision is then split based
on the availability of PV production. If there is currently no PV generation, and there are
currently EVs charging, the inverter is connected to the lowest SOC dual-string. If there is
no PV production or EVs charging, the inverter is connected to the dual-string with the
highest deviation in SOC from the target, which is 0.5 pu. If there is no PV generation,
but EVs are charging, the inverter is assigned to the available string with the lowest SOC.
If the PV system is generating power, and both EV chargers are active, the PV generation
is assigned to the only available dual-string. Otherwise the inverter is assigned to the
highest deviating SOC dual-string, and the PV generation to the remaining dual-string.

2.4.8
Thermal Model
The microgrid model contains an extensive thermal model, the purpose of which is to
model the temperature dynamics within the container, and the resultant auxiliary power
consumption of the thermal management system. This is an important metric to consider
as it needs to be accounted for in the calculation of the overall energy eﬃciency of the
microgrid.
Within the system there are two temperature-dependent consumers of auxiliary power, the
heat pump that pipes cold air into the middle of the container and the battery and control
rack fans that circulate air within the system. Both regulate their operation depending on
the temperature of the component they are associated with. The heat pump is modeled
as operating at 0% of its capacity at a lower temperature of 25◦C, and at 100% of its
capacity at 35◦C, with a linear characteristic between the two. The fans are modeled as
operating at a 50% capacity when a lower temperature of 30◦C is reached, and linearly
increasing their duty cycle until they reach 100% of their capacity at 55◦C. The fans also
have a hysteresis with a lower limit of 25◦C implemented to prevent continuous switching.

are the internal temperatures of the three battery dual strings, Tcr the internal temperature
of the control rack, TC the temperature in the middle of the container and Tout, the exterior
ambient temperature, which is constant and depended on PV scenario. The temperature
of each zone is calculated by using a heat balance equation,

FORECASTING OF PV & EV CONDITIONS
This chapter describes the source of the forecasts used to predict the PV generation, how
these forecasts were converted into usable metrics and an analysis on the accuracy of
the used forecasts. It also presents an analysis of the EV charging patterns seen in the
Bornholm microgrid, and from this formulates a statistical probability of future charging
patterns that can be used to oﬀer a degree of prediction of EV Demand. This forecast
PV generation and predicted EV demand will be used as an input to the designed system
EMS, creating a system that predicts and optimises for future conditions.
3.1
Forecasting of PV Generation
Solar irradiance forecasting can help to mitigate the inherently uncertain nature of operating
PV generation. Having a reliable forecast on upcoming solar irradiance, and hence PV
generation, can facilitate more reliable planning and operation of the systems the solar
generation is connected to. As such, a system to forecast the solar irradiation, and PV
generation, was developed for the campus Bornholm DC microgrid system.
3.1.1
Solcast Forecasting
The source of solar forecasting used for this project was Solcast, a global solar forecasting
and data modelling company [57]. Solcast develops forecasts of solar irradiation designed
for use with PV generation based on numerical weather models and satellite imagery from
ﬁve geostationary weather satellites. These geostationary satellites gather imagery every 5 -
15 minutes, and by comparing these satellite images to images of the same location with no
cloud cover the current satellite images can be separated into cloudy areas and cloud-free
areas. A 3D cloud modelling approach is then applied to the areas found to contain clouds,
to give a measure of the current cloud opacity over a given area. By combining this with
the REST2 clear-sky radiation model [58], which estimates the solar radiation with no
cloud cover, an accurate measure of the current solar irradiance at a point is gathered.
To provide forecasts of solar irradiance, Solcast uses wind forecast by numerical weather
prediction, in conjunction with current cloud opacity measurements to create predictions
on future cloud opacity, out to a range of 4 hours ahead of the current time, which is again
combined with the RSET2 model to give a forecast of solar irradiance. Beyond this 4
hours ahead horizon, only numerical weather prediction data is used to give a forecast, out
to a range of 7 days. This results in a forecast that is more accurate and updates more
frequently for a 4 hour horizon, and a forecast that updates less frequently and, while still
conventionally accurate, is less accurate beyond this out to seven days.

3.1.2
Solcast API
To access the forecast solar irradiation provided by Solcast for the application on the
Bornholm DC microgrid, their Application Programming Interface (API) [59] was utilised.
On the Solcast website, an account was registered and the latitude and longitude of the
Bornholm site was used to create a measurement point, and associated resource id. With
this id, a script was written in the Python programming language [60] that could make a
request to the Solcast API and pull from it the forecast data available for the Bornholm
site. These API pull requests are made automatically at the dictated times of day, and the
script has been running continuously, collecting data from the Solcast API, since October
2021, and continues to collect forecast data for the Bornholm site.
This data takes the form of a .csv ﬁle containing forecast values for the Direct Normal
Irradiance (DNI), a measure of the irradiation directly from the sun to a surface normal to
the irradiation, Diﬀuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI), a measure of the radiation incident
on a horizontal surface from light scattered in the atmosphere, and Global Horizontal
Irradiance (GHI), the total irradiance from the sun on a horizontal surface. All these
forecasts are given in W/m2. GHI is a metric found by the combination of the DNI and
DHI according to the formula
GHI = DHI + DNI · cos(z)
(3.1)
where z is the solar zenith angle, the angle made between the solar radiation and the
vertical. GHI accounts for all solar irradiation on a horizontal surface and as such, this
was the forecast measurement used. These forecasts also provided a forecast temperature
measurement for the Bornholm site.
This forecasts data was provided with a time resolution of 30 minutes, with a measurement
representing the expected average value over the given 30 minute period. Each forecast
had a scope of seven days, with the ﬁrst four hours considering the cloud cover data from
satellite imagery. There was a limit of ten forecast requests per day and as such these
ten requests were spaced throughout the day with no more than four hours between each
request, with more frequent forecasts requests during and preceding the middle of the day
to ensure more accurate forecasts are available during the times of peak solar production.

Prediction of EV Demand
To allow for a level of prediction of the EV demand that is expected to be seen by the
microgrid, a process is used whereby historical charging trends and patterns are analysed
to give a statistical measure of likely future charging patterns. While this is not an active
forecasting process where factors that could aﬀect the charge demand are taken into
account directly, when examining historical charge data clear patterns begin to emerge,
patterns which can be used to give an indication of expected EV demand at a given time.
Such a process becomes more accurate and detailed the more historical data is available.
3.2.1
Recorded EV Charging
When EV charging data was gathered at the beginning of this project, the EV chargers
on the Bornholm DC microgrid had been active for 71 days. During this period, there
were 186 occasions when an EV charged from the system, with a total of 1697.45 kWh of
energy charged. There were also 41 occasions where an EV connected to a charger but no
energy transfer was made. These connection with no energy transfer are either due to the
connection being very brief or no power is requested by the EV, as once a connection is
made the charging is controlled by the EV’s EMS. It was also observed that there were
often multiple separate charges of the same EV within a short space of time, likely due
to the driver plugging the car in and out. Such occurrences are considered as separate
charging sessions by the microgrid, and in this analysis. During this time, there were 19
occasions when two EV’s were charging simultaneously, requiring the use of both charging
stations.
The mean energy transfer made for each charge was 9.12 kWh, with the largest transfer of
energy to an EV in a single charging session being 49.16 kWh, occurring on 29/07/2021 at
13:37. The mean power at which the EV’s drew energy from the system was 53.27 kW.
The highest power demand from an EV was 164.85 kW, which occurred on 08/07/2021 at
08:13. This charge also had the highest average power drawn from the system, at 136.18
kW. The average time an EV was charging was 9.84 minutes, but on average the EVs
were connected to the charger for a total of 57.42 minutes, indicating that it is a common
occurrence for an EV to be left connected to the charger long after it has ﬁnished charging.
Congestion leaving drivers waiting to use a charging station does not appear to be an issue
however, as both charger are only in use simultaneously 19 times.
These ﬁgures show that the EV fast chargers in the Bornholm DC microgrid see relatively
little use, with there only being active charging for 1.79% of the time the charger is active.
The energy requested in each charging session tends to be small relative to the capabilities

DEVELOPMENT OF DYNAMIC ENERGY
MANAGEMENT SYSTEM
The EMS of the Campus Bornholm DC Microgrid is responsible for controlling the ﬂow of
energy into and out of the grid. It dictates how the three battery strings are connected
to the grid components at any time, and controls the setpoint of the grid connection
inverter, with the aim of maintaining a battery SOC that best enables all of the connected
components to achieve there individual objectives. As described in section 2.3.2 the EMS
has multiple objectives that often have contradictory expectations for the system state,
particularly the battery SOC. To best fulﬁll the objective of harvesting PV energy the SOC
of the batteries should be kept low, to ensure there is always space to collect harvested
energy. To best fulﬁll the objective of supplying EV charging requests the SOC of the
batteries should be kept high, to ensure there is always enough energy available to meet
these requests. The EMS is also responsible for ensuring the batteries operate in an eﬃcient
and reliable manor, by ensuring the operation of the batteries remains within acceptable
limits and minimise battery cycling and switching to reduce battery aging and wear on
system components.
To manage these multiple conﬂicting objectives, the EMS designed in this project is based
around a system of weighted priorities whereby the user or operator of the DC Microgrid
gives a numerical value representing the priority of a system objective. The EMS then
adjusts its operation based on these values to fulﬁll multiple objectives with contradictory
expectations. To this end, the three core objectives of the EMS are deﬁned as follows:
1. Fulﬁll EV Charging Needs - The DC microgrid should be able to supply the
energy demand of any EV that connects to the fast chargers.
2. Harvest PV Energy - The DC microgrid should maximise the usable energy
harvested from the connected PV system.
3. Maximize Revenues - The DC microgrid should aim to minimise costs of operation
and maximise revenue generated.
The priority weighting system ask the operator to set a numerical value to represent the
priority of each objective, UEV , UPV & URev for the three objectives listed respectively.
Each of these weightings must have a value between 0 and 1, and the sum of all three
priority weightings must be equal to one,
0 ≤UEV , UPV , URev ≤1
(4.1)
UEV + UPV + URev = 1
(4.2)
Along with the weighted objectives, the EMS designed in this project also dynamically
adjusts its operation in response to forecast conditions. Doing so ensures that the conditions

and control of the DC Microgird are optimal for the fulﬁllment of the prioritised system
objectives. It enables the system to predict and prepare for upcoming conditions, rather

4.1
String Assignment System Level Control
The system level string assignment of the EMS has to ensure that each of the components
of the DC microgrid are connected to the battery dual string with an SOC that best
enables them to achieve there objectives. It is also responsible for ensuring that the battery
strings with a non optimal SOC are connected to components that will return the SOC to
its optimal, i.e. if a battery has a low SOC it is connected to a component that will charge
it, or vice versa.
The assignment of connections between battery strings and components is carried out by a
rule-based system, an overview of which is seen as a block diagram in ﬁgure 4.1. Based on
the forecast system conditions and the operator-set priority weightings, the EMS selects a
decision based algorithm used to assign battery strings to components. This algorithm
becomes the active string assignment algorithm, and dictates the connections between
system components and batteries based on the system conditions. The system conditions
used to make these assignments are the SOC of each of the battery strings, and which
components are currently active.
The EMS has three string assignment algorithms to chose from, as follows
• SAstd - The standard string assignment algorithm that optimally assigns strings in
the majority of cases.
• SAEV - A string assignment algorithm that priorities retaining energy within the
system when high EV charging and low PV generation is expected.
• SAPV - A string assignment algorithm that ensures the PV system is connected to
the optimal battery string at times of high generation.
How the EMS decides which assignment algorithm to use will be described ﬁrst, followed
by a description of each of the algorithms themselves, the SOC protection in place to

4.1.2
String Assignment Algorithm: Standard (SAstd)
The decision system for the standard string assignment algorithm, SAstd, is shown in ﬁgure
4.2. The ﬁrst check that is made is if an EV has just stopped charging, in which case the
EV charger is disconnected from the battery it was charging from, freeing up that battery

string for other connections. Following this, if an EV has begun charging, it is assigned to
the available battery with the highest SOC. This battery then remains connected to the
EV and unavailable for any other assignments until the EV charging ends, either by the
charger being disconnected or the EV’s EMS stopping requests for charge.
With the EV charging aspect of the string assignment handled, the other system components
are assigned to battery strings. If there is currently no PV production, the only remaining
component to assign a battery string to is the inverter. If there is an EV charging and
hence energy leaving the system the inverter gets assigned to the available battery string
with the lowest SOC, as there is currently energy leaving the system and with no PV
generation this energy needs to be replaced via import. If are currently no EVs charging
and no PV generation, the inverter is connected to the highest SOC deviating string, i.e.
the string with a current SOC furthest from the reference SOC (discussed in more detail
in section 4.2). This means that the inverter will be either importing or exporting energy
depending on the state of the batteries, to bring the batteries closer to the target SOC so
they can be in an optimal state for future connections.
If the PV system is currently capable of generating power, and there are two EVs currently
charging, the PV gets assigned to the last available battery string. This leaves the grid
connection inverter disconnected, as when this string assignment algorithm is being used
ensuring the available PV is harvested is seen as a higher priority. If only one EV is
currently charging the inverter is again assigned to the available string with the highest
deviating SOC, with the PV being connected to the last available string.

4.1.3
String Assignment Algorithm: EV Priority (SAEV )
The string assignment used when the conditions of the DC microgrid are expected to be
high EV demand and low PV generation, and the highest priority objective is fulﬁllment of
EV demand, known as SAEV , is shown in ﬁgure 4.3. In a similar manner to the standard
string assignment algorithm, this algorithm begins by disconnecting battery strings from
an EV if it has ﬁnished charging, making the battery available for other connections and
subsequently if an EV has started charging, assigning the highest available SOC battery
to that EV, making that battery unavailable until the charge has ended.
With battery strings assigned to the currently charging EVs, the decision tree splits based
on if 2 EVs are charging and hence there is only 1 unassigned battery string. If 2 EVs are
charging the last available battery string is assigned to the inverter to import or export
power based on the SOC of that string. If there is is only one or no EV charging, the
inverter is assigned to the lowest SOC string, with the PV system, if it is generating, being
assigned to the last available string if there is 1 EV charging or the string with the second
lowest SOC if no EV is charging.
This assignment algorithm prioritises importing energy into the system via the inverter to
best ensure there is enough energy stored within the three batteries to meet the expected
high demand. This is done so at the expense of PV harvesting as within this algorithm
if 2 EVs are charging the last battery string is assigned to the inverter, regardless of if
there is currently PV generation available. This is done as the expected PV generation is
low compared to the capacity of the inverter to import power from the grid, and as the
priority of this string assignment algorithm is to best prepare the system for a high EV
demand the potential of PV generation is sacriﬁced to provide a larger input of energy to
the batteries via the inverter.

The string assignment algorithm used when the highest priority objective of the system
is the harvesting of PV generation, and the expected conditions of the DC microgrid

This algorithm uses the same method of handling EVs seen in the other two algorithms,
disconnecting battery strings from EVs that have ﬁnished charging ﬁrst, and assigning the
available battery string with highest SOC to an EV that connects to a charger. If there is
PV production the PV system is assigned to the available string with the lowest SOC and,

4.1.5
Battery SOC Protection
To ensure the battery operates within a safe range and to prevent the faster battery
degradation that occurs at very high or low SOC values, upper and lower limits of 0.9
p.u. and 0.1 p.u. are implemented within the EMS. While doing so reduces the usable
capacity of the battery by 20% it provides an extra safety margin preventing over and
under charging the battery, helping to extend its lifetime. If a battery string has a SOC
equal to or beyond these limits, and the SOC is currently increasing for the upper limit or
decreasing for the lower, the protection is triggered and that battery string is disconnected.
How this is implemented within the model is shown in section 2.4.3.

4.1.6
Triggering of Active Assignment Algorithm
With the active string assignment algorithm selected the current optimum assignment of
string is known constantly. However, to prevent unnecessary switching the re-assignment
of battery strings using the currently active assignment algorithm is only carried out upon
speciﬁc triggering events. The events that will trigger the re-assignment of battery strings
are as follows
• An EV begins charging at one of the charging stations.
• An EV ﬁnishes charging at one of the charging stations.
• The PV system begins generating energy after having not been generating.
• The PV system stops generating energy after having been generating.
• Any of the battery strings reach the SOC protection limits.
• Every 10 minutes the SOC of the battery string connected to the inverter is checked.
If it falls within the current dead band of the inverter droop control and a battery
string that is not currently charging an EV has an SOC that is outside of this inverter
dead band, string re-assignment is carried out.
This prevents the unnecessary switching of battery strings, lowering the wear on system
components while ensuring the battery strings assigned in an optimal manor for completing
of system objectives when required.
Due to the multiple objectives with contradictory expectations for battery SOC outlined
at the beginning of this chapter, there are beneﬁts for the operation of the DC microgrid
to have a larger deviation between the SOC of the three battery strings. Having a battery
string with a high SOC ideal for EV charging and a battery string with a low SOC ideal

system fall within reasonable bounds.

as k, used throughout. The sensitivity testing was carried out using a range for each
scaling constant k, and then based on the results of this testing the value ultimately used
for each scaling constant was selected.

TESTING AND RESULTS
This chapter presents the testing carried out on the EMS described and implemented in
chapter 4. Firstly the performance metrics used to assess the success of the EMS are
presented followed by the EV and PV scenarios used throughout the testing. Finally
each of the tests, the results and a discussion on what these results mean are presented
individually.
5.1
Performance Metrics
Due to being the EMS designed in this project being a control system with many objectives,
both core and secondary, a wide range of performance metrics have to be considered when
assessing its performance. As such, these performance metrics can be separated into four
types:
• Core Objective Metrics - how well the EMS fulﬁlls its three core objectives as
outlined at the beginning of chapter 4.
• Energy Metrics - how independent the operation of the DC microgrid is from the
wider grid
• Eﬃciency Metrics - how well the EMS minimises losses within in the DC microgrid.
• Degradation Metrics - how well the EMS minimises degradation of system
components.
5.1.1
Core Objective Metrics
These three performance metrics measure the EMS’s success at fulﬁlling its three core
objectives, to fulﬁll EV charging needs, to harvest PV energy and to maximize revenues
generated by the DC microgrid.
EV Success Rate
The EV Success rate is the measure of the success of the DC microgrid at fulﬁlling the EV
charging needs of all vehicles that connect to the chargers. If an EV connected to one of
the chargers and made a request for energy that could not be completely fulﬁlled, the DC
microgrid and EMS have not fully completed this objective. It is deﬁned as

System Eﬃciency
Total eﬃciency is a very important metric of any system, measuring the ratio of the useful
work done by a system to the energy supplied to it. This can be expressed as the general
equation
η = Energy Output
Energy Input · 100%
(5.16)
However, as the Campus Bornholm DC Microgrid is a system that contains energy storage,
the deﬁnition of system eﬃciency needs to reﬂect this. It was considered to include the
energy present within the batteries at the start of a test period amongst the energy inputs,
and the energy present within the batteries at the end of a test period amongst the energy
outputs.
It was found however that doing so made the eﬃciency metric heavily dependent on the
energy throughput of the DC microgrid. As the energy stored in the batteries at the end
of each test was usually close to the energy stored at the start including these values as
separate energy inputs and outputs caused the total eﬃciency to increase disproportionately
when these input and output values were of a signiﬁcant size compared to the other energy
inputs and outputs of the system.
This would not be an issue when considering a longer period of time where the energy
stored in the batteries at the beginning and end of the test are insigniﬁcant compared to
the energy entering and leaving the system throughout the test. However as the majority
of the tests presented in this report take place over a single day with the stored energy
having a potentially large impact on the eﬃciency metric, an alternative deﬁnition was
proposed that considers the batteries only once within the eﬃciency equation as either an
net input or net output of energy. To this end, the value E∆store is deﬁned as the diﬀerence
between the energy stored in the batteries at the start and end of the testing period
E∆store = 3 · Ecap · (SOCave,end −SOCave,start)
(5.17)
where Ecap = 103.68 kWh, the capacity of one of the three battery strings, (SOCave,end is
the average SOC of the three batteries at the end of the test period and (SOCave,start is
the average SOC at the start of the test period. If the value of E∆store is positive, there is
more energy stored at the end of the test period than at the start and as such the batteries
are considered a net output of energy, and conversely if the value is negative the batteries
are considered a net input of energy. The other inputs of energy are considered to be the
PV energy harvested and energy imported through the inverter, and energy outputs the
energy delivered to EVs and exported through the inverter. As such, the system eﬃciency

Importing EV & PV scenarios into Simulink Model
The processing and analysis of EV and PV data described throughout chapter 3 was
carried out within the python programming language [60] with all data being stored in .csv
spreadsheet ﬁles, all with a sample rate of 1 sample per second and time stamps as the ﬁrst
table column. As such the EV and PV scenarios described throughout this section was
available across three .csv ﬁles, the ﬁrst consisting of the actual EV power consumed or PV
power generation measured in W, the second consisting of the predicted EV energy demand
in kWh or forecast PV generation in W for the current 30 minute forecast window, and
the third ﬁle containing the forecast conditions for the subsequent ﬁve 30 minute forecast
windows out to the 3 hour forecast scope, with the reduced accuracy of PV forecast as
described in section 3.1.4.
The .csv ﬁles containing the actual power consumption or generation, along with the ﬁles
containing the forecast for the current 30 minute forecast window, are imported into the
matlab work space and converted into a timeseries variable. This variable type allows
these values to be imported directly into the simulink model.
After importing the EV charging power into simulink model, the value is converted from
W to kW. To detect if a charge session beginning or ending with a variable charge power,
an set-reset ﬂip-ﬂop storage latch [71] is used. The set value of the ﬂip-ﬂop is equal to one
when the current EV charger power is greater than zero, and the reset value equal to one
if the EV charger power has been equal to zero for the last 30 seconds, indicating the EV
has disconnected. The output of this ﬂip-ﬂop latch is a binary value that indicates if an
EV is charging. If this binary value increases, the start of an EV charge is detected and
if this value decreases the end of an EV charge is detected. These are represented in the
values binary EVstart,1, EVstart,2, EVstop,1 & EVstop,2, which are combined to give a single
list that can be used as an input to the EMS.
The PV generation also needs to be converted from W to kW upon being imported into the
model, and the sign of the power reversed to conform with the notation that negative power

are shown in appendix A.1.
The most important result from these tests is that the EV fulﬁllment rate and PV harvesting
rate, EVsuccess & PVsuccess were both 100% for every test, meaning that in all of the basic
operation tests all EV charge was fulﬁlled and all PV generation harvested. This is the
EMS completely succeeding in its ﬁrst two core objectives. As for the third core objective,
the steady amount of proﬁt generated by the DC microgrid is mostly dictated by the
amount of EV fast charging that takes place, as this is the source of revenue with the

being insuﬃcient grid exports to match the cost.
In the majority of cases the DC microgrid imported very little power, with the connected
PV generation being more than suﬃcient to supply the EV demand. There is a signiﬁcant
surplus of energy generated in all but the low PV scenarios that requires exporting from
the DC microgrid to the distribution grid. The only tests with a negative grid balance,
where more power was imported than exported, were the low PV scenarios that contained
EV charging.

with the eﬃciency becoming lower in scenarios with less PV generation or EV demand,

CONCLUSIONS & FUTURE WORK
This chapter contains a brief summary of the work presented in this report and the
conclusions that can be drawn from the presented tests.
It then oﬀers a number of
proposals on how the presented work can be continued and further developed.
6.1
Conclusions
To limit ongoing global climate change a severe reduction in the emission of greenhouse
gasses is required. As the transport sector is a major contributor to these emissions the
electriﬁcation of this sector and the transition to electric vehicles is a core part of this
decarbonisation eﬀort. The improvement of EV charging infrastructure is required to
facilitate this transition. The focal point of this report is a DC microgrid located at
Campus Bornholm that aims to provide EV fast charging supplied by locally generated
renewable energy by integrating a energy storage system consisting of three reconﬁgurable
battery strings, PV generation, two EV fast chargers and an inverter connection to the
distribution grid.
This report proposes an energy management system to control the operation of this DC
microgrid. It utilises forecast PV production and predicted EV demand as inputs and
implements a system whereby the three core, and often conﬂicting, objectives of the EMS
are given weighted priorities by the DC microgrid operator. These forecast conditions and
priority weightings are used by the EMS to implement dynamic and adaptable control that
is split into two levels. The system level control dictates the connections between battery
strings and components by selecting one of three string assignment algorithms based on
the forecast PV and EV conditions and the objective priority weightings. The inverter
level control implements a designed dynamic droop to determine the power at which the
inverter imports or exports energy to or from the DC microgrid. A droop controller dictates
the inverter power based on the SOC of the connected battery, and the EMS continually
adjusts the characteristics of this droop controller, namely the reference SOC, upper and
lower dead bands, based on the forecast conditions and priority weightings.
As the proposed EMS utilises PV generation forecasting and the prediction of EV charging
demand in its control of the DC microgrid, methods to create this forecasting were developed.
Solar irradiance forecasts provided by Solcast and continually collected automatically via
an API were converted into forecasts for PV power generation using a PV panel simulink
model. Accuracy analysis comparing the PV forecasting to measured generation showed it
to be reasonably accurate however the period of time over which this comparison could be
made consisting of generally low PV production and due to the methods of forecasting
used by Solcast it is suspected that that the forecast accuracy would be higher in periods
of high generation.

6. Conclusions & Future Work
To give a prediction on when EV charging demand is likely to occur the recorded charge
data was examined and analysed to establish trends and patterns in when charges occur.
These trends were then converted into a statistical probability of a single kWh of EV
demand occurring within a particular 30 minute period across a day. Due to the small
amount of EV charging data available the trends identiﬁed using this method are not ideal
but they are suﬃcient to test the eﬀect of including EV demand prediction within the
EMS. This testing showed that including a value for predicted EV demand as an input
enables the EMS to implement more informed control and prepare the DC microgrid for
upcoming conditions. This perpetration enabled the EMS to fulﬁll periods of high EV
demand that it would otherwise have been unable to and improves the performance of the
DC microgrid.
The testing of the DC microgrid also showed that the energy generated by the connected
PV system was more than suﬃcient to supply the EV demand in the majority cases and
that the harvesting of PV generation is the largest contributing factor to increasing battery
aging mechanisms. Hence reducing the capacity of the connected PV system could increase
the lifetime of the DC microgrid without impacting its ability to perform its core objectives.
The proposed EMS performed well in all scenarios and when compared to the existing
EMS has noticeable improvements, particularly when tested in stressful scenarios where
the core objectives of the EMS are diﬃcult to fulﬁll completely. The dynamic nature of the
proposed EMS, along with its consideration of weighted priorities for the core objectives
implements a control system that can adapt to best fulﬁll the EMS objectives in situations
where it is limited by the capabilities of the DC microgrid.
This is best exempliﬁed in the tests carried out with the DC microgrid operating at a
reduced grid capacity, which show DC microgrid of similar design utilising the proposed
EMS could operate at a maximum grid capacity as low as 11 kW with almost no drop in
EV charging potential. This would enable DC fast charging of EVs at many locations that
would otherwise be unable to support such infrastructure.
The system of assigning the core objectives weighted priorities around which the EMS is
based enables the operator of the DC microgrid to easily enact changes to how the EMS
operates that result in meaningful diﬀerences in the performance of the DC microgrid.
These priority weightings, along with PV and EV forecasting, the tuning of the inverter
dynamic droop controller and the system constants create an EMS that has a large number
of control handles. These handles allow for additional control over the implementation of
the EMS, either direct control from the operator of the DC microgrid or the integration of
the EMS with other control elements or sources of data. This creates a standardised EMS
that can have its operation tailored to a speciﬁc DC microgrid and the preferences of its
operator.

Proposed Future work
The testing carried out on the EMS and the DC microgrid in this provide a deeper
understanding of the operation, capabilities and limitations of said DC microgrid. The
presented EMS increases the functionality and improves on the performance of the previous
designs, and can be utilised in further tests on the DC microgrid. This EMS has the

6. Conclusions & Future Work
potential to be further developed and integrated with other control systems to further
improve the performance, with the many control handles allowing it to be easily adapted
to fulﬁll more speciﬁc objectives within the DC microgrid. This section contains proposals
for how the works presented in this thesis can be further developed upon.
Optimising the sizing of the components in the DC microgrid can reduce the initial
investment cost required without compromising its capabilities. This thesis shows that a
DC microgrid with the proposed EMS operating at a reduced domestic grid connection
capacity can provide the same EV charging potential. Hence designing a similar DC
microgrid to operate at a reduced, domestic, grid capacity with similarly reduced inverter
and PV capacity and optimised battery sizing, controlled by the presented EMS could
oﬀer the same EV charging capabilities at a reduced investment cost.
The proposed EMS could be adapted speciﬁcally for the operation of such a re-sized DC
microgrid, re tuning the inverter dynamic droop control and to account for the reduced
inverter capacity and altering the battery string assignment algorithms to operate with
battery strings of a smaller capacity.
The priority weighting system of the EMS can be developed further to include the
minimisation of battery degradation as a quantiﬁable and controllable EMS objective could
result in an EMS that increases the lifespan of the batteries within the system it controls.
The accuracy analysis of the PV generation forecasting method described in section 3.1
should be repeated using forecast solar irradiance from throughout the year, as apposed
to a single month. To provide the data for this analysis, the API used to collect solar
irradiance forecast data continues to operate.
Similarly to further develop probability and trend identiﬁcation prediction of EV demand
presented in section 3.2 more EV charge data will need to be analysed to identify additional
trends and increase the accuracy of the trends already found. There is also the potential
for alternate methods for EV charge prediction to be integrated with the EMS. The
implementation of a neural network could provide the EMS with a more complete prediction
of EV demand [54]. Alternatively developing an API, similar to the one currently used
to gather solar forecast data, that extracts traﬃc data from map services such as google
maps [72], could provide a more proactive forecast of EV demand to the EMS.
