Abstract
Filamentous fungi­derived Single­Cell Protein, also known as mycoprotein, presents a
promising alternative to meet the escalating food demands projected for 2050. To en­
hance its competitiveness, the mycoprotein market seeks strategies to decrease produc­
tion costs, including the utilization of cost­effective carbon sources like molasses. Al­
though molasses has demonstrated its potential as a viable option, the considerable vari­
ability in its composition content remains a challenge. This study tries to understand the
interactions among key sugars from molasses (sucrose, glucose, and fructose) to eluci­
date their influence on biomass and protein yield. The objective is to find ways to mitigate
the impact of variations in composition within molasses, thus contributing to the advance­
ment of mycoprotein production with Aspergillus oryzae.
By using a combination of a central composite design and surface response methodol­
ogy, a modified two­factor interaction model was developed. Furthermore, the predictive
capability of the model was tested across a portion of the experimental design space in
terms of biomass productivity. While a complementary model was proposed for the pro­
tein response, its refinement is needed for improving its accuracy.
By using the biomass model, the impact of compositional variability in molasses on pro­
duction yield of was assessed. The investigation revealed that fluctuations in the compo­
sition of complex media can lead to a reduction of up to 38.5% in yield and a decrease
of 19.2% in productivity. Strategic interventions, such as supplementing the media with
alternative carbon sources like fructose and decreasing the concentration of molasses,
were identified as potential remedies.
Furthermore, the study shows the adverse consequences of an increment of glucose
composition in molasses. This adverse impact is attributed to biological factors, specifi­
cally carbon catabolic repression between glucose and sucrose. Notably, the proposed
model was validated using industrial molasses samples, showing that there are signifi­
cant interactions between biomass production and additional unknown compounds within
molasses. This insight open the doors for future experiments to underscore the most sig­
nificant components that affect protein and biomass growth when a complex media like
molasses is used.

1
Introduction
According to the United Nations, the world population is expected to raise by 2050 to 9.7
billion people [1]. Together with an expected income raise in the average population of
developing countries, a food demand increase of 50% from 2020 to 2050 is expected [2].
Moreover, a continuous change on the diet on the developing countries is causing that
the demand of meat­based food increases, expecting a total growth of around 70% [2][3].
The World Resources Institute shares that some technological solutions could have the
potential of closing this gap, for instance: the improvement of the efficiency of natural
resources, reduction of food waste, shifting diets of high meat consumers to other protein
alternatives, the increment of food production without expanding the agricultural land use,
decreasing the greenhouse emissions per calorie produced, among others [2]. One of the
solutions with high growth potential in this field is the use of Single­Cell Protein (SCP) as
a food source as a way to comply the demand of proteins in competition with animal and
plant based proteins in the market [4].
1.1
Single­Cell Protein and introduction to mycoprotein
Single­Cell Proteins (SCPs) refers to the controlled and scaled growth of microorganisms
for human or animal consumption. This term includes the production of crude or refined
protein originated from microbial cells (such as fungi, algae and bacteria) that can be
consumed as a whole or applied as a supplement [5]. Some of the advantages of SCP
versus other protein sources is that SCPs are easier to produce, the interval times of pro­
duction are lower (hours versus months), require relative smaller land use in comparison
to conventional agriculture, do not result in biodiversity losses and are more cost effec­
tive [4]. Additionally, a controlled SCP production is not prone to pests and diseases in
comparison with animal and plant production, where high economical losses exist due to
that reason [4].
Between all the Single­Cell Proteins that can be used for human consumption, the filamen­
tous fungi based SCP, also named mycoprotein, is capturing special attention in research
and food industry because of its capacity of the mycellium to mimic meat structure and
appearance (see Figure 1.1). It has been shown that the high capacity of mycoprotein
to look like meat decreases the acculturation gap between the consumer and the meat
alternatives, showing a clear advantage over other alternatives in the market [6][7]. Addi­
tionally, filamentous fungi SCPs have shown relevant nutrient content, for instance, high
protein, high availability of the essential amino acids and relevant portions of vitamins
and lipids [4]. Other advantages of filamentous fungi based SCPs are that are easier to
harvest, can grow at low water activities and have a higher ability to adapt at different
environmental and substrate conditions [4].

meat. It is observed that the mycoprotein in the market has a high protein content, but
almost a half lower content than beef, chicken and pork. Additionally, the current prices
of mycoprotein are comparable to beef showing a challenge of the mycoprotein industry
to overcome in the future.

1.2
The benchmark: Industrial production of mycoprotein
Marlow Foods was the first company to sell mycoprotein as a food alternative in the En­
glish market in 1985 with the name of Quorn. Today, Quorn is the most popular my­
coprotein meat alternative in the world having a capacity of producing 40,000 tones of
the product per year [15] . It is a Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) product and
is accepted by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in the United States. Also, it is
currently approved and sold in all the countries of the European Union, Taiwan, Canada,
South Africa, among others [22]. The facilities of Quorn have one of the biggest continu­
ous flow bioreactors in the world.
The airlift reactors that Quorn is using for growing the mycoprotein are 155 m3 in volume
and have the capacity of producing 7000 tones per year [22]. In practice, the reactor
works a as a loop where the different parameters are controlled around it. By taking
advantage of the 50m height of the reactor, in one bottom side of the loop a gaseous
nitrogen and oxygen source is placed. Because of the high hydrostatic pressure and
turbulence in that spot a liquid­gas phase mixture is created, causing excellent conditions
for oxygen transfer . At the same time, the addition of gas lowers the density, which
consequently results in the product floating and the liberation of part of the dissolved gas
and the produced CO2 (lower pressure at the top of the reactor causes that liberation).
Thanks to that differential pressure created by the difference of densities from the gas
source to the top of the fermenter, the product can circulate around the loop in cycles
of two minutes. The same airlift reactor has a heat exchanger that cools the system to
30°C, the pH is regulated to 6 with the same nitrogen source (ammonia) and the dilution
rate of the continuous reactor is around the 18.5 h−1. The fungus is always growing
at the maximum rate because the glucose used is always on excess [22][9]. Relevant
parameters from the Quorn fermentation process can be observed in Table 1.3:

Despite the manufacturing of Quorn has been developed for years, there are some issues
that the process is facing in the modern days. For instance, the production of high quan­
tities of ribunocleic acid, the treatment of derived process side­streams and the life­cycle
analysis that states that Quorn mycoprotein is comparable with chicken [23][7]. Other
relevant concern of the Quorn process is the carbon source that represents an important
cost for the company [23].
Quorn’s fermentation uses a highly refined starch­derived glucose for fermentation. De­
spite using alternative substrates was proposed in the past, there is evidence that the use
of different sugars can alter the protein content and biomass growth in Fusarium venena­
tum. In addition, the main concern of using alternative substrates with Fusarium venena­
tum is that the permission for selling Quorn is only granted under strict process conditions.
Moreover, it has been proved that other Fusarium species produce mycotoxins under cer­
tain carbon substrates [23]. Indeed, Fusarium venenatum produces tricothecenes in the
presence of alternative sugars such as sucrose, 1­kestose and nystose [23].
1.3
Key Criteria in the upstream production of mycoprotein
There are several control parameters that encompasses a food mycoprotein that is able to
be sold in the market. For instance, the appearance, texture, flavor, nucleic acid content
and safety parameters [9]. Some of those parameters are controlled by the downstream
process, quality assurance, strain selection, among others [9][22]. However, there are
some critical quality parameters that must be controlled from the design and execution of
the upstream process. Between those parameters are the biomass yield, titer, productiv­
ity, protein content, and fungi morphology [4][22].
• Biomass Yield: Mycoprotein is a product that is produced in high volumes and at low
costs. In other words, the cost of the substrate is highly significant in the total cost of
the product. For that reason, it is important to ”produce more using less”. Yield is the
ratio between the biomass produced over the sugar used for producing that quantity
of biomass [24]. Typically, the yield in mycoprotein production is bellow 0.5 (c­mol/c­
4
==PAGE:11==
mol) because the cell uses a high part of the carbon source in respiration [9]. The
yield is probably one of the most important criteria in this kind of bio­processes. As
a reference, the yield of the Quorn process is showed in Table 1.3. In single­cell
protein the yield can be measured in terms of wet or dry biomass. Wet biomass
is relevant in industry because the product is sold on a wet basis, however, the
water content of the cell could change in terms of the separation process (usually
filtration or centrifugation) [22]. For that reason, the dry weight yield is typically used
in research as it allows the comparison between experiments.
• Titer and productivity: Also known as product concentration, titer is the amount of
biomass present in a defined volume. This parameter is important to define the size
and cost of the downstream process. On the other hand, productivity is the amount
of biomass produced per amount of time and volume. The productivity concept
is essential for defining the size of the reactor and the dilution factor (in case of
continuous fermentation) [24].
• Protein content: As mycoprotein is often sold as an ”alternative protein” the protein
content per mass quantity of biomass is an essential parameter to control [22]. Usu­
ally, the protein content is measured by analytical methods of nitrogen such as the
Bradford and Kjeldahl methods [25]. Another method for analysing nitrogen content
in biomass is the Elemental Analysis (EA). EA is a quantitative analysis that de­
termines the amount of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen elements in a sample using
a high­temperature and high­oxygen combustion. In that process, the combustion
gases (CO2, H2O, N2 and NO) are quantified by a thermoconductivity analyser [26].
• Fungi morphology: In the submerged fermentation of filamentous fungi, the mycel­
lium can create two morphological structures: the dispersed and pellet structures
[9]. Having a dispersed structure is essential for the growth of mycoprotein because
those dispersed hypha are the ones that are mechanically treated for texturizing the
alternative meat [22]. Most of the fungal strains grow naturally on pellet structure
in SmF. Usually, the disperse morphology is dependant on different process and
environmental factors such as the presence of some compounds in the media, the
mixing technology, pH, growth phase and nutrient content in the media [9].
1.4
Alternative substrates and molasses
Despite most organisms are able to use a variety of carbon sources, glucose is the most
known carbon source in bio­processes, as it is rapidly and directly assimilated by the phos­
photransferase system (PTS) in most cases [27]. However, there are some concerns with
the use of glucose as carbon source such as the high use of arable lands, the competi­
tion with the food supply chain and the increment in costs that this demand implies [28].
Therefore, it is imperative to switch the current carbon sources to alternative sustainable
ones, encompassing agricultural and industrial residues.
Recent research efforts have been directed towards the bioproduction of mycoprotein
using alternative substrates, signifying a notable shift in focus [28]. Despite many agroin­
dustrial sidestreams could be used as carbon sources for mycoprotein, many of them
consist on solid lignocellulose materials that require a significant amount of pretreatment
before the hydrolyzed material could be applied on submerged fermentation. Neverthe­
less, there are some ”plant juices” from various food processes that contain available sug­
ars that do not need any intensive pretreatment [28]. Among those available are potato

production) and molasses [28].

Other important challenge is the high variability of sugars composition that has

1.5
Complex and variable substrates
The use of molasses has been a high success in ethanol production despite the men­
tioned substrate variability. That component variability has been overcome by the low
price of molasses and by the capacity of downstream processes to purify the alcohol [32].
However in the case of food mycoprotein production, the main actors for controlling pro­
tein and biomass production are in the upstream process conditions [22]. As mentioned
before, the type of substrate is highly significant for the final protein content and yield in
the mycoprotein processes [22]. Having a high and controlled protein content is essential
for selling alternative proteins in the market, so substrate variability has to be controlled
[23].
There have been different approaches for overcoming the effects of substrate variability
in complex media. While some state that the selection and the metabolic construction of
robust cell factories is essential [33], other authors recommend to purify or pretreat the
complex substrate before use [34][35]. The methodology employed in this study involves
comprehending the interactions among significant components. This approach aims to
mitigate substrate variations even before the fermentation starts [33][35]. There are sev­
eral ways for understanding the interactions of the media components in the fermenta­
tion. One of them is the Design of Experiments that uses a mathematical and statistical
approach for finding correlations between independent and dependent variables, in that
way create a model that could be further used for optimization [36]. Diverse studies have
use the experimental design approach for optimizing biomass production of SCP with a
molasses sample [8][11][12]. However, to the best of my knowledge, a study that tries
to understand the media components interaction as a way to optimize and overcome the
possible substrate variability in molasses has not been done.
In order to execute the experiments the selection of a robust strain (different than Fusar­
ium Venenatum), safe and capable to grow in molasses was necessary. In the next para­
graphs a short summary and justification of the selected strain is showed.
1.6
About the selected organism
Aspergillus oryzae is an aerobic filamentous fungi that is well known for its high capacity of
consuming different kinds of carbohydrates and polipeptides by the production of enzymes
[37]. It has a long history of safe use in food and fermentation processes, particularly in
traditional Asian foods like soy sauce and koji [37]. It is Generally Recognized as Safe
(GRAS) by the Food and Drug Administration in the United States, due to its appeal for
food production [38].
Other relevant advantage of this strain versus the Quorn strain is that the industrial As­
pergillus oryzae strains are not able to produce high toxic mycotoxins such as aflatoxins,
cyclopiazonic acid and maltoryzine, converting it a more robust strain for substrate vari­
ability in food production [37]. In addition, different reports state that Aspergillus oryzae
is able to grow in a variety of substrates yielding dry biomass protein contents between
20% to 62% [39]. Also, Aspergillus oryzae is capable to grow in the desired disperse
morphology at certain fermentation conditions (being influenced by pH, inoculum level,
medium composition and agitation) [40].

1.7
Summary
As it has seen in the last paragraphs, one of the main challenges on the production and
sales of mycoprotein is to reduce production costs. Being the current carbon substrate
a significant cost for this industry, the proposal of using cheaper carbon sources as mo­
lasses has been explained. In the current work, the interaction between the three main
sugars of molasses (sucrose, glucose and fructose) in the biomass and protein production
of Aspergillus oryzae is studied. The intention of understanding the interaction between
those sugars is to understand the effect of the variability between batches of molasses,
as well as finding optimal mediums and propose a way for neutralize those effects. The
main tool for executing the experimentation will be using a Design of Experiments and
Response Surface Methodology for the statistical analysis.

2
Methodology
As mentioned in the introduction, the main objective of the current work is to elaborate a
model that predicts the biomass and protein content of the fermentation based on the three
carbon source interaction. The idea of that model is to be used as a way for overcom­
ing the variations of a multicomponent carbon source as molasses. In the next diagram
(Figure 2.1) an overview of the followed process is showed:
Figure 2.1: Summary of the followed process steps.
First, a design of experiments based on surface response methodology of the three sug­
ars (sucrose, glucose, fructose) was made. For each of the executed experiments the
biomass, dry biomass and protein content were measured as dependent variables. The
construction of a surface response model was executed afterwards. After the verifica­
tion and detection of a relevant point the synthetic optimal experiments were transferred
to molasses experiments to evaluate the applicability of the current model. An analysis
of the obtained information, including the reason of the observed interactions, molasses
results and application in the molasses variability are also discussed. The details of the
design and the experimentation are explained in the following sections.

2.1
Design of Experiment
Based on the three main carbon sources found in molasses (sucrose, glucose and fruc­

2.2
Organism and spore suspension
The microorganism used for this study was Aspergillus oryzae DSM 1147, obtained form
the Leibniz Institute in Germany. The obtained freeze dried culture was activated with
sterile water and inoculated in potato dextrose agar plates. After that, the culture was
grown for six days at 30°C and then, maintained in the same agar at 4°C.
11
==PAGE:18==
The cells for inoculating the shake flask experiments were obtained by growing the mi­
croorganism in potato dextrose agar plates for 5 days at 30°C. After that, the culture was
flooded in 20 mL of a sterile solution of Tween 80 (2 % v/v) and shaken until observing that
the spores were suspended in the liquid. The spore concentration was measured using
a Neubauer chamber. Usual spore concentrations of the suspensions were between the
3X107 to 6X107 CFU/mL. After that, the necessary suspension volume was added to the
shake flasks to obtain a spore concentration of 1X106 CFU/mL in the media.
2.3
Shake flask experiments
All the experiments were executed in 2L shake flask with 400mL of culture media. For this
study the nutrient composition of the culture media was based on experiments from Ottoni
(2012) [42]. The nutrient composition was NaNO3 (10g/L), KH2PO4 (2g/L), MgSO4:7H2O
(0.5g/L), MnCl2:4H2O (0.3g/L), FeSO4:7H2O (0.0.01g/L) at pH 4.5. The pH was corrected
by using 1M solutions of sulfuric acid and sodium hydroxide. The carbon source type and
concentration was variable, depending on the experiment. After the media sterilization
(121°C, 20min) and inoculation, all the shake experiments were fermented at 30°C, 180
RPM for 72 hours.
2.4
Wet and dry biomass concentration
The obtained biomass at the end of each shake flask experiment was separated from
the media by vacuum filtration with a cotton filter (Sartorius, 12 µm –15 µm). The total
biomass was rinsed with at least 800mL of distilled water and filtered again to avoid the
interference of the media components during the biomass analysis. The weight of that
filtered biomass was defined as ”wet biomass”.
For obtaining the dry biomass, the wet biomass was frozen at ­18°C for at least 48 hours.
After that, the samples were freeze dried at 0.001mbar and 20°C for 72 hours.
2.5
Elemental and protein analysis
The carbon and nitrogen weight content ratio of the dry biomass was measured by using
an Elemental Analyzer (FlashSmart™Elemental Analyzer, ThermoScientific at DTU Bio­
engineering). The carrier gas for the analysis was helium (flow 140mL/min) and the gas for
combustion was oxygen (flow 250mL/min) . The left and right furnaces had a temperature
of 950°C and 530°C respectively. The standard component used for the analysis was 2,5­
Bis(5­tert­butyl­2­benzo­oxazol­2­yl) thiophene (BBOT) based on the recommendations
for similar biomass compounds in the equipment manual [43]. The EagerSmart™Data
Handling Software was used for determining the weight composition of both elements.
The protein content of the dry biomass was calculated by multiplying the weight nitrogen
ratio (N %) times the factor 6.25 [44][45].
2.6
Molasses characterization
An industrial sample of beet molasses were donated by Nordzucker AG (Nykøbing Falster,
Denmark). The concentrations of the three main sugars of molasses (sucrose, glucose
and fructose) were determined by High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC). The

samples were isocratically separated at 0.4 mL/min, using a cation exchange resin­ col­
umn (Bio­Rad Aminex HPX 87H, 300x 7.8 mm), phosphoric acid 0.005 N as eluent at 25°C
and detected using a refractive index detector (RI 830) [46]. For the samples preparation,
two dilutions of molasses were prepared (20 g/L and 40 g/L) and five standard solutions
per compound were prepared based on the expected molasses dilution composition [47]:
sucrose (1, 2, 3, 10 and 15 g/L), glucose (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8 and 1 g/L) and fructose (0.05,
0.1, 0.2, 0.4 and 0.8 g/L). The RI area bellow the reference peaks were compared based

analysis as independent values instead of the compositions in percentages and a total
sugar concentration as defined in the initial design (Table 2.1). This validation was made
by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient (3.1) between all the design variables
and run number.

3.3
Wet Biomass
The average water content in wet biomass obtained was 93.06% with a relative standard
deviation of 1.04%. Similar water contents of 87% and 75% have been reported for filtered
and centrifuged A. oryzae biomass, respectively [49][46]. The reported results, showed
that the water content of wet biomass is highly dependant on the mechanical treatment
that was used for its separation. In our experiments, variations in the water content were
observed during the execution of the experiment specially between the filtration of high
and low biomass content samples. It was observed that high biomass samples tend to
15
==PAGE:22==
clog the filter more, so the applied pressure by the vacuum filtering process was lower. In
the same way, low biomass samples tended to be more pressurized by the vacuum force
so the samples liberated more water. This mechanical effect can be shown numerically
by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient (3.1) between the moisture content and
wet biomass of 0.594 (moderately positive).
Figure 3.2: The plot shows that low wet biomass samples tended to have low water con­
tents due to the mechanical separation process.
Because of the effect of the separation process in the wet biomass value, it was decided
that the surface response methodology was going to be executed in terms of the dry
weight data. However, it is important to state that wet biomass is an important indicator
to consider. Wet biomass is a direct and fast way for measuring the performance of the
SCP while the fermentation is running [22].
3.4
General observations in shake flask experiments
Before starting with the statistical analysis of the results it is important to mention some
factors that were observed during the execution of the experiments. First, it is relevant to
state that some adhered biomass in the internal walls of the shake flasks was observed.
That factor could have effects in the formation of biofilm, mixing , nutrient and oxygen
availability in each experiment causing variations inside the biomass and protein content.
These ”biomass adhesions” have been reported in other shake flask experiments of fila­
mentous fungi and it is mostly attributed to the formation of foams and to the smoothness
of the walls of the container [51]. Actions like decreasing the the medium volume in the
shake flask, and the use of antifoamers could help to reduce this adherence in future
experiments.

3.6
Discussion on the obtained dry weight response
The behaviour of the prediction model can be explained by the metabolic regulation of
the sugars consumption. Aspergillus oryzae metabolizes the sucrose by two enzymes:
beta­fructofuranosidase and alpha­glucosidase [52]. Those enzymes are produced by the
transcription of the genes SucA and agdA and consequently, regulated by the transcription
factors InuR and AmyR, respectively.
The main regulation player in the interaction between sugars is the regulatory mechanism
of the carbon catabolite repression (CCR). CCR is a regulatory mechanism that prioritizes
the utilization of glucose over less preferred carbon sources [53]. This repression is me­
diated by transcription factors like CreA that are induced in the presence of glucose (more
than 10mg/L) [54]. It has been demonstrated CreA acts as a repressor to the AmyR and
InuR transcription factors in filamentous fungi like Aspergillus oryzae and Aspergillus niger
[55][56]. That repression system explains the negative interaction that it is observed be­
tween the mixture of sucrose and glucose. This repression has been observed in other
complex medias such as beet pulp and other molasses [55] [56].
It has been reported that fructose is considered a non­repressive carbon source because
is not able to activate the CCR [57]. That explains that when sucrose and fructose are
mixed no interactions are observed. However, that does not explain the negative interac­
tions between glucose and fructose. A carbon source competition that results in a higher
affinity for glucose than sucrose has been reported in other filamentous fungi [52][55].
One reason of the higher affinity could be explained by the higher specificity of the sugar
transporters to glucose than fructose [58]. It has been documented in Aspergillus niger
that in the presence of mixtures of glucose with other monomeric sugars (as fructose and
xylose), there is a preference for glucose that is caused by the transporter system more
than the CCR system [59].

The capacity of the two factor interaction model (equation 3.5) to mimic the interaction
of the sugars when molasses are used is going to be evaluated in this section. The
main hypothesis is that the calculated interactions on the model are strong enough for
being replicated in non­pure media (as molasses). As a first attempt, two experiments
were executed and then replicated using molasses as main carbon source. The first
experiment consisted on having just sucrose as carbon source. The second experiment
was composed by replicating the optimal point found in sections 3.7 and 3.7 (sucrose=7.08
g/L, glucose=2.31 g/L, fructose=18.46 g/L). The third and fourth experiments replicate the
compositions of the first two, but this time using molasses as main carbon source. In order
to make all the experiments comparable, the initial total sugar carbon mole composition
was maintained constant in all the experiments (total sugar composition was 0.94 c­mol/L).
A comparison on the composition of the four mentioned experiments is showed in Figure
3.16.
Figure 3.16: Carbon source composition (c­mol/L) of the four experiments used to explore
the model applicability in complex media.
It is observed in Figure 3.16 that for the used molasses experiments, the total sucrose
composition was shifted for the relative concentration of the same sugar in molasses. In
the case of the optimal composition experiment with molasses, pure glucose and fructose
was supplemented to achieve the desired concentration. All the experiments were exe­
cuted in triplicates, by reproducing the shake flasks methodology used for the rest of the
experiments in this work and described in section 2.

It is relevant to mention that the productivity and yield results showed in Figure 3.17 are
proportional because in all cases the carbon mole concentrations and biomass carbon
mole weights were constant. By comparing the results, it is shown that in the case of
the pure sugars experiments the optimal concentration resulted in a higher productivity
and yield than the pure sucrose experiment. This was an expected result and it has been
proved around this work during the discussion and generation of the models. However,
when comparing the molasses experiments versus the pure sugars it is observed that the
molasses had a better performance in all the cases. And even more, it seems that the
molasses in the high sucrose experiments performed better than the optimal concentra­
tion ones. It is believed that the reason of this behaviour is that the molasses have other
non­considered components that are interacting positively to the production of biomass
in the system. In that way, the molasses­high sucrose experiment would have more con­
centration of those non­considered components than the optimal experiments because
the relative amount of molasses in each one.
It is probably that the non­considered components are amino­acids (see Table 1.4) or a
kind of organic acid. It has been proved that also organic nitrogen sources have a pos­
itive significance in the biomass production of mycoprotein [12][64]. It is recommended
to analyse the other composition of the other components of the molasses or at least the
nitrogen content of them (via Kjeldhal or by Elemental Analysis) to verify the presence
and quantity of those nitrogen components. An even better future proposal is to elabo­
rate a Plackett­Burmann design with all the possible components in molasses. The two
level factorial Plackett­Burmann design can be used for determining which are the most
significant components that affect the biomass growth in the experiments [36]. After that,
a similar design of experiments approach than the used in this work can be executed.

4
Conclusion
In the present work, an evaluation of the effect of the variable composition of molasses
in production of mycoprotein was made. This effect was studied by modeling and under­
standing the interaction of the three most common carbon sources in molasses (sucrose,
glucose and fructose). The use of a Central Composite Composite design allowed to ob­
tain a significant response of biomass productivity with a minimum number of laboratory
experiments (21). A considerable variation of the duplicates was observed experimentally
because the biomass was not able to grow evenly inside the shake flasks experiments. It
is recommended to make some modifications in the experimentation such as decreasing
the amount of medium per experiment, decrease the agitation rate of the incubator and
the use of antifoamers. Nevertheless, the obtained information was enough for creating
a model that predicts the produced biomass based on the three sugar concentrations.
The generated two factor interaction model predicted well the biomass productivity at con­
siderable sugar concentration levels. However, the model needs some prediction correc­
tions specially when the sugar concentrations are low. Additionally, an analysis of the
significant terms and their coefficients helped to understand the interaction of the three
sugars during their consumption by Aspergillus oryzae. It seems that that their consump­
tion is ruled by the presence of glucose in the media. In the case of glucose with sucrose,
possible a carbon catabolite repression is causing the negative observed interactions. At
the same time, a competition transport and enzymatic competition that has preference to
glucose over fructose could be the one that causes a negative interaction when the two
sugars are present in the medium. However, it would be interesting to observe if there is
a carbon catabolite repression also between glucose and fructose.
The total crude protein content response of the three sugars was also studied. When
executing the analysis of variance, it seems that only one model resulted significant. A
diagnosis of the model was executed. In it, a low accuracy and reliability on the prediction
of the response was observed. Nevertheless, the construction of the model showed the
significance that glucose has in the protein content of SCP. However, it was not possible
to understand the effect of the other sugars and their interactions in the biomass protein
content with the obtained data. A future design of experiments with a full factorial design
(with more number of experiments) is recommended for understanding that relation.
The effect of the composition variability in complex media was studied by the elaboration
of three scenarios. In that exercises, the worst and best variability cases were studied in
terms of the reported variation limits in molasses. The results offered an understanding
on the effect that increments in glucose together with sucrose could have in the biomass
yield (being able to decrease the yield down 38.5% and the productivity 19.2% of its initial
value). It is relevant to mention that this variations are based on the extreme cases, so
an analysis of the variation in each process or geographical zone should also be made.
This information is valuable for understanding that the most harmful variation was the
increment of glucose and open the doors to future creations of quality indicators in SCP
that uses molasses.
Finally, a comparison of the performance of the biomass yield and productivity when the
organism was grown in pure sugars versus molasses was executed. Surprisingly it seems
that there were other unknown components that positively interacted in the formation of
more biomass when the molasses were used. An elaboration of a Plackett­Burman design

of experiments is recommended for identifying the components that affect the production
of biomass and crude protein the most. After that, a similar approach of understanding
the interaction between the detected most significant compounds is recommended.

Filamentous fungi­derived single­cell protein, or mycoprotein, presents a promising alternative to
meet the escalating food demands projected for 2050. To enhance its competitiveness, the mycopro­
tein market seeks strategies to decrease production costs, including the utilization of cost­effective
carbon sources like molasses. Although molasses has demonstrated its potential as a viable option,
the considerable variability in its composition content remains a challenge. This study tries to un­
derstand the interactions among key molasses sugars (sucrose, glucose, and fructose) to elucidate
their influence on biomass and protein yield. The objective is to find ways to mitigate the impact
of compositional variations within molasses, thus contributing to the advancement of mycoprotein
production with Aspergillus oryzae.
