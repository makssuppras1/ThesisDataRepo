Abstract
This study investigates the noise differences between electric vehicles (EVs) and internal
combustion engine vehicles (ICEs) at low speeds, focusing on both objective measurements and
subjective evaluations. Pass-by experiments were conducted under controlled conditions, with
noise levels analyzed across various speeds and correlated to frequency content. Listening tests
were designed to evaluate perceived annoyance, loudness, and other psychoacoustic descriptors
using sound recordings from the pass-by experiments.
Results show that EVs emit lower overall noise levels compared to ICEs, with a mean dif-
ference of 1.15 dB(A) in LAFMax. However, this difference diminishes when diesel cars are
excluded, regardless of speed. Psychoacoustic evaluations reveal a disconnect between mea-
sured loudness and subjective annoyance, emphasizing the importance of integrating perceptual
characteristics into noise regulation frameworks.
The study highlights the limitations of current ISO standards for low-speed noise mea-
surements and suggests methodological adaptations for more accurate and comparable results.
Insights from this research can inform urban noise management strategies and support the de-
velopment of AVAS systems that balance safety with acoustic comfort.

1
Introduction
In recent decades it has become increasingly recognized that traffic noise not only constitutes
a problematic contributor to sleep disturbance and stress, but additionally presents societal
health implications such as contributing to cardiovascular diseases, Alzheimer’s disease, cancer
and diabetes (Barregard, Bonde, & Öhrström, 2009; Jephcote et al., 2023; Sørensen et al., 2013).
Research that establish these connections involves comprehensive approaches that includes using
models to map estimated noise exposure levels by integration of traffic volume, vehicle types,
road layouts and geographic factors. The models are then cross-examined against health data
analysis from hospital records and long-term, large-scale cohort studies, controlling for factors
like air pollution and pre-existing health conditions (Engelmann et al., 2023).
The results underscore the need for action towards regulation of noise in areas with heavy traffic
and nearby residents.
Simultaneously, an increasing emphasis on sustainability is happening worldwide, and con-
sidering the World Health Organization (WHO)’s sustainable development goals, it can be ar-
gued that sustainable transportation can have a positive impact on several of these; in particular
number 3, 7, 9 and 11 which regards health, renewable energy, infrastructure and sustainable
cities (United Nations, 2024).
While there are several approaches to sustainable transportation, Electric Vehicles (EV)s
presents as one of the cornerstones due to the widespread impact they will have on a global
scale. The main reasons most usually listed as the environmental benefits of electric vehicles
are; Reduced greenhouse gas emissions based on zero tailpipe emissions, improved air quality
due to less emission of polluting particles, conservation of finite natural resources, energy ef-
ficiency based on high conversion from grid to wheels, of energy to power, and reduced noise
pollution based on the lack of an engine. These benefits are supported by reports from the In-
ternational Energy Agency (IEA) and WHO (International Energy Agency, 2023; World Health
Organization, 2018).
That EVs inherently reduces traffic noise is however mostly an assumption. Current knowl-
edge about road noise coming specifically from electric cars is very limited since structured mea-
surements and analysis are not readily available (Teknik og Miljøforvaltningen, 2024). There
are assumptions about the positive impact it will have on environmental noise levels in traffic
zones with low speed limits when the current fleet of cars are eventually exchanged for a larger
percentage of electric cars but this is not substantiated due to the lack of verifying data.
1.1
Known noise parameters
Ever since the battery capacity of EVs has increased and optimized mileage opportunities, their
popularity have gained traction and sales have started going up noticeably over the last few
years as illustrated in figure 1 where data from Statistics Denmark show that the number of
EVs in Denmark have tripled in the past two years (Danmarks Statistik, 2024).
1
==PAGE:7==
Figure 1: Stock of motor vehicles in Denmark from 2022 - 2024 taken from Danmarks

The relative recent increase in demand has resulted in more research funded by both car man-
ufacturers and public authorities concerned with user experience, safety and health (Alanazi,
2023; Volkswagen Group, 2022). But even before the research started focusing on internal and
external noise emission from EVs specifically, the existing research on external sound emission
from vehicles with Internal Combustion Engine (ICE)s could conclude that noise coming from
the friction between tires and the ground surface, is in itself dominating the noise emission levels
of a car when it reaches and is going at a certain speed. The exact speed varies depending on
multiple parameters such as type of tires, type of surface, or if the car is accelerating or moving
at a stable pace, but the results often mentioned are speeds in the range of approximately 35
- 40 km/h and the literature determines that tire/road noise is always dominating the overall
sound from 50 km/h (Ling, Yu, Sun, Sun, & Xu, 2021; Sandberg & Ejsmont, 2002). Adding
to this, there appears to be consensus that heavier vehicles and larger area of contact between
tires and surface correlates with increased noise emission due to greater tire wear and friction.
Because of the battery, electric cars are heavier than conventional fuel-based cars and their tires
wider and/or larger in diameter (Schweizer et al., 2023). Finally, EVs require a noise emitting
part which is not relevant for ICEs, known in the (European Union (EU)) as Acoustic Vehicle
Alertness System (AVAS). It is mandatory that this system is actively emitting synthetically
added sounds during forwards or backwards movement until and including speeds of 20 km/h
(United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE), 2021).
In conclusion, while there will be reduced noise emissions from EVs when solely considering
engine versus battery, the extent of a significant difference between EVs and ICEs might not

hold true in all scenarios.
1.2
Gaps in literature
A noticeable gap exists in the literature regarding external noise emissions from vehicles oper-
ating at speeds below 50 km/h, particularly concerning the comparative analysis of EVs and
ICE vehicles.
The current research predominantly focuses on high-speed traffic noise, driven by the well-
established correlation between elevated sound levels and adverse health effects.
This knowledge gap is particularly relevant given two concurrent trends in urban trans-
portation: the rapid electrification of vehicle fleets and the growing implementation of low-
speed zones in city centers as part of sustainable urban planning initiatives like ’København
Ned i Fart’ (’Copenhagen Slows Down’). The introduction and evolution of AVAS regulations
adds another layer of complexity to the urban soundscape, yet our understanding of its impact
remains limited.
These changes in urban mobility patterns and vehicle technology raise important questions
about the continued applicability of existing traffic noise models. As cities work to develop
evidence-based noise policies and effective urban soundscaping strategies, there is a need to
address this gap and better understand the acoustic impact of EVs operating at low speeds.
1.3
Research objectives
The goal of this study is to conduct a small case study revolving around structured measure-
ments and analysis of road noise induced by both electric and conventional passenger cars driving
at low speeds. The aim is thereby to provide insights to some of the unknown noise differences
between the two classifications of cars and if possible determine whether the assumption about
lower noise levels from electric cars holds true. Additionally, recordings from the measurements
will be used to explore potential differences in subjective annoyance from the various noise.
The primary research questions are
1. How do the objective noise levels of electric cars compare to conventional cars at low
speeds in a a controlled environment?
2. How do people perceive the noise annoyance generated by electric cars compared to con-
ventional cars at low speeds?
3. Is there value in making extensions to current International Organization for Standard-
ization (ISO) standards regarding noise emissions to include standardized measurements
of slow moving vehicles?

Background
2.1
Standards and regulations
An observation of what could seem to cause a challenge in the recent and current research, is
the lack of a structured method that takes electric cars and/or low speeds into account when
conducting measurements.
ISO standards are globally recognized guidelines developed to ensure quality, safety, effi-
ciency, and uniformity in utilized methods across various industries and sectors. The primary
purpose of ISO standards is to provide a common framework for businesses and organizations
to follow, ensuring that products, services, and systems meet consistent performance criteria
(International Organization for Standardization, 2024). While ISO standards benefit from be-
ing used to harmonize methods on a global scale, it happens that EU directives or national
regulations are used instead, either for legal reasons or due to specificity that might not be the
focus of existing ISO standards.
2.1.1
Pass-by methods
Pass-by methods refer to standardized techniques for measuring noise emitted by a vehicle as it
drives past a specific location where sound measurements are conducted. Several ISO standards
includes different methods of pass-by measurements for different purposes. These methods are
commonly known as: the Statistical Pass-By (SPB) method from ISO 11819-1, the Controlled
Pass-By (CPB) method from ISO 362-1 and the Coast-By (CB) method from ISO 13325 (In-
ternational Organization for Standardization, 2019, 2022a, 2023). A French standard called NF
S31-119-2 is also citepd in some literature as a version of CPB, but this has not been available for
this study (Ling et al., 2021; Moreno et al., 2023). What all of these methods have in common, is
that they are intended for speeds from 45 km/h and up, with microphones placed at a distance
of 7.5 meters from the center of the measured lane. By having cars move at lower speeds while
keeping the microphone distance as required, more of the external sound from the vehicle will be
masked by the background noise of the environment if the measurements are done outdoors in
natural surroundings. Especially for EVs, the larger distance can invalidate the measurements
by not fulfilling additional requirements of the standard, specifying that there shall be at least
10 dB(A) difference between the background noise and the maximum measured sound level of
the quietest vehicle pass-by (International Organization for Standardization, 2023). The Close
Proximity Method (CPX) method from ISO 11819-2 is, like the name suggests, in contrast used
for measurements at very close proximity to the tires. This however, limits the analysis to be
concerned with tire/road-noise only (International Organization for Standardization, 2017b).
Since neither electric cars nor low speeds are accounted for in the requirements and methods
of the various pass-by methods, the closest thing to a standardized process that researchers
4
==PAGE:10==
worldwide currently have, is to choose one of the existing methods and adapt it accordingly.
The consequence is that the adaptations are very individualized and thereby loose their uniform
trades and comparability to some extent. An alternative approach for using a method directed
specifically at electric cars and low speed, would be to use the test regiment from the EU
directive on AVAS. In this, a type of CPB is used where the microphone(s) is/are placed at
distances of only 2 meters from the vehicle midline and the measured speeds are only for 10
and 20 km/h (United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE), 2017). While a
shorter microphone distance is necessary for EVs moving at low speeds, this method possess
limitations of its own, especially for having the option of more speed variations without the risk
of compromising the equipment.
2.1.2
EU directive on AVAS
The content of United Nations (UN) Regulation No. 138 was initially proposed in UN Reg-
ulation No. 540/2014 and entered into force in 2017. It concerns "... the approval of Quiet
Road Transport Vehicles with regard to their reduced audibility" and the resulting installment
of AVAS has been a legal requirement in all European EVs since September 2021. Revisions
over the years include changes to specifications of both minimum and maximum sound level
values within a range of frequency bands and since 2022, the driver has been prohibited from
"pausing" the system, which essentially means having the capability to turn AVAS off (United
Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE), 2021).
For the focus of this study, a particularly curious phrase from the last amendment made
to Regulation 540/2014 in 2017 is found in Annex 8, section 2.3 (a) and states that "The
sound to be generated by the AVAS shall be a continuous sound that provides information to the
pedestrians and other road users of a vehicle in operation. The sound should be easily indicative
of vehicle behaviour and should sound similar to the sound of a vehicle of the same category
equipped with an internal combustion engine."(The European Commission, 2017). Though this
point is vague enough to allow for interpretation of what makes something sound "similar", it
fundamentally determines that it should theoretically be possible to detect similarities of some
kind in the sound emission from EVs and ICEs moving at low speeds.
2.1.3
Annoyance and loudness
In addition to the standards concerned with methods for objective measurements, others are di-
rected at standardized testing of subjective parameters such as ISO 15666 for assessment of noise
annoyance and ISO 16832 for loudness scaling (International Organization for Standardization,
2006, 2021). Finally, there are standards where overlap exist as they are used as reference for
both objective measurements and subsequent subjective assessment. This is the case for ISO
1996 that provides a framework for environmental noise measurement and assessment of the
measured data (International Organization for Standardization, 2017a). In Denmark, regula-
tion MST 6/1984 is the national counterpart used for regulatory compliance of noise control
5
==PAGE:11==
(Miljøstyrelsen, 1984). The regulation parts relevant to this study are the considerations of
annoyance caused by tonal noise content.
2.2
Included cars
While the study focus is on the differences between conventional and electric cars, it was con-
sidered whether Hybrid Electric Vehicles (HEV) and Fuel Cell Vehicle (FCV) were relevant to
include as well.
There are two kinds of hybrid cars. Both are using their internal combustion engine as the
main source of propulsion while the electric battery acts as an additional aid, but for what is
known as a Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle (PHEV), the battery holds a larger electric capacity
and can be charged in the same way as a standard EV. For this type of HEV it is also possible
to force the car to drive on electricity only though to a more limited extent speed and distance
wise compared to a standard EV. Since the non-plugin hybrid can generally not be forced to
use only one or the other type of propulsion system for generating movement, they were not
useful to include in this study where distinguishing between the two is of importance. It was
determined to include PHEVs if they were capable of being forced to drive on electricity only up
to and including a speed of 50 km/h. As for FCVs, the internal powertrain layout is so different
from EVs that they would have had to be considered a category on their own. Because of how
marginal the representation of this vehicle type is, it did not seem relevant to include it.
Both petrol and diesel cars are included in the category of ICE despite known differences
in their overall noise emissions. The reason for this is firstly that they are still both utilizing
internal combustion engines as their means of propulsion, and secondly because it presented
as a good opportunity to validate the study part concerning subjective perceptions against
expectations based on results from literature on this topic.
2.3
Acoustic modeling of vehicle noise
When analyzing vehicle noise, it is essential to consider how the vehicle and its components
are conceptualized as acoustic sources. Two commonly applied models in acoustics are point
sources and line sources, both of which serve as approximations of real-world conditions.
A point source represents a single, localized origin of sound, such as an engine, AVAS, or
a specific contact point between tire and surface. This model assumes that the sound radiates
isotropically in all directions, simplifying analysis of localized noise contributions. In contrast, a
line source treats the sound as being spread along a continuous line, and is often used to model
vehicles in motion or noise sources distributed across the dimensions of the car, like tire noise
combined with engine noise.
The choice of model depends on the scenario. At low speeds or for individual vehicles in con-
trolled environments, the point source model is often sufficient, as individual noise components
like propulsion, tire/road noise or AVAS can be distinctly measured and analyzed. Conversely,
6
==PAGE:12==
the line source model is more suitable for higher-speed scenarios or traffic noise, where multiple
sources blend into a pattern distributed along the vehicle’s length.
In traffic noise modeling for urban areas, line source models are typically applied to represent
roads as continuous noise sources. This approach simplifies the prediction of noise exposure
across broader areas. However, for controlled experiments like the pass-by measurements in
this study, where single vehicles are analyzed at varying speeds, the noise emission aligns more
closely with a point source model.
This distinction is particularly relevant when comparing EVs and ICEs. While ICEs often
exhibit more distributed noise sources due to contributions from the engine, exhaust, and tires,
EVs might behave more like point sources at low speeds, where AVAS would be expected to
dominate. At higher speeds, both vehicle types increasingly resemble line sources as tire/road
interaction becomes the primary noise contributor.
For the sake of repeatability and ability to compare vehicles with varying specifications, this
study focuses on measurements of vehicles driving at steady pace.
2.4
Psychoacoustics and perception
The purpose of including the aspect of perception in this study, is that objective measures
correlates poorly with auditory perception and noise annoyance. Results from objective mea-
surements are mainly useful as descriptors because of all the psycho-acoustic research that have
bridged our understanding between the physical stimuli and the experiences which typically
follow from this. A good example is loudness perception which is both influenced by frequency
content and if the onset is presented gradually or impulsive. A sound characteristic like loudness
is perceptual, meaning that how it is perceived correlates with a physical property , which in
this case is sound intensity. Stevens’ power law presents an empirical framework within psy-
chophysics, which goes beyond the world of sound, and maps out a simplistic understanding
of the ratios between perceptual characteristics and their counterparts of physical stimuli. The
ratios are based on extensive research and commonly known as the numeric power to a specified
physical intensity in the form of equation 1, where P is the perceived magnitude, c is a scaling
constant, I is the physical intensity and the exponent b is the power which the intensity is raised
to.
P = cIb
(1)
For the loudness perception of a sound, the exponent varies slightly depending on frequency
content and binaural vs monaural conditions, but for a stimulus of 3000 Hz we have P = I0.67.
This signifies that perceived loudness is not increasing linearly with the sound intensity but that
it e.g. takes more than a doubling of sound intensity to perceive the loudness as being doubled
(Chen, Tian, Wang, Mao, & Zhao, 2021). Considering the empirical basis, this correlation is
something that can be determined as a normal distribution among people with normal hearing,
making it a very valuable characteristic to inspect in comparison to the objective measured
7
==PAGE:13==
values.
While inspection of loudness can give a good indication of the correlation between the measured
and the perceived sound, it does not however say much about how the sound affects someone.
Rather than only being a perceptual characteristic, ’annoyance’ is also an affective one,
meaning it relates to a personal, emotional or interpreted experience of the specific sound
attributes. This is a type of characteristic that can vary significantly across individuals and
therefore particularly interesting to use for investigating if tendencies arise across groups of
people in response to certain sounds (Moon et al., 2019).
Though annoyance is a sound characteristic of particular interest as it can have an effect both
in relation to health and to the associated experience towards a product (in this case the cars),
it can seem contrived to focus on a negative characteristic only. To account for this, a part of the
investigation into the subjective aspect, will also consider additional subjective descriptors of
both neutral and positive characteristics in an attempt to present a more encompassing overview
of the noise differences between electric and conventional cars at low speeds.
2.5
Methodological Relevance
With section 2.1 in mind, a structured methodology is desirable to use as basis of the mea-
surements. The purpose of the measurements requires a method where sound measurements of
singular passing cars are obtained in order to distinguish between the overall noise emissions
from each of them. Only a few ISO standards deals with this type of methodology and only ISO
11819-1 is concerned with vehicles moving at steady pace. Since the difference in noise between
specific types of asphalt is not the objective of these measurements, the methodology of ISO
11819-1 is not designed or optimal for the purpose, but its framework provides a structured and
reliable methodology which is used to the possible extent and altered when necessary. While the
applied SPB method from ISO 11819-1 does not address low-speed conditions, adaptations to
the methodology, such as adjusted microphone placements and controlled speed ranges, makes
it natural to consider the vehicles as point sources.
Noise perception goes beyond objective measurements, as psychoacoustic and affective char-
acteristics influence how individuals experience sound. While loudness offers an initial bridge
between physical intensity and perception, it does not capture the broader impact of sound,
such as annoyance or its potential to evoke emotional responses. This highlights the importance
of including subjective evaluations alongside objective measurements. In the context of vehi-
cle noise, especially at low speeds, incorporating perception-based analyses allows for a more
comprehensive understanding of the differences between EVs and ICEs.
The methodological adjustments made in this study such as considering vehicles as point
sources, adapting standardized techniques, and integrating subjective descriptors, ensure that
the results provide meaningful insights into both the physical and experiential aspects of low-
speed vehicle noise.

3
Experiment 1: Pass-by measurements
The purpose of the pass-by measurements, is to use a structured methodology to obtain objective
measurements that can be directly compared to results found in literature. Recordings of the
pass-by measurements is also the basis for the later investigation into sound perception.
As previous research have already established that there is no significant differences between
noise from electric and conventional cars at higher speeds where tire noise is the main contribut-
ing source (Iversen, Marbjerg, & Bendtsen, 2013), the focus of these measurements is about
exploring an extent of objective differences between noise from electric cars and conventional
cars driving at low speeds, here meaning up to and including 50 km/h.
3.1
Method
3.1.1
Location
A section of Venlighedsvej in Hørsholm, marked with a pin in figure 2a, was chosen as the
location as it generally fulfilled the following requirements necessary to follow the ISO standard:
• Relatively quiet area and road background noise
• Speed limit of 50 km/h (categorized as a "low" speed road), with good view for the driver
of the road and surroundings, assuring acceptable circumstances for safety considerations
• Level and straight road with a gradient of less than 2%
• Enough width for the purpose of having at least 3.75 meters of the hard road surface
between the microphone and the midline of the cars
• Plane and even surface, neither too new nor too worn and without patching or other

3.1.5
Instructions and speeds
Previous to the day of measurements, participants received an e-mail with information, along
with attached instructions for the experiment and maps showing the two routes. Upon arrival
on the day of their participation, information and instructions were repeated orally for the
sake of reinforced memory and printed maps of the routes were handed out to each driver
after additional explanation on how to recognize the various turning points on the real life
routes in accordance with the maps. 2-3 drivers attended the same session and each driver
received a walkie-talkie for communication during the session and instruction on how to use it.
All walkie-talkies were on the same channel so instructor used the name of each driver when
communicating. After instructions and questions, photo-documentation were taken of each car
and their tires.
To guide the drivers on where to place their cars on the road, a chalk line was drawn to
illustrate where the midline of the car should be. Based on feedback from pilot testing, some
drivers preferred to look for a line by the left wheel rather than midline. Another chalk line was
drawn approximately 92.5 cm to the left of the midline, as this would illustrate where the left
wheel should be considering the average width of a car to be 185 cm. Before the cars started
driving, the drivers were instructed to place their car in accordance with the midline in order to
give them a sense of where both of the lines would be in their line of sight when placed correctly.

4
Experiment 2: Listening tests
As a mean to explore the subjective differences between the external sound from electric and
conventional cars at low speeds, a protocol for listening tests were designed and conducted using
SenseLabOnline as the platform. The purpose of the subjective tests is to collect coherent data
that can be cross-examined against the objective measurements as a mean to base analysis and
conclusions on a more solid foundation.
4.1
Method
4.1.1
Stimuli
For the purpose of direct comparison to the objective measurement results, a selection of the
pass-by recordings were used as the sound files in the tests. Selected recordings were all from
microphone 4 which was located at 5 m. distance to the cars midlines and with driven speeds
deviating less than 10% from instructed speeds. No recordings from the first day of measure-
ments were included due to the audible corruption of the natural signal by broadband electric
noise. While it was possible to use signal processing to get rid of the most noticeable disruption,
the use of this would then also need to be applied to the recordings without the noise in order
to maintain method consistency for the sake of comparable results. When using the same signal
processing on the non-noise affected recordings, parts of the natural signal were wrongly con-
sidered as noise and removed, leading to obstruction of the recording that could not be ignored.
This led to only use the recordings from the 12 cars that participated on the second day of
measurements. Since all recordings were obtained by the use of single microphones, the original
sound files were converted from mono to stereo sound by the use of Adobe Audition. Of the
three existing recordings from microphone 4 of each car moving at each of the respective speeds,
the one that presented with the most stable/uninterrupted pass-by sound and the least sounds
from the background noise was chosen. The most noticeable sounds from the background noise
that could not be avoided came from various birds.
Based on a hypothesis that bird sounds could potentially create bias for the experienced sound,
a large variation of signal processing filters were tested for bird sound reduction. Because the
bird sounds covered frequencies from approx. 4 - 10 kHz and presented both as close to pure
tones and broad-spectrum sound it was only possible to remove a selection while still keeping
the natural signal as intact as possible. The best option were determined to be a Chebyshev 1

Test administration

Discussion
5.1
Experiment 1: Objective measurements
5.1.1
Limitations
As mentioned in section 2, this study focuses on measurements of vehicles driving at steady pace
for the sake of repeatability and ability to compare vehicles with varying specifications. While
some studies have included start/stop and acceleration measurements, these are driving con-
ditions introduce significant variability due to individual driving behaviors and vehicle-specific
characteristics (Ibarra, Cobo, Calvo, & Román, 2012). The focus on steady-pace driving does
create certain limitations for the application of results, particularly in urban contexts. While
steady-pace driving is common on highways and long, uninterrupted road stretches, urban ar-
eas with low speed limits typically involve more complex driving patterns.
These patterns
are shaped by various interruptions such as: intersections, pedestrian and cyclist crossings,
speed bumps, traffic signals and turning. Nevertheless, a methodological focus on steady-pace
measurements provides a solid foundation for understanding baseline noise differences between
electric and conventional vehicles. The results might serve as a starting point for future re-
search examining more complex driving scenarios, while maintaining their own validity within
the specified scope of steady-pace conditions.
Since surface type of the road and environmental surroundings can impact the measurement
results significantly, only one location was utilized to avoid too large a variety of these parameters
to account for during data analysis. Environmental variance in weather and background noise
was the basis of keeping the number of days for measurements as low as possible and duration
of the collective sessions in a day as compact as possible. Despite only using two days for the
measurements and the environmental conditions being quite similar in terms of temperature
and wind speed, the minor differences concerning especially air humidity, wind direction and
debris from trees, were all noticeable factors which to some extent affected the difference in
background noise during the measurements. These are however the terms of environmental
measurements in general, and even within the span of a single day, there is a risk of variation
that can alter the homogeneity of the background noise surrounding the measurements. In
this case it was determined that the environmental variations were negligible in terms of data
comparison.
While the surface type being fixed is good for robustness, it is important to note that a
change of surface could potentially lead to an entirely different outcome of the results due to
the impact the combined tire/surface contribution might have on the overall noise.
To know what the SPL values of this contribution was in the conducted experiment, would
have required several rounds where each driver turned of their means of propulsion before
reaching the measurement area, to only measure the rolling noise.
This addition was not
prioritized as it would have increased the complexity of the experiment and added to the time
39
==PAGE:45==
duration of each driver and session.
Though it is not possible to directly determine the contribution, an estimation of the value
range can be deduced from literature. In research using a comparable method, the maximum
difference found between 9 set of tires mounted on the same electric car while driving at low
speeds was 3.6 dB at 19 km/h when comparing the LAFastMax values. Considering the mea-
sured differences listed in table E.1 and E.2 with the LAFMax difference for EVs being 5.90
dB at 15 km/h and 6.59 dB at 20 km/h, this would suggest that the tire/road contribution is
not by itself responsible for the overall difference in the low speed noise levels (Czuka, Pallas,
Morgan, & Conter, 2016).
5.1.2
Method
Though the ISO 11819-1 were followed for the location guidelines, a few parameters had po-
tential for improvement. The standard specifies a gradient of the road that is less than 2%
and though the incline that existed in the latter part of the measurement area was hardly even
visible, it did present as a slight challenge for keeping the pace steady, especially at the low-
est speed. For length of the road, nothing is specified in the standard as it assumes that the
measurements are taking place at a point were vehicles are already moving at a steady speed.
Despite the length for acceleration had been considered prior to the measurements as described
in sub-subsection 3.1.1, the calculation for minimum length did not take into consideration how
it feels for a non-professional driver to make a car accelerate fast. For this controlled setup
it would have been favorable with more road length before the measurement area in order to
avoid any driver rushing the acceleration from 0 to 50 km/h. Despite this does not seem to
have affected the results, it should not feel stressful to volunteer as a driver in an experiment
like this.
Another issue caused by the choice of location, were the road access to other vehicles not
taking part in the experiment. Had it been possible to use a location closed off to the public,
the setup could have been optimized in terms of the alternative microphone distance being
smaller. But like it is pointed out by Moreno et al., the applicability of pass-by methods from
standards that are not targeted at an urban context and low speeds, will inherently involve
setup compromises which in turn either compromises the repeatability of the experiments, or
leaves them confined to experimental road sites only (Moreno et al., 2023).
For a method that takes EVs and quiet ICEs driving at low speeds into account, the two
most important considerations are the microphone distance to the car midline and the differ-
ence between background noise and measured LAFmax values. In a proposal for a new series of
amendments to the EU directive on AVAS, it is suggested that the required difference between
background noise and lowest measured LAFmax value is revised to 6 dB instead of the current
10 dB (Task Force on Quiet Road Transport Vehicles, 2023). The test conditions are here with
a microphone distance at 2 meters. When evaluating the results of the different microphone
distances used in this project, it is estimated that it would have been necessary with a micro-
40
==PAGE:46==
phone distance of 4,3 m at the most to ensure measurements of at least 6 dB difference to a
background noise around 53 dB like on day 1 and a microphone distance of 3,7 m at the most
to a background noise around 54 dB like on day 2. While these are still very close distances for
road measurements, it would be more realistic to oblige to than the 10 dB difference to most
background noise levels.
Even if optimal setup conditions existed, the drivers on their own presents a variable that is
to some extent uncontrollable and/or unpredictable. Each driver might to some extent change
the measurable results based on their driving experience with a specific car and general driving
style. Some studies using CPB as method considers a professional driver as part of the controlled
method as a means to fix the driving style to the possible extent (Ibarra et al., 2012). In the
measured values from the benchmark car as shown in figure ?? it is not possible to distinguish
if any differences are caused by the change of driver, the environmental changes between the
two measurement days or a combination of both. It should however be of less importance in an
EV or a car where automatic transmission is used compared to cars where manual gearshift and
thereby variation in noise from rpm can vary. Considering how small the measured benchmark
differences are and how variables such as type of tires or wear and tear on tires might influence
measurement results to an extent that is comparable of higher significance, the variation of
drivers is not considered problematic.
5.1.3
LAFmax and Frequency content analysis
The results of this study regarding LAFmax values and frequency content can be examined in
the context of existing research, revealing several important insights about noise emissions from
EVs and ICEs at low speeds.
Impact of Measurement Methods and Standards
The findings demonstrate the critical importance of standardized measurement approaches
when comparing vehicle noise emissions. While this study utilized the method of ISO 11819-1
to the possible extent, previous studies have employed various methods including CPX and
modified versions of ISO 362, leading to different absolute values but similar relative patterns
(Dudenhöffer & Hause, 2013; Iversen & Skov, 2015). The variation in measurement methods
presents a challenge for direct comparisons, highlighting the need for standardized approaches
specifically designed for low-speed measurements.
Evolution of EV Noise Characteristics
A key temporal factor emerging from the literature comparison is the introduction of AVAS
requirements.
The results of this study show a linear trend in LAFmax values correlating
with speed for both EVs and ICEs, which differs notably from pre-AVAS studies [19, 15] that
reported 3-4 dB lower noise emissions for EVs at 10-20 km/h. This difference is particularly well-
illustrated by Laib & Schmidt’s findings, where AVAS activation resulted in 1-11 dB variations
at 10 km/h (Laib & Schmidt, 2019). These comparisons suggest that modern EV noise profiles
41
==PAGE:47==
have evolved significantly due to regulatory requirements.
Surface Type and Environmental Factors
The influence of surface type and environmental conditions on noise emissions is identified as
an essential factor when comparing the results with other studies. Recent research in China and
Switzerland respectively, reported values 3-6 dB(A) lower than the results found here, likely due
to different surface types and environmental conditions (Lan, Yuan, Shao, & Li, 2023; Schweizer
et al., 2023). This aligns with Pallas et al.’s findings of up to 3 dB(A) variations from tire-surface
interactions and Cesbron et al.’s observation of up to 6.9 dB variations across different surfaces
(Cesbron et al., 2021; Pallas et al., 2016).
Vehicle Classification and Matching
The finding of no significant difference between EVs and ICEs (excluding diesel) at constant
speeds aligns with recent Swiss research, though their study showed smaller variations, possibly
due to the matched-pair methodology they used where there was a corresponding model of EV
to each ICE vehicle (Schweizer et al., 2023). This suggests that vehicle matching strategies may
be important for future research design, particularly when comparing across vehicle classes.
Implications for Traffic Noise Modeling
The evolution of EV noise characteristics, particularly post-AVAS implementation, chal-
lenges earlier predictive models such as Campello-Vicente et al.’s projection of 1-2 dB reduc-
tions in urban traffic noise with the fleet conversion to EVs (Campello-Vicente, Peral-Orts,
Campillo-Davo, & Velasco-Sanchez, 2017). The results of this study suggest that such models
may need revision to account for modern AVAS implementations, regional variations in AVAS
requirements (EU vs. US), surface type influences and vehicle matching considerations.
5.2
Experiment 2: Subjective evaluations
5.2.1
Method
On the first day of the listening tests, the necessary equipment for sound card calibration was
not accessible and though the sound card volume knobs were placed in accordance with a
picture from documentation of the initial calibration, it was found that ‘gain’ had mistakenly
been active on one of the sound cards, which might have affected three of the trials. Later the
effect of activating ’gain’ was tested and it showed that mV/dB were doubled compared for the
same volume knob placement, which means that the volume have been increased by 6 dB for
those tests were it was active.
Disregarding if gain was active, the general response from most participants after being told
during debrief what speeds they listened to, was that it seemed much louder and they would
have estimated speeds of up to between 80 and 100 km/h. This experience can be explained
by multiple factors, with the most important one being that the listening test in several ways
differs physically from the real life situation of having a car pass by at close range, like when
walking on the sidewalk. In the test-situation, the participants are hearing the sound in stereo
42
==PAGE:48==
with equal volume for both ears and directed straight into the ears from the headphones while
sitting inside a listening booth. In real life, walking on a sidewalk, the sound would reach one
ear more than the other while also being partially absorbed by the body and scattered by the
external parts of the head and ears. Furthermore, the sound of the passing car would most
likely not have the focused attention of the person walking unless it seemed alarming in some
way. To account for some of these differences, it could potentially have been advantageous to
play the sound from external loudspeakers in an array setup rather than through headphones.
In addition to this it would potentially also have been beneficial to provide participants with
a scenario for the experienced sound as a means to have everyone base their assessments on the
same initial idea. Though the instructions were that it was the sound on its own that should
be assessed disregarding context, it was evident from the debriefings that several participants
had still considered specific scenarios to have context for their assessments. As an example,
one person had considered the context of walking next to a road, and because the car sounds
were then expected, annoyance ratings were automatically lowered, opposite the participant
who considered the context to be sounds outside the bedroom window when trying to sleep.
For the annoyance test it was considered to use bipolar scales to assess the degree of an-
noyance vs its counterpart pleasantness rather than annoyance only. The point being to avoid
a bias of participants already considering the sound as annoying due to it being the focus of
the scale. The one-sided scaling was however chosen because of it being in line with ISO 15666
and due to the risk that a bipolar scale could lead to a higher percentage of "neutral" choices,
which would not be useful for the purpose of comparing annoyance between the sounds. One
participant did however mention during debrief, that they had found it counterintuitive to first
rate experienced annoyance and then in the CATA test have to decide if they wanted to add
the word ’annoyance’ as an adjective, which was a testament to the actual occurrence of the
bias from the one-directional scale.
The CATA test ended up being the least informative which was likely due to the intention
behind it not corresponding well with data output that could be of best use in analysis. In
hindsight, the options of words could have been small specific statements rather than single
words and using descriptors such as ’futuristic’ and ’artificial’, focused on AVAS investigation.
Instead, "Contains tones" is an example of a descriptive statement that could have been com-
pared directly with the objective data and useful to investigate for correlation with annoyance.
Though it is interesting to see that some cars were considered ’pleasant’ or ’calming’, it is not
possible to distinguish if this simply had to do with them being more quiet, or if something
else was the reason behind these choices.
By directing statements to match psychoacousti-
cal parameters like tonality, the results would also be easier to validate against literature like
the study done by Steinbach & Altinsoy who tried to determine annoyance of EV sounds at
constant pass-by speed of 10 km/h by using AI to analyze the patterns between synthetically
produced stimuli containing specific psychoacoustic parameters with annoyance ratings from
listening tests (Steinbach & Altinsoy, 2019).
43
==PAGE:49==
5.3
Integrations of objective and subjective results
While it was clear from the results of the loudness and annoyance test results that strong
correlation exists between loudness and annoyance, the tests did not help identify what other
attributes contributes to annoyance. Bolkovac et. al who came up with the same results in
2014 based on listening tests using stimuli from ICE vehicles, and proposed an idea for future
experiments where stimuli loudness is normalized in order to remove that attribute when testing
for annoyance (Bolkovac, Horvat, Jambrošić, & Domitrović, 2014). Another option would be to
include a test where AVAS sounds from the included vehicles are isolated, to perform separate
annoyance rating on those like done by Lee et. al (Lee, Baek, Han, & Lee, 2018). This could then
be cross-examined with the psychoacoustical parameters mentioned in the CATA and measured
frequency content for AVAS only, as the combination of frequency requirements and mechanical
design of the car, according to the study by Fortino & Kotzian, will influence the sound quality
of AVAS, which could in terms affect annoyance (Fortino & Kotzian, 2023). By inspecting the
AVAS frequency content on its own, it will also help determine if conclusion made by Poveda-
Martínez in 2017 seem to hold true or not. The conclusion suggested that high spectral density
bands combined with pronounced tonal components would be well suited as AVAS sounds due
to good detectability that should not cause too high annoyance (Poveda-Martínez et al., 2017).
From the tonal inspection in this project, it could be theorized that this approach is used in
AVAS for a few of the EVs, however without correlation to identifiable lower annoyance.
No matter the approach for further investigations of these topics, it is important to notice
that part of the results might be outdated within a scope of 5-10 years, simply because of the
rapid evolution that EVs are still going through and the high focus on new battery development
that naturally affects the current boundaries of EV design. Concurrently, there are ongoing
proposals of changes to the EU directive concerning AVAS and so far the revised amendments
have mainly lead to more noise emission. Despite the resistance that these kind of changes
meet from the perspective of those against environmental noise pollution, statistics exist with
relative higher numbers of people being run down by EVs than by ICEs, and especially the World
Blind Union advocate strongly for requirements that resembles the ones of the United States
(US) more. In an informal Document from 2023 three key scenarios are described in which the
stationary sound of vehicles is essential for the safety of blind and low vision pedestrians (World
Blind Union, 2023).

==PAGE:50==
6
Conclusion
This study explored the differences in noise emissions and perceived sound characteristics be-
tween EVs and ICEs at low speeds.
By combining objective measurements and subjective
evaluations, key insights were obtained into the unique acoustic profiles of EVs and ICEs, high-
lighting areas of interest for future urban noise management and regulatory frameworks.
The results show that EVs generally emit lower noise levels than ICEs, with a significant
difference of 1.15 dB(A) in measured LAFMax across all speeds.
This difference, however,
diminishes when diesel cars are excluded, regardless of speed.
Psychoacoustic evaluations revealed that though strong correlation exists, perceived annoy-
ance is not solely determined by measured loudness. Notably, while AVAS ensures the audibility
of EVs at low speeds, its contribution to annoyance was not significant in the subjective tests,
suggesting that current AVAS designs are unlikely to pose a major challenge for public accep-
tance. Significant differences does however exist between perceptual assessments of EVs and
ICEs.
The findings underscore the limitations of existing ISO standards in capturing the nuances
of low-speed vehicle noise.
Adaptations, such as shorter microphone distances and revised
speed categories, are necessary to ensure reliable and comparable measurements. Furthermore,
the study highlights the need for integrating psychoacoustic descriptors like annoyance into
regulatory frameworks, ensuring that future AVAS designs align with both safety and public
comfort.
Future work should focus on determining the specific contributions from AVAS, propulsion
and tire/road noise at low speeds, including more diverse road conditions to validate these
findings further. Additionally, a deeper investigation into how AVAS contributes to perceived
annoyance could inform improved design standards for EV noise emission, cultivating a balance
between functional safety and acoustic quality in urban environments.
This research provides a foundation for understanding the acoustic differences between EVs
and ICEs, offering insights for policymakers, urban planners, and vehicle manufacturers as they
navigate the challenges of a transitioning vehicle fleet.
