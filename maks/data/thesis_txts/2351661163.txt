Abstract
Hearing loss can influence the quality of life (QOL) greatly for the hearing impaired,
as well as relatives and friends. Being able to provide more accurate solutions to each
individual user could potentially increase the user experience, and thus QOL.
A hearing aid can be adjusted to accommodate different acoustic scenarios using dif-
ferent software settings, i.e. programs. Whether the general user prefers to have a single
program, as in the current solution from Oticon, or to choose between several programs
has been investigated in this thesis. Further, it has been tested if an automation of the
program change could be possible.
Previous studies regarding hearing aids have been performed in laboratories, but in
this thesis real-life data is obtained from five test users. They provide the data using
their smartphone, the app If This Then That, and the hearing aid OPN. This is possible
as the hearing aid provides connection to the Internet of Things. The data was cleaned
and sorted previous to several visualisations regarding the use of programs and patterns
in the data.
Based on these visualisations it was found that the test users preferred the opportu-
nity to choose between several programs. Furthermore, a proof of concept was provided
regarding the automation of the program change.
As the proof of concept is very simple there are several ideas for future work. E.g.
additional features to increase the accuracy of the model.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Humans rely on their senses; seeing, smelling, feeling, speaking, and hearing. The loss
of any of those senses changes the way we communicate, interact amongst others, how
we feel about ourselves, and how others see us. Usually this change is to the worse, as
we are now disabled and not able to do the same as before. In short, it decreases the
quality of life (QOL). The loss or decrease of the ability to hear affects in particular the
communication and socialisation of the person, which can lead to confidence loss, social
isolation, anxiety, and even to depression.[1, 2]
A hearing aid can increase the QOL for a hearing impaired considerably if it is fitted
properly. Many people experience troubles with their hearing aids and are not satisfied
with them, hence they stop using them.[3, 4] Hougaard and Ruf [4] found, in their study,
that between 4.7 % and 12,4 % of the hearing aid users quit the use of the aid. This can
have many reasons, but the frustrations about the looks and the functionality are some
of them. On the other hand, a study found that elderly people are generally satisfied
with their hearing aid.[2]
To increase the QOL it is crucial that the users actually wear the hearing aid. Studies
have shown that the QOL increases more for the people wearing their aids often than
for the ones that only wear the aid occasionally.[5]
1.1
Usability
The connection between the user and the hearing aid has apparently only been inves-
tigated in a laboratory.1 Currently, the audiologist can extract summary statistics of
how frequently each setting or program in the hearing aid is used. This gives a very
limited understanding of the users and their preferences. From a research perspective it
is important to understand how the user interacts with the hearing aid during a normal
day, week, month, etc. Further, it is important to understand the changes in settings
made by the user, and even more important to understand why and when these changes
in settings occur. Previously, the goal for hearing aid companies has been to create a
common profile for the majority of the hearing aid users - a one size fits all solution,
but as hearing losses are individual this might not be the most optimal solution. Each
user needs a personal fitting of the chosen hearing aid in order to obtain the highest im-
provement in QOL. This leads to the following hypotheses divided into manual change
and automatic change.

Manual change:
• Given the opportunity to adjust the directionality and noise reduction of their
devices using a smartphone, users will prefer to actively change programs and
volume, rather than rely on one single setting.
Automatic change:
• Analysing user generated data that captures the selection of programs and volume
will make it possible to predict the user’s preferred settings for directionality and

State of the art technology is needed to be able to verify these hypotheses. Oticon has
created the first Internet of Things (IoT) applicable hearing aid called OPN.[6] OPN
provides the opportunity to seamlessly change the program of the hearing aid using
Bluetooth. This is done e.g. as the user changes location or a calendar appointment
is scheduled. By combining OPN with a service like If This Then That (IFTTT) on a
smartphone, it is possible to customise OPN to the specific user in order to get the best
and most seamless experience possible.
To understand the problems described above it is essential to understand hearing
and hearing loss.

CHAPTER 2
What is hearing loss?
Millions of people all over the world have troubles hearing[7], but there are different
types of hearing impairment. To understand the research in this thesis it is necessary to
understand the background of hearing loss and hearing disabilities. Some might question
the use of the word ”loss” in this situation as a person born without hearing abilities
cannot be losing what was never there. Nevertheless, throughout this thesis hearing loss
will be used to cover all kinds of impairments or disorders.
There have been several attempts throughout time to create an international stan-
dard describing hearing loss, hearing impairment, hearing disorder, etc., but researchers
still have problems when comparing results as they are based on different values.[8, 9]
2.1
Characteristics of hearing loss
Even though there are no international standard for describing hearing loss, the general
opinion is that it can be divided into different categories.
Severity
The severity is divided into mild, moderate, severe or profound. Mild hear-
ing loss is defined as a person not being able to keep up with the conversation in noisy
environments. A person with moderate hearing loss cannot keep up with a conversation
without using a hearing aid. A person with severe hearing loss cannot hear anything
without a powerful hearing aid and uses lip reading and/or sign language to commu-
nicate. The profound hearing loss patient relies on lip reading and sign language for
communicating with others.[10]
Origin
Hearing loss can be congenital, i.e. something you are born with, or it can be
gradual, i.e. something a user acquires during his/her life.[7, 10]
Further, hearing loss can originate from two separate parts of the ear. The conduc-
tive hearing loss is due to problems in the middle ear. It can be as simple as too much
ear wax jamming the canal, and thereby making it hard or impossible for the ear to
conduct the sound. The sensorineural loss originates in the inner ear. It can be either
age-induced, called presbycusis, or noise-induced. Common for both is the lack of or
damage to the inner hair cells, which transmit the signals of the sound to the brain. If
the hearing is affected by both types it is called a mixed hearing loss.[11]
==PAGE:12==
4
2 What is hearing loss?
Another way to define the severity of a hearing loss is by the hearing ability at different
sound pressure levels, normally measured in decibel (dB). The normal spectrum for
speech lies between 20 dB and 50 dB, and a normal-hearing person can understand
vague speech or whisper below this threshold. A person with a hearing loss has problems
hearing sounds below the specified level in dB. The thresholds for a hearing loss are
defined as [8]:
• Mild: > 20 and ≤40 dB
• Moderate: > 40 and ≤60 dB
• Severe: < 60 and ≤90 dB
• Profound: > 90 dB
It should be noted that these thresholds are not a solid definition as Smith [9] uses
the levels from the ISO 8253-1 standard which are 26-40 dB, 41-60 dB, 61-80 dB, and
above 81 dB respectively.
2.2
Documenting a hearing loss
How come some people find the hearing aid working fine and helping as it should, whereas
others keep coming back because they do not feel any difference or help at all? The
hearing can be tested regarding both sounds and speech. Sounds are documented in an
audiogram, whereas speech is documented using the Signal-to-Noise-Ratio (SNR)1 loss2.
2.2.1
Testing
There are several different tests to figure out if a person has a hearing loss. Using nearly
no equipment it is possible to count the ”yes” answers to a simple questionnaire as the
one in Appendix A[13], check the reaction to voice, check the ability to locate sounds,
or to use a tuning fork when performing a Rinne or Weber test.[9] These all give a sense
of whether the person has a hearing loss or not. To get a more exact picture of the loss
a pure tone audiometry test is performed. Pure tones are played and the person marks
when (s)he can hear the tone. The tones can be played through earphones, but the most
precise results are obtained if the sound is directly in the ear, as the form of the actual
ear can alter the sounds. The test is usually performed in different frequency ranges and
at different sound levels.[14] In addition to the pure tone test a speech audiometry test
can be done to determine how well a person can understand speech at different sound
levels. It is conducted as the pure tone test using words instead of tones.[15]

2.2.1.1
Speech intelligibility
As the biggest problem for hearing impaired people is understanding speech when in a
noisy environment[12], testing this is essential. Some widely used tests are the Speech In
Noise (SIN) test, the Hearing-In-Noise Test (HINT), and the Danish logatomes.[16] As
these tests are time consuming Killion and Niquette [16] created a shorter SIN test - the
Quick SIN test. This provides a ±3 dB SNR loss estimate in just over one minute.[12]
The person hears a sentence at six different SNR levels starting at 25 dB dropping by
five until 0 dB has been reached. Each sentence has five keywords. The total number
of correctly remembered keywords from all six sentences are subtracted from 25.5 to get
the SNR loss.[17]
2.2.2
Audiograms
The audiologists usually get the results of a hearing test as an audiogram. An audio-
gram is a simple scatter plot showing the lowest sound levels heard for several different
frequencies. It is usually visualised for both ears on the same plot; red circles for the
right ear and blue crosses for the left ear, each connected with a line in the matching
colour.
Audiograms can be categorised, just like the severity of the hearing loss[8]:
• Normal: the general hearing loss is not more than 20 dB in all frequencies
• Flat: all losses are between 20-30 dB in all frequencies
• Sloping: a decreasing slope. It can be partly flat or rising, but the overall slope
is falling
• Rising: the reverse of sloping
• Through: clear dip in the middle frequencies
• Peaked: clear peak in the middle frequencies
• Other: the audiograms which do not fit the other categories
An example of a fictive audiogram showing both the slope and the through category can
be seen on Figure 2.1. Normally an audiogram has the frequencies varying from 125 Hz
to 8000 Hz and the sound levels varying from -10 dB to 120 dB.
When a hearing loss has been identified, the amount of amplification needed to help
the user can be found. From the Figure 2.1 it can be seen that the right ear needs
amplification between the frequencies of 500 Hz and 4 kHz. The amount varies between
8 dB to 40 dB to reach the normal hearing threshold of 20 dB. This is usually referred
to as the gain. Adjusting the gain of the hearing is a relatively simple procedure, but it
can cause the user to experience the sound unnatural. If the gain is too high the speech
can be heard as bright, sharp, or metallic, whereas if the gain is too low the speech can
be hard to hear, full, or mumbling.

2.2.2.1
Sounds
Different sounds, including the pronunciation of the letters, are spread wide across the
frequency range. Figure 2.2 shows some of the common sounds from everyday life along
with most letters in the alphabet on an audiogram. It is seen that vowels are located in
the lower frequencies, whereas consonants are scattered over all frequencies. A person
with a hearing loss removing the highest frequencies will have trouble understanding
words with f, s, th, and have trouble hearing chirping. Not being able to hear the sound
of specific letters may greatly alter the understanding of speech. The word ”speech”
might be understood as ”peach” if the s-sound is not audible.
2.2.3
SNR loss
An audiogram can tell a great deal about in which frequencies and how severe a hearing
loss is regarding sounds, but it does not tell everything about how well the person
can understand speech in noise. Several studies even found no correlation between the
audiogram and the SNR loss, in fact two persons with the same audiogram was found to
have 15 dB difference in their SNR loss.[12] Furthermore, it was found that it is possible
to keep a 0 dB hearing loss based on a pure tone test, but not be able to understand
speech at all.[12]
Killion and Niquette [16] suggest four categories for SNR loss:
• Mild: 0-4 dB
• Moderate: 5-10 dB
• Severe: 11-19 dB
• Profound: > 20 dB

2.3
Summary
As there are no correlation between an audiogram and the SNR loss of a person, both
are important in order to describe the hearing loss and to be able to provide the best
support.
Hearing losses can be described through categories of type and severity. To define
which type and how severe a hearing loss is, several tests are possible to conduct. Having
done the tests, a personal audiogram is usually made to help tune the hearing aid
specifically to the user. An audiogram helps setting the gain, but each user has different
preferences and the sound might be perceived as too bright or too full. Furthermore,
some sounds, as vowels, are located around a specific frequency range. Having a hearing
loss at that range may alter the understanding of speech a great deal.

3.1
Hearing aids
In general, all digital hearing aids have the same five basic components; a microphone,
an amplifier, a speaker, a battery, and a computer chip. Based on these components
different types of hearing aids have been developed. [18, 19]
BTE
Behind-the-ear aids are placed behind the ear and provides the sound through
a hollow plastic tube connected to a personalised ear mould. BTE is the most common
hearing aid even though it might be cosmetically undesired by some. It can be used by
persons with mild to profound hearing loss.
RITE / RIC
Receiver-in-the-ear or receiver-in-the-canal aids have most components
BTE, but keep the speaker in the ear. The sound is transferred through a tiny wire and
a custom ear-bud keeps the speaker in place.
ITE, ITC, CIC
In-the-ear, in-the-canal, or completely-in-the-canal aids are all small
ear-buds located in the ear as in-the-ear headphones. These are smaller than the BTE
aids, hence includes smaller batteries and less computing power. Regarding the size
these might seem undesirable to elderly people, whereas the computing power only ben-
efits persons having a mild to moderate hearing loss.
Oticon provides solutions for all types of hearing loss and makes it possible to personalise
the hearing aid by colours and size.

3.2.2.1
OpenSound Navigator™
To hearing impaired people increasing the speech intelligibility in noise is crucial, hence
an important part of OPN is the OpenSound Navigator™(OSN). It
’... is a new speech-enhancement algorithm that preserves speech and reduces
noise in complex environments’[22]
and it consists of four different modules[22]:
• Analyze
• Balance
• Noise removal
• Fitting
Analyze
The analyze module is the first step in the process of better speech under-
standing. Using two microphone inputs it creates a 360◦noise estimate along with a
back-facing cardioid direction. This is used as input for the following modules which
end up as the actual help to the user.
==PAGE:19==
3.2 Oticon
11
Balance
The key work of this module is to keep the balance between the noise sources
and the target speech sources. This is done by using a minimum-variance distortion-less
response (MVDR) beamformer to improve the SNR. As the target speech is only present
in the omnidirectional signal, the improvement of SNR is done by subtracting the noise
signal from the omnidirectional one. This further creates so called null directions which
are suppressed noise signals towards the most dominant noise sources. Each of these
null directions are adjusted 125 times per second in 16 frequency bands. As the noise
data is initially processed by the Analyze module, the balance module does not know
the actual noise level.
Noise Removal
This module works as a secondary noise remover as all noise might
not have been removed in the previous modules. Further, if the noise source is placed
directly behind the person speaking, i.e. the target talker, it makes it impossible to
filter based on direction. By having a more accurate signal it is possible to remove noise
between words up to 9 dB suppression.
To prevent talking from being interpreted as noise the Voice Activity Detector
is used. If speech is detected in one of the 16 frequency bands the Balance and Noise
Removal modules are frozen in their current state in that band. This freeze is updated
500 times per second.
The fitting module has many possibilities for personalisation and it is complex. Based
on personal choices the above modules are personalised using GENIE.
3.2.3
Fitting with GENIE
GENIE is the fitting tool for hearing aids from Oticon, and it was updated to GENIE
2 along the release of OPN. It is a help to the audiologist in the fitting process and to
the user in the sense of a more personalised first fitting. It is a simple tool that creates
a personal profile based on the answers to a few simple questions. The fitting process is
divided into two parts; the selection and the fitting.
After completion of the initial questions about age, gender, and hearing aid expe-
rience in the selection part, more specific questions about sound preferences are asked
aided by sound samples. The answers to these questions affect the settings of the hear-
ing aid in regards to brightness, compression, sudden sounds, directionality, and noise
reduction. All the questions can be found in Appendix B. Based on these preferences
GENIE 2 assigns one of three help profiles; low, medium, or high. No profile is better
or worse than the others as they mimic the user’s preferences. If the personalisation is
skipped, GENIE 2 will by default choose LX medium as profile.

Noise reduction
The noise reduction can be deactivated, but it is recommended that
it stays active. Using the noise reduction the hearing aid can be customised for both
simple and complex environments.
Simple sound environment
A simple sound environment can be quiet, but it is
not a necessity. There is a high SNR, low resonance, and if it contains multiple speech
sources they are spatially separated.
Complex sound environment
A complex sound environment is the opposite of the
simple. There is low or changing SNR, possibility of resonance and wind noise, and
there are multiple speech sources which are difficult to separate. An example of both
the environments can be seen on Figure 3.1.
Transition
Transition is the connection between the two sound environments. This is
how fast the noise reduction will kick in when in a simple or complex environment. Low
transition keeps the sound realistic until the environment gets complex, while the high
transition kicks in from the start.[22] The different transitions can be found in Appendix
C where the grey area shows the different possibilities. The transition is maintained by
the use of the Balance module. At a high SNR the module keeps sounds realistic, but
as the SNR decreases the sounds are rebalanced.
Directionality
Directionality is per default set to Open automatic, which is the only
directionality where the transition can be changed. It is further the recommended choice
in regards of the full potential of OPN and OSN. Other possibilities are Pinna Omni
which keeps the natural sound of the human ear or Full Directionality which keeps the
front in focus. The latter two influence all environments and therefore the transition
cannot be altered. As the noise is still present the simple and complex noise environ-
ments can still be set.
If the personalisation is skipped the profile is per default set to YouMatic™LX medium.
This leads to a problem as people with the same audiogram but 15 dB difference in SNR
will encounter very different experiences. The Balance module increases the SNR based
on the profile setting, therefore the result of the adaptations might not fit the user. It
could be that some sounds are removed too early to try to keep the natural feeling or
the opposite; that the help kicks in too late and the user loses the speech understanding.
The result of these adaptations is a personal YouMatic™LX profile fitted to the user’s
individual need and hearing aid.

4.1
Theory
4.1.1
Internet of Things
The Internet of Things (IoT) is expanding at a great pace. Just in the past couple of
years IoT has gone through three generations; the RFID tags, sensors and actuators,
and Virtual Object and Real World Object.[26] A major problem when discussing IoT
is the lack of a definition describing the term. No definition makes it impossible to
compare different objects in the many possible fields of application. The primary fields
are healthcare, personal and social, smart home/ smart building, smart city, smart grid
and metering, smart environment, smart business/ inventory and logistics, transporta-
tion, security, and surveillance[26], which again show the huge market potential. Only
recently IEEE has attempted to define the IoT:
’Group of infrastructures interconnecting connected objects and allowing their
management, data mining and the access to the data they generate.’[27]
Having this definition in mind OPN is a clear part of the IoT as the possible connection
to the internet offers the possibility of data gathering from the device. This opportunity
further enables the data to be obtained in real life and not in a laboratory. A hearing
aid, like OPN, is not the most common IoT-connected device to carry around, but
a smartphone might be. By combining the data from OPN with the data from the
smartphone a much larger dataset can be created. Gathering the data is done by the
help of the app called If This Then That.
4.1.2
If This Then That
If This Then That (IFTTT) is a smartphone app that enables connection and control
of many apps and IoT-connected devices. It consists of applets and services.[28]
==PAGE:26==
18
4 Materials and methods
Applets
This type of connection requires a trigger and an action. The trigger is the
”if” part of the applet, whereas the action is the ”then” part. Creating an applet can be
done by saying ”If something happens then do this”. With these applets it is possible
to track when entering or exiting a defined location, start the coffee machine when the
alarm goes off, get the weather status every morning at a specific time, etc.
Services
The services are all the apps and devices used to create the applets. Besides
apps and physical devices it can be features on a smartphone such as the location, bat-
tery status, photos, etc. By combining services it is possible to create applets that can
do a great variety of things as described above.
This project only looks into the applets that can help a hearing aid user, but there are
endless possibilities with the hundreds of possible connections using the service. With
the right equipment one can monitor and automate most of the interior of a house from
locking the door to knowing if something moves inside, to tell of the fridge is left open,
or to turn on the light just before you arrive home.
IFTTT is a crucial part of the data gathering hence very important for the completion
of the project. To get a feeling of the possibilities of the IFTTT app, it was tested
previous to the instructions for the test users (TUs).

4.3
Obtaining the data
As an experiment six users were equipped with OPN if they did not have it beforehand.
They were told to install the Oticon ON App and the IFTTT app on an iPhone. Using

explained the possibilities of generating a calendar event with a specific keyword to log
data. Lastly, access was needed to be granted to a Google Sheets file collecting the data.
One of the TUs does not collect data, hence only five TUs are used in this project.
It should be noted that IFTTT made an update which stopped the upload of data
from most TUs during the project. Therefore, only the initial four weeks of each TU’s
period have been used in direct comparisons. Furthermore, the TUs did not begin the
test simultaneously, but at different dates during three weeks.
As a background for this test it is expected that the TUs use the hearing aid and use
their smartphone on a regular basis. The data might be disregarded if the smartphone
has a bad connection or other faults.

Instead of using the current solution, with a somewhat personal fitting with YouMatic™LX
in GENIE, users could be offered four different directionality and noise reduction profiles.
These profiles will mimic the possibility of choosing between different SNR thresholds

The data was imported and stored in data frames using the pandas library. As IFTTT

The initial data from IFTTT results in five columns; date, action, changeTo, extraIn-

4 Materials and methods
4.4.1.1
Time
The timestamp from IFTTT is in the format ”Month Day, Year at hh:mmAM” or PM.
This was split into four new columns; timestamp, date, time, and hour. The timestamp is
created based on the original date from IFTTT, i.e. day, month, year, hour, and minutes,
as a UNIX timestamp. The hour column is simply the time without the minutes. This
column is used in the visualisation to determine timeslots. delta and deltaProgram are
calculated from the timestamps.
4.4.1.2
Midnight
By investigating the data from the first visualisations it was found that the sorting was
faulty. From the beginning it was, naively, assumed that TUs use their hearing aid every
day, hence there would exist a data entry on each date during the test period. As this
was not the case some calculations for the total time spent in a program exceeded the
total time of a day, i.e. 24 hours.
The solution became to add fake data entries at every midnight to be able to separate
each day by 24 hours. As the five datasets for the TUs all consist of the initial four weeks
of the period the amount of fake data entries are the same for all.

It has now been made clear that people use the ON App to change program and volume

Predicting the future is hard, but some machine learning tools can assist in this difficult
task. In this project a simple machine learning algorithm called Decision Trees will be
used as a proof of concept.
7.1
Theory
7.1.1
Decision Trees
Decision trees (DTs) are models that try to predict a class of a test data point based
on evidence from training data.[29, 30, 31] DTs are easy to interpret as the result can
be visualised. From a visualisation of a DT it is easy to find the predicted value by
following the decisions made down the tree from the root. Each node asks a question
which can be either true or false. This continues until the data in the node cannot be
split further, i.e a leaf. DTs can be grown fully until all training data has been classified
perfectly, but this usually makes the tree overfit.
7.1.1.1
Overfitting
If the tree is grown to fit the data it was trained on perfectly, it will most likely not
work well with new data. This yields faulty new decisions, hence the model cannot be
used; a concept called overfitting.
As a tree can grow very big very fast, different parameters can be set to reduce the
size and to specify on which decisions to split.
==PAGE:56==
48
7 Automating the change
7.1.1.2
Parameters
Only a subset of the possible parameters to affect the growth of a DT will be described
in the following. These are the parameters used in the project.
Impurity
A DT does not split the nodes based on random decisions, but several
calculations lay the base for these splits. Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup [31] specifies that
both balance and purity are important in the decision of what to split on. The first is
needed to keep balance between the two new nodes regarding their respective amount
of data points. The latter to get the purest nodes as possible. A pure node is a node
where all data points in a leaf belong to the same class. To get a mix of both, they are
combined into a measure of impurity. The most common choices for impurity are Gini
and Entropy. These are two different formulas, where Gini focusses on splitting in order
to create one large pure node as a class keeping the rest as the other. Entropy, on the
other hand, focusses on splitting in order to divide the classes equally.[32, 33]
Maximum depth
To limit the size of the tree the max depth of the tree can be
specified. This parameter tells the classifier to stop growing the tree when the number
of nodes on the branches reach the specified limit.
Minimum sample split
Another way to limit the size of the tree is to set the min-
imum sample split value. This stops the tree from growing any further when a node
cannot be split into two new nodes each containing the specified minimum.
7.1.1.3
Cross-validation
As stated very clearly by Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup [31]:
Never, ever should you estimate how well a model performs by its predictions
on data it was trained upon.
To avoid this, cross-validation (CV) can be used.
From the multiple ways to cross-
validate a method, K-fold CV has been chosen. When cross-validating a model using
this method the data is divided into K parts. One part is used as test set, whereas the
rest, (K-1)-parts, are combined as training data. The model is fitted on the training-data
and tested on the test set to calculate the accuracy.[31]

learn includes, amongst other machine learning algorithms, Decision Trees. To obtain a
visualisation of the generated decision tree Graphviz was used.

Data amount
As the datasets obtained in this project are relatively small, CV with a K-fold of five
might have introduced some skewed accuracies. As mentioned earlier, when dividing a
small dataset into five parts, the amount of data in each split might be small. Circum-
venting this problem can be done by using the entire dataset for a single accuracy score.
This is done in a similar way as the K-fold, but only one time - having K equal one. The
problem with the so called Hold-out method[31] is that only a part of the full dataset

is used for testing. The most precise CV is done by Leave-one-out[31]. A single data
point is taken out for testing where the rest of the data is used to fit the model. This
is done for each data point in the dataset. The drawback of this is that it takes much
longer. As the datasets are relatively small this would be a reasonable solution.
Cross-validation is not only used to calculate the accuracy of the model, but it can
be used to find the best parameters for tuning the model.
7.3.2.3
Tuning the model
A thorough way to find the best model with the features available is to test them all.
This can be done either by starting with one feature and then gradually adding features
while calculating the accuracy of the model, or the other way around. I.e. starting with
all possible features and removing one at a time while calculating the accuracy. The
described method is called sequential feature selection.[31] If the accuracy is calculated
by a CV it is important to use the same splits for all calculations. If this is not the case,
as with this project, the accuracies are calculated based on different datasets, hence
cannot be compared equally.
Another way to find a better model is pruning. The previous described methods for
limiting the size of a three are easy, but by doing this it is uncertain if an additional
node would increase the accuracy substantially. To be sure of this, a DT can be made
using pruning. When pruning a tree it is first outgrown fully, i.e. to the limit where all
leaves are pure. As this often results in overfitting, some of the branches are collapsed
into a single leaf.[31] There are multiple strategies in pruning which will not be covered.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Hearing loss is a large problem worldwide. It can be described through categories of
type and severity. As hearing losses are unique to each person, several tests are possible
to perform in order to categorise them correctly. An audiogram is the most used result
of a hearing test. It provides insight into the problematic frequency ranges and loudness
thresholds. Besides this the SNR loss is essential, as this describes the possible problems
understanding speech in noise.
A hearing aid can increase the QOL for a hearing impaired person. As many different
makes and models exist it is important to choose the right one. People only having a
mild hearing loss might be satisfied with the cheaper PSAPs, whereas a more severe
hearing loss requires more functions. The newest hearing aid from Oticon is called OPN.
It is unique in the sense of possibilities and has state of the art functionalities. In order
to get the most out of any hearing aid from Oticon the user has to go through a personal
fitting session. When the fitting is complete, the user has obtained a personal profile
with settings regarding noise, directionality, etc. As many users stop using the hearing
aid the user experience could potentially be improved by analysing the users’ behavioural
patterns.
User generated data is crucial in order to improve the user experience with hearing
aids. The data used in this thesis is obtained continuously through time in order to get
the most precise picture of the daily use. To be able to access this data it is stored in
Google Sheets with the help of the smartphone app IFTTT. As the data contains some
irregularities it is initially sorted and cleaned. Due to a problem with IFTTT only the
initial four weeks for each user are used for direct comparisons.
When analysing the data it was quickly realised that all TUs are different and change
behaviour after that!
However, the initial hypotheses and hopes for the use of the
possibilities in the IFTTT app have not been verified. The TUs did not specify any
calendar events nor did they use the locations. As the information regarding the TUs
are very limited, there are close to no limits when suggesting behavioural patterns based
on the analysis.
The TUs tend to change to programs never actually used, while all TUs have two
or three favourite programs. Most TUs favours the least helping program and no major
changes were seen over time. Several TUs seem to have trouble finding a suitable pro-
gram during lunch. Again, it is clearly seen that the five TUs are very different in their
use of the hearing aid and in their daily routines. From the analysis it has been verified
that the TUs prefer to be able to manually change programs, hence the first hypothesis
is validated.

To further improve the user experience an automatic change could be attractive. To

that the time of day is generally not sufficient to predict the correct program.
To reach even more exact conclusions regarding the hypotheses generated throughout

Future work
As nothing similar have been tried before there are endless possibilities with this data.
This project has only scratched the surface, but proposed several ideas for future work.
Having more information regarding the TUs would increase the possibility of correct

When focusing more on the ”Pinna Omni to Full dir spectrum” more data cleaning
