Abstract
This master thesis deals with an investigation of how well the non-linear force computations gen-
erated using potentially fully-nonlinear code (OceanWave3D) for a wide span of non-dimensional
water depths and signiﬁcant wave heights, along with the pressure impulse model developed for
computing the loads associated with slamming events compare against the experimental DeRisk
project 2D test database undertaken at DHI (Denmark) test facility. Appropriate scaling method
was used to scale up the required parameters from lab scale to real scale corresponding to ocean
depth of 20[m] and 33[m]. Furthermore, the later stage of the thesis investigates a recipe for
integrating the pressure impulse model into the OceanWave3D simulations to include slamming
events over the wave kinematics. Finally, an aero-servo-hydro elastic tool HAWC2 was used to
understand the dynamic response of the entire structure with the inclusion of the slamming forces.
The results obtained with and without slamming forces were compared to validate how well the
model captures the slamming events.
The validation of OceanWave3D simulations against the DeRisk experiments gave encouraging
results with the potential wave solver able to replicate the time-series, exceedance probability and
the power spectrum density to a satisfying extend. Cornerstone to the validation was expanding
the number the simulations over the non-dimensional space and selecting the right simulation to
compare against the experiments. Furthermore, Froude scaling avoided the need to replicate the
exact sea-states for comparison.
It was found that the pressure impulse model was able to compute the slamming loads from the
DeRisk experiments with reasonable accuracy. The best possible method to calculate the slamming
loads directly from the force time-series was to ﬁlter out the non-slamming portion of the force
peak. The validation was carried out for all tests with a depth of 20[m]. Along with geometric
parameters and the velocity of the wave, a calibrated variable corresponding to the angle of impact
of π/5 gave good results. This aligns well with previous studies undertaken in this regard.
Successful integration of the pressure impulse model computed slamming events was possible into
OceanWave3D generated kinematic simulations, with a few key assumptions. The slamming events
in the wave kinematics were identiﬁed as instances where wave breaking occurred. The resolution
of the sampling depth point in the horizontal direction of the simulation domain limited the
accuracy regards to the estimation of geometric parameters associated with the pressure impulse
model. Furthermore, an investigation was carried out for the estimation of wave velocity with
diﬀerent approaches explored.
Numerical study involved the challenge of how to integrate the slamming events. Combining the
knowledge gained during the course of the thesis with Wienkea and Oumeraci model
[12] for
computing the slamming component of the wave load, it was possible to successfully generate
results with and without slamming events and compare.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The ﬁrst batch-produced Danish wind turbines were installed in late 1970s and had an output of 22
[kW]. 40 years on, the size of turbines have grown exponentially. Projections show that the wind
penetration in Denmark will be around 50[%] by 2020 [2]. The European wind energy scenario is
also expected to grow at a steady pace in the coming years as seen in ﬁg. 1.1. With the sector
growing, a signiﬁcant innovation and research has been going on with the wind turbine system
becoming bigger and powerful with parallel reduction in the levelized cost of energy. Another
trend, visible in recent years, is that the developers are moving oﬀshore for their large projects
because of various advantages that oﬀshore wind farms bring - higher eﬃciency due to consistent
wind speed and directions, no visual impact or noise disturbances, terrain roughness is less limiting
and also better logistic methods for larger turbines compared to onshore installations. But as the
wind farm moves further away from the shore, the total developmental cost increases with it.
Figure 1.1: EWEA 2030 SCENARIOS: Capacity installed, power generation and percentage of
EU demand met [4]
Therefore, reducing the cost of oﬀshore wind energy is a key factor that can facilitate this growth.
As seen in ﬁg. 1.2, large contribution of the cost comes from foundations. Therefore, it is critical
to optimise the design of substructures through correct estimation of ultimate load states (ULS)
coming from extreme waves without using over conservative safety factors or non-conservative
designs that would not last the expected turbine lifetime.

1.1
Oﬀshore wind turbine foundation concepts
Unlike in the case of onshore wind, diﬀerent types of foundations are used depending mainly on
water depth and seabed conditions are used in oﬀshore wind ﬁg. 1.3.
• Monopile foundations are the most frequently used type. They are installed in depth around
25m and, are made of steel and is driven into the seabed by hammering. The diameter of
the monopile can be between 5 to 7m, with a wall thickness of 500mm. Example: Horns
Rev by Vattenfall
• Gravity foundations are installed at depths below 25m and are concrete structures made with
or without small steel skirts. It also requires scour protection as it maintains its stability
solely by means of its own gravity. Example: Vindeby by SEAS and Elkraft
• Tripod foundations are relatively lighter in weight with a three-legged jacket structure.
Erosion and scour can be less signiﬁcant compared to other foundation types. Example:
Alpha Ventus by Deutsche Oﬀshore-Testfeld und Infrastruktur
• Tripile foundations have a structure with monopile connected to the upper part with three-
legged jacket structure in the lower section. Example: Bard Oﬀshore 1 by Bard Engineering
• Jacket foundations come in diﬀerent varieties with three or four legged jacket piles with
interconnected bracings. They are ideal for depths between 20 and 50 metres. Example:
Thornton Bank Phase II by C-Power
• Floating foundations are based on a mooring system along with a ballast. They are key to
unlocking wind farm potential in regions with water depth over 50m. Example: Hywind by
Equinor

1.2
Project Scope
The design of oﬀshore substructures for ﬁxed bottom wind turbines requires careful analysis of
all possible wave kinematics and forces that can occur at the desired location. Through, Ocean-
Wave3D, a fully non-linear potential wave solver, several possible wave computations are generated
covering a large non-dimensional space. This data is provided to potential wind farm developers
who can pick the kinematics they require, apply the necessary scaling and compute the wave
distribution and forces at a particular location using suitable load model. Validating whether it
is possible to generate the desired result by OceanWave3D is critical in ensuring that the open-
source source database can be reliable for the interested companies. Improving their conﬁdence
in the database can avoid the need for extensive experimental tests and prototyping, leading to a
decrease in overall cost in the oﬀshore wind energy sector.
Another important factor that inﬂuences the the design of oﬀshore substructures is the slamming
wave force calculation. These pressure impulses which impact the cylinder wall results in structural
responses that interests the designer. For a designer, it is important to estimate ultimate load
states (ULS) as accurate as possible so it does not overestimate the safety factors or results in
non-conservative designs. Extreme wave forces and slamming events have large inﬂuence on the
ULS. Regular force models - Morison and Rainey - do not account for these particular slamming
events. Several engineering models [12] [8] have been tried on top of these force models but has
not been able to accurately replicate the slamming wave loads. Through the pressure impulse
model [1] Ghadirian A. and Bredmose H. tries an approach which focuses on the time integral
of the pressure - the pressure impulse - which depends on the overall momentum conservation of
momentum. This avoids the problem linked with depending on the peak of the pressure impulse
which can vary with impacts having the same nominal characteristics. Combining the above two
methods, the designer can compute the extreme wave loads on the substructure thereby vastly
improving the overall accuracy.
Once satisfactory validation is achieved in the functions of both OceanWave3Da and the pressure
impulse model, a recipe for integrating the generated non-linear kinematics with the impulse model
is developed so as to accurately compute the impulsive force that can be added on top of the force
time series calculated from the wave kinematics. Including the slamming events to the kinematics
will make the generated database more reliable and closer to real scenario.

Post-processing
Once the wave kinematics were generated from OceanWave3D, the next step was to post-process it
to extract the required data. This involved converting the data stored in the OceanWave3D output
’.h5’ ﬁle format to MATLAB arrays. The required data variables were - free surface elevation (η),
velocity, acceleration, time span and position in the z direction.
This set of parameters were
available for each cluster, each seed and each depth that were mentioned earlier in the chapter. A
function which takes in these three inputs and gives out the required set of data was created. The
wave kinematics were utilized to compute the time series, exceedance probability and the power
spectrum density of η and force. Finally, same parameters were computed from experimental data
set.

response and that of the simulations were computed using the velocity and acceleration time series

Power spectrum density
Power spectrum density converts the input data series into the frequency domain. This provides

In this chapter, a pressure impulse model that can compute the slamming force is validated against

4.3
Slamming events and their isolation
Initially, while assessing the experimental test data, it was important to detect and isolate slam-
ming events from the force time series and their corresponding waves. A complete automation
for performing this was impossible as we are yet to understand the entire physics of slamming
events. Moreover, manual detection was less time consuming and less error prone. Force peaks
corresponding to every wave in the time series, i.e, between two down-crossings of the wave, were
isolated. Then, the highest 30 force peaks were picked. The decision to observe 30 peaks and not
more was due to the belief that a reasonably good number of slamming events would be detected
among them. All these waves were carefully checked manually to see if any of them exhibits slam-
ming behaviour. The rule of thumb was to look for sudden increase in peak force in a short time
span. Fig. 4.3 shows an example of slamming peaks extracted from tests 23 and 24 respectively.
Looking at these cases further, it was understood that the structural response inﬂuenced the ob-
served forces causing peaks which cannot be classiﬁed as slamming. To avoid this scenario, the
force intervals were ﬁltered using Butterworth ﬁlter to remove any structural responses (see also
chapter 4.6). In some test cases, this further brought down the number of slamming events.

4.6
Experimental impulse force
As stated in previous chapters, the slamming events were detected by looking for abrupt increase
in force time series resulting in peaks. Once these intervals were known, it would be possible to
compute the force corresponding to the peak alone - which is inferred to be the slamming force.
But, before proceeding to that step, the force time-series intervals were ﬁltered using Butterworth
ﬁlter to remove any structural responses. Once the structural responses were removed, the ﬁlter
was used again to get a second curve. The cut-oﬀfrequency is adjusted so that this second curve
would accurately represent the non-slamming force history.
Once a clear distinction between
non-slamming force history and force history that includes slamming is known, it is easy to ﬁnd
the diﬀerence in area under these curves which would give us the experimental impulse force.
Furthermore, similar to the previous section as examples for each test case two slamming events
corresponding to the highest force peaks are only shown.

5.1
Identifying breaking instances in simulation
A breaking model is included in the fully-nonlinear wave solver, as it cannot directly model slam-
ming events. It is set-up to subtract certain amount of energy whenever a condition is met as
the waves move forward in the domain. Whenever the vertical downward particle acceleration at
the surface of the wave overcomes a certain threshold smoothing is applied. For the simulations
used in this thesis, the threshold was set at 0.5g, where g is the acceleration of gravity [6]. This
essentially means that we assume that the wave particles on the surface exhibits free-fall, as the
downward acceleration is close to g.
The result of this technique is recorded in a plot deﬁned by the ’position in space in the x-
direction/forward direction [m]’ and the ’time [s]’. Whenever a breaking event occurs, a marker
is placed on this plot. After the entire simulation is complete, the plot can be assessed to ﬁnd out
where and at what instance the breaking occurs. More detailed look into these plots for two tests
(each with high and low number of breaking respectively) is below.

5.3

5.3.1
Time instant of parameter extraction
As established in the previous chapter, ﬁnding the time instant of parameter extraction is crucial
to identify the right geometric or spatial distribution of wave before it slams or in this case breaks.
Diﬀerent approaches were tried to get accurate results.
Method #1 - Spatial slope between monopile location and wave gauge in front
For estimating the time instant, the method established in the previous chapter where we validated
the pressure impulse model against DeRisk experiments was to look at the time series of slope
between the two wave gauges in front of the monopile. This method cannot be used here due to
the larger distance between wave gauges which might skew the results for b and µ (see also table
5.1). This method can be implemented in the future if the resolution of sampling depth points is
increased.
Method #2 - From δη/δx distribution at the monopile location
One of the output of the simulation is δη/δx which was then utilized to get accurate time instant.
Initially, the corresponding time instant where the wave begins to break is identiﬁed. This was
described in section 5.1. The goal is to understand what happens approximately before and after
this time instant in the force and η time series. Therefore, a time interval around this instant is
assessed which starts 250 time steps before the instant and ends 250 time steps after the instant
under consideration. For this interval, η and δη/δx at the location of the monopile is computed
along with η at ﬁve positions in front of the monopile (see ﬁg. 5.3). Fig. 5.4 shows the distribution
of δη/δx for slamming corresponding to the highest force peak for tests 25 and 25. The time instant
when the wave begins to break is also denoted on the ﬁgures. Following similar idea of the previous
chapter, it was realised that the best solution would be identify a threshold for δη/δx. If the value
crosses this threshold the time instant of crossing would act as the time instant for extracting
parameters.

slamming occurs - test 24 (left) and test 25 (right)

6.1
HAWC2 overview and model set up
HAWC2 - Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine simulation Code 2D - is an aeroelastic code capable
of calculating the response of onshore wind turbine with 1,2 or 3 blades, oﬀshore turbines on
monopiles, tripods or jackets, vertical axis wind turbine or ﬂoating wind turbines with mooring
lines, in the time domain [10].
6.2
Aerodynamic modelling
The aerodynamic model is based on Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory that includes three
dynamic stall models - Stig ye model, a modiﬁed Beddoes-leishmann model and model for Active
Trailing Edge Flaps (ATEF). The other basis for the aerodynamics are skew inﬂow model, shear
eﬀect on the induction, dynamic inﬂow model and tiploss correction model [11]. For implementing
the BEM theory in HAWC2 rotor plane is discretized into independent concentric annular elements
and azimuthal variation of loading on which the 1D assumption is applied. The aerodynamic forces
acting on the structure of the wind turbine can be classiﬁed into two groupd - forces on the blades
and drag forces on the tower and nacelle. The aeodynamic input to HAWC2 consists of a main
.htc ﬁle with a blade centre line deﬁnition which contains two sub-ﬁles - The ﬁrst one being a .ae
data ﬁle containing the geometric characteristics of the airfoil sections (span-wise position, chord
length and relative thickness), and the second being a .pc data ﬁle containing the aerodynamic
properties of the airfoils (lift, drag and moment coeﬃcients of the airfoils as function of angle of
attack).
6.3
Structural modelling
The structural modelling in HAWC2 accounts for the main structural components like tower, the
nacelle, the bearings, the main shaft, the hub and the blades. Multibody formulation method
is used to transform these components into main bodies (main body) consisting of a number of
elements, where each element is considered a 6 degree of freedom Timoshenko beam element.
These bodies have their own coordinate system and position, and orientation according to the
global coordinate system. Bodies are connected by constraints. Components like blades are further
divided into a number of bodies (nbodies) which allows large deﬂections and linear formulation or
small deﬂections inside each body. The main input is a .st specifying the Timoshenko beams at
every section. They contain 19 structural parameters representing stiﬀness and damping properties
which can be seen in [10].
6.4
Wind and turbulence
The mean wind speed is deﬁned at the centre of the hub at a height of 119[m] from sea level.
The mean wind speed is ramped up to go from 4[ms−1] to 25[ms−1] in 600[s]. Once 25[ms−1]
is achieved, it is allowed to remain at that wind speed for the rest of the simulation as seen in
ﬁg. 6.2 . The said wind speed was selected to be close to the cut-oﬀwind speed, replicating
the storm condition. Turbulence was not included in this initial model to maintain simplicity.
The simulations were carried out with rotor allowed to rotate and also at standstill. This was to
understand the impact of aerodynamic damping on the response due to impulse force.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
In this thesis, a validation was carried out to check the reliability of potential non-linear wave
solver OceanWave3D in generating wave kinematics corresponding to required two-demensional
sea-states. To achieve this, the simulations were compared to experiments performed at the Danish
Hydraulic Institute (DHI) under the project ”DeRisk”. The method of Froude scaling was also
veriﬁed so as to compare waves with diﬀerent sea-state parameters but are close to each other
on the non-dimensional LeMehaute plot. Encouraging results were observed with the simulations
able to closely replicate the experimental data, with the exception of few tests were the force was
over-predicted. There is a possibility of tuning the force computed from the simulation kinematics
by adjusting the added mass or inertia coeﬃcient (CM) which could be explored in the future.
Understanding the physics of slamming wave events was another crucial objective carried out
in this thesis. The pressure impulse model developed at DTU was veriﬁed on the experiments
conducted at DHI. The model was satisfactorily able to compute the pressure impulse, which is
the time integral of the wave impact that occurs in a very short time span. The time instant at
which the input geometric parameters of the pressure impulse model is extracted was instrumental
in reducing the error in the ﬁnal pressure impulse force. It would be ideal if the method depended
on the time-series of wave surface elevation as the user of the OceanWave3D kinematics would
ﬁnd it simpler. But the method suggested in this thesis, gives reasonable accuracy.
From the knowledge gained by verifying that the OceanWave3D can replicate wave kinematics
accurately and the pressure impulse model can compute slamming loads, a recipe was formulated
to integrate the slamming loads on top of the force computed through OceanWave3D kinematics.
Resolution of the hypothetical wave gauges or the location of sampling depth was critical in this
analysis as ﬁnding the time instant of geometric parameter extraction depended on it. Multiple
investigation was carried out to ﬁnd the right method for computing relative impact height, crest
length and velocity of the wave. Overall, the validation gave encouraging results. One major
observation was that the number of slamming events in the simulation was diﬀerent to that ob-
served in the experiments. This could be down to the simple breaking ﬁlter used in the simulation.
Improving the breaking ﬁlter might help identify slamming instances better.
An initial attempt was also made at integrating the computed impulse force with OceanWave3D
simulations and carrying out a numerical analysis using HAWC2. The impulse force was placed
on the monopile as an external force. The magnitude and time-span of the impulse was computed
by combining the already gained knowledge with the conclusions of Wienke-Oumearci model for
slamming on slender cylinder. The accuracy of combining these two methods was not investigated
in this thesis, but reasonable results were generated considering it was the ﬁrst attempt. Another
mismatch was in terms of the time step of wave kinematic simulation and the numerical model,
72

with the former ten times smaller leading to interpolations.
For future investigations it is recommended to:
• expand the OceanWave3D simulations to three-dimensional cases
• improve the breaking ﬁlter in OceanWave3D to get more accurate breaking instances
• improve the method of integrating pressure impulse for numerical analysis
• carry out a DLC numerical study with the impulse force included for diﬀerent test cases
In conclusion, the present work has contributed to a successful validation of the potential wave
solver OceanWave3D in replicating sea-states and the pressure impulse model in computing the
loads corresponding to slamming events. It adds reliability and conﬁdence in both OceanWave3D
and the pressure impulse model which will directly contribute towards the oﬀshore wind energy
industry research.
