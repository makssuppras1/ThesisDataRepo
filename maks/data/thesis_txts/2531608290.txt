Abstract
In 2013, Vietnam and Denmark established a long-term cooperation agreement to
strengthen Vietnam’s transition towards a low-carbon economy.
The Danish Energy
Agency (DEA) cooperates with the Ministry of Industry and Trade in Vietnam through
the joint Energy Partnership Program between Vietnam and Denmark (DEPP). Among
the many topics, the program covers long-term scenario modeling and analysis of the
energy sector. This thesis aims to enrich such analyses.
With the signiﬁcant increase in demand expected in Vietnam in the future, the gen-
eration ﬂeet is going to grow its capacity substantially. Vietnam is prosperous in natural
resources. Solar and wind technologies will be less costly in the upcoming future. In-
deed, with the RE targets in force in Vietnam, the share of RES in the generation mix
is expected to increase from the current decade. A high level of RES requires ﬂexibility
of the system to operate optimally. For this purpose, interconnections with neighboring
countries could be a partial solution.
This thesis serves as a ﬁrst attempt to analyze the impact of interconnectors as an
alternative source of ﬂexibility to exploit RES. The extensive modeling of Laos and Cam-
bodia energy systems is implemented, and their interactions with Vietnam are studied in
detail. The study focuses on the impact of such a feature on RES share of generation,
internal transmission, system costs, electricity price, and CO2 emission.
Vietnam could gain several beneﬁts from interconnections with Laos and Cambodia.
Primarily, in terms of RES generation share of total demand, which increases of around
30%, from the BAU case. Further, the reduction of conventional unit capacity leads to
lower dependency on fuel imports, one of the main issues highlighted in the EOR19.
Further, Vietnam could lower its yearly CO2 emissions from the energy sector up to
7%. Even though the high cost of imports leads to greater system costs, the average
electricity price would decrease and gain uniformity with Laos and Cambodia. However,
an integrated market needs robust regulations to be reliable for its participants.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
For the last decades, Vietnam has been one of the fastest-growing economies in Asia,
with a GDP growth rate above 6%. The energy sector plays a critical role in the contin-
ued development of Vietnam, and access to affordable and reliable energy will be essen-
tial to sustain its economic growth. The achievement of the Paris Agreement goals, for
greenhouse gas emission reduction, depends on the development pathway that emerging
economies like Vietnam will take. Considering the domestic resource potential for solar
and wind power, Vietnam has a signiﬁcant opportunity to embark on a sustainable devel-
opment path. The cost of these technologies, as well as battery storage, is declining, and
consequently generates an opportunity for a green energy transition in Vietnam. How-
ever, such a pathway entails certain challenges to expand and integrate renewable energy
into the energy system in the most affordable way. These challenges have to be addressed
by policy action.
Developing countries lack knowledge and tools to embark on the green transition,
making essential the cooperation with countries, that already have a reliable and advanced
energy system with a large share of renewables. Since 2013, Vietnam and Denmark have
entered into a long-term cooperation agreement to strengthen the Vietnamese transition
towards a low-carbon economy. The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) and the Ministry
of Industry and Trade in Vietnam cooperate within the framework of the joint Energy
Partnership Program between Vietnam and Denmark (DEPP). Currently approaching the
end of the second phase (DEPP II, 2017-2020), the Program covers, among the topics,
long-term scenario modeling of the energy sector and the integration of renewable energy
in the power grid.
Among all the studies and reports released within this cooperation, one is of particular
importance for this thesis, the EOR19 [1]. The Energy Outlook is grounded in a well

documented and detailed modeling of the energy system and was developed with the aim
of providing a scenario-based foundation for policy action by exploring different least-
cost development pathways of the Vietnamese energy system towards 2050. The EOR19
aims to guide policy makers and inspire deliberation on green transition while being the
starting point for the forthcoming National Power Development Plan 8 (PDP8) and the
Energy Master Plan.
In any modern energy system, interconnections with neighboring countries have a
key role in guaranteeing ﬂexibility and reliability to grid operations. It is also thanks
to the improvement of interconnections and multilateral trading systems that RES have
become technically and economically feasible around the world. In the previous works,
including the EOR19, this feature was analyzed in a simpliﬁed manner, the reason why
various stakeholders have requested for more insights on the topic. Moreover, it is of
particular importance to include it in the PDP8 since a National plan has to consider all
the opportunities for the future development of a national energy system.
Vietnam is currently connected with Laos, Cambodia, and China, without proper reg-
ulation, being the power trade only through bilateral contracts. On the other hand, the
ASEAN market develops fast, the demand for energy grows accordingly, and Vietnam,
like all the other countries, could gain various beneﬁts from a multilateral power trad-
ing system. For instance, it could allow to exchange power with the neighbors when it
is economically convenient to do so or when there is an excess or a lack of generation,
especially during the dry season when water in the hydropower reservoirs is scarce. In ad-
dition, the renewable technologies potential could be fully exploited, and the investments
in conventional plants and the import/export of fuel reduced to the minimum. Therefore,
improving interconnectors is imperative not only for Vietnam but all the South-East Asia
Nations.
This thesis serves as a ﬁrst study of this feature in the Vietnamese context.
Owing to the mentioned methodologies and objectives, this Master’s thesis aims at
providing answers to the following core research questions:
• How would the Vietnamese energy system develop if interconnectors will improve?
• which opportunities, in terms of RES exploitation, could interconnections with
neighboring countries offer to Vietnam?
• What would be the impact of interconnections on CO2 emissions, system cost and
electricity price in Vietnam?

2.1
Energy Markets Fundamentals
This section introduces the traditional day-ahead energy markets, which describes the
theory behind the Balmorel model. Here the basics for understanding and analyzing the
results are presented. Every hour, according to the classical theory of supply and demand,
Day-ahead markets are cleared, as shown in Figure 2.1.
The optimization process of most energy system models (see Section 2.2), includ-
ing Balmorel, integrates only the day-ahead market. Day-ahead markets are cleared 24
hours in advance to the day of delivery. In essence, producers make their bid around
noon of day d-1, and, the merit order includes them, the upcoming day, they will deliver
a precise amount of energy on an hourly basis. This mechanism forces generators to dis-
patch a speciﬁc volume 12 to 36 hours in advance; apart from unexpected outages and
other unplanned breakdowns that can always happen to conventional units, the impor-
tance of intra-day and balancing markets increases when there is a large share of RES.
The ﬂuctuating nature of renewable energy sources and the resulting challenge for accu-
rate forecasting is the main reason for it.
Even though Intra-day markets work according to the merit order curve, they are
designed in different ways across the globe, while the representation of real-time markets
is challenging. Despite an increase in the renewable energy share, the cash ﬂows of intra-
day and real-time markets are foreseen to remain a few percents of the day-ahead also

2.2
Energy system models
2.2.1
Generalities
The ﬁrst attempts to develop large-scale energy system models for policy design are
documented in the 1970s when their importance was recognized, and computational time
reached a high-enough level to process extensive simulations [12]. It is primarily from the
1990s and the signing of the Kyoto Protocol in 1997 that ESMs (Energy System Models)
have focused on the integration of renewable energy in the energy system and green-
house gas (GHG) reduction, which introduced parameters without the pure economic
convenience (externalities excluded). In Europe, renewable energy has been subsidized
already in 1991, ﬁrst in Germany and the UK [13], and conventional units discouraged
with the use of market instruments such as the EU ETS-Emission Trading System (Di-
rective 2003/87/EC [14]). From an energy point of view, Europe is a very interlinked
entity technical- and political-wise. There are transmission lines, which allow multilat-
eral power exchange between the member States. While the EU shares tools to achieve
pre-determined, common targets, such as the ETS mentioned above, South-East Asia has
==PAGE:19==
2.2. ENERGY SYSTEM MODELS
7
no electricity market structure and not every country is well interconnected with its neigh-
boring countries. There have been studies already about the development of the ASEAN
market, such as the ASEAN Energy Outlook (AEO5) [7], the Southeast Asia Energy Out-
look (WEO17) [9], the ASEAN Plan of Actions for Energy Cooperation (APAEC) [8].
However, there are no country-speciﬁc studies for Vietnam and how it could beneﬁt from
the multilateral exchange of power with neighboring countries. This thesis serves as the
ﬁrst analysis of this topic.
ESMs are grounded on both technical and economic input. It is impossible to aim at
complete information, and a detailed representation of the reality, and, therefore, simpli-
ﬁcations are unavoidable, and less complicated relationships are needed to describe it. In
any energy model, a simpliﬁcation of a past or present system provides a robust but lean
characterization of existing structures; next, assumptions help designing future trajecto-
ries. The two main approaches used in large-scale ESMs are top-down and bottom-up.
Top-down models are especially suited when different sectors are under consideration
[15]. On the other hand, bottom-up models, such as Balmorel, are more convenient and
informative when only the energy sector is analyzed since this approach focuses on a nar-
row part of the economy. These models choose the most efﬁcient or least costly solution
employing from a set of disaggregated data and a structure. In most of the cases, large-
scale models obtain convergence using Linear Programming (LP) techniques, which are
the state-of-the-art for problems of such a size, since more advanced methods demand
high computational power. Nevertheless, ESMs employing only LP techniques neglect
operational constraints that describe the operation of energy plants. In order to make
the modeling closer to reality, such constraints need the use of integer variables (Mixed
Integer Linear Programming, MILP), which can solve a classic unit commitment. The
common goal of an optimization process is to ﬁnd the structures s (i.e., the size of the
energy system) and the relative operations so that they minimize the total system costs.
These can be decomposed into capital (CAPEX) and operational expenditures (OPEX):
minZ = min(a · CAPEX(s) + OPEX(s, o))
(2.1)
Where z represents the problem’s objective function and the factor a the annualizes capital
expenditures. This function drives a large-scale ESM like Balmorel towards the optimal
solution. However, each problem has a different design of structural parameters and of
the framework where the system operates.
2.2.2
Challenges
The analyst faces a series of difﬁculties in order to represent the reality of being op-
timized. The study [16] explains the four major challenges related to energy system
==PAGE:20==
8
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND KNOWLEDGE
models. In essence, they are related to the representation of time and space, to the system
complexity, to the transparency and the uncertainty of the results, to behavioral and social
aspects. This work treats all the problems apart from the latter, which is difﬁcult to assess
and rarely accounted for. The details can be found in the next Chapters.
Time and space are paramount parameters in ESMs. An hourly resolution is needed
to provide an accurate representation of both storage technologies and energy markets.
The Day-Ahead market, described in Section 2.1, is cleared in advance for every hour
of the next day. A year is composed of 8760 hours, which makes it challenging to run
investment decisions in rolling horizon run mode due to the high computational time
required. Time aggregation becomes necessary in order to combine investment decisions
and hourly energy dispatch within the same model. A simpliﬁed but enough informative
aggregation is also needed space-wise since geography sets the system boundaries for
energy ﬂows. The model recreates zones where the markets are cleared or where local or
national policies are implemented.
Energy systems have a high degree of complexity that increases based on the level
of modeling detail to be represented. Neglecting technical modeling of power and heat
grids and the extensive functioning of systems for energy generation can reduce system
complexity.
The main aim of large-scale ESMs is essential to support decision-making and pol-
icy design, and, therefore, these features can be neglected. The aggregated transmission
between regions is enough to analyze market and grid issues, such as bottlenecks and
congestion prices. However, a combination of different tools can be employed to provide
a more detailed analysis of the desired topics. For instance, for the publication of the
EOR19, described in the later sections, Balmorel was combined with the power system
model PSSE to assess fault analysis and operational details of the electric grid.
The future evolution of costs, prices, demand trends, and other parameters come from
uncertain assumptions or analysis, which lead to uncertain results. The use of scenar-
ios to test different choices of the exogenous variables becomes essential to understand
the reliability of the results thoroughly. The modeler chooses an exogenous variable (or
parameter) and gives it as an input. Typical examples are renewables proﬁles and techno-
logical costs. Every ESM has different types of exogenous variables. For Balmorel, the
relevant ones are presented in the next Section.
ESMs consider the societal perspective, which turns into a set of parameters that re-
ﬂects risks, interests, costs, and beneﬁts for the community as a whole. For instance,
common welfare accounts for environmental consequences, that requires the inclusion of
externality cost, such as costs related to GHG emission.
In the following, a description of Balmorel is carried out by brieﬂy discussing its
strengths, weaknesses, and the features directly connected to this project.

2.2.3
Balmorel model in brief
Balmorel, the “BALtic MOdel of Regional Electricity Liberalized,” saw the light at
the beginning of the century to represent the combined power and heat sector around
the Baltic Sea [17]. Originally, ELkraft (now Energinet.dk) and the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark (DTU) developed the model. Currently, research centers, universities,
and private companies operating mainly in the Nordic region make extensive use of it.
Speciﬁcally, the version used for this thesis is the one developed by EA Energy Analysis
[20] for Vietnam, which carries all the features and structures of the original model, al-
though with different geography and data input. The Balmorel model is an open-source,
bottom-up, partial-equilibrium model that optimizes the operations and the investments
of a large-scale energy system. The code is developed in GAMS, the General Alge-
braic Modeling System, a high-level modeling tool for mathematical programming and
optimization [19]. The model converges to the optimal solution through linear program-
ming techniques, and it integrates integer variables when the problem size allows for
it and when the analysis needs speciﬁc operational characteristics of the technologies.
Figure 2.2 shows a representation of the model setup. Regarding the input data, Bal-
morel requires a set of parameters to be deﬁned exogenously for the years considered
in the simulations. These are demand projections, fuel prices, existing and committed

Different options for model runs
Balmorel can be run in four different ’Balbase’ (Bb) options that make it ﬁt a variety of
scopes; the Bb4 option is not described here since it is not in use at EA Energy Analyses.
The other three options are presented below:
• BB1 - Operation Optimization with yearly resolution: the problem is linear, and
the model optimizes operations and chooses to dispatch the cheapest generations.
The capacity installed is an exogenous parameter.
• BB2 - Operation and Investment Optimization: include in the model investments
in generation and transmission capacity, and the outcome is a result of joint opti-
mization of operations and investments. The new capacity added to the system are
the most proﬁtable ones, while a plant is decommissioned if it is more convenient
to invest and operate others instead.
• BB3 - Operation Optimization with weekly resolution: equivalent to BB1, be-
sides a difference in the time horizon, which in this case is a single week. A sim-
ulation of a year corresponds to 52 weekly optimizations. The main difference
is how hydropower operations are optimized, with a much lower time horizon of
perfect knowledge. Furthermore, some speciﬁc features, e.g., solar daily peak, de-
mand proﬁle or storage operations, require an hourly resolution to be realistically
represented.
Geography and time
Geography offers a sufﬁciently wide array of spatial entities were to deﬁne parameters
and constraints. These entities can act as a control volume when related to physical
quantities, or as space where economic structures and policies are established.
There are three different spatial layers in Balmorel: Country, Region, and Area in
decreasing order of size [18]. Policies, targets, and fuel prices are deﬁned for every
Country, Energy markets are cleared in each Region separately, and transmission lines
connect Regions to evaluate possible congestions and bottlenecks.

In the Balmorel representation, there can be only one line linking two separate Re-
gions. In order to keep the balance between supply and demand, the model can choose
to invest in new transmission infrastructures. Depending on the scope of the analysis and
on the type of quantity, Areas can, for example, represent pieces of land characterized by
high wind speeds. Depending on the type of analysis conducted, the degree of spatial res-

The ASEAN Centre for Energy (ACE) is an intergovernmental organization, estab-

A secure and reliable electricity infrastructure is essential to support regional eco-

1. ensure regional security by promoting the effective utilization and resource sharing
for common regional beneﬁt;
2. facilitate cross-border electricity trading to obtain:
– reliable, efﬁcient, and economical operations;
– effective utilization of resources across geographical locations;

Political framework
In Vietnam, the government drafts plans and strategies towards the objectives in the

together with EREA for the EOR 2019, which will be the baseline for the PDP8 .
Other plans to establish the development path of competitive power markets in Viet-
nam, such as The Electricity Law (2005) and the PM’s Power Market Roadmap (2006),
are already in place.

2.4.2
Electricity market status
This part covers the electricity market functionality in the South-East Asian country.
This section is necessary to have a better understanding of the real status of the Viet-
namese electricity market.
Electricity of Vietnam (EVN) is the local and only utility, which is state-owned. In
addition, EVN is in charge of operating the grid as system operator. EVN is responsible
for smaller entities that are in charge of speciﬁc tasks.
The Transmission and Distribution System Operator form the entity called National
Power Transmission (NPT). On the other hand, there are Power Corporations (PCs) which
work as electricity retailers. Finally, also the National Load Dispatch Center (NLDC) is
under the management of EVN. This entity is responsible for keeping the demand and
supply balance.
Apart from EVN, there are many other producers known as Independent Power Pro-
ducers (IPP). These producers can monetize the energy they produce in three ways:
1. Feed-In-Tarif. This support is available for some technologies and it works as in
Europe.
2. Power Purchase Agreements. It is possible to do it with EVN. However, PPAs are
not in place nowadays.
3. Electricity Market. Explained in detail below.
The market structure in Vietnam is different compared to European standards. In Novem-
ber 2013, the Prime Minister approved a road map to liberalize the electricity market
(Decision No 63/2013/QD-TTg). Until then, the generator revenues were determined by
the government with PPAs. This new plan started in 2016 with the introduction of a pilot
Electricity Wholesale Electricity Market (VWEM) for suppliers, that today is in full oper-
ation. Every producer can bid in the spot market, which works under the same principles
as in the European Union. The liberalization is planned to be completed in 2023 when
the Electricity Retail Market will enter in full operation.

2.4.4
Interconnections with neighboring countries
Multilateral trading allows higher electricity ﬂows from regions with lower generation
costs, smoothing the electricity price level of neighboring regions. The ﬂexibility that
interconnections can offer is essential for future energy systems with a large share of
RES. The ﬂuctuations of RES are a signiﬁcant issue when integrating these technologies
into the system, and the possibility to use transmission lines as a virtual storage can,
in part, solve it. Therefore, interconnections play a key role in integrating renewables
in fast-growing energy systems, where signiﬁcant investments in storage systems and
transmission, depending on what is more convenient in the different areas, are essential
to enhance ﬂexibility, cost-effectiveness, and security of the system.
Vietnam’s borders offer a connection to Laos, Cambodia, and China. These regions
have a variety of energy sources that are different in each region. To fully exploit the
potential of such natural sources, there is a need to exchange energy with each other.
For instance, Cambodia is rich in solar irradiation, which unfortunately has a variable
daily pattern 2.7. During the mid-day peak, the power in excess could be exported to
Laos, which has a huge potential for hydropower and could store this energy in one of

CHAPTER 4
Scenario design
Taking into account both the focus and framework of the analysis and the modelling
choices, the different scenarios analysed are presented in this section. The scenarios
described are three, ﬁrst the business as usual and then the two scenarios designed to
further investigate the interconnections with Laos and Cambodia.
4.1
Business As Usual (BAU)
The results found with the new Balmorel setup discussed in Section 3 have to be
compared to a reference scenario, in order to see the advantages generated by intercon-
nections with neighboring countries and demand response. For the purpose of this thesis,
the BAU scenario is the C1_RE_target scenario developed by EA, DEA and IE within
the framework of the EOR19. This theoretical scenario simulates a future development
of the Vietnamese energy system, and, in order to do so, the model employs all the data
and modeling features of the Balmorel setup used for the EOR19 [1]. The scenario also
includes the renewable energy (RE) target for the power sector set out in the current Re-
newable Energy Development Strategy (REDS) shown in Figure 2.6 and described in
Section 2.4.3. With this setup, the ﬁxed import with hourly proﬁle, mentioned in the
previous Sections, constitutes the modeling of neighboring countries.
4.1.1
Security of supply (SOS)
One of the main concerns, when developing multilateral energy trading with other
countries, is to supply your own demand if there is any issue with the interconnectors.
This is an essential theme since countries, that start now to exchange energy with others,
have to guarantee power supply to the population.

4.1.2
Limited interconnectors capacity (LIC)
In the base case, interconnections expansion have no limits for new investments in
transmission lines between Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia, in order to study the best case
scenario, when they can freely expand. However, to be closer to reality, there should be
some limitations on the interconnectors expansion. Indeed, there are studies that highlight
the potential of transmission in the ASEAN region, such as [35], that can be a reference
for the maximum interconnection capacity. Table 4.1 shows the transmission potential in
the ASEAN region from [35], speciﬁcally between the countries studied in this paper.

4.1. Beginning from null capacity in 2024, the missing data from 2025 to 2034 and
from 2036 to 2049 are retrieved through linear interpolation. For Laos, that is linked
with more than one Vietnamese region, the maximum capacity is split proportionally
according with the demand of the relative Vietnamese regions. This division leads to
76% towards North, 13% towards North-Central, 8% towards Center-Central, and 3%
towards Highland. In Balmorel, there is a parameter called XMAXINV which allows to
set maximum investments in transmission capacity for every year.

CHAPTER 5
Results Analysis
This Section discusses the results of the neighboring countries’ modeling. First, Cam-
bodia and Laos are analyzed to better understand their interactions with the Vietnamese
energy system. Further, Vietnam and the cooperation with its neighbors are studied com-
pare to the BAU scenario explained in Chapter 4. For this concern, the scenario regarding
the modelling of neighboring countries as explained in Chapter 3 is called INT for sim-
pliﬁcation. In the second part of the chapter, the results from the Security Of Supply
(SOS) and the Limited Interconnectors Capacity (LIC) scenarios are discussed and com-
pared to the BAU and INT cases. Finally, the last part explains the sensitivity analysis on
interconnection investment cost.
5.1
Laos and Cambodia
The energy sector development in Laos and Cambodia expects considerable growth
in terms of capacity and generation in the future, in order to meet their energy demand
and to trade energy with the neighboring countries. Generation capacity in Laos doubles
from 2020 (12.6 [GW]) to 2030 (25.7 [GW]), and later faces a more moderate increase in
2040 (31.2 [GW]) and 2050 (31.8 [GW]) (Figure 5.1). This capacity is more than enough
for Laos since it always covers at least ﬁve times the peak demand. Considering the peak
of energy export to Thailand is 3.2 [GW], there is a signiﬁcant capacity left for exports
to Vietnam. Hydropower dominates the scene and increases its capacity throughout the
years until the resource is fully exploited (26 [GW]) in 2050. There are not investments
in coal plants, indeed substituted by biomass and municipal waste in the energy mix. The
reason why is that, even though these technologies have slightly higher investment and
operational costs than coal technologies, they have lower fuel costs, which appear to lead
the investment choice towards them. Indeed, the fuel price of biomass is around 12 %
the one of imported coal, while fuel cost of municipal waste are null. The characteristics

Discussion
6.1
Results Overview
6.1.1
Interconnections neighboring countries
The modeling of interconnections with neighboring countries seems to bring several
beneﬁts for Vietnam. First of all, the demand covered by RES generation is always at
least 32% (see Figure 5.4, while in the BAU scenario, the maximum was 19%. Solar PV
is the most advantaged technology, with 34% more capacity in 2030 and 2040. On the
other hand, wind power better exploits its potential in 2050, when the cost of the invest-
ment decreases enough to lead 56% more investments in the technology compared to the
BAU scenario. This increase of RES capacity comes along with a reduction in conven-
tional units and batteries capacity. Indeed, the ﬂexibility given by interconnectors allows
the system to have lower peak capacity installed, primarily reducing the dependency from
imported coal. The decrease of battery capacity happens mainly in the regions with the
biggest imports from neighbors, North and South (see Table 5.2. Indeed, Cambodia and
Laos are expected to be net-exporter towards Vietnam. Furthermore, the energy ﬂows
explained in the Table show the convenience for Vietnam to send energy from Highland
through Laos in order to reach the North, instead of building internal transmission. In-
deed, generation, especially from solar PV, increases in Highland while it decreases in the
North. The only drawback of interconnections is the increase in system costs compared to
BAU. Even though fuel and capital costs decreased, the cost of imports have a signiﬁcant
impact on the overall costs, which lead to the increase explained in Figure 5.7. However,
the interaction with other markets makes the average electricity price lower than the BAU.
The whole purpose of integrating RES into the energy system is to lower CO2 emission
levels. Developing multilateral energy trading with Laos and Cambodia, Vietnam could
lower its yearly CO2 emissions by around 6-7% compared to the BAU case.

6.1.2
Security of supply restriction
The different levels of security of supply rates lead to interesting variation compared
to the INT scenario. When the minimum supply with the national generation is 85% or
lower, the ﬁnal result is the same as INT, while for higher rates, it changes. Capacity
and generation vary according to the percentage of demand covered by its own supply,
shown in Table 5.7. Indeed, they are higher if the percentage is higher, which leads to an
increase in capacity and generation along with the increase of the SOS rate. The tech-
nologies facing the most signiﬁcant variations are coal-ﬁred based(see Figure 5.8), that
can guarantee cheap energy anytime. When the SOS rate increases, power exchange with
other countries decreases, and, as a consequence, the capacity and generation of imported
coal technologies increase. The greatest variations are in North and South, the regions
with the highest demand and energy imports. The capacity of batteries is already lower in
the INT than the BAU scenario. With the security of supply limitation, their employment
reduces with higher SOS rates. The reasons why are that more energy is directly used
to cover the Vietnamese demand, and there are more conventional units installed com-
pared to INT. Wind and solar technologies have nearly the same generation capacity and
generation in any SOS scenario, that is another signal of how much Vietnam can bene-
ﬁt from interconnectors to integrate RES. Indeed, even though Vietnam can not receive
big energy amounts from Laos and Cambodia, he still can send them the excess gener-
ation from RES, minimizing their curtailment. Indeed, while imports decrease, exports
towards adjacent countries increase the higher the SOS rate (see Figure 5.9). Even though
the cost of imports reduces with higher SOS rates, the system costs are still higher than
the BAU scenario when transmission with Laos and Cambodia is allowed. However, the
electricity price seems to beneﬁt from the lower generation and energy exchange between
countries. Indeed, it decreases with the increase of the SOS rate due to lower generation.
With such limitation, another drawback arises; indeed, CO2 emissions appear to increase
with the higher SOS rates, being higher than BAU in any case but SOS85 (same as INT).
Therefore, lower SOS rates imply lower generation and capacity, lower emissions, higher
imports, higher system costs, higher average electricity prices.
6.1.3
Transmission capacity restriction
When the amount of energy import and export is reduced to the minimum, the ef-
fect of power interconnections is still positive in terms of RES exploitation. Indeed, with
a reduction of over 80% of the energy ﬂows between countries, solar power still has a
similar capacity and generation to the INT case, which are even higher for wind tech-
nologies. With such a limitation on interconnections capacity, in order to always supply
the energy required and guarantee the necessary ﬂexibility to the system, Vietnam installs

more imported coal and battery units in the LIC than the INT scenario. The technology
which beneﬁts the most from this combination is wind onshore, which has more variabil-
ity than solar power throughout the day. The total capacity installed and power generation
in Vietnam in the LIC scenario is the largest of all scenarios. As a consequence, the in-
crease in capital cost in the overall system costs is greater than the decrease in the cost
of imports, which makes the LIC scenario the one with the highest costs for Vietnam.
The effect on electricity price is the loss of uniformity within the countries, due to the
reduction of power exchange. Also CO2 emissions face a negative effect since the gen-
eration from fossil fuels increases. They are, indeed, the highest of all scenarios. This
scenario is the only one respecting the Memorandum of Understanding between Laos and
Vietnam, which foresees 5 [GW] of interconnection capacity between those countries in
2030. This fact, combined with the limitations applied, which restrict investments in the
interconnector capacities, makes this scenario the most tangible.
6.1.4
Sensitivity on investment cost of transmission lines
The increase in investment cost of the most used transmission lines connecting Laos
and Vietnam do not present a signiﬁcant variation on the results if analyzed on a national
level. The main effect is exclusively on the power exchanged between the countries,
which increases between Vietnam and Cambodia, while it decreases between Vietnam
and Laos. The most signiﬁcant implications happen, as expected, to Highland and North,
which trade considerably lower amounts of energy with Laos. The result is to have less
solar capacity installed in Highland and more coal and solar plants in the North. This
fact demonstrates that Highland uses Laos to transfer and store energy, from solar and
wind generation, towards North that has worse natural resources. At the same time, solar
power capacity in the South and South-Central increases, leading to additional power
ﬂows towards Cambodia compared to the INT scenario.
6.1.5
Limitations
One of the main limitations of this analysis is the scarce differentiation of local re-
sources. Laos and Cambodia have accurate local data for solar proﬁles and full load
hours. However, the characteristics of wind and hydro are the same as those deﬁned in
the most similar Vietnamese region. It is difﬁcult to quantify the impact of this assump-
tion, which probably slightly changes the resulting investments and the operations of such
technologies. However, the most signiﬁcant limitation is to have just one region repre-
senting a whole country, especially for Laos, which can be interconnected with several
Vietnamese regions. The lack of internal transmission in Laos leads to high energy ﬂows

from Highland to the North going through Laos. It would be interesting to analyze the
convenience of this action if cost and losses for internal transmission in Laos were con-
sidered. Further, the existing and planned power plants are represented as an aggregation
of more units, which limits the variability of generation and does not consider different
timing for decommissioning. Another substantial limitation regards the demand proﬁles

whole year, which consequently does not consider peaks and reduces the precision of the

emission does not account for the direct emissions released by Laos and Cambodia to
generate the energy exported to Vietnam. The emissions released by them but accounted
as Vietnamese are based on the percentage of yearly net-export to Vietnam on their yearly
generation. However, the emissions from Laos and Cambodia are considerably low, due
to the high hydropower generation; therefore, the emissions from imports cover a tiny part
of the total Vietnam emissions and should not have such an impact on the ﬁnal result.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The stringent climate issues and the ambitious decarbonization targets declared in the
Paris Agreement will challenge the future development of the Vietnamese energy system.
In a system that heads towards high levels of renewables, the need to stabilize and balance
the generation from intermittent sources will promote the installation of transmission lines
to connect adjacent countries. While the future interactions with the Chinese energy
system are still uncertain, a multilateral trading system within the ASEAN countries is
planned to enter in operation in the upcoming future. This work has analyzed the potential
beneﬁts that improved interconnections with neighboring countries within the ASEAN
organization could offer to Vietnam, primarily in terms of RES integration.
This study shows that Vietnam could gain several beneﬁts from interconnections with
Laos and Cambodia, primarily, in terms of RES generation share of total demand, which
increases of around 30% in all the scenarios analyzed in the thesis. Further, multilateral
trading could bring along a reduction of storage capacity and conventional units, which
are usually employed to guarantee ﬂexibility and peak capacity to the energy system.
The fossil fuel capacities, which decrease, are mostly run with imported fuels, especially
coal. Indeed, this reduction means lower dependency on fuel imports, one of the issues
highlighted in the EOR19.
The employment of RES in the energy system has the primary purpose of lowering
CO2 emissions. With the possibility to exchange power with other countries, Vietnam
could lower its CO2 emission around 6-7%. However, if the security of supply rate has
to be at least 90%, they would increase due to more conventional unit generation. The
level of GHG released in the air by Vietnam would also increase in the case when the
interconnection capacity available for investments is restricted to a limited amount.
The main drawback of this feature is that the cost of imports would lead to an increase
in the overall system costs in Vietnam. On the other hand, the average electricity price
would decrease and gain uniformity with Laos and Cambodia, while interconnections

improve. However, by limiting the trading within countries, either by increasing the SOS
rate or by restricting the interconnection capacity available for investments, the price
would lose uniformity within the different countries. To avoid the strategic behavior of
market participants, the parties have to establish a series of policies to regulate power
exchange ﬁnancially.
This study has assessed the future perspectives of RES in Vietnam when multilateral
trading with neighboring countries will be efﬁcient.
7.1
Further work
This thesis has proved that interconnections with neighboring countries could offer
great beneﬁts in terms of RES integration in Vietnam. The generation ﬂeet is in fast evo-
lution, and the climate issue pushes for new, constantly updated national plans. These
strategies require an ongoing revision of the models, e.g., with planned shut-downs of
power plants to be permanently replaced. Recently, Vietnam has developed a Demand
Side Management (DSM) program, which aims to develop in the next years. This feature
could also generate ﬂexibility for the Vietnamese energy system and help the advent of
renewables. Indeed, it would be interesting to study the impact of such a feature and to
combine it with interconnectors. Regarding the analysis of interconnections with neigh-
boring countries, the modeling of Laos and Cambodia could be further developed. The
level of details could be improved, especially with more insights on the speciﬁc power
plants and with a regional subdivision of the countries, in order to describe more in-depth
resources and interactions with the Vietnamese system. A revision of the parameters reg-
ulating the power transmission between neighboring countries is needed to conﬁrm that
the national results are not inﬂuenced by the exploitation of resources in other States. Fi-
nally, the energy exchanged between Laos and Thailand is substantial. Full-on modeling
of Thailand could provide further improvement to the study of the Vietnamese advantages
from interconnections between ASEAN countries.
