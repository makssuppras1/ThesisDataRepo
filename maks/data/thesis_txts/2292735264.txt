1.0 Introduction  
1.1 Tilapia 
Tilapia is a common name describing pure fish species and hybrids belonging to genera 
Tilapia, Sarotherodon and Oreochromis of   family Cichlidae. Tilapias are largest group of 
warm freshwater fish in tropical and subtropical countries. According to Wohlfarth & 
Hulata (1981) Tilapia are native to Africa, the Mediterranean and the Middle East, but 
currently are most farmed fish species all over the world. Looking at the production status, 
China is the world’s largest producer. Other significant producers are Egypt, Philippines, 
Thailand, Indonesia, Latin America, Ghana Germany, Belgium, Spain, Canada, Korea and 
Japan(FAO, 2014). The most significant and plentiful in aquaculture production is Nile 
tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus). The suitability of this species is attributed by several 
factors: Ease breeding in captivity, tolerance to both to high stocking density and poor 
water quality parameters and less susceptible to fish diseases(Watanabe et al., 2002). 
1.2 Metabolism in fish 
Metabolism refers to a sequence of biochemical processes that occur in living organisms. It 
comprises of catabolism (breakdown of larger substances into small constituents e.g. food 
substrates) and anabolic (buildup of large substances from small constituents e.g. proteins 
from Amino acids) processes. Catabolism processes convert food energy into adenosine 
triphosphate commonly called ATP. ATP is then used for fuelling anabolism processes. 
Quantifying the ATP turnovers (termed as direct calorimetry) would provide a best 
estimate of metabolic rates in fish. Unfortunately it is not the case due to: high heat 
capacity of water, low heat production and low fish metabolic activity, which collectively 
restrict the metabolic measurements in fish. Consequently, alternative approach termed 
‘’indirect calorimetry’’ which involves the use of oxygen consumption rates has been 
employed for measuring metabolic rates in fishes(Cech Jr and Brauner, 2011; Nelson and 
Chabot, 2011).  
Oxygen is need for oxidizing food substances to release ATP during aerobic metabolism 
(Aerobic respiration). However, in some cases (predator escaping, prey capture, hypoxia 
environments, intense exercise) anaerobic metabolism (anaerobic respiration) is necessary 
for ATP production (Frappell & Butler 2004). Oxygen consumption rates of fish, hence 
metabolic rates can be described with respect to fish activities.

The Standard metabolic rate (SMR) is the minimum oxygen consumption rate vital to 
keep a resting fish alive.  Resting fish needs energy for biosynthesis of new body tissues 
(chiefly protein synthesis), potential energy gradient work (ion pumps) blood pumping by 
heart (Clarke & Fraser 2004). SMR is usually measured in unfed, resting, non- reproducing 
or growing fish.  
The oxygen consumption rate  of fish due to spontaneous activities is termed as Routine 
metabolic rate (RMR) (Fry 1957). According to Fry (1971) and Brett (1972) ,the oxygen 
consumption rate above the RMR is called Maximum metabolic rate, (MMR). The 
MMR reflects the maximum aerobic tolerance capacity of fish during prolonged exercise.  
The difference between MMR and SMR is called metabolic scope, (MS) and it describes 
the available power for all energy-demanding activities (e.g. growth, locomotion, 
swimming and reproduction) above the animal basic requirements (Fig. 1)

1.3 Effect of temperature on metabolism 
Endotherms are able to maintain constant body temperature of 37°C which is independent 
of their environmental temperatures. These animals maintain their body temperature at 
expenses of high energetic cost. By contrast, Ectoderms are incapable of regulating their 
body temperature independent to their environmental temperatures. Consequently, their 
metabolic activities are intensely influenced by environmental temperatures. Some fish 
species such as Yellow fin (Thunnus albacaris), Skipjack tuna (Katsuwonus pelamis) and 
White shark (Carcharodon carcharias) are able to regulate their internal body temperature 
by vascular counter current heat exchanger (Brill et al., 1994; Carey and Teal, 1969, 1966; 
Carey et al., 1981; Carey and Scott, 1972; Goldman, 1997). These fish species possess the 
thermoregulatory blood capillaries system called ‘Rete mirabile which enables them to 
regulate their internal temperatures.  Rete mirabile is located between the deep red 
swimming muscles. Muscles create heat due to physical activities. Then, the heat is 
transported by blood vessels carrying deoxygenated blood towards the gills. While the 
Blood vessels carrying oxygenated blood from the gills flows in opposite direction towards 
the muscles therefore regulating the temperature by counter current exchange. Still these 
fish species are not able to entirely regulate their body temperature as endotherms do. 
Hence are called Heterothermic ( Carey and Teal,  1966).

Temperature affects physiological processes by changing activity of enzymes. 
Experimental works have shown that, activity of enzymes increase with increasing 
temperature towards the optimum temperature. Above this, any temperature increase 
produces not significant increase in activity of enzyme (Johnston et al., 1998; Peterson et 
al., 2007; Pörtner, 2001). Biochemical processes are enzyme dependent processes; 
therefore temperature affects these processes by changing the activity of enzymes.  Besides, 
Dorsey (1968) and von Herbing (2002) described that, at low temperature body fluid 
viscosity of blood, water and intracellular is high and therefore energy demanded by blood 
flow and gill ventilation is high.  Schurmann & Steffensen (1997) and Steinhausen et al. 
(2008) observed reduced  cardiac and swimming performance  of Sockeye salmon and  
Atlantic cod due to low muscle contraction rate at low temperature while Casselman et al. 
(2012) documented comparable evident due to oxygen limitation at high temperature.

 Following the work by Ege & Krogh (1914), numerous  academics have studied the 
quantitative relationship between SMR and temperature. Among others are Ivleva (1980), 
Johnston et al., (1991), Clarke & Johnston (1999), Ikeda et al. (2001), Clarke (2004),Frisk

Agreeing to Gillooly et al. (2001) the standard metabolic rate of fish is high at high 
temperature because of great kinetic energy of cellular components at this temperature.   
The MMR of fish increases with temperature towards a temperature the optimum 
temperature. An increase in MMR with temperature is contributed by high oxygen 
diffusion rate across the fish gills (Dejours (1990).

Above the optimum temperature, the MMR decreases significantly with temperature 
increase due to insufficiency of cardiac output (Brett, 1964; Pörtner and Knust, 2006) and  
reduction of heart rate (Steinhausen et al., 2008; Farrell et al., 2008; Farrell 2009; 
Casselman et al., 2012). The metabolic scope as a function of temperature followed the 
bell- shaped curve (Fry curve) described by Fry (1947). The curve consists of critical 
temperatures that define the temperatures at which the aerobic scope disappears, leading to 
anaerobic activities. Fry curve consists of Pejus temperatures (lower and high critical 
temperatures), the optimum temperature (where the maximum MS occurs and optimum 
temperature range defining temperatures at which 80% of maximum MS appears(Fry, 
1971; Neuheimer et al., 2011; Pörtner, 2001; Wang and Overgaard, 2007).  
RMR of fish increases exponentially with temperature increase (Patterson et al., 2013; 
Wieser and Forstner, 1986). The effects of temperature on SDA have been viewed by 
several scholars (review studies by McCue 2006 and Secor 2009). Animal exposed to 
warmer temperature have, high-energy expenditure and SDA peak, short SDA duration and 
time to peak. While the contrary is true for animals exposed to cooler temperatures (Fig.3)

1.4 The need for metabolic and respiratory physiology data  
Metabolic and respiratory information are needed for developing of bioenergetics models 
necessary for growth and reproduction under different rearing conditions (Van Nes et al. 
2002; Hölker & Breckling 2005;Farrell et al. 2008). Metabolic data are fundamental for 
estimating stocking density in aquaculture systems. Metabolic scope have been used to 
infer the optimal temperature necessary for fish performances in both natural habitats and 
aquaculture systems in general (Frisk et al., 2012).  
Metabolic scope is an approach to assesses how fish respond to changing climate(Pörtner, 
2001; Pörtner and Knust, 2006; Pörtner and Peck, 2010). Moreover, Respiratory data have 
been used to assess altered environments (Dube, 2010).  
1.5 Objectives of the master thesis 
The main objective of this master thesis was to investigate the effects of temperature on 
metabolism, growth, ventilation frequency and Ammonia excretion in Juvenile Nile tilapia 
(Oreochromis niloticus). Specifically: 
 
1.5.1 To determine the thermal optimum for Juvenile Nile tilapia 
(Oreochromis Niloticus) and the use of ventilation frequency as an 
accurate indicator of metabolic rate 
 
1.5.2 To investigate the effects of temperature on specific dynamic action, 
growth performance and Ammonia excretion in Juvenile Nile tilapia 
(Oreochromis Niloticus)

Abstract  
Measurements of Standard dynamic Action (SDA) under rearing temperature conditions 
are becoming important for understanding the functional relationship between costs 
associated with post-absorptive activities and growth performances of fish. To investigate 
this link, SDA and SDA parameters were measured in Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis 
Niloticus) at five different temperatures ranging from 18C to 34C with feed given to their 
satiation. SDA parameters were then compared to Feed intake (FI), Feed conversion ratio 
(FCR), and Specific growth rate (SGR) under similar temperature and feeding regimes.  
The oxygen consumption rate measured prior to feeding was significantly higher at 34C. 
The SDA duration and time to peak were significantly longer at 18C and 22C compared 
to other temperatures. The SDA magnitude and SDA peak were significantly high at 34C 
resulting into high SDA cost at this temperature. SGR was linked to SDA costs at all 
temperatures. There was inconsistency relationship of SDA cost - SGR at 34C compared 
to other temperatures and possibly due to high-energy demand for physiological processes 
associated with routine activities. 
As a secondary objective of this study, protein utilization was estimated before and after 
feeding using nitrogen quotients. The results indicated that protein utilization before 
feeding did not differ significantly at all temperatures. However there was an increase in 
protein utilization following feeding at 18C, 22C and 34C indicating a change in energy 
source that could limit protein available for growth. It was therefore proposed that, the 
optimal temperature for feeding metabolism and growth of Juvenile Nile tilapia is between    
26C, and 30C. 
 
Key words: Standard dynamic Action, Nile tilapia, Growth, Temperature

1. Introduction  
An abruptly increase in oxygen consumption rate (MO2) which occurs in animals following   
meal ingestion integrates the energy used by physiological and chemical processes 
associated with digestion, absorption and assimilation of a meal(McCue, 2006). It is named 
as Standard dynamic Action (SDA), which additionally, it comprises the energy cost for 
protein synthesis, protein turnover and excretory products. The key parameters used to 
quantify the SDA include the baseline MO2 (Standard, or routine metabolic rate) SDA 
peak, the time to peak, the factorial scope, SDA magnitude, SDA coefficient and the SDA 
duration (Jobling, 1993) 
Scientific works that examined the SDA have described that, these parameters depend upon 
the characteristics of fish species (Jobling and Davies, 1980; Secor, 2009), meal 
composition (Fu et al., 2005a), fish body size (von Herbing & White 2002;Luo & Xie 
2008; Pirozzi & Booth 2009), relative meal size (Jobling 1981;Fu et al. 2005b; Secor & 
Boehm 2006; Fu et al. 2006; Jordan & Steffensen 2007) meal type (Fu et al., 2005b; Secor 
and Boehm, 2006; Secor et al., 2006), feeding frequency (De La Gándara et al., 2004, 
2002; Sebastian et al., 2003).  
Additionally, temperature has been mostly pronounced to affect the SDA response in 
ectotherms( Kalarani & Davies 1994; Guinea & Fernandez 1997; Secor & Boehm 2006; 
Luo & Xie 2008; Pirozzi & Booth 2009;Secor 2009; Pérez-Casanova et al. 2010;Vanella et 
al. 2010).  
Understanding the SDA cost across a range of rearing temperatures has concrete 
implications in the context of aquaculture production mainly for intensively farmed fish 
species whose growth and FCR have to be optimized to enhance production performance.  
Looking at previous works, approximately 60 - 80% of SDA energy is linked with post- 
absorptive process mainly anabolic activities (protein and lipid synthesis, protein turnover 
and growth) (Coulson & Hernandez 1979; Brown & Cameron 1991).  While about 1 – 3 % 
of the SDA energy is invested in meal processing(Cho and Slinger, 1979; Peck, 1998). This 
suggests that, SDA cost can be used to infer the energy paid for growth and consequently, 
huge SDA energy would provide faster growth (Coulson et al., 1978; Grigoriou and 
Richardson, 2008).

Ammonia excretion rate in fish accounts for protein break down (Brett and Zala, 1975;

 Our objectives in the present study were to investigate: i) the effects of temperature on 
SDA in Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus); ii) how SDA energy is linked to 
growth of Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) under these temperature conditions 
.iii) the protein metabolism based on the measurements of total ammonia excretion and 
oxygen consumption rates, hence it was important to calculate Nitrogen quotient under 
these temperature conditions.  
2.0 Materials and methods  
2.1 Experimental animals and acclimation  
Juvenile Nile tilapia, Oreochromis niloticus used in the present study was acquired from a 
commercial producer (Til-Aqua International, Velden, Netherlands) and transport to the 
Technical University of Denmark in Hirshals. Fish were weighted and randomly distributed 
into 5 experimental aquariums each with three replicates (Total = 15 aquariums, 43.35L 
each). Each experimental aquarium was stocked with 8 fish. The water temperature in each 
experimental aquarium was 25C when fish were stocking. Water temperatures were 
gradually changed at the rate of 1C per day until the preferred experimental temperatures 
were reached (i.e. 18C, 22C, 26C, 30C 34C).  
Water temperatures were maintained either by titanium aquarium heater (TH -100W) or 
graduated scale aquarium Heater (50w-20/ 50L) connected to temperature controllers (T 
2001 HC, Aqua medic or built T controller). Pumps (EHELIM compact 300) were installed 
in all experimental aquariums to mix the water.  
 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
 
 
The water oxygen level in each experimental aquarium was kept above 75 % saturation by 
cylinder aquarium bubble air stones connected to HI BLOW AIR PUMP (TYPE HP- 40/ 
AC 220- 240V). Oxygen, temperature, and Ammonia were daily monitored prior to water 
replacement. Approximately 50% of water by volume was replaced in each experimental 
aquarium on daily basis. Fish were acclimated to experimental temperatures for a minimum 
of three weeks prior to the start of the experiment and fed a commercial Tilapia feed pellets 
(EFICO Alpha 845F N 4.5, 32 % Crude Protein; BioMar A/S, Denmark) between10:00 
and 16:00 hours on daily basis.  
2.2 Growth trial  
Prior to the start of growth trial, fish were fasted for 24 h and weighed to obtain the initial 
body weight. During 24-days growth trial, fish were offered a commercial Tilapia feed 
pellets (EFICO Alpha 845F N 4.5, 32 % Crude Protein; BioMar A/S, Denmark) to their 
apparent satiations (not greater than 4% of their body weight) and the remaining feed 
pellets at the end of each feeding day were weighed for estimating feed intake. The ration 
was adjusted based on the fish body weight after 10 days of feeding. Fish were fasted on 
the day prior to the end of growth trial and weighed to determine the final body weight 
The specific growth rate, SGR (% day-1) in each experimental aquarium was computed 
based on the initial (Wi) and final total body weight (Wf) of the fish (Hopkins 1992) 
SGR =100* (ln Wf –ln Wi)/ number of feeding days.  
Where, ln is the natural logarithm. The feed intake, I (fish-1 day-1) was calculated as total 
weight of dry feed pellets consumed by fish in each experimental aquarium divided by 
number of fish in the experimental aquarium per feeding days. The feed conversion ratio 
(FCR) was computed as feed intake of the fish divided by fish weight gain during the 
growth trial experiment.

2.3 Oxygen consumption measurement 
Measurements of oxygen consumption rates were performed for 36 hours for all 
experimental temperatures accept at 34C where the duration was shorter (24 hours). Prior 
to Measurements, fish were fasted for, weighed and acclimated to the experimental 
conditions overnight. Oxygen consumption rates were measured for15 minutes prior to 45 
minutes aeration period. Programmed timer (JOE®, JO-EL Electric A/S – DK 52200 
Odense SØ) was used to control the periods of measurement and aeration.  
Decline in oxygen partial pressure was measured by a 4- channel fiber optic oxygen 
transmitter (OXY 4-Mini, Pre-Sens, GmbH, Germany).  The metabolic rate of Juvenile 
Nile tilapia prior to feeding for each experimental temperature was determined based on the 
oxygen consumption rates measured during the acclimation period 
To measure postprandial oxygen consumption rates, fish were fed to satiation a single meal 
of a commercial feed pellets (EFICO Alpha 845F N 4.5, 32 % Crude Protein, 6% crude 
lipid, 8% crude fiber, 7% ash and 14.7MJ/kg   digestible gross energy ; BioMar A/S, 
Denmark). The oxygen consumption rate was determined following the equation provided 
by Steffensen et al., (1984). 
 
MO2 = α*Vrem*β/BM,  
 
Where MO2 is the oxygen consumption rate (mg O2 kg -1 h-1), α is the decline in oxygen 
content over time determined by linear regression (∆po2∆t-1), Vrem is the volume of the 
respirometer minus the volume occupied by fish (L), β is the oxygen solubility at a given 
temperature (mg O2L-1), BM is the fish biomass (kg) 
2.4 Ammonia excretion measurements.  
Total Ammonia Nitrogen (TAN) excretions were measured alongside with oxygen 
consumption rate measurements. Prior to measurements, fish were fasted for 24 hours and 
all experimental aquariums were thoroughly cleaned to remove fecal materials. Fish were 
allowed to acclimatize to the experimental conditions overnight. Approximately 5ml of 
water sample was drawn two times prior to feeding.  
 
 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
 
 
During the oxygen measuring period (see section 2.3), water samples were retrieved at the 
rate of 1h, 2h, 4h, 6h, 9h, 12h, 18h, 24h, 30h and 36h following feeding for all temperatures 
except 34C which ended for 24 hrs.   
All water samples were store at 2°C prior to analysis. The accumulative concentrations 
were determined in duplicate by spectrophotometric technique ( = 680) following the 
Danish standards for water analysis (DS, 1975). Total ammonia excretion was computed 
according to the equation provided by Frisk et al. (2013) 
 
 
 
TAN = Vrem x ∆ [N]/ BM x ∆t 
 
Where, Vrem is the volume of the aquarium (L) minus the volume occupied by fish. ∆ [N] is 
the change in N- concentration (mmol/L) over the time course, ∆t is the time period (hours) 
and BM is the fish biomass (kg) 
In the present study, only total ammonia excretion was measured since it represents a major 
component (80% - 99%) of nitrogenous excretion in freshwater fish. The theory underlying 
this idea is discussed by Brett & Groves (1979); Mommsen & Walsh (1991) and Wood 
(1993). 
 2.5 Calculations and statistical analyses 
The following SDA parameters were computed: MO2 prior to feeding (MO2,-P,  mmol O2 
kg-1 h-1), SDA peak, (SDA peak) (mmol O2 kg-1h-1), Time to peak (hours), Factorial 
postprandial scope, (SDA peak divided by (MO2,-P), SDA duration (SDA duration, hours), the 
SDA (Accumulated oxygen consumption rate (mmol O2 kg-1) above MO2-P over the SDA 
duration. SDA was calculated as area under the curve (AUC)(Motulsky and Christopoulos, 
2003). By using a oxycalorific factor of 14.06kJ g O-1(Gnaiger 1983), SDA values were  
converted into energy expenditure(SDA cost, kJkg-1) above MO2.-P,  SDA coeffient (%) as 
SDA energy cost divided by meal energy.  
From TAN excretion the following parameters were computed: TAN excretion prior to 
feeding (TAN - P, mmol NH3-N kg-1h-1), TAN peak (TAN peak, mmol NH3-N kg-1h-1), TAN 
factorial scope (TAN scope) (TAN peak divided by TAN-P), Time to peak (hours), TAN 
duration (TAN duration, hours), TAN SDA (TAN SDA, mmol NH3-N kg-1)(total ammonia 
excreted above the TAN-P) (mg N kg-1).

0.27 is a theoretical maximum value when protein is only used as energy source (Van den

4.0 Discussion  
4.1 Specific dynamic action 
The temporal change in oxygen consumption rates (MO2) of Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis 
niloticus) measured at different temperatures were in agreement with the observations that have 
been testified in other fish and ectothermic species ( Luo & Xie 2008; Luo & Xie 2009; Frisk et al. 
2013). MO2 increased rapidly subsequent to meal ingestion and returned slowly to pre-feeding level 
after some hours depending on the temperature (Fig. 4). Oxygen consumption rates prior to feeding 
(MO2 - P) and SDA peak increased with increasing temperature while the contrary was true for Time 
to peak (Table 1). Similar evidences have been reported by earlier studies (Robertson et al. 2002; 
Toledo et al. 2003; Wang et al. 2003; Luo & Xie 2008; Pang et al. 2010). SDA duration in the 
present study was 14h to 31h which fall within the values documented for other fish species 
(Reviews by McCue 2006 and Secor 2009).  
The effect of temprature on factorial scope in fish  has been testified to be minimal (Jobling & 
Davies 1980; Johnston & Battram 1993; Vanella et al. 2010). It was also revealed by Juvenile Nile 
tilapia in  the present study. The  computed factorial scope  (1.99 – 3.53)  was within the middle 
range of values (1.8 -5.9) reported by Fu et al. (2006) but lower than the range ( 5.7 – 6.2) 
described by Wang et al, (2003). Pirozzi & Booth (2009) documented lower factorial scope 
(1.3 -1.5) relative to the values reported in this study. Three clarifications may be assigned for this 
disparity:  
First, the magnetude of SDA response greatly differs with meal size (Boyce and Clarke, 1997; Fu et 
al., 2006, 2005b; Hamada and Maeda, 1983). In present study, Juvenile Nile tilapia were fed 4% of 
their body weight which is higher  than 2%  fed to Mulloway  species (Pirozzi & Booth 2009 ) 
,although it is lower than  24%  fed to chenese  catfish (Fu et al. 2006), 20 % fed to Python molurus 
(Wang et al. 2003).  Seond, the calculated  factorial scope in the present study was based on MO2 
prior to feeding (typically routine metabolic rate), which differs greatly between fish species 
depending on the level of fish activity. Third, factorial scope is sometimes computed in relation to 
resting metabolic rates ( Beamish 1974;Chen et al. 2008) which normally increases values.  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:39==
31 
 
 
In agreement with previous studies(Guinea & Fernandez 1997; Toledo et al. 2003; Secor & Boehm 
2006; Luo & Xie 2008; Luo & Xie 2009; Khan et al. 2015) the  SDA coefficient (amount of 
energy devoted to SDA response  as a  fraction of meal energy)in the current study was 9 .2 – 
14.3  and influenced by temperatures (Table 1). Contrasting to this are Powell et al. (1999);Wang et 
al. (2003); Pirozzi & Booth (2009) and Frisk et al. (2013) who jointly reported no effects of 
temperature on SDA energy and coeffient. Two reasons maybe assigned for these two opposing 
observations. First, the SDA coeffient is greatly dependent on body size (Beamish 1974);meal size 
(Carter & Brafield 1992; Fu et al. 2006);meal type (Secor and Boehm, 2006). Second, energy 
expenditure among species is highly linked to species’s history strategy (Bacigalupe and Bozinovic, 
2002). It is therefore not surprising that,  the SDA cost and coeffient differ  among fish species.   
4.2  TAN 
TAN excretion prior to feeding (TAN – P) increased with increasing temperature while the contrary 
was true for Time to peak and TAN duration. TAN peak ,TAN SDA and TAN scope was unaffected by 
temperature (Table 2). These observations have been noticed by earlier findings in areolated 
grouper (Epinephelus areolatus) and Mangrove snapper(Lutjanus argentimaculatus)(Leung et al., 
1999), Inland silverside (Menidia beryllina)(Peck et al., 2003), Pikeperch (Sander lucioperca) 
(Frisk et al., 2013).  
Moreover, TAN excretion (Fig 5) and MO2 (Fig. 4) operated independently from each other, 
although the patterns of response following meal ingestion are comparable. It has been suggested 
that, plasma amino acids transport and protein synthesis followed by deamination of excess amino 
acids are major controlling factor for ammonia production, therefore contributing to high peak of 
TAN prior to the peak of MO2 (Brown and Cameron, 1991; Frisk et al., 2013). 
 Values for NQ-P (0.089 – 0.097) which were further used for estimating PM – P (32.4 – 36.06) fell 
within values  reported in Atlantic salmon (Forsberg, 1997).  The NQ-P and estimeted PM – P were 
lower than the values reported in Southern catfish (Luo and Xie, 2009), this may probabily be 
because carnivorous fish use protein as a key energy source (Van Waarde, 1983).  
Moreover , NQ-P values documented in this study are relatively higher than the values described by 
Frisk et al. (2013). This suggests  that nitrogen quotient and estimated protein utilization depends on 
the level of fish activity from which TAN and MO2 were measured (review by Van Waarde 1983). 
High quotient  and protein metabolized  occurred during SDA indicating  a shift in energy source 
following feeding(Alsop & Wood ,1997; Lyytikäinen & Jobling ,1998; Luo & Xie ,2009; Frisk et al 
,2013).

4.2  The link between  SDA and growth perfomance 
 The present study aimed at determining how SDA is affected by temperature and how this could be 
linked to growth performances of Juvenile Nile tilapia. A positive relationship has been documented 
in either fish species (Vahl 1984;Carter & Brafield 1992;Leggatt et al. 2003) or non- fish 
species(Carefoot, 1990). Results from this study suggest that, both SDA magnitude and energy 
(Table 1) increase across all experimental temperatures, although growth (SGR) results revealed 
that, these parameters at 34C were not related to each other. It can be attempted to explain that, the 
significantly high MO2-P at 34C perhaps requires a larger energy investment to maintain the 
physiological processes associated with routine activities and consequently, for the same feed intake 
(Fig. 1) only small fraction of energy is retained for growth(Cuenco et al. 1985; Guinea & 
Fernandez 1997) 
5.0 Conclusion  
In principle, short SDA duration and time to peak are necessary for frequent delivering of feed in 
aquaculture production systems and possibly 34C would be a favorable rearing temperature for 
Juvenile Nile tilapia. But, if large quantity of SDA energy is essential for biochemical processes 
that are important for growth performance, it therefore indicate that 18C, 22C, and 34C are 
limiting rearing temperatures for growth of Juvenile Nile tilapia.  
Additionally, our study indicated that protein utilization increased rapidly following feeding at 
18C, 22C, and 34C suggesting that, availability of amino acids necessary for protein synthesis 
(growth in general) is limited by these temperature conditions.   
Since growth is the focal point for any aquaculture production, thus this study suggest that, the 
optimal temperature for feeding metabolism and growth of Juvenile Nile tilapia is between    26C, 
and 30C.

Thermal optimum for Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis Niloticus) and the use of ventilation 
frequency as an accurate indicator of metabolic rate 
Jonas Ndau Leonard1*  
 
 1Technical University of Denmark, DTU Aqua, National Institute of Aquatic resources, Section for 
Aquaculture, the North Sea Research Centre, P.O.Box 101, DK-9850 Hirshals, Denmark 
 
Abstract  
 Metabolic scopes (MS) have received a greater attention to address questions related to 
performances, optimal temperature and temperature tolerance of fish species.  The present study 
was carried out to determine the optimal temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia by means of 
metabolic scope. Therefore measurements of standard metabolic rate (SMR) and maximum 
metabolic rate (MMR) were done at five different temperatures (18 -34).  
In the present study, absolute metabolic scope, AMS (MMR- SMR) and factorial metabolic scope, 
FMS (MMR/SMR) were also determined, but only absolute metabolic scope was used for 
determination of optimal temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia.  SMR increased exponentially with 
increasing temperature with highest Q10 value of 2.38 between 22C and 26C while the lowest Q10 
value was observed between 18C and 22C (1.17). MMR was affected by temperature and 
increased significantly from 233.2 - 370.6 at 18C and 30C and decreased from 370.6 -354.8 
between 30C and 34C causing a significant differences in AMS among temperature treatments.  
The maximum AMS (234.38 mg O2kg-1h-1) was observed at 28.2C describing the optimal 
temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia. The optimal temperature range was found at 20.7C and 
35.7C that corresponded to 80% of Maximum AMS (187.5 mg O2kg-1h-1). FMS did not differ 
significantly between temperatures. Therefore the study showed that, the optimal rearing 
temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia is 28.2C. However, Juvenile Nile tilapia can be reared at 
20.7C - 35.7C without substantial effect on the availability of oxygen capacity.  
As a secondary objective of this study, measurements of ventilation frequency (Vf.) at normoxia 
conditions were carried out under similar temperature conditions to investigate if it could be used to 
predict metabolic rates (MO2). There was a stronger relationship between MO2 and Vf.  
Accordingly, Vf can be used as an accurate predictor of MO2 provided that, the averaged Vf is used 
instead of individual Vf. 
Key words: Metabolic scope, Oxygen consumption rate, Temperature, Ventilation frequency

1. Introduction  
Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) belongs to tilapia members of the Cichlidae family. It is a 
species with great economic interest and substantial role in freshwater aquaculture in  many parts of 
the world particularly where animal protein sources are limited (Wohlfarth and Hulata, 1981) . Nile 
tilapia is a valued species for both extensive and intensive aquaculture(Bucur et al., 2012). An 
acceptability of this species for aquaculture is accredited by: ease breeding in captivity, tolerance to 
both to high stocking density and poor water quality parameters and less susceptible to fish 
diseases. In natural habit, Nile tilapia occurs in shallow water of lakes and rivers (FAO, 2012). 
Measurements of metabolic and respiratory physiology have received greater attention to address 
questions related to bioenergetics model for growth and reproduction (Cech Jr and Brauner, 2011; 
Fitzgibbon et al., 2007),Carry capacity or stocking density(Dalla Via et al., 1998), oxygen demand 
in rearing systems(Fitzgibbon et al., 2007). Additionally, measurements of metabolic scopes are 
gaining interest in fish ecology because of the hypothesis (Oxygen- and capacity – limited thermal 
tolerance, OCLTT) which links the metabolic scope of fish species  to all activities related to fish 
performances (growth, reproduction, swimming and locomotion)(Claireaux and Lefrançois, 2007; 
Fry, 1947; Pörtner, 2012). It has documented(Clark et al., 2013, 2011; Frederich and Pörtner, 2000) 
that, performances of fish are maximized when metabolic scope is high at a given temperature 
condition (termed optimal temperature) whereas it is true that, performances vanished when 
metabolic scope decreased at lower or upper temperatures (lower and upper critical temperatures). 
Metabolic rates in different groups of tilapia species have been described by previous findings. 
Example, in Tilapia rendalii (Caulton, 1977), Nile tilapia, Oreochromis niloticus(Farmer and 
Beamish, 1969; Turker, 2011),Tilapia mossambicus, Oreochromis mossambicus(Caulton, 1978). 
Despite the great economic interest of Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) in aquaculture industry, 
there is limited information on how metabolic scope is linked to rearing temperature condition of 
this species. Therefore, the primary objective of the current study was to determine the optimal 
temperature of Juvenile Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) by means of metabolic scope. 
Consequently, measurements of standard and maximum metabolic rates were done under five 
different temperature conditions. 
 Additionally, since metabolic rates of other fish species have been linked to respiratory variable 
primarily ventilation frequency( Millidine et al., 2008; van Rooij and Videler, 1996), the secondary 
objective was to investigate the use of ventilation frequency as an accurate indicator of metabolic 
rates and therefore it was important to measure opercula beat frequencies together with metabolic 
rates under similar temperature conditions.

2.0 Materials and methods  
2.1 experimental animals and acclimation 
Juvenile Nile tilapia, Oreochromis niloticus used in the present study was acquired from a 
commercial producer (Til-Aqua International, Velden, Netherlands) and transport to the Technical 
University of Denmark in Hirshals. Fish were weighted and randomly distributed into 5 
experimental aquariums each with three replicates (Total = 15 aquariums, 43.35L each). Each 
experimental aquarium was stocked with 8 fish. The water temperature in each experimental 
aquarium was 25C when fish were stocking. Water temperatures were gradually changed at the 
rate of 1C per day until the preferred experimental temperatures were reached (i.e. 18C, 22C, 
26C, 30C 34C).  
Water temperatures were maintained either by titanium aquarium heater (TH -100W) or graduated 
scale aquarium Heater (50w-20/ 50L) connected to temperature controllers (T 2001 HC, Aqua 
medic or built T controller). Pumps (EHELIM compact 300) were installed in all experimental 
aquariums to mix the water.  
The water oxygen level in each experimental aquarium was kept above 75 % saturation by cylinder 
aquarium bubble air stones connected to HI BLOW AIR PUMP (TYPE HP- 40/ AC 220- 240V). 
Oxygen, temperature, and Ammonia were daily monitored prior to water replacement. 
Approximately 50% of water by volume was replaced in each experimental aquarium on daily 
basis. Fish were acclimated to experimental temperatures for a minimum of three weeks prior to 
experiment start and fed a commercial Tilapia feed pellets (EFICO Alpha 845F N 4.5, 32 % Crude 
Protein; BioMar A/S, Denmark) between10:00 and 16:00 hours on daily basis.  
2.2 Respirometry set up 
Respirometry consisted of four resting respirometers (45ml each) immersed into a 1060L water 
holding tank. The water from the holding tank was circulated through the trickling filter, UV 
sterilizer (9W UV-C Aqua Cristal GmbH, Neuhofen, GERMANY) and aerated. A 1600W heater 
was used to maintained water temperature.  Oxygen consumption rates were measured by 
computerized intermittent flow through respirometry (Steffensen, 1989).  Measurements were made 
in a loop of 7-10 minutes comprising of 3-4 minutes flushing period, 1 minute waiting period, and 
3-5 minutes measuring period depending on the experimental temperature.  
 
==PAGE:52==
44 
 
 
Respirometers were flushed by water from the holding tank. A 4- channel fiber optic oxygen 
transmitter (OXY 4-Mini, Pre-Sens, GmbH, Germany) was used to measure the oxygen tension in 
the water inside the respirometer. All collected data were stored directly to a computer (AutorespTM 
version 2.2.0 software, Loligo system, Denmark). The oxygen consumption rate during measuring 
period was automatically determined by the software following the equation provided by Steffensen 
et al. (1984) 
 
MO2 = α*Vrem*β/BM,  
 
Where MO2 is the oxygen consumption rate (mg O2 kg -1 h-1), α is the decline in oxygen tension 
over time determined by linear regression (∆po2∆t-1), Vrem is the volume of the respirometer minus 
the volume occupied by fish (L), β is the oxygen solubility for defined temperature (mg O2L-1), BM 
is the biomass experimental fish (kg). 
2.3 MO2 measurement protocol 
Prior to MO2 measurements, feed was withdrawn for 24 h and continued after fish had been 
sampled. Four fish were randomly sampled from each experimental aquarium with 24 h interval 
from one experimental aquarium to the next. Fish were individually weighed and introduced into 
four respirometers. Fish were acclimated to the new experimental conditions overnight. Afterwards, 
the maximum metabolic rate (MMR) of individual fish was determined following the protocol 
similar to previous studies(Reidy et al. 1994; Killen et al. 2007). Concisely, a continuous flush 
mode of the loop was activated. Then, one fish was acquired from the one respirometer and placed 
in the container with appropriate water temperature and hand-cased (approximately 5 min) to a 
point that it was possible to handle it with no escape. Finally, the fish was instantly transferred back 
to the respirometer and the wait period of the cycle was skipped for quick post- exercise MO2 
measurements.  
MO2 was measured for at least one hour following chasing practice. The procedure above was 
repeated for fish from respirometer 2, 3 and 4. At the end of the protocol, all fish were removed 
from respirometers and all respirometers were thoroughly cleaned prior to the introduction of new 
fish. The temperature of the system was changed and allowed to stabilize overnight when changing 
from one treatment to another treatment. Then, SMR was quantified from MO2 measured during the 
acclimation period by fitting the MO2 values into frequency distribution (Skov et al. 2011) while 
MMR was calculated by using quantile approach (q = 0.99) (Dupont-prinet et al., 2013).  
==PAGE:53==
45 
 
 
 To avoid overestimating of MO2, values due to measurement error were removed prior to the

4.0 Discussion 
4.1 MO2  
Temperature has a substantial influence on both physiological and biochemical processes in 
ectotherms. Consequently, it governs physical and performances of ectothermic species (Bennett, 
1980). In the present study, Juvenile Nile tilapia exhibited an exponentially increase in SMR with 
increasing temperature (Fig 1). This relationship has been reported by previous findings (Beamish, 
1964; Clarke and Johnston, 1999; Frisk et al., 2012; Ikeda et al., 2001; Johnston et al., 1991). 
Increased SMR with temperature also corresponded to Vf.SMR within similar temperature conditions 
(Fig 4). Evident has been marked in Sharpsnout sea, Diplodus puntazzo(Cerezo & Garcı 2004), 
Silver perch, Bidyanus  bidyanus and Rainbow trout, Oncorhynchus mykiss(Black et al. 1991; Patra 
et al. 2009 ). High values of SMR at high temperature is due to high kinetic energy of cellular 
components(Gillooly et al., 2001). 
 Agreeing with previous findings (Claireaux et al., 2000; Johnston et al., 1998), Juvenile Nile tilapia 
showed an increased MMR towards optimal temperature (Fig.1) beyond which decreased with 
increasing temperature.  An increase in MMR with temperature is due to high oxygen diffusion rate 
across the fish gills (Dejours, 1990) while decreased in MMR above the optimal   temperature has 
been linked to insufficiency of cardiac output(Brett, 1964; Pörtner and Knust, 2006) and a reduction 
of heart rates(Casselman et al., 2012; Farrell, 2009; Farrell et al., 2008; Steinhausen et al., 2008). 
4.2 Metabolic scope 
Metabolic scope of an organism can be termed as absolute metabolic scope, AMS (MMR –SMR) or 
factorial metabolic scope, FMS (MMR/SMR). Absolute metabolic scope describes the energy 
available to power all aerobic activities above animal’s basic requirements (Schurmann and 
Steffensen, 1997). Contrasting, the factorial metabolic scope reflects a factor by which an animal 
can increase its aerobic capacity above the basic requirements to power all energy demanding 
physiological processes(Bishop, 1999). All scopes are useful in the study of animal respiratory 
physiology. However they are contradicting to each other as a result, they have been reported to 
yield different conclusions from similar data set. For example, the estimated optimal temperature 
for Pink salmon (Oncorhynchus gorbuscha) was 21C when AMS was used and 11C when FMS 
was used (Clark et al. 2011). Other similar cases have been detailed by  Cutts et al. (2002),Clark et 
al. (2005),(Killen et al. 2007).  
 
 
==PAGE:62==
54 
 
 
In the present study, only absolute metabolic scope was used for estimating the optimal temperature 
of Juvenile Nile tilapia due to the fact that: Factorial metabolic scope changes with small change in 
SMR compared to absolute metabolic scope; Absolute metabolic scope is more informative when 
comparing diverse species(Clark et al., 2011).  
4.3 Optimal temperature and optimal temperature range 
The primary objective of the current study was to determine the optimal temperature for Juvenile 
Nile tilapia based on measurements of metabolic scope. Accordingly, the optimal temperature 
(28.2) of this species was estimated by using the maximum AMS (234.38 mg O2kg-1h-1). 
Following an approach provided by Huey & Stevenson (1979). Knowing that, ectotherms can live 
in a specific temperature range (termed optimal temperature range) without robust change in their 
physiological performances(Huey & Stevenson 1979; Frisk et al. 2012), the 80% of maximum 
AMS was estimated and it corresponded to 20.7 and 35.7(Fig. 2, letter B and D).   
The estimated Q10 values were around 2.4 and 2, between 22 and 26, and between 22 and 
34respectively. These values were within the range previously described by Casselman et al. 
(2012),Frisk et al. (2012) when fish were within their thermal optimal range. Thus, it is possible 
that the estimated thermal optimal range (20.7 - 35.7) is a cormfort zone for this species. 
Additionally, supporting this , is a scope of ventilation frequency which peaked at the upper value 
of  thermal optimal range (35.7) ( Fig. 5) indicating that, Juvenile Nile tilapia can increase their Vf 
beyond optimal temperature.  
Previous findings on growth performance of Nile tilapia, Oreochromis niloticus (Azaza et al., 2008; 
El-Sayed and Kawanna, 2008; El-sherif and EL-Feky, 2009; Jun et al., 2012; Likongwe et al., 1996; 
Mirea et al., 2013) suggested that, the optimal growth for this species were achieved  within 
temperature range of  20 to 36. These findings correlated well with optimal temperature range 
reported in the current study and this is perhaps, metabolic scope of an animal is linked to 
performances (e.g. growth, locomotion and reproduction) and optimization of these performances 
occurs within a given range of metabolic scope and vanished when metabolic scope extinct (Clark 
et al., 2013, 2011; Frederich and Pörtner, 2000). Therefore, this results suggested that, the optimal 
temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia is close to 28  
 
 
 
==PAGE:63==

4.4 Critical temperature for Nile tilapia 
The critical temperature of an animal is defined as a temperature at which metabolic scope and 
associated performances disappeared (Sommer et al. 1997; van Dijk PL et al. 1999; Frederich & 
Pörtner 2000; Pörtner & Knust 2006). In our study, the estimated lower and upper critical 
temperatures were 11.2 and 44.5 respectively. In natural habitat, the survival limits for this 
species is between 11 and 42(FAO, 2012). Bucur et al. (2012), Mirea et al. (2013) described that, 
Nile tilapia stoped feeding at 16 and 20 was a limiting temperature for spawning while high 
mortality was inevitable at 12. In the present study, Juvenile Nile tilapias were able to survive at all 
experimental temperatures except 18 where Nile tilapia died (n =6) during acclimation. Again, 
poor feed intake was observed at lower temperatures during acclimation but there were no mortality 
at the upper temperatures.  
4.5 The relationship between MO2 and Vf 
Our secondary objective was to determine whether ventilation frequency of Juvenile Nile tilapia can 
be used as an accurate indicator of oxygen consumption rate at resting and following intense 
exercise across a range of thermal conditions. Oxygen consumption rates and ventilation 
frequencies are presented in fig. 6. Equations 7, 8 and 9 were used to describe a correlation between 
oxygen consumption rates (SMR, MMR and overall MO2) and ventilation frequencies (Vf.SMR, 
Vf.MMR. and Vf in general). There was a strong correlation between SMR and Vf.SMR, MMR and 
Vf.MMR (R² = 0.981 and R² = 0.919 respectively) at a specific temperature. Subsequently, the 
correlation of actual SMR or MMR with predicted SMR or MMR was described (fig. 6). Again 
there was a strong correlation between the actual SMR or MMR and predicted SMR or MMR (R2 = 
0.989 and R2 = 0.989 respectively). These findings are in agreement with earlier studies (Dalla 
Valle et al., 2003; Millidine et al., 2008). Opposing this is Frisk et al (2012) who found poor 
correlation between MMR and ventilation frequency. It was argued that, MO2 - Vf relationship is 
weight dependent(Cerezo and Garcı, 2004; van Rooij and Videler, 1996) 
 On the other hand, there was fair good (R2 = 0.795) correlation between overall MO2 and overall 
Vf in comparative to SMR and MMR (fig. 6) suggesting that, predicting MO2 using individual fish 
ventilation frequency measurements is not valid than when Vf measurements are averaged(van 
Rooij and Videler, 1996).  
 
 
 
==PAGE:64==
56 
 
Additionally, MO2 can depend on: oxygen solubility, saturation, extraction and ventilation 
frequency (i. e MO2 = (. PO2 .EO2) Vf (Dejours, 1981). In principle, oxygen extraction (EO2) and 
solubility () vary with rising temperature and therefore, independent relationship between 
ventilation frequency and MO2 is predictable.  Increased demand in MO2 may be controlled by 
change in other respiratory variables (buccal volume and gill perfusion)(Brauner and Randall, 
1996). It would therefore be interesting for aquaculturist to know the contribution (%) of each 
respiratory variable to MO2 at any given temperature condition. Since measurements of other 
respiratory variables were done, only the percent contribution of Vf to MO2 was computed at each 
temperature condition using an equation provided by Millidine et al. (2008). 
 
 
 
%Vf = (Log(Vf.MMR) – Log(Vf.SMR))/( Log.(MMR) – Log(SMR))*100  
 
The percent contribution of Vf to MO2 in Juvenile Nile tilapia increased from   68 to 95 % when 
temperature increased from 18 to 30 , then decreased (95 – 75 %) as temperature rose from 30  to 
34 (Fig. 8). Since there was an increase in Vf relative to MO2 with increasing temperature, It  was 
therefore suggested that, Vf is a key respiratory variable controlling MO2 in Juvenile Nile tilapia 
and contemplate the decreased %Vf at high temperatures was due to decreased MMR, following a 
reduction in oxygen extraction and solubility at these temperatures.  
 
5. Conclusion  
The present study showed that, the optimal temperature for Juvenile Nile tilapia is 28.2 which is a 
favorable rearing temperature particularly in the intensive recirculating aquaculture where the 
entirely temperature control is possible. However, it is reasonable to conclude that Juvenile Nile 
tilapia can be reared at 20.7C - 35.7C without substantial effect on the availability of oxygen 
capacity 
Moreover, MO2 and averaged Vf were strongly correlated within a temperature range reported in the 
current study, accordingly, Vf can be used as an accurate predictor of MO2 provided that, the 
averaged Vf is used instead of individual Vf. and all measurements are taken in normoxia 
conditions.
