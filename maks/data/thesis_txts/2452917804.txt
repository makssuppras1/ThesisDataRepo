Abstract 
 
Oral drug delivery is the most attractive route of administration with a high patient compliance. The 
oral route, however, has certain obstacles to overcome in order to be absorbed in the small intestine, 
where most drugs are absorbed. The harsh pH environment, digestive enzymes and mucus barrier 
are some of the variables that hinder the absorption of the pharmaceutical active ingredient (API). 
Recently, in the field of nanotechnology, a microfabricated device – microcontainers (MCs) have 
become attractive as an advanced oral drug delivery system. MCs can overcome some of the 
challenges with oral delivery, and can protect the drug, which will avoid losses of the API in the gastro 
intestinal tract (GIT).  
         
          In this work MCs are functionalized as a drug vesicle loaded with furosemide, which is chosen 
as a model for a poorly water-soluble drug. The furosemide loaded MCs will be explored with three 
different mucoadhesive polymers as a lid, namely poly ethylene glycol (PEG), chitosan and alginate. 
Three coating thicknesses are explored for each polymer; PEG coating thicknesses of 6, 22 and 30 
μm, chitosan coating thicknesses of 3, 6 and 8 μm, and alginate coating thicknesses of 1, 2 and 3 μm. 
MCs with a mucoadhesive coating facilitates a targeted delivery towards the mucus and provides the 
possibility of protecting the drug from degradation in the GIT, which can enhance the overall 
therapeutic efficacy of the API. PEG and alginate coated MCs presented a faster onset of release, 
while MCs coated with chitosan displayed a sustained release. Thus, chitosan presents an advantage 
in providing a controlled release. Ex vivo studies evaluated the mucoadhesive property of the 
different polymers and different coating thicknesses to see how the MCs interact with the mucus 
layer. The closed perfusion model was not successful, and studies were continued with an open 
perfusion model. MCs with a PEG coating enhanced the mucoadherence with around 20%, while MCs 
coated with chitosan increased mucoadherence up to 30-50%. The MCs with alginate coating induced 
the mucoadherence with 7-11% in the open perfusion study. These results indicate that MCs as a 
drug carrier coated with a mucoadhesive lid increases the mucoadhesiveness and mucus penetration. 
Furthermore, the adhesion force of the polymer coatings was studied on a Texture analyzer (TA). TA 
enables by measurements of a peak force of a single coated MC. However, more research has to be 
performed to design and improve the experimental procedure, in order to obtain a correct analysis 
of the data.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
In the pharmaceutical industry oral drug delivery has a tremendous growth, and oral formulations 
constitute nearly 70 % of the US pharmaceutical drug delivery market (1). The oral dosage forms is 
the most convenient and attractive route of drug administration. It offers high patient compliance as 
a non-invasive route and is more cost effective compared to injections (2–4). Following the oral 
administration, drug absorption take place in the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), where most drugs are 
absorbed in the small intestine (1,3).  
 
          However, there are several physical barriers in the body to overcome for oral drug delivery to 
be successful. The first obstacle after the API has been administered, is that the drug needs to get 
through the highly acidic environment in the stomach. Next, when the drug passes through the 
stomach it enters duodenum in the small intestine, where the API faces degradation by the enzymes 
(2,4,5). In addition, there is also the mucus barrier. The drug has to penetrate the mucus barrier and 
cross the epithelial cell layer to get to the systemic circulation for absorption. Furthermore, the 
continues secretion of mucus, also hinders the drug uptake (6,7). These obstacles result in poor 
solubility and low bioavailability of the pharmaceutical ingredient. Thus, these challenges limits and 
reduces the drug formulation to cross the mucus layer over the epithelial layer, in order to be 
absorbed in the small intestine and reach the systemic circulation (2,6,7).  
 
          The market for the poorly soluble drugs has grown tremendously in the past decade and make 
up to 40% of the oral market in the US. In addition, the tendency for new drug compounds, accounts 
for 75% under development as well as up to 90% of new chemical entities which exhibit poor 
solubility and low permeability (1,7,8). Poorly soluble drugs do not get dissolved in the GIT fluids thus 
the drug does not get absorbed, which lead to a low bioavailability. To overcome some of the hurdles 
with the physical barriers of the oral route, it can be necessary to employ a new drug delivery system 
(DDS). A micro fabricated delivery device – microcontainers (MCs) show promising potential as an 
advanced oral DDS. MCs can protect the API inside the cavity of the MCs and reduce drug loss in the 
surrounding environment in the GIT, hence induce the oral bioavailability of drug (1,7).

1.1. 
Gastrointestinal tract (GIT) 
The GIT comprises of an upper and lower parts, the upper part includes the mouth, pharynx, 
esophagus, stomach and the first part of duodenum of the small intestine. While the lower parts 
consist of the small intestine (jejunum and ileum) and large intestine (cecum, colon and rectum) (9) 
(Figure 1). The function of the GIT are digestion, absorption, excretion and protection (10). The 
obstacles for the oral route in the GIT are due to the harsh pH environment (pH 1-8) in the stomach 
and digestive enzymes. The digestive enzymes include protease enzymes cathepsin and pepsin, 
which digest proteins into peptides. Afterwards, the peptides get digested into amino acids by 
trypsin, chymotrypsin, dipeptidase, and aminopeptidase. Hence, it will make it a challenge for protein 
and peptide-based drug formulation to reach and be absorbed in the small intestine due to a high 
degradation in the stomach (2,6). The pH in the stomach ranges from pH 1-2 in fasted state and pH 
3-7 in a fed state, hence the pH in the intestinal fluid greatly variates. Ultimately, the drug is exposed 
to a changing pH environment, which influences the bioavailability of the drug (11–13). Moreover, 
oral formulations tends to be coated with an enteric coating, such as Eudragit L100, which is a pH 
sensitive polymer used to prevent disintegration of the oral medication in the stomach  (1), in order 
to avoid the gastric release in the stomach. Upon reaching a higher pH (alkaline pH) in the small 
intestine the pH sensitive coating dissolves and are available for absorption (2,12,14).

1.2. 
Small intestine  
The small intestine is responsible for the uptake of nutrition and the absorption of most drugs, as the 
small intestine have a bigger surface area as compared to the stomach (1,16). The small intestine 
consists of duodenum, jejunum and ileum, where the highest absorption capability takes place in the 
jejunum and ileum (9,10). The duodenum mixes the food intake and break down compounds from 
the stomach by enzymes.  The primary function of jejunum is absorption, while the ileum absorbs the 
rest of the nutrients from jejunum. The surface area in the GIT is established by villi with a surface 
area of 300-400 m2 and the villi are surrounded by epithelial invaginations (crypt) (Figure 2, A) (17,18). 
The intestinal epithelium is made up by various cell types, such as the absorptive enterocytes, mucus 
secreting goblet cells, enteroendocrine cells, Paneth cells and microfold or membranous cells (M-
cells). The villi are covered by the absorptive enterocytes and mucus secreting goblet cells which are 
interspersed with Follicle Associated Epithelium (FAE) (Figure 2, B) (6,9,18,19). M-cells are important 
and attractive as a drug target due to lack of mucus secretion, as well as exhibiting transcytosis 
pathway of antigens through a non-degradative manner. Besides, the M-cells have a negatively 
charged surface, which induces the interaction with a positively charged drug delivery system (18). 
The administered drugs can be absorbed in four different pathways: transcellular, paracellular, 
carrier-mediated transcellular and facilitated transport, where the transcellular pathway is being the 
predominant mechanism (9).

1.3. 
Mucus  
Mucus is a hydrogel that coats all of the wet epithelial surfaces of the body, such as in the eyes, lungs 
and the GIT (22). Mucus is an adhesive and viscoelastic gel, and the primary role of mucus is 
protecting and lubricating the epithelial cells from mechanical forces like digestion and blinking 
(1,22,23). The lubrication facilitates food transport and function as a selective physiochemical 
protection layer. Thereby, minimizes exposure to foreign pathogens and particles or harmful 
molecules, while allowing  passage of nutrients absorption (22,24). The primary components of 
mucus are water (95%), and mucin which are elongated with O-linked glycoproteins molecules. The 
viscoelastic and adhesive properties of mucus is owed to the O-linked glycoproteins, which are 
covered with proteoglycans, being responsible for the negative charge of mucus (9,23,25).  The 
glycoprotein mucin belongs to the MUC gene family, and there are up to 21 different mucin 
glycoproteins. In addition, glycoprotein is the most dominant component of the mucus gel layer 
(19,22).  In the small intestine, the primary mucin present is MUC2, which are secreted from the 
goblet cells and are large macromolecules (0.5-40 MDa) made of monomers. The macromolecules 
are crosslinked through disulfide bonds between amino and carboxy termini, which build up the 
mucosal layer (23). The mucin macromolecules have a rich content of sialic acid and sulfate residue, 
resulting in a polyanionic nature (26). Moreover, mucus is constantly secreted by the goblet cells, 
thus there is a fast turnover of mucus, which protect the epithelium against harmful pathogens in 
the GIT. This mucus turnover (50-270 min) plays a significant role as a barrier, which results in a short 
residence time for drug absorption (1,23). The protein backbone of the mucin molecule consists of 
tandem repeats of proline, threonine and serine (PTS) domains, and intermixed with these domains 
are cysteine regions, as wells as carboxy terminus. Additionally, the PTS domains are characterized 
by the O-linked glycosylation, from which are branched oligosaccharide chains or glycans (22). 
Moreover, the mucosal layer comprises of two layers, a thick outer loosely adherent layer and a thin 
inner firmly adherent layer adjacent to the epithelial cell surface (26). The thicker outer loosely 
adherent mucus acts as a barrier and can be a major hindrance for the bioavailability of therapeutics. 
The inner layer is made up by glycoproteins, glycolipids and cell-bound mucin, the layer is impervious 
to bacteria and aids in the uptake of drugs (9,27,28). In humans, the mucus layer is thickest in the 
stomach 180 μm (ranging 40-450 μm), and in the colon (110-160 μm), this increases protection from 
acid in the stomach and bacteria in the colon (see Table 1 for more characteristic of the human GIT).

1.4. 
Poorly water-soluble drugs 
 
In oral formulations, the solubility and permeability through the GIT are the primary factors, 
determining the oral bioavailability. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has provided the 
Biopharmaceutical Classification System (BSC), which predict the drug absorption.  The BSC is divided 
into four categories based upon their aqueous solubility and intestinal permeability (see Figure 3)  
(29,30). The BSC Class I drugs have high solubility in water and high permeability, whereas Class II is 
poorly soluble. High solubility of drugs exhibit a rapid dissolution and absorption, and a high 
permeability means the drug is completely absorbed through the transit time in the small intestine 
(31).  If the drug has low permeability, it is classified as Class III with high water solubility and is Class 
IV when the drug exhibit poor water solubility (32). Thus, it is necessary to induce the bioavailability 
of Class II and IV to increase the drug’s efficacy to reach a desired drug concentration (30,32,33).

into four classes depending on their aqueous solubility and permeability through the intestine (32).

1.5. 
Theory of mucoadhesion 
 
Mucoadhesion is defined as the adhesion contact between two surfaces, where one of them is the 
mucosal layer (35,36). Mucoadhesive systems can deliver a drug at the mucosal surface and prolong 
the retention time. Mucoadhesion is divided into two steps: the contact stage followed by 
consolidation stage. The contact stage entails the contact between the mucoadhesive agent and the 
mucus membrane, whereas the consolidation state refers to the adhesive interaction (25,36). There 
are six theories that have been proposed to describe the mechanism of mucoadhesion: the 
electronic, the adsorption, the wetting, the diffusion, the fracture, and the mechanical theory.  
 
• The electronic theory explains the transfer of electrons between the mucoadhesive system 
and the mucus, and results in an electrical double layer charge at the interface between them 
both.  
• The adsorption theory accounts for the adhesive chemical interactions of hydrogen bonds, 
Van der Waals, electrostatic attraction or hydrophobic interactions (25).  
• The wetting theory is applied to the liquid system and explains the spreading property of the 
liquid measured on a surface.  
• Diffusion theory is the adhesion by interpenetration and entanglement of the polymer and 
mucin. The penetration rate is dependent on the diffusion coefficient, flexibility of the 
polymer and contact time.  
 
==PAGE:15==
 
Page 15 of 79 
• The fracture theory described the mechanical measurement of mucoadhesion. The 
separation or detachment from the polymer and mucus corresponds to the strength of 
adhesive bonding.  
• Mechanical theory considers the diffusion of the liquids that fill up the rough or irregular 
surface leading to adhesion (25,36).  
 
Thereby, the mucoadhesive interaction formed between the polymer and mucus cannot be defined 
by one of the aforementioned theories, and instead requires a combination of all the theories to 
account for the full picture. 
 
1.6. 
Mucoadhesive polymer 
Mucoadhesive polymers are commonly used to prolong the residence time at the target site. There 
are both water soluble and water insoluble mucoadhesive polymers. Some mucoadhesive polymers 
can swell under wetting, thus interpenetrate into the mucus network. Mucoadhesive DDS presents 
various characteristics that promote the mucus interaction. In the subsequent list, favorable 
characteristics of a mucoadhesive polymer are described, the polymer should be: 
1. Nontoxic and nonabsorbable from the GIT 
2. Nonirritant to the mucus layer 
3. Adhere quickly  
4. Allow for easy incorporation of drug 
5. Not decompose during storage 
6. Be relatively cheap 
Factors to consider for a great mucoadhesive property (37): 
1. Hydrogen bonding groups (alcohol and carboxyl) 
2. Anionic charges 
3. Flexibility in order to penetrate and entanglement of the mucus network 
4. High molecular weight 
5. Polymer concentration

1.6.1. Polyethylene glycol 
 
Polyethylene glycol (PEG) is a nonimmunogenic, nontoxic and US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) 
approved polymer (Figure 5). PEG is an uncharged hydrophilic polymer that has a high solubility in 
water. The mucoadhesive properties of PEG is due to its ability to form hydrogen bonds by interacting 
between of the electronegative and electropositive atoms (38).  
 
          Moreover, the molecular weight and concentration of PEG determines whether the polymer 
have mucoadhesive or mucus penetrating properties (39). A lower molecular weight (MW) of PEG 
has a minor mucoadhesive property, while higher MW of PEG show increased mucoadhesiveness  
(38).  A higher MW of PEG can penetrate into the hydration sphere of mucus network, to form 
hydrogen bond with the mucus. In addition, a longer chain length of the polymer results in a greater 
number of conformational sites, which aids the penetration and entanglement of the polymer chains 
(38,40). Furthermore, the concentration of the polymer has a crucial role for mucoadhesion, as lower 
concentration has demonstrated an inadequate interaction leading to a less mucoadhesive effect. 
According to Mythri. et al., a higher polymer concentration of approximately  1-2.5 wt%  would lead 
to more sufficient mucoadhesive property (41).

1.6.2. Chitosan 
 
Chitosan is a biocompatible and biodegradable polymer approved by the FDA, and the EU for dietary 
use and wound dressing applications. The polymer is widely used as a carrier for drug delivery, due 
to its low toxicity, antimicrobial activity, mucoadhesive properties and permeation enhancing abilities 
(4,42,43). Chitin is a naturally occurring polysaccharide derived from crustacea, which includes 
shrimps, crabs and lobsters. Chitin is also found in the cell walls of fungi. Chitosan is a derivative of 
chitin and is produced by deacetylating the acetic moiety from chitin with diluted acids shown in 
Figure 6. It is also one of the most abundant polysaccharides after cellulose (36,42).

The mucoadhesive effect of chitosan is attributed to several mechanisms, due to the abundant 
presence of amino (-NH2), carboxyl (-COOH) and alcohol (-OH) groups in the molecule. When 
chitosan comes into contact with water it swells and becomes protonated, leading to a positive

The drug release from the chitosan coated MCs is due to diffusion, swelling or erosion. When chitosan 
comes in contact with water it swells and become sticky and creates pores, which release the drug 
in a sustained manner (4).  
These adhesive properties sustain the retention time of API, which gives the advantage of a 
controlled release system. This can improve the therapeutic bioavailability of the drug, which makes 
it an excellent drug carrier (43). Moreover, chitosan can induce penetration by facilitating trans- and 
paracellular transport of the drug by opening the tight junctions of the epithelial cells (4).

1.6.3. Alginate 
Alginate is a natural biodegradable, non-toxic and an FDA approved anionic polymer derived from 
brown seaweed and is soluble in water. Alginates are extensively used in the cosmetic, food, and 
pharmaceutical industry due to its lack of toxicity. In pharmaceutical research sodium or calcium salt 
of alginate is the most used complex (36,44). The polymer is a versatile biomaterial for viscosity 
enhancement, drug encapsulation, and bioadhesive film for drug delivery. Formulations with alginate 
can sustain the therapeutic efficacy of the API, resulting in a reduced dose frequency (35).  
          Sodium alginate is a linear hydrophilic polysaccharide, composed of two types of monomers 
residues (GM) of 1,4-linked β-D-mannuronic acid (M) and 𝛼-L-guluronic acid (G) (35,45). The 
mucoadhesive feature of alginate is caused by the presence of the carboxylic acid moiety, which 
create a hydrogen bond with the glycoprotein of mucin through carboxyl-hydroxyl interactions. Thus, 
alginate has demonstrated to have excellent bioadhesive effects (36,46).

1.7. 
Mucoadhesive interaction 
Mucoadhesive polymers can be divided into three types: anionic, cationic and non-ionic polymers. 
The mucoadhesive interactions that can occur are ionic bonds, covalent bonds, hydrogen bonds, Van 
der Waals bonds and hydrophobic interactions.  
• Ionic bonds are when two opposite charges attract each other by electrostatic interactions 
and forms a strong bond.  
• covalent bonding, the electrons are shared between atoms, through which the orbitals of 
these become filled resulting in strong binding.  
==PAGE:19==
 
Page 19 of 79 
• Hydrogen bonding is when a hydrogen atom is attracted to the other electronegative atoms 
(oxygen, nitrogen, fluorine) (47). The hydrogen atom is shared, and the bond formed is 
weaker than covalent and ionic bonds.   
• Van der Waals bonding describes dipole-dipole and dipole induced attraction in polar 
molecules, as well as non-polar compounds (electrostatic interaction). Van der Waals bonding 
is one of the weakest bonding interactions.  
• Hydrophobic bonds (hydrophobic effect) are indirect bonds, which occur when non-polar 
groups are in an aqueous solution (47). Some of the mucus interactions with the mucin 
glycoproteins are displayed in Figure 9.

1.8. 
Advanced Drug Delivery Systems 
 
 
Traditional oral formulations such as tablets and capsules have multidirectional release meaning 
release of drug in all directions. This results in a loss of the API and therefore, decreases the 
absorption into the systemic circulation (3). 
 
          To overcome the obstacles with oral delivery, microfabricated drug delivery devices such as 
MCs have come to the forefront as an advanced delivery vesicle (1,4). MCs have the potential to 
facilitate a unidirectional release due to the structural design with only one side of the reservoir 
exposed, which allows for a better control of drug release as compared to micro- and nanoparticles 
with multidirectional release see Figure 9 (1,49).

1.8.1. Microcontainers 
 
The SU-8 MCs are a negative epoxy photoresist-based polymer that are light sensitive, and the 
material comprises of 8 epoxy moieties, such as the solvent cyclopentenone and a photo acid initiator 
(triarylsulfonium hexafluoroantimonate) (50). MC sizes are in the range of 300 μm in diameter with 
a reservoir depth of 270 μm with capacity of 10 nL in volume Figure 10 (7). Furthermore, the SU-8 
material has  certain thermal, mechanical and chemically characteristics, which makes it an excellent 
model for protection against the harsh environment in the stomach (1,49).  
 
          MCs henceforth protect the therapeutic inside an enclosed micro-reservoir from the presence 
of chemical and enzymatic degradation in the stomach and minimizes the drug loss (3). In addition, 
MCs have been  demonstrated to exhibit mucoadhesive capabilities due to engulfment in the mucus, 
this result in sustaining the contact period for the uptake of epithelial cells leading to an enhanced 
absorption time (3,7). To improve the delivery to the small intestines, the MCs can be coated with a 
mucoadhesive polymer, which enables to directly target the MCs toward the epithelium and 
enhances the bioavailability (1).

1.8.2. Loading of the Microcontainers 
There are several methods for loading a drug into the SU-8 MCs. These methods include inject 
printing, super-critical impregnation and powder embossing (see Figure 11) (7). Inkjet printing 
dispenses solutions into the MCs with high accuracy and minimal waste. However, this technology 
results in a low drug efficiency due to multiple deposition and are time consuming (7,51). The 
impregnation method reduces drug waste and avoids the use of organic solvents, but requires a 
solubility of the drug in a matrix (7). By powder embossing, a shadow mask is used to cover the space 
between each MC on the chip, to reduce residuals between each MC. Here, a shadow A bonding 
pressure is applied, which presses the drug into the cavity of the MCs and allows for a high efficiency 
of drug loading (7,52). Another method is by a manual brush technique, where first a shadow mask 
aligns the chip with MCs to prevent drug residues between the MCs. A brush is used to press the drug 
into the MCs, followed by air pressure to remove excess residue (1,7). This method is utilized for 
loading of the MCs, as it offers a high yield and is a fast technique.

1.8.3. Spray coating 
 
The current methods for polymer coating techniques includes, spin coating, inkjet printing, dip 
coating and spray coating (53,54). The dipping technique is one of the first and simplest techniques 
for achieving thin polymer films, which include three simple steps: immersion, dwell, and withdrawal 
(54). The spin coating method, a solution is applied onto the center of a substrate, the substrate gets 
rotated with high rpm (rotations per minute), which distribute the solvent and creates a film layer. 
Spin coating, however, has low coating yields since the process has to be repeated to build up layers.  
It also creates an excessive amount of waste during the process and has disadvantages of coating 
three dimensional structures. Spray coating has the advantage of coating multidimensional 
structures and has shown great transfer efficiency and potential for scalability (7,53,54). Spray 
coating by ultrasonic atomization deposits micro sized droplets by a nitrogen carrier gas on a 
substrate on a hot plate. The hot plate promotes an evaporation of the solvent, which forms the thin 
film. The film thickness can be controlled by the concentration of the polymer solvent and spray 
coating process parameters to achieve the desired coating layer. The spray coating parameters taken 
into considerations are the nozzle path speed, temperature of the hot plate, number of passes in 
total, infusion rate, distance between nozzle and sample as well as air pressure (53,55).

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is a versatile instrument to characterize surface structure, 
topography and surface morphology. SEM uses high energy electron beams and a probe to scan the

surface morphology, by emitting beam of electrons, which comes in contact with the specimen under 
vacuum. The interaction between the sample surface and electron bean results in transmission of 
primary and secondary electrons. When the primary beam interacts with the sample surface, it 
caused ionization of specimen atoms, in which the loosely electrons gets reflected back as secondary 
electrons. These secondary electrons provide the topographic information and generates a high-

1.8.5. Dissolution 
The µDISS profiler is an optic UV system for measuring dissolution and solubility in small volume 
concentration (7,58). The µDISS profiler is set to a computer to collect data, and the apparatus can 
analyze up to eight samples at the same time. The instrument uses UV spectroscopy to measure 
concentration of release over time. The UV probes with a mirror attached are emerged into vials 
containing the biorelevant dissolution medium that simulates the fluids of the GIT. The probes 
measure the absorption of the solution, in which passes through the UV light. The samples for 
measurements are attached to a magnetic stirrer with carbon tape. Prior to measuring the 
concentration of the samples, a standard curve is first performed with a known concentration (59). 
In vitro dissolution testing is required for oral dosage forms to assess drug release from formulations. 
It is a great tool for quality control procedure. It is used in development at early stages before 
experiments on cell studies or in vivo experiments (7,60).

1.8.7. Texture analyzer 
Texture analyzer (TA) is an instrument used to measure physical product characteristic, such as 
hardness, gel strength, drug, properties of foods, adhesiveness, cosmetics etc.(62) In this project, the 
TA system is used to measure the adhesive force and detachment of the MC, coated with a 
mucoadhesive polymer from a substrate (intestinal tissue) (63). Adhesiveness or stickiness is essential 
for the product quality, and are affected by various factors such as temperature, moisture and 
different products and used repeatedly. The TA measures adhesion peak forces to verify if it is 
possible to measure a single coated MC, and measure the difference between different coating 
thicknesses (64).

2. HYPOTHESES  
 
It is hypothesized that when coating the cavity of microcontainers (MCs) with a mucoadhesive 
polymer, it will exhibit a higher mucoadhesion compared to uncoated MCs. Coating the cavity of MCs 
with chitosan will provide a sustained drug release at the site of target, and the thicker the coating 
layer deposited on the MCs, it will lead to a slower drug release profile with a higher adherence. 
Coating with PEG and alginate does not hinder release as they are a water-soluble polymer, they will 
exhibit higher mucoadhesion than uncoated MCs.  
 
3. AIM 
The objective of this work was to explore and investigate three different mucoadhesive polymers 
with three different coating thicknesses, as a lid for MCs for oral drug delivery and compare it to 
uncoated microcontainers. The following aims to be carried out were: 
• The MCs were loaded with furosemide and are sealed with a mucoadhesive lid 
• In vitro studies were performed to measure drug release from the coated MCs.  
• Ex vivo studies were performed on intestinal tissue to investigate mucoadhesion of the 
drug-loaded and coated MCs.

Drug loading into the SU-8 microcontainers

5.5. 
Release of furosemide from the microcontainers 
 
5.5.1. Preparation of medium 
 
The phosphate buffer solution (1L) was prepared by dissolving Sodium phosphate monobasic 
dihydrate (NaH2PO4) and sodium chloride (NaCl) in Milli-Q water. The buffer solution was adjusted to 
pH 6.5 with a pH meter (Hanna Instruments Inc., Woonsocket, USA) with 1M natrium hydroxide 
(NaOH). The phosphate buffer was used to prepare the FaSSIF medium. The stock solution of the 
standard curve was made by dissolving furosemide with purified water with 1M NaOH of pH 
(10mg/mL). 
 
5.5.2. In vitro studies 
 
The in vitro drug release from the drug loaded MCs without and with coatings (three different coating 
thicknesses of PEG, chitosan and alginate) were carried out by a µDiss profiler (pION INC, Woburn, 
MA, USA) in FaSSIF medium (pH 6.5). The experiments were performed under 37°C, with a stirring 
rate of 100 rpm with an in situ UV probe of 1 mm attached. The chips with the MCs were adhered on 
a cylindrical magnetic stirrer with carbon tape and added to the vials (1,3). Next, 10 mL of FaSSIF 
medium was poured into the vials, and the UV probes in each vial detects the absorbance of drug 
release at 330-350 nm for furosemide. A standard curve was employed prior to the experiments, the 
measurements were collected with 2 spectra: 200 spectra in 10 sec. and 400 spectra in 1 min. with 
the total duration time of 7.5 h. and the release study were carried out in 4 replicates.

5.6. 
Mucoadhesive studies 
 
The mucoadhesive features of the polymer coatings on MCs were explored in ex vivo studies with a 
closed perfusion model, open perfusion model and with a Texture analyzer (TA) to determine a single 
MC adhesive strength with the different mucoadhesive coating tested. Titanium-gold (Ti/Au) release 
MCs were used for the ex vivo studies for the ease of scraping the MCs of the chip. 
 
5.6.1. Ex vivo closed perfusion model  
 
For the closed perfusion model, empty Ti/Au release MCs, with and without drug and with polymer 
coating were weighed and stated with the average weigh and SD. Next, a gelatin capsule size 9 
(Torpac ®, USA) was weighted before and after filling with drug loaded MCs (300-450 MCs per 
capsule). A 18 cm long frozen piece of porcine small intestine was thawed for 40-50 min., and was 
placed on an angled holder at 20°C, which were held together with a cord. In the starting end of the 
intestine was a peristaltic pump connected.  Whilst a beaker with a filter paper were put at the other 
end. The humidity in the closed set-up was held in the range of 30-70 % (39). Which is similar to the 
one found in the literature for characterization of bio-adhesion (67). The intestine was washed with 
FaSSIF for 15 min. with a flow of 4.1 mL/min. Afterwards, the angle holder was set at 10°C and washed 
for an additionally 5 min. with the pump set at a flow of 1.55 mL/min. The tube connected to the 
intestine was removed, and with a capsule containing the MCs was placed in the intestine (approx. 2 
cm from the start end). For 15 min the capsule was allowed to dissolve, and the tube was again 
connected to the intestine, and the angle holder was placed back to an angle of 20°C. The experiment 
was continued for 30 min with the pump set at a flow of 1.55 mL/min. Next, the intestine was cut 
open and divided into 9 pieces and arranged on microscope glass slides. The height and length of the 
tissue pieces were documented, and where the MCs were found (tissue piece no.1-9) and their 
orientations (see Figure 17) were observed by a light microscope (Carl Zeiss, Germany) and noted.

5.6.3.4. 
Design of experiments  
 
The next step was Design of experiments (DOE) for increasing and optimizing the experiment design

thickness of mucus, and measurements were also performed on different location on the tissue.

randomized complete block design (RCMD) a Latin Square design.

The topography of the PEG coating of 6 μm thickness is referred to as a mushroom conformation

6.5.1. Improving parameters for adhesion test on single microcontainers

mucoadhesion with 0.075N with a lower SD compared to the contact force of 0.1N. A too high contact 
force may damage the tissue, thus leading to a higher standard deviation (68). In addition, testing the 
contact force in tissue where the loose mucus has been scraped off a higher peak force was obtained

reached a plateau. Furthermore, the measured peak force of the nail tip did not show a high adhesion

adhesion test (Figure 43). Additionally, it is hard to differentiate the measured peak force of the glued 
MCs on the nail tip with UV glue, as it is similar to the measured adhesion force of the glue.

6.5.4. Chitosan

properties. Thus, a pre-test was first carried out to test various contact times by the TA on mucus to

300, 360 and 420 sec. were examined to evaluate its influence on the mucoadhesive strength. The 
highest peak force was obtained at 360 sec.; hence this contact force was chosen to allow the coating 
to swell, which may enhance the mucoadhesive performance (see Figure 49).

7. CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
The initial aim of the project was to load the MCs with furosemide, this was successfully done by a 
simple manual brush method. Three mucoadhesive polymers with three different coating thicknesses 
have been explored to seal the furosemide loaded SU-8 MCs. By sealing the MCs with a mucoadhesive 
lid, a maximum and targeted delivery towards the mucus is achieved, hence result in a greater 
bioavailability.  
==PAGE:72==
 
Page 72 of 79 
          The subsequent aim of this project was to measure the drug release by in vitro studies. These 
polymer coatings provided different release profiles. The in vitro release of PEG coated MCs exhibited 
a rapid drug release. The PEG coating thickness of 22 μm showed a faster release rate compared to 
control, due to the hydrophilic nature of PEG, which enhances the observed rapid release. The 
release of chitosan coated MCs showed a sustained release, with different release profiles due to the 
swelling properties. The release from the alginate coatings exhibited a burst release and induced the 
release of furosemide. From these results, it can be concluded that PEG and alginate can induce the 
release rate, whereas MCs with chitosan coating provides a sustained release. Depending on the 
polymer coating, an enhance the release of a poorly soluble drug or promote a controlled release. 
          The final aim of the project was to investigate mucoadhesion of the coated and uncoated 
(control) MCs in porcine small intestines. Ex vivo studies were performed with a closed and open 
perfusion model to assess how the MCs interact with the mucus layer. Another ex vivo study was 
performed with a TA to measure the adhesion peak force of a single MC. The closed perfusion model 
frequently had problems with MCs sticking together. Especially for MCs coated with chitosan, where 
it was observed that all the MCs were mainly stuck together in a lump. The closed perfusion model 
was not successful in assessing the mucoadhesive property of the different polymers and coating 
thicknesses. Therefore, an open perfusion model was utilized. In the open perfusion model, the MCs 
coated with PEG lead to a 20% higher mucoadhesion than the control. It is also noticeable, that the 
MCs with the lowest PEG coating of 6 μm thickness had an adherence almost 40% higher than the 
control. Overall, the MCs with a PEG coating thickness of 22 μm had the best mucoadherence, as 
demonstrated by these experiments. For the MCs coated with chitosan there was a 30-50% higher 
adherence to the mucus than control. The thicker the coating thickness the higher the adherence 
was observed for the chitosan coatings. The MCs with alginate coatings lead to a 7-11% higher 
mucoadherence compared to the control, which is also due to the low coating thicknesses tested. 
The perfusion models have shown that the MCs with a mucoadhesive lid can be functionalized to 
enhance mucoadhesiveness and mucus penetration. For future studies, different concentrations of 
PEG and alginate solutions can be explored and compared to improve the mucoadhesion 
performance.  
==PAGE:73==
 
Page 73 of 79 
The Adhesion measurements were conducted by the TA, where the parameters for the contact force 
and time have been explored. A Latin Square Design was presented to eliminate tissue variability and 
the time factor, which may influence the measured adhesion. For MCs coated with PEG, all three 
different coating thicknesses had an induced adhesion. A higher adherence was observed with the 
MCs coated with three different coating thicknesses. However, for the alginate coatings, a higher 
adherence than control was only observed for a coating thickness of 2 μm. This DOE demonstrated 
that for some of the MCs, with polymer coatings, it was possible to measure the adhesion force. 
However, the tissue location influenced the adhesion peak force greatly for all experiments. This 
tissue location is thus an important factor that will have to be taken into consideration when 
optimizing the DOE. The DOE is crucial to obtaining repeatable results and a more accurate analysis 
of the results of the experiments.  
The adhesion measurement of a single MC is a novel approach, and more research has to be done to 
find an optimal contact force and time. It could also be interesting in comparing different contact 
forces instead of only testing one contact force. For polymers that have swelling properties, here it 
would be beneficial to test different contact times since the swelling improves mucoadhesion and 
entanglement in the mucus layer. Moreover, the UV-glue used to glue a single MC on a nail tip, had 
an adhesion peak force measured for some of the experiments. This factor also has to be eliminated 
or taken into consideration in future design experiments. The TA has proven to be able to measure 
the adhesion force for different coating thicknesses, which is important to determine which coating 
thicknesses have the best mucus adherence. 
           As a final remark, the SU-8 MCs used in this study is an epoxy-based resin and therefore can 
accumulate in the GIT. Thus, in order to ensure excretion of the MCs from the body, the material of 
these reservoirs have to be made of biodegradable polymers in order to be utilized as a successful 
drug carrier. Such biodegradable and biocompatible polymers that are FDA approved could 
potentially be made of polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), Polymers poly (L-lactic acid) (PLLA) and 
poly(caprolactone) (PCL) (7,78).
