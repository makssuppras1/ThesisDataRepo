DTU Campus Development
Abstract
The main subject of this thesis is the future of urban development at DTU’s
campus in Lyngby. Current urban development in the area is taken into account, as
well as the history and values of the institution. General trends in urban planning
throughout the world are examined, both on a larger scale as well as focusing on
universities, and those trends used as an inspiration for the future of DTU. A weighted
factor comparison, with a focus on the three pillars of sustainability, is applied to a
series of options regarding urban development on Lyngby campus. The option that
obtained the highest grade was the relocation of parking spaces to the outskirts of
campus to free up area for building projects and a green environment.
As urban
development of the campus is a broad research area, possibilities for future studies
are also presented.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Urban plans are an important part of construction, whether it is changing existing area
or building new towns from nothing. Even though urban plans can change drastically
in just a few years, e.g. as new political forces come to power or when new technology
emerges [127], the time scope has generally increased, i.e. we are looking further into the
future and creating plans that hopefully will be satisfactory on the long run. Some have
tried to guess what the future will hold with relatively good predictions, e.g. the city
center not being only one for a whole city, but instead many smaller centers inside the
neighborhoods. At the same time, cities are being planned more for the pedestrians in it,
rather than for their cars as the case has been in the past decades [36].
Looking back to the time before the private car became a popular choice, the streets
were shared by pedestrians, children playing, horses and streetcars [90] and when the ﬁrst
cars emerged some cities even discussed passing laws against them [89]. We can see that
only one century ago, the private car was not considered a necessity [94]. In the future the
current trends will become increasingly important, especially when we consider the mega
cities which have been constantly growing, and are expected to more than double, from
14 in 1995 to over 30 in 2025 [88]. Therefore it is important for all regions to aim towards
constantly improving their urban plans.
The main concept of sustainability is to drive our innovations and not compromise
our way of life, while protecting our natural environment, ecological and human life. Sus-
tainability is usually deﬁned as "development that meets the needs of the present without
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs". Three pillars have
been deﬁned, which all need to be fulﬁlled in order to achieve sustainability. Those pillars
are Economic Development, Social Development and Environmental Protection. These pil-
lars are often known as the triple bottom line and they state that in order to be sustainable
a project should be economically beneﬁcial and not cause harm to the inhabitants nor the
environment [84]. An example of a sustainable area is the state of California, which is
leading in environmentally friendly practices in the United States and has GDP growth
more than 1 percent higher than the rest of the United States [100,112].
Urban planning projects should now be aiming to be more sustainable than before, as
cities need to take action to meet the ambitious goals of the Paris agreements [64] and

to compete against other cites, both regarding attracting businesses as well as talented
inhabitants [93]. India is an example of a country which is becoming more sustainable,
where plans are to make every car sold after 2030 an electric car [145]. In the United
States, over 1000 mayors, businesses and universities spread over the country have signed
a declaration in order to "pursue ambitious climate goals" in order to keep up with the
demands of the Paris agreement [146]. Some cities have been transforming themselves into
knowledge cities and/or smart cities. A knowledge city aims to encourage knowledge to
grow by investing in education, research and training [58]. A smart city is a city that
invests in their information and communication technology (ICT) to make it more eﬃcient
for their inhabitants and businesses. The concept of a smart city goes beyond ICT as it
can contain smarter transport networks, improved water supplies and waste disposal, and
even improved eﬃciency of heating and illuminating buildings [42].
DTU can trace its history back to 1829 to the initiative of Hans Christian Ørsted [72].
They started in central Copenhagen and moved around a few times before building up their
current facilities in Lyngby in the 1960s and inaugurating the ﬁrst buildings in 1974 [69].
As of 2016, just over 11 thousand students are enrolled at DTU and close to 6 thousand
persons employed by the institute [25]. The number of students has been increasing rapidly
over the past years from just over 7 thousand in 2010 [19], partly due to a merge with
IHK in 2013 [128]. DTU aims to become one of the top 5 leading universities in Europe,
and they are well on their way to achieve that, as DTU is in top 6 leading universities in
Europe, according to some rankings [13,56].
DTU’s main campus is located in Lyngby and is spread over 106 hectares [55]. The
campus is split into four quadrants by Anker Engelunds Vej and a parking lot going
north-south through the campus.
Most of DTU’s building were built within the ﬁrst
years of Lyngby campus, i.e. back in the 1960s-1970s. In 2010 they started an eleven
year construction plan [34] and as of January 2017, they had 355 thousand sq. meters of
available facilities on Lyngby campus [46].
In the beginning of 2017, DTU was spread over 13 locations, but with the ambitious
eleven year construction and refurbishing plan DTU is investing heavily in their infrastruc-
ture on their main campus in Lyngby. DTU expects, after these eleven years, to decrease
their locations down to six, one in Greenland, two test sites for wind turbines in Høvsøre
and Østerild, and their three campuses, in Lyngby, Ballerup and Risø [15,129]. In addition
to the vast eleven year plan, more new buildings are being introduced to Lyngby or being
proposed [21], as well as the arrival of a light rail train ready for use in 2023/2024 [47].
With the new building 310 and remodeling of 228, DTU is planning to relocate employees
from Risø campus to Lyngby [21,29].
DTU faces the same major obstacles as many other institutions when it comes to their
growth, i.e.
being sustainable while making a good urban plan.
How they choose to
proceed and tackle these obstacles can have a signiﬁcant aﬀect on their competitive status
in the future. Urban planning and sustainability must go hand in hand, and DTU should
consider sustainability when making any drastic changes to their urban plan.
If DTU

2.1
History of DTU’s Infrastructure
When DTU was founded in its original form in 1829, it was located at two professors’
manors in Studiestræde and Sankt Peders Stræde in central Copenhagen [72]. The school
was there until 1889 when it was moved to Sølvtorvet. The new facilities could however not
keep up with the rapid increase of students [70], and in 1929 they started working on a new
building complex at Østervoldgade. The building process was delayed signiﬁcantly, mostly
due to the second world war and its consequences, and oﬃcially the facilities began to be
used in 1954 [71]. It soon became clear that with their rapid growth, DTU would have to be
moved outside of central Copenhagen to be able to continue growing. The ﬁrst cornerstone
in Lundtofte, Lyngby, was taken in 1960 and the ﬁrst buildings were inaugurated in 1974
[69]. From 1974 to 2010 there was no major constructional development on the grounds
of DTU’s campus in Lyngby, but in 2010 an eleven year construction plan was started.
Multiple new buildings have and will be opened in this time period and others renovated.
Over 4 billion DKK are assigned to over 20 building projects around the campus [34], and
change in aerial view of Lyngby campus can be seen on Figure 2.1.
When comparing these two ﬁgures, we can see that many of the new buildings are
located on existing parking lots, e.g. buildings 128, 130, 220, 225 and 324, as well as
parking spaces being changed into green areas, e.g. Matematiktorvet. In order to fulﬁll
the criteria regarding parking spaces in relation to square meters built in the local plan,
DTU has proposed multiple new parking lots that they want to build between 2016-2020.
The locations for these new parking lots can be seen in yellow on Figure 2.2 [46]. On
Figures D.13-D.18 in Appendix D we can see how the area currently is for locations 1,2,4,5
and 7. As for the other two, planned parking lot number 6 is currently within building
grounds and parking lot number 3 has already been built.

Research Question
The main focus of this master thesis is to analyse and oﬀer ideas regarding the urban
development at DTU’s campus in Lyngby.
To analyse DTU’s urban plan for Lyngby campus it is essential to consider what DTU

How can DTU increase their usable ﬂoor space to accommodate future growth

4.1
Urban Planning
Urban planning is a task that every city and every town has to master in order to optimize
the usage of their land. It has been around for millenniums, from planning ancient Rome
to building Lusail, a city in Quatar for 450 thousand inhabitants in 10 years [115].
When creating a new urban plan, it is important to look at the needs of all stakeholders
involved, e.g. those who will live or work within the area as well as those ﬁnancially involved
in the project. Therefore urban plans diﬀer, both between cities and with time, as not
all cities have the same needs. An example are the highways going through Boston [5]
compared to many bike lanes in Copenhagen [17]. Both political parties and inhabitants
can have an inﬂuence on the making or changing of an urban plan. In Boston a highway
through the city was cancelled due to protesting by inhabitants [5], and in Reykjavík,
Iceland, the urban plan changed drastically as new political forces came into power [127].
When looking back, for instance to ancient Rome, we can see how the challenges of
urban planning have changed throughout the centuries. In the times of ancient Rome,
security, administrative eﬃciency and economic exploitation were important aspects when
founding new towns. The grid pattern was widely used in towns ruled by the Romans,
as well as the Greeks. Towns with this grid pattern are centered around a square where
two larger streets meet at a right angle. These streets often lead north-to-south and west-
to-east and at the ends they often had the only gates into the city, as it was otherwise
surrounded by large walls for its protection [37]. Another signiﬁcant part of the urban
plan in ancient Rome were bridges they built to carry their water supply into the cities,
reducing the manual labor needed [45].
When looking closer to the present we can see that the architect Max Chanowitz [36]
has collected what he considers the main trends happening in cities around the world, e.g.
how they are changing from being planned for the automobile and focusing on walk-ability
and bikes. Since cities are not being planned for automobiles anymore, building out to the
suburbs is becoming decreasingly popular [36]. With more people moving to cities [85], at
the rate of 3 million per week [86], the number of cities with population over 3 million will
more than double, from 70 to 150, before 2050 [98]. The density will increase as buildings
17
==PAGE:28==
Literature Research
become taller to accommodate more people per square kilometer of land. Green spaces
and natural features are being seen as an asset and an important part of a living city. With
the increased density, spaces that used to be considered unusable [36], are now being seen
in a new light, e.g. the High Line in New York, an old, elevated, unused railroad track has
been transformed into a green park for residents to enjoy [44].
Another trend is to not have strictly deﬁned neighborhoods, but rather mixing together
residential area, businesses and light industry to have alive and attracting neighborhoods.
As a result from this, the concept of a one city center does not apply anymore, as each
neighborhood can have its own center. It can be beneﬁcial that the city center is not as
popular, as it often forces the prices up and kills all diversity [36].
Looking closer at what is happening in new neighborhoods in Denmark, more precisely
three new areas that are all on diﬀerent stages in their construction, i.e. Ørestad, Nordhavn
and the city of Vinge. Ørestad is still under construction and has been for a while. When
it will be fully constructed, it will contain a mixed urban area, with 60% commercial and
oﬃce buildings, 20% residential area and 20% for cultural facilities, services, retail and
institutions. The area will also have a high environmental quality, containing lakes and
canals [96]. A similar story can be told about Nordhavn, as green and blue areas are kept
in focus, as well as plans being made to favour to pedestrians and bicycles over cars [124].
The city of Vinge is designed around a public park, where residents can walk the town
from end to end without needing to cross a single street. This town is located on the
outskirts of Copenhagen and is connected to the city center through an S-train. The train
station in Vinge will lie through the center of the public park and will be lower than its
surroundings. The residents will therefore be able to cross over the station on their way
throughout the city. The city will be placed on 370 hectare land and when it will be
completed in 2033 it will be the residence for around 20 thousands [43, 52]. We can see
from these three new areas that new neighbourhoods, in Denmark at least, are going to
become more mixed into the nature.
With better understanding of the nature and its limitations, sustainability has become
increasingly important when a city is being improved or revised. The Danish Architecture
Centre has introduced 12 themes where cities can improve to become more sustainable,
and have published cases where cities have improved themselves [18]. One of those cases is
from Mexico City, in which a market was set up where inhabitants could come with their
recyclable waste and trade it for fresh food. This market is set up once a month and each
time there are on average 12 tonnes of material handed in to be recycled. Mexico City’s
main problem with the market is that too much waste is being returned, as they often
run out of products to give back to the inhabitants, but the project helps the unemployed
inhabitants of Mexico City get quality food products [26].
French Polynesia is a cluster of islands located in the Paciﬁc Ocean [144]. They are
facing one of the challenges of climate change, i.e.
the rising sea water level.
Their
solution is a radical one as they are planning to build a ﬂoating city oﬀthe coast of Tahiti.
These new kinds of cities consist of two types of ﬂoating platforms that are rectangular or

Not all cities work out as they are planned. One example is the city of "California

4.2
Knowledge Cities
There is no agreed deﬁnition of a knowledge city [97] and therefore many diﬀerent deﬁni-
tions exist. One states that it is "a city that was purposefully designed to encourage the
nurturing of knowledge" [98], while another deﬁnes it by "The term knowledge city is short
hand for a regional economy driven by high value added exports created through research,
technology and brainpower. Compared to other cities in advanced economies, knowledge
cities invest signiﬁcantly more of the community’s income (GDP) in education, training
and research" [58], and the third as a place where "knowledge is discovered, created, nour-
ished, exchanged, and transformed into a new form" [97]. No matter how diﬀerently they
are put into words they all imply the same concept, i.e. that a knowledge city needs to be
able to share information with their inhabitants and between them. How a knowledge city
is deﬁned is not the important part, but rather how they operate and increase knowledge
within themselves. According to Ergazakis [58] a knowledge city should be able to:
• provide access to new communication technologies.
19
==PAGE:30==
Literature Research
• have good research that can provide a platform for new knowledge based services
and goods.
• provide instruments to make knowledge accessible to its residents, both eﬀectively
and eﬃciently.
• generate, attract and retain highly skilled workforce.
• have open civil service centers that can foster face to face relations.
Beneﬁts of a knowledge city are many, both for the urban government as well as
the residents. Some examples are an increase in good jobs, more sustainable economy,
attractiveness of foreign investors, increase in tourism, improved educational service and
more tolerant environment for minorities [58].
There are multiple cities that identify themselves as knowledge cities [57]. One success-
fully implemented knowledge city is the city of Barcelona, Spain. Barcelona is one of the
largest university communities in Europe with its eight universities, and in addition there
are multiple technology centers and a network of scientiﬁc parks that is used by companies
working in technology and engineering ﬁelds. In the case of Barcelona, knowledge attracts
knowledge to the degree that over 28% of all companies working with high or medium high
technology industries in Spain are located in this region [40]. Other successful knowledge
cities include Stockholm in Sweden which was the sixth most competitive region in the
world and the most competitive outside USA in 2008 [7], and Singapore, winner of the
MAKCi award in 2007 [40].
Early in 2012, an organization called Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge and Urban
Development was founded in Lyngby-Taarbæk commune to develop "one of the leading
knowledge-intensive university towns in Northern Europe" [132]. This organization has
grown during its ﬁve years in operation and now consists of over 70 businesses, educational
institutions and public authorities [134] compared to the original eight founding members
[132]. Their vision is in four parts: First to develop Lyngby-Taarbæk commune into a
leading university town, with the best conditions for researchers, students, businesses and
entrepreneurs. Second to be a center for development for a knowledge-intensive business
cluster. Third to be an international hub and fourth to be a living laboratory for sustainable
urban and business development, [132]. By doing so they will "transform the city of Lyngby
into a thriving knowledge-intensive innovation district around the DTU campus" [132].
Many knowledge intensive organizations have relocated themselves into the city of
knowledge, e.g. Microsoft, Aquaporin, Maersk Drilling, Maersk Supply Service, Visma
Consulting and Copenhagen Business Academy. In addition, Novozymes and H.C. Ørsted
Gymnasie are building new facilities close to DTU [131,140]. DTU is among other compa-
nies highly interested in making Lyngby into a knowledge city and Claus Nielsen, Executive
Vice President of DTU, is the chairman of the Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge and Ur-
ban Development board [135]. The organization has conducted surveys, created awareness
about mobility management, and are encouraging people to use alternative transportation,

When developing any area, whether it is building from scratch or renovating, it is sensible

5.2
Weighted Factor Analysis
We will apply a weighted factor analysis to answer the research question How can DTU
increase their usable ﬂoor space to accommodate future growth while focusing on sustain-
ability and maintaining a beautiful and functional campus? presented in Chapter 3.
First we will present some scenarios regarding the future growth of DTU. Second we will
present indicators which DTU values, categorized into the three pillars of sustainability.
Then we present various options for DTU to consider, which are graded based on indicators
and scenarios. Those grades are then used to evaluate the gain of diﬀerent options for DTU.

5.2.4
Weighted Factor Comparison
For each option, scenario and indicator we will give a grade on scale ranging from -3 to 3,
where the grades are as follows.
• The grade -3 represents that the change will lead to much worse circumstances.
• The grade -2 represents that the change will lead to worse circumstances.
• The grade -1 represents that the change will lead to a little bit worse circumstances.
• The grade 0 represents that the circumstances will not change.
• The grade 1 represents that the change will lead to a little bit better circumstances.
• The grade 2 represents that the change will lead to better circumstances.
• The grade 3 represents that the change will lead to much better circumstances.
These grades compare the change to how the situation is in the beginning of 2017. In
the case of ﬂoor area and parking spaces, a lower grade will mean that it would be harder
to build and a higher grade that it would be easier. In the same manner, less traﬃc will
receive a higher grade than more traﬃc.
As the grades are a subjective evaluation we will go through the indicators and present
their grading criteria to ensure that all options follow the same standard. The grades can
be seen in Tables 5.12-5.16.

Chapter 7
Future Studies
Several questions and ideas regarding DTU and their future came to mind during the
process of this master thesis.
Some were directly related to urban development while
others were on the aspect of a sustainable university. In this chapter we will discuss some
of them and elaborate on how DTU could implement them in their future development.
7.1
Parking Spaces on Lyngby
First we will consider the possibilities that emerge if DTU decides to apply option 1 from
this thesis and move all parking lots to the outskirts of Lyngby campus. This would result
in many beneﬁts in addition to those that have been previously discussed. One beneﬁt
when parking lots are not scattered throughout the campus could be the reduction in
costs of maintaining and cleaning the parking lots, e.g. snow removal would be simpler in
a parking house rather than parking lots scattered over a large area. The wear of streets
would also decrease as cars would be outside of the area, resulting in a lower maintenance
cost. Building new parking facilities could also be easier as they would not need to be
planned around the existing, or new, buildings.
DTU also should consider the technical development in the world and the diﬀerent
needs that it brings, e.g.
electric vehicles.
The Swedish car manufacturer Volvo has
decided to stop developing diesel engines and instead moving their focus to electric and
hybrid engines [39]. India also plans to make every car sold there after 2030 an electric
vehicle and China wants one-ﬁfth of new cars sold after 2025 to be powered by alternative
fuel [145]. Even though the Danish government has not published such a drastic claim they
are encouraging people to buy an electric vehicle rather than a gas powered one, with a tax
reduction [108]. If the amount of electric cars continues to rise in Denmark, more parking
spaces will need to be transformed to contain charging stations for electric vehicles. If
DTU decided to build up new parking facilities they could include many charging points
for electric cars, which could be a simpler implementation than connecting them to existing
parking lots throughout the campus.
Furthermore DTU could use the fact that they were a car free campus to promote
themselves as an institute that is willing to make large changes towards sustainability as
47
==PAGE:58==
Future Studies
they can present an annual CO2 saving as a result of reduced traﬃc within their premises.
Even though the local plans specify a certain amount of parking spaces, future research
could include estimating whether all the spaces are needed. This could for example be if
the arrival of the light rail train lowers the number of personal vehicles coming to campus.
If Olli, the self driving bus, is coming to stay an optimization could be done regarding the
best route to drive around campus to spread those that still drive to campus to their oﬃces
or classes from the parking lot at the outskirts of campus. DTU could also conduct an

an additional distance every day, as health beneﬁts could lead to employees using fewer sick

7.2
Ballerup campus
Another idea that came up at the start of this thesis was the possibility of combining the
two campuses, i.e. Ballerup campus and Lyngby campus, on DTU main grounds in Lyngby.
This idea came from hearing about people that travel back and forth between Lyngby and
Ballerup for their studies, and even some classes being held in both locations [24]. In
addition DTU is currently closing most of their smaller locations along with Risø and
moving their operations to Lyngby campus, i.e.
with buildings 201-208, 228 and 310.
This was an important idea when the scope of this thesis was originally deﬁned, but when
researching this topic, i.e. moving campuses, a number of diﬃcult questions came up,
which are impossible to answer suﬃciently in a project like this as they require high level
of interdisciplinary collaboration as well as access to various information regarding DTU’s
facilities and a platform to gather the data that does not currently exist, e.g. surveys to
estimate utilization. These questions can be placed in a few categories, the ﬁrst one being
space.
If DTU were to consider this as a serious option they would ﬁrst of all need to look
into if and how they could possibly accommodate all the employees that would move to
Lyngby campus, along with facilities for additional classes resulting from the relocation.
That would require a thorough analyses of the utilization of classrooms, as well as deciding
whether to eliminate some classes, e.g. calculus and algebra 1 and calculus and algebra
2 are taught simultaneously in both locations and might be joined [22, 23]. Analysis of
other facilities, e.g. the cafeterias and study areas outside the classrooms should also be
conducted in order to see whether they can handle the addition. We saw in chapter 2 that
the institutions that are currently in buildings 451 and 358 are relocating themselves oﬀ
Lyngby campus. DTU could use the opportunity with the space freed up to combine these
locations and would perhaps not need as much additional space. It would however need
to be looked into whether the free space were applicable since diﬀerent types of facilities
are needed to accommodate diﬀerent things.
Even though combining these campuses will cause high initial expenses and a lot of

7.3
Sustainability
There are few ways DTU can try to become more environmentally friendly. Some are
related to the development of the campus while other address sustainability in a more
general way.
When DTU decides to build on existing green areas they can relocate the trees instead
of cutting them down with a machine called Tree spade [35]. This machine can relocate
trees with their roots and a tree spade in action can be seen on Figure 7.1. Using this
machine, DTU could relocate their trees and therefore maintain their old, and high, trees
and locate them in places where they can ﬂourish, either to a new place within campus or
give them away, e.g. to Lyngby commune or to another institution.
Figure 7.1: Tree being relocated with a tree spade [113].
Another idea would be to increase the recycling system within campus. Today there
are multiple garbage cans spread over campus and they are often in close proximity within
the buildings, e.g. as is in building 324 or the garden within building 101. These staggering
amounts of garbage cans usually have a small capacity and therefore need to be emptied
often. There also does not seem to be any classiﬁcation on the diﬀerent waste categories,
except in the cafeterias and Skylab, where food and deposit bottles are collected separately.
DTU should see whether they could implement a better classiﬁcation of waste categories
and possibly reduce their costs of waste disposal as some wastes can be sold, e.g. paper

We can consider the cost associated to building diﬀerent types of parking spaces. The
cost of building a parking space in a parking house rather than on the ground has a factor
of 5, while locating them in a parking garage has a factor of 10, i.e. 10 parking spaces
can be built on the ground for every two parking spaces in a parking house or for each
parking space in a garage [50]. At the same time a parking house or a garage can store
more cars per sq. meter of ground area as they will be stacked on top of each other. A
parking house can also be used in a more compact manner than parking on the ground, e.g.
excluding trees without aﬀecting the overall appearance. DTU therefore needs to consider
the associated costs as well as beneﬁts, i.e. evaluate the value of their land compared to
the initial cost of a large parking facility. If DTU were to build a parking house, they
should design the ground ﬂoor to make it easy to change from parking area to campus
service facilities and the other way around, e.g. by placing columns sensibly to be usable
as a facility as well as having enough height for campus service machines.

We have seen that a big institution like DTU can have many role models when it comes
to organizing their campus, e.g. Stanford and MIT, but in order to be a leader themselves
they need to be relatively fearless and open minded when it comes to critical changes. One
of those changes could be relocating the parking lots. If DTU were to relocate their parking
lots to the outskirts of the campus they would not be the ﬁrst university to have a car free
area within their campus, as we can see from Stanford university. They would however
be the ﬁrst one to dedicate all their campus to pedestrians apart from special needs,
e.g. maintenance and handicapped. We need to acknowledge that restricting cars to the
outskirts of campus requires investing in some new infrastructure to transport students
and employees within the campus area, especially here in the northern climate.
If we look into Olli, the self driving bus that is going to roam the streets of DTU, we
can see that he is the ﬁrst step in relocating the parking spaces to the outskirts of campus,
as he will transport people within campus. The problem is that Olli is only a one bus with
eight seats available, while thousands of drivers will be moved to the outskirts of campus.
DTU could invest in larger electric buses, either self driving or with a driver that would
drive a ﬁxed route through campus and transport majority of DTU’s drivers. Another way
DTU could distribute the people around campus would be by introducing a bike sharing
system that would contain drop oﬀlocations for bikes around the campus. In order to
not cause extra costs for those that choose to use that transportation mode the ﬁrst few
minutes could be free of charge, to enable even the slowest of riders to manoeuvre around
the campus.
Another challenge that rises when considering the relocation of parking lots is the
numbering of buildings at DTU. The campus is currently split into four quadrants by
Anker Engelunds Vej and the large parking lots, and the buildings are numbered according
to their position within the quadrants. If we were to build on the parking lot between the

quadrants we need to consider how to number the buildings in that area, e.g. what number
would a building between buildings 101 and 201 get? One option would be to rename all
buildings on campus, but that would most likely cause more harm than good. DTU could
also have a new numbering system on that area, e.g. starting with zero or ﬁve. That
system could for example have odd and even numbers on the opposite sides of Anker
Engelunds Vej or have positive or negative numbers depending on the position as in a
coordinate system. Another numbering system would also need to be considered if DTU
were to build outside of area A on Figure 2.2 in Chapter 2.
We can see that even though there are many beneﬁts associated with relocating the
parking lots, it is also a great challenge. Many aspects need to be taken into account and
the amount of planning that goes into a project of this scale is tremendous. The relocation
of the parking spaces would completely alter the current structure of the campus and
measures would have to be taken to ensure that it would not reduce the quality of the
research and education. Relocating the parking spaces requires a long term vision and
accurate estimations of future needs. Investing in a project like this and ending up with
either a parking facility that quickly reaches its capacity, or wasting resources on something
that is not used, would be a complete failure. As this option requires some modiﬁcations to
the current plans, this project would have to be in close cooperation with Lyngby-Taarbæk
commune.
