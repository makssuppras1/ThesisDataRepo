Abstract
This project was aimed at studying the effect of adding tip extensions to the IEA 10 MW Reference Wind
Turbine. The objective of adding a tip extension was to increase Annual Energy Production (AEP) of the
turbine while not exceeding the loads of the baseline rotor. Three different types of tip extensions were
considered - Plain tip extensions which just increased length of the blade, swept tip extensions which
were bent in the rotor plane and dihedral tip extensions which were bent out of the rotor plane. Unsteady
simulations were performed for Design Load Cases DLC12 and DLC13 according to the third edition of IEC
61400-1 standard for wind turbine design requirements using the aeroelastic code Horizontal Axis Wind
turbine simulation Code 2nd generation (HAWC2). Simulations revealed an increase in AEP for all tip
extensions, due to an increase in blade length. Maximum AEP rise was 2.78% for plain tip extensions, 2.65%
for swept tip extensions and 2.18% for dihedral tip extensions. Increase in blade length was followed by
increase in blade loads. Forward swept tip extensions exhibited a very high increase in blade root torsion due
to bend twist coupling inducing pitch to stall moment. Upwind dihedrals produced higher AEP compared
to downwind dihedrals. Controller tuning was carried out for the IEA 10 MW rotor using pole placement
method. It was found that low pole natural frequencies made the controller sluggish and slow to respond to
wind step changes while high natural frequencies made the controller aggressive and led to high overshoot.

Introduction
With the ever increasing energy demand and the need of the hour to meet that demand primarily with
renewable and environment-friendly sources of energy, it is vital to invest time and resources into inves-
tigating and optimizing energy systems all around the world. Wind energy is one of the most feasible
renewable energy source, and the potential that can be garnered from wind is unparalleled. In order to
maximize the potential and profitability of wind energy, proper utilization of the available wind resource
must be done. One way of achieving this is by having new blade designs or modifying existing blade
designs to capture extra power.
The scope of increasing the Annual Energy Production (AEP) of a wind turbine lies in the region
between cut-in speed and rated speed. As wind turbines grow in size, the power that can be ex-
tracted by the wind turbine increases. However, it is not possible to increase the size of a wind tur-
bine rotor beyond a certain value due to limitations in current technology.
While upscaling wind
turbines, the Square Cube Law must be kept in mind. Square Cube Law states that if the size of an
object is doubled, its volume, and thus the mass, increases by eight times. Proper counter-measures
must be taken while upscaling in order to limit the increase in mass of the turbine [1].
As an al-
ternative to increase power production without having to upscale the turbine beyond a limit, mod-
ifications on existing turbines can be done to improve the efficiency of converting the energy from
wind.
1.1
Objectives
In this study, the main objective is to increase the AEP of the wind turbine by adding tip extensions to an
existing rotor. The counterpart of increased power production is the possibility of increased loading on the
turbine structures. Thus, the change in loading on various turbine structures should be kept to a minimum.
This can be realized by first analyzing the change in AEP and loads of rotors with various tip extensions,
and control strategies can then be implemented to balance the change in loads with changes in AEP. The
main objectives can be summarized as shown below :
• Use existing design implementation tools and strategies to prepare tip extensions of three kinds - a
plain extension designed to simply extend the length of the blade, swept extension bent in the rotor
plane and dihedral extension bent out of the rotor plane.
• Attach the tip extension so generated on the baseline rotor - the IEA 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine
and determine change in AEP, Lifetime equivalent fatigue loads and Ultimate loads by running
unsteady turbulent simulations as specified in the Design Load Basis for onshore wind turbines.
• Controller tuning for tuning the gains of PI torque and pitch controller to have smooth setpoint
tracking.

Literature study
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) studies
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) study of adding winglets to a wind turbine blade was performed
by Johansen and Sørensen [2]. Results from this study showed that adding a rectangular winglet to an
existing blade increased the forces towards the tip of the blade and thus led to increased power production
of around 1.4% for wind speeds greater than 6 ms−1, with the drawback of an increased thrust of around
1.6%. A downstream pointing winglet produced higher power compared to upstream pointing winglets,
but care must be taken to consider the tower clearance.
Zahle et al [3] used a high fidelity 3D Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) code, EllipSys3D to re-
design the tip of the IEA 10 MW rotor with sectional load constraints along the blade. There were
12 design variables that were used to redesign the blade tip. There was a 1.6% increase in mechani-
cal power at lower wind speeds when the winglet height was 3% of the rotor radius, while winglet
sweep produced an increase in mechanical power at lower wind speeds. It was also found that it is
beneficial to have a winglet as opposed to a straight extension of the blade around the tip at a station
located at 95% of the blade radius. A winglet shape increased the mechanical power by almost 2.6%
without increasing flapwise loads close to the tip, while it required an additional 2% extension to the
straight blade to obtain the same increase in mechanical power with the downside of 10% increase in
loads.
Numerical investigation on winglet design and optimization using CFD was done in [4], with an objective
of maximizing the torque, thereby increasing the power produced by adding an optimized winglet. An
increase in power of 9% was attained by the optimized winglet. Numerical analysis of winglets using CFD
done by Johansen and Sørensen [5] showed that adding a winglet increased power by 2.8% while having
a thrust increase of 3.6%. The parameters varied to study different tips were winglet height, curvature
radius, sweep and twist. It was found that increasing the winglet height increased mechanical power and
thrust, twist angle had a very small effect on the power and thrust and sweeping the winglet did not lead
to increase in power.
Studies on winglets
A project called InnoTIP [6], conducted in collaboration between LM Wind Power and Energy Research
Center of the Netherlands (ECN), explored the possible increase in efficiency and increased turbulence in
the wake to enhance wake recovery in offshore wind farms, by developing three different blade tip designs.
An estimated 2-9% increase in power for a conventional tip free from the constraint of aerodynamic noise
and up to 10% increase in power produced for turbulators was observed on a 2.5 MW turbine with field
measurements.
According to Niels Pettersen [7], having a controllable winglet orientation with the help of sweep, skew and
cant angles provide an extra degree of freedom that significantly lowers blade root flap loads and causes
a small increase in thrust. It was also found in this study that a high level of sweep must be followed by
a high level of skew angle. Sessarego et al [8] studied the effects of winglets and sweep by simulating a
Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine using a vortex based aerodynamic model in simple and complex inflow
conditions including turbulence and extreme shear on an aero-elastic vortex code, MIRAS (Method for Inter-
active Rotor Aero-elastic Simulations). It was found that for simpler inflow conditions without turbulence,
swept blades and wingletted blades performed better and for complex inflow conditions, shorter swept
blades and wingletted blades produced lower loads on the turbine. Thrust force reduced for a downwind
winglet because of the reduction in normal loads at the tip as the winglet angled away from the incoming
2
==PAGE:13==
wind.
Gaunaa and Johansen found out that increase in power by using winglets was not due to downwind
vorticity shift, but rather due to the reduction of tip effects [9]. Winglets must be designed such that the
additional drag from the winglet is lesser than the induced drag on the blade, thus reducing the total drag.
In this study, it was found that having downwind winglets was effective compared to upwind winglets,
while an interesting conclusion was that the increase in power by having a winglet was lesser than the
increase in power by extending the blade radially by the same length.
Having non-conventional tip designs like a winglet has proven to be effective in increasing the power
captured by the turbine. This feature, however, must be useful in commercial applications, like a wind
farm where the wake generated by a turbine has a very large influence on neighbouring turbines. A study
done by Tobin et al [10] on the effect of winglets on the wake and performance of a model wind turbine
showed an increase in power and thrust coefficients of 8.2% and 15% respectively. The higher thrust caused
enhanced shear and turbulence in the far wake, which reduced the strength of the tip vortex and also
led to higher energy flux in the far wake. Care must be taken while increasing CP, higher wake deficits
downstream may reduce the power production in downwind turbines. Another takeaway from this study
is that the winglets themselves do not alter the strength of the tip vortices appreciably, but shifting the tip
vortices downstream rather than reducing their strength might be effective in enhancing the aerodynamic
performance in a wind farm.
A study done by Imamura et al [11] on the aerodynamic effectiveness of winglets on a 2 bladed HAWT
showed a large increase in power coefficient CP and a relatively lower increase in blade root flap bending
moment can be obtained by having smaller installation angles of the winglets. The main reason for increased
aerodynamic efficiency was the increase in mass flow and circumferential component of the lift force due
to reduced induced axial velocities, thus improving the circulation at the tip. There are a lot of aerodynamic
devices that can alleviate the loads on wind turbines and reduce the fatigue loads. Winglets are one such
aerodynamic load shedding device that reduces spanwise flow, diffuses and moves the tip vortex away
from the rotor plane, thereby decreasing total drag [12].
Studies on swept blades
Zuo et al [13] performed an optimization study on the effect swept blades have on aerodynamic per-
formance at high Tip Speed Ratio (TSR). It was found that at TSR higher than 7, the swept blade had
a higher CP compared to straight blade, pointing to the fact that swept blades can capture more energy
at lower wind speeds. An added advantage of having swept blades was the reduction in thrust ob-
served, especially at higher wind speeds which leads to a reduction in cost of the components. Angle
of attack for swept blades at the tip and inboard stations was found to be higher compared to straight
blades.
Verelst and Larsen [14] studied the effects of sweep on a pitch controlled 5 MW NREL rotor, where it
was shown that backward sweep lowers blade root flap fatigue and ultimate load. The shape of the
considered sweep geometries produced an inherent penalty in blade root torsion, increasing the fatigue
and ultimate loads by up to 400%. A boomerang shape was suggested to counteract this side-effect
of sweeping blades. Torsional deformation of the blades lead to pre twist distribution not being op-
timal any longer which leads to lower power production for backward swept blades in partial load
region.
An understanding of the flow occurring at the tip of the wind turbine blade is key in determining ways
to improve aerodynamic performance of the turbine. Blade tip modifications have the potential to enhance
3
==PAGE:14==
the aerodynamic performance of a wind turbine and this is a particularly interesting area for wind turbine
manufacturers. Chattot J-J [15] performed a study on some tip modifications using a vortex method
based on the Goldstein model. The basis of this study is that induced velocities at the lifting line is no
longer zero and the flow along the blade changes according to the Biot-Savart law. The aerodynamic
performance of different tip modifications indeed exhibited an increase, with the drawback of structural
penalties.
Studies on wind turbine controllers
Tibaldi et al [16] developed a method to automatically tune a classical wind turbine PI controller based
on numerical optimization. In this study, effects of changing the controller parameters on wind tur-
bine parameters using a cost function was analyzed and it was found that aggressive tuning of the
PI controllers by increasing the proprtional gains led to higher tower load oscillations, while a slug-
gish tuning method led to an increase in generator load oscillations. Lower gains from the torque
controller lead to smoother transition from constant speed variable torque region to constant speed
constant torque region. Another study performed by Tibaldi et al [17] on the effects of gain schedul-
ing suggest changes in pole placements of the speed regulator mode using different aerodynamic gain
scheduling methods. In both [16] and [17], the reference wind turbine used was the National Renewable
Energy Labaratory (NREL) 5 MW turbine. This study provides a new gain scheduling method which
better places the poles of speed regulator mode at high wind speeds, in the region of the PI pitch con-
troller.
Robustness is important while designing and tuning a wind turbine controller. A wind turbine is a non-
linear system in operation while the behavior is approximated with the help of a linearized model around
the operating points. In the study done by Mahmood et al [18] on the DTU 10 MW reference wind turbine,
it can be seen that the instantaneous operating points of a wind turbine deviate from the steady state
operating points, meaning the parameters of the model deviates from the operating points used to design
the controller. This paper used maximum sensitivity and complementary sensitivity functions as a way
of maintaining the robustness of the model.
Pole placement is one of the methods employed to tune a classical PI controller. In addition to pole
placement methods, Tibaldi et al [19] studied a fatigue based method in order to tune the Basic DTU
Wind Energy Controller. The linearized models of the DTU 10 MW reference wind turbine was ob-
tained by using HAWCStab2, and tuning was done using an optimization framework. The fatigue based
method was evaluated with a frequency domain approach due to the computational time required to
run a time domain evaluation. A spectral method was then employed to evaluate the fatigue loads for a
load sensor. The advantage of running a fatigue based method is that there is no need to set initial values
for parameters. This method however, is still slower than the pole placement methods and in case of
running an extensive controller tuning, pole placement with a rotor speed filter is the primary method
employed.

Simulation tools and models
3.1
Simulation tools
The simulation tool used for running aero-elastic simulations was HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine
simulation Code 2nd generation), which calculates wind turbine response in the time domain. HAWC2
was developed at DTU Wind Energy and is now a widely used platform for aero-elastic simulations
[20]. HAWC2 consists of many models describing the behavior of external effects (wind, waves and
soil), applied loads, structural dynamics and control system response. The structural nucleus of HAWC2
is based on a multibody system, where each body is an assembly of Timoshenko beam elements. A
linear Timoshenko beam model cannot capture the non-linear effects of a wind turbine blade due to
violation of rotation assumptions of beam cross sections. However, the multibody system formulation
enables a wide range of model capabilities and inclusion of non-linear geometric effects. The basic aero-
dynamic model used for simulations was the BEM model. But with the development of new blade
designs and control strategies, new aerodynamic models like Near Wake Model (NWM) have been in-
corporated. For this thesis, HAWC2 was used to simulate aero-elastic response of the turbine for DLC12
and DLC13, and to determine the AEP, extreme loads and fatigue loads for the rotors with different tip
extensions.
A simple structural and aeroelastic modal analysis was carried out using HAWCStab2, which is a
frequency based aero-servo-elastic code for steady state computations and stability analysis of wind
turbines, developed at DTU Wind Energy. HAWCStab2 employs an analytical linearization of a non-
linear finite beam element model, coupled with an unsteady Blade Element Momentum aerodynamic
model [21]. HAWCStab2 conisders uniform inflow to provide an isotropic loading (independent of
azimuthal position of the blade element) for steady state aero-servo-elastic calculations. Detailed infor-
mation about the working of HAWCStab2 and steady state calculations can be found in [22] and [23].
The structural and aeroelastic modal analysis was performed for the IEA 10 MW reference wind tur-
bine.
Controller tuning for the gains of the torque PI controller and pitch PI controller in regions 2 and 3 respec-
tively was then performed using HAWCStab2 and a time step simulation to determine the effect of the
tuning parameters was done using HAWC2. The regions of operation for a wind turbine within the scope
of this project can be seen in Figure 3.1.

3.4
Aerodynamic models
The conventional aerodynamic model used for aero-elastic simulations is the Blade Element Momen-
tum (BEM) model. Blade Element Momentum theory was derived by Glauert in 1935 and it couples
the 1D momentum theory for an ideal rotor with the local events happening at the blade elements
[28]. The BEM model operates under the assumptions that the blade elements are independent of one
another, meaning that there is no radial dependency and the forces from the blade on the flow is con-
stant. For a straight blade, BEM provides a good level of accuracy to calculate blade loads, power
and thrust for a range of wind speeds, rotational speeds and pitch angles. However, the model has
some discrepancies while modelling a curved blade with add-ons like vortex generators and tip exten-
sions. This occurs because of the radial independence of the blade elements in the BEM model. A
study done by Gopinath and Braaten [29] on aerostructural analysis of wind turbine blades with sweep
and winglets compared the BEM approach implemented in AeroDyn with a vortex line method to
account for the 3D shape of the blade. This comparison was made due to the fact that radial indepen-
dence provided no distinguishing feature while modelling a straight blade and a blade with sweep or
winglets.
The shortcomings of BEM model can be attributed to the derivation of the model itself.
The rela-
tionship between induction and axial thrust is derived for a single stream tube for the entire rotor,
but used for differential stream tubes at different radial positions in the BEM model. This feature
proves to be a limitation while analyzing loads at the tip and root. Another feature with the con-
ventional BEM model is that it is a steady state model [30], but with inclusion of dynamic inflow
and dynamic stall models, BEM model has the ability to capture highly dynamic loads due to tur-
bulence and eigen motions [20]. BEM theory, however, does not provide flow details close to the
blade, and this becomes problematic while analyzing blades with vortex generators and tip exten-
sions.
BEM model assumes the rotor as an actuator disc, in other words, a rotor with an infinite number of
blades. This assumption is not physically valid as a rotor with finite number of blades shows non-
uniformity in the flow which goes against the assumption that an infinite number of blades causes
a uniform flow through the rotor. A cylindrical wake for uniform flow then transforms into a heli-
cal wake, and the non-uniformity that arises is considered as losses [31][32]. Prandtl derived a cor-
rection factor to account for the losses at the tip for a rotor with finite number of blades. BEM does
14
==PAGE:25==
not accurately capture some of the aerodynamic effects occurring at the tip even with correction fac-
tors.
BEM model however, is the fastest method to calculate the forces on a wind turbine. Instead of dis-
regarding BEM altogether, modifications in the BEM code using some features of vortex methods in
order to better capture the flow around the tip would allow reasonable computational time with better
results compared to the conventional BEM model. To this effect, Madsen and Rasmussen proposed a
coupled aerodynamic model consisting of a BEM based far wake model and a lifting line based trailed
vorticity, also called Near Wake Model (NWM) developed by Beddoes, and further development was
done by G R Pirrung [33]. Near Wake Model serves to account for root and tip vortices, while also
accurately modeling the near wake induction by coupling radial sections. BEM model is used to com-
pute the induction in the far wake, where effects of dynamic inflow is slower than at the blade sec-
tions.
Ang Li et al [34] modified the coupled near wake trailed vorticity with BEM model done in [33] to account
for induction due to trailed and curved vorticity due to blade sweep. The modified model was compared
with original near wake model, BEM model and a CFD model for the IEA 10 MW reference wind turbine.
For four different swept blades, the extended model had closer agreement with CFD than with the original
near wake model. The calculation of normal forces from BEM model was wrong due to the inability to
account for sweep in BEM calculations. This error extends to the near wake model through far wake BEM
model.

Aeroelastic evaluation of different tip extensions
An aero-elastic simulation process was carried out to analyze the effect having a tip extension on the
IEA 10 MW rotor. Intuitive thinking would point to a reduction in some loads and an increase in
other loads. Sweeping induces additional torsion when the blade is loaded, twisting the blade lo-
cally and a bend twist coupling is achieved. Dihedral tips lower the strength of the tip vortex and
reduce total drag on the blade while not necessarily reducing the loads on the turbine. Aero-elastic
simulations would conclusively depict the change in loads and AEP of the new rotor with tip exten-
sions. In order for the ensuing results to be relevant, the blade and tower loads for the rotor with
tip extensions have been compared with the original IEA rotor and explained in detail in this sec-
tion.
Initially, the baseline rotor was simulated on HAWC2 with the Blade Element Momentum aerodynamic
model and rotors with tip extensions were simulated using Near Wake Model on HAWC2. The compar-
ison thus made showed the behaviour of certain loads, but the variations were due to model differences
and design differences. The author realized this too late in the project schedule and was able to res-
imulate the baseline rotor using NWM and make the comparison fairer but unfortunately was out of
time to investigate the behaviour of certain loads and would like to defer it to future work along these
lines.
5.1
Procedure followed for evaluating different tips
The procedure involving all input files for the simulation of different tips have been explained in this
section. The master htc file for the IEA 10 MW rotor was taken as the basis for creating master htc
files for the different tips, which were created using an existing python script, as explained in Chap-
ter 4. The resulting htc file was the master htc file for the particular tip extension being evaluated.
A DLC master excel file, which included details and values of different tags for aero-elastic simula-
tion of the entire DTU Design Load Basis (DLB), was simplified to simulate DLC12, DLC13 and a
steady state simulation. The next step involved creating htc files for each case as prescribed in the
DLCs excel file. For the scope of this study, DLC12, DLC13 and the steady state simulations were car-
ried out, which resulted in 455 simulation files for each tip extension. Because of the large number
of simulations required for the evaluation of each tip extension, the simulations were carried out on a
Linux computer cluster with 320 compute nodes, each having 20 cores. The results of the simulations
were extracted and analyzed with the help of a Matlab script, and the required plots were then gener-
ated.
5.2
Load channels
To have a clear aero-elastic response study for a wind turbine, a number of turbine loads have to be analyzed.
In this section, the different load channels being considered in this project will be explained. HAWC2
calculates the loads on the turbine components according to the coordinate system of the component in
question. The coordinate system can be specified in the input file according to requirements of the user.
The coordinate system used in HAWC2 is as shown in Figure 5.1, where subscript ’G’ stands for ground
coordinate system, subscript ’T’ stands for tower coordinate system, subscript ’S’ stands for shaft coordinate
system, subscript ’H’ stands for hub coordinate system and subscript ’B’ stands for blade coordinate system.
32
==PAGE:43==
Figure 5.1: Coordinate systems used in HAWC2, taken from the user manual [20]
5.2.1
Blade load channels
One of the many important components of the wind turbine are the blades. This is primarily because
energy is extracted from the wind with the rotation of the blades, and the fact that the loads on the rest
of the turbine are transferred through the blades. Another reason for the importance of studying blade
loads is the scope of optimization that it presents, as is being done in this project. The areas of the blade
being dealt with in the load analysis are the blade root and overlap region. Overlap region loads are to be
considered since the tip extensions are fitted over the existing tip, thus there is a region of overlap that must
be strong enough to withstand the loading caused by the tip extensions. The blade loads being analyzed
are the bending moments around three different axes, as shown below. It must be noted that the loads
described for the blade are considered in the blade coordinate system.
33
==PAGE:44==
• Flapwise bending moment (BR and Overlap - F) : About x axis
• Edgewise bending moment (BR and Overlap - E) : About y axis
• Torsional bending moment (BR and Overlap - T) : About z axis
5.2.2
Tower load channels
In operation, there is a strong interaction between the blades and the turbine structure, and blade loads
influence loading on other turbine components to a high level. As far as the scope of this project is con-
cerned, the main turbine components are the tower, shaft and blades. Thus, the loads on these components
are analyzed in detail over other turbine components like the gearbox, motors and actuators. In reality, the
loads on these components must be considered while making a detailed loads analysis. The tower loads
being analyzed in this project are as shown below. It must be noted that the loads considered for the tower
are in the tower coordinate system.
• Tower bottom fore-aft moment (TB - FA) : About x axis
• Tower top tilt moment (TT - Tilt) : About x axis
• Tower bottom side-side moment (TB - SS) : About y axis
• Tower top yaw moment (TT - Yaw) : About z axis
Another load channel, the shaft torsion moment (Shaft - T) at the end nearest to the blade was also con-
sidered as part of the aeroelastic evaluation. The shaft torsion is the bending moment of the shaft around
the z axis in the shaft coordinate system. Load on the shaft end opposite to the direction of flow was
a load channel that was analyzed as a substitute for aerodynamic thrust force. This load sensor was
considered instead of the aerodynamic thrust force because this is the actual load that is acting on the shaft
and shall be referred to as thrust force in this report. The reason for considering thrust was to determine
necessity of peak shaving, where pitching will be done aggressively around rated speed to bring down
the loads.
5.3
AEP and load analysis of the tip extensions
AEP of the turbine was determined using a Weibull distribution function with Weibull scale param-
eter of 11.28 and Weibull shape parameter of 2, following from definitions set in the IEC Standard
61400-1.
Unsteady simulations are carried out on HAWC2 to determine the behavior of the rotor
with tip extensions in conditions closer to reality. The results of these simulations are compared with
the original rotor to quantify the change in Fatigue (DLC12) and Ultimate (DLC13) loads by adding
tip extensions.
The lifetime equivalent fatigue loads are calculated as described in DLC12, with a
W¨ohler coefficient of 10 for the blades and 4 for all other components and 97.8% of the 20 year life-
time operation was considered. Ultimate loads were taken as the maximum of absolute maximum
values for each load channel under consideration. For the scope of this project, changes in loads un-
der 1% were considered insignificant, due to uncertainties in load measurements in unsteady condi-
tions.

7
Conclusion
This section covers the overview of the project, key learning points and points to the scope of research that
can be carried out in regards to modifications on existing turbines.
7.1
Summary and key learning points
This project aimed to investigate the effect of adding tip extensions to the existing IEA 10 MW reference
wind turbine, by using existing tip shape parametrization tools and models. The objective of adding a tip
extension was to increase the energy output without increasing the loads on the turbine. Three different
types of tip extensions were generated- plain tip extensions which served to increase the length of the
blade, swept tip extensions where the tip was bent in the rotor plane and dihedral tip extensions where
the tip was bent out of the rotor plane. Th effect of adding these tip extensions was studied through
unsteady simulations carried out on the in-house aeroelastic simulation tool HAWC2, for a reduced De-
sign Load Basis consisting of a steady state simulation, DLC12 and DLC13, and determining the AEP of
the turbine with tip extensions in accordance with the IEC standard wind conditions for class IA wind
turbines.
Initially, a structural and aero-elastic modal analysis was performed on the IEA 10 MW rotor, hereafter
referred to as the baseline rotor. This was done in order to identify the frequencies and damping ratio of
principal modes in operating conditions and this was done on the aer-servo-elastic tool HAWCStab2, also
developed at DTU Wind Energy.
The design process for developing tip extensions was used from existing implementation. Structural
data for the tip extensions was modified using a simple scaling law, that scaled the existing values
with the curved length of the blade based on the existing relation between the two. It was chosen
that the chord length at the end of the tip extension would be half the chord length at the end of
original tip, and twist distribution would be constant. Based on these values, and the x, y and z co-
ordinates in the blade coordinate system, new files were generated that represented the IEA rotor with
tip extensions. Unsteady state simulations were then performed and lifetime equivalent faatigue loads
and ultimate loads were obtained from the simulations. The loads obtained for the rotor with tip ex-
tension were compared with the baseline rotor, and changes in fatigue and ultimate loads were calcu-
lated.
The aerodynamic model used to simulate unsteady state for the baseline rotor was initially Blade Element
Momentum model. After a literature survey for employing different aerodynamic models while inves-
tigating 3D tip shapes, it was decided that BEM model did not sufficiently provide accurate results due
to complexity in aerodynamics due to tip extensions. Near Wake Model combined with a far wake BEM
based aerodynamic model was used for unsteady state simulations for rotors with tip extensions. The
comparison was performed for two different aerodynamic models and the author realized this towards
the end of the project. The baseline rotor was then resimulated using the Near Wake Model in order to
have a fair comparison, but the author was unable to investigate behaviour of some loads due to time
constraints.
Adding a plain tip extension increased the AEP of the turbine by increasing power capture in the region
before rated speed. This was due to the increased length and swept area of the blade. It also led to
an increase in blade fatigue and ultimate loads, with blade root torsion being affected the most. The
reason for increase in blade root torsion could be because the blade is flexible and lightweight. Tower
fatigue loads were not as significantly affected by extending the rotor. The thrust force was found to
decrease by attaching a plain tip extension, which went against the behaviour observed in steady state
conditions.
72
==PAGE:83==
AEP of the turbine increased upon adding swept tip extensions due to the increased length of the blade.
Sweep is generally done to alleviate the loads on the turbine due to the fact that sweeping the blade
in the rotor plane induces a bend twist coupling. Forward swept tips induce a pitch to stall moment,
increasing the angle of attack and thus some loads, while backward swept tips induce a pitch to feather
moment, decreasing the angle of attack and decreasing the loads. Thus, forward swept tips had rela-
tively higher AEP compared to backward swept tips, with the drawback of higher loads on the turbine.
Swept tips greatly increase the blade root torsion loads, due to the shape of the tip. The thrust force
behaviour for some swept tips in unsteady conditions were different from the behaviour observed in
steady conditions, pointing to the fact that unsteady conditions caused a drastic change in turbine be-
haviour.
Dihedral tip extensions caused an increase in AEP, due to the elongated curved length of the blade. Di-
hedrals are generally used to lower the strength of the tip vortex, and move the vortex away from the
rotor plane. This reduces the total drag over the blade and improves the lift drag ratio which leads to
higher energy capture. There were some extreme dihedral tip extension cases where the simulation failed
due to instabilities in the Near Wake Model on HAWC2, and there were some common turbulence seeds
that caused the simulation to crash. Results for these seeds were replaced with another turbulence seed.
Upwind dihedral tip extensions increased the AEP of the turbine more than downwind dihedrals, while
also having a higher increase in turbine loads. This increase however, was not as significant as for swept
tips. Thrust force reduced on the turbine by adding dihedral tip extensions, due to lower angles of attack
around rated speed.
With the loads and AEP for rotors with different tip extensions, a simple weighting function was made
to determine the optimum tip extension parameters. The weights for AEP, loads and thrust was given
arbitrarily, a detailed optimization procedure was out of the scope of this project. The weights were
given keeping in mind the objective of the project, increasing the AEP of the turbine while attempt-
ing to maintain constant or lowering the loads. It was decided to give AEP 30% contribution, loads
50% contribution and thrust 20% contribution. Two cases were obtained from this weight function, one
which was minimum value of the function to limit the change in loads and one which was maximum
value of the function to detemine point of highest AEP gain. As expected, the case which showed
highest AEP change also had a very high increase in loads, which pointed to a bad combination de-
sign.
Controller tuning using pole placement method was performed on the baseline rotor, using HAWCStab2
to obtain controller gains and HAWC2 to run wind step simulations to study the response of the system
to changes in wind speed. HAWCStab2 employs BEM model to perform aero-servo-elastic simulations,
and thus was unsuitable to simulate the rotor with tip extensions. Pole placement was done based on the
rule of thumb for 10 MW turbines, where the pole natural frequency should be around 0.06 Hz and pole
damping ratio should be around 70%. Pole frequencies ranging from 0.01 Hz to 0.07 Hz were chosen with
a constant damping ratio of 70%. Wind steps were carried out at three different wind speed, 8 ms−1 which
was below rated speed, 12 ms−1 which was just after rated speed and 20 ms−1 which was closer to the
cut-out speed. System responses were studied for these three wind steps and in region 2 of operation, a
pole frequency of 0.03 Hz was selected for both torque controller and pitch controller because it provided
lesser overshoot and tracked the setpoint faster. In region 3, pole frequency of 0.04 Hz was chosen for the
same reason as mentioned before. In the transition zone between region 2 and region 3, pole frequency
for torque and pitch controller were kept the same, however, different pole frequencies can be employed
to make for smooth transition.

7.2
Scope for future work
Since retro-fitting aerodynamic load shedding devices on existing turbines is gathering pace, the scope for
research and development of tip extensions is ever expanding. To this end, from the results obtained in
this project, there are some areas that could benefit from future research.
• Performing an exhaustive DLB to determine the performance of the turbine to all events that occur
during the lifetime. This project concentrated on DLC12 and DLC13, normal events that mostly occur
during the lifetime of the turbine. However, there is an inherent uncertainty of just these events
occurring, thus an extensive DLB covering all possible events can be performed.
• Investigating differences in steady state and unsteady state simulations by adding tip extensions.
In this project, it was seen that plain tip extensions and swept tip extensions exhibited differing
behaviour of thrust force in steady state and unsteady state conditions. Lack of time available to the
author owing to the need for resimulating the baseline rotor with a different aerodynamic model
prevented the investigation into these differences.
• Optimization of the cost function using an optimizing tool to determine the best tip extension param-
eters. In this project, the weights of the function were chosen arbitrarily, since optimizing the design
fell outside the scope of the project. In order to arrive at a tip extension design that maximizes the
requirements, a thorough optimization is required.
• Tuning the controller for rotors with tip extensions. Controller tuning was performed on the baseline
turbine due to the fact the BEM model was employed in HAWCStab2 and results from BEM for
3D tip shapes do not account for the shape of the tip. This can be seen in Figures 11.3 and 11.4 in
Appendix 11. The difference arising from the two models can be calculated to perform a full system
aero-servo-elastic analysis of the turbine. Tuning the controller reduces the chances of failure by
tracking the setpoints accurately, and especially in the transition region between region 2 and 3, where
smooth change from variable power to constant power needs to be achieved. This could be done
by having different pole frequencies for the partial load and full load controllers.

Appendix D - Bad system response for higher pole frequen-
cies
Figure 12.1: Response of rotor speed (top) and pitch angle (bottom) to change in pole frequencies for a wind
step at 12 ms−1
Figure 12.2: Response of electrical power to change in pole frequencies for a wind step at 12 ms−1
From the above two figures, it can be seen that higher pole frequencies lead to aggressive set point tracking.
This leads to large overshoots and instability induced by the controller. Thus it is of vital importance to
find the balance between having a sluggish controller which does not overload the generator and tracks
the setpoints slowly and an aggressive controller that overloads the generator but tracks the setpoint faster.
