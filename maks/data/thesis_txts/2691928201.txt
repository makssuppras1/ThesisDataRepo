Abstract
A new field of robotics is emerging. Robots are making their way into environments
that people inhabit - hospitals, oﬀices, homes and other cluttered and uncontrolled en-
vironments. - Khatib et al., 1999. This shows that researchers have been investigating
and defining autonomous capabilities and norms for robots coexisting with humans
for more than twenty years. Since then, technology has evolved exponentially. People
have engineered robots with extraordinary mechanical capabilities, such as perform-
ing back-flips or climbing walls. However, the number of commercial applications for
human-centred robots is still limited to the current date.
Thus, this project addresses the technological and entrepreneurial aspect of in-
troducing robotic solutions to environments where humans are present. Technology-
wise, experimentation of the currently available technology and the problems that
prevent it from being commercialized are addressed. To achieve this, research has
been done around the previous work in this area and exploration of new state-of-the-
art methodologies that can improve the commercialization of robotic solutions in such
environments.
At the same time, a new technology oriented towards robotic development made
by NVIDIA, offering photo-realistic simulated environment and state-of-the-art algo-
rithms, appears to show promising results. Despite this fact, there is no evidence
supporting that it can successfully be used in real-world scenarios. Many people who
have tried have given up applying it due to the lack of documentation, support and
technological immaturity. Thus, this project aims to be a pioneer in exploring this
technology.
Given the fact that a robot can autonomously navigate and manipulate, what
are the use cases this robot can be applied to so that it has the opportunity to
be transformed into a scalable business? This question is addressed as part of the
entrepreneurial side of the thesis by identifying a market fit at the same time the
technology is being developed. The investigations carried as part of the project lay
the foundations of an endeavour that can later commence into a startup, Yuman.

Preface
This thesis is written as a conclusion of our MSc in Computer Science and Engineering
at the Technical University of Denmark, Department of Applied Mathematics and
Computer Science. The project was conducted during the spring semester of 2021,
from February to June 2021.
This project results from an entrepreneurial venture of three Computer Science stu-
dents looking into opportunities to apply technical solutions to real-life problems.
Hence, technical and entrepreneurial guidance was provided by professor Lazaros
Nalpantidis from the Department of Electrical Engineering and Diana Landaes Lund-
gren, programme manager at DTU Skylab.
The thesis document describes the entrepreneurial as well as the obtained tech-
nical achievements. It starts with an introduction to the overall topic, stating the
motivation and goals of the project. This is followed by a list of theoretical concepts,
both technical and entrepreneurial, necessary for the reader to understand the project.
After these theoretical concepts are explained, the foundation of the entrepreneurial
aspect of the project will be presented. Then, a detailed overview of the exact pro-
cess followed during the entrepreneurial side of the thesis to transform the formulated
hypotheses into a business case will be given. Hereafter, the project presents the re-
search done during the inception phase of the project, both for commercialized robotic
solutions and technology researched by academia. The entrepreneurial and technical
work carried out in parallel is presented in two separate chapters. The outcome of the
Thesis is analyzed in the chapters that follow. Lastly, the reader is presented with a
conclusion and future work.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Initially, robots operated entirely in industrial environments with restricted human
access and were programmed to perform repetitive work that was frequently danger-
ous for humans[2], just as the original meaning of the word robot itself. It derives
from the word robota, present in several Slavic languages, and the original meaning
is forced labour or heavy, monotonous work.
The word’s original meaning has been carried out until our current days, where
thanks to the advances in sensing and computing technology, robots capabilities have
been extended. As a result, new types of robots have emerged, and robots can no
longer only be seen as a replacement for humans but also as collaborative assistants
- better known as cobots. As a result, robots started to be present also in dynamic,
open environments co-occupied by humans, helping them with activities commonly
experienced as ’boring’ or ’monotonous’. Hence, collaborative robots create a safer
and better environment for people, allowing them to focus on more challenging tasks.
Such robots are equipped with sensors that enable them to be aware of their sur-
roundings. By interpreting the sensor data, the robot actions can be planned and
executed. However, such flow can be easy to understand but not so trivial to imple-
ment due to the uncertainty, dynamicity and complexity of the environment where
they operate. To overcome this, over the years, the robotic community has managed
to build a solid framework for a great variety of specific work tasks - the Robot Oper-
ating System - ROS[3]. And recently, research has been oriented towards developing
groundbreaking technologies such as Deep Reinforcement Learning algorithms - able
also to solve complex robotic tasks that match the new requirements set by the in-
dustry needs. This shows that it is possible to develop robotic applications. However,
maturing such applications to commercial products is quite limited in comparison.
Therefore, the introduction of RL to robotics has moved the focus away from
continuing the development of new groundbreaking technologies and algorithms to
accelerating the deployment of AI-powered robots to the real world. Creating photo-
realistic physics-based simulated environments able to model the changing aspects
of the real world is one way to do so [4]. Thus, in June 2020, NVIDIA released a
state of the art simulation platform for building 3D virtual robotic worlds - capable
of rendering high-quality, photo-realistic environments that contain textured objects

and domain randomization capabilities.
However, this platform is still at a very early stage, and it has not yet been used at
a professional level but primarily for testing and research experiments at universities.
Therefore, one focus area for this project is the analysis of this simulation platform
and the exploration of the built-in navigation and object manipulation applications.
Instead of inventing new robotic algorithms, this project aims to exploit the existing
technology and algorithms by creating a solid development and testing foundation in
a photo-realistic simulated environment and possibly accelerating their deployment to
the real world. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first project published that
addresses the development, integration and deployment of existing mature robotic
algorithms, with the newest RL influence in a photo-realistic simulated environment.
With the creation of such a solid development and deployment tool, this project
aims to close the gap between the simulation and the real world, which makes it possi-
ble for the impressive obtained results in many robotic applications to be transferred
to the real world. For this reason, the project aims to contribute to the transition
into a more automated world, in which humans work alongside robots while focusing
on delivering a better service.
Due to the great variety of robotic technologies currently being researched, this
project does not aim at only introducing yet one more technology research project
to the list. Instead, it is crucial to first focus on identifying robotic applications in
industries where the potential of automation is not fully exploited and then apply
the existing research to these areas. With this idea in mind, this project is an En-
trepreneurial Thesis that aims to develop a technology-based start-up, Yuman, that
uses state-of-the-art technologies and introduces a social aware mobile robot system
that can help automate tasks in an indoor environment.
Subsequently, the project is divided into two research areas: entrepreneurial and
technological. Technologically, the project focuses on developing and proving that
robotic algorithms can be applied to navigation and object manipulation using RL
algorithms tested in a photo-realistic simulated environment with easy knowledge
transfer to real-world robots. Two commercial products: a MiR100 moving base and
a UR5 object manipulator, were used for testing this assumption. Entrepreneurial-
wise, the project aims at finding commercial applications for autonomous mobile
robots by validating the technical approach of having a solid simulated foundation
and easy transfer to the real world. It is then needed to validate if this approach
could be a pioneer in breaking the ground between academia and the industry and
hence help introduce robotic applications to automate some of the most elementary
human’s day-to-day activities. To achieve this, exploring how to best find a market
fit is required to define an exact business case where robots could have a potential
impact.

1.2
Motivation
Throughout our education in computer science, it has become evident that the cur-
rently available technologies are outstanding and evolving at an exponential rate, even
as this thesis is being written. Human programmed systems have achieved expert lev-
els where humans are outperformed in image and object recognition, board games,
and video games. The research community is even competing on training computers
to beat humans at almost any game one can think of [5].
In recent years, a new game designed to challenge human intellect, considered to
be one of the most challenging real-time strategy games, has emerged as the ”grand
challenge” for artificial intelligence researchers. Unlike board games, the player has no
perfect information about the state of the game and has to discover it by continuously
exploring it actively. Moreover, just as in life, there can be an indefinitely long time
horizon when one makes a decision and sees its result. Hence one can not evaluate it
immediately - since one simple decision can diverge on different levels and time scales.
Consequently, winning in such dynamic and complex environments account for the
perfect competitive challenge. Yet, researchers have created a program that outplays
99.8% of human players [6].
They have used a machine learning technique that teaches computers to do what
comes naturally to humans - learn by trial and error. But what if the same technique is
used to train robots with one single mission - to help and assist humans in performing
various tasks?
In theory, this can also be achieved by letting the robots explore the world and
learn how to perform a certain task by being punished or rewarded. This implies
having a prototype of the robot and a simulation of the real world. Subsequently, one
can have one thousand simulations running in parallel, where one thousand robots
share information while trying to learn the same thing.
Does this sound like the
future? No, people can already do this nowadays.
One may then wonder, why are there no robots going around humans and do what
they want? The main problem is the diﬀiculty of transferring the learned experience
from the reality of the simulation to the reality of the physical world, often called
the ”reality gap” [7]. The spectrum along which one can measure the size of the
gap is the spectrum of how large the world is.
If the world is very tight, i.e.
if
the robot is put in a room and is told where everything is and is only programmed
to do one specific task, then it can do things in a superhuman way: it can move
faster, it can be stronger, perform at a constant rate. However, as soon as anything
starts to vary visually in the environment, unexpected events can happen. Since it
is known that visual perception constitutes the most extensive part of this gap, can
a photorealistic simulation reduce it? The answer is yes [8], [9] However, despite the
significant number of investigation results, and the promise showed by using such
approach, we believe that a comprehensive analysis of using a photorealistic solution
to transfer the learned experiences to a real robot does not exist.

1.2 Motivation
4
Even though machine learning and artificial intelligence applications are promis-
ing, nowadays, they are applied in limited [10] robotics applications for commercial
use. More specifically, meaningful robotic solutions are not very common in social
environments such as shopping centres, hospitals, airports, etc. Therefore, all these
industries lack the potential of the automation that robotics brings together with this
powerful technology. However, it is without a doubt that all the current tools and
technology provide a big opportunity to advance human prosperity towards a future
where machines empower humans.
Nevertheless, future greatness does not come with just the evolution of technology.
The best robot solution is not the newest one, [11] and technology is not the criterion
that raises the need for the adoption of robots in certain environments. Many star-
tups fail when they are not solving a real market problem[11]. A robot can execute
complex mechanical tasks like climbing walls or doing backflips[12]. Yet, it may have
no meaningful use in real-life applications. The key to developing a technological
startup is using the technology to solve meaningful problems that customers have.
Understanding the customer should come first, and the product should follow - not
the other way around [13]. Therefore, towards building a great future, finding the
right use cases for the technology is of great importance.

1.3
Goal
This project is at the intersection of two distinct, extensive areas: entrepreneurship
and technology. Therefore, there are combined into one for a simple purpose: to
leverage the advanced technology in meaningful use cases. Considering the complexity
of such an ambitious goal, explicit milestones must be specified to determine the
environments and the definition of a meaningful use case, together with the exact
technology required for its realisation.
As such, the goals for the project are the
following:
Entrepreneurship-wise: find a market fit for the robot and define a business model:

2.1
Entrepreneurial Methodology
This section outlines the theory and models surrounding the business side of the
project. The general approach follows the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop, Fig-
ure 2.1, presented in the Lean Startup Methodology [14]. This methodology pro-
vides a scientific approach towards creating a startup around a product that is
customer-driven. The first step of this approach is to create a hypothesis: identify an
idea, i.e. a problem that needs to be solved. Once identified, a solution, also referred
to as the minimum viable product, must be built and presented to the customers.
Next, the customers test the product and share their feedback with the entrepreneur.
By measuring how customers respond, an entrepreneur may choose to pivot or perse-
vere. If the entrepreneur decides to pivot, the old hypothesis is discarded and changed
into a new one. Then, a new MVP has to be built to prove the validity of the new
hypothesis. Otherwise, the entrepreneur may make small adjustments to the product
and persevere with the old hypothesis. Measuring customer feedback minimizes the
risk of building an irrelevant product, preventing the risk of failure. This methodology
enables entrepreneurs to build a product on real data instead of assumptions.

2.3
Robot Operating System (ROS)
Consider the case when a team of engineers have been designated the task to control
a robot. The robot is equipped with two laser scanners, proximity sensors, and a
3D camera to sense the world around. A gyroscope is embodied to sense inertial
force, acceleration and rotation. Depending on the robot’s mechanical capabilities,
a couple more speed feedback sensors or force-torque sensors could be incorporated.
Not to mention different controllable actuators that put the mechanical parts in
motion. These are just some of the sensors that a robot is commonly equipped with.
Each sensor has to be controlled and monitored. Therefore, shortly, a robot becomes
a complex cyber-physical system where the software and hardware components are
deeply intertwined.
To avoid the need to have different implementations, for every robot, of low-
level device control, commonly-used functionalities, and message passing between
processes, the robotic community has gathered all the developed tools and software
packages into an open-source platform - the Robot Operating System (ROS)[23]. The
origin of the name accounts for the fact that it provides a structured communication
layer between the hardware and computer software. However, in addition to that,
the platform has become the standard framework for writing robot software that
facilitates sharing of research and algorithms across different teams and institutions.
The underlying concept behind the communication protocol represents a graph,
where the information transferred is identified as messages. A message can be passed
from node to node via topics or directly, via services or actions. A topic repre-
sents an unidirectional communication link between nodes and identifies the content
of the message being routed through. Nodes represent the message processing units.
Figure 2.4: ROS Communication paradigms.
Figure 2.4 contains trivial graph configurations illustrating the ROS communica-

tion concepts. Messages can be streamed using publisher-subscriber communication,
requested on-demand using request-response communication or sent in the form of
actions. When streamed, a node can subscribe or publish to a topic. This is gen-
erally used for sending continuous data such as sensor data or robot states. In a
publish-subscribe mechanism, the messages is routed independently of the sender

2.4
Reinforcement Learning (RL)
Reinforcement Learning is an area of machine learning in which an agent learns to
perform a specific behaviour by trial-and-error, learning from interaction[24]. Just
as humans, the agent has a direct sensorimotor connection to its environment that
continuously needs to be exercised to help it reason about cause and effect - about
the consequence of its actions. These consequences come in the form of rewards and
punishments. Thus, the goal is to make the agent reason about how the environment
responds to what it does and learn to change these responses through his behaviour
so as to obtain the highest possible future reward.
This concept is illustrated in
Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: A general concept behind Reinforcement Learning: The agent interacts
with its environment in a series of episodes. An episode runs for a series of time steps.
At each time step t, the agent is in state St ∈S. From this state, the agent can
perform a series of actions A(st). It chooses to perform action At ∈A(st) and as a
result it moves on to the next state St+1, receiving a reward Rt+1. This process is
repeated for a predefined number of episodes and time steps[24].
The RL policy defines the agent’s behaviour at any point in time. Given a per-
ceived state in the environment, the policy maps it to actions to be taken in that
state. Therefore, the policy is the foundation of a reinforcement learning agent and
determines the agent’s behaviour. For every action determined by the policy, the
agent receives a single number, called reward. Rewards can be compared to feelings
of pleasure and pain. Based on the agent’s current state and the action taken at that
state, a reward will be computed. Thus, its actions can positively or negatively affect
the reward, and the only way to change this reward is through its actions. The policy
must then be modified according to the received reward such that the learned policy
allows the agent to get the maximum reward over time. For example, suppose the
action selected by the policy gives the agent a punishment or low reward. In that case,
the policy may be adjusted to choose a different action if such a state or situation is
encountered in the future.
Since RL environments tend to be stochastic, this adds a degree of uncertainty
to the learning. It makes it diﬀicult for an agent with partial observability of his
surroundings to estimate the future reward. Consequently, a good strategy for RL

2.4.1
Reinforcement Learning Taxonomy
At a high level, reinforcement learning methods can be divided into two categories:
Model-Free and Model-Based.
Model-Based methods learn the model of the world. By interacting with it, the
agent slowly builds an estimate of how the world is perceived. Once such a model has
been estimated, the agent can estimate and plan actions into the future by predicting
how different actions will affect the world - how the world would react to its actions.
Given a state and an action, a model produces a prediction of the altered world
and outputs a new state and reward[24].
Therefore, model-based predictions can
be represented as a table with one entry for each state-action pair. However, due
to the time it takes to accurately find value estimates for every state-action pair,
model-based approaches are limited to low-dimensional environments.
To be able to generalise over larger environments, model-free methods or Deep Re-
inforcement Learning (DRL) emerged as a combination of the classical reinforcement
learning methods and existing generalisation methods[24], where the generalisation
methods replace the state-action pair table with vigorous function approximation rep-
resented as deep neural networks (DNN)[5]. Model-free models can be Value-based
or Policy-based.
2.4.2
Value-Based Methods
Value-based Methods are off-policy methods that learn to estimate the value of taking
an action in a particular state. The values of state-action pairs are learned and then
used to implement a policy and select actions. Hereafter described the concept of Deep
Q-Networks, which has outperformed humans in various ATARI games by learning
directly from raw input images, using deep neural networks as function approximators.
Improvements of this algorithm are then presented by covering the concept of Double
Deep Q-Networks.

2.4.3
Policy-Based Methods
Policy-Based methods move away from the concept of learning values for state-action
pairs and using them to implicitly compute a policy. Instead, a policy is represented
directly and is independent of any value function. Thus, policy-based methods learn
a stochastic policy πθ(a|s) that maps state to actions based on the reward obtained
for taking action a in state s.
2.4.3.1
Actor-Critic Methods
Actor-Critic methods are TD methods consisting of a two-level architecture: the actor
and the critic. The actor is policy-based; it is responsible for selecting an action a
for a given state s (π : S →A) by sampling the actions from the policy network
and choosing the one which maximises the network output. The critic is value-based,
and it criticises the actions made by the actor by computing the expected value for
the given state s (Q : S x A →R). It is trained to satisfy the Bellman equation
under the policy π given by the actor, and it aims to assess the quality of the chosen
action based on the expected result. This assessment is termed as the TD error, as
illustrated in Figure 2.6.

2.4.3.3
Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG)
DQN and DDQN can only solve problems with finite discrete action spaces. In most
RL applications, continuous actions are required. For continuous control, DDPG can
be used. DDPG is an off-policy or local path planning method that uses the Actor-
Critic method that learns both a Q-function and a policy to iterate over actions
in continuous action space environments. It employs the use of off-policy data and
the Bellman (2.2) to learn the Q function and from it derive and learn the policy.
However, one failure of DDPG is that the learned Q-function tends to overestimate
Q-values, [26], leading to suboptimal policies. In order to solve this overestimation
problem, Twin-Delayed DDPG is introduced.
This overestimation does not affect the overall performance in most scenarios, as
the agent manages to learn an optimal policy. However, there are applications in
which a good value function estimation is required. In such cases, TD3 should be
preferred over DDPG.

2.4.3.5
Hindsight Experience Replay (HER)
DDPG also suffers from sample eﬀiciency, where many episodes end in a non-goal
state. The goal is for the agent to reach a goal state in a finite length of an episode.
However, in the majority of cases, before this happens, the agent ends in a non-goal
state, producing a failed episode. In such cases, the agent does not obtain significant
rewards. Since RL finds the optimal policy by maximising the cumulative reward,
sparse rewards are critical in RL. Hindsight Experience Replay (HER)[27] can be
employed together with other RL methods to solve this problem [28].
After every episode, HER stores every transition st →st+1 in the replay memory,
not only with the original goal but also with a series of an intermediate set of goals.
In the simplest version of the algorithm, each state is repeated with the goal achieved
in the final state of the episode. However, another more advanced set of goals can
also be used for the reply. Considering the simplest case in which the goal is set
to the last state at the end of the episode, by using HER, every episode ends in a
goal state. Therefore, the agent’s policy can reproduce the set of actions that took
the agent to that particular state. Hence, HER is an implicit method for curriculum
learning, where the replayed goals are initially recorded in the first episodes of the
training and are therefore easy to achieve even by a random agent and slowly start
to get more complicated and can only be achieved by trained agents.

4.1
Robotics in Healthcare
Robots have provided assistance in the healthcare sector since 1980, emerging in
this domain with the robotic arm technologies[37]. Since this moment, companies
from all around the world have been following the trend of automating tasks in the
medical industry, designing robots with assistive applications for nurses, doctors and
patients in hospitals and care facilities [38]. One of the countries pursuing the path
of automation in the healthcare industry is Denmark, with a significant role in both
producing high-level technologies recognized world-wide [39, 40] and applying the
existing gems of the robotic industry in hospitals with an increasing interest [41, 42].
Generally, it is anticipated that in the upcoming years, Denmark will invest con-
siderably in new hospitals and healthcare services. Moreover, it is expected that the
number of patients and elderly requiring more treatment will increase. With an in-
creased number of patients, the hospitals are forced to increase their eﬀiciency [43].
Therefore the healthcare workers will have to keep the same quality of patient care,
while conducting eﬀicient processes in clinical settings and providing a safe environ-
ment for the patients [37]. Robotic solutions are an useful tool to the aforementioned
need as the robots can take care of some of the more time-consuming tasks and
thus let the healthcare professionals focus on value-adding medical services oriented
towards the patients [44].
Having identified an opportunity within the Danish healthcare industry, this sec-
tion aims at presenting a general review of the currently available commercial robotics
solutions with a focus on hospital service robots, which are widely covered in the cur-
rent project. The purpose of this review is to give an understanding of the current
standing/situation of the robotics catering to the needs of the healthcare institutions
and staff, as well as to assess the trends and the gaps associated with this matter.
First of all, the term robotic solutions is vaguely defined, as it encompasses a broad
variety of robots of different sizes and technologies, built for specific purposes [45, 46].
Hence, one would need to draw a clear distinction between the types of robots used
in healthcare, and define their characteristics:
• Surgical-Assistance Robots: clinical surgical systems that assist surgeons
in performing complex micro procedures without making large incisions [47]. A

4.2
Robotic Simulations
Training robots in real-world environments is slow and resource-intensive. For this
reason, thanks to the advances in computer graphics, training robots in realistic
simulators to later transfer the learned skills to reality is more eﬀicient than real-world
training[80, 81]. As a result, nowadays, simulated environments can help to bypass
many challenges, including robot safety [82]. Currently, a large number of physics-
based robotic simulations are available, which makes choosing a simulation nontrivial.
To facilitate the selection of the right simulation, surveys have been conducted over the
years, providing a comparison on stability, speed, resource utilization and available
documentation [83, 84, 85, 86, 87, 88].
Based on these surveys, it can be seen that the most popular simulation envi-
ronment for robotics and reinforcement learning is Gazebo. This has been the case
since 2009, when John Hsu, a Senior Research Engineer at Willow, integrated ROS in
Gazebo, which made it the primary tool used within the ROS Community. Thanks to
this close integration, the same control interface can be used for both the simulation
and the real robot. Thus, this makes Gazebo the recommended simulation when the
goal is to develop not only in the simulation but also with real robots. Besides, it is
also the best simulation choice for beginners. The ROS community has managed to
develop and integrate many of the most popular robots with Gazebo, meaning the
vast majority of the available drivers come with Gazebo support, and the simulation
can be run out of the box. A clear example of this fact is the drivers used for this
project. There exist open-source MiR and UR drivers that developers and researchers
can use without any serious modifications.
Thus, Gazebo can be seen as the classical simulation, enhanced by the extensive
documentation, big community and ROS integration. However, according to the sur-
vey conducted in 2014[84], the three most important criteria for choosing a simulator
are 26% closeness to reality, 22% open-source and 17% same code for both the real
and the simulated robot.
Although the second and third criteria are remarkably
fulfilled by Gazebo, the first one is not. Particularly, Gazebo is not well known for
providing realistic simulations which are close to reality.
This can be a problem
when training reinforcement learning models that need to be transferred to the real
world since most successes are in those models whose environments are simulatable.
To solve this issue, there are two directions of research: (i) improving the ability of
the reinforcement learning algorithms to form policies that are transferable across all
domains, especially the real world, and (ii) improving the fidelity of the simulation
to the point where the gap between reality and simulation is such that the models
learned in the simulation are directly and trivially transferable to the real world.
An example highlighting the importance of realistic simulations and transferable
learning was carried out by Peter Dykas [9] from NVIDIA. When training deep learn-
ing robotic perception models, he outlines the importance of using a simulation for
such cases in which, without a simulation, it is infeasible to collect large labelled
==PAGE:41==
4.2 Robotic Simulations
31
datasets that can generalize to reality. In the same way, without a photo-realistic
simulation, it is very time consuming (if not impossible) to achieve the proper results
for a model to be used in the real world. To break the gap between simulation and
reality when training robotic perception deep reinforcement learning models, he uses
domain randomization, photo-realistic simulation, and accurate physics imitation in
the NVIDIA Omniverse Isaac Sim[89]. Using these techniques offered by the sim-
ulated environment, he successfully managed to create a model that generalizes to
reality and, more importantly, can be transferred to the real world.
Domain randomization is important when working with such models since most
CNNs are texture learners, and a diverse set of domain randomized textures for the
objects the model needs to detect is required. He proved that without these accurate
randomized textures on the objects of interest, the model would fail to generalize to
the real world. Moreover, randomizing lighting is crucial for the success of a model
since shadows and brightness can easily confuse deep neural networks (DNNs). It is
therefore essential to eliminate the simulation inconsistencies that models can misuse.
Thus, this example proves that a simulation that is close to reality can highly
influence the performance and results of deep reinforcement learning robotic solutions,
and even in 2014, this was a priority for researchers in this field. Unfortunately, this
was barely achievable until the release of NVIDIA Isaac Sim in 2020. Although other
physic engines existed, such as Unity 3D, they generally require previous experience
with simulation environments and physics engines. This is also the case when working
with Isaac Sim. However, the main difference is that Isaac Sim has been specifically
created for robotic applications, and examples of how deep reinforcement learning are
applied to robots can be found.
Hence, a possible alternative to Gazebo that solves the photo-realistic problem
could be NVIDIA Isaac Sim. Although not everything in NVIDIA is open source and
many components are at an early stage, they provide a framework, Isaac SDK[90],
with algorithms and DNNs for perception and planning, and machine learning work-
flows for supervised and reinforcement learning. Besides, they have state-of-the-art
GPU examples for navigation, multi-LiDAR obstacle avoidance and manipulation
that can be adapted to different robots. Although there is no documentation about
this process, they state it is possible. Unfortunately, when this project was started
in February 2021, there were no successful open-source projects developed in Isaac
SDK and Isaac Sim. The only available examples are the ones developed by NVIDIA.
Monteiro et al. summarizes the main ideas behind Isaac Sim and Isaac SDK and
possible use cases and application[91].
We do not claim that NVIDIA Omniverse Isaac Sim is the best simulator for all
robotic applications. In fact, we do not believe that one single simulator can satisfy
all robotic applications. However, we believe that NVIDIA’s robotic software is a
pioneer in creating a realistic simulator for training RL agents eﬀiciently. With the
appropriate knowledge, advanced robotic applications can bridge the gap between
simulation and the real world.

4.3
Navigation
In robotics, the goal is for autonomous driving vehicles to navigate, without any
human intervention, from any arbitrary start position to any target position in any
environment, by following a proper path that avoids both static and dynamic obstacles
of arbitrary shapes. Thus, the navigation problem can be solved by answering the
three following questions [92]: Where am I? Where am I going? How do I get there?.
At a general level, these three questions can be answered by following two different
approaches: the modular approach or end-to-end learning.
Following the modular approach [93] to autonomous navigation, these three ques-
tions are answered by the four self-contained but inter-connected modules of mobile
robots [94],Figure 4.1: (i) mapping, the robot uses its sensors to extract and interpret
information about the environment; (ii) localization, the robot determines its position
in the environment; (iii) path planning, the robot determines how to steer to reach
the target position while avoiding obstacles and reducing the path length; (iv) motion
control, the robot executes the path while adjusting its motion.
Figure 4.1: Modular pipeline.
Consists of four self-contained but inter-connected
modules.
On the other hand, the end-to-end learning approach consists of a single neural
network, Figure 4.2, that processes the sensory input and generates steering com-
mands. The neural network is modelled either by mimicking the human driver ac-
tions using imitation learning[95, 96, 97, 98, 99] or through policy exploration using
reinforcement learning [100, 101, 102, 103, 104, 105, 106, 107, 108].

Although the modular approach to navigation is considered a classical approach,
it is widely used in the industry as it is known to be reliable and easy to debug - by
having four independent modules, in case of a failure in the robot’s behaviour such
failure can be easily identified. End-to-end approaches are theoretically ideal and of
growing trend in literature but not yet fully reliable. Some recent surveys examine
the different en-to-end approaches to autonomous navigation [109], and individually
compare those to the different existing modular approaches [110].
For this reason, the modular approach will be chosen for this project so as to
implement autonomous navigation.
4.3.1
Mapping
A major research topic in the robotics community is related to learning and using
maps [111] of the surrounding environments. Usually, mapping means finding a way
to integrate the robot observations about its surroundings into a compact and useful
representation.
In other words, it aims at getting a clear image of how the envi-
ronment looks for a robot by using its sensors as the main source to precept the
world.
There are several points of view regarding a practical way to represent the environ-
ment, depending on the specifics of the scenario. The most frequently used formats
are feature-based maps, geometric maps, and grid maps [111]. Feature-based map-
ping involve using line segments [112] and points [113] (called features) as presented
==PAGE:44==
4.3 Navigation
34
in Figure 4.3a. The approach is to store a list of feature types along with the cor-
responding coordinates and a rate of uncertainty occurring in sensor measurements.
The geometric representation tries to fit each obstacle around the robot in a ge-
ometric figure like polygons or circles. The grid-based approach creates a bridge
between the continuous environment and a discrete world, where each coordinate
from reality corresponds to a few integers as locations in the map. This possibility is
provided by encoding the map as a grid with cells of the same size [114] corresponding
to a 2D square from the environment. Even though feature-based and geometrical
maps proved to be more compact and less memory consuming [112], the grid repre-
sentation has an accuracy advantage, because it does not rely on pre-defined features
the obstacles have to fit in, and it also has an increased performance when accessing
a cell of the grid. Therefore, the grid maps are used to encode the environment in
the context of this project, specifically as occupancy grid maps as displayed in
Figure 4.3b, which store a single value for each cell of the grid - the probability that
the cell is occupied. However, the probabilities are often converted to binary repre-
sentation by rounding the values to 1 or 0, suggesting that the cell is occupied or not,
respectively.
(a) Feature-based map
(b) Occupancy grid map
Figure 4.3: Representations of the environment
Besides the cases when the environment is completely static, an occupancy map
(or any other global map representations) is not accurate enough to show the real
state of the surrounding world. Maps created from a single measurement are often
sparse and subject to occlusion [115], with precision constantly decreasing every time
the obstacles are moved, or dynamic agents appear while a robot is navigating. In
this context, a way to constantly increase the knowledge about the environment has
to be defined.
Several ideas on the problem were proposed by the researchers in the robotic area
- for instance, to use the errors from odometry measurements (velocity and accelera-
tion) [116] for estimating the pose of the robot and an area of the map which has to
==PAGE:45==
4.3 Navigation
35
be updated when the uncertainty increases. However, periodic updates of the robot
knowledge domain do not cover most situations, and the community started using
evidence grid maps.
This term was initially proposed by CMU Mobile Robot
Laboratory in 1983 [117] to describe the representation created by transforming ac-
cumulated information from sensor readings into an increasingly confident map. The
idea behind evidence maps is to improve robot knowledge by using sensor observations
to update the map locally in real-time.
A widely used approach in generating the evidence grid map has been created
by Hans Moravec [118], which is presenting the environment as a 3D occupancy
grid, besides the coordinates also storing the belief that this cell is occupied from
different readings of the same area.
The belief is computed by implementing the
theory of evidence of Dempster-Shafer[119]. Basically, it suggests to represent the
uncertainty about a cell occupancy with probability[120] and provides a way to resolve
the conflicts occurring when a scan of the same area yields different results, which
guarantees updated information about a cell occupancy, thus an occupancy grid map
displaying a real state of the environment.
4.3.2
Localization
An important aspect of navigation is to answer the question: Where is the robot
now? [121] Determining the current location of a robot is essential to perform further
tasks like path planning and the motion itself.
The classical definition of robot
localization covers this topic, describing the process of estimating a robot pose
(position and orientation) relative to a given coordinate frame [121]. In other words,
when an origin point in space and the direction for XY axes are defined, the goal of
localization is to find how far is the robot on each axis from the origin and which
rotation around xy axes coincides with the robot orientation.
The localization process involves collecting information about the environment,
coming from robot perception of the world. The main source of knowledge about
the surroundings of a robot is its onboard sensors. According to their specifications,
there are two classes of sensors used in localization: proprioceptive and exteroceptive
sensors [122]. An example of proprioceptive sensors are Inertial Measurement Units,
which measure the motion of the robot (odometry) and, thus, are used in practice
to track the robot pose [123] by evaluating the displacement from the starting point.
However, such an approach has a major drawback as the localization error grows over
time. The exteroceptive sensors include range scanners and cameras. The approach
when using their observations is constantly determining the correlation between in-
coming measurements to update the estimation of the current robot pose. In practice,
the exteroceptive sensors use Global Positioning System (GPS) [124] for outdoor nav-
igation and map matching methods (adjusting sensor data to a provided global
map)[125] for indoor scenarios.
Apart from the type of sensors used for localization, various algorithms to estimate
==PAGE:46==
4.3 Navigation
36
the robot pose has been a research topic throughout the study of robotic navigation
[126]. A comparative review of main problems when designing a localization strategy
[127], yielded results displayed in Figure 4.4. As a result different setups of localization
caused a separation of the subject in three main problems: global localization, position
tracking and the kidnapped robot problem.
Global localization introduces the
situation when a map of the environment is available beforehand, and the initial pose
has to be determined [128]. Position tracking starts with an assumption that the
initial pose of the robot is known and aims at keeping track of the robot pose every
time the observations are updated [129]. The kidnapped robot refers to a situation
when an autonomous robot is carried to an arbitrary location, and its position is
no longer accurately reported[130] by a position tracking algorithm. Such an event
does not often happen in practice, however it shows the ability to reset after a failure
in global localization and when the sensors have technical issues [131].
Thus an
exhaustive algorithm for localization has been characterized as approaching all the
mentioned issues optimally.

There are two main perspectives on accomplishing localization with previously
mentioned subtasks: autonomous map building and probabilistic approaches [127].
An instance of autonomous map building is Simultaneous Localization and Mapping
(SLAM) process, which attracted attention from the robotic community in the past
two decades [132]. The main objective of SLAM is to simultaneously detect the robot
pose in an unknown environment and construct a map by using its sensors [133].
Even though the idea of accomplishing localization and mapping at the same time
sounds promising, SLAM is considered a complex problem, and its estimations become
increasingly inaccurate due to the lack of global information [132].
Additionally,
autonomous map building solutions are trying to achieve both a global pose and
position tracking, thus missing modularity and the kidnapped problem is not covered,
so it has to be considered separately [134].
Global pose identification based on a probabilistic approach is a grid-map-based
==PAGE:47==
4.3 Navigation
37
localization, where the position is estimated by a map matching technique between
a priori grid map and current range sensors values [135]. Normally, using the occu-
pancy grid map, the sensors are used to compute a set of Hypothetical locations of the
robot and eventually select the pose with highest likelihood to correspond with the
reality. In this context, Markov localization [128] is an algorithm that uses a proba-
bilistic distribution to eﬀiciently find the pose with 90-95% accuracy. Recently, other
probabilistic methods have emerged, for instance, using grid submaps of possible
states [136] and match them to the sensor measurements, which try to improve the
performance and accuracy of the currently used solutions. The probabilistic methods
for global localization (especially Markov localization) have been eﬀiciently used to
solve other problems like wake-up robot problem (when the robot starts the navigation
without prior knowledge about its location) and the kidnapped robot problem, which
is more generic and frequently explored [126].
When a robot has been initially localized in the map, its pose has to be tracked over
time. Most of the existing probability-based tracking methods are trying to optimize a
density function conditioned on the sensors measurements to find the location with the
highest probability. Based on that, techniques like the Kalman filter [137] and Monte
Carlo localization [138] have emerged. The Kalman filter tries to describe the motion
model with Gaussian density. However, it does not cover the non-linear motion, which
often occurs in practice. Monte Carlo localization uses a sample-based method,
considering samples as representatives of the density function and applying a separate
recursive density estimation at each time step, thus being flexible at motion models.
Due to its accuracy of 95% [139], it was eﬀiciently used for localization in real world
scenarios as, for instance, RHINO robot[140] in oﬀice environments and MINERVA
mobile robot [141] in the Smithsonian National Museum of History.
4.3.3
Path and Motion Planning
Path planning approaches can be divided into three categories, depending on the
robot’s knowledge about the environment, the environment nature and the approach
used to solve the problem[92].
Environment Nature: An environment can be static or dynamic. The former,
as its name suggests, is constant over time.
However, in the latter, the location
of the goal and the obstacles is allowed to change. Thus, the planning algorithms
need to adapt to any unexpected changes in a formerly planned path. The current
commercialized mobile robots operate well in static environments but experience non-
reasonable behaviour in complex dynamic environments. This is the case of the AGV
introduced in the Herlev Hospital, as explained in Section 3.1.
Map Knowledge: The knowledge the agent has about the environment, gen-
erally modelled as a map, divides the path planning algorithms into two categories:
Global and local path planning.

4.3.3.1
Sampling-Based Methods
For static environments, where the map is known beforehand, and global planning
can be applied, Sampling-Based methods can be used. Given a configuration space,
samples are taken to create a collision-free path, modelled as a tree or a graph. This
path is then used to approximate the connectivity of the underlying configuration
space. However, most sampling-based algorithms do not guarantee to find a solution,
even if one exists. Instead, they provide probabilistic completeness guarantees [142]:
if a solution exists, the probability that the planner fails to find a path asymptotically
approaches zero.
Sampling-based planners are not a novel concept in robotics. Bruce R. Donald
[Donald, 1987] in 1987, introduced the implementation of a complete algorithm for
the full six degrees of freedom Mover’s problem[143].
Since then, sampling-based
motion planning has continued to develop. Improvements have been made to correct
the shortcomings of previous planners, and new exploration paradigms have been
studied [144]. Examples include the Randomized Path Planner (RPP), Probabilistic
Roadmap planners (PRMs), and Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRTs), which
have been extensive literature reviewed [144, 145, 146, 147].
In general, these planners are well known for solving static high-dimensional path
planning by approximating the configuration space. However, in dynamic environ-
ments, as the dynamicity of the motion planning problem increases, so does the
sampling-based planners’ computational complexity, which hinders the robot’s navi-
gation capabilities. For example, PRM planners are required to validate if the con-
==PAGE:49==
4.3 Navigation
39
nectivity of every path in the road-map holds, which is computationally expensive. A
solution to this issue is to combine the existing sampling-based planners with Deep
Reinforcement Learning (DRL)-based methods as proposed in [148], where PRM is
combined with TD3. The combination of such methods, and therefore, the creation
of a two-level hierarchical approach for planning and motion control, allows for long-
range navigation in complex and dynamic environments.
Figure 4.5: Performance comparison of Classic PRM and Visibility PRM methods in

Another approach to using Sampling-Based Methods in high-dimensional spaces
without using DRL methods was proposed by Nissoux, Simeon, and Laumond[149],
where the focus is switched to obtaining a small solution with high visibility coverage.
Classic PRM planners generate collision-free sampled configuration spaces, connect
them using a local planner and later generate a road-map capturing the connectiv-
ity of the collision-free configuration space. This comprises a two-step process: the
sampling strategy used to generate samples in the configuration space and the lo-
cal planner validation to connect samples to the roadmap. Nissoux, Simeon, and
Laumond use a more expensive strategy for sampling the configuration space but out-
perform other PRM algorithms in the collision checking procedure. As the size of the
==PAGE:50==

roadmap increases, the computational complexity of PRM algorithms is completely
dominated by the testing step. Their approach, taking advantage of the visibility
notion, computes relatively smaller visibility roadmaps and therefore increments the
performance of creating the roadmap connections, which highly compensates for the
slow sampling procedure used.

4.3.3.2
Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement learning has proven to be applicable and eﬀicient in path planning due
to its ability to map large sensor data to actions. For instance, considering the fact
that DQN has been able to outperform human in various Atari games, Wolf et al.[150]
uses DQN to solve the autonomous-driving problem. The goal of this approach was
to teach the agent to find and follow the right-hand lane and drive around the track
without leaving the lane. Even though the proposed approach and obtain results
are favorable, DQN only handles discrete action spaces, and therefore, Wolf et al. is
limited by the fact that only five steering angles were mapped to discrete steering
command, and adjustments to the vehicle direction could not properly be made.
A solution to this problem is either to increase the dimensionality of the discrete
action space or to use a continuous action space. Scaling the dimensionality of the
discrete action space is not feasible for real-world models, and a learning model with
such characteristics would not be able to scale to high-dimensional spaces as the
ones required by the real world. All in all, it would not be able to generalize on
novel complex environments. Even though experiments [100] showed that continuous
actions are harder to learn, they tend to provide smoother motions. Kendall et al.[100]
uses DDPG to map a series of raw images to a two-dimensional continuous action
space: the steering angle in the range [−1, 1] and speed in km/h.
Kendall et al. work was training in a real-world 250-meter section of road, which
made it the first application of deep reinforcement learning to a full-sized autonomous
vehicle. The results show that in under 30 minutes, the model learns to follow a lane.
Another important factor to note about this work is the fact that the reinforcement
learning algorithms were trained in a simulated environment.
According to their
results, the simulation proved essential for tuning the network hyper-parameters, and
the network could reliably learn from their network could learn to follow a lane in
simulation from raw images within 10 training episodes.
Given that training RL agents to autonomously drive in a real environment is
costly and resource-intensive, Pan et al.[151] investigated possible options to train an
RL agent to be able to generalize on the real-world novel environments without the
==PAGE:51==
4.3 Navigation
41
need of real-world training. As a result, a novel realistic translation network to filter
virtual images from a simulator to realistic images was created. Once filtered, the
realistic images were used to train a driving Actor-Critic model that could predict
steering angles. All in all, the trained policy was able to generalize better in a real
environment than pure training with virtual data or training with domain randomiza-
tion. This is an approach that should be considered when working from raw images
as input so as to improve the training speed. Although the same results might be
achievable with a significant amount of images from a simulation with domain ran-
domization capabilities, such as NVIDIA Isaac Sim, it is an approach that should
nevertheless be considered.
Twin-Delayed-DDPG has also been widely literature reviewed for path planning
approaches [148] due to its faster convergence rate. Gao et al. compared the conver-
gence rate of SAC, PPO, DDPG and TD3 for a model where mobile robots learn
to plan a path to a target position while avoiding obstacles faster. By comparison,
TD3 converged faster, which means that the mobile robot can learn the planning
ability much faster. Besides, methods to speed up the learning of the agents, such as
Hindsight Experience Replay (HER), have also been proposed [108].
However, even though TD3 has demonstrated its applicability as one of the most
optimal approaches to path planning, it still fails to find a solution in large-scale
environments. In other words, they are incapable of global path planning. When
using LiDAR or vision sensors as the input to the TD3 planner, the robot only has a
notion of its local environment, and it is not able to obtain observations in a larger
scenario, which can limit the overall global decision making. In other to overcome
this problem, the target position can be broken down into a series of intermediate
waypoints. Assuming that the algorithm for selecting such waypoints ensures that all
the selected way-points are connected to each other, to the start and target points, and
the connections are obstacle-free. To achieve this, a two-level hierarchical approach
where RL is combined with Sampling-Based Methods can solve this issue. Gao et al.
proposes a PRM+TD3 path planner, which uses PRM as a global planner to select
intermediate way-points and decompose the path into multiple local sub-paths, and
integrates with TD3 for local planning.

4.4
Manipulation
Since the invention of robotic arms by George Devol in 1962,[152], the robotic com-
munity has been interested in developing various techniques to identify a motion plan
for the arm and ensure reliable execution of the plan by autonomous manipulators
[153]. Starting with the classical definition of manipulation as the robotic ability to
physically interact with and modify the environment [154], researchers have focused
on the question: how should a robot learn to manipulate the world around it? [155].
Specifically, the experiments included in the category of manipulation scenarios were:
picking and placement alongside conveyor belts (industrial applications) [156], au-
tonomous pruning and seeding (agriculture) [157], cleaning surfaces for disinfection
(medicine) [158] and many other use cases in various practical domains.
Due to the complexity of the presented tasks, a few generations of robotic arms
were created to satisfy the requirements. The most advanced and thus frequently used
robotic arms belong to the third generation - intelligent arms [156] with a meaningful
understanding of the events occurring in the environment as, for instance, object
detection and, depending on that, assessment of the collision risk. The key sub-task
constantly performed during manipulation is making the end effector (normally a
gripper or suction cup) reach the desired 3D pose (location and orientation), dealing
at the same time with some constraints in the environment, like avoiding obstacles
and keeping a safe velocity [159]. In order to accurately accomplish this goal, safely
reaching a target has been divided in two essential problems: motion planning and
motion control. Motion planning covers the objective of computing a sequence of
intermediate points between the start position and the target, thus achieving a smooth
transition of the arm towards reaching the goal [160]. It normally receives information
about the target pose for the end effector, considers the environment constraints
(static and dynamic obstacles), physics constraints (velocity and acceleration) and
produces a trajectory (points and corresponding velocities) to be sent for motion
control, as presented in Figure 4.6. Motion control describes the ability to follow
a command trajectory generated by a motion planning algorithm in real-time and
accurately.

5.2.4.1
Transport of Blood Samples
Søren Sünksen proposed a new use case: Blood sample delivering to the Tempus600
pneumatic tube systems[177]. Currently, Nordsjælland hospital takes around 10 mil-
lion blood samples a year (≈27400/day). Therefore, pneumatic tube systems have
been integrated into the new Nordsjælland hospital to fast deliver blood samples.
With such systems, a blood sample is sent from the wards to the lab in a matter
of seconds. However, the problem arises in the ward, where the blood samples are
collected.
Due to the number of samples taken daily, nurses have to collect suﬀi-
cient samples before sending them. This introduces a bottleneck in the collection of
samples in the ward. Hence, it is then ineﬀicient to have such fast pneumatic tube
systems if samples are not sent fast enough. As a solution, a mobile manipulator
could collect samples and eﬀiciently send them to the pneumatic tube system sending
stations.
This use case was presented to Odense Universitethospital. This hospital deals
with a lower amount of blood samples a day, only an average of 8000 compared to
the 27400 in Nordsjælland. Consequently, they have not considered or experienced a
bottleneck when delivering blood samples. However, it could be something that they
would like to automate in the future as the number of samples increases. However,
it was outlined that there exists a case in which blood samples have to be contained
at a specific temperature when transported. In such cases, a MiR250 robot with a
mini-fridge with a lock is used. Nurses go to the robot’s location and put the blood
samples in the fridge. Then the robot delivers the blood samples to a location near
the lab, where another clinician extracts the samples.
Bispebjerg is also using the Tempus 600 pneumatic tube system, where 13 million
blood samples are taken every year.
In the Neurological department, the nurses
outlined that they are not using the Tempus600 pneumatic tubes systems. Instead,
every time a blood sample is taken, a nurse has to register it in their system so as
to notify other employees (not necessarily nurses) that the sample is ready to be
taken to the lab. Then another person is collecting and taking the samples to the
lab. However, in two years, they are moving to a new hospital, where they might get
pneumatic tube systems.
To sum up, this use case was of great interest to the Nordsjælland hospital, but
it was not of interest to Bispebjerg and Odense. Since Nordsjælland performs three
million blood samples less than Bispebjerg and Frederiksberg Hospital to complete
the validation of this use case, it could be of interest to discuss if such turnaround
sample bottleneck exists with a ward within Bispebjerg hospital where the Tempus600
system is in use. As for now, a problem statement and a business model was created
around this use case and can be found in Appendix A.

visually identify and grasp the right medicine container, open and close containers of
different shapes and types, take out medicine from blister packs, crash and dilute the
medicine.
Grasping and object recognition are trivial tasks that humans can perform remark-
ably regardless of the characteristics of the environment and the task. To date, no
commercial robot has been capable of doing any of these tasks autonomously. Thus,
for a robot to autonomous be in charge of preparing medicine and delivering to the
nurses when ready, the structure of the current medicine room needs to be changed
such that the complexity of the medicine preparation tasks is reduced. As for now,
the medicine room setup, and storage of pills, is designed for humans, but not for
robots. The nurses mentioned that the medicine room layout cannot be changed, and
the pills cannot be stored outside their original packaging. Therefore, for robots to
handle the delivery of medicine autonomously - the entire process - starting with the
medicine storage needs to be improved and standardized.

the patient or generally visiting the patient, interruptions can occur. In such cases,
the nurse must go to the corridor, find the storage cabinet for general medicine,

tasks. A survey was created to have a statistical overview of the impact that these
routine tasks have on the nurses’ daily activities. This survey aims at presenting an
analysis of the most time-consuming tasks of a nurse during a shift. The results will
create the foundation for the argumentation on the choices made for the problem

The survey was created in Danish and was answered by 22 nurses from Bispebjerg
Hospital. The analysis of the survey is reported hereinafter.
5.4.1
Most Time-Consuming tasks during a shift.
In the survey’s first question, nurses were asked to indicate the most time-consuming
tasks during their shift. Out of seven tasks, they were asked to rate each from one
to seven, where one was the least time-consuming activity, and seven was the most
time-consuming activity.
As illustrated in Figure 5.7, grouping the answers, the
three most time-consuming tasks for the nurses are: spending time with the patient

small cup. This is repeated for every patient, which again introduces a lot of walking.

CHAPTER 6
Technical Methods
The analysis presented in Chapter 5 started from the possibility of introducing social-
aware robotic solutions to perform different repetitive tasks currently performed by
healthcare professionals autonomously. These tasks included medicine delivery, food
delivery and transport of laundry.
For a robot to autonomously operate in a human-populated environment, such as
the hospital, it needs to master several autonomous capabilities. These capabilities
include mobility and manipulation[1].
Therefore, the technical part of this thesis
focused on exploring the different navigation and manipulation approaches that can be
suitable for a Hospital environment. Furthermore, intending to develop a commercial
application and the well-known problem of the gap between the research and the
commercial application due to the diﬀiculty of transfer methods from the simulation
to the real work, it is of fundamental importance to develop and test the capabilities
of the robot, not just in any simulated environment, as explained in Section 4.2, but
in a photo-realistic environment that can perfectly accommodate to the variability
and complexity of real-world environments.
Current research shows that transferring learned experiences from a simulation to
the real world suffers from the reality gap problem. The majority of the simulations
fail to capture all the physical intricacies of the real-wold perfectly. Therefore, it is
believed that the use of photorealistic environments to develop and evaluate the robot
performance can facilitate the integration of the solution in real-world environments.
In recent years, NVIDIA has been focusing on solving this problem by creating a
photo-realistic environment, NVIDIA Omniverse Isaac Sim, with perfect fidelity to
the real world. Thus, for this project, NVIDIA Omniverse Isaac Sim was chosen as
the simulated environment.
All in all. this chapter then focuses on the various methodologies used for develop-
ing navigation and manipulation algorithms tested in NVIDIA Omniverse Isaac Sim
simulated environment and evaluated in real-world environments. Besides, a detailed
description of the bridge between the simulated world and the real robots is given by
introducing the digital twin concept. It is also important to note that this project
is a pioneer in exploiting the capabilities of the NVIDIA Omniverse platform. Up
to this date, there are no open-source documented projects that manage to integrate
NVIDIA Omniverse with custom algorithms and frameworks successfully.

6.1.1
Defining Custom Robots
The fundamental representation of the assets in the simulated environment is Pixar’s

represented in Isaac Sim, a conversion to .usd is needed.
Simulated robots models are generally represented in an XML format in ROS,
defined as the Unified Robot Description Format (URDF). Most simulated robots,
or at least the most common ones, have a publicly available URDF or 3D model. If
no such model is available, either a URDF file or a 3D model representing the exact
three-dimensional rendering will have to be created. For the MiR100 and the UR5,
Xacro description files were available in their respective drivers[179], [180]. Xacro is a
scripting mechanism that allows a URDF model to be defined modularly[181]. These
xacro files were converted to URDF and then imported into Isaac Sim[182].
Besides, these models were extended, i.e. the arm was extended with a Robotiq
85 Gripper and A RealSense camera D435i. The RealSense model is attached to the
UR5 arm using a custom mount. The result of the two complete 3D models is shown

A crucial part when working with imported simulated robotic models is setting

However, it may be time-
consuming due to different run-time crashes and instabilities. As a rule of thumb,
whenever performing any physical change to the robot, one would have to save the
USD model as a new flattened model to perform further configurations. This was
the case for adding the camera mount and attaching the fixed joints between the end
effector and the arm.
Moreover, one needs to specify the Revolute joints where needed and configure
their damping, stiffness and maximum force. As a general rule, every Revolute joint
supports two drive modes: position and velocity. For position drive, which is the
case of mobile manipulators, as the UR5, the drive strength is determined by the
joint’s stiffness. To ensure a natural, smooth movement, position joints need to have
a relatively high stiffness in regards to the damping. On the other hand, for velocity
drive, in the case of the actuated wheels of mobile robots like the MiR, the joint drive
strength is set by the stiffness. To ensure that the robot is grounded and does not
perform supernatural movements, both the stiffness and the damping of the joints
need to be set to zero. A reasonable maximum force needs to set for both drive modes.
More documentation on the specific configuration settings when importing a URDF
model into Isaac Sim can be found here. [181]

6.2
Development Approach for Navigation
A realistic simulated environment cannot be fully exploited without the appropriate
algorithms. Similarly, the feasibility at which the gap between the simulation and
the real world can be reduced depends not only on realistic simulations and mature
algorithms but also on the framework used. The correct framework should enable a
nearly seamless transfer from simulation to real robots, which has been an utopian
idea in robotics. Robotic applications have mostly been restricted to simulated envi-
ronments and only a handful of them have been transferred to commercial real world
applications in the service sector.
For this reason, this project aims to take advantage of the photo-realism offered
by Omniverse Isaac Sim, state-of-the-art algorithms available in Isaac Software Devel-
opment Kit (Isaac SDK) and trainable RL algorithms to create a robotic framework
that blends the differences of simulated and real robots into a distributed, scalable
solution for manipulation and navigation. Manipulation and navigation have been
addressed separately and are therefore two independent solutions.
The framework was designed following a modular approach. At a high level, it is
divided into four interconnected modules: (i) the simulated robot present in Isaac Sim
simulated environment, (ii)the real robot, (iii) Isaac SDK and (iv) a DRL framework.
i Simulated Robot in Isaac Sim: This module contains the simulated robot,
sensors and the simulated environment running in Omniverse Isaac Sim. The
MiR100 sensor data is published through ROS Services for the RL framework
to request it on demand and through a TCP connection for Isaac SDK.
ii Real Robot: The real MiR100 robot has been used to transfer the learned
models and algorithms applied in simulation to the real world. Besides, a digital-
twin was built using Isaac Sim simulation platform and the Robot Operating
System (ROS) so as to simultaneously control the real and simulated robot in
real time. To enable a feasible implementation of the digital-twin, the existing
ROS driver was adapted to match the simulated robot requirements, i.e. the
ROS connectivity was adapted accordingly to match the ROS services in Isaac
Sim, Isacc SDK and the RL framework.
iii Isaac SDK: As part of the software platform for robotics offered by NVIDIA, a
set of high-performance GPU-accelerated algorithms for manipulation, percep-
tion and navigation are included in NVIDIA Isaac SDK. These algorithms are
refereed to as Isaac GEMS[190] and have been developed to enable the creation
of modular applications that can be connected to simulated and real robots -
which is exactly what this project aims to achieve.
To enable the integration of Isaac SDK GEMS with simulated robots running in
Isaac Sim, communication between these two platforms has been made available
over TCP using the Robot Engine Bridge extension (developed by NVIDIA).

using pre-defined Robot Engine Bridge Components[191].

6.3
MiR100 Navigation
The MiR100 mobile base was used in simulation to experiment with a modular nav-
igation approach that was later transfer to the real MiR100 robot. Mapping, local-
ization, path planning and motion control were therefore considered as four different
but interconnected modules. The overall navigation approach focuses on a two level
architecture. For each module, the high-level approach relies on an eﬀicient global
estimator whereas the low level is based on a robust local estimator. In other words,
each module uses a global and a local estimator to correctly map, localize, plan and
adjust the trajectory of the robot.
For mapping, localization and global path planning, the Isaac SDK Navigation
GEM was used and adjusted according to the requirements of the project. For lo-
cal path planning and motion control, a local RL path planner was trained. The
approaches taken to implement each module is explained in the next sections.
6.3.1
Mapping
For accurate localization and path planning, the autonomous robot needs to combine
its prior knowledge about the surroundings with a real-time representation of the
environment [111], and create what in the navigation context is considered a map.
Consequently, the mapping process is done in two distinct phases, offline and online.
The offline mapping, or global mapping used to estimate the initial location of the
robot to later generate a global path towards the goal. Thus, a static global map
is elaborated and provided to the robot beforehand, constituting its prior knowledge
about the surroundings. The real-time representation of the environment is formed
online from the sensory data[111].
This forms a local map, used to continuously
localize the robot, as well as create a local plan reacting to dynamic obstacles and
previously unknown static obstacles.
6.3.1.1
Global Static Mapping
In the current setting of the navigation problem, the environment where the robot
navigates is known beforehand. This grants the possibility of encoding it in a format,
such that the initial localization can be correctly identified and later used by the
global planner to determine an initial path towards the goal.
A standard format to represent the environment is the occupancy grid map. A 2D
map formed by discretizing the environment into congruent cells and encoding them
as binary values, each displaying the presence of obstacles at respective locations in
the environment [192].
In order to generate the global map of the surroundings of the real robot, MiR
provides a native mapping tool that allows to intuitively generate a map by manually
driving the robot around the environment [193]. Hence, to generate the occupancy

map of a custom environment, the robot was controlled to navigate around our liv-
ing room, and using its LiDAR sensors it was able to reconstruct the living room
environment.
MiR100 native interface offers tools that allow users to manually edit the generated

smoothened map were exported as PNG images. Figure 6.19a is a clean representation

The occupancy map for the real robot is used only after the RL local path planning

Thus, the only difference between a real to a simulated environment is the origin of
the occupancy map. In a real environment, the robot needs to scan its surroundings
to progressively generate an occupancy map.
On the other hand, in a simulated
environment, the 3D environment needs to be converted to an occupancy grid map.
Due to the tight connectivity between Isaac SDK GEMs and Isaac Sim, the simulation
tool integrates a mapping feature to convert 3D environments to occupancy maps.
Unlike the most mapping tools which are interpreting robot sensors, NVIDIA relies
solely on the physics collision geometry [194].
To generate the occupancy map from a 3D environment, one would need to define
a rectangle boundary spanning the entire environment, as shown in Figure 6.10a. One
very important aspect is the height at which the rectangle is defined in respect to the
environment ground. Since, in a real case scenario the robot maps the environment
from the height of its sensors, the height of the defined rectangle needs to be exactly
the same as the height of the sensors of the simulated and real robot, i.e. the height
at which the mapping occurs in real life.

tool samples points from a 2D plane at the specified height and releases rays 360
degrees from each sample point. An eventual collision indicates an occupied location,
thus the distance from each sampled point to an obstacle is projected to an image.
Due to the immense amount of such rays (one million with possible increase), the set
of all detected points marks the boundaries for the whole environment, as well for
the internal obstacles, forming the occupancy grid map. The generated occupancy

6.3.1.2
Local mapping
In addition to the initial global map, the robot can use feedback from the reachable
surroundings to update its belief about the environment in real time. This information
is provided in a grid-based representation of the immediate environment [197], referred
to as local map. Updating the local map is a repetitive process, tending to support
==PAGE:94==

avoidance of dynamic obstacles and increase the safety, precision when navigating
around static objects.
For the MiR100 moving base, the front and back LiDAR data are combined to
generate an evidence grid map. This grid is generated using Dempster–Shafer The-
ory[198]. Using the LiDAR data, this theory creates a three-channel image where
each channel represents the degree of certainty of each cell in the grid. A cell can
be free, occupied, or ambiguous. Subsequently, this image is converted to a single
channel binary map, similar to the occupancy grid map, but covering only the part
of the environment that is covered by the LiDAR scans.

6.3.2
Localization
Once the occupancy map is in place, the robot needs to be properly localized so
as to start the navigation tasks. Determining the exact pose of the robot relative
to the provided map of the environment is executed in two sequential steps: global
localization and local position tracking. This approach combination was chosen in
this project to allow the robot to plan and navigate autonomously and reliably in
complex dynamic environments.
In the context of global localization, the objective is to identify the robot’s lo-
cation without any prior knowledge about its initial position apart from an a priori
or previously learned map. This issue is addressed in the so-called kidnapped robot
problem [127], which covers the case of first time deployment in the environment, as
well as relocation without eventually providing any pose information.
To solve this problem, the Isaac SDK Global Localization[199] is used. At the start
of the navigation process, the initial pose of the robot is calculated by performing
an exhaustive search in the occupancy grid. Based on the LiDAR scans, every pose
in the map is checked by comparing the scans at that position and the given exact
==PAGE:95==
6.3 MiR100 Navigation
85
scans. The similarity coeﬀicient between both scans is calculated. The pose with the
highest similarity is then selected as the initial pose of the robot.
Once the initial position of the robot has been estimated, it is written to the Pose
Tree as world_robot_init_global_localizer. The pose tree is a graph representa-
tion of the stored coordinates used to transform poses between different coordinate
frames, as shown in Figure 6.13. Due to the time complexity of the global grid search
localizer, the global localization is resolved only once, at the start of the applica-
tion. It then relies on a continuous scan matching localization to correctly update
the position of the robot as it navigates around the map.
Figure 6.13: Pose Tree of the MiR100 simulated robot
The scan matching localization, as it name suggests, localizes the robot based on
the front and back LiDAR scans using the Monte Carlo localization[200], normally
denoted as the MCL-method. Monte Carlo Localization is based on particle filters,
where each particle represents a possible state, i.e. a hypothesis of where the robot
could be.
Using a subset of the whole set of possible positions, it constructs an
approximation of the robot’s state. More specifically, every time a new scan message is
received because the robot has moved, following a Bayesian distribution, the particles
are sampled to compute an estimation of the current robot pose and eventually update
its pose in the map and the Pose Tree.
Since the global localizer is executed only once, this becomes a problem when it
comes to using it in a RL setting where the robot, for training purposes, needs to be
teleported to positions drawn uniformly at random from the map. This happens one
per training episode, and then the kidnapped problem needs to be addressed. How-
ever, this would mean that, every training episode, an expensive global localization
search would need to be done.
To avoid such expensive computations, modifica-
tions to the MCL-method would suﬀice. Hence, two custom Codelets were developed
and integrated in the Isaac SDK Navigation stack. First of all, the poses were the
robot is teleported need to be directly injected into the pose tree by resetting the
world_robot_init_global_localizer. This is achieved by implementing a ROS
==PAGE:96==
6.3 MiR100 Navigation
86
Service that is called on-demand every new training episode.
After teleporting the robot to a new pose and updating its position in the Pose
Tree, the MCL local localizer needs to be notified of the update. Here the second
developed codelet comes in place and implements another ROS Service that reseeds
the particles of the Monte Carlo Localization after Isaac Sim has fully completed the
robot teleportation.
Before concluding this section, another important factor to mention is how Isaac
SDK receives the LiDAR scan data from Isaac Sim. As explained in Section 6.2, this is
achieved by creating so-called Robot Engine Bridge (REB) Components which creates

created for publishing the front and back scans through TCP. Besides, a Differential
base REB component was created to send the state of the MiR100 base from Isaac
Sim to Isaac SDK so as to allow proper localization.

6.3.3
Path Planning
A correct localization of the robot in the map allows for path planning algorithms to
start planning a path towards the goal. For this project, the path planning problem
was considered as follows: on an a priori known map, a MiR100 mobile robot equipped
with front and back LiDAR sensors, needs to autonomously navigate following an
obstacle-free path.
The approach taken was, once again, a two-level architecture
consisting of a global and a local planner. The global planner is based on Visibility
Probabilitstic Roadmaps(PRM), inspired from [201], and a RL local planner.
Isaac SDK provides two global planners: a planner that focuses on finding the
shortest possible path from the robot to a goal while avoiding obstacles by using
Visibility PRM, and a costmap planner where restricted areas and traﬀic rules can
be defined. This project is aimed to test the hypothesis of combining a Sampling-
Based global planner with a RL local planner, where the global planner would serve
as guidance for the local planner by providing it a set of sub-goals to achieve before
the final target. Therefore, the standard approach using Visibility PRM was deemed
suﬀicient. However, future versions of the project could benefit from exploring the
Costmap planner, as the possibility of defining custom restricted areas could be of
use in a hospital environment. In the same way, traﬀic rules could be employed to
teach the robot to behave among doctors or in emergency situations.
6.3.3.1
Global Path-Planning
Following the localization approach in Section 6.3.2 the robot is correctly localized at
any point in time and a global planner to plan a trajectory from the robot’s position
towards a goal. Thus, the global planning consists of two steps: global and plan
generation. The communication flow from the goal generation to the plan execution
is illustrated in Figure 6.15
1. Goal Generation:
Isaac SDK provides three ways of defining a goal: manu-
ally creating custom waypoints in the occupancy map, manually selecting cus-
tom poses in the occupancy map and programatically generating random poses
in the occupancy map. The later is done by default in Isaac SDK using the
RandomWalk component, while the former two can be customizable in Isaac

Sight or directly set in a configuration file. These two approaches allow the cre-
ation of static goals, that do not vary from run to run. However, in this project,
the goals were generated automatically using the random walk approach. By
default, a new is generated after the robot is idle for a predefined period of time.
For this project, new goals needed to be generated on demand by the RL local
planner. Therefore, a ROS Service was implemented not only to generate them
on demand but also to forward them outside the Isaac SDK framework. More-

2. Global Plan Generation:
The computed goal is then sent to Isaac SDK
GlobalPlanner component. The global planner is itself a three step process
based on: a Visibility PRM planner, a plan optimizer and a plan validator.
First, the Visibility PRM planner computes a path from the robot’s position
to the defined goal.
As described in Section 4.3.3, the first step in a PRM
algorithm is to sample the configuration space, the occupancy map in this case.
For better performance, the algorithm was configured to build an initial dense
graph by considering a set of 20000 initial samples. In case that a valid path is
not found, an extra set of 500 samples is redrawn.
The path produced by this algorithm generally contains deviations and tends
not to be optimal[202].
Hence, the produced path is fed into an optimizer.
For this project, Isaac SDK’s GlobalPlanSmoother was used. The optimizer
takes care of smoothing the initially generated rough path and simplifies it by
short-cutting redundant waypoints. A redundant waypoint, is that which can
be found in a direct path between other waypoints. Besides, when a path is
generated, the allowed distance from any point in the path to an obstacle and
hence the maximum threshold distance from the robot to an obstacle can be
configured. All the configured parameters for the Global planner can be found
in Appendix B.1.
The smoothened plan is validated by the PlanCorrectionValidation compo-
nent. In case that a plan is invalid, the robot will be prevented from re-planning
its global trajectory. Finally, a valid smooothed plan is returned to Isaac Sigth
for display purposes, to the Isaac SDK local trajectory planner and to out cus-
tom RL framework. An example of this can be seen in Figure 6.14c.
6.3.3.2
Local Path-Planning
A path planning strategy uniquely consisting of a global planner is not suﬀicient for
real world environments were dynamic obstacles are present. A robot equipped with
LiDAR sensors, such as the MiR100, can expand its planning capabilities by using its
sensor data to have a notion of its surroundings and hence perform real-time planning.
Isaac SDK offers a trajectory planner, DifferentialBaseLqrPlanner, that cre-
ates a local plan using the global plan computed by the GlobalPlanSmoother, the
==PAGE:100==
6.3 MiR100 Navigation
90
robot local position and the information of its surroundings. Using the robot’s odom-
etry (current speed and acceleration), it represents as a set of linear differential equa-
tions the robot’s dynamics: position of the base, orientation, linear and angular
velocity, angular acceleration of each wheel [203].
Additionally, the evidence grid
map is converted to a distance map containing the distances from each cell to the
closest obstacle. The distance map is then sent to the planner for a quadratic cost
function generation, which considers the distance to the closest goal. As a result,
the local planner computes a trajectory as a list of consecutive robot states (pose,
velocity, acceleration), which serves as a valid input to the motion control stack.
Isaac SDK trajectory planner was used as a baseline to test the Isaac Navigation
stack with the real MiR100 robot. By providing the odometry data from the real
robot to the DifferentialBaseLqrPlanner through ROS, it was possible to make
the robot navigate in the living room using a local and global planner.
Besides, a reinforcement learning framework was created to combine Isaac SDK
global planner (Figure 6.15) with a custom trained local planner. A detail presenta-
tion on the RL framework setup is given in Section 6.3.5.
6.3.4
Motion control and collision detection
Another domain of the navigation problem includes controlling the motion, i.e. gener-
ating valid commands based on the trajectory plan [204]. The DifferentialBaseControl
component from Isaac SDK was used for this purpose, considering that its main ob-
jective is to take a trajectory plan and output a valid mobile robot command. Thus,
the first step accomplished was connecting the LQR Planner with the control compo-
nent and provide the trajectory for motion. Subsequently a proper configuration of
the DifferentialBaseControl has been performed: the PID controller was enabled as a
control loop mechanism to minimize the error from the current state to the next one
in the plan and a delay of 0.2 seconds was added between the command sent and the
execution to ensure the action has been applied. As an output the algorithm yields
the next state of the robot, including its resulting speed to apply on the wheels.
According to the oﬀicial Isaac SDK documentation, the navigation stack intends
to safely navigate the robot [205], thus the obstacle avoidance and real-time collision
detection is crucial for the path planning and motion control. Another GEM used be-
fore sending the motion command to the robot is the StateValidation, which forwards
the command if the desired robot state is valid, otherwise it stops the robot by send-
ing zero commands [202]. The estimated odometry (presented in the simulation and
driver of the real robot), which is tracking the velocity and acceleration was connected
to the StateValidation component in order to check if the desired speed of the wheels
is possible with the current direction and acceleration. Additionally, the algorithm
verifies the applicability of speed in the context of environment surroundings, when
a dynamic obstacle creates a risk of collision and the command will be to stop the
robot, with eventual retry of executing it. The applicability check of the command is

the concluding element of the navigation stack, which is followed by physically visible
motion of the mobile robot after the validated command has been applied.
6.3.5
Reinforcement Learning Framework for Local Planning
A reinforcement learning framework was created to train different reinforcement learn-
ing approaches to experiment with new trained models and compare to the existing
Isaac SDK trajectory planner. Moreover, the objective was to explore the creation of
a framework where reinforcement methods could be tested in Isaac Sim and at the
same time be deployed to real robots.
In order to achieve this, a custom RL environment using OpenAI Gym bench-
mark[206] was implemented.
OpenAI Gym has been the key motivator for the
development of DRL and its applications to robotics by providing an easy way to
create a custom environment, using a common interface, that receives actions from
the agent and computes rewards based on the agents state and observations. For
the RL algorithms, the stable-baselines[207], an open-source library that provides a
set of improved algorithms, was used. Then ROS was used to integrate the Isaac
Sim simulated environment, the Isaac SDK global planner, the RL environment and
stable-baselines.
Three different Actor-Critic methods approaches were investigated. Based on the
discoveries presented in the related work, continuous action spaces are proven to be
more advanced, since they cover a wide variety of cases. However, because of this
reason, training such algorithms is very time consuming.
For this reason, it was
decided to experiment with both continuous and discrete state spaces. Having this in
mind, Twin-Delayed DDPG (TD3) and a combination of Hindsight Experience Replay
(HER) and TD3 were considered for continuous action space. PPO was deemed as a
suitable candidate for discrete action spaces.
The environment has the following general characteristics:
1. Every time the environment is reset, after every episode, the robot is teleported
to a different position, randomly selected in the map. Besides, a new random
goal, and hence a new global plan is generated. The new pose is sent to Isaac
Sim and Isaac SDK through a ROS Service. First Isaac Sim spawns the robot
at the given pose, and then Isaac SDK, calculates a new global plan based on
the new pose and a newly generated goal.
2. An episode terminates in case the robot collides with an obstacle, reaches the
goal or the maximum number of steps per episode is exhausted.
It is important to note that out of the three communication paradigms available
in ROS, ROS Services were selected. Since the robot teleportation and control are
delegated from the RL environment to Isaac Sim, it was necessary to know when Isaac
==PAGE:102==
6.3 MiR100 Navigation
92
Sim has finished teleporting or applying an action before the training could continue.
This was achieved using ROS Services thanks to their blocking-call mechanism. By
trial-and-error, it was discovered that using simple ROS topics was not suﬀicient for
such a distributed framework since it is used for continuous data-flow, and Isaac Sim
would return back to the RL environment as soon as the ROS message was received.
When creating the RL environment, four other factors were considered: observa-
tion space, action space, reward function and Neural Network Architecture.
6.3.5.1
Observation Space
The observation space contains the current state of the environment at any point in
time. It is updated after every action is performed and after the environment is reset
and hence the robot is spawned at a new position. For the project characteristics,
the following data was included in the observation space:
• LiDAR scans: The LiDAR scans give the agent awareness of his surroundings,
i.e. information about the obstacles and free space around him. The MiR100
robot has a front and a back LiDAR of which Nlidar values from each are
sampled in simulation.
Hence, both LiDARs are combined together into a
Nmerged_lidar-dimensional vector. The values come in mm from Isaac Sim and
are converted to m.
• Distance towards the subsequent waypoints based on direction: The
global plan contains a series of waypoints that the agent aims at reaching, ideally
sequentially. The angle and the distance from each waypoint to the robot are
calculated. The number of waypoints varies from path to path. Thus, to provide
a uniform waypoint size in all episodes, the distance list has a constant length
of Nmerged_lidar, where each distance is indexed based on angular intervals of
360◦/Nmerged_lidar = Ainterval◦.
This way, the distance to the waypoint is
stored based on its direction in respect to the robot. If there are more than
one point within the same direction interval, the succeeding logical waypoint
is considered.
By default, if there is no waypoint within a specific angular
interval, the distance value is 0. Therefore, in case when all the waypoints are
oriented in a single direction, the list would contain at least one non zero value,
representing the distance from the robot and the next waypoint to be reached.
Note that this waypoint may not necessarily be the closest. Since the objective
is to teach the robot to follow a certain trajectory, it is important that the
subsequent waypoint is always prioritized over the others. An example of the
indexing based on angle interval is ilustrated in Figure 6.16.
The observations are the normalized and combined into a single Nobservation-
dimensional vector.
The LiDAR and the waypoints are separately normalized by
scaling the values individually to unit form[208].

6.3.5.2
Action Space
For each of the different algorithms considered, both discrete and continuous action
spaces were used. The continuous action space consists of a 2-dimensional vector
containing the linear and the angular velocity. The linear velocity can take values in
the range [0.0, Vmax] and the angular velocity [−Wmax, Wmax].
For the discrete action space, six different actions were considered based on differ-
ent combinations of the linear and angular speed: [0.0, 0.0] (stop), [0.0, Wmax] ( full
right turn), [0.0, −Wmax] (full left turn), [Vmax, 0.0] (straight), [Vmax, Wmax/2] (half
right turn and full speed) and [Vmax, −Wmax/2] (half left turn and full speed).
6.3.5.3

One reward function for static environments was considered for this project.
For static environments, three factors are considered: the goal, the closest way-

6.4
Development Approach for Manipulation
NVIDIA provides a strong collection of manipulation examples with the Franka
Emika Panda and UR10 arms. Therefore, the goal was to adapt their existing exam-
ples to work with the UR5 arm with the overall goal of creating a digital-twin. More
specifically, the focus was on motion planning by applying the Riemannian Motion
Policy (RMP) to the UR5, so as to allow the UR5 to plan a trajectory towards a
specified target object. In order to achieve this, a custom extension was created in
Isaac Sim, the details of which are elaborated in Section 6.5.
Once the motion planning extension was in place, the objective was to reproduce
the planned motion in the real UR5 and hence achieve the concept of digital-twin
between the simulated and the real robot.
This was accomplished by constantly
sending the joint state values of the simulated robot to the real one by extending the
UR5 ROS Driver[209].
The target object, around which RMP plans its trajectory, was initially a simple
sphere defined in simulation. However, this was later extended with an object de-
tection algorithm. Using the RealSense D435i camera, it was possible to send raw
camera images through ROS to the object detection algorithm. These images were
then used to detect an object, find its position relative to the camera and send it
to the extension created in Isaac Sim. Lastly, RMP plans and executes a trajectory
using the new position as the target.
Thus, this creates a two-way digital-twin, where the simulated robot plans a trajec-
tory towards a target, which position was previously calculated based on the position
of a real object. An illustration of this process can be seen in Figure 6.18.

problem was approached to create a digital-twin. A more detailed explanation of how
exactly this approach was conducted is provided in the next, and last section of this
chapter.

6.5.1
Motion Planning
RMP was chosen as the motion planner due to its ability to generate smooth trajecto-
ries that include sophisticated collision avoidance techniques[210]. NVIDIA provides
UR10 and Franka Emika Panda examples where RMP is used to plan a trajectory
such that the end-effector can reach a predefined target. The trajectory planning
happens in real time, meaning that the position of the target is continuously being
tracked. As the position varies, it is sent to RMP for a new trajectory to be planned
and executed.
To implement the motion planner, a custom extension was created in Isaac Sim,
following the existing examples. To do this, a basic scene with a ground plane was
created. Inside this scene, the UR5 robot and a cube object target were included.
These assets constitute the simulated environment the robot operates in.
In a usual extension, without RMP, creating such an environment would suﬀice.
However, RMP must have its own definition of the environment and the robot model.
To properly create the RMP environment, one would need to supply the URDF used
to create the robot model in the simulation, a robot description yaml file, and another
yaml file including custom parameters used to tune the RMP planner. Unfortunately,
it is again the case that there was no documentation on what these parameters mean
and how to modify them so as to make them work with a custom robot, UR5 in
this case. Therefore, based on the UR10 RMP parameters provided by NVIDIA, the
UR5 parameters were set using a pattern-matching and trial-and-error approach. The
overall insights gained after creating a custom model description and RMP parameters
are the as follows:
• The robot model description contains a definition of the robot joints and the
links used to connect them. Both the joints and links specified in the robot
description need to correspond to the ones defined in the URDF, used to create
the robot in the simulation.
• The RMP parameters were left as in the original UR10, since after experiment-
ing with them, the planned trajectory was not significantly influenced. How-
ever, the accel_p_gain and accel_d_gain values were reduced as to have a
smoother trajectory - this helped with creating more realistic movements when
transferring to the real robot.
• The radius of the joints, six in the case of the UR5, and the gripper finger tips
need to be defined in the yaml file where the custom parameters are specified.

needs to sequentially achieve. As these points are achieved, the robot’s joints move to
a new state. The goal is for the real robot to receive these states from the simulated
robot so as to sequentially reproduce them.
From the simulation perspective, the state of the six joints, including velocity and
position, is published as sensor_msgs/JointState on the ROS topic /joint_states.
==PAGE:112==
6.5 UR5 Manipulation
102
This is achieved out of the box by using the ROS_JointState component provided
by NVIDIA.
As soon as a new simulated joint state is published in this topic, it is forwarded to
the real UR5 robot by creating a ROS message of type control_msgs/FollowJoint-
TrajectoryGoal.
In this message, the joint position and velocity are set.
These
values are taken from the received simulated joint state. Then, it is necessary to
specify the duration at which the real robot can get to the specified position using
the specified velocity. In this case, to achieve a smooth and safe transition between
states in the real robot, a duration of 0.6 seconds was deemed optimal.
This message is then made available to the real robot by using the
scaled_pos_joint_traj_controller/follow_joint_trajectory action controller
implemented in the UR Driver. This process is repeated for every state the joints go
though during an entire trajectory.

7.2.2.1
Navigation with Isaac 2020.2 SDK
The first navigation experiments were conducted using Isaac SDK Global and local
planner. Some of the results of these experiments can be seen in Figure 7.4. In the
experiments conducted, the Isaac SDK Global Planner yielded stable results, and
always planned an obstacle-free path towards the goal. Nevertheless, this comes with
a trade-off. Computing the graph is resource-intensive, depending on the number of
samples to be considered when pre-computing the graph. A large number of initial
samples might result in a too slow global plan computation that is not optimal not
only for real world examples but also for simulated environments. A solution might
be to reduce the number of initial samples. However, in some cases reducing this
number compromises the quality of the global plan generated. This is the case in
complex environments, where the planner does not have enough initial information
to compute a plan.
All in all, the experiments proved that Isaac SDK Global Planner is able to plan
an obstacle free trajectory able to adapt to dynamic obstacles. Every tick_period
Hz, the global plan is validated. If the global plan the robot is following is still valid,
i.e if there are no obstacles interfering with the the plan between the goal and the
robot, the same plan will be followed. Otherwise, a new plan will be calculated.

techniques such as reducing and increasing the complexity of the neural network or
varying the observation space were experimented. However, the results did not change.
For this reason, it was considered that in order to successfully train a continuous
action model, more time should be allocated for the exploration phase. An alternative
to the multiple environment training setup was to use Hindsight Experience Replay
(HER) in addition to TD3 so as to make the agent learn faster.
Thus, HER+TD3 was trained in the Simple Room (178 x 163) for 189.8k steps,
a total of two days and ten hours of training, with interruptions.
However, the
same problems as in TD3 were experienced. It should be noted that in RL with
continuous action space, 189.8k steps by no means can yield something meaningful.
Such number of steps is just not suﬀicient for training a model of this kind. As an
example, Suchsland et al. trained a RL agent to autonomously navigate using RGB-D
input in 2.5 Billion steps, equivalent to 80 years of human experience.
As an alternative to continuous action spaces, experiments were conducted on
discrete action spaces with a PPO model, where only five actions, with the same
constant speed, were considered: stop, forward, backward, left and right. The per-
formance of PPO, as shown in Figure 7.9 was significantly better than TD3, and
after 452k steps, the reward started to gradually improve. Besides, in multiple cases
after those 452k steps, the robot was able to consecutively successfully reach the goal
following the pre-established global plan. Overall, PPO was trained for eight days,
with interruptions.

The signals, sent from the real robotic sensors, needs to be recognized and properly
handled by the software providing a solution for problems like navigation and object
manipulation. Similarly, a way to transfer output the of the system (commands) to
the robot has to be defined. In other words the first goal is to create a bridge between
the robotic hardware and the decision making entity.
D.1
MiR100 Driver
In order to configure and and manipulate the MiR100 robotic base a suitable driver
needs to be identified. The robotic base configurations were defined in the context
of a community project by DFKI (the German Research Center for Artificial Intelli-
gence) [223]. The list of all robot components, their properties and action descriptions
have been selected from this open-source project [224], while ROS publish/subscribe
semantics is playing the role of a bridge to send commands and interpret its sensors
signals.
Robot description structure
The robotic structure is defined in the Unified Robotic Description Format (urdf),
which is a standard way to describe all elements of a robot using an XML syntax.
The proposed format can only specify the kinematic and dynamic properties of a
single robot in isolation[225], therefore the pose of the robot in a given environment
has to be handled in a separate way, usually through a ROS topic publishing map
details and the position of the robot at the very beginning.

specifications of all links (rigid bodies) and joints (connections) both for the robotic
base and its sensors. In the presented format, robot consists of:

words, it is an interface for configuring, running, and interacting with the navigation
stack on a robot [226]. When move_base receives updates about sensor values, odom-

publishing local and global costmaps, each being a grid map where cells are assigned
a specific cost: higher costs display a smaller distance between the robot and nearby
obstacles[228]. This ensures a possibility to use complex path planning algorithms,
which consider an initial global plan from the static map, as well as navigation among
dynamic obstacles or realtime changes in the environment.

D.2
UR5 Driver
Since the release of UR5 robotic arm, the robotic community has been interested in
integrating the robot with ROS, which is a standard operating system in the industry.
The idea was to create an interface, able to receive important information about the
state of UR5, mainly the pose of each joint (six in total) and send motion commands
for a valid path for the arm. The driver for UR5 was oﬀicially released by Universal
Robots [22], which decided to make the code publicly available [209], such that the
developers can configure the necessary parameters and add missing components.
The features included in the driver satisfy all the necessary requirements to setup
and customize the communication with UR5 via ROS topics.
Firstly, the driver
incorporated factory calibration for the robot in order to make it reach the desired 3D
coordinates more precisely. Additionally, the functionality supports realtime-enabled
communication to handle the 2ms cycle time of the robotic arm.
In order to use the driver, UR5 needs to be properly calibrated, which ensures a
proper forward and inverse kinematics for the arm. Fortunately, the parameters for
the arm configuration (specific for each Universal Robots product) can be requested
from the real arm by accessing a corresponding tool from the UR driver. Thus, by
determining the IP address of the robot and specifying a location for the generated
configuration file, the calibration is copied on the local machine and communication
will be established with increased accuracy of Cartesian coordinates.
Another adjustment that was performed for a better compatibility with NVIDIA
Isaac framework is starting a ROS Action server to listen for commands to execute
for each joint of the arm and translate them to the hardware, thus enabling constant
reaction with corresponding velocities (real time response). The only remaining step
was specifying the name of the action server on Isaac side, such that it can post
positions and velocities to the robotic arm for each joint: shoulder_pan_joint, shoul-
der_lift_joint, elbow_joint, wrist_1_joint, wrist_2_joint, wrist_3_joint. Finally,
if the Usniversal Robot arm is running, the calibration parameters had been defined
and joints pose publisher (Isaac in the context of this project) had been started, the
UR driver ROS node can be started. In the run time the communication through a
ROS node occurs as displayed in Figure D.3.

D.2 UR5 Driver
139
Figure D.3: ROS nodes communication ensuring a bridge with UR5 hardware
As presented in the diagram, ROS Driver for the UR robotic arm has two main
roles: to receive detailed information about the current state of the arm and publish
the joints information on the ROS computation graph level.
First of all, due to the access to real hardware, the ur_hardware_interface
node is able to get safety mode, robot mode and whether a program is currently
running on the robotic arm.
The topic called safety_mode checks if the robot
did not enter one of its emergency states, the possible outcomes being one of the
following modes: NORMAL, REDUCED, PROTECTIVE_STOP, RECOVERY, SAFEGUARD_STOP,
SYSTEM_EMERGENCY_STOP, ROBOT_EMERGENCY_STOP, VIOLATION, FAULT. Each of them
corresponds to a possible situation, which might occur with the robot, such as a
technical failure, inability to move or software errors. Another topic on which ex-
tra information about the arm is published is robot_mode.
The value can be
an element of the list: NO_CONTROLLER, DISCONNECTED, CONFIRM_SAFETY, BOOTING,
POWER_OFF, POWER_ON, IDLE, BACKDRIVE, RUNNING or UPDATING_FIRMWARE. The mode
names define such event as: robot not reacting to commands (idle), turned off or
currently updating its firmware.
And the last topic related to hardware state is
robot_program_running. It is a boolean published on the topic whenever an ex-
ternal control program has been started (manually from the touchscreen or remotely),
thus providing information if the robot is currently available for new commands.
On the other side, an important role of the interface is publishing the desired joints
state to be fulfilled by UR5. Specifically, on the joint_states topic a message which
describe names of the joints, a corresponding pose and velocity for each of them is
published. Sequentially, the robot_state_publisher receives the joint states, and,
using its knowledge about the robot description (supplied at the beginning in URDF
format), updates the tf with new poses of the joints, maintaining the possibility to get
their positions and orientations relative to any coordinate frame a user is interested
in.
