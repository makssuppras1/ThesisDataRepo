Abstract
This project explores the topic of Terrain Relative Navigation, in the the form of an optical
(camera­based) system. The proposed system is intended for autonomous spacecrafts,
operating far from supporting infrastructure and relying on internal measurements and in­
terpretations of their surroundings.
An approach based on image features is examined, and a solution for the problem is
derived from first principles. With the system intended for space applications, great con­
sideration has been given to its efficiency and computational complexity in every step.
Aiming for robustness, effort has been put into ensuring minimal dependence on fixed
parameters and similar magic numbers, but also without creating an overly adaptive sys­
tem. Since the output of the system is intended to be used for navigational purposes,
the design is centered around constant execution time in order to ensure termination of
operations within the frame budget. While no design choices consider a specific type of
terrain, a Lunar soft landing mission was speculated for completeness purposes.
Finally, the proposed system has been developed in conforming C without external de­
pendencies, and its performance has been evaluated. In order to do that, datasets have
been acquired from a simulated Lunar terrain model at a DTU space laboratory as well as
from a synthetic dataset generated using 3D modeling software and topographical data
from NASA’s Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter spacecraft.

1
Introduction
The year is 2020 and instead of flying cars we have settled for WiFi controlled washing
machines. Nevertheless, navigation capabilities and computational capacities are being
taken for granted, often forgetting or even being downright oblivious about the fact that the
computers in our pockets have thousands of times the computational power and memory
of the Apollo Guidance Computer that took humans to the Moon, or that with the help of
GPS we can resolve our position globally with an accuracy of a few meters. The situa­
tion is different for space applications though, where a combination of radiation effects on
electronics, risk aversion practices and long mission planning/executing timelines have
led to a situation where decades old computing hardware is routinely used in current and
future missions (e.g. RAD750 used in Mars 2020 rover). In a rapidly evolving technolog­
ical environment of the 21st century, this means that development of systems and their
software for space applications offers a unique and very interesting challenge, quite differ­
ent from their general purpose contemporaries. The computational power limitations are
accompanied with strict robustness requirements, where mission critical systems that are
responsible for millions of dollars worth of hardware and man­hours are simply expected
not to fail.
At the forefront of human exploration, infrastructure is scarce and problems that rely on the
existence of aiding systems have to be solved in different ways. Navigation, which consti­
tutes the topic of interest of this thesis, has been solved terrestrially for most applications
with the advent of Global Navigation Satellite Systems in the form of GPS almost forty
years ago; however, the situation in space still remains different. Spacecrafts typically
rely on information relayed to them from Earth, by an Orbit Determination process. While
this is sufficient for a lot of applications and specific mission phases, there are certain
scenarios where the temporal constraints of such methods are prohibitive. This project is
built around one such scenario, that of a spacecraft performing a so called soft landing
on a planet or a natural satellite of one. Such a spacecraft has to constantly monitor and
evaluate its surrounding environment autonomously, in order to perform the necessary
maneuvers and decelerations that will ensure a soft landing and the survival of the mis­
sion. The control systems of a spacecraft in such rapidly varying conditions require fast
inputs and equally fast actions, and the inherent delay of communications with the Earth
means that a navigation system can only rely on on­board sensors and local interpretation
of the acquired data.
The aim of this project is to delve into the topic of optical Terrain Relative Navigation for a
soft landing spacecraft without prior knowledge of the target terrain, propose a system that
achieves relative localization using mainly optical stimuli from a camera (image feature
matching), and provide a reference implementation and evaluation of the system.

2
Concepts and Literature review
2.1
Terrain relative navigation
The term Terrain Relative Navigation (TRN) pertains to systems that use terrain infor­
mation for Guidance, Navigation and Control (GNC) purposes. An overview of TRN ap­
proaches is provided by Johnson and Montgomery (A. Johnson and Montgomery 2008),
where the important distinction between two categories is noted; Global position estima­
tion and Local position estimation. The former is also being referred to as Absolute while
the latter as Relative.
Absolute position estimation
Absolute position estimation provides knowledge of the spacecraft’s position in a global
coordinate system, placing it within a global map where the position of areas of inter­
est (e.g. landing site) is also present. Some examples of such systems and their basic
principles:
Autonomous Precision Landing and Hazard Detection and Avoidance Technology (AL­
HAT) (Epp and T. Smith 2007) currently being developed by NASA for use in autonomous
lunar landers with landing accuracy design requirements of 90m, 3σ global error and 3m
3σ local error (Striepe, Epp, and Robertson 2010), 3σ meaning that the probability of the
spacecraft landing within this area is 98.8%. The suit of sensors in the current design
of ALHAT includes “a 3­D Flash LIDAR used to image the surface for hazard detection
and avoidance as well as hazard relative navigation, an inertial measurement unit, star
tracker, altimeter, and doppler velocimeter” as well as an additional sensor for TRN pur­
poses which has not been defined yet (Striepe, Epp, and Robertson 2010). The data
acquired from those sensors will be correlated with lunar maps made by the Lunar Re­
connaissance Orbiter to achieve global localization.
Terrain Contour Matching (TERCOM) deployed in cruise missiles as an aid to Inertial Mea­
surement Units (IMU) which uses a RADAR altimeter and a pre­recorded terrain contour
map to localize the missile (Golden 1980).
Crater Pattern Matching for Position Estimation. This approach utilizes a previously con­
structed map of crater locations and a passive imaging sensor (camera) to acquire the
position estimation. (Kariya et al. 2018, Cheng et al. 2003).
Additional approaches include matching an image acquired from a camera to a global
map, also used in cruise missiles as Digital Scene Matching Area Correlator (Carr and
Sobek 1980) or extracting Scale Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) features from an
image and comparing them to a pre­computed map (Leines 2014).
Relative position estimation
Relative position estimation refers to systems that do not provide a global referenced po­
sition of the spacecraft, but rather a position relative to its surrounding environment. Such
systems form a local map using onboard sensory input, and localize the spacecraft in that
local frame of reference. Data acquired by onboard sensors allow for a higher resolu­
tion map which in turn enables more accurate positioning and better hazard avoidance,
although at the cost of no absolute localization. Examples of relative position systems:
Onboard Image Reconstruction for Optical Navigation generates surface patches that can
be used to create a synthetic image which is in turn correlated with sensory input to place
the sensor within the local map (Gaskell 2001).

Descent Image Motion Estimation Subsystem correlates image patches between frames
acquired from a camera, estimating the velocity of the lander with the capability of being
extended to provide local position estimation (A. Johnson, Willson, et al. 2007).
Some of the methods mentioned for the absolute position estimation can also be used
in relative estimation, most notably generating a local map of craters on the fly (Wetzler
et al. 2005) or by correlating data acquired from a scanning laser rangefinder (A. Johnson
and San Martin 2000).
Sensor type
Another important distinction for TRN methods is the type of sensor being used, with the
approaches being also divided in two categories, yet irrespective of the previous cate­
gorization. These two categories are systems using active sensors, and systems us­
ing passive sensors. Any sensor that emits electromagnetic radiation, whether within or
near the visible spectrum (laser rangefinders/LIDAR) or within the radio frequency domain
(RADAR) and measures the reflected or “returned” signal belongs to the active category.
The passive sensors on the other hand measure the emissions already present on the
environment, and take the form of a camera. The trade­offs are mainly higher accuracy
and higher robustness (smaller sensitivity to external factors e.g. lighting conditions) for
active systems, but higher power consumption and computational requirements.
The system under investigation in this project belongs to the relative position estimation
category with a passive sensor, namely a camera in the visible spectrum. The main tech­
nique used is keypoint detection and matching.
2.2
Image keypoints
In the context of computer vision, the term keypoint (or feature) refers to specific points of
an image that are easily discernible. The definition of what is discernible or not depends
entirely on the method of the keypoint extraction, but that definition typically coincides
with what is considered noticeable by the human vision system, e.g. sharp corners or
high contrast circles. It is important to note that the higher level of understanding and
interpretation capabilities of visual stimuli that the human brain possesses typically tracks
entire entities (regions in an image) and not their individual features. This referred to
as blob detection, and feature extraction is conceptually considered as a subset of blob
detection.
Feature detection
Feature detection is the process of algorithmically locating keypoints within an image, a
topic which has attracted wide interest in research as it is the basic foundation for solving
multiple computer vision problems. Several approaches has been proposed, with varying
degrees of complexity and different properties (Nixon and Aguado 2012). The output of
such algorithms is primarily the assessment if the image pixel under investigation is a
feature or not (binary variable), and is usually accompanied with a score variable which
dictates how discernible the feature is (corner saliency measure).
Feature description
Techniques and algorithms that characterize a feature and effectively build a representa­
tion which represents it belong in the category of feature description. This can be thought
of as a dimensionality reduction process, where from an input with high informational
content (the image around the feature), a small­scale representation is produced which
still describes the input sufficiently. The produced output is called a feature vector and
typically is an array of values.

Feature matching
The process of correlating points of interest between two frames containing the same ob­
jects or terrain areas captured from different locations and orientations, is called feature
matching, or the correspondence problem. In order to do that of course the same concep­
tual features must be detected and their descriptors extracted in both frames. The entire
premise of correlating (matching) features depends on the fact that satisfactory descript­
ing methods will characterize said features as uniquely as possible, i.e. some similarity
measure will be higher when two descriptors from the two frames checked correspond
to the same physical feature. Instead of calculating similarity it is easier to define a dif­
ference or distance measure, and the most common ones are the L2 norm (Euclidean
distance) for real­valued descriptors and the Hamming distance for binary­valued ones,
which is simply the number of differing bits. From an optimization standpoint, this dis­
tance is called the distance metric and the matching a Nearest neighbor search. For
large datasets, efficient approximation methods are presented in the literature where not
the exact match is returned, but a “good enough” one based on a threshold. These meth­
ods, dubbed approximate nearest neighbor are usually based on hashes, k­dimensional
trees or lattices (Andoni and Indyk 2006) and are performant on large datasets where
exact matching is not critical (Liu et al. 2005).
2.3
3D reconstruction
The subset of computer vision which deals with extracting three dimensional information
about the surrounding environment from sensory input is referred to as 3D reconstruction.
Those methods can be classified into a taxonomy based on different basic principles, such
as passive or active systems (similar to the TRN categories), and single vantage point
versus multiple vantage points (Moons 2008). Different 3D reconstruction methods apply
to different problems, ranging from digitization of art and cultural heritage in a controlled
environment (Gomes, Bellon, and Silva 2014) or city reconstruction using aerial images
and laser scans (Früh and Zakhor 2003), to extracting 3D anatomy models from medical
scans (Zachow, Zilske, and Hege 2007). A subset of 3D reconstruction is the category
of photogrammetry, where more weight is given in producing an accurate 3D point cloud
of the target object or scene, often combined with textural (color) information (Mikhail,
Bethel, and McGlone 2001).
A closely coupled topic is the one of Structure from Motion, which typically pertains to
monocular (single camera) applications where movement of the camera is involved be­
tween the acquired frames, also referred to as multi­view stereo. Terrestrially these meth­
ods find applications in topography and geosciences, providing 3D reconstructions at a
lower cost than active methods (Westoby et al. 2012).
Finally, the concept of Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) approaches the
same problem with the main aim of localizing the camera within unknown surroundings,
and producing a local map (Durrant­Whyte and Bailey 2006). Most commonly used for
robotic applications, this approach focuses on real­time performance and navigation.
The lines between those intertwined topics are sometimes blurred, and the approach of
this project lies somewhere between SLAM and Structure from Motion.
2.4
Pinpoint/soft landing
The notion of pinpoint landing in the context of space exploration refers to landings on
planetary bodies or natural satellites thereof with sufficient accuracy, typically meaning
landing ellipses with width in the order of meters instead of the customary tens or hundreds
of kilometers. A landing ellipse is the area of the astronomical body within which the
Fast and robust Terrain Relative Navigation
5
==PAGE:18==
spacecraft will land with a certain statistical confidence level. Achieving a pinpoint landing
requires a closed loop controlled power descent, where the landing trajectory is contained
within the desired path (Kluever 2008). This is in opposition to an uncontrolled descend
where aerodynamic effects and their uncertainties are left untamed, leading to much larger
landing ellipses.
A soft landing is one where the spacecraft has very low vertical and horizontal speeds
upon touchdown, ensuring that no damage will be sustained to the structure and the
on­board systems. Alternative designs include impactors, spacecraft specifically built to
withstand a powerful impact upon landing, which of course adds limitations to the type of
payloads they can carry.
It becomes apparent that achieving a soft, pinpoint landing requires a powered descend
controlled by closed loop systems throughout the descend trajectory. The combination
of localizing the spacecraft, defining the desired path and acting in order to follow it, is
referred to as Guidance, Navigation and Control.

3.1
Homogeneous coordinates
When working with projections it is beneficial to use the Homogeneous coordinate sys­
tem, as an extension to the Cartesian coordinate system. This offers the advantages that
even points at infinity can be represented with finite coordinates, and that it allows certain
transformations to be expressed as matrix multiplications. Converting a point from the
Eucledian geometry (R) to Projective geometry (P) is as simple as adding one extra di­
mension with a unit coordinate. Intuitively, one point in R2 represented in homogeneous
coordinates is similar to a ray in R3. Finally, to convert a point from P to R, one only needs
to divide all the coordinates by the last one and discard it. This is of course not possible
for points whose last coordinate is zero, as those points represent points in infinity and
they cannot be represented in R.

3.5
Computational complexity
In order to quantify the complexity of algorithms, Big O notation will be used. Big O nota­
tion characterizes functions’ complexity as a function of their inputs, whether that complex­
ity is computational cost or memory cost. For example, the execution time of an algorithm
with a computational complexity O(n) scales linearly as a function of the input size, i.e
if the average execution for an input of size k takes τ seconds, the average execution
for an input of size 2k will take 2τ seconds. Functions with exponential computational
complexities arise frequently in mathematical problems, making it very easy to write slow
programs if their sensitivity to input size is not taken into account.

Problem Formulation
In this chapter, the problem will be framed and the constraints and limitations imposed by
its nature will be examined. The limitations define the approach taken in order to arrive
to the solutions of the sub­problems that arise, and the general problem framing provides
context in arguing that such an approach is actually possible.
4.1
Limitations
User temporal restrictions
Since the system is intended to provide navigation for the control of the spacecraft, a
relevant time constant is required. A typical attitude control system sample rate of 0.5s
(Sidi 1997) dictates an example upper limit for execution time, the so called frame budget.
User resources restrictions
In order for the final system to be able to provide real­time solutions, the computational
power constraints will be the main deciding factor in a lot of the design choices. The imple­
mentation should be in a form that could be easily verified and ported to a space­capable
platform, therefore no external libraries should be used and all the required functionalities
should be developed from scratch.
The language of choice is C, the dominant language in the embedded/real­time system
domain, with minimal overhead and high portability. No dynamic memory allocation will be
used, a requirement often found in flight software specifications in order to avoid memory
leaks and to enable precise memory management. Everything is developed for a target
32­bit platform with a floating point unit (FPU), namely the Elan SC520 microcontroller,
which is the one present on the micro­ASC instrument, more details for which will be pro­
vided in the next section. The code for all the subsystems can be found in the Appendix.

4.3
Scene analysis
In order to postulate the characteristics of the optical system, it is necessary to define and
examine the scene that the camera will be pointed at.
Assumptions:
• No spacecraft parts or appendages will be within the camera’s Field of View (FOV)
• The Earth will not be visible in the background throughout the landing trajectory
• The camera will never be pointed towards the Sun
• The presence of plumes or smoke from the rocket engine will not be considered
• The relevant mission phases (last part of the transfer orbit and powered descent
phase) will be performed on the lit hemisphere of the Moon
• The traces of radioactive particles on the image sensor will not be taken into account
• No extra information can be extracted from different wavelengths, therefore grayscale
images are considered
The scene that the camera observes will therefore include (partially or fully) the surface of
the Moon. In the beginning of the powered descent phase where the spacecraft initiates
the braking burn to shed the orbital velocity, the spacecraft will be pointed close to the
astronomical horizon, thus the lunar surface will not cover fully the FOV of the camera. In
the subsequent pitch over phase as the horizontal velocity is decreased and the retrograde
vector (inverse of the velocity vector) rotates towards Nadir, the Moon will cover the entire
FOV.

5.1.2
Keypoint description
After the detection methods, different keypoint description algorithms are benchmarked.
Some of the detection methods have built­in description capabilities, while also stand­
alone methods have been proposed in literature, such as BRIEF (Calonder et al. 2010).
An important distinction between description methods is the type of their output. Typically
a keypoint descriptor is a vector of values which can be integer, real (in reality floats),
or binary. In order to calculate the similarity between two descriptors, some kind of dis­
tance function must be used. Described previously in the theory section, for integer and
real valued vectors the 2­norm (Euclidean distance) is used, while for the binary vectors
packed as bytes the Hamming distance (number of differing bits) can be used. As will be
delved into in the next section, binary descriptor distances can be calculated much faster
than the rest, an important property to take into account when selecting the descripting
method.

5.1.3
Conclusion
It is apparent by now that multiple factors have to be considered in order to decide upon
the different methods, with no method displaying clear advantage over the others but
trade­offs based on the desired use. For this system real time performance is critical, so
computational complexity will be given high weight on the deciding process. Furthermore,
scale and rotation invariance are less of a consideration since the successive frames will
not exhibit significant scaling (vertical velocity of the spacecraft relative to the ground) or
rotation (spacecraft rotation along the boresight axis).
It is therefore decided to select FAST as the keypoint detection method, and BRIEF as
the description method. Those two methods have consistently demonstrated lower com­
putational costs, and combined with the fact that BRIEF computes a binary descriptor
which will result in performance gains in the next step, they are advantageous for real­
time applications. It is important to point out that while ORB is a combination of those two
methods too, its additional step of adding rotational invariance is deemed unnecessary
since BRIEF demonstrates good performance for differences in angles up to more than
10 degrees (fig 5.9).
The main drawback of this approach is the inability of FAST to produce features centered
with sub­pixel accuracy, an extension which will have to be implemented.

5.2.1
Non­max suppression
The previously mentioned effect of multiple detected keypoints for a single conceptual
feature can be seen in fig. 5.11, which is a synthetic image created using the original
frame for the red and blue channels and the corner saliency for the green channel. The
intensity has been set to zero on pixels detected as keypoints in the red and blue channels,
to increase the visibility of the keypoints. It is evident that for a single real­world feature

different scores (as indicated by the varying values of the green channel).
Figure 5.11: Feature clumping of FAST
Also evident is the prevalence of some keypoints within those groups, which are deemed
as “stronger” corners by the FAST saliency score. The process of reducing those groups
to single keypoints is called Non­max suppression (NMS), and multiple different approaches
have been suggested in literature. For this application since the number of keypoints will
be relatively low due to the computational power constraints, the simplest NMS approach
was implemented where for each keypoint all the “neighbors” within a square bounding
box are checked, and the one with the highest score is kept. A tuning parameter is the
dimension of the square bounding box, which dictates the minimum proximity of output
keypoints. The computational complexity of the NMS algorithm is weakly exponential, in
the form of O(NM) where N are the numbers of keypoints and M the average number
of keypoints within the bounding box.

the number of pairs nd influences how distinct each descriptor is. Useful descriptors can
never uniquely identify keypoints, as dictated by the Pigeonhole principle. A value of 32
pairs has been selected, as this is the width of one integer variable for the target system.

The first step is implementing the distance function, and as previously mentioned due to
the binary nature of the descriptor Hamming distance will be used. Conveniently, and
leading to very efficient implementations, the Hamming distance can be calculated just by
performing an exclusive or (XOR) logical operation on two descriptors, and counting the
number of set bits. Modern processors and instruction sets can achieve that with only two
instructions, a XOR and a POPCNT, which directly counts the number of set bits. The
presence of such instruction on a processor for space applications is very unlikely, thus
different methods were implemented.

5.4.2
Matching
With a multitude of efficient and complex matchers presented in the literature with ver­
ified performance, the simplest possible algorithm was developed in order to evaluate
the performance of the total system. This approach is colloquially referred to as a Brute­
Force matcher (BF) or as a linear search, where every single keypoint of the first frame is
compared to every single keypoint of the second frame, resulting in a computational com­
plexity of O(NM) with M, N the number of keypoints in each frame. The complexity also
depends on the selected length of the BRIEF descriptor, which effectively is the dimen­
sionality of the space. For large descriptors, therefore high dimensionality, the naive BF
approach can even outperform more involved methods (Weber, Schek, and Blott 1998).
Despite the exponential complexity of such an implementation, it proved to be satisfac­
tory due to the very efficient Hamming distance approach. Additionally, the more efficient
methods proposed in the literature are typically based on k dimensional trees and approx­
imate nearest neighbors, where for a small number of keypoints the cost of building the

5.6
Outlier rejection
Any matching approach is bound to also return non­valid matches (outliers), and a method
to robustly discard those is required. Another topic which has seen significant interest in
the literature, with various proposals and approaches. A proven and frequently used such
method is Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC ­ Fischler and Bolles 1981), in which
the parameters of a model describing the relationship between the two frames are esti­
mated multiple times using the least amount of matches, and the total number of matches
which fit on this estimated model (inliers) is calculated. If the selected model can repre­
sent the actual relationship between the valid data in the dataset, the iteration which has
the most inliers represents that actual relationship thus effectively discarding the matches
which do not fit in that.
Since the features detected will be 3D points on a terrain with varying elevation, no trans­
formation can describe the relationship between two subsequent frames perfectly. The
terrain though will be sufficiently far away, and the rotations small enough, that the rela­
tionship between the two frames can be represented by an Affine transformation with six
degrees of freedom. Selecting a more complex transformation e.g. a perspective trans­
formation with eight degrees of freedom would increase the complexity of its estimation,
and reduce the successful rejection probability as more points would have to be used
(this will become more clear in the calculation of this probability in an analysis presented
below).

5.7
Relative pose recovery and 3D reconstruction
Having finally robustly acquired a reliable set of point correspondences between two
frames, the problems of pose recovery and 3D reconstruction can be undertaken.
Traditional approach
The traditional approach to solving this problem using point correspondences is utilizing
the so­called epipolar geometry, which describes the relationship of those correspond­
ing points between the two captured frames centered on the fact that every point on one
frame can lie only on a line of the second image plane. This concept enables the formu­
lation of a Fundamental matrix (Quang­Tuan Luong 1992), a 3 × 3 matrix encompassing
the epipolar relationship between two frames. An extension of the fundamental matrix
for calibrated cameras is the Essential matrix, which therefore contains the information
about the 3D relationship between the two captured frames. Estimating those matrices
from point correspondences is done using the Eight­point algorithm or a variation thereof,
which usually involves a form of constrained optimization since multiple correspondences
are used to alleviate the effects of noise. After having estimated the essential matrix, a
singular value decomposition is required in order to recover the relative pose of the two
cameras (extracting R,⃗t ­ Hartley and Zisserman 2003).
While this approach has been proven to be effective, it is usually employed in fixed
dual camera stereo setups (fixed location of epipoles and non­changing essential ma­
trix) where those steps do not have to be performed on every frame. In this case of a
monocular stereo, those matrices constantly change between frames as different rota­
tions and translations of the cameras are performed every time, making this approach
slow and inefficient (since it also estimates unneeded quantities e.g. the essential ma­
trix). Furthermore, since we can rely on other systems to provide the rotation between
the cameras each time, this additional information can be utilized on the solution. The
3D reconstruction problem has thus been approached from first principles, developing a

5.7.5
Hazard map
Since hazardless areas are flat and have no discernible features, FAST detection by de­
sign will not detect keypoints within such, since they appear as relatively homogeneous
areas within the image plane. An efficient way to generate a hazard map can therefore
utilize this information, avoiding the need to reprocess the original image. By increasing
a “hazard score” on an area around each keypoint, regions of the image plane with a
high number of keypoints display a high hazard score, while featureless areas have a low
hazard score. By integrating this hazard scoring approach directly into the FAST detec­
tion algorithm, the reprocessing of the image can be avoided and the previously unused
information from the keypoints discarded by the NMS can now be utilized.

discarded by the NMS). While the output clearly highlights areas with a lot of hazards,
more sensitivity is required as for example the craters in the mid­upper section of the

situation regarding the relative error being different since the accumulation of the error is
not a concern.

Conclusion
Concluding, a complete optical TRN system has been designed from first principles with
computational efficiency, real­time capabilities and robustness as the main concerns. The
proposed system has been developed in conforming C without external dependencies,
aiming to prove the feasibility and efficiency of the design. Datasets were acquired from
the lunar terrain model at the CalVal facility of DTU space, and also generated using 3D
rendering software and terrain information provided by NASA. The performance of the
system has been validated on these datasets, and the results have been evaluated.
Overall, the proposed system appears to be a viable candidate for providing a solution to
the originally posed problem of Terrain Relative Navigation under the imposed constraints,
although further verification and characterization can be carried out.
7.1
Future work
Throughout the course of this project, multiple interesting pathways that could be explored
emerged. These additions can improve the performance of the system, validate the ex­
isting approach or enhance its robustness.
Test the system on the Micro­ASC hardware
While initially it was thought that the system would be tested on the micro­ASC platform
consisting of an Elan SC520 microcontroller, SONY ICX039DLA CCD sensor, optics and
accelerometers, this was not achieved due to time constraints. It would be very interesting
to see the performance on a flight proven processor that is currently operating on multiple
active missions.
Quantify the effect of different parameters on the performance of the system
The relationship between the different tunable parameters and the system has been dis­
cussed, but not quantified. Performing an analysis and actually measuring those effects
would be useful in tuning the system for a specific case / platform.
Use the extracted motion information to improve keypoint detection
Since the relative motion of the spacecraft is estimated, that information can be used to
calculate where will the previously tracked keypoints appear within the image plane of the
next frame. Using this information the detection of the keypoints and the matching could
be sped up, albeit with implications on the hazard map generation method.
Investigate different methods for absolute scale estimation
The subsystem of scale estimation could be enhanced by including additional information
sources, such as known movements during maneuvers or structured light approaches
for the final moments of the landing. These methods could be of interest as they could
increase the robustness of the system.
Conceptual grouping of features
A method to classify the detected features would provide additional information about the
terrain encountered, which would increase the efficacy of the hazard map or signify areas
of interest.
Variable frame time division
In some cases it might be beneficial to allocate more weight in some subsystems at the
expense of others. For example, during the last moments of the touchdown more weight
could be given in feature detection and the 3D reconstruction at the expense of hazard
map generation, since the touchdown velocity is critical and the landing area is more or
less fixed on those final moments.
