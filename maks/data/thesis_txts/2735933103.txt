Process
Writing this thesis has been a journey filled with perplexities, excitements, discomforts,
realizations and hope. I have personally dwelled between the advocacy of capitalism and the
advocacy of sustainability. Realizing the two things did not seem to possibly coexist, I decided
to delve into the paradox of sustainable innovation in a capitalistic society (Sustainable
capitalism/Capitalistic sustainability). It started off from debates I was having with the people
that I respect the most. Questions like: How bad is the current state of our climate? Can
innovation save the world? Can we question Growth? Are we inherently greedy? Does
capitalism serve us well? Am I evil for desiring to be a millionaire? Did I lose hope in believing
we can prevent irreversible climate degradation?
The thesis initially aimed at formalizing a strong base of knowledge on the above mentioned
topics. It further aimed to discover the foundations that make it seem so naive to imagine a
world where instead of growing our economies, we stabilize them in the pursuit of shared
global equilibrium and wellbeing. It further aimed to gather the points of view of key
stakeholders in order to delineate the problems and opportunities that arise from this goal. It
dives into shaping foundational knowledge we need to share in order to start building
sustainable innovations. Further research is needed to create actionable models that could be
used by startups to succeed in this endeavor.
I wrote this thesis in conclusion of the Masters in Technology Entrepreneurship, for myself and
for the people I love with the hope to inspire an ethical shift in our values that could promote
the development of sustainability oriented regulations, taxes, business practices and personal
compasses to strive for a sustainable future that prevents the decline of our current precarious
climatic equilibrium.
This thesis is a critique to our disbelief that we can make the world a better place rather than a
worse one. This thesis is also a call to action to change what seems to be unchangeable.

Preface
My name is Paco Puylaert, and I am a Digital Product Designer, Entrepreneur and Degrowth
movement supporter. Having worked with many startups and having founded my own, I have
felt on my own skin the pressures and expectations that entrepreneurs are subject to in their
pursuit of success. I have learned the terminology and methodology that startups use to
conquer the markets that they aim to revolutionize. Having successfully raised capital with my

startup that is asking for funding. I encountered both the visionary and the mercenary aspects

Throughout my entrepreneurial journey and personal life I have met extraordinary visionaries
that also happen to be extraordinary capitalists. I have seen millions being made through more
or less questionable approaches. Having founded a blockchain-powered company I have seen
scams of all sorts and witnessed the brutality of anonymity on the web. I have seen greed in
many forms but I also have seen incredible visionaries sincerely aiming at changing the status
quo for the better. Throughout the Master in Technology Entrepreneurship I have seen great
innovations coming to existence and met inspirational professors that believe in a future built
by Entrepreneurs. I have the fortune of having a partner that dedicates her life to climate

planetary climate. The consequences of continuing with “business as usual” are highly debated

With this thesis I aim to bring a holistic perspective on the cultural context we currently operate

operates and in the way we think. This thesis tries to define a foundational consensus that we
can implement to strive towards what promotes impactful innovation rather than mere profit
maximization.

Abstract
Entrepreneurial Degrowth: Addressing the Paradox of Sustainable Innovation in a Capitalistic
Society, aims to shine light on the origin of our growth centric economy while also looking at
ways in which we can correct our current profit maximizing system into an impact maximizing
one.
The thesis starts by urging its reader to acknowledge and realize the current outlook of our
climate condition by providing an overview of what we are at risk of losing if we continue with
“business as usual”. The thesis continues by delving into how we have innovated over-time to
create a near-perfect agreed upon system that facilitates global interconnection and propels
innovation forward. However, after analyzing its origin, the thesis delves into the shortcomings
of today's capitalistic system and calls for a long overdue revision of it. The Thesis analyzes the
fundamental values of Degrowth, a movement that demands a post-growth economy that
strives for balance rather than perpetual growth. Degrowth’s provocative and rebellious
characteristics are often looked down upon in today's hyper capitalistic consensus. Because of
that, the thesis looks into ways that Degrowth could plausibly be applied to the context of
Entrepreneurship, where to this day, growth is king and fundamental.
Paradoxical at its core, Entrepreneurial Degrowth looks to provoke a reaction in the reader that
needs to confront the paradigm shifts required if we are to meet our goals to keep global
warming under the 2 degree threshold. Entrepreneurial Degrowth looks at regulations as the
globally required compass to propel impactful innovation above profit maximizing innovation.
After delving into hypothetical approaches on how to apply this new paradigm to
entrepreneurship, the thesis analyzes 7 different interviews with key stakeholders in the fields of
Innovation, Degrowth, Entrepreneurship and Sustainability. The analysis of the interviews
further enhances the previously derived findings from the literature review. The spotted patterns
and agreed upon thinking support the foundations of Entrepreneurial Degrowth and provide a
strong starting point for future elaborations of actionable frameworks.

Context introduction
The Urgency of Climate Change
Many of the things you will read in Chapter 1.1 are things you already know. I urge you to read
them anyway to remind yourself of what’s at stake with the accelerated transformation of our
climate. These are not fictional scenarios or rough estimations. These are scientific projections
made by the The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC 2023) based on the most
updated planetary data. Being aware of them is crucial to establish common ground on the
reality we are headed towards and to shape new paradigms to preserve pleasant life on earth.
It’s 2024 and the global surface temperature now reads 1.1°C warmer than it did 150 years ago
(Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2023). The rate at which the temperature has
increased every year is also growing. The scientific projections of this phenomena's
consequences are causing severe concerns. The widespread understanding that not only
global warming is real, but also that beyond certain thresholds it carries catastrophic
consequences with it, is activating policy makers globally to look for solutions. That said,
Gounaridis and Newell (2024) estimate that we have a large amount of the global population
still not believing in climate change. In fact, it is estimated that between 12 and 26% of
Americans are still Climate Change Denialists.
How often have you heard people say that the climate has always changed and always will?
Although it’s true that climate evolves over time, according to Kim Cobb, a climate scientist at
Georgia Tech, none of these natural causes can explain the Earth’s current warming trend,
which is significantly influenced by human activities, particularly the emission of greenhouse
gasses from burning fossil fuels (Yale Climate Connections, 2021). And while this common
belief is founded on the truth, i.e., that the Earth’s climate has undergone dramatic
9
==PAGE:11==
transformations over time, it overlooks a key fact: that the rate at which our climate is changing
today is so fast that it might beat our capacity to adapt to it (IPCC). The pace of global warming
has been the fastest in the last decade with no indications that it’s about to halt (IPCC). We
have accelerated climate change to an extent that is likely to push our current geological
epoch, the Holocene, towards its end (Hooton, 2014), marking the beginning of a new one,
often referred to as the Anthropocene. What this means is simply that the kind of climatic
global equilibrium that has allowed us to live beyond comfortably for the past thousands of
years is coming to an end if we do not take immediate and radical action to preserve and
regenerate our climate conditions. The window of time for preventing climate calamities is now
firmly shut behind us. The global surface temperature will certainly reach 1.5°C and will most
probably touch the 2°C mark (if not erratically cross it) sometime between 2040 and 2050. The
IPCC warns of the urgency required for a rapid and sharp fall in greenhouse gasses, which
would allow for the limiting of global temperatures' rise and would thus avert the worst
consequences (IPCC 2023).
But what are the most severe impacts? Why aren’t they all problems we can easily react to
rather than prevent? According to the IPCC 2023 these are just some of the most concerning
consequences of hitting 2°C.
Resource Scarcity Conflicts
Water Stress and Scarcity: Increased competition for water resources due to changing
precipitation patterns, glacial melt, and droughts, particularly in already water-stressed regions.
Globally, an estimated 2 to 3 billion individuals already face water scarcity issues, a situation
expected to intensify in the future decades (UNESCO, 2023).
Food Security: Diminished agricultural productivity in many parts of the world due to heat
stress, drought, flooding, and pests, leading to food shortages and price spikes. (IPCC 2023)
Energy Security: Conflicts over energy resources may intensify as demand for cooling
increases and hydroelectric power generation becomes more variable. (IPCC 2023)

Migration and Displacement
As you probably know, climate refugees are a growing phenomenon. This is due to rising sea
levels and extreme weather events that when summed up with degraded climatic patterns is
expected to displace 1 Billion people in the next 3 decades. More elaborate recent projections
are pointing at 1.2 billion by 2050, and 1.4 billion by 2060 (International Organization for
Migration, n.d.). To understand the scale this problem might create, let's compare it to a recent
event. The current Syrian refugee crisis, which has seen severe impacts in the south of Italy
and Greece, “only” displaced 12 million people throughout 13 years (United Nations High
Commissioner for Refugees [UNHCR], n.d.). In the next three decades we will witness the
largest forced migration of humans in the history of our species. This will cause dramatic
tensions in receiving areas, where, as we already see today, not everybody will be welcoming
to the refugees. If we will not be prepared to welcome and sustain such an injection of
refugees, we simply will not do that.
Urbanization Pressures:
When individuals migrate from rural zones impacted by various factors to urban areas, the
strain on city infrastructure and essential services can intensify. This surge can lead to an
increase in poverty and escalate social conflicts (IPCC 2023).
Economic and Financial Strains
The rising occurrence of natural disasters puts significant pressure on insurance sectors and
can trigger financial turbulence, especially in areas frequently hit by severe weather. The global
financial impact of such extreme weather events from 2000 to 2019 was around $2.86 trillion,
highlighting the looming economic challenges. For reference, this figure far exceeds the
financial toll of the 2008 crisis (Nature Communications, n.d.) that put our economy on its
knees. With these disasters becoming more common and expected to continue on this
trajectory, the concern for future economic stability grows.
Environmental Degradation and Biodiversity Loss
The World Wildlife Fund's Living Planet Report 2022 paints a shocking picture in terms of the
development of our biodiversity in the last 50 years. The studies, showing a 69% drop in global

Social Tensions: Increased stress on resources and economic disparities can lead to social

Political Instability:
Governments facing pressure from the impacts of climate change may experience increased

Planetary boundaries
The complexity of our climate crisis does not stop at climate change. In fact, the reason we are
faced with such risks is that we have been compromising multiple pillars that form the climatic
equilibrium we are used to living in. These pillars are also called Planetary boundaries (Figure
0). Planetary boundaries are a useful tool that allows us to scientifically evaluate how well we
are performing in the face of the climate crisis. Each parameter represents a pillar that forms
our climatic stability. According to recent research by Richardson et al. (2023), we have
unfortunately crossed 6 out of 9 planetary boundaries, which, amongst climate change, are
now causing the consequences mentioned in the first chapter of the thesis. In fact, climate
change is just one of nine planetary boundaries that are highly interconnected with each other.
We are in urgent need for action to reverse these trends and restore global equilibrium
(Richardson et al., 2023).

Climate Change:
The consequences of climate change are severe and are driven mainly by the amount of
greenhouse gasses that are present in the atmosphere. The way this happens is that as
we emit greenhouse gasses (Including C02), we are strengthening an invisible layer in
the atmosphere that absorbs heat from the sun and sends it back to earth. This cycle
strengthens itself and is one of the main causes of increased temperature (Richardson
et al., 2023).
●
Biodiversity Loss (Biosphere Integrity):
As mentioned by The World Wildlife Fund's Living Planet Report 2022, almost 70% of
biodiversity has been lost in the past half century. At this phase we are increasingly
limiting the amount of genetic diversity, which can undermine ecosystems' resilience
and functionality.
●
Nitrogen and Phosphorus Cycles:
According to Bas Fransen, 2021, “modern agriculture methods were adopted to
increase yield to feed growing populations. However, the use of fertilizers has added
many gasses to the atmosphere. The industries where fertilizer production takes
place—and the use of these in agriculture—are equally responsible for increasing
greenhouse gasses”.
●
Land-System Change:
“Forests, wetlands, and grasslands are being converted into agricultural land to sustain
the world’s population and be fit for human use. This is a change in land use — instead
of reforestation, humans are removing forests to make available more land for
cultivation, and the Earth can’t adjust itself according to the growing needs of
humanity” (Bas Fransen, 2021).
●
Freshwater Use:
“Research shows that by 2050 almost half a billion people will experience water
scarcity. Freshwater resources are being affected by excessive usage by industries.
Human modification of water bodies has a catastrophic impact on the environment,

from changes in river flows and irregular vapor movements on a global scale” (Bas
Fransen, 2021).
●
Chemical Pollution (Novel Entities):
“Novel entities are defined as new chemicals, new forms of existing substances, and
changed life forms that can have undesirable geophysical and biological consequences.
Many of these have been introduced into the environment due to fundamental
human-driven changes. The effects of these entities are long-term: they reduce fertility
and increase the potential of permanent genetic damage in the generations to come.
They’ve already been linked to the decrease in bird populations and hampered
development in marine animals” (Bas Fransen, 2021).
●
Ocean Acidification:
“The increased acidity has ramifications on global marine environments — it mitigates
the carbonate ions necessary for corals, shellfish, and many other species to grow.
Transgressing these boundaries and losing these species would mean a drastic change
in ocean ecosystems and shortages in fish stocks” (Bas Fransen, 2021).
●
Atmospheric Aerosol Loading:
“Atmospheric aerosols affect global atmospheric circulation systems. The aerosols can
formulate clouds that can cool and warm the Earth outside of standard natural cycles.
Aerosols especially tend to affect the monsoon systems in tropical areas. The presence
of aerosols also affects the reflection and absorption of solar rays in the environment”
(Bas Fransen, 2021).
●
Stratospheric Ozone Depletion:
“The ozone layer is responsible for filtering out the harmful ultraviolet rays from the sun.
In the 1980s, the increased concentration of chemicals created a hole in the layer. When
the layer gets thin, increased amounts of Ultraviolet rays make their way towards the
Earth. And this, in turn, threatens the marine and terrestrial biological systems” (Bas
Fransen, 2021).

The brutal realization here is not that accelerated climate change is real and that science
proves that humans are the driving factor of it. We knew that for decades. ExxonMobil, one of
the world's largest publicly traded energy providers and chemical manufacturers, knew that
since the 50’s (Supran, Rahmstorf, Oreskes, 2023). The brutal realization is that the rich

economic forum, 2024). This is a consequence of us being too greedy and egoistic to be willing
to put our capital gains at risk as we work towards a sustainable present. In fact, the only way
we are actively working on solutions is with the pursuit of capital gain, not out of genuine
interest.
The pursuit of constant economic growth has not only been the driving factor of Material
extraction and C02 emissions (Hicklel, 2020), but it’s also the ultimate obstacle to protecting

A brief history of money
We love money and honestly, it’s an incredible invention. It’s one of the greatest inventions of
humankind. Throughout time we have evolved from barter to digital currencies and that
transformation is a testament to humanity's ingenuity in solving complex economic challenges.
Before the widespread use of money, trade was done through a direct exchange of goods and
services, also called barter. However, this system had significant limitations, primarily the
necessity for a coincidence of wants, difficulties in dividing goods for trade, and the absence of
a standard measure of value (Hardy, 2024).
The introduction of money as a universal medium of exchange revolutionized trade by
overcoming these barriers. The first iterations of money were objects of value within societies,
such as livestock, grains, or shells. In 600 BCE, the Lydians had the brilliant idea of introducing
24
==PAGE:26==
the first metal coins, which offered a standardized and widely accepted medium for
transactions, marking a pivotal shift in economic history (Hardy, 2024).
Money's evolution continued with the minting of coins and the printing of paper currency, each
innovation facilitating trade, enhancing economic complexity, and expanding the scale of
global human interaction. These forms of money relied on a shared belief in their value, a trust
that throughout time was underpinned by the authority of temples, banks, and eventually
states.
According to Hardy, 2024, the concept of money as a cognitive and cultural construct
highlights its role as more than just a physical object; it is a product of human trust and belief in
shared myths. It’s through this transformation from barter to money that has enabled the
development of sophisticated economies, the great expansion of cities, and the expansion of
international trade networks.
Today, money continues to evolve with digital currencies such as bitcoin, that are reinventing
the foundations of money and that have been created as a response to the 2008 great
recession. Through decentralized digital currencies with unchangeable and anti-inflationary or
even deflationary characteristics, we might witness the further abstraction of money from
physical objects to incorruptible digital entries (Félez-Viñas, Foley, Karlsen, & Svec, 2021). This
ongoing evolution reflects the adaptability of economic systems to human needs and
technological advancements, underscoring money's role as a fundamental aspect of human
civilization.

Capitalistic practices
As money matured to become a widespread and recognized tool, new applications for it have
been invented to facilitate making (or losing) more of it.
In the context of investing and capital accumulation, The Netherlands played a pioneering role
in the development of capitalism with huge early contributions to economic practices, financial
innovation, and global trade from the 16th century onwards. With the invention of joint-stock
companies The Netherlands is attributed the debated title of "first capitalist nation"
(Emmanouel/Marios/Lazaros and Kyriazis, Nicholas, 2019). Joint stock companies protect
personal assets with limited liability, meaning if the company struggles, your own belongings
as an owner of shares aren't at risk. This sounds obvious now, but was a first at the time.
These companies can also gather large amounts of money by selling shares, allowing them to
grow and invest in further development easily. Because these companies can accrue such a
vast amount of capital, they can produce goods more cheaply and handle large projects that
smaller companies can't. The Dutch were pioneers in the creation of joint-stock companies,
with the Dutch East India Company (VOC) established in 1602 to carry out trade activities in
Asia, being one of the most notable examples. This allowed for the pooling of capital for large
ventures, spreading the risk among shareholders, and is considered a fundamental practice in
capitalism (Stringham, 2015).
On top of joint-stock ventures, The Amsterdam Stock Exchange was established and by so
doing the world's first stock exchange was born. This new institution provided a centralized
location for trading shares of companies, and this conception laid the groundwork for modern
financial markets and the concept of investment into private markets in the pursuit of profits.
The Netherlands also established extensive global trade networks, and amassed wealth
through trade, especially in commodities like spices, textiles, and later tulips. The concept of
Dutch colonial ventures, notably in the Dutch East Indies (modern-day Indonesia), driven by

Inflation + debt
From the introduction of joint-stock companies to today lot’s has evolved in our financial
systems. We have managed to establish global trade and expand cooperation amongst most
countries in the pursuit of more profits and capital accumulation. Throughout this time we have
even detached from the intuitive notion of backing our money with gold and replaced that with
trust and authority of the issuing governments. This phenomenon is also known as the end of
the gold standard (Chen, 2020). This choice, mainly led by the end of convertibility from US
dollar to gold, set an inspiring precedent that created a ripple effect in accelerating the
widespread adoption of Fiat (money that is not backed by precious metal) currencies globally.
Since now, countries did not have to back their money with gold, they assumed control over
the money supply in circulation. Central banks can decide with governments how much money
is printed. Although this may sound as a good thing, it also means that central banks control
the amount of money in circulation and we “paesans” have no say in this practice. And when
we print too much money we inflate our economies and devalue our currency. One danger of
fiat money is that governments could print too much of it, resulting in hyperinflation (Chen,
2020). When we devalue our currencies we end up with more expensive products and services.
What is inflation?
Inflation means that the value of money reduces (Wolla, 2013). Basically, we have inflation
when we inflate the amount of money in circulation by minting (printing) more money into
existence. When we do that, we are augmenting the amount of money in circulation but are not
changing the amount of buyable goods. This discrepancy leads to price increases, as more
28
==PAGE:30==
money chases the same amount of goods (pushing the price of goods to augment). The
COVID-19 pandemic, as detailed in recent research, exemplifies this phenomenon, where
monetary and fiscal policies introduced to mitigate economic downturns have, in some
economies, led to inflation by increasing the money supply without a proportional increase in
goods and services (Li, Sun, & Chen, 2020).
When we introduce more money but don’t increase the amount of things to buy, prices rise.
Imagine we start with 100 dollars in everyone's pockets and 100 items in the store. Then,
suddenly, we print 20 more dollars and give it out, so now there's 120 dollars out there. But the
store still has only 100 items. With more money trying to buy the same number of things, the
price of each thing starts to go up. It's like more people bidding on the same item in an auction
– the price naturally increases because the market can afford to grow the value of the desired
asset. More money chasing the same amount of goods leads to higher prices. (Inflation has
more layers to it, but for the sake of this thesis we don’t need to dive into them.)
And here's a twist – sometimes, the extra money does help to produce more, like 20 new
products. But not always; Oftentimes, that extra money will end up in the hands of the wealthy
that will lock that surplus into capital accumulation assets (e.g. property), or invest it in financial
markets.
Why do economies undergo inflationary measures?
Economic inflation is often a deliberate policy choice aimed at stimulating spending and
investment, crucial drivers for economic growth (Li, Sun, & Chen, 2020). For instance, to
address unemployment, governments might embark on significant projects—like constructing
public universities or bridges—that create jobs and enhance national value. These projects,
due to their extensive costs, frequently necessitate substantial capital, which might not be
readily available through conventional means.
In such scenarios, governments, in collaboration with central banks, may opt to increase the
money supply, a process also known as "printing money." This influx of capital can indeed
29
==PAGE:31==
finance valuable infrastructural developments, subsequently boosting employment and
attracting talent from abroad. However, this method introduces more money into the economy,
leading to inflation. As a result, while the economy benefits from growth and reduced
unemployment, the general populace may experience a decrease in purchasing power,
effectively bearing the cost of economic expansion.
Governments also want inflation because if the value of stationary money (e.g. in your bank
account) decreases, while the value of things (e.g. houses) increases, we will be incentivised to
invest our money (Wolla, 2013). So again, this will grow our economies.
On top of that, our governments might want to spend more than what they earn through taxes
and other revenues and they have the power to print that money and finance those initiatives

Understanding Entrepreneurial Degrowth
Degrowth
Degrowth is an idea that critiques the global capitalist system which pursues growth at all
costs, causing human exploitation and environmental destruction (Hicklel, 2020). The degrowth
movement of activists and researchers advocates for societies that prioritize social and
ecological well-being instead of corporate profits, over-production and excess consumption
(Hicklel, 2020). This requires radical redistribution, reduction in the material size of the global
economy, and a shift in common values towards care, solidarity and autonomy. Degrowth
means transforming societies to ensure environmental justice and a good life for all within
planetary boundaries (Hicklel, 2020).
Degrowth signifies radical political and economic reorganization leading to drastically reduced
resource and energy throughput. Put simply, the degrowth hypothesis is that it is possible to
organize a transition and live well under a different political-economic system that has a
radically smaller resource throughput (Hicklel, 2020).
Flip it however you want, what degrowth claims is that we cannot maintain perpetual economic
growth while also managing to prevent environmental degradation (and the consequences that
come with it). In order to improve our global conditions in terms of welfare we have to give up
some of the ways and costumes we have come to believe are not possible to debunk.

system, means questioning principles that are deeply ingrained in our culture (G. Buzzao,

Degrowth is purposefully provocative
The term degrowth often elicits negative reactions upon first hearing the word. It is a purposely
provocative term that, in our current growth-centric society, is immediately demarcated as
negative and radical. This is only the case if the underlying assumption is that perpetual growth
is good. The basic premise of degrowth, on the other hand, is far more agreeable and clear cut
than its name suggests: we need to reduce energy and material throughput to stay within the
physical limits of our planet.
So, why use the term degrowth at all - why not just refer to what the word stands for and avoid
the confusion?
According to Hickel, 2020, there are a few reasons for this. While most economists agree with
the basic premise of degrowth, they assume that we can bring our economy into the limits of
our planet while continuing to pursue economic growth at the same time. Some even believe
that economic growth can be absolutely decoupled from any underlying energy and material
throughput. These widely held beliefs are housed under the paradigm of green growth.
However, as Hickel notes, there is no empirical evidence that supports the hypothesis of green
growth (Hickel, 2020). With this in mind, degrowth is a useful term as it directly calls into
question the fallacies of green growth and draws our attention to the core issue: perpetual
growth.
Hickel believes that the negative initial reaction to degrowth does not harm its adoption, but
rather, does quite the opposite: “In many cases, negative initial reactions give way to
contemplation (do high-income countries really need more growth?), and then curiosity
(perhaps we can actually ﬂourish with less throughput, and even less output?), and then
investigation (what is the relevant empirical evidence?) that eventually leads people to change
their views. This kind of intellectual transformation is enabled, not inhibited, by using a
provocative term. Trying to avoid provocation, or trying to be agnostic about growth, creates a
39
==PAGE:41==
milieu where problematic assumptions remain unidentiﬁed and unexamined in favor of polite
conversation and agreement”.
Degrowth ≠Recession
A common misconception about degrowth is that it is tantamount to a recession. In reality, the
two could not be further apart. Where degrowth is a planned reduction in material and energy
throughput that aims to reduce ecological impact, inequality and improve overall human
well-being, recessions are unplanned economic crises that harm people. Moreover, degrowth
discriminates (in a good way). Where recessions often destroy socially important sectors while
empowering socially less necessary sectors, degrowth wants to restrict ecologically destructive
industries (e.g., SUVs, beef, advertising etc.) and expand beneficial sectors like healthcare and
education (Hicklel, 2020). In short, degrowth is an economic and social paradigm shift, with an
ethos that prioritizes social and ecological wellbeing. Degrowth policies aim to prevent
unemployment, reduce wealth inequality, and improve access to nature and universal public
goods and services. When did a recession leave us collectively better off than where we
started? As Hicklel, 2020 notes, “Recessions happen when growth-dependent economies stop
growing: it is a disaster that ruins people’s lives and exacerbates injustices. Degrowth calls for
a diﬀerent kind of economy altogether: an economy that does not require growth in the ﬁrst
place, and which can deliver justice and well-being even while throughput declines”.
Degrowth does not trap countries into poverty either. Degrowth is focused on reducing excess
resource and energy use. This means that high-income countries, which are consuming far
more resources than they need to provide their citizens with a comfortable life, would need to
‘degrow’ more. Poorer countries, ones whose populations have yet to achieve fair living
standards and which do not use an excess of resources and energy, can continue to grow their
socially beneficial industries (Hickel, 2019). This premise addresses the social injustices of
climate change, wherein the global North is responsible for 92% of global CO2 emissions in
40
==PAGE:42==
excess of the safe planetary boundary, while the South suﬀers the vast majority of climate
change-related damages (in terms of both monetary costs as well as loss of life) (Hicklel, 2020,
Dorninger et al., 2020).
Entrepreneurship
In its simplest form, entrepreneurship means actually pursuing innovation.
While most people have ideas, the vast majority of them will never act on these. The very few
that actually start acting on their ideas and start creating innovation with viable business
models are entrepreneurs. Being an entrepreneur means committing to an idea that brings
innovation to society and aims to provide financial returns to the business. As an entrepreneur
you usually have to take on a lot of risk (no income, low income) in order to work on an

entrepreneur as long as you pursue your vision (Diandra, D., & Azmy, A., 2020).
Some entrepreneurial ventures aim to be profitable with few resources and scale with no

they attempt to establish and build a company from personal finances or the operating
revenues of the new company (Kenton, 2024). While some businesses prefer to bootstrap,
others prefer to raise money before having a feasible business model and scale faster while
promising high returns to their investors in the long term. A company's capital funding consists

Entrepreneurial degrowth
Entrepreneurial Degrowth aims to bridge the strong community of entrepreneurs with the
rebellious and provocative values of Degrowth. Entrepreneurial Degrowth does that by
urging a reevaluation of how innovation can be promoted under the paradigm of non-perpetual
growth. To connect the innovative spirit of entrepreneurship with the principles of Degrowth, a
shift is necessary where we move away from the traditional metrics of success, marked by
continuous growth and profit maximization and shift towards a model that values sustainability,
equality, and community well-being where value can be created in degrowth oriented
organizations (Froese et al., 2023). Entrepreneurial degrowth lays its foundations on the work of
Tobias Froese, and Oscar Haumann.
Degrowth-oriented organizational value creation is not primarily focused on economic
efficiency, accumulation, and expansion. Instead, it is aimed at realizing equality, participation,
and ecological sustainability (Froese et al., 2023). According to Froese, there is no such thing
as always positive transformation, especially when innovations are aimed at extracting more
profits, often at the expense of global welfare, climate, and social well-being.
44
==PAGE:46==
Froese et al. (2023),s findings support the idea that Entrepreneurial Degrowth has to create a
crucial distinction between profit-driven innovation and innovation that is genuinely designed to
have a positive impact on global welfare, climate preservation/regeneration, and social
well-being. Their research suggests that for an innovation to be aligned with Degrowth
principles, it must challenge the traditional capitalist motivations and instead contribute to a
more equitable, sustainable, and participatory societal model.
In other words, within the framework of Entrepreneurial Degrowth, not all innovations are
created equal. In fact, the concept of Entrepreneurial Degrowth critically examines the
traditional notion of innovation as an always positive transformation that is both novel and
useful (Boyels, 2022). It posits that if innovation is pursued with the sole goal of extracting more
profits, then it is merely optimizing for capitalistic returns rather than aiming for positively
impactful innovation, which actively promotes non-harm to global welfare, climate preservation,
and social wellbeing (Froese et al., 2023). In essence, Entrepreneurial Degrowth delineates
between innovation that positively impacts global welfare, climate preservation/regeneration,
and social wellbeing, and innovation that is driven solely by the pursuit of profits, detached
from the consequences of such pursuits.
Through the lens of Entrepreneurial Degrowth there is a clear distinction between an innovation
aimed at obtaining clean energy, and an innovation that optimizes processes of natural
resource extraction solely for profit maximization. When thinking about ways to make raw
material extraction processes better, it is crucial to evaluate if we can decrease or eliminate
extraction by exploring options that do not rely on limited or damaging resources (Froese et al.,
2023). This critical perspective highlights the importance of creating innovations that are in line
with Degrowth principles - innovations that are both sustainable, fair, and beneficial for both
society and the environment.

The paradox
The paradox we live in is that even when good intentions are our core drivers to innovate, we
are forced to compete in a landscape that prioritizes profits to impact.
Reflect on this statement:
Sustainable innovation requires capital for execution. Venture capitalists invest in sustainable
innovation with the goal to make profits. For a sustainable Innovation business to be viable,
the highest profit margin needs to be seeked. The quest for the most profitable deal pushes
businesses to adopt practices that exacerbate the exploitation of countries’ resources
(environment) and workforces (people). As long as in a competitive landscape and in a
growthist system where we must provide shareholder value, businesses will be forced to
seek cost arbitrage* to stay in business, maintain and grow sales and profits. These
fundamental capitalistic practices prioritize profit over impact or sustainability and incentivize
exploitation of environmental resources and workforces - going against the initial goal of
building sustainable innovations.
Cost arbitrage*
Cost arbitrage refers to the strategy of taking advantage of the cost differences between markets or locations to achieve
financial savings or gain. In the context of businesses striving for profitability, especially those in the green innovation sector,
cost arbitrage involves seeking out lower-cost environments for production, labor, or resources. This could mean moving
manufacturing to countries with lower wages, sourcing materials from regions where they are cheaper due to lower
environmental regulations, or exploiting tax differences between countries.

The complexities of pursuing sustainable innovation within a capitalist framework, which often
prioritizes profit over societal or environmental impact, can be analyzed through the lens of
paradox theory, which refers to “the simultaneous existence of seemingly contradictory
elements in a given situation” (Studysmarter). This theory highlights the inherent contradictions
between the need for capital to drive sustainable innovation and the profit motives of investors.
Such contradictions often result in practices that undermine the goals of sustainability.
A study by Luo, Tang, Chen, Li, Luo, (2020), elaborates on how organizations have long
prioritize economic goals but now also need to integrate social welfare and environmental
concerns to achieve sustainable development. This integration of economic, social, and
environmental goals presents a paradoxical challenge, as organizations must navigate the
tensions between these often conflicting objectives to maintain long-term competitiveness. The
paradox perspective, as discussed in the study, suggests that embracing these tensions can
be challenging but also lead to innovative solutions and sustainable outcomes​(Luo, Tang,
Chen, Li, Luo, 2020)
According to Carmine and De MArchi, 2022, “Change toward more sustainable business
models requires simultaneously building upon and destroying current activities to create novel,
more sustainable organizational forms and practices. From a paradox perspective, this tension
between existing business models and the radical innovations is necessary to achieve systemic
innovation towards long term sustainable development can be addressed through
organizational ambidexterity, i.e., the ability to simultaneously pursue explorative and
exploitative activities for sustainable innovations”.

Findings
Our current system of capitalism is rooted in 500+ years of history and emphasizes constant
economic growth while shaping our approach to innovation and presenting a significant
challenge when considering environmental sustainability and social equality. Historically,
capitalism has been intertwined with the idea that economies must always grow, a concept
that dates back to religious and accounting practices that favored profit and expansion
(Sangster, 2021; Ruge, 2017).
Despite the prevailing and capital backed global narrative of Techno-optimism advocating for
technological solutions and an increase in energy consumption (Marc Andresseen, 2023),
Entrepreneurial Degrowth questions the need for increased energy consumption and endless
50
==PAGE:52==
growth and suggests a new way of thinking about innovation in line with the scientific
projection from IPCC (IPCC 2023). This approach suggests that innovation should not just be
about making more money or growing the economy. Instead, it should focus on creating
solutions that are good for the planet and fair to all people, aligning with ideas of reducing
environmental harm and promoting social justice (Hicklel, 2020; Froese et al., 2023).
Currently, It’s paradoxical to have “green” innovation within our capitalist system, suggesting
that we might need to change how capitalism works to truly achieve sustainability and equality
(Hicklel, 2020; Lüdeke-Freund & Froese, 2020). Not all innovations are beneficial. A
shortcoming of our current system is that if innovation only serves to increase profits without
considering its impact on the environment and society then it’s failing to innovate successfully
(Froese et al., 2023). Successful innovation should be judged by more than sheer yielded
profits.
This thesis argues for a big shift in how we think about capitalism and innovation. It suggests
moving towards "Capitalism 2.0," where the goal is not economic growth but rather making
sure that our innovations help the planet and are fair to everyone.

What are the key principles and foundations of Entrepreneurial Degrowth?
Redefining luxury goods:
Under the lens of Entrepreneurial Degrowth, the concept of luxury evolves into a new
paradigm. Luxury today is anything that elevates one’s life experience to new levels of comfort
and pleasure. Though luxury should not cease to exist, luxurious products should be evaluated
for their level of pollution. The more a luxury is polluting the more it is taxed. By doing this we
would realign incentives to create non-polluting luxuries or reduce the amount of people able to
pay for the most polluting of luxuries.
Sustainability and Ecological Integrity:
A foundational principle of Entrepreneurial Degrowth is prioritizing ecological sustainability and
reducing environmental impact. This involves rethinking production and consumption patterns
to align with the planet's ecological limits (Buzzao, Froese).

Compatibility and Challenges:
Green innovation can exist within a capitalistic landscape, but it faces challenges, such as the

For green innovation to thrive in a capitalistic system, there needs to be a shift towards
alternative financing and investment models that prioritize environmental and social returns
alongside financial returns. This includes support for not-for-profit models and investment
models that ensure equity is returned to the owners of the company rather than shareholders

by an individual or entity which are required to be redeemed for cash or another such property
at a stated time or following a specific event. Essentially, they are shares with a built-in call
option that will be exercised by the issuer at a predetermined point in the future (Kenton, 2022).

The feasibility of green innovation within capitalism also hinges on supportive policy and
regulatory frameworks. Incentives for sustainable practices, penalizing pollution, and fostering a
culture of responsibility towards the planet are crucial (Winge-Petersen, Broeng).

Discussion
The unachievable needed Systemic Change:
The consensus amongst all participants on the problems related to our current growth centric
system make it evident that the desire to change is there. However, the lack of capacity to
actually look beyond the centuries strong capitalist mindset seems to make systemic change
unachievable within a relevant time frame to prevent climate collapse. This realization is
important as we need to build an important compromise between the inherently capitalistic
entrepreneurial values and the radically rebellious degrowth values. The urgency of climate
change, highlighted by the IPCC's dire projections, necessitates a reevaluation of our
economic paradigms. As mentioned, the relentless pursuit of economic growth is deeply
ingrained in global capitalist practices and stands in stark contrast to the ecological and social
imperatives of our time. The concept of Entrepreneurial Degrowth is not reinventing the wheel
by any means, yet makes a strong statement by clarifying that for climate collapse to be
prevented, we need regulatory changes. We cannot expect individuals to solve climate change
out of their personal concerns for the future. We cannot expect companies alone to build the
needed innovation for catastrophes to be avoided (especially when profit maximization is their
core driver of innovation). We need Governments to steer innovation and personal behaviors in
the right direction with penalization and incentivisation.

embrace new rules to the game that reshuffle the playing field. We shall celebrate the most
impactful entrepreneur, not the most profitable. When we do that - we start to change the
world.
Rethink Success and Wealth Accumulation:
Advocate for a societal reevaluation of success, suggesting measures such as capping
personal wealth to promote a more equitable distribution of resources. Encourage narratives
that celebrate contributions to sustainability and regeneration as the pinnacle of achievement.
Final thoughts
At its core, the persistent cultural emphasis on maximizing profits, deeply embedded in global
society, cannot be quickly unlearned within the urgent timeframe required to prevent the
potential end of the Holocene epoch. However, we have the capacity to redirect the
motivations behind this profit-driven mentality towards prioritizing impact over profit. Changing
our values might only happen when faced with the immediate and personal impacts of climate
change, highlighting the urgency of transforming the detrimental economic forces currently in
100
==PAGE:102==
place. This demands a significant shift in perspective: from perceiving the exploitation of
natural resources and emission generation as acceptable side effects of economic activities, to
acknowledging the real costs these practices incur. Such a shift would redefine what is
considered luxury, steering investments towards regenerative and sustainable initiatives.
Success, therefore, would not be measured by mere wealth accumulation but by the significant
contributions to sustainability and regeneration. Gaining wealth through environmentally
harmful practices would become increasingly difficult, bordering on illicit. As indicated in this
thesis, innovation needs to be incentivised by regulations that encourage the creation of
sustainable and regenerative businesses. This transition, though gradual, begins with laws that
diminish the profitability of unsustainable enterprises. While this transition may initially appear
financially challenging in contrast to maintaining current practices, it lays the foundation for a
future that significantly improves our odds of mitigating severe climate change and its related
challenges. This proactive approach not only positions us to better navigate the impending
environmental crises but also enhances our collective potential to continue thriving on this
planet. Ultimately, embracing such a shift is not just an investment in sustainability and
regeneration but a crucial step towards securing a pleasant future for all inhabitants of Earth.
