Abstract
Neural imaging studies have shown that musicians exhibit higher cortical activation
when presented with the sound of their trained instrument. Behavioural studies
however do not show the same eﬀect, with changes in timbre not evoking signiﬁcant
changes in pitch discrimination.
In contrast to previous behavioural studies that used unnatural stimuli, this
study implemented synthesised stimuli based on audio recordings of real instru-
ments to investigate the behavioural eﬀects of timbre on pitch discrimination. The
aim was to clarify whether the musicians ability to discriminate pitch increased
when presented with stimuli based on their own instrument.
Three behavioural
experiments were implemented to measure this eﬀect for non musicians, violinists
and pianists.
The ﬁrst experiment measured pitch discrimination of synthesised
stimuli based on the spectral shape of instrument recordings. Experiment two used
a mistuned melody task with the same instrument conditions, providing musicians
with a more natural pitch discrimination task. Experiment three measured pitch
discrimination using stimuli that included both the spectral shape and the temporal
envelope of the instrument recordings.
Results from these experiments showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in thresholds
between instrument conditions. Hence, musicians did not show an increase in their
ability to discriminate pitch when presented with stimuli based on their trained
instrument. These ﬁndings suggest that the increase of cortical activation found in
musicians when presented with recordings of their own instrument may not be seen
behaviourally.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The human ability to discriminate pitch has been a widely researched topic, with
numerous studies investigating the primary cues used by the auditory system when
performing pitch discrimination tasks [1]. These studies have led to diﬀering con-
clusions, with some studies favouring the dominance of spectral cues [2, 3], some
favouring temporal cues [4, 5], and some arguing the importance of the combined
eﬀect of both sets of information [6,7].
Although opinions regarding the dominant cue are still controversial, a consistent
result found across numerous studies is that musicians repeatedly outperform non
musicians when performing pitch discrimination tasks [8–13]. This enhancement
had previously been linked to an intrinsic disposition in the genetic make up of
musicians [14]. An alternative theory suggests that an eﬀect of neuroplasticity could
enhance a musicians ability to discriminate pitch [15–18].
This eﬀect allows for
additional synaptic connections to be formed to better reﬂect the sensory input
experienced; for example when undergoing musical training.
Findings from a study conducted by Shahin et al. [17] support the second of these
two arguments, with passive listening tests of complex tones producing stronger au-
ditory evoked potentials of late components for musicians when compared to non
musicians. From these results, Shahin et al. suggests that "the tuning properties of
neurons are modiﬁed in distributed regions of the auditory cortex" with suﬃcient
musical training. These ﬁndings are also supported by Pantev et al. [18] who in-
vestigated the cortical activation of trained musicians when presented with audio
recordings of their own instrument. Using Magnetoencephalography (MEG) with
a spherical array positioned over the temporal lobe, Pantev found that musicians
exhibited higher cortical activation when presented with stimuli of their own instru-
ment in comparison to an instrument they were not trained in. This suggests that
the modiﬁed tuning properties of musicians neurons are also adapted to be more

sensitive to the speciﬁc timbre of their trained instrument.
Although these studies show strong results favouring neuroplasticity as the pri-
mary cause of enhanced pitch discrimination in musicians, they do not provide a
deﬁnitive explanation, as they only measure the activation of the auditory cortex.
To fully investigate the neuroplastic eﬀect on pitch discrimination, the response of
the entire auditory system must be taken into consideration.
Looking at behavioural studies carried out within this research area, arguments
favouring the eﬀect of neurplasticity on pitch discrimination for musicians are also
shown. Kishon-Rabin et al. [19] conducted a study where a 3-Interval Forced Choice
adaptive procedure using non musical pure tones was carried out to obtain the
pitch discrimination thresholds for musicians and non musicians.
The result of
this study found the diﬀerence limen for frequency (F0DL) for musicians to be
approximately half that of non musicians. This suggests that the higher cortical
activity of musicians found in electrophysiological studies may be linked to the
signiﬁcantly lower thresholds observed behaviourally.
A second behavioural study by Spiegel and Watson [13] presented pure tone
stimuli to musicians and non musicians in a pattern discrimination test, where par-
ticipants were to specify whether two single tones were the same or diﬀerent, or
whether two 10 tone sequences were diﬀerent. The results from this study once
again showed that musicians had better pitch discrimination abilities overall. Musi-
cians that tuned their instruments compared to those who did not were also shown to
perform better at the task. This in turn supports the argument of musical training
eﬀecting performance in behavioural pitch discrimination tasks.
An additional behavioural study carried out by Oxenham et al. [8] scrutinised
this argument further by measuring the eﬀects timbre in pitch discrimination tasks.
Using a 2-Alternative Forced Choice (2-AFC) adaptive procedure, diﬀerence limens
(DLs) were measured for both the fundamental frequency (F0) and the spectral
centroid of a bandpass-ﬁltered harmonic tone complex. Harmonic complex tones
were increased in frequency for the pitch task and were shifted in centre frequency
for the spectral centroid task, measuring pitch and timbre discrimination thresholds
for musicians and non musicians.
The pitch discrimination thresholds obtained
conformed with previous research, showing a signiﬁcant increase in performance for
musicians compared with non musicians. However, for the spectral centroid task,
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was measured between musicians and non musicians. This
suggested that behaviourally musicians do not perform better in pitch discrimination
tasks for diﬀerent timbres.
This contradicts Pantevs results [18], where stimuli
with diﬀerent timbres showed signiﬁcant diﬀerences in cortical activation between
musicians and non musicians.
==PAGE:15==
3
Although Oxenham et al. showed an insigniﬁcant diﬀerence between non musi-
cians and musicians in relation to changes in timbre, the stimuli used was arguably
too unnatural to evoke a notable response from the musicians. A single harmonic
complex tone added in sine phase was implemented and ﬁltered for diﬀerent centre
frequencies in order to change the stimulus timbre. Similarly, behavioural studies
by Spiegel and Watson, and Kishon-Rabin et al. also implemented unnatural and
unrealistic stimuli in their pitch discrimination tasks, using either pure sine wave
tones or square wave tones. Based on all aforementioned studies, if an eﬀect of
neuroplasiticity exists within musicians, then it would be logical to carry out pitch
discrimination tasks with stimuli that accurately represent the timbre of the musi-
cians instrument of training. Although this has been implemented in electrophysi-
ological studies [17,18], no behavioural study has addressed this factor. Therefore,
this study focused on using stimuli that had the timbre of real instruments in order
to investigate whether a musicians ability to discriminate pitch is speciﬁc to the
timbre of their own instrument. The research question addressed in this study was
deﬁned as:
• Do trained musicians perform better in behavioural pitch discrimination tasks
when presented with stimuli based on the timbre of their own instrument?
Based oﬀof previous studies [8,13,17–19], the hypothesis of this study was:
• Due to the learning eﬀects over years of training, musicians should be more
sensitive to the speciﬁc timbre of their own instrument, thus we should expect
to see an increase in performance for musicians when presented with stimuli
based on their trained instrument.
In this study three experiments were conducted to investigate this research ques-
tion. A total of three subject groups were tested across these experiments: Non mu-
sicians, violinists and pianists. The ﬁrst two experiments used stimuli synthesised
from the spectral shape of real musical instruments. These experiments aimed to
measure pitch discrimination thresholds; experiment one being a classical F0DL task
and experiment two a mistuning detection within a melody task. Experiment one
was implemented in order to provide direct comparisons of F0DLs obtained in this
study to those previously measured behaviourally. Experiment two was designed to
provide a more realistic task for musicians when discriminating pitch. As musicians
do not normally listen to the diﬀerence between a single note and a reference, an
experiment was designed where a pitch discrimination task was incorporated into
a melodic sequence. This provided a more diﬃcult task, with the aim of evoking
==PAGE:16==

larger diﬀerences between stimuli based on diﬀerent timbres. The third experiment
introduced the temporal envelopes of the musical instruments synthesised in exper-
iments one and two. This created a second set of complex stimuli synthesised from
both the spectral shape and temporal envelope. The aim of this experiment was to
measure what aﬀect adding the temporal envelopes of the instruments had on the
pitch discrimination thresholds. A pitch discrimination task measuring F0DLs was
implemented in this experiment, allowing for a direct comparison of the results from
experiments one and three.

Chapter 2
Method
The subject groups, stimuli and experimental procedures are discussed in this chap-
ter. The subject groups chosen for this project are deﬁned, as well as the methods
used for creating various types of stimuli.
2.1
Experimental Subject Groups
Three subject groups were recruited; non musicians, violinists and pianists. These
musician groups were selected based on previous research which had shown signif-
icant diﬀerences in cortical activation between musicians and non musicians [17].
They also represented two contrasting instrument types; the violin a string instru-
ment that requires both initial tuning by the musician and tuning whilst played,
and the piano a string-percussion instrument that requires no initial tuning by the
musician and no tuning whilst played. These musician groups also represented two
contrasting timbres, with the piano being an impulsive hammered instrument, and
the violin being a low impulse, bowed instrument.
All test subjects were screened for normal hearing, with a normal threshold of
20dB HL or below for frequencies 125Hz, 250Hz, 500Hz, 1kHz, 2kHz, 4kHz and 8kHz
being assigned. Any subject with a hearing threshold above 20 dB HL was omitted
from any testing carried out within this study (See A.1 for the mean audiogram
across participants).

2.1.1
NonMusicians
For the purpose of this study, non musicians were classiﬁed as individuals who had
no prior musical experience in any capacity. This not only included having no formal
training, but having no informal experience either.
2.1.2
Musicians
Musicians who participated in this study had an average start age of 8 years old
(±3.2 yrs), an average of 13 years (±3 yrs) of training for their main instrument and
practised their instrument for at least 3 hours per week at the time of this study.
Table 2.1 shows the start age, training and main instrument for each subject tested
in this study. Nine non musicians, seven violinists and eight pianists were tested.
The average age of non musicians was 27 years old (± 2.8 yrs), violinists 25 years

start age of 7 years old (± 2.5 yrs), an average training period of 14.5 years (± 3.4
yrs) and all had conservatory training. Piano players had an average start age of 9
years old (± 3.3 yrs), an average training period of 12 years (±2.2 yrs) and had a
mixture of conservatory, private and informal training.

2.2
Stimuli
Two sets of stimuli, each containing 5 synthesised harmonic complex tones were
used in the three experiments carried out in this study. The ﬁrst set of stimuli
was generated using the spectral shapes extracted from audio recordings of musical
instruments. This stimuli was used in experiment one and experiment two. The
second set of stimuli contained additional temporal envelope information, extracted
from the same audio recordings. This second set of stimuli was used only in the
==PAGE:20==
8

third experiment. The ﬁve synthesised instrument conditions are referred to as the
sine, violin, piano, clarinet and voice conditions. The Sine condition was a digitally
synthesised harmonic complex tone, having a ﬂat spectral shape and was used as a
reference for all other conditions. The violin, piano, clarinet and voice conditions
were also digitally synthesised harmonic complex tones, but were created based on
the spectral shape and temporal envelope extracted from the corresponding audio
recordings for each instrument. All audio recordings used were approximately one
second in duration and taken from Ableton Live 8 Suite 8 [20].
2.2.1
Synthesis Method and Parameters
To synthesise a complex tone based on the audio recording of instrument, the am-
plitude and phase values were extracted for speciﬁc frequencies contained within the
audio signal. The frequencies at which these parameters were extracted were based
on the harmonic series of the note played. Thus, the synthesised harmonic complex
tones generated contained the phase and amplitude of the fundamental frequency
of the note played, along with the values of 20 additional harmonics.
To calculate multiples of the harmonic series, the following formula was used:
Fn = F0 · n, where F0 is the fundamental frequency, n is the harmonic number to
be calculated, and Fn is the frequency value obtained from the calculated harmonic
value. A visual representation of the harmonic series used is shown in Figure 2.1,
with Table 2.2 providing an example of the ﬁrst nine computed harmonic values for
the note C4, with a F0 of 261.6Hz.

2.3
Experiments
Three experiments were conducted in this study. Experiment one was a classic F0DL
experiment using stimuli based on instrument speciﬁc spectral shapes. Experiment
two a mistuning detection within a melody task, and experiment three an F0DL
experiment using stimuli based on instrument speciﬁc spectral shapes and temporal
envelopes. All experiments were carried out monaurally, where test participants
were presented with stimuli at their right ear.
All experiments used Sennheiser
HDA-200 headphones for the stimuli presentation. Stimuli across all experiments
was presented at 70dB SPL.
2.3.1
Experiment I
Experiment one measured the fundamental frequency discrimination limens (F0DLs)
of non musicians and musicians to obtain their pitch discrimination thresholds for
the ﬁve test conditions (sine, violin, piano, clarinet and voice). This experiment
implemented a 3-AFC paradigm with a weighted 1-Up-1-Down method [21] to track
the 75% point on the psychometric function. At each trial, test participants were
presented with three tones; two references (F0 = 261.6Hz) and one target with an
increased F0. Test participants were asked to listen to the stimuli and identify the
target tone which had increased in pitch. The initial diﬀerence in F0 between the
reference and target tone was 20%. This was then logarithmically decreased with a
varying step-size for every second reversal. The reference F0 was roved in a uniform
distribution of +/-2.5 dB around the presentation level for each trial. Each tone
had a duration of 250ms and a linear ramp of 20ms at the start and end of each
tone. A gap of 200ms was present between the three tones. The target variable was
selected randomly trial-by-trail. Figure 2.11 provides a visual representation of the
task used within this experiment.
Six thresholds for each instrument condition were measured.
The average of
the last three measurements were then used as the test participants total threshold
for a given condition. The ﬁrst three values were used as training. In total, the
test participant completed 30 measurement blocks; 5 conditions and 6 repetitions.
Instrument conditions were randomised for each measurement block.

4.1
Discusion
4.1.1
General Analysis
The results obtained in this study suggested that altering the spectral shaping of
stimuli to represent the spectral and temporal properties of real instruments did not
decrease the pitch discrimination thresholds for musicians. Speciﬁcally, thresholds
were not shown to decrease when musicians were presented with stimuli based on
their own instrument. This contradicted the hypothesis of this study, which expected
musicians to show an increased performance in pitch discrimination tasks when
presented with stimuli based on their instrument of training.
Experiment one exhibited signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the thresholds of non
musicians and musicians as found in previous studies [8,13, 19], however no diﬀer-
ences were shown across instrument conditions within subject groups. This was con-
ﬁrmed by comparing the performance ratios of the instrument conditions, where no
signiﬁcant increase in performance was observed for any subject group in comparison
with the sine reference condition. This suggests that although subject groups varied
in their overall thresholds, they performed equally across all conditions, contradict-
ing the hypothesis of this study. F0DLs measured for musicians were consistent
with those found previously for participants with musical training [8]. For non mu-
sicians, F0DLs were lower than previous research, potentially due to the inﬂuence
of high psychoacoustic experience (actively listening to music) in the non musicians
tested [13].
With experiment two implementing a mistuned melody task, musicians were ex-
pected to show greater separation in thresholds of instrument conditions due to the
more realistic nature of the experiment. However, no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in perfor-

mance was observed across instrument conditions. Musicians performed signiﬁcantly
better than non musicians in experiment two between 10 and 60 cents, however no
signiﬁcant diﬀerence was found between violinists and pianists. This supports the
results obtained in experiment one, where the only signiﬁcant diﬀerence observed
was between the pitch discrimination thresholds of non musicians and musicians.
Violinists and pianists who were expected to perform better with stimuli based on
the timbre of their own instrument instead showed no bias to their own instrument
condition. This once again contradicted the hypothesis, which expected an increase
in percentage correct thresholds to be shown for the two musician groups respective
instrument conditions.
Adding the temporal envelope of the instrument recordings to the synthesised
stimuli in experiment three obtained similar results to experiments one and two.
A signiﬁcant diﬀerence was observed between the mean F0DLs (across conditions)
for non musicians and musicians, however no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was shown be-
tween instrument conditions within subject groups.
This was supported by the
performance ratio analysis, which showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the per-
formance of conditions in relation to the sine condition.
If the addition of the
temporal envelope of musical instruments to the stimuli used in experiment three
had eﬀected the pitch discrimination thresholds of musicians, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in performance ratios between the sine condition and all other conditions would be
expected, as the sine condition did not change between experiments. As this was not
shown, this suggests that using stimuli containing the spectral shape and the tem-
poral envelope of an instrument does not have an eﬀect on the pitch discrimination
thresholds for musicians or non musicians. Therefore, results from experiment three
also contradict the hypothesis of this study, as musicians were not found to perform
better in pitch discrimination tasks for conditions representing their instrument of
training.
Comparing experiments one and three showed a signiﬁcant decrease in the mean
F0DLs across instrument conditions for non musicians and pianists (see ﬁgure 3.7).
Violinists did not show a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the mean thresholds across
instrument conditions for experiment one and three. This potentially was due to
violinists having lower pitch discrimination thresholds on average in experiment one,
resulting in fewer signiﬁcant changes on average between experiments. Analysing
individual conditions, non musicians had a signiﬁcant reduction in F0DLs between
experiments for the sine and violin conditions. Violinists also showed a signiﬁcant
reduction between experiments for the piano condition. The signiﬁcant diﬀerences
found between experiments one and three therefore suggest that a varying factor
must exist. As individual results from experiment one and experiment three both

showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in instrument condition thresholds, the probability
of timbre evoking diﬀerences in pitch discrimination thresholds between experiments
is low. Analysing the performance ratios between the two experiments supports this
argument, as no signiﬁcant improvement was seen between the ratios in experiment
one and three. A second argument as to why thresholds were reduced comes from
the diﬀerence in stimuli duration used in experiment one (250ms) and experiment
three (500ms). Increasing the duration of each tone potentially provided a longer
assessment period for the three tones in experiment three, allowing for the target
tone to be identiﬁed more consistently. In turn this would explain the general re-
duction in thresholds between experiments.
If the increase in duration was the
signiﬁcant factor between experiments, no increase in performance ratios between
conditions would be expected, as the duration was increased equally across all con-
ditions. Results from the ratio comparison between experiments therefore support
this argument, as no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was observed.
Looking at the individual conditions, non musicians showed a signiﬁcant diﬀer-
ence in F0DLs between experiments for the sine, violin conditions. As non musicians
were not previously trained in pitch discrimination tasks, training from experiment
one may have inﬂuenced the thresholds in experiment three [24]. This combined
with the increase in the duration of the stimuli in experiment three could explain
the observed reduction in thresholds between experiments for non musicians. This
argument cannot be used for violinists however, who showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
for the piano condition. This initially suggests that the added temporal envelope for
the piano condition may have aﬀected the thresholds between the two experiments
for violinists. This could be due to the high impulse nature of the sound of the
piano increasing the synchronisation of neurons used for coding the spectral and
temporal information of this stimulus (Schreiner et al 2000). However, for these
results this argument is not strong, as the high impulse nature of a pianos temporal
envelope would also increase the synchronisation of neurons in pianists. Looking at
the F0DLs of pianists between experiments for the piano condition, no signiﬁcant
diﬀerence was shown. Therefore, for these results it is more reasonable to assume
that changes between pitch discrimination thresholds in experiment one and three
were elicited through changes in duration between the experiments.

4.1.2
Electrophysiological Comparisons
An electrophysiological study carried out by Pantev et al. [18] showed signiﬁcant
diﬀerences in cortical activation for musicians when presented with diﬀerent record-
ings of real instruments. Speciﬁcally, musicians presented with an audio recording
of their trained instrument were found to have an increased cortical response in
comparison to a recording of an instrument they were not trained in. Although
these eﬀects were shown by Pantev in the auditory cortex, the results obtained in
this study did not reproduce this same eﬀect behaviourally.
Results from a second electrophysiological study by Shahin et al. [17] also could
not reproduce the eﬀects found by Pantev. Shahin investigated the diﬀerence in
cortical activation of non musicians, violinists and pianists when presented with a
pure tone, a violin recording and a piano recording. The results from this observed
no diﬀerence in cortical activation between violinists and pianists when presented
with the recordings of their respective instruments. This contradicted what Pantev
had previously found. Shahin suggested that an overlap in the musical training
history of the musicians tested within the study could have prevented the timbre
speciﬁc responses previously shown by Pantev. This argument could also be true
for the results found within this study. Six out of the seven violinists measured
within this study had an average of 5.7 years of training in a second instrument (±
3.3 yrs). Five of the eight pianists tested had an average of 4.6 years of training
in another instrument (± 2.9 yrs). This second instrument experience is similar
to that found in Pantevs study (5.5 ± 3.5 years for violinists, 4.4 ± 1.6 years for
trumpeters), however the second instruments played by musicians in Pantevs study
did not correlate with the instruments being compared; violinists were not trained
in the trumpet and trumpeters were not trained in the violin. This was not the
case in this present study, as four out of seven violinists were trained in the piano
(7 ± 0.6 years). This may have had an inﬂuence on the results for the thresholds
of violinists, as the hypothesised reduction in threshold for the violin condition may
not be observed due to their thresholds in the piano condition being similar. Figure
3.5 supports this, as violinists and pianists show similar thresholds for the piano
instrument condition. This particular experiment (experiment three) provides the
most support for this argument, as two out of the three violinists measured had the
second instrument of piano (6.5 ± 3.5 years).

4.1.3
Psychophysiological Comparisons
Looking at similar behavioural studies, ﬁndings from Spiegel and Watson [13] showed
signiﬁcant diﬀerences in pitch discrimination thresholds for musicians that tuned
their instrument compared to those that didn’t. Based on these ﬁndings, violin-
ist should have shown decreased F0DLs in comparison to pianists. However, this
was not observed in the results from this study. In contrast, violinist and pianists
performed equally across experiments. This once again suggests that the violinists
training in their second instrument may have inﬂuenced the thresholds between the
violin and piano conditions. Although violinists discrimination thresholds may have
been eﬀected by their training in piano for these two conditions, it does not ex-
plain the lack of diﬀerence observed between other instrument conditions. Violinists
and pianists had no secondary training in the clarinet or voice, yet musicians still
did not perform signiﬁcantly diﬀerent for these conditions in comparison to their
instruments of training.
These ﬁndings were consistent with that found in Oxenham et al. (2014) [8],
where no signiﬁcant eﬀect of stimuli containing diﬀerent timbres was observed. Ox-
enham suggested that this was due to the unnatural nature of the stimuli presented
in their experiments. This argument ﬁts with results found in Oxenhams study, as
harmonic complex tones added in sine phase with a common band-pass ﬁlter (slopes
of 24 dB/octave around the center frequency) were used. Only the centre frequency
of the band-pass ﬁlter was altered in Oxenhams study to represent a change in tim-
bre. In comparison, this study synthesised stimuli containing amplitude, magnitude
and phase values extracted from live instrument recordings. By doing this, previous
inaccuracies regarding synthesised stimuli were addressed [25]. The stimuli used in
this study therefore better represented the natural sounds of instruments normally
heard by musicians. Based on Oxenhams argument, this in turn should have evoked
signiﬁcant diﬀerences in F0DLs between diﬀerent instrument conditions. However,
the results from this study clearly contradicts this hypothesis, with no diﬀerence
across conditions observed in any experiment. A suggested reason for this comes
from the lack of inharmonic spectral values used in the synthesis method of this
study. Inharmonic values alter the periodicity of a signal and have also been shown
to introduce diﬃculties when identifying pitch [26]. By not including inharmonic
values in the stimuli synthesis, conditions may have been too similar to show dif-
ferences in F0DLs for the experiments implemented in this study. This additional
spectral information could be the deﬁning factor required for the eﬀect of timbre to
be observed behaviourally within musicians.
An additional reason why no signiﬁcant diﬀerence was found across instrument
==PAGE:52==
40
CHAPTER 4. DISCUSSION
conditions was due to the inaccuracies in the instrument recordings. As musicians
train with their primary instrument, they are exposed to the sound of that instru-
ment in the position in which it is played. The learning eﬀect of the timbre of their
instrument is arguably then based on the spectral shape observed at that playing
position. Instrument recordings implemented within this study do not take this fac-
tor into account. Combining this with the lack of inharmonic values in the stimuli
synthesis suggests that the stimuli in this study may not be accurate enough to
evoke changes in F0DLs across instrument conditions.
4.2
Future Work
Based on the results found in this study, three main aspects could be improved
in future work to increase the likelihood of observing a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in
musicians pitch discrimination thresholds when presented with stimuli representing
the timbre of their own instrument.
Firstly, the recruitment conditions of musicians could be more strict. Musicians
that play instruments matching with more than one selected timbre measured within
a study should not be used as test participants.
This eliminates the overlap in
training between instruments, which could potentially inﬂuence the timbre speciﬁc
pitch discrimination thresholds of musicians.
Secondly, the stimuli used within this study could be changed, either by present-
ing natural stimuli (audio recordings) or by using a more detailed synthesis method.
Natural audio recordings are ideal stimuli, however these recordings are diﬃcult to
manipulate in experiments without changing the spectral and temporal character-
istics of the original audio ﬁle. Synthesised stimuli based on audio recordings of
instruments are easy to manipulate, thus a wider range of experiments could be
created through this method. To improve the accuracy of the synthesis method
used within this study, the addition of inharmonic spectral values should be im-
plemented. This additional spectral resolution would produce a complex tone that
better represents the timbre of the real instrument. A further improvement that
should be made to the stimuli would be using the entire temporal envelope for the
original signal instead of only the ﬁrst half. This once again would better represent
the timbre of the real instrument, potentially providing the information required to
produce results in support of the hypothesis of this study. Basing this synthesis on
recordings taken from the musicians listening perspective may also increase accu-
racy. The use of binaural dummy head recordings would also provide the correct
lateralisation experienced by the musician.
==PAGE:53==
4.2. FUTURE WORK
41
Finally, the experiments used within this study could be improved to potentially
evoke the hypothesised eﬀect of instrument condition. By randomising the instru-
ment condition per trial, instead of per block, participants may be forced to assess
both pitch and timbre cues, instead of only pitch. The comparison between experi-
ment one and three should be repeated with equal tone duration across experiments.
This along with increase test participants for this comparison would clarify the eﬀect
the temporal envelope has in pitch discrimination tasks. Additionally, including an
objective measurement such as a mismatch negativity task using EEG within the
same study would provide a direct comparison between measurement methods with
controlled variables. This would help bridge the gap between behavioural and elec-
trophysiological measures within this research topic, identifying more clearly the
response of the respective parts of the auditory system.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Using synthesised stimuli based on the spectral shape and temporal envelope of au-
dio recordings of musical instruments, this study investigated the eﬀect of timbre
in pitch discrimination tasks for musicians and non musicians. Results from three
behavioural experiments showed that the two musician groups (violinists and pi-
anists) had signiﬁcantly lower pitch discrimination thresholds than non musicians,
in agreement with results from previous behavioural studies. However, no timbre
speciﬁc eﬀects were observed in either group of participants. Speciﬁcally, violinists
and pianists did not perform better when presented with synthesised stimuli rep-
resenting their instrument of training, as initially hypothesised based on previous
electrophysiological ﬁndings. Overall, the results from this study suggest that the
increased cortical strength found in musicians when presented with stimuli of their
trained instrument may not be reﬂected in increased behavioural performance.
