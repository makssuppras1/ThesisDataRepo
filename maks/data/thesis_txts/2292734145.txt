Abstract
Background: Fast spectroscopic magnetic resonance imaging has gained a large foothold clinically. It is
used as a non-invasive tool to investigate physiological processes and changes for a large number of diseases.
Improvements within the п¬Ғeld are therefore essential. The purpose of this project was to investigate the
effects of optimising gradient trajectories to match the sampling density to the п¬Ғlter. The basis for this
investigation is the fundamental idea that the point spread function can be shaped as desired using the
appropriate п¬Ғlter.
Methods: Throughout the thesis theoretical calculations of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) improvements
obtainable by matching the sampling density are presented. An algorithm to design gradient trajectories
with a given sampling density is also presented. Furthermore, an algorithm to interpolate an arbitrary
trajectory in k-t-space is implemented. Methods to simulate metabolite signals and attenuate the trajectories
to approximate the sampling density along the temporal axis are also presented.
Results: Several different trajectories were designed and corresponding metabolite signals were simu-
lated for evaluation. The simulations conп¬Ғrmed that an SNR gain is obtainable by matching the sampling
density to the п¬Ғlter, both along the spatial and the temporal axis. It was also conп¬Ғrmed that the choice of п¬Ғlter
dictates the shape of the point spread function. Furthermore, it was found that an exponentially decaying
sampling density is best approximated along the temporal axis by attenuating the trajectory oscillations
with an exponentially decaying function. The designed trajectory was translated to gradient waveforms to
ensure that it can be implemented on a scanner. Finally a method to extend the designed trajectory to three
dimensions was presented.
Conclusion: The п¬Ғndings of this thesis implies that there are signiп¬Ғcant improvements to fast spectro-
scopic magnetic resonance imaging obtainable by optimising the gradient trajectories. This has the potential
to both improve current applications as well as enable development of new techniques and diagnostic tools.

1.1
Historical Review
MRI relies on the fact that certain atoms exhibit the phenomenon of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR),
or just magnetic resonance (MR). This stems from the fact that nuclei with an odd number of protons and/or
an odd number of neutrons possess a property known as spin angular momentum. This was п¬Ғrst described
1
==PAGE:22==
2
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
for molecular beams by Rabi in 1938[1], and expanded to condensed matter in 1946 independently by
Bloch[2] and Purcell[3]. In 1973 Paul Lauterbur[4] created the п¬Ғrst image using MR and linear gradient
п¬Ғelds, as well as back-projection to reconstruct the image. The same year, Mansп¬Ғeld[5] demonstrated
recording of spatial and spectral information, and in 1977, he and Maudsley[6] produced the п¬Ғrst in vivo
magnetic resonance image of a human п¬Ғnger, thereby laying the foundations for the ongoing research into
and clinical application of MRI.
Chemical shift imaging (CSI) was п¬Ғrst described by Brown et al in 1982[7]. It was realised as single
voxel MRS (SV-MRS), where a localised voxel of tissue was excited and the transversal magnetization was
recorded over time. This captures frequency information of the chemical environment inside the voxel, which
when demodulated with the precession frequency, can show small chemical shift variations. The frequency
spectrum showing these variations is then used to approximate concentrations of the different chemical
compounds present in the voxel. The technique was further developed by Maudsley et al in 1983[8], which
drew on the work of Mansп¬Ғeld in 1973[5]. Maudsley et al scanned a 31phosphorus-containing phantom,
recording both spectral and spatial information, and thus acquiring a spectrum of frequency variations for
each voxel in a three-dimensional structure. In 1984, Mansп¬Ғeld[9] presented a method for rapid acquisition
of spatial-spectral information, based on a scanning sequence known as echo-planar imaging (EPI). The
technique was further advanced by Twieg[10] in 1989, where he suggested applying an oscillating trapezoidal
gradient during readout, to simultaneously record spatial and spectral information. This new technique
to spatially divide tissue and obtain frequency spectra for each division, allows for comparison e.g. of
metabolite concentrations in different parts of the brain, and leads to a better understanding of physiological
variations throughout the body.
Clinically CSI has gained a large footing, although not as a routinely used diagnostic tool. It is used as
a non-invasive means to investigate physiological processes and changes for a large number of diseases.
For instance, it is used when diagnosing a large range of cardiac diseases[11], to measure hepatic lipid
accumulation for investigation of liver disease[12], to classify epilepsy in patients suffering from mesial
temporal sclerosis[13] as well as characterise metabolism, cancer diseases and ischaemia[14,15] to mention a
few.

1.2.4
Radio-frequency Field
When the magnetisation vector M is aligned with the main п¬Ғeld, it is nearly impossible to measure it,
as M is many orders of magnitude smaller than B. However, as B is only non-zero in the z direction, a
signal from the magnetisation can be recorded if M is tipped away from the longitudinal direction. To rotate
M into the transversal xy-plane a radio-frequency п¬Ғeld B1 is applied. The rotation of the magnetisation
is effectively due to the torque that B1 applies to M, which will rotate it with an angle dependent on the
duration and amplitude B1. Applied in concordance with a gradient, only a selected number of nuclei
can be rotated and provide signal. After the excitation, B1 is turned off and M will precess around B0,
while it will return to thermal equilibrium. This relaxation of the magnetisation is described by two time
constants T1 and T2. T1 describes the regrowth of longitudinal magnetisation, while T2 describes the decay
of transversal magnetisation. The rotation of the magnetisation induces an electromotive force in a receiver
coil, as described by FaradayвҖҷs law of induction, and will yield a signal called a free induction decay (FID).
This signal will contain information about the amount of nuclei at the spatial position selected by gradients.

resolution of the reconstructed image. The distance between samples in k-space is denoted as вҲҶk, and this
distance determines the п¬Ғeld-of-view (FOV) along the corresponding spatial direction. The central regions
of k-space holds the coarse features (low frequencies) of the image while the outer regions holds the details
(high frequencies). For certain sequences k-space is hermitian symmetric with respect to the center, and
thus partial k-space coverage can be considered for reduced scan time.
1.2.8
MRI Sequence
An MRI sequence is a term used to describe the generation and recording of transverse magnetisation.
It consists of a set of RF pulses, used in conjunction with gradients to record MR signal and reproduce
images. Different sequences exist for each application of MRI, and later sequences speciп¬Ғc to MRSI will
brieп¬Ӯy be discussed. A pulse sequence is generally deп¬Ғned by some basic parameters including; repetition
time (TR), echo time (TE) and п¬Ӯip angle. The п¬Ӯip angle is the rotation angle of the magnetisation during
application of an RF pulse. TR is deп¬Ғned as amount of time between successive RF pulses applied to the
same tissue, while TE represents the time between the excitation pulse and the center of k-space is sampled.

1.2.10
Point Spread Function
The point spread function (PSF) in MRI describes the blurring of an object as a result of the imaging
process. The PSF is the response of the imaging system to a inп¬Ғnitesimally small point source, also referred
to as the impulse response. As any object can be considered a collection of point sources, and the Fourier
transform is a linear operation, knowledge of the PSF will allow one to predict how an object will be
imaged. The PSF of an unп¬Ғltered reconstruction can be described as the convolution of a point source
with the Fourier transform of the continuous sampling density. As a sampling density is usually a constant
rectangular function, the Fourier transform of this will be a sinc function, which when convolved with the
point source will blur it. As the width of a sinc function is inversely related to the width of the rectangular
sampling function, an inп¬Ғnitely large coverage of k-space will yield an inп¬Ғnitely narrow sinc function (a
delta function), which will result in perfect reconstruction of the point object. However, this is not possible,
as only п¬Ғnite sampling is realisable, which will inevitably result in contribution of signal in one voxel to
neighbouring voxels. This effect is called bleeding. Bleeding is usually sought minimised through the use
of a п¬Ғlter, but at the cost of image contrast and signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). A more thorough discussion of
the PSF related to MRSI will be conducted in Section 1.3.2.
1.3
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopic Imaging
This thesis denotes CSI as a subtechnique of MRSI, where the same phase encoding procedures are
used as in MRI to map the spatial distribution of MR signals. The technique is separate from SV-MRS as
it aims to map the concentration of metabolites from several voxels, rather than a single voxel. For this
thesis mainly 1H-CSI is considered, where proton MR signals produced by metabolites within the tissue
are imaged. As the concentration of said metabolites are approximately 10,000 times lower than water
concentration, they produce much lower signal strengths, and thus larger voxel sizes must be used to detect
enough signal above background noise. At a п¬Ғeld strength of 3 Tesla for instance, voxel volumes on the
order of 0.3 cubic centimetres can be measured, and the use of surface coils allows voxel volumes around
0.1 cubic centimetres[20].
CSI is very sensitive to local magnetic п¬Ғeld inhomogeneities within a voxel. These will result in a
regional spectral broadening, which will disperse the MR signal as a function of time, as local gradients

will impose local k-space trajectories for each voxel. Conclusively, this will result in a destruction of the
signal from the voxel, as k-space will be shifted with time, and signal decay will be accelerated. Another
way to perceive the accelerated signal decay, is that the local magnetic п¬Ғeld inhomogeneities will cause
intra-voxel de-phasing of spins. This will lead to accelerated decay of transversal magnetisation, and thus an
accelerated signal decay. Furthermore, a central problem in CSI is artefacts that emerge as a consequence of
water and lipid signals. As water produces a lot higher signal than the metabolites, the water is presaturated
using selective RF pulses. However, this method is not perfect, as some water signal, especially at tissue
interfaces, remains. Certain tissues have a large amount of lipids, and thus lipid signal will be signiп¬Ғcantly
higher than other metabolite signals. Solutions include lipid nulling using short T1 inversion recovery
(STIR)[20].
A typical CSI sequence uses phase encoding only before, and not during, signal readout, as conventional
MRI methods. The use of phase encoding during signal readout would interfere with the chemical shift,
and result in spectral line broadening due to the applied gradient. Phase encoding is therefore done before
signal readout, and only modulates the phase and amplitude of the resulting signal, but not the frequency.
This means that the reconstruction is straightforward, as a simple Fourier transform will transform k-space
information to spatial information, and the temporally recorded signal to spectral information. However,
phase encoding will have to be applied once per TR, which will result in very long scan times, as the
sequence will have to be repeated and the signal measured once for every desired voxel. Shortening of the
scan time is highly desirable, as it allows increased volume coverage and reduces motion artefacts. Several
approaches exist, but the most commonly used include echo-planar spectroscopic imaging (EPSI) and spiral
k-space encoding[20]. EPSI uses a series of periodically inverted gradients, which generate a train of echoes
that contain both spatial and spectral information[21]. This method introduces a fast coverage of k-space,
while simultaneously recording spectral information. Consequently, the sampled data does not lie on a
Cartesian grid, which means that the fast Fourier transform (FFT) cannot be used to reconstruct an image.
Usually this is overcome by using a gridding algorithm to interpolate the data onto a Cartesian grid, so FFT
is possible. The gridding algorithm used in this thesis is described in Section 2.5.

1.3.5
Advances in the Field of CSI
A brief review is here given of advances within the п¬Ғeld of CSI, although focus is put on advances that
are pertaining to the scope of this thesis.
Adalsteinsson et al. [27] implemented a sequence to improve scan time by acquiring multiple contiguous
slices in the same acquisition time as required for a single slice, without a loss of SNR. As described earlier,
Posse et al. [21] introduced EPSI which enabled acquisition of high resolution spectroscopic images with a
short TE. It is a modiп¬Ғcation of the echo-planar spectroscopic imaging method proposed by Mansп¬Ғeld [9]
and utilises time-varying readout gradients to simultaneously encode spatial and spectral information. The
authors report a reduction of scan time from the traditional 30 minutes to 64 seconds, although at a loss
of spatial SNR of 10-20% for a phantom and a decrease of a factor of 5-6 in vivo. The spectral resolution
==PAGE:31==
1.3. MAGNETIC RESONANCE SPECTROSCOPIC IMAGING
11
was retained along with artefacts, or lack thereof. Pohmann et al. [28] performed a comparison study of the
different fast spectroscopic imaging methods. The sensitivity, which was deп¬Ғned as the ratio of SNR and
the square root of the acquisition time, was used to quantify the performance of the different methods. It
was shown that conventional CSI is the gold standard and sensitivity was not improved for any fast CSI
sequences. However, the fast CSI methods were found to be useful to avoid blurring caused by motion
artefacts. Adalsteinsson et al. [29] presented a sequence where the spatial frequency components are sampled
through slice selection in one dimension and a spiral-based trajectory in the remaining two dimensions.
This sequence was designed to improve acquisition time, but suffered a loss of SNR. The sequence allowed
for water and lipid suppression.
Other studies introduced several sequence designs, all with various advantages and disadvantages;
Cunningham et al. [30] designed a variant of EPSI, called п¬Ӯyback EPSI, where signal was only recorded
while traversing k-t-space in an increasing direction, unlike normal EPSI. This sequence yielded a large
k-t-space coverage, but at the cost of SNR (10-30%); Gurney et al. [31] presented a study that suggested
using multiple cone-shaped trajectories to cover k-t-space. Designing the trajectories with a given resolution
and FOV in mind resulted in increased SNR efп¬Ғciency and the authors concluded that a better PSF was
obtained; Uribe et al. [32] suggested to intentionally undersample k-t-space, while sampling it uniformly
with concentric spheres in all three spatial dimensions. Comparing this sequence with the one suggested
by Adalsteinsson et al. [29] yielded a narrower PSF when using the same number of interleaves for both
sequences.
For all the described sequences, the trajectories have been designed from competent guesses, and the
consequences of the design subsequently analysed. More recently though, algorithms have been developed to
design trajectories that meet speciп¬Ғc restrictions. For example, Sabat et al. [33] proposed Genetic Algorithms
optimisation on three-dimensional trajectories in k-t-space, given gradient hardware limitations. The
resulting PSF was compared to one obtained with the stack of spirals suggested by Adalsteinsson et al. and
yielded a narrower main lobe under similar conditions. Pipe and Zwart [18] introduced a п¬Ӯexible numerical
algorithm for designing spiral trajectories in two and three dimensions based on deп¬Ғnition of the resulting
radial undersampling and limited by gradient hardware characteristics. The algorithm analytically designs
how to best utilise the hardware to create the fastest k-t-space coverage.
So far the discussed studies only deal with how to traverse k-t-space during data acquisition with no
regard to sampling density. As this study will show, the sampling function is also of importance. Tsai and
Nishimura [34] proposed a variable-density sampling of k-t-space for spiral trajectories and a conventional
two-dimensional phase encoding trajectory. By sampling the more energy heavy centre of k-t-space more
densely than the outer parts, a better reconstruction was obtained, and reduced aliasing was found, despite
violating the Nyquist criterion in the outer parts of k-t-space. Greiser and Von Kienlin [35] developed an
algorithm that varies sampling density according to a given weighting function. Furthermore, they compared
conventional uniform weighting with acquisition weighting and density weighting, and obtained a better
PSF with density weighting and no resulting SNR penalty. Geier et al. [36] implemented effective density
weighting and parallel imaging and found improved SNR (17-34%) and a reduction of Gibbs ringing,
compared to standard Cartesian imaging. Zeller et al. [37] found signiп¬Ғcant SNR improvement (43Вұ13%)
when implementing density weighting for turbo spin echo sequences, while retaining comparable contrast
behaviour.
A few studies also directly try to optimise the PSF, such as Tan and Zheng [38], which uses linear matrix
inequality constraints. The authors п¬Ғnd that a trade-off exist between width of the main lobe of the PSF and
the amplitude of side lobes. The resulting reconstruction of simulated data demonstrates more accurate and
visually better looking images.

The discussed studies mainly focus on designing k-t-space trajectories to improve various parameters.

1.4
Project Outline
Having established the basic foundations of MRSI and brieп¬Ӯy described the recent advances within the
п¬Ғeld, an overall statement of intent can be given. The fundamental idea of this study is that by selecting a
desired PSF, one must utilise a speciп¬Ғc п¬Ғlter. Matching this п¬Ғlter to the acquired signal will result in the
optimal balance between resolution and SNR. The thesis seeks to ascertain that by optimising the sampling
density to the matched п¬Ғlter a better SNR may be obtained. Initially expressions for the behaviour of noise
are derived theoretically and theoretical SNR gains are estimated. Thereafter simulations of k-t-space
trajectories are generated to conп¬Ғrm the theoretical п¬Ғndings. For this purpose, an algorithm that allows
the design of a k-t-space trajectory with varying sampling density is developed. Furthermore, the thesis
translates the designed k-t-space trajectories to gradient waveforms that adhere by hardware limitations,
which ensures that the designed trajectories are feasible in practice.
In the following chapter, Chapter 2, the theoretical background of the methods employed throughout
the thesis are discussed. Basic concepts such as the signal evolution and matched п¬Ғltering are discussed,
and the interpolation method called gridding is introduced. Finally, theoretical calculations of the expected
SNR gain by optimising the sampling density are done. Chapter 3 describes the methodological strategies
used to generate results. Both implementation of the gridding algorithm as well as algorithms to generate
arbitrary trajectories are introduced. In Chapter 4 the generated k-t-space trajectories are shown and
evaluated for different situations. Both theoretical trajectories, to verify the gridding algorithm and illustrate
obtainable PSFs, as well as critically sampled trajecories, to reconstruct metabolites precessing at realistic
frequencies, are generated and tested. The results are followed by a discussion in Chapter 5, where the
different evaluations are analysed and future investigations are suggested. Finally, the thesis is rounded off
in Chapter 6, where a conclusion of the project is reached.

In this chapter the theoretical background for the calculations and results presented in this thesis is introduced.
First some frequently used distributions and their Fourier transforms are presented, followed by a general

both one and two dimensions. The derivations establish that SNR gains should be obtainable by matching

effect, in the metabolite signal is given. At the end of the chapter a tentative conclusion of the theoretical
derivations is presented, along with a plan on how to verify the results.

2.2
Signal Evolution in MRSI

separability of spins at a given spatial position from spins at other positions, and thereby enable the ability

precession frequency. In MRSI, the trajectory is propelled by two different engines. First and foremost time,

was disregarded for this study. Therefore, a constant sampling frequency is assumed throughout the thesis.

2.4
Sampling Density
When the acquired signal is п¬Ғltered some recorded information, as well as the noise, is suppressed.
As time is important, it is essential to use it wisely in MRSI. It is not beneп¬Ғcial to spend time acquiring
a lot of signal, which is then suppressed by a п¬Ғlter. A way to avoid wasting time acquiring signal, which

it is not sought. Given that the matched п¬Ғlter follows the deп¬Ғnition in Eq. 2.20, the sampling density in both

2.8
Separation of Lipid and Other Metabolites
A common problem with in vivo MRSI is the ubiquitous presence of lipid peaks. These peaks may
obscure the peaks of less abundant metabolites. Lipid contamination due to non-ideal slice proп¬Ғles can be
handled by outer volume lipid suppression[14]. Lipids give rise to other problems though. As lipids usually
have a very short T вҲ—
2
[23], signal will decay fast, and thus the spectral peaks will be broad. Furthermore,
lipid peaks are present at several frequencies all across the spectrum. Finally, as lipids are usually deп¬Ғned
by sharp transitions in tissue, extensive k-space coverage is needed to ensure proper spatial localisation.
One way to counter this problem is to increase TE, as the lipid signal will then decay away before signal
acquisition begins. However, this will result in a reduced SNR, as signal originating from the metabolites
of interest will also decay away during the waiting period. Furthermore, the experimenter may want to
actually quantify the lipid at its spatial position. To demonstrate the problem with the broad lipid spectra, a
spin echo signal containing lipid and an arbitrary metabolite is simulated. The metabolite and lipid is set to
precess at the same frequency; 1.33 ppm, and the two spectra will overlap. The initial amplitude of the lipid
signal was chosen to be three times that of the metabolite signal, which was simulated with a T2 of 240
ms and a T вҲ—
2 of 70 ms. Parameters used for the lipid signal are taken from Table 1.1. The sequence was
modelled with a TE of 36 ms. Figure 2.8b illustrates the two signals separately, and the quick regrowth and
decay of the lipid signal is obvious. The summation of the two signals is show in Figure 2.8c, where both
contribution from both signals are clear. The corresponding spectra are illustrated in Figure 2.8a, where it is
clear that the broad spectra of the lipid metabolites inп¬Ӯuences the metabolite spectra, and thus jeopardises
estimation of metabolite concentration.
The above example does not take any spatial separation into account. However, when doing in vivo
experiments, lipids are usually separated from other metabolites spatially, as lipids are mostly situated
subcutaneously in the outer parts of the skull. As the lipid signal is present for only a short amount of
time, k-t-space must be covered extensively during this time, both to obtain sufп¬Ғcient signal to reconstruct
a spectrum, but also to spatially localise the lipid, so it does not bleed into metabolite signals of interest.
Apart from approximating the desired sampling density, attenuating the trajectoryвҖҷs k-t-space coverage with
time has another advantage. Extensive coverage of k-t-space in the beginning of the measurement may
ensure spatial localisation of the fast-decaying lipids, while central areas of k-t-space will be covered at the
end of the measurements to improve SNR for the slowly decaying metabolites.
As discussed earlier, there is a trade-off between width of the PSF and SNR. By attenuating the
oscillation amplitude of the trajectory, it may be possible to spatially separate the lipids from the metabolites
of interest, while retaining the same SNR as obtained with a non-attenuated trajectory, which has a lower
spatial resolution.

3.1.4
Extension to Three Dimensions
The methods and algorithms discussed so far only design a two-dimensional trajectory in k-t-space.
Utilising this trajectory in a slice selective sequence will only yield a one-dimensional projection of
the metabolite signals present in the slice. To design a trajectory that will be able to reconstruct three-
dimensional voxels another spatial frequency axis must be considered, meaning that kx-ky-t-space must
be traversed. This thesis does not offer any simulations on three-dimensional trajectories, as the gridding
algorithm was only implemented for two dimensions. However, it does propose a method to extend the
designed k-t-space trajectory to a kx-ky-t-space trajectory.
The idea is to design the trajectory as described earlier and then rotate it around the time axis using
a rotational matrix. This would mean that several samples still have the same time stamp, which could
be solved by implementing the sequence such that one trajectory would be traversed per TR, and that the
number of needed scans to cover the entire three-dimensional trajectory would be equal to the number of
rotations generated.

3.5
Gradient Waveform Design
To estimate gradient waveforms from the designed trajectories an algorithm developed by Chauffert
et al. [40] was used. The algorithm projects a given trajectory onto the convex set of admissible curves. To do
this it employs convex optimisation, which iteratively minimises the distance between the input trajectory,
and the approximated trajectory. Furthermore, the algorithm aims to preserve the sampling density of the
input curve, which is essential for this study. As the developed trajectories in this thesis only deal with one
spatial dimension, the hardware limitations were deп¬Ғned as rotational invariant. The algorithm requires
only an input sampling time, a maximal gradient amplitude and slew rate. Number of iterations were kept at
5000, as the algorithm converged well within this range.

4.4
Spatial Localization of Spectra
To investigate the trajectoryвҖҷs ability to spatially localise spectra, an arbitrary signal containing contribu-
tions from water, lactate, NAA, creatine and choline, all with equal amplitude, was simulated. The signal
for all metabolites was shifted one centimetre from the center of the FOV. All precession frequencies and
relaxation parameters were taken from Table 1.1.
To successfully reconstruct the signal from the precessing metabolites, the trajectory must be sampled
critically. The trajectory was therefore designed such that the time spacing between crossing of k = 0 was
0.36 ms, resulting in an effective sampling frequency of approximately 5.6 kHz along the k-axis. As the
bandwidth of the metabolites signal is around 500 Hz, this was considered sufп¬Ғcient to reconstruct the
spectra. The trajectory was designed such that the kmax/Пғ relation described earlier was retained, using
Nk = 16 and sampled at 50 kHz for 572 ms and with a TE of 144 ms. The resulting trajectory is seen in
Figure 4.14a. The simulated metabolite signal was interpolated onto a 16 Г— 3180 Cartesian grid, with a
voxel size of 2 cm and a FOV of 32 cm, which was п¬Ғltered with the two-dimensional п¬Ғlter in Eq. 2.20,
where a = 100 ms, and the spectra were reconstructed. In accordance with the intra-voxel dephasing
requirements speciп¬Ғed earlier, the trajectory was not attenuated to less than 0.1025 cmвҲ’1. The interpolated
k-t-space and corresponding reference are shown in Figure 4.14. The interpolated k-t-space has a lower
peak amplitude than the reference, which results in a worse reconstruction. This is further illustrated
in Figure 4.15, where the reconstructed spectra and reference are shown. During the reconstruction the
spatial dimension was interpolated so the resulting grid was 128 Г— 3180 voxels. The spectral line shown
in Figure 4.16 depicts the reconstructed and spatially shifted spectrum. Although the spectrum has been
successfully reconstructed at the correct spatial location, the amplitude of several metabolites is much
lower than for the reference. Especially for the metabolites precessing at the highest frequencies. Some
oscillations are present in between the peaks, but these are relatively low compared to the peak heights.
Furthermore, Figure 4.17 shows the spatial distribution of each metabolite, which emphasises that the
metabolites have been reconstructed at the correct spatial position.
To counter the excessive averaging, which was the suspected reason for the bad reconstruction of
metabolites precessing at higher frequencies, the RCF of the gridding algorithm was changed, and the signal
simulated and reconstructed again. The RCF used for this interpolation was half of that determined by
Eq. 2.30. The resulting metabolite spectrum at the spatially shifted location is illustrated in Figure 4.18. This
clearly shows a better reconstruction of the spectra, with especially the NAA peak increased in amplitude.

Temporal Filter Effect
To investigate the effect of the exponential decaying п¬Ғlter applied in the temporal direction a brief
study was conducted. Noise with an amplitude of 40% of the metabolite amplitude was added to the above
simulation, as described in Section 3.4, and the п¬Ғlter time constant a was varied between 50 ms and 250 ms.
The spectra resulting from the different п¬Ғlters are shown in Figure 4.19. The short п¬Ғlter clearly п¬Ғlters away
more noise than the longer ones, but the peaks are widened and the peak value lowered. The longest п¬Ғlter
results in very well deп¬Ғned narrow peaks, but a lot of noise is present. For this simulation the RCF of the
gridding algorithm was set to half of that estimated by Eq. 2.30.

4.5

Section 2.8 describes how it might be possible to obtain better spatial localisation of lipid metabolites,

and thus a voxel size of 2 cm. The decaying trajectory had a peak kmax = 0.5 resulting in a voxel size of 1
cm. Both were п¬Ғltered with an exponentially decaying п¬Ғlter with a = 100 ms, and reconstructed onto a
FOV of 32 cm. For this experiment the RCF parameter for the gridding algorithm was set to half of that
estimated using Eq. 2.30 to counter excessive averaging. The trajectories are illustrated in Figure 4.20.
The metabolite signal was composed of the same metabolites as described above, shifted two centimetres
from the center of the FOV, as well as a broad lipid peak, precessing at 1.5 ppm[14] and shifted 4 cm from
the center of the FOV. Relaxation times of the lipids are taken from Table 1.1 and the amplitude of the

Here follows an interpretation and discussion of the results that have been obtained throughout the thesis. The
impact of the implementation of the algorithms, as well as choice of parameters are evaluated. Furthermore,
suggestions for improvements to the used procedures will be given throughout the chapter.
5.1
The Gridding Algorithm
The reconstruction of the data obtained from Stanford University conп¬Ғrms that the gridding algorithm
works as intended. The parameters chosen were validated and they provide a good reconstruction of
the scanned phantom. However, as the data from Standford University was MRI data and not MRSI
data, the choice of RCF was not validated. The algorithm proposed by Jackson et al. [42] has earlier been
validated in several studies[45,29,34,36,39] and thus mainly the computational implementation was validated.
As described earlier, the implementation was very similar to that of Marcussen [39] apart from the choice
of parameters. Some averaging of k-t-space is present, but this is unavoidable when interpolating and the
chosen convolution window, convolution width and overgrid factor are considered adequate.
The results throughout the thesis illustrate how essential the determination of gridding parameters are
for the success of the reconstruction. Unfortunately, there is no ground truth when choosing the parameters,
and several authors have taken different approaches. Both optimisation[39,46] and analytical[47] solutions
have been proposed with various results. This thesis presents estimation methods for the convolution width
and relative contribution factor, while the convolution window choice and corresponding overgrid factor
were inspired by past research. The advantage of consequently estimating the convolution width and RCF
in the same way was comparability between the spatial and two-dimensional simulations. However, when
interpolating the trajectories which were sampled critically, there was evidence that suggested the estimation
of RCF resulted in a too large convolution width along the time axis. This correspondingly resulted in too
65

much averaging of the samples along the time axis, which erased some of the fast changing signal that
stemmed from the metabolites precessing at higher frequencies. The RCF was therefore changed for the
critically sampled trajectories, which did accommodate the problem to a certain extend, although it was not
removed.
One could argue that a more thorough study of how the parameters affect the actual estimation of spectral
quantiп¬Ғcation should be done, but this was considered outside the scope of this study. One way to perform
such a study could be to design a speciп¬Ғc trajectory, and then optimise the parameters for that speciп¬Ғc
trajectory on a known dataset. This could either be a simulated dataset or a dataset obtained from a phantom.
One of the main problems of the optimisation approach however, is that it is very hard to determine when
a trajectory has been interpolated in an adequate way. It is not very hard to optimise it to successfully
interpolate a signal originating from a single metabolite as the expected outcome is known. But that set of
optimised parameters are not likely to perform as well on data containing several metabolite signals, which
all precess at different frequencies. A likely solution would be to validate the gridding parameters for a
speciп¬Ғc trajectory against phantom data, where the signal originates from a realistic metabolite distribution.
This way a trajectory to solve a given clinical task would be provided with an optimal set of gridding
parameters, which would ease the clinical implementation.
5.2
Spatial Simulations
The spatial simulations were conducted for several reasons. First and foremost, the gridding algorithm
was thereby tested on MRSI signals, albeit simulated. Furthermore, it was conп¬Ғrmed that a Gaussian PSF
could be obtained in the spatial direction by п¬Ғltering with the matched п¬Ғlter designed from a Gaussian
function.
The reconstructions of different trajectories all resulted in an accuracy gain when using a Gaussian
sampling density rather than a uniform one. This implies that the gridding algorithm performs better with a
higher sampling density. The gridding algorithm also beneп¬Ғts more from the trajectories with slew rates
taken into account than those without, due to the higher sampling density at the edges of k-t-space. The
results also illustrate that the averaging exercised by the gridding algorithm lowers the signal peak amplitude,
as the convolution kernel smears the signal out on the Cartesian grid. The density compensation factor
does not compensate adequately for this. Apart from the described problems, the interpolation of k-t-space
enables reconstruction to a satisfactory degree.
As expected, there is an SNR loss when taking the hardware limitations into account, which is due to
the fact that it is not possible to retain the Gaussian sampling density across the entire k-axis. The time used
to perform the reversals at the edges of k-t-space will cause a higher sampling density there than desired.
But there is still a signiп¬Ғcant SNR gain obtainable when matching the sampling density to the п¬Ғlter. This
supports the theoretical calculations done earlier in the thesis, and one of the fundamental theories of the
project. Furthermore, it conп¬Ғrms п¬Ғndings done earlier by Greiser and Von Kienlin [35]. No further SNR gain
is obtained when using a spin echo trajectory, rather than a gradient echo trajectory, but a better accuracy is
obtained. This is further conп¬Ғrmation that the gridding algorithm handles more densely sampled areas better.
In the spin echo trajectories the time TE, where a lot of signal is present, is sampled more densely than for
gradient echo. This was the reason that spin echo trajectories were used for the remainder of experiments
done in the thesis.
Finally the spatial simulations ascertain that the matched п¬Ғlter controls the shape of the PSF. The
theoretically estimated PSF matches the PSF from the reconstructed spectra, obtained using the spin echo
trajectory with a Gaussian sampling density, which has been interpolated and п¬Ғltered. This conп¬Ғrms the

5.3
Two-dimensional Simulations
The two-dimensional simulations were performed both to ensure that an SNR gain was obtainable in
the spectral dimension as well as investigating how an exponentially decaying sampling density is best
approximated. Two methods of attenuating the trajectory was evaluated and the highest SNR gain was
observed when attenuating the oscillations with an exponentially decaying function. In fact a loss of SNR
was estimated when using the Gaussian contour as amplitude for the oscillations.
It is not possible to shape the spectral PSF in the same fashion as the spatial PSF. This is because
the signal will inadvertently decay exponentially with time constant T вҲ—
2 , meaning that even though k-t-
space is gridded to a uniform sampling density in the time direction, the signal will п¬Ғlter itself with an
exponentially decaying function. The shape of the spectral PSF is therefore a Lorentzian peak convolved
with a sinc function per default. One can alter the shape of the spectral PSF, e.g. by using the Lorentz-Gauss
transformation[41]. The Lorentz-Gauss transformation п¬Ғlters the acquired signal such that the exponential
decay is countered and the signal is near constant throughout time. However, this has a signiп¬Ғcant SNR
penalty, as noise at the end of the acquisition will be massively enlarged. The constant signal can be
shaped as desired though, and is subsequently shaped as a Gaussian function, hence the Gaussian part
of the transformation. The matched п¬Ғltering done in this thesis aims to improve SNR and thus results in
convolving the spectral PSF with yet another Lorentzian, which leads to broadening of the spectral peaks.
It is curious that there is no accuracy gain identiп¬Ғed for the exponential attenuation while there is for the
Gaussian attenuation method. It could be because the exponential function attenuates the trajectory faster
than the Gaussian method, such that the signal is less spread out in k-t-space.
The noise in the background of the reconstructed PSFs is probably a consequence of truncation of
the FID, before the signal has decayed away completely. This could be avoided by sampling for a longer
period of time, which is usually done when using visual identiп¬Ғcation and peak integration for spectral
quantiп¬Ғcation. Some of the noise could be a consequence of the gridding algorithm, but as the reference
image is not interpolated with the gridding algorithm and still contains the noise, this is unlikely. It could
also be caused by the sinc interpolation along the spatial axis.
For the experiments conducted here, the gridding algorithm again averages the signal to a degree such
that the resulting PSFs have a peak amplitude well below the true spectrum, which in turn will result in a
worse SNR than could be obtained.
Alternative ways of approximating the sampling density along the time axis should be evaluated. One
possible solution could be to vary the sampling frequency during acquisition. This would give complete
control over the sampling density along both axes, but might be hampered by hardware limitations. Another
approach could be to record different trajectories for each TR and then use all data acquired when gridding.
This way the sampling density could be approximated by shifting the trajectory oscillations in time between
each TR. However, both methods require extra time spent acquiring data from the same metabolites, which
would increase overall scan time.
Due to the SNR gain obtained when attenuating the oscillations of the trajectory with an exponentially
decaying function and a Gaussian sampling density along the k-axis, along with the fact that no noticeable
loss of gridding accuracy was observed, that particular type of trajectory was used for more realistic
simulations.

5.4
Spatial Localisation of Spectra
The simulation and reconstruction of the spectrum with spatially shifted metabolites that precessed
at realistic frequencies presented a number of problems. The gridding algorithm clearly had problems
successfully interpolating k-t-space such that metabolites precessing at higher frequencies were adequately
reconstructed. It was veriп¬Ғed that this decrease in amplitude was not a consequence of attenuating the
oscillations of the trajectory with time.
The interpolated k-t-space visibly lacked amplitude of the metabolites precessing at higher frequencies,
which is attributed to the choice of RCF. When performing the simulation and interpolation with an RCF,
which would lessen the degree of averaging performed, the reconstruction became visibly more accurate.
The peaks of the metabolites precessing at higher frequencies are signiп¬Ғcantly closer to the reference peaks
than before the adjustment. There is still a bias present, which implies that the gridding algorithm results in
underestimation of peaks spinning at higher frequencies. A possible solution is to approximate this bias,
and correct after interpolation. This suggests that a more thorough investigation of the gridding parameterвҖҷs
effect should be performed, as discussed in Section 5.1.
Apart from the described problems, the simulation of the metabolite spectra illustrates that the designed
trajectory can successfully reconstruct metabolites which are spatially shifted. For in vivo experiments the
metabolites are not present as point sources, but distributed spatially. However, when the spatial PSF of
the imaging procedure is known, then the performance of the imaging procedure on any arbitrary spatial
distribution of metabolites can be estimated. The trajectory provides good separation of the spectral peaks,
but in vivo more metabolite signals would be present in the signal. For example, macromolecules and lipids
would interfere with the spectrum, and artiп¬Ғcially rise or lower the amplitude of the peaks. Moreover, noise
will be present in the signal.
The illustration of the effect of different п¬Ғlter lengths merely serves to conп¬Ғrm the expected outcome.
As discussed earlier, varying the п¬Ғlter lengths determines the width of the spectral peaks and the amount of
noise present in the spectrum. The п¬Ғlter with a short decay time effectively removes almost all of the white
noise, but the peak amplitudes are severely lowered, as signal will also be attenuated. This will complicate
the spectral quantiп¬Ғcation. Furthermore, the spectral peaks are broadened, as the Fourier transform of the
rapidly decaying п¬Ғlter is a broad Lorentzian function. On the contrary, the п¬Ғlter with a long decay time does
not suppress the noise as effectively, and therefore more noise is present in the spectrum. Consequently, the
width of the spectral peaks are narrowed, so they become more well-deп¬Ғned.
The п¬Ғlter with a 100 ms decay time is considered the matched п¬Ғlter and is meant to provide the optimal
balance between spectral peak width and amplitude. However, in vivo the signal is composed of a lot of
different metabolite signals, and thus picking a time constant which will yield the optimal matched п¬Ғlter
is challenging. Therefore, matching the п¬Ғlter in the temporal direction requires beforehand knowledge of
which metabolites that are present in the tissue of interest, or at the very least an idea of which metabolites
the experiment should quantify. In practice, a matched п¬Ғlter would not be a generally matched п¬Ғlter, but
rather a п¬Ғlter matched to adequately quantify a speciп¬Ғc metabolite or a range of metabolites.
5.5
Localisation of Lipid Peaks
The simulation of lipids placed alongside other metabolites yielded some interesting results. However,
before concluding too much on the background of these results, one should bear in mind that the SNR
estimation is a very rough data reduction, and perhaps a more thorough investigation of the SNR of the two
trajectories should be performed.
==PAGE:89==
5.6. DESIGN OF GRADIENT WAVEFORMS
69
Considering the results though, they illustrate that a better spatial separation of lipids from metabolites
can be achieved by starting with large oscillations in k-t-space which gradually decrease with time, as
opposed to a constant amplitude of the oscillations throughout the data acquisition. The attenuated trajectory
will spatially localise the rapidly decaying lipid signals at the beginning, and then subsequently the slowly
decaying metabolite signals will be acquired to retain the SNR. This method is equivalent to enlarging the
voxels with time, which means they encompass more and more signal, as the signal decays away. The use
of the matched п¬Ғlter ensures that the constant noise is suppressed. As described earlier, the voxels should
not be enlarged too much, as this will cause them to contain too much dephasing, which will decrease
the signal. This result is interesting, as it suggests that by designing trajectories this way lipids can be
adequately separated from surrounding tissue without a signiп¬Ғcant loss of SNR. This means that shorter
echo times can be utilised, which in turn will yield a higher SNR. Furthermore, it will make quantiп¬Ғcation
of rapidly decaying metabolites easier. However, it does depend on improvement of the gridding algorithm,
or utilisation of another interpolation method.
In opposition to this sampling strategy, Posse [48] recently suggested that instead of attenuating the
trajectory, one should increase the amplitude of the k-t-space oscillations with time. This is equivalent to
shrinking the voxels with time, such that less noise and less dephasing is encompassed in a voxel. The
idea is that it would increase the signal. It could be described as chasing the signal through k-t-space, and
would certainly avoid the problem with a voxel containing too much dephasing. However, a challenge could
be to not shrink the voxel so much that too little transversal magnetisation is present to quantify a signal
from the voxel. This approach is the exact opposite of the strategy offered in this thesis, but may be viable
nonetheless.
5.6
Design of Gradient Waveforms
The projection of the trajectory and translation to gradient waveforms conп¬Ғrm that it is possible to
implement the trajectory on a scanner with the given hardware limitations. As illustrated by the gradient
waveforms generated by the algorithm, the maximal slew rate is reached for each reversal in k-t-space, while
the gradient waveforms are well within the maximal amplitude range. The short time spacing between the
reversals does not allow the gradient amplitude to reach its maximal capacity. For a classic EPSI sequence,
there would be a gradient plateau part, where the gradient is held constant, such that position probed in
k-t-space travels at a constant speed across the k-axis. For this sequence however, it is possible to identify
that the gradient amplitude is gradually ramped down as the probed position approaches the center of the
k-axis, such that the trajectory is traversed slowly, and a higher sampling density is obtained. As the probed
position travels further away from the center of the k-axis, the gradient is ramped up, such that the sampling
density decreases.
The projected trajectory is very similar to the input trajectory at the central regions of k-t-space, but at
the edges, there is an increase of sampling density. This implies that the approximation of the gradient ramp
time done in the trajectory design is not sufп¬Ғcient. As the results show, the projected trajectory actually
retains the sampling density very well for the areas surrounding the center of the k-axis. This should ensure
that the simulated SNR gain is also possible to obtain on a real scanner.
5.7
Extension to Three Dimensions
The extension of the trajectory to three dimensions, or two spatial ones, means that it can acquire
signal to reconstruct a three-dimensional image where each voxel contains its own spectrum. This enables
advanced spatial localisation of the metabolite signals. The rotational approach seems viable with regards

to retaining the sampling density along the spatial frequency axes. However, the scanner implementation
might be complicated by the fact that several samples have the same time stamp. The designed trajectory is
thus not possible to acquire in a single TR, but rather several excitations must be utilised.
The gridding of the three-dimensional trajectory is not handled in this thesis. However, an approach
could be to simply use the existing gridding on algorithm on two-dimensional planes of the acquired signals
and then reconstruct the last dimension using a back-projection. Another approach would be to simply
extend the gridding algorithm to interpolate in three dimensions. This should not be an extremely difп¬Ғcult
venture, as the convolution kernel could simply be extended from an ellipse to an ellipsoid and samples
could be evaluated in three dimensions rather than in two. However, the errors caused by the gridding
algorithm would be increased when interpolating higher dimensional data.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The main purpose of the project was to investigate techniques for optimisation of gradient trajectories
for fast spectroscopic magnetic resonance imaging. More speciп¬Ғcally, the purpose of the project was
to investigate the effect of matching the sampling density to the applied п¬Ғlter on the SNR. A secondary
objective was to develop an algorithm which could design k-t-space trajectories with varying sampling
density along both dimensions, thereby enabling evaluation of the main purpose.
Based on the theoretical derivations, it was estimated that SNR gains were obtainable by matching the
sampling density to the п¬Ғlter. An algorithm that can design k-t-space trajectories with varying sampling
density along the k-axis was built. Trajectories with both uniform and Gaussian sampling density were
designed and the spatial SNR gain was conп¬Ғrmed by п¬Ғltering with a Gaussian п¬Ғlter. Furthermore it conп¬Ғrmed
that by applying a Gaussian п¬Ғlter, the resulting PSF would have the shape of a Gaussian. Subsequently, the
sampling density along the time axis was approximated to the matched п¬Ғlter by attenuating the trajectory
with an exponentially decaying function, where the time constant was matched to the decay of the metabolite
signal. This resulted in an SNR gain.
Thereafter, the attenuated trajectory was sampled critically and used to reconstruct a spatially shifted
spectra, which contained a realistic distribution of metabolites. The trajectory reconstructed the spectra at the
correct spatial location. However, metabolites precessing at higher frequencies were visibly underestimated,
which results in inaccurate quantiп¬Ғcation of metabolite concentrations. Adjustments of the gridding
algorithm parameters improved the reconstruction, but did not remove the bias completely. An investigation
of the separation of lipid peaks from metabolite peaks was also performed. This resulted in conп¬Ғrmation
that the attenuated trajectory could be used to double the spatial resolution, as compared to a non-attenuated
trajectory, without any registered loss of SNR. Subsequently, the attenuated k-t-space trajectory was
translated to gradient waveforms to demonstrate that the trajectory is realisable in practice. The projection
algorithm was able to design gradient waveforms that retained the sampling density along both dimensions.
Finally, the designed k-t-space trajectory was extended to acquire signal in three dimensions, that being
two spatial and one spectral, by rotation around the temporal axis. An acquisition method was also proposed.
No simulations or further analysis of the three-dimensional trajectory was performed.
The п¬Ғndings presented in this thesis are applicable to several п¬Ғelds within MRI. With improvements
in hardware limitations the described techniques has the potential to both improve scan time and the
quality of data acquisition. For clinical implementation practicable scan times is a strict demand and thus
shortening scan times will likely broaden the clinical scope of MRSI. Furthermore the application of arbitrary
trajectories throughout k-t-space is a viable approach for many п¬Ғelds within MRI. It could be utilised to
ensure high spatial resolution, or perhaps change the FOV during a measurement if circumstances demand it.
Limitations are dictated by hardware restrictions, as well as the need of a satisfactory interpolation routine

The concept of optimising the gradient trajectories to match the sampling density to the п¬Ғlter remains a
promising approach within the п¬Ғeld of fast magnetic resonance spectroscopic imaging.

function k_train = greiser_algorithm(NA,sigma,kmax)
%greiser_algorithm - Generation of sample train of k-coordinates as
%dictated by a Gaussian weighting function. It is a computational
%implementation of the algorithm described by Greiser et. al. (2003)
%"Efficient k-Space Sampling by Density-Weighted Phase-Encoding"
%
% Syntax:
[k_train] = greiser_algorithm(NA,sigma,kmax)
%
% Inputs:
%
NA - Number of samples to place along k-axis
%
sigma - Width of Gaussian weighting function
%
kmax - Maximal k-coordinate to sample
%
% Output:
%
k_train - Generated sample positions
% Author: Andreas Nexmann Nielsen
% email: andreas.nexmann@gmail.com
% January 2015; Last revision: 24-January-2016
%% Initialize weighting function
% Sample k-vector for 1e6 points to insure high resolution
k_length = 1e6;
kvec = (0:kmax/k_length:kmax)';
% Gaussian sampling density function
Dk = 1/(sqrt(2*pi)*sigma*erf(kmax/(sqrt(2)*sigma))) ...
*exp(-kvec.^2/(2*sigma^2));
% Normalize to obtain NA samples
Dk = Dk/sum(Dk)*NA;
%% Generate sample train
% Sum of Dk
Dk_sum = Dk(1);
% Initialize variables
k_samples = zeros(k_length+1,1);
k_samples(1) = 1;
% Loop through kvec and find sample points
for k=2:k_length
if(Dk_sum >= 1)
k_samples(k) = 1;
Dk_sum = Dk_sum-1;
end;
Dk_sum = Dk_sum+Dk(k);
end;
% Ensure that k=kmax is sampled if needed
