Abstract
This thesis investigates crack growth in 2D problems. Through topology optimiza-
tion it is possible to manipulate certain parameters, which have great inﬂuence on
crack propagation. When a crack is spotted in a structure or machine element, it is
sometimes possible to repair the part temporarily by increasing the stiﬀness around
the fracture with a patch. However, this may not always be an eﬃcient solution,
which leads to the motivation of this study.
First, a Finite Element code is developed utilizing isoparmetric elements. The anal-
ysis is set up to simulate a cracked specimen and measure the energy release rate
at the crack tip. This is done by introducing the simple stress intensity factors and
by implementing J-integral into the code. The Interaction-integral utilized as well
in order to measure the distribution in the separate stress ﬁeld.
The topology optimization will be based on the SIMP material model and using the
optimality criteria as well as the method of moving asymptotes, a series of optimized
patches will be designed. Three diﬀerent objective functions will be investigated;
Compliance, J-integral and the crack face displacement.
To ensure a patch like design, the parametrized model is expanded to contain a base
plate with the design domain on top, but still only be in 2D. Passive elements are
further introduced in order to ensure the patch is well away from boundaries and
load eﬀects.
It is concluded, that when optimizing the patch for compliance, the energy at the
crack tip is reduced signiﬁcantly. The results are almost as good as the ones obtained
for the J-integral. However, the robustness of the patches designed for compliance
are signiﬁcantly better. The results from the crack face displacement shows a small
decrease of to the energy release rate.
Keywords: Domain integral · Energy release rate · Stress intensity factors · Topol-
ogy optimization · Patch Designs · Mechanisms design

Preface
This report represents the work of a master thesis conducted at the Technical Univer-

of Fracture Development using Topology Optimization” documents the work carried

Problem Description
Failure of engineering parts are always an undesirable event since human lives or eco-
nomic losses may be at risk, while interfering with the ability to use the machine or
services (William D. Callister and Rethwisch [29]). A majority of the observed fail-
ures are often due to cyclic loading, causing the parts to experience fatigue, resulting
in cracks starting to propagate in the part. This failure occurs slow, compared to a
brittle or ductile fracture. This makes these types of failures detectable by regular
inspections and makes it possible prevent a complete failure, due to the time to
react and counteract the crack propagation. This type of failure is approximated
to be the cause of 90% of all the failures in metal, polymers and ceramics (William
D. Callister and Rethwisch [29]).
The initial cracks caused by fatigue can temporarily be stopped by either drilling a
hole (Kang et al. [12]) or using a patch repair (Davis and Bond [9]). When using
the patch repair, a large uniform patch is often applied to strengthen the cracked
area. In modern days, fabrication often tries to reduce cost in order to maximize the
proﬁt. This makes the idea of a weight optimized patch that is designed speciﬁcally
to a certain task very attractive. In order to save material, the method of topology
optimization will be utilized. This makes it possible to optimize the patch with
respect to a given objective function, while keeping the material usage as low as
possible.
For fractures in isotropic material, the J-integral is a good and reliable measure of
how much energy that is present at the crack tip. Hence by combining the J-integral
with topology optimization, a patch that will minimize the propagation of the crack,
while using a small amount of material, is investigated. To the authors knowledge
there have been no attempts to use topology optimization to design a patch that will
directly decrease the energy at the crack tip. Kang et al. [12] optimizes a structure
using compliance and in areas where cracks a most likely to appear, they set up a
small domain where the optimization is based on the J-integral. This will make the
structure less sensitive to a fractures at these areas and make them more reliable.
Their combination of compliance and J-integral results in a mixed optimization for-
mulation, but still with the compliance as the main objective function and not a
truly optimization of the energy at the crack tip.

The Finite Element Method
The ﬁnite element method (FEM), also called ﬁnite element analysis (FEA), is a
widely used method, which principals are the fundamentals for many commercial
softwares today. The point is to obtain a numerical solution of ﬁeld problems by
utilizing a set of individual ﬁnite elements, which can be seen as small pieces of a
structure. The ﬁnite elements are connected at points, deﬁned as nodes and the
assemblage of elements is called the ﬁnite element structure (Cook et al. [8]). The
particular arrangement of the ﬁnite elements is deﬁned as the mesh. The FE mesh is
represented by a system of algebraic equations to be solved for unknowns at nodes.
The value of the ﬁeld quantity is deﬁned as nodal unknowns and depending on the
element type. It is important to note that the solution for nodal quantities, when
combined with the assumed ﬁeld in any given element, completely determine the
spatial variation of the ﬁeld in that element (Cook et al. [8]). The ﬁeld quantity
over the entire structure is approximated element by element meaning that the FE
solution is not exact. In general, the solution can be improved by using more ele-
ments to represent the structure.
The principle of virtual work can be utilized to formulate the ﬁnite element formu-
lation. By use of the the virtual work principle it is possible to establish a relation
between variations in internal and external work. This is based on displacement
variations from which the residual equations can be derived.

Isoparametric Elements
Rectangular elements are easy to formulate, but are usually impossible to use, since
it is diﬃcult to mesh a complicated geometry using only rectangular elements. It
is especially a problem if the mesh is graded from coarse to ﬁne in order to make a
detailed study of critical regions (Cook et al. [8]).
This problem can be solved by introducing isoparametric elements. These elements
can be non-rectangular and can even have curved sides. This study will utilize bi-
linear quadrilateral elements, also called Q4 elements (Cook et al. [8]). When using
the Q4 element it is important to note that the Q4 element cannot exhibit pure
bending. When bent, it displays shear strain as well as the expected bending strain,
Cook et al. [8] calls this behavior shear locking.

Fracture Mechanics
This section will present and discuss the theory of fracture mechanics used in the FE
model. First a brief introduction to the basic fracture modes will be presented ([1]).
Next, the J-integral developed by Rice [18] which makes it possible to investigate
further crack growth will be discussed. Furthermore, the numerical implementation
of the J-integral (Shih et al. [19]) is utilized in order to change the J-integral from
a line integral to a surface integral. At last, the Interaction-integral is presented
and implemented into the numeric code, in order to make it possible to evaluate the
individual contributions of stress intensity factor in an arbitrary stress ﬁeld.
3.1
Fracture Modes
Figure 3.1a shows the three basic modes of crack initiation and growth. The ﬁrst
and most studied fracture mode, Mode-I, is also the simplest. In Mode-I, the load
is applied normal to the crack face, giving it the name of the opening mode. In the
second mode, Mode-II, the load is applied parallel in-plane, leading to a sliding crack
face. The third mode of fracture, Mode-III, is deﬁned as the out-of-plane shear (or
tearing mode). In this case, the load is applied out of the plane and parallel to the
crack surfaces. As the numeric analysis is in 2D, only the two ﬁrst fracture modes
are considered further.
The stress ﬁeld in linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) is governed by the sin-
gularity 1/√r at the crack tip where r is the distance to the next point of evaluation.
In order to scale the stress singularity to the modes, a parameter called the stress
intensity factor Ki is considered. The stress intensity factor is denoted by a sub-

5
Topology Optimization
This Chapter will discuss and present the theory and the implementation of topology
optimization. In modern times the focus is often set to reduce cost in an eﬀort to
increase the proﬁt. This has resulted in an increased focus on reducing especially
the use of material in products. To achieve this the topology optimization is used.
First the topology optimization method will be deﬁned and explained. Next, the
density approach will be described which will be used to distribute the material in
the design domain. The sensitivities in this study are all found utilizing the adjoint
sensitivity analysis, which will be shown here. A brief look at the solvers used in
the optimization are then presented and, ﬁnally, this chapter will present a mesh-
independency ﬁlter developed by Sigmund [20], which is used to avoid checkerboard
structures.
5.1

a number of diﬀerent directions (Sigmund and Maute [25]). Munthe [15] argues that
the reason for choosing the topology optimization method is because it will not only
optimize the topology but also the shape and size of the structural members at the
same time. Thus, this method often results in better designs compared to only using
shape or size optimization.

5.4
Optimization
In Figure 5.1 the step called Optimization, is where the design values are redis-
tributed.
This study, utilizes the OC solver (Bendsøe [3]) and the MMA solver
(Svanberg [26]).
5.4.1
Optimality Criteria
The Optimality Criteria (OC), is one of the simplest optimization procedures. The
OC was developed and described by Bendsøe [3], but was already discussed in [4]
where a method was sought that was as simple as the sizing problem, but would
use the approach related to the shape design optimization.
The OC allows the
density to take any value between 0 and 1, instead of only one of these (Bendsøe
and Kikuchi [4]). Furthermore, the OC solver is a very eﬃcient solver as it updates
each design variable independent of the rest (Bendsøe and Sigmund [5]). The only
real drawback with the OC solver lies in the restriction of constraints, as it can only
handle problems with one constraint. The numeric procedure for the OC solver used
in this thesis can be seen in Appendix H.
5.4.2
Method of Moving Assymptotes
In 1987, Svanberg [26] developed a method, that in contrast to the Optimality Crite-
ria, is more ﬂexible and general, called the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA).
The MMA can handle all kind of design variables and constraints provided only
that the derivatives of the constraint functions with respect to the design variables
could be calculated. The MMA solver will in general solve the standard topology
optimization problem using less iterations, but use a slightly increased CPU-time
pr. iteration (Sigmund [22]).
This method presented by Svanberg [26] is based on the assumption, that for me-
chanical problems the solution will follow a convex function. In order to obtain the
minima or maxima of the objective function, a upper and lower asymptote is deﬁned
and compared to the calculated value of the objective function. By adjusting the
asymptotes and updating {x} in an iterative process, the minima or maxima can
be found.

7.2.1
Penalty factor
When using the SIMP approach, the relation between the density design variable
and the material property is given by Eq. (5.3), where p is the penalization param-
eter. The advantage of the SIMP formulation is seen in the possibility of aiming
at solutions of solid or void elements (Mlejnek [14]). Working with the SIMP, one
should choose p > 1, which will make the porous densities unfavorable, meaning
the extra stiﬀness obtained is small compared to the cost in volume (Bendsøe and
Sigmund [5]). This means that when a higher p is used optimized structures will
contain much less porous material as it becomes to expensive. Sigmund and Maute
[25] states that by choosing p too low or too high the optimizer can converge to a
local minima. Bendsøe and Sigmund [5] found that in order to obtain ”0-1” designs,
p ≥3 is usually required. However it should be noted that the proper choice of the
penalization factor p strongly depends on the physical problem being solved and
that the penalization eﬀect only works in the presence of a volume constraint or
some other constraint that indirectly limits volume (Sigmund and Maute [25]).

Optimization of Mechanism Design
This chapter will investigate the structural optimization problem for a compliant
mechanisms. The mechanism can be optimized to minimize the crack face displace-
ment, which intuitively makes good sense, when trying to reduce crack propagation.
This study will utilize the structural topology optimization approach developed by
Bendsøe and Sigmund [5]. First the compliant mechanism is studied for a simple
plate, hence a model only containing the design domain. Next, to obtain a patch-like
mechanism, the model will be equipped with both a base plate and the design do-
main. At last, these results will be compared to the results obtained for optimization
of compliance and the J-integral.

Limitations and Future Work
This chapter will discuss the limitations of the parametrized model. Furthermore,
it will discussed how this study could be improved and further expanded, with both
the existing fracture analysis and by enhancing it.
10.1
Limitations
When modeling the real world, it is always necessary to simplify it by using diﬀerent
assumptions. Hence the limitations of a model often lies in the choice of simpliﬁca-
tions and assumptions. In the model established for this study, isotropic material
was assumed. This resulted in an energy release rate (J-integral) without the capa-
bility to handle graded material, thus the J-integral can only be evaluated on the
base material. As a consequence, it is assumed that crack growth is not possible in
the patch material.
Cohesive elements were introduced in order to make the optimization able to dis-
tribute patch material on top of the crack. When utilizing cohesive elements to glue
the two faces together, it is necessary to make the cohesive element stiﬀness go to-
wards inﬁnity, in order to ensure the correct material response. However, since this
thesis models a 3D problem in 2D, it do not make sense to have cohesive elements
with inﬁnite stiﬀness. If this was the case, the crack in the basis material would
be completely removed. Nevertheless, since the cohesive elements have the same
stiﬀness as the patch material, the material response of the patch is weaker, since
the cohesive elements are deforming under the load as well.
The simulation utilized passive elements in order to make a design domain for the
patch. This was mainly done in order to minimize the eﬀects from the boundary
conditions and load forces. However, when this design constraint was introduced,
the optimization moved all of the energy outside the patch area, making the patch
edge the new eﬀective crack tip. As a consequence, the J-integral was evaluated in
a larger radius than the design domain radius in order to catch the energy of the
new eﬀective crack tip. Nevertheless, since the eﬀective crack tip is relocated, so
should the J-integral. One may argue, that since the patch optimization leads to
multiple crack tips, the J-integral should be evaluated multiple places through the
optimization.

10.2
Further Work
This thesis have been a prove of concept, and it is clear that topology optimization
using the SIMP approach is a strong tool that can be utilized in order to optimize
structures against crack growth. The obtained results raises the possibility to com-
bine the topology optimization with the fracture mechanics in other areas as well.
Some of these possibilities are listed below along with improvements to the current
patch optimization.
• Instead of adding material to the plate in form of a patch, it would be inter-
esting to study if it is possible to lower the J-integral by removing material
from the model. This is a technique that is widely used in the industry, since
drilling a hole, at the crack tip, is a fast and eﬃcient way of lowering the stress
intensity factors.
• In this thesis it is assumed that the patch is perfectly glued to the base ma-
terial. However, it will be very interesting to obtain a patch design which
utilizes bolt connections instead. This would further lead to design where the
distributed patch material needs to be connected to each other. Opposite the
results obtained in this thesis, using cohesive elements.
• The J-integral can be formulated so it can handle graded materials. So that
the J-integral can be evaluated in the base material and the patch material at
once.
• By introducing a 3D model many of the limitations discussed previously can
be removed. There would be no need of cohesive elements, since the patch
material can be a separate mesh on top of the base material.
• It is possible to estimate further crack growth by using Crack Tip Opening
Displacement, (CTOD). However, this method is not very accurate using linear
elastic fracture mechanics, thus a nonlinear model must be implemented into
the FE analysis.
• Through heat treatment, it is possible to design a patch with prescribed dis-
placements.
By use of topology optimization it may be possible to design
patches that tries to close the crack faces or lowering the J-integral when
cooled.

Discussion and Concluding Remarks
A ﬁnite element program capable of determine the energy release rate in a cracked
specimen was developed. It was further capable of establishing the composition of
the applied stress ﬁeld to determine the purity of the stress ﬁelds. The program
was validated using an analytic stress ﬁeld applied on a cracked structure, obtaining
the same ﬁelds and energy release as the scientiﬁc literature. When expanding the
program to utilize the topology optimization, cohesive elements was implemented in
order to have a patch that could cover the crack in the base material. The following
conclusions are achieved by using this numeric program and diﬀerent parameter
studies.
First a patch was developed using the compliance formulation and it was found that
the increased stiﬀness had a positive impact on the energy at the crack tip. In
order to enhance this eﬀect the cohesive elements was introduced. To ensure these
elements have the right stiﬀness, a small study was conducted. Here it was found,
that to obtain a patch which can deform over the crack the stiﬀness should be like
the rest of the patch material. When utilizing the cohesive elements a reduction
in average of 81% was found, while the compliance in average was reduced with
41%. In order to get a better reduction in the energy release rate, the J-integral was
used as the objective function in the optimization. Here the energy at the crack tip
was reduced with 82% in average, while the compliance was increased with 120% in
average. It concludes that the energy is better reduced, but the stiﬀness is greatly
decreased when using the J-integral as objective function.
On the basis of these ﬁndings it can be concluded that to obtain the best patch
designs, one should choose the compliance optimization, as this will result in a
robust patch, while reducing the energy at the crack tip. It should be noted that
when utilizing these optimizations it was found that the crack tip was eﬀectively
moved to the patch edge and as the evaluation point of the J-integral did not, the
optimization actually just moved the energy out of sight, when using the J-integral
as objective function. It furhter meant that the new crack tip was located at the
patch edge, which was not included in the J-integral. If the J-integral was moved
along with the energy one could end up with other patch designs and conclusions.
At the end, a mechanism design was generated to test out if it was possible to reduce
the energy at the crack tip, by reducing the displacement of the crack faces. It was
found that the reduction of the displacement only resulted in an energy reduction
of 53%, which was signiﬁcantly less than the other patches.
