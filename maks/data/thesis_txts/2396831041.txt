Abstract 
The human cytomegalovirus (HCMV) causes mostly asymptomatic infections for immune-
competent individuals, however it causes severe and even deadly consequences for immune-compromised 
patients. Immuno-naive pre-natals and newborns, patients taking immuno-suppressive drugs, organ 
transplantation and surgery patients, cancer chemotherapy, hemo-dialysis as well as HIV-infected patients 
are at high risk of developing an HCMV disease. The currently available drugs suffer from toxicity, 
resistance issues as well as not being able to target the latent viral infection. Even though there is high 
demand and interest for novel drugs against HCMV, the task remains elusive for chemical and 
biopharmaceuticals drugs as well as vaccines.  
In this thesis, drug developments against HCMV and more specifically its viral G protein-
coupled receptor US28 are reported: a focused small molecule compound library was screened for binding 
to the US28 receptor and for antiviral activity; a fusion toxin protein’s production was optimized, up-scaled 
and the activity was characterized by binding to the US28 receptor, in vitro cell killing and viral plaque 
reduction activity. 
Several chemical ligands were identified which displace 125I-CCL2 and 125I-CCL4 radioligands 
when binding to US28 and some were identified to exhibit antiviral properties. The insights from their 
chemical structures can be used as a first step in the optimization of specific antiviral ligands. The protein 
production of the described fusion toxin protein against HCMV was optimized to have higher yield and 
purity with production up-scaling in mind. The optimization included lowering of protein concentration 
during refolding to 1 mg/mL, changing the first chromatography step to anion exchange chromatography, 
purification buffer pH lowered to 6.5, additional salt was added for protein stability, endotoxin removal 
method was introduced and optimized. 277 mg of F49A-FTP was purified which in functional experiments 
has matched the previously reported binding, killing and antiviral IC50 values. The next step of the FTP 
development should entail in vivo studies in different model systems to provide a convincing proof of 
concept for human treatment.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The human cytomegalovirus (HCMV) overview 
The human cytomegalovirus is an enveloped double stranded DNA virus with a genome of 
around 236 kilobase pairs [1]. By nomenclature it belongs to the herpesvirus family, beta-herpesvirus 
subfamily and the cytomegalovirus genus [2]. HCMV can be found throughout all geographic locations and 
socioeconomic groups at about 90% infection rate between adults [3]. The virus spreads by saliva, urine, 
blood and breast milk and the primary target cells for infection are monocytes and epithelial cells. After 
infection by HCMV, it remains latently in the body and can be reactivated any time. HCMV infections in 
immune-competent hosts are usually asymptomatic, while they are most severe and dangerous in immune-
compromised patients [2].  
1.1.1 HCMV life cycle 
HCMV replicates in endothelial cell culture at a rate of about 3-4 days [4]. The virus 
expresses its genes in a time-controlled manner. Immediate early genes are responsible for the regulation 
of transcription, followed by early genes which process viral DNA replication and further transcriptional 
regulation [5]. The viral late genes typically code its structural proteins. HCMV encodes its own 
functional  polymerase (UL54), but uses the host’s for its transcription [6].  
The first stage of HCMV infection is cell entry and it involves distinct pathways and different 
glycoprotein complexes (see Figure 1) [7]. The two main different pathways are entry into fibroblasts which 
involve fusion with the plasma membrane and requires the gH/gL complex, while entry into epithelial, 
endothelial and monocyte cells involves the process of macropinocytosis and fusion with endosomes which 
requires a larger complex - gH/gL/UL128-131. There is a variety of HCMV receptors described, but it is likely 
that other receptors also mediate entry of the wild type HCMV into epithelial, endothelial cells and 
monocytes/macrophages [7].

After entering the cell the early genes are transcribed and come into play. HCMV infection 
disrupts the ND10 (nuclear domain 10) sites, disperses the associated proteins and a subset of HCMV 
genome is deposited at those sites and provides the template for early transcription [8].   
Blocking of the host cell’s apoptosis is achieved by the two viral immediate early proteins [8]. 
The first one, vMIA, blocks the activation of caspase 8 and cytochrome c release into the cytoplasm [9] 
while the second, vICA, acts further upstream to block cleavage of pro-caspase 8 and its subsequent 
activation [10]. 
Viral DNA lytic replication depends on the oriLyt origin sequence [11]. Some of the important 
genes expressed early are UL54, the viral DNA polymerase; UL44, the polymerase processivity factor; UL57, 
a single-stranded DNA binding protein; UL97, the viral kinase; and three proteins that comprise a helicase-
primase complex: UL70, UL102, and UL105 [12][13]. 
After the DNA is synthesized it has to be cleaved into genome-length segments and packaged 
into preformed capsids. Four early–late products (major capsid protein, minor capsid protein, minor capsid 
binding protein, and small capsid protein) are required for capsid formation. Capsid assembly occurs in the 
nucleus of the host cell by employing the capsid shell proteins as well as a precursor of the assembly 
protein - a protease (UL80) [14]. 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Once viral DNA has been packaged, the nucleocapsid interacts with the nucleus membrane 
proteins in a complex two-stage envelopment and egress process which starts in the nucleus and leads to 
the virion release by exocytosis to the plasma membrane [14].  
The final stage of the life cycle is the secondary envelopment which occurs in the cytoplasm. 
Once the final envelopment has occurred, the mature virion is transported inside a vesicle to the cell 
surface for release by fusion of an exocytic vesicle with the plasma membrane [15]. 
1.1.2 HCMV immune evasion and latency 
HCMV infection generates a primary immune response in the host - natural killer cells as 
well as humoral immunity are induced, which includes neutralizing antibodies specific for several viral 
proteins. Additionally, both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells are induced for a very broad range of viral proteins. 
For an immune-competent individual this response effectively eliminates the replicating virus, however 
HCMV always managed to establish latency, therefore it is never completely cleared. 
HCMV encodes multiple immune evasion mechanisms which have been shown to work 
potently in vitro. However, as mentioned earlier, in vivo these immunoevasins fail to help the virus 
escape the host anti-viral immune response [16]. Therefore, it is thought that the function of these 
immunoevasins is actually more specific to the latent virus infection phase - to stop the immune system 
from targeting the latent virus efficiently, thus resulting in viral persistence which is one of the reasons 
HCMV infection relapse happens after successful treatment with the currently available drugs [16]. 
Some of the most interesting immunoevasins are the immunosuppressive IL-10 
homologue - UL111A [17], which acts as a powerful inhibitor of Th1 cytokines and also inhibits 
inflammatory cytokines produced by monocytes and macrophages [18]. The viral G protein-coupled 
receptor (GPCR) US28 acts as a receptor for host inflammatory cytokines, which scavenges, internalizes 
and destroys the cytokines. A number of other HCMV encoded genes such as pp65, US2, US3, US6, and 
US11 can interfere with both major histocompatibility complexes one and two, thereby reducing the 
effectiveness of CD4+ and CD8+T-cells [19] as well as other T-cell signalling [20]. 
1.1.3 Different HCMV strains 
The next-generation sequencing technologies have allowed more extensive studies of the 
wild-type HCMV. They have revealed that there is a large variation detected in the different isolated viruses 
[21]. When HCMV research had first begun the very first clinical isolate was termed AD169 [22]. It has been 
the most extensively studied strain, but it was later found out that HCMV strains do not propagate well in 
vitro, but quickly acquire mutations in the UL128 locus and often the UL/b′ region [21]. The Towne HCMV

strain was studied extensively for use in vaccination, however it could not create a strong immune-
protection for the vaccinated patients [23]. 
The Merlin strain was isolated and cloned into a bacterial artificial chromosome after 3 
passages in cell culture [24]. Few genes that were lost in the passaging were restored by gene engineering. 
It is thought to be the closest resemblance to wild-type HCMV strain and is used as the standard in 
diagnostic laboratories [25].  
1.2 G protein-coupled receptors  
The G protein–coupled receptors constitute a large protein family of receptors which 
function by detecting molecules outside the cell and activating internal signalling pathways [26]. The 
receptors can be bound and activated by numerous different ligands such as light-sensitive compounds, 
pheromones, hormones and neurotransmitters. They can vary in size from small molecules (and chemical 
drugs) to peptides and large complexes. Signalling and therefore GPCRs are important in many diseases, 
thus they have been the most drug targeted protein class and approximate to about 34% of all currently 
marketed drugs [27].

– phosphatidyl inositol 4,5-biphosphate.

When an agonist binds to the GPCR it causes a conformational change, which allows a 
subsequent activation of an associated G protein. This in turn results in an exchange of the guanosine 
diphosphate bound to the α-subunit of the G protein for a guanosine triphosphate (GTP) molecule. The G 
protein's α subunit, together with the bound GTP, can then dissociate from the βγ subunits to further affect 
intracellular signalling proteins or target functional proteins [28].  
There are four main G protein-mediated signalling pathways determined by the four sub-
classes of Gα proteins which have slightly different sequences from each other -Gαs, Gαi, Gαq, and Gα12 (see 
Figure 2). Even though most GPCRs can activate more than one Gα-subtype, they have a higher preference 
for one subtype than the other. The preference can by bypassed by different ligands, regulation (such as 
phosphorylation) or the availability of one type of proteins more than the other, however regardless these 
nuances literature defines GPCRs by their most obvious G protein preference [29]. 
The Gαs pathway induces the enzyme adenylate cyclase (AC) which in turn generates cyclic 
adenosine monophosphates (cAMP) which act as secondary signalling molecules. In contrast, the activity of 
the Gαi pathway inhibits AC from generating cAMP. Thus, these two different Gα subunits counteract each 
other. The concentration of cAMP determines the activity of various ion channels as well as protein kinase 
A thus sending the signal further to the cell [28]. 
The Gαq pathway activates the is phospholipase C (PLC), which in turn cleaves and activates 
the phosphatidylinositol 4,5-biphosphate into its messenger molecules - inositol (1,4,5) trisphosphate (IP3) 
and diacylglycerol (DAG). The IP3 molecule acts on IP3 receptors which are on the membrane of the 
endoplasmic reticulum and releases Ca2+ ions, while DAG activates the protein kinase C, which in turn signal 
molecules further down the line. 
The Gα12 pathway first activates three Guanine Exchange Factors, which in turn can 
allosterically activate the Rho GTPase. Once GTP diffuses to Rho it can further activate signalling through 
the Rho-kinase [30].  
The Gαi-coupled GPCRs can also work through the Gβγ signalling. In this pathway the Gβγ 
proteins disassociate from the membrane-bound receptor and can inhibit the Gα signalling as well as 
enable different ion channels, such K+ or Ca2+ channels, as well as some isoforms of AC and PLC [31]. 
In order to turn off the GPCR response or adapt to a persistent stimulus the arrestin pathway 
comes into play. First a G protein-coupled receptor kinase phosphorylates the receptor which allows for the 
arrestin protein to bind. Upon binding, the arrestin blocks the G protein-mediated signalling and moreover 
induces internalization of the receptor through clathrin coated pits. The internalized receptor can be 
directed to either degradation by lysosomes or recycled back into the plasma membrane [32].

One example of a human GPCR is the CX3C chemokine receptor 1 (CX3CR1) which is also 
known as the fractalkine receptor [33]. The fractalkine ligand CX3CL1 is involved in the adhesion and 
migration of leukocytes [34]. This receptor  is a 373 amino acid protein and is most commonly found in 
neural cells and in subsets of leukocytes [35][36]. 
1.2.1 HCMV’s viral GPCR - US28 
HCMV encodes three chemokine receptor homologs: UL33, US27, and US28 and additionally 
UL78 which does not have homology to chemokine receptors [37], but seems to have a GPCR structure. 
US28 recognizes a broad-spectrum of chemokines such as the CC-chemokines, CCL2-5, as well as XCL1 and 
CX3CL1 [38]. An interesting feature of US28 is that chemokines are recognized differently and the binding 
of CX3CL1  is superior to the other CC-chemokines, and was shown that it can only be displaced by itself 
(CX3CL1) and not by the CCLs, whereas the opposite is not the case [38].  
While endogenous GPCR signalling is quite specific through few intracellular pathways and 
has low constitutive activity, the viral US28 acts through a variety of different signalling pathways e.g. the 
production of inositol phosphate through Gαq activation, activating further downstream signalling in a 
constitutive manner, etc. [39]. On top of that, US28 is constitutively internalized and located intracellularly 
in the membranes of intracellular organelles such as late endosomes/lysosomes and multivesicular bodies, 
which are preassemblies of the HCMV virions [17][18]. As 
mentioned earlier, the broadspectred chemokine binding and 
diverse signalling properties of US28 has led to the 
suggestion that it helps the virus to evade the immune 
system by functioning as a chemokine scavenger [38].

1.3 HCMV caused diseases, diagnostics and treatment 
The severity of the HCMV infection varies with the age and immune-competence of the 
individual [2]. Healthy individuals can usually fight HCMV asymptomatically and only rarely the primary 
HCMV infection will inflict an infectious mononucleosis-like syndrome, with prolonged fever and mild 
hepatitis. However, the biggest threat of HCMV is when the host is unable to mount an adequate immune 
response. HCMV infection can lead to death in immune-compromised patients, including organ transplant 
recipients, patients undergoing hemo-dialysis, patients undergoing chemotherapy, HIV infected patients 
and others [2].  
The main diseases associated to HCMV infection are hepatitis, retinitis, colitis, pneumonitis, 
esophagitis, polyradiculopathy, transverse myelitis, and encephalitis [2]. 
For successful prevention of HCMV in immune-compromised patients it is extremely 
important to diagnose the HCMV infection [2]. Blood and bronchioalveolar lavage specimens have the best 
predictive value for severe infection by using the cell culture or rapid diagnostic methods. The real-time 
polymerase chain reaction (real-time PCR) assay is currently the gold standard for diagnosing HCMV 
infection. More rapid diagnostic methods are being increasingly used for the diagnosis of HCMV infection, 
which include rapid culture methods such as the detection of early antigen fluorescent foci test, detection 
of HCMV antigen from polymorphonuclear cells, and the detection of HCMV DNA by PCR from white blood 
cells or serum [43]. Other possible diagnostic methods are histopathology, tissue immunofluorescence, 
electron microscopy, enzyme linked immunosorbent assay for HCMV antigen in urine, however due to 
reliability and convenience they are currently used less often [44]. One risk associated with diagnostics is 
that the primary therapy resistant HCMV strains are usually only spotted after the therapy has failed to 
induce a positive response [45]. 
This thesis aims to investigate novel therapeutics for the treatment of HCMV infections. The 
most obvious initial target patients for such novel treatment options are recipients of organ 
transplantation, but in principle novel antivirals are needed for all patients with severe HCMV infections.  
For the treatment of HCMV in organ transplantation five main strategies are employed. They 
are used in different combinations of drugs as well as combination of strategies depending on the patient 
and the transplantation or therapeutic procedure to be carried out [2]: 
1. True prophylaxis - before and after transplantation 
2. Delayed prophylaxis – before acute infection but after graft rejection 
3. Suppression - if HCMV is diagnosed at any peripheral site 
4. Pre-emptive treatment - if HCMV is diagnosed from systemic sites 
5. Treatment of the established disease 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
Prophylaxis and pre-emptive treatment have become routine as they have shown to reduce 
the incidence of HCMV [45]. Treatment of an active infection and especially HCMV pneumonitis is very 
difficult and often unsuccessful. According to medical treatment guidelines, the prophylaxis options include 
intravenous ganciclovir (GCV), oral valganciclovir, and high doses of oral valacyclovir. The current gold-
standard drug for treatment is intravenous GCV, while foscarnet is an alternative choice for the GCV 
resistant cases. Anti-HCMV immunoglobulin is often added as a complimentary treatment [45]. 
In organ transplantations a number of factors increases the likelihood of HCMV infection and 
makes the treatment more difficult [45]. With the high prevalence of HCMV most often the donor has a 
latent infection which can reoccur when given immunosuppression, especially if the organ recipient is 
seronegative. Combination of other therapies such as OKT3/ATG, MMF, Alemtuzumab or mTOR inhibitors 
also increase the risk of acute HCMV infection. Acute graft rejection, co-infection of different viruses (e.g. 
HHV-6 or different genotype HCMV) or weaker immune system in general are also important to monitor 
[46] [47].  
The successful prevention and treatment of HCMV disease in immune-compromised patients 
has greatly improved in recent years due to additional clinical studies and refinements in prophylactic and 
pre-emptive therapy and an expanding number of new antiviral agents [2]. However, it is reported that as 
many as 50% of solid organ transplant recipients develop symptomatic HCMV disease resulting in 
significant morbidity and even with optimal therapy, mortality from HCMV pneumonia in hematopoietic 
stem cell transplant recipients may exceed 40% [48]. With the growing number of transplantations 
worldwide and the limited number of donors - successful HCMV treatment is of the highest necessity. 
1.3.1 HCMV infection in hematopoietic stem cell transplantation recipients 
Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) or more specifically bone marrow 
transplantation (BMT) is a therapy for aplastic anaemia, various haematological malignancies, 
immunodeficiency syndromes and thalessaemia [49]. In patients receiving HSCT, every aspect of the 
immunological function is depleted. Major complications after HSCT are graft versus host disease (GvHD) as 
well as toxicity from the therapeutics and infection. Immunosuppression drugs are given for 100 days or 
more after the operation to prevent GvHD, while patients who still develop GvHD are treated with further 
immunosuppression. Both GvHD and the immunosuppression increases the risk of infection [50]. 
BMT recipients are at very high risk of developing severe viral infections and in particular – 
HCMV, which is the single greatest cause of failure of the transplant and occurs 1 to 3 months following 
transplantation [51]. It occurs in 30 to 40% of HCMV sero-negative patients given and up to 80% of 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
seropositive patients who reactivate the latent virus. 15% of infected patients develop HCMV pneumonitis 
which has a very high mortality rate [51].  
1.3.2 HCMV infection in solid organ transplant recipients  
In renal transplant cases HCMV infection occurs up to 80% of all patients and is the most 
common pathogen [52]. Even though, most of the infections are due to reactivation of the latent virus and 
are asymptomatic, but it can also lead to acute deterioration of the graft [52]. Sero-negative patients who 
receive transplants from seropositive donors as well as patients who are seropositive for HCMV are also at 
risk, with 20 to 30% developing clinical illness [48].  
In heart transplant cases most fatal HCMV infections arise in sero-negative recipients of 
hearts from seropositive donors [48], where the incidence rate is between 9-23% [53]. The severity of the 
HCMV disease is dependent on the degree of immunosuppression and heart-lung transplant recipients 
sero-status [46].  
In liver transplantation both primary and recurrent infection are common. Even with a long 
prophylactic treatment late-onset HCMV disease occurs at a rate between 17%-37% [45] [53].   
1.4 Current therapy and drug developments against HCMV 
1.4.1 Vaccination 
When considering the feasibility and design of a vaccine against HCMV, it is important to 
understand main methods of infection: primary infection, latent virus reactivation, or reinfection by a 
different strain of HCMV. Since immune-competent hosts can normally fight the disease off it is only of 
highest importance for the pregnant mothers facing primary infection [2]. Current vaccine development 
efforts are not able to prevent the virus from establishing latency. The focus of this thesis is HCMV 
treatment in immune-compromised individuals therefore an enhanced immune response by vaccination is 
mostly irrelevant. Even though, it has been shown that vaccines in trial might have a small effect on 
reducing the relapse of infection in post-transplant patients, however this does not eliminate the need for 
novel antiviral treatments  [54]. 
1.4.2 Antiviral drugs for HCMV treatment 
There are currently several antiviral drugs licensed for the treatment of HCMV infections: 
ganciclovir and valganciclovir (oral drug - valine ester of GCV), acyclovir (ACV) and valacyclovir (oral drug – 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
valine ester of ACV), foscarnet, cidofovir, fomivirsen, cytogam and others [55] [56] [57] [58]. Letermovir will 
soon be the latest addition to the list as it has recently been approved for market [59]. 
Ganciclovir and acyclovir are related nucleosides which are metabolized into the active 
substances, meaning they are prodrugs (see Figure 4) [60]. After activation in the body, they are inhibitors 
of the HCMV encoded DNA polymerase. In cells infected with HCMV, the drugs first get phosphorylated by 
the UL97 protein kinase, then the cellular enzymes convert the drugs into triphosphates – the active drug 
form. Selectivity for virus-infected cells is achieved by UL97 activation and because GCV/ACV triphosphates 
are more specific inhibitors of the HCMV-encoded DNA polymerase than the host’s [2]. The main difference 
between these two drugs it the long intracellular half-life of GCV, which causes a lot of side-effects, while 
the significantly shorter ACV-triphosphate intracellular half-life means that high drug levels and frequent 
dosing are needed for ACV to successfully control HCMV replication 
in vivo [60].  
 To achieve high drug tissue concentrations, GCV is 
administered intravenously at a dose of 5 mg/kg every 12 h for at 
least 14 days [61]. Treatment efficacy is reported to be up to 98%, 
however relapse, resistance and toxicity are all severe problems in 
the treatments with this drug. [62] 
Ganciclovir is eliminated by secretion from the 
kidneys, therefore patients with renal insufficiency need appropriate 
dosage adjustments [61]. A lower dose can be ineffective at treating 
the infection and promote HCMV resistance to emerge. Ganciclovir is 
known to have hematological toxicity, neurotoxicity, and possibly 
hepatotoxicity [63].  
One study indicates that 28% of 607 lung transplant 
patients developed an HCMV infection and 9.4% of these were GCV 
resistant [64]. The 16 patients with GCV-resistant HCMV infection 
were treated with foscarnet regimens and two of the treatments 
were successful, 5 failed and 9 has a relapse infection. The additional 
treatment of the 16 patients resulted in toxicity for 11 patients; 11 
out of 16 patients developed HCMV pneumonitis, which lead to the 
deaths of 4 patients [64]. 
Foscarnet is structural mimic of the pyrophosphate and inhibits the HCMV DNA polymerase 
by binding to its active site where pyrophosphate would bind (see Figure 5). Therefore, foscarnet inhibits

1.4.3 Specific antiviral chemical compounds against US28: 
In the continuing search for better antiviral therapies, specific treatments are highly wanted. 
The HCMV vGPCR US28 is thought to be a good target candidate for specific treatment as it is encoded by 
the virus, and thus only expressed in virus-infected cells. Moreover, it is expressed in both lytic and latent 
phase [74][20].

[75]. In 2017, one group demonstrated that VUF2274 can induce latently 
infected HCMV cells towards the lytic state, therefore allowing the immune 
system to fight off the infection [76].

1.4.4 Immunotoxins against HCMV 
Immunotoxins are a new type of therapy made possibly by gene engineering. These 
biological molecules, so-called biopharmaceuticals are designed to have a very specific targeting moiety 
such as an antibody, a growth factor or a chemokine, which allows delivering the drug only to the desired 
target (see Figure 11). In the case of immunotoxins, the recognition domain is fused through a linker with a 
lethal toxin which can kill the target cell. The strategy was first designed for use of targeting cancer cells, 
but it is just as important to kill viral cells before they release millions of particles to infect other cells [79].  
 
Figure 11. Schematic overview of the design of immunotoxins. The target is a surface protein encoded by the host or a virus; the 
targeting molecules are usually antibodies, growth factors or cytokines; there can be a linker, which joins the targeting molecule 
to the toxin; the toxin molecule is usually a known plant, bacterial or fungal toxin or a protein which can induce apoptosis. 
Figure taken from [76]. 
One of the first immunotoxins against HCMV was reported in 1995 [80]. Barnett et. al. had 
used polyclonal human immunoglobulin against HCMV, which they had connected through a disulphide 
linkage to gelonin, a ribosome-inactivating enzyme. For experimental purposes they had made a similar 
immunotoxin just against MCMV and had shown an EC50 of infected cells at 35 µg/mL while for uninfected

cells at 200 µg/mL [80]. The required drug concentrations were high compared to modern standards and 
the specificity difference between the EC50 values was less than 6-fold, but the newer technological 
advancements today allow creation of much more potent drugs.   
1.4.5 Fusion toxin protein (FTP) against US28 
The first chemokine-based immunotoxin targeting a vGPCR has been reported recently 
where the targeting molecule was based on the fractalkine ligand (CX3CL1) with a series of modifications 
for enhanced specificity against US28 over the endogenous CX3CL1 receptor[81].  
In that publication, Spiess et. al. had summarized why US28 would be suitable for targeting 
of HCMV in an immunotoxin-based strategy [81]: US28 shows high selectivity and enhanced binding of the 
CX3C chemokine CX3CL1. The large structure of CX3CL1 and the domain architecture suggests that the 
chemokine recognition part can be engineered to bind with high-affinity to US28 and replace the C-terminal 
domain by another protein (e.g., the cytotoxic domains of Pseudomonas Exotoxin A). Lastly, the 
constitutive internalization of US28 and subsequent localization to endosomes allows for efficient 
intracellular delivery of the toxin. Based on these molecular characteristics of US28 and its defined ligand 
profile, an immunotoxin was designed consisting of the chemokine domain CX3CL1 and the translocation 
and cytotoxicity domains of PE [81].  
The initial created immunotoxin was termed CX3CL1-FTP and it was shown to bind US28 with 
high affinity and kill US28-expressing cells with higher potency than ganciclovir [81]. Since CX3CL1 is a well 
described agonist to CX3CR1, the CX3CL1-FTP also killed non-infected cells, which expressed the human 
chemokine receptor. Therefore, a single-point mutation (Phe49 to Ala) was made, which was shown to 
have reduced affinity to CX3CR1 and retain the high-affinity for binding to the viral US28. The selectivity of 
the new immunotoxin termed F49A-FTP exhibited a 10-fold superior viral load reduction during HCMV 
infection in vivo in a mice model with a human graft (for the possibility of HCMV infection) compared 
against ganciclovir [81].  
The efficacy, potency and selectivity of the new drug paves the way for an interesting new 
therapy against HCMV, however due to the nature of the immunotoxin safety is of the highest importance. 
Further selectivity optimization as well as toxicity experiments in other model organisms, organ transplants 
or even humans is necessary to push the drug development to the next stage.

2. Aim of study 
The aim of this project is to develop novel therapeutic agents against HCMV via targeting of 
the vGPCR US28. A biological and chemical drug development approaches were used to reach this aim and 
the subproject goals were as follows: 
1. Up-scale and optimize the production procedures for the fusion toxin protein and test for binding to 
US28, killing of cells expressing US28 and antiviral activity against HCMV.  
2. Screen an in silico designed small chemical compound library to find lead candidates which exhibit 
binding to US28 and antiviral properties against HCMV.

3.1 Mammalian cell culturing 
A human embryonic kidney cell line (HEK-293) was used. HEK-293 cells were grown at 10%

GlutaMAX™ with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 180 units/mL penicillin and 45 μg/mL streptomycin.  
The stable inducible HEK-293 clones of US28 and CX3CR1 were kindly provided by Hjortø et.

HEK-293 cells, but with the addition of hygromycin B 300 μg/mL and blasticidin 50 µg/mL to the cell media

Human foreskin fibroblasts cells (HFF) (CRL-1634) were purchased from the American Type 
Culture Collection. HFF cells were grown at 5% CO2, 90% humidity and 37oC. They were cultured in GIBCOR 
DMEM GlutaMAX™ with heat inactivated (55⁰C for 30min) 10% FBS and 180 units/mL penicillin and 45 
μg/mL streptomycin. 
All cells were passaged using 0.25% trypsin in phosphate buffer saline (PBS) containing

3.2 Production of virus stock samples 
The Towne HCMV strain was purchased from the American Type Culture Collection (VR-977). 
The Merlin HCMV strain was kindly provided by Rich Stanton from the Cardiff University, while the 
recombinant MerlinΔUS28 HCMV strain was kindly provided by Associate Professor Barry Slobedman from 
the University of Sydney. 
To create a large sample stock of the virus, about 100 million of HFF cells were grown. To 
infect the cells the media was changed by adding fresh media without FBS and with viral particles at about 
MOI of 0.01. The cells were incubated for 1 hour to allow the virus to penetrate the cells. The media was 
then changed to the growth media but with only 2% of heat inactivated FBS. The cells were grown for 9-11 
days until about 80% CPE was visible. The supernatant was collected and centrifuged at 4000g for 30min at 
+4oC to remove the cell debris. The supernatant was transferred to new tubes while the cell pellet was 
discarded. Afterwards the supernatant was centrifuged at 18000g for 6 hours at +4oC to concentrate the 
viral particles. The supernatant was discarded and the virus pellet was resuspended in 5 mL of PBS. The 
sample was carefully applied in a 24 mL ultracentrifugation tube on top of an 18 mL 20% sucrose cushion 
and centrifuged at 18000g for 3 hours at +4oC. The supernatant was discarded and the purified virus

and 6th day post infection (dpi) until the 11-13 dpi when the plaques become visible by microscopy. The

A single colony was used to inoculate 5-200 mL of LB medium and grown overnight at +37oC.

5. Discussion 
5.1 Fusion toxin protein purification 
Chemokine purification has been a widely studied topic in the scientific literature because 
chemokines are important actors of the immune system during the inflammatory response [89]. 
Chemokine production in literature is described in different hosts, but Escherichia coli is the first choice due 
to being the cheapest, fastest and a very versatile recombinant protein production host. E.coli has been 
used in research laboratories to successfully produce different chemokines, however due to the bacterial 
translation machinery being different from eukaryotes, the common practice has become to produce 
chemokines from the insoluble fraction - the inclusion bodies. This option is good enough for academic 
study of chemokines, however for the production of large quantities e.g. for use as a biopharmaceutical, 
up-scaling such a process becomes very expensive.  
Different host organisms are being genetically engineered for better, faster and cheaper 
industrial protein production [90]. It would be an interesting topic to explore chemokine production in 
fungi, however this would require a separate study. The current consensus is to either use mammalian cells 
for production of glycosylated chemokines or E.coli for non-glycosylated. There are attempts to produce 
chemokines in the periplasm of E.coli where better disulphide bond assembly can occur, however only in a 
few cases this was commercially viable, while usually the secretory machinery gets overloaded and protein 
starts to be produced in inclusion bodies [91]. When refolding is the chosen option for protein production, 
then defining the right conditions is critical to limit the cost of the process. For this purpose, miniaturized 
screening techniques are being developed for faster and efficient condition screening by the use of the 
nanodroplet technologies, however they are only in development of specialized laboratories [92] [93]. 
One of the main issues which raise the cost of refolding in e.g. 10000 L bio-reactors is the 
need to use large amounts of expensive chemicals. In our case it was required to use at least a three molar 
solution of urea or GdmCl to solubilize the inclusion bodies. The cheaper alternative – urea when exposed 
to proteins can also cause peptide modifications, therefore often the more expensive denaturant, GdmCl, is 
chosen. In addition to the selection of denaturing agents, the oxidative and reductive agents are also 
expensive chemicals, which are required for the efficient disulphide bridge formation during the refolding 
process [94]. The best scientific practice suggests using oxidized and reduced forms of glutathione, however 
cheaper alternatives can also be used successfully. One strategy to overcome the expense of chemicals 
could be reuse or recycling, however researchers are struggling to find the balance  between cost-efficient 
recycling of intermediate purification reagents [90].   
==PAGE:42==
41 
 
All of the aforementioned chemokine production issues are considered in this study, because 
the biopharmaceutical’s recognition moiety is the fractalkine CX3CL1, therefore as described by Inagen, this 
recombinant protein expresses and purifies similarly to a chemokine as also described in the literature [89]. 
As a positive aspect of this method, inclusion body washing usually eliminates a large majority of 
contaminants therefore chromatographic purification is required only minimally, mostly for separating the 
correctly folded proteins from the misfolded. However, the problem faced using the initial protocol was 
almost no purification during IEXC, just capture of the product together with contaminants, therefore 
protein purification method development techniques were applied. 
Throughout this study initial experiments were carried out in smaller scale, however 
considerations for the up-scaling for preclinical protein production were taken into account and afterwards 
carried out. As shown in the results 4.1.1 section, the protein is overexpressed abundantly and after 
washing the inclusion bodies it is also greatly purified, however the yield from the protein refolding step to 
the IEXC capture has reached only about 6% at 2 mg/mL protein concentration. It was shown that reducing 
the protein concentration during the refolding step can increase the yield, however at the cost of using 
more chemicals and in too large volumes to handle in our laboratory. Considering our experimental setup, 
after producing 80g of wet weight of cell culture which amounted to about 6 g wet weight of inclusion 
bodies. Which means to dilute 6 grams of protein to 1 mg/mL concentration already makes it about 6 L of 
volume, which by using the dialysis refolding technique with at least the bare minimum of 20 times the 
dialysis buffer to sample ratio the total volume amounts to 120 L dialysis size. For this procedure a tank of 
40 litres was acquired to perform the dialysis and the dialysis was still had to be carried out over 3 
procedures. Lowering the protein concentration further to e.g. 0.5 mg/mL would have doubled the total 
volume and would have become unpractical.  
The first purification step in chromatography is intended to be the capture step [95], where 
the intent is to capture as much of the target sample without much regard for protein purity. However, 
since the protein is already quite pure after the washing of inclusion bodies, our first chromatography step 
would need to focus on capturing the correctly folded protein as well as purifying. To this regard, 
empirically the standard strong cation and anion exchange chromatography resins with different pH buffers 
were chosen to screen the binding and eluting fraction (see Figure 17). The anion exchange resin at pH of 
6.5 successfully captured and purified the protein. When up-scaling the IEXC another issue had come 
apparent – our target protein requires more binding capacity. Even though our setup used three 5 mL 
HiTrap SP FF columns, which by the manufacturer’s provided specifications has a binding capacity of 50 mg 
of protein per mL of resin (meaning a total of 750 mg of protein could potentially be captured), it seemed 
to be far from the case. In the large-scale IEXC runs, the 280 nm wavelength absorbance chromatogram 
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
showed to run over capacity immediately (see Appendix E). One hypothesis was that the incorrectly folded 
protein from the refolding step is interfering or binding to the resin at an increasing amount. To overcome 
this obstacle and to maximize the yield of our setup the purification method was programmed to perform 
IEXC in cycles with the column undergoing cleaning in place procedures every 300 mL of loaded and eluted 
sample. This still lead to some loss of the sample, however it was concluded that due to practicality reasons 
this was the optimal speed to capture ratio. 
When creating biopharmaceuticals in E.coli it is important to purify the protein sample from 
the lipopolysaccharides which are present in the bacterial membrane. This is most often done by using 
detergent wash steps, some biotechnological processes achieve this by a simple IEXC [96]. For the 
purification of our target FTP the endotoxin removal step was not documented in Inagen’s protocol, 
therefore implementing it was another developmental goal. Testing the endotoxin concentration after IEXC 
as well as after different endotoxin removal methods (detergent washing during IEXC; using Pierce™ 
Endotoxin removal resin) the endotoxin levels had remained high. The only option which managed to lower 
the endotoxin amount to an acceptable limit (less than 5 EU/mL) was the Triton X114 phase separation 
method [97]. This raised the hypothesis that the LPS stick to the F49A-FTP, which is why only a rigorous 
method managed to purify it. However, after the LPS removal the following SEC revealed that the majority 
of the protein has aggregated. Two hypotheses were formed – that the LPS were stabilizing the FTP’s 
monomeric form; that the detergent in the protein solution physically damaged the FTP. Both of these 
hypotheses were investigated further.  
Specific buffer, pH, salt or other additives are required to stabilize proteins in research as 
well as in biopharmaceutical formulations [98]. Since the half-life of this product was reported by the 
former company Inagen to be over several weeks in room temperature without the removal of LPS, it could 
be that LPS was stabilizing the protein and there is the need to change the stabilizing function into a 
different additive. For this reason, the Thermofluor technique was used to screen for additives as it is a 
popular tool in molecular biology to increase protein stability [99]. The Tm of the final purified FTP has 
resulted to be 37.8oC, which is in addition dangerously close aggregating during the Triton phase separation 
protocol when exposed to 30oC. The identified 8oC additional thermostability with the additional phosphate 
salts were identified and could ideally fix the aforementioned issue. However, this did not prove to be the 
case, as with the increased salt in the buffer the additional thermostability did not result in lower the 
protein aggregation during endotoxin removal. The thermostability parameter does not necessarily convert 
to general protein stability, however it may prove to help the shelf life of the formulation.  
The second option to reduce aggregation during endotoxin removal was to try to optimize 
the procedure. It was hypothesized that minimizing the time the protein is in contact with the detergent 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
could minimize the aggregation caused. This was proven to be the case by changing the protocol as 
described in the results section 4.1.2, however when thinking in the context of process scale biologics 
manufacturing, this LPS removal technique would not scale up, therefore would have to be redeveloped.  
Lipopolysaccharide removal is an important topic in biopharmaceutical development as 
removing them completely from the formulation can be tricky, however it is not an impossible task [96]. A 
crucial aspect is having a particle-free laboratory as viral and bacterial contaminations are almost 
impossible to avoid otherwise. As was the case in our laboratory setting: when the LPS purified protein 
sample was subjected to the size exclusion chromatography step, it was re-contaminated by endotoxins.  
Therefore, without changing facilities, endotoxins in the final purified sample could not be avoided and was 
not further investigated. 
The Pharmaceutical development Q8 (R2) guidelines by the ICH explains the two different 
methods of biologics manufacturing – the minimal approach as well as the enhanced, quality by design 
approach [100]. With the continuing efforts to reduce batch to batch variability as well as the final cost it is 
promoted to research the design space of different parameters in protein production. The method 
described in this study would of course still need to be transferred to a GMP facility and validated, however 
the additional knowledge will allow a faster process. 
5.2 Fusion toxin protein functional activity 
The newly created F49A-FTP was evaluated on several functional characteristics such as 
binding to US28, cell killing, antiviral activity and where possible compared to the previously published 
data. Recombinant protein production manufacturing strategy is important for the cost effectiveness, final 
purity and validation of the process, therefore while the optimization of production led to use of different 
resins and buffers, these changes should not affect the characteristics of the enzymatic activity.  
The competition binding experiment was carried out on inducible HEK293-US28 cells with 
the 125I-CCL2 radioligand, which is known to be displaced by the CX3CL1, but not the other way around [38]. 
Meaning that in this experimental setup the FTP is more likely to displace the radioligand, therefore the 
competition binding assay should be more accurate in terms of the actual binding affinity. 
The in vitro cell killing experiment was carried out on inducible HEK293-US28, HEK293-
CX3CR1 and HEK293 cells by incubating the FTPs for 24 hours with the cells. The approach aimed to identify 
the how well does the biopharmaceutical kill cells which it is supposed to target as well as how good is the 
specificity of the viral receptor targeting versus the endogenous human CX3CR1 receptor as well as to see 
the non-specific toxicity. The HEK293 cell line of course also expresses the CX3CR1, however at much lower 
amounts than does the inducible counterpart.  The therapeutic index (TI)  of the current compound was 
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
measured to be 121, which is a good value, however it does need to be assessed in vivo to have a more 
realistic toxicity representation [101]. To compare the FTP’s TI to the current therapy, in lymphoblastoid 
cells ganciclovir’s toxic IC50 is 20 mg/L, while results with the HCMV AD169 strain show a typical IC50 of 0.9 
mg/L, giving a therapeutic index of about 22 [63], meaning as long as the FTP’s values are true in vivo it 
could be considered more safe than ganciclovir. 
The final activity experiment was the plaque reduction assay carried out in HFF cells infected 
by the Towne virus, which showed the FTP to be 100% effective at a 10 nM dose. This experiment is the 
best representation of the FTP’s function in vitro and it is also considered the classic standard method of 
virology [102][103]. 
When comparing the values experimentally tested of the newly produced F49A-FTP with 
those reported previously by Spiess et. al. they all match or are within the standard error of mean, meaning 
that the activity of the enzyme is fully intact [104]. The binding and killing experiment potencies are just 
slightly higher and these small differences may be due to the fact the final purity of the FTP is over 99%, 
which may lead to more precise target protein concentration quantification, meaning the contaminant 
absorbance at 280 nm wavelength would be almost non-existent. However, the variance cannot be 
disregarded for the nature of cell biology as numerous factors can affect the assays such as the livelihood of 
the cells, the induced expression of the receptor, not to mention different equipment used to detect the 
values. 
5.3 Identified SMCs which bind to US28 
Screening of millions of SMCs against various diseases has since 1980s been one the method 
for large pharmaceutical companies to identify and add new drugs to their development pipelines [105]. A 
variety of assays are now being employed to screen for interesting interactions or activity. In silico drug-
protein interaction modelling comes to aid to the screening process whenever the structures of the target 
are available. The first library against US28 was designed on the basis of the partial inverse agonist 
VUF2274 [75], while the compound tested in this thesis were further refinements of the previously 
identified inverse agonist – LC13 [78].  
The binding assay by the use of radioligands is a classical method, which is popular due to its 
ease of use and effectiveness [106]. The competiton binding experiments have identified compounds which 
have superior properties to displace chemokines compared to its parent compound (see Figure 26). The 
two compounds in particular, 167 and 176, have been shown to be more efficacious at displacing CCL4 than 
VUF2274. In addition, 176 was the only compound capable of displacing the 125I-CX3CL1 radioligand in 
binding to US28 [data unpublished]. This ability demonstrates that these ligands binding centre overlaps 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
different chemokines binding pockets with high affinity. 
When looking into the chemical structures of 167 and 176 
to their parent compound LC13 differ only slightly – they all 
have different halogenated moieties in the left hand side 
(see Figure 27). In contrast, compounds without the 
halogen on the right hand side did not exhibit binding 
properties. This gives an indication of the importance of a 
strong nucleophile for the binding to the receptor and gives 
a good lead for further investigations of the chemical atom 
interactions with the US28 receptor. However, to analyse 
these results in more detail an in silico docking study is 
required, which was not the scope of this thesis.  
In this study, the binding to US28 screening 
was intended to supplement the antiviral activity screening 
of these compounds. Trying to design specific drugs against

of the chemical compounds. The binding and antiviral activity screening has given leads which need to be 
characterised further. 
5.4 Identified SMCs which exhibit antiviral activity 
The regulatory guidance document for testing a compound’s antiviral activity is well 
described by the FDA [107]. They recommend identifying the mechanism of action, testing the antiviral 
properties in different clinical isolate strains, different cell lines, determining the cytotoxicity and 
therapeutic indexes as well as looking into the effects of serum protein binding on antiviral activity. It is 
recommended to determine the antiviral activity, the compound’s IC50 and the suggested assays are: the 
virus inactivation assays, plaque reduction assays, cytopathic effect inhibition assays and peripheral blood 
mononuclear cell assays. The plaque reduction assay is a classic method which was first used in 1952 [102]. 
The assay directly measures the infectivity of a viral sample and any compound which can reduce infectivity 
will be observed.  
In this thesis the chemical compound library was screened in a multiple-dosage experiment 
at a 10µM doses for chemical compounds and at 10nM for the FTP. Eleven statistically significant (p<0.002) 
hits were identified by one-way ANOVA analysis: 114, 130, 166, 167, 168, 171, 173, 176, 181, 183, 186. The 
observed direct therapeutic effect is a positive achievement, however it is yet completely unknown how

This thesis investigated two very different drug development approaches - chemical and 
biological. The production methods, molecule size, structural characteristics and functional activity are 
unique to each of the approaches (see Table 3) [110]. Chemical compounds have the advantage of being a 
much older and well-studied technology, being much cheaper to produce, being stable and possibly usable 
in an oral drug format, while biological drugs have the upper hand in providing either a more specific

often solve therapeutic challenges which could not be addressed [113]. Since it is becoming harder to add

the future development of the FTP. 
When comparing a novel chemical drug to the current therapies, the main successful 
business case requirements are higher efficacy, lower toxicity and cost efficiency. If one of the lead 
molecules identified in this study proves to have a novel mechanism of action it will have to be proven to 
be better in the three aforementioned factors. One hypothesis is that targeting US28 and altering the 
signalling in the virus infected cell such a drug can serve as a combination therapeutic. As shown with the 
VUF2274 compound, it might also be able to push the virus from latency into the lytic phase, where the 
conventional therapy would effectively eliminate the virus [76]. The combination therapy might allow the 
dose reduction of the primary therapeutic, resulting in both a cost and efficiency driven business case. As a 
final scenario it might be an effective therapy with the possible newer vaccine developments, but in this 
case it could be hypothesized that any of the current drugs would be just as useful. 
In the case of a new biological drug, it needs to be significantly better than the current 
counterparts or ideally - completely change the way this disease is treated. The FTP targets US28 which is 
present in both lytic and latent phase, which could potentially lead to complete eradication of HCMV from 
immune-compromised patients. This could be the first drug to achieve such a feature, albeit a slim chance.

6. Conclusion and future perspectives 
To summarize the first subproject, the production of the fusion toxin protein was 
investigated in several areas: refolding, endotoxin removal, purification by different IEXC resin, buffer 
concentration and pH. An empirical approach trying to expand the known limits of each step has helped 
identify favourable conditions and add to the knowledge of the design space.  
Lower protein concentration during refolding gives a higher protein capture yield, however 
increases the scale and cost of the procedure – a further cost, benefit analysis in a designated facility would 
be required to have the optimal conclusion. 
The leftover contaminants from an E.coli inclusion body recombinant protein purification can 
be cleaned by using a buffer with a pH between 6 to 7 with a strong anion exchange resin. The target 
protein is captured in these conditions and when eluted at 320 mM NaCl step gradient contains mostly 
monomeric protein. This allows for an efficient purification step with good recovery and speed. 
The lipopolysaccharides stick to the FTP, therefore rigorous endotoxin removal method is 
required. The Triton X114 phase separation method has been optimized to be done in two cycles, 1,5 mL 
eppendorf tubes with minimal time in the +4oC and +30oC treatments. This method works well in the 
research setting, however since this method does not scale efficiently it will have to be completely re-
worked when transferred to a facility with controlled number of particles in the air. Due to academic 
laboratory facilities it was impossible to protect the sample from endotoxin recontamination. 
The optimized FTP production proved to yield 11.54 mg of protein per liter of grown E.coli 
cell culture and a total of 277 mg were produced. It was shown to be as active as reported previously 
therefore, the sub-project of the thesis is deemed successful. 
In summary of the second subproject, several chemical antiviral compounds have been 
identified. The binding to US28 and antiviral screening study has shown that there might be a correlation 
between these two attributes, however it remains to be determined what exactly is the mechanism of 
action. The identified compounds give insight into the important structural characteristics and areas how to 
further refine and possibly later perform a quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) study. So far 
the identified antiviral compounds exhibit a lesser effect than of ganciclovir, while the FTP has the highest 
antiviral potency and efficacy. 
As mentioned previously, the chemical compound screen was the first step in the search for 
specific antivirals against HCMV. Defining the mechanism of action of the chemical compounds would be 
beneficial. The first step required would be to establish high-throughput system for testing the compounds 
in different assays, such as for G protein or beta-arrestin signalling, unspecific binding. In silico modelling of 
the current compounds to the binding pockets and investigating the structure to the observed effect 
==PAGE:51==
50 
 
relationship would be the second step. Lastly, designing a QSAR study to find the further optimized 
compounds would hopefully lead to novel promising antiviral agents. 
With the successful production of the FTP the next step would be to test it in different 
systems, e.g. in vivo mouse models, transplant organs, rhesus macaque models to achieve as close to in 
human experimental conditions as possible. If these experiments would provide positive results, moving 
the manufacturing to a certified facility would be required to prepare for in human trials. Such facilities are 
difficult to develop from scratch without the know-how and funding, therefore collaboration with an 
industrial partner should be established. As safety of a fusion toxin protein is of the utmost importance, 
further improvements in the specificity would be beneficial. To improve the recognition protein 
establishing a high-throughput random mutagenesis phage display library could be one option. Second 
option, with the recently available published structures of the US28 receptor perform in silico protein-
protein interaction modelling, could reveal new clues to selective mutagenesis of the receptor for increased 
specificity. 
Two very different drug development approaches were investigated - a chemical and 
biological compound. The development, governmental regulations and possible future of these drugs are 
also very different. Since the drug development has a very small chance of getting to the market, therefore 
both of the options should be investigated further. Rationally designed biopharmaceuticals can often 
bypass a lot of the pitfalls, but the conclusion can only be found after conducting in vivo experiments.   
To conclude this thesis, lead chemical compounds were identified and the production of the 
FTP was improved, therefore a significant contribution to the search for HCMV therapeutics was 
accomplished.

Appendix D – F49A-FTP large scale purification by the optimized protocol 
 
Appendix figure 5. 24 L of F49A-FTP induced E.coli in LB medium were expressed, lysed and the inclusion bodies were washed. 
The induction of expression can be seen slightly. A large amount of FTP is seen in the pellet fraction. Afterwards the pellet was 
washed 8 times – P1-P8 indicates the insoluble fractions, while W1-W8 indicates the wash fractions. The final pellet was 
solubilized in 2,5L of solubilization buffer. The sample was then refolded overnight over three separate dialysis procedures. 
 
 
Appendix figure 6. IEXC and the following SEC of the refolded F49A-FTP. A large amount of the target protein remains in the flow 
through, however an equally large amount is captured and eluted in the 20% buffer B elution step, while a much smaller and 
slightly dirtier fraction is eluted in a 100% buffer B elution step. The 20% pool was concentrated to about 10mg/mL, it was 
treated with Triton X114 to remove LPS, then it was incubated with SM2 beads to remove leftover Triton X114 and loaded onto 
SEC column over multiple runs due to the limitation of the SEC column size. The multimeric and monomeric fractions are of even 
size when integrating the 280 nm wavelength absorbance chromatograms. The final eluted monomeric protein is over 99% pure. 
The upper panels depict the chromatograms zoomed into the elution peaks while the lower panels depict the SDS-PAGE of the 
appropriate fractions. Load – sample which was loaded onto the column, FT – flow through, 20% - sample eluted with a 20% 
buffer B step elution; 100% - sample eluted with a 100% buffer B step elution; 2-26 – fraction number; M – marker.
