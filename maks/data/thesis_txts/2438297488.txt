Abstract
This report details the design and testing of a system capable of measuring the magnetic ﬁeld
gradient. The AMR magnetometer HMC1001 from Honeywell was chosen for the system
after market analysis.
The system was simulated and an approximation algorithm was
implemented in MatLab to estimate the location of a source. Diﬀerent simulated sensor
arrangements were tested using the approximation algorithm.
Support systems needed to collected measurements from the magnetometers were designed,
and formed into sensor units. A Central controller was implemented to collected data and
control the measurement parameters on the sensor units. Two sensor units prototypes were
built, and the prototype system developed is capable of measuring the ﬁeld gradient along
a single axis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Humans have used magnetic ﬁelds for hundreds of years, in the earliest day’s sailors used
simple compasses for navigation, and later improvements upon these simple instruments
aided in the exploration of the earth. With the increase in understanding of the relation-
ship between diﬀerent materials and magnetic ﬁelds, the mapping of earths ﬁeld has found
increasingly varied uses.
Today measurements of magnetic ﬁelds are used in science to understand the inner work-
ings and history of earth and other planets.
Magnetic mapping of large areas help ar-
chaeologist determine locations of ancient civilisations, and possible locations for new digs.
Measuring the local magnetic ﬁeld can aid in understanding the world around us, or help lo-
cate hidden or obscured objects. A change in the local magnetic ﬁeld might indicate an area
of archaeological or geological signiﬁcance, or it could indicate the presence of a power-line
buried under the ocean ﬂoor.
The goal of this project is to design a system that using multiple sensors, can measure the
gradient of a magnetic ﬁeld, and is capable of detecting magnetic sources. This system should
use multiple sensors to increase sensitivity, by providing additional sources of measurements.
The project will investigate what eﬀect the number of sensors and their positioning, have
on estimated performance, The system should be self-contained by including all necessary
components for operation. These components and the sensors should be chosen considering
weight and cost compared to performance. The project aims to develop a lightweight and
low cost alternative to traditional instruments.

==PAGE:18==
1.1
Objective
Designing a multi-sensor platform for measuring magnetic ﬁeld gradients, and build a pro-
totype to verify the design.
This project will focus on lowering price and weight, while maintaining a high sensitivity.
1.1.1
Requirements
The ﬁnal solution will be measured on the following parameters:
Resolution: The resolution of the ﬁnal system should be 10nT or less.
Time resolution: The system should be able to deliver data at a rate of 1Hz or faster.
Noise: The noise inherent in the system should be no greater than 50nT peak to peak.
Weight: The system should be man portable and weigh no more than 3kg.
Additional parameters that should be considered:
The system should contain its own power system to increase portability.
The system should deliver data to an end user in a convenient fashion.
To complete these requirements the following objectives need to be completed:
Investigate available magnetic sensors, and sensor types.
Determine theoretical system performance for detecting magnetic sources.
Investigate what optimal arrangement and/or geometry and structure of sensors is needed.
Design electronics to support and operate the magnetometer.
Design a system to support multiple magnetometers, and data collection.
Build a prototype system.
Calibrate the system.
Test the system.

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Magnetometers
In order to determine which type of sensor and model the system should use, the market
of available magnetometer is investigated and the characteristics of each are noted. As the
system is intended for ﬁeld usage, the focus will be on portable systems, and individual
sensors. This section will brieﬂy list several common types of survey magnetometers, and
their strengths and weaknesses.
2.1.1
Proton precession magnetometer
Proton precession magnetometers measure the ambient magnetic ﬁeld by exciting a proton
rich ﬂuid with a strong magnetic ﬁeld, usually generated by running DC current through
a solenoid. The generated magnetic ﬁeld causes some of the protons in the liquid to align
themselves with the ﬁeld.
When the ﬁeld is turned oﬀ, the protons will return to their
original state (aligned with the ambient ﬁeld) and as they do they precess with a frequency
dependent on the ambient ﬁeld.
Using the frequency of this precession to calculate the
magnetic ﬁeld result in a precise scalar measurement of the ﬁeld.
Pros: High sensitivity.
Cons: Heavy, power-intensive, expensive.

Caesium/Potassium vapour magnetometer
Alkali vapour magnetometers use speciﬁc wavelengths of light to excite a sample of gaseous
alkali metal. Once the gas is excited it will not absorb any more light, and allow it to pass
through to a photon detector. Applying an RF ﬁeld to the gas will cause the gas atoms
to change excitation state, and the gas sample will start to absorb more of the light. The
frequency of RF ﬁeld needed to change the excitation state is dependent on the ambient
magnetic ﬁeld. By measuring the light passing through the gas sample and controlling the
RF frequency, the magnetic ﬁeld can be determined with a high resolution.
Pros: Very high sensitivity.
Cons: Heavy, expensive.

From the market research it is apparent that the MEMS magnetometers score better in terms

makes them unsuitable for use in a project of this scope.
The HMC1001 is the best performing sensor in terms of price and sensitivity of the AMR
and MEMS magnetometers listed. It will therefore be the magnetometer chosen for this
project. The HMC1001 has a resolution of 27µGauss or 2.7nTesla, sensitivity of 3.2/mV/V/-

expected of the magnetometer, in order to not degrade the ﬁnal resolution of the system.

2.3
Using multiple sensors to measure the gradient
In order to measure the gradient with only AMR sensors, multiple sensor-units are needed.
To measure the gradient along a single axis, two units are needed with an oﬀset distance
between then. Two such sensors would then when compared give the gradient along the path
formed by the two sensors. Measuring the gradient along multiple paths require multiple
sets of sensor units, two along each axis needed.
The simplest arrangement that will result in a 3-axis gradient, is one sensor unit at the
origin, and one placed along each axis, resulting in 4 total sensors.

Having two or more three axis measurements, at diﬀerent known coordinates, provides

measurements, and any error in the gathered data can result in unsolvable equations. Due
to this a best ﬁt approximation need to be used instead of algebraic solution.
Monte Carlo Method
Estimation of the location of the source can be done by using a Monte Carlo method. The
Monte Carlo approach used in this project relies on taking a random guess at sensor location
and magnetic source, then through increasingly smaller random steps, approaching the result
resembling the measurements most accurately.
Using only a single measurements forces an assumption of either magnetic dipole moment
or location. Assuming the vector potential, results in a relative direction to the source. If
the source in known, or only the direction is desired, this method can be used.
If the source is not known, or direction and distance is desired at least two measurements
taken with a known oﬀset is needed. Using MatLab a Monte Carlo approximation algorithm
is implemented, taking a variable amount of measurements and their sensor’s position in
relation to each other as arguments. The version implemented operates only in two dimen-
sions, in order to prove the concept in the time allotted. The two dimensional version will
still allow an approximation of the source and location in two dimensions.
The algorithm takes ’n’ measurements from ’n’ sensors, and the sensors position relative to
the position of sensor 1. The implementation in MatLab (included in appendix B.1.3) can
be described as follows:
1. Generate a large amount of random magnetic dipole moments [mx, my, mz], the range
of generation should be large enough to include the true value.
2. Generate a large amount of random [x, y, z] coordinates, the range of generation should
be large enough to include the true position.
3. Calculate the x/y/z components of the B ﬁeld for all potentials in all coordinates for
all sensors.
4. Calculate the absolute diﬀerence of the calculated B ﬁeld from the measurement, this
is the error.

Chapter 3
Design & Implementation
To construct the multi-sensor system necessary for measuring the ﬁeld gradient with the
HMC1001 AMR magnetometer, several support systems and components are needed. The
HMC1001 outputs an analog voltage diﬀerential from the internal Wheatstone-bridge, which
have to be interpreted and then collected for all three axis. Measurements from one point
should be done for all three axis simultaneously, meaning that three single axis sensors should
be placed close together with the necessary supports to form a single 3-axis sensor unit. This
sensor unit can then be replicated for each sensor wanted in the ﬁnal conﬁguration. To collect
the data from all the sensors, they should be able to communicate using a standard bus, to
a central control unit managing sampling rate and time.
In ﬁgure 3.1 an overall block diagram of the system is shown. The "Central Control Unit"
(CCU), is a Micro Control Unit MCU that is used as the main control unit, and communicates
with the sensor-units using SPI. The "Power circuit" is a collection of sub-circuits delivering
the necessary voltage lines needed by the system.
This project will build two sensor units as a proof of concept prototype.

3.1
The Sensor Unit
The sensor unit consists of the three HMC1001 magnetometers needed for a 3 axis measure-
ment. To read the HMC1001’s analog output, analog to digital converters (ADC) are needed
for each axis. Having an ADC for each axis enables the system to acquire measurements
from the three axis simultaneously, instead of having to measure each axis in rapid succes-
sion. This allows for more time for each conversion, increasing precision and achieves higher
sample rate compared to a single ADC setup.
Communicating with the ADC’s directly from the central MCU(the CCU) pose a problem
as ADC’s most often use SPI or I2C. This is because I2C requires a unique address for each
ADC, and most ADC’s come with few or a single hard-coded address.
This becomes a
problem when the system contains multiple sensor units with three ADC’s each, as there are
not enough unique addresses. SPI suﬀers from a similar problem as it needs a GPIO pin as
Chip Select (CS) for each ADC, resulting in a very high (sensor units x 3) use of GPIO pins.
To solve this issue and ease the burden of coordinating the sampling of the magnetometers,
a micro-controller is used to handle the communication with the ADC’s, and function as
’middleman’ collecting data and doing some initial processing for the CCU. Figure 3.2 show
a block diagram of the sensor unit.
Figure 3.2: Block diagram of the 3-axis sensor unit.
The sensor unit is divided into two parts: the Sensor Control Unit (SCU) and the sensor
PCB. The sensor PCB contains everything but the SCU and the SN74LVC2T45 buﬀer
(mentioned in section 3.1.1). The SCU is excluded from the sensor PCB to simplify and
speed up PCB design.
3.1.1
AMR magnetometer: HMC1001
The Honeywell HMC1001 contains 4 permalloy magneto-resistor arranged in a Wheatstone-
bridge, with an oﬀset coil and a set/reset coil integrated into the package. The oﬀset coil is
used to manually oﬀset the permalloy resistors by running a current through it, imposing a
magnetic ﬁeld on them. In this project the oﬀset-coil will not be used. The set/reset coil is
used to ﬂip the sensitive direction of the permalloy magneto resistors, reversing the polarity
of the output. Performing a set/reset operation has the added eﬀect of reconditioning the
magnetic domains of the permalloy, thereby erasing magnetic disturbances, and increasing
accuracy.
The output from the HMC1001’s Wheatstone-bridge is 3.2mV/V/Gauss (typical), and
with a total range of ±2Gauss. In order to ensure accurate readings, and lower the re-

Set/Reset circuit
Performing a Set or reset operation is done by running a 2-5A current through the S/R strap
for 2µSeconds, as seen in ﬁgure 3.3. A positive current forming a ’Set’ pulse, and a negative
current a ’Reset’ pulse. Pulsing the S/R coil produces a magnetic ﬁeld across the permalloy
elements in the sensor improving performance.
Figure 3.3: Description of the set pulse from the HMC1001 datasheet[7].
As the only diﬀerence in output after a set or reset pulse is the polarity of the output,
the oﬀset of the sensor can be eliminated. This is done by taking two measurements, one
after a set pulse and one after a reset pulse, and comparing the two. A measurement after
a set pulse (mSet) will be the oﬀset + the ﬁeld, and the measurement after a reset (mRset)
will be the oﬀset - the ambient ﬁeld:
mSet = Off + x
mRset = Off −x
(3.4)
x being the measured ﬁeld, and Oﬀbeing the oﬀset.
With these two measurements
the oﬀset can be eliminated, without having to ﬁnd the oﬀset for each individual sensor in
calibration. This also eliminates error due to a change in oﬀset after the calibration is com-
pleted. This of course only work if the ﬁeld does not change between the two measurements,
requiring either a fast sample speed, or a slow changing/static ﬁeld.
Producing the current pulse needed for the set/reset operation, needs its own dedicated
circuit. Pulsing 2A through the S/R coil, with a (typical) resistance of 1.5Ωrequires 3V over
the coil. To ensure some safety margin the S/R coil should be driven with 3A, requiring
4.5V diﬀerence over the coil. To avoid adding an additional power line (4.5V) to supply
the S/R circuit, it is driven from the 6V line. This also solves the problem of voltage drop
across the MOSFETs, and allows the capacitor sourcing the current pulse to drop in voltage
without ruining the pulse. Using a standard H-bridge setup to pulse current through the
coil, requires strict control of the H-bride control inputs. As a failure to shut oﬀthe current
at the correct time, will lead to much longer pulses and potentially damaging the device. To
avoid this the coil will be pulsed through a capacitor placed immediately before the S/R+

the necessary current needed for the SR pulse.

Operating the ADCs
Communication with the ADCs is done through an SPI bus, connecting all the ADCs with
the SCU. Controlling the ADC is done by either setting the ADC register or sending a
command byte. The command byte allows the ADC to either begin self calibration, start
conversion at a rate speciﬁed in the command byte or perform an immediate power down.
The register address command allows for reading and writing to the ADC registers.
The ADCs are conﬁgured to operate in ’single-cycle’ conversion mode, where they start
the conversion upon receiving a command-byte (with a start conversion instruction), and
goes to idle once completed. This allows the SCU to start the conversion simultaneously on
all the sensors by sending the command byte to all three ADC’s at the same time. Once a
conversion is started the ADC will use the time speciﬁed by the conversion rate to convert

Performing the measurement
Performing the measurements can be divided into three parts: Instructing the ADCs to start
conversion, reading the data from the ADCs and preparing the data for transmission to the
21
==PAGE:38==
CCU. The measuring process is only started when the CCU sends the command containing
the sample number and conversion rate. The maximum sample size is 20 samples, due to
limited RAM available on the PIC24F16kl401. One sample is one measurement-set (ms) for
each axis, and a measurement-set is one measurement after a ’Set’ pulse and one after a
’Reset’ pulse. It is therefore important to note that the eﬀective sample rate is at best half
the conversion rate.
General structure
The general structure of the measurement software is shown in ﬂow chart format in ﬁgure
3.10. Once a command to measure is received the software begins collecting the speciﬁed
amount of samples.
As each measurement-set should be taken as close to each other as possible, the Set/Re-
set measurements are taken one after another. Before a single measurement of a set is taken
the processor toggles the SR pins, making the S/R circuit perform the corresponding ’Set’
or ’Reset’ pulse. After an S/R pulse the processor waits for 20ms in order to left the mag-
netometer stabilise in the new state. After the S/R pulse the processor send the conversion
command corresponding to the conversion rate speciﬁed by the CCU.
Once the conversion is started, the processor will continuously check whether the conver-
sion is completed and the data is ready, this is shown in section 2 of the ﬂowchart. Checking
whether the data is ready, is done by pulling the Chip Select low on the ADC in question,
and if the ADC DOUT pin (SPI MISO line) is low the data is ready. If the DOUT pin is
high the data is not ready yet, and the next axis is examined. If the data is ready the ADC’s
data registers are read and the data stored in holding arrays. This check is performed until
data has been acquired from all axis, or until it has spent more than 1.5 second checking. If
more than 1.5 second has been spent checking an error has occurred, and the program skips
the axis on this iteration. Once it has acquired the data (or skipped some due to an error),
the program performs the next measurement in the set, or if the set is complete continues
to the next sample.
In section three on the ﬂowchart in ﬁgure 3.10, the program checks if the target sample
number has been reached or more is needed. If no more is needed the program proceeds to
process the data for sending back to the CCU.
The packing process includes calculating the oﬀset, described in section 3.1.1 and sub-
tracting the oﬀset from the ’Set’ measurement. This is done for each sample and all sample
are then averaged, and the result stored as 3 bytes in the send buﬀer. After all axis data
has been processed, the processors enable send-mode and is now ready to send the gathered
data to the CCU.

Chapter 4
Calibration & Test
4.1
Calibration
As the HMC sensor output is linear in the region +-2Gauss (ﬁgure 2.1), the sensor units
are calibrated assuming a linear behaviour.
To calibrate the sensors measurements of a
known reference is needed, the more precisely the reference is known the higher the resulting
accuracy will be. The best reference available is the earths magnetic ﬁeld, which is assumed
to be 50µT in Denmark. While this is hardly ideal, it is the best available and will be used.
Using the Earths magnetic ﬁeld as a reference provides only one immediate reference
point, and as at least two is needed for a linear calibration, the reference is measure twice
at opposite directions. This provides one measurement where the sensitive axis is pointing
along the local magnetic ﬁeld vector, and one where it is pointing opposite the local ﬁeld
vector. This results in the two equations:
50µT = a · m+ + b
(4.1a)
−50µT = a · m−+ b
(4.1b)
With a being the scale factor, b being the oﬀset, m+ being the measurement oriented along
the local ﬁeld vector, m−being the measurement oriented opposite the local ﬁeld vector, and
the ±50µT is the strength of the local ﬁeld. Once the two reference measurements (m+ and
m−) are gathered, the two equations can be solved for the scale (a) and oﬀset (b) calibration
factors. This is then repeated for all axis on all sensors.
4.1.1
Obtaining the reference points
Each sensor unit is calibrated individually, it is assembled and connected directly to a PC
through UART-USB converter. The CCU is bypassed as it only provides control over mul-
tiple sensor units.
The sensor units were calibrated immediately after one-another with
identical conﬁguration: to output the raw ADC data with an ADC conversion rate of 10sps
and ADC gain of 4. The data collection from the z-axis had to be redone, due to errors in
the way the data was printed. The process was redone within an hour of the other axis.
This should not impact the ﬁnal result, as earth magnetic ﬁeld can be regarded as constant
within short time-spans. All the data collection for the calibration was done at 24°C.
The alignment along the magnetic ﬁeld vector was done by hand, by slowly rotating the
sensor unit around one axis at a time, ﬁnding the local maximum each time by observing the
data. The process was repeated several times, using smaller and slower rotations each time.
While this process does not result in a perfect alignment for long, it does produce several

measuredField = cos(angleError) · fieldMagnitude

full ﬁeld. This is believed to be within the realm of possibility of measurement by hand,

4.2.1
Set/reset pulse test
To ensure the S/R circuit was providing the needed pulses to the HMC’s, the system was
setup up and an oscilloscope was used to monitor the voltage across the pulsing capacitors.
The system was instructed to measure, and the set and reset pulses was recorded.
The initial test (before the buﬀer was added), showed an unsatisfactory reset pulse. The
reset pulse shown in ﬁgure 4.2, did not reach the 4.5V as it was designed to do. The reset
pulse reaches -2V and maintains -2V for approximately 4µs. -2V across the S/R strap (1.5Ω)
results in only 1.33A, compared to the minimum 2A needed and 3A desired. The cause of
this was the N-channel MOSFET needing at least 4.5V to conduct the current needed for
the pulse. To remedy this the buﬀer was added to the circuit as described in section 3.1.1.
The set pulse (shown in ﬁgure 4.2), reaches 4V without the buﬀer, providing a current
of 2.67A. This is a bit lower than the desired 3A, but within the required current of 2-5A.
After adding the buﬀer to the circuit the ﬁnal pulses were within the desired parameters,
and are shown in ﬁgure 4.4. The reset pulse reaches -4.8V, resulting in a peak current of 3.2A,
and the set pulse reaches 4V with a peak current of 2.66A. Both pulses reach the minimum
of 2A. The Set pulse does not maintain a voltage above 3V for the 2µs recommended, but
testing showed it to be functional, and was thus not improved due to time constraints.

Detection range
To test the practical applications and sensitivity, the system was setup on a table and
measurements were taken as a magnetic source was placed at varying distances from the
system. The system was secured to a laboratory table and aligned by hand.
Three diﬀerent sources were placed in front of the system: an iron cylinder weighing
1694g, a strong magnet and a paperclip. The system was set to measure with a conversion
rate of 10sps and 5sets per sample. All three test were done in succession over the course of
one hour, they were all done without changing or moving the system. Only the sources were
moved/introduced to the system, and no other objects were moved during the test. The
system was powered from the USB port of the laptop used for data logging.
The source was moved closer to the system in discreet steps, and the system was allowed
to settle for two measurements (approximately 3 seconds) before the measurement result
were logged. The system was left to measure at least ﬁve samples before the source was
moved. The data points are converted to µT has calibration constants applied, and the
local gradient (without the source) is subtracted. Subtracting the local gradient is done to
visualise the change as the source is moved.

Chapter 5
Discussion and Improvements
5.1
Hardware Performance
5.1.1
Noise and resolution
The ﬁnal resolution of the system is 2.5nT. This is observed in the testing, with the minimum
step size taken during the magnetic shield test was 2.5nT.
The calibration and testing showed the ADC’s having an output that was 10x higher than
anticipated. This 10x factor was not found documented in the datasheet, but was used as it
was necessary. Not using it would have caused the calibration constants be 10x higher. Due
to the lack of temperature compensation of the sensor data, the system will only function
accurately at 24°C, which is the temperature it was calibrated at.
The noise for diﬀerent conversion/MS settings is listed in table 4.4. If a sample rate of
1Hz is desired the lowest noise is gained at a conversion rate of 10sps and with a 3MS aver-
age. These settings have an inherent noise with a standard deviation of 1.87nT, at a sample
rate of 0.97Hz. These setting should ensure that 99.7% the noise of the system falls within
±5.61nT (3 std deviations) of the measured ﬁeld. The peak-to-peak noise at these settings
is measured to 10.71nT. While not evident under the settings described here, the y-axis
measurements on SU2 showed to be signiﬁcantly more noise than its SU1 counterpart. This
along with outliers in the measurements might indicate that this sensor is partly defective.
It is interesting to note that the 60sps conversion rate performs worse than the 120sps
conversion rate. The two fast conversion rates also end up with similar eﬀective sps, 8.21Hz
and 9.48Hz for 60sps and 120sps respectively. These eﬀective sample rates shows a major
weakness of the system: time lost in communication. With a fast conversion rate the system
spends more time waiting and transmitting data than it does measuring.
The CCU can in principle manage up to 18 sensor-units, limited by the SPI communi-
cation needing a GPIO pin for each sensor unit. The power circuit needs to be upgraded
before this number is reached, as the current power circuit only supports up to 4 SUs.

5.2.1
software improvements
Currently the SSU checks whether the ADC’s have completed the conversion continuously
after the command to start the conversion is sent. This is not necessary as the time until
conversion completion is known. The activity on the SPI lines can be a source of noise in
the system, it is however not know to which extend this is an issue, and it has as such not
been a priority.
The current iteration of the system requires the CCU to be reprogrammed if a change of
settings is desired. A future software iterations should allow a user to change these setting
via an interface, for example could the serial interface be used to send commands to the

Add a feature to subtract a ’zero-ﬁeld’ from future measurements until reset. The user
would place the system in the ﬁeld desired to be ignored, and by a simple command the sys-
tem should measure and save this ﬁeld for later use. This will aid in making the presented

5.2.2
Hardware improvements
A potential source of noise is the magnetometers proximity to the power-lines supplying
them. This is reinforced by the higher noise on the Z-axis sensor in the test. The Z-axis
sensor have the power lines running close to far more than the other two axis. Redesigning
the PCB to increase the distance from power lines to sensor is a way to mitigate this issue.
Another improvement would be to replace components close to the sensor with non magnetic
versions. If the components used produce a ﬁeld it will manifest as an oﬀset in the result. If
the components are magnetically soft, and change they magnetisation over time, this oﬀset
will change. As long as the oﬀset’s changes slower than the sample rate, the set/reset mea-
surements should remove this oﬀset.
The S/R circuit should be improved, to produce a better set pulse to ensure the system
operates at equal standards for both set and reset measurements. This could be done by
removing the BJT transistor from the S/R circuit, and use the buﬀer to provide a 0-6V input
to the MOSFET. This was not done in the prototype as removing the BJT would require
some diﬃcult PCB modiﬁcation or complete redesign.
The ﬁnal version of the sensor unit PCB should include the SCU and the buﬀer. The
Sensor units should still be discrete entities from the CCU, in order to allow for modularity,
and the potential for custom sensor arrangements. Adding a thermometer to the sensor
units would allow the system to compensate for the HMC1001’s sensitivity drift due to
temperature.
The thermometer would be controlled by the SCU, and temperature data
could be acquired while the ADC conversion was in progress, thereby not slowing the system
down with more SPI communication. This would slightly slow the system, by adding another
data processing step.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The project found the HMC1001 AMR magnetometer from Honeywell to be the best candi-
date for a small gradient measurement system, based on its high sensitivity and low weight.
The electronic systems necessary for supporting the HMC1001 were designed to ensure no
bottleneck in the system would lower the magnetometer performance.
The eﬀective detection range of a theoretic system was calculated and shown in ﬁgure
2.2. Furthermore an Algorithm based on Monte Carlo approximation was developed to es-
timate the direction and distance to a magnetic source in two dimensions. The algorithm
could estimate the location of the simulated source with respect to the system. The location
estimation when operating using data with white noise with a standard deviation of 20nT,
produced an error in estimation with a standard deviation of 0.06m, or <13.5% of the range
to the source. Running the Monte Carlo algorithm for diﬀerent sensor arrangements showed
a decrease in estimation error for an increase in sensor count.
Using the HMC1001 as the magnetometer, a 3-axis sensor unit were designed using the
PIC24F16KL401 as the controller. A PCB was designed to hold the magnetometers, the
ADCs and other support electronics, for the prototype sensor units. Two prototype sensor
units were constructed, and integrated into the system. The system was designed to sup-
port multiple sensor units, and to control the sensor units via SPI. The sensor units accept
commands specifying ADC conversion rate and sample size to be averaged.
The Set/Reset feature of the HMC1001 was used to compensate for oﬀsets while measuring.
The system have inherent noise with standard deviation of 1.87nT with measurements at
0.97Hz. Further system performance at a sample rate of 0.97Hz (listed values are for worst
performing axis):
• Resolution of 2.5nT.
• Maximum noise of 10.71nT peak-to-peak.
• Maximum drift of 15.16nT over 2.22 hours, after initial warm-up.
The systems temperature drift were not determined, but will be no better than the magne-
tometer’s (HMC1001) drift of 0.30%/°C.
The system prototype weigh 438g, though is unwieldy due to its lack of support structure.
