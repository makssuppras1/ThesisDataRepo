Abstract
The transition to sustainable energy systems is crucial for reducing greenhouse gas emissions and mitigating cli-
mate change. Methanol, a versatile chemical and fuel, can be synthesized from CO2 and green hydrogen, offering a
promising alternative to fossil-based production. This thesis investigates the feasibility of e-methanol production using
biogenic CO2 from the Vesthimmerland Biogas facility and hydrogen produced via alkaline water electrolysis (AWE),
powered by renewable energy sources. A detailed process model was developed in Aspen Plus to optimize methanol
synthesis, focusing on carbon efficiency, heat integration, and reactor performance. Seven production scenarios were
analyzed, including a fully grid-supplied system, a grid-supported hybrid renewable system, and an off-grid hybrid
renewable system, testing different shares of wind and solar installed capacity. The economic results show that grid-
supported hybrid configurations achieve the lowest methanol production cost when considering capital expenditures,
operational expenses, and electricity costs over the plant’s lifetime. Conversely, the fully grid-dependent scenario had
the highest production cost due to its reliance on fluctuating electricity prices. The environmental impact of the dif-
ferent scenarios was evaluated through a Life Cycle Assessment. The off-grid renewable configurations noted lower
overall environmental impacts compared to grid-connected scenarios, despite the bigger scale of their systems in order
to cover the system’s energy requirements. Lastly, a prospective LCA suggests that grid-connected scenarios could
become even more environmentally favorable as the renewable share in the electricity mix increases in the future.
The overall findings demonstrate the potential of this alternative methanol production process to contribute to a more
sustainable chemical industry, provided that energy integration strategies, technological advancements, and supportive
policies continue to drive its development and deployment.

Introduction
One of the most urgent challenges of these days is global warming, a crisis largely driven by the rise in greenhouse gas
(GHG) emissions since the pre-industrial era, with human activities being the primary cause of the significant climate
changes observed over the past century [1]. The increasing and accelerating emissions of carbon dioxide, the most
dominant contributor of greenhouse gases, have resulted in a reported concentration of 425 ppm in the atmosphere [2].
This means a 52.5% increase from the level in the pre-industrial era (i.e. 278 ppm) [3] and a 14.7% increase from the
year 2000 (i.e. 369.7 ppm) [2].
To address this crucial matter, the Paris Agreement was adopted in 2015 and was signed by 196 countries, aiming to
limit the global rise in temperature to less than 2 ◦C [4]. As a result of this agreement, countries have embraced re-
newable energy as a vital way to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions. This international movement towards sustainable
energy systems is illustrated by initiatives such as the Green Deal of the EU and the proposed New Deal of the US [5].
Denmark’s commitment to sustainability began with the 1973 oil crisis, which sparked the country’s shift to renewable
energy. The nation has greatly reduced the relationship between economic growth and energy consumption over time
through broad political agreement, public-private sector cooperation, and strong social involvement [6]. Denmark
aims to have all main sectors, such as manufacturing, transportation, heating, and electricity, completely powered by
renewable energy until 2050 [7].
According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), in order to achieve net-zero emissions in the
industrial sector, a wide range of mitigation strategies should be adopted. Enhancing energy and material efficiency,
implementing circular material flows, and transforming industrial methods in conjunction with low- and zero-GHG
energy sources such as renewable electricity, hydrogen, and green fuels are some of these [8]. However, the increasing
global energy demand, projected to potentially double by 2050 [9], coupled with the continued dominance of fossil
fuels in the energy, chemical, and transportation sectors as reported by the U.S. Energy Information Administration
(EIA) [10], intensifies the challenge of reducing emissions. Among industrial sectors, the chemical industry ranks as
the most significant energy consumer and the third-largest industry subsector in direct CO2 emissions. Within this
sector, methanol production holds a significant position, contributing to 27.1% of the direct CO2 emissions generated
by primary chemical manufacturing processes [11].
Methanol is one of the most widely used and valuable chemicals in industry, serving as both a fuel and a significant
chemical feedstock. It is a liquid energy carrier with applications in various sectors including transportation, steel
production and the chemical industry [12]. It is commonly used as a fuel additive, a potential drop-in fuel replacement
[13], and an industrial solvent, while also acting as a platform molecule for the synthesis of heavier alcohols, dimethyl
ether, gasoline, olefins, formaldehyde, acetic acid, methyl tert-butyl ether, and other complex chemicals [14][15].
Methanol is traditionally produced on an industrial scale from syngas, a mixture of CO, CO2, and H2, using a
copper-based catalyst under high temperature (200–300°C) and pressure (50–100 bar) [15]. Syngas is primarily ob-
tained through steam reforming of natural gas or coal gasification. This fossil-based production process is highly
carbon-intensive, emitting approximately 0.6–1.6 kg CO2 per kg of methanol for natural gas-derived syngas and up
to 3.8 kg CO2 per kg of methanol for coal-based processes [9][16]. As a result, the necessity of introducing and
advancing greener methanol production processes has become increasingly evident to mitigate environmental impacts
and reduce dependence on fossil resources.
Over the last two decades, the utilization of captured and purified industrial CO2 as a feedstock for the production
of methanol has received great attention. According to the process stoichiometry, the synthesis of 1 ton of methanol
requires 1.373 tons of CO2, of which 0.87 tons is directly integrated into the methanol molecule. This represents
one of the largest potential CO2 uptakes among chemical processes [15]. Methanol is synthesized through a process
called CO2 hydrogenation, where CO2 is mixed with hydrogen and undergoes a catalytic reaction to produce the final
product.

Hydrogen is considered a key energy carrier for delivering sustainable energy and it can be produced through a variety
of methods, with water electrolysis being one of the most promising ones in terms of low-carbon emissions [17]. This
hydrogen production pathway forms the foundation of Power-to-X (P2X) technologies, which enables the conversion
and storage of energy into carbon-neutral chemical energy carriers. One of the most promising applications of P2X is
the synthesis of e-methanol, a sustainable methanol variant using green hydrogen derived from renewable electricity.
The further conversion of hydrogen into an energy carrier such as methanol offers significant advantages in terms of
storage and transportation over pure hydrogen [18]. Moreover, in comparison with the gaseous energy carries, liquid
fuels like methanol pose fewer challenges in transportation both from a safety perspective and in terms of existing
infrastructure requirements [9].
The effective implementation of Power-to-X (P2X) technologies is significantly dependent on the incorporation of re-
newable energy sources (RES), such as wind and solar. These energy sources supply the electricity required for water
electrolysis, guaranteeing that hydrogen and its subsequent transformation into fuels such as methanol are considered
green. By utilizing surplus electricity from intermittent renewables, P2X systems offer an effective way to stabilize
the energy grid and enable long-term energy storage. [9].
As the global energy transition accelerates, countries leading in renewable energy deployment are seizing the oppor-
tunity to integrate Power-to-X technologies into their energy systems. Denmark has positioned itself at the forefront
of this shift, and while its current hydrogen consumption is quite limited, there are ambitious plans of scaling up P2X
projects and expanding green hydrogen production by 2030 as a replacement for fossil fuel-based methods. To make
P2X’s large scale-up and competitiveness possible, the Danish government has outlined four objectives, including
cost-effective CO2 reduction, regulatory support, enhanced energy system integration, and export expansion to con-
tribute to emissions reductions beyond its borders [19].
The growing industrial and scientific interest in alternative methanol production pathways and Power-to-X technolo-
gies has driven extensive research into CO2 hydrogenation using green hydrogen for e-methanol synthesis. This
process has been the focus of design optimizations to ensure high conversion rates, selectivity, and minimal energy
losses. Battaglia et al. [14] developed a comprehensive process model integrating carbon capture, electrolysis, and
methanol synthesis, applying thermal integration strategies. Similarly, Khojasteh-Salkuyeh et al.[20] conducted a de-
tailed process simulation of CO2-based methanol production, evaluating direct CO2 hydrogenation, dry reforming,
and tri-reforming pathways. Their results showed that while CO2 hydrogenation demonstrated promising thermal
efficiency, its viability was highly dependent on the availability of low-carbon electricity. Further advancements in
system integration were explored by Lonis et al. [21], who assessed two Power-to-Methanol systems using alkaline
and solid oxide electrolysis, concluding that alkaline electrolysis is currently more viable.
Apart from the technical advancements, it is essential to assess the economic viability of this process to ensure that
the production process of e-methanol remains competitive with conventional production pathways. Understanding
the economic feasibility of e-methanol production requires evaluating capital and operational costs, energy consump-
tion, and potential revenue streams generated through this process. Over the past years, numerous techno-economic
analysis (TEA) studies have been conducted on renewable methanol synthesis via CO2 hydrogenation, assessing its
cost competitiveness, scalability, and economic feasibility under various process configurations and market conditions
[4][9][22][23].
Another important aspect to be considered is the environmental impact of e-methanol production. Quantifying the
environmental benefits of such a process can provide valuable information on its long-term sustainability. Through an
LCA (Life Cycle Assessment), the overall carbon footprint, resource consumption, and potential emissions reductions
of e-methanol production can be systematically evaluated. Chen et al. [24] and Liu et al. [25] performed comparative
cradle-to-gate LCA assessments, demonstrating the high environmental burden of coal-based methanol production
compared to other pathways. Studies on alternative methanol synthesis routes [26][27] have shown that CO2-derived
methanol can achieve negative emissions when coupled with low-carbon hydrogen production, particularly through
CO2 hydrogenation and biogas reforming. However, the overall environmental impact of these processes is highly
dependent on the electricity mix used for electrolysis, with renewable energy playing a crucial role in minimizing
emissions.

While previous studies have extensively explored process design optimizations, economic feasibility, and life cycle
assessment of e-methanol production, most of these analyses have been conducted independently, focusing on spe-
cific aspects rather than a holistic evaluation. This thesis aims to bridge these gaps by integrating those aspects into
a unified framework. Conducted in collaboration with BioCirc, this study utilizes biogenic CO2 sourced from the
Vesthimmerland Biogas facility, which is part of BioCirc’s operations, located in western Denmark. The hydrogen
required for the CO2 hydrogenation process was assumed to be produced via an alkaline water electrolyzer (AWE).
The methanol production process was designed and modeled, with a focus on carbon conversion efficiency, energy
efficiency, and reactor operating conditions. A detailed process simulation was developed to analyze how tempera-
ture, pressure, and the H2-to-CO2 ratio impact methanol yield and overall system performance. Additionally, heat
integration was incorporated to enhance thermal efficiency and minimize energy losses, optimizing the system’s en-
ergy consumption. Multiple e-methanol production scenarios were evaluated, including grid-connected and off-grid
renewable energy configurations. Additionally, an economic analysis was conducted to assess the feasibility of these
different production scenarios. The analysis examined various cost factors, such as capital investment, operational
expenses, and electricity costs, to determine the minimum production cost of e-methanol in order for the project to be
viable. Lastly, a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) was applied to evaluate the environmental impact of the assessed e-
methanol production setups across several impact categories. Beyond this, a prospective LCA was carried out, aiming
to investigate how future energy transitions, technological advancements, and policy developments may influence the
environmental performance of the production process.

Methodology
2.1
Process description and modeling
A primary objective of this project is the design of a system that utilizes biogenic CO2, along with green hydrogen,
to achieve the sustainable production of methanol. For this purpose, the process route was modeled in Aspen Plus
Process Simulator. More specifically, the simulation includes multiple process steps, such as CO2 vaporization, water
electrolysis, a methanol synthesis loop, and a purification section to produce a high-purity product. The overall process
was designed to prioritize resource efficiency, heat integration, and recycling streams. The methanol plant is assumed
to operate continuously for 8760 hours per year. The basis of the system’s design is set to be the utilization of 50,000
tons of CO2 on a yearly basis. This section delineates the essential steps in the process, with each subsection specifying
the individual operations and their corresponding contributions to the system.
2.1.1
Overview of the process
The methanol synthesis process commences with the conversion of liquid CO2 into its gaseous form through vaporiza-
tion. Simultaneously, hydrogen is being produced from water via electrolysis, powered by renewable energy sources.
These two streams are mixed to create a syngas mixture, which functions as the feedstock for the methanol synthesis.
The crude methanol generated in this phase is subjected to purification to meet high-purity standards. A simplified
visual representation of the overall process is provided in Figure 1 below.
Figure 1: Flow chart of the systems’s main processes
2.1.2
CO2 vaporization
The vaporization process facilitates the conversion of biogenic CO2 from its initial liquid to a gaseous form. This way,
the resulting stream is in the right phase for its subsequent mixing and reaction with hydrogen. The CO2 used in this
system is obtained from BioCirc’s biogas facilities, where it has been captured and liquified before being incorporated
into the methanol production process.
In this procedure, liquid CO2 is introduced into the system at a flow rate of 1.585 kg/s, with an initial temperature of
-27 ◦C and a pressure of 16 bar. The liquid stream first passes through a pump with 80% efficiency, which increases
its pressure to 30 bar [28]. Following that, the stream enters a phase-changing heat exchanger, where it is vaporized
by raising its temperature to -5°C while sustaining a pressure of 30 bar. The resulting CO2 stream maintains a mass
flow rate of 1.585 kg/s, ensuring a steady input for the upcoming processes.

Electrochemical water electrolysis technologies are classified based on the type of electrolyte used and their operating

AWE has been the dominant commercial technology for over a century and operates at low temperatures between
30°C and 90°C. One of its main advantages compared to the other technologies is its relatively low investment and
operational costs, as it does not require noble metals for cell fabrication [32]. However, its performance is limited by
low current densities and an energy efficiency ranging between 62% and 82% [33].
PEM electrolysis has been commercially available for approximately 25 years, and it is mainly used in specialized
industrial applications [34]. It operates at higher current densities, with efficiencies ranging from 67% to 82%. Its
typical cell lifespan can extend up to 80,000 hours, making it the most durable electrolysis technology. However, its
reliance on noble metal catalysts for electrode surfaces significantly increases capital costs [30].

SOEC is a high-temperature technology, operating between 500°C and 900°C, where water is supplied in the form of
steam. An advantage of this technology is that a portion of the required energy can be provided as heat rather than
electricity, allowing for efficiencies of up to 92% [35]. Additionally, SOEC is unique among electrolysis technologies
as it can operate in a reversible mode, functioning both as an electrolyzer and a fuel cell [36]. However, its typical
lifetime is limited to less than 20,000 hours, due to the degradation of catalytic performance because of the high tem-
peratures [30].
AWE was selected for this thesis because of its technological maturity, cost efficiency, and the fact that it is suitable
for large-scale production. This study puts emphasis on scalability and economic viability in hydrogen production for
e-methanol synthesis, which makes AWE the most practical and well-established choice.
For the modeling of the electrolysis process, water was assumed to enter the electrolyzer at 30 bar and an ambient
temperature of 25 ◦C. The unit was modeled as an RStoic reactor operating at 90 ◦C with no pressure drop, followed by
a separator to split the hydrogen and oxygen streams. The electrolyzer was assumed to operate with a 70% efficiency.
After electrolysis, the hydrogen and the oxygen stream have a molar flow rate of 0.104 kmol/sec, and 0.052 kmol/sec
respectively.
2.1.4
Gas mixing and compression
In this process, vapor-phase CO2 and hydrogen, both being at a pressure of 30 bar, are combined to form a synthesis
gas (syngas). The produced syngas serves as a feedstock for the upcoming methanol synthesis reactions. Subsequent
to the mixing phase, the syngas is discharged at a temperature of 49◦C and then directed through a compressor, where
its pressure is increased to 90 bar to meet the requirements of the methanol synthesis loop. The high-pressure syngas
stream exits the compressor at a temperature of 161◦C as a result of compression heating. To model the compressor’s
performance, a polytropic efficiency of 80% was assumed, following the ASME method for compression efficiency
calculations. This approach provides a more accurate representation of realistic energy losses.
2.1.5
Methanol synthesis loop
In the methanol synthesis loop, the syngas reacts within a catalytic reactor to produce methanol. At this step the raw
feedstocks are converted into the desired product through a series of thermochemical reactions. The unreacted syngas
is recycled within the loop and mixed with new syngas to obtain the reactor’s feed, enhancing the overall efficiency
and sustainability of the system.
Within the methanol synthesis reactor, several reactions occur concurrently, such as the formation of methanol from
CO2 and from CO. The latter is being produced during the process and recirculated within the system. Since it
is assumed that the reactions in this system are in equilibrium, the reverse water-gas shift reaction should also be
included, as it influences the overall equilibrium due to the presence of its reactants in the process. Those reactions
are presented as follows:

Concerning the modeling of the methanol synthesis loop, the reactor feed is heated to 210 ◦C before entering the
reactor at a pressure of 90 bar. The reactor was modeled as an equilibrium reactor (REquil), with a pressure drop
of 3 bars while the outlet temperature was specified at 240 ◦C. The model assumes that the reactor operates at ideal
equilibrium, without any divergence from the theoretical equilibrium point. In this setup, the temperature approach is
0 ◦C, meaning that equilibrium is achieved precisely at the specified outlet temperature. This guarantees that the outlet
stream composition corresponds to the optimal outcome for methanol production under the given conditions. It should
be mentioned that reaction kinetics were not included in this model, as the simulations aimed to attain thermodynamic
equilibrium in order to assess the highest possible conversion and product composition under the specified parameters.
The output of the reactor contains a mixture of gases with molar compositions of roughly 57% H2, 18% CO2, 11%
H2O, 11% CH3OH and 1% CO. Recycling the unreacted reactants is crucial for achieving a high methanol yield.
Therefore, the stream after the reactor is cooled to 30 ◦C and enters a flash (2-phase separator) to separate the unre-
acted gases from the products. The temperature is carefully chosen to maximize the condensation of methanol into
the liquid phase, preventing its return to the reactor which could disrupt the equilibrium. Merely 1.6% of methanol
leaves with the unreacted gases, while 98.4% is headed to the purification stage. The liquid stream after the separation
mainly consists of methanol and water, along with a minor fraction of 2% CO2.
The gas stream after the separation is compressed to the reactor’s input pressure (90 bar) and then mixed with the
fresh syngas in the feed. Additionally, a small fraction (1%) of the flow is purged, to prevent the accumulation of inert
gases in the process. The purge stream consists of 75% and 23% mole fractions of H2 and CO2 respectively, while
the methanol losses through this stream are negligible.
2.1.6
Methanol purification
After the methanol synthesis loop, the crude methanol stream contains impurities such as water, unreacted gases
and trace by-products. To achieve the desired purity, a purification section is employed, which usually consists of dis-
tillation columns that facilitate the separation of different compounds with respect to their different boiling points [38].
This model employs two fractional distillation columns (RadFrac), after dropping the pressure to 11 bar. The first col-
umn, referred to as stabilizer, is intended to remove the residual CO2 and any remaining gases after the loop. It utilizes
a partial vapor condenser at a pressure of 10 bar and a kettle-type reboiler. The gases, being the lighter components,
are obtained on the top of the column, whereas methanol and water are collected as liquids at the bottom. To achieve
an optimal separation, a design specification was created in order to adjust the bottoms rate in the column so that the
condenser operates at 30 ◦C. This temperature was chosen to enable the use of cooling water as the utility. The reflux
ratio of the stabilizer was set at 1. The gas concentration at the bottom outlet is reduced to below 10−9 molar fraction,
while the methanol content in the distillate stream reaches approximately 2% molar fraction.
The liquid mixture of methanol and water, which is exiting the reboiler at a temperature of 148 ◦C, is then headed to the
second distillation column. In this column, water, as the heaviest component, is collected at the bottom, whereas pure
methanol (99.99% mole fraction) is obtained at the top under conditions of 1 bar and 64 ◦C, with a total condenser.
These conditions are suitable for storing methanol as a liquid at atmospheric pressure, without the need for further
processing. Lastly, a design specification was implemented to adjust the reflux ratio in order to minimize the methanol
content in the bottom stream to just 1%.

2.2
Economic Analysis
2.2.1
Overview of the economic analysis
The economic analysis focuses on evaluating the financial feasibility of the proposed methanol production system.
This section applies techno-economic principles to analyze capital and operational expenditures, revenues as well as
the overall economic performance of the process. The analysis examines multiple production scenarios, that are an-
alyzed in the following section. Each scenario was evaluated in terms of capital investment, operational costs, and
the economic performance indicator of the Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM). All calculations were conducted in
Danish Krone (DKK), using a conversion rate of 1 EUR to 7.46 DKK wherever applicable.
2.2.2
System Analysis
This section describes the different methanol production scenarios evaluated in this study, each designed to meet the
plant’s power requirements through different electricity-sourcing strategies. In certain scenarios, a renewable hybrid
park, consisting of onshore wind turbines and solar PV, was integrated, where different configurations and renewable
energy shares were tested. While all scenarios assume a consistent input of 50,000 tons of CO2 per year, leading
to a fixed methanol output of 34,286 tons per year, the variations in electricity supply configurations influence both
the sustainability classification and economic performance of the system. It should be noted that for all the scenarios
the methanol plant and the accompanying units are projected to be built in Vesthimmerland Municipality, located in
western Denmark. The assessed scenarios are presented thoroughly below.
Fully Grid-Supplied system (Scenario 1)
In this scenario, the methanol plant’s energy requirements are met entirely by electricity from the Danish national grid.
The plant’s total annual electricity consumption is 319,554 MWh, with an assumed 8,760 operating hours per year,
reflecting continuous, uninterrupted operation. No planned shutdowns or maintenance periods are accounted for in
this assessment, meaning the facility is modeled as running at full capacity year-round.
A crucial factor in this scenario is whether the produced methanol can be classified as a green fuel, which is closely
linked to the respective classification of the hydrogen utilized in its synthesis. According to EU regulations on renew-
able fuels of non-biological origin [41], hydrogen qualifies as green only if the electricity used for its production comes
from a grid that meets strict sustainability criteria. Specifically, the regulation states that a hydrogen producer must be
located in a bidding zone where the average share of renewable electricity exceeded 90% in the previous calendar year.
The proposed methanol plant is located in bidding zone DK1, for which the average share of renewable electricity in
the grid in 2023 was 82% [42]. This means that under current conditions, hydrogen produced using Danish grid elec-
tricity does not meet the classification for green hydrogen, thereby affecting the sustainability status of the methanol.
However, projections from the International Energy Agency (IEA) indicate that by 2030, the share of variable re-
newable electricity in the Danish national grid is expected to reach 117%, mainly due to the expansion of offshore
wind projects [43]. Based on this forecast, it is assumed that by 2028, when the methanol plant is expected to begin
operations, the renewable electricity share in DK1 will exceed 90%, allowing the hydrogen—and consequently the
methanol—to be classified as green.
This assumption has significant implications for both the regulatory compliance and the economic feasibility of this
scenario. If the projected increase in renewable energy share in the Danish grid takes place, the plant could qualify for
green fuel certifications, making it eligible for incentives and premium markets that require sustainability credentials.
On the other hand, if the grid’s renewable share does not reach the required level, alternative measures such as Power
Purchase Agreements (PPAs) [44] with renewable energy suppliers may be needed to ensure compliance, which could
have an impact on the viability of the project.

Grid-supported Hybrid Renewable System (Scenarios 2A, 2B, 2C)
In Scenarios 2A, 2B, and 2C, the methanol plant functions at full capacity, same as in Scenario 1, resulting in an
annual power usage of 319,554 MWh, based on an expected 8,760 operational hours per year. The difference is that
this system does no depend solely on grid electricity, but rather uses renewable energy sources to directly power the
electrolyzer and methanol production unit. Due to the varying nature of renewable energy, there are periods when the
hybrid park cannot entirely satisfy the plant’s electricity requirements. In such instances, the grid provides the addi-
tional electricity needed for continuous operation. Conversely, when renewable energy surpasses the plant’s electrical
requirement, the excess electricity cannot be stored and is thus curtailed. The management of the curtailed energy and
its potential utilization was not examined in this study.
The three assessed scenarios in this section, represent three different configurations of the hybrid park. The scenarios
differ based on the installed capacity ratio between onshore wind and solar PV. Each configuration influences the
extent to which the plant relies on grid electricity or experiences curtailment and are presented below:
• Scenario 2A: Wind-Dominant Configuration – 70% installed wind capacity and 30% installed solar PV capacity.
• Scenario 2B: Balanced Configuration – 50% installed wind capacity and 50% installed solar PV capacity.
• Scenario 2C: Solar-Dominant Configuration – 30% installed wind capacity and 70% installed solar PV capacity.
Off-Grid Hybrid Renewable System (Scenarios 3A, 3B, 3C)
In Scenarios 3A, 3B, and 3C, the methanol plant including the electrolyzer functions in an off-grid setup, relying
exclusively on power produced by the hybrid renewable park. Compared to the previous systems, the specific one is
not functioning at a constant full capacity. The plant operates with a dynamic load, adjusting its input and therefore its
production rate according to the hourly availability of wind and solar energy. When renewable generation adequately
satisfies the electrical requirements of the electrolyzer and methanol plant, the system functions at full capacity. How-
ever, when the availability of renewable resources is lower, the plant modifies its input of CO2 and water as well as its
methanol output to align with the available generated power.
Similar to the Grid-Supported Hybrid Renewable System, this setup also experiences curtailment. The extent of
curtailment depends on the renewable capacity mix and the overall alignment between energy generation and plant
demand. An important constraint for this system is the minimum plant operation load. This threshold was set at 10%
of the nominal capacity, based on projections provided from Campion [46]. This means that if the available renewable
electricity is insufficient to maintain at least 10% plant load, the system cannot operate at that hour. It ensures that
the methanol plant runs within technically feasible limits, avoiding inefficiencies or suboptimal operation at very low
loads.
• Scenario 3A: Wind-Dominant Configuration – 70% installed wind capacity and 30% installed solar PV capacity.
• Scenario 3B: Balanced Configuration – 50% installed wind capacity and 50% installed solar PV capacity.
• Scenario 3C: Solar-Dominant Configuration – 30% installed wind capacity and 70% installed solar PV capacity.

2.2.3
Optimization of Renewable Sizing
To determine the optimal installed renewable capacity for the assessed systems, a structured approach was employed
to minimize the Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM) in each scenario. LCOM represents the breakeven selling price
of methanol, ensuring that all expenses over the plant’s lifetime are covered. The calculation of the LCOM was con-
ducted through a detailed cashflow analysis which is described in chapter 2.2.7. The optimization process was applied
to both the grid-supported hybrid scenarios (2A, 2B, 2C) and the off-grid scenarios (3A, 3B, 3C).
For the grid-connected hybrid scenarios (2A, 2B, 2C), the optimization process involved testing various installed
renewable capacities for each scenario, evaluating their impact on LCOM. The renewable mix (wind-to-solar ratio)
was fixed according to each scenario’s definition, and the configuration that resulted in the lowest LCOM was selected.
For the off-grid scenarios (3A, 3B, 3C), the optimization process was more complex due to the dynamic operation of
the methanol plant. Unlike the aforementioned scenarios, the plant load is not fixed at 100%, and the sizing of both the
methanol plant and electrolyzer was allowed to vary. The optimization was carried out using a scaling factor, which
proportionally increased the methanol plant and electrolyzer capacity. For each scaling factor, the capacity of the
hybrid park that produced the required methanol output of 34,286 tons per year, was identified. Once the renewable
capacity corresponding to each plant sizing was determined, the LCOM was evaluated across the different plant sizes,
and the configuration that yielded the lowest LCOM for each off-grid scenario was selected.

2.2.4
Capital Expenditures (CAPEX)
Capital expenditures or capital costs refer to the expenses related to the construction of a new plant or the modifica-
tions of an existing one. The Total Capital Investment (TCI) represents the total assets required to establish a plant,
comprising of two primary components, namely the Fixed Capital Investment (FCI) and the Working Capital (WC),
as detailed by Towler [47].
Fixed Capital Investment (FCI) is the total cost necessary for the design, construction, and installation of a chemical
facility, including related alterations to the plant site. It can be divided into the following categories:
1. Inside Battery Limits (ISBL):
• Major Equipment Costs: Procurement and installation of process equipment such as reactors, distillation
columns, heat exchangers, pumps, compressors, and furnaces.
• Bulk Material Costs: Piping, valves, wiring, insulation, instrumentation, and structural components.
• Civil Works: Foundations, roads, sewer systems, and other site-specific requirements.
• Installation Labor and Supervision: Skilled and unskilled labor for equipment assembly, commission-
ing, and testing.
2. Outside Battery Limits (OSBL):
• Electrical Substations, Transformers, and Power Distribution Systems: Includes main substations,
transformers, and power lines.
• Utilities: Water treatment, cooling systems, and steam generation.
• Storage Facilities: Raw materials and finished products storage.
• Buildings: Administration, maintenance, and laboratory buildings.
• Site Preparation, Landscaping, and Security Installations: Includes fencing, gatehouses, and landscap-
ing.
OSBL costs are typically estimated as a percentage of ISBL costs, ranging from 20% to 50%, depending on
the project scope and infrastructure requirements. They are divided into engineering and construction costs and
contingency charges as shown below. In this project, 30% of the aggregate ISBL and OSBL costs were allocated
to engineering and construction and 10% to contingencies.
3. Engineering and Construction Costs:
• Detailed Engineering and Process Design: Includes the creation of process flow diagrams and equipment
layout.
• Procurement and Project Management: Management of materials and logistics for the construction.
• Construction Supervision and Administrative Charges: Supervision of labor, equipment installation,
and project progress.
• Temporary Facilities and Site Preparation Costs: Infrastructure used only during the construction
phase.
4. Contingency Charges: Contingency charges account for uncertainties and unforeseen expenses during the
project lifecycle.
Working Capital (WC) refers to the financial reserve necessary to sustain daily operations, including raw material in-
ventory, utilities, and spare parts. In this project, working capital is estimated to be 15% of the FCI, which guarantees
adequate liquidity for the operation and management of short-term financial requirements.

2.3
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) LCA is a way of quantifying the environmental impact of a product/process/system
throughout its life Cycle [64]. Resource use, emissions, and environmental burdens are analyzed in a data-driven man-
ner to evaluate sustainability and identify potential improvements. The current LCA study is conducted in accordance
with the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) standards, specifically ISO 14040:2006 [65] and ISO
14044:2006 [66].
Those standards include four key phases, which structure the assessment process:
1. Goal and scope definition: It defines the aim of the study, including the comparative evaluation of functionally
equivalent systems, the functional unit, and the system boundaries.
2. Inventory Analysis: Involves the collection of data related to input and output flows of materials and energy
within the system.
3. Impact Assessment: This phase employs characterization models to convert inventory data into potential envi-
ronmental impacts. It quantifies the resulting environmental burdens and compares the assessed scenarios using
standardized impact assessment methodologies across different impact categories.
4. Interpretation: The final phase where results from the inventory and impact assessment are analyzed. It seeks
to identify key environmental hotspots, compare alternative methanol production pathways, and make recom-
mendations for sustainable improvements.
2.3.1
Goal and scope definition
The goal of this study is to compare the environmental performance of the different system configurations for methanol
production. As analyzed in 2.2.2, the primary variation between those systems is the energy source used to power the
process. The methanol synthesis pathway remains identical across all scenarios, but the electricity mix varies.
A summary of the system configurations is provided below:
• Scenario 1: 100% grid electricity.
• Scenario 2A: 70% wind – 30% solar + grid.
• Scenario 2B: 50% wind – 50% solar + grid.
• Scenario 2C: 30% wind – 70% solar + grid.
• Scenario 3A: 70% wind – 30% solar, off-grid.
• Scenario 3B: 50% wind – 50% solar, off-grid.
• Scenario 3C: 30% wind – 70% solar, off-grid.
A gate-to-gate Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) approach is applied, focusing exclusively on the methanol production
system and its associated processes. The assessment begins with the receipt of purified CO2 and extends to the final
methanol production. Processes beyond this boundary, such as upstream CO2 capture and downstream methanol dis-
tribution, storage, and use, are excluded from the analysis.

The system includes CO2 liquefaction, hydrogen generation by electrolysis, and methanol synthesis and purification.
The electrolyzer is explicitly addressed, as it is essential for providing hydrogen for methanol production. As previ-
ously stated, although all configurations depend on the same methanol synthesis routes, there are primary distinctions
among scenarios which are due to the energy mix utilized to operate the system and the scaling of the methanol plant
and electrolyzer. In off-grid configurations, where the system depends entirely on renewable electricity without grid
support, greater capacity for methanol production and electrolyzers are necessary to offset variations in energy supply.
A schematic representation of the system boundaries is provided in Figure 5, illustrating the included and excluded
processes.
To ensure fair comparability across all scenarios the functional unit (FU) was defined to be: ”production of 1 ton of
methanol at 99.9% purity, from a plant with a yearly production capacity of 34,286 tons/year.”. This mass-based
functional unit provides a consistent basis for impact assessment, allowing for a direct comparison of environmental
footprints across the scenarios. It guarantees that all impacts are normalized per ton of methanol produced.
It should be mentioned that all processes are assumed to occur on-site, as the methanol plant, electrolyzer, and renew-
able energy farms are projected to be co-located next to the existing biogas plant of Vesthimmerland. For this reason,
the environmental impacts of transportation and storage are neglected in the analysis. The extraction of raw materials
for the CO2 production is also outside the system boundaries. Similarly, the end-of-life phase of methanol, including

Given this focus on direct environmental impacts, an attributional modeling approach is employed in the Life Cycle
Inventory (LCI) analysis. Attributional modeling is well-suited for this study as it quantifies the resource use and emis-
sions directly associated with each production scenario, without attempting to model potential large-scale changes or
indirect effects. Thus, there is a clear distinction between foreground processes (directly analyzed) and background
systems (which provide inputs such as electricity and raw materials).
Moreover, the ILCD framework recommends that attributional LCA should be combined with the use of average
processes in the background system, as marginal processes could have uncertainties and affect the reliability of the
results [64]. Attributional modeling is also particularly relevant in cases where system expansion is applied to address
co-products, where subdivision is not feasible.
In this study, oxygen and waste heat generated from the electrolyzer are not included within the system boundaries,
meaning no system expansion or environmental credits are applied. The assessment only considers the environmental
impacts associated with the production of hydrogen from the electrolysis process without accounting for potential
substitutions of those co-products.

The CO2 captured and purified from the biogas plant is projected to undergo liquefaction before being utilized in
methanol synthesis. Since this intermediate step is necessary for storing and handling CO2 in a liquid state, it was
decided to include the liquefaction process in this evaluation. The input to the liquefaction process is biogenic CO2,
which has already been captured and purified from the biogas plant before entering the system. In SimaPro, this CO2
is considered to have zero environmental impact because its removal from the atmosphere has already been accounted
for at the capture stage. Since biogenic CO2 is part of the natural carbon cycle, its uptake and release do not contribute
to an increase in atmospheric levels. If its reduction benefits were included again at this stage, it would result in double
counting, which would distort the assessment. Therefore, the environmental burden of this input does not influence
the overall results of this study.
To model the liquefaction process, a pre-existing dataset from the Ecoinvent v3.8 database was used. This process
originally included both purification and liquefaction, but since the CO2 in this study is already purified, it was modi-

includes electricity consumption at medium voltage levels in Denmark, which powers compressors and refrigeration
units. It also involves cooling water as well as the infrastructure-related impacts of the unit. Lastly, wastewater emis-
sions linked to cooling water use were included in the inventory.

carbon dioxide, which is released through purge streams during methanol synthesis. Additionally, a very small amount

2.3.4
Prospective Life Cycle Assessment
Prospective Life Cycle Assessment is used to assess how environmental impacts may evolve in the future based on ex-
pected changes in technology, energy systems, and policies. Unlike conventional LCA, which focuses on present-day
conditions, prospective LCA integrates future scenarios to evaluate long-term sustainability trends. With this approach
possible decarbonization opportunities can be recognized, showing how different energy transitions can impact differ-
ent environmental categories.
To model future environmental impacts, two baseline Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) were used. These path-
ways describe possible global development scenarios based on varying levels of technological advancement, economic
growth, and policy implementation:
• SSP1-Baseline represents a sustainability-driven future, where strong climate policies, efficiency improvements,
and renewable energy expansion lead to lower emissions. This pathway aligns with an estimated global mean
surface temperature (GMST) increase of +2.3 to +2.8°C by 2100.
• SSP2-Baseline Reflects a continuation of current global trends, with moderate technological advancements and
climate policies, resulting in a higher GMST increase of approximately +3.5°C by 2100.
The background life cycle inventory (LCI) databases for the future projections were generated using the Premise
Python library [73]. The ecoinvent v3.8 database (cut-off version) was adapted into prospective datasets by incor-
porating REMIND Integrated Assessment Model (IAM) scenarios, covering the period from 2025 to 2050. These
datasets include anticipated changes in energy grid composition, resource availability, emission regulations, and effi-
ciency improvements [74].
The generated databases were imported into SimaPro, where both midpoint and endpoint indicators were analyzed
using the ReCiPe methodology, following a cut-off life cycle inventory modeling framework to assess the potential for
future emission reductions. The midpoint impact results presented in this study focus on climate change impacts, while
the endpoint analysis assessed potential damages to Human Health and Ecosystem Quality, measured in Disability-
Adjusted Life Years (DALY) and species loss per year, respectively.

3.1.3
Effects of the operating temperature and pressure
To investigate the effect of temperature and pressure on methanol yield, a sensitivity analysis was conducted in Aspen
Plus, varying the reactor temperature between 210–260◦C and pressure between 40–100 bar. Furthermore, two differ-
ent recycling ratios of 98% and 99% were tested at the splitter, where purge gases are released. The objective was to
understand how these operating conditions influence methanol production and to identify potential limitations.
At a 98% recycle ratio, the methanol synthesis generally follows established thermodynamic principles as shown in
Figure 12. As temperature increases, methanol yield decreases according to Le Chatelier’s principle. Since the reac-
tion is exothermic it takes higher temperatures to move the equilibrium away from methanol production. Conversely,
increasing pressure typically enhances methanol yield. However, at lower temperatures (e.g., 210◦C), increasing the
pressure to 100 bar does not result in a higher methanol yield compared to 90 and 80 bar. This can be attributed to
the high solubility of CO2 in the liquid phase at higher pressures, so a greater fraction is absorbed into the liquid
phase rather than participating in the reaction. As a result, the benefit of increasing pressure is diminished at lower
temperatures.
The difference in methanol yield at 210 ◦C between the examined operating pressures is minor, as the system quickly
reaches equilibrium, limiting the influence of pressure on methanol production’s improvement. However, at higher
temperatures, the impact of operating pressure becomes more significant, as reduced CO2 solubility in the liquid
phase allows a greater fraction to remain in the gas phase, where it can actively participate in the reaction. Under such
conditions, higher pressure causes a greater yield of methanol because equilibrium is shifted toward the liquid state.

The analysis of the contribution of various processes of the system to Human Health and Ecosystem Quality across

understanding of which processes are the most significant contributors to the overall damage, helping to identify the
critical areas of the system.
The largest contributor to Human Health impacts across all scenarios is the electricity required to power the alkaline
electrolyzer. In Scenario 1, this process has the highest contribution, with approximately 85% of the total impact,
while in Scenario 3A it records the lowest, at 67.9%. The process with the second highest impact across all scenarios
is CO2 liquefaction. In Scenario 1, it accounts for 12.1% of total Human Health impacts, while in the hybrid grid-
supported scenarios (2A, 2B, and 2C), its contribution increases to 16.3%–18.3%. The highest contribution of this
process is observed in Scenario 3A, where liquefaction represents 24.7% of the total Human Health impact.
Moreover, the construction of the electrolyzer plays a more significant role in off-grid scenarios, due to the larger
size of the unit. Particularly in Scenario 3A, it contributes 5.3% of the total impacts. The rest of the processes show
relatively minor contributions to Human’s Health damage. The electricity used for compressors and pumps in the
methanol plant shows a consistent impact across all scenarios, contributing between 1.2% and 1.5%. The processes
of methanol plant construction, deionized water, as well as the direct emissions of CO2 from the plant are negligible,
accounting for less than 1% of the total impact across all cases. Lastly, cooling water needed to cover the external
cooling demands does not contribute to impacts associated with Human Health.

trolyzer remains the dominant contributor across all scenarios, followed by the process of CO2 liquefaction. The

Prospective Life Cycle Impact Assessment
Figure 26 presents the projected climate change midpoint impacts (kg CO2-eq/ton MeOH) for the assessed scenar-
ios under the SSP1-Baseline (2.3–2.8°C) and SSP2-Baseline ( 3.5°C) trajectories from 2025 to 2050. Across both
pathways, a declining trend is evident for all scenarios, reflecting the anticipated decarbonization of the global energy
system over time. However, the rate of reduction and final emission levels vary significantly depending on the scenario.
Under the SSP1-Baseline trajectory, the off-grid scenarios (3A and 3B) maintain the lowest climate change impacts
up to 2030, reinforcing their initial advantage in emission reduction. However, all the off-grid configurations exhibit
the smallest decline in emissions over time compared to grid-supported scenarios. Among all scenarios, Scenario 3C
shows the slowest reduction in climate change impact, with a 17% decrease from 2025 to 2050. In contrast, Scenario 1,
which initially has the highest emissions, undergoes the largest reduction of approximately 80% over the prospective
timeframe, benefiting from the decarbonization of the grid. Scenarios 3A and 3B show moderate decreases of 23%
and 20%, respectively, while the hybrid grid-supported scenarios (2A, 2B, and 2C) exhibit reductions of 69%, 65%,
and 63%, respectively.
By 2050, the scenario with the lowest emissions is 2A and 3A, both reaching approximately 393 kg CO2-eq/ton
MeOH produced. They are followed by Scenario 2B, while Scenario 1 is ranking fourth with 470 kg CO2-eq/ton
MeOH. Interestingly, from 2045 onwards, Scenario 3C emerges as the worst-performing scenario, and by 2050, it is
by far the highest-emitting one, with 811 kg CO2-eq/ton MeOH. The second worst scenario at the end of the time-
frame is 3B, further reinforcing the pattern that solar-heavy configurations are projected to result in higher emissions
compared to wind-based alternatives.
A similar downward trend is observed under the SSP2-Baseline projections, for all the scenarios. However, the overall
emission levels remain higher than in SSP1, which could mean a slower decarbonization process and a more carbon-
intensive energy mix. Scenario 1 remains the highest-emitting scenario until 2040. From 2040 onwards, Scenario 3C,
has the highest climate change impact, resulting in 800 kg CO2-eq/ton MeOH by 2050. The main difference compared
to the SSP1 pathway is that, despite its lower reduction rate as opposed to grid-supported configurations, Scenario 3A
remains the one with the lowest climate change impact throughout the entire prospective time scale.
Overall, the results indicate that grid-connected scenarios are projected to experience a greater reduction in climate
change impact over time compared to off-grid configurations. This outcome suggests that the progressive decarboniza-
tion of the Danish grid is expected to significantly contribute to lower emissions, gradually making grid-supported
configurations more competitive in terms of climate impact. The reason for that is the fact that Simapro’s process of
high-voltage electricity from the grid includes a mix of various energy sources, such as wind, hydropower, heat and
power co-generation (biogas, natural gas, oil, coal, wood chips), and electricity imports from neighboring countries.
As the grid evolves, it incorporates a wider variety of low-carbon sources, such as bioenergy, nuclear, and cleaner
imports, leading to a larger decline in emissions over time. In contrast, off-grid scenarios which rely exclusively on
solar and wind and their emissions are already near zero, leave little room for further reduction.
Moreover, scenarios with a greater share of solar PV tend to have higher climate change impacts over time compared
to those dominated by wind or with a more balanced energy mix. The reasons for that are the higher emissions linked
to solar panel manufacturing and the lower efficiency due to the solar capacity factor in Denmark, leading to a lower
reduction in global warming potential.

Discussion
4.1
Interpretation of results
The results of the process simulation demonstrated a carbon conversion efficiency of 94.18%, which is within the same
order of magnitude as most of the reports that analyzed methanol production via CO2 hydrogenation. Specifically,
Meunier et al. [26] and Lee et al. [4], reported both carbon conversion efficiencies of 93.4%e. The overall system effi-
ciency of 59.3% is also in agreement with similar literature values [12] [15] [27], indicating that the energy utilization
strategy in this study aligns well with established Power-to-Methanol processes. Concerning the specific electrolysis
energy consumption, the 47.60 kWh/kg H2 obtained is lower than similar works utilizing Alkaline Water Electrolysis
(AWE) for methanol production [12] [26]. This difference is likely due to the high efficiency of the electrolyzer used
in this study. However, Lonis et al.[21] reported a specific energy consumption of an alkaline water electrolyzer equal
to 45.5 kWh/kg H2, which is very close to the value obtained in this work. The observed trend of temperature and
pressure effects on methanol yield, where increasing pressure at low temperatures (210◦C) does not enhance yield due
to CO2 solubility effects, is supported by the findings of Lee et al.[4], which reported similar limitations on pressure
effects at lower temperatures. Finally, the heat integration savings of 69.45% are significantly higher than those in
comparable studies, which typically range between 30% and 50%. This discrepancy is likely due to a potential over-
estimation, as the results were obtained using the Aspen Plus Energy Analyzer rather than a detailed Pinch Analysis.
A more detailed heat exchanger network (HEN) optimization could reveal additional constraints, inefficiencies, and
practical limitations.
The economic analysis revealed that the electrolyzer constitutes the largest share of investment costs, which is in line
with most reports analyzing Power-to-Methanol plants. For example both Gu et al. [23] and Battaglia et al. [14] report
that electrolyzer costs account for over 50% of total CAPEX, confirming the capital-intensive nature of electrolysis
technology. The utility cost of electricity represents the highest part of Operational Expenditures in grid-connected
scenarios. This observation is consistent with the conclusions of Gu et al. [23] and Atsonios et al. [12], who mention
that the electricity cost is the most significant factor affecting methanol production’s feasibility. The Levelized Cost
of Methanol (LCOM) obtained in the different scenarios is within the range of values reported in similar studies but
positioned at the higher end [12][23]. The report of IRENA [75] on renewable methanol, estimates that the expected
selling price of e-methanol using renewable energy sources ranges from 730 to 1,460 EUR/ton, which implies that
the off-grid scenarios analyzed in this study (1,658 - 1,721 EUR/ton) are slightly higher than the expected cost bench-
marks.
Concerning the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), direct comparison with literature is difficult because of differences in
geographical location, methodologies, and processes for evaluating the environmental impact of methanol production.
In many studies carbon capture and storage is included, which produces negative emissions, reducing the overall im-
pact of the methanol production system. To contextualize the results, this study compares the impact on GWP of the
different scenarios with those for conventional methanol production. In an LCA conducted in China, Chen et al. [24]
observed that the methanol produced from natural gas produced 790 kg of CO2 equivalent per ton of methanol. In
a similar manner, Khojasteh et al. [20] analyzed the CO2 hydrogenation for methanol production in five provinces
of Canada versus conventional production pathways. The carbon footprint of conventional methanol production on
average was found about 600 kg of CO2 equivalent per ton of methanol. So the results of this thesis on GWP show
that for most scenarios the calculated emissions outweigh those from conventional methanol production. Neverthe-
less, Scenario 3A and 3B, which are off-grid configurations, produce lower emissions connected to Global Warming
compared to fossil-based conventional methods of methanol synthesis. That emphasizes the importance of electricity
sources on the impact of CO2-based methanol production to climate change. This conclusion aligns with the findings
of Khojasteh et al. [20], who stated that CO2 hydrogenation can only be considered an environmentally viable option
if powered by renewable or low-carbon electricity sources.

4.2
Limitations and Recommendations
4.2.1
Process Modeling and Simulation Limitations
• Thermodynamic Property Models: The Aspen Process Simulator was used to obtain mass and energy bal-
ances, but the study did not investigate alternative thermodynamic property models such as Soave-Redlich-
Kwong (SRK) or Non-Random Two-Liquid (NRTL). The choice of thermodynamic model can influence phase
equilibrium calculations, energy requirements, and reaction behavior, which could potentially affect process
efficiency..
• Operating Conditions Selection: The pressures and temperatures used in different process units were derived
from literature sources. In real-world applications, these parameters are adjusted based on plant-specific con-
straints, operational safety, and economic feasibility.
• Reaction Kinetics Exclusion: The process model assumes thermodynamic equilibrium and does not incor-
porate reaction kinetics. In reality, reaction rates depend on catalyst performance, residence time, and reactor
conditions, which can influence conversion efficiency and energy demand. However, the use of catalysts was
expected to have a minimal impact on the economic and environmental results.
4.2.2
Economic and Market Uncertainties
• Electricity Price Assumptions: The study used hourly spot prices from 2023, adjusting only for inflation,
without considering future electricity price projections. Given the volatility of energy markets, electricity costs
could be significantly affected by various factors. This could impact the economic feasibility of methanol
production process.
• Renewable Energy Production Profiles: The study relied on historical 2023 production data for wind and solar
availability, assuming that these generation patterns remain constant over time. In reality, climate variability and
technological improvements in renewables can alter future production profiles.
• Lack of Detailed Capital Cost Breakdown: While the study estimated the Purchase Equipment Cost (PEC)
for the methanol plant, a detailed cost breakdown of individual components (e.g., reactors, compressors, heat
exchangers) was not conducted. A more granular cost analysis could improve the accuracy of the capital invest-
ment estimates.
4.2.3
System-Level Constraints and Operational Simplifications
• Assumption of Continuous Plant Operation: The analysis assumes that the methanol plant operates con-
tinuously (8,760 hours/year) without shutdowns. Nevertheless, in reality, scheduled maintenance, equipment
failures, and unexpected downtimes occur, affecting production efficiency and economic performance.
• Curtailment in Renewable-Integrated Scenarios: In scenarios that incorporate wind and solar energy, cur-
tailed electricity (i.e., surplus renewable energy that cannot be utilized or stored) was not utilized.

4.2.4
Recommendations for Future Research
• Methanol Yield Optimization: The stream exiting the first distillation column could be recirculated into the
methanol loop to enhance methanol yield. This stream is primarily composed of CO2, which could act as
an additional input to the methanol synthesis loop. By implementing this recirculation, the overall carbon
conversion efficiency would increase to 98.3%, improving the overall process sustainability.
• Water Recovery and Reuse: The stream exiting the second distillation column, primarily composed of water,
could be further purified using reverse osmosis or additional distillation, in order to be used as an input to the
electrolyzer. A heat exchanger would also be required to lower the water temperature to the operational range of
the electrolyzer. Currently, this stream has a methanol concentration of 1,800 ppm, while the required threshold
for impurities concerning the water that is used as an input to electrolysis is below 10 ppm [76]. Future studies
could therefore investigate purification techniques to enhance process circularity.
• Heat Integration and Energy Efficiency: A detailed Heat Exchanger Network (HEN) could be designed using
Pinch Analysis. Heat integration has been performed using Aspen Energy Analyzer, while a detailed Pinch
Analysis could provide a more realistic and comprehensive approach to identifying which hot and cold process
streams could be effectively connected through a HEN.
• Incorporation of Carbon Credit Tax: Future research should assess whether this system qualifies for carbon
credit incentives, which could provide an additional revenue through CO2 reduction. The eligibility criteria,
legislation, and economic impact of such an action should be explored in detail.
• Revenue from Curtailment Energy Sales: In hybrid renewable scenarios (Scenarios 2A, 2B, and 2C), surplus
electricity that is now being curtailed could be sold back to the grid. The estimated yearly revenue from selling
the excess electricity to the grid, using the spot prices and energy production of 2023 is:
– Scenario 2A: 11.33 million DKK
– Scenario 2B: 12.55 million DKK
– Scenario 2C: 18.67 million DKK
While the revenue may not be substantial, it still presents an opportunity for financial optimization.
• Sensitivity Analysis on Economic Parameters: Given the volatility of electricity prices, future studies should
conduct a sensitivity analysis on electricity pricing trends, as well as on CAPEX variations. This would make
the results of the economic analysis more robust.
• Alternative Impact Assessment Methods in LCA: The current LCA was conducted using the ReCiPe impact
assessment method. Additional methodologies such as IMPACT World+ could also be tested to compare results
and evaluate potential methodological uncertainties.
• Integration of More Prospective Databases: This study used the prospective databases of SSP1-baseline and
SSP2-baseline to estimate the environmental impacts on expected changes in technology, energy systems, and
policies. Future research could incorporate more Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs), such as:
– SSP2-RCP1.9: A highly ambitious scenario with rapid decarbonization, targeting a 1.9 W/m2 radiative
forcing by 2100.
– SSP2-RCP2.6: A scenario aligned with the Paris Agreement, limiting global temperature rise to well below
2◦C.
• Contribution Analysis for Prospective LCA Results: In the results of the prospective LCA, a detailed contri-
bution analysis for the year 2050 could identify the processes within each scenario that contribute most to the
final environmental impact. This could be done for both midpoint impacts (e.g., Global Warming Potential) and
endpoint damage categories (e.g., Human Health and Ecosystem Quality).

5
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the techno-economic and environmental feasibility of methanol production using biogenic
CO2 and renewable hydrogen. It integrated process modeling, economic analysis, and life cycle assessment. Seven
production scenarios were assessed, including a fully grid-connected system, grid-supported hybrid renewable config-
urations, and fully off-grid renewable setups. The aim of this study is to analyze the effects of process optimization,
financial viability, and environmental impacts on the competitiveness of e-methanol as a sustainable fuel alternative.
The process modeling and simulation were conducted using Aspen Plus, focusing on key unit operations such as CO2
vaporization, electrolysis, gas mixing and compression, methanol synthesis, and purification. The system exhibited a
high carbon conversion efficiency of 94.18%, showing that the carbon input in the CO2 feedstock was successfully
converted into methanol. Additionally, the overall system efficiency was evaluated, showing that 59.3% of the total
input energy was retained in the methanol product. Various operating conditions, including the temperature and pres-
sure of the reactor, and the recycling ratio within the methanol synthesis loop, were tested to assess their impact on
system performance. The results revealed the crucial role of the recycling ratio in enhancing methanol yield. They
also indicated that at lower temperatures, high pressure on the reactor increases the solubility of CO2 which results in
a slightly lower methanol production. Furthermore, heat integration was performed using the Aspen Energy Analyzer.
All heating demands could be met internally through process heat recovery, with only external cooling utilities re-
quired. The overall utility requirements of the system are reduced by 10.17 MW, which represents a 69.45% decrease.
The economic analysis marked that the CAPEX of the electrolyzer constitutes the most significant investment cost in
the system for all the examined scenarios. Thus, technological advancements and cost reductions in electrolysis are
highly important in order to improve the overall feasibility of such a project. In scenarios connected to the grid, elec-
tricity costs represent the major component of operational expenditures (OPEX). Specifically, in the hybrid renewable,
grid-supported scenarios (2A, 2B, 2C), electricity utility costs account for approximately 64% of total OPEX, while
the corresponding percentage for Scenario 1, which relies entirely on grid electricity, is 80%. For the calculation of the
Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM), a detailed cash flow analysis was conducted over a 25-year period, assuming an
NPV of zero for all scenarios. The analysis included the revenues generated from the sale of oxygen and waste heat
from the electrolyzer. Among the examined scenarios, the hybrid renewable, grid-supported configurations resulted
in the lowest LCOM, followed by the off-grid setups. Within both of these categories, the balanced scenarios (2B,
3B), having an equal distribution of wind and solar installed capacity (50% wind – 50% solar), achieved the lowest
methanol production costs. Conversely, Scenario 1, which relies entirely on grid electricity, was the least attractive
option economically-wise, yielding an LCOM of 13,375 DKK/ton of methanol.
The Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) revealed significant environmental differences among the scenarios. Scenario 1
had the highest impact across most midpoint categories and all three Areas of Protection (Human Health, Ecosystem
Quality, and Natural Resources), while Scenario 3A had the lowest. Off-grid configurations generally showed lower
midpoint impacts, leading to reduced Global Warming Potential (GWP) compared to grid-connected scenarios. No-
tably, only Scenarios 3A and 3B had lower impacts than conventional methanol production from natural gas reforming.
For endpoint impacts, wind-dominant and balanced off-grid setups (3A and 3B) consistently had the lowest environ-
mental damage, whereas grid-supported and solar-heavy configurations showed higher impacts due to Denmark’s low
solar capacity factor. Scenario 2C relied more on grid electricity, while Scenario 3C required a larger solar capacity,
increasing its overall footprint. The contribution analysis identified electricity used to power the alkaline electrolyzer
as the largest environmental burden, responsible for over 60% of impacts on human health and ecosystem quality
across all scenarios.
Lastly, a prospective LCA was conducted using databases that consider advancements in technology, energy systems,
and policies. The findings indicated a more significant reduction in environmental impacts for grid-connected sce-
narios, particularly in climate change and endpoint damage, under both SSP1-BASE and SSP2-BASE trajectories.
Overall, this study aimed to provide an analysis of e-methanol production, exploring its feasibility under different
energy-sourcing strategies. The results emphasize its potential as a sustainable fuel alternative, with off-grid and hy-
brid renewable configurations showing the most promise. As technology advances and the costs of renewables and
electrolysis continue to decline—coupled with supportive policies and market incentives—e-methanol is expected to
become increasingly competitive and play an important role in the transition to low-carbon energy systems.
