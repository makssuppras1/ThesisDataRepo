Abstract 
Freight transportation systems involve all modes of transportation, of which each has 
advantages and disadvantages. To combine the advantages and reduce the disadvantages of 
two, intermodal transportation can be used to combine multiple modes. The most visible one 
is road transportation in the form of trucks, which leads to external effects including pollution 
and congestion on roads. Rail freight transportation on the other hand has much lower impacts 
for the environment than road. However, freight trains on their own are unable to provide door-
to-door solutions for customers. Intermodal rail and truck transportation allows an optimal 
usage of each mode’s advantages. In an intermodal system, rail can be used to connect ports 
with so-called dry ports in the hinterland. These systems are commonly used in Sweden and 
Germany. In Denmark, the market share of rail freight is rather low in comparison to other 
European countries, most importantly its two neighbour countries Germany and Sweden. Both 
countries differ from Denmark in several aspects, most importantly size and population. Rail 
freight is generally separated into bulk freight, containers, and semi-trailers. Different types of 
containers exist worldwide, therefore, standards have been developed for increase 
compatibility. Trucks and semi-trailers can also be combined with rail by letting a freight train 
transport the road units for a part of their trip. The Trans-European Transport Network and the 
East West Transport Corridor are projects for improved transportation corridors. Denmark has 
three public intermodal terminals – one in Zealand, two in Jutland. In addition two ports in 
Jutland are equipped with rail terminals for transhipment from ships. Most of the terminals deal 
with complications that make an efficient operation difficult. The Port of Gothenburg is served 
by an efficient shuttle train system from its hinterland. Starting with the liberalisation of the 
Swedish railway system, the shuttle train system boomed, and customers experience positive 
effects of the results. The Danish railway network has several capacity issues making operation 
of heterogeneous traffic difficult. Infrastructure projects are expected to improve the conditions 
for trains in the network. 
Several domestic and international routes could show a potential for shuttle trains. With the 
new rail terminal at the Port of Esbjerg, a shuttle train from the port to Zealand can become 
attractive if the handling costs can be reduced. An international route between Høje Taastrup 
and Hamburg shows potential for being competitive depending on the development of the 
conditions for road and sea transportation.

1. 
Introduction 
Freight transportation is one of the major drivers for economies and plays a critical role in 
global and local trade. The shipment of goods by road, rail, sea, and air all have strengths and 
weaknesses depending on the requirements of each mode. 
Road transportation is the most simple and convenient form of transportation, but also leads to 
several external effects such as noise and air pollution, but also congestion in the cities and on 
highways. Road transportation is the major mode for freight all over Europe, and Denmark is 
a country in which the share of road is significantly larger than elsewhere. Rail has a rather low 
relevance. Its neighbour country Sweden, on the other hand, has a large and efficient shuttle 
train network running from the Port of Gothenburg to various destinations all over the country. 
The same applies to Denmark’s second neighbour country Germany, where the Port of 
Hamburg is equipped with a wide shuttle train system to several countries in Europe. 
The potential for domestic and international shuttle trains in Denmark is analysed here and 
inspired the shuttle train system of the Port of Gothenburg. This research contains several 
aspects relevant for the operation of intermodal trains, comparing the possibilities with their 
feasibility in Denmark and identifying which conditions must apply. 
This document is organised as follows. Chapter 2 gives a basic introduction into freight 
transportation systems, intermodal terminals, and dry ports. It also contains an explanation of 
the regulations by the European Union and the consequences. Finally, the chapter ends with a 
comparison of the freight transportation systems of Denmark, Sweden, Germany, and the 
Netherlands. Chapter 3 presents different methods of rail freight transportation in detail and 
shows the differences between bulk freight, containers and semi-trailer transportation on rail. 
In chapter 4, two different corridor projects are presented with a focus on the Danish sections. 
A detailed presentation of the intermodal freight terminals in Denmark is given in chapter 5. 
Chapter 6 deals with the shuttle train system of the Port of Gothenburg, which was the inspiring 
aspect for working on this topic. The difficulties and problems of rail freight in Denmark along 
with a list of projects relevant for the freight market are shown in chapter 7. Finally, chapter 8 
introduces of a list of potential shuttle train routes, followed by a deeper analysis of two strong 
candidates on the list: shuttle trains from Høje Taastrup to the ports of Esbjerg and Hamburg. 
The candidates are analysed by attractiveness of routes, terminals, and potential freight 
volumes. The documents ends with a conclusion in chapter 9 and the appendix in chapter 10.

2. 
Literature Research 
In this chapter a basic introduction into several topics relevant for this thesis are given. First 
the different aspects of costs are described and the differentiation between internal and external 
explained. Then the different possibilities and modes for transporting freight are introduced. 
The third subchapter contains an explanation of the Danish railway network, followed by a 
definition of different types of trains depending on their route. Next the various methods of 
operating freight trains between two or more terminals are explained, followed by a description 
of the operation of mixed rail traffic, which is common in Europe and affects the punctuality 
and capacity of railway lines.  
2.1. 
Costs of Transportation 
Costs are an important and complex topic in transportation. However, as the focus of this thesis 
is not the financial aspects, they are not described in detail. Instead, a short introduction of 
relevant expressions is given here. 
Among others, Ricci and Black (2005) and Forkenbrock (2001) divided the overall social costs 
of transport into internal and external costs, explaining that internal costs are costs that are paid 
in relation to the transport activity by the one who creates them. Ricci and Black (2005) go 
more into detail and list six groups of internal costs in the transportation sector: staff, fixed 
assets including maintenance, energy, inventory turnover, management costs, and insurance, 
taxes and charges. External costs, on the other hand, are explained as costs that are paid by 
everyone else, and include non-monetary costs such as pollution, noise, accidents including 
injuries and fatalities, etc. The separation into internal and external costs is used to enable a 
perspective on the true and overall costs that occur. 
In order to create more transparency and fairness between different modes of transportation, 
the external costs can be internalised, as explained by Vierth et al. (2013). They 
comprehensively analysed the internalisation of external costs, which varies greatly between 
the countries and transport modes: internalisation can be implemented by taxes and/or charges, 
e.g. fuel taxes, infrastructure usage charges, etc. They add, that the relation of external costs to 
taxes and charges that have to be paid by the initiator of the transport service is called the 
internalisation rate. The result describes how much of the external costs are covered by those 
additional costs. An analysis they performed about freight transport from Oslo to Rotterdam 
==PAGE:14==

via Denmark shows that railway transport has a very high internalisation rate in Norway, 
Denmark and Germany (more than 160%), but very low in Sweden (less than 50%). On the 
road, the internalisation rate in this study varies depending on the route, but is generally lower 
than for rail. It is worth mentioning that in many cases the fees for fixed link crossings like the 
Øresund Bridge (connecting Copenhagen, Denmark and Malmö, Sweden) have a high impact 
on internalisation rates of the overall transportation chain (Vierth et al., 2013). In addition, 
while the ground-based modes internalise at least a small part of the external costs through fees 
and infrastructure charges, sea transport usually has hardly any internalisation of external 
effects: they are often not charged for pollution and emissions by governments. Besides the 
unequal charging systems, the varying level of internalisation between and within a country 
makes it difficult to properly compare transport modes.  
2.2. 
Transportation of freight 
In general, five different modes are used for external transportation of goods. “External 
transportation” means transportation outside of a company – compared to internal 
transportation that takes place within the company, e.g. by forklifts. The following modes for 
external transportation are nowadays used: 
 Road 
 Rail 
 Sea 
 Inland waterways 
 Air 
All of these modes are commonly used all over the world and have several advantages and 
disadvantages and had different developments in the past. Kummer et al. (2009, pp. 285–286) 
lists various types of advantages and disadvantages, which are explored for each mode here. 
Road transportation is performed by using trucks that can carry different kinds of cargo. They 
are very flexible in terms of operation, as they do not require any special infrastructure. Trucks 
simply use the existing road network and have therefore an extremely large and dense network 
to use, which is one of the main advantages. Due to the high flexibility and the high speed of 
direct door-to-door delivery – which is something no other mode allows. The main 
disadvantage is the low capacity that can be transported by a single truck. They are mostly used 
==PAGE:15==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 15 of 105 
 
for short or medium distances, on which they can be operated with low fixed costs and medium 
variable costs. 
Rail transportation uses one or multiple locomotives that pull (or also push) freight wagons of 
different types. The train is limited to the railway network, which is far less dense than road 
networks and in most cases does not allow a direct shipment from and to the customers, except 
for the few number of companies whose facilities are connected to the railway system. As a 
result, rail is used for transportation from station-to-station instead of door-to-door, requiring 
pre and post haulage by another means of transport, usually by truck. Especially for door-to-
door deliveries within 24 hours or during the night, rail has serious disadvantages compared to 
road transportation. The capacity that can be transported by train is high (on average in Europe 
around 20 standard containers can be transported by one train) and the variable costs are low. 
Although the fixed costs for setting up a train are high – the operation itself is significantly 
lower than road transportation and greatly benefits from economies of scale.  
Ships are used for freight transportation on seas and inland waterways. The focus on the types 
of goods transported varies: while inland waterways are mostly used to deliver bulk goods, 
such as coal, wheat, scrap metal, etc., ships on the sea are used for all types of goods. Water 
transportation has, similarly to rail, high fixed costs, but very low variable costs and offers a 
lot of capacity. These characteristics are comparable to rail. 
The importance of freight transportation by air planes has grown rapidly in the recent years. It 
is mostly used for high-class and time-critical goods, as the price is many times higher than the 
slower sea transportation, especially in the case of heavy goods. In comparison to all other 
modes, planes are by far the fastest, allowing intercontinental overnight shipping. Due to the 
dependence on airport infrastructure, they can be used for station-to-station transportation only. 
As with trucked traffic, air transport does not have very high capacity.  
The main differences between the modes introduced are in terms of cost, speed, capacity and 
flexibility. Due to the different advantages and disadvantages, there is no clear “best” mode of 
transportation, and all have situations that make them necessary. Road transportation is the 
only mode that allows door-to-door delivery without combining with another mode. Therefore 
there is no alternative for short haul transportation, but only for long-haul (Egyedi, 2000). Rail 
freight has a high capacity and is, if the transported volume is high enough, cheaper and has 
fewer environmental impacts than road transportation, meaning both internal and external costs 
are lower (Kreutzberger et al., 2003; Molemaker and Pauer, 2014; Port of Esbjerg et al., 2013).

Ships have similar advantages (capacity, overall costs) and disadvantages (limited availability 
of infrastructure, high fixed costs) as rail. Concluding from the history of transportation and 
globalisation explained by Rodrigue (2007), globalisation at the current scale would be not 
possible in this form without the existence of sea and air transportation, while road and rail are 
more practical modes for shorter distances. 
2.3. 
Rail network in Denmark 
In 2014, the Danish rail network had a total length of 2,636 km, of which 946 km had two or 
more tracks and 642 km were electrified (Danmarks Statistik, 2014a). A map of the network is 
shown in Figure 1. The main lines connect cities in most parts of the country to the Danish 
capital city Copenhagen, which is located in the east. In the north of the peninsula of Jutland, 
the two major cities connected are Aarhus and Aalborg, in the west the city of Esbjerg, and in 
the centre Odense on the island of Funen. Besides the national connections, the main lines have 
two direct links to foreign countries: one to Sweden via the Øresund Bridge close to 
Copenhagen, the other one to Germany on Jutland close to the town of Padborg. In addition to 
these two, there is another connection to Germany via the ferry between the Danish town of 
Rødby and the German town of Puttgarden. However, this is not considered a main line at this 
point, which might be changed after the construction of the Fehmarn Belt Fixed link has 
finished (see chapter 7.3.1).  
The railway network in Denmark is generally operated with mixed traffic, with several 
different types of trains such as fast non-stop connections between the major cities, slow 
regional trains, and freight trains operated on the same infrastructure (Landex and Nielsen, 
2012). By doing this, the different types of trains have access to most of the railway network. 
Therefore it is not required to build infrastructure separately for different types of trains. 
However, the whole network is not open for all types of trains and traffic. Besides rail systems 
for which the exclusive usage of one train type is clear, such as the metro network in 
Copenhagen, some lines are operated exclusively for passenger trains, while others are used 
only by freight trains. In Denmark, this separation is differentiated between the main lines 
(explained earlier), regional lines, local lines, the suburban railway lines (S-tog), and freight 
lines. These different characteristics of the railway network are also shown in the map in Figure 
1.

2.4. 
Types of freight trains 
Freight trains have a different level of importance for a country depending partly upon the 
origin and destination of the train. The following groups have been defined for Denmark by 
the Trafikministeriet (2005): 
 National freight trains 
 International freight trains 
 Transit freight trains 
The Trafikministeriet (2005) defines the group of national freight trains as all trains that have 
both their origin and their destination within Denmark, e.g. a train from the Port of Aarhus to 
the freight terminal in Høje Taastrup. International freight trains, they explain, have either their 
==PAGE:19==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 19 of 105 
 
origin or destination in Denmark, e.g. a train from the Port of Hamburg in Germany to the 
freight terminal in Høje Taastrup or vice versa. Finally, they explain transit freight trains, which 
have both their origin and destination in different countries, but their route crosses Denmark, 
e.g. a train from the freight terminal in Jönköping in Sweden to the freight terminal in Hamburg. 
An important detail for the relevance of freight transportation for the Danish government 
mentioned by Grontmij and Region Syddanmark (2011) is that only national and international 
freight are considered to be of economic interest, as they have an impact on Danish markets. 
Transit trains, they say, only cross the country without having any major impact on the 
economy, as they support neither import nor export of goods to and from Denmark.  
2.5. 
Operation of freight trains 
The operation of freight trains is a complex process and a simplification of the different 
operation types ignores several details. Nevertheless, four major types have been identified by 
Ballis and Golias (2004): 
 A direct train connects two terminals to each other. The train delivers all the containers 
from terminal A to their destination terminal B. The composition of the train can be 
flexible: Either it is a fixed set of wagons of which some may be empty if there is not 
enough demand for goods or containers transported; alternatively, the composition of 
the train can be changed at the terminal depending on the required number of wagons. 
 
Figure 3: Direct Train (Ballis and Golias, 2004) 
 A feeder train transports freight from two or more different origins to one destination, 
or vice versa. In the first case, two trains leave from two different terminals and are 
combined at a convenient point after the two lines merge. From there, one train 
continues to the destination. In the opposite way, one train leaves the station with 
wagons for two or more different destinations and is split into multiple trains at a 
convenient location. 
==PAGE:20==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 20 of 105 
 
 
Figure 4: Feeder Train (Ballis and Golias, 2004) 
 A liner train travels between two terminals and has one or multiple stops at other 
terminals during its trip – similar to a common passenger train. At the intermediate 
terminals it is possible to unload and load freight from and to the train. In some systems 
instead of loading and unloading freight, the wagons are added or removed. This 
operation type allows service to terminals where the freight volume is too low for a 
single train to be operated profitably.  
 
Figure 5: Liner Train (Ballis and Golias, 2004) 
 Finally, a hub and spoke system connects multiple terminals with a central transfer 
terminal and therefore creates a connection between all the terminals with one transfer 
at the central hub – comparable to the hub network of conventional commercial 
airliners. 
 
Figure 6: Trains operating in a Hub Network (Ballis and Golias, 2004) 
The actual operation is much more complex than those four groups and the different methods 
can be also used in combination. For example, a train connecting a hinterland terminal with a 
hub is a direct (shuttle) train between those two terminals, even though the transported freight 
has a different origin or destination (or both) than the train. Another possible mix is a liner hub 
==PAGE:21==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 21 of 105 
 
that connects multiple hubs with each other, or a feeder train connecting two small terminals 
to a hub. 
The different operation methods have different advantages and disadvantages in terms of costs 
and simplicity of operation. One important aspect for increasing efficiency of rail freight is to 
increase train lengths. Janic (2008) created a model to evaluate the performance of intermodal 
transport with road-only transport. Two different types of freight trains were compared: 
conventional and long intermodal freight trains (CIFTs and LIFTs). The main difference 
between these types is the train length (500 – 600 m for CIFTs, 800 – 1000 m LIFTs). The 
research shows that intermodal transport benefits not only have lower costs with increasing 
distances between origin and destination, but also have a higher reduction of costs than road 
transport. As LIFTs have a higher efficiency than CIFTs, they can be used to create a better 
competitive advantage of intermodal transport. On the other hand, Rutten (1998) explained that 
a shorter minimum length of shuttle trains increases the attractiveness of railways and the 
tonnage that is delivered. In order to increase profits with short trains, a dense network of 
freight terminals is required. 
2.6. 
Mixed train operation 
The operation of trains varies from country to country. Depending on the requirements and the 
conditions, the traffic on the railway infrastructure can be either homogeneous or 
heterogeneous. As  Landex et al. (2006) explain: completely homogeneous railway traffic 
means that all the trains go with the same speed and stopping pattern, while in a heterogeneous 
system, also referred to as “mixed traffic”, different types of trains use the same infrastructure. 
For example, this can be fast, non-stop intercity trains using the same tracks as regional trains 
with stops every few kilometres. Other examples are different categories of passenger train 
services on the same tracks as freight trains. The varying operating speed of the trains can 
reduce the capacity of the infrastructure, and can make it necessary to let trains overtake each 
other; by doing so slower trains do not brake faster ones behind them (Landex et al., 2006). 
Landex and Nielsen (2012) show in their research, that due to interdependencies in the railway 
network, a delay of a train can increase and also have consequences all over the network. They 
say that especially due to bottlenecks in the infrastructure a small delay can lead to measurable 
impacts on the punctuality of trains in the whole network. This means that a delayed train in 
Zealand might affect a train in Jutland, even though the train in Jutland is not directly related 
==PAGE:22==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 22 of 105 
 
(coming from or going to) to the rail traffic in Zealand. The lack of sufficient buffer times in 
the timetable supports these network effects: if trains are operated without sufficient buffer 
times and have a very high usage of the available railway capacity, a delay easily affects the 
following trains, as the track cannot be cleared in time (Landex and Nielsen, 2012). 
Landex and Nielsen (2012) also explain the network effect for passengers: for passengers 
transferring at a station between two different trains, the delay of their first train can result in 
the situation that the connecting train has already left at the point of arrival. An increased 
transfer time therefore reduces the risk of missing a connection, but also increases the total 
scheduled travel time for the passenger, as their research shows. They add, that the network 
effects for passengers also depend on the frequency of the lines, as well as several other 
conditions. Finally, thy mention the possibility of passenger delays, which can differ from the 
train delay: for example, passengers travel by a train from A to B, and another train from B to 
C of which both trains have a frequency of two hours. If the first train is delayed for 15 minutes 
and the passengers lose the connection in B, they need to wait for the following train and has 
a delay of two hours at their destination. 
Although research is lacking on the impacts of network effects on rail freight, it is reasonable 
to assume that these trains are also subject to such impacts. If a freight train arrives at the 
terminal too late, some of the goods might not be able to be transhipped to the next train (or 
other means of transport) they were scheduled to be on. The previous train can either leave in 
order to reduce further network effects in the rail network, especially in case of single track 
lines, or wait for a reduction of the delay of the freight from the first train. 
2.7. 
Intermodal Transport 
“Intermodal transport” is the transportation of freight with at least two different modes of 
transport, such as rail, sea, air, or road (United Nations Economic Commission for Europe, 
2001). In addition to that, the transhipment of freight between different modes requires special 
terminals, supporting the loading and unloading of e.g. both trucks and trains. An important 
aspect of intermodal transport is that the actual goods are not directly handled at the terminals 
for transhipment, but are located inside a loading unit, such as a container or semi-trailer 
(United Nations Economic Commission for Europe, 2001).  
Besides the term intermodal transport, two more definitions are commonly used in the literature 
and are defined by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (2001): “Multimodal 
==PAGE:23==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 23 of 105 
 
transport” is a more open expression and contains any kind of transportation chain that involves 
at least two different modes. On the opposite, “combined transportation” is a more specific 
mode of intermodal transport. The main mode of transport has to be rail, inland waterways or 
sea, but not road. At the same time, the pre- and post-haulage, if necessary, should be kept very 
short.  
The advantages of intermodal transportation compared to unimodal transportation are in the 
reduction of internal and external costs, as explained by Ricci and Black (2005): the costs for 
the actual transportation can be reduced for the operator, but also pollution and congestion can 
be reduced in cities due to fewer trucks running through them. However, they also explain that 
there is also the disadvantage of additional costs occurring as part of an intermodal transport 
chain: the loading and unloading processes at terminals take time and need additional resources 
in labour and facilities. The additional costs consist mainly of pre- and post-haulage to and 
from the terminals as well as transhipment and transfer in the terminals, like movement of 
containers between vehicles, rearranging train wagons, etc. 
Figure 7 shows the composition of different costs on the overall price of different routes. The 
graph shows that, depending on the route, the actual transportation is not the only noticeable 
cost: In the three examples shown the main haulage is only causing around 75% (Barcelona-
Warsaw), 55% (Genova-Preston), and 80% (Athens-Gothenburg) estimated based on Figure 7. 
 
Figure 7: Composition of cost factors on selected intermodal transport routes (Ricci and Black, 2005) 
Several studies, such as those by Kim and Van Wee (2009) and Kreutzberger et al. (2003), 
have shown that combined transport – intermodal transport with rail as the main mode – results 
in less damage to the environment than unimodal transport with road. However, they also 
==PAGE:24==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 24 of 105 
 
clarified that there are some conditions that need to be fulfilled in order for this to be actually 
the case: the main source to environmental damage of freight transportation is the engine of 
the vehicle and its energy source. If the locomotive used for the freight train is electrical, the 
environmental-friendliness of the transportation depends greatly on the method used for 
producing the consumed electricity. Electricity from a coal power plant, for example, can lead 
to higher emissions than diesel powered trains or trucks (Kim and Van Wee, 2009). Besides 
the energy source the route used for the transportation is also relevant. Depending on the route 
chosen for comparing unimodal to intermodal transport, road transport can in some cases offer 
much shorter transportation distances due to the road network, which has a much higher density 
than rail networks, and due to its higher flexibility.  
In combination with rail, intermodal transport is usually done in three different ways, as 
explained by Woxenius and Bergqvist (2009, 2008). The most common method is to move 
containers from other modes of transportation to rail freight wagons at the intermodal 
terminals, for example from trucks or ships onto the wagon. The two alternatives to that are the 
transport of semi-trailers on their own, and the so-called “roll-on roll-off” traffic. In this system, 
trucks with semi-trailers are loaded as a whole onto the freight train at a terminal and then 
transported to another terminal from where they continue their trip by road. A more detailed 
explanation of the operation modes can be found in chapter 3.  
There are advantages and disadvantages for those modes, as there are several differences in the 
way they are operated and integrated in the transport chain. While container trains are created 
in order to benefit from economies of scale, roll-on-roll-off and semi-trailer trains are more 
convenient for customers, as explained by Woxenius and Bergqvist (2008). They state that 
container trains, mostly coordinated by shipping lines or forwarders, have a limited number of 
hub and feeder ports and the dwell time of cargo in the ports are counted in days. Intermodal 
transport involving roll-on-roll-off and semi-trailers, on the other hand, are coordinated by 
shippers, road hauliers or general forwarders (Woxenius and Bergqvist, 2008). 
2.7.1. Sea ports 
Seaports are the connection between the transportation on the sea and the ground. As sea 
shipping is the cheapest mode of transportation and the only way to connect all continents to 
each other without involving air transportation, sea ports (together with airports) play a major 
role in the global trade and globalisation (Rodrigue, 2007). 
==PAGE:25==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 25 of 105 
 
The ports that cargo containers are delivered from and to are in many cases not the origin or 
destination within the whole transport chain. Therefore, pre and post haulage are required, 
which makes an intermodal transport chain according to the definition earlier in this chapter. 
Rodrigue (2007) explains that for this the ports need to be equipped with facilities for 
transhipping the containers between the ships and trucks and trains, which major ports have 
been adapted to support since the containerisation began. 
The transhipment of containers between ships and trains requires special infrastructure at the 
seaport. Schwarz (2006) explains four different terminal types for this purpose, which are based 
on the ports of Hamburg, Bremerhaven, Rotterdam and Antwerp: 
 A direct transhipment terminal allows the movement of the container between the two 
modes without any intermediary storage. This type would allow an optimal situation 
for hinterland traffic of the train. However, due to the aim of maritime terminals for 
maximising the transhipments, it is not applied in any of the four analysed ports. 
 An integrated terminal has a storage area with rail access. This requires splitting of the 
train into smaller sections for loading and unloading. 
 An adjacent terminal uses gantry trains for loading and unloading and has different 
facilities for the ship and for the trains. 
 Finally, a port terminal requires transportation of the containers between two different 
parts of the terminal: the maritime terminal and the hinterland terminal. In most cases 
this is done by trucks, but rail is theoretically also a possibility.  
2.7.2. Dry ports 
Rodrigue and Notteboom (2012) explain dry ports as more specialised versions of intermodal 
terminals that act as an extension of a seaport and with a high capacity transport connection to 
a seaport. They point out that in most cases this connection is implemented by rail. The dry 
port is used as a hub for distribution of the freight in the hinterland (Rodrigue and Notteboom, 
2012). 
There are several reasons that explain why dry ports have been developed. Bergqvist and 
Woxenius (2011) cover this topic in their research. First of all, seaports – especially in urban 
areas – suffer from a lack of space for expansion, or from limitations in capacity. They say that 
dry ports can be used to solve these restrictions by expanding the port at a different location. 
==PAGE:26==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 26 of 105 
 
Furthermore, they explain that a dry port can also increase the accessibility to the seaport from 
the hinterland of the country, furthering its development. 
Dry ports have different characteristics that influence the efficiency and service level provided. 
According to Roso et al. (2009, 2006), the efficiency is influenced by the possibility of the 
terminal providing repositioning of containers and rolling stock and by the speed of cargo 
rotation. They also point out that compared to standard intermodal terminals, they can offer 
additional services such as customs clearances.  
Depending on the location of a dry port in relation to the seaport it serves, three categories of 
dry ports have been defined by Roso et al. (2009): distant, mid-range, and close. They explain 
the differences as follows: 
 A distant dry port (as shown in Figure 8) is the most common form of implementation. 
It allows the seaport to connect to regions further away of the port, and therefore to 
increase the catchment area to a much larger part of the hinterland by still offering low 
costs. Due to the high distance to the seaport, freight trains are very competitive for 
connecting distant dry ports, compared to trucks. The operation of distant dry ports also 
helps reduce external costs, e.g., traffic in the city of the port can be decreased and 
emissions for the transportation reduced. 
 
Figure 8: A seaport with a distant dry port (Roso et al., 2009) 
 A mid-range dry port has a lower distance to the actual seaport, but generally offers the 
same functionality of extending the seaport than the distant dry port. The benefits of 
having the terminal closer, even though this could reduce the competitiveness of rail, is 
to still have necessary facilities and technical equipment concentrated at one place. 
==PAGE:27==

 A close dry port, such as the one symbolised in Figure 9, helps for dealing with traffic 
problems close to the port area. By locating the close dry port at the other end of a city, 
“facing” the hinterland, road traffic through the city can be avoided. In addition, close 
dry ports can also help with problems regarding capacity of ports and potential 
difficulties for expanding it. One could be located at the other end of a city and help 
avoiding road traffic to cross the city for reaching the port, for example. 
 
Figure 9: A seaport with a close dry port (Roso et al., 2009) 
According to a survey performed by Jaržemskis and Vasiliauskas (2007), the reasons for 
companies to become involved in dry ports include the goal of internationalisation of the 
hinterland, giving the hinterland better access to international trade. They add that the supply 
of value-added services would be a motivating power for operators to make use of a dry port, 
especially in case of services that are not offered by the usual ports themselves.  
2.8. 
European Union regulations 
In order to move the European transportation system more towards sustainability, the European 
Union (EU) created a regulation for developing the internal rail market of the member states. 
The overall objective of the regulation defined by the European Commission (2011) is the 
creation of an efficient and competitive transportation system in Europe. In order to achieve 
that, emissions have to be reduced and existing borders removed. The result is the so-called 
“Single European Transport Area”. 
Ten goals have been defined by the  European Commission (2011) in order to achieve the goal 
of a 60% reduction of CO2 emissions. The goals are separated into three groups, as follows: 
 Actions to improve the fuel and propulsion systems of road and air traffic 
==PAGE:28==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 28 of 105 
 
 Optimisation of the intermodal transportation systems for both passengers and freight 
 Efficiency improvements by using IT systems. 
In terms of freight, the European Commission (2011) defined clear targets for the optimisation 
of intermodal transportation systems. By the year 2030, the aim is to have 30% of the freight 
which is transported more than 300 km to be delivered on rail. By the year 2050 the rail market 
share should have increased to at least 50%. In order to reach these numbers, the developments 
and investment of the railway infrastructure are a necessity. In addition, the Trans-European 
Transport Network (TEN-T) with high capacity and performance should be fully implemented 
by the EU and the respective countries. The TEN-T network is explained in chapter 4.1. 
Gómez-Ibáñez (2004) analyses the liberalisation of railways and shows that it resulted in 
different approaches of maritime operators and shipping lines involvement regarding the 
inclusion of the railway mode into their transportation chain. He points out that, as the railway 
infrastructure became free to be accessed by private operators, some of the shipping companies 
chose to provide the railway service themselves, including the organisation of the required time 
slots, rolling stock, and staff. The rolling stock can be either rented or bought by the operator. 
Alternatively, the companies can cooperate with existing freight rail operators, who perform 
the haulage (and have more skills in this sector), while the shipping company reduces the 
necessary investment and can keep to its core business (Gómez-Ibáñez, 2004). 
As part of the railway liberalisation in the European Union, governments of EU countries 
created reforms that resulted in the possibility for competition on the rail network.  According 
to Gómez-Ibáñez (2004), this led to a vertical unbundling of the railway network. He explains 
that originally the railway companies were public organisations working with an integrated 
system. They were both responsible for the infrastructure and the operation of trains. The goal 
of the liberalisation was the vertical unbundling, so that infrastructure management and train 
operation are done by two different, independent organisations, Gómez-Ibáñez (2004) states in 
his research. He adds that nowadays the infrastructure companies do the track maintenance and 
management of the traffic for any operator running on their network. One or multiple train 
operators run their trains on the public infrastructure along with potential other operators who 
can, but do not have to be, competitors. 
This separation of infrastructure manager and train operator also led to an opening for other 
operators has also been done in Denmark. As stated by Banedanmark (n.d.), 14 railway 
operators are running trains on their railway network. This list includes public national 
==PAGE:29==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 29 of 105 
 
passenger train operators such as the DSB, but also foreign operators, such as the Swedish SJ 
and Arriva. In addition, the list contains different freight train operators such as TXL Logistik, 
CFL Cargo Danmark, Hector Rail, and DB Schenker Rail Scandinavia. All these companies 
run trains next to each other on the Danish railway infrastructure managed by Banedanmark.  
In their research Gouvernal and Daydou (2005) find that the liberalisation also showed different 
approaches by shipping companies and maritime operators towards freight rail, which can be 
separated into the categories of integration and cooperation. They describe that a full 
integration of railways into the transportation chain of a shipping company means that the rail 
freight service and marketing are provided and performed by the shipping companies 
themselves. The alternative approach they mention is cooperation with existing freight 
operators, such as the national railway operators: the railway operator provides the service to 
the shipping companies, who are the planning the transportation chain. In both cases, however, 
big players dominate the market, as only they are able to supply the required capacity and 
resources for running the service (Gouvernal and Daydou, 2005).

contrast, is much smaller and has limited interconnection possibilities due to the number of 
islands and the sea. These differences were also stated by Kristiansson (2015). In comparison 
to the similarly sized Germany, which is much more densely populated than Sweden, the 
differences in the size of the highway and rail network become also noteworthy: while the 
Swedish highway network is only the tenth of the size of Germany’s, the railway network is 
more than a fourth. Unlike Denmark, the Swedish freight system depends much more on rail: 
the efficient container shuttle train system to and from the Port of Gothenburg and the bulk 
freight trains in the northern part of Sweden signifies a higher interest in rail freight. 
The differences between Germany and Denmark are quite large:  there are no similarities 
between the countries in size, population, and length of rail and road network. However, the 
prioritisation of passenger traffic in Denmark compared to Germany can be seen from the data. 
While in Denmark passenger rail has more than 10% and freight 13.2%, in Germany passenger 
rail is only used by 8.5% of the journeys. Almost a quarter of the freight transportation, on the 
other hand, is done by rail. These numbers for freight market share are more meaningful when 
considering that Germany also has a large inland waterways transportation network.

3. 
Methods of rail freight transportation 
The way freight can be transported by rail varies. Depending on the type of goods to be 
transported, different kinds of rolling stock are required or can be used in order to efficiently 
and safely deliver the freight to its destination. Besides that, several other aspects play an 
important role as well, such as flexibility and the requirement for special equipment or rolling 
stock, especially in combination with intermodal transport. 
3.1. 
Bulk freight 
The Australian Department of Infrastructure and Regional Development (2009) splits freight 
as a general term into the groups “bulk” and “non-bulk”. It states that bulk freight usually 
occurs as large amounts of goods that are transported without any packaging and often have a 
low unit value. The loading of bulk freight is done by pumping or dumping the goods into the 
freight car, and same is done for unloading. In general, bulk freight is not perishable or fragile. 
According to UNCTAD (2015) the most common bulk commodities are iron ore, coal, grain, 
bauxite and alumina, which are central drivers for the continuous growth of sea transport. 
Due to the nature of bulk freight, it is not considered relevant for the topic of this thesis. The 
focus of the thesis is intermodal transportation, to which bulk freight does not count according 
to the definition given in chapter 2.7. 
3.2. 
Container and Swap Bodies 
Due to the high relevance of the research Egyedi (2000) has done regarding the standardised 
container, this subchapter discusses his findings and statements. 
The transportation of (semi-)processed goods can, independent of the mode, be done by putting 
them into a container. Compared to the unloading and loading of bulk freight, the transhipment 
of containers takes less time and is more efficient, which also helps to reduce both costs and 
congestion in the ports and the cities, if trucks have to cross them for accessing the port. When 
containerisation became a new trend in the 20th century and more containers were used for 
international freight transportation, methods of freight transportation systems changed. In 
addition, the possibility of multimodal transportation was created. With the invention of a 
standardised container it became possible to transport any types of goods without the need for 
==PAGE:37==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 37 of 105 
 
specific equipment. Instead, cranes for taking the containers and putting them onto a ship, train, 
truck, or storage area are used, independent of the actual type of goods in the container. By the 
1950’s, companies proved the benefits of using containers that can be unloaded from a ship 
and loaded onto a truck.  
One of the major benefits of standardised container transportation is compatibility. It is not 
necessary for a terminal or a road/train/ship operator to know what is inside the containers, as 
they only need to be able to handle the container itself. In order to guarantee that the containers 
actually can be handled in different terminals, a common standard needed to be defined for use 
by all the involved stakeholders. Therefore, several constraints needed to be considered for 
creating a standard, such as the maximum height, width and length of a vehicle on a public 
road or a train. As a result, in the 1960’s, a standardisation initiative was started at the 
International Standardisation Organisation (ISO). The defined standards not only included the 
size of the container, but also the methods to secure them, as well as several other aspects. Two 
common standard freight container types are the TEU (Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit) and the 
FEU (Forty-foot Equivalent Unit).  
Another standard commonly used in Europe are the so-called swap bodies. They have different 
characteristics than the ISO containers and have equipment for lifting and detaching the 
container on-board. In order to support the compatibility within the European Union and 
intermodal transport involving railways, the European Commission defined a standard for swap 
bodies (CEN/TC119).  
3.3. 
Double stack 
A system common in the United States of America is the so-called double stack transportation. 
This is a system in which freight train wagons are able to carry two containers on top of each 
other. By using this approach it is possible to transport twice as many containers as in the 
original way without stacking. The result of this operation method is a reduction of the average 
transportation costs per container. Strawbridge (1994) shows that, considering the low variable 
costs, this has clear and very positive effects on the profitability of a freight train line. In their 
research Rodrigue and Notteboom (2012) analyses the differences between European and 
North American intermodal systems, stating that in North America double stack trains are 
extremely visible, and that trains are significantly longer. They state that due to double stacking 
==PAGE:38==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 38 of 105 
 
and long trains a capacity of 300-500 TEUs per train are possible, while in Europe trains have 
a capacity of 40-95 TEUs. 
Even though double stack container shipping has obvious cost advantages, it is a system that 
is not applied in Europe, and according to Woxenius (1997) this is unlikely to change. The 
reasons for this are mostly of a technical nature and a large investment into infrastructure, 
adjustments would be necessary. The UIC (2009) listed differences between the US and the 
European railway systems. While Europe mostly has a maximum axle load of 22.5 t, the US 
allows up to 31.8 t, resulting in significantly fewer limitations for freight transportation. They 
also state that the clearance of the loading gauge for double stack trains in the US required 
large investments since the 1980’s. 
3.4. 
Roll-on-roll-off 
One alternative to reduce road traffic is to move the trucks from the road onto the rail. This can 
for example be done by the so-called roll-on-roll-off method, also called piggyback transport 
or rolling highway. 
In freight terminals equipped for this mode, trucks drive onto special wagons that have a 
continuous loading area. Trucks simply drive onto the train and continue on it until the end. 
Then the train transports the wagons with the trucks to a different terminal. The truck drivers 
take the same train in a separate passenger wagon (which can be a couchette wagon for 
overnight rides) and have their compulsory break. Seidelmann (1997) explains that even though 
the transhipment of the trucks is very simple and has low costs, the overall system is often not 
profitable. This is in part due to limited clearance area of tunnels requiring special wagons, and 
also costs of driving personnel. Due to its characteristic of transporting the whole truck 
(including the driver) this method belongs to the group of “accompanied combined transport” 
(Seidelmann, 1997) 
Wagener (2014) states that this mode of transport is not very common in Europe and is 
declining in use by companies involved in intermodal transport. Mostly it is used due to 
intervention of the governments, and on the lines crossing the Alps due to the strong traffic 
limitations on the road (Wagener, 2014).

3.5. 
Semi-trailer 
Another alternative for intermodal transportation is the transportation of semi-trailers. 
Seidelmann (1997) explains that at the terminal the semi-trailer is moved from or onto the train. 
Unlike the roll-on-roll-off method, the tractor unit and the driver are not transported. Therefore, 
Seidelmann (1997) points out that this method belongs to the group of “non-accompanied 
combined transport”, and that due to the transhipment this way is more complicated than the 
roll-on-roll-off system. The difference between intermodal transportation with semi-trailers 
instead of containers is the possibility to transport also swap bodies and other types of freight 
that doesn’t need to be unloaded from the truck and loaded onto the train (Seidelmann, 1997). 
According to Seidelmann (1997) and Wagener (2014) the market of intermodal transport in 
combination with semi-trailers is slowly expanding, unlike the roll-on-roll-off traffic: On the 
lines crossing the Alps this system is found to run successfully. This is explained by with the 
strict regulations regarding the road traffic along these routes, similar to roll-on-roll-off traffic. 
Even though the transportation of semi-trailers on rail has similarities to container 
transportation, there are still many differences. Among other reasons, this leads to the situation 
that container trains are much more present on trains than semi-trailer trains, as Woxenius and 
Bergqvist (2008) say. They add that due to different conditions regarding time precision of 
semi-trailers, rail is often not a solution on shorter distances, as the strict schedule of the semi-
trailer is simply not compatible with the trains. Besides that, a lack of infrastructure or 
concentration of available infrastructure makes the transhipment in terminals difficult and less 
attractive.  
One of the issues regarding the transportation of semi-trailers by rail is the technology of the 
majority of the existing semi-trailers. Many semi-trailers in operation today are not crane-able 
(Wagener, 2014), making it difficult to load them onto a train. For this purpose, several 
different approaches have been invented in order to be able to put those non-crane-able trucks 
on trains as well. A few examples for technologies are introduced in the following sub-chapters.

3.6.2. Road Railer 
One other alternative that involves the transportation of road vehicles on rail is the so-called 
“Road Railer”. According to Ferreira and Sigut (1995), this technology was invented in the 
1950’s and, in comparison to the previously two methods of putting trucks onto a train, does 
not include a whole wagon on which a road vehicle is parked. Instead, it consists of either a 
single bogie that can be attached and detached to a semi-trailer, or a permanently fixed single 
rail axle to allow for flexible usage.  
The German railways implemented this system in the 1990s, but it did not become very 
successful, as the magazine Der Spiegel (1996) describes. They explain that the Road Railer 
technology would offer one great advantage to the operator: reduced deadweight of the rolling

Trans-European Transport Corridors

member states, to enhance intermodal transportation, and to reduce technical differences 
between member states. They also explain that the corridors connect major European transport 
nodes by different means of transport, such as rail, road, inland waterways, etc. A map 
visualising the various corridors is shown in Figure 21.

The TEN-T corridor relevant for Denmark is the Scandinavian-Mediterranean corridor,

5. 
Intermodal terminals in Denmark 
There are three public intermodal terminals in Denmark that have a connection to the Danish 
railway network. The largest terminal and the only one in Zealand is close to the Copenhagen 
region in Høje Taastrup. The other two terminals can be found in Jutland: one in Taulov and 
one in Padborg, which is close to the German border. Besides those intermodal terminals, there 
is also a private terminal owned and operated by the Danish beer brewer Carlsberg in 
Fredericia, close to the terminal in Taulov. In addition to the terminals, a couple of the Danish 
ports are also equipped with railway infrastructure to allow transhipping between ships and 
trains. These ports are the ones in Esbjerg and Aarhus.  
5.1. 
Høje Taastrup 
The intermodal freight terminal in Høje Taastrup around 25 km to the west of Copenhagen is 
the only intermodal terminal on the island of Zealand. Greinert et al. (2012) performed an 
analysis of the terminal, which is explored here. 
The terminal is owned by the government, but operated by DB Schenker, a logistic and freight 
train operator from Germany. Since its extension it has a size of 122,000 m², of which 40% is 
unused. In 2011 it had a handled volume of around 75,000 TEU with an overall capacity of up 
to 120,000 TEU. The majority of the traffic handled at the terminal is for the brewery Carlsberg 
and for the shipping company Mærsk, who transport semi-trailers and containers by freight 
trains. The goods at the terminal are mostly handled by Carlsberg and APM Terminals.  
The location of the terminal is strategic: it is within the Greater Copenhagen area and has good 
connections to the road network. In addition, several companies are settled in close proximity 
to the terminal facilities, which makes the usage of the terminal more attractive for them. One 
of the two main customers, Carlsberg has the distribution centre right next to the terminal and 
connects it to the brewery in Fredericia with shuttle trains. In the future, the terminal has the 
chance of becoming an important hub for Scandinavia. However, in case of a growth of handled 
volumes, the terminal would require extensions.  
The railway part of the terminal is equipped with four tracks, two with a length of 800 m and 
two with a length of 650 m. However, not the whole length of each track can be used – only 
600 m of the 800 m tracks and 280 m out of the 650 m tracks are usable for loading and 
unloading. The terminal has an area available that can be used for expansion of the facilities in

order to increase the track capacity in the station. A map of the layout of the terminal is shown 
in Figure 23. 
 
Figure 23: Plan of the Høje Taastrup Terminal layout (COWI A/S, 2012a) 
The railway infrastructure suffers from two problems that reduce the efficiency of the terminal 
for the arrival and departure of trains. First, it does not come with a shunting yard. This means 
that the wagons need to be put into the right position before the arrival of the train. Second, the 
connection between the terminal and the rail network is only implemented towards 
Copenhagen. Freight trains leaving the station towards the west of Zealand, Jutland or Germany 
have to turn around outside the terminal. The same applies for trains arriving from the west to 
the terminal.  
For increased efficiency and lower operating costs it would be therefore in the interest of freight 
forwarders and their customers to have easier access to the terminal by being able to approach 
it from the west. A very simple estimation for the costs is done with results from the research 
of Trabo et al. (2013). Their calculations list a price of around 1.5 million Euro per kilometre 
of track constructed in Denmark based on the new line between Copenhagen and Ringsted. 
Switches are listed with a price between around 176,000 and around 303,000 Euro. 
According to an analysis done by COWI A/S (2012a), the intermodal terminal in Høje Taastrup 
already is able to interact as a dry port. Due to its location and available infrastructure, it could 
be used for shuttle trains to the ports in Aarhus, Hamburg, or Bremerhaven. One issue is the 
attractiveness are the Copenhagen Malmo Ports, also referred to as CMP, which create a 
competitive situation due to sea freight availability. A result of the analysis stated that the 
attractiveness of the Høje Taastrup terminal will most likely not increase as long as the port in 
Copenhagen is operating.

Summary 
The intermodal terminal in Høje Taastrup is Denmark’s is especially important due to its 
proximity to Copenhagen and the lack of alternatives on Zealand. The location has been 
evaluated as very good, as it is close to a large number of companies. The operation of freight 
trains at the terminal leaves room for improvement, but is generally considered to be very 
feasible. The capacity of the terminal is sufficient for the current operations, but might require 
an expansion in the future if more traffic is handled. After the opening of the Fehmarn Belt 
Fixed Link it is expected to have an increase importance for international freight. 
5.2. 
Taulov 
Taulov is located in the eastern part of Jutland close to the Little Belt Bridge to Funen. It is 
operated similarly to the terminal in Høje Taastrup and is currently used as a gateway between 
Central Europe and some parts of Scandinavia (Greinert et al., 2012). Due to its location along 
the main rail freight line through Denmark, the terminal is quite attractive for businesses: it is 
situated in the central meeting point of the line from Copenhagen and Sweden, the line from 
Germany, the line from Esbjerg, and the line from Aarhus and Aalborg. The closest 
metropolitan regions around this terminal are Copenhagen and Hamburg, both with a distance 
of around 200 km (Greinert et al., 2012).  
Jutland is the Danish export region, which means that there is a clear demand for freight 
transportation. While in other countries like Sweden the transportation of semi-trailers is 
significantly lower than containers (Woxenius and Bergqvist, 2008), in the terminal in Taulov 
semi-trailers represent 50% of the handled freight. Only 10% of freight is standard containers 
and the remaining 40% are swap bodies. A possible reason for this anomaly could be the 
comparably high market share of road in the Danish freight transportation market (Greinert et 
al., 2012). 
The terminal in Taulov has two handling tracks which are situated parallel to the main line 
tracks. Both ends are connected to the main network, which gives freight trains the possibility

to enter and leave the terminal from both directions. In addition, a shunting yard is located to 
the east of the terminal. 
Summary 
Taulov is an important terminal for trains going between Germany and Sweden. It is mainly 
used for loading and unloading of semi-trailer and swap bodies. The location is very beneficial, 
as the terminal is situated next to the major horizontal lines from north to west, and from east 
to west. 
5.3. 
Padborg 
The terminal of Padborg is located in the very south of Jutland, only 2½ km from the border to 
Germany. COWI A/S (2012b) performed an analysis of the terminal, which is elaborated here. 
Even though the terminal was constructed in the year 1987, after a period of being not in 
operation it has been only reopened in 2007 by TX Logistik AG, which started operating freight 
trains to the terminal in that year and increased the number of handled units at the terminal 
from 640 in 2007 to 14,136 in 2011. With an estimated maximum capacity of around 30,000 
TEUs per year and an area of around 1,500 m², Padborg is the smallest of the three public 
intermodal terminals in Denmark (COWI A/S, 2012b). 
The terminal is not used for any national freight trains, but rather mostly for destinations in 
Italy, Norway and Sweden. It acts as a gateway for the southern part of Jutland as well as the 
northernmost part of Germany, the state of Schleswig-Holstein. As the volume transported to 
and from the terminal is not high enough to allow a profitably running freight train, many of 
the trains stopping at the terminal do not have their origin or destination in Padborg. Instead, 
they just have an intermediary stop on their trip from Italy to Sweden or Norway (and vice 
versa).  
The terminal in Padborg is not only the smallest of the three in Denmark, but it also has several 
technical problems that make the operation more complicated and less attractive for freight 
train operators and shipping companies. It comes with only two rather short handling tracks 
with a length of 350 and 300 m, besides two depot tracks (600 m and 650 m). In addition, only 
trains with a maximum length of 650 m can be handled in the terminal facilities, requiring 
additional splitting in the case of longer trains. However, the usual length of the trains going to 
the terminal is between 400 and 520 m. Besides the capacity restrictions on the rail side, the 
==PAGE:53==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 53 of 105 
 
road infrastructure has limitations as well. Only a small number of trucks can be on the facility, 
which frequently results in congestion and illegal parking outside the terminal, due to the lack 
of parking areas for the trucks. Due to the lack of power supply, containers and semi-trailers 
that need to be refrigerated need to have a unit running that ensures the refrigeration (e.g. 
having a sufficient amount of fuel in the tanks), as there are no power supply or refuelling 
facilities available in the terminal. 
The connection of the terminal to the rail network is not optimal – due to the lack of 
electrification of the depot, a special diesel operated locomotive needs to be used for shunting 
between Padborg station and the intermodal freight terminal. As part of this is done on the main 
line, the capacity on the line between Central Jutland and Germany is affected by the shunting 
process, which is done with a speed of 10 km/h.  
Summary 
The intermodal terminal in Padborg does create issues for an efficient operation. Several 
technical problems create limitations in the possibilities for using the terminal on a larger scale. 
Besides the low efficiency, the list of services it offers are rather low. Therefore it is not able 
to act as a dry port. This result does not imply that the terminal is not usable for intermodal 
transportation – which it is currently used for – but that the requirements for an efficient 
operation are not fulfilled.

Port of Esbjerg 
Esbjerg is a city in the western part of Central Jutland with an international port at the North

6.1. 
History 
6.1.1. Political steps for formation of the shuttle train system 
The history of the shuttle train system at the Port of Gothenburg starts in the 1980's, in which 
several political changes regarding the organisation of intermodal transport resulted in new 
opportunities for rail freight transportation in Sweden (Roso et al., 2006). 
First, the operation of trains and the management of the railway infrastructure have been 
separated from each other (Roso et al., 2006). In the next step, the possibility for private 
competition in the rail freight market on Swedish railway infrastructure has been created (Roso 
==PAGE:60==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 60 of 105 
 
et al., 2006). Finally the former railway company of Sweden, SJ, has been split into multiple 
parts, so the freight sector is now a limited company (Roso et al., 2006). 
Bergqvist (2015) states that due to the opening of the railway infrastructure for other operators 
than the state-owned freight, road hauliers saw potential for the operation of shuttle trains. He 
adds that a few low number of small operators started running shuttle trains in cooperation with 
the road hauliers, and these trains turned out to be very successful. However, he also mentions 
that the operators and road hauliers came to the conclusion that there is a requirement to 
improve the location of the intermodal terminals used for the shuttle trains, for which they need 
to cooperate together with the municipalities. 
6.1.2. The boom of shuttle trains 
Bergqvist (2015) explains that in the beginning of the 2000's, the shuttle train systems had an 
upswing and became very popular. He also describes that at the same time, the intermodal 
terminals that are the destinations of those trains, developed very quickly, providing the 
viability of this concept of shuttle trains in Sweden. This boom resulted in more interest in the 
system by different stakeholders involved in the freight transportation business. Finally, 
Bergqvist (2015) states that it also increased the awareness of the municipalities in offering an 
intermodal terminal for shuttle trains within their territory. 
Bergqvist (2015) argues that the peak of the shuttle train systems in terms of the growth rate 
was in 2009 and 2010, at which the most new shuttle trains have been started. Nowadays, new 
lines are still implemented, but much fewer than before. He points out that although growth 
declined, the existing lines are still operating and are very successful. As they cover the 
majority of the containers going to and from the Port of Gothenburg, new trains would be 
required to meet a new market that is not served yet, such as semi-trailer transportation 
(Bergqvist, 2015). In a personal interview, Kristiansson (2015) stated that the efficiency at the 
Port of Gothenburg is one of the main reasons why the shuttle train system in Sweden works. 
6.1.3. The role of the Port of Gothenburg 
The importance of the Port of Gothenburg is extremely high due to its size and volumes 
handled. Although the port has a market share of 65% in containers handled within Sweden, 
on a European scale, the port’s volumes are rather low in comparison with other major ports.

operators own the locomotives and have the knowledge and ability to run trains on the Swedish

Experiences 
The experiences with the shuttle train system have been quite positive for customers. In a 
personal interview by phone, Bergqvist (2015) gave an example about the customer satisfaction 
of the service level. After a couple of storms in the 2000's, Sweden's railway infrastructure 
suffered from major damages resulting in the temporary closure of lines. These made it 
impossible for freight trains to transport the goods from their origin to their destination. Due to 
this extraordinary situation, customers were not expecting delivery of the freight. However, the 
responsible shipping company decided to move the freight onto trucks temporarily and deliver 
them on the road instead of waiting for the railway tracks to be cleared for operation again. 
Bergqvist (2015) explains, that this is a service level that the state-owned railway company did 
not have, and by providing it the road hauliers created a new way of thinking in the market. 
This experience shows the advantages of a more open transportation system. The actual 
advantage in the case of storm damage resilience is not that the transportation is generally done

Danish railway network: Problems and future 
7.1. 
Bottlenecks in the Danish rail network 
One of the problems for freight trains in Denmark is the limited availability of capacity on the 
railway tracks. Due to a high number of passenger trains and several infrastructural bottlenecks, 
the capacity in Denmark is limited and the chance for increasing delays on the main freight line 
of Denmark (between the Øresund Bridge, Copenhagen airport in Kastrup, Høje Taastrup, 
Odense, Taulov and Padborg) is very high, as explained by Landex (2009): 
Kastrup station 
Three stations are located along the railway line between Copenhagen Central Station and the 
Øresund Bridge. One of them is the station for the Copenhagen Airport in Kastrup. Landex 
(2009) explains that in this station, a bypass for freight trains has been implemented, so that 
freight trains do not cross the station area. This bypass requires trains going the opposite 
direction to cross the track, and then continue on a short single track section. He points out that 
a freight train going from Denmark to Sweden therefore blocks the traffic for trains towards 
Denmark, independent of whether they are passenger trains leaving the station, or they are 
freight trains waiting at the short single track section. A schematic track layout of the station is 
shown in Figure 30. Mod København shows the line towards Copenhagen; Godstog the bypass 
for freight trains and Passagertog the route for passenger trains through the airport station. 
 
Figure 30: Schematic track layout of the western part of Kastrup station (Landex, 2009) 
Main line on Zealand and Funen 
Landex (2009) argues that the railway line between Copenhagen and Ringsted, and the line 
between Odense and Snoghøj have a very high capacity usage and is therefore not very flexible 
==PAGE:68==

if a train is delayed or early. Due to the high frequency of trains going on this line, a delay of 
one train easily affects following trains as well. Freight trains can create even more delays in 
case of an unscheduled stop, as they have a lower acceleration than passenger trains which can 
slow down following trains (Landex, 2009). 
Triangle on Jutland 
Close to the Little Belt Bridge between Funen and Jutland the railway line diverges into two 
lines. One line leads to the north of Jutland towards Aarhus and Aalborg, the other one to the 
south towards Germany and to the west towards Esbjerg. Landex (2009) also points out 
bottlenecks in this triangle. As there is also a connection between the line to the north and the

tracks into the opposite direction twice in this junction, as shown on Figure 31. Mod Middelfart

Landex (2009) states that in the southern part of Jutland, two sections of the line, between 
Vamdrup and Vojens, as well as between Tinglev and Padborg close to the German border, 
offer only single track. These sections reduce the line capacity significantly, and also increases 
the problems of network effects in case of train delays.

deny applications for four more trains due to the unavailability of time slots (Iwersen, 2008).  
7.2. 
Punctuality of transit freight trains 
In order to increase the punctuality of transit freight trains, the railway infrastructure manager 
Banedanmark and the operator DB Schenker cooperate for an improved traffic flow, as Richter 
(2014) writes in his research. One possibility for improving it is by relocating delayed freight 
trains to a different time slot, so it operates “on time” according to the new slot. This method 
has a positive influence on the predictability of the train, especially if it is leaving Denmark 
bound for Germany or Sweden. Another alternative that has been analysed by Richter (2014) 
is the operation of freight trains without a timetable. However, his results showed that this led 
to a reduced traffic flow. In order to ensure a proactive cooperation, Banedanmark and DB 
Schenker organise different types of regular meetings (daily, weekly, and monthly) for the 
analysis and planning of the transit traffic (Richter, 2014).  
7.3. 
Impacts of the future Danish railway infrastructure 
projects 
Currently, several railway projects in Denmark are considered, planned or already under 
construction. Even though many of the projects focus on advantages for passenger railways, 
they can also create new advantages and better conditions for freight trains, for example, by 
removing existing bottlenecks and increasing the available capacity on the rail network. 
7.3.1. Fehmarn Belt Fixed Link 
At this time, the only rail connection between the capital city of Copenhagen on Zealand (and 
therefore also the intermodal terminal in Høje Taastrup) and Germany is via the Great Belt 
Bridge to Funen and further to Jutland. This train line from Copenhagen to Padborg is one of 
the few lines in Denmark which is fully electrified (Banedanmark, n.d.). 
There is an alternative route for passenger trains between Copenhagen and Hamburg that goes 
via Lolland and the German island of Fehmarn. Roll-on-roll-off ferries connect the two 
countries on the 19 km section of sea in around 45 minutes. Due to the limited capacity and the 
==PAGE:70==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark

bad conditions of the railway infrastructure on most parts of the line, this route is not considered 
to be attractive for rail freight transport at this point. The main reason for this is the requirement 
of double transhipping freight between rail and sea at the ports for crossing the Fehmarn Belt. 
Trucks using the roll-on-roll-off ferries are not affected by this. 
In order to eliminate this bottleneck on both road and rail, a project has been initiated to connect 
Denmark and Germany with a fixed link accessible on both modes. By 2021, a tunnel is 
expected to be finished and open for traffic with a cost of more than 10 million Euro (Femern 
A/S, 2012). The tunnel will consist of 89 elements with a length of 217 m each. The underwater 
portion of the tunnel will have a length of 17.6 km, making it the longest immersed tunnel of 
the world (Femern A/S, 2012). The allowed maximum speed on the road will be 110 km/h, 
while freight trains can go up to 140 km/h, and passenger trains up to 200 km/h (Femern A/S, 
2012).  
The overall project also contains the infrastructural upgrades on the Danish and German side 
of the Belt. Banedanmark (2014) states that the upgrade will increase the allowed maximum 
speed on the line from 120 km/h and 160 km/h (depending on the section) to 200 km/h, and 
that the whole track alignment will be double track for increased capacity. In addition they also 
list benefits for rail freight, such as three overtaking tracks with a length of 1 km that ensure an 
increased capacity for a mixed operation of freight trains, and high speed passenger trains. The 
new line crossing the Fehmarn Belt is expected to have 78 daily freight trains (Banedanmark, 
2014). 
The increased quality and capacity of the railway infrastructure after the complete construction 
of the Fehmarn Belt Fixed Link is expected to increase the freight traffic on this line. Not only 
does it increase the attractiveness to freight transport via Jutland, but it creates also an 
alternative for traffic that currently uses the direct ferry from Sweden to Germany. Instead of 
the ferry, transporting the goods by rail or road via Denmark is more attractive than in the 
current situation. Femern A/S (2014) expects that, based on the EU’s Trans-Tools model, more 
than 60% of all rail freight between Scandinavia and the rest of Europe will be routed via the 
tunnel, resulting in relatively large growth on this line compared to the general traffic growth 
between the two areas.

7.3.2. Copenhagen-Ringsted High Speed Line 
In order to reduce travel time for passengers and increase capacity on the railway line between 
Copenhagen and Ringsted, a new high-speed railway line between those cities is currently 
under construction. Banedanmark (n.d.) explains some data about the project: the line is 
expected to be finished and open for traffic at the end of 2018 and will allow a maximum speed 
of up to 250 km/h. This will mainly allow a travel time reduction for passengers from 
Copenhagen to western parts of Denmark. They show that compared to the current main line 
between Copenhagen and Ringsted via the city of Roskilde, the new high speed line will be 
aligned along the coast and pass by close to Køge.  
It can be expected that after opening several of the passenger trains between Zealand and 
Jutland will take the new line. This reduces the traffic on the old main line via Roskilde. 
Banedanmark (n.d.) explains that this resolves capacity issues and supports the “one hour 
model”. This model is a plan for reducing the travel time of passenger trains between the largest 
cities in Denmark to one hour (Landex, 2009). Due to the reduced capacity usage on the line 
between Copenhagen and Ringsted via Roskilde, there is more capacity for the operation of 
freight trains instead. The new line increases the overall capacity between Copenhagen and 
Ringsted and therefore eliminates the bottleneck that currently limits freight train operation. 
7.3.3. New signalling system 
In 2009 the Danish government decided in an investment of 3.2 billion Euro into a new 
signalling system in Denmark, as Banedanmark (2010) describes. The reason for this 
investment was aging and unreliable existing signalling infrastructure, some with technology 
dating before World War I. Banedanmark (2010) estimates that almost 40,000 trains are 
delayed every year due to signalling system failures. They state that the upgrade involves more 
than 3,000 km of tracks of the Danish railway network. Banedanmark (2010) also explains the 
new system for the main lines will be ERTMS Level 2. The benefits expected by implementing 
ERTMS Level 2 are not only increased reliability by reducing failures, but also higher 
maximum speeds and more capacity on some routes. 
An issue regarding the implementation of a completely new system is the requirement for 
operators to equip their trains with new technology. Søndergaard (2015) explains that trains 
used for existing public transportation services for passengers will be equipped with the 
necessary technology by Banedanmark. For new trains, on the other hand, the operator is

commercial train services such as freight trains have to do the investment on their own.

7.3.4. Electrification 
A significant part of the Danish long-distance railway network is currently not electrified. In 
fact, only the so-called Kystbanen between Copenhagen and Helsingør, the short section from 
Copenhagen to Sweden, and the main line from Copenhagen via Funen and Jutland to Germany 
are currently electrified (Banedanmark, n.d.). Note that the isolated suburban rail network and 
metro network in Copenhagen are also electrified. However, the Danish government and 
Banedanmark plan to change this within the next decades. 
To the currently 1,321 km of electrified tracks for the “normal” railways, Banedanmark (n.d.) 
plans to add 1,362 km, summing up to a total of 3,118 km. Besides the two major railway line 
projects currently in planning or construction – the Fehmarn Belt Fixed Link and the high-
speed line between Copenhagen and Ringsted – the line between Lunderskov and Esbjerg and 
between Køge and Næstved has the highest priority. Banedanmark (n.d.) also intends to 
electrify the lines from Roskilde to Kalundborg, from Fredericia via Aarhus and Aalborg to 
Frederikshavn and from Vejle to Struer.  
The advantages Banedanmark (n.d.) expects from the electrification are, e.g., a higher 
maximum speed of up to 250 km/h, shorter travelling times due to better acceleration of electric 
locomotives, a reduction of noise especially in station areas, and lowering the operation and 
maintenance costs for train operators.

Possible Shuttle Train Routes 
Many of the existing shuttle trains lines have in common that they connect a major port with 
high volumes of handled freight (such as the ones in Hamburg or Gothenburg) to intermodal 
terminals of varying sizes in the hinterland. One reason for this is the possibility to bundle 
volumes and complete additional transportation with other means of transport than rail. This 
means that a train line does not actually require the same origin or destination as the goods 
transportation, as long as the costs for pre- and post-haulage and handling make the overall 
transportation chain still attractive for the operators and customers. 
For the routes introduced in this chapter, the usage of ports and/or intermodal terminals as 
gateways for freight trains has been considered as the most economical and logistically feasible 
approach. One of the reasons for this approach is the lack of precise data about the freight 
flows’ specific origin and destination locations within a country. When volumes are used as 
part of the analysis, the data analysed here refers mainly to year 2013 reports from Danmarks 
Statistik (2015a, 2015b, 2014b, 2014c), including transported volumes of all types of freight 
between Denmark and other countries for rail, sea and road modes. Road transportation is 
further distinguished in trucks owned and operated by Danish companies and foreign 
companies. Analyses of other data sources are noted as such.

8.1. 
Requirements for the routes 
The list introduced in the previous section shows a selection of possible routes. The basic 
requirement for a route to be part of the list is a train operation performing part of an intermodal 
==PAGE:76==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 76 of 105 
 
transport chain. This can be container or semi-trailer transportation. The route needs to be 
technically possible (railway infrastructure exists or is in planning) and can be assumed realistic 
without the knowledge of specific additional information, such as a train between two specific 
companies instead of public terminals, as those routes cannot be evaluated with the available 
data. A valid example is a train from the Høje Taastrup terminal to the Port of Gothenburg. An 
example for a route that does not fulfil this requirement is a train from the Høje Taastrup 
terminal to Finland, as there is no feasible direct train connection possible due to geographical 
limitations. 
The following criteria are used to evaluate the routes: 
 Distance: 
In general, the profitability and competitiveness of freight train operation increases with 
higher distances, and is very low for short distances. Therefore, longer routes are more 
relevant and likely to be implemented than shorter ones. Very short routes of only a few 
kilometres are only used in rare cases and not for financial reasons, but for reasons such 
as avoiding road traffic through a city. Therefore they are not considered here. 
 Volume: 
The more volume is transported on a train, the more profitable it is for the operator. 
Therefore routes on which high volumes would be transported make most sense to be 
analysed. 
 Maximum train length: 
Due to different regulations and infrastructure characteristics in European countries, 
train lines have different maximum lengths. Similarly to the volume criterion, this 
affects the profitability of the train, as longer trains have lower average costs than 
shorter ones. 
 Existing truck traffic: 
If a route already has a high number of trucks running between the two end points, this 
could become potential traffic for a freight train, either by transporting containers by 
train instead of trucks, or by putting the semi-trailers onto trains. 
 Competition by other means of transport: 
At the same time, existing (or potential) traffic by other means of transport or even 
existing railway lines can also be considered as competition. Especially ships on seas 
or inland waterways offer higher volumes for lower costs and therefore have a clear 
==PAGE:77==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 77 of 105 
 
competitive advantage to trains. Therefore routes connecting two ports might not be 
competitive enough. 
In addition to these criteria, the following aspects about the railway and terminal infrastructure 
been taking into consideration as well: 
 Technical feasibility of route: 
Due to high capacity usage of the tracks and/or capacity-limiting track layout, some 
routes might be hard to be implemented due to the lack of capacity on the railway 
infrastructure. Routes along lines with less capacity issues might therefore be more 
reasonable to be implemented. 
 Terminal feasibility and features: 
In order to have a profitable service level for the shipping company and rail operator, 
an efficient transhipment of containers, trucks or semi-trailers needs to be offered. This 
features can for example include the provision of power for refrigerated container, but 
also much more basic ones, such as a good length for the loading and unloading track 
for little requirement of splitting trains.

(2015a, 2015b, 2015c, 2014c). The feasibility of routes and terminals is mostly based on 
volumes and infrastructure within Denmark. 
In order to calculate the distance of routes, the route planning software “HERE” 
(http://www.here.com) has been used. Even though it uses the road network, it allows an 
estimation of the length of the route and is more accurate than the linear distance. As the exact 
distance is not relevant for this level of evaluation, deviation of the railway tracks from the 
road tracks are not seen as relevant. The values are rounded to 25 km. In the case of multiple 
destinations on one end (e.g., Hamburg/Bremerhaven) the shorter distance has been chosen.

8.2.1. National routes 
The introduced routes for national freight trains are all connections between the terminals and 
ports introduced in chapter 5. One of the major issues for national routes is the size and 
geography of Denmark. Due to the short distances between the major cities in comparison to 
other countries and the characteristics of intermodal rail freight to be profitable only on longer 
routes, the potential for domestic freight trains in Denmark is not easily. 
Looking at the flows of freight in containers within Denmark shown in Table 6 on page 94, it 
can be seen that the majority of the trucked traffic is transported within the same regions or to 
and from the nearby regions, while the volumes to distant regions are much lower. Considering 
the advantages of freight transportation by road, this is in fact very reasonable, as a large 
amount of freight movement happens on very short distances. As stated earlier, rail is not 
competitive on very short distances and does not offer the possibility for door-to-door delivery. 
This can also be seen in the data about container transportation by rail, as the existing container 
freight trains in Denmark transport the majority of the goods between terminals in southern 
Denmark and the capital region. 
In addition to domestic freight transportation, feeder shuttle trains for ships are also an 
alternative. As two of the possible destinations in Denmark are port terminals, the requirements 
for an intermodal transport chain involving shuttle trains as a feeder for a ship are fulfilled. 
Depending on the services of the terminals, they act as a dry port for one of the seaports. 
All of the ports and terminals on Jutland have a distance to each other of at most 175 km. This 
is a very short distance for the operation of an intermodal freight train. The distance required 
for running a freight train on a competitive price level varies, while Rutten (1998) expects a 
minimum distance of 100 km to be sufficient, Wagener (2014) recommends at least 300 km to 
500 km, depending on the operational concept. Due to the short distance between most 
domestic terminals, the problems at some of the terminals (as described in chapter 5) and the 
high convenience of truck transportation in Jutland, a profitable operation is not seen as a likely 
possibility. The more relevant routes are therefore the ones connecting Høje Taastrup with 
Jutland.

8.2.2. International routes to neighbour countries and major ports 
The second group of routes run between the Danish ports and terminals and locations in 
Germany, Sweden, Norway, Poland, and the Benelux countries. Already now, the majority of 
international trains transporting goods to Denmark come from Germany. However, looking at 
the transported volumes to Germany or Denmark’s other neighbour, Sweden, the railway has 
a very low market share compared to road and ship: the import and export share of rail is 1.18% 
and 0.3% for Sweden and 3% and 0.51% for Germany. 
The highest volume of freight to and from Germany is transported by ships (more than 50%) 
and trucks (more than 40%). Data on details of origin and destination within the countries is 
unavailable, therefore a separate analysis for different destination terminals in Germany cannot 
be conducted. As a result, the following assumption is made: the Port of Hamburg and 
Bremerhaven and the geographic location of Hamburg are very convenient hubs, not only from 
a Danish perspective. Major railway lines and roads between Denmark and Central Europe 
pass by the city. This aspect in combination with the attractiveness of having the port (or an 
intermodal terminal nearby) as a destination of a shuttle train from Denmark creates a great 
potential. Not only does it allow the transhipment between international freight ships, but also 
bundling of freight coming from different parts of Europe. 
The volume transported between Sweden and Denmark is lower than between Germany and 
Denmark, but also depends mainly on ships and trucks. In comparison to the road traffic to 
Germany, the share of Danish trucks in the overall international road volumes is much higher 
(around 86% compared to 23% to Germany). The geographic conditions, separating Denmark 
and Sweden by the sea, implies a high amount of sea transportation. Indeed, more than 75% of 
the imports and more than 87% of the exports are done by ship. Almost all of the remaining 
share is transported by truck, even though the geography hardly allows direct road access. One 
reason for this is considered to be the convenience of roll-on-roll-off traffic for trucks, which 
is simpler to tranship than containers or semi-trailers. Examples for ferry routes are the 
connections between Helsingør and Helsingborg, Grenå and Varberg, and Frederikshavn and 
Gothenburg. The potential for freight rail is limited by the Øresund Bridge being the only fixed 
link to Denmark which already has a high capacity usage. Due to the high competition by ships, 
shuttle trains between the Swedish ports and the Danish ports are not further analysed.

An operation of a shuttle train as described would, nevertheless, run into a number of 
complications. First of all, as the traffic at the port is roll-on-roll-off traffic, trucks arriving by 
sea are “ready to go” as soon as the ship arrives. It is not necessary to tranship the semi-trailer 
by an external operator in order to continue the transportation after the ship has docked. The 
same applies for the opposite direction. Therefore loading and unloading semi-trailers on and 
from freight trains creates additional handling costs. In addition, as mentioned in chapter 3.5, 
only a low share of semi-trailers can be used by a crane for transhipment onto a train. This 
creates difficulties for handling the semi-trailers at a port or intermodal terminal. As an upgrade

of every semi-trailer possibly using this route is unlikely, one of the technologies introduced in 
chapter 3.5 can be used for transhipment. The disadvantage with this solution is, besides the 
additional costs, the lack of a technology standard. Deciding on one of the technologies today 
can create a potential problem of incompatibility in the future, if another route is started to a 
terminal with a different technology. 
The facilities of the Port of Esbjerg can be considered another reason for concern. As 
introduced in chapter 5.5, the Port of Esbjerg recently has been upgraded with a new railway 
terminal. However, the originally planned level of profitability for operators cannot be 
achieved with the actual implementation. This is due to several design flaws that make the 
operation too expensive, according to an interview with DB Schenker Rail Scandinavia 
performed by Larsen (2015). In the article the reason for the high operating costs were 
explained with the lack of inclusion of the operators into the planning process. The main issue 
is that the handling tracks only have a length of 400 m, which is very short. Djørup (2015) 
explains that due to this, freight trains need to be split in the train station of Esbjerg before 
being moved to the port terminal. In the opposite directions additional coupling is required for 
departing freight trains. Another issue is the short distance between Esbjerg and Høje Taastrup. 
With less than 250 km linear distance between the two terminals, the route is below the 
recommended minimum distance for profitable intermodal transport from Wagener (2014).  
Summary 
Based on this analysis it is clear that an efficient operation for freight shuttle trains between the 
Port of Esbjerg and Høje Taastrup is not possible without difficulties. The source of those 
difficulties is the same as for intermodal transport in general: the handling costs. Even though 
the railway operation itself is competitive, the prior and following parts of the transport chain 
influence the profitability of the overall system. Therefore, it would be beneficial to reduce 
these costs as much as possible, which requires efficient handling operation at the terminals. 
The short distance of the route does not increase the competitiveness for the route compared to 
road either. A competitive shuttle train operation on this route, therefore, is assumed to be 
possible if the disadvantages of the terminals can be resolved or their impacts minimised.

in terms of number of handled TEUs. In 2014, more than 5 million TEUs have been imported

million TEUs, China is by far the largest trade partner at the Port of Hamburg, followed by 
Russia, Singapore, South Korea, and the United States. One reason for the continuous growth 
of the Port of Hamburg is China’s entrance into the World Trade Organisation (WTO) in the 
end of 2001 (Bundesamt für Güterverkehr, 2007). Besides this strong position of Hamburg as 
an intercontinental and European port, it also is supported by a large hinterland transportation 
system, dealing with almost 60% of the handled containers at the port. Of this hinterland traffic, 
the modal split of rail is nearly 40%. The rail shuttle system also experienced growth, as the 
volumes increased by 7% between 2013 and 2014. 
The shuttle train system is quite extensive and connects large number of terminals with the Port 
of Hamburg. According to data provided by Hafen Hamburg Marketing e.V. (2015b) there are 
more than 30 domestic lines, more than 10 destinations in Poland, and 8 in the Czech Republic. 
The shuttle trains also serve Denmark, Austria, Switzerland, and Italy. A few services also 
connect the deeper European hinterland in Slovakia and Hungary. Overall, more than 60 
terminals are connected to the Port of Hamburg (Hafen Hamburg Marketing e.V., 2015b). A 
table containing a list of destinations and operators of these lines to the Port of Hamburg can 
be found in the appendix on page 100 in Table 11. 
With more than 12.5 million tons of imports from Germany to Denmark and almost 11.5 
million tons of exports, Germany is by far the largest trade partner of Denmark. As explained 
in the analysis of Høje Taastrup by COWI A/S (2012a), a shipment from an overseas origin to 
Zealand is generally transhipped in one of the major European ports before being delivered to 
Denmark. One of the reasons they identify this is the size of intercontinental container ships. 
Ports on Zealand do not have the capacity for handling this size of ships. Therefore the freight 
is shipped to its destination either on a smaller feeder ship, or on trucks. 
Similarly to the national route analysed in the previous section the distance between the two 
terminals is not very high. The linear distance is around 280 km, which is only slightly longer 
than the route between Høje Taastrup and Esbjerg. Frailey (2015) wrote about a rather short 
intermodal train route in the United States. The line is less than 250 km long, but is used 
profitably for the transportation of freight from China. He argues that the reason for this is that 
the railway part is only a very small part of the overall transportation chain. This argument is 
supported by most shuttle train routes from Gothenburg to the southern part of Sweden, and by

some short routes from Hamburg, e.g. Hamburg-Wismar (less than 110 km), and Hamburg-
Wittenberge (less than 140 km). 
The transhipment between sea and rail at the port of Hamburg is done with an adjacent terminal, 
as introduced in section 2.7.1. At this type of terminal, the railway terminal is situated next to 
the intermediary maritime terminal and transhipping containers between a ship and a train 
requires additional handling, as the facilities are separated (Schwarz, 2006). 
As direct rail freight between Høje Taastrup and Hamburg is already possible since the opening 
of the Great Belt Bridge, the potential for operating freight trains has already been analysed by 
rail operators and freight forwarders. However, recent upgrades in the infrastructure may 
change the conditions to benefit the potential profitability of rail freight. Besides the Fehmarn 
Belt Fixed Link the new line between Copenhagen and Ringsted affects the route as well due 
to the increased capacity between Høje Taastrup and Ringsted. 
COWI A/S (2012a) analyses about the impacts of the Fehmarn Belt on the freight flow. They 
compare three scenarios based on different ways of potential development of the freight rail 
market along this line. The first scenario has unchanged conditions with a constant freight 
volume flow. In the second scenario, they assumes higher costs for sea transportation due to 
environmental regulations, leading to a switch from freight to rail. The third scenario assumes 
a closure of the freight port in Copenhagen, moving its activities completely to Malmö. Even 
though a more thorough analysis of the routes is not part of the project, they are able to provide 
volume estimations. Based on their scenarios, they estimate potential volumes of 8,000 TEUs 
(two half-full trains per week) for the first scenario, 35,000 TEUs (three full shuttle trains) for 
the second scenario, and 70,000 TEUs for scenario 3. 
In addition to this analysis, Intraplan Consult GmbH and BVU Beratergruppe Verkehr Umwelt 
GmbH (2014) also performed a detailed analysis of the freight flows along the Fehmarn Belt 
Fixed Link, which is discussed in this paragraph. Among others, they calculated the estimated 
traffic between different regions in Northern Europe and the rest of Europe for two scenarios 
of different levels of population growth in the future. For this analysis, the data for scenario 1 
with a lower expected population has been chosen. The relevant data are the traffic forecasts 
affecting the international traffic between the eastern part of Denmark and Germany as well as 
the rest of Europe. The forecast shows an increase in freight for all modes between 2022, before 
the link is opened, and 2035. The share of rail freight is, however, not expected to change very 
much: In 2022, about 5.41% of the goods are estimated to be transported by rail (conventional

Summary 
A shuttle train between the Port of Hamburg and Høje Taastrup is a reasonable route to 
consider. With the opening of the Fehmarn Belt Fixed Link, the route the route becomes more 
attractive for rail operators – but so does road transportation via the tunnel. One of the analyses 
summarised here shows a lack of potential for a significant increase of rail freight after the 
opening of the tunnel. However, demand for intermodal trains on routes to international routes 
to European countries other than Germany has been shown and is worth further investigation. 
Nevertheless, another analysis shows that depending on the conditions, there is a great potential 
for shuttle trains. The data about trade between Denmark and Germany show the demand for 
high capacity, but competition comes from not only road, but also sea transportation. The 
greatest potential for rail freight is in the case of stricter environmental regulations by 
governments.

9. 
Conclusion 
Denmark is a small country surrounded by water, making the conditions for freight rail more 
complicated than in many other countries. Freight railway operators not only compete with 
road transportation, which dominates the market more than in most other European countries; 
they also compete with sea transportation, which is available for most major cities in the 
country. At the same time, the requirements for efficient operation at the intermodal terminals 
in Denmark are not optimal. Nevertheless, there is potential for an increased shift of freight 
from road to rail. New infrastructural projects remove bottlenecks, increase capacity and create 
new possibilities for freight operators. In combination with technologies for transporting semi-
trailers by rail, new opportunities are created. Finally, potential government regulations for a 
reduction of emissions can increase the competitiveness of rail freight. 
Chapter 2 presented an introduction into freight transportation in general and on rail, and 
explained the benefits and disadvantages of intermodal transport and dry ports. It also gave an 
insight into the Danish railway network and presented the idea and impacts of the regulations 
by the European Union for a liberalised railway market, and it introduced the freight 
transportation systems of Denmark, Sweden, German, and the Netherlands and pointed out the 
major differences. In chapter 3 different methods for transporting freight have been introduced, 
including a list of different technologies created so that more semi-trailers can be transported 
by rail. The technologies fulfil the same purpose, but have a few differences in the way the 
work. Chapter 4 explained the transport corridors that affect Denmark. Chapter 5 presented the 
intermodal terminals in Denmark. Høje Taastrup, Taulov, and Padborg are the only cities in 
Denmark with public intermodal terminals. While Padborg has several issues that limit an 
efficient freight train operation, Taulov and Høje Taastrup provide a quite acceptable service 
level. Nevertheless, all terminals are served by freight train operators. In addition to those 
terminals the Port of Esbjerg has been introduced and a short overview to the Port of Aarhus 
and the private terminal in Fredericia was given. 
In chapter 6 the shuttle train system of the Port of Gothenburg was presented. The largest port 
of Scandinavia experiences a continuous growth of volume of rail and benefit from the system 
due to reduction of external effects in Gothenburg and the rest of Sweden. The organisation 
and the involved stakeholders have been introduced and the experiences and satisfaction with 
the system presented. Chapter 7 showed a list of bottlenecks in the Danish railway network. 
==PAGE:92==
 
Matthias Schett – Container Shuttle Train Feasibility in Denmark 
Page 92 of 105 
 
Kastrup station, the triangle on Jutland and other sections with high capacity usage or single 
track elements reduce the overall capacity and lead to congestion on the rail. An approach for 
resolving the issues of punctuality of transit freight trains as well as the possible impacts of 
future infrastructure projects have been presented. 
Finally, chapter 8 introduced a list of possible domestic and international shuttle train routes 
based on freight volumes of different modes of transportation. Based on criteria such as 
distance, volume, and potential competition by rail the potential routes have been evaluated in 
order to identify two strong candidates: first a domestic route from the terminal in Høje 
Taastrup to the Port of Esbjerg, second an international route from the terminal in Høje 
Taastrup to the Port of Hamburg. Both routes offer potential in terms of freight volumes, but 
have difficulties due to terminal handling, reducing the competitiveness to road transportation.  
The on-going railway infrastructure projects will help to increase the competitiveness of rail 
freight in Denmark. The issue of low efficiency in the Danish intermodal terminals is a point 
that could definitely be improved. Future research could also focus on a more thorough 
investigation of the potential routes for getting a better overview about the freight flows within 
Denmark, but also between Denmark and other countries. Another topic that would be 
interesting to find out more about are possible locations for new intermodal terminals.
