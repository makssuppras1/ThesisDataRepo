Abstract
With new sharpened energy requirements regarding the building envelope, resulting in
more air tight buildings and a higher degree of thermal insulation, fire compartment propôÙ
erties used to calculate design fire development are changing. One of these properties,
the opening factor, is often determined based on the assumption that window glazing
breaks and falls out completely before flashover. In order to gain a better understanding
of the whether, when and to which degree modern glazing types can be assumed to break
and create a ventilation opening in case of fire, a total of 41 experiments are conducted to
investigate the effect of insulted glazing units (IGUãs), lamination, thermal tempering and
integrated solar shading on the breakage behavior of glazing subjected to thermal load
simulating fire conditions.
The effect of fire is simulated by use of a radiant panel as heat source, delivering a radiant
heat flux to the 400 x 400 mm glazing at a level corresponding to flashover conditions,
namely 15ôÙ20 kW/m2. Due to nonôÙuniformity of the received heat flux, the heat flux at
the center of the glazing is 19.5 kW/m2, while the average over the exposed surface is
estimated to be 17.3 kW/m2. All glass panes have a thickness of 4 mm. For the samples
with multiple panes and/or laminated panes, gas cavities of 16 mm and lamination foils of
0.76 mm are used. For the samples with integrated solar shading, a MicroShadeôÛ MSôÙF
60/14 solar film is used. All glazings are framed with a shading width of 20 mm at all four
edges. The glazings are subjected to the heat flux until cracking or for a total exposure
time of 30 minutes. The primary conclusions are as follows:
ãÂ No cracking was observed in toughened glass panes, with or without solar shading
foil, thus no ventilation opening were created for these glass types
ãÂ Cracking was observed in all nonôÙtoughened glass panes, monolithic or laminated,
in all layers of multiôÙpane glazing.
ãÂ Laminated glass panes will retain enough stiffness after cracking to remain in the
frame and not fall out, thus not creating a ventilation opening.
ãÂ Front nonôÙtoughened panes break at about the same time, independent of laminaôÙ
tion and subsequent layers in the IGU.
ãÂ In laminated layers, the time to crack of the pane furthest from the fire is on average
twice the time to crack of the pane closest to the fire.
ãÂ In insulated glazing units, the time to crack of a pane succeeding a gas filled cavity
is on average 5:20 minutes after crack of the preceding layer. The time is shorter
when the preceding layer falls out.
ãÂ No significant effect on the time to first crack was observed in MicroShadeôÛ glazing.
ãÂ A strong correlation between fracture stress and time to first crack or temperature
difference at crack was not observed.
The research shows that even though the time to first crack of the individual panes in
multiôÙlayer glazing is delayed, they do crack and thus create the possibility of a ventilaôÙ
tion opening. Thus, the time for a ventilation opening through windows to occur should
be included in fire modeling. Though an actual ventilation opening was not observed in
the experiments, other effects of a compartment fire, such as overôÙpressure, were not
included in the study and should be investigated in order to establish to which degree a
ventilation opening is created in multiôÙlayer windows.

Introduction
With new sharpened energy requirements regarding the building envelope, resulting in
more air tight buildings and a higher degree of thermal insulation, fire compartment propôÙ
erties used to calculate design fire development are changing. Fully developed fires were
analytically described by Pettersen et al. [1] by use of three such fire compartment propôÙ
erties:
ãÂ Fuel load density, q [MJ/m2]
ãÂ Thermal inertia of the enclosing surface, b [Wsô§/Km]
ãÂ Opening factor, O [mô§]
Fuel load density, q, measures the energy content in the material in the compartment
uniformly distributed on the entire enclosure area. The thermal inertia of the enclosing
surface, b, measures the heat stored in the room enclosure, and the opening factor, O,
measures the level of ventilation in the room. The parameters are used for calculating the
temperatureôÙtime curve of the fire progression [2]:

It is seen that a higher fuel load density leads to a more severe fire by increasing the fire
duration, tmax, as well as the peak gas temperature in the compartment, Tg. A decrease in
opening factor results in a longer fire, but decreasing the peak gas temperature. Lastly, a
decrease in thermal inertia results in a higher peak gas temperature. It can be shown that
insulated steel and concrete elements reach higher peak temperatures if the enclosing
thermal inertia or the opening factor decreases [1].
On February 1st 2021 new stricter requirements were effectuated in the Danish building
regulation [3] regarding the UôÙvalue of windows in regular building rooms, which means
that going forward, primarily 3ôÙlayer glazing with lowôÙemissivity coatings will be used in
buildings in Denmark. In contrast to the old oneôÙpane window glazing, which would typiôÙ
cally break before flashover and give way for a higher degree of ventilation, these multiôÙ
layer glazings will resist the fire much longer and might not give way for additional venôÙ
tilation during the fire [4]. In turn, the thermal solicitation of structural elements can be
severely increased in modern buildings.
Additionally, more complex glazing that is designed to mitigate problems of overheating
in buildings are entering the market, e.g. glazing where solar shading or solar control
coating is integrated between the glass layers. This, too, potentially affects the windowãs
fireôÙinduced breakage behavior.
Whether, when, and to what extend multiôÙlayer glazing commonly used in buildings today
will break during a fire is at present unknown. As a consequence, the decrement of the
opening factor and increase in fire severity is also unknown. This project will investigate
the fireôÙinduced breakage behavior of such multiôÙlayer window glazings. The knowledge
gained will serve as a step towards a reôÙcalibration of the calculation method of the openôÙ
ing factor for design fires.

1.1
Literature study
An extensive amount of research has been conducted on single layer glazing since the
need for it was stressed by Emmons in 1986 [5]. The research has sought to establish
the effect of different factors, such as shading width [6, 7], heat rate [6, 8], pane thickness
[9], pane type [10], and edge conditions [11], on breakage of the glass pane during fire.
The body of research, including the work conducted by Skelly [12] and Pagni and Joshi
[13, 14], has established that the thermal gradient between the area directly exposed to
radiant heat and the area shaded by the frame is the main cause for crack initiation.
In 1988, KeskiôÙRahkomen [15] deduced the relation

Research pertaining to multiôÙlayer glazing has been more limited, especially concerning
tripleôÙlayer glazing. Shields et al. [16] studied the behavior of double glazing exposed
to enclosure fire. The test pool comprised two window sizes of 845 mm x 845 mm and
1897 mm x 845 mm, using 6 mm clear float glass panes separated by a 12 mm air filled
cavity and with a shaded edge width of 20 mm. The test setup simulated a two zone and
one zone fire environment, making the location of the windows in the facade significant.
The experiments showed that the small top glazing in the window assembly, which was
irradiated with the highest heat flux, cracked after the shortest period of time. Even though
the large window was subjected to the smallest incident heat flux, this glazing cracked
next. This trend was found for both interior and exterior pane in the glazings. Cracking
times were estimated by use of a lumped heat transfer model with experimentally derived
input data, showing good agreement.
Klassen et al.[17] analyzed doubleôÙ and tripleôÙpane glazingãs reaction to large fires outôÙ
side of buildings. The test pool comprised seven types of both laminated and insulated
glazing of three different sizes: small (305 mm x 305 mm), medium (609 mm x 1219 mm)
and large (1219 mm x 2438 mm). All glass panes had a thickness of 6.35 mm, the air gap
in the three layer glazing was 12.7 mm and the type and thickness of the lamination inôÙ
terlayer was varying. A total of 113 experiments were conducted. The small and medium
samples were subjected to radiant heat from two different sources: A radiant panel and
a pool fire. Incident heat fluxes ranged between 5ôÙ50 kW/m2. It was found that the two
heat sources produced very similar results and therefore suggested that the radiant panel,
which is more economic and simpler to reproduce, be used as a test standard. The study
showed that the glazing with an airspace transmitted less of the incident radiation, since
the additional optical surfaces absorbed and reflected a larger fraction of the incident raôÙ
diation. As a general trend, the larger samples could withstand a higher incident heat
flux for a longer time before cracking. Generally for the small samples, cracking occurred
when subjected to an incident heat flux of 20 kW/m2 for toughened glass after 192ôÙ456
seconds and a temperature difference of 81ôÙ157ãÎC. For heat strengthened glass, crackôÙ
ing occurred at a lower heat flux, 10 kW/m2, after 116ôÙ120 second and a temperature
difference of 214ôÙ268ãÎC. Since the glass panes were tinted gray or green, a larger proôÙ
portional of the incident heat flux is absorbed rather than transmitted compared to a clear

Wang et al.[18] investigated the critical breakage conditions for double glazing in encloôÙ
sure fire. The glazing consisted of two layers 6 mm float glass panes separated by a 6
mm air gap and measured 600 mm x 600 mm. The frame was made of stainless steel
with a shading width of 20 mm. A total of nine experiments were conducted. The samôÙ
ples were subjected to radiant heat from a pool fire. The distance between the glazing
and the burner, as well as the mass of the fuel, were varied in order to achieve different
heating rates. The critical heat fluxes of the exposed and ambient panes were found to be
6 kW/m2 and 25 kW/m2 respectively, though only one of the nine tests resulted in breakôÙ
age of the ambient pane. The study found that the crack initiated from the edge with the
largest temperature difference between the shaded and exposed area at temperatures
from 48ôÙ78ãÎC. It was proposed that the exposed pane behaves as a single pane glazing
with a critical temperature difference of 60ãÎC, whereas the second layer has a different
breakage behavior, partly due to the frame not shading the ambient layer in the same way
as the exposed one. It was recommended to further investigate the effect of the thickness
of the air gap, as well as the radiation transmitted through and the breakage behavior of
the exposed pane.
Wang et al. [19] further studied the effect of insulated glazing compared to laminated
and single layer glazing. All glass panes had a thickness of 6 mm. The air gap in the
insulated glazing unit was 6 mm and the interlayer in the laminated glazing was 0.38 mm
PVB. Analogous to the study described previously, the size of the glazing was 600 mm x
600 mm, the shading width was 20 mm and the radiant heat source was a pool fire. In
total, ten experiment were conducted. The study confirmed that the exposed pane in the
insulated glazing had a breakage condition similar to single layer glazing, whereas the
laminated glazing stayed in place after breakage, and thereby did not form a new vent.
At the time of breakage of pane 2, the total heat flux on the insulated glazing unit was
55 kW/m2 and the central temperature 84ãÎC, while on the laminated glazing it was 13ôÙ14
kW/m2 with a central temperature of 61ôÙ73ãÎC. Since the test pool was very small, more
experiments are needed to confirm these findings.
The experiments later formed the basis for a numerical study on the influence of lamination
on the glazingãs fire resistance, which found that both number of glass layers and thickness
of the interlayer have a significant influence on the breakage time [20].
Huizinga et al.[21] studied the effect of doubleôÙ and tripleôÙglazing on fire safety using an
ISO 834 standard fire furnace, a test setup similar to the one used by Shields et al.[16].
Two sizes of glazing were tested, 924 mm x 924 mm and 1897 mm x 924 mm. A total of 16
glazings were tested. All glass panes were clear float glass and had a thickness of 4 mm.
Air cavities were 12 mm with a 90 % argon filling. The study found indications of the size
of the window being more normative than the number of panes with regards to fallout, with
fallout happening earlier and to a larger extent for larger windows. This finding contradicts
that of Klassen et al.[17], leaving the effect of glass pane size uncovered. The study found
no correlation between the temperature difference and individual glass fallout, which was
explained by a large influence of imperfections in the glass. Temperature differences at

Fracture mechanics
The breakage of glass often happens instantaneously and without warning. This brittle
response to load stems from the materialãs random molecular structure, which has no slip
planes and therefore is unable to yield. Without the possibility to yield, the fracture of glass
is brittle and cracks propagate at a characteristic speed of 1500 m/s [25]. The material
behavior is perfectly linear elastic from zero strain to the point of failure at temperatures
below 500 ãÎC. The compressive strength of glass can be as high as 10,000 MPa, while
the tensile strength is less than 100 MPa [41]. According to the Danish building research

Since glass allows for no plastic redistribution of stress, stress is concentrated around
surface flaws. A glass pane will have a number of flaws from the production process, so
small that they are not detectable by the naked eye. The flaws are sometimes referred
to as Griffith flaws [42]. Tensile stress in the glass pane will lead to growth of the flaws,
as they are pulled apart. This growth will not necessarily lead to breakage of the glass,
why it is referred to as subôÙcritical crack growth. This effect makes the tensile strength of
the glass very dependent on its stress history. The extent of undetectable surface cracks,
stress history, and also environmental conditions such as humidity, have a large impact
on the tensile strength of the glass, making it is very hard to predict.
At elevated temperatures, the linear elastic behavior of glass changes and the response
becomes viscoôÙelastic. The point at which the response changes is around 500 ãÎC [25].

glazing. Shields et al [16] found temperatures peaking at around 400 ãÎC after 25 minutes
of exposure to a heat flux of about 20 kW/m2.

Effect of integrated solar shading
A total of 14 tests were conducted which included a MicroShadeôÛ integrated solar shading
(sample codes T and S). None of the solar shaded glass panes cracked during a 30 minute
exposure to the radiant panel.
4.3.1
Time to first crack
Pane 1 in the glazings comprising a MicroShade solar shading film cracked an average
time of 64 s with a standard deviation of 8.6 s. This is somewhat faster than the general
average of 80 s and with a smaller variability. Though one might attribute this difference
to the higher absorptance (70%) of the solar shaded layer, and thus higher reôÙradiation,
the crack happens so fast that the temperature rise of the subsequent layers is not large
enough to make a significant impact on the temperature of the first layer. Had this been
the case, the effect would be assumed to be most pronounced in the two layer glazing
(S2b), where the solar shaded layer directly succeeds the first layer, but the average time
to first crack for this glazing type is in fact slower, with an average time to first crack of 77 s.
Calculations show that the absorptance of the nonôÙsolar shaded layers in a MicroShadeôÛ
glazing are the same (to 3 significant digits) as the corresponding layers in regular glazing,
see appendix A.3.

faster, e.g. for test T3a.2 where crack of pane 2 happens after about 4 minutes. It is asôÙ
sumed to be due to the softening of the lamination foil with temperature, which decreases
the resistance, allowing cracks to propagate faster..
It is also observed that multiple thermal breaks are happening in the same glass pane, as
seen in figure 4.12. This was not observed in the monolithic glass panes. It is assumed
to be made possible by the continued integration of the glass pane due to the lamination
foil. Thereby, the tension is not released by the cracking.

5
Conclusion
5.1
Summary and highlights
A series of experiments were conducted to investigate the effect of insulated glazing units,
lamination, thermal tempering and integrated solar shading on the breakage behavior of
glazing in fire conditions.
The effect of fire is simulated by use of a radiant panel as heat source, delivering a radiant
heat flux to the 400 x 400 mm glazing at a level corresponding to flashover conditions,
namely 15ôÙ20 kW/m2. Due to nonôÙuniformity of the received heat flux, the heat flux at
the center of the glazing is 19.5 kW/m2, while the average over the exposed surface is
estimated to be 17.3 kW/m2. All glass panes have a thickness of 4 mm. For the samples
with multiple panes and/or laminated panes, 90% argon gas cavities of 16 mm and PVB
lamination foils of 0.76 mm are used. For the samples with integrated solar shading, a
MicroShadeôÛ MSôÙF 60/14 solar film is used. All glazings are framed with a shading width
of 20 mm at all four edges. The glazings are subjected to the heat flux until cracking or
for a total exposure time of 30 minutes. After 30 minutes the temperature of the exposed
glass pane had reached a steady temperature and it was was deemed unlikely that the
glazing break even with longer fire exposure.
The primary conclusions are as follows:
ãÂ Cracking was observed in all nonôÙtoughened glass panes, monolithic or laminated,
in all layers of multiôÙpane glazing.
ãÂ No cracking was observed in toughened glass panes, with or without solar shading
foil, thus no ventilation opening were created for these glass types. A peak bulk
temperature difference of ãTbulk = 120 ãÎC between the exposed and shaded areas
of the glazing was observed after 7 minutes of exposure in the single toughened
glazing without solar shading. The expected minimum critical temperature differôÙ
ence was ãTc = 168 ãÎC based on the characteristic tensile strength of toughened
glass.
ãÂ Front nonôÙtoughened panes break at about the same time, independent of lamiôÙ
nation and subsequent layers in the IGU. The time to first crack is approximately
normally distributed with mean value of 80 sec and standard deviation of 20 sec.
The average calculated fracture stress, derived from measured fracture mirror radii,
was 47 MPa, which was comparable to literature. The measured temperature difôÙ
ference were not as high as expected with the given fracture stress, indicating that
the temperature measurement were inaccurate.
ãÂ In laminated layers, the time to crack of the pane furthest from the fire is on average
twice the time to crack of the pane closest to the fire. It was observed that laminated
glass panes retain enough stiffness after cracking to remain in the frame and not fall
out, thus not creating a ventilation opening.
ãÂ In insulated glazing units, the time to crack of a pane succeeding a gas filled cavity
is on average 5:20 minutes after crack of the preceding layer. The time is shorter
when the preceding layer falls out. No fallout of the ambient pane in the IGU was
observed, thus no ventilation opening was created.

ãÂ No significant effect on the time to first crack was observed in MicroShadeôÛ glazing.
The crack initiation in the first pane in MicroShadeôÛ glazings was on average 62
seconds, which was faster than the general average, but the observations of pane
directly preceding the solar shaded pane were not faster than the average, and thus
the difference is explained by inherent variability.
ãÂ A strong correlation between fracture stress or time to first crack and temperature
difference at first crack was not observed. The variability in temperature is assumed
to be epistemic rather than intrinsic.
Based on the experimental results it is concluded that the creation of ventilation openings
through multiôÙlayer glazings are at best delayed. The results indicate that, for the given
glazing specimen and experimental setup, ventilation openings through laminated and
toughened glass panes are unlikely.
5.2
Future research
The experimental research conducted for this thesis indicated that all panes in multiôÙlayer
glazing with up to 3 panes will crack at flashover conditions, when the panes are not
thermally tempered. However, a clear correlation between the time to first crack, fracture
stress and temperature difference was not confirmed. It would be interesting to investigate
how to model the glazing behavior with finite element modeling. This would enable the
possibility to monitor temperature and stress conditions in support of the experimental
work, as well as provide the opportunity to do more parametric studies.
It would be interesting to establish a correlation between the results when applying the
measured temperature vs when applying the heat flux, since the heat flux is more easily
estimated in design situations. In this work, the calculations of total glazing transmittance,
shown in appendix A.3 would likely be useful.
Other areas for future research
ãÂ A study focused on improving the experimental setup should be conducted. This
should include the development of a method for attaching the thermocouples such
that they stay in the designated place in the shaded area of the glazing, possibly with
adhesive. It would also require development on the frame assembly to more easily
confirm that thermocouples are placed where they are intended. Furthermore, a
more thorough mapping of the heat flux output from the radiant panel of the HôÙTRIS
should be conducted.
ãÂ In order to establish a new method of calculating the opening factor, which takes
into account the degree of fallout as well as the temporal delay of first crack in multiôÙ
layer glazing panes, it is necessary to conduct more experimental work focused on
fallout. This study must better mimic the pressure conditions in a real compartment
fire.
ãÂ The size of glazings used in this study is not representative of glazing used in buildôÙ
ings today, where large windows are often used. It would be interesting to study
effect of changing the size of the glazing and possibly establish a correlation beôÙ
tween size and time to breakage.
ãÂ Even though 4 mm is the most commonly used thickness of glass panes today,
varying thicknesses are often used in IGU for purposes of sound reduction. It would
be interesting to study the effect of changing the glass pane thickness and possibly
establish a correlation between thickness and time to breakage.

B.1
Experiment checklist
Every test performed follows the same procedure as described in this appendix.
Fundamental setup
1. Place the HôÙTRIS and window frame structure under the ventilation hood
2. Confirm that the window frame structure is placed in the correct position, 430 mm
from and parallel to the radiant panel
3. Turn on the ventilation hood
Preparation of test element
1. Examine the test sample for visible flaws. Document if any.
2. Attach pieces of aluminum tape to the surface of the sample which will face the
radiant panel at the center of each edge 30 mm from the edge, and at the center of
the glazing
3. Place the specimen on the setting blocks in the frame and ensure that all edge
aluminum tape pieces are 10 mm away from the frame edge
4. Place the back frame behind the test specimen
5. Place the thermocouples in the assigned locations behind the frame through the
holes drilled in the frame, bending the tip behind the frame to ensure location
6. Fix the back frame by tightening the wing headed bolts evenly
7. Place the thermocouples in the assigned locations on the glass back surface ensurôÙ
ing that the tension in the wires keep them in place
8. Attach thermocouple underneath the aluminum tape on the front side of the samples
9. Assess that the front frame and reflective tape is flush
10. Install heat flux gauge in the designated position
11. Ensure that the glass sample is within field of view of the camera
12. Place the shield between the HôÙTRIS and the window frame structure
Heat flux gauge
1. Connect tubes to the water supply connectors on the heat flux gauge
2. Fill a container with water and submerge the water pump
3. Turn on the water pump and ensure flow through the heat flux gauge
Preparation of data acquisition
1. Turn on PC connected to the data acquisition system
2. Open BenchLink date acquisition software
3. Ensure that all relevant channels are properly configured:
