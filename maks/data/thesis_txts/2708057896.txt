Abstract
Modern long-span bridges are subjected to substantial dynamic interaction in relation
to high-speed train passage. The dynamic eﬀects can aﬀect both passenger comfort and
train runability and safety.
This thesis is a continuation of an ongoing collaboration
between the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and Rambøll Group A/S with the
primary goal of developing a vehicle-bridge interaction (VBI)-programme, which can be
used as a tool for assessing passenger comfort and runability of train passage on long-
span bridges. Commercial tools for doing VBI-analysis exists, but the underlying theory
and documentation are not readily available, resulting in the need for a well documented
numerical tool for this purpose.
The present thesis aims to continue the development of the VBI-programme, which was
ﬁrst created by Groes [1] and later further developed by Indrebø [2]. When assessing the
runability of trains on long-span bridges, the eﬀect of a ship impact on a bridge pier should
be considered. The scope of the present thesis is to model the structure-soil interaction
through non-linear springs, such that a non-linear simulation of train passage on long-span
bridges is made possible in the framework of the VBI-programme. Initially, the theory
behind the VBI-programme is presented in the current state, and the general theory behind
the vehicle-bridge coupling is presented. The non-linear Newmark time integration is then
implemented based on [3], alongside a general structure for the spring implementation
adopted from [4]. By extending the static 3D Finite Element (FE)-programme MaxiFrame
provided by DTU [5] to a dynamic programme, the non-linear Newmark time integration
and non-linear spring implementations are by modelling through simple two degrees of
freedom (DOF) systems.
The non-linear time integration is modiﬁed to be used in
the modal space, as the bridge model in the VBI-programme is described by modal
coordinates. A simple T-structure, which through boundary conditions resembles a part
of a bridge structure, is modelled in the FE software SOFiSTiK and used for the input in
the VBI-programme. Through careful changes and testing, the non-linear time integration
and hyperbolic tangential softening spring model is implemented as an add-on to the
existing VBI-programme. The implementation is veriﬁed, and by modelling a ship impact
as an impulse load, the eﬀect of the non-linear simulations compared to linear simulations
concerning overturning of the vehicle is assessed. It is shown that the critical impulse
load concerning overturning is 6 % larger for the non-linear simulation than for the linear.
The downside of the non-linear simulations is the runtime, which is increased by 50 % for
the chosen parameters. In the end, the non-linear simulations are found to improve the
critical load concerning overturning for the chosen bridge model and spring parameters.

Introduction
Modern long-span bridges have become increasingly longer and more slender resulting
in the structures being prone to dynamic eﬀects. The combination of the long slender
structures with highway and railway traﬃc, the dynamic eﬀects are of signiﬁcant
importance. When examining the train passage of long-span bridges, the passage might
be associated with substantial dynamic interaction. This interaction can aﬀect both the
safety of the bridge structure and the safe runability of the train, and combined with
external excitations such as ship impact or natural loads such as earthquakes and wind,
the need for advanced analysis tools is eminent. The analysis of high-speed train passage
on long-span bridges exists in diﬀerent commercial frameworks. However, the underlying
theory and documentation of implementation are not readily available, resulting in the
need for a well documented numerical tool for this purpose.
The present thesis is the third thesis in continuation of an ongoing collaboration between
Rambøll Group A/S and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The collaboration
aims to develop a well documented vehicle-bridge interaction (VBI)-programme, which can
be used to assess the dynamic interaction due to the train passage of long-span bridges
and analyse the runability of the train when the bridge is exposed to external loading
such as ship impacts. The present thesis aims to further develop the programme initially
made by Jacob Groes in the MSc Thesis "Numerical Analysis of Vehicle-Bridge Interaction
during passage of High-speed Trains across Long-span bridges" [1], and later modiﬁed by
Andreas Indrebø in the MSc Thesis "Derailment Analysis for High-speed Trains on Long-
span Bridges" [2].
The VBI-programme is designed to function as a numerical tool, which relies on the
input from a commercial Finite Element (FE) software, capable of modelling the dynamic
characteristics of a bridge structure by using spatial linear-elastic beam elements. The
commercial FE model can then provide the input used for the VBI-programme. The input
consists of node coordinates, mode shapes and natural frequencies resulting in the VBI-
programme modelling the bridge in modal coordinates. The vehicle model implemented by
[2], which is based on a realistic train model from the Storestrøm Bridge Design Basis [6],
is a 3D vehicle model, which is coupled to the bridge through location-dependent system
matrices, which are assembled based on kinematic relations. The vehicle model, which is
a 13 degree of freedom (DOF) model, is modelled in the geometric space, resulting in the
VBI-programme having modal and geometric coordinates.
The current thesis aims to model the structure-soil interaction through non-linear springs.
It is well known that the behaviour of soil is non-linear [7], and therefore the relevance
of non-linear modelling of the interaction is highly relevant. Initially, the Matlab based
3D static FE programme MaxiFrame provided by DTU [5] is extended to a dynamic FE
programme and tested to verify the extension. The framework is then used to implement
the non-linear Newmark time integration scheme based on [8], which is needed for solving
the non-linear equation of motion. The time integration scheme is also implemented in the
modal space, which is needed for implementing the non-linear spring model into the VBI
programme. Through simple dynamic systems, both the non-linear spring model and the
non-linear time integration are tested and veriﬁed before ﬁnally being implemented in the

VBI programme
This chapter will present the relevant theory used for the development of the current
vehicle-bridge interaction (VBI) programme developed ﬁrst by Groes [1] and later by
Indrebø [2]. This section is created based on [1][2], and these heavily inspire both text
and ﬁgures.
The VBI-programme is designed to rely on the input of an existing FE software. The
requirements for this model is that the vehicle path is modelled by linear-elastic beam
elements and that the programme can capture the vibration modes of the structure. The
VBI-programme requires three input parameters; node coordinate matrix Xin of the path
of the vehicle motion, mass normalized mode shape matrix Sin describing the same nodes
as in Xin, and a vector fin containing the natural frequencies of the corresponding mode
shapes. It should be noted, that all modes have the same modal mass, as the mode shapes
in Sin are normalized independently to a modal mass of m = 1000. This is done to simplify
the input needed, as well as to represent the bridge with just one modal mass.

Non-linear implementation
Physical systems are inherently non-linear.
We often use linear approximations of
system’s, as the systems operating range is limited, and a linear model is then suﬃciently
accurate.
Sometimes, a linear approximation will not be suﬃciently accurate, and a
non-linear model of the system needs to be established.
The non-linearity can occur
in diﬀerent ways.
Geometrical non-linearities typically occur from large deﬂections or
rotations of a system. An example here can be the Taylor approximation of a sine function
sin(θ) = θ −1
6θ3 + . . ., where for small rotations, the approximation made in section 2.2.1
(sin(θ) ≈θ) is suﬃcient, but it is no longer valid for large rotations. The non-linearities
can also be presented by a material non-linearity. A requirement for the input of the
VBI-programme is that the FE-model of the bridge must be done with linear-elastic beam
elements. However, some materials might not have a linear stress-strain relation, which
must be considered for large strain variations [9].
For this project, the structure-soil
interaction is of interest. Often, this interaction is modelled with springs, as the stiﬀness
of the support will be between a ﬁxed and free connection. For large deformations, the
stress-strain relationship of the soil will be non-linear. Large deformations might occur due
to ship impact on the piers, whereby a non-linear model of the springs is relevant. When
dealing with non-linear systems, the EOM eq. (2.1) is no longer valid. The non-linear
EOM for a system can, in general terms, be written as
M¨u(t) + g (u(t), ˙u(t)) = F(t)
(3.1)
where g (u(t), ˙u(t)) denotes the systems internal energy and contains both the damping
and stiﬀness terms of the system. The exact equation for the internal energy is dependent
on the system, and the modelled non-linearity
3.1
Non-linear Newmark time integration
As the EOM of the system is non-linear, the Newmark algorithm currently implemented
in the VBI-programme is not suﬃcient, and a non-linear time integration scheme is to be
implemented. Multiple schemes are available for non-linear time integration as described
in, e.g.
[8], [14].
The chosen scheme is the non-linear Newmark time integration as
described by [8]. The non-linear Newmark algorithm predicts the velocity and acceleration
of the system, while the displacement is solved for through an iterative solution of the
EOM. The linear Newmark time integration predicts the velocity and displacement from
the acceleration and then solves for the acceleration. The Newmark algorithm is satisﬁed

The implementation of the springs in the system can be done in diﬀerent ways. The chosen
approach is from [4], which is for dampers, but the approach is the same for springs. The
approach uses a spring placement matrix Bs with the dimensions (n × p) where n is the
number of DOFs in the system, and p is the number of springs. The spring placement
matrix Bs consists of 0, 1 or −1 depending on the inﬂuence of the spring on the DOF.
The value of 1 or −1 corresponds to the spring aﬀecting the DOF in either a positive or
negative direction relative to the global axes. For a spring with a symmetric work curve,
the sign of the entry is not important, only the value. A 0 entry means the spring does not
aﬀect the DOF. A diagonal matrix Γ is also introduced, containing the spring stiﬀnesses
in the diagonal elements, resulting in a (p×p) matrix. For a linear spring, the spring force

softening behaviour/eﬀect.

increasing deformation, and the spring is said to be hardening. A softening eﬀect is the
opposite, where the stiﬀness decreases for increasing deformations [9]. The eﬀect can be

often p-y curves are used, which gives the force-deﬂection relation of the speciﬁc structure
site. A model can then be ﬁtted to the site speciﬁc data, resulting in a realistic spring
model. For this project a model based on a hyperbolic tangent is used, where the spring

well. For a sand layer, the hyperbolic tangent can be used to generate p-y curves according

3.5.1
Spring parameter values
The inﬂuence of the two spring parameters has been examined. In general terms, it can
be said that the amplitude parmater, or spring amplitude f0 models the capacity of the
soil/spring. The slope parameter, or spring stiﬀness ks describes how steep the work curve
is. It can safely be assumed, that the amplitude parameter f0 is much larger than the
slope, i.e. f0 >> ks. Considering a monopile for an oﬀshore wind turbine, if the spring
force would primarily be in the ﬂat part of the work curve, the monopile would either
tip over, or drift away for constant loading. The spring parameters will always be site
speciﬁc, whereby a set of values is chosen, which are in the same order of magnitude, as
typical parameters used to model sand. The chosen parameters are shown in table 3.4.
The spring stiﬀness ks is assumed equal for rotational and translational springs, while the
spring amplitude is assumed halfed for the rotation springs compared to the translational.

The work curves of the non-linear springs have until now been equal to the backbone curve
of the chosen work law, which for the hyperbolic softenening spring was given by eq. (3.38).
It is well known, that when soil is exposed to cycling loading hysteresis loops are formed.
THe hysteresis loops dissipates energy in the system system [20][21]. For this project,
the model suggested by Pyke [22], hereby denoted Pykes model, is implemented. Pykes
model adopts the two ﬁrst rules suggested by Massing [23]. The rules are as described
by [22]: "(1) The shear Modulus on each loading reversal assumes a value equal to the
initial tangent modulus for the initial loading curve: and (2) the shape of the unloading
or reloading curves is the same as that of the initial loading curve, except that the scale is
enlarged by a factor of two.". Pykes model, explained in it simplest form, suggest ﬁtting a
hyperbola from the last reversal point to the asymptote deﬁned by τr/τy = ±1, where τr
is the shear strain at the latest reversal point, τy is a reference strain, and the sign of the
1 depends on the direction of loading, where it is positive for reloading, and negative for
unloading. Pykes model is equivalent to the second Massings rule, but instead of using a
factor two, a factor of cp which is described by the relation

5.1
T-structure implementation in VBI
In the current state, as delivered by [2], the VBI-programme is only capable of using deck
structure as input to conduct the analysis. Changes are therefore needed in the proramme,
so that structural elements besides the deck can be modelled. In this case a column for the
T-structure, which can reasemble either a pylon or a pier for the bridge. The principles
used are though valid for other structural elements as well. As described in section 2.5,
the function used for assembling the interaction matrix b and submatrices here oﬀ, which
are the matrices used for coupling the vehicle and bridge structures, uses the four matrices
• Node coordinate matrix X
• Third node coordinate matrix X3
• Topology matrix T
• Mode shape matrix S
In the current state, these matrices only contains information regarding the deck nodes,
as this is what the FE model input contains. The principle used to model additional
structural alements are therefore to divide the above mentioned matrices into two sets of
matrices. One set which contains the full structure which will be denoted with subscript
f, and one set containing only the the deck nodes denoted with subscript d. When this is
achieved, two changes are to be made
1. The load vector is converted from geometric to modal coordinates by the full mode
shape matrix Sf
2. The modal bridge coordinates xb are transformed to the geometric space with the
full mode shape matrix Sf
theese two changes are to only changes needed to model additional structural elements.
The input for the main function which computes the Newmark time integration is the
four matrices Xd, X3, d, Td, and Sd. By doing this, the functions which assembles the
interaction matrix b still works, and only ﬁnds the nodes related to the bridge deck.
The seperation of the input matrices into the sub-matrices only containing the bridge
deck is done in the VBI programme. For the T-structure, the procedure is quite simple,
but for more complex bridge structures containing multiple piers, pylons and cables, one

5.4
Eﬀect of the non-linear springs
The eﬀect of the non-linear hyperbolic springs is to be examined. The springs will dissipate
energy from the system at the bottom of the column, but how this aﬀects the train
response, especially the overturning and derailment, is not evident.
The eﬀect of the
non-linear simulation of train passage compared to the linear simulation is done with the
simulation parameters from table 5.3 and modelling a ship impact at the bottom of the
column. The ship impact is modelled as an impulse load with constant amplitude in the
transverse (y) direction. As the span of the model is only 80 m, and the train velocity
is large, the duration of the impulse load is very short. Two scenarios are examined; 1)
Where the front wheels are positioned at node 21, and 2) where the front wheels are
positioned at node 41, i.e. over the column. The load intensity is varied from 1000 kN
to 10 000 kN, with an increment of 1000 kN, as to ﬁnd the critical load intensity. The
spring parameters used are the ones described from table 3.4. Both linear and non-linear
simulations have been computed, and the overturning of the vehicle is, as expected, found
to be the governing ratio of the two. Therefore the derailment plots are omitted, and the
overturning plots are shown for each boogie’s two most critical wheels. The overturning
of the train as a function of the load intensity can for both cases be found in ﬁg. 5.5,
where the top ﬁgures are for case 1 and the bottom for case 2. It can be seen that the
relation between the overturning ratio and the applied load is linear. It can also be seen
that for the non-linear simulations, the critical load with respect to overturning for two
boogies are almost equal, and around 2.9 % for case 1 and 5.7 % for case 2. The later case
where the train is located above the pier at ship impact is found to be the critical case,
with the load for the linear simulation being 5.1 MN and for the non-linear simulation
5.4 MN.
For the critical case, the non-linear simulation results in a load almost 6 %
larger then for the linear simulation. This improvement of course depends on multiple
factors as both the spring parameters, impulse loading, vehicle velocity and the bridge
properties all inﬂuences the response. Though as a preliminary result, the capabilities
and advantages of the non-linear simulation can be sad to exist. If the hysteresis eﬀect
had been implemented, the improvement would be expected to be even greater.

5.5
Evaluation and future work
Through the validations of the non-linear time integration from sections 3.4 and 3.6, and
the simulations done in chapter 5, the implementation of the non-linear add-on to the
VBI-programme is assumed to be correct.
The original plan was to use the Matlab
created in the 3D beam programme Maxiframe to verify the implementation, though as
described in section 4.2 this, unfortunately, was not possible. Nevertheless, the results
from the VBI-programme have not led to any concerns regarding the implementation.
The eﬀect of the non-linear dynamic simulation of the train passage, when compared to
the linear simulation exists. It has been found that with the non-linear simulation, it is
possible to increase the impulse loading for the given spring parameters and model by
almost 6 %. Therefore, it can be concluded, that for the given model, with the chosen
spring parameters, the non-linear simulation is advantageous to the linear simulation.
This advantage comes with a disadvantage regarding the runtime. Through the analyses
performed, is was found that in general the non-linear simulations, for the chosen ranges of
parameters required 50 % longer runtime per analysis, compared to the linear equivalent.
This was also the case for the linear analysis in section 5.3.
Ship impact
Implementing the non-linear springs opens up for more detailed modelling of the ship
impact. It makes it possible to model non-linear ship impacts on both the bridge deck,
piers and pylon, and the dynamic inﬂuence of the non-linear ship impacts, for a fully
coupled model, can then be analysed. This is essential to get a more detailed derailment
and overturning analysis, as it is expected that a fully coupled model results in dynamic
interaction, and loading, of the bridge structure. The results should then be compared to
ship impacts modelled as simple impact loads.
Non-linear contact model
In the current framework, the contact between the wheel and rail is assumed to be
constant. This assumptions though being valid for many scenarios, are not necessarily
valid for high speed passage of trains. When there is no contact between the wheel and

6
Conclusion
As part of the thesis, a thorough understanding of the current VBI-programme from
[2] was developed.
In chapter 2 the general theory behind the VBI-programme was
presented as well as the general concept regarding the vehicle model was developed, and the
coupled system matrices for a vehicle with an arbitrary number of both DOFs, wagons and
trains were developed. The non-linear Newmark time integration function was developed
in section 3.1.
The calculations in the non-linear time integration scheme which are
dependent on the springs was made as separate functions, making it easy to change the
spring model. The implementation of the springs was adopted from [4] and resulted in an
implementation that is easily modiﬁed for diﬀerent spring models. The Matlab-based FE
programme Maxiframe from [5] was then extended to a dynamic FE-programme through
the element mass matrix from [15]. Through a simple static system, the implementation
of the functions related to creating the system mass matrix was veriﬁed by comparing
with an analytical solution and a model created in commercial FE software.
The non-linear Newmark time integration and the spring implementation were veriﬁed
through simple two DOF systems in section 3.4. The response was veriﬁed by comparisons
to [17], [18], as well as tests with increasing non-linearities, in order to evalute the eﬀect.
The non-linear time integration was then computed in the modal space. In oppose to the
linear Newmark time integration, which works in both the geometric and modal space, the
non-linear solver does not. The spring forces from the non-linear spring are determined
based on the geometric displacement, whereby modiﬁcations to the algorithm was needed
to account for this. This was done in section 3.6, where the responses from section 3.4 was
used to verify the implementation. Following the modal implementation of the non-linear
solver, an extension to the solver was examined. It is well known that hysteresis eﬀects
occur in soils, whereby the hysteresis model known as Pykes model [22] was implemented.
The implementation was found relatively complex, which led to the implementation only
being ﬁnished shortly before the thesis deadline. The eﬀect of the hysteresis eﬀect, when
compared to the response computed without the eﬀect, was signiﬁcant, and the peak
amplitude was reduced by up to 43.5 %. Unfortunately, due to the late implementation,
it was not possible to implement the hysteresis eﬀect into the VBI-programme.
The ﬁnal step before implementing the non-linear add-on in the VBI-programme was to
create a simple bridge structure, such that a structure-soil interaction could be modelled
in the VBI-programme.
For this purpose, a T-structure was created, with boundary
conditions corresponding to the behaviour of a bridge structure. The model was created
in the commercial FE-software SOFiSTiK, where the natural frequencies was compared
to a model created in MaxiFrame. The natural frequencies for the two models, which were
supposed to be identical, were not equal. Even though extensive checks were conducted, it
was not possible to determine why the models did not have the same dynamic properties,
which resulted in the model created in MaxiFrame being unable to be used to verify
the VBI implementation.
The SOFiSTiK model was used for the implementation, as
a framework was needed. For the implementation, the full T-structure was needed in
the VBI-programme, but as the current framework from [1] only modelled the bridge
deck, some changes were needed to be able to model the full T-structure, and hereby
the structure-soil interaction.
