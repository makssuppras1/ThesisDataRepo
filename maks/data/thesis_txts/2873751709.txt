Abstract
Cold pools are mesoscale meteorological phenomena that form during convective rain-
storms, producing a sudden decrease in near-surface temperature and a sharp increase in
wind speed within the atmospheric boundary layer. This study demonstrates that such
events can be observed in Denmark using high-frequency meteorological mast measure-
ments from Test Centre Østerild, located on the west coast of Jutland. A detection algo-
rithm, adapted from [1], identifies cold pool events based on rapid temperature drops, coin-
cident rainfall, and wind anomalies. The detected events are characterized in terms of wind
speed, direction, and turbulence, revealing typical surges exceeding 5 m s−1 and directional
shifts over 30◦. Turbulence during cold pool passages is quantified by fitting parameters
of the Mann spectral model, enabling realistic representation of the observed flow fields.
A representative composite cold pool event is then constructed from the characterized data
and implemented in Dynamiks, a dynamic wind farm simulation tool. Simulations across
different inflow directions and mean wind speeds show that cold pools can temporarily
enhance farm power output of over a 100% increase depending on the initial background
conditions, but also modify wake structures and yaw alignment due to abrupt directional
changes and elevated turbulence. These effects have implications for both short-term power
forecasting and long-term energy yield estimates. The results suggest that incorporating
cold pool climatology into wind resource assessment and operational strategies can im-
prove the accuracy of annual energy production estimates and better account for transient
atmospheric phenomena affecting Danish wind farms.
1
Introduction
Wind energy is a prominent source of power for Denmark, accounting for 59% of the country’s
total electricity consumption in 2024 [2], and despite challenges with intermittency, it continues
to supply an increasingly substantial portion of the national demand. Potential improvements
are being explored to optimize energy harvesting. Wind variability can make it difficult to
accurately predict annual energy production (AEP), balance the grid, and adjust turbine control
settings. These inaccuracies can become costly in the long term [3], [4]. Even relatively small
prediction errors can have substantial financial consequences—for instance, a 1 GWh shortfall
in AEP due to a P50 bias, i.e., a systematic overestimation of the median expected AEP, could
correspond to a revenue loss on the order of EUR 50,000–70,000 [5].
In addition to its high wind resource, Denmark also receives substantial rainfall, with precipi-
tation occurring on roughly half of the days each year [6]. Among the various weather systems
1
==PAGE:9==
contributing to this rainfall are convective rainstorms, which can give rise to meteorological
phenomena such as cold pools (CPs). CPs are localized regions of cool, dense air formed be-
neath convective clouds due to the partial evaporation of precipitation. They are characterized
by a near-surface drop in air temperature and a surge in horizontal wind speed, typically in
the direction of CP outflow [7]–[9]. Since convection plays a key role in CP formation, these
events are more likely to occur in Denmark’s summer months [10]. Due to climate change,
convective storms are increasing in frequency and intensity, so studying and understanding this
phenomenon is growing in importance [11].
1.1
Motivation
If Denmark wants to prepare for the anticipated 50% increase in its electricity demand by
2030, wind farms should be optimally designed to operate under all meteorological conditions,
including extreme and transient wind events like CPs [12]. The ability to predict CP-induced
wind ramps and adjust turbine control strategies accordingly will become increasingly impor-
tant as the country transitions toward greater reliance on wind energy. Without incorporating
CP dynamics into wind energy models, there is a risk of underestimating turbine fatigue loads
and power production variability, leading to potential inefficiencies in grid integration and en-
ergy dispatch planning.
The guiding research questions for this work are as follows:
• What are the defining characteristics of CPs in Denmark in terms of frequency, intensity,
and duration?
• How can CP effects be incorporated into wind energy forecasting models and wind farm
design?
• How do wind farms respond to CP events with respect to wind variability, power fluctu-
ations, and AEP estimation?
1.2
Background
CPs are a well-recognized atmospheric phenomenon that have been studied for decades. Early
systematic investigations into thunderstorms and their associated outflows date back to the mid-
20th century, with significant advances in the 1980s when researchers began performing time-
dependent analyses of gust fronts, now understood as CPs, to document their vertical structure
2
==PAGE:10==
and life cycle [8]. Subsequent work has highlighted the importance of CPs in organizing precip-
itating convective systems. For example, surface observations from 39 mesoscale convective
system (MCS) events revealed systematic changes in pressure, temperature, and wind speed
throughout different life cycle stages of the parent storms [13].
High-resolution numerical modeling has also been used to simulate CP evolution and colli-
sions, showing that such interactions can enhance gust front strength and moisture transport,
increasing the likelihood of new convective rain events [14].
It is important to note, however, that accurately resolving the strength and structure of CPs
requires model resolutions on the order of hundreds of meters Fivet2023. By contrast, oper-
ational numerical weather prediction (NWP) models, such as the HARMONIE system used
at the Danish Meteorological Institute, typically operate at horizontal resolutions of about 2
km [15]. At these coarser resolutions, CPs are subgrid-scale phenomena and are therefore not
directly simulated with sufficient accuracy. Furthermore, due to the stochastic nature of con-
vection, even when CP–like features are present in NWP output, they are often displaced from
their true location in space and time. These limitations underscore the need for targeted high-
resolution modeling or specialized parameterizations when assessing the potential wind energy
impacts of CPs.
Despite their relevance, detailed knowledge of CPs has only become possible in recent years
thanks to the availability of high-resolution meteorological data capable of capturing short-lived
events [16]. Tall meteorological masts, which are required to observe CP impacts throughout
the rotor layer, are rare. Less than 50 towers worldwide are known to have sensors positioned
above 100 m [17]. This scarcity of infrastructure has historically limited observational datasets
for CP studies, particularly in complex or data-sparse regions. Recent uses of these advance-
ments involve the detailed detection and characterization of CPs. For example, long-term mast
and radar datasets in the Netherlands have linked CP structure to parent rain-cell properties,
confirming theoretical density current relationships and quantifying typical temperature drops
and wind surges [7]. Similarly, dense measurement networks in Germany have provided valu-
able insights into CP morphology, identifying median diameters of around 8.5 km and gener-
ally elliptical shapes [18]. While such findings improve our understanding of CP structure, the
overall three-dimensional shape and spatial variability of cold pools remain poorly constrained.
Point measurements from a single mast can capture the temporal evolution at one location, but
they offer limited information on the broader spatial geometry of a CP.
Although our understanding of CPs has improved, several knowledge gaps remain. Research
3
==PAGE:11==
over the past two decades has shown that, to a first approximation, the average downwind prop-
agation speed of a gust front can be estimated from the mean wind speed within the cloud layer
or from the motion of air parcels that contribute to the CP. This relationship can be useful for
approximating the movement of forward-propagating MCSs [19]. However, in practice, pre-
dicting CP motion is challenging because gust fronts behave as gravity currents whose speed
depends on the density contrast between outflow and the surrounding air, as well as the depth of
the outflow. These quantities are difficult to measure in real time, and factors such as evapora-
tive cooling and precipitation drag vary in both time and space, limiting the operational utility
of theoretical estimates [19].
More recent work has begun exploring machine learning approaches to CP prediction. For
instance, Kruse et al. (2024) developed a nowcasting tool for Dakar, Senegal, using high-
frequency weather station data and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks trained on
Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) simulations. The model achieved skill in predicting
CP arrivals with a 30-minute lead time, offering a potential pathway for improved short-term
forecasting in data-sparse regions [20].
While Denmark’s meteorological network is extensive, there has been no dedicated observa-
tional study of CPs in the country. Previous work has primarily focused on extreme wind events
and ramp-like fluctuations rather than explicitly identifying CPs. For example, Hannesdóttir et
al. (2019) developed a method combining continuous wavelet transforms with analytical fitting
to characterize wind ramps at multiple sites in Denmark, but these events were not specifically
classified as CPs [21]. In addition to detection and characterization, extreme wind events rele-
vant to turbine loading can also be represented through simulation frameworks for wind farm
analysis. For example, Hannesdóttir et al. (2023) developed a probabilistic gust model based
on a decade of high-resolution measurements from a coastal Danish site. By integrating the
model into aeroelastic simulations, they were able to compare site-specific gust scenarios with
standard IEC load cases. In contrast, nearby countries such as the Netherlands and Germany
have conducted detailed CP investigations, providing useful benchmarks for comparison since
they have similar climates[7], [18].
The interaction between CPs and wind power production remains understudied, despite the
potential operational relevance. A recent preprint from Thayer et al. (2025) analyzed four
years of observations at the WiValdi research wind park in northern Germany, detecting and
characterizing 120 CP passages. They reported hub-height wind speed increases of up to +4
m/s during gust front passage, accompanied by directional shifts of 15°, increased turbulence,
4
==PAGE:12==
and changes in shear and stability across the rotor layer. These disturbances led to estimated
power boosts of up to 50% lasting around 30 minutes, depending on the background wind
regime. Vertical profiling also showed that CP impacts can extend to 650–700 m in height
highlighting their capacity to influence turbine inflow conditions for extended periods after
passage [22].
The ability to predict CP events could have substantial benefits for wind farm operation and
electricity market participation. In day-ahead markets, power producers must commit to hourly
generation bids before market closure. If actual generation during delivery deviates from the
submitted bids, producers incur financial penalties—known as deviation penalties or imbalance
costs—which are determined by the market’s settlement rules and the real-time supply–demand
balance [23].
For wind power producers (WPPs), the inherent uncertainty in wind forecasting makes them es-
pecially vulnerable to these penalties. Rapid, unanticipated wind speed changes—such as those
induced by CPs—can cause significant deviations from scheduled output. Such deviations of-
ten comprise a large proportion of WPP operating costs, reducing revenue and operational
efficiency [24]. Enhanced understanding and prediction of CP dynamics could therefore play
a role in reducing imbalance costs, improving market participation strategies, and supporting
overall grid stability.
1.3
Objectives
The aim of this thesis is to detect CPs in time series data from weather masts in Denmark,
characterizing their temperature drops, wind gusts, and other meteorological variables. Us-
ing high-frequency measurements, this study will identify CPs, analyze their properties, and
incorporate them into simulations to assess their influence on wind energy ramp-up in wind
farms.
The impact of wind-related meteorological events on wind turbines can be simulated for wind
farm design or operation. Depending on the frequency and intensity of CPs, a farm layout and
control strategy can be optimized. Meteorological mast data from DTU’s Test Center Østerild,
located on the west coast of Jutland, are used to simulate the atmospheric conditions of CP
events in Denmark and estimate their effect on wind power production. From there, important
performance indicators can be analyzed. Dynamiks, DTU’s dynamic wind system simulator,
is a Python-based framework used to model the dynamic wind speed and direction needed to
obtain the AEP. The key contributions of this study can be summarized as follows:
5
==PAGE:13==
• A validated CP detection algorithm for meteorological data
• Characterization of CP frequency, intensity, and impact on wind farms in Denmark
• Dynamiks simulation results illustrating how CP events influence wind energy ramp-up

Outline

future research.
2
Theory
2.1
Cold Pools
CPs are localized regions of cool, dense air formed beneath convective clouds due to the partial
evaporation of precipitation. The cold air sinks to the ground and spreads out like a density
current, and flows underneath the warmer surrounding air [25].
2.1.1
Formation
In order for a CP to form, the following atmospheric conditions need to be present:
6
==PAGE:14==
• Dry, unsaturated boundary layer
• Convective precipitation
The boundary layer is the lowest part of the atmosphere, typically extending up to 1-2 km above
the ground. Here the effects of surface heating, surface roughness, and turbulence are most pro-
nounced. When the boundary layer is dry and unsaturated, that means the air near the surface
lacks moisture. If the humidity is low, then during precipitative events there is effective evapo-
ration of the falling precipitation [26]. This evaporation cools some of the air in the boundary
layer which can lead to CP formation. Convective precipitation develops in unstable atmo-
spheric conditions, where warm, moist air rises rapidly, cools, and condenses to form clouds.
This process is often followed by short, intense rainfall, known as a convective rainstorm. The
cooled air beneath the precipitation cell is denser than the surrounding environment, causing it
to sink toward the surface under its own weight. Downdrafts within the convective cloud can
enhance this descent, delivering a surge of cool air to the ground that can spread outward as a
CP[8], [14].
A CP, as described in [8] and illustrated in Figure 1, can be divided into four main stages.
Figure 1: Schematic illustration of the four primary stages of a convective CP, adapted from [8].
Stage 1: evaporation of precipitation cools and densifies downdrafts aloft; Stage 2: the cooled
air reaches the surface, spreading horizontally as a gust front with peak winds and turbulence;
Stage 3: outflow weakens as rainfall subsides, with reduced wind speeds and a less distinct
front; Stage 4: the CP gradually dissipates and mixes with the surrounding air, though lifting at
its edges can trigger new convection.
The first stage, given the required atmospheric conditions, is when the precipitation from a
7
==PAGE:15==
rainfall event begins to evaporate before hitting the ground. The dry air gets cooled from the
evaporation of precipitation and begins to sink to the ground as a denser, colder outflow.
In the second stage, the rainstorm begins to dissipate meanwhile the cold air reaches the ground
and spreads out horizontally away from the source as a density current. Vortical motion can be
observed at the leading edges as the cold air pushes into the warm air. This can be observed
near the surface through a drop in air temperature and increase in wind speed. This gust front is
strongest during this stage which represents the most energetic and turbulent phase of the CP,
especially when the parent storm is still actively raining.
During the third stage, as the rainstorm continues to weaken, the CP also weakens since there
is less precipitation to cool more air for the CP. The cold air absorbs heat from the surface and
the surrounding air. While the CP continues to propagate, its wind speeds decline, and the gust
front becomes less defined. This stage marks the transition from active to residual outflow.
In the fourth stage, once the rainstorm has fully dissipated, the remainder of the CP continues
to spread at a slower rate until it becomes fully mixed with the surrounding air. As the outflow
becomes more diluted, convective forcing within the CP interior decreases; the cold, stable air
suppresses vertical motion, leading to a temporary reduction in convective activity across the
footprint. However, convective activity can be enhanced along the CP’s edges, where mechani-
cal lifting at the gust front and increased moisture accumulation can help trigger new convective
cells [27]. Over time, wind and temperature anomalies gradually fade into the background at-
mospheric conditions. While the CP has largely lost its structural identity, these edge effects
can continue to influence downstream convection and surface conditions if environmental in-
stability persists.
In environments with multiple convective cells, CPs can collide. When two or more CPs inter-
sect, the convergence at the boundary can force air upward, triggering new convective updrafts
and rainfall [14], [27]. This renewed convection may in turn generate a new CP, effectively
restarting the life cycle. In some cases, these interactions contribute to the formation of squall
lines: elongated, long-lived storm systems sustained by continuous CP–wind shear interactions.
As the CP outflow spreads, its leading edge interacts with low-level environmental shear, en-
hancing lift along one side of the line and fostering the growth of new cells as older ones decay
[28]. This process enables squall lines to persist for hours, with the storm continually regener-
ating along its advancing edge. Such dynamics play a significant role in sustaining organized
convection over large areas and prolonging severe weather impacts.

2.1.2
Scale
Temporally, CPs typically last from 30 minutes to several hours [1], [7]. The most pronounced
wind and temperature anomalies usually occur within the first 10 to 30 minutes after onset,
during the active spreading of the gust front. This influence of the CP can persist long after the
core has dissipated. CP activity can reinvigorate if multiple CPs converge and form new rain
cells, so CPs can extend their lifetimes beyond several hours.
Spatially, CPs can extend across tens of kilometers from the original location beneath the parent
convective cell. Their shape has not been sufficiently evaluated and has often been assumed to
be circular due to the expectation of uniform radial expansion. However, recent observations
suggest otherwise: aspect ratios between 1.5 and 1.6 indicate that CPs are generally elliptical
rather than perfectly circular [18]. It has been theoretically derived that a CP can expand up to
14 times its original radius, with radar imagery and simulations showing diameters commonly
ranging from 5 to 50 km [18].
Vertically, the depth of a CP remains a subject of debate and appears to vary considerably
with environment. Over land, depths between a few hundred meters and nearly 2 km have
been reported, with a median value of 746 m [1], consistent with earlier estimates of around 1
km for continental convective outflows [26]. In contrast, oceanic CPs are generally shallower,
averaging approximately 300 m in depth [29]. Regardless of the environment, CPs typically
extend well into wind turbine rotor heights, ensuring that their influence is directly relevant for
wind farm operations.
2.1.3
Common Environments
CP behavior varies significantly between land and ocean environments due to differences in
boundary-layer depth, surface characteristics, and precipitation regimes.
Land: Midlatitude land-based CPs are most frequently associated with deep convective pre-
cipitation and show strong seasonal and diurnal cycles. Observations from the Netherlands, a
region influenced by both maritime and continental climates, documented 189 CP events over
ten summers (2010–2019) [7]. For another study in Germany, on average 35 events occur per
year with a clear peak in July (7.6 events/month) and a sharp decline by September [1]. These
events primarily occur during the afternoon, with a maximum frequency near 1500 local time,
reflecting their link to daytime convective heating. Terrain can also influence CP characteristics
and evolution. For example in the Sierras de Córdoba, Argentina, terrain can block the horizon-
9
==PAGE:17==
tal spread of CPs. This blocking traps cold air near the source, allowing it to deepen and persist
for longer. The resulting stronger CPs accelerate the transition from isolated convective storms
to MCSs [30]. Such terrain–CP interactions highlight the importance of local geography in
determining CP strength, depth, and role in storm organization.
Ocean: CPs are also prevalent over tropical and subtropical oceans, where their formation
is primarily linked to the partial evaporation of precipitation into subsaturated air near the
surface. Unlike land-based CPs, which can also be generated by nocturnal radiative cooling,
oceanic CPs are almost exclusively precipitation-driven [31]. They are most often associated
with precipitating cumulus and stratocumulus systems, although they can form under shallower
cloud regimes.
Observations from the VOCALS Regional Experiment in the southeast Pacific stratocumulus
region provide some of the most detailed measurements of oceanic CPs to date [29]. Ninety
CP edges were identified from in situ aircraft data at approximately 150 m altitude, with sizes
ranging from 2–16 km (middle 50th percentile). The largest CPs were associated with the
greatest surface temperature drops, and most formed in clusters rather than as isolated features.
These events tended to occur beneath thicker clouds, higher liquid water path, and heavier
precipitation, and were more common in cleaner atmospheric environments.
From pressure perturbation estimates, the average oceanic CP depth is around 300 m [29], sig-
nificantly shallower than many land-based counterparts but still deep enough to influence wind
turbine inflow in offshore environments. Thermodynamic composites along the CP edges re-
veal increased humidity, equivalent potential temperature, coarse-mode aerosols, and dimethyl
sulfide concentrations within the pool. These enhancements are consistent with increased static
stability, which traps surface fluxes in the lowest layers of the boundary layer.
Over tropical oceans, CPs can span hundreds of kilometers and typically persist for less than
a day before dissipating into the large-scale flow [31]. Their density-driven outflows can orga-
nize surrounding convection into arc-shaped mesoscale cloud structures, enabling deeper cloud
development (4–5 km) even in otherwise dry environments. This mesoscale organization is
particularly common in the northern hemisphere trade wind regimes, where the flow toward
the Intertropical Convergence Zone is unobstructed by the equator. The influence of these CPs
is maximized when the lower free troposphere is moist but the air above is dry, a profile that
supports both mechanical lifting and thermodynamic enhancement at the CP edges.

2.1.4
Density Current
A density current is a horizontal flow of dense fluid beneath a lighter fluid, driven by a hor-
izontal pressure gradient resulting from a density difference. In the atmosphere, a primary
example is the outflow of evaporatively cooled downdrafts from thunderstorms [8], [25]. When
precipitation falls into unsaturated air, partial evaporation cools the air, increasing its density
and causing it to sink. Upon reaching the surface, this negatively buoyant air spreads radially
outward as a density current [25].
Density currents play an important role in convective initiation. The planetary boundary layer
is often metastable, meaning that air must be lifted a finite distance before convection can
occur. Density currents are particularly effective at producing this lift, with their leading edge,
known as the gust front, forcing warm, moist environmental air upward. This mechanism is
frequently invoked in squall-line formation, where the orientation of the density current is often
perpendicular to the low-level flow, aligning with observed storm structures [33].
The strength and vertical motion associated with a density current depend on both the CP
temperature deficit and the environmental vertical shear. Numerical experiments show that
colder CPs and stronger shear generally lead to faster ascent of cold-pool air [34]. However,
stronger negative buoyancy can also reduce the vertical depth of cold-pool air transport, as the
gust front may advance too quickly relative to deep convective updrafts.

2.1.5
Vertical Structure
CPs display a distinct vertical profile that evolves over their lifetime. At formation, the center
of the CP can exceed 1 km in depth [8]. As the leading edge develops, often marked by a
precipitation-driven “roll” or overturning circulation, it can initially deepen to between 1–2
km. Over time, both the center and the leading edge gradually lose vertical extent as the CP
spreads and entrains surrounding air, eventually dissipating when its thermodynamic contrast
is erased.
The head of the CP, located at the gust front, is dynamically distinct from the trailing interior.
Here, the frontal ascent lifts warm ambient air upward, but the dense air within the front sinks
back down, creating the shallow overturning “roll” circulation that suppresses vertical mixing
inside the pool. The center behind the front is generally shallower than the head once the initial
deep phase passes, as the gust front remains the primary driver of vertical motion.
Observations show that the strongest thermodynamic signals, particularly temperature deficits,
occur near the surface and decay with height. Kruse et al. (2022) found that over land, tem-
perature anomalies measured at 10 m decrease approximately linearly to zero around 500 m,
providing an estimate for mature CP depth in midlatitude conditions. Kirsch et al. (2024)
reported that horizontal wind speed perturbations tend to increase with height up to around
50 m before leveling off, likely due to frictional damping near the surface and more uniform
momentum transport aloft.
The gust front is characterized by a pronounced positive horizontal wind anomaly, which in
composites peaks at intermediate heights ( 80 m in Kruse et al. 2022) due to the combined
effects of front propagation and internal circulation. Ahead of the front, vertical velocity per-
turbations show a brief upward pulse followed by subsidence, consistent with lifting-induced
overturning circulation.
CP vertical extent varies with environment. Over continental regions, depths can exceed 1 km,
with deep events reaching up to 2 km at the leading edge [1], [8]. Over the ocean, CPs are
generally shallower, averaging around 300 m (Terai et al., 2013). Regardless of location, tur-
bulent mixing above the CP gradually erodes its temperature and momentum contrasts, leading
to weakening and eventual dissipation.

2.1.6
Meteorological Signature
CPs are typically identified through a combination of sharp temperature drops and accompa-
nying wind surges. Observations show that the magnitude of the temperature drop generally
ranges from 2–11 K, with median values near 3 K in midlatitude land environments [1], [7].
Wind surges are of comparable significance, with median height-averaged increases of 3–5 m
s−1, and extreme cases exceeding 15 m s−1 [1]. These thermodynamic and kinematic signals
form the basis for objective detection methods
Figure 3 compares composite cold-pool wind and temperature anomalies from two long-term
continental datasets: Kirsch et al. (2021) in Germany and Kruse et al. (2022) in the Nether-
lands. Both show a coherent wind surge across all measurement heights, with magnitude in-
creasing above the surface due to reduced frictional drag. The strongest cooling occurs near the
surface, with a decreasing temperature anomaly aloft. Kruse et al. (2022) additionally high-
light an enhancement of wind anomalies near 80 m, interpreted as the influence of gust-front
vortical circulation, while Kirsch et al. (2021) emphasize the vertical gradient in cooling and
wind decay. Together, these profiles illustrate the vertically structured and temporally coherent
nature of cold-pool signatures, which are fundamental to both detection and characterization.
Detection algorithms often combine a temperature criterion—adapted to local conditions—with
a horizontal wind anomaly requirement to ensure that events represent genuine gust fronts. Ad-
ditional indicators include abrupt changes in wind direction, small pressure rises on the order of
2–5 hPa, and the timing of rainfall relative to CP onset. For example, some studies report that
the characteristic sequence involves a pressure rise, wind surge, and temperature drop occurring
within tens of minutes before rainfall begins [1].
Moisture changes during CP passages remain an active topic of debate. While some studies
report a moistening within the CP interior [1], others find a drier interior [7]. This discrepancy
may be linked to differences in environmental humidity, storm type, or measurement method-
ology, and highlights the variability of CP microphysics.
Together, these meteorological signatures not only enable reliable detection in tower and sta-
tion data but also help characterize the intensity and potential impacts of CP events on the
atmospheric boundary layer and, in the context of this study, on wind farm operations.

2.1.7
Simulations
Simulating CPs (CPs) has been an active area of research, as they play a central role in convec-
tive organization and mesoscale dynamics. Large-eddy simulations (LES) have been the pri-
mary tool for resolving the fine-scale processes at CP gust fronts, including free propagation,
frontal collision, and gust front merging Fivet2023. At coarse resolutions, CP gust fronts suffer
from excessive numerical dissipation, which weakens mechanical lifting and underrepresents
gust front dynamics. Resolutions finer than 100 m horizontally and 50 m vertically are of-
ten necessary to capture the small-scale instabilities—such as the lobe-and-cleft patterns—that
influence CP structure and longevity [35]. We need extremely fine model resolutions to cap-
ture the sharp edges and complex shapes of CP fronts; otherwise, the simulated CPs will look
weaker and less realistic.
Model convergence studies have shown that gust front merging can sustain or strengthen out-
flows, and that azimuthal instabilities may arise in horizontally circular CPs within shear-free
15
==PAGE:23==
environments. These instabilities, and their contribution to turbulent mixing, remain a chal-
lenge to reproduce consistently, even at high resolution. Additionally, subgrid parameteriza-
tions have been proposed to represent CP effects in coarser models, but the representation of
CP–convection feedbacks remains limited [36], [37]. Even with powerful simulations, we still
struggle to consistently model how CPs combine, spin, and mix air, and coarser models have
to make rough guesses about these effects.
Beyond idealized LES, case study simulations have investigated CP variability across environ-
ments. Over land, CP depths and intensities vary with background shear and surface fluxes,
with strong shear enhancing frontal propagation speed in the downshear direction but limiting
upshear movement. Over the ocean, CPs tend to be shallower and more influenced by thermo-
dynamic effects from sea surface conditions. However, high-resolution case studies still tend
to underestimate the magnitude of sub-mesoscale temperature variability compared to dense
observation networks, suggesting incomplete representation of formation and dissipation pro-
cesses [38]. Simulations can capture many differences between land and ocean CPs, but they
still underestimate some of the real-world variability measured by dense sensor networks.
Sensible heat fluxes (SHFs) also influence CP dissipation rates, primarily by altering near-
surface stability and turbulent entrainment. Idealized simulations show that while bulk SHF
formulations have modest impacts, constant SHFs can substantially change entrainment rates
and CP intensity. The effects, however, are sensitive to grid resolution, with coarser simulations
often misrepresenting or even reversing the SHF influence [35]. The way heat flows between
the surface and the air can speed up or slow down CP decay, but lower-resolution models often
get this wrong.
Operational numerical weather prediction (NWP) models, such as HARMONIE used by the
Danish Meteorological Institute, typically run at 2 km resolution, far too coarse to explicitly
resolve CP gust front dynamics. This subgrid nature means CPs are often too weak, misplaced,
or entirely absent in such forecasts, partly due to the stochastic nature of convection [15], [39].
Weather forecast models are usually too coarse to capture CPs accurately, so they might show
them in the wrong place, make them too weak, or miss them entirely.
While traditional prediction relies on theoretical gravity current relationships, in practice CP
motion is difficult to forecast accurately due to uncertainties in density contrast, depth, and
the temporal–spatial variability of evaporative cooling and precipitation drag [19]. More re-
cently, machine learning has emerged as a promising tool for CP nowcasting. For example,
[20] developed a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model trained on high-resolution WRF
16
==PAGE:24==
simulations and automatic weather station data, achieving 30-minute lead time skill in Dakar,
Senegal. Such approaches could complement high-resolution simulations by providing rapid,
data-driven predictions in operational settings.
The lessons from previous CP simulation studies directly inform the approach taken in this
work. While large-eddy simulations at sub-100 m resolution are ideal for capturing fine-scale
gust front structure, such computational demands are impractical for extended wind farm sim-
ulations. In this thesis, the Dynamiks simulation framework is used to incorporate CP signa-
tures—derived from high-frequency meteorological mast data—into wind farm inflow scenar-
ios. This approach bridges the gap between high-resolution atmospheric research and practical
wind energy applications, enabling the assessment of how CP-driven wind surges and direc-
tion shifts influence turbine performance, wake interactions, and power ramp-up under realistic
farm layouts.

Extreme wind events can affect wind turbines in multiple ways, from direct structural loading

performance and planning are power sensitivity, yaw alignment, and turbulence intensity.
Power Sensitivity: Accurate power prediction is essential for both operational scheduling and
financial planning. In power systems, production and consumption must remain balanced in
real time, requiring reliable forecasts to guide scheduling, dispatch, and reserve allocation [41].
Because of the intermittent nature of wind energy, even relatively small prediction errors can
have substantial financial consequences. For example, a 1 GWh shortfall in annual energy
production (AEP) due to a P50 bias could correspond to a revenue loss on the order of EUR

50,000–70,000 [5]. Such deviations can occur if an extreme wind event disrupts normal power

Yaw Misalignment: Wind turbines are designed to orient their rotors perpendicular to the
incoming wind to maximize energy capture. Sudden shifts in wind direction during extreme
events can cause unintentional yaw misalignment, which reduces power output. The effect
is exacerbated in wind farms, where downstream turbines operate in the wakes of upstream
machines. In such conditions, the spatially non-uniform inflow can alter the power–yaw loss
relationship, and neglecting these effects can lead to overestimation of power production by
several percent. For example, a 30◦misalignment can lead to a 3.5% overestimation if wake
interactions are ignored [42]. These errors directly influence wind farm optimization, particu-
larly when using simplified wake models for planning and control.
Turbulence Intensity: Extreme events can also alter turbulence intensity over extended pe-
riods. Measurements from the coastal Danish site Høvsøre show that variance in wind speed
during certain events can exceed the IEC 61400-1 extreme turbulence model (ETM) limits [43].
While many of these fluctuations are not caused by traditional turbulence, ramp-like increases
in wind speed can drive large transient loads. Aeroelastic simulations indicate that while most
components see lower loads than IEC ETM cases, events where wind speeds ramp from below
to above rated values (8–16 m s−1) can produce extreme rotor thrust and tower-base fore–aft
loads that exceed IEC ETM design cases. These findings highlight the need to account for
prolonged, non-stationary turbulence patterns when assessing structural safety during extreme
meteorological events.
2.3
Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM)
When a wind turbine extracts energy from the wind, it creates a wake downstream—a region of
reduced wind speed and increased turbulence compared to the inflow. This wake results from
the kinetic energy extracted by the rotor and directly impacts downstream turbines, causing
power losses and increased structural loading [44].
In wind farms, the performance and loading of a turbine depend not only on the free-stream
conditions but also on its position relative to other turbines. Accurate wake modeling is there-
fore essential for predicting energy production, turbine lifespan, and the effectiveness of control
strategies [44]. The evolution of a wake is highly dynamic and time-dependent, requiring mod-
els that can represent both its generation and its transport by the atmospheric boundary layer
(ABL).

2.3.2
Wake Meandering and the DWM Approach
Wake meandering is the large-scale, low-frequency lateral and vertical motion of the wake as
it is advected by the ABL. These slow sweeping motions are driven by large turbulent eddies
and are essential for reproducing the correct temporal behavior of wake–turbine interactions.
The DWM model represents the wake as a passive tracer plume, transporting the velocity deficit
(from Section 2.3.1) downstream at the mean advection velocity [44]. This follows Taylor’s
frozen turbulence hypothesis, assuming that large-scale turbulent structures are convected with
the mean flow over short timescales. Crucially, DWM does not generate the wake—it models
its dynamics and deformation in response to background turbulence. By combining the wake
deficit with the meandering motion, DWM captures realistic spatial and temporal variations in
wind speed, direction, and turbulence intensity for downstream turbines.
2.3.3
Turbulence and the Mann Model
The Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) model requires a large-scale turbulence field to ad-
vect the wake deficit downstream. This field must preserve realistic coherence and anisotropy
19
==PAGE:27==
characteristics of the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) while remaining computationally fea-
sible to generate for many realizations. High-fidelity methods such as Large Eddy Simulation
(LES) or Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) could in principle provide such turbulence, but
their computational cost is prohibitive for engineering applications where hundreds or thou-
sands of runs are needed. Instead, synthetic turbulence generation using spectral models offers
a practical compromise, and the Mann model [46] has become the standard choice for DWM.
Role in DWM.
In the DWM framework, the Mann model generates a three-dimensional
turbulent velocity field (u,v,w) that is statistically consistent with measured ABL turbulence.
Only the low-frequency components of this field (typically f ≲0.3 Hz) are used to drive the
wake meandering, corresponding to time scales longer than the advection time through the
near wake Madsen2010. Higher-frequency, small-scale turbulence is filtered out, as it does not
significantly contribute to wake displacement.
Theoretical basis.
The Mann model is based on:
• Stationary, homogeneous, Gaussian turbulence: Statistical properties are uniform in
space and time, with velocity fluctuations following a Gaussian distribution.
• Rapid Distortion Theory (RDT): Linearization of the Navier–Stokes equations to model
how turbulence is distorted by mean shear in the ABL.
• Spectral tensor formulation: The model defines a 3D spectral tensor Φi j(k) in wavenum-
ber space, from which turbulent velocity fields can be generated via inverse Fourier trans-
forms. This tensor preserves anisotropy and coherence between velocity components,
unlike simpler isotropic models.
The tensor is calibrated using atmospheric measurements, ensuring that the simulated fields
reproduce realistic second-order statistics (variances, cross-spectra, coherences).
Model parameters.
The Mann model is defined by three parameters:
• αε2/3 [m4/3/s2]: Controls the overall turbulence intensity, with ε the turbulent kinetic
energy dissipation rate and α the Kolmogorov constant.
• L [m]: Turbulence length scale, representing the characteristic size of energy-containing
eddies. Larger L produces more coherent, slowly varying structures.
20
==PAGE:28==
• Γ: Anisotropy parameter describing the relative strength of horizontal vs. vertical turbu-
lence. Γ = 0 corresponds to isotropic turbulence, while larger values indicate stronger
horizontal structure typical of shear-generated turbulence.
Practical implementation in this study.
Turbulence boxes are generated using the open-
source Hipersim tool [47], which implements Mann’s spectral method with efficient FFT-based
algorithms and parallelization. The generated domains are 3D Gaussian fields with anisotropic
variance and negative correlation between the u (streamwise) and w (vertical) components,
consistent with ABL measurements. Each box is filtered to extract only the low-frequency
motions relevant for wake meandering, while the high-frequency part of the field can be used
separately for modeling added wake turbulence.
Limitations.
The Mann model assumes a neutral ABL and Gaussian turbulence statistics,
which may not hold under strongly stable or unstable conditions. It also assumes horizontal
homogeneity, while vertical inhomogeneity is only approximately treated. These limitations
can lead to discrepancies in extreme or non-neutral events, but the model remains robust for
typical wind farm simulation scenarios. The Mann model provides an efficient and physically
grounded way to generate large-scale turbulence fields that preserve realistic ABL coherence
and anisotropy. In the DWM framework, these fields supply the slow, sweeping motions that
drive wake meandering, allowing for computationally tractable yet physically consistent wake
dynamics.
2.3.4
Implementation in Dynamiks
In this study, the DWM model is implemented using the simulation tool Dynamiks. Dynamiks,
which employs Hipersim, simulates the transient aeroelastic response of wind turbines un-
der time-varying inflow conditions. The need for a dynamic model arises because CP events
evolve on short time scale where the inflow velocity and direction change rapidly. Therefore,
steady-state models are insufficient. The DWM model enables capturing both the temporal de-
velopment of the wake and the interaction with realistic, time-varying inflow generated using
the Mann model.

3.2.1
Data Filtering
In certain cases, sensor malfunctions were evident, typically during intense rainstorms, where
the output of anemometers became constant for the remainder of the event. Such instances
were flagged and excluded from analysis.
No additional filtering was applied, as the detection algorithm is inherently insensitive to most
short-duration spikes or dropouts; these do not satisfy the CP detection criteria and are therefore
excluded implicitly. However, it is important to note that extended sensor outages could result
in genuine CP events being missed. The total proportion of excluded data was not quantified in
this study due to time constraints, and therefore the possibility of underestimating the number
of events cannot be ruled out. This limitation should be considered when interpreting the
frequency statistics presented in later sections.
3.3
CP Detection
3.3.1
Temperature Criterion
The data was analyzed one month at a time. First, the 1 Hz data is fetched from the database
and converted into one-minute averages to reduce noise. Then, using an algorithm from [1],
potential CP events are detected based on the time series data for temperature and rain measured
at a height of 7 m. This algorithm was chosen because it is a robust method that uses clear
threshold criteria to isolate CP signatures while minimizing false detections. The threshold on
the temperature drop was chosen to be -1.5 K. This value was chosen to match with the criterion
used in Kruse et al., whose study in the Netherlands is also situated in flat coastal terrain and
thus provides a relevant comparison [7]. The threshold was adjusted from the original 2 K used
by Kirsch et al. to 1.5 K to account for increased environmental moisture over coastal Denmark
[1], which likely reduces the strength of evaporative cooling during convective rainfall events.

The temperature criterion algorithm identifies CP events by scanning high-frequency meteoro-

decreases by at least 1.5 K within a 20-minute window, marking these as candidate events. For
each candidate, it determines the exact onset time (t0) as the point where temperature first drops

• Lack sufficient data, or
• Are not followed by rainfall within 60 minutes after t0.

The wind criterion algorithm evaluates the strength of wind anomalies associated with each
detected CP event. For each event timestamp, it:
• Retrieves a full day of high-frequency wind data,
• Computes both a 1-minute average and a 2-hour smoothed wind speed at multiple heights,
• Calculates the wind anomaly as the difference between the two,
• Determines the standard deviation of the wind anomaly across the day,
• Iteratively tests a range of impact multipliers to check if the anomaly exceeds thresholds
within a 90-minute window centered on the event,
• Returns the impact value (i.e., normalized strength of the anomaly), the extended time
range for further analysis, and the associated 3D wind vectors.

the day of the event. By applying this wind criterion, only the stronger impact CPs remain in
the pool of detected CP passages.
3.3.3
Sensitivity Analysis
To determine what wind and temperature changes to detect, a sensitivity analysis was per-
formed. For time efficiency, only two years were analyzed to conduct this analysis: 2018 and
2021. Using the 10-minute standard deviation table, the top 1% timestamps with the highest
7 m temperature standard deviations were identified. 2018 and 2021 were the most and least
represented years respectively in this percentage compared to all of the years in the dataset.
Analyzing both years provides a balanced perspective on the variability of CP events under
different climatic conditions. This approach ensures that the chosen detection thresholds are
robust and applicable under both frequent and rare CP scenarios, improving the robustness of
the results. Three parameters were adjusted for analysis: measurement height, temperature
change, and wind speed standard deviation. Looking at the upper row of the figures in Fig-
ure 7, it can be observed that generally fewer events are detected if the temperature is measured
at a higher height. This matches expectations since CPs have been shown to have the highest
temperature anomalies close to the surface [1], [7]. To ensure the algorithm detects as many
potential CP events as possible, the lowest height of 7 m was chosen. Now looking at the
middle row of the figures in Figure 7, if the temperature drop threshold used for detection is
decreased, then the events detected increases significantly. For both years, the events double
for every 0.5 K reduced from the temperature change threshold. While the goal is to detect as
many CP events as possible, lowering the temperature change too much likely detects too many
false events. For example, in Figure 7c, applying a threshold of –1 K for temperature change
results in the detection of over 30 events in September, which is unlikely to reflect actual at-
mospheric conditions. The -2 K change ensures strong CP events are detected, but is perhaps
overly conservative for this inital round of detection. The -1.5 K change strikes a balance be-
tween detecting proper CP events without being too lenient. Lastly, the effect of adjusting the
wind criterion is analyzed. In the lower row of the figures in Figure 7, it is seen that increasing
the wind speed standard deviation threshold greatly reduces the amount of CP events detected.

3.3.5
Composite Wind Anomaly
The high-frequency wind data of the detected CP events was processed to compute a composite
time series of wind anomalies, along with associated percentiles. This composite anomaly is
later used as input for wind farm simulations, while the percentiles quantify the variability
between events.
The characterization procedure is as follows:
1. For each CP event, a rolling average is applied to all three wind components to downsam-
ple the time series to 1 Hz, reducing high-frequency noise while preserving meaningful
trends.
2. For each CP event, the mean of the u-component is calculated over the first 10 minutes.
The anomaly onset time t0 is defined as the time where the u-component deviates most
from this mean, ensuring temporal alignment of all events.
3. For each CP event, a time window is extracted spanning 25 minutes before and 45 min-
utes after t0.
4. The mean wind speed components before t0 are computed, and the anomaly is obtained
by subtracting this pre-t0 mean from the entire time series of the event.
5. The anomalies from all events are aggregated by calculating the mean anomaly at each
time step, along with the 10th and 90th percentiles to represent ensemble spread.
A potential consideration is to normalize each event’s time series by its pre-t0 mean wind speed,
allowing direct comparison of relative surges across events with different background wind
conditions. We tested this normalization and found it had negligible impact on the resulting
composite and percentile bounds, indicating that the anomaly patterns were consistent across a
range of pre-event wind speeds. Because the normalized and non-normalized composites were
nearly identical, we present the non-normalized results for simplicity, while noting that this
implies the CP wind surge signal is robust to variations in background wind speed.

7. Final parameter set and uncertainty The optimized (αε2/3,L,Γ) values are stored for

Parameter fitting was initially attempted using statistical likelihood-based approaches, specif-
ically exponential and gamma likelihood functions. In practice, these methods frequently en-
countered convergence issues, where the optimizer failed to reach a stable solution within the
allowed iterations, and overfitting, where the fitted spectra matched noise fluctuations in the
high-wavenumber range at the expense of capturing the overall spectral shape. These behav-
iors were particularly common for events with lower turbulence intensity or irregular spectra.
To avoid these issues, a penalty-based approach was adopted. Unlike likelihood methods, this
approach does not attempt to maximize a statistical probability; instead, it deterministically
minimizes a weighted sum of the differences in total spectral energy and spectral shape between
the observed and modeled spectra over the relevant wavenumber range. This provided stable
and physically consistent parameter estimates across all events without the sensitivity to noise
that was observed in the likelihood fits.

4.1.3
Limitations
Although the algorithm appears to successfully detect CP events, there are several limitations
that may lead to false positives or missed detections.
First, the temperature criterion is applied before the wind criterion rather than concurrently. As
a result, events with clear wind surges but without a strong enough initial temperature drop may
be missed. This sequential filtering reduces sensitivity to CPs with atypical or gradual thermo-
dynamic signatures. Furthermore, the use of fixed thresholds for both temperature anomaly
and wind anomaly inherently filters out certain events based on predefined rules. This means
that the resulting CP ensemble is partly shaped by the detection parameters chosen, and caution
must be taken when comparing the “composite CP” in this study to those from other works that
used different thresholds or detection criteria. For example, Kirsch2021 define t0 as the start of
the temperature drop, whereas in this work it is defined as the time of maximum deviation in
the u-component from the pre-event mean. Such differences in t0 definition can influence the
apparent duration and temporal structure of the detected CP.
Second, the algorithm rejects events occurring within 60 minutes of one another, which can
exclude overlapping or rapidly successive CPs often observed in convective storm environments
. This choice simplifies the event catalog but may omit complex multi-front passages.
Third, the analysis is limited to point measurements from a single mast, constraining the ability
to resolve the spatial structure or propagation path of CPs. Events that pass near but not directly
over the sensors may go undetected, particularly if the core outflow misses the measurement
location. The fixed-height vertical profiles further restrict the ability to capture horizontal vari-
ability or map the full gust front extent.
Additional limitations include occasional sensor outages during heavy precipitation or strong
44
==PAGE:52==
winds, which can cause data gaps or corrupted records. Moreover, while the analysis focuses
on the northern mast to minimize turbine wake effects, local flow distortion from nearby infras-
tructure or terrain features may still influence the measured wind field.

Not all CP events result in equally strong wind anomalies, and the operational impact may
differ dramatically from case to case. Some events may introduce only modest surges, while
others can generate intense gusts exceeding 15 m/s. This variety underscores the need for
event-specific forecasting and classification methods.
The composite anomalies of the u- and v-components reveal that CP events do not induce a
uniform increase in wind speed along a fixed direction. Instead, the two components evolve
differently—exhibiting varying peak magnitudes and decay patterns—indicating that CPs are
often accompanied by directional shifts in the wind field. If the anomaly were strictly aligned

the deviation suggests a dynamic reorientation of the wind vector, consistent with gust fronts
or outflow boundaries. In this study, CP passages frequently introduced directional shifts of
up to 35°, posing additional challenges for yaw alignment and turbine control. These abrupt
and short-lived changes can result in yaw misalignment and corresponding energy losses .
Furthermore, the gradual recovery of the wind vector back to its original direction indicates that
the system does not immediately return to equilibrium—extending the CP’s influence beyond
the initial front and reinforcing its relevance in transient wind resource modeling.
Compared to the wind anomaly composite in Kruse et al., the maximum anomaly is around the
same value at the closest height (80 m). Both studies show a clear decay in wind speed anomaly
after t0, though the composite presented here returns to baseline levels slightly earlier—around
12 minutes after t0—whereas the composite from Kruse et al. remains elevated for approxi-
mately 20 minutes. One possible explanation is that CP momentum in this study is dissipated
more quickly due to differences in terrain roughness or surface friction at the Østerild site,
which may dampen the duration of wind surges. Both plots show that CPs produce reliable,
structured, and temporally coherent surges in the horizontal wind speed. Their composite plot
includes the standard deviation of the anomaly which also resembles the range depicted in 18.
Both plots show a relatively narrow band of variability of around 3 m/s before t0, then after-
wards the range expands to approximately 5 m/s. This expansion reflects the highly variable
wind response of a CP which can differ substantially based on the local environment. All CPs
might start similarly, but the magnitude and duration of a CP can differ greatly. This general
pattern being consistent across both studies supports the validity of the CP detection and char-
acterization method. The mean anomaly was used for the subsequent simulations to represent
a typical CP event in the area.
Compared to the horizontal wind anomaly composite shown in Kirsch et al. (2021, Fig. 3e), our
results exhibit a similar temporal structure and peak intensity. Both studies report a sharp surge
in ∆U immediately following CP onset (t0), with similar peak magnitudes at similar heights.
Again like with the composite in Kruse et al., the Kirsch et al. profile has a more sustained
decay after t0, lasting around 30 minutes. It is noted that the peak wind speed is at t0 in our
composite while Kirsch et al. denotes t0 as the first time step where the temperature drops by
at least 0.5 K, but even if t0 were shifted to the peak of the wind profile, the increase in wind
speed would still last longer than in the composite we produced. Again, this difference may be

The fitted Mann turbulence parameters further support the findings, which show that CP wind
fields are characterized by relatively high turbulence intensity, large eddy length scales, and
distorted spectral shapes. The length scale L = 57.39 m implies that the dominant turbulent
structures extend over tens of meters—on the order of the rotor diameter—thus directly in-
teracting with turbine inflow. The integral length scale L clusters between 30–80 meters, with
some reaching over 150 m. These spatial scales are significant because they correspond to large
eddies on the order of the rotor diameter, meaning such turbulent structures can directly affect
turbine loads and yaw misalignment. This reinforces the need for dynamic inflow modeling,
particularly during events like CPs, where coherent structures persist across the turbine rotor.
The shape parameter Γ = 4.267 and turbulence intensity αε2/3 = 0.2106 are consistent with ac-
tive convective turbulence, further validating the classification of these events as energetic and
convectively driven. The shape parameter Γ also shows considerable variability, but the median
and upper quartile values exceed 4, which suggests that the energy-containing eddies are more
elongated or sheared compared to isotropic turbulence. This is consistent with observations of
CP gust fronts, where shear layers form along the outflow boundary, enhancing anisotropy in
the turbulence structure. Together, these characteristics highlight the importance of account-
ing for CPs in both wind resource assessment and load simulation frameworks, especially in
regions where convective events are frequent.
The insights of the Mann model parameter analysis underscore that CP-driven turbulence is
not uniform: some events are highly energetic, others more moderate, and the resulting wind
fields exhibit a complex mix of intensity, spatial scale, and structure. This justifies the need for
ensemble-based simulation approaches that account for this variability when modeling wind
turbine responses to convective outflow events.
The characterization process succesfully captured the typical wind speed, direction, and tur-
bulence signatures associated with CP passages at turbine-relevant heights. The composite
anomalies and fitted turbulence parameters not only align well with past studies but also pro-
vide stie-specific insight into the variability of CP intensity, duration, and directional change.
This fulfills the second major objective of the study: to produce a robust, physically consistent
represetnation of CP inflow conditions for subsequent wind farm simulations.

4.2.2
Limitations
While the composite characterization of the CP wind anomalies is useful and aligns with past
studies, there are some limitations to this process. This composite only takes into account wind
speeds at a height of 103 m which is a good assessment of hub height wind speeds, but fails to
account for differences at other heights such as the top or bottom of the rotor’s swept area. In
reality, the magnitude of the wind shear could result in high loads on the turbine. Additionally,
the time normalization across events may obscure differences in the ramp-up and recovery of
the different CP events.
Averaging the turbulence parameters removes any nuance in regards to the variability of the
atmospheric turbulence observed across events. This could limit the realism of the CP inflow.

Simulations
Before the anomaly was incorporated into the simulation, it was first aligned to the mean wind
flow (v-component averages out to zero), and then rotated more based on the CP inflow angle.
The inflow angle of the CP has an impact on the directional change as seen in Figure 21. At
0°, the wind anomaly vectors are primarily aligned with the x-axis, showing a strong horizon-

the flow field, with inflow and outflow regions aligned in parallel bands. At 30°, the vector
field rotates accordingly, and the anomaly appears to strike the wind farm at an oblique an-

variability, with vectors in the gust front zone becoming more curved and spatially scattered.

Across all inflow angles, a sharp transition or "front" is visible in the center of each plot, show-

The quiver plots showing different CP inflow angles reveal several important implications for
wind farm behavior and control. At 0° inflow, the CP anomaly propagates directly through
the wind farm, likely impacting all turbines along a straight column simultaneously. This can
result in stronger and more uniform wake interactions across the array. In contrast, higher in-
flow angles such as 30° and 60° cause the CP to sweep diagonally through the farm, leading to
asynchronous loading. While this may temporarily reduce cumulative wake overlap, it intro-
duces uneven power surges and variable turbine exposure. At steeper angles, particularly 60°,
the directional changes introduced by the CP can challenge turbine yaw systems, especially if
the shifts are abrupt or short-lived. This can lead to yaw misalignment losses or even increased
fatigue loading. The observed directional curvature in the anomaly vectors, most pronounced
in the 60° case, also points to enhanced lateral turbulence transport, which could complicate
wake recovery and downstream flow behavior. These findings highlight the importance of dy-
namic modeling and suggest that farms in regions prone to oblique CP inflows might benefit
from adaptive control strategies to better manage transient wake and loading effects.

4.3.2
Discussion
The power increase trends are consistent with the shape of a wind turbine power curve. Below
rated wind speed, power output increases approximately with the cube of the wind speed, so
a 4 m/s increase from a baseline of 6 m/s produces a proportionally larger gain than from
higher baseline speeds. However, once the wind speed exceeds the rated value (13.7 m/s for
the turbines modeled here), the power output plateaus at the rated capacity. In this regime,
additional increases in wind speed do not translate into significant gains in electrical power,
which explains the smaller relative changes observed at the highest baseline wind speed of 15
m/s.
It is expected that if the wind farm is aligned in the 0° direction, then the CP, which is also
aligned at 0°, will result in the biggest increase in the u-component of the wind vector and thus
a greater power output. While the sharper peaks in the 30◦and 60◦inflow cases briefly boost
instantaneous power output, they also present operational and structural challenges. The rapid
onset of these surges can result in high transient loads on blades, drivetrain components, and
tower structures, particularly if the control system is unable to react quickly enough to modu-
late rotor speed or pitch. From a forecasting perspective, these abrupt, short-lived deviations
54
==PAGE:62==
are problematic for power predictions. Most wind power forecasting models assume gradual
variations in wind speed and direction, and they may not capture the magnitude or timing of
such transient surges. For grid operators, this volatility can introduce additional balancing
challenges causing financial losses on the farm operation.
The influence behind the power peaks and decays is likely due to wake effects. Supporting this
are the field visualizations in Figure 25. The wake effects worsens for the downstream turbines
in the 60° whereas the 0° case shows that the wake effects stay the same or slightly improve
for the farm. The staggered layout of the wind farm plays an important role in this behavior,
as it allows the high-momentum CP inflow in the 0° case to thread between turbine rows with
minimal wake overlap. In contrast, the angled inflow in the 60° case steers the wakes into
downstream turbines more quickly, increasing losses and reducing the sustained power gains.
One possible explanation for this phenomenon lies in yaw dynamics. In the simulations, the
yaw control follows a standard strategy in which each turbine aligns its nacelle to the current
mean wind direction, typically calculated over a short averaging period. This means that the
turbines may require time to realign to a 0◦CP inflow due to prior wind conditions and yaw
system response time, whereas the angled inflows might momentarily align better with the
pre-yaw orientation of the rotors. However, as the angled simulations progress, the wake tra-
jectories bend into the paths of other turbines, increasing losses. This lateral forcing matches

4.3.3
Limitations
While the simulation results provide valuable insight into the potential impact of CP passages
on wind farm performance, several limitations should be noted.
First, the simulations rely on an idealized representation of the CP wind field derived from a
composite anomaly. This approach captures the average behavior but smooths over the event-
to-event variability in magnitude, ramp rate, and directional change. Real CPs may exhibit
sharper fronts, more complex spatial patterns, or secondary surges that are not fully reproduced
in the composite-based input.
The turbulence field is generated using the Mann model with parameters averaged across de-
tected events. While this ensures a representative baseline, it neglects the wide spread in tur-
bulence intensities and length scales seen in the observations, which could influence wake
recovery and turbine loading in ways not captured by a single parameter set. Also mentioned
earlier in the characterization limitations, there is a constant set of parameters for each event.
The Mann box generation cannot accept more than one of each parameter, and so the turbulence
intensity of the base wind field remains consistent temporally.
The CP anomaly was applied uniformly across the inflow boundary, which assumes the gust
front is spatially coherent at the scale of the farm. In reality, CP boundaries may arrive with
non-uniform intensity or timing across the site, producing asynchronous turbine responses.
The wind farm layout, background wind speeds, and inflow angles tested here are specific to
the chosen simulation scenarios and may not generalize to other configurations. The chosen
staggered layout, for example, may respond differently to angled inflows than a different row
spacing or alignment.
To increase the confidence in the findings, more cases should be simulated. More cases capture
the real effect on AEP or grid stability.
Finally, the simulations do not explicitly model turbine control system dynamics such as yaw
rate limits or pitch actuation delays. These factors could significantly affect how turbines re-
spond to the abrupt directional and speed changes of a CP, potentially amplifying or dampening
the observed effects.

5
Conclusion
This study set out to detect, characterize, and simulate CP events to quantify their influence
on wind farm performance. Cold pools are transient but potentially high-impact meteorolog-
ical phenomena capable of producing abrupt wind speed surges, directional shifts, and turbu-
lence changes that can significantly affect turbine loads and energy production. While their
importance in convective storm environments is becoming well established in meteorology,
their operational relevance to wind energy in Northern Europe remains largely unexplored. By
combining a high-frequency detection algorithm with turbulence characterization and dynamic
wind farm simulations, this work addresses a key knowledge gap and demonstrates a practical
framework for assessing CP impacts on wind power systems.
From 2016–2024, the detection algorithm identified 134 cold pool events using a combination
of temperature-drop and wind surge criteria. The majority of events occurred during summer
afternoons, consistent with the diurnal and seasonal cycle of convective activity in Denmark.
Year-to-year variability was substantial, with a notable increase in events over the past two
years, possibly reflecting a broader climatological shift toward more frequent convective insta-
bility. A detailed case study verified the robustness of the detection method, showing strong
agreement between radar imagery and near-surface meteorological signatures of a CP passage.
Characterization of the detected events revealed a composite wind anomaly marked by a rapid
ramp-up in the u-component (≈4 m/s) and v-component (≈2.5 m/s) at turbine hub height
(103 m), followed by a gradual decay over roughly an hour. Directional shifts of up to 35◦
were observed, complicating yaw alignment and increasing the potential for energy losses.
The Mann turbulence model fitting yielded average parameters of αε2/3 = 0.2106, L = 57.39
m, and Γ = 4.267, indicating high turbulence intensity, large eddy length scales on the order
of rotor diameter, and significant anisotropy. These parameters confirm that CP-driven wind
fields are energetic, coherent, and distinct from background atmospheric turbulence, making
them operationally relevant for both load assessment and forecasting.
The simulation campaign, comprising 72 Dynamiks runs, directly tested the influence of CP
anomalies on a model wind farm under varying background wind speeds and inflow angles.
Results showed that CP passages consistently enhanced power output, though the magnitude
and duration of the gains depended strongly on the inflow geometry and pre-event wind speed.
Low baseline wind speeds (6 m/s) experienced the largest relative gains, consistent with the
cubic relationship in the wind power equation.
60
==PAGE:68==
The inflow angle determined the spatial and temporal distribution of the power increase: aligned
inflows (0◦) produced the greatest cumulative energy gain, while oblique inflows (30◦and 60◦)
yielded sharper peaks but faster decays due to wake interference. Field visualizations revealed
that the staggered farm layout allowed aligned inflows to thread between turbine rows with
minimal wake overlap, whereas angled inflows drove wakes into downstream turbines, ampli-
fying losses over time. Turbine-level time series confirmed that downstream units were more
affected by wake persistence in the aligned case.
These findings carry several implications for wind energy applications. For resource assess-
ment, CPs should be considered as intermittent but impactful contributors to site wind statis-
tics, especially in convective climates. For operations, abrupt wind ramps and directional shifts
require fast yaw response and potentially adaptive control strategies to capitalize on transient
high-wind periods without increasing structural loads. For modeling, the integration of mea-
sured CP anomalies into dynamic wake simulations proved effective in capturing realistic,
transient inflow conditions and could be extended to other mesoscale-to-microscale coupling
frameworks.
Several limitations should be acknowledged. The detection process relied on a single me-
teorological mast, limiting spatial coverage and the ability to capture off-axis CP passages.
Threshold-based criteria inevitably filter out events that do not match the predefined signature,
potentially biasing the composite toward stronger or more textbook cases. In the simulations,
a uniform wind farm layout and simplified atmospheric conditions were assumed, omitting ef-
fects such as stability variation or terrain complexity. The decoupling of CP inflow anomalies
from their mesoscale context means that interactions between CP evolution and larger-scale
weather patterns were not directly modeled.
Future work should address these limitations by expanding detection networks with multiple
masts or scanning remote sensing systems, enabling spatial mapping of CP structure and prop-
agation. Classifying CPs by intensity and duration could inform tailored operational responses
and improve ensemble simulation strategies. Coupling mesoscale models such as WRF with
large-eddy simulations or DWM could better capture the generation, growth, and decay of
CPs in realistic environments. Additionally, investigating CP impacts across diverse turbine
technologies and farm layouts would provide a more generalizable understanding of their op-
erational consequences.
In conclusion, this study successfully met its objectives by detecting, characterizing, and sim-
ulating cold pool events in a wind energy context. The combined observational and simulation
61
==PAGE:69==
approach not only validated the detection methodology but also revealed the mechanisms by
which CPs influence wind farm output. The results underscore the importance of including
transient convective outflow events in both resource assessment and operational planning and
increase the demand for CP prediction research. The methodology developed here offers a
foundation for future research into CP–wind energy interactions and supports the development
of control and forecasting tools to optimize wind farm performance during such events.
