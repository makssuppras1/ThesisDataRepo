ABSTRACT 
Unconventional T cells expressing the γδ T cell receptor (TCR) can be divided into 
IFN-γ producing (γδT1) and IL-17 producing (γδT17) cells. These subsets can be 
distinguished by means of differentially expressed surface markers such as CD27, 
CCR6, NK1.1 and CD44. However, these markers do not allow a clear identification of 
the subsets in the gut. Thus, subsets can only be identified by means of transcription 
staining (for Tbet and RORγt, expressed on γδT1 and γδT17 cells, respectively) or by 
using transgenic animal models. We found that CD25, PD-1, CD127 and CD44 are 
differentially expressed on intestinal γδT17 cells and a combination of at least three of 
these markers allow a segregation of the subsets. In addition, we studied the activation 
state of the TCR at steady state and if it may be important for homeostasis of these cells. 
We found that mice with decreased TCR expression and signaling have impaired γδ T 
cell numbers in the gut, with a higher impact in the small intestine (SI) than in the colon. 
We also found that CD73 and Nur77 expression is higher in the SI, indicating that 
intestinal γδ T cells may have different TCR signaling requirements for development 
and/or homeostasis depending on the intestinal compartment. Additionally, we blocked 
CD132 (common γ-chain) in-vivo to elucidate the importance of homeostatic cytokines 
for maintenance of intestinal γδT17 cells. We found an increase in total γδ T cell and 
γδT17 cell proportions in the colon but not in the SI of injected animals, which may 
suggest that mechanisms that control homeostasis of γδT17 cells might differ between 
SI and colon.

INTRODUCTION 
The gastrointestinal tract 
The gastrointestinal tract is constantly exposed to foreign antigens derived from 
food and pathogens and represents a complex system of cells involved in nutrient 
sensing, breakdown and absorption, corresponding to its primary function. The intestine 
in addition constitutes the major immune compartment of the body, where immune and 
non-immune cells orchestrate the balance between oral tolerance (to food antigens), 
commensal tolerance, and inflammatory responses to pathogens (Mowat & Agace, 
2014). The intestinal tract starts at the end of the stomach and ends at the anus, and it 
is composed of the small intestine (SI) (from duodenum to ileum) and the large intestine 
(LI) (from caecum to anus) (Figure 1A). Microscopically, the intestinal wall is composed 
of four layers: serosa, submucosa, muscularis and mucosa (Figure 1B) (Jaladanki & 
Wang, 2011). The mucosa is the inner-most layer and consists of the lamina propria 
(LP), a thin layer of connective tissue, and the epithelium, a single-cell layer that 
separates the intestinal lumen from the LP and acts as a barrier holding commensals 
and pathogens at bay (Jaladanki & Wang, 2011). In the SI, the mucosal layer contains 
folds called villi, that increase the surface area of the epithelium for increased nutrient 
absorption. In both compartments a crypt is present, rich in stem cells that renew the 
epithelium every 4-5 days (Mowat & Agace, 2014). Epithelial cells (ECs) contain pattern 
recognition receptors (PRRs) involved in initiating an immune response, and specialized 
ECs such as Paneth cells produce antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) and regenerative 
compounds for tissue homeostasis. Goblet cells are also present and are involved in 
mucus production which adds an additional barrier to pathogens (Mowat & Agace, 2014). 
The intestinal tract contains its specialized secondary lymphoid tissues which are 
collectively known as gut-associated lymphoid tissues (GALT) and include the Peyer’s 
Patches, isolated lymphoid follicles and cryptopatches (Buettner & Lochner, 2016).

Each compartment of the mucosa has its own organization with different types of innate 
and adaptive immune cells. Within the epithelium, intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs) can 
be found and these are either conventional αβ T cells expressing co-receptors CD8αβ 
or CD4, or unconventional CD8αα cells expressing either an αβ or a γδ T cell receptor 
(TCR) (Ma et al., 2021). In the LP, many different innate and adaptive immune cells are 
present such as B and T cells, innate lymphoid cells (ILCs), macrophages, dendritic cells, 
and granulocytes (Okumura & Takeda, 2016). Rarer types of immune cells such as 
mucosal-associated invariant T (MAIT) cells and invariant natural killer T (iNKT) cells can 
be found (Mowat & Agace, 2014). Although in lower numbers than in the epithelium, γδ 
T cells can also be found in the LP (Asigbetse et al., 2010), and these cells will be 
discussed in the following section.

Gamma delta T cells (γδ T cells) 
Gamma delta T cells (γδ T cells) are an unconventional subset of T cells that 
express a T cell receptor (TCR) composed of a gamma (γ) and a delta (δ) chain. Unlike 
their αβ counterparts, they have a more limited TCR repertoire and mostly reside at 
epithelial barriers such as the skin, lung, and intestine, where they have a major role in 
tissue surveillance, homeostasis, repair, and as a first line of defense against pathogenic 
microorganisms (Parker & Ciofani, 2020). Like αβ T cells, γδ T cells arise from early 
thymic progenitors and are the first T cells to appear in the fetal thymus at embryonic 
day 13.5 (E13.5) (Havran et al., 2005). Evidence suggests that γδ T cell maturation and 
pre-programming occurs in the embryonic thymus, and subsets acquire their effector 
function locally without the need for priming in periphery, thus allowing an innate-like 
response upon infection (Jensen et al., 2009; Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011; Haas et al., 
2012; Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016; Sumaria et al., 2017). Their classification is based on 
ontogeny, anatomical residence and TCR diversity (Jensen et al., 2009). In mice, γδ T 
cells are distinguished by their variable gamma chain (Vγ) while in humans, by the 
variable delta chain (Vδ) (Nielsen et al., 2017). 
Different subsets of γδ T cells arise at different stages of ontogeny in coordinated waves 
expressing different Vγ chains (Table 1). The first wave of γδ T cells appear in the fetal 
thymus and generates a subset that expresses the invariant Vγ5Vδ1 TCR (Heilig & 
Tonegawa, 1986 nomenclature), also known as dendritic epidermal T cells (DETCs). 
These cells populate the epidermis of the skin and produce interferon gamma (IFN-γ). A 
second wave in the fetal thymus generates Vγ6 and Vγ4 cells that populate the genital 
tract, tongue, lungs, intestinal lamina propria, uterus and dermis and mostly produce IL-
17A/F and IL-22. A third wave occurs postnatally and generates γδ T cells with diverse 
Vγ and Vδ that reside in lymphoid organs and epithelial surfaces such as lung and 
intestines (Jensen et al., 2009; Nielsen et al., 2017; Khairallah et al., 2018; Parker & 
Ciofani, 2020).

Due to the nature of γδ T cells, their classification as innate or adaptive lymphocytes 
remains a subject of debate. The first two waves of γδ T cells produce innate-like cells 
with invariant TCRs that migrate and enrich epithelial tissues where they self-renew and 
maintain for a long-time. They sense signals leading to rapid effector responses (Parker 
& Ciofani, 2020). In addition, they do not require antigen presenting cells (APCs) and do 
not depend on MHC molecules (Ferreira, 2013). However, unlike ILCs, γδ T cells express 
a TCR that undergoes rearrangement with some subsets showing junctional diversity 
(Xiong & Raulet, 2007). In addition, γδ T cells may generate memory upon primary 
infection (Sheridan et al., 2013).  
 
==PAGE:10==

Their function depends on the subset, anatomical location, and stimuli (Xiong & Raulet, 
2007). The main types of γδ T cells based on cytokine profile are γδT1, which mostly 
produce IFN-γ and share similarities NKT cells, and γδT17, which produce high amounts 
of IL-17A and will be discussed in more detail in the following section. γδ T cells are 
involved in tissue homeostasis, pathogen clearance, chronic inflammatory responses 
and anti-tumor responses (Shekhar et al., 2012). Indeed, γδ T cell infiltration in the tumor 
environment was found to be correlated with a favorable prognosis in humans (Gentles 
et al., 2015). However, γδT17 cells have been shown to have pro-tumor properties in 
mouse models (Wakita et al., 2010) and thus the cancer response by γδ T cells is subset-
dependent (Park & Lee, 2021). Other functions of γδT17 cells have been discovered 
such as bone regeneration (Ono et al., 2016), adipose tissue thermogenesis (Kohlgruber 
et al., 2018), and promotion of short-term memory in the mouse meninges (Ribeiro et al., 
2019). In addition, many chronic inflammatory conditions in humans and mouse models 
appear to be associated with aberrant γδ T cell activity (Paul et al., 2015). 
 
Development of γδ T cells 
γδ T cells originate in the thymus from a common lymphoid progenitor (CLP). The 
decision between the αβ or the γδ lineage is mutually exclusive and takes place at the 
CD4−CD8− double negative (DN) stage. The exact mechanism by which the cells commit 
to either lineage is a matter of discussion and two models exist: the stochastic model 
proposes that commitment takes place before TCR gene rearrangement (Kang et al., 
2001), and the instructive model (the most accepted one) suggests that lineage selection 
is dependent on the strength of TCR signaling after rearrangement, with cells receiving 
strong signals being promoted to the γδ lineage and cells receiving weak signals being 
committed to the αβ lineage (Haks et al., 2005; Hayes et al., 2005; Coffey et al., 2014). 
 
==PAGE:11==
6 
 
Evidence shows that upon lineage commitment, the TCR strength also determines the 
effector function of γδ T cells (Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011). Cells that receive strong 
signals by means of interactions with putative thymic ligands become IFN-γ producing 
cells (γδT1) while those with that receive weak TCR signaling become IL-17 producing 
cells (γδT17) (Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011). These subsets are characterized by the 
lineage-specific transcriptions factors Tbet and RORγt, respectively. Although no ligands 
for γδ T cells have been identified, it is speculated that DETCs undergo positive selection 
by interactions with skint1 expressed on thymic epithelial cells and keratinocytes, as a 
mutation in skint1 leads to impaired number of DETCs (Lewis et al., 2006; Boyden et al., 
2008; Barbee et al., 2011). In addition, mice deficient for the autoimmune regulator 
(AIRE) have increased numbers of γδT17 cells (Fujikado et al., 2016). In addition to TCR 
engagement, several other signaling pathways may also be involved in determining the 
fate of emerging γδ T cells, such as lymphotoxin β receptor signaling (Silva-Santos et 
al., 2005) and Notch signaling (Shibata et al., 2011). 
 
γδT17 cells 
γδT17 cells are a subset expressing the retinoic-acid-receptor-related orphan 
receptor gamma t (RORγt) transcription factor and produce IL-17A and the related IL-
17F when stimulated with IL-23 and IL-1β (Wencker et al., 2014). IL-17 is a type-3 
cytokine important for extracellular pathogen responses. It acts on epithelial, endothelial, 
and stromal cells inducing the secretion of granulocyte-colony stimulating factor (G-CSF) 
which induces granulopoiesis and neutrophil release into the bloodstream from the bone 
marrow (BM) (Zenobia & Hajishengallis, 2015). It also induces chemokine expression 
(CXCL1, 2, 5 and 8) by these cells which lead to recruitment of neutrophils to the 
inflammatory site via the chemokine receptor CXCR2 (Zenobia & Hajishengallis, 2015).  
 
==PAGE:12==

IL-17 also acts in positive feed-back loop to induce activation and expansion of Th17 and 
γδT17 cells and thus rising IL-17 levels at the inflammatory site (Stutton et al., 2009; 
Benedetti & Miossec, 2014; McGinley et al., 2020). Furthermore, γδT17 cells also 
produce IL-22 that together with IL-17 contribute to induction of AMPs, mucins, 
upregulation of tights junction proteins and barrier repair leading to enhanced protection 
(Valeri & Raffatellu, 2016). γδT17 cells thus play an important role in initiating and 
progressing inflammatory responses and are therefore an interesting population to be 
studied and understood in the context of inflammatory conditions such as psoriasis, 
multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, among others. 
The two subsets of murine γδT17 cells can express either the Vγ4 or Vγ6 chains, 
although some Vγ1-expressing cells have been found to produce IL-17 (Roark et al., 
2008). Vγ6+ cells develop in the thymus during embryonic and neonatal life and mostly 
localize to lung, dermis, uterus, and mouth and gut (Table 1) showing restricted TCR 
diversity (Mckenzie et al., 2017). Vγ4+ cells develop during embryonic life and 
postnatally, localizing to dermis, lung, intestine, and secondary lymphoid organs (SLOs) 
(Table 1). While both subsets are pre-programmed in the thymus, a study by Zeng et al., 
2012 discovered that engagement of the TCR with phycoerythrin (PE), an alga derived 
protein, upregulated CD44, RORγt and cytokines receptors associated with a type-3 
response leading to production of IL-17 in naïve-like Vγ4+ cells in adult mice, providing 
evidence that although most γδT17 cells are innate-like, some Vγ4+ cells can be primed 
in the periphery from naïve precursors, and these cells have been classified as 
“inducible” γδT17 cells (Chien et al., 2013).  
γδT17 cells can be distinguished from their IFN-γ producing counterparts by the 
phenotype CCR6+, CD44high and CD27− (Ribot et al., 2009; Haas et al., 2009). In addition, 
they are NK1.1−, CD122− and IL-7Rαhigh (Haas et al., 2009; Papotto et al., 2017). Between 
the two subsets of γδT17 cells, the main difference is the level of TCR expression: Vγ6+ 
cells have increased TCR expression (and therefore CD3) while Vγ4+ cells have lower

(Paget et al., 2015; McKenzie et al., 2017). However, there is no conclusive evidence 
that TCR expression levels represents any functional difference in the periphery, as both 
subsets seem to be hyporesponsive and mostly respond to cytokines (Wencker et al., 
2014). γδT17 cells can also produce cytokines upon PRR activation, such as when 
stimulated by toll-like receptors (TLRs) 1 and 2 ligands (Martin et al., 2009). In addition, 
γδT17 cells uniquely express the scavenger receptors SCART1 and SCART2, which can 
be used to distinguish them from IFN-γ producing γδ T cells (Kisielow et al., 2008). 
Moreover, SCART1 seems to identify the Vγ6 subset, while SCART2 can be used to 
identify Vγ4+ cells (Tan et al., 2019).  
Despite γδT1 and γδT17 cells being completely different subsets with differences in 
developmental requirements and showing a contrasting phenotype, γδT17 cells have 
been shown to be capable of co-producing both types of cytokines under certain 
circumstances manifesting a certain plasticity (Schmolka et al., 2013; Sheridan et al., 
2013; Corpuz et al., 2017). In the same manner as Th17 cells, γδT17 can co-produce 
IFN-γ and IL-17 in response to a combination of IL-1β and IL-23 in-vitro (Schmolka et al., 
2013). This phenotype was also observed in-vivo in a murine peritoneal cancer model 
(Schmolka et al., 2013) and upon oral Listeria monocytogenes infection (Sheridan et al., 
2013). Interestingly, Kadekar et al., 2020 discovered that γδT17 (mostly Vγ6+) cells 
predominantly co-express Tbet and RORγt in the small intestinal and colonic lamina 
propria where they co-produce IFN-γ and IL-17 at steady-state in a STAT3 and retinoic 
acid-dependent manner, and this phenotype was shown to be regulated by a balance of 
the two isoforms of STAT5 (A and B) (Kadekar et al., 2020). The role of this population 
in the intestine is not known and while it is speculated that it may provide an advantage 
during infection by producing both cytokines, more research is needed to elucidate its 
contribution during inflammation and the role it plays at steady state.

Homeostasis of γδ T cells 
Upon development in the thymus, γδ T cells migrate to peripheral organs where 
they establish and remain throughout adult life (Papotto et al., 2017). Naïve αβ T cell 
survival depends on weak interactions (referred as tonic signals) of the TCR with self-
antigens and by means of homeostatic cytokines (Boyman et al., 2009). Basal 
homeostatic proliferation of CD4+ cells depend mostly on IL-7, while CD8+ cells seem to 
require predominantly IL-15 (Boyman et al., 2007). A study by Baccala et al., 2005 and 
French et al., 2005 reported that the same cytokines are important for homeostatic 
proliferation of γδ T cells, although they do not seem to require MHC recognition as the 
αβ counterpart. In fact, French et al., 2009 reported that β2 microglobulin (β2m) negatively 
impacts homeostatic proliferation of γδ T cells. In addition, they seem to compete with 
αβ T cells and NK cells for survival (Baccala et al., 2005; French et al., 2005). Michel et 
al., 2012 reported that IL-7 seems to be mostly important for CD27− (γδT17) cells in the 
periphery, while IL-15 mostly acted on CD27+ (γδT1) cells. This observation is consistent 
with the phenotype of the γδ T cell subsets, as IL-7Rα (CD127) is highly expressed on 
γδT17 but not in γδT1 cells, while IL-15Rβ (CD122) expression is absent in γδT17 but it 
is a marker for γδT1 cells (Papotto et al., 2017). Furthermore, an age-dependent 
increase in accumulation of Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ cells in peripheral lymph nodes (pLNs) of 
mice was observed, and this was found to be correlated with a local increase in IL-7 
levels (Chen et al., 2019). Moreover, increased infiltration of accumulated Vγ6+ cells from 
pLNs to tumor sites lead to tumor progression (Chen et al., 2019).  
Activated T cells produce IL-2, another known homeostatic cytokine. Li et al., 2020 found 
that a murine strain that spontaneously develops lupus has an age-dependent decrease 
in γδT17 cells in the spleen, correlated with an age-dependent decline in IL-2 production 
by T cells. The importance of IL-2 was confirmed in-vivo by neutralization of IL-2 leading 
to decreased IL-17 producing γδ T cells. In-vitro, IL-2 deficient medium had increased 
apoptosis of γδT17 cells, while a decrease in apoptosis was observed when IL-2 was 
==PAGE:15==
10 
 
supplemented. However, IL-2 seem to be mostly involved in survival rather than 
proliferation, with only IL-7 supplementation leading to proliferation of γδT17 cells (Li et 
al., 2020). This was found to be mostly via a STAT5-dependent maintenance of RORγt 
(Li et al., 2020). Furthermore, it had been previously shown that CD28 activation is 
important for IL-2 production by γδ T cells, regulating survival in an autocrine mechanism 
(Ribot et al., 2012). Michel et al., 2012 also found that both subsets require IL-2, although 
CD27+ cells to a major level. 
McIntyre et al., 2020 found that β2-integrins seem to play a tissue-specific role in 
homeostasis of Vγ6+ cells, as β2 (CD18)-deficient mice have decreased numbers of Vγ6+ 
cells in the lungs, uterus, blood, and spleen but normal numbers in the gut and other 
lymphoid tissues. As β2-integrins do not seem to play a role in γδ T cell homing (McIntyre 
et al., 2020) and development was not affected, it indicates a role in homeostasis of Vγ6+ 
cells. In addition, an increase in pro-survival proteins and a decrease in pro-apoptotic 
proteins was observed, with normal proliferation kinetics, indicating a role in survival 
(McIntyre et al., 2020). 
In addition to cytokines, immune checkpoint receptors may play important roles in 
regulating γδ T cell numbers in the periphery. For instance, Bekiaris et al., 2013 observed 
that the B and T lymphocyte attenuator (BTLA) receptor plays an important role in 
controlling homeostatic numbers of γδT17 cells in LNs and skin by downregulation of IL-
7R, while increased IL-7R expression led to an increase in BTLA expression in a feed-
back loop. It was also observed that RORγt acts as a negative regulator of BTLA 
(Bekiaris et al., 2013), indicating that RORγt most likely sustains survival of γδT17 cells. 
The V-domain Immunoglobulin Suppressor of T cell Activation (VISTA), another 
inhibitory receptor, has been shown to regulate homeostatic numbers of γδT17 cells by 
means of restricting IL-7R signaling (Li et al., 2017).

PART 1 – Immunophenotyping of intestinal γδT17 cells 
 
Problem and aims 
The first part of this master thesis consists of immunophenotyping γδT17 cells in the 
gut in order to find a surface marker or a combination of surface markers that are 
differentially expressed between γδT1 and γδT17 cells and would allow a discrimination 
of the subsets by means of flow cytometry (Figure 2). γδT17 cells in SLOs and in the 
thymus can be identified by expression of CCR6, SCART1/2, CD44high and by the 
absence of the classical marker CD27. Nonetheless, CCR6 expression is low on non-
lymphoid tissues (Haas et al., 2009) and CD44 is expressed by a diversity of cells in the 
intestine, including some IFN-γ producing γδ T cells. Moreover, the use of Liberase 
Thermolysin Medium leads to cleavage of CD27 in the gut, and the use of other 
collagenases has poor cell yield. SCART1 and 2 have been shown to allow a distinction 
between the two subsets (as γδT1 cells do not express these receptors) (Kisielow et al., 
2008), but no commercial SCART1/2 antibodies are available. Thus, the only available 
options to identify the subsets are by means of transcription factor (TF) staining or by the 
use of transgenic models. However, the identification of the subsets in wild-type (WT) 
mice by surface staining is desired as intranuclear staining does not allow recovery of 
live cells for cell sorting and RNA sequencing. Moreover, TF staining leads to loss of 
fluorescent proteins (FPs) such as green fluorescent protein (GFP) and red fluorescent 
protein (RFP) (Grupillo et al., 2011) which poses a drawback when these subsets are 
sought to be identified in a model that reports gene expression by means of FPs. 
Furthermore, the majority of γδT17 cells in the intestine have been found to co-express 
Tbet and RORγt (Kadekar et al., 2020). Although not the main focus of this thesis, it is 
also sought to find a surface marker (or markers) that allow a distinction of 
RORγt+Tbet−  and RORγt+ Tbet+ cells.

Mouse model 
In order to easily identify the subsets of γδ T cells without TF staining, a reporter 
mouse model was used (Figure 3). These mice report GFP under the control of the 
RORγt promoter and Amcyan under the control of the Tbet promoter. Thus, γδT17 cells 
can be identified by being GFP+. Therefore, we tested several surface markers to see if 
they were differentially expressed between GFP− and GFP+ cells. Intranuclear staining 
for RORγt and Tbet was also carried out in some instances (e.g., when the use of the 
reporter model was not possible).

RESULTS 
Development-associated surface molecules: CD25, CD24 and CD73 
CD25 and CD24 are maturation markers expressed on early γδ T cell thymic 
progenitors. These cells are highly proliferative and express low levels of the γδ TCR 
(Coffey et al., 2014). Throughout development, CD25 is downregulated while the TCR is 
upregulated. Shortly after, CD24+CD25− cells transition to a more mature state marked 
by the CD24−CD25− phenotype (Coffey et al., 2014). Upon thymus egress, the majority 
of γδ T are expected to be negative for both markers. However, Shibata et al., 2008 
identified CD25+ IL-17 producing γδ T cells in the peritoneal cavity of mice, while IFN-γ 
producing cells were not found to express this molecule. These cells seemed to acquire 
the CD25 phenotype in the periphery upon tissue localization (Shibata et al., 2008). 
CD25 expression in γδT17 cells has also been characterized by Colpitts et al., 2015 and 
Corpus et al., 2017 in both Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ cells and CD25 was found to be higher in this 
last population (Corpus et al., 2017). Expression of CD25 was thus evaluated at steady 
state in intestinal γδ T cells, and it was detected in both the small intestinal lamina propria 
(siLP) and colonic lamina propria (coLP) (Figure 4A). Expression of this marker was 
indeed higher in GFP+ (RORγt+) cells, although GFP− cells also had some staining. This 
may point towards a role of IL-2 in survival of both subsets (Michel et al., 2012). With 
respect to CD24, we included this marker in the flow cytometry panel as it was available 
in our lab and it was detected in the RNAseq (on RORγt− cells), although no staining was 
expected based on literature (Coffey et al., 2014). Expression of CD24 was indeed not 
detected in γδ T cells. CD24 was examined in B cells to confirm that the staining worked, 
as this lymphocyte population is known to express this marker (Duperray et al., 1990).

CD73 is a marker for strong TCR and it was found to be a marker for γδ lineage 
commitment (Coffey et al., 2014), in accordance with the instructive (TCR strength) 
lineage commitment model. As the TCR strength also plays a role in effector function 
programming, it was speculated that maybe a difference in CD73 expression between 
the two subsets would be observed. Nevertheless, CD73 staining was observed at 
similar levels in both subsets, with γδ T cells isolated from the siLP showing higher CD73 
expression than those isolated from the coLP (Figure 4B). Some RORγt+CD73− cells 
were observed in the colon, and these have been discussed by In et al., 2017 as cells 
that develop via an alternative pathway without CD73 upregulation.

cannot be used as potential markers to identify the subsets. However, it confirms that 
these cells are fully committed, mature γδ T cells. CD25 expression was found to be 
higher in RORγt+ cells, although this marker alone did not allow a separation of the two 
subsets (Figure 4C) as upon gating on TCRγδ+CD25high cells significant contamination 
from Tbet+ cells is present.

Inhibitory receptors: BTLA, PD-1 and CD244 
BTLA is an inhibitory receptor expressed on T and B cells (Gavrieli et al., 2006). 
Bekiaris et al., 2013 found that BTLA controls homeostasis of γδT17 cells by decreasing 
IL-7R signaling. In addition, RORγt was reported to be a negative regulator of BTLA 
(Bekiaris et al., 2013). Due to these findings, it was tested whether BTLA expression 
could differentiate between γδT17 and γδT1 cells in the gut. However, no BTLA staining 
was detected in any subset at steady state. BTLA is known to be highly expressed on B 
cells (Gavrieli et al., 2006) and to confirm that the staining worked, cells were gated on 
this population and they were indeed BTLA+. Expression of the Programmed cell death 
protein 1 (PD-1), another inhibitory receptor, was detected in the double positive (DP, 
RORγt+Tbet+) population in an independent RNAseq experiment (data not shown). This 
receptor has been studied in human (Iwasaki et al., 2011) and murine (Lopez et al., 2012) 
γδ T cells. Our results indicate that PD-1 expression is high on RORγt+ cells at steady 
state, especially in the coLP (Figure 5A). Similar to CD25, gating on TCRγδ+PD-1+ 
enriches for γδT17 cells but contamination from RORγt− cells is still present (Figure 5C), 
so PD-1 alone cannot isolate γδT17 cells but it could be combined with an additional 
marker to eliminate contamination from RORγt− cells. 
CD244 is a receptor that has been identified in several cells including γδ T cells 
(Schuhmachers et al., 1995; Hatano et al., 2017). Expression of this receptor was 
identified in an RNAseq (data not shown) of intestinal γδ T cells and was found to be 
higher on Tbet+ cells in the SI. Cells were then stained for this receptor to see if a 
difference in expression among the subsets would be discerned. This marker however 
was found to be expressed at high levels in both RORγt+ and RORγt− cells in the gut with 
no significant difference in expression levels (Figure 5B).

Homing and tissue retention: CD11b, CD103, CD69 and CD44 
CD11b is a β2-integrin important for extravasation of neutrophils and monocytes 
to the site of inflammation and has been discussed in the context of γδ T cells by Hoshino 
et al., 1993 and Smith & Barnum, 2008. As this marker was available, we stained γδ T 
cells to characterize CD11b expression levels, but all cells were CD11b− (not shown). 
Efficient staining was confirmed by gating on monocytes. While McIntyre et al., 2020 
found that β2-integrins may play a role in homeostasis of Vγ6+ cells, this seemed to be 
mostly dependent on CD11a as no CD11b or CD11c expression was observed in any 
subset (McIntyre et al., 2020), which is in accordance with the data we obtained. 
CD103 is the αE integrin involved in cell retention and homing and it is expressed in 
many intestinal leukocytes. CD103 has been studied in human γδ T cells (Peters et al., 
2019) and in mice CD103 has been characterized in intestinal and dermal γδ T cells (Do 
et al., 2017; Woodward Davis et al., 2015). We speculated if CD103 could separate 
RORγt+ cells from RORγt− and so cells were stained for this molecule. A population of 
Tbet+CD103+ was found in the siLP but not in the coLP (Figure 6A). The majority of 
RORγt+ cells were CD103− with a small percentage of cells being positive for this marker 
(Figure 6A), and these RORγt+CD103+ were mostly single positive (SP, RORγt+Tbet−) 
cells (not shown). 
CD69 is a C-type lectin expressed on tissue resident cells. This molecule is also a well-
known early T cell activation marker upregulated shortly after TCR/CD3 stimulation 
(Cibirán & Sánchez-Madrid, 2017). It was then speculated if a difference in the activation 
state between the two subsets would be present and if it would allow a separation of IFN-
γ and IL-17 producing γδ T cells by means of CD69 staining. Both RORγt+ and RORγt− 
cells expressed CD69, with a few cells from both subsets being negative for this marker 
in both the siLP and coLP (Figure 6B), with most RORγt+CD69− cells corresponding to 
the SP population (not shown).

γδT1 associated markers: NK1.1 and Ly6C 
NK1.1 is a C-type lectin and a marker for NK cells. This molecule has been shown 
to distinguish between IFN-γ and IL-17 producing γδ T cells in lymphoid tissues, with 
expression restricted to only CD27+ (γδT1) cells (Haas et al., 2009). We then speculated 
if the same would be true in the SI. While NK1.1 staining was found to be higher in Tbet+ 
cells (Figure 7A), many Tbet+NK1.1− cells could also be found. In addition, few 
RORγt+Tbet+ cells were also NK1.1+ in both siLP and coLP. As Haas et al., 2009 reported 
that NK1.1 and CCR6 is expressed at low levels in non-lymphoid organs, NK1.1 staining 
was somewhat expected to be low in the gut.  
Ly6C expression in γδ T cells has been previously reported (Lombes et al., 2015; Lino 
et al., 2017). Lombes et al., 2015 found that Ly6C−CD44high cells corresponded to 
CD27−CD44high (γδT17) cells in SLOs, liver, lungs, and siLP, and CD27+ (γδT1) cells can 
be identified by the phenotype Ly6C−+CD44low and/or Ly6C+CD44high. They also reported 
CD25high in CD27− cells. We thus found Ly6C a promising marker to distinguish the 
intestinal γδ T cell subsets. However, only few cells were found to be Ly6C+ in the gut 
(Figure 7B). A combination of Ly6C with CD44 did not allow a clear distinction of the 
subsets as reported by Lombes et al., 2015 (Figure 7C), although the few Ly6C+ cells 
were indeed Tbet+. 
In summary, NK1.1 expression was found to be low in the gut, in accordance with Haas 
et al. 2009 where it was shown this marker, together with CCR6, was preferentially 
expressed in SLOs. While Ly6C seemed like an interesting marker for Tbet+ cells based 
on Lombes et al., 2015, expression of this marker was low in the gut but restricted to 
Tbet+ cells.

We stained γδ T cells in the gut for cytokine receptors IL-7Rα (CD127), IL-1R 
(CD121a), IL-23R and IL-15Rα (CD215) to see if γδT1 and γδT17 cells could be 
distinguished based on their cytokine receptor expression profile. IL-7, IL-2 and IL-15 are 
homeostatic cytokines that have been shown to play a role in peripheral maintenance of 
γδ T cells (see homeostasis section). In addition, IL-1R and IL-23R are correlated with a 
type 3 immune response and these markers have been shown to be expressed by γδT17 
cells (Awasthi et al., 2009; Chien et al., 2014). Nevertheless, IL-1R, IL-23R and IL-15Rα 
staining was not found in any subset of γδ T cell (not shown), and it was neither found in 
conventional αβ T cells indicating that the staining did not work. These antibodies had 
been tested in our lab before and were shown to stain inefficiently.  
IL-7Rα (CD127) has been shown to be highly expressed in γδT17 cells (Baccala et al.,

efficient staining in the gut being higher in the RORγt+ subset, as expected (Figure 8A). 
We then tried to segregate the subsets based on CD127 expression (Figure 8B). Similar 
to CD44, some RORγt− cells were IL-7Rα+ which did not allow enrichment for only γδT17 
cells. In addition, some RORγt+ cells were IL-7Rαlow, so purification of γδT17 cells based

FINAL COMBINATION 
CD25, PD-1, CD44 and CD127 were found to be differentially expressed between 
γδT1 and γδT17 cells in the gut. However, these molecules by themselves did not 
efficiently separate the subsets as would be achieved by CD27 staining. We then 
combined these markers in a flow cytometry panel and checked how efficiently each 
combination isolated RORγt+ cells from RORγt− cells. In the colon the majority of cells 
are RORγt+ and thus a combination of two of the aforementioned markers was enough 
to get a final cell population composed of approximately 97-98% γδT17 cells. The two 
best combinations in the colon were CD127 together with CD44 (97.8% γδT17 cells, 
phenotype CD44highCD127high) and PD-1 together with CD44 (98.2% γδT17 cells, 
phenotype CD44highPD-1+). This proportion can be increased up to 99% γδT17 cells if a 
third marker is added (Figure 10). In the SI, where more RORγt− cells are found, at least 
3 markers are required for a high separation of the subsets, and a gating strategy

DISCUSSION  
Several markers were tested to see if they were differentially expressed between 
γδT1 and γδT17 cells and would allow a segregation of the subsets, which is commonly 
achieved by means of the classical marker CD27 (expressed by γδT1 cells only). It was 
confirmed that CD25 is upregulated again in the periphery upon establishment in organs 
as found by Shibata et al., 2008 and it can be used as a marker for γδT17 cells. PD-1 
was found to be highly expressed on γδT17 cells at steady state and expression is higher 
in the colon, where a larger part of RORγt+ cells express this receptor. CD44 and CD127 
(IL-7Rα) are common γδT17 markers, and were indeed found to be highly expressed by 
this subset in the intestine, with higher expression of both markers in the colon. However, 
by gating on TCRγδ+CD44+ or TCRγδ+CD127+ contamination by γδT1 cells can be found, 
siLP 
coLP 
==PAGE:35==
30 
 
so these markers alone cannot be used to isolate only γδT17 cells. As CD25, PD-1, 
CD44 and CD127 were differentially expressed on γδT17 cells, we tried several gating 
strategies based on these markers to see how efficiently they would separate intestinal 
RORγt+ from RORγt− γδ T cells. We found that a combination of at least 3 of the 
aforementioned markers enriches for γδT17 cells for up to 96-98% in the small intestinal 
and up to 98-99% in the colon. The optimal combination for both intestinal compartments 
was found to be PD-1 together with CD44 and CD127. This enriches for 97.6% in the SI 
and 99.3% in the colon with very few RORγt+ cells lost in the gating process. 
In addition to discovering a panel for isolation of γδT17 cells, the staining carried out 
provide insights on the biology of these unconventional T cells. By CD24 and CD73 
staining it was confirmed that these intestinal γδ T cells are fully committed and mature. 
CD25 is downregulated during maturation but it was found to be upregulated again by 
intestinal γδT17 cells. CD25 is the α chain of IL-2R, and this cytokine may play a role in 
survival of these cells (see homeostasis section). While CD25 alone can bind IL-2 with 
low affinity, it does not allow IL-2R signaling as it requires a trimeric structure composed 
of CD122 (IL-2/15R β-chain) and CD132 (common γ-chain) (Raker et al., 2020). 
Paradoxically, γδT1 cells express CD122 but no CD25. As CD122 and CD132 form a 
structure with an intermediate affinity for IL-2 (Raker et al., 2020), it may be plausible to 
believe that IL-2 plays a more important role in homeostasis of the IFN-γ producing cells. 
Il2−/− and cd25−/− mice have reduced γδT17 cell numbers, but the impairment is higher in 
the CD25 deficient animals (Shibata et al., 2008). It is thus possible that CD25 in γδT17 
cells has additional roles beyond IL-2R signaling, but this has yet to be elucidated. As 
CD25 is upregulated after activation (Bajnok et al., 2017) and together with the data 
displaying that most γδT17 cells are CD69+ and CD44high this may indicate that these 
cells have an active state and the phenotype of tissue-resident memory cells, although 
most γδT17 cells were found not to express CD103, another tissue residence marker. 
Some RORγt+Tbet−CD69− cells were found in both compartments of the intestine and it 
==PAGE:36==
31 
 
may be plausible that this population corresponds to Vγ4+ cells, as some Vγ4+ were found 
to be “inducible” (Zeng et al., 2012) and this might explain the lack of activation status 
marked by CD69 expression. Furthermore, Kadekar et al., 2020 found that most 
RORγt+Tbet+ cells corresponded to the Vγ6+ population and upon gating on 
RORγt+CD69− cells roughly 90% of them were single positive (RORγt+Tbet−). 
Nevertheless, intestinal γδ T cells were not co-stained with CD69, CD44 and Vγ4 to 
confirm this. Finally, high PD-1 expression was observed in γδT17 cells, and 
substantially higher in the colon. PD-1 had already been shown to be overexpressed in 
dermal γδT17 cells during psoriasis in both mice and humans (Kim et al., 2016), but in 
this thesis murine intestinal γδT17 cells were shown to express high levels of PD-1 at 
steady-state. As PD-1 is expressed upon continuous TCR stimulation in order to regulate 
inflammatory responses (Agata et al., 1996), it may be possible that γδT17 are being 
constantly stimulated by putative intestinal ligands thus explaining the activated 
phenotype (CD25+CD69+PD-1+) (the activation state of γδT17 cells is studied in more 
detail in the next part of this thesis). Treatments based on recombinant PD-1 ligands for 
chronic inflammatory diseases in which γδT17 play an important contribution are thus a 
prospect and have been shown to be effective in rheumatoid arthritis (Raptopoulou et 
al., 2010), inflammatory bowel disease (Song et al., 2015) and psoriasis (Kim et al., 
2016) in both mice and humans.

PART 2 - Homeostasis of intestinal γδT17 cells  
 
Problem and aims 
In the second part of this master thesis, we try to provide some light regarding the 
activation state of the γδ TCR in the intestine at steady state, and if the TCR may play a 
role in homeostasis of γδT17 cells. An independent RNAseq study (data now shown) 
indicated high TCR signaling in the double positive RORγt+Tbet+ population while 
RORγt+ cells isolated from pLNs had lower TCR signaling. This data is in agreement with 
a paper by Tan et al., 2019 showing that TCR signaling is present in the skin but not in 
the LNs. In addition, it has been previously shown that TCR signaling does not appear 
to be prominent in γδT17 cells in SLOs (Wencker et al., 2014). We therefore speculated 
that this active state in intestinal samples might be important to sustain γδT17 numbers 
throughout adult life by a homeostatic mechanism, as it is also known that naïve 
conventional αβ T cells require weak interactions with self-ligands for survival (Boyman 
et al., 2009). To test this, a Nur77 staining was carried out as a surrogate for TCR 
signaling and γδ T cell numbers were assessed in a mouse model with decreased γδ 
TCR expression and signaling. We also conducted an in-vivo common γ-chain (CD132) 
blocking experiment with the purpose of investigating cytokine requirements for 
peripheral maintenance of γδT17 cells.

CD3γ and CD3δ double haploinsufficient (CD3DH) mouse model  
The CD3γ and CD3δ double haploinsufficient (CD3DH) mouse is a model with 
reduced γδ TCR and CD3ε expression and signaling (Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016). This 
model has been discussed in the context of development in the thymus and lymph nodes. 
As the haploinsufficiency only affects γδ T cells without influencing conventional αβ T 
cells numbers, this model can be used to study signaling strength requirements for the 
development of the subsets of γδ T cells and potentially during homeostasis. As no data 
for intestinal γδ T cells exists for this model, γδ T cell numbers were assessed in the 
lamina propria of both intestinal compartments of adult CD3DH mice. 
 
RESULTS 
Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016 reported a decrease in numbers of both γδT1 and γδT17 
cells in the thymus during development and in the spleen and pLNs of adult mice. In 
addition, adult Vγ4 numbers remained unaffected, with a major impact in the Vγ6 
population. In this thesis, the numbers of γδ T cells were assessed in the small intestine, 
colon, and lymph nodes of adult CD3DH mice at steady state, and a decrease in 
intestinal γδ T cells was observed (Figure 11 and Figure 12). The decrease was also 
observed in pLNs (data not shown). While both intestinal compartments were affected 
by the reduced γδ TCR signaling, the impact was higher in the SI than in the colon. A 
decrease in the proportion of γδ T cells with respect to CD45+CD3+ cells was observed 
(Figure 13) as the haploinsufficiency has a major effect on γδ T cells while αβ T cell 
numbers remain unaffected. The impaired TCR expression and signaling affected all 
subsets equally, with no significant changes in the proportion of γδT17 cells in neither 
the SI nor colon (Figure 13). Within the γδT17 subset, a decrease in both Vγ4+ and 
Vγ6+ cells (defined as Vγ4−) numbers was observed, with a slightly higher impact on Vγ6+ 
cells (Figure 12), with no changes in proportions of Vγ4+ cells (Figure 13).  
==PAGE:39==
34 
 
A decrease in proportion of Vγ6+ cells was observed in the SI while in the colon it was 
insignificant. Together, this data shows that the haploinsufficiency affected all 
populations of intestinal γδ T cells equally, including Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ cells, with γδ T cell 
numbers being more affected in the SI than in the colon, providing evidence that the TCR 
is indeed required for both γδT1 and γδT17 cells. However, it cannot be presumed from 
the CD3DH model data that this decrease is due to affected homeostasis of cells, as 
impaired γδ T cell numbers was already observed in the embryonic thymus (Muñoz-Ruiz 
et al., 2016) and the decrease in TCR signaling and expression most likely affects 
development.

Nur77 
The Nr4a1 (Nur77) gene encodes a transcription factor shown to be involved in several 
pathways depending on the cell type and tissue location (Pearen & Muscat, 2010). The 
peculiarity about this protein is that it is upregulated shortly after TCR stimulation and it 
is commonly used to study TCR signaling both in-vitro and in-vivo in mice and humans 
(Ashouri & Weiss, 2017). In addition, the level of Nur77 expression is proportional to the 
TCR signaling strength, with thymocytes undergoing positive selection showing a 10-fold 
increase in expression with respect to basal levels (Moran et al., 2011). As Nur77 
expression does not seem to be dependent on co-stimulatory signals (such as CD28), 
inflammatory cytokines or general STAT5 signaling (involved in homeostatic cytokines 
IL-2, IL-7, and IL-15 signaling), this marker is preferentially used instead of the classical 
CD69 (Moran et al., 2011). Naïve conventional αβ T cells are known to require tonic 
signals with self-ligands for survival in the periphery (Boyman et al., 2009) and this can 
be confirmed by means of Nur77 expression (Moran et al., 2011). This marker is thus 
compelling to study the activation state of the γδ TCR at steady state in the intestinal 
lamina propria.  
 
RESULTS 
In order to provide more light with respect to the activation state of the γδTCR at 
steady state, intestinal γδ T cells were stained for the transcription factor Nur77. As 
stated in the previous section, Nur77 is an early T cell activation marker, upregulated 
shortly after TCR triggering. With the intention of confirming efficient Nur77 staining and 
that it can be used to detect TCR signaling, lymphocytes were isolated from pLNs and 
stimulated with 5 µg/mL anti-CD3 for 3 hours. Both CD4+ and CD8+ αβ T cells showed 
an increase in Nur77 staining upon stimulation, which was correlated with CD69 
expression, another well-known marker for T cell activation (Figure 14). These results 
confirmed that Nur77 staining works and can faithfully mark TCR activation.

In-vivo common γ-chain (CD132) blocking 
CD132 is known as the “common γ-chain” since it is shared by several cytokine 
(IL-2, 4, 7, 9, 15 and IL-21) receptors and it is required for functional signaling (Schwarze 
et al.. 2013). As mentioned in the homeostasis section, IL-2, IL-7, and IL-15 have been 
shown to be important for γδ T cell survival and proliferation. In order to confirm the 
importance of these cytokines for maintenance of γδ T cell numbers in the gut, we 
performed an in-vivo blocking experiment with 25 mg/kg of αCD132 blocking antibody 
every other day for 2 weeks. At the end of the experiment mice were culled to check if a 
difference in γδ T cell numbers would be observed between injected and non-injected 
controls.  
 
RESULTS 
We hypothesized that blocking IL-2R and most importantly IL-7R would lead to a 
decrease in γδT17 cell numbers based on literature (Baccara et al., 2005; French et al., 
2005; Chen et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). Surprisingly, we observed an increase in 
proportions of total γδ T cells and γδT17 cells in the colon but not in the SI nor in pLNs 
(Figure 17A and B). We did not observe any significant change in proportions of αβ T 
cells in neither the SI, colon nor pLNs (Figure 17C).

DISCUSSION 
Impaired γδ TCR expression and signaling led to a decrease in all subsets of 
intestinal γδ T cells in the CD3DH mouse model. We found that the decrease was higher 
in the small intestine than in the colon. We also observed a decrease in γδ T cell numbers 
in pLNs. In addition, both Vγ4+ and Vγ6+ (defined as Vγ4−) populations were affected in 
both intestinal organs, with a higher effect on Vγ6+ cells indicating that this population of 
γδT17 cells require higher TCR signaling strength for development than Vγ4+ cells as 
discussed by Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016. Although the reduced TCR expression in the 
CD3DH model may affect homeostasis of γδ T cells in the periphery, the decrease in 
numbers is most likely due to an impaired development as TCR signaling strength is 
important for γδ T cell commitment and effector function programming (Jensen et al., 
2008; Turchinovich & Hayday, 2011; Haas et al., 2012; Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016; Sumaria 
et al., 2017). In addition, the impairment is already noticeable in the embryonic thymus 
(Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016). This difference between the two intestinal compartments may 
indicate that γδ T cells targeted to the colon do not depend on stronger signals for 
development as cells to be localized to the SI do. In addition, CD73 is a marker for strong 
TCR and it is upregulated during γδ T cell development in the thymus (Coffey et al., 
2014) and we have shown (in Part 1 of this thesis) that γδ T cells from the SI have higher 
expression of this molecule. However, it may also be possible that γδ T cells require 
higher TCR signaling in the SI by means of interactions with putative intestinal ligands to 
maintain homeostatic numbers during adult life after thymus egress. This speculation 
should be confirmed by means of experimental designs that affect TCR expression and 
signaling in adult mice without influencing development. We intended to perform and 
experiment based on treating adult mice in-vivo with the GL3 (anti-γδ) antibody, which 
has been shown to internalize the γδ TCR and thus makes these cells unable to signal 
(Koenecke et al., 2009). However, treatment with GL3 does not allow detection of γδ T 
cells, but a mouse model that reports Tcrd expression by means of GFP is available 
==PAGE:49==
44 
 
(Koenecke et al., 2009) and could be used to identify the cells. In addition, as we found 
a panel to identify γδT17 cells based on CD25, CD127, CD44, PD-1 and CD69 
expression (Part 1 of this master thesis), intranuclear staining for RORγt and Tbet would 
not be needed, as this would lead to loss of the GFP signal (Grupillo et al., 2011). Due 
to time constraints we could not perform this experiment, but it is an example of 
experimental design that would allow us to study the importance of the TCR for 
homeostasis of intestinal γδT17 cells in adult mice without influencing development. 
Nonetheless, the CD3DH model provides further evidence that intestinal γδ T cells, 
similarly to γδ T cells from the LNs and spleen, arise from a common progenitor in the 
thymus that require TCR strength for development (Muñoz-Ruiz et al., 2016) but the 
biology of these cells can be altered upon entering peripheral organs, as it has been 
shown with CD25 upregulation in γδT17 cells (Shibata et al., 2008; and this report) and 
co-expression of RORγt and Tbet in the gut (Kadekar et al., 2020). 
Based on Nur77 staining, our data indicates that γδT17 cells are somewhat more active 
in the SI than in the colon, and in the colon γδT17 and γδT1 cells have similar levels of 
Nur77 expression. Even so, Nur77 staining was found at comparable levels in the lymph 
nodes, where γδ T cells have been shown to be hyporesponsive (Wencker et al., 2014; 
Tan et al., 2019; our RNAseq data) and is thus hard to conclude based on our Nur77 
staining how active the TCR is in the periphery.  
In order to elucidate the importance of cytokine requirements for homeostasis of 
intestinal γδT17 cell, we treated adult mice with a CD132 blocking antibody and it was 
hypothesized based on literature (Baccara et al., 2005; French et al., 2005; Chen et al., 
2019; Li et al., 2020) that a decrease in γδT17 numbers would be observed due to an 
impaired survival and proliferation of these cells. Surprisingly, an increase in proportions 
of γδT17 cells was observed in the colon while proportions remained equal in the SI and 
in pLNs. Proportions of αβ T cells did not change in any organ nor in the LNs. As the 
common γ-chain is shared by many cytokine receptors (IL-2, 4, 7, 9, 15 and IL-21) 
==PAGE:50==
45 
 
(Schwarze et al.. 2013), it is hard to interpret the data as these receptors are expressed 
in many cell subsets which may have an influence on γδ T cell numbers. It is also peculiar 
that blocking the γ-chain only affected colonic γδT17 cells. An alternative experiment 
blocking individual cytokine receptors could be carried out to see if this phenotype is 
caused by a single cytokine receptor and/or cell subset that may affect homeostasis of 
intestinal γδT17 cells.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
Media and buffers 
Collection buffer HBSS/Hepes was prepared by adding 15 mM of Hepes to 1x HBSS 
(10x HBSS diluted with sterile, distilled water). Washing buffer HBSS/HEPES/EDTA was 
prepared by adding to 1x HBSS (10x HBSS diluted with sterile, distilled water) 15 mM of 
Hepes, 2% FBS, 1% Pen Strep, 50 µg/mL of Gentamycin and 2 mM of EDTA. Complete 
(R10) medium was prepared by supplementing 20 mM Hepes, 10% FBS, 2% glutamine, 
2% Pen Strep and 50 µM of β-mercaptoethanol to 1650 RPMI medium. Isotonic Percoll 
was prepared by mixing 9 parts of Percoll with 1 part of 10X HBSS and supplementing 
with 10 mM Hepes. Isotonic Percoll was diluted with HBSS/HEPES/EDTA to 70% and 
40% for layering. 
 
Mice 
All animals were adults bred and housed in a barrier facility at the Technical University 
of Denmark following local regulations in accordance with the Directive 2010/63/EU. Both 
males and females were used. All transgenic mice were of C57BL/6 genetic background. 
The CD3DH model was provided by Natacha Gonçalves-Sousa (Instituto de Medicina 
Molecular, Universidade de Lisboa, Portugal). The Nur77-GFP model was provided by 
Bengt Johansson Lindbom (Faculty of Medicine, Lund University, Sweden).

Lymphocyte isolation from the intestinal laminal propria 
The small intestine and colon were harvested. The caecum was discarded. Organs were 
flushed with ice-cold HBSS/Hepes twice to remove fecal waste and mucus. Remaining 
fat and Peyer’s Patches were removed. Organs were cut longitudinally and shaken in a 
50 mL tube with ice-cold HBSS/Hepes to further clean mucus. They were cut in small 
pieces (around 1 cm) in a pre-warmed (37ºC) tube with 15 mL of HBSS/Hepes/EDTA 
and tubes were placed in a shaker at 37ºC, 400 g for 10 minutes. Afterwards, tubes were 
shaken rigorously for 10 seconds and poured over a 250 µm nylon filter. Pieces were 
transferred to a new pre-warmed tube and placed in the shaker once again for 15 
minutes. This washing step was carried out 4 times alternating 10 and 15 minutes. After 
the final wash, pieces were placed in a digestion tube with 5 mL complete medium, 10 
µL of DNAse I (stock 20 mg/mL) and 60 µL of Liberase TM (stock 5 mg/mL). The 
intestinal pieces were digested in a magnetic shaker for 45 minutes at 37ºC and 800 g. 
Then, cells were filtered through a 70 µm strainer and centrifuged. The pellet was 
resuspended in 40% Percoll, layered on top of 70% and centrifuged for 20 minutes, 800 
g at room temperature with brake off. The interphase was collected and transferred to 
complete medium. Cells were centrifuged and the pellet was stained. 
==PAGE:53==
48 
 
Lymphocyte isolation from peripheral lymph nodes 
Inguinal, brachial, and axillary lymph nodes were removed and remaining fat pieces were 
pulled out. Lymph nodes were crushed with a syringe plunger on a 70 µm cell strainer 
and resuspended in 5 mL complete medium. Cells were centrifuged, resuspended in 2 
mL complete medium and filtered through a 40 µm cell strainer. Cells were counted in a 
Sysmex XP-300 Hematology Analyzer and cell concentration was adjusted to 1 x 107 
cells/mL. 250 µL of adjusted cell suspension was transferred to a 96 well plate, followed 
by centrifugation and staining of the pellet. 
 
Surface staining 
Cells were stained in 100 µL Live/Dead viability stain for 10 minutes in a 96-well plate on 
ice dark. Afterwards, cells were washed with PBS and centrifuged. The pellet was 
stained in 75 µL antibody cocktail (1/200 dilution) for 30 minutes on ice dark. Afterwards, 
cells were washed with FACS buffer and centrifuged. Intranuclear staining was carried 
out or the pellet was resuspended in 300 µL of FACS buffer and transferred to flow 
cytometry tubes with a 35 µm strained on top. 
 
Intranuclear staining 
Following surface staining, the pellet was resuspended in 100 µL eBioscience Fixation 
buffer and incubated on ice dark for 1 hour. After the incubation, cells were washed with 
eBioscience Permeabilization buffer and centrifuged. The pellet was stained in 75 µL 
antibody cocktail containing the transcription factors (RORγt, Tbet and in some instances 
Nur77, 1/200 dilution in eBioscience Permeabilization buffer). Intranuclear staining was 
carried out for 1 hour on ice dark and afterwards cells were washed with eBioscience 
Permeabilization buffer, centrifuged, resuspended in 300 µL of FACS buffer and 
transferred to flow cytometry tubes with a 35 µm strained on top. 
==PAGE:54==

In-vitro T cell stimulation 
A 5 µg/mL solution of anti-CD3 (145-2C11 clone) was prepared by diluting the antibody 
in sterile PBS. 50 µL of the prepared solution was dispensed into wells in a 96-well plate 
for stimulated samples and 50 µL PBS for unstimulated controls. The plate was 
incubated at 37ºC for 2.5 hours. Afterwards, the liquid from the wells was removed with 
a pipette and wells were washed twice with 200 µL sterile, room temperature PBS. Upon 
washing, 250 µL lymph node cell suspension was transferred to the wells and the plate 
was placed in an incubator (37ºC) for a 3-hour stimulation. Cells were then harvested 
and stained. 
 
In-vivo anti-CD132 blocking 
4 adult mice were injected intraperitoneally with 0.5 mg (25 mg/kg) anti-CD132 blocking 
antibody (clone 3E12) 3 times a week for 2 weeks. Animals were then culled and 
intestines were harvested. Uninjected mice (also 4) were used as control. 
 
Flow cytometry and data analysis 
Samples were analyzed in a BD LSRFortessa and data was acquired with BD FACS 
Diva software. The obtained .fcs files were analyzed using FlowJo 10.7.1. Graphs and 
statistical analysis were made in the GraphPad Prism software version 9.0.2.
