Abstract
Bound infragravity waves are gravity waves, which are formed due to wave envelope
modulation in a progressing wave group. Infragravity waves propagate along with the
wave group until they are released due to breaking of the short waves. The aim of this
thesis is to use MIKE 21 to model these bound infragravity waves, which is a project
initiated by Danish Hydraulic Institute.
The model is based on the assumption, that runup is dominated by infragravity waves,
thus only the wave envelope of the short waves is simulated. The waves are simu-
lated based on a coupling between a spectral wave model and a hydrodynamic model.
The wave model determines the time varying radiation stresses and the hydrodynamic
model determines the surface elevation change based on the radiation stresses.
The empirical formulation of swash characteristics from Stockdon et al. (2006) is com-
pared to results from simpliﬁed model set-up. The results showed, that the numerical
model produced runup extremes in the same order of magnitude as the empirical for-
mulation. The model was furthermore investigated in regard to a ﬁeld experiment.
By measuring bathymetry, runup and oﬀshore surface elevation, the model was set-up
based on the conditions during the experiment.
The ﬁeld experiment showed, that the swash energy is not entirely based on infragrav-
ity waves, and that the short waves are also present in the swash zone. However, the
comparison between the numerical model and the ﬁeld experiment indicated consis-
tency between the measured and simulated swash motion. The model proved to be
sensitive to variation in the beach proﬁle.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Coastal protection has in many years been used to reduce the erosion of beaches and
protect urban areas from wave runup and overtopping. The runup is related to the
increased surface elevation (tide and surge) and the height reached by the swash. This
project concerns the contribution to the swash motion from infragravity waves.
1.1
Background
Diﬀerences in the frequency of dispersive waves causes them to travel in group with
a modulating wave envelope. This envelope form a harmonic varying mean sea level
known as bound infragravity waves (Longuet-Higgins and Stewart, 1962). These are
long period waves, which are bound to the wave group, hence the name bound in-
fragravity waves. When the incident waves approach shallow water they break and
dissipate but the low frequent energy remain causing the infragravity wave to propa-
gate as a free infragravity wave. The infragravity waves are more dominant in shallow
water but are also present in deep water (Webb et al., 1991). The mechanisms behind
the generation and propagation of infragravity waves are further described in Chapter
2.
Many diﬀerent hydrodynamic or morphodynamic features occurs inside the surf zone
due to wave breaking, current interaction with sandbars, up-rush etc. For the sake
of convenience a deﬁnition sketch of the inner coastal area are included in Figure 1.1.
The ﬁgure describes the terminology in the inner coastal area.

1.2
Research Question
The wave runup is an important coatal process, which causes dune erosion and cross-
shore sediment transport (de Bakker et al., 2016). The runup is random and depends
on many coastal mechanisms, which makes it hard to predict. It is therefore often
estimated based on parameterization of ﬁeld measurements (Stockdon et al., 2006).
The numerical model XBeach has previous been used to simulate the runup based on
infragravity waves (Palmsten and Splinter, 2016). If models are capable of accurately
estimating the runup, they can be used in cases, where geometry may reduce the ac-
curacy of empirical formulations or to simulate morphological evolution. The swash
motion is found to be dominated by infragravity waves in dissipative beaches condi-
tions (Ruessink et al., 1998), thus encouraging the development of models to simulate
these infragravity waves.
MIKE 21 is a numerical software program developed by DHI. It is based on the same
wave formulations as XBeach, and if the conﬁgurations are made in the same manner,
MIKE 21 is expected to simulate infragravity waves similar to XBeach. This is inves-
tigated by comparing the model results from MIKE 21 to empirical parameterizations
and to measurements performed in the swash zone and surf zone during this project.
The work presented in this thesis can be therefore divided into an experimental and

Chapter 2
Infragravity Waves
Infragravity (IG) waves were ﬁrst noticed as surf beats by Munk (1949) and later inter-
preted and formulated by radiation stresses by Longuet-Higgins and Stewart (1964).
IG waves are gravity waves with long periods (0.005 - 0.05 Hz) and are formed due to
modulation of the wave envelope.
Longuet-Higgins and Stewart (1964) hypothesised that the IG wave is released when
the incident waves are breaking. Hence the wave moves forward as a free wave. The
energy of the incident waves dissipates due to breaking and the infragravity wave
remain. Because of the relatively large wave length it will not become steep enough
to break, but instead it will continue up the beach as run-up, become reﬂected, and
propagate back into the sea as a free IG wave (leaky wave).
2.1
Signiﬁcance of IG waves
Since the discovery of IG waves, an extensive amount of study has been dedicated to
investigate the signiﬁcance of these waves. Because the waves travels at a far lower
frequency and have a much smaller amplitude than wind waves or swell, they are not
clearly visible, but hidden within the incoming wave train. They do, however, play
an important role in the nearshore coastal wave dynamics.
They contribute quite
signiﬁcantly to the beach uprush which is especially signiﬁcant for dissipative beaches
(Ruessink et al., 1998). The beach run-up is relevant for dune erosion but studies have
shown, that they also contributes to sediment transport and development of sand bars
(Aagaard and Greenwood (2008), de Bakker et al. (2016)). The low frequency waves
are relevant in many scenarios but this thesis only concerns the inﬂuence of infragravity
waves across the surf- and swash zone.

2.3
Observation of IG waves
Because of the diﬀerent generation mechanisms and locations, waves are in general
irregular and are typically statistically described. Description and assessment of a sea
state is typically carried out in either the time domain or frequency domain (Mangor
et al., 2017). In an irregular wave train the individual waves are often deﬁned by zero
down-crossing. Since the period of an IG wave is long compared with incident waves,
many zero crossing occurs during one period which makes it impractical to study in the
time domain. The long waves are much easier observed from analysis in the frequency
domain.
An irregular time series can be divided into frequency based sinusoidal components
performed by Fourier analysis. The surface elevation is in that way represented as a
superposition of diﬀerent waves with diﬀerent amplitudes, frequencies and directions
illustrated through a wave spectra. For further reading consult Appendix A.
As previously explained the bi-chromatic wave is made up of two diﬀerent frequencies
but identical amplitudes. A Fourier analysis of such signal will provide a spectrum
with energy located at two frequencies corresponding to the frequencies of the two
waves. Fourier and spectrum analysis will be used as a tool to investigate the energy
at diﬀerent frequencies in surface elevation time series later in this project.

Chapter 3
Numerical Model
MIKE 21 is a tool for coastal modelling developed by DHI. The model consist of diﬀer-
ent modules that separately deals with diﬀerent coastal and estuarine processes. The
spectral wave (SW) module, the hydrodynamic (HD) module and the sand transport
(ST) module are the three modules which are relevant for the coastal processes related
to infragravity waves. This thesis considers the interaction between the SW and HD
module alone, thus the ST module is therefore not included in the analysis. It should
be noticed, that it is not possible to investigate the morphological evolution without
applying the ST module.

To aid in the understanding of how the interaction between the HD- and SW modules
have been used to simulate IG waves, some fundamental theory behind the modules
will be reviewed. Only the part of the modules, which are relevant for the simulations
carried out in this thesis will be reviewed.

3.1.2
SW module
The SW module is a phase averaged wave model which essentially solves the wave
action conservation equation. The model simulates the growth, decay and transfor-
mation of wind generated waves and swell in oﬀshore and coastal areas and can be
applied with two diﬀerent conﬁgurations.
 Directional decoupled parametric formulation
 Fully spectral formulation
The directional decoupled parametric formulation is a parameterization of the wave
action conservation equation made in the frequency domain by introducing the zeroth
and ﬁrst order moment of the variance spectrum. The fully spectral formulation in-
cludes the direction-frequency wave action spectrum as dependent variable.
In general, phase-averaged models are advantageous at slowly varying sea states, how-
ever, should the phase averaged properties vary rapidly (order of a few wave lengths),
it is necessary to use a phase resolving model (Young, 1999). The numerical simulation
in this thesis will treat rapidly varying phase properties for the phase averaged model.
This is explained in detail later in this chapter.

3.2.3
Coupled model
The interaction between the SW-, and HD-module is the very thing which the ’IG wave
model’ is based upon. When both modules are switched on in the coupled model, fol-
lowing three interaction can be speciﬁed in the HD,- and SW-module respectively:
 Wave Radiation: Wave radiation from SW simulation
 Water level condition: Water level variation from HD simulation.
 Time: Time step interval
This implies, that the radiation stresses in the NSWE given in Eq. (3.2) and Eq. (3.3)
are constantly updated by the SW simulation. This is usually used to account for the
set-up and set-down related to the sea state simulated in the SW-module. However,
the boundary in the model is formulated as a constantly changing energy level designed
to describe the energy level in a wave group (this will be more carefully explained later
in this chapter). The idea with the ’IG wave model’ is to use the change in radiation
stresses from the SW module to formulate the change in the water level in the HD-
module.
By including the water level variation in the SW-module from the HD-module, it ac-
counts for the propagation of the IG wave in the SW simulation. Because the water
level variation is fairly small this coupling is not expected to be equally important as
to the wave radiation coupling.
The last important feature is the time step interval. This speciﬁes the frequency of
which the diﬀerent modules should synchronize with each other. This has to be fairly
low to proper resolve the surface elevation in the HD-module from the Hs in the SW-
module. The sensitivity is discussed later in this section.

The Hilbert transformation returns a complex helical sequence from a real data se-

3.3
Discussion
The interesting part of the described model is how the SW-module is used to describe
a constant changing signiﬁcant wave height. This is quite contradictory taken into
consideration that the signiﬁcant wave height is an expression for a sea state within a
time frame. It is thus a bit strange to have a signiﬁcant wave height that varies during
just one wave period. However, the signiﬁcant wave height is often expressed through
an energy spectrum, and thus related to the energy of the waves. It makes sense, that
this energy is allowed to modulate. The model uses the envelope of the waves, hence
the energy of the wave group, and calculates the phase averaged parameters for each
time step. Instead if using an averaged seastate condition, Hm0, to describe the wave
energy, the envelope of the incoming wave series is used instead. It can therefore be
considered a phase averaged model describing a phase resolved wave energy. This is
very eﬃcient since the model is much faster to estimate the surface elevation than a
full 3D phase resolving model.
By interpreting the surface elevation output for three identical boundary condition in
the cross-shore direction, but diﬀerent coupling frequency, it can be seen, that the sur-
face elevation depends on the coupling frequency between the HD- and SW- module.

coupling frequencies, 1, 10 and 100 Hz respectively, are illustrated in Figure 3.3.

Numerical Estimation of Runup
Numerous hydrodynamic and morphological processes occurs in the surf zone which
complicates the estimation of runup. The runup is often expressed as a 2% exceedence
value of runup, R2. This means, that only 2 % of the runup exceeds this values. A
comprehensive analysis of R2 has been conducted by Stockdon et al. (2006), where
measurements from 10 diﬀerent ﬁeld experiments have been used to formulate a pa-
rameterization of the R2 value. In their analysis, the runup, R(y, ti), is deﬁned as the
set of discrete water level maxima measured at the foreshore of the beach with respect
to still water level (SWL). It is the combination of the setup and the time varying
vertical ﬂuctuation about the setup that makes up the runup values, R. This chapter
presents a comparative analysis between the empirical parameterization of runup and
runup values extracted from MIKE 21 simulations. The same deﬁnition of R2 is there-
fore applied to the numerical model results.
The literature review performed by Stockdon et al. (2006) indicates, that the runup
depends on the slope and the deep water wave parameters H, T and L0. Based on
their studies they proposed a general relationship for the extreme 2% runup elevation
for any given conditions:

Chapter 5
Field Measurements
A ﬁeld experiment has been executed between 21th and 25th of April 2017 at the North
Coast of Zealand by Asger Bendix Hansen and Myself. The experiment is performed in
order to investigate the coastal hydrodynamics with respect to wave transformation, IG
waves and the runup. Previous chapter showed, that the ’IG wave model’ is capable
of predicting runup values and the results from this experiment are used to obtain
further insight in the accuracy of the model. Based on interest of gaining experience in
the execution and processing of ﬁeld measurements, the model is not compared with
previous conducted experiments. The experiment furthermore constitutes a signiﬁcant
part in the validation of the equipment, which Asger has developed during his phd.
study. The mutual beneﬁts associated with the experiment is the main reason why it
has been carried out.
5.1
Study Site
As previous mentioned the IG waves are related to the incident waves, thus large in-
cident waves will magnify the IG waves. Various of simulations in MIKE 21 have also
conﬁrmed that the low frequency energy is dependent on the wave climate. The study
site should therefore be located in an area which is subjected to large waves in order
to enhance the magnitude of the IG waves.
The wind in Denmark is in general dominated by western winds (Cappelen, 2009).
The West Coast of Jutland is therefore often exposed to a vigorous wave climate, thus
a good location to carry out the experiment. However, the West Coast is limited by
the lack of calm conditions, which are needed to safely install the equipment and to
perform bed level measurements. Because of that and transport time, the experiment
is preferably carried out close to Copenhagen. The west coast of Jutland will, however,
be a great future candidate to test the equipment and collect surf zone measurements

LiDAR
Video imaging has been used to measure the swash motion for many years but the
method is not very feasible in low light conditions and continuously requires mor-
phological updates in order to accurately determine the runup. Recent studies have
acknowledged the use of laser technology to analyse the beach topology and runup
during storm conditions (Almeida et al., 2013). By using a LiDAR (light detection
and ranging) scanner to detect the swash motion, the experiment can be carried out
any time of the day, and by ﬁxating the scanner the constant need for topographical
update is absence.

at distinct peaks, but are spread over several peaks. These peaks are expected to be
related to the phenomenon bore merging, which is basically when one bore catches
another bore and merge together in the inner surf zone. Tissier et al. (2017) suggested
that the wave envelope modulation is expected to enhance the diﬀerence in the celeri-
ties between the waves located at the infragravity crest and trough, thus contributing
to the bore merging. Based on that the bore merging should be visible two times dur-
ing an infragravity wave period - once during the crest and once during the trough. For
both time series there are peaks around 20 s (approximately half the IG wave period),
which may be due to the acceleration and de-acceleration of the incoming waves from
the IG waves. Furthermore the study sites contained two sandbanks, thus the waves
breaks at diﬀerent locations contributing to the bore merging. Additionally (S´en´echal

5.4.2
Bathymetry
As previous mentioned the LiDAR data can only be used to detect the bed level if
the bed is not submerged in water. This is approximately 8 and 11 m oﬀshore of the
LiDAR tower for TS21 and TS23 respectively. As seen on Figure 5.15 and Figure 5.16
the LiDAR scanner can detect wave fronts up to 50 m oﬀshore of the tower. By eval-
uating a wave front propagating towards shore it is possible to determine the celerity
at diﬀerent locations. This can be converted to a water depth from the simple shallow
water expression c = √gh. However, Matsuba et al. (2017) used the linear dispersion
relation to evaluate the submarine bathymetry by extracting wave crest lines from
aerial photos. They found, that the error in shallow water regions was 0.3 m at most.
This is considered to be accurately enough compared to not having any bathymetry
data at all. They did furthermore comment, that they had some issues with estimating
the wave fronts in the run-up area, but since the LiDAR provides a clear picture of the
incoming wave front, their method is used to estimate the bed level within the range
of the detected wave front from the LiDAR measurements.

Discussion
In this chapter the results from Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 will be discussed with respect
to the validity of the results and the uncertainties associated with the performed ﬁeld
experiment. The discussion is divided into topics related to the hydrodynamics and
topics related to the bathymetry.
7.1
Hydrodynamics
7.1.1
Coast Sensor
One sensor managed to perform measurements of the surface elevation but without
associated time. Since this sensor was the only one which has logged during the ex-
periment, the time stamp was attempted restored by comparing the entire time series
with buoy- and MWL measurements. The alignment is based entirely on a visual com-
parison, hence the time stamp is not exactly correct. The measurements can therefore
not be used to compare the wave climate in a very short timespan. However, since
the concerned comparison is based on time series, which both exceeds 80 minutes in
duration, the error related to the time oﬀset is diminished.
Figure 7.1 indicates that the wave period is increasing during TS21. The peak period
in the coast sensor in that time series is 0.3 second less that the buoy. It may be
related partly to the fact that the time series is assumed to be concurrent and that the
waves detected at the buoy will not have reached the sensor yet. Since the wave period
is increasing in the time span, the larger periods at the buoy will not have reach the
coast sensor. It may also imply that the assigned time stamp is delayed up to almost
an hour. This can not be known for sure, but the reader should bare in mind that this
oﬀset may be present.

7.1.2
Swash Lens Extraction
The swash lens has been extracted both from the model and from the LiDAR mea-
surements. The swash position in the model is based entirely on a ﬂood versus dry
consideration. If a cell is empty it is land and if not, it is water. Since the mesh only
consisted of one element in the longshore diretion, there was no challenges associated
with the extracting swash position from the ’IG wave model’.
The swash extraction from the LiDAR measurements was not as straight forward. The
method used to extract the swash motion involved specifying a threshold to ensure that
the swash position always intersected this threshold. It is advantageous in terms of
full automatic determination but the method will reduce the maximum uprush and
downrush. The threshold was set to 4 cm. This implies, that the uprush is under-
estimated by a maximum of 4 cm. This will most of the time be smaller than the
threshold because the bed is based on the 1 % lowest value during the concerned time
series. Morphological evolution requires, that this method is used on short time series
(depending on the rate of bed level change) to ensure, that changes in the topography
are accounted for.
The main advantages of the LiDAR is that it is quickly installed and can measure
during day and night. The disadvantages is that the scaﬀold tower becomes vulnerable

7.1.4
Power Spectrum’s
Power spectrum’s have extensively been used to examine the surface wave variance
contained at diﬀerent frequencies for both the experiment and the numerical model.
The experimental spectrum’s showed, that furthest oﬀshore the spectrum is dominated
by short period waves, but the energy shifts to longer periods as the wave progresses
towards shore. Furthermore, the incident wave energy is present in every spectrum’s
indicating, that the runup in not entirely based on infragravity energy. This is an
important ﬁnding in regards to the ’IG wave model’. The model is based entirely on
wave envelope modulations, thus excluding the incident wave energy from the model,
which may be a contributing factor to why R2,fluc is slightly smaller in the model
results. On the other hand, the small diﬀerence indicate the most of the runup is based
on infragravity waves. The diﬀerence in the swash characteristics between the model
and the experiment is aﬀected by the previous discussed obliquity, which complicate a
direct comparison between the model and the experiment.

Bathymetry
7.2.1
Vertical Alignment
Malfunction in the equipment used to perform the bathymetry survey has lead to
a quite comprehensive analysis in order to estimate the bathymetry during the ﬁeld
experiment. The procedure of aligning the survey with the beach proﬁle measured by
the LiDAR gives a good estimate of the proﬁle but it is still an approximation based
on MWL, satellite images and visual comparison. The error related to the alignment
is expected to be of less signiﬁcance compared to the error related to the longshore
variability and the morphological change between the experiment and the survey.

7.2.2
Bed Estimation from Dispersion Relation
Observation of the swash zone during the experiment did not match the very shallow
water over the ﬁrst sandbar. An estimation of the bed level from the dispersion relation
did increase the water depth and maintained the indication of a sandbar similar to the
satellite images. The bed level is based on only three wave fronts which questions the
reliability of the estimated water depth, however, the three wave fronts produced very
similar results. Furthermore, the numerical model results indicated better correlation
with the experimental results, when the estimated proﬁle was used.
7.2.3
Uniformity
Besides assuming that the wave approach the coast perpendicular, the coastal proﬁle
were assumed to be uniform in the alongshore direction. This assumption was made
to keep the simulation as idealised as possible and reduce the computational time by
considering a narrow strip of the coast. This has signiﬁcantly increased the amount of
model runs in the investigation of the model. Based on variability of three extracted
proﬁles from the survey the assumption is considered to be valid for the study site.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
By comparing the surface elevation in the HD-module for three identical bathyme-
tries and boundary conditions but diﬀerent coupling frequencies between the SW- and
HD-module it can be concluded, that the surface elevation becomes more smooth with
increasing coupling frequency. The study showed, that at least 10 Hz is needed to
remove the oscillations however this may be increases even further without compro-
mising the computational time signiﬁcantly.
144 simulations with diﬀerent boundaries and beach slopes have been compared to the
empirical parameterization from Stockdon et al. (2006). The study showed, that the
R2 values from the model is scattered around the empirical formulation with r2 = 0.84.
Based on this correlation it can be concluded, that the model is capable of predicting
runup values by neglecting incident waves and only simulate the modulation of the
wave envelope.
The swash motion has been measured by a LiDAR scanner mounted at the top of a
scaﬀold tower. Based on the analysis of the measurements it can be concluded that
the inﬂuence wind is a signiﬁcant factor in terms of the accuracy of the swash position.
The maximum error in the swash motion is less than 4 cm.
From the numerical simulation of the ﬁeld experiment it can be concluded, that changes
in the bathymetry induces signiﬁcantly changes in the peak frequencies.
However,
based on the swash energies and runup ﬂuctuations the model produces quite similar
results for both applied bathymetries.
Spectrum analysis has shown, that the IG wave increases towards shore and contributes
signiﬁcantly to the swash motion. The analysis did furthermore indicate, that energy
at incident frequencies are present in the swash motion.

CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
Overall it can be concluded, that Hilbert transformation of an oﬀshore surface elevation
time series can be used as boundary condition to model the surface elevation change
in the HD-module for perpendicular waves. The analysis has shown, that the surface
elevation is modulating with periods close to the measured infragravity period from
the experiment and produces similar swash ﬂuctuation R2,fluc.
8.1
Recommendations for Future Work
The investigation in this thesis has shed light on validity of the IG wave model for
perpendicular waves. This is a signiﬁcant step towards modelling the inﬂuence of IG
waves with respect to sediment transport. The model need further investigation for
perpendicular waves in order to investigate why the peak frequencies are sensitive to
bed level changes.
Further work is required to investigate the behaviour of the model for waves approach-
ing at an oblique angle. The coast sensors used in ﬁeld experiment are quite cheap
and by deploying multiple sensors in a 2-D study area, the behaviour of oblique waves
can be measured and used to calibrate a 2-dimensional model. This can furthermore
be used to investigate the eﬀect of a longshore variability in the bathymetry. The
coast sensors contain the ability of measuring morphological development which is
valuable knowledge in terms of calibrating the sediment transport.
By conducting
several experiments in diﬀerent wave climates it may be possible to develop a general
model conﬁguration which can be used to describe the morphological evolution based
on infragravity waves.
