Summary
Understanding the methodologies behind the production of numerical wind atlases
(NWA) is essential to exploit their potential as planning tools. Their ability to esti-
mate wind resources in a fast and robust manner is limited by a number of reasons,
including their resolution and the variable error of their predictions. Such issues are
related to the numerical simulations used in the production of a NWA, which can be
outputs from reanalyses or mesoscale models. These coarse simulations do not cap-
ture microscale features of wind flows, which are essential to predict wind resources
at specific sites.
Numerical simulations are corrected with the wind atlas method embedded in the
Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program (WAsP). This is called the numerical
wind atlas method (NWAM). To account for the microscale effects caused by the ter-
rain, WAsP requires topographic maps. Maps can be derived from multiple sources,
but some lead to more reliable predictions than others. An evaluation of the NWAM
with multiple inputs was carried out, aiming to identify the requirements that lead
to the most accurate and consistent predictions.
Six sites in Northern Europe with at least 2 years of observational records were
used: 2 offshore and 4 onshore. For each site, a total of 8 numerical simulations
from ERA5 reanalysis and the WRF mesoscale model were used.
Multiple maps
were available at each site. Predicted and observed wind climates were compared
through their overall mean wind speeds and power densities. Vertical wind speed
profiles were compared visually and using the rotor-equivalent wind speed (REWS).
The REWS-based power density was introduced as another metric. For each metric,
all the prediction errors were calculated and the mean average error (MAE) was used
to quantify uncertainty.
For onshore sites, it was found that selecting the best map reduces the error in
the NWAM by more than 50%. The maps that consistently led to the smallest errors
were derived from GlobCover2009. At 3 sites, NWAM predictions using simulations
from ERA5 at 100 m height result in the lowest errors and show the best resemblance
to the observed wind profiles. However, at the fourth site it caused 67% power den-
sity overpredictions. Such variability in the errors do not occur when using WRF
simulations. The NWAM with WRF simulations at 150 m height leads to the lowest
MAE: 2.5%.
==PAGE:4==

Summary
For offshore sites, interpolations from ERA5 and WRF simulations at different
heights resemble wind profiles better than the NWAM. The NWAM errors are min-
imized when using simulations from WRF at heights below 100 m. Considering all
results from this study, the use of WRF simulations at 100 m height is recommended
for the production of a NWA including offshore and onshore sites.

Introduction
A wind atlas is a collection of statistics representing the wind climatology of a region.
From an energy-systems perspective, wind atlases are important tools to be consid-
ered by decision-makers and project developers. They can be used to estimate the
total potential of a country for wind power production, which is essential to provide
an outlook of the future energy mix [1]. They can also lead to the identification of
areas for wind measurement campaigns and the subsequent installation of new wind
capacity and power transmission lines [2, 3].
The wind atlas method is a resource assessment technique used in the production
of wind atlases [4, 5, 6]. It is originally based on microscale models and observational
data. This technique is applied through the Wind Atlas Analysis and Application
Program (WAsP), which is a software developed and maintained by DTU Wind En-
ergy. The method and the software are also used in the planning process of upcoming
wind farms [7, 8, 9].
Another alternative to obtain regional wind climatologies is through numerical
weather prediction (NWP) models. Their simulations can be more or less accurate,
but they do not account for microscale effects at a site. Therefore, the sole use of a
NWP model for registering long-term wind climates can lead to large errors [1, 10].
However, outputs from NWP models can be coupled with WAsP. Substituting obser-
vations in the wind atlas method with simulations from NWP models has enabled
the production of numerical wind atlases (NWA) [11]. The development of such tools
has evolved for almost 20 years [6, 12, 13, 14]. However, they still present uncertainty
issues due to the variability in the quality of their predictions across different kinds
of terrains [1].
In a wind-farm-development context, a 1% uncertainty in mean wind speed can
result in a 3% uncertainty in mean power output [1, 8, 9]. This means that 5% in-
accuracy in wind speed estimations can lead up to 15% error in revenue estimations.
The construction of wind farms by utilities and other project developers is subject to
their economical feasibility, which is limited by their access to financial support. In
fact, the lack of rigor when assessing wind resources leads to inaccuracies that cannot
be tolerated by banks and financing institutions [8, 9, 15].

1 Introduction
Given the importance of reducing uncertainty in wind resource assessment tools,
international efforts to establish improved and verified methodologies are being car-
ried out [1, 16, 17]. The improvement of NWAs requires the effective understanding
of the methodologies involved in their production, as well as their limitations. In line

Theoretical Framework
Wind resource assessment demands a thorough understanding of the atmosphere and
the integration of numerous concepts, models and tools. The ones used for this project
are presented below. As of now, there is no international standard for wind resource
assessment, but efforts to elaborate one are being conducted [17, 8, 18].
The troposphere is the lowest part of the atmosphere, where weather events take
place. It contains about 75% of the atmosphere’s air and almost all its water vapour.
Near the Equator, the troposphere’s height is approximately 18 km above the Earth’s
surface, while at the poles it can be as low as 7 km [19]. Past and future conditions
in the troposphere can be estimated and prognosed by using numerical weather pre-
diction (NWP) models, which are described in Section 2.1.
The atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) is the lowest part of the troposphere,
where the motion of air is influenced by the Earth’s surface [19]. It is a turbulent
zone with mixing air due to the heat and momentum fluxes from the surface. De-
pending on the stratification, the ABL can generally be classified into stable, unstable,
or neutral. The stratification also influences the height of the ABL, which can vary
from tens of meters to several kilometers above the ground. Section 2.2 explains how
topographic maps can describe the Earth’s surface, which is essential to understand
the behavior of the ABL.
The surface layer is the atmosphere adjacent to the ground, where turbulent fluxes
of heat and momentum are nearly constant with height. It accounts for approximately
10% of the ABL[20, 21]. Within the surface layer, the vertical profile of the wind can
be described by the logarithmic wind profile. The wind atlas method uses the logarith-
mic profile and other models to derive regional wind climates from local observations.
Regional wind climates can be downscaled to estimate wind speed distributions at
specific sites. The wind atlas method is explained in detail in Section 2.3.
The wind industry requires the estimation of wind speeds at multiple heights. The
power output of a wind turbine has traditionally been calculated using the mean wind
speed at the rotor’s hub height. The concept of a rotor-equivalent wind speed (REWS)
has been recently introduced to better account for wind speed and direction variations
across the whole rotor. The REWS has been also proposed to as an indicator to
evaluate wind resource assessment techniques [16]. The calculation of the REWS is
explained in Section 2.4.

2.1
Numerical weather prediction models
The atmospheric surface layer has been described since the 19th century, when ob-
served atmospheric pressures at ground level were used for drawing weather charts
[22]. In the early 20th century it was recognized that upper-air and large-scale atmo-
spheric circulation are closely related to weather changes. To predict such changes,
Lewis F. Richardson suggested the numerical integration of the equations that govern
atmospheric circulation [22]. He traced an equally-spaced grid over central Europe
and tried to forecast pressure, air density and wind velocities at five vertical levels
for the 6 hours following his original records. The original grid is shown in figure 2.1.
.
Figure 2.1: Horizontal grid proposed by Richardson. Predictions for air pressure
(P) and momentum (M) were attempted alternately for the center of each grid cell.
Taken from Kimura. [22].
Richardson’s attempt was unsuccessful, but it set the basis for the first NWP
performed in 1950 in Princeton, USA [23]. The Electronic Numerical Integrator and
Computer (ENIAC) enabled the successful prediction of a 24-hour pressure change
at the middle level of the troposphere [22]. According to Smagorjnsky [23], the first
36-hour forecast was delivered in April 18, 1955. It required a three-level model, along
with an operational system for receiving, processing and disseminating data. During
the following years, major developments of numerical integration techniques, along
with the identification of energy sources and sinks, enabled not just weather but also
climate forecasting. Nowadays, global climate models consider many processes in the
troposphere beyond atmospheric circulation. These include ocean circulations, land-
sea interactions, ice melting events, major nutrient’s cycles and other phenomena in
the biosphere [24].

2.1.1
Technicalities
The modeling process starts by discretizing the geographical space for which the
forecast will be issued, i.e. defining land cover characteristics, elevation, underground
porosity, etc. Figure 2.2 shows an example visualization of the 3-D gridboxes used for
modern NWP models. To ease the solution of meteorological equations, the vertical
levels and upper boundaries are defined by pressure and not by height. Parametriza-
tion schemes are needed to approximate solutions to physical processes that are not
completely understood, have no analytical representations, or simply cannot be re-
solved at the selected grid spacing. The increasing nonlinearity in the interaction
of more complex processes can yield results that are apparently correct but for the
wrong reasons [23].
Therefore, understanding the discretization of space and the
parametrization of physical processes at sub-grid scales, as well as isolating their
contributions to final outcomes, is crucial for the continuous improvement of NWP
models.
.
Figure 2.2: Visualization of gridboxes for a NWP model. Taken from Hahmann et
al. [12].
Depending on the relevant parameters to study and the temporal and spatial
scale of the model, different approximations can be used for discretizing the differen-
tial terms in the atmospheric equations of motion. These approximations are called
dynamic solvers and are divided into finite difference methods and spectral methods.
The former ones expand the differential terms into polynomial series in the temporal
and spatial domains; while the latter ones do so in the frequency domain.
Limited area models (LAM) are typically solved with finite difference methods.
LAMs commonly use staggered quasi-regular grids with lateral boundaries [24]. This
means that, unlike Richardson’s original attempt, vector quantities are not calculated
at the center of the grid, but instead at vertexes or edges, as shown in figure 2.3. The
term quasi-regular is used because depending on the projection over which the three-
dimensional grid is defined, the points may not be equally spaced.

.
Figure 2.3: Example of Arakawa-C staggered single-level, horizontal grid. Variables
calculated at the center points are called mass fields q, e.g. temperature, humidity
and pressure. Vector quantities such as wind speeds u, v are called momentum fields.
Vectors quantities at mass points are obtained through interpolation. Taken from
Warner [24].
Global NWP models typically use spectral methods. These require spectral spaces
instead of staggered grids [25, 26]. Spectral methods are good for evaluating phenom-
ena with large associated energy, which is transferred into lower scales. The effective
resolution of a NWP model is the finest scale at which the model can represent the
energy distribution in the atmosphere. At scales below the effective resolution, NWP
models simulate larger energy dissipation than what the atmosphere really experi-
ences [27]. The effective resolution varies from model to model, and shouldn’t be
confused with the grid resolution. In figure 2.4 the atmospheric scales of motion are
shown together with the typical grid resolution of the corresponding NWP models in
a wind-energy-application framework.
Even though the advancements in computing capacity keep growing at an expo-
nential rate, NWP models are still limited at describing the physical systems at every
timestep. A common limitation is the extreme sensitivity to the initial conditions,
known as atmospheric chaos [22, 24, 25]. For a proper initialization of the model, the
grid must faithfully represent the current conditions of the atmosphere. There are
two main methods to achieve such resemblance: static and dynamic initialization [24].
Static initialization, also called data analysis, consists of interpolating observa-
tions into the model grid. These observations come from a number of sources such
as satellites, weather balloon radiosondes, near-surface weather stations, aircrafts,
radars, lidars, etc. All observations should be adjusted to make them consistent with
the model equations, as described later.
==PAGE:22==

.

For dynamic initialization, not all the observations are considered. Instead, some
variables are set to zero and the model itself is used for calculating the initial condi-
tions. This requires a so-called spin-up period, for which the forecasts are not useful
because the atmospheric conditions represented in the model are not balanced yet.
Once the model is said to be dynamically consistent, it can be used for forecasting.
Due to the availability of data and the imminent need for adjustments to fit them in
the model, the limits between static and dynamic initialization are blurry [24].
The data analysis process defined above can be performed at multiple time steps
to incorporate observations distributed in time.
In this case, the interpolation of
observations into the model grid is called a 4-dimensional data assimilation (FDDA).
In practical terms, once an observation is assimilated, the state of the atmosphere
is upgraded from a background state to an analysis state. The analysis state serves
as the initial condition for a subsequent forecast [25]. The assimilation is said to be
sequential if the analysis state matches the atmosphere’s chronological evolution. A
non-sequential FDDA is able to move back and forth in time to adjust previous time
step’s analyses as new observations become available. Additionally, the assimilation
can be continuous or intermittent depending on whether the observations are inserted
at the times they are made, or whether they become available to the model by batches
[24]. The combination of these assimilation schemes is depicted in figure 2.5.
In data assimilation systems, observations are rarely inserted directly into the
model as output variables.
Instead, different methods are used for adjusting the
ingested observations into the grid, allowing the models to calculate their own outputs
[25]. These methods are:
• Nudging or Newtonial relaxation, which adds non-physical terms to the model
prognostic equations, hence constraining the analysis towards observations.
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Numerical weather prediction models
9
• Variational assimilation, which globally minimizes a cost function that includes
the error between the analysis and the observations. Depending on whether it
is done intermittently or continuously, it is referred to as 3D-Var and 4D-Var,
respectively.
• Ensemble Kalman filters (EKF), which correct the background sequentially and
intermittently based on the ensemble of various simulations. Each simulation is
perturbed by unique initial and lateral boundary conditions. Thus, an EKF can
be considered a random sample of the model’s state probability distribution.
• Hybrid methods, which are a combination of the previous three.
.

2.1.2
Meteorological reanalyses
The same data assimilation systems used for providing initial conditions for opera-
tional forecasts are also used to generate long-term numerical records of the atmo-
sphere’s state [24]. By incorporating observations to the models, FDDA systems allow
the reconstruction of past atmospheric conditions. Each reconstruction is called an
analysis, and the collection of past analyses obtained by the same FDDA system is
known as reanalysis. Reanalyses can cover time spans as large as multiple decades,
and the time step between each analysis can be as short as some hours [30].
A global climate reanalysis is produced for all locations on a spectral space model
of the Earth; and contains estimates of a number of variables such as rainfall, soil
moisture content, sea-surface temperature, air temperature, pressure and wind at dif-
ferent altitudes. According to Dee et al. [30], in the production of a global reanalysis,
between 7 and 9 million observations are ingested at each time step. Even though
the FDDA system producing the reanalysis remains unchanged, the sources of the
ingested observations will most-likely vary along the reanalysis period, which can in-
troduce artificial variability and trends. Therefore, reanalysis outputs should be used
with caution [30, 31].
Due to the large amounts of stored data, reanalyses products can take up to several
petabytes of space. Accessing reanalysis data sets is typically done through national
and regional weather forecasting agencies, as well as other research and educational
institutions, e.g:
• European Center for Medium-Range Weather Forecasting (ECMWF)
• USA National Center for Environmental Prediction (NCEP)
• USA National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)
• USA National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR)
• USA National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)
• Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences in the University
of Colorado at Boulder (CIRES)
• Japanese Meteorological Agency (JMA)

spectral spaces into Gaussian grids or regularly spaced lat-lon grids [26]. As a con-
sequence, gridboxes may be irregularly sized on a flat projection. Some of the most

2.1.2.1
ERA5
ERA5 is the fifth-generation reanalysis product from the ECMWF. Once it is com-
pleted, its reanalysis period will extend from 1950 to the mid-2020s [34].
It will
replace the older ERA-Interim, which is scheduled to stop its production on August
the 31st, 2019, after having produced a 40-year long data set. According to its official
documentation [35], ERA5 is produced using the 4D-VAR data assimilation system
employed by the CY41R2 operational forecast model, within ECMWF’s Integrated
Forecast System (IFS). “High resolution” analyses and forecasts (HRES) are available
hourly on a spectral space model of Earth equivalent to a Gaussian grid with 31 km
latitudinal gridbox length at the Equator. Ensembles of forecasts at 3-hourly time
steps on a 62 km resolution grid are also available under the name of EDA.
The atmospheric model in the IFS has 137 model levels and is coupled to a soil
model and an ocean wave model. ERA5 data are available on all model levels, as
well as 37 interpolated pressure levels, 16 potential temperature levels, 1 potential
vorticity level, and 1 surface level. The interpolated surface level data set is called
ERA5-sfc and contains 2-D parameters that can be classified as:
• Invariants: Input parameters related to the grid description, e.g. vegetation
covers, type of vegetation, geopotential, land-sea mask, etc.
• Instantaneous: Calculated parameters valid at the specified time stamp, e.g.
friction velocity, wind speed components at 10 and 100m height, forecasted
surface roughness, instantaneous surface heat fluxes, boundary layer height,
etc.
• Accumulations: Calculated parameters showing accumulation since the previous
timestamp, e.g. surface heat-fluxes, snowfall, evaporation, etc.
Within a wind resource assessment framework, reanalysis data can be used as an
input for mesoscale models, or directly in the wind atlas method described in Section
2.3. Due to their importance in such methodology, the calculation of two parameters
in ERA5 are of particular interest: geopotential and forecasted surface roughness.
2.1.2.2
Forecasted surface roughness
Forecasted surface roughness (FSR) is the calculated aerodynamic roughness length,
typically denoted as z0 and expressed in meters. It is a measure of the surface resis-
tance against the air flow. It’s scale is logarithmic so higher values cause significantly
slower near-surface wind speeds. In ERA5, FSR is obtained in two different ways,
depending on whether the grid point for its calculation represents a land section or
==PAGE:27==
2.1 Numerical weather prediction models
13
a sea section. This requires a previous classification of the gridbox, which is done by
the following procedure described thoroughly in the IFS official documentation [36]:
1. At the surface level, each gridbox is divided into 2-D tiles of 1km per side.
2. Each tile is classified into a type of biome using the Global Land Cover Char-
acterization (GLCC) data base, which is derived from Advanced Very High
Resolution Radiometer (AVHRR) observations during 1992 and 1993. There
are 20 types of biomes a tile can be classified into, as shown in table 2.2.
3. Every time step, snow accumulation is estimated for each tile. Accumulations
depend on the calculation of energy fluxes, which rely on numeric factors as-
signed to each biome.
4. According to the snow accumulation and the type of biome in the tiles, 8 pa-
rameters are calculated for the entire gridbox. These are measured as fractions
of the gridbox and may vary at every time step:
• bare soil fraction
• high vegetation fraction
• low vegetation fraction
• snow on bare ground fraction
• snow under high vegetation fraction
• sea ice fraction
• intercepted water fraction
• ocean and lakes fraction
5. The land-sea mask is calculated using the fractions above. The whole gridbox
is classified as a land section when the land-sea fraction is above 0.5. Below 0.5,
the whole gridbox will be seen by the model as a sea section.
Depending on the gridbox classification, there are two ways of determining each
time step’s FSR:
• Over land: The dominant biome is determined from the tiles and the roughness
length is fixed according to table 2.2.
• Over sea: The IFS model has multiple parametric equations to calculate FSR
over water together with 3-D wind speed components and other coefficients.
This is useful to couple the ocean model with the atmospheric model, reflecting
the energy exchanges between both media.

2.1.2.3
Geopotential and orography
Geopotential is the graviational potential energy of a unit mass relative to the mean
sea level. Dividing the geopotential at a point by Earth’s gravitational acceleration,
g=9.80665 m s−1, will show the point’s height in m asl. As such, the geopotential in
ERA5-sfc is directly linked to the height of the surface, which is also called orography
or elevation.
Each gridbox in ERA5-sfc has a constant geopotential, meaning that the surface
elevation within the gridbox is considered homogeneous. This unrealistic assumption
allows the model to capture phenomena in the synoptic scale, but leaves smaller scale
events unresolved. To arrive to the geopotential of a gridbox at the surface level, the
following procedure is executed [36]:
1. A grid with 30 arcsec resolution is defined over the globe. This is equivalent to
a separation between gridpoints of approximately 1 km at the Equator.
2. An elevation value is assigned to each gridpoint using five different data sets.
The selection on the data set depends on the location of each grid point, because
neither of them are available as global products.
==PAGE:29==
2.1 Numerical weather prediction models

3. A smoothing filter is applied to gridpoints every 5 km, except close to the poles
for which it is applied every 2 km. This leads to a grid with less resolution than
the original one.
4. The filtered grid is smoothed again by averaging the neighbouring gridpoints
until the target resolution of the model resolution is achieved, as visualized in
figure 2.6.
5. The grid-point orography is transformed to its equivalent spectral space, smoothed
in the frequency domain, and then transformed back to grid points.
6. Orography is multiplied by Earth’s graviational acceleration to get geopotential.

2.1.3
Mesoscale models
Fifth-Generation Penn State/NCAR Mesoscale Model
Mesoscale models are LAMs extended over domains of tens to a few hundreds
of kilometers. They can provide important insights to mesoscale weather systems
because their model grids have higher resolution than global models [39]. With the
advanced parametrization and filtering of sub-grid events in mesoscale models, their
effective resolution can be less than 7∆x, where ∆x is the spacing between gridpoints
[27]. According to Warner [24], there is a tendency to think that the effective resolu-
tion of a mesoscale model is 2∆x.
As any LAM, mesoscale models require the definition of the atmosphere’s state
at the domain’s edges, which are the so-called lateral boundary conditions. These
are generally updated during the simulated period, which is a process called “forcing”.
Forcing a mesoscale model can be done with data from global operational forecast-
ing models, reanalysis data sets, or even larger domains of the same or a different
mesoscale model [40]. Forcing a model that is nested into a larger one can be done
through one-way or two-way communication schemes, as visualized in figure 2.8. In
the former, it is the largest (so-called “parent”) domain that determines the boundary
conditions for the nested domain. In the latter, feedback from the nested domain sets
new boundary conditions for the parent domain. A two-way communication scheme
requires the model at both domains to be solved simultaneously.
Figure 2.8: Nested domains and communication schemes for forcing mesoscale mod-
els. Taken from Knievel [40].

decade with research as their main purpose. Their potential to decentralize real-time
weather prediction encouraged their rapid development and usage by numerous groups
all around the world. The increase in availability of high-performance workstations,
the accessibility of analyses and forecasts from major weather forecasting institutions,
and the sharing of models itself within the scientific community have been key to the
development of state-of-the-art mesoscale models [39]. The following is a list of well-
known mesoscale models, some of which have been used to generate open-access time
series [41]:

2.2
Topographic maps
A topographic map is the representation of an area by depicting the geographical
features of its surface. In other words, a topographic map refers to the description of
the terrain in terms of elevation, vegetation cover, water bodies, human constructions,
transport routes, and many other characteristics [51, 52].
To get a topographic map, a top perspective is uttermost useful. Therefore, in-
struments in satellites are nowadays the standard sources for getting high-resolution
topographic maps. Other remote sensing sources, such as aerial lidars, have also been
tried in recent years [53, 54]. Satellite images are by default stored as raster maps [55].
In raster maps, the space is divided into a grid and each gridcell or pixel is assigned
an individual value for the recorded feature. The size of the grid will impact the res-
olution of the map, leading to a trade-off between the quality of the description and
the size of the files. An alternative to this is to convert and store topographic maps in
vector formats. Instead of using pixels, vector maps represent space through points,
lines and polygons on a coordinates system. Among other benefits when working
with Geographical Information Systems (GIS), vector maps allow more geographical
accuracy than raster maps [55].
Without topographic maps, both NWP models and the wind atlas method would
not be possible. Both tools rely on two topographic characteristics: land cover ,which
is directly linked to the aerodynamic roughness length, and orography.
2.2.1
Orography maps
Elevation or orography is the height of the surface, typically relative to the mean sea
level. On two-dimensional maps, it is represented by closed lines that join points at
the same height. These lines are called contour lines and allow for the visualization
of the shape of ground reliefs, as shown in figure 2.10. The height difference between
contour lines determines the vertical resolution of an orography map. Since it is lines
and not surfaces that compose the map, the conversion between raster and vector
maps is simple.

shuttle Endeavour in the year 2000 [7, 56]. SRTM data for different regions have
been processed and published gradually. SRTM data have been the base for other
products, such as the European elevation model EU-DEM[57].

products contained no-data areas, including oceans, deserts and mountainous regions

Land cover and roughness maps
Roughness length is not a feature that can be mapped directly from measurements
because it is an integration constant. However, it is related to irregularities on the
terrain’s surface such as vegetation covers, water bodies, roads, and other features
that are captured in satellite-based land cover maps, as the one shown in figure 2.11.
Roughness length maps derived from lidar measurements follow a different principle
than the one described below. For such cases, Floors et al. [54] described the process
in detail.
Land cover or land-use maps are two-dimensional raster maps, which use image
classification techniques to assign a land cover class to each pixel [60]. Traditionally,

allows for the identification of shapes and sizes among groups of pixels, leading to

Roughness maps are obtained by assigning a roughness length to each land cover
class in a land-use map. The accuracy of a roughness map does not only depend on
the resolution of the land-use map, but also in the quality of the classification steps

classes may still present differences due to the time in which the original images were
captured. The way seasonal and inter-annual variability is handled in the production
of a map will certainly affect its usefulness.
Raster roughness maps can be transformed into vector maps by describing as lines
the boundaries between regions of pixels with the same roughness length. In this case
they are called roughness-change maps, but the name distinction tends to be ignored.

2.3
The wind atlas method
First described by Troen and Petersen with the publication of the European wind
atlas (EWA) in 1989 [4], the wind atlas method aims to describe the wind climatol-
ogy of a region and allow for the estimation of wind resources at specific sites. It is
based on the integration of various physical and statistical models for the meso and
microscale, and its practical execution is achieved through the use of a limited range
of software applications [5]. The application that resulted from the development of
the EWA is the Wind Atlas Analysis and Application Program (WAsP), which has
become an industry-standard software suite not just for wind resource assessment,
but also for siting and energy yield calculation of wind turbines and wind farms [7, 1,
8, 68].
The first part of the wind atlas method embedded in WAsP is the “analysis”, also
known as “generalization”. The purpose of the generalization is to describe the wind
climate on an area or region by removing site-specific effects on the local wind with
a microscale flow model that considers the influence of:
• effects from the orography
• effects of roughness and changes in roughness
• shelter from near-by obstacles.
Neither mesoscale variations of the flow nor microscale thermal effects are ac-
counted for in the method, so the generalized winds will be valid only over areas
with a similar mesoscale forcing and thermal influence [5, 69]. The generalized wind
climate can later be used to estimate the wind resource at specific locations and at
various heights. This is achieved by the second part of the wind atlas method: a
downscaling method called “application”, which is essentially an inverted generaliza-
tion. By adding the local effects of the topography using the same microscale flow
model, the downscaling step allows for long-term estimation of wind resource at a
specific location [70].
2.3.1
Generalization
The generalization and downscaling can be performed dynamically on a time-series of
wind speed and direction, i.e. for each individual 10-min, 30-min or 1-hour record, or
statistically, i.e. using wind speed and frequency distributions. The way atmospheric
stability is accounted for may also vary depending on the generalization method [50].
Even though they do share the same physical principles, both the statistical and dy-
namical method may yield different results [69]. Here the default statistical approach

Downscaling
The “application” or downscaling process uses the GWC to estimate wind resource
at a selected height, at a specific location within an area over which the GWC is
assumed to be valid.
.
Figure 2.15: Visualization of a EWC in WAsP 12.
Once the map for the area has been defined, all the Aw,sec and kw,sec for the new
site are obtained by logarithmic interpolation from the GWC, using the closest zstd
and z0,std to those describing the corresponding sector. For each sector, the inter-

generalization step. Then, using the same microscale flow model as in the generaliza-

used for getting the final Weibull parameters and the frequencies of each sector. From
these, new overall and sector-specific mean wind speeds and power densities can be
calculated for the new site. This is called a estimated wind climate (EWC) and is
shown in figure 2.15.

For the error of a EWC to be minimized with respect to the real wind climate, both

the surrounding area, needs to be similar[71]. Otherwise a correction based on the
quantification of the ruggedness, called ruggedness index (RIX),might be needed [74].
2.3.3
Extensive implementation
Traditionally, the information on the local wind comes from observations. In fact, the
EWA is an observational wind atlas (OWA), which collects data sets that were gen-
eralized using high-resolution terrain descriptions. However, with the advancement
of computational power, wind simulations started to be used for the same purposes.
This has allowed for the development of numerical wind atlases (NWA), which are
a collection of GWCs and EWCs at the grid points of numerical weather prediction
models (NWP). Depending on which NWP it is based, a NWA can cover extensions
that go from parts of a country to the whole world.
NWAs can be developed using data collected from reanalysis datasets or mesoscale
models. In such cases, the generalization is a vital step, whose main objective is the
removal of the influence of the coarse topographical inputs on the numerical simula-
tions because of their limited resolution. NWAs have been developed from mesoscale
simulations since 1994 [11]. A large number of them were based in the Karlsruhe
Atmospheric Mesoscale Model (KAMM), which has been substituted with the use
of the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model in recent years [49, 50, 14].
The state-of-the-art example of a NWA developed directly from reanalysis data is
the Global Wind Atlas (GWA) project [2]. As of version 2.0, the GWA uses WRF
between the reanalysis data and the wind atlas method.
In the GWA, a similar generalization method to the one described in section 2.3.1
is performed, but major changes were adopted. First, the use of the LINCOM flow
model instead of those in WAsP to calculate the topographical correction factors, as
demonstrated by Badger et al. [70]. Moreover, the data processing before the Weibull
fitting is not based on the modification of the bin boundaries within the wind speed
histograms, but in the central values of the bins. Also, turning changes between the
original and the generalized winds are dismissed. This assumption relies on the initial
definition of 12 azimuth sectors, so a deviation larger than 15◦would be needed for
the wind bins to be classified into a different sector; which according to Badger et al.
[2] rarely occurs. Additionally, some average heat fluxes and ABL heights were fixed

is expected to be published by September 2020 [17]. The first section of the 12th
part of the series, the “61400-12-1: Power performance measurements”, was reviewed
in 2017. Among other updates, the second version introduced a new definition for
wind speed: the rotor-equivalent wind speed (REWS) [80]. Under the context of the
NEWA, the REWS has been used as a relevant indicator to evaluate techniques that
couple mesoscale and microscale models for wind resource assessment and turbine
siting Sanz et al.
The REWS is the wind speed corresponding to the kinetic energy flux passing
through the total swept area of a wind turbine [81, 16]. It accounts for the verti-
cal wind shear and the changes of wind direction with height; i.e. the wind veer.
To calculate a generic REWS, no specific turbine model needs to be proposed, only
a hub-height and a rotor radius.

Objectives and Methods
The wind atlas method in WAsP can estimate historical wind resource at specific
sites by taking time series from numerical wind simulations as an input. This will be
called the numerical wind atlas method (NWAM). The accuracy of its predictions is
expected to be imperfect due to several factors:
• The coarseness of the terrain description used by NWP models and their limited
effective resolution.
This impacts the ability of the NWP model to capture
synoptic and mesoscale features of the flow.
• The capabilities of the linear flow models in WAsP beyond its original opera-
tional envelope. WAsP is challenged to get a regional wind climate by remov-
ing the unresolved effects in NWP models without removing their mesoscale
features.
• The ability of the linear flow models in WAsP to add microscale effects to a re-
gional wind climate when using a high-resolution map. Even if the performance
of the method has been long proved over simple terrain, it may be limited in
complex terrain, e.g. coastal, mountainous, steep or forested areas [2][49][1].
The main objective of this study is to quantify the total deviation incurred by
using the NWAM to estimate wind climates, as compared to those obtained directly
from observations. It is also the purpose to identify the requirements with which
the NWAM works best. Assessing its usefulness as opposed to other tools is also
envisioned.
To achieve the objectives, six sites in Northern Europe with at least 2 years of
observational records were used: 2 offshore and 4 onshore. The sites are introduced
in section 3.2. For each site, 8 different numerical time series were used as an input
for the NWAM: 2 series from ERA5 and 6 series from WRF simulations. This will
show whether the performance of the NWAM is improved by the use of mesoscale
simulations as opposed to reanalysis data. ERA5 and WRF time series are explained
in sections 3.6 and 3.7.
It may be argued that the evaluation of the NWAM should be done at the re-
gional level of the wind atlas, by comparing the numerical and observational GWCs
[13]. This would avoid the possible error introduced by interpolations in the down-
scaling stage of the wind atlas method. On the flip side, the complexity of comparing
GWCs, each composed by 25 wind speed distributions, is much higher. Selecting just
one distribution on a single generalized level seems rather arbitrary. To allow for a
==PAGE:54==

more understandable evaluation, the comparisons were done through an estimated vs.
observed scheme. The issue of interpolation in the downscaling stage is addressed by
replacing the standard roughness and heights in WAsP with a set of roughness and
heights tailored specifically for each site, as explained in section 3.1.
For downscaling GWCs obtained from numerical simulations, at least two differ-

the best map for downscaling the NWAM at each site was selected.
After downscaling a GWC, the correspondence of the observed and estimated

The error of predicted sector-wise
mean wind speeds was calculated for every height with available measurements. The
same was done for the predicted sector-frequency-weighted (so-called “overall”) mean

indicators was calculated as described in section 2.3.4. The MAE aims to provide
a comprehensive metric for each time series’ performance across the 6 sites, and it
allows for the comparison between this study and existent wind atlases. Due to their
relevance for the wind industry, only predictions at heights between 40 and 200 m
were considered.
Data from NWP models cannot be directly compared to observations due to the
discrepancy between heights at which they are available. To include the direct out-
puts from ERA5 and WRF in the evaluation, these were fitted into wind profiles as
explained in section 3.8. The procedure was also followed with the multiple NWAM

These metrics will show the accuracy of the different methods at estimating wind
resource for a hypothetical wind turbine defined in section 3.8.

3.1 WAsP setup

3.1
WAsP setup
The wind atlas method was executed using the PyWAsP library for Python, which
has the same generalization and downscaling capabilities as the WAsP software ap-
plication. PyWAsP also allowed for the agile retrieval of topography maps and air
densities from external databases. Air densities at each location were used to calcu-
late power densities as explained in section 2.3.1.
Roughness maps available in PyWAsP are based on land-use raster maps and built-
in land-use-to-roughness lookup tables. The maps used in this study were based on
CORINE CLC2012 and GlobCover2009, hereafter called CORINE and GlobCover.
Orography maps were obtained from SRTMv3, with a vertical resolution of 10 m.
Every map generated with PyWAsP is square-shaped, with the evaluation site placed
at the center.
The distance to the borders for each specific site was determined
by multiplying the tallest height with available measurements (in meters) times 115.
This is in line with WAsP best practices, allowing for the IBZ model to calculate
the appropriate speed-ups and turns due to orography and roughness changes in the
surroundings. In all maps, the roughness length of water areas was changed to 0.0002
m, which is required by PyWAsP to account for land-sea changes. Two examples
of maps obtained with PyWAsP are shown in figure 3.1. All maps are visualized
in appendix A.1. The sector-wise roughness changes considered by PyWAsP for the
calculation of each map’s upstream roughness, can be seen in appendix A.2.
The model type in PyWAsP was set to the default values of WAsP version 12. The
results in chapter 4 correspond to the model with default values for boundary layer
heights and heat flux values listed in section 2.3.1. Modifications to default values
were just used in the sensitivity analyses to verify the robustness of the method at
each site. For all cases, 12 wind direction azimuth sectors were used.
For each generalization, the standard heights zstd were modified according to the
site’s available real measurements, making sure that the GWC always included the
minimum and maximum heights with measured records. Similarly, the set of stan-
dard roughness z0,std in each GWC was changed to cover the same range as the 12
sector-wise upstream roughness ˆz0 given by the site’s downscaling map. Tailoring the
GWC reduces the interpolation errors throughout the methodology, thus achieving
more accurate predictions than with the default standard values. This enables a fair
comparison of predictions at the same site with different downscaling maps.
PyWAsP is still under development at DTU Wind Energy and will be made avail-
able alongside the rest of the WAsP suite once it reaches its version 1.0 [83]. Multiple
Python routines were developed for the collection and post-processing of PyWAsP
outputs, allowing for the visualizations and analyses presented further in this docu-
ment.

3.4
Best map selection
For this study, all numerical winds were generalized using the coarse-resolution maps
derived from their corresponding NWP model, as shown in sections 3.6 and 3.7. To
ensure the best approximation to observations, the selection of the appropriate high-
resolution downscaling map was needed. For all sites, the CORINE and GlobCover
roughness maps were available. Maps from an aerial lidar with 20 m horizontal reso-
lution were available at Høvsøre and Østerild. A fourth map for Høvsøre, referred to
as the ALDI map, was provided by Alfredo P. Díaz. The upstream roughness for each
sector in the ALDI map was derived by fitting a logarithmic profile to wind speed
observations at 40 and 10 m [85]. All maps are available in appendix A.1. The follow-
ing selection process only relies on observations, for it is independent from simulations.
When executing the wind atlas method over any map, there should be no differ-
ence between the observation used as an input, and the estimation obtained for the
same height. This is called self-prediction. An accurate self-prediction indicates that
the microscale effects removed by the generalization can be correctly added in the
downscaling step. Differences between estimations and predictions can be quantified
through their overall wind speeds and power densities. For each downscaling map,
the self-prediction errors for both indicators were calculated. To provide certainty to
the evaluation of the NWAM, maps leading to the highest self-prediction errors are
discarded. If no map leads to outstanding errors, all maps are kept for the next step.
The map should not influence the precision of the wind atlas method. A com-
patible map should lead to identical estimations at the same height, regardless the
observation used as input. Plotting indicators at different heights enables a simple
visual comparison between vertical profiles. Overall power density is the preferred
indicator because it relies on cubed wind speeds, making differences more noticeable.
The convergence of power density profiles estimated from different observations val-
idates the compatibility of a map for its use with the wind atlas method. The final
selection of the best map for each site is done visually, in favor of the one that shows

3.5
Sensitivity of the wind atlas method in WAsP
The accuracy of the wind atlas method is variable at each site, depending on the
specific terrain and atmospheric conditions. As explained in section 2.3.1, parameters
related to atmospheric stability can be modified in WAsP. To understand the impact
of such parameters in the wind atlas method estimations, a sensitivity analysis was
performed for every site. The following WAsP parameters were modified:
• Average heat flux over land. Default value: −40Wm−2
• Average heat flux over water. Default value:: −8Wm−2
• Factor on geostrophic height for stab envelope limiting. This WAsP coefficient
is proportional to the ABL height. Its default value, both over land and water,
is 1.
For each sensitivity analysis, WAsP parameters were independently modified by
+10%, -10%, -20% and -30%. Therefore, the values used for the sensitivity analysis
represent 110%, 90%, 80% and 70% of the default values. The sensitivity analyses
were repeated with each observation available as an input. The set of multi-input
sensitivity analyses was repeated using every possible downscaling map.
Sensitivity analyses with the same input, but estimating at different heights,
present different deviations from the base model. Similarly, same-height estimations
using the same inputs, had different deviations depending on the map used. All de-
viations from the base model were recorded. This resulted in a large set of values
describing the sensitivity range to each modified parameter. Figure 3.5 shows an
example of the sensitivity range for overall wind speeds at one site, considering only
the possible variations within one map. The sensitivity of each individual estimation
will fall within the two limiting lines, representing the maximum deviations from the
base model. The wind speed and power density sensitivity ranges for all sites are
condensed in section 4.7. The individual sensitivity ranges for each site are available
in appendix A.5.
A combinatorial analysis was also carried out. New sets of estimations were ob-
tained by modifying all parameters simultaneously, using all combinations within
the 70-110% range. This analysis pretends to give an insight about the extent to
which modifications on the atmospheric stability in WAsP can compensate for the
selection of a non-compatible map. Figure 3.6 shows an example of the estimated
profiles using all combinations with two different observations as input, overlaid by
the profiles obtained from the sensitivity analyses. Visualizations of the sensitivity
and combinatorial analyses for every map on each site can be appreciated in appendix
A.5.

4.7.2
Numerical wind atlas method
Results in this section consider errors for predictions between 40 and 200 m. This
includes the evaluation of 19 onshore and 14 offshore wind climates. The average
height for both is 97 m. The following analysis considers the usage of the best map
for each site. The CORINE map proved to be the best option for Høvsøre, while the
GlobCover map showed more compatibility for the remaining onshore sites.
The goodness of the NWAM depends on the simulations used as input. Simula-
tions at taller heights lead to less overall mean wind speed than simulations at lower
heights. As seen in figure 4.45, there is a large spread in the prediction’s accuracy
when using ERA5 inputs. Out of these, ERA5-100m shows the most consistent over-
all wind speed estimations, with half of the errors between -0.9% and 1.1%; and a
median of 0.2%. Such balance between over and underpredictions is not achieved
with any other simulation. However, using it for the production of a numerical wind
atlas may lead to excellent predictions at certain sites and extreme, unrealistic val-
ues for others. On the flip side, using WRF simulations as an input for the NWAM
reduces the spread of the prediction errors. A reduced spread in the production of
a NWA would ensure a reasonable level of certainty for most locations. From the
WRF simulations, the best balance between small over and underpredictions was
achieved with WRF-150m and WRF-100m. These two inputs also show the lowest
MAE across all sites: 3.02% and 3.28%. The remaining MAEs are show in table 4.19.
MAEs with most inputs are smaller than the ones in table 2.3 (Section 2.3.4). The
improved performance of the NWAM is surely related to the selection of the best map
and consequent adjustment of standard heights and roughness for the generalization.
A direct comparison with the Danish wind atlas is not possible because error were
quantified with RMSEdiff instead of MAE.
Due to the variable errors in sector-wise estimations, each input leads to different
overall power density errors. For such indicator, the best inputs across all sites are
WRF-50m, WRF-75m or WRF-100m. These three inputs concentrate half of their
errors between -5.7% and 7.22%, as shown in figure 4.46. All three lead to the low-
est power density MAEs, shown in table 4.20. The good performance of WRF-50m
and WRF-75m occurs mainly at offshore sites. For onshore sites, WRF-150m and
WRF-100m are the best inputs. The consistently small errors from these two inputs,
especially WRF-100m, suggest they are the best options for the production of a NWA.
The evaluation of the NWAM was executed again with the less-compatible satellite-
based maps. Tables 4.19 and 4.20 show the MAE for each case. Since 3 out of 4 sites
have GlobCover as their best map, it is reasonable to see similar MAEs. Using WRF-
50m as an input, wind speed and power density MAEs with GlobCover maps are
smaller than with the best maps. On the flip side, using only CORINE maps doubles
the MAEs with most inputs, as opposed to using the best map. Only when using
WRF-250m, CORINE maps lead to a smaller MAEs than GlobCover maps.

from the generalization map. This is the case for Østerild and Cabauw.
The REWS is not a typical indicator for wind resource assessment, and its use
to compare downscaling methodologies is relatively new [16]. Individual site evalu-
ations in sections 4.1 to 4.6, showed that relying on the REWS without looking at
the whole wind profile may be misleading. REWS and REWS-based power densities
are affected by the balance between over and underpredictions around the hub-height.
Very accurate REWS can be achieved with a set of wind climates that do not resemble
observations. This occurs at Høvsøre, where large errors from NWP models balance
each other out below and above 120 m. Profiles with a wind shear similar to the
observed one can lead to larger REWS errors than very deviated profiles that inter-
sect observations at the right height. Such is the case for offshore sites, where NWP
models resemble the wind profile better than NWAM outputs but both show equally
small error values. The fairest comparisons with REWS-based values are found for
Cabauw and Østerild. In such cases, errors from NWP models are not balanced so
REWS-based errors reflect the accuracy of each method at describing the wind profile.
Any REWS-based evaluation should be interpreted with caution.
The distributions of REWS-based errors shown in figures 4.47 and 4.48 reflect the
same results as the boxplots in section 4.7.2 and allow some comparison with NWP
models. The NWAM with WRF inputs shows less spread among errors than WRF
interpolations. In contrast, the NWAM with ERA5 inputs does not show a reduction
in the error spread compared to ERA5 interpolations. WRF interpolations show less
error spread than ERA5 interpolations. The error distribution from the two NWP
models do not resemble each other. This is a consequence of WRF’s modifications to
ERA5 inputs.

the goodness of each method at forecasting resource for a turbine with hub-height of
120 m. For offshore sites, profiles from the NWAM with WRF-50m, WRF-75m and
WRF-100m lead to the smallest deviations from the observed REWS. This occurs
despite the similarity between the observed profiles and interpolations from WRF
and ERA5. As in section 4.7.2, the NWAM with WRF-100m and WRF-150m as an

Conclusions
The wind atlas method using simulations as an input was called the NWAM. The right
atmospheric stability setup in WAsP is a precondition for the NWAM to achieve good
predictions. To improve inaccurate estimations from NWP models with the NWAM,
the generalization and downscaling maps need to be different. If the NWP model
already shows good estimations, similar generalization and downscaling maps are
needed.
The NWAM was evaluated for 2 offshore and 4 onshore sites. A total of 33 observed
wind climates between 40 and 200 m were considered. In all cases, the predicted wind
roses showed very close resemblance to those from observations. The evaluation of
predicted wind speed distributions was based on their mean wind speeds and power
densities. Different sector-wise and frequency-weighted results were obtained with
different inputs.
The selection of an appropriate downscaling map is of the utmost importance for
getting accurate predictions. The usage of a non-compatible map cannot be entirely
compensated by the modification of WAsP parameters for heatflux and ABL height.
Maps with high resolution and geographical accuracy do not guarantee compatibility
with the wind atlas method. Land-use-based roughness maps were preferred over
lidar-based maps. From these, maps derived from GlobCover2009 were a better op-
tion for 3 out of the 4 onshore sites. Executing the NWAM at all sites with maps
derived from CORINE CLC2012 led to values of MAE that were more than two times
larger than with the best map at each site. Therefore, the use of CORINE-derived
maps to downscale a full NWA is not recommended without further studies.
Using the best maps, the NWAM with ERA5-100m achieved the most accurate
predictions at 3 onshore sites.
Predicted wind profiles at Høvsøre, Østerild, and
Cabauw follow observations better than with other methods. However, using it at
Risø led to wind speed errors of over 17% and power density errors of over 67%.
Instead, the NWAM with WRF inputs leads to more consistent results. Using WRF-
150m simulations, the MAE for overall wind speeds across all heights of the 4 sites
is 2.5%. Based on these results, the NWAM with WRF-150m as an input appears
to be the safest choice for evaluating site-specific conditions if no measurements are
available.
Interpolations from NWP models resemble offshore wind profiles better than the
NWAM. WRF interpolations appear to be the best way for estimating offshore re-
source. At these sites, the smallest errors for the NWAM are achieved with WRF-

50m, WRF-75m and WRF-100m as inputs. For onshore sites, the performance of the
NWAM with WRF-100m is close to that with WRF-150m. Based on the relatively
good performance for both onshore and offshore results, WRF-100m confirms to be
the least uncertain input for the production of a NWA.
The accuracy in the estimation of the wind profile does not necessarily affect the
resource estimation for a wind turbine. The multiple averaging processes involved
in the estimation of the REWS can lead to very small rotor-based errors with very
deviated wind profiles. With such indicators, the NWAM with WRF-150m and WRF-
100m as inputs outperform any other method. Overall, the ERA5-WRF-WAsP model-
chain is an effective methodology to downscale free-access global meteorological data
for wind resource assessment.
5.1
Future work
Throughout the elaboration of this study, possible improvements to the methodology
were identified. Data filtering according to stability conditions could be executed.
This would require the use of time series for additional variables, such the surface
heatflux. Seasonal analyses could be carried out without the need for more data. The
Högström equation for extrapolating estimations could be replaced by other methods,
such as the logarithmic profile or the power law. More importantly, a selection of the
best-quality observations would result in more precise evaluations.
The results of the study open new paths that are worth investigating. Concerning
the map selection, analytical processes to identify the best downscaling maps could be
developed. Using additional error metrics could avoid relying on visual checks. Other
approaches focused on land classification could lead to a selection process in which
no observations are needed. Moreover, it is important to understand which terrain
conditions lead to better performance with lidar maps than with satellite-based maps.
Analyzing sites over complex terrains and at other latitudes is also left as future
work. Sensitivity analyses on the size of the generalization map could also be carried
out. Both proposals could lead to understanding for which locations or site charac-
teristics the NWAM performs better with ERA5 inputs than with WRF inputs. This
would be relevant to define a framework within which mesoscale modeling can be
safely avoided.
Finally, it would be interesting to evaluate the performance of a dynamical NWAM.
Given the multiple errors that balance out with the statistical NWAM enabled by
WAsP, a dynamical approach might not be as accurate.
Otherwise, it would be
promising to couple it with operational forecasts for applications beyond resource
assessment.
