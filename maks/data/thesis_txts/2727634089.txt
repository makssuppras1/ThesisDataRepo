Abstract 
This thesis studies the motivations of individuals joining startups, also named “joiners”, and the 
strategies founders employ to attract and retain talent within the Danish startup ecosystem. 
Through nine semi-structured interviews, it explores the varied roles and aspirations within this 
dynamic sector. The comprehensive literature review identifies gaps in the understanding of 
startup motivations and retention strategies, establishing a basis for the study's unique 
contributions. The methodology and research design follow a qualitative approach using semi-
structured interviews to explore the intricate dynamics of startup employment and talent 
management. The empirical findings present a nuanced understanding of the motivations of 
different startup roles and the strategies founders implement for talent management. The 
results, discussed in relation to existing literature, reveal theoretical and practical implications 
for startup founders, employees, and stakeholders. The thesis concludes by acknowledging 
its limitations and suggesting avenues for future research. It underscores the importance of 
understanding complex motivations and effective talent management strategies, offering 
practical strategies and a fresh perspective to the academic discourse on startups especially 
in the Danish context.

1. Introduction 
Denmark's startup world is booming like never before, fuelling an exciting economic surge and 
sparking a wave of fresh, inventive thinking. This growth has not only strengthened Denmark's 
economy but has also created a lot of job opportunities, attracting individuals with diverse skills 
and expertise. However, these promising ventures are coupled with the substantial challenges 
of uncertainty and risk, especially in the context of attracting and retaining talent. These 
obstacles, often referred to as “Liabilities of Newness”, are cleverly pointed out by Alfred 
Stinchcombe in his classic article "Social Structure and Organizations." (Stinchcombe, 1965). 
Notably, Denmark is regarded as a flourishing environment for entrepreneurship, ranked 4th 
in the world by the Global Entrepreneurship Index in 2019 (Ács et al., 2019). Furthermore, 
Denmark's conducive business environment, ranked 4th globally, makes it an attractive 
destination for aspiring entrepreneurs and startup employees (World Bank, 2020). Despite 
these favourable conditions, a deeper understanding of the incentives and motivations 
attracting individuals to join startups, specifically in the Danish context, remains largely 
unexplored. 
Addressing this gap presents a significant real-world problem impacting a broad range of 
stakeholders, from startups to potential employees, investors, and policymakers. Intriguingly, 
there has been an observable shift in career preferences among millennials, who, as (Lillelund, 
2023) reports, are seeking more purpose in their careers. High-profile directors are 
increasingly pivoting from traditional corporate roles to join the startup and scaleup sector, 
driven not only by the entrepreneurial spirit but also the pursuit of more meaningful, purposeful 
work. This trend underscores the importance and timeliness of examining the factors that 
influence this decision-making process, with the notable emergence of purpose as a key 
motivator. 
This thesis aims to dive into this unexplored area, investigating the motivations and incentives 
prompting people to become part of startups in Denmark, in roles ranging all the way from 
unpaid interns to co-founders with equity. In addition, it seeks to shed light on the often-
overlooked perspective of startup founders, who play a critical role in shaping the startup's 
culture, hiring practices, and overall work environment. These factors significantly influence 
talent attraction and retention, thereby determining the success and survival rate of startups. 
Unlocking the secrets of Denmark's startup hiring scene, this study bridges academia, and the 
real world, infusing fresh perspectives into talent strategies. The overarching motivation for this 
thesis is to be a thoughtful guide aimed at supporting a successful Danish startup ecosystem.

1.1 Preliminary Literature Review 
The majority of existing research on startup joiners predominantly concentrates on highly 
skilled professionals, with motivations such as the desire for autonomy, intellectual challenge, 
and potential financial rewards typically cited (Sauermann, 2018). Empirical evidence indicates 
that some startup employees experience greater financial success in the long run compared 
to those employed in established firms (Ouimet & Zarutskie, 2014; Sorenson et al., 2021). 
However, the literature largely leans towards quantitative methodologies, relying on specific 
demographic data such as PhDs and post-docs, limiting its scope (Roach et al., 2018; Roach 
& Sauermann, 2015).  
The founder's perspective on talent acquisition and retention is notably underrepresented in 
current research, despite its crucial role in the startup ecosystem (Cardon & Stevens, 2004; 
Wasserman, 2012). Startup founders grapple with the dual challenges of attracting and 
retaining talent amidst the constraints of limited resources and the inherent uncertainty of early-
stage ventures (Honore & Ganco, 2016). Moreover, the human capital theory underscores the 
vital role talent plays in the performance of startups (Brown et al., 2019; Crook et al., 2011), 
creating a pressing need for comprehensive research in this domain.  
This thesis aims to fill existing gaps in the literature by investigating a broader demographic of 
startup joiners, targeting individuals from unpaid interns to co-founders. The research design 
incorporates a qualitative approach, leading to an in-depth exploration of the motivations and 
incentives of these joiners (Batra, 2017; Frick, 2015; Ganco et al., 2019; Leung et al., 2006; 
Lewis & Cardon, 2020; Noack et al., 2018). Further, the thesis seeks to clarify founders' 
perspectives on talent acquisition and retention, a relatively underexplored area in startup 
literature (Choi et al., 2022; Sauermann, 2018).  
In the dedicated literature review chapter, I will take a closer look and dig deeper into all the 
research that's been done so far. This will help us better understand the many factors that 
shape the startup talent scene and will guide the focus of this study. 
1.2 Problem Statement 
This thesis will investigate the incentives and motivations that drive individuals to join startups 
in the Danish context, as well as the founders' perspectives on attracting and retaining talent. 
The study focuses on a broad spectrum of startup joiners, from unpaid interns and hourly-paid 
student workers to full-time employees and equity-holding co-founders. Through qualitative 
methods with an inductive approach, it aims to offer a comprehensive understanding of the 
startup hiring landscape and its impact on the Danish startup ecosystem.

1.3 Research Questions 
The following research questions will guide this study: 
 
What are the primary incentives and motivations for individuals to join startups in 
Denmark? 
 
How do founders in the Danish context perceive the challenges and opportunities related 
to attracting and retaining talent? 
 
How do the incentives and motivations of startup joiners differ based on their roles, such 
as unpaid interns, hourly-paid student workers, full-time employees, or co-founders with

 
How can the findings of this study inform talent attraction and retention strategies for

1.4 Scope and limitations 
This study will focus on Denmark, and while the findings may have implications for other 
countries with similar cultural and economic contexts, the primary focus will be on the Danish 
startup ecosystem. The study will explore the incentives and motivations of startup joiners and 
founders, providing a comprehensive understanding of the startup hiring landscape. Therefore, 
this thesis’ scope is limited to the Danish context with technological startups. 
The influence of gender in entrepreneurial pursuits needs to be acknowledged and its potential 
impact on this study. Gender differences in personality can significantly affect behaviours and 
preferences (Feingold, 1994). Furthermore, gender roles within the entrepreneurial ecosystem 
can differentially shape future career choices of startup joiners, with female founders, in 
particular, acting as influential role models for their female employees (Rocha & van Praag, 
2020). However, this investigation is not designed to differentiate the motivations and 
incentives across genders or explore the complexities of these possible gender dynamics. 
This study's intention is to provide an overarching understanding of the themes and incentives 
attracting individuals to startups, irrespective of gender differentiation. However, the 
implications of gender-specific incentives and motivations, as suggested by Feingold (1994) 
and Rocha & van Praag (2020), provide solid ground for future research in this context. 
This study will use a qualitative approach to explore the incentives and motivations of startup 
joiners and founders, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. While providing rich 
insights, semi-structured interviews may also be subject to researcher bias and participant self-
reporting limitations. Nevertheless, the in-depth insights derived from the interviews will 
contribute to a richer understanding of the subject.

2. Literature Review 
This chapter critically examines existing literature about startup joiners, the different 
hierarchies of entry for a joiner and the dynamics of attracting and retaining talent in the startup 
context. Additionally, I will identify gaps in the current body of knowledge. 
2.1 Defining Startup Joiners 
To get a clear picture of the literature a definition of the term "startup joiner" is needed. For the 
purposes of this research, the definition offered by Michael Roach and Henry Sauermann 
(2015), widely credited as pioneers in researching this specific employee group, will be used. 
They define a startup joiner as: “individuals who join founders as entrepreneurial employees 
but who have little interest in becoming founders themselves” (Roach & Sauermann, 2015, p. 
2160).  
This definition leaves little room for alternative interpretations. Ouimet & Zarutskie (2014) noted 
that joiners tend to be young and recent graduates, aligning with startups' need for the newest 
scientific knowledge.  
Although "early employee" is another term sometimes used interchangeably with "joiner" in the 
literature, it emphasizes the timing of the individual's arrival in the startup rather than the 
motivations that underpin the choice to join, making "joiner" the more appropriate term for this 
study. Therefore, the term joiner will be used for the remainder of this thesis.  
As it will be described in the next section, a joiner can join the startup at different levels in 
terms of commitment and responsibility. 
2.2 Different Startup Roles 
The world of startups is filled with a broad range of roles, each with its unique incentives and 
motivations. Venturing into the startup scene can be an exciting journey, one that's full of 
opportunities and challenges. The driving motivations behind unpaid interns, hourly-paid 
student workers, full-time employees, and co-founders with equity will be investigated. For 
clarity, these four categories of joiners are identified and defined by me, though it is probable 
that more roles exist. 
Unpaid interns join startups to gain hands-on experience, build their network, and perhaps 
even get a foot in the door for a future job (Batra, 2017). They are often driven by the potential 
to learn new skills and gather valuable insights about entrepreneurship (Lewis & Cardon, 
2020). Their motivation is rooted in the growth and learning opportunities that startups provide,

rather than immediate financial gains. The thrill of being in an innovative and fast-paced 
environment adds to the startup’s appeal (Noack et al., 2018). 
Student workers often get involved in startups to support their studies, both financially and 
academically. They appreciate the flexible working hours and the opportunity to earn while 
learning (Batra, 2017). Moreover, they may see their participation as a steppingstone to a 
potential full-time role post-graduation, aligning their current role with their career goals 
(Sauermann, 2018). They're not just motivated by their paycheck, but also the potential for a 
more significant role in the startup's future. 
Full-time employees are commonly attracted to startups by the unique blend of challenging 
work and meaningful contributions (Choi et al., 2023). The prospect of having a substantial 
impact and being part of the startup's growth trajectory serves as a significant motivator (Roach 
& Sauermann, 2015). Additionally, the potential for rapid career advancement and 
opportunities to broaden their skill set are key factors that attract and retain these employees 
(Brown et al., 2019). 
Co-founders with equity are primarily driven by the potential for significant financial rewards if 
the startup succeeds (Frick, 2015). However, they're also motivated by the opportunity to turn 
their vision into reality and have a substantial influence on the startup's strategic direction 
(Cardon & Stevens, 2004). In addition, the autonomy and freedom that come with running a 
startup, as well as the satisfaction derived from building a company from scratch, serve as 
powerful incentives for co-founders (Ganco et al., 2019). 
Understanding these varied motivations can be key for startups as they seek to attract and 
retain the right talent for their growth and success. As indicated by the referenced studies, 
tailoring incentives, and work environments to align with the unique motivations of each role 
can lead to greater satisfaction, commitment, and productivity in the startups.  
Now the focus shifts towards how to attract the talents to the startups. 
2.3 Attracting Talent to Startups 
Attracting talent to startups is a complex process, with unique challenges and opportunities 
arising from the context of these young, resource-limited companies (Stinchcombe, 1965; 
Leung et al., 2006). However, such companies must navigate these issues to compete 
effectively with established firms. Founders play a crucial role in this endeavour (Wasserman, 
2012; Lewis & Cardon, 2020), crafting their startup's value proposition to align with the 
==PAGE:12==

Page | 7 
motivations and expectations of potential joiners of the startup (Ganco, Honoré, and Raffiee, 
2019). 
To counterbalance their inherent "Liabilities of Newness", startups offer alternative incentives 
such as equity, flexible organizational structures, and an environment rich in learning 
opportunities (Batra, 2017). The relatively flat hierarchies typical of startups enable greater 
decision-making autonomy, which can be particularly appealing if the organization's vision is 
shared between the founding team and the joiner (Batra, 2017). Further, a founder's ability to 
foster a passionate connection to the product can significantly heighten startup attractiveness 
(Lewis & Cardon, 2020). 
However, it is not just about what the startups can offer. Ganco et. al (2019) asserts the 
importance of aligning joiner's values and operational style with the founding team's, ensuring 
both parties' mutual success. This alignment is very important for the talent acquisition 
process, effectively bridging the startup's offering with the joiner's personal and professional 
aspirations. 
A well-rounded understanding of joiners motivations and expectations can help frame effective 
strategies for attracting talent. Joiners in a broad definition are typically attracted to 
independence, responsibility, and intellectually challenging work, more than financial stability 
commonly associated with established firms (Roach and Sauermann, 2015; Sauermann, 
2017). Studies find that individuals willing to trade off financial security for the opportunity to 
make a significant impact, rapidly learn, and engage in innovative problem-solving are more 
likely to join startups (Sauermann, 2017; Roach, Sauermann, and Berlin, 2018). 
Additionally, Noack, Miller, and Guidice (2018) emphasize the varied expectations among 
joiners, ranging from career progression opportunities to contributing to a visionary project. 
The extent to which these expectations are fulfilled significantly influences joiner satisfaction 
and, consequently, talent retention. 
The responsibility falls on the founders to maintain a balance between a flat hierarchy and 
transparency, while simultaneously promoting an inclusive decision-making process (Batra, 
2017). This balance, combined with an ability to maintain a shared vision and a passionate 
connection to the product, can substantially enhance startup attractiveness (Lewis & Cardon, 
2020). 
Lastly, the founder's skill in managing the 'Liabilities of Newness' (Stinchcombe, 1965) plays a 
significant role in attracting talent. To overcome limited resources and high uncertainty,

founders must strategize effectively to showcase the potential rewards a startup can offer to 
joiners (Leung et al., 2006). 
In conclusion, talent attraction in startups is a multifaceted process, with founders playing a 
critical role in defining their company's attractiveness. They must understand the expectations 
and motivations of potential joiners and align these with the startup's value proposition. A 
strategic balance between offering unique opportunities and managing inherent liabilities is 
key to attracting talent to startups, ensuring their growth, and ultimate success. 
The next section will dive into the relevant literature with regards to retaining the attracted 
joiners in the startups. 
2.4 Retaining Talent in Startups 
Retaining talent in startups is an intricate task shaped by multiple factors, ranging from 
individual motivations and expectations to the unique vision and culture fostered by the 
founders. The dynamics of talent retention in startups are markedly different from those in 
established companies due to the distinct organisational attributes and operational constraints 
that typify early-stage ventures. 
Primarily, talent retention is based on meeting joiners' motivations and expectations. These 
are multi-layered, influenced by personal inclinations, professional aspirations, and the 
perceived trade-offs of working in a startup (Roach & Sauermann, 2015). Joiners tend to value 
independence, responsibility, and intellectually stimulating work over the financial stability 
commonly associated with established firms. They are drawn to the flexibility and autonomy 
that startups offer, which allow them to carve out their roles and engage in diverse tasks that 
provoke intellectual curiosity (Sauermann, 2017). 
These inner drives - a thirst for autonomy, the allure of intellectual challenge, and the chance 
to make a tangible impact - become key players in the game of employee retention. Startups 
may be high-risk, but they offer something different. They offer a rich ground where these 
intrinsic rewards can flourish and often, these elements can tip the scales in favour of job 
satisfaction and employee retention, even outshining financial compensation (Sauermann, 
2018). 
Intriguingly, many people who join startups are willing to make a trade-off. They're prepared to 
swap out the certainty of financial stability and job security for a shot at making substantial 
contributions to something new and exciting. They're attracted to the rapid pace of learning 
and the opportunity to get stuck into innovative problem-solving (Roach, Sauermann, & Berlin,

2018). In essence, they're drawn by the chance to grow, learn, and make a real difference, 
which can often provide a powerful motivation that surpasses traditional career incentives.  
It is imperative, therefore, for founders to effectively address these expectations to retain talent 
successfully. Founders are not only charged with defining the strategic trajectory of the startup 
but also play a vital role in shaping its culture, values, and human capital strategy (Wasserman, 
2012). The vision and culture of the organization are central to employee retention, as startup 
employees often identify strongly with the venture's mission (Choi, Goldschlag, Haltiwanger, & 
Kim, 2022).  
Achieving alignment between the founders' vision and the joiners' motivations is therefore key 
to talent retention. Founders must effectively communicate their vision and cultivate a culture 
that resonates with their team. The need for congruence between the joiners' motivations and 
the startup's mission is highlighted by Ganco, Honoré, and Raffiee (2019). They emphasized 
that this alignment helps ensure that joiners not only contribute effectively to the startup's 
growth but also find personal and professional fulfilment in their roles. 
Moreover, the founders must navigate the 'Liabilities of Newness' (Stinchcombe, 1965)—
limited resources, high uncertainty, and a lack of legitimacy in the market—while maintaining 
the promise of high rewards that attract talent to startups in the first place (Leung et al., 2006). 
This challenge underscores the role of equity compensation in retention. Despite its inherent 
risk due to the uncertain future of startups, equity compensation can be a potent tool to retain 
talent as it promises potentially significant future returns, keeping employees committed 
despite current hardships (Honore, F., & Ganco, 2016). 
Furthermore, nurturing a positive and supportive work environment is crucial in talent retention. 
Founders must foster an atmosphere characterized by a flat hierarchy, transparency, and 
inclusive decision-making. Regular feedback, recognition, and opportunities for employees to 
voice their opinions contribute to a sense of belonging and foster long-term commitment 
(Cardon, & Stevens, 2004; Batra, 2017).  
Notably, talent retention is not solely about catering to the individual needs and expectations 
of employees but also necessitates the establishment of a satisfactory work-life balance. 
Contrary to the popular understanding of the relentless 'startup grind', a balance that respects 
the personal lives of employees while maintaining necessary productivity levels is essential for 
retention (Leung, Zhang, Wong, & Foo, 2006). 
In conclusion, retaining talent in startups is a multifaceted challenge, requiring founders to 
balance the different elements—from intrinsic motivators and personal growth opportunities to

work-life balance and equity compensation. Founders play a pivotal role in every stage of this 
complex process. Their ability to understand, respond to, and align with the motivations of their 
team members significantly contributes to the overall success and sustainability of the startup. 
2.5 Gaps in the Literature 
While current literature presents valuable insights into the dynamics of talent attraction and 
retention in startups, it does leave some room for expansion and further investigation. One 
significant area where research could expand is the methodology employed in these studies. 
Most existing research, including Roach and Sauermann (2015), Ganco, Honoré, and Raffiee 
(2019), and Batra (2017), among others, is largely quantitative in nature. They rely on surveys, 
statistical data, and numerical analysis to explore patterns and trends in startup talent 
management. 
Quantitative approaches are beneficial in providing broad, generalizable insights. However, 
they can sometimes overlook the intricate, nuanced, and contextual aspects of the startup 
joiner phenomenon. Consequently, a qualitative approach, such as in-depth interviews, focus 
groups, or ethnographic studies, could complement these quantitative findings and provide a 
more in-depth, nuanced understanding. 
For instance, the role of personal factors, such as an individual's risk tolerance, values, and 
career aspirations in shaping their decision to join a startup, is still not fully understood. 
Personal interviews could provide a more detailed picture of these dynamics.  
Much of the current research focuses on the founders' perspective, often neglecting the equally 
crucial perspective of the joiners. Using a qualitative approach, future studies could dive 
deeper into joiners' experiences, motivations, and perceptions, offering a more balanced 
understanding of the talent acquisition and retention processes in startups. 
Lastly, while the existing literature identifies job attributes that attract joiners to startups, there 
is limited understanding of the relative importance of these attributes, and how they may 
change over time or differ across individual joiners. Qualitative research could offer more 
personalized insights into these areas, aiding startups in tailoring their strategies to attract and 
retain a diverse talent pool. It is important to note that the gaps highlighted in this section are 
not exhaustive, and there is plenty more ground to cover in the quest to fully understand this 
space. 
While existing literature provides a solid foundation for understanding the dynamics of talent 
attraction and retention in startups, it seems clear that integrating more qualitative methods in 
future research could contribute to a richer, more comprehensive understanding of joiners.

3. Methodology 
In this chapter, I present my chosen methodology, a qualitative research design, aimed at 
providing an in-depth and nuanced understanding of the motivations and incentives driving 
individuals to join startups in Denmark. This understanding is sought from both the joiners and 
founders perspectives, using one-to-one interviews. A majority of these interviews are 
conducted via the online platform Microsoft Teams, except for one which takes place in-person 
with the audio recorded. I elaborate on the semi-structured nature of the interviews, the 
transcription and thematic analysis process, as well as the ethical considerations undertaken 
in the study. This methodological approach enables a comprehensive exploration of the 
complex dynamics in the Danish startup ecosystem. 
3.1 Rationale 
Several key factors influenced my decision to employ a qualitative research approach for this 
study. At the forefront is the desire to capture in-depth, personal perspectives that lie at the 
heart of individuals' motivations and incentives to join startups in Denmark. The personal and 
contextual nature of these motivations necessitates a methodology that allows for a nuanced 
exploration of individual experiences and perceptions. 
While quantitative methods may offer broad patterns or correlations, they often fall short in 
capturing the richness and complexity of subjective experiences and the unique, personal 
meanings that individuals ascribe to their actions. Therefore, a qualitative approach was 
deemed the most effective in addressing the exploratory nature of this study's research 
questions. The use of semi-structured interviews offers a degree of flexibility that is invaluable 
in this research context. It allows for the exploration of emergent themes and unexpected 
insights during the interviews, which could be pivotal to understanding the motivations and 
incentives in the dynamic Danish startup ecosystem. 
In essence, the qualitative methodology was chosen as it provides the necessary flexibility and 
depth to understand the complex dynamics of motivations and incentives in an under-
researched and evolving context such as the Danish startup scene The chosen methods thus 
align closely with the research objectives and offer the potential to uncover rich, valuable 
insights into this unique context. 
3.2 Qualitative Research Approach 
Building upon the rationale provided, my research embraces a qualitative approach for its 
inherent potential to dive into the complex and multifaceted experiences, thoughts, and 
motivations of individuals. A qualitative approach is uniquely suited to tap into subjective 
==PAGE:17==
Incentivising Startup Joiners 
 
Page | 12 
realities and provides room for the richness, depth, and nuance that underlie personal 
experiences and perspectives, which often get overlooked by quantitative methods (Fossey et 
al., 2002) 
This study's focus lies in uncovering the nuanced understanding of individuals' motivations and 
incentives, making the qualitative method indispensable. Here, rather than treating motivations 
as pre-set categories, as quantitative methods often do, I treat motivations as emergent, multi-
dimensional constructs that can be interpreted in light of the unique personal experiences and 
social contexts of the individuals involved. 
Moreover, considering the predominant use of quantitative methods in existing research on 
startup joiners, this qualitative approach offers a refreshing change in perspective. By choosing 
this approach, I am not merely looking for the 'what' and 'how many' but also trying to uncover 
the 'why' and 'how' of the motivations and incentives of individuals joining startups in Denmark. 
This could potentially open up new dimensions of understanding and unveil previously 
unexplored insights, thus contributing significantly to the field. 
To guide the research process, I adopt an inductive approach, which is highly compatible with 
the qualitative method chosen. Given that the research area of motivations and incentives of 
joiners in the startup scene remains under-researched, an inductive approach proves 
beneficial. It permits theories to emerge and evolve organically during the analysis of the 
findings, rather than forcing the data into predefined theoretical frameworks (Bryman & Bell, 
2007). This flexibility is essential for capturing the unique dynamics in the Danish startup 
ecosystem. 
In conclusion, a qualitative research approach, underpinned by an inductive stance, forms the

3.6 Ethical Considerations and Limitations 
In this research, I follow the ethical guidelines for qualitative research, making sure to uphold 
the confidentiality and anonymity of the participants. (Fossey et al., 2002) I inform all 
participants about the purpose of the study, their role in it, and how I will use the data. I also 
assure them that they can withdraw from the study at any point, and I ensure an informed 
consent from all participants. 
Simultaneously, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of this study. While the research 
provides valuable insights into the motivations and incentives of startup joiners and founders, 
the findings may not be generalizable to all individuals in the Danish startup ecosystem. This 
limitation arises from the qualitative nature of the study and the purposive sampling method 
employed. Additionally, the reliance on self-reported data could introduce recall bias and reflect 
the personal interpretations of the participants. Despite these constraints, the research 
contributes rich, in-depth data to the understanding of the startup landscape in Denmark.

From all nine interviews, one thing is abundantly clear - the unique work culture of startups is

4.6 Conclusion of Findings  
In summarizing the findings from the four themes it is clear that the decision to join a startup 
is multifaceted and deeply personal, involving considerations of passion and entrepreneurial 
spirit, organizational culture and environment, roles and learning, and the individual joiner’s 
own perception of risk and reward. 
The first theme, 'Passion and Entrepreneurial Spirit,' signifies the intrinsic motivation and 
passion that individuals carry when they join startups. This entrepreneurial spirit, often 
underpinned by a deep-seated desire to innovate, disrupt, and challenge the status quo, is a 
compelling factor that draws talents towards the startup environment. The allure of carving out 
new paths and the thrill of creating something from scratch emerges as a strong determinant 
for joining startups. 
The second theme, 'Organizational Culture and Environment,' underscores the importance of 
a vibrant and flexible culture in the startup, one that is built on mutual trust, transparency, and 
a shared sense of purpose. This theme illustrates that many joiners are drawn to startups for 
their flat hierarchies, innovative work environments, and the opportunity to contribute directly 
to the startup’s growth and success. Startups' unique ability to foster a sense of community 
and belonging, despite the inherent uncertainties and challenges, can be a powerful attraction 
and retainment for startups. 
'Roles, Skills, and Learning,' the third theme, sheds light on the rich learning experiences and 
the multifaceted roles that individuals often encounter in startups. The startup environment, 
characterized by its dynamism and agility, allows talents to wear multiple hats, gain a variety 
of skills, and grow both personally and professionally. While this requires a degree of 
adaptability, it also offers opportunities for rapid career progression and continuous learning, 
making startups an attractive career option for many talents. 
The fourth and final theme, 'Risk and Reward Perception,' encapsulates the high-risk, high-
reward nature of startups. While potential financial gains can be a significant lure, it is often 
the chance to make a real impact that resonates deeply with many joiners. Despite the inherent 
financial risks and the element of uncertainty of startups, the potential to make a significant 
personal contribution and societal impact is a powerful motivator for the interviewees. 
In conclusion, these themes collectively highlight the key factors that attract joiners to startups. 
They reflect a complex interplay of personal motivations, cultural fit, learning opportunities, and 
the balance between risk and reward.

A2. Interview plan – Joiner 
1. Introduction (2-3 minutes) 
Thank you for taking the time to participate in this interview. My name is Per Jensen, and I 
am conducting this research to explore the incentives and motivations of individuals who join 
startups in Denmark. Your input will be valuable in helping us understand the factors that 
drive people to join and work in startups. I will ensure your anonymity and protect your data 
throughout this research. May I have your consent to record this interview? 
2. Background information about the participant (5-10 minutes) 
Understanding your background will help put your experiences and motivations into context.  
 
To start, could you please tell me about your current role and responsibilities in the startup? 
 
And now some stats about the startup, age, sector of business, no. of employees, any 
revenue yet? 
 
Would you mind sharing a bit about your previous experiences and background before 
joining this company? 
3. Motivations and incentives for working in a startup (15-20 minutes) 
Identifying the key drivers for joining a startup can help companies tailor their strategies for 
attracting talent.  
 
What initially motivated you to work in a startup? 
 
Which incentives did you find most attractive at your current company? 
 
How has your motivation and incentives evolved over time? 
4. Challenges and opportunities associated with working in a startup (10-15 minutes) 
Gaining insights into the challenges and opportunities faced by startup employees can help 
founders develop supportive work environments.  
 
Can you describe some challenges and opportunities you have experienced while working 
in a startup? 
 
How have you handled these challenges and taken advantage of the opportunities? 
 
How does your work in a startup differ from your previous experiences? 
5. Talent attraction and retention in startups (10-15 minutes) 
Understanding the talent attraction and retention process can help startups create effective 
strategies to build strong teams.  
 
Can you share your experiences with attracting and retaining talent in your company? 
 
In your opinion, what makes a company attractive to new talent? 
 
How does your company work to retain existing talent? 
6. Conclusion and follow-up (2-3 minutes) 
 
Do you have any final thoughts or questions you'd like to share? 
I'd like to thank you for your time and insights today.
