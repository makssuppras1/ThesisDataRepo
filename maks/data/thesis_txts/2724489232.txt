Abstract
This thesis is the implementation and evaluation of a Lidar Inertial Odometry (LIO) sensor-fusion
model between a Livox MD-70 solid-state Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) 3D-scanner and
a Xsens MTi-100 Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), for more accurate and robust odometry and
undistortion of point clouds over non-fusion Lidar Odometry (LO) for use in Global Navigation Satellite
System (GNSS) restricted or totally denied environments. The fusion consists of a bounded a priori
transformation prediction via the preintegration of IMU measurements of accelerations and angular
rates. The predicted transformation is refined by scan-to-local map registration using the Point-to-
Plane-ICP (P2Pl-ICP) algorithm. The process is controlled through a Geometric Observer (GO) that
optimises the initial preintegration state by the translational and rotational residual of the scan match
refinement. A Madgwick filter has been implemented for determination of the initial gravity referenced
orientation and for removal of gravitational influence in accelerometer measurements. The evaluation of
odometry is focused on fitness metrics such as refinement residuals and scan registration fitness as well
as a proposed method for covariance estimates of odometry transformation. A procedure named Hot-
Start that initialises the local map from stationary updates is devised and shown to stabilise initial
odometry estimates. Simulations of Inertial Navigation System (INS) corrected by either an Error
State Kalman-Filter (ESKF) or a Geometric Observer (GO), a simplified Contracting Hierarchical
Observer (CHO), has been conducted to determine viability of each for bias tracking and correction
under harsh noise. The fusion model has been tested and evaluated on data from both indoor and
outdoor environments. The results are mixed as the model is subject to not being able to maintain
proper odometry after deviation from the initial heading by more than 90◦due to unknown reasons.
Attempts of mitigation by conical crop of the local map has been tried with no improvements. This
problem also persists with non-fusion LO test. Odometry from linear sections with only slight turning
show great performance.

Introduction
This thesis is the result of the implementation of a Lidar Inertial Odometry
(LIO) localisation system, attempting to fuse the egomotion estimation from a
Lidar Odometry system using solid state LiDARs, a Livox MD-70, and an Iner-
tial Navigation System system with off-the-shelves Inertial Measurement Units
(IMUs), namely the Xsens MTi-100.
The main aspect of the fusion is a prediction of the next Lidar Odometry step
by pre-integrating the INS. The Lidar Odometry will then provide an error or
residual which is then used to correct the INS state and biases, leading to better
a priori prediction. The INS is corrected using a simplification of a Contracting
Hierarchical Observer, referred to as a Geometric Observer. The corrected pre-
integration is then used for point cloud undistortion, providing locally accurate
LiDAR scans.
The LIO implementation is made entirely in Robotic Operating System 2
(ROS2) in C++ with prototyping and simulations made in Python. ROS2 is a
very popular toolbox for creating entire robot operation system using a modular
approach. This means that an implementation like this localisation system can
be transferred to another system without a complete software rebuild.

Both sensors are considered commercial grade, but combined in a fusion algorithm they can potentially
perform én par with LO from high-end research grade LiDAR-scanners, at a fraction of the cost.
Another interesting aspect of the Livox MD-70 is its non-repetitive scan pattern fig. 1.0.1. Because it
is non-repetitive the cloud density will continuously increase with time. Within 1 s of scan time the
Livox MD-70 will have accumulated the same point density as as full 64-line spin scanner. This allows
for the creation of very dense and detailed point clouds.
Figure 1.0.1: The non-repetitive scan pattern of the Livox MD-70. The scanner is projecting a single
scan-line that oscillates from edge to edge while also spinning about the center, almost like an ultra
fast Foucault-pendulum.
The small size of the scanner is also a great selling point making it much more viable for use as a
payload on drone surveys, where spin-scanners are often awkward to implement and half the points of
the scans are lost to the heavens anyway.
For testing, data from both indoors and outdoors environments have been collected around Campus
Lyngby of The Technical University of Denmark, mimicking environments that are GNSS denied. The
outdoors data is both under foliage and in close proximity to multistory office buildings, which is very
prone to cause issues with GNSS systems for precision tracking. The indoors data is from an office
building lobby and hallway, and the outdoors data is sections of the Campus area. Ground truth of the
outdoors data set was attempted to be recorded but the data was apparently corrupted and therefore
not available. This is, however, a testament to the issues of GNSS which is greatly covered in [1].
1.0.1
Motivation and Purpose
With drones and robots becoming more autonomous, they also require reliable localisation systems if
they are to be integrated into a dynamic world of humans. Or be entirely on their own on the surface of
another planet. The success of the martian rovers, the latest being Curiosity, relies heavily on reliable
localisation in order to navigate the dunes and cliffs safely, and not compromise the mission - which
would be a hugely expensive write-off.
In the near time to come it is also not difficult to imagine robots and drones alleviating labour-intensive
and hazardous jobs such as mining and construction work and inspection. For instance is inspection of
bridges important and necessary to maintain it, but can be very expensive if personnel has to traverse
and inspect every part of the surface, due to the inaccessibility. Having a UAV to fly around and map
the bridge in 3D while documenting locations of issues, can both speed up the process and ensure the
safety of the personnel.
With regards to research, with in-the-field data collection using drones, accuracy and precision of the
data localisation is always desired to be as good as possible, as this directly affects the results.
The project is also a study in the feasibility of using solid state LiDARs for LO. The concern being
that they only scan directly in front of them as opposed to spin scanners that achieves a full 360◦
scan in every frame. This poses the problem of easy degeneration of the odometry estimation due to
various reasons.
Sci-Fi movies are often also an envisioning of what might be in the future, and here robots are often
seen integrated seamlessly into the world of humans. Such a vision sets some requirements of robots

1.0.1.1
Why fuse LOAM and INS
Systems Complements each other
The idea of fusing data from a Lidar Odometry and Inertial Navigation System into what is known as
Lidar Inertial Odometry is not novel, and is in fact researched extensively. All of [2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9,
10] is built as Lidar Inertial Odometry systems in one form or another, and this is because the systems
complement each other. LO can provide quite accurate pose estimation but at a limited frequency (5
to 10 Hz) while INS has very high rate of pose estimation (100 to 1000 Hz) and is fairly accurate in
short time intervals, but will drift exponentially if not corrected. The INS is for this reason a great
way to provide good a priori information, i.e. a short term prediction, for the scan matching. And
the accurate pose estimation of LO can then correct the INS.
Localisation Independent of Reception
Another popular fusion model is a INS/GNSS configuration, which is used in great effect in maritime
and aerial navigation. Here it makes sense to do so, due to the unrestricted availability of GNSS
signal. But as drones are becoming more prevalent in research and business, and with the introduction
of self-driving cars, having to navigate and localise themselves in GNSS denied environments, more
robust and independent localisation systems are sought after.
This is the great advantage of the LIO, the system is kept independent of outside tracking making it
available for use indoors, under dense foliage, or in mines for the mining industry. In fact much of the
research on scan matching is from a mining industrial incentive [11, 12].
1.0.2
Previous Work
The thesis is a partial continuation of a previous project of the author which is comprised of the im-
plementation of a Pose-Graph (PG) LIDAR Odometry And Mapping (LOAM) algorithm utilising a
Velodyne VLP-16 scanner. The experiments consisted of large scale trajectories around DTU campus
with results that showed strong short-term odometry at typical city driving speeds, but long-term
revealed a biased drift in orientation. This was, at the time, attributed to misalignment of the scanner
and motion-distortion of point-clouds and was never fully resolved. But, from research and implemen-
tation of this project, this error has hypothesised to most likely be caused by the degeneration of the
orientation rotation matrix in the poses, caused be the many recursive transformations without nor-
malisation of this matrix. The LOAM of that project was built as a pose-graph enabling loop-closure
capabilities that was able to correct for this drift however. The LOAM theory and implementations
of this project is mainly build and iterated upon that project, [13], but adapted to handle the Livox
MD-70 LiDAR-scanner and rebuild in ROS2 in C++. The abstract is enclosed in appendix A.1.

LiDAR- and Inertial Navigation
The integration of LiDAR and IMU for navigation purposes is based on the fu-
sion of two separate navigation models namely Inertial Navigation System (INS)
and LIDAR Odometry And Mapping (LOAM) or Lidar Odometry (LO). The
fusion model is usually named Lidar Inertial Odometry (LIO).
INS is a dead-reckoning model that uses the continuously measured accelerations
and angular rates of a system to propagate its location by time-wise integration.
LO operates by matching consecutive LiDAR scans of the the surroundings to
infer the motion of the system. The LOAM algorithm allows for optimisation
of previous odometry when revisiting areas previously scanned or using recent
local maps to make odometry more robust.
Both models are recursive navigation solutions meaning that they use the previ-
ous navigation solution as the foundation for the next and are therefore subject
to drift for various reasons. They can therefore also be referred to as relative
navigation models as they propagate in relation to a previous solution. The
advantage, however, is that these models do not rely on communication or con-
nection to separate units or systems as it is the case in GNSS positioning. This
allows these inside-out navigation systems to be used in environments such as
dense woods, caves and extra terrestrial worlds that are restricted or denied of
outside-in tracking systems.

As mentioned earlier, a pose consists of a position and an orientation. The representation of a position
is quite straight forward and is always noted as a 3-vector (R3), symbolising the three cardinal axes
of 3D-space. This easy to work with, continuous, and every point in space is represented uniquely.
Representations of orientations, however, is not trivial.
There are several ways representing orientations, and some are better than others. The most commonly
known is the Roll, Pitch and Yaw system, which is used for aircrafts. This representation is very
intuitive but not very robust and is not differentiable.

so there is no unique representation of orientations.

2.2.1
Scan-Matching
The process of scan matching is the extrapolation of the relative pose transformation that align the
source cloud with the target cloud.
This is a highly researched field as it is deemed a keystone
in autonomous vehicle navigation, as it requires ultra robust estimation of odometry due to safety
concerns. For this reason more and more algorithms are being developed and published, ranging from
expansions of the ICP algorithm to entirely new approaches. To give a comprehensive outline is not
within the scope but a narrow study of the simpler and well recognised methods is, however, relevant
to the project as it has a considerable effect on the performance of the LOAM algorithm. Algorithms
such as ICP and NDT are called local registration methods as they require an initial guess of the
sought transformation. In LOAM this is usually acquired from robot odometry.
2.2.1.1
Iterative Closest Point - ICP
Iterative Closest Point [18, 19] has long been a stable go-to for scan-matching. It works by iteratively
searching for point correspondence pairs and then minimise the euclidean distance between them. The
transformation can be found using the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) approach which utilises
the decomposition of the cross-covariance matrix of the centralised point sets. A simple and good
reiteration of this method can be found in [20].
Figure 2.2.2: The iterative process of aligning two point clouds, Image from [21].
If the two clouds have perfect point correspondence, e.g. that one cloud is the the result of the rigid
body transformation of the other, the transformation can be perfectly recovered.
This method is
therefore very susceptible to outliers i.e. points pairs that are in fact not point pairs, hence the search
for correspondences. This is usually determined as the closest neighbouring point in the other cloud and
is limited by a max-correspondence-distance parameter. This parameter is therefore also controlling a
trade-off between accuracy and convergence. If too small compared to the cloud density the algorithm
cannot converge as proper point pairings might be filtered out, and if too large false pairings will cause
error in the estimated transformation. The iterative process thus consist of updating the point pairs
from the previously estimated transformation with the anticipation of finding more accurate point
==PAGE:25==
2.2. LIDAR ODOMETRY AND MAPPING - LOAM
11
pairings and consequently a better transformation. The algorithm therefore needs an initial guess of
the transformation in order to find the first pairings.
In Visual Odometry, when these point pairings are determined by feature correspondences, the SVD
approach can, with proper filtering, estimate the odometry transformations. The points are defined
from actual features in the scene and can therefore be tracked and relocated in later frames. In LO a
point is only defined by the laser shooting angle and the return time, and where the laser point hits
has no distinctness that can be used to find the point again in later scans. In this sense a LiDAR point
is just a sample of the small local area where it hits, and given the point density which drops with
distance from the scanner, this area might be relatively large. For this reason the Point-to-Point-ICP
ICP might only be viable for dense and uniformly sampled point clouds. Distinct scan patterns like
the flower pattern of the Livox MD-70 might even present a bias towards fitting the pattern rather
than the scanned 3D structure, fig. 2.2.6a. Alterations like Point-to-Plane-ICP ICP or NDT should
mitigate such problems as this takes into account that points are a representation of actual surfaces of
structures.

Feature Matching
The limiting factor in ICP is the process of finding correspondences pairs, which can require looping
over all the point many times, if done naively. By instead reducing the point cloud data to a more
sparse structure, by some defined features, it can potentially be speed up.
reducing or removing
the requirements of the initial transformation. This process of fitting extrapolated features is called
feature matching. An implementation of this can be found in [4]. Here surfaces and edges features
are extracted from the point cloud creating a more sparse data structure, which is then used in an
optimisation model. The optimisation model uses two loss functions namely a point-to-plane metric
and point-to-edge metric. So essentially a P2Pl-ICP with and extra criteria of edges. This method can
potentially have high output rates due to this sparsity, and also be more invariant to a proper prior
transformation.

2.2.1.5
Coarse-to-Fine Two-step Registration
When using local scan-matchers like ICP that relies on correspondence pairs the choice of max-
correspondence-distance parameter, dmax, has a big effect on the accuracy of the registration and
whether it converges to the correct solution at all. If set too high the transformation results will be of
high noise, and if set too small the correct solution might never be reached due to limited iterations.
Therefore, in order to increase both the probability of a correct convergence and the accuracy of the
final solution the ICP process can be split into two sweeps of iterations. The first sweep is a coarse
fit with few iterations and a quite high correspondence distance, about an order of magnitude higher,
dC
max ≥10 · dmax, that should find a good but unrefined general fit. The second sweep is then meant
to refine the solution, by using the alignment from the coarse fit as the initial guess. This is a general
implementation in many of the referenced LOAM systems using ICP algorithms for scan-matching,
and in the earlier project [13] it not only improved robustness of the odometry but also improved
correctly identifying loop-closure candidates.

testing. Keyframing too often will not have the full benefit of removing noise accumulation, as this
is more akin to no keyframing at all, while scarce keyframes will eventually bring rise to too vast
disparity between the target and source, leading to degenerate registrations. Criteria like a fitness
threshold is therefore a good-to-have criteria but will most likely not be the main criteria to trigger a
new keyframe.
Keyframe Selection-Scheme
Another aspect to consider is which frame to select as the new keyframe when a keyframe-criteria is
met. The straightforward way is to just make the current source cloud as the new keyframe cloud and
apply the registered transformations to the odometry and continue from this keyframe. However, due
to the geometric criteria, a degenerate registration can thus cause a triggering of a new keyframe, if
the e.g. translation is way off, and beyond the criteria. The new keyframe would then be the product
of this degenerate transformation, which is not good. A selection-scheme to safeguard against this
issue is to chose the previous source as the the new keyframe, and redo the registration by the newly
established keyframe. The degenerate transformation would then be rejected and re-attempted with
a stronger keyframe as the base of the registration.

2.3
Inertial Navigation Systems INS
Inertial Navigation System (INS) is based on the principle of dead-reckoning where if position and
velocity is known the future position can crudely be predicted for some point in time after the last
known state. This assumes no dynamics of the system except for the change in position, why it is
called "dead". This has often been used in navigation of aeroplanes in-between course corrections.
By implementing Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs) the dynamics, specifically the acceleration and
angular rates, can now be measured on-top of the dead-reckoning integration giving more precise
tracking. But because of this the positional uncertainty will increase exponentially with time from
the double-integrated sensor noise and biases, and recursively updating the navigation solution. For
this reason the reliability of INSs is very time dependent. Depending on the grade of the IMU this
varies a lot, where low grade will tend to be unusable after few moments and high grade can have only
relatively small drift even after hours.
The INS implementation of this project is build up around the theory of [1], with a focus on the
stapled-down Inertial-Frame-Navigation model as this is simpler and makes more sense when fused
with the LOAM algorithm. Even though this frame ignores the fact that the surface of the Earth is
not a proper inertial frame the errors prescribed from this, such as the fictitious forces and the rotation
of the Earth, are negligible considering the small scale of the time ranges, velocities and trajectories
of the experiments.
INS is not limited to accelerometers and gyroscopes but these are essential. Additionally magnetome-
ters and even barometers can be included. A magnetometer specifically can mitigate the gyroscopic
drift as this is an absolute sensor measuring the ambient magnetic field. By measuring a constant
field, such as that of Earth, one can assume that the INS is not rotating if there are no changes in the
measured field. Magnetometers are, however, quite easily influenced by the magnetic fields induced by
electronics and highly ferromagnetic materials, typically present on modern devices and vehicles. For
this reason they can be hard to implement properly.
2.3.1
Inertial Measurement Units - IMU
IMUs are units consisting of several inertial sensors arranged along the three orthogonal axes so that
movements and rotations are fully resolved in 3D space. The sensors are mainly accelerometers and
gyroscopes with an internal processor that handles the raw data to a calibrated output and some
storage for a calibration profile. Most commercial IMUs are fully factory calibrated from slight axis
misalignment and electronic variability. IMUs can also carry thermometers as the calibrations are
usually temperature dependent, or rather the output of the electronics are, and therefore need a
temperature-value to correct for this.
2.3.1.1
Accelerometers
The main principles of accelerometers are a mass-spring system wherein a proof-mass is suspended/-
controlled by springs enclosed in a case, see fig. 2.3.1. When the unit is at rest the mass will be in
a equilibrium position relative to the case. When under are constant non-gravitational acceleration
the mass will be displaced from this position. The displacement of the mass will proportional to the
acceleration applied to the case. The displacement can then be measured be other means such as a
capacitative measure or potentiometers.

Gyroscopes
Gyroscopes are internally referenced sensors that register the angular rates of the sensor. The definition
has been but is no longer limited to the types using a spinning mass. The basic gyroscope is based
on the property of spinning masses having angular momentum and will strive to preserve this when
subjected to a torque. When the torque is not coinciding with the angular momentum of the spin it
will result in a precession motion i.e. the spinning axis will rotate about the mutually perpendicular
axis of the torque and the angular momentum. This is what makes a spinning top start to wobble
around when slowing down. More modern gyros use other means such as a beam of light bouncing in a
ring and measuring its offset from the its origin (Ring Laser Gyro, RLG), or Vibratoy Gyros that have
a harmonically oscillating elements that will be subject to fictitious forces when the frame is rotating.
For various reasons gyroscopes are sensitive to rapid changes in linear acceleration and can therefore
produces false readings when subject to bumps or shocks.

3.1
The Kalman Filter
3.1.1
Introduction
The Kalman-Filter (KF) is a tool to estimate the state of a dynamic system. What is a dynamic
system? Well anything that is not constant, and its state is a mathematical quantisation of whatever
properties that is of interest, or are needed to predict the next state. The Kalman-filter is therefore
applicable to almost any process that is linearly predictable and is perturbed by white noise, also known
as Gaussian noise. It balances the output of a predictor in the filter against outside measurements
from sensors, by comparing the uncertainties of both the filter will output the most optimal solution.
The measurements will have uncertainties defined by the sensor properties, while the predictor will
gain more more uncertainty from noise as it tries to predict the next state. The term "filter" is used
because it removes/filters-out what is unwanted. And what is unwanted is errors and noise so what is
left should be a more accurate and precise estimate of the state.
It is also very lightweight in its implementation on processors as it does not need to retain all the
previous states, saving memory, and the update steps are simple because of the linearity. The book
"Kalman Filtering: Theory and Practise Using MATLAB" [27] has much more history and details
about Kalman filters and their applications, while [1] provides great insight to its application with
navigation and localisation.

3.2.3
Undistortion of Point Clouds by IMU Preintegration
Because of the way that current LiDAR-scanners scan in time, movement of the scanner during the
scan period, tk to tk + τ, will cause a distortion of the scan, known as motion-distortion. When an
object is hit by a laser of the LiDAR it creates a point in space relative to the position of the LiDAR at
that point in time, tk. At the end the scan, tk +τ, the same object might be hit again, but now located
differently in the frame of the LiDAR, and the latest scan point will therefore not correlate with the
initial scan point. Much like motion-blur in photographs. The points are basically in two different
reference frames and the difference is the distortion. The distortion will have different magnitudes
depending on the velocity of the motion and the frequency of the scan-rate. Higher velocities and
rotation rates will cause greater distortion while high scan rates will reduce the distortion. Because
the distortion can be viewed as a transformation between reference frames it can split into a rotational
and a translational part.

is mounted on a car going approximately 50 km

3.2.5
Madgwick Orientation Filter
The Madgwick filter [14] is an orientation fusion filter that uses both the gyroscopes and the accelerom-
eters, and potentially also the magnetometers, to estimate a gravity referenced orientation. This means
it can be used to find the initial gravity referenced pitch and roll of the LiDAR odometry and the INS.
Yaw is arbitrarily set to zero at initialisation and be corrected for in post processing.
Having a proper initial orientation is important for the Kalman-filter or other fusion method such that
the measured gravity vector from the IMU is actually parallel with the z-vector of the local navigational
frame established by the odometry. The orientation estimations of the Lidar Odometry are therefore
better aligned compared to that of the world frame with respect to roll and pitch.
Since the filter determines an orientation with respect to the gravity vector it can also be used to
remove the gravity contribution from the accelerometer measurements. The residual from this is then
absorbed/mitigated by the estimated accelerometer bias along the z-vector.
3.2.6
Summary of the Sensor Fusion Implementation
Because the a priori is embedded into the point cloud via the undistortion, the transformation found
from scan matching is a residual transformation meaning the difference between the preintegration
transformation and the LiDAR optimised final transformation. The information from this residual is
used to update the GO and thus the biases of the gyroscope and accelerometer.

The LiDAR was set to record at 10 Hz while the IMU recorded at 100 Hz. This frequency was chosen

sampling rates.

Fusion differences
The two estimators fuse with the INS in different ways. The GO-corrected INS is supplied with the
bias-corrected inputs, and is simultaneously directly corrected from the output of the GO, whereas the
ESKF is given the raw biases readings and is then corrected by the errors estimated in the filter. This
is quite counter-intuitive, one could think that it might be more benificial to use the biases in the INS,
==PAGE:64==
50
CHAPTER 5. RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
but this only causes the estimated bias value to be half of the true value. Inside the ESKF the modelling
of the biases servers to better estimate a correction, which is then applied at the update/measurement
step. The halving effect stems from the fact that the biases is then actually modelled in two places.
The filter is trying to optimise the prediction of the error and therefore it needs the proper error (i.e.
not corrected by as bias estimate) to compare against in the measurement step.
Another difference is the inputs. The observer is given the residual in position and orientation while
the ESKF is given the residual in the position and velocity and therefore no direct update of the
orientation.

Geometric Observer
In all of the scenarios the gains where adjusted to accomplish a satisfying result, table 5.1.2, and it
shows that even in situations of considerable noise the estimation of the biases will converge towards
the true values. It is also robust to variances, as can be seen in the "long run" simulation, fig. 5.1.2. It
is however necessary to lower the gains to maintain stability which in turn makes convergence slower.
In practice, it might be difficult to know if the observer is tuned properly when the true values are
not known. It might be effective to implement some sort of adaptable gain that will decrease the
gain values after an initial "warm-up" period where the observer still needs to converge on the biases.
This would allow for initial high gains that leads to a fast rough convergence of the biases. Then, by
lowering the gains, the small variations temporal in the biases would tracked with more finesse.
So initial high gain for rough convergence on off-set / turn-on biases, and then lower gains for tracking
the bias walk.

The ESKF estimates accelerometer bias quite well in the "low noise" scenario, though it has settled

5.3
Evaluation of LiDAR Inertial Odometry Implemention
First of it should be noted that the odometry solution has good performance for straight line move-
ments, i.e. when the general heading is consistent with the initial heading. But, unfortunately, a weird
problem occurs whenever the rover has turned about 90◦or more from the initial heading. For some
reason not identified the ICP registrations becomes highly irregular and outright erroneous, this goes
for all the implemented scan matching methods (P2Pl-ICP, GICP, and P2D-NDT). The problems is
therefore not with the scan matching algorithms it seems, but rather a problem in the implementation.
This is investigated in section 5.5. For this reason the system has not been able to run through an
entire data set, making end-point to end-point evaluation unavailable. To get around this issue, with
regards to odometry evaluation, the recordings are cropped to only feature the parts where a general
heading is maintained. The evaluation is therefore mostly focused on the odometry fitness-metrics such
as the scan-match fitness, the translation covariance estimation, and the scan-matching residual from
the preintegrated IMU prior estimation, as well as bias estimation and correction via the Geometric
Observer. The lack of a proper ground truth is really a great shame as ground truth comparison is
best way to convey the accuracy of an odometry solution intuitively, and is the standard in evaluation
of odometry. The second go-to evaluation measure is then end-to-end evaluation, which is often used
when ground truth is hard to establish. But since this is also not available it has been problematic
to evaluate and convey the results of the algorithm. Hopefully the detail in the point cloud maps will
convey the accuracy of the odometry, that is, when it is not deteriorating.

Test Metrics
The fitness metrics is a tool to show how well the preintegrated prior has predicted the final transfor-
mation. Another point of looking at the metrics is to look into whether they can be used to detect
what [1] calls "bad data". A quick reiteration of the metrics:
1. The Linear Residual Norm is the norm of the translational part of the scan match transformation
∆t where ∆T L = T (∆t, ∆q). It is then also normalised by the the norm of the full odometry
translation in order to not bias low velocity with better residuals.
2. The Rotational Residual is the norm of complex vector of the residual quaternion. ∥∆⃗q∥where
∆q = (∆q◦, ∆⃗q). This and the linear residual metric show how well the INS preintegration fits
with the LO.
3. The Scan Match Fitness is a measure of the average distance between correspondence points
at the estimated transformation, and is therefore a measure of the correctness of the estimated
registration transformation. This is relayed from the ICP implementation in C++.
4. The translation covariance is an attempt at a measures that shows if a translation is erratic and
therefore likely erroneous. It does so by comparing the estimated translation in the local frame
to average of the past three translation as a variance calculation, σt2 = (t −µ(3)
t )2
All metrics should be as small as possible with zero meaning no error at all. Because they usually are
quite small, with huge spikes from bad estimations, they are plotted on a log scale. Boxplots, however,
are in linear scale to show the true scale and variation.

it happens it shows quite clearly, as the effective distortion scales with the depth of the scan point.

it is important to correct for.

Not Demeaning Points Clouds before Scan Matching
Another hypothesis for the source of the error is that the point clouds are not demeaned before scan
matching, i.e.
because the Livox MD-70 scanner only scans in front of itself the produced point
clouds bary-center will not be close to origo of coordinate frame. The ICP therefore struggles in the
optimisation of the scan matching, as the rigid body transformation is such that it rotates all the
points about origo. If the bary-center of the point cloud is not origo the rotational estimate is then
analogous to aiming a laser pointer at a distant point. The point disparity error is enhanced by the
distance from origo which potentially skews the optimisation gradients.
This makes sense because the error is mostly rotational, seen by the down-pitching but with still
moderate translation estimates.
If this is the case, this issue would not be very profound with spin-scanners, as the scanner is located
in origo, scanning points all around. This effectively balances the point clouds.

hits a single wall. In this instance the scan-matching process degenerates as this ultimately relies on
the uniqueness and the features in the points clouds. The fitting of a single plane to another plane in
a local map is highly ambiguous. This ambiguity is also the most likely cause of the zig-zagging, seen
by the high noise of the covariance in the y-axis, fig. 5.3.22 and the covariance bubbles elongated along
the y-axis in fig. 2.2.7. When Livox-scanners are used for LOAM they are therefore seldom used alone,
and if they are, the systems rely on qualitative feature matching schemes rather than ICP, [37]. The
advantage of the scanner, however, is that with the non-repeating pattern the scanner can accumulate
very dense point cloud maps, which is great when used for surveying and mapping. An example is
the surveying of bridges for maintenance, mentioned in the introduction, chapter 1. Spin-scanners, in
contrast, rarely have problems with degeneration as there will most likely be a great deal of features in
each scan. This allows for the use of quantitative matching methods, such as ICP that do not require
extrapolation of features. For instance in the previous project of the author, [13] the Velodyne VLP-16
scanner along with the P2Pl-ICP method was used with much success.
Dense vs Sparse Keyframing
These differences of the scanners also affects the keyframing approach. In the previous project with
the Velodyne VLP-16-scanner the keyframing was a Sparse approach, meaning that it was in fact
disadvantageous to keyframe to often, as this would accumulate more drift. And because the scans
encompass the entirety of the environment in every frame, there was not much gained by including
more scans in the local map, which only had a width of 3. With the Livox MD-70 it is the polar
opposite, i.e. a Dense approach, that works the best. If not including all of the most recent frames
in the scan matching the bias towards fitting the scan pattern, rather than the structure, wins out
leading to wrong odometry, fig. 2.2.6. This also requires the local map to be much wider, set to 100
frames in the test, including all scan in the past 10 seconds.
Normal estimation is Difficult
Many of the scan matching methods utilises the normals of the points, as they describe the surface
from which the point is sampled. These normals are, however, not inherent to the raw cloud and
need to be established in preprocessing. The usual method of eigen-decomposition of the covariance
matrix is great when the clouds are uniformly dense. But the scans from either of the scanners are not
very uniform. They have very high sample density along their scan-lines, but in the normal direction
to them, it is very sparse. A novel idea for estimating normals from singular scans is to utilise the
==PAGE:93==
79
knowledge that the points in the scan data structure are ordered along the scan-lines. Then it is a
matter of locating the closet point that is not in same scan-line as the query point. By assuming that
the tangent to the scan-line is also tangent to the surface, and the vector to the closet point in the
neighbour scan-line is also tangent to the surface, and close to orthogonal to first vector tangent, the
surface normal can be established from the normalised cross product of these.

Popular field of research
At the time of writing, the subject of sensor-fusion implementations for Simultaneous Localisation And
Mapping (SLAM) with LiDAR and IMU, and also cameras, is immensely popular, especially in China.
This popularity is definitely a product of the requirements set by robust autonomous systems, a main
contender being self-driving cars. The prospect of being chauffeured every day to work brings joy to
the the minds of many in the working force of the modern world. This brings focus and attention,
and subsequently more research. For this reason the wave-front of research on the subject is moving
very rapidly and is very far along already. It is therefore challenging to compete with the most recent
innovation on the matter.
An example being one of the newest papers encountered in the research for this thesis. The paper is
"Point-LIO" by He et. al. [3] from this spring, which is just utterly impressive. They have managed
to make a hyper-robust LIO system that can output odometry updates at rates of 4 to 8 kHz, yes, 4-8
thousand Hz, far beyond the rate of most IMUs. They do this by assuming that every single point from
the LiDAR-scanner is its own data point in time, instead of being a part of a cloud. With an Extended
Kalman Filter it is predicted where it should be located. If there are enough points to constitute a
plane, in the vicinity of the new point, the point is fitted to that plane, else it just integrated into the
cloud at the predicted location. With LiDAR-scanners producing 100 000 points per second, the high
rate of output is understandable. The solution is even robust towards saturation of the IMU-sensor,
shown in tests where the rig is spun by a motor at an eye-watering 75 rad
s , which is about 700RPM.
By modelling every point in this manner, it also entirely eliminates the problem of motion distortion,
as each new point is referenced to the updated odometry estimation by the earlier point. It is strenuous
to imagine something better than this with the current technologies, but such is the veil of the future.
Low point of View
The low point of view of the scanner is thought to have created some sort of bias towards the section
right in front of the UGV. Because of the very close proximity to the scanner a lot of the scan points
have been congested in this area, creating an over-representation. Also, from the specification of the
scanner it says that the error of the point depth increases when within a meter or so from the scanner.
In any case the relative error is bigger for closer vs farther points.

CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION
Individual point timestamps
In the prepossessing step the timestamp of each point in the point clouds are inferred from their order
and the full scan time delta. However, if say only half of the projected beams receive a return single,
many points in the cloud would be missing. This means that the inferred timestamps are wrong, which
will affect the undistortion process as this is done from the timestamp. This, though, was the only
way of getting timestamps of the individual points without access to the raw data packets.
6.1
Future Work and Implementations
• The main point for future work is an improvement to the robustness and speed of the scan-
matching. Mainly to resolve the error when turning, and the scan-matching process-time is the
only bottleneck that prevents real-time operation. An idea inspired by the [3] is to cut/split the
the scans into IMU-synced sub-scans. This way the rate of output would match that of the IMU
and the scan-matching have a lot less points to work lowering process-times.
• The implementation of a Pose-Graph (PG) backend for further optimisation of the fusion, as
well as enabling loop-closure capabilities.
• Having previously also worked with Visual Odometry (VO) it would be interesting to also im-
plement a camera in fusion model. VO is often quite robust to outright degeneration but has
more frame-to-frame drift.
• Implementation of the Geometric Observer properly so that it is not bounded by the LO but
instead working more like an independent INS. Also, make an ESKF implementation designed
for use with LO, as the simulated one is mainly designed for use with GNSS.
• Maybe even attempt to fuse the concepts of the Contracting Observer with a KF, by adjusting
observer gains from a Kalman controlled approach. But this is purely speculative.
• Implement and test proposed method for normal estimation.
• The max velocity of the rover never exceeded 1 m
s in all tests, so more rigorous testing, with
higher velocities and also aerial would be very interesting - once the scan matching is improved.
This would also require more testing with higher IMU-rates.
• For more rigorous testing it will also be necessary with an extrinsic calibration between the
LiDAR and the IMU. In this implementation, lack of calibration has not caused issues due to
the short mounting distance in-between the the two sensors, and the rather slow speeds of the
rover.

CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
The objective of this thesis has been to implement and evaluate a method for integration of Light
Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) and Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) for localisation in Global
Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) denied environments.
The implementation created is mostly
inspired by [2] where a preintegrated Inertial Navigation System (INS) a priori prediction works really
well to estimate the transformation in-between LiDAR scans, with normalised translational residuals
in the range of 0.12 m
m for indoors environments, and 0.25 m
m for outdoors. Due to an error the ground
truth data was unfortunately corrupted, so the evaluation has been based mostly on various fitness
metrics in order to detect bad estimations.
Two methods of undistortion has been tested and compared in order to show the improvement of the
fusion implementation. The first being a non-fusion method that interpolates the previous odometry
estimation to undistort the next scan. The second method is using the preintegration of the IMU for
point-wise modelled undistortion.
A Madgwick orientation filter has been implemented for initial gravity referenced orientation of the
odometry, and removal of the gravity measurements in the accelerometer measurements. This means
that if odometry starts on a slope, it will be corrected for in the odometry estimation. Both intentions
of the filter has worked really well.
A method for covariance estimation of the translational odometry updates is proposed. The method
was made for implementation with Kalman-Filters (KFs).
The method is shown to provide good
estimation of bad translation.
In order to determine the best fusion-model, extensive simulations of various noise scenarios has been
conducted. The tests simulated an INS corrected by a Geometric Observer (GO) and an Error State
Kalman-Filter (ESKF) through pose measurement of other sources such as LO. The GO showed much
better performance in both tracking of IMU biases, and maintaining the INS stable and true in even in
the harshest simulated scenario. For this reason the GO has been used for bias tracking and correction
of initial preintegration states, in the real implementation.
The implementation was fully made in Robotic Operating System 2 (ROS2) for later use at DTU
Space. With improvement to the scan match processing times, the system should be able to operate
in real-time as all preprocessing is handled within the time of a single frame.
On some of the more strenuous data the non-fusion Lidar Odometry model was tested, where it
diverged and failed to maintain odometry while the Lidar Inertial Odometry (LIO) fusion model showed
accurate odometry all the way through, underlining the increased robustness. All in all the developed
implementation has been shown to be more robust and more accurate than aLiDAR-equivalent non-
fusion LO.
In the last hours of writing the U-turn error has been localised to an error in the update of odometry
pose. The error was introduced in the code during the conversion to quaternion orientations, when cor-
recting for the degeneration of the orientation matrix. As deduced by the analysis the error was never
with the scan matching, which always made correct estimations. It was just propagated incorrectly.
The error is gone but there is no time to correct the report. Everything in the report still holds true
however.
