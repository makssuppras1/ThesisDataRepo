Abstract
G/G/k queues are everywhere in daily life, present in call centers, hospital emergency
rooms, manufacturing lines, and traffic systems, where arrival rates and service times are
generally distributed, and there are k servers to serve customers. These systems prove
difficult to predict and are, therefore, traditionally simulated to obtain system KPIs. While
recent studies have explored neural networks’ ability to predict patterns in G/G/k queuing
systems, none have predicted the related KPIs, representing a significant research gap.
This study developed a three­stage approach to address this challenge. First, a com­
prehensive G/G/k Battery Electric Vehicle (BEV) charging simulation incorporated four
sequential stations with real­time information sharing and Orthogonal­Maximin Latin Hy­
percube Designs (OMLHD) to generate robust training data across multiple charger con­
figurations. Second, a bidirectional Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) neural network
using a 2­hour sliding window predicted marginal wait times across all stations. Third,
an optimization framework leveraged ML predictions to identify the optimal infrastructure
configuration satisfying maximum wait time thresholds.
The ML model achieved near­instantaneous predictions with 8× computational speedup
compared to traditional Monte Carlo simulations – a speedup that increases with dataset
size. The neural network successfully captured wait time patterns and identified Station
1 as the bottleneck, correctly predicting elasticity relationships between wait times and
charger count modifications.
Key limitations include difficulties predicting high wait times due to zero­inflated data,
though this improved with higher arrival rates and inclusion of queue lengths as features.
The neural network and optimization framework demonstrated broad application oppor­
tunities across multiple sectors with G/G/k dynamics, offering valuable decision support
in long­term strategic infrastructure planning, and efficient resource allocation in rapidly
changing operational environments.

Introduction
Machine Learning (ML) is rapidly transforming how we analyze and predict complex sys­
tems across various domains, including healthcare, manufacturing, transportation, and
telecommunications. ML models are increasingly used to provide near­instantaneous
support and insights, which previously was achieved through computationally heavy and
time­consuming methods (Alowais et al., 2023; Tercan & Meisen, 2022; ScienceLogic,
2024; Saki & Soori, 2025).
Many systems and their Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) are challenging to predict and
aggregate due to their underlying G/G/k dynamics, where arrivals and service times follow
general (non­exponential) distributions and k parallel servers are available (Shortle et al.,
2018). These systems are present in various sectors and industries, including patients
arriving at the hospital emergency room, customers queuing at customer service, orders
arriving at manufacturing lines, and vehicles arriving at charging stations – all of which
experience varying service times. A common characteristic of these scenarios is their
sensitivity to congestion, i.e., wait time, affecting the overall system flow and general user
satisfaction.
Unlike simpler M/M/k systems with Poisson arrivals and exponential service times, en­
abling analytical solutions, G/G/k systems require complex Monte Carlo simulations to
predict dynamics and KPIs such as wait times, queue lengths, and system utilization,
which are impractical for real­world applications due to their computational complexity
and time requirements (Shortle et al., 2018).
If the G/G/k dynamics could be captured by ML models, it would enable near­instantaneous
predictions of critical KPIs. It would provide a powerful tool for real­time operational man­
agement support, optimizing staffing schedules in emergency rooms, allocating resources
in manufacturing systems, and guiding user decisions in service systems and traffic.
Recent research has begun exploring the potential of ML predicting single­server G/G/1
system patterns. Studies show that recurrent neural networks can predict complex queu­
ing systems with near­instantaneous accuracy using minimal data input, demonstrating
substantial computational improvements over traditional methods (Baron et al., 2024;
Sherzer, 2024). However, research gaps exist as no studies have explored applying
these ML models to multi­server G/G/k systems, which represents the majority of real­
world scenarios.
This research addresses this gap through a comprehensive study that simulates a G/G/k
system and develops and evaluates ML models to predict KPIs. Applying a deductive
framework, this study begins with the general theoretical premise that ML can predict
G/G/k dynamics, then validates this with a specific application. Using Battery Electric
Vehicle (BEV) charging dynamics as a case study, a neural network is developed to predict
wait times in a G/G/k queuing system across multiple charging stations with information­
sharing capabilities. The case study represents an ideal test laboratory for G/G/k ML
model prediction application, as charging stations exhibit general vehicle arrival patterns,
service times vary, and there are multiple parallel servers. It has practical importance
for infrastructure planning and user experience, demonstrating its possible application in
other sectors and scenarios.
This leads to the research questions:

Primary Research Question:
How can machine learning models be designed to predict KPIs in G/G/k queuing systems,
and what performance and computational trade­offs exist compared to traditional Monte
Carlo simulations?
Case Study Research Question:
How can machine learning models predict wait times at electric vehicle charging stations
within a G/G/k framework with information­sharing capabilities, and what insights does
this provide for infrastructure optimization and user decision­making?
The methodology applies a two­stage approach. First, the study creates a comprehensive
discrete­event simulation framework combined with Monte Carlo methods to generate a
robust dataset capturing human decision­making behavior in G/G/k charging dynamics
with information­sharing capabilities (Vandet & Rich, 2023). Second, the dataset is used
to train a bidirectional Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) neural network to predict marginal
wait times across multiple charging stations, enabling near­instantaneous predictions suit­
able for real­world application, providing a computationally superior and significantly faster
alternative to traditional simulation approaches.
In addition to analyzing the computational efficiency of the ML model, this study proposes
an optimization framework that utilizes the ML predictions to determine the optimal in­
frastructure configuration. Specifically, the study develops an optimization model that
determines the minimum number of chargers required to maintain a reasonable service
level, given a specific vehicle arrival pattern.
This study demonstrates the practical value of ML­based G/G/k predictions, extending
beyond the specific case study, to showcase possible applications in multiple sectors,
optimizing system KPIs, and potentially, along the way, improving the triple bottom line of
sustainability: people, planet, and profit (Elkington, 1997).
The following chapters present: Chapter 2: Literature Review, Chapter 3: Theoretical
Foundation, Chapter 4: Case Study, Chapter 5: Methodology, Chapter 6: Results, Chap­
ter 7: Discussion, and Chapter 8: Conclusion.

Literature Review
2.1
ML for Queuing System Predictions
Recent studies have explored the feasibility of applying ML models for G/G queuing pre­
diction.
Soldan et al. (2021) developed a logistic regression model for predicting short­term charg­
ing station occupancy. They combine historical data with real­time updates from charging
stations to make predictions, thereby enhancing existing models that rely solely on histor­
ical patterns. The model predicts the charging station occupancy probability 15 minutes
ahead and demonstrates the importance of updating parameters according to real­world
dynamics.
Sherzer (2024) studies how neural networks can be used to approximate the steady­state
number of customers at two stations in a G/G/1 tandem queuing system based on the ex­
ternal inter­arrival time and service time distributions. The study develops three neural
networks: one approximating the departure process of a G/G/1 queue, another the sta­
tionary probabilities of the number of customers in the system, and a third predicting the
departure process of a G/G/k system. With the neural networks, he achieves an error
below 5% in most scenarios with the near real­time inference superior to more computa­
tionally heavy methods like simulations.
Baron et al. (2024) developed a neural network to predict the stationary system­length
distribution of a G/G/1 queue. They generate an extensive library of training instances by
developing a sampling algorithm that covers a wide range of distributions and utilize this
in ML training. A significant finding of the study is that the neural network can be trained
using only the first five moments of both arrival and service time distributions. The model
outperforms available methods in both accuracy and runtime. While the study focuses
on a single­server G/G/1 system, the methodology demonstrates the feasibility of using
neural networks to predict queuing systems, paving the way for potentially expanding this
approach to G/G/k systems.
Sherzer et al. (2024) developed a distinct neural network, called Moment­Based Recurrent
Neural Network (MBRNN), to predict transient customer distribution in a G/G/1 queuing
system with time­dependent arrivals. Unlike stationary systems, they develop a system
that never reaches steady state. They utilize simulated data both as their ground truth
and to train their ML model. The MBRNN model excels as it only requires the first four
moments of arrival and service time distributions to train, achieving an error of less than
3%, even under the worst performance errors.
The different ML models developed all perform almost instantaneous predictions com­
pared to computationally heavy, time­consuming simulations (Baron et al., 2024; Sherzer,
2024; Sherzer et al., 2024). However, research gaps exist in G/G/k systems with k>1
servers using deep learning approaches.

2.2.1
Charging Dynamics
Thorhauge et al. (2024) present an adaptive stated choice experiment and develop a
mixed logit model to study charging behavior during long­distance travel. They analyze
how the probability of charging is affected by the remaining range, which relates to range
anxiety. They create a realistic decision­making environment where options are dynam­
ically removed as drivers progress along the route. The study reveals that BEV drivers
often choose to charge early in the trip, even when the SoC level is high, due to range
anxiety and concerns about wait time. The findings support the need for wait time predic­
tion models to improve both system­wide charger utilization and range utilization of the
BEVs which is backed up by Hipolito et al. (2022).
Hipolito et al. (2022) study the steady­state distribution of SoC levels for EVs. They de­
velop a generic parametric model to determine whether to charge based on the SoC and
the daily relative range. They simulate charging dynamics and observe the SoC and
charging events. The study reveals that drivers typically use only around 40% of the bat­
tery before charging. Range anxiety leads to early charging behavior. Furthermore, the
steady­state SoC distribution follows a beta distribution with mean levels between 40%
and 80% at the start of the day.
This study’s simulation incorporates the shared information principle demonstrated by
Vandet and Rich (2023), where real­time communication between BEVs and charging
stations enables optimal station selection, reducing the overall system wait time through
better vehicle dispersion. Information­sharing provides vehicles with system­wide infor­
mation, creating a self­organizing system, enabling drivers to utilize their battery flexibility
in combination with geographically distributed charging infrastructure to avoid congested
stations. The system performance with information sharing approaches optimal levels
even when only 50% of the population has access to shared information (Vandet & Rich,
2023).
Rich et al. (2022) conducted a comprehensive welfare economic analysis of charging
infrastructure investments in Denmark by developing 24 scenarios to examine possible
charging dynamics for year 2030. They evaluate the trade­offs between infrastructure
costs and wait time benefits by applying a simulation­based optimization approach that
combines G/G/k queuing systems with agent­based modeling. They find that a maximum
wait time guarantee of 10 minutes represents the most efficient solution from a welfare
economic perspective, balancing infrastructure costs with user time savings.
The behavioral studies above demonstrate the complexity of charging decisions and how
patterns are influenced by various factors like SoC levels, range anxiety, and wait times.
Due to this complexity, brute­force Monte Carlo simulations are often applied to simulate
charging systems. However, as will be explained in Section 3.1.1, they are computa­
tionally heavy and time­consuming. This has led researchers to explore the possibility of
approximating these queuing systems with machine learning to achieve faster, real­time
predictions.

and service times are generally distributed with k agents in parallel handling the calls. Nik­
far and Mohammadzadeh (2025) predict the number of calls in a call center using deep
learning, developing both a unidirectional Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) model, a bidi­

2.2.3
Hospitals
Hospital emergency rooms are another excellent example of classic G/G/k queuing sys­
tems, where patient arrivals and treatment times are generally distributed, and k employ­
ees attend to the patients in parallel. Hijry and Olawoyin (2021) predict the patient wait
times in a hospital emergency room using deep learning, developing four different ML
models using different optimizers, trained on data from a single hospital. The best per­
forming optimizer (the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)) achieved a Mean Absolute
Error (MAE) of 10.80 minutes, corresponding to 24% error reduction compared to the
traditional approach used in the hospital.
Pak et al. (2021) have likewise developed different ML models (ridge regression, LASSO,
and Random Forests) to predict the wait time in hospital emergency rooms, comparing
it to traditional methods of rolling average or median estimates. Their ML model outper­
forms the rolling average by 20% based on mean squared prediction error (MSPE) and
mean absolute prediction error (MAPE). They also demonstrated that quantile regression
reduced the number of underpredictions by 42%.
Hijry and Olawoyin (2021) and Pak et al. (2021) demonstrate that ML approaches can sig­
nificantly enhance real­world wait time predictions for hospital emergency rooms, show­
casing ML’s potential as an operational tool that can improve patient flow and experience,
staff allocation, and overall system performance.
2.2.4
Limitations and Challenges
Even though recent literature shows great potential for predicting complex queuing sys­
tems using ML, some systems still prove too complicated to predict accurately. For in­
stance, wait time prediction in busy cities is extremely challenging as dynamics are af­
fected by erratic station availability, sporadic wait periods due to high variability in traffic
conditions, charger utilization patterns, and random arrival rates (Kumari, 2024). The ge­
ographical dispersion of the chargers and the directions of vehicles in a city add to the
complexity of the system. Hüttel et al. (2023), furthermore, find that 61% of demand is
hidden due to unavailable chargers, risking ML models trained on historical data being
heavily biased, underestimating the actual demand, as unobserved data is not included
in the training process.

Theoretical Foundation
3.1
Simulation Theory
3.1.1
Discrete­Event and Monte Carlo Simulations
Stochastic simulations are methods used to model complex systems that evolve under
uncertainty, such as stock markets, weather patterns, or, as in this case, BEV charging
dynamics. These simulations incorporate uncertainty and randomness (the stochastic el­
ement) that characterize real­world charging behavior and arrival patterns (Graham & Ta­
lay, 2013). Different classes of stochastic simulations address different modeling needs.
Discrete­event simulations model the temporal evolution of a system by simulating dis­
crete events that change the system’s state and advance time from event to event rather
than operating with a fixed time interval (Banks et al., 2010).
Monte Carlo simulations are a class of stochastic simulations characterized by repeated
random sampling from probability distributions to estimate system behavior and outcomes
(Gobet, 2016). While Monte Carlo simulations often require extensive iterations to achieve
statistical convergence, making them computationally heavy and time­consuming, they
capture the essential randomness in arrival processes, charging durations, and routing
decisions (Graham & Talay, 2013).
This study will combine the temporal dynamics of discrete­event simulation with the prob­
abilistic sampling of Monte Carlo, creating a robust framework, particularly well­suited for
G/G/k queuing systems, where arrivals and service times follow a general distribution,
and k servers are available at each station (Banks et al., 2010).
3.1.2
Queuing dynamics
Queuing systems are characterized by three key components: stochastic arrival process,
stochastic service process, and system configurations. In Kendall notation, a G/G/k queue
represents a system with:
• General (G) arrival process
• General (G) service process
• k parallel servers
While M/M/k queues assume Poisson arrivals and exponential service times, enabling
analytical predictions through a Markov chain analysis, G/G/k queues have arbitrary dis­
tributions, requiring simulation or approximation methods for analysis, making them sig­
nificantly more computationally complex (Shortle et al., 2018).
Little (1961) defined the long­term average number L of customers in a stationary system
as equal to the arrival rate λ multiplied by the average time W spent in the system:
L = λ · W
(3.1)
As Little’s law applies regardless of arrival process distribution, service distribution, and

Integer Programming and Black­Box Optimization
Integer programming is a class of mathematical optimization problems where the decision
variables are constrained to take integer values (Wolsey, 2020). The objective is to find
the optimal solution to a problem while satisfying specified constraints.
When the objective function cannot be expressed in closed form (explicit mathematical
expressions), as is often the case in neural networks, the problem becomes a black­
box optimization challenge. This situation commonly arises in complex systems such
as neural networks, where the numerous internal non­linear transformations cannot be
simplified into analytical expressions.
Black­box optimization refers to optimization problems where the objective function can
be evaluated at specific inputs, but its analytical form, gradients, or derivatives are un­
available (Conn et al., 2009). In these cases, optimization algorithms rely on function
evaluations instead of mathematical analysis, making black­box approaches necessary
when dealing with machine learning and complex simulations.

Methodology
5.1
Research approach overview
This study employed a two­stage approach to develop an ML model that predicts the
marginal wait time at charging stations for BEVs. First, a discrete­event simulation frame­
work, combined with Monte Carlo components, was used to generate comprehensive data
by modeling realistic charging dynamics patterns on a highway corridor with four stations.
Second, the simulated data was then used to train a bidirectional LSTM neural network to
predict the marginal wait time at each charging station in real­time given realistic inputs,
providing a computationally dominating alternative to typical Monte Carlo simulations.
Discrete­event simulations model the temporal evolution of a system by simulating dis­
crete events that change the system’s state, here exemplified by car arrivals on the high­
way, arrivals at stations, initiation of charging sessions, and transitions between stations
(Banks et al., 2010). The random sampling component and stochastic elements of car
properties from the Monte Carlo simulation represent the stochastic nature of vehicle flow
on a highway. This hybrid approach generates a realistic representation of the complex
dynamics of the BEV charging system.
The simulation incorporated the shared information principle demonstrated by Vandet and
Rich (2023). Its purpose was to replicate a real­world scenario demonstrating the effect
of information sharing. It was used to analyze the impact of different arrival patterns and
station configurations creating different G/G/k dynamics.
The design of the simulation and the development of the neural network integrated and
were based on insights from the Theoretical Foundation, Literature Review, Market Con­
text & Statistical Trends, and Current Market Solutions to ensure realistic charging dy­
namics and capture current and upcoming evolutions within the field.
A comprehensive overview of the various parameters and variables were defined in the
methodology section can be found in Appendix A.
The following methodology section consists of three main components: (1) Simulation
framework development including application of Little’s law to charging dynamics, vehicle
behavior modeling, and data generation, (2) Machine learning framework covering data
preprocessing and LSTM model architecture, and (3) Evaluation framework establishing
the performance metrics and computational complexity analysis.

a closed system where vehicles cannot enter or exit the system at intermediate points.

5.3
Decision­Making Framework
This section outlines the decision­making framework from a top­down approach, first pro­
viding a conceptual overview of the vehicle journey and then diving into the technical
details of utility function calculations and decision­making mechanisms.
5.3.1
Vehicle Journey Process
Upon entering the system, each vehicle follows a multi­stage decision­making process to
determine optimal charging behavior. They evaluate available charging stations at key
decision points, considering factors such as the current SoC level, range requirements,
and expected station­specific states. Based on this evaluation, vehicles select their des­
tination and travel accordingly.
If arriving at a station where all chargers are occupied, vehicles enter a queue and share
information about their expected waiting and charging times. Simultaneously, all the sta­
tions broadcast their current occupancy status. This information­sharing framework in­
tegrates principles from current market solutions: real­time occupancy sharing similar
to ChargeFinder (2025) and EasyCharging (2025), and predictive occupancy features
comparable to Tesla’s software (Hertz, 2023). This comprehensive information sharing
enables drivers throughout the system to make informed decisions about expected wait
times.
Upon completion of a charging session, vehicles re­evaluate their options and may choose
either additional charging stops or exit the system based on their updated SoC level and
shared information across the system. Figure 5.1 visualizes the described infrastructure
setup. The specific decision­making mechanisms, mathematical framework, and utility
function calculation are detailed in the following subsections.
5.3.2
Utility function development

5.6
Model Architecture
This section details the development of the bidirectional LSTM neural network for predict­
ing wait times across multiple stations. The methodology includes the hyperparameter
tuning framework, model architecture design, and training procedures including regular­
ization techniques, preventing overfitting and ensuring robust performance.
5.6.1
Hyperparameter tuning
This study applies two complementary hyperparameter optimization approaches to sys­
tematically explore parameter combinations and identify optimal configurations that mini­
mize prediction error for wait times: Bayesian optimization and Hyperband.
The hyperparameters and their respective search ranges were:
• Number of LSTM layers: 1–3 layers
• Neurons per LSTM layer: 32–256 neurons (step size: 32)
• Dropout rate: 0.1–0.5 for regularization
• Learning rate: 0.0001–0.01 (logarithmic sampling)
The model architecture features bidirectional LSTM layers with batch normalization (mean
of 0 and standard deviation of 1) and dropout for regularization. The output layer consists
of 4 neurons with linear activation functions – one wait time prediction for each station.
The effect of the activation function will be discussed in Section 7.3.1.
The Bayesian optimizer was configured with a maximum of 20 trials, while Hyperband
used a reduction factor of 4 for successive halving. Both optimization methods used a
consistent batch size of 16.
5.6.2
Bidirectional LSTM Design
The final bidirectional LSTM model is configured applying the optimal hyperparameters
identified in the hyperparameter tuning process. The specific values are presented in
Section 6.2.2.
The LSTM model is executed with:
• Input layer: 24 time steps (2­hour sliding window) with 22 features.
• Batch normalization after each LSTM layer.
• Adam optimizer and gradient clipping (norm=1.0)
The predictions are post­processed to eliminate wait time predictions below 5 minutes, to
reflect a realistic threshold below which wait times have insignificant importance for users’
decision­making.
The training process is evaluated by implementing several metrics and regularization tech­
niques, explained in the following section.
5.6.3
Metrics and Regularization Techniques
This study employs three different metrics: Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared
Error (MSE), and Relative Absolute Error (RAE) to evaluate model performance.
Since the ML model predicts wait times for four different stations, metrics are calculated
for both the overall model and for individual stations, providing a comprehensive picture
of the model performance across different outputs.

The simulation parameters were calibrated through iterative testing to reproduce realistic

The calibration process involved:

2. Testing simulation scenarios with different parameter combinations.
3. Evaluating results against target behavioral patterns
4. Iteratively adjusting parameters until desired dispersion across stations was achieved.
The primary calibration objective was to reproduce the vehicle dispersion across stations

The simulation parameters (base rate and peak multiplier), along with the ML model fea­

This study conducts an elasticity analysis to understand the relationship between charging

variable.

Computational Efficiency Analysis
This section presents the computational efficiency analysis for the simulation, ML model

6.4.1
Simulation Computational Efficiency
As mentioned previously, the full simulation contains 102 simulation runs in total (6 ar­
rival patterns across 17 different charger configurations) and the simulation complexity is
approximately O(2, 63 · 106). The runtime for Version 1­4 and that for Version 5 differ sub­
stantially due to the increased number of simulation runs, while the difference between
Version 1 and 2­4 is minimal.
Version 1 has a runtime of 5 seconds, corresponding to 0.83 seconds per simulation run.
With a total of 12,417 vehicles simulated in total, this corresponds to 0.40 milliseconds
per vehicle.
Version 2­4 has a runtime of 6.1 seconds, corresponding to 1 seconds per simulation run,
reflecting the slightly higher traffic intensity. With a total of 18,252 vehicles simulated in
total, this corresponds to 0.33 milliseconds per vehicle.
Version 5 has a runtime of 2 minutes and 8 seconds, corresponding to 1.25 seconds per
simulation run. With a total of 310,110 vehicles simulated across all runs, this corresponds
to 0.41 milliseconds per vehicle.
With this simulation complexity as a baseline, the complexity for the ML model during
training and inference is now evaluated.
6.4.2
Machine Learning Efficiency
The training of the ML model has a complexity of O(4.59 · 109), with an actual runtime
of 34 minutes, and 41 seconds for 17 epochs. This computational intensity is expected
and acceptable, and bears little weight, as training is performed only once curing model
development.
The real­time inference complexity for the ML model is O(2.87·106), with an actual runtime
of 16.3 seconds for all 102 configurations, corresponding to 0.16 seconds per simulation
run. It represents an 7.8× speedup compared to the simulation runtime for Version 5.

Elasticities for the simulation results are calculated using the average of the maximum

In the BEV charging system case, negative elasticities indicate the expected relationship
where adding chargers reduces wait times or removing chargers increases wait time,
while positive elasticities represent the counterintuitive scenario where adding chargers
increases wait times or removing chargers decreases wait times.
The 95% confidence intervals (CI) are calculated using the bootstrap method with 2000
resamples of the daily maximum wait times, taking the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the
resulting elasticity distribution.
The G/G/k BEV charging system embeds great stochastic variability and a non­monotonic
relationship between the charger count and the wait time which influences the elasticity
calculations presented below. The implications of these characteristics are further dis­

Discussion
This section evaluates the methodology, findings, and implications of this research on the
application of ML to predict G/G/k dynamics. The discussion begins by examining key de­
sign decisions that affect the simulation framework and ML architecture and analyzes how
these choices influence the results and performance. The chapter then interprets primary
findings and addresses whether ML can replace Monte Carlo simulations for real­time
wait time predictions in G/G/k queuing systems. System performance metrics and com­
putational efficiency gains are evaluated within a broader context of queuing theory and
practical applications. The discussion then examines how the developed ML framework
addresses existing gaps in the literature and in current market solutions for charging in­
frastructure. Furthermore, the broader implications of ML­based G/G/k predictions for
multiple domains are explored. Lastly, the section reflects on the study’s limitations and
proposes directions for further research to advance ML­based approaches for predicting
G/G/k queuing dynamics.
7.1
Simulation
This section discusses the different simulation implementations, the reason for including
them, and if it could be done another way.
The two warm­up days were implemented to ensure a steady state of the system, thereby
minimizing the risk of initialization bias, which could potentially produce unrealistic dynam­
ics. It can, however, be discussed whether the two warm­up days are important for this
case study as traffic is low during nighttime and warm­up days can, therefore, be redun­
dant.
The utilization of the chargers at a station is logged, reflecting digital real­world capabili­
ties. Vehicle arrivals are logged, representing traffic cameras capabilities.
The two­way data collection, which combines the 5­minute intervals of periodic logging
of the system’s state in one Data Frame with individual vehicle dynamics in another Data
Frame, captures the underlying G/G/k queuing dynamics.
The expected wait time Ws in the simulation represents near­perfect information based on
current state of the station including queuing and charging vehicles. However, the model
does not account for vehicles en route to the station, reflecting realistic limitations, where
drivers make spontaneous charging decisions.
It is important to stress that the ML model is predicting the marginal wait time at each
station i.e., there is only wait time at a station when all chargers are occupied. Comparing
the actual wait times with the marginal wait times, thus shows significantly different results.
This makes sense considering the utility function and shared information, as drivers will
not choose stations with high marginal wait times, but instead select another station if the
SoC level is sufficient. This results in reduced overall experienced wait times for vehicles.
7.1.1
Service time modeling
The service time approach differs from Hipolito et al. (2022)’s formulation in two ways: (1)
the base parameters are set to Beta(8, 2) instead of their decision­focused parameters,
and (2) the scaling factor is simplified to a multiplication of 100 minutes. This was the
result of iterative calibration to achieve realistic service times.

The 1:1 ratio of the service time and charging efficiency relationship is a simplification,
which falls within the realistic range of DC fast chargers (GoElectra, 2025), representing
an average charging rate of 60kWh/hour. In reality, charging speed varies dramatically
depending on battery type and age, charger specifications, and environmental conditions.
7.1.2
OMLHD
The OMLHD method efficiently explores the parameter combinations and ensures robust
data generation for ML model training across multiple charging scenarios avoiding the risk
of redundant configurations. As the OMLHD method creates distinct pattern with unique
charger count for each station, it was necessary to manually add seven specific configu­
rations to train the model on dynamics across desired patterns, such as the [12,12,12,12]
and [6,6,6,6] configurations (See Table 6.5).
To ensure that the ML model was not trained on unrealistic scenarios demonstrating un­
acceptable maximum wait times for users, which could influence the performance of the
model on realistic test sets, a threshold of maximum 90 minutes wait time was imple­
mented. If unrealistic configurations were included, the ML model would learn wrong
patterns affecting its ability and effectiveness to predict wait times for realistic patterns.
7.1.3
Utility function
This study implemented a multinomial logit framework with fixed coefficients (β1 = 1, β2 =
0.005, β3 = ­20), which differs from the mixed logit framework developed by Thorhauge et
al. (2024). While mixed logit frameworks allow for reflecting realistic preference hetero­
geneity among drivers, such as range anxiety or wait time sensitivity (e.g., (β3 N(−20, σ2)),
fixed coefficients were chosen for this study. The calibration process revealed that min­
imal changes to the coefficient values led to considerable changes in station selection
behavior i.e., vehicle dispersion patterns. Thus, fixed coefficients were chosen to ensure
stable and realistic representation of charging dynamics, thereby enabling the generation
of a robust dataset for ML training.
Future research could explore the implementation of a mixed logit framework to represent
the real­world heterogeneity of preferences among BEV drivers.
7.1.4
Model Calibration and Validation
Calibration Methodology and Objectives
All simulation parameters, including utility function weights, penalties, and incentives, ar­
rival rates, peak multipliers, and service time parameters were calibrated through iterative
testing to generate realistic charging dynamics consistent with real­world observations
and existing literature. The primary calibration objective was to reproduce the vehicle dis­
persion demonstrated by Vandet and Rich (2023), where system­wide information­sharing
capabilities minimize congestion.
Parameter Sensitivity and Behavioral Validation
The penalty and incentive parameters required careful tuning to prevent excessive charg­
ing behavior and maintain realistic patterns. Initially, many vehicles charged 3­4 times
during their journey, demonstrating the necessity for the Frequency Penalty in the util­
ity function. Similarly, smaller absolute weights for the wait time parameter β3 resulted
in unrealistic congestion scenarios where individual stations experienced high utilization
despite other stations remaining underutilized.
The final calibrated parameters successfully reproduced key behavioral patterns docu­
mented in the literature: early charging due to range anxiety (Thorhauge et al., 2024) and
effective vehicle dispersion under information sharing (Vandet & Rich, 2023), along with
realistic charging frequency patterns that avoid excessive charging as seen in Figure 6.2.

Impact of Arrival Rate Calibration on ML Performance
The calibration process revealed that system dynamics and ML performance, particularly
for extreme values, were extremely sensitive to small parameter variations. A clear ex­
ample emerged during the development of Version 2, where all base rates initially were
multiplied by 15 to increase traffic flow. A crucial point was that the ML model struggled
to predict high wait times, capping at around 15 minutes, while actual wait times reached
30 minutes.
Through multiple experimental iterations, this limitation was addressed by reducing the
base rate for Pattern 2 from 0.3 to 0.25, while maintaining the enhanced rates for the
other patterns. This resulted in significant improvements in ML performance, enabling it
to predict wait times of up to 30 minutes, where it with a base rate of 0.3 only reached
18 minutes. This calibration demonstrated its importance and the need for exhaustive
calibration techniques, testing multiple scenarios. It clearly illustrated how a small portion
of the entire dataset can impact the entire training process, diminishing ML performance.
Relationship Between Traffic Intensity and Information Sharing Effectiveness
Another crucial finding concerned the relationship between arrival rates, range anxiety,
vehicle distribution, and shared information. In Pattern 1, with relatively low arrival rates,
range anxiety was evident, as demonstrated in Figure 6.2, reflecting that with low or no
marginal wait times, range anxiety dominated driver decisions. When increasing the base
rates from Version 1 to Version 2, wait times increased correspondingly, enhancing the
importance of shared information. In Version 2, vehicles dispersed more evenly across
stations due to higher occupancy levels and increased wait times, which supports the
findings of (Vandet & Rich, 2023). To further test this dynamic, base rates and peak
multipliers were raised additionally to: Base Rates = [0.35, 0.35, 0.35, 0.15, 0.4, 0.35]
and Peak Multipliers = [4.1, 5, 2, 4.1, 3, 4.1]. This resulted in nearly uniform vehicle
dispersion across all four stations, with Patterns 1 and 2 showing the following vehicle
counts for a representative day:
Pattern 1: Station 1: 110, Station 2: 114, Station 3: 108, Station 4: 103
Pattern 2: Station 1: 121, Station 2: 120, Station 3: 119, Station 4: 112
This dynamic illustrates how increased traffic, combined with shared information, reduces
range anxiety behavior, enhancing wait time’s importance in the decision­making process,
thereby increasing the utilization of chargers across all stations and optimizing system
performance.
7.2
Data Preprocessing
Different approaches and features were analyzed and tested to determine how to create
the best and most robust ML model for G/G/k wait time predictions. The chosen final
features will now be discussed.
7.2.1
Final Features
The arrival rate At is the most crucial feature describing the flow of the cars affecting
most of the other features except the time feature. The arrival rate is, furthermore, the
first part of the G in the G/G/k Kendall notation, representing the general stochastic arrival
rate. It is thus an essential feature, given that this study seeks to predict the wait time that
occurs as a result of the G/G/k queuing dynamics.
Cyclical transformation was implemented because it enhances the ML model’s under­
standing of the temporal aspect and is a common approach to processing time in RNNs
Machine Learning for G/G/k Queuing System Predictions
53
==PAGE:61==
(Weerakody et al., 2023). It is furthermore important for the temporal dependencies of
the ML model’s understanding of the charging dynamics.
Utilization rate features capture the occupancy levels at each charging station, which
are essential for accurate wait time predictions. While marginal wait time predictions only
occur when stations reach full capacity, near­full utilization patterns provide valuable pre­
dictive information to anticipate future wait times and imminent congestion.
Full utilization of chargers always results in a positive marginal wait time, which is why
this feature is important. The effect of this feature improves the model’s ability to predict
when positive wait times occur. Without this feature, the model sometimes predicted wait
times when the feature was False, corresponding to no wait time in reality. Even though
it might seem redundant given the inclusion of the other utilization rate features, it thus
encourages the ML model to focus on utilization features.
Queue length was initially considered an unrealistic input feature for the current charging
infrastructure, and multiple features were, therefore, tested to obtain the best possible
predictions, making the data preprocessing one of the most time­consuming aspects of
this study. In today’s charging infrastructure, queue length tracking is not implemented
at the charging stations. Such capabilities could, however, be easily implemented by in­
stalling cameras that track queue lengths or via charging apps, where drivers self­report
their queuing status. As queue lengths directly correlate with wait times, following Lit­
tle’s law (Little, 1961), incorporating this feature was expected to improve predictions,
especially for the higher wait times, which proved to be correct. There are other G/G/k
scenarios where queue lengths are realistic and easily obtainable, such as call centers
where queue lengths are tracked. To capture the broad application of the ML framework
beyond charging infrastructure, the queue length was included as a feature.
The inclusion of queue length also highlights its significance when compared to the base­
line model without the feature. As demonstrated in Table 6.6, the metrics from Version 2
did not improve by including the queue length in Version 3. However, the most distinct
difference is the prediction of high wait times, as shown in Figures 6.13 and 6.14, where
including the queue length improves the ML model’s prediction of high wait times signif­
icantly. The queue length was therefore included in Version 3 despite the slightly worse
metrics compared to Version 2.
System Stress was included as a feature in Version 4 due to the results of the feature
importance analysis. Comparing Version 4’s metrics to those of Version 3 in Table 6.6,
the RAE and MAE improve, and the MSE is slightly worse. The higher MSE is expected
as the model’s ability to predict high wait times improves. This is because the instances
of the model predicting high wait times when the true values are low also increase, which
is subsequently reflected in the MSE, where larger errors have more importance. Due to
the improved MAE and RAE, System Stress was included as a final feature.
Number of chargers is a crucial feature in this study, as it represents a decision variable
in the optimization framework, and the k in the G/G/k Kendall notation. This feature is
included both as the total system capacity and as individual charger counts for each sta­
tion. The objective of the optimization model is to minimize the total number of chargers
across all stations while maintaining acceptable service levels. Incorporating this feature
is therefore essential to identify the relationship between charger count, arrival rates, and
wait times.

7.2.2
Feature Engineering and Lessons Learned
The feature engineering involved extensive experimentation with various features, includ­
ing duration at full capacity, the time since the newest/first charging session began, cumu­
lative arrivals across different intervals, arrival acceleration, and inter­station dependency
indicators (e.g., Stations 1 and 2 simultaneously at full capacity). Multiple mathematical
variants of these candidates were tested, including cubed, quadratic, sigmoid, logarithmic,
and exponential transformations. However, none of these improved model performance,
and several produced worse results.
Dimensionality and Feature Selection:
A critical finding was when testing high­dimensional feature spaces, where the model, at
one point, incorporated around 100 features. The extensive feature dimensionality did
not produce good results, prompting the testing of incorporating only the most fundamen­
tal features described in the previous sections. This dimensionality reduction improved
prediction accuracy, as well as the computational requirements, including model training
time, demonstrating that feature selection is just as important as feature creation in ML
model development. This demonstrates that data preprocessing and feature engineering
are crucial phases when developing an ML model. Some features can confuse and mis­
lead the model training, causing it to look for patterns where no meaningful relationships
exist. Two examples illustrate this:
First, the simulation framework tracked the days to maintain realistic temporal patterns.
Initially, both day number and pattern ID were included as discrete variables. However,
pattern ID represents a distinct arrival rate, e.g., in real life, Mondays might exhibit Pattern
1, while Tuesdays to Thursdays might exhibit Pattern 4. The inclusion of the day num­
ber caused the model to look for non­existent similarities between Mondays in different
arrival patterns, and the day number was thus removed as a feature. The pattern ID was
then one­hot encoded, and the day number was removed, allowing the model to identify
similarities within the same pattern, demonstrating significant improvements in wait time
predictions.
Second, the charger configuration variations, derived with the OMLHD method, were ini­
tially one­hot encoded. However, the one­hot encoding prevented the model from recog­
nizing patterns between similar configurations (e.g., configurations differing by only one
additional charger at a single station). Removing the one­hot encoding and instead imple­
menting the feature as discrete variables allowed the model to learn relationships between
all configurations within the distinct arrival patterns.
7.2.3
Splitting and scaling of dataset
The 80/20% split is the most commonly adopted ratio in ML practice when dividing data
into training and validation sets (Sivakumar et al., 2024), which is why this split was also
used in this study. The test set thus remained completely unseen during both the scaling
parameter estimation and the ML model training phase to avoid biased evaluation and
prevent data leakage risking contaminating the performance metrics. Scaling both the
input features and target variables (wait times) was essential to ensure consistent gradient
magnitudes across the features preventing features with large values from dominating
during the learning phase (Hastie et al., 2009).
7.2.4
Sliding window
A 2­hour sliding window was implemented for several practical and technical reasons. For
real­world applications, the model must predict wait times using limited historical input.
Furthermore, given the average service time of approximately 50 minutes plus potential
wait times, system dynamics are often unaffected by events occurring more than 2 hours

55
==PAGE:63==
prior.
During model development, different sliding window sizes were tested. A 1­hour window
produced slightly worse results, probably due to the highway corridor structure where
travel time from Station 1 to Station 4 is 90 minutes, combined with the average service
time of 50 minutes and potential wait times. The 2­hour window was, therefore, selected
as most optimal for capturing relevant temporal dependencies. For other G/G/k scenarios,
such as healthcare dynamics optimization, it is likely that another window size is more
optimal due to different service times compared to charging dynamics.
With 5­minute time steps, the sliding window contains 24 data points spanning 2 hours of
historical data. For the initial 23 time steps with insufficient historical data, zero­padding
was applied, which is a common approach to handling sliding windows (Dwarampudi &
Reddy, 2019). Zero­padding can, however, drastically affect initial predictions, as the ML
model operates with deficient input.
7.2.5
Parameter and Feature Variation Implications
The arrival rate (base rates and peak multipliers) was increased from Versions 1 to 2­5
to increase the instances of positive marginal wait time. This created a less zero­inflated
dataset, which in theory should improve the prediction accuracy on positive wait times,
which proved to be true as shown in Table 6.6. An increased arrival rate increases the
marginal wait time mean, as it causes more congestion. It, furthermore, produced more
non­zero instances of marginal wait times, which gives the model more data on which to
learn the pattern for non­zero wait time predictions.
7.3
Hyperparameter Tuning and ML architecture
The patience of the callback function in the hyperparameter tuning process was lower than
in the training of the final LSTM model as the hyperparameter tuning process is meant to
provide an initial best estimate of the hyperparameters. Thus, it is not meant to fully train
the model and reach the optimal solution, hence the patience was lower.
Due to computational limitations (8GB Ram), neither of the optimization algorithms (Hy­
perband and Bayesian) completed the full search space. At one point the speed of each
step decreased drastically from 200ms to 2 seconds per step, but in reality, it took be­
tween 1–2 hours to conduct a single step, thus the algorithm was terminated manually to
stop the search. The process required around 3­5 hours of computational time before the
speed dropped, and the process was manually terminated.
Due to computational limitations, the batch size and the sequence length were not simu­
lated in the hyperparameter tuning process. Other values of the batch size were tested,
but with no significant difference from the final result.
7.3.1
Linear vs. ReLU Activation Functions
The choice of activation function in the output layer has a significant influence on model
performance, as demonstrated in Figure 6.10. Linear activation produces significantly
better results compared to ReLU activation for this application (see Section 6.3.3), which
relates to the applied scaling method. ReLU activation clips all negative predictions to
zero, which creates problems when the predictions are inversely transformed back to the
original scale. During the inverse transformation, some predictions that appear nega­
tive on the normalized scale become positive, corresponding to positive wait times. The
ReLU activation, thus, introduced systematic bias, preventing the model from learning the
full range of possible outcomes. The linear activation layer preserves all predicted val­
ues, including negative ones on the normalized scale, allowing the intended effect of the

better prediction output (see Figure 6.14), which is why it was chosen.
7.4
Model Performance and Validation
7.4.1
Overall Performance Assessment
A key distinction of this study is that the ML model does not predict the G/G/k queue
patterns themselves but the wait times resulting from the G/G/k dynamics. This approach
is significantly more complex than pattern prediction because arrivals at individual stations
are continuously influenced by the system­wide state resulting in distinct G/G/k dynamics
at each station. This differs from recent research (Soldan et al., 2021; Sherzer, 2024;
Sherzer et al., 2024; Baron et al., 2024), which focuses primarily on predicting arrival
patterns (the first G in G/G/k notation) rather than operational KPIs.
Version 5 generally has either best­performing metrics or is one of the best­ranked ver­
sions across both the station­specific and overall metrics. The RAE metrics across all
versions show that applying either version is superior to using the overall wait time mean.
The lower metrics for Version 5 can be explained by the model predicting more conser­
vative values, as Version 5 does not reach as high wait time magnitudes compared to
Version 4. Previous calibration tests showed that some datasets for Version 5 limited
the ML model’s ability to predict high wait time magnitudes. Whether this is due to the
larger training set increasing the zero­inflated characteristic or due to another reason is
unknown.
7.4.2
Station­Specific Performance Variations
The ML model performs significantly better at Station 1 compared to downstream stations,
as shown in Table 6.6 and visualized in Figure 6.16. A notable finding is that the predic­
tion magnitude of wait times decrease for downstream stations. These findings can be
attributed to several factors related to the sequential order of the charging stations.
Temporal Information Advantage
Predictions for Station 1 benefit from the most up­to­date arrival information, as vehicles
enter the system immediately before reaching this station. In contrast, downstream sta­
tions receive vehicles that entered the system 30 minutes to 2 hours prior, depending
on charging durations and potential wait times at upstream stations. This temporal lag
creates increasing uncertainty in wait time predictions for stations further downstream.
Cumulative Uncertainty Propagation
Downstream stations inherit prediction errors from all preceding stations. Station 2 relies
on predictions of Station 1, while Station 4 relies on predictions for Stations 1­3. This
compounds prediction errors and uncertainty progressively through the system.
Decision­Making Complexity
As vehicles progress through the system, their charging decisions affect the system state
and dynamics for all subsequent stations. Early stations primarily exhibit range­anxiety­
driven decisions, while later stations must account for vehicles’ updated SoC levels and
routing decisions based on real­time system conditions, increasing the prediction uncer­
tainty for these stations.
These findings and station­specific uncertainties affect the optimization framework, as
discussed in Section 7.7.1, where the reliability of the predicted maximum wait time in­
fluences the reliability of the optimization result and, consequently, the effectiveness of
operational decision support.

7.5
Computational Efficiency and Practical Implications
7.5.1
Computational Complexity Analysis
As established in Section 6.4, the computational requirements of the three phases ­ sim­
ulation, ML training, and ML inference ­ reveal distinct trade­offs that are essential to
understanding the practical value of the ML­based approach.
Training vs. Inference Trade­off
While the computational complexity of ML model training is significantly higher (O(4.59 ×
109)) than running the simulation (O(2.63 × 106)), it is a one­time investment. The trained
ML model produces real­time inference predictions at O(2.87 × 106) complexity, corre­
sponding to a 7.8× speedup over the simulation based on actual runtimes. This advan­
tage grows as the frequency of predictions increases, making the approach particularly
valuable for real­time operational applications.
Algorithmic Efficiency and Scaling
A critical finding is the differential scaling behavior between simulation and ML inference.
As vehicle arrival rates increase from Version 1 to Versions 2­5, simulation runtime in­
creases proportionally (from 0.67 to 1.25 seconds per run), reflecting the O(n) relationship
with the vehicle count.
In contrast, the ML inference runtime remains constant at 0.16 seconds per run, regard­
less of the traffic intensity in the dataset used for training. The bidirectional LSTM archi­
tecture exhibits efficient parameter utilization, with complexity scaling depending on the
sequence length (24 time steps) and hidden layer dimensions, rather than with system
complexity variables, such as vehicle count. This architectural characteristic ensures con­
sistent inference performance, increasing its computational superiority over the simulation
when increasing the size of the dataset.
Scaling Impact Example: If arrival rates increase by a factor of 10, simulation runtime
would increase to approximately 12.5 seconds per run, while ML inference remains con­
stant at 0.16 seconds, resulting in a speedup increase from 7.8× to 78×.
This scaling behavior becomes increasingly important in G/G/k systems exhibiting high
throughput, such as charging networks during peak hours, hospital emergency rooms
during crisis events, or manufacturing lines operating at maximum capacity.
Resource Requirement Trade­off
The simulation requires minimal memory usage but intensive computation that scales
with vehicle volume. On the other hand, the ML model requires higher memory for storing
trained parameters as well as higher memory to execute complex neural network com­
putations with multiple hidden layers and features. This memory investment of the ML
model, however, enables near­instant predictions with constant computational cost.

7.6
Elasticity
7.6.1
Elasticity Characteristics in G/G/k Charging Systems
The BEV charging system exhibits significant stochastic variability due to random arrival
times, initial SoC levels, charging durations, and Gumbel noise in routing decisions. This
variability, combined with the non­monotonic relationship between charger count and wait
times, creates complex patterns in the elasticity calculations. The non­monotonic relation­
ship occurs because, when wait times increase, drivers will eventually cease charging at
that station due to congestion, unless forced to do so by low SoC levels. This behavioral
pattern creates significant station­specific differences in elasticity calculations, as Station
1 effectively becomes a bottleneck for some cars.
Three different configurations were used in the elasticity calculations, as they reflect differ­
ent charger distributions, leading to varying vehicle behavior and different elasticities. For
the simulation­based elasticities, all calculations used the average of the daily maximum
wait times across a week, as this produced more stable results with respect to changes in
the total number of chargers across the system. For the ML model­based elasticities, the
calculations used the maximum prediction value across a week, as this produced more
stable results. This rationale for this is further explained in Section 7.6.5.
As explained in Section 6.5, negative elasticities indicate the intuitive relationship where
adding chargers reduces wait times or where removing chargers increases wait time. In
contrast, positive elasticities represent the counterintuitive scenario where adding charg­
ers increases wait times or removing chargers decreases wait times.
This section analyzes and discusses two different elasticity aspects: first, the pattern­
dependent elasticities, demonstrated in the simulation­based elasticity calculations, which
are executed on Patterns 1 and 3 to show the effects of higher arrival rates in the system,
and second, ML­based elasticities, which are only conducted on Pattern 1 to evaluate the
ML model’s ability to replicate the underlying dynamics of the BEV charging system.

The reason why using the maximum wait time values for the ML­based elasticities pro­

7.7
Optimization Framework
7.7.1
Strategic and Operational Applications
The near­instantaneous ML KPI predictions offer huge opportunities for both strategic
planning and real­time operational optimization when combined with the optimization model
framework, which will now be discussed.
Long­term Strategic Applications:
From a strategic perspective, the ability to quickly evaluate various scenarios using the
optimization model provides effective data­driven decision support for planning. Whereas
traditional Monte Carlo simulations can take hours or days, ML predictions enable plan­
ners to evaluate hundreds of scenarios in a fraction of the time, supporting optimal re­
source allocation and informed investment decisions. However, long­term strategic plan­
ning often spans a longer period. One may therefore argue that the rapid ML predictions
are not as important for this application, and traditional Monte Carlo simulations can be
used, as there are no day­to­day urgent time requirements. This is, however, another
case in day­to­day operations.
Day­to­day Operational Applications:
For day­to­day operations, the optimization model is a crucial tool for decision support
across multiple domains. In healthcare settings, the system can optimize personnel allo­
cation by predicting bottlenecks and system dynamics based on recent patient flow and
current staffing levels, taking into account factors such as sick leave and available equip­
ment. For BEV charging infrastructure, the framework can calculate wait times consid­
ering unexpected occurrences such as unavailable chargers due to charger failures or
the increasing problem of charging cable theft (Ritzau, 2025). Likewise, in manufacturing
lines, the framework can reassess optimal resource allocation and predict system perfor­
mance if equipment breaks down. The optimization model thus enables the minimization
of bottlenecks through proactive management, demonstrating the operational advantages
for ML model­based optimization frameworks.

tructure configurations for a specific arrival pattern to satisfy a certain maximum wait time
threshold. Front­end heavy configurations enable natural charging habits, allowing more
cars to charge at the first stations, while back­end heavy configurations result in increased
wait times.

7.7.3
Methodological Validation
Due to the stochastic variability in maximum wait times, it was considered using the 95th
percentile for the elasticity calculations. However, as previously presented, the data is
zero­inflated, and the majority of the positive wait times have moderate magnitudes. The
95th percentile differs significantly from the average maximum wait time, e.g., 13.0 versus
32.8 minutes for the baseline configuration in Pattern 1. As the optimization framework
relies on the maximum wait time threshold, using the 95th percentile as a constraint would
not be viable, as it would significantly underestimate the critical model constraint and result
in suboptimal resource allocation decisions.
As in the elasticity calculations, the optimization framework also utilizes the ML model’s
maximum prediction value across 7 days, while the results are compared to the average
maximum wait times across the 7 days from the simulation; the reason for this was ex­
plained in Section 7.6.5. This is also done in an attempt to minimize the gap between the
magnitude of the ML predictions and the true simulation results.
Greedy Heuristic vs. Optimal Solutions
As described in Section 5.10, solutions to the optimization problem, can be obtained either
by applying optimization software such as Julia for optimal solutions, or by applying greedy
heuristics, obtaining fast (sub­)optimal solutions. While greedy heuristics do not always
guarantee optimal solutions, it is likely to offer improved solutions compared to baseline
configurations and offer fast insights into optimization opportunities, making them valuable
for real­time operational decision­making. For the ML­based optimization example, the
greedy heuristic identified configuration [8,7,7,6] as the optimal solution using 28 chargers
with a wait time of 15.75 minutes, as seen in Table 6.10. This is however not the optimal
solution, as the [8,7,6,7] configuration also uses 28 chargers but only has a wait time of
13.33 minutes.

7.7.5
Optimization Model Performance and Limitations
A critical limitation of the optimization model is the wait time constraint (Constraint 5.41).
To ensure the effectiveness of the optimization model, the ML model must be able to pre­
dict the high wait times of the G/G/k system, particularly the maximum wait times that occur
during peak periods. While the ML model does not need high accuracy for all predictions
to be efficient in the optimization framework, it must be able to predict the magnitude of
the maximum wait time to ensure that the optimization model identifies feasible solutions.
If the ML model underestimates the maximum wait time, the optimization framework will
suggest sub­optimal optimization configurations, leading to constraint violations in prac­
tice.
As shown in Figure 6.17 and Table 6.6, the performance of the ML model varies across
the four stations. Generally, the model is better at predicting higher wait times for Station
1 compared to the other stations. This is a crucial finding, since the optimization model
relies on the magnitude of the prediction of the maximum wait time. Hence, if the maximum
wait time occurs at Stations 2, 3, or 4, this affects the performance and reliability of the
optimization model, as the model may be unable to reach the same magnitude, leading
to suboptimal configuration solutions.
These are critical limitations that must be considered, and the ML model performance
across all servers in the specific G/G/k system must be analyzed before applying the
optimization model in real­world scenarios.
To address this limitation, proportional differences in wait time predictions between the
simulation­based true average maximum wait times and the ML­based predictions along
with their elasticities should be analyzed.
Introducing the average correction factor (ACF) did not improve the results. This demon­
strates that even though the model generally underpredicts wait times, a simple multiplica­
tion factor may not be applicable, as the ML model in some cases demonstrates significant
deviations from the general proportional difference, compared to the true simulation val­
ues. A more complicated configuration­based multiplication factor might be more suitable,
but this was not tested. A multiplication factor might improve the overall maximum wait
time accuracy, but the more moderate values, which are more precise than the higher
ones, would then be overestimated compared to the true simulation values. This over­
estimation would, however, not negatively affect the ML model’s reliability in relation to
the optimization framework, as it only utilizes the maximum value, but it would affect the
overall accuracy of the ML model predictions.
If the simulation were not a highway corridor with four stations, but a continuous highway,
the SoC levels might look slightly different. This could result in a more even dispersion of
vehicles across the stations. Thus, Station 1 would not be the bottleneck, as there would
be a station before that as well. The optimal configuration might not be a front­end heavy
one, but rather one with the same number of chargers at all stations, assuming the vehicle
flow remains the same along the entire highway. Dynamics in the charging infrastructure
in a city will also look significantly different, as cars can arrive from every direction, in­
troducing even more stochastic variability. At the same time, there are spill­over effects
between queues and stations, which makes it more complex and unpredictable.

7.7.6
Threshold Selection and Stakeholder Perspectives
The optimization model requires setting a maximum wait time threshold as a constraint
(Constraint 5.41). Determining this parameter contains strategic choices that vary de­
pending on the stakeholder perspective. From a user’s perspective, overall lower wait
times, e.g., maximum 10 minutes, might be necessary to address range anxiety and en­
courage BEV adoption, as demonstrated by the 29% of potential buyers who fear long
wait times (CSR, 2024). Looking at the threshold from other perspectives, however, gives
insights into need opportunities and challenges.
Welfare Economic Perspective
Rich et al. (2022) found that a maximum wait time guarantee of 10 minutes represents
the most efficient solution from a welfare economic perspective, balancing infrastructure
costs with user time savings. There is, however, a distinct difference between finding the
optimum from a welfare economic perspective and from a profit optimization perspective.
Profit Optimization Perspective
Public chargers are run by private companies, making the charging infrastructure depen­
dent on companies’ business strategies. A specific charger infrastructure plan might rep­
resent the optimum from a welfare economic perspective. However, if this is not the case
from a profit optimization perspective, companies will likely not implement that specific
solution, as they seek to optimize their financial profits.
Balancing Stakeholder Needs
When applying the optimization model, the different actors on the market and their respec­
tive business strategies must be considered to set a reasonable threshold. However,
setting the threshold to satisfy wait times from a welfare economic perspective, rather
than a profit maximization perspective, can also encourage companies to comply with
state and municipal goals, leading to increased goodwill. This can benefit companies,
as the Ministry of Transport in Denmark in 2022 established a public funding pool where
private companies could apply for subsidies when installing public chargers (Transport­
ministeriet, 2022). The state­funded subsidies can both accelerate the expansion of the
charging infrastructure and influence where and how chargers are implemented, hope­
fully ultimately reaching the welfare economic optimum. This public­private partnership
demonstrates how threshold selection can become a strategic policy tool for achieving
societal objectives while strengthening the private sector.
The above­mentioned dynamics are specific to the charging infrastructure but can be
easily applied to other sectors. A call center must balance reasonable wait times for
callers (e.g., industry standard of 2­3 minutes) with financially sustainable staffing allo­
cation, where each additional agent represents fixed labor costs. Production companies
must, likewise, balance delivery time commitments with production line capacity, where
e.g., reducing lead times from 5 to 3 days might require significant equipment investments
or additional workers.
These different perspectives highlight the optimization framework’s ability to accommo­
date various thresholds and analyze the trade­offs between different stakeholder needs,
costs, service levels, and policy objectives, delivering support for informed decisions
across multiple implementation contexts where G/G/k queuing dynamics are present.

7.8.1
Beyond Charging Infrastructure
As explained in Section 3.1.2, the absence of services that predict wait time through ML
likely comes from the complexity of modeling the G/G/k dynamics like traffic patterns. This

study addresses this gap by developing an ML framework that integrates information­
sharing capabilities ­ an upcoming feature in traffic infrastructure that is likely to be ex­
panded to more services and providers. It provides an efficient tool for drivers to make
informed decisions about station selection, leading to reduced system­wide congestion
and potentially optimized infrastructure utilization and development.
The information­sharing approach also reflects the capabilities of other G/G/k systems
where real­time system monitoring is already feasible due to existing infrastructure. Ex­

7.8.2
Sequential vs. Parallel Server Architecture:
Another significant distinction is that this G/G/k charging system represents a sequential
arrangement of parallel server clusters, resulting in substantially more complexity than
traditional G/G/k systems such as call centers, which operate with k parallel servers. The
sequential nature of the G/G/k charging system introduces station interdependencies as
driver decisions at each station affect both upstream and downstream demand patterns.
It can, therefore, be argued that if ML can successfully predict KPIs in sequential G/G/k
charging systems with k parallel servers, it should be even more feasible to predict sim­
pler parallel­only G/G/k systems, potentially with higher accuracy due to reduced system
interdependencies.
The behavior pattern for the BEV charging system is, furthermore, complicated because
vehicles can opt out and skip charging if the wait times are too long. This would not be the
case in manufacturing lines or hospital emergency rooms, where units or patients must
go through the service processes. It would thus likely be easier to predict those scenarios
as the arrival rate to the servers would be more consistent.
7.9
Study Limitations and Future Directions
7.9.1
Dataset and Computational Limitations
The results in Section 6.3.2 demonstrate that an increased arrival rate yields improved
model performance, which are the most important predictions from a user perspective.
This improvement is likely the result of several factors:
First, a higher arrival rate generates more instances with non­zero wait times, reducing
the challenge of zero­inflated datasets where extreme value prediction is a common issue.
As the proportion of positive wait times increases, raising the average mean, the model
is more likely to predict higher wait times rather than conservative, near­zero predictions.
Second, as the arrival rate increases, the dataset grows and contains more diversity. The
ML model thus has more data on which to fine­tune its parameters, enabling the model
to learn the underlying patterns more effectively and optimize its parameters accordingly.
Due to computational limitations, especially in the hyperparameter tuning phase, further
dataset expansion was not feasible. However, as described above, larger datasets con­
taining more non­zero wait times improve model performance. Additional computational
power is thus needed to fully explore the opportunities and potential of applying ML for
G/G/k dynamic prediction.
7.9.2
Alternative Modeling Approaches
In the optimization framework, real­time wait times are unimportant, but the maximum
wait times are critical. To improve the optimization framework, the ML model could be
Machine Learning for G/G/k Queuing System Predictions
67
==PAGE:75==
modified to only predict maximum values across various configurations, and not predict
wait times at all instances. Some appropriate models for this could be:
Quantile Regression Forests, which, instead of focusing on the mean, could predict the
99th percentile, which would prove more relevant for maximum wait time prediction.
A neural network that focuses only on predicting the maximum wait times, or a neural
network for each of the four stations, which could improve the model’s performance and
accuracy across all stations.
Another interesting ML model is symbolic regression, which is on the rise and demon­
strates impressive model performance across multiple domains substituting for common
ML techniques (Radwan et al., 2024). Unlike neural networks, symbolic regression pro­
vides interpretable results in the form of explicit mathematical equations, which has sev­
eral advantages for G/G/k dynamics prediction.
The interpretability of symbolic regression enables sensitivity analysis and elasticity cal­
culations for each term in the derived equations. This transparency addresses the ’black­
box’ issue of neural networks, enabling a better understanding of how each feature con­
tributes to model prediction and how they interact with each other.
Symbolic regres­
sion would visualize the importance of different inputs (arrival rate, arrival slope, service
time, queue length, charger count etc.), providing valuable insights into and improving
the feature engineering phase (including feature importance analysis, which is very time­
consuming), bridging the gap between theoretical, analytical research and operational
usage.
It is unknown whether symbolic regression can handle the high­dimensional feature space
of the sequential arrangement of parallel server clusters in the G/G/k charger infrastruc­
ture, but this would be an exciting area for future research.

Conclusion
Machine Learning and AI have entered everyday life, and their possibilities are still being
discovered and explored. Businesses and individuals are leveraging their potential for
multiple purposes, achieving significant optimization improvements in operational chal­
lenges and finding rapid, easy solutions to everyday problems. G/G/k queues, character­
ized by generally distributed arrival rates and service times, and k servers to serve cus­
tomers are present everywhere: in call centers, hospital emergency rooms, manufacturing
lines, and traffic systems. These systems prove difficult to predict and are, therefore, tra­
ditionally simulated to obtain system KPIs. While neural networks have been explored to
predict pattern types in these systems, none predict the related KPIs, making this study
a significant contribution to this field.
This study developed a bidirectional LSTM ML model that demonstrated that it can cap­
ture the underlying dynamics of G/G/k queuing systems with limitations, achieving near­
instantaneous predictions with 8× computational speedup compared to traditional Monte
Carlo simulations. The BEV charging system case study demonstrates that the ML model
can generally predict wait time patterns, but struggles to predict high wait times, which is
common for RNNs with zero­inflated data.
The methodology demonstrated robust simulation model development as the simulation
successfully replicated observed real­world behavior: range anxiety, wait time sensitivity,
and the effects of information sharing. The OMLHD method proved to be a valuable tool,
ensuring low correlation and sufficient coverage of configurations when combined with
manually selected ones to create a comprehensive training set for the ML model.
Several methodological insights emerged both during the simulation and ML model de­
velopment. Iterative calibration of simulation parameters was essential to replicate robust
and realistic charging patterns and to create a robust dataset for ML model training. Fea­
ture engineering was the most time­consuming phase of the study, where the feature
importance analysis identified the System Stress as the most important feature across all
stations. Including station queue lengths as features drastically improved model perfor­
mance. Including the queue length increased the maximum wait time predictions from 12
minutes to around 60 minutes, depending on the specific configuration.
Building on this technical foundation, several key insights emerged, revealing that Station
1 is a critical bottleneck in the BEV charging system, and changes in charger count to
this station have significant implications on the wait times, as some vehicles are forced to
charge at it, due to low SoC levels. Station 1, therefore, also often exhibits the highest wait
times of the four stations. The ML model demonstrates better performance when predict­
ing station wait times closest to the input perform better, with uncertainty compounding
at downstream stations. The ML model generally captures the underlying patterns in the
elasticity calculations, where removing a charger leads to higher wait times and adding
one reduces wait times. Elasticities for the simulation range from ­14.5 to +2.8, with the
highest value occurring in the back­end heavy configuration. In contrast, the ML model’s
elasticities range from ­23.26 to +1.72, with the highest value occurring in the front­end
configuration.
As the ML model demonstrates that it captures the underlying patterns of the sequential
arrangement of parallel server clusters (with some significant limitations for high wait time
predictions), ML models can likely predict simpler parallel­only G/G/k systems. These

==PAGE:77==
can possibly be predicted with higher accuracy, as there is no opt­out opportunity, and
customers must be served at the servers.
Despite these promising results, several limitations must be acknowledged for the ML
model. The zero­inflated data hinders the ML model’s ability to predict high wait times.
This limitation is mitigated when increasing the arrival rate, as this creates more non­zero
observations. Other G/G/k queuing systems might not exhibit the same zero­inflation,
which might improve the ML model predictions of these systems. However, more complex
systems, such as a charging infrastructure in a city, will be even more complicated than
the highway corridor, as cars can arrive from every direction, and there can be spill­over
effects between queues and stations, making it even more unpredictable.
The hyperparameter tuning phase was computationally intensive, resulting in the incom­
plete exploration of the search space for the hyperparameters. Further computational
power is thus needed to fully explore the hyperparameter space.
From a computational perspective, the ML model is superior to the traditional Monte Carlo
methods, as simulation runtime increases proportionally with the number of customers. In
contrast, the ML model runtime remains consistent due to the model architecture, which
relies on the size of the sliding window input, rather than customer volume. The opti­
mization framework, despite its limitations, can provide a valuable decision­support tool
for both long­term strategic and day­to­day operational decisions. The rapid ML model
predictions have several operational advantages: drivers can choose the stations with the
lowest wait time, and managers can efficiently allocate resources at the right place in hos­
pitals or manufacturing lines. From a strategic perspective, the ML model predictions can
provide rapid decision support when evaluating different infrastructure scenarios, thereby
speeding up the planning phase and enabling more scenario evaluations in a fraction of
the time.
The optimization framework relies on the ML model’s ability to predict high wait times,
which is a significant limitation. If the ML model underpredicts the maximum wait times,
the optimization framework produces suboptimal solutions. Furthermore, the ML model
correctly identified that Station 1 generally exhibits the highest wait times. If the highest
wait times, however, occur at another station, the ML model might not be able to predict
this, which again negatively affects the effectiveness of the optimization framework.
While the current framework shows promising results, real­world applications require higher
accuracy and addressing the high wait time prediction limitation to ensure reliable decision­
making. Future research should explore the capabilities of other ML models to use both
for wait time predictions and in the optimization framework. It should be explored whether
models, such as Quantile Regression Forests, can produce better maximum wait times,
which can then be utilized in the optimization framework. Furthermore, as symbolic re­
gression is on the rise, it would be interesting to explore its ability to predict the G/G/k
system KPIs. This would provide valuable insights into variable dependencies, thereby
easing the understanding of their interdependencies.
In conclusion, this study demonstrates that while challenges remain in predicting ex­
treme events in zero­inflated G/G/k systems, the computational advantages and near­
instantaneous predictions offer great potential for operational decision­making. The frame­
work’s ability to support both long­term strategic planning and real­time operations, espe­
cially in large­scale datasets, shows promising results, which, with some improvements,
can outweigh the limitations. This approach can strengthen the triple­bottom line by im­
proving employee well­being by reducing stress through efficient resource allocation (peo­

technology adoption through optimized infrastructure utilization (planet), as well as im­
proving cost­efficiency through optimized resource allocation (profit). With continued re­

settings, where rapid response times are critical.
