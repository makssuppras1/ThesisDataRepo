Abstract
In recent years there has been an ever increasing demand for autonomous underwater robotic systems
to perform several subsea applications such as exploration, inspection, recovery etc. With the increased
utilization of remotely operated vehicles, the development towards more autonomous vehicles that enhance
operator efﬁciency becomes very desirable.
Experimenting on a real robot can be costly and time consuming and therefore a mathematical model
that describes the system dynamics is necessary. Such model is useful in order to simulate a control oriented
behavior of the robotic system and formulate the control algorithms in respect to the system dynamics.
This thesis presents a linear model identiﬁcation of the system dynamics of an underwater remotely
operated vehicle. The identiﬁcation scheme is based on speciﬁc experimental tests in order to estimate the
manoeuvring and diving dynamics of the vehicle. In this work a light weight, low cost and open-source
ROV is used as test platform. For the identiﬁcation of the linear model parameters a non-linear least squares
method is used based only on-board sensor data.
The system behavior of an underwater vehicle can be greatly inﬂuenced by the dynamics of the vehicle
thrusters. In this project a characterization of the delivered force of each thruster has been carried out for
forward as well as for backward movement.
Achieving good maneuverability of an underwater vehicle depends on an accurate hydrodynamic model
and a robust control system. For this purpose a horizontal and a longitudinal model is identiﬁed and served
as the basis for the design of optimal linear robust controllers that control the depth and the heading angle of
the vehicle during a task. The choice of a robust control algorithm was made under the necessity to satisfy
the desired speciﬁcations in terms of performance and robustness under the inﬂuence of a model parametric
uncertainty that the identiﬁcation process entailed.

1.1
Motivation
Worldwide there always has been a demand for energy consumption. Some of the main world’s energy
sources comes from the harness of resources such as oil, wind, sea, or solar power. The facilities that extract
or convert these resources to usable energy sources are mostly located at offshore areas. As an example,
Figure 1.1(a) illustrates the North West Hutton platform which is located in the UK North Sea and situated
at a water depth of 143m ( North West Hutton Field United Kingdom (n.d.)).
(a) North West Hutton Field platform diagram ( North West
Hutton Field United Kingdom (n.d.)).
(b) Underwater inspection of a support structure by human
diver ( Ranger Offshore (n.d.)).
Figure 1.1: Oil platform facility and under water inspection of support structure by a human diver.
However, these offshore installations imply challenges in respect to their inspection, maintenance and
repair. As it can be seen in Figure 1.1(b) the conﬁned spaces in those underwater structures make these tasks
very difﬁcult and costly to be carried out by human divers. At the North Sea oil platform, Siri, the discovery
of cracks in the platform support structure have cost million of euros since 2009 due to reduced production
( Christensen et al. (2014)).
In recent years the focus on applications for Unmanned Underwater vehicles (UUVs) performing a

variety of tasks has increased. These tasks include applications such as: underwater mapping, habitat
exploration, different types of inspections, rescue missions and many more.
UUVs can be divided in two main categories: Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs) and Autonomous
Underwater Vehicles (AUVs). On the ﬁrst, the robot is connected through a tether with the operator, which
transfers power and data such as control signals, sensor readings and video streams. On the latter, the robot
is untethered and conducts a mission autonomously without operator intervention.
Both ROVs and AUVs have been developed for different types of applications according to their
maneuverability, range of operation, power autonomy and task complexity. Because of their endurance to
operate in different environment conditions, as well as for economic and safety reasons unmanned vehicles
can replace the use of human divers or manned submarine vehicles to conduct the various missions.
This project is a part of a larger research in which the objective is to create a system of collective modular
underwater robots in order to perform several tasks such as inspection of offshore installations in harsh
environments ( Christensen et al. (2014)). In such cases like the Siri platform where the support structure,
located under the sea, is a conﬁned hollow steel structure, small AUVs give signiﬁcant potential to carry out
such tasks.
The system is comprised of modular robots with different characteristics, namely swimming or legged
robots, in order to collaborate and perform speciﬁc tasks. The swimming robot shown on Figure 1.2. is
the OpenROV ROV and is used as the test platform in order to conduct this project. Its characteristics are
further discussed in Chapter 2. A main advantage of this heterogeneous modular system is that the robots
can reconﬁgure themselves according to the particular need. More speciﬁcally a swimming robot can carry a
legged one to the area of inspection and afterwards take it back to the docking station where it can recharge
and transmit the inspection data, Figure 1.2(a). Another example is to perform a collective transport of heavy
payloads which could not be carried only by one robot, Figure 1.2(b).
On the other hand, these functionalities and coordinated missions give rise into solving problems with
different aspects such as communication, localization and control. The term ’control’ can be classiﬁed in
three main categories ( Yildiz et al. (2009)).
• Motion control: The focus is on the system response to an input and the stability of the vehicle.
• Mission control: It focuses on the execution of the behavioral modeling of an AUV in a parametrically
predeﬁned behavior.
• Formation control: It focuses on coordinated behavior of multiple AUVs.
From a control perspective in order for each robot to be able act autonomously the need of control
algorithms in a high level such as path planning, or obstacle avoidance is obvious. For that purpose
it is assumed that the needed maneuvers generated from these algorithms can be rapidly and precisely
implemented by the lower level of control, namely motion control. This thesis is concerned on the motion
control of an underwater vehicle and more speciﬁcally it focuses on heading and depth control.
1.2
Problem Statement
In order to conduct a mission with a ROV that requires precision maneuvering in the proximity of
a marine structure, the utilization of a robust control system would be useful. During a task it is very
difﬁcult for an operator to catch up the external time-varying disturbances such as waves or currents as the
micro-operation needed to compensate those disturbances is troublesome to handle and can be costly in
terms of thruster action. To that end, with a maneuvering controller on board, the pilot can maintain a desired
heading angle and depth during the mission.

1.3. LITERATURE SURVEY

A mathematical model is essential for simulating purposes and for formulating control algorithms which
take into account the system’s dynamics and uncertainties. A model identiﬁcation is carried out in this project
using only the on-board sensors of the vehicle, namely the Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) and the pressure
sensor. The vehicle is excited to perform different type of maneuvers such as forward/backward movement,
zigzag, submerge and emerge at different speeds. The data are collected, pre-processed and according to the
corresponding model structure to be identiﬁed, the model estimation routines are implemented. The outcome
of this procedure is the evaluation of the manoeuvring and diving dynamics of the vehicle, in decoupled
horizontal and longitudinal models. The information from the identiﬁed models are ﬁrst used to formulate
the control system and after to simulate the resulting behavior of the system.
The thrusters of an underwater vehicle can greatly inﬂuence the overall system behavior. For that matter
a characterization for each of the three thrusters has been conducted.
The general underwater vehicle control system design problem includes a variety of nonlinearities and
modeling uncertainties. These include hydrodynamic and inertial nonlinearitites and problems related to
coupling of the system states. For linear control design, which is the scope of this project, many of these
dependencies are neglected in order to obtain a simple model which can be used for the design of a motion
control system that is robust to parametric uncertainties and external disturbances and guarantees for the
desired system stability and performance.
1.3
Literature Survey
Different kind of identiﬁcation techniques of UUVs dynamic models as well as motion control strategies
have been extensively researched. In Yang et al. (2015) a computational ﬂuid dynamic software (CFD) is
utilized in order to acquire a control-oriented hydrodynamic model that is used for H∞and classic PID
design for heading control. A simpliﬁed decoupled model of an underwater robot is identiﬁed by least
squares and parameter optimization techniques using on-board sensors and speciﬁc experiments in Ridao
et al. (2001). Another approach for the identiﬁcation of the hydrodynamic parameters is used by Yuh (1990),
where a parameter adaptation algorithm estimates the parameters at each sampling time of a discrete-time
model which are then used by an adaptive velocity controller in order to control the sway, surge and heading
rates of an ROV. In (Guo et al. 1995) an on-line learning neural network controller is used for heading
control of an AUV. A multi-layered feedforward neural network is implemented as a motion controller and
showed adaptation towards constant and sudden disturbances and slow varying AUV dynamics. Neural

networks are used in (Van De Ven et al. 2007), in order to identify the drag terms of an AUV over full region
of operation. A neural network is used in parallel with a model of the known part of the system dynamics.
Online learning of neural networks is tested under noisy conditions and changing of system parameters and
showed that can alleviate the noise effect and adapt on system parameter alteration. In (Healey and Lienard

waypoints but not accuracy, while disturbance rejection such as ocean currents is compensated by adding a
feedforward term on the steering controller.

Scope and Contribution
The outline of this report can be brieﬂy described as follows:
• Chapter 2: Description of the vehicle and it’s characteristics; Theoretical background necessary to
describe the dynamics of an underwater vehicle.
• Chapter 3: Thruster characterization and estimation of the mathematical models that describe the
steering and diving dynamics of the vehicle.
• Chapter 4: Design of robust control systems for heading and diving control.
• Chapter 5: Conclusions and future work.

2.1
OpenROV Characteristics
The test platform of this project is an underwater ROV called OpenROV. It is a fully open-source vehicle
that is built and improved upon by a community of users. It is intended to be easy for use and repair without
having speciﬁc technical knowledge on hardware or software. Since it is open source there is a continuous
development of ideas about its further improvement. It is highly manoeuverable due to its lightweight design,
also neutrally buoyant and hydrostatically stable due to its weight distribution. It weights ∼2.6kg with
dimensions: 30cm long x 20cm wide x 15cm high. Its maximum speed is 2knots and it can submerge to the
maximum depth of 100m.
Figure 2.1: OpenROV.
It is comprised by a Beaglebone Black board, which is an open-source single-board computer which
runs Linux operating system. It handles the video from the camera as well as the communication, as it hosts
a web server that provides the OpenROV interface to the surface computer. A screenshot of the OpenROV
interface is shown in Figure 2.2 where one can see a video stream, readings from sensors, vehicle status
information and also can control the ROV from the keyboard. The Beaglebone is connected through a
Universal Asynchronous Receiver/Transmitter (UART) protocol to an Arduino Mega board that controls the
robots actuators through the Electric Speed Controllers (ECSs). Figure 2.3 illustrates an overview of the
hardware architecture.

2.2.3
Decoupled models
The six DOF equations of motion can be divided into two subsystems which facilitate the design of the
manoeuvering controllers. In favor of these simpliﬁed models the following assumptions are made:
• The thrusters effect only motions in surge, heave and yaw. Heave motion is provided by the vertical
thruster, surge motion is provided by equal thrusts on both horizontal thrusters, while yaw action is
provided by differential thrust. Hence, the system can be decoupled into two decoupled subsystems.
• The vehicle velocity is lower than 1 m/s, thus the Coriolis and centripetal forces and quadratic damping
are neglected.
• It is assumed that the CG and CB are located vertically in the z-axis, that is xb = xg and yb = yg.
Therefore the hydrostatic forces on yaw are zero.
• It is assumed that a certain amount of thrust is delivered in a certain DOF, thus the thruster allocation
matrix is ignored.

• Longitudinal model: with heave velocity state, ν = [w]T .

3.2
System Identiﬁcation
3.2.1
Theoretical introduction
The idea of system identiﬁcation is to estimate an approximate model of a system for a desired application
that is based on observed input and output data and prior system knowledge. The main components of this
procedure are the prior knowledge of the system structure, the application objective of the system and the
available data. These notions are interdependent with the prior knowledge being the most prevalent on the
choice of model structure (Keesman 2011). The identiﬁcation process is summarized in Figure 3.4, in where
==PAGE:32==
16
CHAPTER 3. THRUSTER CHARACTERIZATION & SYSTEM IDENTIFICATION
it can be seen that the choice of a model is determined only on the prior knowledge of the system.
Figure 3.4: System identiﬁcation process (Keesman 2011).
Depending on which source of information is used, prior knowledge or experiment data, different
approaches on the model structure can be classiﬁed into the following three categories:
• ’White-box’ model: The model is based on the prior knowledge of the system, physical laws and
additional relationships with corresponding physical parameters.
• ’Black-box’ model: The model does not necessarily refer to the underlying physical laws and the data
is used to estimate model structure and parameters.
• ’Grey-box’ model: The model is based both on prior knowledge of the model structure and the
experimental data.
The ’grey-box’ approach is chosen for this project because the structure of the dynamics of the system is
already known by the mathematical models. In addition to model structure, a parameterization of the model
along with a criterion function which solves numerically the estimation problem is deﬁned.
The last step is to validate the estimated model and determine whether it is appropriate for the intended
application.
Next follows a short description of the strategy followed for the system identiﬁcation:
• Select a model structure that is appropriate for a control application.
• Design and carry out speciﬁc experiments in order to excite the system for the identiﬁcation of the
steering and diving dynamics.

• Collect the input-output data, inspect it and if required preprocess it applying ﬁlters, removing trends
and attenuating noise.
• Perform parameter estimation using the chosen model.
• Assess the quality of the obtained model by using model validation methods.

Kalman ﬁlter
The purpose of the discrete Kalman ﬁlter is to estimate the state of a discrete-time process that it cannot
be measured directly without error (Hendricks et al. 2008). In many cases this occurs because the states
or the measurements of the states are perturbed by noise. The properties of that noise is critical for the
performance of the ﬁlter. Both sensor and process noise are assumed to be white and Gaussian distributed
with zero mean.
In order to conﬁrm these assumptions a bench test was performed by having the OpenROV in a stationary
condition for 10min. The data were sampled at 20Hz for both sensors. The pressure sensor data, as it
can be seen from the histogram in Figure 3.11(a) it is Gaussian distributed with zero mean. Figure 3.11(b)
shows that the samples are not correlated, placed inside the 3σ conﬁdence interval. For the acceleration
data as it is shown in Figure 3.11(c) the assumption of Gaussian distribution with zero mean is conﬁrmed,
while the autocorrellation function in Figure 3.11(d) shows that the samples are highly correlated indicating
a sinusoidal component. In order to see at which frequency range this variation is dominant the power
spectral density of this signal is illustrated in Figure 3.12(a) with 12027 samples and a frequency resolution
of 0, 0017Hz/sample. It can be seen that the sinusoidal component is present at the range of frequencies
from 0.28Hz to 0.4Hz. In Figure 3.12(b) is the power spectral density plot of a diving experiment with 10%
thrust with the red line, while with the blue is the static experiment but with the same frequency resolution
of the diving experiment; That is 0.041Hz/sample. It can be seen that the sinusoidal component that was
present on the static test does not appear on the data from the underwater experiment. It is noted that the
bench test was performed after the experiments for the identiﬁcation process had been ﬁnalized.

CHAPTER 4
Diving & Manoeuvring Control System
Design
The analysis described in Section 2.2 about modeling and decoupling the ROV dynamics, indicates that
control can be achieved independently by two decoupled linear control subsystems namely, heading and
depth control. The objective of a robust control system is for the closed loop system to remain stable and
achieve a certain performance up to a certain bandwidth in presence of uncertainties and to counteract the
effect of disturbances. For that matter as it is discussed further, the assessment on how well the system
responds to the control objectives is described by speciﬁcations in the time domain. Taking into consideration
the existence of model parameter uncertainties in both identiﬁed models, lateral and longitudinal, the H∞
control design methodology was chosen.
4.1
H∞Control Design
Background on H∞control
A control design problem for a nominal plant can be formulated as one degree of freedom negative
feedback structure shown in Figure 4.1. The objective is to design a controller K such that the output y

reasons for using feedback control is due to the presence of model uncertainty and unknown disturbance

further, for robust stability of the system with respect to uncertainty modeled as additive plant perturbations.

stability and robustness by applying speciﬁc weights that shape accordingly the sensitivity functions. In this
project a multivariable synthesis methodology is applied, namely the mixed-sensitivity H∞method.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this thesis two linear models are estimated using only on-board sensors in order to describe the
manoeuvring and diving dynamics of the OpenROV. The identiﬁed models were then used for the design of
optimal robust control systems for diving and heading control.
Regarding the diving dynamics, different models are estimated depending on the operating speed of the
vehicle. Twenty models were estimated in total in order to cover the entire range of operation which are then
grouped into three models describing operations at low, medium and fast speed. Regarding the manoeuvring
dynamics, two models were identiﬁed with respect to two operating speeds. As expected, the results from
the identiﬁcation procedure showed that the linear model is an approximation of the true non-linear system
that cannot be described perfectly only by linear terms. The results of the thruster characterization in
different water depth did not show any signiﬁcant variation. The performance of the ROV in a surge course
was improved for a speed of 40% and above, while on lower speeds the robot would move along a curved path.
For the development of the diving and manoeuvring control system the mixed-sensitivity H∞control
design methodology was chosen. The results from simulation showed that the design speciﬁcations were
fulﬁlled and the control is robust in respect to model uncertainty and constant external disturbances on
the output. The order reduction of the controllers from four to three states showed a slight impact on
the closed-loop step responses on the diving control, while on the heading control it introduced a small
overshoot.
5.1
Future work
While this thesis presents a manoeuvring and diving control design approach for the OpenROV that was
designed based on linear identiﬁed models, different topics for extending the scope of this thesis may be
considered. This section presents indicatively some of these directions.
• Apply and validate the control system on the OpenROV.
• Extend the sensor package with ultrasonic range ﬁnders and in combination with image processed
data from the camera, design an obstacle avoidance and target recognition algorithm.
