Abstract
A central goal in the development of cell factories is to improve the volumetric produc-
tivity. The magnitude of impact that the genetic engineering oﬀers is immense; however,
the targeted metabolic engineering strategies do not always give the anticipated result.
It is diﬃcult to predict the phenotype from the genotype, due to our current knowledge
of the complex regulatory and metabolic networks. Therefore, it is favorable to exploit
large mutant libraries for cell factory engineering. Furthermore, when the ideal strain is
identiﬁed the productivity is often further increased by overexpression of selected genes
that are key for the production of the compound of interest. Currently, the introduction of
genetic elements in ﬁlamentous fungi is done by transformation, which requires generation
of protoplasts and can be extensive work when large libraries of strains need to be engi-
neered. Also, the methods for over-expression are limited, using autonomous maintenance
AMA1-derived plasmids, multiplexed random integrations in genome or integrations at
speciﬁc site in the strain. The ﬁrst method results in heterogeneous expression levels in
the population of cells and requires selective pressure to be applied. Random integra-
tions are not controlled, undeﬁned copy numbers and integration can result in changes
in production of other unanalyzed metabolites/molecules. While site speciﬁc integrations
are controlled, however over-expressing by repeating sequences will increase the genome
instability. For these reasons, we have initiated development of two platforms adapted
for use in ﬁlamentous fungi. CRI-SPA platform is a combination of CRISPR/Cas9 and
Selective Ploidy Ablation (SPA), the platform is mating-based and oﬀers high-throughput,
multi-gene editing in ﬁlamentous fungi strain libraries. The other platform is CASCADE,
which enables gene ampliﬁcation crating strains with deﬁned gene copy numbers through
simultaneous ampliﬁcation. The resulting strains from both platforms can be propagated
on selection-free medium.
The study sets the stage for the construction of the donor strain, which requires mul-
tiple integration sites that support:
high gene expression, are eﬀectively targeted by
CRISPR/Cas9, does not phenotypically aﬀect the growth and are located on each of
the platform strain chromosomes. The platform development was initiated in the model
organism, Aspergillus nidulans, and the platform designs adapted from yeast, Saccha-
romyces cerevisiae, with respect to the diﬀerences in the life cycle. Nine new integration
sites were located in A. nidulans genome that fulﬁlls the stated requirements, which al-
lowed initiation of the platform strain construction. Through selection free, multiplexed
gene integration, four copies of the required genetic constructs were integrated into the
speciﬁc loci by a single round of transformation and thereby allowing construction of the
CRI-SPA and CASCADE platform strain to be completed in a round and third round of
transformation, respectively, in the future.

Introduction
1
Introduction
The fungi kingdom includes microorganisms such as yeasts, mushrooms and ﬁlamen-
tous fungi. They are heterotrophs and acquire food by absorbing dissolved molecules.
Many fungi are both excellent at degradation of biomass and production of industrially
relevant compounds, making them good candidates for cell factories. Today, ﬁlamentous
fungi are successfully used as industrial cell factories for production of organic acids (e.g.
citric acid and kojic acid), enzymes (e.g. cellulase, amylase, glucanase, rennet, lipase and
laccase), secondary metabolites (e.g. penicillin) and other metabolites [1]. Fungi oﬀer
outstanding capacity in expression and secretion of homologous and heterologous proteins
[2, 3]; for instance industrial Trichoderma reesei strains can produce homologous cellulases
reaching titers of 100 g/L [4, 5].
The productivity of cell factories can be enhanced by order of magnitude by employing
metabolic engineering strategies to levels resulting in a economically feasible bioprocess.
Today, the development of fungal cell factories is an extensive process, due to complex
regulatory and metabolic networks that not always allow the designed engineering strategy
to be eﬃcient. With the growing strain libraries and genome databases, and the demand
for enzymes and pharmaceutical compounds, a high-throughput method is favourable for
ﬁnding the best strain to produce the desired product. It is therefore of interest to develop
a method that allows to introduce genes at speciﬁc loci in a high-throughput setting. The
method would be applied onto multiple strains, which would allow identiﬁcation of the best
one for the production of the compound of interest. The method will allow identiﬁcation
of novel genetic interactions or production advantages that cannot be predicted by our
current understanding of the host organism.
This study was performed to address these issues and initiate the implementation
process of two large platforms, termed CRI-SPA [6] and CASCADE [7], in ﬁlamentous
fungi. Both platforms have been implemented in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, however never
advanced to other fungal organisms. The CRI-SPA platform is mating-based and combines
clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR associated
protein-9 nuclease (Cas9) and Selective Ploidy Ablation (SPA) [8].The platform allows
high-throughput gene integration into speciﬁc loci of an acceptor strain genome. It can
be applied to transfer genes into library of diﬀerent strains, allowing to select the best
candidate to produce the desired product or to study gene interactions. CASCADE is an
amplicon based platform applied to increase desired gene/pathway copy numbers, over the
whole genome and into speciﬁc, stable and highly expressed regions. The platform comes
with a variety of strains containing diﬀerent numbers of amplicons, allowing ﬁne-tuning
of the gene expression to optimize the ﬂuxes over the pathway and improve the overall
productivity.
The genetic construct requirements for the two systems are similar and can be con-
structed simultaneously, allowing functionality assesment of two diﬀerent platforms in ﬁl-
amentous fungi using the same strain. The well studied cell factory and model organism,
Aspergillus nidulans was chosen to be ﬁrst ﬁlamentous fungi to have CRI-SPA/CASCADE
platforms developed, with primary advantage of the mapped genome.
1.1
Aim
The aim of this project is to initiate the CRI-SPA/CASCADE strain construction,
that can be used as proof of concept for establishment of functional expression platforms
in A. nidulans and subsequently in other ﬁlamentous fungi.
To succeed, new integration sites (IS) must be identiﬁed that support eﬃcient CRISPR/
Cas9 single chimeric guide RNA (sgRNA) targeting and high expression levels and do
not phenotypically aﬀect the growth.
The integration of genes is increasingly diﬃcult

1.2
Research goals
The CRI-SPA/CASCADE platform implementation in ﬁlamentous fungi requires de-

Design adaptation for functional CRI-SPA and CASCADE platforms from

Multiplexing of sgRNAs for gene integration in A. nidulans and
construction of CRI-SPA/CASCADE platform strain.

Background
2
Background
The following sections cover the background information needed to understand the
mechanics behind the CRI-SPA and CASCADE platforms in ﬁlamentous fungi and why
it is of interest to use and engineer ﬁlamentous fungi as a cell factory.
2.1
Cell factories
Filamentous fungi are diverse class of multicellular eukaryotes with exceptional se-
cretion capacity compared to other eukaryotes [3]. They have been widely applied for
production of proteins, enzymes, secondary metabolites and organic acids.
European
companies such as AB Enzymes, BASF, Bayer, Chr. Hansen, DSM, DuPont, Novozymes,
Puratos and Roal Oy are global leaders in using fungi for bulk manufacturing [9]. The en-
zymatic cocktails, which are used to convert waste lignocellulosic biomass to fermentable
sugars, had an estimated value of over e4 billion in 2016 [9], while the global sale of white
biotechnology products are estimated to reach e450 billion by end of next year [9]. Several
species of Aspergillus are widely used for industrial enzyme production, of which A. niger,
A. oryzae and A. terreus [9, 10], and it is therefor of interest to develop new platforms that
can be used for development of new products in these established production organisms.
2.2
Aspergillus genus
Aspergillus is ubiquitous and phenotypically diverse genus of ﬁlamentous fungi, con-
taining scientiﬁcally, medically and industrially important species. These include A. nidu-
lans - a model organism for fungal cell biology, A. parasiticus and A. fumigatus - medically
signiﬁcant molds, and industrially relevant A. niger and A. oryzae [2]. The species belong-
ing to this genus have saprophytic lifestyle, making them excellent secretors of enzymes for
degradation of biomass. The secretion in industrial applications is highly valuable, as it
allows to bypass many of the downstream steps, such as disruption of cells and puriﬁcation
from intracellular debris, and move straight to product puriﬁcation and polishing. The
reduced number of processing steps also improve the over all product recovery. Aspergillus
and other ﬁlamentous fungi stand out for their ability to grow in wide range of pH and
temperatures and degrade and consume a wide range of substrates. The genus is a part
of Aspergillus Whole-genus sequencing project, initiated by DOE Joint Genome Institute
(http://genome.jgi.doe.gov/). The goal of the project is to sequence, assemble and
annotate approximately 300 species of genus Apsergillus. The sequencing data can be used
to increase the diversity in the available ﬁlamentous fungi industrial production strains;
as the data allows to identify gene targets that need to be knock out in order to facilitate
genetic engineering.
2.2.1
A. nidulans
Studies of A. nidulans dates back to 1940s-1950s, where notable work was done by
Pontecorvo et al., describing cell life cycle and classical genetics through meiosis and mi-
tosis in A. nidulans [11]. A. nidulans is an established ﬁlamentous fungi model organism,
with well-developed toolbox for genetic engineering. It was the ﬁrst ﬁlamentous fungi to
be whole genome sequenced in 2005 [12]. A. nidulans is an attractive cell factory being
able to produce a plethora of biosynthetically diverse secondary metabolites [13]. The
A. nidulans produces unicellular spores, that makes it easy to handle and the growth
requirements are simple and inexpensive [14].
Implementation of CRI-SPA and CASCADE system in ﬁlamentous fungi is initiated
in well studied A. nidulans FGSC A4. The FGSC A4 is Glasgow wild type (veA+) [11].
The veA is a key gene in regulating sexual development [15]. The genome consist of eight

2.2.2
Life cycle
The life cycle of A. nidulans is diverse and the organism can reproduce through asexual,
sexual and parasexual, with the asexual being dominant [17, 18].
A. nidulans is also
homothallic [11], allowing for sexual cycle to initiate from two nuclei that are derived from
the same individual. The three cell cycles of A. nidulans are presented in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Life cycle of homothallic A. nidulans.
The organism can reproduce through asexual cycle
(Orange arrow), sexual cycle (pink arrow) or parasexual cycle (green arrow). Anastamosis takes place when
heterokaryon is formed (Blue arrow), during fusion of two strains with genetically diﬀerent backgrounds.
Figure adapted and modiﬁed from Casselton and Zolan, 2002 [18].
The asexual cycle forms identical haploid cells called conidium, produced at the heads
of conidiophores. Once the spores spread and reach favourable growth conditions, they
Page 4
==PAGE:17==
Background
germinate forming mycelium networks from which new conidiophores develop. Alterna-
tively, two mycelium can fuse together with another strain of diﬀerent genetic background
through anastomosis. The result is the generation of a heterokaryon. Heterokaryons carry
nuclei of both strains and complement one another in situations of auxotrophic deﬁcien-
cies in one of the strain. The heterokaryon is rather unstable [18] and can return back to
the haploid homokaryon state, or undergo sexual or parasexual reproduction. The coni-
dium can only contain a single nucleus, which also means that the spores developed by
heterokaryon carries only one of the two heterokaryon nuclei.
The parasexual reproduction is an alternative to meiosis. This cycle is initiated with
formation of diploid homokaryon through nuclei fusion of heterokaryon nuclei. The diploid
nuclei carries 16 chromosomes, twice as many as in haploid state. The cell can then undergo
mitosis. The mitotic cell cycle results in two cells with nuclei from either of the parent
[19].
The sexual cycle is possible for both heterokaryon and homokaryon. During the sexual
cycle, thousands of ascospores are produced inside a a special cell called ascus[18]. Inside
the ascus, karyogamy (nuclear fusion) and meiosis takes place. The fruiting bodies of A.
nidulans, cleistothecia, can contain 104 asci [18]. The screening of these cells for gene
conversion allowed the ﬁrst molecular models for genetic recombination to be developed
[20], as each ascus is the result of separate meiotic event from a single, the same, nuclear
pair [18].
2.3
Double stranded break DNA repair
Genome stability is very important for ﬁtness and viability of living organism. Eﬃ-
cient and accurate repair system is required when DNA damage occurs. There are many
types of DNA damage, however the most severe are the DNA double stranded breaks
(DSB) that result in chromosomal rearrangement, cell cycle arrest or cell death. [14, 21]
The three dominant DSB repair pathways include non-homologous-end-joining (NHEJ),
microhomology-mediated end joining (MMEJ) and homologous recombination (HR). DSB
initiates the deployment of evolutionarily conserved Mre11-Rad50-Xrs2 (MRX) complex,
that holds the broken strands tethered to one another and activates the DSB signalling
pathway through checkpoint kinase Tel1 [22, 23]. The choice of the repair pathway is
inﬂuenced by the availability of sister chromatid that diﬀers in diﬀerent stages of the cell
cycle [21].
NHEJ is a DSB repair pathway that ligates two DNA strands without the use of
extended homology to guide the repair [23, 25]. NHEJ is known to have high potential for
error, with small insertions and deletions at the break site [26]. The NHEJ is dominant
during G1 and early S phase when no sister chromatid is available to serve as a repair
template.
The pathway is dependent on Ku, DNA ligase IV and other proteins [25].
Ku is a heterodimer of two related proteins, Ku70 and Ku80, that binds the ends of the
DSB strands. The binding results in conformation change of Ku allowing it to interact and
activate the Lig4 complex, that ligates the two DNA strands together [25]. The DSB repair
through NHEJ can be error free, however a few bases are often lost in the processing of
the ends prior to ligation of the two strands resulting in mutation. In an event of multiple
DNA DSBs, the broken ends of DNA can be mislinked resulting in insertion or deletion
of DNA.
Alternative to NHEJ, the cell can undergo MMEJ repair of DSB that functions simi-
larly, but employs diﬀerent enzymes to repair the break. The enzyme machinery behind
this process is not as well-deﬁned as in case of NHEJ. In an event of Ku protein not
binding, or removal of it from the DSB site, the MRX complex will deploy Exo1 or Dna2
nucleases that removes nucleotides from the strand that has 5’ end facing DNA DSB. Re-
section leaves a 3’-end single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) tail [27]. The restriction can reveal

HR is active during late S and G2/M stage, when sister chromatids have been synthe-

result in loss of heterozygosity. Through the process of HR, DNA can also exchange large
segments of two double-stranded DNA molecules and the sequence diﬀerences between

resolution can result in either DNA strand crossover or non-crossover, while dissolution
results in non-crossover and no modiﬁcation of sister chromatid, see Figure 3.C [20, 24].

2.4.1
Methods of introducing DNA
The genetic transformation of ﬁlamentous fungi is either done on chromosome level or
by introducing an autonomously replicating plasmid, such as autonomous maintenance in
Aspergillus (AMA1). The AMA1 plasmids have showed replicability in many Aspergillus
[32], Penicillium chrysogenum [33] and other fungi. They are mitotically unstable with
average copy number of 10-30 per haploid genome [32]. The instability of the plasmid
can be seen as an advantage, allowing for fast loss of plasmid when not under selective
pressure. It is therefore ideal to carry a system, such as CRISPR/Cas9, that is intended
to be removed from transformed cells.
DNA can be introduced into a speciﬁc chromosomal location through recombination
between the targeting sequences (TS) ﬂanking your favorite gene (YFG) and the homol-
ogous sequences in the genome [31]. For eﬃcient targeting in ﬁlamentus fungi, these TS
must be of at least 1000 bp [34]. The eﬃciency of gene targeting can be facilitated through
induction of DSB using CRISPR/Cas9 or homing endonucleases and thereby increasing
pressure to integrate the targeted construct.
2.4.2
Markers
The selection markers are essential for selection of transformed cells. These markers
can either be auxotrophic or antibiotic. Auxotrophic markers allow the cell to synthesize
Page 7
==PAGE:20==
Background
essential compounds, such as amino acid or vitamin, while antibiotic resistance markers
allow to inactivate antibiotics.
Common auxotrophic markers include argB and pyrG
[35], required in arginine and uridine synthesis, respectively. Fungal antibiotic resistance
markers include among others hygromycin [36] and bleomycin [37]. While selection mark-
ers are available for ﬁlamentous fungi, only two counter-selectable markers are currently
known, pyrG and amdS. Counter-selectable markers have bi-substrate activity, meaning
that the enzyme transcribed can act on more than one substrate. They function as regular
auxotrophic markers, but can be counter-selected by a speciﬁc compound. The counter-
selectable marker can act on this speciﬁc compound, turning it from growth non-inhibiting
into a compound that is either lethal or inhibits the growth of the host organism. The
fungal counter-selectable markers, pyrG [35] and amdS [38], can be counterselected by
5-ﬂuoroorotic acid (5-FOA) and 5’-ﬂuoroacetamide (FAA), respectively.
2.4.3
Expression sites
The gene expression in ﬁlamentous fungi is dependent on the integrated loci and the
promoter used. The chromosomal location for gene integration has great impact on gene
expression, as these locations can be methylated in heterochromatin structures, resulting
in blocked binding of transcription factors and lowered gene expression. The neighboring
genes of the integration loci must be considered, as the integration can result in changes
in their expression levels and thereby can have eﬀect on strains health and ﬁtness. Seven
integration sites (IS) in A. nidulans are described in literature: IS1 [39], IS2 [40] and IS3
to IS7 [41] and an unpublished IS8 [in-house]. These IS are located on Chromosome I to
IV, VI and VIII and support high gene expressions, except for IS3 [41].
2.4.4
Promoters/terminators
The expression of the gene is regulated by promoter and terminator. There are two
types of promoters, inducible and constitutive. Inducible promoters are only activated in
the presence of a speciﬁc compound, therefore allowing for inducible gene expression. An
example of inducible promoters is glaA, that is induced in presence of maltose [3]. The
constitutive promoters result in continuous gene expression. A wide range of ﬁlamentous
fungi promoters are available that have also been applied in heterologous gene expression,
allowing to select the appropriate promoter for the expression of the genes, considering
control mechanism and functional properties [2, 3].
2.5
CRISPR/Cas9
CRISPR is one of the key scientiﬁc discoveries of the 21st century. The technology
originates from prokaryotic and archaeal immune systems to counter phage invasions [42].
CRISPR was discovered ﬁrst in 1987 [43], however the function of the observed short
palindromic repeats in the Escherichia coli genome were unknown.
Only in 2007 [44]
the ﬁrst experimental evidence for CRISPR adaptive immunity was acquired. Increased
number of laboratories studied the system and in 2012 the in vitro characterization of DNA
targeting by CRISPR associated protein-9 nuclease (Cas9) derived from Streptococcus
pyogenes and S. thermophilus was published [44, 45], followed by the ﬁrst demonstration
of CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing in eukaryotic cells in 2013 [46]. The functionality of
CRISPR/Cas9 in eukaryotes has allowed the technology to expand to a broad range of
living organisms.
The CRISPR/Cas9 system is composed of cas9 gene and a CRISPR array consisting
of unique DNA-targeting sequences interspersed with identical repeats [47]. Once tran-
scribed, the precursor CRISPR RNA (pre-crRNA) molecules are formed. The pre-crRNA
binds the trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA) and, in the presence of the Cas9, the newly
Page 8
==PAGE:21==
Background
formed double-stranded RNAs are processed by ribonuclease RNase III, separating the
pre-crRNA into intermediate crRNA [48]. The intermediate crRNA is processed by endo-
or exo-nuclease resulting in mature crRNA in duplex with tracrRNA [49], known as guide
RNA (gRNA).
The CRISPR/Cas9 system used today for genetic engineering consists of two compo-
nents, a Cas9 nuclease and a single chimeric guide RNA (sgRNA). The sgRNA is composed
of the same two RNA molecules as the gRNA, however, synthetically linked by a GAAA
tetraloop [49, 50]. Once the sgRNA binds to Cas9, the Cas9 nuclease is guided to a speciﬁc
site in the genome.
The guidance by sgRNA occurs through Cas9 complex interaction with protospacer
adjacent motif (PAM) sequences, 5’-NGG, in the genome. During interaction, the double
stranded DNA is melted and the 20 nucleotide (nt) protospacer sequence, present upstream
of the PAM sequence, binds the 5’-end of the sgRNA sequence [51]. A complete binding
results in Cas9 nuclease activation and a blunt DSB, approximately 3-5 nt from the 5’-end
of the PAM sequence [52].
The Cas9 is a single, multidomain eﬀector protein that consists of two regions, nucle-
ase (NUC) lobe and the recognition (REC) lobe. The NUC lobe consists of the PAM-
interacting domain and two unrelated nuclease domains: RuvC responsible for cleavage
of the displaced DNA strand and HNH responsible for cleavage of the target DNA strand
[50]. The PAM-interacting domain recognize the PAM sequence resulting in Cas9 inter-
acting with the site [50]. The binding requires complete complementarity of the ﬁrst 10-12
nt from the 3’-end of the protospacer sequence to the DNA sequence [46, 53, 54]. This
area is known as the ”seed-sequence” of the protospacer. Missmatch in this segment can
abolish Cas9 binding and cleavage activity [55, 56]. The binding of the sgRNA to the
DNA is mediated by the REC lobe and during the process, the HNH domain undergoes
conformational transition [51]. The completed binding of the protospacer results in the
HNH activation and positioning, which enables cleavage of the DNA [51]. The mode of
action is illustrated in Figure 4.
The CRISPR technology applications are very wide, as diﬀerent variants of Cas nucle-
ase exist, that can have diﬀerent PAM recognition sequence, protospacer sequence length
requirement or diﬀerent nuclease activity. The nuclease can also be mutated to alter their
function [42]. The removal of nuclease activity creates deactivated Cas (dCas), that binds
DNA without nicking it [57, 58]. This can prevent for example RNA polymerase from
binding to the DNA and thus silencing a gene expression. Furthermore, the dCas can be
fused with transcription regulators to overexpress [59, 60, 61] or silence a gene [61].
2.5.1
CRISPR/Cas9 in ﬁlamentous fungi
CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing in ﬁlamentous fungi is possible by a plasmid based system
[52].
The system has been developed allowing for multiplexing of sgRNAs in a single
plasmid by Nødvig et al., 2018 [62]. These plasmids contain the AMA1 sequence and
have been developed with commonly used markers, such as Afum pyrG, Anid argB, bleR
or hygR.
The Cas9 used in the construct originates from Streptococcus pyogenes, and
therefore is codon optimized for expression in industry relevant A. niger with a SV40
nuclear localization sequence fused to the 3’-end of the gene [52]. The expression of Cas9
is regulated by the Anid tef promoter and terminator [52].
The plasmids contain the
PacI/Nt.BbvCI cassette, allowing to introduce a gene cassette containing one or more
user deﬁned sgRNA sequences spaced by tRNA and regulated by Afum U3 promoter and
terminator [62]. Once transcribed, the sgRNAs are liberated by RNase P and RNase Z
that cleaves pre-RNA transcript at the 5’- and 3’ linkages between the sgRNA and tRNA,
respectively [62]. This allows Cas9 to be delivered together with multiple diﬀerent sgRNA
using a single plasmid. The plasmids can be assembled through USER cloning [62, 63].

2.5.2
Technique to assess protospacer eﬃciency (TAPE)
The targeted delivery of the Cas9 to the locus depends on the protospacer-PAM se-
quence nucleotide motifs, as the lack of their sequence speciﬁcity can result in oﬀ-targets
[64]. At the moment, reliable in silico models of sgRNA eﬃciences or oﬀ-targets predictions
do not exist for A. nidulans. However, the eﬃciencies of the CRISPR/Cas9 sgRNA se-
quences can be assessed using a technique to assess protospacer eﬃciency (TAPE) [62, 65].
The technique relies on comparison of a number of colonies formed after transformation
of NHEJ deﬁcient and proﬁcient strains with a CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid encoding either
sgRNA or no sgRNA (empty plasmid; control). The NHEJ proﬁcient strains can repair
the DSB and create point mutations through the process, that can prevent sgRNA from
binding to the targeted site and thus no further cleaving can occur. On the other hand,
the induction of the DSB will be fatal in the NHEJ deﬁcient strains, as no repair template
is present to initiate the HR. Therefore, by comparing the transformants acquired, the eﬃ-
ciency of the sgRNA can be predicted. The TAPE can therefore be used to identify highly
eﬃcient protospacer sequences that can be used in NHEJ deﬁcient strains for marker free
gene editing and/or multiplexed experiments [62].

CRI-SPA in S. cerevisiae
CRI-SPA is a mating-based platform for transformation and genomic integration in
yeast, S. cerevisiae. The platform was introduced by was introduced by Coumou, 2017 [6]
and is currently under development at the Technical University of Denmark by PhD stu-
dent Hel´en E. Olsson. The method combines CRISPR/Cas9 and Selective Ploidy Ablation
(SPA) in yeast. The SPA is a method developed in yeast for high-throughput introduc-

The UDS chromosomes are conditionally unstable, as they contain a strong inducible pro-
moter, PGAL10 [66], near each of the relatively short S. cerevisiae centromeres (of 125
nt) and an counterselectable Kl URA3 marker on each of the chromosome [8]. The SPA
method allows to transfer the plasmid in three steps, Figure 6. First, the UDS is mated

yeast can be performed through only three replica transfer steps, taking less then a week

In the SPA methods perspective, the CRI-SPA introduces DSB in the library
chromosome when cells are in the diploid state. Using homology directed repair (HDR),

strain. The platform can also be applied to delete a part of the genomic sequence, a gene
or a whole pathway. The locus of gene-expression cassette in the UDS will deﬁne the
chromosomal IS in each of the recipient library strains.
One of the major advantages of the CRI-SPA is the possibility to transfer a speciﬁc
genetic feature in a deﬁned location to a library of strains without the requirement of com-
petent cells. The protoplastation of ﬁlamentous fungal cultures is one of the bottlenecks
in gene introduction through transformation. Furthermore maintenance of protoplast of
a large strain library is labour intensive. It is therefore of great interest to implement
the mating-based CRI-SPA platform in ﬁlamentous fungi for high-throughput transfer of
genes or mutations into gene libraries to identify novel genetic interactions or production
advantages that cannot be predicted by our current understanding of a host organism.

CASCADE in S. cerevisiae
The CASCADE platform was developed by Strucko et al., 2017 [7] for high, tunable
and selection-free gene expression in S. cerevisiae. The platform allows fast construction of
a stable strain containing multiple copies of a gene, or multiple genes, that are integrated at
diﬀerent loci in intergenic regions of the genome. The platform development was initiated
for possibility for genome expression of large, multistep pathways [67]. The creation of
the CASCADE platform was inspired by the group I introns homing endonucleases [68]
and the mating type switching in yeast [69]. The homing endonucleases are highly speciﬁc
due to their long DNA target site. The same class of enzyme is involved in the mating
type switching in yeast [68, 69], where the process is initiated by the homing endonucleases
activity resulting in DSB followed by HDR. These are the bases of the CASCADE platform
in which a gene acceptor strain (GA strain) is created with a deﬁned number of gene
acceptor cassettes (GA cassettes) and a donor amplicon sequence that may contain one
or multiple genes.

Results and Discussion
This project was aimed to initiated the development of two platforms simultaneously,
adapted for use in ﬁlamentous fungi, A. nidulans. The results are presented by ﬁrst gibing

3.1
Compatibility of CRI-SPA and CASCADE
The advantages of implementing CRI-SPA and CASCADE in ﬁlamentous fungi are
diﬀerent. CRI-SPA allows high-throughput transfer of genes or mutations into a library
of strains, thus opening possibility to identify novel genetic interactions and production
advantages that cannot be predicted by our current understanding of a host organism.
Despite that they are more complex organisms than S. cerevisiae, two features make
it feasible to implement CRI-SPA in ﬁlamentous fungi.
Firstly, it contains only eight
chromosomes [70] as compared to the sixteen in S. cerevisiae [71]. This fact simpliﬁes donor
strain construction. Secondly, ﬁlamentous fungi are typically haploid lifeforms [72] whereas
S. cerevisiae prefers to be diploid. This fact simpliﬁes haploidization. The CASCADE
platform oﬀers ampliﬁcation of genes into stable and deﬁned IS on the genome. The gene
expression can be controlled through selecting a strain with desired number of amplicons.
Despite the diﬀerent application of the two platforms, the genetic requirements allow to
construct them in the same strain, while using separately of one another. CRI-SPA strain
only has one requirement, a counterselectable marker on each chromosomes. CASCADE
requires that the IS used support high gene expression, are stable and does not aﬀect
phenotypically the strain. The requirements of the two platform strains are summarized
in Table 1. Similarities in the requirements allows to construct both platforms in the
same strain. The CASCADE construct contains the CRI-SPA required counterselectable
marker, and if integrated on all chromosomes, can serve in both platform applications. The
CASCADE construct consists of host unique TSs, A and B, ﬂanking the counterselectable
marker, pyrG, and a homing endonuclease, I-SceI, restriction site.

3.2
CRI-SPA platform in A. nidulans
The implementation of CRI-SPA in ﬁlamentous fungi is of particular interest since it
allows gene transfer through parasexual cycle. The CRI-SPA platform oﬀers transfer of
a speciﬁc genetic feature from the donor strain to recipient strain through the parasexual
cycle. The platform oﬀers possibility to genetically engineer ﬁlamentous fungi, that are
absent of sexual cycle, through mating. In many of industrially relevant fungi sexual cycle
has rarely, or never observed for instance in A. niger [17]. A. nidulans was chosen as
the model organism for CRI-SPA platform in ﬁlamentous fungi, because of the mapped

genome. Sexual reproduction is possible in A. nidulans, however the frequency of sexual
reproduction is very low [73].
The platform functions by ﬁrst integrating YFG in the donor strain by transformation,
this strain is then mated with a library strain and selected for diploid formation.
In
the diploid cell, the YFG gene is transferred. The Diploid is then induced to forced to
haploidize by missegregation during mitosis. The mechanisms of the YFG integration in
the donor strain and gene transfer from the donor strain into the library strain by CRI-
SPA platform are presented in Figure 8 and Figure 8, respectively, and explained in the
following paragraphs.
The platform demands the strains to be NHEJ deﬁcient, have diﬀerent auxotrophies
and the proposed design requires that the strains with deﬁcient pigmentation are used. The
NHEJ deﬁcient strains are required for targeted gene integration by CRISPR/Cas9 [62]. As
mentioned in the section 2.3, the repair by NHEJ can induce mutations in the protospacer
sequence, resulting in no further targeting of CRISPR/Cas9 and no gene integration. The
NHEJ strains can be created by the knock out of the key genes in the NHEJ repair pathway,
for example ku70 [25]. Diﬀerent auxotrophies, such as pyrG and argB, allow the selection
for heterokaryon formation by complementarity [17]. The selection is possible because
a heterokaryon contains both parental nuclei and is able to synthesize both uridine and
arginine. The heterokaryotic stage is broken during sporulation, because spores contain
single parental nuclei, which depending on the parental nuclei will either be pyrG- or
argB-. Alternatively, nuclear fusion can take place inside heterokaryon, forming a diploid
nuclei that can be selected by the spore color. Two genes are responsible for the green
colour of A. nidulans spores [74], the wA and yA, and knockouts of either of these result in
white or yellow spores, respectively. As mentioned earlier, the spores contain only single
nucleus and, as a result of complementation, only spores of a diploid will be green. In the
platform design, a white (wA-) donor strain was used and a yellow (yA-) library strain,
however, it could have just as well been a yellow donor and a white library strain. The
mechanisms behind the CRI-SPA platform are explained in the following paragraphs.
For CRI-SPA platform application, the donor strain must contain counterselectable
marker integrated onto all eight chromosomes.
For this purpose, multiplatform donor
strain will be used with CRI-SPA/CASCADE cassette, Figure 7 panel a, integrated on all
eight chromosomes. The pre-stage of CRI-SPA application involves integration of YFG
construct into the donor strain using high eﬃciency CRISPR/Cas9 sgRNA, Figure 7 panel
b. The gene must be integrated onto the donor strain chromosome, between two pyrG
counterselectable markers on the same side of the centromere, thus preventing possible
crossover events as a result of DSB repair through dHJ resolution. The native pyrG is
located on the chromosome I. It is important that the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid is retained,
therefore selective pressure must not be removed after the transformation.
The ﬁrst stage involves mating of the created donor strain with the library strain,
formations of heterokaryons and diploids, Figure 8 panel a. To complete this state, two
fungal strains are inoculated, using approximately equal amounts of spores, in liquid com-
plete medium (CM) with essential supplements and grown overnight [17]. The diﬀerence
in the auxotrophy allows the selection for heterokaryon formation. The resulting mycelium
is plated on minimum medium (MM), allowing the heterokaryon to form conidium and
generate spores. The nuclear fusion of heterokaryon nuclei will result in formation of green
spores containing diploid nuclei. During the ﬁrst stage, additional care must be taken,
because cleistothecia can be formed. Cleistothecia contains thousands of asci [18] that
are formed through meiosis and each chromosome is unique and contains a mix of genetic
information from the parental chromosomes. The formation of cleistothecia is relatively
slow process, therefore the green spores must be selected as soon as possible to initiate
the second stage of the CRI-SPA platform.

sette and native pyrG marker (indicated in orange). The YFG is integrated in the strain by HDR of

During the diploid state, the gene transfer using CRI-SPA takes place, Figure 8 panel b.
In a diploid cell, each nucleus contains two copies of each chromosome: one from the donor
strain and one from the library strain. The diploid nucleus also contains the CRISPR/Cas9
plasmid from the donor strain, which was introduced in the pre-stage during integration
of YFG. The CRISPR/Cas9 targets the same locus in the library strains chromosome
where the YFG was integrated in the donor and creates DSB. The DSB initiates the
DSB repair pathways. The additional chromosome copy available in the diploid cell will
favour HR pathway using the donor chromosome as template. The repair results in loss
of heterozygosity at the speciﬁc site [14] and, thereby, integration of YFG from the donor
chromosome into the library chromosome. Integration disrupts the protospacer sequence
which prevents CRISPR/Cas9 from further targeting.
During the second and ﬁnal stage, the diploid cells are haploidized, which in ﬁlamentous
fungi can be achieved by exposure to low concentrations of tubulin destabilizing agent, such
as benomyl [17, 75]. Benomyl (Methyl 1-(butylcarbamoyl)-2-benzimidazolecarbamate) is
a fungicide that has been commercially introduced by DuPont in 1968 and widely used
in agriculture. The drug acts on fungal microtubule structures, depolymerizing them into
monomers. Benomyl is a reversible drug, making it ideal for CRI-SPA platform. [75] This
is done by spot plating of the green spores on CM, supplemented with benomyl, 5-FOA
and the required amino acids and nucleobases. The exposure to the drug will cause the
strain to undergo haploidization through chromosome missegregation during mitosis re-
sulting in strains containing random distribution of donor and library strain chromosomes,
Figure 8 panel c. Each of the donor strain chromosomes contains a CRI-SPA/CASCADE
cassette, as described previously. These chromosomes can be counterselected on medium
containing 5-FOA, which will prevent development of all haploid nuclei containing any
number of donor strain chromosomes. The parental library strain is counterselected by
CRISPR/Cas9, as the protospacer sequence is not disrupted. Therefore the growth will
be only possible for the library strain with YFG integrated at the deﬁned locus. It is
possible that this stage will be required to be performed as two stages, ﬁrst induce hap-
loidization on medium containing benomyl and, second, counterselect for the donor strain
chromosomes, because stress on the cell might be too large when applied simultaneously.

3.3
CASCADE platform in A. nidulans
The CASCADE platform allows to amplify a single gene construct throughout the
genome after two rounds of transformation. The CASCADE platform strain is derived
from the CRI-SPA strain through knock out of the native pyrG gene. This can be done
during diﬀerent stages of the CRI-SPA strain construction, resulting in wide range of strain
containing diﬀerent number of amplicon constructs. The CASCADE strain is referred to
as CASCADE-X strain, where X deﬁnes the number of the amplicon cassettes the strain
carries. The strains containing varying number of amplicon cassettes allows to optimize
the ﬂux through pathway by adjustment of gene copy numbers. The CASCADE platform
can be used to amplify large multi-gene constructs in four steps, visualized in Figure 9
using eight amplicon strains, and explained in the following paragraphs.
The ﬁrst step is to integrate the gene or whole pathway, referred to as YFG, to be
ampliﬁed into one of the available integration sites of CASCADE-X strain. The YFG must
be ﬂanked by the CASCADE TS A and B, same TS as used in the amplicon cassettes
and a TS for integration of this construct at speciﬁc locus. The integration is mediated
by CRISPR/Cas9 encoding single sgRNA, targeting speciﬁc loci.
In the second step, the strain is transformed using a plasmid encoding I-SceI homing
endonuclease. The expression of endonuclease is controlled by inducible promoter, using
for example A. niger glucoamylase inducible promoter glaA that is active in presence of
maltose or starch [3]. A deviation from the original platform design could be made here
by expressing I-SceI using a constitutive promoter, allowing to reduce the number of steps
by one.
The induction of I-SceI takes place in the third step. The nuclease targets the re-
striction sites in the integrated amplicon cassettes, which results in a DNA DSBs. The
DSBs are repaired through HDR, recognizing the homology between the DSB site and the
TS A and B integrated together with the YFG. This results in marker free ampliﬁcation,
replacing all of the amplicon cassettes with a YFG copy. Through the repair the pyrG
marker is lost, as it is located in between the homology recognized sequences, TS A and
B.
The fourth and ﬁnal step is counterselection of any colony that carries intact ampli-
con cassette. This is done on medium containing required supplements and 5-FOA. The
resulting strain carries X+1 copies of YFG and is pyrG deﬁcient.
The design can be altered for the CASCADE platform in ﬁlamentous fungi.
The
platform can be operated using only CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids, by replacing the I-SceI
encoding plasmid with CRISPR/Cas9 encoding single sgRNA that targets the integrated
CASCADE cassettes. Alternatively, if I-SceI functions eﬀectively in ﬁlamentous fungi, it
would be beneﬁcial to adjust the designs and reconstruct the CASCADE platform strain
without using CRISPR/Cas9. This would allow industry to use the technology without
CRISPR/Cas9 license.

CRI-SPA/CASCADE strain construction
The construction of CRI-SPA/CASCADE platform strain requires nine ISs, one on
each of the A. nidulans chromosome, for the integration of the CRI-SPA/CASCADE
cassette and an additional IS for application of the CRI-SPA and CASCADE platforms.
The additional IS for the CRI-SPA platform must be located between two pyrG markers,
to counterselect the colonies that resulted in crossover event during HR, by dHJ resolution.
To reduce number of required cassette integrations, this site located between the native
pyrG, located on chromosome I, and integrated CRI-SPA/CASCADE cassette. All sites
must support high expression and eﬃcient targeting by CRISPR/Cas9 sgRNA. Currently,
insuﬃcient ISs are described in A. nidulans, which demands the identiﬁcation of additional
sites for implementation of these platforms. In this project, the ISs were selected based on
transcriptomics data of A. nidulans grown on wide range of substrates [76], data available
on Gene Express Omnibus (GEO) database, accession no. GSE9298. The data was sorted
using 3 stage, gradient color coding, allowing quick visualization of chromosomal areas that
support high transcription levels. Within these areas, ISs were selected with the criteria
that the IS is located in intergenic region, between the ORF of opposite orientation.
An example for the IS selection on chromosome V, based on the transcriptomics data,
Figure S1. The process of testing the selected IS is presented in a ﬂow diagram, Figure 10.

Platform strain construction through multiplexing
Two constructs were considered in the CRI-SPA/CASCADE platform strain construc-
tion, presented in Figure 16. It was preferred to create the strain without markers, as this
simpliﬁes the construct, does not use limited fungal markers and also increase the speed
of the strain construction, compared to if markers were to be removed post integration.
It also promotes the marker free genetic engineering in ﬁlamentous fungi. Marker free
strain construction required high eﬃciency CRISPR/Cas9 sgRNA to mediate the integra-
tion, which also allows for multiplexing of the integrations. An alternative construct that
contains antibiotic resistance gene, for example HYG, ﬂanked by LoxP sequences. The
construct was assembled in case of selection free multiplexing was not possible. The LoxP
sequences in the construct allows to loop out the marker using the Cre recombinase, that
recognize LoxP sequences [79]. These alternative constructs were constructed as a backup
plan.
Figure 16: The two considered CRI-SPA/CASCADE cassette designs. Both cassettes consist of two TS

2017 [7]), I-SceI restriction site and a counterselectable pyrG marker. In the case of required selection,
alternative cassette was considered with addition of antibiotic marker (Hygromycin resistance) that can
be looped out using Cre-Lox recombination.

repair templates for each of the targeted IS. Successful integrations in all eight sites results
in CRI-SPA platform strain, presented in Figure 17 (blue area).

allowing to generate a variety of CASCADE amplicon strains.

because the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid argB marker originates from A. nidulans and the
strain used encodes mutated argB that has lost its function. The ORF of argB 1.2 kb,
which is suﬃcient for recombination to take place and repair the mutation that caused
the deﬁciency.
There are multiple approaches how to cope with the previously described problem, one
is knock out of the native argB, other possibility is used of CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids with
antibiotic resistance marker instead of argB, the third possibility test more colonies that
appeared later, if argB integrated and/or recovered its function the strains would have
growth advantage and the strains that were tested in the study were from the very ﬁrst
transformants.
To cope with this, a CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid encoding two sgRNAs and targeting the

plasmid encoding four sgRNAs with HYGR marker was assembled, allowing to repeat the
transformation with hygromycin selective pressure.
Due to limited time, no further investigation could be performed. However, all of the
required plasmids for both platform strain construction and testing were assembled, of
which most are sequenced, allowing to proceed with the strain construction once unknown
issues with argB marker are solved.
4
Future perspectives
The following sections covers the strength and weaknesses of the CRI-SPA and CAS-
CADE platform in ﬁlamentous fungi, proposed ideas for the CRI-SPA and CASCADE
platform functionality assessment in ﬁlamentous fungi and a strategy for expansion of the
platform into A. niger.
4.1
Strengths and weaknesses of CRI-SPA/CASCADE platform
The CRI-SPA platform is powerful at transfer of genes into large library strains through
the parasexual cycle. The platform can be applied on strain knock-out libraries to study
gene interactions, both by gene integration and knock out. The platform can be applied
in industry to identify the strains with advantages in terms of the desied compound.
However, the platform comes with some drawbacks. The platform can only be applied to
engineer library recipient strains that are pyrG-. Furthermore, the proposed designs of the
CRI-SPA platform in ﬁlamentous fungi is dependent on selection of the diploid formation
based on the spore pigmentation, white (wA-) and yellow (yA-). This additionally reduces
the pool of strains for which platform can be used. One could think of a reporter system
which is only located in the donor strain and that is only activated once the donor has
Page 30
==PAGE:43==
Future perspectives
mated to form a diploid, independent of the genotype of the library strain.
The CASCADE platform is a powerful gene ampliﬁcation system, that allows inte-
gration of large gene constructs in multiple targeted loci in the genome. These loci are
well characterized for expression levels, stability and phenotype.
A draw back of the
CASCADE platform is that it can only be applied in developed CASCADE strains.
Another advantage that CASCADE oﬀers is the possibility to alter the integrated
cassettes into all eight chromosomes. For example, the CASCADE could be applied to
amplify another fungal counterselectable marker, such as amdS. The resulting strain can
be applied in the CRI-SPA platform as a donor strain, where counterselection of the donor
strain chromosomes are achieved by counterselection of amdS and allowing allowing the
platform to be used with amdS- library strains instead of pyrG-.
4.2
Proposed CRI-SPA platform test
To test the CRI-SPA platform, it is suggested to transfer the uidA gene from the donor
strain to a small recipient library. The uidA gene encodes the β-D-glucuronidase (GUS)
enzyme that can be applied to hydrolyze the chromogenic substrate, X-Gluc (5-bromo-4-
chloro-3-indolyl-beta-D-glucuronic acid, cyclohexylammonium salt). The hydrolysis of the
X-Gluc results in formation of colorless glucuronic acid and a colorless indoxyl derivative,
which in presence of atmospheric oxygen undergoes oxidative dimerization forming insol-
uble indigo dye (blue color). Therefore, uidA can be used as a reporter gene to indicate
successful application of the CRI-SPA platform. The construct for integration of the uidA
in the donor strain is presented in Figure 19. After the CRI-SPA transfer of this construct
into the library strain chromosome, the strain can be grown on MM supplemented with
required supplements and X-Gluc. A successful platform implementation and application
will result in blue color formation during growth of the resulting yellow library strains. It
is suggested to use an isogenic library in the proof of principle studies, ensuring that the
genome will be close to identical for the donor and recipient strains.

4.3
Proposed CASCADE platform test
The CASCADE platform should be tested through similar experiments as it was tested
in yeast, S. cerevisiae. The initial stage experiments performed by Strucko et al., 2017 [7]
includes expression of lacZ and RFP::CFP gene pair. In ﬁlamentous fungi, the expression
at speciﬁc IS is assessed by heterologous production of lacZ and by RFP. However, the use
of bidirectional expression of cyan and red ﬂuorescence protein would provide more detailed
analysis. The RFP strains developed in this study cannot be used for the comparison
of the expression, as they contain pyrG marker, and the presence of it could result in
altered expression level. This prevents comparing expression levels of these strains to the
CASCADE amplicon strain, because it is not possible to amplify a constructs encoding
pyrG with a CASCADE strain, due to counterselection in the fourth step of CASCADE
platform application. For the comparison of the ampliﬁed expression to the respective
single site expressions requires creation of new strains without use of the pyrG marker.
Page 31
==PAGE:44==
Conclusion
4.4
Platform expansion to A. niger
A. niger is the ideal ﬁlamentous fungus to have the CRI-SPA platform implemented.
The organism is one of the most important ﬁlamentous fungi cell factories in industry,
producing more than 80 % of all citric acid in the world [80, 81]. More importantly, the A.
niger does not have a sexual cycle and is dependent on gene integration through transfor-
mation. It is therefore favorable to implement the CRI-SPA and CASCADE platform in
this organism, once the mapped genome is publicly available and the ﬁrst results of func-
tionality in A. nidulans are acquired. The described designs in this study can be directly
applied in A. niger, with only slight modiﬁcations in the color selection system. The A.
niger black spore color can be altered to fawn (fwnA-), brown (brnA-) and olive (olvA-)
or other colors [82]. This allows the same method for selection of diploid formation in A.
nidulans to be used also for A niger.
5
Conclusion
In this study, nine new IS were identiﬁed in A. nidulans that support high expression
levels and are eﬀectively targeted by CRISPR/Cas9. Eﬀective target sites for CRISPR/Cas9
were also identiﬁed for six out of the eight previously described A. nidulans IS. The study
presents the platform strain designs and the adapted designs of the CRI-SPA/CASCADE
platform from S. cerevisiae to A. nidulans. The study was concluded by a successful, se-
lection free, multiplexed gene integration of four copies of the required genetic constructs
into the speciﬁc loci by a single round of transformation; allowing construction of the CRI-
SPA and CASCADE platform strain to be ﬁnalized in a second round and third round of
transformation, respectively, in the future.

6.1
Strains and cultivation media
A. nidulans FGSC A4 was used as model organism for the implementation of CRI-SPA

6.3.1
BioBrick synthesis
The BioBricks were PCR synthesized using commercial Thermo Scientiﬁc Phusion U
Hot Start PCR Master Mix or in-house PfuX7 [84] polymerase. Thermo Scientiﬁc U Hot

The PCR reactions using Thermo Scientiﬁc U Hot Start PCR Master Mix were per-
formed according to supplier speciﬁcations. The reactions were down-scaled to 25 μL ﬁnal
volume, containing 0.4 mM of each forward and reverse primer. The PCR reactions with
in-house PfuX7 polymerase were performed in 50 μL ﬁnal volume, containing 1 μL of
polymerase 0.2 mM dNTP, 2x CxL buﬀer and 0.4 mM forward and reverse primer. For
both polymerases, the plasmid and gDNA template were provided at 0.5-2 ng/μL concen-
tration. A. nidulans gDNA was kindly provided by Zoﬁa D. Jarczynska, extracted using
Soil DNA extraction kit (MP Biomedicals, USA). The PCR program used for BioBrick
ampliﬁcations for the two polymarases are summarized in Table 4.

Gel Band Puriﬁcation kit according to supplier speciﬁcations. The list of PCR reactions for
generation of each BioBrick are summarized in Table S2 and visualized on electrophoresis
gel in Figure S18.
6.3.2
Plasmid assembly by USER cloning
Plasmid construction was performed through USER-cloning and -fusion [63, 85]. The
CRISPR-Cas9 plasmids were designed according to Nødvig et al., 2018 [62].
In total
four types of plasmids were created, CRISPR/Cas9-tRNA plasmids expressing sgRNA,
targeted repair vectors with PacI/Nt.BbvCI restriction site, RFP plasmids and CRI-
SPA/CASCADE plasmids. All plasmids were extracted using Sigma-Aldrich GenEluteTM
Plasmid Miniprep kit according to supplier speciﬁcations. A list of plasmids created and
used in the study are presented in Table S3.
The design of the repair templates.
The repair plasmids were designed to be con-
structed through sub-cloning, by ﬁrst creating a plasmid with targeted repair sequences
that can be re-opened to integrate the desired construct. Each of the target sequences are
approximately 1 kb long and have homology to the integration site. They are joined by
a PacI/Nt.BbvCI restriction enzyme target sequence, allowing plasmids to be reopened

==PAGE:47==
Materials and methods
after USER cloning [63]. The plasmids can thereafter be used to integrate the RFP ex-
pression cassette, CRI-SPA/CASCADE cassette or other construct. The repair templates
were linearized by PCR reaction, using the plasmids as a templates. The reaction was gel
puriﬁed following the same instructions as for purifying BioBricks. The puriﬁed product
was concentrated to 300-500 ng/μL by ethanol precipitation. The visual representation of
the plasmid construction is presented in Figure 20.
6.4
Fungal protoplastation and transformation
Fungal protoplasts were generated as described by Nielsen et al., 2006 [86]. The trans-
formations method used was adapted from yeast by Johnstone et al., 1985 [87] and per-
formed according to Vanegas et al., 2019 [88]. The concentration of protoplasts was not
standardized by cell-count, however, each batch was tested for qualitative eﬃciency as-
sessment. The provided DNA in the transformations was dependent on the number of
fragments/plasmids provided, 1 μg of each. When using many fragments simultaneously,
they were concentrated to not exceed total volume of DNA provided above 25 μL.
6.5
Strain validation by diagnostic PCR
Screening of fungal transformants
was performed by diagnostic PCR reactions us-
ing DNA from conidia as template and Bioline MyTaqTM Plant-PCR Kit. Primers for
detecting precise gene editing were designed to bind up- and downstream from the gene-
targeting sequence or within the PCR construct. Prior PCR, the spore suspension was
prepared according to Vanegas et al., 2019 [88]. Standard reaction volumes were 10 μL
including 0.5 μM primers, 5 μL MyTaqTM Plant-PCR Kit, 2 μL of spore suspension and
MilliQ water to reach the ﬁnal volume.
Screening of E. coli colonies
was performed by diagnostic PCR reactions using DNA
from single colonies of E. coli and PfuX7 polymerase.
Primers for detecting plasmid
assembly were designed to bind up- and downstream from the gene-integration site on the
plasmid. Prior PCR, a suspension of each single E. coli colony was made by transferred
to 20 μL of sterile Milli-Q water. The suspension was kept refrigerated, as it was used
as inoculum when a colony was conﬁrmed to contain the plasmid. The PCR reactions
were performed as described previously using PfuX7 in BioBrick synthesis section. The
reactions were modiﬁed to down-scaled volume of 25 μL and 1 μL of E. coli suspension
was used as a template.
6.6
Camera setup and imaging
All photos were taken using a Nikon D90 SLR camera, equipped with AF-S Micro
Nikkor 60mm lens. The bright ﬁeld images were captured using the following settings:
ISO - 250, aperture — f/7.1, shutter speed — 1/3 s and 35 mm Focal length of 90. Photos
were saved in JPEG format with a 300 dpi resolution.
Red ﬂuorescence was examined using an in-house build digital camera setup, described
by Vanegas et al., 2019 [88]. The camera setup was supplemented with Light and Filter
set (NIGHTSEATM). The ﬂuorescence images were captured with extended shutter speed
to 1 s, remaining settings remained the same as for bright ﬁeld imaging.
