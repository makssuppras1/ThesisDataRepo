Abstract
Greenland is the largest non­continental island in the world. As Greenland is mostly cov­
ered by ice it is sparsely populated, even though the island is 2,166,000 km2 the population
is around 56.500 people. Despite Greenland‘s isolation, over 80% of its population has
some European ancestry. The degree of European admixture varies between different
regions of Greenland, with more European ancestry in the south and west but less in the
north and east. The genetic component of the Inuit population varies based on their ge­
ographical location, allowing differentiation between individuals from each region. With
this in mind, analyzing data from Greenland has some special features that need to be
taken into account, for example that admixture is different between locations and genetic
drift has more impact on smaller populations. Also, due to Greenland’s isolation, coastal
towns and harsh climate the traditional Inuit diet mostly consists of marine mammals and
fish which can lead to the potential for strong selection signals.
The aim of this project is to conduct a genetic association analysis on a set of metabolic
markers measured in serum samples from participants in a previous study conducted
by Skotte et al. [7] in the Greenlandic population. Although the data has already been
analyzed in the past, this thesis introduces three new elements: (1) updated metabolic
marker measurements provided by the laboratory have been analysed for association,
(2) genetic variants that were not observed in the study samples have been imputed, and
association tests have been performed on the imputed data, and (3) the data has been
examined using a recessive genetic model.
23 phenotypes were analysed under both an additive model and a recessive model. The
top variants in the additive analysis were rs80356779 and rs730882082 but they are both
missense variants. Under the recessive model only one phenotype had a genome­wide
significant signal that was not present in the additive analysis and one phenotype had
signal with much lower p­value in the recessive analysis versus the additive one.
Certain limitations were to this study and the biggest being that only 23 phenotypes were
analysed. Also, the sample size was on the smaller side, consisting of only 1570 individu­
als. Future work could include further analysis of all the phenotypes under both recessive
and additive models. It would also be interesting to do a similar study with a larger sam­
ple size. Overall, the goal of the project was to conduct a genetic association analysis on
a set of metabolic markers measured in serum samples from participants in a previous
study and that was achieved. The analysis performed in this project have demonstrated
the value of analysing imputed data in the Greenlandic population and performing asso­
ciation tests under a non­additive model.

1
Introduction
Greenland, also known as Kalaallit Nunaat, is the world’s largest non­continental island
and home to a unique and diverse population. It is estimated that Greenland was first
populated around 2500 BC, however, the Inuit ancestors of the present day population
are believed to have arrived in Greenland around 1000 years ago [1]. Despite its isolated
location, Greenland has had many visits from other countries over its history, especially
from Europe. It is estimated that most of the present­day Greenlandic population, over
80%, have some European ancestry [1] and that most of the European ancestry is Danish
[2]. Not all towns are equally admixed, as there is less European ancestry in the north
and east and small villages in the south [1]. Additionally, the study by Moltke et al. [1]
found significant difference in the genetic component of Inuit people based on their geo­
graphical location, that is east, west, north. As a result, individuals from each region can
be differentiated from each other based on genetic data.
Even though Greenland is more than 2,166,000 km2 it is sparsely populated, and the
population is estimated to be around 56.500 people, with the majority living in the capital
city of Nuuk [3]. Greenland is largely covered by ice, which makes it difficult to build towns
and cities on the interior of the island. Therefore, most of the towns are located on the
coast where Inuits could easily access the resources they needed to survive.
Over the past years, several studies have been published highlighting the consequences
of traditional Inuit diet. Recent studies, for example by Senftleber et al.[4], have found
signs that selection on the CPT1A gene in the ancestors of the Inuit population which is
probably as a result of their traditional diet.
Due to Greenland’s isolation, coastal towns, and extreme arctic climate, the traditional
Inuit diet is primarily based on marine mammals, such as whales and seals, and fish,
which are rich in fat, particularly omega­3 polyunsaturated fatty acids, and protein but
have low levels of carbohydrates [5]. A high­fat diet can be beneficial for those living in
cold environments because fat is a great source of energy as it provides more than twice
as much energy per gram as carbohydrates and proteins do and in cold environments,
like in Greenland, the body requires more energy to keep warm. Therefore, having a high
fat diet, like the traditional Inuit diet is, can help to provide the body with enough energy
to stay warm but also provide sufficient nutrition. Overall, the traditional Inuit diet is an
example of how indigenous communities have adapted to their environment, using the
resources available to them to create a diet that is both sustainable and nutritious.
Over the past decades a change has been in Inuit diet, both due to increased consump­
tion of imported foods and climate change [6]. The consumption of imported foods is
different between towns in Greenland. Towns with good transportation like big harbours
and airports often provide year­round access to imported foods. In contrast, other towns
and villages that are more isolated and harder to reach rely more on locally sourced food
supplies and have limited access to imported food [5]. This difference contributes to di­
etary differences between locations. Climate change also has an effect on the traditional
diet of Inuits by altering the availability of traditional food resources. Rising temperatures
have resulted in the melting of sea ice, making it more difficult for Inuits to hunt for ma­
rine mammals such as seals and whales. This has led to a shift in their diet, with an
increased reliance on store­bought food and a decrease in the consumption of traditional
food sources.

Over the past years the field of genetics has made significant progress and the rapid ad­
vancements in genetic technology have facilitated a deeper understanding of the complex
relationship between genetics and phenotype. As a result, genetic association studies
have become a crucial tool in uncovering the underlying causes of complex phenotypes
and identifying new avenues for their management and prevention. Greenlanders tend to
have causal variants in high frequency, which means that fewer samples are needed to
have the power to detect them compared to typical studies for example in Asian popula­
tion.
The aim of this project is to perform a genetic association study in the Greenlandic pop­
ulation on a set of metabolic markers measured in serum samples from participants in a
previous research study by Skotte er at [7]. While the data has been analysed previously
[7], the current thesis includes the three following novel aspects: (1) the metabolic marker
measurements have been updated by the laboratory providing the data and I performed
association analyses of the updated data, (2) imputation of unobserved genetic variants
has been performed for the study samples, and I conducted association tests based on
the imputed data, and (3) I analysed the data under a recessive genetic model. By using
imputed data the hope is that it can lead to the identification of novel loci and/or better
candidates for the causal variants in the loci that were identified by Skotte et al. [7]. The
idea behind doing a recessive analysis is that several of the causal variants that have so
far been identified in Greenland have been recessive, as can be seen in studies by Moltke
et al. [8] and Andersen et al. [9] and therefore it made sense to do a recessive analysis
as well.

2.1
Data Set Description
In 2013, individuals from seven different towns across Greenland were recruited to partic­
ipate in a study. To be eligible for the study the subjects needed to be born in Greenland
and be older than 16 years old [7].
In this study original data used by Skotte et al. [7] and imputed data were used to con­
duct further analysis. The data set from Skotte et al. [7] contained samples from 1570
individuals, 597 males and 973 females (supplementary figure B.2), from seven differ­
ent towns across Greenland (figure 2.1a). Most individuals came from Nuuk, and fewest
came from Upernavik (figure 2.1b). Year of birth ranged from 1997 to 1944, which corre­
sponds to age 16 ­ 69 with median year of birth being 1981 which corresponds to age 32
(see supplementary figure B.1).

As described by Skotte et. al. [7] blood samples were collected from all participants.
Serum samples were generated from the blood samples and used for metabolomics anal­

with the HumanOmniExpressExome­8v1­2_A array from Illumina. It contains information
on about 950000 variants, including about 700000 tag variants optimised for genome­wide
association studies (GWAS) and >250000 exonic variants [7]. In addition to the data, de­
scribed above, used by Skotte et. al. [7] imputed data was also used. The imputed data
contain information about chromosomes 1­22. This imputed data was then used to de­
termine whether any new signals appeared in comparison to previous results from Skotte
et.al [7].

[10]. For this project imputed data was used in contrast to the original data used in the
study by Skotte et al. [7] which may have a different impact on the results and interpreta­
tion of the study. As a part of an ongoing research, data imputation had been done before

European population. Output from ADMIXTURE was used as an input for RelateAdmix.
The results showed that there were 1570 individuals, 15 families, 14 duos and one trio.
All this resulted in a .vcf.gz file containing data for chromosomes 1 to 22. The data from
this .vcf.gz is the imputed data I used for analysis in this project.
2.2
Quality Control
The data used in this study is from a 2017 study by Skotte et al. [7] and from imputation.
As described by Skotte et al. [7] originally 1630 of the recruited individuals were randomly
selected for genotyping. They used the software PLINK to remove samples that were not
suitable for further analysis due to sex discordance, duplicates or high rates of missing
data. This resulted in 1570 genotyped samples that were then used for further analysis.
For the imputed data some cleaning was needed. PLINK was used and the best results
were obtained by setting the missingness to 0.05 and MAF to 0.01. The imputed data was
used as an input for GEMMA analyses to determine whether any new signals appeared
in comparison to previous results from Skotte et al. [7].
2.3
Phenotype Selection
The data set contained measurements for 233 phenotypes but only 23 phenotypes (see
table A.1) were selected for further analysis. The reduced subset of phenotypes was
selected because many of the original 233 phenotypes were highly correlated, leading
to overlapping information. This reduction helps to simplify the analysis and reduce the
computational burden, while still maintaining the important information needed to achieve
the goals of the study. These 23 phenotypes were selected among phenotypes that al­
ready had strong association signals to assess if imputed variants at these loci would
show stronger association, and potentially be the causal variants at the loci. Other of
those 23 phenotypes were selected to see if there would be a genome­wide significance
at these loci for imputed variants as they previously had association signals approaching
genome­wide significance. Measurements for the phenotypes were quantile transformed
before further analysis took place.

this project a PCA was done using the software R [15]. A PC1 vs PC2 plot coloured by

2.5
Estimation of Admixture Proportions and Ancestry
Specific Allele Frequencies
The software ADMIXTURE [12] was used to estimate the admixture proportions for the
genotyped samples. When running ADMIXTURE, K was set to 2 (K=2) as K refers to the
number of ancestral populations being modelled in the analysis and it was expected that
the ancestry of all individuals in the study were either of the European population or the
Inuit population. To make sure that convergence was accomplished, we ran ADMIXTURE
20 times, each time with a random starting point. These 20 results were then sorted by
highest likelihood and, in this case, did the 5th run yield the best results. The results
from ADMIXTURE were used as input for RelateAdmix [13] to determine the relatedness
between individuals in the study. RelateAdmix was used as it also takes admixture into
account when determining the relatedness between individuals.
2.6
Genome­Wide Association Analysis
Genome­Wide Efficient Mixed Model Association (GEMMA) is a statistical software tool
used for performing genome­wide association studies [16]. GEMMA uses mixed linear
models to account for relatedness among individuals and to control for the confounding
effects of population structure. It is capable of fitting a univariate linear mixed model with
the following structure:
y = Wα + xβ + u + ϵ; u ∼MV Nn(0, δτ −1K), ϵ ∼MV Nn(0, τ −1In)
Here, y is a vector of size n that contains quantitative traits. W is a matrix of size n by
c that represents covariates, α is a vector of size c that contains the coefficients asso­
ciated with the covariates, including the intercept. x is a vector of size n that represents
marker genotypes whilst β represents the effect size of the marker. Lastly, u and ϵ are
both vectors of size n but u is a vector of random effects and ϵ is a vector of errors. MVNn
indicated the n­dimensional multivariate normal distribution. Both u and ϵ have τ −1 which
stands for the variance of the residual errors. For u there is λ and K, λ represents the
ratio between the two variance components and K is an n by n relatedness matrix. For ϵ
the In is an n by n identity matrix.
Four main input files containing genotypes, phenotypes, relatedness matrix and covari­
ates were used when running GEMMA. All phenotypes had been quantile transformed
to a standard normal distribution before analyses. PLINK was then used to generate a
binary ped file (.bed) for each phenotype which GEMMA recognizes as an input for both
genotypes and phenotypes. The estimated relatedness matrix was generated by using
GEMMA . Lastly, age of individuals and sex were used as additional covariates. The
imputed data was used as an input for GEMMA analysis to determine whether any new
signals appeared in comparison to previous results from Skotte et al. [7].
For each of the 23 selected phenotypes (see overview in supplementary table A.1) a lin­
ear mixed model (lmm) was run using GEMMA. First analysis were performed using an
additive genetic model but secondary analyses were performed using a recessive genetic
model. Same input files were used for both additive and recessive genetic model analy­
ses. GEMMA generates two output files: .log.txt file which contains detailed information
about the computation time and the running parameters, and .assoc.txt which contains
the results from GEMMA. The .assoc.txt file was used to generate Manhattan and Q­Q
plots for every phenotype, both additive and recessive, and, if applicable, find top SNPs.

3.1
Population Structure and Relatedness
It is well known that population structure and relatedness can affect the results of standard
association testing methods like linear regression [18]. Therefore, I first explored how
much population structure and relatedness was present in the data set using the tools
ADMIXTURE, PCA and RelateAdmix in order to be able to choose the most appropriate
model for performing association analyses with. When running ADMIXTURE I expected
that most individuals in the study had both European and Inuit ancestry based on study by
Moltke et al. [1] so K=2 was used to represent those two ancestries. Figure 3.1 shows the
admixture proportions for the 1570 individuals in the study with with ancestry proportions
ranging from 0, that is full European ancestry, to 1, that is complete Inuit ancestry. As
expected figure 3.1a shows that most individuals have both European and Inuit ancestry.
Next, I wanted to look better at the admixture proportions in each town. Based on study
by Moltke et al. [1] European ancestry varies between location, being far smaller in more
isolated areas like in the north or east. As Tasiilaq is the the only sampling town located
on the East of Greenland it would be expected that Tasiilaq had the most Inuit ancestry.
In figure 3.1b it can be seen that indeed the least admixed town is Tasiilaq where the
majority of the town are of Inuit ancestry.

3.2
Comparison of NMR data
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a technique that can be used to study
metabolites. It can detect and quantify a wide range of metabolites, for example amino
acids and lipids. The technique works by applying a strong magnetic field and radio­
frequency pulses to the sample, which causes the metabolites to emit signals that can
be detected and analysed [20]. We had NMR data from 2015 that was used in the 2017
study by Skotte et al. [7] and also new NMR data from 2022. As described by Skotte
et al. [7] a high­throughput serum NMR metabolomics platform was utilized to analyse
all samples. The metabolomics results for all samples had been updated by Nightingale
Health, who ran an optimized bioinformatics pipeline to quantify metabolites from the raw
NMR data [21]. In supplementary figures B.5 and B.6 histograms of the 23 phenotypes
analysed can be seen both from the NMR 2015 data and the NMR 2022 data. Scatter
plots of the 2015 NMR data vs. the 2022 NMR data can be seen in supplementary figure
B.4. The phenotypes seem to be relatively correlated and comparison between 2015 and
2022 NMR data did not show any major changes between the phenotypes I looked at.
However, that does not rule out that there could be differences between the 2015 and
2022 NMR data for other phenotypes.

3.3.2
CPT1A
For four phenotypes, the top associated SNP was found in the Carnitine Palmitoyltrans­
ferase 1A gene, (CPT1A) on chromosome 11.
The top SNP for estimated degree of unsaturation (UnSat) has chromosome position
chr11:68780662:G:A. By looking at the gnomAD website, the rsID was determined to be

CPT1A gene. For most of the transcripts this change is predicted to be probably/possibly
damaging and deleterious by PolyPhen and SIFT tools. The European allele frequency is
0.00, whereas it is much more common in the Inuit population where the allele frequency
is 0.99 [27] [23].
The top SNPs for phenotypes omega­3 fatty acids (FAw3) and 22:6 docosahexaenoic

Two of the transcripts have HGSVc consequence of c.­14+7127G>C and the other two
have HGSVc consequence of c.­14+9497G>C. Both HGSVc consequences indicate a
G to C substitution. The European allele frequency is 0.01108 whereas it is much more
common in the Inuit population where the allele frequency is 0.99 [28] [23].
The top SNP for the total cholesterol to total lipids ratio in very large VLDL (XL­VLDL­C%)
has chromosome position chr11:68828403:G:GCC. By looking at the gnomAD website,
the rsID was determined to be rs34854628 and found to be located on the CPT1A gene.

of the transcripts have HGSVc consequence of c.­13­12918_­13­12917dup and one has
HGSVc consequence of c.­14+11147_­14+11148dup. This means that there is a duplica­
tion of two bases at positions 11147 and 11148 and duplication of two bases at positions
12918 and 12917. The European allele frequency is 0.8704 whereas it is more common
in the Inuit population where the allele frequency is 0.99 [29] [23].
Carnitine Palmitoyltransferase 1A gene, (CPT1A), is found on chromosome 11. It is a pro­
tein coding gene which encodes the enzyme carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1A and plays
a vital role in maintaining energy homeostasis in the body. The enzyme is responsible for
the transfer of long­chain fatty acids into the inner membrane of the mitochondria. Once
inside the mitochondria they can be metabolized which can produce energy [30] [31].

main reason for doing that is because several of the causal variants found so far in Green­

SNP and chromosome position for each phenotype that had p­value below genome­wide
significance were identified. This resulted in 13 phenotypes with p­value below genome­

4
Discussion
In this thesis, I have performed genetic association analyses of 23 metabolic markers
measured in samples from 1570 study participants from the Greenlandic population. Com­
pared to previously published analyses of the data [7], the novel aspects of the work pre­
sented here were: (1) that metabolic marker measurements had been updated and was
association analysis performed on this data, (2) imputed data was used but as a part
of another ongoing study, imputation of unobserved genetic variants had been done for
the study samples (3) a recessive analysis was performed. Overall, the analyses of the
imputed genetic data under an additive model resulted in different top SNPs at the associ­
ated loci, which in most cases had lower p­values compared to the additive model results
based on observed genotype only. Also, the recessive analyses identified genome­wide
significant associations at one locus that were not observed when analysing the data un­
der an additive model. Lastly, the new values for the metabolic markers were highly cor­
related with the previous values, and the fact that new metabolic marker measurements
had been generated did not result in new associations in itself.
4.1
Imputation
Imputation is a good technique to improve the power and resolution of genome­wide as­
sociation studies. In European ancestry population, large reference panels are usually
available, making the imputation more successful [52]. When studying an isolated popu­
lation, like the Greenlandic population, one of the challenges is that a suitable reference
panel does not exist. For this reason, imputation was not done in the previous study by
Skotte et al. [7]. However, for this project I was very fortunate that imputed data had been
generated for the study samples as a part of an ongoing research project and I could use
that data for my analysis. As expected, most phenotypes had an imputed SNP with a
lower p­value than the best genotyped SNP.
For the imputed data, two different missense variants were identified, that is, variants
rs80356779 and rs730882082. Variant rs80356779 is located on the CPT1A gene and
is a good candidate to be the causal variant at the locus. In my analysis the top SNP
for UnSat was rs80356779 with p­value 4.07E­33 making it the third lowest p­value of
all in the analysis (see table 3.1).
In the previous study by Skotte et al.
[7] UnSat
had top SNP with p­value 2.13E­27 and variant rs1017640. However, targeted geno­
typing of variant rs80356779 was performed and it resulted in the p­value for UnSat being
1.16E−34.Previous studies by Skotte et al. [7] and Clemente et al. [53] both reported
finding strong proof of positive selection at the locus [7]. It is thought that the variant con­
fers a selective advantage for individuals living on a traditional Inuit diet containing mostly
fat and protein and only little carbohydrate [7] [53]. Variant rs730882082is located on the
LDLR gene and is likely to be a causal variant at the locus. This variant was not reported
in the previous study by Skotte et al. [7] as it was found by imputation an was not on the
genotyping chip. On the other hand, this variant has been targeted genotyped in the co­
hort and studied for the potential of use as a marker for early intervention against familial
hypercholesterolemia in Arctic populations [54].
4.2
Association Analysis
It is a common practice in genome­wide association studies to test for association as­
suming that the genetic variants have an additive effect on the phenotype, but that this is
A Genetic Association Study in the Greenlandic Population
17
==PAGE:26==
not necessarily true. For example, if the model is misspecified, the statistical power can
be severely reduced [55], therefore I chose to analyse the metabolic phenotypes under
a recessive model. The additive analysis resulted in 19 phenotypes having a genome­
wide significant signals and the recessive analysis resulted in 13 phenotypes having a
genome­wide significant signals. The results showed a highly significant association for
acetate (Ace) in the recessive analysis that was not detected in analysis under an additive
model. Only one phenotype (Serum­TG) had a genome­wide significant signal that was
only found in the recessive analysis and not the additive analysis
The new bioinformatics pipeline for measuring the values of the 23 metabolic markers that
I focused on did not result in any substantial changes compared to the previous data. As
the focus was only on those 23 phenotypes it is a possibility that there is a large differ­
ence between the other phenotypes but in regards to this study there were no significant
changes observed when compared to the previous data.
Certain limitations were to these analysis but the biggest was only analysing 23 pheno­
types. There were 233 phenotypes in total but do to time limitations it was decided to focus
on these 23, but also many of the 233 phenotypes were highly correlated which leads to
overlapping information. It would have been interesting to analyse more phenotypes but
that can maybe be done in the future. Another limitation is that the sample size is not big,
there are only 1570 individuals in this study. For future work it would be beneficial to have
larger sample size.
Analysing data from a Greenlandic population requires special considerations due to the
unique characteristics of the population, for example due to the historical isolation and
small population size. Genetic drift has more significant impact on smaller populations
such as the Inuit, compared to larger populations [56]. Additionally, the Greenlandic pop­
ulation exhibits a notably higher level of linkage disequilibrium (LD) compared to Euro­
peans, indicating that genetic variations are more strongly associated with one another
and have an increased likelihood of being inherited together [1]. Admixture results also
need to be taken into account as it is estimated that over 80% of the present day Green­
landic population have some European ancestry but the admixture percentage varies be­
tween locations (see figure 3.1) [1]. Due to Greenland’s isolation and harsh living condi­
tions the traditional Inuit diet is primarily based on marine mammals and fish and therefore
there is the potential for strong selection signals. However, analysing small and isolated
populations also poses challenges for example on the statistical model used to analyse
the data. Nevertheless, it can be beneficial as it gives the opportunities to detect associ­
ations that could not be found in the usual large European ancestry populations used in
genome­wide association studies. The analysis performed in this project have provide ex­
amples of such associations and have demonstrated the value of analysing imputed data
in the Greenlandic population and of performing association tests under a non­additive
model.
