Abstract
Deep learning has shown remarkable success in detecting objects within 3D volumetric
images. However, these achievements are largely reliant on the presence of extensive
labeled datasets, the generation of which is a very laborôÙintensive task. In scenarios
where labeled data is scarce, one approach is to generalize from one task to another.
This, however, introduces the challenge of domain shift.
To address this issue, we have attempted to solve the domain shift challenge posed by the
creators of the BugNIST dataset. This challenge involves learning to detect and identify
insects in volumetric images of insect mixtures, using training data from volumetric images
of individual insects.
Our proposed solution involves the use of synthetic data, created from the individual insect
volumes. We developed and iteratively refined a twoôÙstage process for synthetic data
generation, progressively narrowing the visual gap between synthetic and authentic data.
Our investigation focuses on the correlation between the quality of the generated synthetic
data and the performance of the model.
Our findings reveal a strong correlation between the visual quality of synthetic data and
model performance, indicating the potential of synthetic data in improving model perforôÙ
mance in the face of domain shift.

Introduction
In the field of 3D image analysis, the issue of domain shift presents a substantial chalôÙ
lenge. This study explores the potential of synthetic data generation as a strategy to tackle
this problem, with a specific focus on the semantic segmentation of volumetric images.
Historically, the focus of 3D image analysis research has been predominantly on medical
imaging, primarily due to the majority of data available being medical in origin. This focus
could potentially impede the longôÙterm development of 3D image analysis techniques,
as it remains uncertain whether techniques developed for medical imaging will generalize
well to other 3D images. Therefore, expanding the research scope to include nonôÙmedical
imaging is a crucial step towards advancing the field of 3D image analysis.
To facilitate this expansion, BugNIST[1] introduced a novel 3D volumetric image dataset
of ôçCT scanned insects, which includes both individual insect scans and mixtures. The
task of classifying individual insects was found to be relatively straightforward, prompting
the creation of the insect mixtures and the posing of a domain shift challenge: learnôÙ
ing from individual insects to semantically segment the mixtures. This study addresses
this challenge by employing synthetic data generation and investigating various domain
adaptation strategies.
Training a semantic segmentation model on individual insects volumes and testing on
insect mixtures yielded a precision of 13.4% (see table 5.1). This result highlights the
domain shift problem the model faces, as it performs only marginally better than random
guessing. Consequently, there is a clear need for another approach. For this we propose
generating synthetic mixtures using the individual insect scans, with the aim of creating a
training dataset that can enhance the modelãs performance on authentic insect mixtures.
To achieve this a twoôÙstage process for synthetic data generation was developed. The
first stage involves the isolation of individual insects from singular insect volumes, while
the second stage entails the packing of these individual insects into a volume to simulate
authentic mixtures. This process was iteratively refined, with each resulting synthetic
mixture progressively bridging the gap between synthetic and authentic data.
The use of synthetic data is not a novel concept, and the problem of domain shift when
using synthetic data is wellôÙdocumented[2, 3, 4]. Previous research has leveraged video
game engines to generate synthetic data for the semantic segmentation of street views, in
this research the issues of domain shift is highlighted and a CycleGANôÙbased approach is
proposed to address the issue[5, 4]. More recently, similar approaches using a CycleGAN
have been applied to translate between MRI and CT images[6]. In this study, we will atôÙ
tempt to use this domain adaptation technique to further improve our generated synthetic
data. Additionally, we will explore methods for selecting the most promising generated
samples.
The rest of the paper is structured as follows: In chapter 2 we will go over some of the
research into synthetic data generation and domain adaptation. Chapter 3 will have an
overview of BugNIST[1] the dataset used. Chapter 4 we will explain the data generation
pipeline, model selection, and our domain adaptation strategies. Chapter ?? analyse and
shows the results. Chapter 6 we discuss the implications of our results and limitations of
our approaches.

Related work
2.1
Synthetic data generation
The efficacy of deep learning models, particularly for semantic segmentation, is signifôÙ
icantly contingent upon the volume of annotated training data [7, 8, 3]. However, the
manual annotation of images, especially in the context of 3D volumetric images, is a
laborôÙintensive and timeôÙconsuming process.
To mitigate this challenge, the generation of labeled synthetic data has emerged as a
valuable tool [9, 10]. However, this process introduces a challenge known as domain
shift [11, 12, 13, 14].
Domain shift, in this context, refers to the discrepancies in the data distribution between
the synthetic (source) and realôÙworld (target) datasets. Synthetic data, despite being genôÙ
erated to mimic realôÙworld data, inherently possesses its own unique distribution due to
the differences in the generation process and the realôÙworld complexities it attempts to
replicate [11, 12, 13, 14].
When a model is trained on synthetic data and then applied to realôÙworld data, its perforôÙ
mance may be subôÙoptimal due to these distributional differences. This underscores the
need for effective domain adaptation techniques to bridge the gap between synthetic and
realôÙworld data distributions [3, 12, 14, 13].
2.1.1
Domain adaptation
Domain adaptation (DA) is a machine learning strategy designed to mitigate the issue of
domain shift. The objective of DA methods is to adapt a model, which has been trained
on a source domain, to perform optimally on a target domain, despite the differences in
their respective data distributions.
DA approaches can be broadly classified into three main categories, especially in the
context of semantic segmentation [3]. These categories are:
1. ImageôÙlevel adaptation: This method mainly applies data augmentation through
imageôÙtoôÙimage translation techniques.
2. FeatureôÙlevel adaptation: This approach alters the learning process of the network
through additional losses and adversarial training.
3. OutputôÙlevel adaptation: Similar to featureôÙlevel adaptation, this approach uses adôÙ
versarial training to align with the target domain. However, unlike featureôÙlevel methôÙ
ods that attempt to influence the highôÙdimensional feature space within the network,
outputôÙlevel adaptation focuses solely on the lowôÙdimensional label prediction outôÙ
put space.
In the context of this work, imageôÙlevel adaptation is of primary relevance as the work
revolves around data creation, augmentation, and selection. We will briefly discuss some
methods for featureôÙlevel and outputôÙlevel adaptation before delving into a more detailed
examination of imageôÙlevel adaptation.
FeatureôÙlevel adaptation encompasses methods such as domain confusion [15] and
maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) [16, 17].

Domain confusion is a method that employs a domain discriminator for adversarial trainôÙ
ing. The goal is to create confusion between the source and target domains during the
training process, thereby enabling the model to learn domainôÙinvariant features [15].
MMD is a statistical distance metric used in domain adaptation to measure the discrepôÙ
ancy between the source and target domain distributions. MMDôÙbased methods aim to
minimize this discrepancy, thereby aligning the data distributions of the two domains [16,
17].
OutputôÙlevel adaptation uses methods similar to featureôÙlevel adaptation as previously
mentioned. One such method, as used in [18], involves training a discriminator on the outôÙ
put. This discriminator is designed to distinguish whether the segmentation predictions
were derived from the source domain or the target domain. Subsequently, this discrimiôÙ
nator is utilized as an adversarial loss to render the output of the segmentation network
more domainôÙinvariant.
ImageôÙlevel adaptation primarily employs image style transfer (IST) techniques. The
fundamental idea is to transfer the domain ãstyleã or appearance from the target to the
source, from the source to the target, or both. Post style transfer, the source images
retain the semantic content of the original source, including its pixelôÙlevel labeling, but
their appearance becomes more similar to the style of the target. This assists the network
in learning a model that is better suited for the target domain.[4, 19, 20, 21, 22, 5]
The introduction of a cycle consistent generative adversarial network for unpaired images,
CycleGAN[5], in 2017, paved the way for numerous research papers on the adaptation of
this IST technique within Domain Adaptation (DA). CycleôÙConsistent Adversarial Domain
Adaptation (CyCADA), introduced in 2018, was among the first to incorporate the use of
CycleGAN into DA. It utilized both featureôÙlevel and imageôÙlevel adaptation for translating
between images from GTA5 and images from CityScapes for semantic segmentation.
In the context of medical imaging, CycleGAN has been used for various applications such
as translating between different modalities of medical images[23, 6]. For example, CycleôÙ
GAN has been used to translate between MRI and CT images[6].
The use of CycleGAN in semantic segmentation has been explored in various studies.
One study by LûÑhdefink et al.[24] discusses how noise injection into the latent segmentaôÙ
tion space can help the performance of CycleGANs for semantic segmentation. Another
study by Mondal et al.[25] uses CycleGAN for semiôÙsupervised segmentation to train deep
networks for semantic image segmentation using only a fraction of annotated images, sigôÙ
nificantly reducing the need for human annotation efforts. Mû¥tze et al.[26] introduces a
downstream task loss in the training of CycleGAN to improve semiôÙsupervised DA.

Methods
In order to train a model capable of detecting and classifying insects in mixtures, synthetic
mixtures were created from individual insect scans. These synthetic mixtures were deôÙ
signed to mimic the appearance of the scanned mixtures. The methodology employed for
this synthesis is detailed in Section 4.1.
Three model architectures were utilized to evaluate the synthetic mixtures. The rationale
behind the selection of these specific models is elaborated in Section 4.2.
In an effort to enhance the synthetic data, four different domain adaptation strategies were
implemented and tested. The procedures for these domain adaptation approaches are
outlined in Section 4.3.
4.1
Data synthesising
The data synthesis process was comprised of two primary stages. The initial stage inôÙ
volved the isolation of individual insects from singular insect volumes, while the subseôÙ
quent stage entailed the packing of these individual insects into a volume to simulate
authentic mixtures.
The isolation stage was characterized by volume analysis to determine an estimated
bounding box that encapsulated the insect. This was followed by background thresholdôÙ
ing to generate a reduced image devoid of background noise and the cotton separators
located at the top and bottom of each volume. The outcome was a volume exclusively
containing the insect.
The specifics of this stage are as follows: the center of mass (where voxel intensity is
equivalent to mass) in the image was first identified. The xôÙvalue (height) was then utilized
as a starting point to locate a window containing only the insect. Subsequently, every voxel
outside this window and every other voxel below the intensity threshold of 40 was set to
0. The bounding box was finally narrowed down from each direction by identifying the first
slice containing a voxel with an intensity of 75 or higher. The full code can be referred to
in A.1.
This method relies on several assumptions, such as the insect maintaining a consistent
intensity above 80 with no gaps in the xôÙaxis, and the voxel with the highest intensity
being located within the insect. While these assumptions generally hold true, there are
instances where they fail to yield a wellôÙfitted bounding box, resulting in the final volume
having portions of the insects cut off. Additionally, this method encounters difficulties
with lowôÙintensity and long appendages such as antennas and wings, resulting in them
sometimes being cut off. An example of the results of the insect isolation method can be
seen in figure 4.1.
The combining of the now isolated insect into a synthetic mixture was approached like a
3D bin packing problem i.e. the problem of packing varying sizes of threeôÙdimensional
rectangularôÙshaped items into a bigger rectangular bin, like the logistics problem of packôÙ
ing a shipping container. The 3d bin packing algorithm use is based on the paper [27]
and implemented in the python library py3dbp [28]. 15 isolated insects volumes were
randomly sampled and then packed using the 3d bin packing algorithm randomly picking
between whether to pack the largest or smallest shapes first. This returns a position for
each volume the algorithm was able to fit into the bin of size 256x128x128, to pack it as

objective was to align the density of insects in synthetic mixtures more closely with that in
authentic mixtures.
The denser packing is achieved by first identifying an initial valid position for all insects,
ensuring no overlap. This is facilitated by the 3D bin packing algorithm employed in the
first version. Subsequently, the insects are progressively moved closer to each other by
reducing the x, y, and z coordinates in steps with a step size of half the initial x, y, and z
coordinates of the starting position1.
After each step, a check is performed to identify any overlapping labels. If overlap is
detected, the step is reversed, and the new step size is set to half of the previous step
size. This process is executed separately for each xôÙ, yôÙ, and zôÙdimension, beginning with
the xôÙdimension and continuing until movement results in overlap, followed by the yôÙ and
zôÙdimensions. A visual representation of this process can be found in Figure B.1.
Once all the volumes of single insects have been compacted, a new set of volumes is
sampled, and the 3D bin packing algorithm is used again to determine starting positions.
However, as the starting position may not necessarily be valid, as the mixture volume
now contains insects, a check is performed to identify any overlap between the insect in
the volume and any insect already in the mixture. If overlap is detected, the volume is
skipped and the next volume is tested. This process continues until all volumes in a set
are skipped.

4.2
Model selection
Three distinct model architectures were employed during the testing phase: two were
based on the UôÙNet architecture[29, 30], and one was based on the Swin UNETR arôÙ
chitecture[31]. The two UôÙNet models, referred to as ãUôÙNet smallã and ãUôÙNet largeã,
consist of 126 K and 28.7 M trainable parameters, respectively. The Swin UNETR model
comprises 18.3 M trainable parameters.
The rationale behind the selection of these architectures is as follows:
4.2.1
UôÙNet small
Initially, the UôÙNet small model was utilized due to its ability to achieve a test precision
of 88.2% and a training precision of 95.9% on the single insect volumes, and 83.9% and
85.5% on LP (v1) with 5,000 training samples. It appeared that the model had reached its
learning capacity with the single insect scans and had only marginal room for improvement
with LP (v1). Consequently, there was no compelling reason to transition to a larger model,
which would potentially increase the risk of overfitting with minimal benefit. Moreover, the
significant drop in precision to 17.3% on the authentic mixtures indicated ample scope for
improvement through dataset enhancement and domain adaptation.
4.2.2
UôÙNet large
With the introduction of DP w/bg (v3), the training precision of UôÙNet small decreased
to 66.5%, indicating that the model was no longer capable of fully exploiting the dataãs
quality. As a result, the UôÙNet large model was introduced, which achieved a training
precision of 98.4% and a test precision of 91.7%, suggesting that this new model could
effectively extract all the information from the dataset.
4.2.3
Swin UNETR
Until this point, only 5,000 training samples from each dataset iteration were generated. To
evaluate the impact of a larger dataset, 20,000 and subsequently 50,000 training samples
were generated. Given the general consensus that transformerôÙbased models require
more data compared to CNNôÙbased models, the Swin UNETR model was introduced
upon availability of more data, to assess the performance of a transformerôÙbased model
on the synthetic datasets.

4.3
Domain Adaptation
In an effort to enhance the detection and classification capabilities of models on the auôÙ
thentic mixtures, a range of domain adaptation strategies were examined with the obôÙ
jective of refining the synthetic data used for training. Two primary approaches were
undertaken: unsupervised domain adaptation using a CycleGAN to enable translation
between synthetic and authentic images, and a quantitative evaluation of synthetic data
quality to guide data selection. Three quantitative metrics were employed for this purpose,
namely UMAP, PCA, and Feature Distance. Further details on these metrics can be found
in Section 4.3.2. Each strategy resulted in a subset or transformed dataset, which was
subsequently used to train three distinct model architectures. These models were then
evaluated on the authentic mixtures to assess the effectiveness of each strategy.
4.3.1
Unsupervised Domain Adaptation
An unsupervised domain adaptation technique was investigated, which involved the use
of a CycleGAN to achieve pixelôÙlevel alignment between synthetic and authentic data. A
CycleGAN model, incorporating the UôÙNet large architecture for the generator, was trained
on both the DP w/bg (v3) dataset and the authentic mixtures. The generator, specifically
trained to perform transformations from DP w/bg (v3) to authentic mixtures, was subôÙ
sequently utilized to modify the DP w/bg (v3) dataset. This method aimed to improve
the compatibility between synthetic and authentic data, thereby potentially enhancing the
performance of models trained on the transformed dataset.
4.3.2
Data Selection
Three different methods of quantitatively evaluating the quality of the synthetic data for
data point selection were examined: Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection
(UMAP), Principal Component Analysis (PCA), and the computation of raw feature disôÙ
tances. The rationale behind this strategy is as follows: certain synthesized data points
may deviate significantly from the authentic data, potentially impairing the performance
of a model trained on such data. Therefore, by identifying and excluding these outlier
data points (as determined by the chosen metric), the performance of the model could be
enhanced.
For the purpose of quantitative evaluation, a variant of the UôÙNet architecture, without skip
connections, was trained on the third version of the dataset (dense packing with backôÙ
ground, DP w/bg (v3)). Both the authentic and synthetic mixtures were then processed
through this model. The deepest features, extracted immediately prior to the upscaling
process, were then utilized in the three aforementioned methods (UMAP, PCA, and raw
feature distances) to identify the most promising data for the purposes of this study.

Test methodology
The BugNIST dataset does not provide comprehensive semantic segmentation labels for
the authentic mixtures. Instead, only the centroid of each insect is provided, as previously
mentioned in Chapter 3. Therefore, it is necessary to establish a performance metric for
these authentic mixtures.
An automated pseudoôÙlabeling process was implemented using a combination of image
analysis techniques. The first step involved applying a series of morphological operations.

The ãclosingã operation was applied initially to eliminate the air holes that appear in the
mealworms and other small gaps. This was followed by erosion and opening operations
aimed at separating any insects that were in close proximity to each other.
The resulting volume was then skeletonized, and any region with an intensity below 75
was classified as background. Subsequently, a small cube was added around the centroid
of each insect, labeled with the corresponding class label. These labeled cubes served
as markers for the application of the watershedôÙfromôÙmarkers algorithm, using the image
foresting transform method. This step was performed to label the skeleton.
The labeled skeleton was subsequently employed as a new set of markers for the applicaôÙ
tion of the watershed algorithm to the original volume. This process resulted in a labeled
volume, where each voxel was assigned a label based on its proximity to the nearest
marker. Ideally, each voxel would be labeled according to its closest insect.
However, in practice, this is not always the case, and most volumes contain minor erôÙ
rors. A common issue is the mislabeling of the extremities of elongated insects, which
often end up being closer to the centroid of another insect. Despite these limitations, this
method provides a more accurate estimation than simply considering the distance to the
centroid. See 4.4 for an overview of the performance difference between the pseudoôÙ
labeling method and the distance to centroid method.
In addition, a set of 18 authentic mixtures were labeled manually. This was not merely to
compare with the automated pseudoôÙlabeling method, but primarily to assess the modelãs
performance. The handôÙlabeled data served as a more reliable benchmark, enabling a
more precise evaluation of the modelãs effectiveness when applied to authentic mixtures.
However, given the extensive time required to manually label all the authentic mixtures, we
continue to depend on pseudoôÙlabels as a representative measure for the entire dataset.
The metric reported in the results is computed as the ratio of the number of voxels correctly
predicted to belong to an insect to the total number of voxels that were predicted to be part
of an insect, i.e. precision
TP
TP+FP . This provides a measure of how accurately the model
is able to identify and classify insect voxels in the given data. When calculated background
voxels are disregarded, as they are not of interest in this context. Given that the models
are proficient at distinguishing insect voxels from background voxels, this metric offers a
reasonable approximation of the actual precision. Only precision is employed due to the
lack of known correct labeling and outlining of insects for the majority of the data, thus
making it impossible to calculate false negatives. Consequently, standard measurements
such as the recall, and Dice score cannot be applied.
In an effort to obtain smoother prediction outcomes from the models during testing, a
connected component analysis was performed on the softmax predictions. This process
involved identifying connected components for each class in the softmax prediction output
of the model. The values within the identified regions were then replaced with the mean
value of that region. This approach aimed to smooth out the predictions with the reasoning
behind showcased in the following two examples. For instance, in a scenario where a
few voxels are misclassified due to a small area with high probability, this method would
balance it out with the surrounding low probability, potentially resulting in the correct label
having a higher probability. Another potential benefit of this approach is the possibility
of extending high probability to the extremities of an insect, thereby ensuring consistent
labeling across the entire insect.

Results and Analysis
The models trained solely on individual insects serve as a performance baseline for each
model in the absence of synthetic data creation. The results, as shown in Table 5.1,
indicate that the models face challenges with the task, performing only marginally better
than random guessing. Thus giving a reason to pursue the creation of synthetic data.
The results of models trained on the synthetically created data are presented in Table 5.2.
A significant improvement is observed with the first synthetic mixture LP (v1) compared
to the models trained only on volumes with single insects. Notably, the Swin UNETR
model performs exceptionally well, achieving a precision of up to 45.4%, with the two UôÙ
Net based models achieving approximately half of that. However, this still represents a
doubling of performance over the single insect volumes. With the visual enhancements to
the synthetic dataset, the UôÙNet based models catch up to the Swin UNETR model, even
surpassing it in performance on DP w/bg (v3). The results clearly indicate that creatôÙ
ing more densely packed mixtures and adding the background, thereby visually aligning
the synthetic mixtures closer to the authentic mixtures, significantly impacts the perforôÙ
mance of the models, i.e. each iteration increases the precision by 10ôÙ20 percentage
points depending on the model. It is noteworthy to mention the difference in the effect of
the tightening of insect packing between the UôÙNet based models and the Swin UNETR
model. The UôÙNets benefit significantly more from this change, which might be attributed
to the fact that as fully convolutional neural networks with a limited kernel size, modeling
of longôÙrange information is challenging. On the other hand, the Swin UNETR model, as a
transformerôÙbased model, which has been shown to capture such longôÙrange information
well, has less to gain from moving the insects closer to each other. This is supported by
its superior performance on the lightly packed synthetic mixtures.
The outcomes of the domain adaptation strategies are presented in Table 5.3. It is obôÙ
served that none of these strategies significantly influence the precision of the models.
This observation is understandable for the three data selection strategies (UMAP, PCA,
and Feature Distance). These strategies would only enhance a modelãs performance if
the additional data, which is further away according to the distance metric, had an adôÙ
verse effect on the modelãs performance on the authentic mixtures and offered no benefit
to the modelãs generalization capability. The lack of a significant impact on precision from
these strategies suggests two possibilities. Either the additional data does not have any
detrimental effects, or these three strategies are not effective metrics for data selection.
Moreover, the implementation of these data selection strategies requires the synthesis of
additional data, as such the benefit of the selection has to outweigh the possible benefit
of using the additional data.

6
Discussion
In the study, we have explored the generation of synthetic data as a solution to the chalôÙ
lenge of domain shift. Our focus has been on the transition from creating simple volumetric
representations of multiple insects to generating volumes that are nearly indistinguishable
from realôÙworld volumes to the human eye, barring the occasional insect extremity extendôÙ
ing beyond the cylindrical background noise, and how this effects semantic segmentation
of 3D volumetric images. Furthermore, strategies to enhance the synthetic data using
domain adaptation techniques were explored.
Our findings suggest that the performance of models improves as the synthetic data more
closely mirrors the authentic data. This is an intuitive outcome, but one that is substantiôÙ
ated by our results. For models trained on single insect volumes, the estimated precision
on the authentic mixtures was around 10%, only marginally better than random guessing.
However, this precision increased to 20ôÙ40% for simple synthetic mixtures (LP (v1)), 30ôÙ
50% for slightly more advanced mixtures in DP (v2), and finally to 50ôÙ67% for our most
realistic synthetic mixture DP w/bg (v3).
These results underscore the importance of creating realistic synthetic data that incorôÙ
porates the context of the real data. By doing so, we have demonstrated a remarkable
improvement of 54 percentage points in our estimated precision measure compared to
not using synthetic data. This highlights the potential of synthetic data generation as a
viable strategy for improving model performance in the context of domain shift.
The results of our investigation into the enhancement of synthetic data using domain adapôÙ
tation techniques were not as promising. As discussed in the analysis of the results preôÙ
sented in Table 5.3, none of the techniques tested demonstrated consistent improvements
across all model architectures. In fact, most exhibited a decrease in performance when
implemented.
These results, however, should be interpreted with caution. The observed changes in
precision when these techniques were applied fall within the expected variance for the
models, using the change between training on 20,000 and 50,000 samples as a referôÙ
ence. Due to constraints in computational resources, it was not feasible to train multiple
instances of all the models to obtain the mean and standard deviation for each experiment.
Therefore, definitive conclusions cannot be drawn from these findings.
The task of evaluating semantic segmentation on a largely unlabeled dataset poses a conôÙ
siderable challenge. The quality of our synthetic data is inferred from the performance of
the models on this task, underscoring the importance of obtaining an accurate estimation.
However, the use of pseudo labels and the precision metric in conjunction with these
labels is not without its pitfalls, as briefly discussed in previous chapters.
One such pitfall is the dependence of the estimated precision metric on the modelãs ability
to distinguish insects from background materials. In the pseudo labels, each voxel in the
volume is labeled according to what is presumed to be the nearest insect. As a result,
when the model erroneously classifies background materials as part of an insect, it can
occasionally contribute to the true positive count when it should invariably be counted
towards the false positive count. This scenario is exemplified in the second example for
the UôÙNet small model in Figure 5.1.

This, in combination with other inaccuracies arising from the pseudo labels, and the fact
that a misclassification of an extremity of a larger insect could impact the estimated preôÙ
cision metric as much as correctly classifying the entirety of a smaller insect, due to the
significant difference in the number of voxels that constitute these insects. On visual
inspection, the misclassification of an extremity could easily be disregarded, unlike a misôÙ
classification of an entire insect. For this reason, in our opinion, weighted precision and
recall is a superior metric, and the calculation of these on the 18 real labeled authentic
mixtures, as seen in Table 5.4, was crucial for understanding the true performance of the
models and thus the quality of the synthetic data.
Upon examination of Table 5.4, assuming that performance on the 18 labeled authentic
mixtures is representative of how the models would perform on the rest of the dataset,
while not directly comparable to the estimated precision metric using the pseudo label, it
appears that the models perform better than previously indicated by the estimated preciôÙ
sion metric.
Transitioning from training on single insect volumes to synthetic mixtures, the increase in
weighted precision is comparable to that of the estimated precision metric. As previously
noted, the estimated precision metric saw an increase of 53ôÙ54 percentage points. SimiôÙ
larly, the weighted precision experienced an increase of 57ôÙ60 percentage points for the
two topôÙperforming models, UôÙNet large and Swin UNETR. This suggests that the conôÙ
clusions drawn would be consistent, whether using weighted precision or the estimated
precision metric.
The capability to compute recall has offered new perspectives on the performance of the
models. The recall is lower than the precision, suggesting that the models may classify
uncertain parts as background more frequently than misclassifying them as other insects.
This is corroborated by the observation that the models generally only detected the highest
intensity parts of the two fly types (blow fly and curly winged fly), which lowers recall but
not precision.
A potential explanation for this behavior may be rooted more in the modelãs training than in
the synthetic data. During training, the models try to minimize the focal loss, where each
class is weighted. This weight is lower for the background class, but otherwise equal
for all insect classes. This could lead to the model prioritizing the detection of insects
composed of more voxels, and neglecting the detection of flies, which consist of thin legs
and a mostly hollow body, and thus a low number of voxels.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this study has demonstrated the potential of synthetic data generation as
a strategy for addressing domain shift in 3D image analysis.
The iterative process of creating increasingly complex synthetic mixtures has shown a
clear correlation with improved model performance.
While the exploration of domain
adaptation techniques did not yield consistent improvements, these techniques remain
a valuable area for future investigation.
The complexities encountered in this study underscore the challenges inherent in semanôÙ
tic segmentation of largely unlabeled datasets and highlight the importance of continued
research in this field.
The findings of this study pave the way for future work in improving model performance
in the context of domain shift and broadening the scope of 3D image analysis beyond its
traditional focus on medical imaging.
