Abstract
Urbanization and industrialization have made comfort an affordable commodity and to-
day, we cannot imagine our day-to-day activities without this luxury. However, along
with reaping benefits, unarguably, burning fossil fuels to generate energy is one of the
primary causes to a 1°C rise in global temperatures over the past 150 years. This contin-
uous increase in energy consumption and production led to global problems like climate
change and ecological destruction. The shift towards by introducing VRES into the power
grid reduces carbon based emissions and accelerates the transition towards green energy.
However, the power electronics used to connect the VRES to the grid substantially affect
the power quality of the grid. This thesis aims to investigate the role of grid-connected
BESS in improving VRES power quality. The power quality parameters chosen for this
study were 5th and 7th harmonic distortion and THDi.
The investigation was carried out in two steps. The first step involved modelling the
power system with BESS equipped with an active filter and VRES in power factory (PF)
and comparing its performance to a system with passive filters at PCC. A Harmonic
Load Flow analysis was performed on the power system for two scenarios that differed
in the location of passive filters. The second step was a lab experiment to observe the
performance of LCL filter connected to DTU’s lab-scale Xolta battery (55 kW/ 80 kWh)
at PowerLabDk. The experiment was divided into three parts that differed in how the
battery was charged and discharged.
The simulation results showed that the BESS active filter performed successfully mitigated
5th and 7th harmonics as well as the THDi at the PCC. Moreover, in the scenario where
the passive filters were connected to same BESS bus, the performance of the BESS active
filter was better. The experimental results showed that the LCL filter mitigated harmonics
and consequently reduced THDi during charging and discharging for all three parts.

1.1
Global Energy Consumption and GHG Emissions
As the human population increased since 1900, humans began using natural sources to
satisfy their needs and wants which eventually led to the industrial revolution. Both, the
first and second industrial revolution that harnessed energy from coal and oil respectively
have considerably eased fundamental human necessities like food, transport and shelter
thereby resulting in higher economic development and better living standards during the
last century. However, along with reaping benefits, unarguably, burning fossil fuels to
generate energy is one of the primary causes to a 1°C rise in global temperatures over the
past 150 years [20]. Fig 1.1.1 shows the exponential increase in coal and oil consumption
since 1940s and the corresponding rise in annual CO2 emissions from each country.
This continuous increase in energy consumption and production coupled with pollution
caused by conventional sources of energy has resulted in global problems like climate
change, ecological destruction and degradation of human health. Acknowledging the grav-
ity of such impending issues in the Paris Agreement signed in 2016 [21], nations all over
the world consequently set the target of limiting the rise of average global temperature to

1.2
VRES Challenges and Energy Storage in Power
Grid
In the context of this thesis, the first characteristic on unpredictable output generation
becomes very important. Besides uncertainty on the demand side, this unpredictability
results in uncertainty on the supply side of the power grid.
Additionally, the power
electronics used to connect the VRES to the grid substantially affects the power quality
of the grid.
For instance, it may lead to disturbances in the distributed system like
excess reactive power at certain buses or zones, transients, reduction in power factor, large
current and voltage fluctuations, noise, harmonics and exceeding total harmonic distortion
(THD) limits which could result in various unwanted consequences like overheating and
failure of transformers and other power equipment, false tripping of protection relays,
interference with communication signals, errors in operation of sensitive microcontrollers
[29, 30].
ESS decouples the generation and consumption and hence are possible solutions to such
challenges [31]. In ESS, the generated electrical energy is converted into a storable form
and stored in various mediums which can subsequently be converted back into electrical
energy when needed [32]. As the energy is stored, they stabilize the power grid by main-
taining load balance. Electricity storage technologies can primarily be classified by the
form of storage into: (i) electrical energy storage using capacitors and super-capacitors to

Chapter 2
Role of BESS in Power Systems
Fundamentally, BESS in conjunction with VREs are used to store the generated energy
for later use. In order to ease the integration of VRE + BESS system into the power
grid, [35] identifies a few key characteristics of BESS: (i) rated power capacity; (ii) energy
capacity; (iii) storage duration; (iv) cycle life/lifetime; (iv) self-discharge; (v) state-of-
charge; (vi) round-trip efficiency. Due to the increasing needs for flexibility within the
power system combined with rapid decreases in battery technology costs in recent years,
the role of BESS has expanded in the power system. Section 2.1 talks about the different
services that a grid-connected utility scale BESS offers in a power grid. The next section
2.2 reviews literature on using BESS in improving power quality and expands on its
role in mitigating harmonics generated from VREs. Section 2.3 discusses the problem
statement(s) of this thesis. Lastly, section 2.4 outlines the structure of this report.
2.1
BESS Services
The various services provided by BESS are mentioned below.
2.1.1
Arbitrage
As the name suggests, arbitrage involves charging the battery when energy prices are low
and discharging it during peak hours when the prices are high. This takes advantage
of the price variations throughout the day and acts as a source of income for the BESS
owner. Additionally, storing energy when prices are low during off-peak hours also helps
mitigate curtailment of VREs thereby minimizing the energy generation from conventional
generators. Another advantage of arbitrage which does not include energy markets is load-

leveling. The surplus energy generated is stored in the battery. The battery is discharged
during periods of excess demand thus enabling efficient dispatch of generated power [35].
2.1.2
Peaking Capacity
Power systems experience periods maximum and minimum demand in a given year.
Though the minimum demand is the base load and is easily covered, system operators
need to ensure adequate supply of generation capacity to cover the periods of maximum
demand (or peak demand). This capacity is generally supplied by higher-cost generators.
As the peak demand can be estimated from the load curve, the energy stored from VREs
in BESS can be used instead of the expensive generators to ensure that the peak demand
is covered thus countering for their unpredictable generation [35, 36].
2.1.3
Black Start
During the rare event of a complete failure of the grid, when large generators start up,
they need an external source of electricity which is conventionally supplied via an on-site
diesel generator. By replacing the diesel generation with an on-site BESS, it can provide
the same service and support restarting the system. Further BESS with large capacities
can provide other services to the grid in addition to its black start service. [37, 36].
2.1.4
Congestion Reduction in Transmission and Distribution Sys-
tems
Congestion occurs in the transmission and distribution systems when the power demand
exceeds the capacity of the system as the demand continuously fluctuates throughout the
year. A grid-connected BESS addresses this issue as the stored energy can be released
when the demand peaks. This avoids installation of new infrastructure specifically to
cater to such high demands thereby reducing costs [35, 36].
2.1.5
Frequency Regulation
In a power system, the power generated must exactly match the demand to ensure reliable
operation of the system. Any mismatch in demand and supply directly affects the syn-
chronous frequency of the system thus disturbing the stability of the system. Frequency
control service provides corrective measures to any critical change in the frequency instan-
taneously and is traditionally provided by synchronous generators. Since there are several
categories of operating reserves and ancillary services based on timescales, a BESS can
be appropriately sized to operate in the best suited category. For instance, higher power

Traditionally, on-load tap changers, shunt capacitors and step voltage regulators are used

Conventional Techniques to Improve Grid Power Quality
Installation of volt-var components such as on-load tap changers (OLTCs), voltage regula-
tors (VRs) and capacitor bank is one of the conventional techniques to improve the grid’s
capability to provide a stable supply to the end-users [16, 46]. [7] classifies power quality
improvement broadly into three main generations: (i) the first generation devices are sim-
ple and cheap. Passive, active and hybrid filters fall under this category; (ii) the second
generation devices typically include the popularly used flexible alternating current trans-
mission system (FACTS) devices; (iii) the third generation devices are multi-functional
devices capable of performing advanced functions such as smart impedance and electrical

First Generation Devices
The primary function of first generation devices is to intercept the spread of harmonics
into the grid or cancel the harmonics generated on the consumer side. The structure of
first generation devices is illustrated in Fig 2.2.2.
• Passive Filters - They are basically a combination of incductances and capaci-
tances connected either in series or in parallel to eliminate current harmonics and
compensate reactive power. When connected in parallel, they provide a path for the
harmonic currents to flow as the impedance for the desired harmonic is reduced by
selecting appropriate inductance and capacitance values (this process is called tun-
ing). On the other hand, in a series connection they prevent the harmonic current
from entering the device it is protecting [47, 48].
• Active Power Filters - Unlike passive filters, whose performance is directly related
to the degree of tuning, quality factor and source impedance, active power filters
inject a harmonic current/voltage to cancel the desired harmonic generated in the
grid [7]. They are further classified into shunt and series active filters [49, 50].
– Shunt Active Filters - Shunt active filters compensate current harmonics by
injecting a harmonic of the same magnitude but with a phase difference of 180
°with respect to the current harmonic. This maintains the sinusoidal nature of
the grid current [51].
– Series Active Filters - It is connected in series to the bus/component to be
protected and compensates for voltage harmonics. The underlying principle is
same as shunt filters except that instead of current, a voltage harmonic of the
same magnitude (as the voltage harmonic being cancelled) and having a phase
difference of 180 °(to the voltage harmonic being cancelled). One disadvantage
is that production of same power to compensate harmonics increases the cost
of this type of filter [7].
• Hybrid Active Power Filters - As the name suggests, it is a combination of both,
passive and active filters therefore having both their advantages. They offer a com-
promise between passive filters requiring fine tuning which has physical limitations
and the high costs of active filters [7].

Thesis Problem Statement
As mentioned earlier in section 1.2, the power electronics used to connect the VRES to
the grid affect the power quality. Review paper [16] discusses three broad categories to
improve power quality issues: (i) voltage regulation devices and strategies by installing
capacitor bank and tap changer; (ii) using FACTS devices like static compensators (STAT-
COMs) and D-STATCOMS to improve the active and reactive power as well as the phase
imbalance in the system; (iii) installing shunt active power filters for charging stations.

Although existing literature studies [62, 63, 64, 65, 66, 67, 68, 69] suggest using a BESS

power quality alongside its primary of active power exchange has not been explored much.
This work focuses on using grid-connected BESS to enhance VRE power quality
thereby enhancing the BESS utility in a distribution network by observing
the effect of BESS on parameters that characterize power quality at the PCC.

by power electronics. Moreover, Denmark is the leading country in wind energy

3.4.1
Overview of WTGs
Until 1990s, the wind turbines were generally designed to operate at a fixed speed. This
meant that regardless of the wind speed, the wind turbine’s rotor rotated at a constant
speed and was bound to the frequency of the power system. An induction machine was
used as the electrical generator that converter the rotor’s rotational energy into electrical
energy. However, the presence of an induction machine also meant that it required more
reactive power from the stator to be magnetized implying that the reactive drawn increases
with the active power produced leading to voltage regulation issues. While these machines
did not generate harmonics, the fluctuations in active power were relayed to the grid
because the stator was directly connected to the grid (called as flicker).
The intention to generate maximum power by varying the rotor speed of the wind turbine
led to the development of type C wind turbines. They are popularly known as Doubly Fed
Induction Generators (DFIG) because both, the stator and rotor of the induction machine
are connected to the grid. While the stator is directly connected to the grid, the rotor
is connected to the grid using power electronic devices (usually a pair of back-to-back
pulse width modulated VSCs). Although the flicker from type C WTGs is prevented due
to power electronics controlling the rotor speed, the pulse width modulated converters
generate harmonics.
It must be noted the type D WTG where the generator is entirely decoupled from the
grid by means of power electronics is not discussed here.

The BOSS Project
The concept of smart grid emphasizes on flexible electricity production and consumption
by small consumers.
The idea is to stress on using VRES to transition from passive
and predictable generation/consumption to an interactive and dynamic generation/con-
sumption controlled and automated by intelligent digital systems thus resulting in socio-
economic benefits. In this context, the BOSS (or Bornholm Smartgrid Secured by Grid
Connected Battery Systems) project is an attempt to demonstrate the use of the largest
battery energy storage (1 MW/MWh) in Denmark to increase grid flexibility [82]. BESS
have great potential to provide grid services that are currently provided by conventional
generators driven by fossil fuels and hence providing another reason to retire them. Tests
pertaining to grid stability when the battery is connected to and disconnected from the
grid will be performed in Bornholm. The Bornholm island was chosen as the testbed for
this project because of its ability to operate in islanding mode as well as in interconnected
mode (to the Nordic Power System).
As the simulations performed in this thesis focused on mitigating harmonics generated
from type C WTG using the battery (both installed in Akikerby) and the 55kW/80kWh
scaled-down version of the battery was used for the experiment, it expands the scope of the
grid stability tests from ancillary services and energy storage to power quality. Further,
it would also aids Bornholm’s regional municipality’s vision towards sustainability.

Methodology
Modeling and simulations play an important role in identifying power quality issues and
henceforth improving them. They can broadly be classified into:
• Data driven models based on Artificial Intelligence (AI) have become pop-
ular due to the inclusion of digital infrastructure that achieves a higher degree of
automation in the grid. AI is a computational technique that automates and in-
creases the accuracy of a given task by learning and making decisions like humans
[83]. The basic idea is to feed data collected from sensors installed in the grid into
an AI algorithm that "learns and takes the best decision" in the control strategies
governing electrical components to improve one or more power quality parameters
(for which the AI algorithm is designed). Fig 4.0.1 illustrates some of the popular AI
based techniques used to address challenges concerning power quality. The primary
drawbacks of this model are twofold – the need for large amount of accurate data
and the inability to verify the model as it is essentially a black-box.
• Grid modelling techniques that perform dynamic simulations where each
grid component is comprehensively modelled with all details to ensure that the
model is as close to the practical situation as possible thus minimizing errors. [62,
63, 64, 67, 68, 69] are a few examples that perform dynamic simulations on the
grid model comprising of wind farm for energy generation, BESS for storage and
a FACTS device to address the power quality issues. [22] also performs dynamic
simulations to demonstrate the use of BESS as an independent-entity (without any
FACTS device) to improve harmonics in the microgrid. However, a high degree of
31
==PAGE:39==
Master thesis
emphasis on detailed modelling is the primary drawback of this model as it cannot
capture all dynamics of the system.
• Scenario/Case(s) Modelling where the desired simulations are performed on
the modelled grid for various scenarios and the performance is compared.
This
thesis uses this technique where the different components (mentioned in section 4.1)
connected to the BESS bus in region 2 are turned ON/OFF and their performance
is compared. Unlike the above two models, this model eliminates the need for large
amounts of data.

Experiment Description
In a real scenario, the battery is connected to a VRES and would be charged or/and
discharged according to the intermittent generation of the VRE or/and the current de-
mands of the power system. The lab-experiment conducted using DTU’s lab-scale BESS
at PowerLabDK was an attempt to emulate this scenario. The aim was to observe the
performance of LCL filters used in the BESS on harmonics and THDi when the battery
48
==PAGE:56==
Master thesis
4.2. Experimental Setup
was being charged and discharged at different rates. The experiment was divided into
three parts namely (i) incremental; (ii) intermediate and; (iii) full.
In the incremental part, the battery was charged in "increments" (or steps) of 5 kW
starting from the minimum value of 5 kW to the maximum value of 40 kW and the
measurements were taken after the system stabilized at each step. It was ensured that
the initial charging rate for this part was set to 0 kW. The battery was then discharged
from 40 kW to 0 kW in intervals of 5 kW. Similar to the aforementioned charging case,
the measurements were taken after the system stabilized at each interval.
In the intermediate part, the battery charging rate was varied in two steps: (i) from 0 kW
to any intermediate value between 5 to 40 kW; (ii) from the intermediate value to 40 kW.
The intermediate value chosen for this experiment was 20 kW to maintain symmetricity.
The initial condition was set 0 kW. Similarly, the battery discharging rate was changed
from 40 kW to 20 kW in the first step and subsequently from 20 kW to 0 kW. Finally
in the full part, the battery charging rate was varied from 0 to 40 kW (with the initial
condition at 0 kW) in a single step. Similarly, the discharging rate was varied from 40 to
0 kW in a single step.
Unlike the incremental part, the measurements in intermediate and full parts were taken
during the switch before the system stabilized. The attempt was to capture the distur-
bances caused during the transition. The recording time of each measurement for all the
three parts was 2 minutes.

Experimental Observations Relevant to Mitigate Harmon-
ics from VRES
As mentioned earlier, the experiment was conducted to emulate a real scenario where
the battery would be charged and discharged at varying rates due multiple reasons like
intermittent VRES power generation and the dynamic situation of the power grid. Some
of the key observations from the experiment relevant to harmonic mitigatio from VRES
are listed below:
• Optimal Charging/Discharging Rate - The above experiment showed that the
5th, 7th, higher order harmonics and the THDi were minimum when the lab-scale
battery was operated at 30 kW and above (i.e. at higher active power setpoint).
Moreover, setting the rate to 40 kW provided best results. Thus, the rate of charg-
ing/discharging is an important factor that affects the amount of harmonic content
being mitigated.
• DC Current Variation - In extension to the previous point, the variation in
DC current decreased as the charging/discharging rate increased. Moreover, the
variation was lower during discharging. Though the DC current does not directly
affect harmonic content and THDi, it provides information on battery health that
supplements the overall "mitigation functionality".
• Filtering - During charging the harmonic content of 5th and 7th orders were greater
before the filter. On the contrary, the harmonic content was greater after the fil-
ter during discharging. The difference in distortion indicated that the LCL filter
mitigated harmonics during charging as well as discharging of the battery. Thus,
a battery paired with appropriate filters plays a crucial role in enhancing power
quality from VRES.
• Transitions - The experiment showed that the transition from 0 to any intermediate
rate until 25 kW (for this battery) led to significantly higher harmonic content (see
section 5.2.2). This helps in selecting the optimal transitions involving turning the
BESS ON/OFF for rare events. The results of the anomaly case are excluded for
this observation.
• Higher Order Harmonics - Knowledge of the most frequently occurring higher
order harmonics helps in improving filters (and other power quality improving de-

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The work carried out in this thesis was explored the role of BESS in enhancing VRES
power quality and enhance its services in the power grid. Particularly, the main goal
was to analyze how BESS can be used to mitigate 5th and 7th current harmonics and
THDi originating from VRE. The Aakikrerby grid was modified to suit the needs of this
investigation and modeled in PF.
• PF Model and Simulation – In the context of the two power quality parameters
that were chosen for this work, a Harmonic Load Flow analysis was performed on
modeled the Aakikerby grid for two scenarios that differed in location of passive
filters. The BESS active filter was modelled using an AC source element. Subse-
quently, the results from the two scenarios were compared. The comparative study
of the two scenarios indicated that a BESS equipped with an active filter mod-
elled using an AC source could successfully mitigate harmonics and reduce THDi at
PCC as an independent unit. Additionally, the BESS active filter performed better
than other passive filters in scenario 1 which is a better representation of a realistic
situation.
• Lab-Experiment – The experiment conducted using the BESS comprising of a
55 kW/80 kWh Xolta battery at PowerLabDk emulated a realistic scenario where
the battery would be charged/discharged according to the dynamics of the power
system. The performance of the LCL filter was observed in all three parts – in-
cremental, intermediate and full. The results showed that the filter mitigated 5th,
7th and higher order current harmonic content as well as THDi during charging
