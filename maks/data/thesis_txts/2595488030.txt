Abstract
An applicable method to quantify and elaborate on the uncertainty in contaminant
mass discharge (CMD) estimations would make it possible to improve risk-assessment
and prioritise remedial activities for soil- and groundwater contamination. It would
furthermore allow for a more eﬃcient and sustainable use of the allocated resources,
which is pivotal for managing the vast amount of contaminated sites.
Here a geostatistical method for quantiﬁcation of CMD uncertainties in a multilevel
control plane is proposed. The method explicitly accounts for the spatial heterogeneity
of the interpolated variable, and estimates both the most likely spatial distribution
and corresponding uncertainty.
The method employ a Box-Cox transformation on
residuals generated by applying a spatially varying mean given by an analytical solution
of a macrodispersive transport equation.
This ensures Gaussian and non-negative
data. From this, conditional simulations of the spatial distribution of concentration
and hydraulic conductivity are generated, which are combined to give the full mass
discharge probability distribution. The developed method is tested and validated on
three V2-surveyed Danish contaminated sites, and shown to fully determine the CMD
uncertainty. Site conditions vary in sampling density, plume delineation, number of
measurements and deﬁnition of hydraulic conductivity ﬁeld. The method is further
developed to incorporate multiple plumes to reﬂect sites with more complex source
zone situations.
Additionally the method has the advantage of low computational
requirements and the possibility of locating the peak of the mobile contaminant mass.
Results validated the method on a site with a sampling density of 0.005 sample points,
n, per m2 and an uncertain plume delineation, with a relative uncertainty of 48%.
Using larger or more dense datasets reduce the uncertainty. A sampling density of
0.024 n/m2 and 0.003 n/m2 but well-deﬁned plumes result in relative uncertainties of
36% and 38%, respectively. Assuming a moderately homogeneous aquifer (σ2
lnK =
0.34) and a constant ﬂow ﬁeld for the most densely sampled site (0.024 n/m2) is found
to constrain the uncertainty to 30%. The method proves overall consistent with CMD
estimations by practitioners, but determine a considerably higher CMD for the heavily
contaminated site Lundtoftevej. For none of the test-sites, the assigned risk change
based on the CMD estimated by the method. Dilution of the most conservative CMD
estimate (95%-quantile) in the existing abstraction at Industrivej still classiﬁes the
site as "no risk towards the existing abstraction". Comparison between diﬀerent site-
conditions reveals that for maximum eﬀect of resource allocation, properly deﬁning
the contaminant distribution constrains the uncertainty. This is however only valid if
a constant ﬂow ﬁeld can be justiﬁed from site-investigations. A conceptual framework
for method implementation is ultimately proposed, which can be used as an indicator
of where to locate additional measurements to minimise the CMD uncertainty.
The method can provide decision-makers with the possibility of integrating the incon-
testable spatial variability and corresponding uncertainty of CMD estimations in risk
assessment of contaminated sites. Based on the successful application of the developed
geostatistical method, it is therefore considered a potential tool in the transition to-
wards an uncertainty-driven management practice in risk-assessment of contaminated
sites in Denmark

5.1
Overview of the developed geostatistical method quantifying the CMD
uncertainty in a transect located downstream a contaminated site, us-
ing a set of MLS measurements of hydraulic conductivity and contam-
inant concentration.
The method is divided into three overall steps.
In Step 1 the explanatory data analysis is performed, which aims to
determine a suitable transformation and spatial correlation structure
(i.e. variogram) of the measurements. In Step 2 the method generates
an ensemble of conditional simulations of the spatial distribution of the
concentration and the hydraulic conductivity.
In Step 3 these simu-
lations are combined using equation 2.2 yielding an ensemble of mass
discharge simulations, from which a CMD probability distribution can
be derived.

Chapter 1
Introduction
With an estimate of 2.8 million contaminated sites in Europe, and increasing political
attention on sustainability and soil-protection in the European Union (Payá Pérez and
Rodríguez Eugenio, 2018) and the world (FAO and ITPS, 2015; Brevik and Sauer,
2014), the need for cost-eﬀective risk-assessment tools to prioritise remedial activities
is pivotal.
There are 37,000 conﬁrmed contaminated sites in Denmark, which means that a com-
prehensive priority-eﬀort is required in order to secure the Danish drinking water re-
sources from point-source soil- and groundwater contamination. The current decision-
practice has an inverse relationship between expenses and number of investigations.
Initially a high number of low-cost investigations are conducted, followed by a selection
process gradually sorting out the sites found not to be a risk, ultimately ending up
with a few sites that require expensive remedial eﬀorts (Danske Regioner, 2019). This
process calls for several risk assessments, for the most eﬃcient and sustainable use of
the allocated resources (Miljøministeriet, 2016; Overheu et al., 2014).
The decentralised Danish drinking water distribution network depends solely on ground-
water, with the water only undergoing a simple treatment process before being dis-
tributed (The Capital Region of Denmark, 2019c; Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2020;
Damvad Analytics, 2018). This leads to very high water quality requirements for the
Danish aquifers, which consequently makes them vulnerable to soil and groundwater
contaminating activities (Locatelli et al., 2019; Aisopou et al., 2015).
In addition to the inherent physical-chemical properties, the fate and transport of con-
taminants in soil- and groundwater environments are governed by the local geology
and hydrogeology (Worsztynowicz et al., 2008; Gavrilescu, 2014; Bohling et al., 2012;
Lee et al., 2018). Complex heterogeneous glacial formations makes up the majority of
the geology in the Eastern part of Denmark (Klint et al., 2012), resulting in large local-
scale geologic heterogeneity at many contaminated sites (Kessler et al., 2012). This can
result in large uncertainties in characterising and understanding site-speciﬁc contam-
inant plume transport, as the sampling location highly aﬀects the critical parameters
used in risk-assessments (Lee et al., 2018; ITRC, 2010; Troldborg et al., 2010). The
complex nature of comprehensive site-delineation, forces practitioners to draw conclu-
sions from often incomplete datasets (Kitanidis, 2015). Uncertainty quantiﬁcation is
therefore needed, to focus site assessment and remedial activities on the contaminated
sites posing the largest risk towards the groundwater resource and human health.
Contaminant mass discharge (CMD) integrates two important features in contaminant
risk; concentration and mobility (Brooks et al., 2015; Suthersan et al., 2010), and is
increasingly being incorporated into risk assessments of contaminated sites as an alter-
==PAGE:22==
2
native to point-value concentration- and ﬂow-based risk assessment (Locatelli et al.,
2019; Cremeans et al., 2018; Schwede and Cirpka, 2010; Hadley and Newell, 2012),
also in Denmark (Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020; The Capital Region of Denmark,
2019a).
The importance of uncertainty estimations within CMD calculations have
been long acknowledged (Nichols, 2004; Li et al., 2007; Rifai et al., 2010; ITRC, 2010).
CMD estimation interpolates and integrates concentration- and ﬂow measurements
across the control plane, with the uncertainty being a result of subsurface heterogene-
ity in terms of spatially and temporarily varying ﬂow ﬁelds and contaminant distri-
bution combined with often limited available site-measurements (Brooks et al., 2015;
Troldborg et al., 2012; ITRC, 2010; Basu et al., 2008). Given this complexity of the
sampled parameters in subsurface investigations, it is not possible to determine exact
or error-free estimates (Kitanidis, 1999). Geostatistics renders the possibility of inter-
polating and statistically describing spatial variability and distribution of parameters
in unsampled regions of the investigated area (Webster and Oliver, 2007; Isaaks and
Srivastava, 1989).
In order to oblige to legislative limits of aquifer contaminant concentrations, variabil-
ity and uncertainty considerations should dominate decision-making (Kitanidis, 2015).
This complicates the decision-making process for public authorities, as uncertainty in
estimations implies a risk of being wrong (Beven, 2018). Implementation of quantita-
tive uncertainties and CMD estimation in risk assessment practices calls for a paradigm
shift in the evaluation of groundwater remediation eﬀorts (Hadley and Newell, 2012),
and is a novel approach within Danish Regional work (The Capital Region of Den-
mark, 2019a; Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020). However, the transition away from a
concentration-based practice poses several challenges; increasing demands on the avail-
ability and quality of sampled data (The Capital Region of Denmark, 2019a). The
need for higher-resolution site characterisation to reduce uncertainty is also challenging
(Kitanidis, 2017; Dijkshoorn et al., 2014).
If the Capital Region of Denmark aspires to integrate CMD uncertainty into risk-
assessment of contaminated sites, a method which can be applied on locations with
diﬀerent characteristics and sampling density is required. This study will contribute to
the existing risk-assessment practice, by estimating the most likely spatial distribution
of CMD along with the associated uncertainty. This will be done using a kriging and
conditional Monte Carlo simulation of hydraulic conductivity, K, and concentration, C,
distribution in a 2-dimensional transect downstream a source zone. This will generate
equally likely realisations of ﬂow and concentration, used to obtain a mass discharge
probability distribution displaying the full range of CMD estimations.
The main objectives of this study are to: (1) Determine the challenges within the
current practise applied for contaminated site investigations in The Capital Region of
Denmark; (2) review the diﬀerent methods currently applied for uncertainty estima-
tions in contaminant mass discharge calculations; (3) develop a geostatistical method
to improve the quantiﬁcation of uncertainties and the prioritisation of remedial ac-
tivities within the Capital Region of Denmark; (4) apply the developed geostatistical
approach on 3 case studies to evaluate the reliability of the current contaminant mass
discharge estimates at these speciﬁc sites; and to (5) give a perspective on the need
for site-speciﬁc knowledge of governing parameters required for contaminant mass dis-
charge estimations.

Chapter 2
Contaminant Mass Discharge Estimation
This chapter provides an overview of the current practise for contaminant mass dis-
charge estimations and the corresponding uncertainties. Initially the current approach
to risk assessment of contaminated sites in Denmark will be brieﬂy introduced. This
will be followed by an introduction to the contaminant mass discharge concept and the
diﬀerent methods for determining it, and the corresponding overall uncertainties which
contaminant mass discharge estimations are subject to.
2.1
Risk Assessment of Contaminated Sites in Denmark
The ﬁve Regions of Denmark have the overall responsibility of protecting and securing
the Danish drinking water resources against soil- and groundwater point-source con-
tamination (Danske Regioner, 2019). This is implemented through an extensive set of
guidelines and requirements that the companies conducting the site investigations and
proposing potential remediation activities must meet (The Capital Region of Denmark,
2020; Miljøministeriet, 2016). All done to uphold the law as stated in the legislative
act from Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet (2017) to "prevent, remove or limit the harm-
ful impacts from point-source soil contamination upon nature, environment and public
health" and with "special aim at securing the national drinking water resources." The
Regions currently have knowledge of 37,000 contaminated sites in Denmark (and an
additional 18,000 still waiting to be investigated) with the largest proportion being
situated within The Capital Region of Denmark. The large regional diﬀerences calls
for regional solutions (Danske Regioner, 2019). The highest priority of The Capital
Region (from here simply stated as The Region) when ensuring clean groundwater, is
to secure the areas where 85% of the groundwater is abstracted from by 2030 (The
Capital Region of Denmark, 2019c).
The investigated sites are classiﬁed according to the potential risk towards human
health, groundwater resources, surface-water and the environment (Miljøministeriet,
2012).
Initially historic data of potential polluting site-activities is collected, and
if site-contamination is suspected, the site is surveyed on Knowledge Level 1 (V1
- ’Vidensniveau 1’).
This is followed by a preliminary investigation to conﬁrm or
disconﬁrm an actual site-contamination, and if conﬁrmed, the site is surveyed on
Knowledge Level 2 (V2 - ’Vidensniveau 2’). The next phase is further investigations,
to describe and outline the contamination to an extent where a more detailed risk
assessment is possible.
Finally remedial activities will be carried out on the sites
posing a risk, starting with the ones constituting the largest risk. The priority eﬀort
currently in use by The Region is visualised in Figure 2.1.

are conducted and a selection process gradually sorts out the sites that are not a risk,
ultimately ending up with a few sites where expensive remedial activities is required.
This eﬀort calls for several risk assessments throughout the process, so the right sites
are discarded. The Danish Regions have categorised 121 of the sites to be ’Large’,
where 9 are determined as a ’Generation Contamination’, all being subject to very
high remediation costs (Danske Regioner, 2019).
A ’Generation Contamination’ is
characterised as the most comprehensive and complicated contaminated sites, which
are extremely expensive to remediate. They are typically built up over generations, and
the high complexity of the contamination will take generations to remediate (Danske
Regioner, 2019). This creates a tension between the high-proﬁled largest sites and the
potentially de-prioritisation of the numerous smaller sites. The largest contamination
are not always the worst in terms of the risk towards groundwater, health and the
environment, and contaminated sites with smaller remediation costs can potentially
pose a higher risk than the large sites (Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020). A lack
of action on these smaller sites, can thus potentially result in closure of nearby water
works and detrimentally aﬀect the groundwater resources. To focus site-remediation on

the sites posing the largest threat to the groundwater resources and ensure an eﬃcient
and sustainable utilisation of The Regions resources, a highly eﬃcient prioritisation is
needed. This requires the contaminant mass and corresponding risk at the investigated
sites to be determined with a suﬃcient certainty (Miljøministeriet, 2012).
2.2
Contaminant Mass Discharge in Risk Assessment
Due to the spatial variability of ﬂow ﬁelds, a high aqueous concentration does not nec-
essarily equal a high risk, as concentration measurements lack information on mobility
(Miljøministeriet, 2012). The majority of remediation projects work with the aim of
restoring the groundwater to drinking water standards safe for human consumption.
However, multiple site-remediation performance studies have found this goal to be
diﬃcult to achieve (Hadley and Newell, 2012; Newell et al., 2011). The use of contam-
inant mass discharge is therefore increasingly being incorporated into risk assessment
of contaminated sites (Suthersan et al., 2010; Brooks et al., 2018; Troldborg et al.,
2012; Newell et al., 2011; Johnston et al., 2014), as the coupling of mass discharge and
source mass is important when assessing long-term impacts upon groundwater, source
longevity and evaluating remedial and attenuation eﬃciency and time-frames.
Diﬀerent implementation strategies exists.
In The United States numerous regula-
tors, consultants, and academics have developed a “Technology Overview: Use and
Measurement of Mass Flux and Mass Discharge” for The Interstate Technology and
Regulatory Council (ITRC, 2010). Verreydt et al. (2012) proposes a risk assessment
method applying a CMD-based risk management strategy to be implemented into
Flemish regulation, in accordance with the European Union Water Framework Di-
rective regulations. Here the concept incorporates a maximum allowed risk, which is
translated into a maximum CMD that can be monitored along a predeﬁned control
plane.
Newell et al. (2011) presents an system organising CMD from sources and
plumes in orders of magnitudes, separating them into 10 categories depending on the
CMD. The given categorisation can then be used to determine risk to a receptor (e.g.
a water abstraction well).
BOX 1: Contaminant Mass Discharge and Contaminant Mass Flux
Contaminant mass discharge, CMD [MT-1], and contaminant mass ﬂux, J [ML-2T-1],
combines two important features in assessment of contaminant risk: Concentration C
[ML-3] and mobility (Brooks et al., 2015; Suthersan et al., 2010). Contaminant mass
discharge (mass per unit time of a deﬁned control plane) can thus be expressed as the
integrated mass of the contaminant ﬂux (mass per unit time per unit area) measured
over the control plane, and the abbreviations are occasionally used without distinguish-
ing the two (ITRC, 2010). In Denmark the term contaminant mass ﬂux (’forurenings-
ﬂux’) is used as the mass per unit time, hence corresponding to the contaminant mass
discharge.
For simpliﬁcation purposes, this project will use the deﬁnitions of con-
taminant mass discharge as CMD [MT-1] and contaminant mass ﬂux as J [ML-2T-1].
==PAGE:26==
6
The incorporation of contaminant mass discharge as a decision tool is currently be-
ing evaluated on concluded sites within the Capital Region as a part of the holistic
site-assessment (The Capital Region of Denmark, 2019a; Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen,
2020).
Current site-delineation in Denmark is mainly based on discrete soil-, pore
air- and water- samples from point-measurements covering the investigated area (The
Capital Region of Denmark, 2019b). The conclusions are however potentially prone to
large uncertainties due to the spatial heterogeneity found at many investigated sites,
as the critical parameters are highly dependent on the local-scale sampling location
(ITRC, 2010; Troldborg et al., 2012, 2010).
This challenges the current sampling
strategies in use; when are enough measurements enough, with the safest conclusion
often being additional investigations (Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020).
Today no quality criteria exist for contaminant mass discharge values in Denmark,
and the associated contaminant risk is determined by diluting the plume mass with a
ﬁctitious water abstraction volume and controlling if the groundwater quality criteria
is exceeded (The Capital Region of Denmark, 2019a, 2020).
The Region currently
implements the concept by using a range of mass discharge categories, based on the
volume of water aﬀected by the cCMD from a site. The reasoning behind being that
the larger the volume potentially aﬀected, the larger the impact upon the groundwater
resources and thereby the higher the risk. The categorisation is divided into two dif-
ferent types; one for existing water abstractions and one for the groundwater resource
with the risk being upon a potential future abstraction well.
BOX 2: Maximum Allowed Concentration
Several terms exist for classifying an aqueous contaminant risk in e.g.
an aquifer.
Threshold value and groundwater quality criteria is the direct translation from the
Danish term of grundvandskvalitetskriterie. Adapted terms as groundwater concen-

Hadley and Newell (2012)) also exists. This project will however use the term Max-
imum Allowable Concentration (MAC) as standardisation, with the MAC being the
maximum concentration that can be justiﬁed in consistency with the objective of main-
taining unimpaired health risk (in e.g. an aquifer used for groundwater abstraction)
in accordance with the values listed in Miljøministeriet (2015).

based on elaborating the need for additional site-investigations and to visualise the
large diﬀerences in knowledge level (Miljøministeriet, 2012). This is done by diﬀeren-

as a parameter covering the conceptual uncertainty and uncertainty from lack of data.
Either by an overall qualitative assessment or a semi-quantitative approach where a
relative uncertainty-score between 0-1 is assigned from a list of qualitative estimations
(for further details, see Miljøministeriet (2012)). The uncertainties embedded within
the site-speciﬁc measurements is thus not currently addressed, e.g. the spatial variabil-
ity of concentration- and ﬂow ﬁelds due to local-scale heterogeneity is not accounted
for.

ITRC, 2010): (1) Integral pumping tests (IPT); (2) passive ﬂux meter measurements
(PFM); and (3) multilevel sampling approach (MLS).

in order to fully deﬁne the plume extent, and to outline the two-dimensional contami-
nant concentration distribution. To characterise the speciﬁc discharge in the transect,
representative measurements are needed for both hydraulic conductivity K [LT-1] and
hydraulic gradient i [LL-1] (as deﬁned by Darcy’s Law q = K · i). The method is
naturally subject to large uncertainties.
2.4
Uncertainties in Contaminant Mass Discharge
Published literature on the subject attributes the large uncertainties of contaminant
mass discharge estimation to generally be a result of subsurface heterogeneity in terms
of spatially and temporarily varying ﬂow ﬁelds and contaminant distribution (Brooks
et al., 2015; Troldborg et al., 2012; ITRC, 2010; Basu et al., 2008). Uncertainty is also
related to the control plane itself, as it assumes perpendicular ﬂow, which could be
subject to the same temporal and spatial variations.
Naturally, the more heterogeneous the CMD distribution is, the ﬁner the monitoring
resolution network is required, to minimise the inﬂuence of the unmeasured areas in
the control plane (Troldborg et al., 2010). Contaminant concentrations and speciﬁc
discharge can vary by an order of magnitude within short distances as a result of
high subsurface heterogeneity (ITRC, 2010). The MSL method described in Section
2.3 lacks a quantitative uncertainty assessment of the potential variation of these
parameters from the un-sampled regions.
2.4.1
Characterising Subsurface Heterogeneity
Stochastic theories describing subsurface ﬂow and transport for applications in sub-
surface environments such as heterogeneous geologic media have existed for several
decades (Sudicky et al., 2010). In 1983 Gelhar and Axness (1983) presented the con-
trolling ﬁeld mechanisms to describe three-dimensional statistically anisotropic het-
erogeneity in geologic settings, by describing the variation of hydraulic conductivity as
a function of its natural logarithm. Characterising the spatial variability of hydraulic
conductivity in heterogeneous porous media by assuming that the natural logarithm
of K follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution, has since been tested on ﬁeld-scale
(Sudicky, 1986; Hess et al., 1992; Zheng et al., 2011; Bjerg et al., 1992). As such, a
variance of σ2
lnK <1 has been found to indicate a moderately homogeneous aquifer.
For highly heterogeneous aquifers this assumption is however challenged, as discussed
in Meerschaert et al. (2013); Bohling et al. (2012); Rehfeldt et al. (1992) and Bianchi
et al. (2011) for an extensively investigated Macro Dispersion Experiment (MADE)
site in Mississippi with σ2
lnK > 4.5.
==PAGE:31==
2.4 Uncertainties in Contaminant Mass Discharge
Sudicky et al. (2010) discusses the spatial correlation in the measured hydraulic con-
ductivity ﬁeld from variogram analysis in the three principle directions. Sudicky et al.
(2010) also summarises the relationship between lnK variances and correlation dis-
tances in each direction for four extensively investigated aquifers (see Table 3 in paper).
Here the connection between a small σ2
lnK (moderate heterogeneity) and correspond-
ing small correlation distances is presented. This relationship is also described by Bjerg
et al. (1992) which investigated the spatial variability of the hydraulic conductivity
in a sandy aquifer, comparable to the one found at Industrivej (Section 3.2). They
report a σ2
lnK = 0.37 and a small horizontal correlation length of 1-2.5 m.
2.4.2
Risk and Uncertainty
The importance of uncertainty estimations within contaminant mass discharge calcu-
lations have been long recognised (Nichols, 2004; Li et al., 2007; Rifai et al., 2010;
ITRC, 2010), but the implementation of these uncertainties into the risk assessment is
a novel approach within Danish Regional and National work (The Capital Region of
Denmark, 2019a; Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020). The decision makers are forced
to draw conclusions based on uncertainty, raising the question of how certain one
should be before taking (and how to take) decisive actions. This calls for an approach
that can address the major uncertainties, but also can be applied to real life cases by
practitioners. It is known that conclusions are subject to high uncertainty, but it calls
for political will to implement them quantitatively into the current risk assessment
and site-management practices.

Chapter 4
Geostatistics in Environmental Modelling
This chapter gives an introduction to some fundamentals of geostatistics. Initially the
variogram will be introduced, which lays the foundation for the applied interpolation
techniques. This is followed by an introduction to ordinary kriging and conditional
Monte Carlo simulation. Ultimately the Log-likelihood function used for optimisation
in Chapter 5 is presented.
4.1
Geostatistics - An Introduction
A common feature of environmental variables is that they often display a degree of
positive auto-correlation in space (i.e. locations in close vicinity tend to have similar
values, and ones farther apart on average diﬀer more) (Webster and Oliver, 2007). En-
vironmental investigations are often limited by a ﬁnite number of parameter samples,
which leads to the need of estimating or predicting the spatial distribution and variabil-
ity of the parameters in un-sampled regions. This is often done by taking advantage
of this auto-correlation in the interpolation (Webster and Oliver, 2007). Geostatistics
is closely related to other interpolation methods, but geostatistical techniques extend
far beyond the simple interpolation problem by considering the variable of concern
at unmeasured locations to be a set of correlated random variables. Unlike standard
interpolation methods, geostatistics is based on statistical models, and can therefore
model the uncertainty associated with the spatial estimation and simulation of a given
variable.
In geostatistics, the sampled data are usually assumed to originate from a multivariate
Gaussian space function. This means that the value of the variable at all unsampled
locations is assumed random and described by a normal distribution. It is furthermore
commonly assumed that this space function is ﬁrst- and second order stationary. First-
order stationarity entails the global mean of the observed data within the domain to
be constant and independent of sampling location and size of the domain (Sidler and
Holliger, 2012). Second order stationarity means that the the domain solely depends
on the distance between the observations and not their absolute position. The esti-
mates are naturally subject to errors, but geostatistics gives the possibility of assigning
uncertainties and conﬁdence levels to parameter predictions of the investigated values
(Kitanidis, 1999; Webster and Oliver, 2007).
==PAGE:44==
24
The ﬁeld of geostatistics consist of a variety of statistical tools, but for this study, an
overview of the following three components will be presented:
• The (Semi) Variogram: Spatial interdependence of measured data.
• Kriging: Linear interpolation for prediction of values in unsampled regions using
a ﬁtted variogram function.
• Conditional simulation: Generation of a large number of equally probable reali-
sations of the spatial variability of a given variable, all conformed to the sampled
measurements using the constructed variogram model.
The aim of this project is to determine the uncertainty of CMD estimation through a
transect. In order to do so, a conditional Monte Carlo simulation of concentration and
hydraulic conductivity along the transect is performed. To perform the Monte Carlo
simulation, the C and K values (or the transformation of these) are assumed to follow a
multivariate Gaussian distribution (a stationary stochastic Gaussian process). Where
a univariate normal distribution is described by two parameters; mean and standard
deviation, the multivariate normal distribution is described by a mean-vector and a
covariance matrix, which needs to be determined in order to generate Monte Carlo
simulations from this multivariate normal distribution. This is done through an ex-
planatory and structural data-analysis, i.e. ﬁtting a so-called experimental variogram
to the data. The theoretical principles of constructing an experimental variogram is
presented in Section 4.2 and an combined explanatory data-analysis and variogram ﬁt-
ting is presented through a step-wise analysis using data from Industrivej in Chapter
5.
4.2

compared to points distanced further apart (Armstrong, 1998). As stated in Isaaks
and Srivastava (1989) the semivariance, γ(h), is half the average squared diﬀerence
between paired data points and is shown in Equation 4.1:

can be computed, which constitutes the experimental variogram (Webster and Oliver,

distances. The semi-variance for a speciﬁed lag-bin is determined by grouping each

experimental variogram is thereby the average variance of all data pairs within the
determined lags, and illustrates the variance between measured values as a function
of distance (and direction) between these values. If there is a spatial correlation in
data, a lower variance would be seen between measurements that are closely spaced
and correspondingly an increasing variance with increasing distance between measure-
ments. One of the advantages of describing the spatial relationship of data through
the semi-variogram, is that when two points do not coincide, their covariance depends
on their separation distance only and not their absolute positions, which applies to
any pair of points within the separation distance h (Webster and Oliver, 2007).

Sill
The sill denotes the maximum variation and is in principle the variation between
observations, which are not spatially correlated (Webster and Oliver, 2007). The sill is
thereby the limiting value of the variance, and the estimated value will approach the
global mean with the sill as the variance when moving away from measured points.
Range
The distance where the variogram reaches its limiting value, the sill, and where sam-
ples are no longer spatially correlated, is called the range (Armstrong, 1998). Values
lower than the range are thus spatially auto-correlated, and if the distance between
samples are larger than the range-value, the observations are (typically) not correlated.
Increasing the lag will therefore not result in a corresponding increase in the variogram
(Isaaks and Srivastava, 1989; Webster and Oliver, 2007).
The range of a variable can depend on the direction of the variogram, reﬂected by
the anisotropy, ϕ (Kitanidis, 1999). An isotropic model does not change with direc-
tion, meaning that the variogram solely depends on the separation distance (lag) h
(i.e. the variance between two samples is the same in both horizontal and vertical
direction). However, the directional dependence of variables are well documented (Ki-
tanidis, 1999), with an example being the hydraulic conductivity (e.g Sudicky (1986)
or Bjerg et al. (1992)). In geologic formations with units formed by glacial deposits of
layers of gravel, sand, clay and silt, the layers of the diﬀerent materials are typically
oriented in the horizontal direction; as a result, the variance of hydraulic conductivity
measured at two points at a short distance h in the horizontal direction is typically
expected to be smaller than the variance for the same distance in the vertical direction
(Kitanidis, 1999). This spatial orientation of the variation in correlation distance is
reﬂected by the anisotropy parameter.
Nugget
In theory the variance at lag distance h = 0 should be zero. A discontinuity at the
origin is called nugget, and is a variable indicating erratic short scale behaviour (Isaaks
and Srivastava, 1989). It states a degree of initial variation, or the variability between
closely spaced samples. In practice, when designing the measurements of a sampling
campaign, the chosen design speciﬁes a single measurement at a number of various
locations. Here the nugget eﬀect can have a dual interpretation as either (or both)
measurement error, or spatial variation on a distance smaller than the smallest distance
between any sampling points in the sample design (Diggle et al., 2007).

Kriging
Kriging can be used to estimate the spatial distribution of a variable in un-sampled
regions, using the existing knowledge of the spatial variation of the variable gained
through the variogram model to interpolate between sample locations (Kitanidis,
1999). A profusion of kriging methods exist (Webster and Oliver, 2007; Armstrong,
1998). As ordinary kriging is the most widely used kriging method (Abzalov, 2016;
Wackernagel, 1995) and the one used in this project, a short introduction will be given.

4.4
Conditional Simulation and Stochastic Modelling
Conditional simulation (or stochastic imaging), is a stochastic simulation process,
where equally probable realisations of a spatial random function are created with
each realisation honouring the discrete data values at speciﬁc locations or geometric
patterns, i.e. each realisation is ’conditioned’ on the data. The objective is to esti-
mate a ﬁeld variable (in this case C, K and ultimately CMD) over a region. However,
because of scarcity of information, it is not possible to ﬁnd a unique solution. The
actual unknown ﬁeld variable can be seen as one out of an ensemble (a collection) of
possibilities (Kitanidis, 1999). This ensemble of possibilities, or realisations, are re-
alised from a normal distribution of the given parameter interval, and thereby deﬁnes
all possible solutions of the estimation.
Assuming the variable of concern (i.e. C or K) is a stationary spatial process deﬁned by
a multivariate Gaussian distribution with known structural parameters, it is possible to
generate unconditional random simulations of that variable over the domain (here: the
transect). Unconditional simulation is however not conditioned to the observations,
but generates the basis for conditional simulation.
The principle of conditional simulation can be seen as a process where the uncondi-
tioned simulations are adjusted to ﬁt the measured data, so the simulated values at
each location agrees with the measured value. This is done using Equation 4.7:

The result of kriging is the best estimate of the variable (i.e. the mean) and the asso-

If the variable is assumed to be from a (multivariate) normal distribution, the result
of kriging can also be said to be a normal distribution. Each simulation point will
therefore be a result of this probability distribution with the estimated value equal
to the mean of the spatial process and the given standard deviation of the variable.
Conditional simulation is N number of random realisations of this normal distribution.
A kriging interpolation will thereby result in one estimation of the variable in each
grid point, whereas conditional simulation will give an ensemble of estimations of the
variable at each point. The mean of conditional simulations will approach the kriged
mean value, when increasing the number of conditional simulations.

Chapter 5
Geostatistical Method for Uncertainty
Estimation in CMD
This chapter presents the developed geostatistical method for quantiﬁcation of contam-
inant mass discharge uncertainty. The method has been developed using the theoretical
principles presented in Chapter 4, using a mixture of conditional simulation and or-
dinary kriging. The method will be simultaneously presented and validated through a
stepwise analysis of the contaminated site Industrivej. In Chapter 6, the method will
be applied at two additional contaminated sites, in order to test the robustness and
potential of the method, at two sites with diﬀerent conditions.
5.1
Overview and Method Assumptions
In the following, a geostatistical method for quantifying the CMD uncertainty in a
transect located downstream a contaminated site, using a set of MLS measurements of
hydraulic conductivity and contaminant concentration is presented. Figure 5.1 shows
an overview of the method. The method assumes that both the hydraulic conductivity
and the contaminant concentration along the transect are spatial random processes
that can be described using a geostatistical model. The method is divided into three
overall steps. In Step 1 the explanatory data analysis is performed, which aims to
determine a suitable transformation and spatial correlation structure (i.e. variogram)
of the variable of concern based on the available observations from the MLS transect.
In Step 2 the method generates equally likely realisations of the spatial distribution of
the concentration and the hydraulic conductivity across the transect using conditional
simulation. These simulations are then combined in Step 3 using equation 2.2 yielding
an ensemble of mass discharge simulations, from which a CMD probability distribution
can be derived.
The advantage of this method is that it explicitly accounts for the spatial heterogeneity
of the investigated variable (for interpolation), and estimates both the most likely
spatial distribution of the variable, and the uncertainty of this estimated distribution.
The method is based on the following assumptions:
• The spatial distributions of hydraulic conductivity and concentration along the
transect are independent. A degree of complex statistical dependence is likely to
exist, but estimation of a such relationship is not straightforward and would re-
quire either a very strong data set and/or heavy computational eﬀorts (Troldborg
==PAGE:54==
34
et al., 2012). In case of a signiﬁcant cross-correlation the two distributions would
then have to be co-simulated using a co-kriging approach (Kitanidis, 1999). This
is beyond the scope of this project and will not be addressed further.
• The heterogeneous distribution of the parameters along the transect is assumed
to be stationary stochastic spatial processes.
• The concentration measurements are subject to uncertainties, which are assumed
normal distributed with the standard deviation σm increasing linearly with the
measured concentration Cmeas:
σm = Eabs + Erel · Cmeas
(5.1)
Where Eabs is the absolute measurement error standard deviation and can be
seen as the uncertainty on the measurements below detection limit while Erel
is the relative uncertainty on the measurement itself. The method is currently
testet with an absolute error equal to 0.1 µg/L, as the data is not strong enough
to determine a clear trend in the variability of measurements. The relative un-
certainty of chemical analyses for chlorinated ethenes and similar compounds are
set to 20% as determined by the analytical laboratory performing the analyses.
• The uncertainty of the hydraulic conductivity is embedded in the uncertainty
given the conditional simulation.
For stronger data sets an uncertainty can
be assigned to the conductivity data similar to done with the concentration
measurements.
The method has been developed using the programming language and open-source soft-
ware environment for statistical computing, R (Homepage: https://www.r-project.
org/), using the integrated development environment RStudio (Homepage: https:
//rstudio.com/) and the geostatistical package gstat for generation of unconditional
random ﬁelds (Pebesma, 2004). The Industrivej transect is 91.8 m wide with a height
of 12 m and has been discritised into grid-points of 0.1 m×0.1 m = 0.01 m2. From Front
et al. (2019) the overall gradient is set to 0.0025 m/m and constant for all calculations.
Validation of Gaussian Data
A range of statistical hypothesis test can be applied in order to validate a Gaussian
distribution (Kitanidis, 1999); chi-square and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests are gen-
eral tests that can be applied to any distribution, but the Shapiro-Wilks test have been
developed speciﬁcally for the normal distribution, and has been chosen as the statisti-
cal test for this method. The Shapiro–Wilks Test was originally developed by Shapiro
and Wilk (1965) and tests the null hypothesis, that the observations were sampled in-
dependently from a normally distributed population. Thereby if the p-value is larger
then the chosen α-level of 0.05, the null-hypothesis cannot be rejected, indicating the
observations indeed are sampled from a normally distributed population. The test is
elaborated in Appendix C.

5.5
Method Summary and Novelties
A geostatistical method for quantifying the uncertainty in CMD has been developed
and presented in this Chapter. The method was applied to Industrivej and was found
to fully determine the CMD uncertainty at the site. The method uses a combination of
Box-Cox transformation and a spatially varying mean given by an analytical solution of
a macrodispersive transport equation. This ensures stationary and Gaussian data, and
furthermore remove the possibility of estimating negative concentrations as reported
by Cai et al. (2011), whom uses geostatistical simulation directly on un-transformed
concentration data without accounting for trends.
To determine the optimal set of analytical transport model- and geostatistical pa-
rameters the method applies a general-purpose optimisation algorithm integrated into
Rstudio to ﬁt the parameters jointly. This approach still takes advantage of a joint
concentration- and spatial correlation parameter-ﬁtting, but avoid the considerably
more advanced model-based Bayesian approach using an acceptance-rejection sam-
pling algorithm as applied by Troldborg et al. (2012).
The developed method assumes the aquifer heterogeneity is solely represented by the
interpolation of the hydraulic conductivity along the transect, which signiﬁcantly sim-
pliﬁes the simulation of ﬂow-ﬁeld properties. This simpliﬁcation is acknowledged to
be a large source of uncertainty in the developed method, as the co-simulation of ﬂow
properties is reported to constrain the CMD uncertainty in Troldborg et al. (2012).
This simpliﬁcation however is believed to greatly increase the application possibility,
as it avoids the need for integrating complex regional ﬂow-models.
A major advantage of the developed method is the considerable decrease in com-
putational requirements. Troldborg et al. (2010) condition the CMD simulations to
measured data, conceptual uncertainty and the variability imposed by the physics of
ﬂow and contaminant transport, which is reported very computationally demanding.
Troldborg et al. (2012) report the high simulation time to mainly be a result of the
co-simulation of hydraulic head and conductivity. When the spatial correlation is de-
termined, 500 conditional simulations of concentration and hydraulic conductivity as
well as 250,000 realisations of the CMD distribution could all be computed within an
hour for the Industrivej transect on a standard laptop (Windows 10, Intel® Core™i7-
7500 processor, 8GB RAM and 64 bit operating system). This is naturally depending
on the grid-size (i.e. de facto the size of the transect).

Summary
The further development of the method for uncertainty estimation in CMD was found
to fully delineate the contaminant distribution at the Lundtoftevej transect. It was
possible to construct a satisfactory variogram-model to describe the spatial correlation
of the data, and decreasing the residual variance by a third compared to the original
method. The reported mean CMD along the transect was found to be higher compared
to the result by Jørgensen and Christensen (2019), but still within the 95% conﬁdence
interval (but not the standard deviation).

6.3
Method Applicability
By comparing the results from Lundtoftevej and Vejlegårdsvej, which both used a
constant hydraulic conductivity ﬁeld, the importance of a site characterisation fully
delineating the plume is evident. In both cases the uncertainty is solely a result of the
concentration distribution of the transect, but the relative uncertainty at Vejlegårdsvej
is higher. As described in Section 6.1 the plume is not assumed to be fully delineated
in the Vejlegårdsvej transect, which thereby results in the high uncertainty.
This
indicates that to reduce the CMD uncertainty, the most important parameter to sample
is the concentration distribution, in order to fully outline the plume extent. It should
however be stressed that this is only valid if the ﬂow ﬁeld parameters can be determined
to a satisfactory degree; either in terms of applying regions with constant hydraulic
conductivity (as done at Lundtoftevej) or assuming a relative homogeneous ﬂow ﬁeld
for the entire transect (as done to some extent at Vejlegårdsvej).
Obtaining useful variogram-models for the sites was generally seen as the biggest
challenge in the method application.
For all sites the spatial correlation distance
was ﬁtted to be relatively long. Nothing necessarily makes it less right to have a very
high correlation distance in environmental investigations, but general knowledge of
subsurface ﬂow-paths and the site in general, makes it highly unlikely that measured
concentrations 353 m apart should be correlated 350 m downstream the source (it
would e.g. require very high dispersivities) as seen for Lundtoftevej in Section 6.2.1.
Though it should be noted that the variograms depict the spatial correlation for the
transformed concentration residuals, and not the concentration measurements itself.
The ﬁtted correlation length was generally seen to be very data-dependent. The ﬁt-
ting of Vejlegårdsvej determined a range of 68 m. The transect is delimited by few
measurements, and the ones outlining the plume span approximately 60 m, so in order
for the algorithm to properly optimise the ﬁt, it correspondingly assigns longer corre-
lation distances. Even though a large dataset exist for Lundtoftevej, the correlation
distances are also long (especially for the automatic ﬁtting), which is a result of the
boreholes being located far apart. It should however be remembered that the ﬁtted
variogram-models (normally) ensure that data in short distances is weighted the most.

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter compares CMD estimations using the developed method with the values
reported by practitioners as presented in Chapter 3. The applicability of the method will
be discussed. Limitations and possible method improvements are presented alongside
a comparison with uncertainty estimation methods presented in published literature.
Additionally a conceptual framework for method implementation is presented.
7.1
CMD Estimations and Site Comparisons
The developed method has been shown to be applicable on all sites reviewed in this
project; densely sampled C/K measurements (Industrivej), sparsely sampled concen-
tration measurements and constant K-ﬁeld (Vejlegårdsvej) and an extensively sampled
transect with two source zones and regions of constant ﬂow properties (Lundtoftevej).
The method was found applicable at Vejlegårdsvej, a site with only 15 concentration
measurements along a transect of 130 m, but gave the most convincing result when
using larger (Lundtoftevej) or more dense (Industrivej) datasets.
Table 7.1 shows an overview of the estimated mean, standard deviation, relative un-
certainty and conﬁdence intervals of the CMD estimations used in this study, along

this study are highlighted with bold font.
For none of the three reviewed sites the risk assigned by the Region or practitioners
towards water abstractions would change based on the mean CMD estimations cal-
culated using the developed method. If the most conservative CMD estimate (upper
limit of the 95% conﬁdence interval) of 296.7 g/yr TCE found at Industrivej would be
diluted in Bogøgård water abstraction located nearby (Table 3.3), the concentration
would increase from 0.079 µg/L to 0.16 µg/L, which would still be below the MAC.
Applying the method on Industrivej therefore enables decision-makers to take actions
from a precautionary approach, as even in the most conservative estimate, taking all
uncertainties into account, the MAC will not be exceeded. On the contrary, looking
at a ﬁctitious water abstraction of 100,000 m3/yr, the concentration would be in the
range of 0.8-3 µg/L. Which implies a high risk of exceeding the MAC if an abstraction
well was to be installed immediately downstream Industrivej.

be classiﬁed as High Risk towards a potential future abstractions of 100,000 m3/yr
even for the lower 5%-percentile CMD estimation. Thereby indicating that using the
least conservative estimate, the contamination at both sites would still be determined

30%, signiﬁcantly reducing computational time and only inducing minor alterations
in the estimated CMD. This also decreased the uncertainty span of the standard de-
viation and the 95% conﬁdence interval, which is again the result of the uncertainty
solely being a product of the concentration uncertainty. Such simpliﬁcation can be jus-

ﬁeld in Figure 5.13. The low σ2

When planning a sampling campaign, it would therefore be most cost-eﬃcient to in-
crease the number of concentration measurements to fully delineate the plume extent
and decrease the hydraulic conductivity measurements. Enough ﬂow-measurements
should however be conducted in order to determine a representative σ2
lnK-value for
the transect; to either deﬁne a mean hydraulic conductivity of the transect or outline
transitions between high- and low-permeability layers. This conclusion is thereby only
valid for moderately homogeneous aquifers such as the one found at Industrivej, or for
transects were it has been possible to delineate sections with equivalent ﬂow-properties
as was done for Lundtoftevej.
The relative uncertainty is found to be in the range of what has been reported in other
studies for similar sampling densities. Troldborg et al. (2010) found a relative uncer-
tainty of 68% for a sampling density of 0.02 points/m2, where the data is conditioned to
measurements outside the transect (and on the boundary conditions). Troldborg et al.
(2012) reports a relative uncertainty of 74% for the lowest sampling density of 0.05
points/m2. However, both studies interpolate the hydraulic conductivity ﬁeld from a
considerably more heterogeneous aquifer (σ2
lnK ∼2 and 1.4 respectively), compared
to this study (σ2
lnK = 0.34). Cai et al. (2011) determine a low relative uncertainty of
19% for a sampling density of 0.63 points/m2, and assumes a constant ﬂow ﬁeld, hence
not considering the impact of a spatially varying ﬂow ﬁeld on the CMD uncertainty
estimation.
They however use geostatistical simulation directly on un-transformed
concentration data without accounting for trends, and reports the major drawback to
be the possibility of the conditional simulation to yield estimates with negative concen-
trations. Balbarini et al. (2018) compares CMD using concentration-based (ordinary
kriging) and geophysics-based (regression kriging) geostatistical interpolation of vinyl
chloride and chloride downstream a landﬁll, and reports the relative uncertainty to be
inversely proportional to the sample density. Balbarini et al. (2018) ﬁnds a relative
uncertainty of ∼50% at the lowest analysed sample density (0.015 samples/m2) in a
mildly heterogeneous aquifer similar to the one found at Industrivej (σ2
lnK <1). Com-
paring the slightly higher sampling density for Industrivej with the correspondingly
slightly lower relative uncertainty, the results are determined comparable.

7.2
Method Applicability
As presented in Chapter 5 and Section 6.1, the developed method was found to prop-
erly outline the contaminant distribution when one source is present in the transect.
The automatic parameter ﬁtting could however not determine an acceptable spatial
correlation when multiple source-zones were present as seen for Lundtoftevej.
The
importance of properly deﬁning the spatial relationship in data was simultaneously
demonstrated at Lundtoftevej, as the simulated concentration distribution here over-
laps with the high-conductivity zones. The method was therefore further developed
to incorporate two plumes by using the law of superposition. From this follows, that
in principle no upper limit of plumes exists, and one can tailor the method to a given
site to reﬂect more complex source zone situations. This however also increases the
number of parameters connected to the ﬁtting of the transport model.
For all sites, the variogram ﬁtting was found to be challenging, and most variograms
illustrate that the accuracy of the ﬁtted variogram-models in short distances is uncer-
tain. In most sampling campaigns carried out by practitioners, the goal is to delineate
the contaminant distribution using the least amount of resources. It is therefore sel-
domly prioritised to locate multiple sampling points within close proximity of each
other. This lack of data within short distances complicates the process of identifying
short-scale spatial variation. This short-scale variation is however partly described by
the measurement error in the method, as it suppresses a nugget eﬀect in the variogram-
ﬁtting. Site-speciﬁc variograms for the contaminant and hydraulic conductivity distri-
bution should in general not be compared, as they are very site-dependent in terms of
the source, contaminant type and the sampling campaign and method. The variogram
models are currently investigated in the horizontal and vertical directions, but nothing
hinders the possibility of investigating multiple directions for a more detailed analysis
of spatial correlation. If detailed geologic knowledge of the investigated site exists (e.g.
sub-vertical high-permeability layers), then this can be incorporated into the ﬁtting
of variogram-parameters. Additionally, the uncertainty related to the eﬀect of ﬁtting
geostatistical parameters was not included in the study. How diﬀerent ranges or sill-
values aﬀect the estimated CMD would therefore be recommended before a potential
future method implementation.
Decreasing the number of parameters for ﬁtting could potentially increase the per-
formance of the optimisation algorithm, as the chance of the function determining
multiple optimums generally increases, for an increasing number of ﬁtting-parameters.
The method was initially developed including four source-zone parameters in the Log-
likelihood optimisation (thereby resulting in a total of 11 parameters for ﬁtting). The
performance was however found to signiﬁcantly increase when ﬁxing individual source-
zone parameters (reducing the total number of parameters to seven). For sites with
in-depth knowledge of source and aquifer properties, it could therefore be beneﬁcial
to determine as many transport-model parameters as possible before optimisation is
performed. This also holds for the geostatistical parameters, where specifying e.g. the
anisotropy could remove a ﬁtting-parameter. The signiﬁcance of removing each ﬁtting
parameter from the optimisation was however beyond the scope of this project.

7.3 Lessons Learned and Method Implementation
Comparing the estimated mean CMD with that reported in literature (Table 7.1) the
method can be seen to estimate CMD in the same order of magnitude for Industrivej
and Vejlegårdsvej, but approximately twice as high for Lundtoftevej. The advantage of
this method is however the possibility of assigning uncertainties to the estimate. The
reported CMD by Jørgensen and Christensen (2019) for Lundtoftevej is interpolated
using a mixture of subjective choices and an adapted nearest-neighbour approach, and
each grid is assigned a concentration incorporating nearby PID- and/or soil measure-
ments combined with a general knowledge of the site. A limitation of the developed
method is therefore the current lack of possibility to integrate subjective choices re-
sulting from in-depth knowledge of a site. An example being the limited extent of
contamination into the clay-aquifer seen for Lundtoftevej, which the geostatistical in-
terpolation does not account for. Such boundary conditions could however easily be
implemented, in the same way the hydraulic conductivity-ﬁeld was divided into high-
and low hydraulic conductivity regions for Lundtoftevej.
For Vejlegårdsvej the method was found to properly reproduce the MTBE distribution
similar to what is depicted in Boe et al. (2019) from a limited amount of samples. The
relative uncertainty is however accordingly high, and the plume extent is governed
by the analytical trend solution, which is correspondingly governed by very few high-
concentration measurements. The amount of measurements available at Vejlegårdsvej
was thereby found to be at the method application limit.
7.3
Lessons Learned and Method Implementation
Overall the method can be seen as a simpliﬁcation of more advanced (i.e. assumed
more precise) methods to evaluate the CMD at contaminated sites, such as Troldborg
et al. (2010) or Troldborg et al. (2012). The developed method should therefore be
seen as a link between these more advanced methods, and what is currently practised.
Even though it is deemed considerably more advanced compared to a Theissen-polygon
interpolation, it still proved relatively simple to adapt the method, and apply it on
locations with very diﬀerent site-characteristics as shown in Chapters 5 and 6.
An essential lesson gained during the work with this project, for future possible im-
plementation of the method, is the diversity of how data is reported by diﬀerent
practitioners. Although the required input-data follows a very simple pattern (mea-
sured values along a x/y grid), it was found very time consuming to adapt reported
data, into something that could be used for geostatistical analysis and interpolation.
Additionally there are large diﬀerences how the CMD approach is incorporated into
the risk assessment of the sites among Regions and practitioners.
Considering that the developed method was successfully tested on sites, where the
risk assessment practice outlined in Section 2.1 is implemented, this study found the
geostatistical method to be applicable as a risk-assessment tool assigning uncertainties
to CMD estimations. Incorporating the developed method would give decision-makers
the possibility of integrating the spatial variability which is incontestably present at the
majority of contaminated sites, when taking decisive actions. It is acknowledged that
==PAGE:98==
78
implementing uncertainties into the rigid interval limits shown in Tables 2.1 and 2.2,
which currently governs CMD practice in The Capital Region, will impose considerable
challenges and require a signiﬁcant change of management practice. Given the vast
amount of contaminated sites in Denmark, the method should only be applied on
sites which are V2 surveyed and with a reasonable suspicion of a CMD imposing a
risk. However, considering the recent allocation of more resources for remediation of

performance.

CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK
Figure 7.1: Conceptual understanding of the potential method implementation on
a site-investigation.
The concept consists of four overall steps: (1) Initially the sampling campaign should
deﬁne a transect consisting of concentration and hydraulic conductivity measurements
used for interpolation; (2) subsequently the spatial relationship must be analysed (step
1 in Figure 5.1) before; (3) the contaminant or hydraulic conductivity distribution is
==PAGE:99==
7.3 Lessons Learned and Method Implementation
interpolated along the transect (step 2 in Figure 5.1). If the results from (3) does
not return acceptable results, the cycle starts over with additional samples taken in
the transect. This can be repeated until a satisfactory interpolation is obtained, after
which the cycle breaks, and ultimately; (4) the CMD and corresponding uncertainty
is calculated (step 3 in Figure 5.1).
An advantage of the developed method is thereby the possibility to continuously update
the CMD estimation. From additional measurements collected on a given location,
the ensemble of conditional simulations of hydraulic conductivity and contaminant
concentration distribution can be directly updated cf. Equation 4.7. Additionally the
method can provide an indication of where to place new boreholes and measurements
in order to reduce the uncertainty of the CMD estimation. Thereby, if a given location
displays a large range on the CMD-estimate, and as a result would be placed in multiple
risk-categories (cf. Table 2.1), the developed method can be used to indicate where to
place additional measurements in order to reduce the uncertainty the most.
How minimisation of structural uncertainty by optimisation of site investigations on
the basis of geostatistical modelling is further discussed in Nowak et al. (2010), but is
beyond the scope of this project. It could however provide the foundation for future
method development.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The developed geostatistical method for quantifying the CMD uncertainty in a tran-
sect located downstream a contaminated site, using a set of MLS measurements of
hydraulic conductivity and contaminant concentration, has been shown to fully deter-
mine the CMD uncertainty and be applicable on all test-sites in this project. Box-Cox
transforming residuals generated using a spatially varying mean given by an ana-
lytical solution of a macrodispersive transport equation, ensured Gaussian data and
non-negativity.
The method explicitly accounts for the spatial heterogeneity of the interpolated vari-
able, and estimates both the most likely spatial distribution and corresponding uncer-
tainty. The method is divided into three overall steps; (1) explanatory data analysis
for determining the suitable transformation and spatial correlation structure in the
observations; (2) generation of equally likely realisations of the spatial distribution of
the interpolated value across the transect using conditional simulation and ﬁnally; (3)
combination of the conditional simulations into an ensemble of CMD simulations used
to express the full CMD probability distribution.
Site conditions ranged from fully delineated by C/K measurements, a sparse concen-
tration dataset with a constant hydraulic conductivity ﬁeld to an extensively sampled
transect with regions of constant ﬂow properties. The method was found applicable
to Vejlegårdsvej, a site with sampling density of 0.005 n/m2 and an uncertain plume-
delineation, but correspondingly gave the highest relative uncertainty of 48%. Using
larger or more dense datasets was found to reduce the uncertainty. Industrivej had
the highest sampling density of 0.024 n/m2 which simultaneously reﬂected the lowest
relative uncertainty of 36%. The relative uncertainty could be further constrained to
30% by applying a constant hydraulic conductivity ﬁeld along the transect, and only
induce minor alterations in the CMD estimation. This was justiﬁable based on the
moderately homogeneous aquifer found at the site. The lowest sampling density of
0.003 n/m2 was found at Lundtoftevej. The large amount of concentration measure-
ments and proper plume-delineation however constrained the relative uncertainty to
38%, which illustrates the importance of outlining the plume extent before geostatis-
tical (or other) interpolation should be performed. The relationship between relative
uncertainty and sampling density was found to be in accordance with similar studies.
The method was furthermore found to delineate where the mobile contaminant mass
is highest, and can therefore also be applied to maximise eﬀect of remedial actions in
a transect.
By assuming the aquifer heterogeneity to be represented solely by the interpolation
of the hydraulic conductivity along the transect, the method could be considerably
simpliﬁed compared to similar studies. This simpliﬁcation is acknowledged to be a
==PAGE:102==
82
source of uncertainty in the method, but simultaneously found to be how practitioners
currently deﬁne ﬂow conditions in CMD estimations. Constraining the ﬂow-properties
to a 2D domain furthermore signiﬁcantly decreased computational requirements, en-
abling the generation of 250,000 realisations of CMD distributions within an hour using
a standard laptop.
The method is overall consistent with what has been estimated by practitioners, but
additionally provides both a standard deviation and conﬁdence intervals for the esti-
mated CMD. For Industrivej a CMD of 160 g/yr TCE ± 56.9 g/yr was determined,
which is in agreement with the CMD of 143 g/yr TCE estimated by Front et al. (2019).
The method determined a CMD of 16.3 kg/yr MTBE ±7.8 kg/yr for Vejlegårdsvej,
and is in the range of the estimated value of 17.3 kg/yr MTBE in Boe et al. (2019).
To account for the existence of two plumes at Lundtoftevej, the method was further
developed to include multiple source zones using superposition of the analytical trend
model. The altered method reduced the residual variance to a third, and rendered
the possibility of deﬁning a spatial relationship to be used for conditional simulation
of the contaminant distribution. This resulted in an estimated CMD of 40.3 kg/yr
sum chlorinated ± 15.3 kg/yr, which is considerably higher compared to the 23 kg/yr
estimated by Jørgensen and Christensen (2019). For none of the three test-sites, the
risk assigned would change based on the CMD estimated using the develop method.
Using the most conservative estimate for Industrivej, and diluting the 95%-quantile
into Bogøgård water abstraction, would still result in a concentration below the MAC.
Correspondingly diluting the 5%-quantile in a potential future water abstraction at all
three sites would classify them as High Risk cf. The Region’s risk-categorisation.
This study concluded, that to maximise the eﬀect of resource allocation in site in-
vestigations, it is essential to properly outline the plume extent from concentration
measurements, as this was found to be the governing parameter for constraining un-
certainty in the CMD estimation. This conclusion is however only valid for moderate
homogeneous aquifers such as the one found at Industrivej. No test-sites in this project
showed signiﬁcant heterogeneous properties, or had enough measurements in order to
challenge this conclusion by testing the method on a more heterogeneous aquifer.
A conceptual framework for method-implementation has been proposed. The concept
follows a simple four-step model, and can be repeated until a satisfactory parameter
interpolation and CMD estimation is obtained. This framework further demonstrated
how the method can be applied as an indicator, of where to locate additional mea-
surements to decrease the uncertainty span of the CMD estimation.
Based on the successful application of the developed geostatistical method on the
three test-sites presented in this project, the method is found to be applicable as an
advanced tool in the risk-assessment practice of contaminated sites in Denmark. It is
acknowledged that changing the current practice will impose considerable challenges
and require a signiﬁcant change of management practices on both Regional and Na-
tional scale.
The method however provides decision-makers with the possibility of
integrating the spatial variability and corresponding uncertainty of CMD estimations,
when taking decisive actions about potential remedial actions.

Appendix A
Reviewed Sites
This Appendix presents the reviewed sites in the project where the developed method
has not been tested on. Additionally enlarged versions of Figures presented in Chapter
3 are presented.
A.1
Industriparken
Industriparken is prioritised for remediation, but awaits nearby investigations to be
concluded for potential remedial actions. If an area contains multiple contaminated
sites requiring remediation in order to protect an abstraction site, it can result in the
area getting a lower priority (Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020). The study site is
described in detail in (Hindrichsen and Møller, 2018).
Industriparken is located in the north-northwestern part of Zealand, Denmark in an
industrial area with several other V2 surveyed sites in an area with high drinking water
interest. An oxygen- and hydrogen factory have had multiple industrial activities at
the location from 1960-1992, where the buildings where torn down and replaced with
administrative buildings. The site is heavily contaminated with chlorinated solvents,
and delineated by an extensive sampling campaign of pore-air, soil-, groundwater- and
tree-core samples, MiHPT-sounding and hydraulic tests. Figure A.1 illustrates the
conceptual understanding of the sum chlorinated solvents contamination at Industri-
parken through the E-W transect. The contamination is primarily situated below the
building and just east of it. There is a high vertical hydraulic gradient from the upper
secondary aquifer in the till, towards the lower primary sandy aquifer, and the con-
tamination is deemed to migrate vertically downward, into the regional sandy aquifer.
Traces of contamination have been detected in the top of the regional aquifer, but is
found to not have fully penetrated the till yet. In the sandy aquifer the ﬂow direction
is south.

A.2
Ørnebjergvej
Ørnebjergvej is an ongoing case. The risk is found too high to close the case, but
too small to remediate (Kerrn-Jespersen and Tuxen, 2020). The case is therefore sent
to the monitoring-team in the Capital Region, meaning it will be monitored over a
period of years to see if the contamination will spread. The study site, investigation
results and conclusions are thoroughly described in Hindrichsen and Munch (2018),
and the potential remediation schemes can be seen in Carlson and Møller (2019),
which describes the considerations about clean-up criteria and potential remediation
techniques.
Ørnebjergvej is located on the northern part of Zealand, Denmark. The contamination
originates from a former dry-cleaning facility which was active in the period 1969-
1988, that used PCE as a cleaning ﬂuid. A number of pore-air, soil and groundwater
analysis have been conducted in order to outline the contamination. Figure A.2 shows
the conceptual understanding of the PCE contamination at Ørnebjergvej along the
N-S transect. As can be seen, the contamination is mainly located in the secondary
aquifer and soil just below the source. The geology and hydrogeology at Ørnebjergvej
is complex, but an overall downward ﬂow direction is seen from the upper secondary
aquifer towards the underlying primary aquifer, and from there down to the regional
limestone aquifer.
The horizontal gradient is generally showing a falling potential
towards north. However, the degree of pavement is very high at the site, which leads
to the ﬂow in the upper secondary aquifer to be very limited and mainly taking place
in sand lenses embedded in the glacial till.
==PAGE:114==
94
Figure A.2: Conceptual understanding of geological and hydrogeological conditions
and migration of PCE in the groundwater along a N-S transect at
Ørnebjergvej. Figure borrowed from Hindrichsen and Munch (2018).
The site is heavily contaminated with PCE which is mainly located in the upper sec-
ondary aquifer, and is not observed to break through to the primary aquifer. Vertical
and horizontal spreading will happen through diﬀusive spreading from concentration
gradients and advective transport with the groundwater, but is deemed to be very
limited as a result of the local low inﬁltration rates and low ﬂow velocities. A verti-
cal downward CMD leaving the source area is estimated to 267 g/yr PCE, but not
found to be a risk towards the local water abstraction, as the contamination have not
reached the primary aquifer. A conservative risk assessment where the total CMD of
267 g/yr PCE will be diluted directly into a future water abstraction of 50,000 m3/yr
will exceed the MAC of 1 µg/L by a factor 5.3, and as a result pose a potential risk
towards a future water abstraction.

The study site, investigation results and conclusions are thoroughly described in Hin-
drichsen and Møller (2020).
Abildgårdsparken is located in the northern part of Zealand, Denmark in an area with
high drinking water interest. The site is contaminated with chlorinated solvents orig-
inating from diﬀerent industrial activities as spray painting of electrical instruments,

A.4
Fabriksvangen
The study site, investigation results and conclusions are thoroughly described in Løj-
mand and Bastrup (2019).
Fabriksvangen is located in the northern part of Zealand, Denmark in an area with
high drinking water interest. The contamination mainly consists of chlorinated solvents
and is believed to originate from storage and production at a former press shop, but
several diﬀerent types of industry have been located at the site. MiHPT and pore-
air measurements were initially conducted in order to delineate the contamination,
followed by a series of soil and groundwater measurements to quantify the extent.
Figure A.4 shows the conceptual ﬁgure of the tetrachloroethylene (PCE) contamination
at Fabriksvangen along the N-S transect. As can be seen the hotspot is located beneath
the building and has not migrated downstream of the site.
Figure A.4: Conceptual understanding of PCE-migration in the groundwater along
a N-S transect at Fabriksvangen. Figure borrowed from Løjmand and
Bastrup (2019).
A small vertical CMD leaving the source area is estimated to 0.001-0.110 g/yr PCE,
but no horizontal CMD is estimated due to low concentrations in screens downstream
the presumed source area. No contamination has been detected in the sandy aquifer,
which is deposited directly upon the regional limestone aquifer constituting the ground-
water resource in the area. Applying the values of Tables 2.1 and 2.2, the detected
contamination at Fabriksvangen is found to not pose a risk towards either the existing
nearby water abstraction site (Hørup Kildeplads) or a potential future abstraction site.

Appendix C
Statistical Testing
This Appendix elaborate the Shapiro-Wilks test used in the project for validation of
Gaussian data.
In principle all data can be kriged, however, the theoretical principles (as described in
Chapter 4) of kriging interpolation assumes the desired variable for interpolation to be
a multivariate Gaussian spatial process. As such, the distribution of the data needs to
follow a Gaussian distribution before optimal kriging-interpolation can be performed
(Kitanidis, 1999; Diggle et al., 2007; Webster and Oliver, 2007).
A range of statistical hypothesis test can be applied in order to validate this Gaussian
distribution (Kitanidis, 1999); chi-square and the Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests are gen-
eral tests that can be applied to any distribution, but the Shapiro-Wilks test have been
developed speciﬁcally for the normal distribution, and has been chosen as the statisti-
cal test for this method. However, all tests can be applied to determine whether data
diﬀers suﬃciently from the null hypothesis, being that the observations are sampled
independently from a normal distribution.
The Shapiro–Wilks Test was originally developed by Shapiro and Wilk (1965) and
tests the null hypothesis, that the observations were sampled independently from a
normally distributed population. The test statistic being:

Where y = (y1,...,yn) denotes a vector of ordered n random observations (i.e. y1 ≤

the test statistic W is smaller than the critical threshold, the assumption of a normal
distribution has to be rejected. In Table 5 in Shapiro and Wilk (1965) threshold values
for n = 50 is tabulated. Originally the test was limited to a maximum sample size
of n = 50. However, this was extended to n = 2000 by Royston (1982), which is the
algorithm integrated into RStudio and used here, but for all sites investigated in this
study the following holds: n < 100.
The test statistic is used to generate the p-value. As the null-hypothesis of the test
is that the observation comes from a normally distributed population, the p-value has
to be less than the chosen α-level in order to reject this null-hypothesis. For p-values
less than this α-level, the hypothesis is rejected, meaning that there is evidence that

the observations are not normally distributed. Similarly, if p is larger than the chosen
α-level, the null-hypothesis cannot be rejected, indicating the observations indeed
are sampled from a normally distributed population. Hence, the lower this value is,
the smaller the chance that the sample comes from a normal distribution. A cutoﬀ
value of 0.05 has been chosen here, i.e. if the p-value is lower than 0.05, the sample
deviates from normality. It should be noted that large p-values do not prove that the
distribution is normal, it is just not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from normal.
As described by Kitanidis (1999), the limitations of the statistical tests for exploratory
data analysis is the assumption that the data was generated independently, thereby
assuming a negligible correlation between observations. Correlation can however be
important sometimes, as two measurements tend to diﬀer by less as the distance be-
tween their locations decreases. Here assuming statistical independence in would not
be valid. However, the Shapiro-Wilks test has been chosen for the method presented
in this project, and will thus be applied continuously throughout the method-testing
to test for normality. This will be done in order to validate (i.e. the test is used as
an indicator) if the data-transformation, generation of residuals and the subsequent
parameter-optimisation ensures a more Gaussian data set, to be used for the following
geostatistical analysis and interpolation.
