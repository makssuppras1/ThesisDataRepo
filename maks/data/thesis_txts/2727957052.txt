Abstract
The world is currently experiencing a demographic shift in which the share of elderly are in-
creasing drastically. This have an impact on nearly all sectors of society, including the need for
adequate senior residences. In the design and construction of senior homes, aspects such as
the ongoing climate change and the building sectors influence are important to consider, as well
as the importance of having an indoor climate that facilities the well-being of senior residents.
Bovieran is a company specialised in the design and construction of senior homes, where the
concept builds on creating a social and including community for seniors. As a part of the com-
pany’s vision, a large atrium is included in the building design to operate as a heated winter
garden to imitate the climate on the French Riviera. The atrium an unique design with plants
and large glazed surfaces, however it is also shown to have a high heating consumption. The
aim of the thesis was therefore to analyse the design and operation of the Bovieran building de-
sign, to minimise energy use while maintaining an adequate indoor climate in the atrium zone.
This thesis was carried out in collaboration with Bovieran Denmark, where the Bovieran building
located in Ishøj was used as the case study. The project is a continuation of two previous master
projects on the subject, and certain materials and results were thereby included in the present
study. This included a reference building model of the Bovieran building design in IDA ICE.
A literature review was carried out to identify design and operational parameters which have
an impact on the energy performance and indoor climate of an atrium building. This included
the evapotranspiration from plants, glazing properties of the facade and roof, and the outdoor
climate conditions.
The first part of the project included a validation process of the building model in IDA ICE.
The materials and measurements provided by Bovieran Ishøj was used to develop the building
model in IDA ICE to simulate the actual building performance. The validated building model was
thereafter evaluated in terms of the building’s sensitivity towards changes in the climate, using
predictions of future climate scenarios in Denmark. As a follow-up on the literature review and
validation process, specific design and operational changes was implemented in the building
model, in an attempt of reducing energy use while maintaining a sufficient indoor climate.
The validated building model was found to correlate with the actual building performance of
Bovieran Ishøj to a great extent. However, deviations in the monthly average relative humidity
levels and hours with overheating were seen. Moreover, it was found that changes in the outdoor
climate could have a major influence on the building performance, both in terms of energy
use by the air handling unit and the thermal comfort in the atrium. The results from analysing
the Bovieran building design showed potential for energy savings, but some of the simulation
scenarios lead to higher indoor air temperatures and more overheating, potentially causing
thermal discomfort for the occupants.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The world is currently experiencing a demographic shift as an increased share of elderly are
seen in most countries in the last decades. An increased life expectancy together with declining
fertility are the key drivers for the ageing population [1]. These changes in the global demo-
graphic impacts nearly all sectors of society, including a growing demand for senior residences.
Senior residences can be defined as homes built to meet certain needs that older people might
have. This can for instance include easy access to elevators, avoidance of obstacles such as
door frames and other adjustments to increase the accessibility in the building.
In the design and construction of senior homes there are however several other aspects to
consider. This include the ongoing climate crisis and the building sectors influence, as well as
the importance of having an adequate indoor climate. The following section present the trends
that the sector of senior residences are currently facing.
1.1.1
Demographic shift
According to data from the United Nations (UN), 25% of the people living in Europe and Northern
America could be aged 65 or older in 2050 [1]. This demographic shift is also seen in Denmark,
where the Danish Ministry of the Interior and Health in 2020 reported of a drastic change in the
number of elderly people in the country. From 2011 to 2020, an increase of 24% was seen in
the number of danish people older than 65 [2].
Having an elderly population causes challenges to the society in several ways. The older people
get, the more vulnerable they are towards illness and injuries. Furthermore, studies have shown
that the elderly often feel more lonely than the younger part of the population. It is reported from
the Danish Ministry of the Interior and Health that about 6% of men and 10% of women over
the age of 65 feel lonely [3]. This can be seen together with the research presented by Ospina
et al [4] focusing on time spent alone across ages. It was reported that older people spend the
most amount of time alone, compared to the rest of the population. Spending time alone does
not necessarily mean that a person is lonely, but in combination with the reported numbers of
loneliness in Denmark, a relation can be seen. It is therefore of great interest for the elderly and
the rest of the society to create senior homes where the social aspect is considered as one of
the focus areas.
1.1.2
Climate change
Climate change refers to the shifts in weather patterns and temperatures over a long-term pe-
riod. These shifts can be caused naturally (activity of the sun or volcanic eruptions) but in the
latest 200 years human activity have been the main drive for climate change. Through burn-
ing of fossil fuel (coal, oil and gas), greenhouse gases (GHG) are emitted to the atmosphere,
where the most influencing GHGs are methane and carbon dioxide. Climate change lead to
higher temperatures on earth and the last decade was reported to be the warmest decade ever
recorded. The changes in temperature are predicted to cause several challenges, such as water
scarcity, rising sea levels, declining biodiversity and more extreme weather.[5]
Research is carried out to predict how the future will look like and several estimations of future
weather scenarios are created, including short-term and long-term predictions. Buildings today
are usually designed using historical weather data to predict a building’s performance. However,
as the climate is expected to change, the future weather scenarios should be taken into account
when designing buildings for the future.

1.1.3
The building sector
The building sector has gained increased attention in the latest decades in terms of the sector’s
potential for energy efficiency as well as as its potential for reducing greenhouse gas emissions.
Regardless of this, UN reported that the building sector reached an all time high in terms of both
energy demand and emissions in 2021 [6]. The sector was then accounting for 34% of the global
energy consumption, where both the energy consumption from residential and non-residential
use were included together with the energy used in the building construction industry [7].
The same tendency was observed in terms of greenhouse gas emissions, where a total of 37%
of the global energy and process related emissions origins from the building sector [7]. These
numbers are obtained despite the increased attention and engagement for the built environment
and its need for improvement to be able to reach the goals of the Paris Agreement.
1.1.4
Building performance and climate change
It is however not only the need for lowering the energy use and GHG emission that is important
in terms of building design and climate change. Buildings should be designed to tackle the
future changes in climate, both when designing new buildings and when renovating existing
building constructions.
Climate change is predicted to cause wetter winters, more heavy rain and a general warmer
climate in Denmark. The Ministry of Environment of Denmark and Environmental Protection
Agency have summarised the main risk and following consequences of climate change on build-
ings, roads and railways [8]. With the prediction of more storms, snow and water encroachment
follows and increased risk of collapse and loss of value. The change in climate is also predicted
to cause deteriorated indoor climate which will influence the overall public health. Several po-
tential adaptions to existing and new buildings are suggested in the report [8], but it is stated
that necessary adaption will only occur if the building regulations and standards are enhanced
to include climate change adaption.
In Denmark today, the building requirements given in BR18 shall be met using a weather file
representing a reference year of weather which is based on historical data [9]. However, the
climate is expected to change and thereby should also the building regulations and requirement
change hereafter.
1.1.5
Indoor climate
The importance of having an adequate indoor climate has been a focus area in society in the
latest years. The recent corona pandemic is one of the reasons, but the fact that people spend a
vast amount of their time indoors has also affected the consciousness on the subject. Research
have shown that the average American spends up to 90% of their time indoors [10], and this
was found to be representative for several European countries. VELUX released in 2019 the
results from a survey, stating that more than half of the respondents spent one hour or less
in nature during a day, which emphasizes the fact that people spend a great amount of time
indoors [11].
This is one of the reasons why the indoor climate is an important area to focus on when designing
buildings. Having a poorly designed indoor environment can cause illness and affect the overall
public health. Some of the most common symptoms related to an inadequate indoor climate
are headaches, irritation of skin, eyes, nose and throat, fatigue and tiredness [12].

Literature review
A literature review was carried out to summarise the existing research on energy use and indoor
climate in atrium buildings to be able to answer the stated research questions. This included
a study on the definition of an atrium space, how the energy use of such spaces have been
investigated in the past as well as a summary of the existing research on evaluating the indoor
climate of an atrium. Moreover, a literature review on the research carried out about the influ-
ence outdoor features, such as plant and ponds, have on the interior space is presented. The
literature review include comments and inputs about the Bovieran building design as well as
findings from the previous projects carried out were it is relevant.
2.1
Definition of an atrium
The inclusion of an atrium or similar spaces have become a popular feature in building design
in the last decades, and it exists several variants and definitions in literature. Examples of
such spaces are winter gardens, semi-outdoor spaces or an atrium. The design, operation and
functioning can vary to a great extent, dependent on the building location and the intended use
of the space.
An atrium can be defined as an large enclosed space, with varying shapes and height. It can be
fully enclosed or partly enclosed by building structures, being protected to outdoor conditions
through glass walls and/or roof.[15, 16]
Tabesh and Sertyesilisik [16] listed four main reasons for including atrium in buildings in their
research; the architectural function, connection to the outdoors, economic function and energy
efficiency. The flexibility of the atrium space is of great value for architects as the space can
be used efficiently for various purposes, such as social gatherings, events or other activities.
Atriums are usually covered with one or several glazed facades and roof, which creates a con-
nection to the outside and makes it possible to include outdoor features inside, such as plants
and ponds. It is nevertheless the energy saving potential that was found to be one of the main
advantages when including an atrium in a building design today.[16]
2.2
Design of an atrium
The design of an atrium is shown through literature to have a great impact on the energy use
and indoor climate. This includes the geometry, the orientation and the type of glazing used in
the roof and/or facade.
2.2.1
Geometry
The geometry of an atrium includes the shape of the space, together with its relation to the
adjacent buildings. Four generic shapes and relations are defined through literature [17], how-
ever classified with various names. Figure 2.1 illustrate the four main types: 1) Centralized, 2)
Semi-enclosed, 3) Attached and 4) Linear, as defined in by Hung et al [17].

Aldawoud [18] investigated the influence of the atrium geometry on the energy performance of
a building, in combination with different climate scenarios and glazing properties. The study
analysed a centralized atrium, by changing the shape and dimensions of the atrium zone. Each
atrium model was simulated with four different glazing types on the atrium skylight, as well
as with variable glazing ratio. Furthermore, the number of floors in the building was changed
together with simulations of four different climate scenarios. The climate scenarios was deter-
mined based on the four primary climatic zones in the US. The concluding results showed that
the energy performance was highly affected by the atrium geometry, however widely dependent
the other modifications where primarily the change in climate was an influencing factor.
The Bovieran atrium design can be classified from Figure 2.1 to be semi-enclosed, as it is sur-
rounded by the three apartment blocks. Moreover, the atrium geometry was analysed by Skotte
and Jespersen [14], where the geometry was changed from semi-enclosed to centralized. This
resulted in reduced heating consumption by the mechanical ventilation system, but a small in-
crease in hours with overheating.
2.2.2
Orientation
The orientation of the atrium decides how the outdoor climate conditions, such as the solar
radiation and wind speed affect the building performance in terms of energy use and indoor
climate. Hung et al [17] state through their study that the location of the atrium should be
dependent on whether the atrium is required to be heated or cooled by the surrounding climate.
The orientation should also be considered in terms of the indoor climate and especially regarding
daylight in the atrium, as the available daylight changes with the atrium’s direction towards the
sun. The influence of atrium orientation was investigated by Vujosevic et al [19], where a hotel
building with an atrium was simulated in the city of Belgrade. Four different orientation was
tested, where the glazed atrium facade was orientated southwest, southeast, northwest and
northeast. The research showed that changing the orientation of the building would change
the solar heat gain and thereby change the energy use for cooling purposes. It is however
important to state that changing the orientation is very location specific, and analysis for each
specific building location should be carried out.
Skotte and Jespersen [14] analysed how changing the orientation of the atrium in Bovieran
Ishøj would influence the energy use and thermal comfort inside the atrium. The atrium is in
reality orientated towards north-east, however no standard orientation is given for the Bovieran
building design. Four various orientations (North, South, East and West) was therefore tested,
where the south-orientated atrium lead to the lowest heating consumption as well as the highest
number of comfort hours.
2.2.3
Glazing
The large glazed facades and roof often implemented in atrium design, influence both the energy
performance and indoor climate. The glazed surfaces are usually included to take advantage
of the daylight, solar heat gain and it creates a potential for natural ventilation.

There has been carried out a great amount of research on the daylight performance in atrium
buildings, as this has been one of the main drivers for implementing the space in the building
design throughout history. Lie et al [20] studied the influence of glazing size, building height
and atrium shape on the daylight performance of a large atrium building. The study showed
that increasing the glazing proportion is an effective way to improve the daylight conditions in
an atrium building, but the visual discomfort increased with increased daylight availability.
The glazing properties are also important to consider in terms of its influence on the thermal
comfort in a building. Suhendri et al [21] investigated different glazing materials for an atrium

included the number of panes and u-value, where a reduced u-value as a result of more panes,

the u-value is a vital factor to evaluate in terms of thermal comfort, but also due to its potential
for energy savings.

It was found that lowering the u-value both reduced the heating consumption and increased the
share of comfort hours.

2.3
Building ventilation system
The main purpose of the building ventilation system is to maintain an acceptable indoor air
quality (IAQ) by providing fresh air or process the indoor air to remove air pollutants. The
ventilation system is at the same time often used for heating and cooling purposes to ensure
thermal comfort and reduce energy consumption in the building. Three types of ventilation
system exists today: natural ventilation, hybrid ventilation and mechanical ventilation.
The Bovieran building design relies on mechanical ventilation for heating the atrium, while the
natural ventilation system is the only means of cooling of the atrium. The mechanical ventilation
system was analysed by Skotte and Jespersen [14], where different operation setpoints of the
air handling unit (AHU) was changed through simulations in IDA ICE. This included the return air
setpoint, various periods of use through changing the heating setpoint as well as implementing
a control option based on the outdoor temperature control.
2.3.1
Natural ventilation
Natural ventilation (NV) is most common to use in buildings with an atrium. NV systems relies
on natural power (passive) driven by the buoyancy force and wind pressure difference, either
separately or in combination. If the ventilation is wind induced, the wind pressure difference

is utilized to provide fresh air into the building. A buoyancy driven ventilation system exploits
the temperature difference between the outside and indoor air, and thereby creating a chimney
effect [22]. This effect was investigated by Holford et al [23] where the research focused on an
atrium that was directly self ventilation trough the degree of flow enhancement by a low-level
connection to the exterior. A theoretical model was developed to predict the steady stack-driven
flow and thermal stratification in the atrium, which was caused by the heat gains in the adjacent
storeys and solar gains in the atrium.
When designing a natural ventilation system for an atrium building, several factors must be
considered in terms of its influence on the thermal comfort of the atrium and the performance
of the ventilation system. Moosavi et al [24] summarised the design parameters that should be
carefully evaluated as they affect the thermal performance of the atrium in question: opening
characteristics, atrium geometry, roof properties, fenestration properties and building materials.
The opening characteristics of an atrium are stated by Moosavi et al [24] to influence both the
ventilation performance and the thermal performance of the atrium in question. This includes
the opening size, number of openings, position and location of the opening. The criteria and
importance of the proportion between the inlet and outlet opening varies with wind-induced and
buoyancy-driven ventilation.
Natural ventilation is not necessarily the most optimum solution for maintaining an adequate
indoor climate all year around, as it is highly dependent on the outdoor conditions and the
specific atrium design. A mechanical ventilation system can therefore be a good alternative or
additive, both for heating and cooling purposes.
2.3.2
Mechanical ventilation
Mechanical ventilation systems relies on power (active), where the air handling unit is the main
part of the system, being the component that supplies clean and fresh air to create sufficient
indoor air quality. Briefly stated, the AHU typically consist of fans, filters, energy recovery sys-
tem (ERS), and a heating and cooling coil. The mechanical ventilation system can be used
for both heating and cooling purposes, dependent on the location of the atrium and outdoor
conditions.[25]
A study by Ratajczak et al [26] was reviewed as the authors looked into how incorporating an
atrium into the ventilation system would influence the energy consumption and thermal comfort
of a university building located in Poland. The analysis showed that the atrium reduced the
overall heating demand of the building, but it was found more difficult to maintain comfortable
indoor temperatures within the room adjacent to the atrium zone.
However, it is found through literature [26, 27] that it is less common to solely rely on a me-
chanical ventilation system in buildings with an atrium. Usually, when mechanical ventilation
strategies are implemented it is in combination with natural ventilation system. Hybrid ventila-
tion strategies must however be implemented carefully to not cause disturbance on the natural
ventilation system by adding mechanical air flows to the system [27].
Fohimi et al [28] investigated the potential for using a hybrid ventilation system in an atrium
building through Computational Fluid Dynamics (CfD) simulation. The atrium was in reality
relying solely on mechanical ventilation. Through their research they investigated the potential
for natural ventilation to fully or partly substitute some of the mechanical operation. From the
simulation results it was concluded that a hybrid solution by combining natural and mechanical
strategies could improve the air distribution and maintain sufficient indoor climate, while solely
relying on natural ventilation was not recommended for the atrium in question.

2.4
Outdoor features in an atrium
One of the aspects that make atrium spaces attractive and valuable for building designers is
the potential for flexible use and design. Some atrium spaces are thereby designed to create
a sense of outside on the inside, all year around. The Bovieran building design is an example
of such an atrium design, with the extensive use of plants and a pond to imitate the French
Riviera. It is however limited with existing research on such atrium designs in terms of the
energy performance and indoor climate. Nevertheless, the plants and ponds that are located
in the Bovieran atrium are shown through [13] and [14] to have a major impact on the energy
performance and indoor climate, especially in terms of the humidity levels in the atrium.
A literature study focusing on energy performance and indoor climate of buildings with similar
characteristics as the Bovieran atrium was therefore carried out, to create a general understand-
ing on the potential influence on the building performance.
The Bovieran atrium design can be interpreted to have similar characteristics as a greenhouse
and a plant factory. The energy performance of greenhouses and plant factories are more rep-
resented in literature, where also factors influencing the indoor climate are included. Graamans
et al [29] carried out a research project focusing on the energy fluxes related to the production
of lettuce in a plant factory, where the energy balance of a plant factory was compared to the
energy balance of a greenhouse. The study included a specific approach for estimating the
crop transpiration, and thereby illustrate the effect the crop had on the sensible and latent heat
transfer in the plant factory.
The energy consumption by crop transpiration was also evaluated by Zhang et al [30], where a
temperature control system was modelled and simulated in a plant factory using energy balance.
The energy balance of a plant factory and greenhouse was presented and compared also in
this study, to illustrate the effect of crop transpiration. From [29] and [30] it can be concluded
that having plants located in the atrium influence the energy balance of the atrium through
evapotranspiration (ET).
Evapotranspiration is the combination of evaporation and transpiration, both being processes
where water moves from a surface to the atmosphere either as latent or sensible heat. Sensible
heat is defined as the exchange of energy between the system and the surroundings, while the
latent heat is related to the transfer of energy to a system when phase transition takes place.
It do not exists an easy way of distinguish between evaporation and transpiration as the two
processes occurs simultaneously.[31]
It is however not only the energy balance that are influenced by the evapotranspiration from
plants. The actual resulting moisture emission from the evapotranspiration effect the humidity
levels in the atrium, often interpreted trough the relative humidity (RH).
2.4.1
Evapotranspiration from plants
For estimation of evapotranspiration from plants, the Penman-Monteith (PM) crop transpiration
model is widely used in literature [29, 30, 31]. It was originally derived from combining the energy
balance with the mass transfer method to calculate the evaporation from an open water surface
from standard climate data. The original PM equation calculates the evapotranspiration from a
one dimensional big leaf, where net radiation is absorbed, heat is exchanged and water vapor
escapes. It has from there on been developed to account for evapotranspiration from cropped
surfaces and adjusted several times to be valid for various types of crops and conditions, by for
instance including resistance factors.[31]
As the surface and aerodynamic resistances in the PM equation are crop specific, The Food and
Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) [31] developed a reference evapotranspi-
ration model using grass as a a reference crop surface. By using a ”Hypothetical reference crop

with an assumed crop height of 12m, a fixed resistance of 70 s/m and an albedo of 0.23” the
reference evapotranspiration ET0 was developed.
The four meteorological factors determining ET are solar radiation, air temperature, air humidity
and wind speed. Solar radiation is the largest energy source available for vaporizing the water,
either from the plant leaves or the surface. The potential amount of solar radiation available
for evaporation is mainly determined by the location and time of the year. The surrounding air
temperature of the respective surface is influencing the rate of evapotranspiration, as higher
temperatures leads to higher evapotranspiration compared to the same surface with lower air
temperatures. The opposite correlation is observed with air humidity, where air close to the
point of saturation (high relative humidity) leads to lower evapotranspiration rates. Wind and air
movement around the evapotranspiring surface influence the evaporation rate as the air above
the surface is gradually getting saturated. If the air is not replaced, the evapotranspiration rate
will decrease.[31]
The study carried out by Graamans et al [29] included a model estimating the energy related to
the crop transpiration using the PM equation. The model was made applicable for the conditions
in the plant factory using various sub-models to estimate the net radiation (from artificial light-
ing) absorbed and the resistance factors. Their model was validated for three different interior
climates, where experiments was conducted in closed systems with artificial lighting.
2.4.2
Evaporation from ponds
Ponds are another feature that are often included in atrium designs, contributing to the overall
energy balance of the room through evaporation. The evaporation rate from a pond can be
estimated assuming the pond to be a type of indoor swimming pool, also known as natatoriums.
The 2011 ASHRAE Handbook - Heating, Ventilation and Air-Conditioning (HVAC) Applications
presents an empirical equation for estimating the evaporation from an indoor swimming pool
[32], presented in Equation 2.3. Estimating the evaporation rate from a pool is dependent on
factors such as water area, wet deck, water temperature and activity level.
wp = A
Y · (pw −pa)(0.089 + 0.0782 · V )
(2.3)
Where wp is the evaporation of water in [kg/s], A equals the area of the water surface of the pool
in [m2] and Y is the latent heat required to change water to vapour at surface water temperature,
given in [kJ/kg]. pw represent the saturation vapor pressure at surface water temperature in
[kPa], while pa is the saturation pressure at the dewpoint temperature of the room air in [kPa].
The air velocity over the water surface is symbolised by V given in [m/s]. The constants 0.089
and 0.0782 have the units of [W/m2 Pa] and [W s/m3 Pa] respectively.
Equation 2.3 is further developed by [32] to account for an activity factor, Fa, and reduced by
assuming an Y-value of 2400 kJ/kg and air velocity values ranging between 0.05 and 0.15 m/s.
Researchers from COWI and the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU) [33]
investigated the importance of assuming an appropriate activity factor in the use of Equation
2.4, where their research validated that a correct estimation of the activity factor is important for
correct determination of the evaporation rate.
wp = 4 · 10−5 · A · (pw −pa) · Fa

2.5
Atrium buildings and climate change
With growing concerns regarding climate change follows a need for building designs that can
handle higher temperatures, flooding and the extreme weather that are predicted to occur in the
near future. As presented in Section 1.1, adaption to climate change is still not a part of building
regulations and standards and it was therefore interesting to review existing literature on atrium
buildings with regards to changes in climate.
As previously stated in Section 2.2.1 where the geometrical influence was studied by Aldawoud
[18], it was found that the changes in the climate had a perceptible impact on the energy per-
formance when changing the geometry of the atrium.
The energy performance and thermal comfort from implementing a courtyard and atrium in a
dwelling located in the Netherlands was analysed with regards to changes in the climate by
Taleghani et al [34]. Four different climate predictions for the Netherlands in 2050 was used in
the analysis, consisting of two main scenarios being a moderate and warm scenario. One of
the key highlights from the study was the increased heating demand when adding a courtyard
to the dwelling. Adding an atrium on the other hand showed to reduce the heating demand,
but increase the number of hours with discomfort. Overall the paper illustrated that climate
change influence the energy demand and thermal comfort in a dwelling, and transitional spaces
(courtyard or atrium) could be a tool in reducing the heating demand.
2.6
Evaluating the indoor climate of an atrium
The broad term of indoor climate consists of four aspects that affect occupants perception of
the indoor environment, and it includes the thermal comfort, indoor air quality, daylight and
acoustics [35]. The indoor climate of atrium buildings are dependent on several influencing
factors, such as the atrium geometry, building ventilation system and glazing properties, as
presented in the previous sections. Therefore, the following section presents relevant standards
where regulations and recommendations for the indoor climate are given.
There exists numerous national and international standards focusing on the indoor climate and
energy performance of a building. A report from [36] compares the most common national and
international standards. Several similarities was found, but common for them all was the lack
of recommendations for atrium spaces.
Thermal comfort is however found through literature [24, 26, 34] to be the focus area when
evaluating the indoor climate of an atrium. The perception of the thermal comfort is influenced
by the indoor air temperature, humidity, ventilation rate and heat radiation. These parameters
leads to energy transfer between the surroundings and the human body, both in terms of latent
and sensible heat transfer.
Operative air temperature
The European Standard EN16798 [37] provide a recommendation for the operative temperature
inside a room without mechanical conditioning. The operative temperature θ0 is given as a
function of a weighted running mean of the outdoor temperature Θrm, presented in Figure 2.2.
EN16798 divide the recommended values into three categories, describing the expectation level
with regards to indoor climate in the room [37]. Criteria within category 1 represent a high level
of expectation of the indoor climate, while category 2 represent a normal level of expectation
and should be used for new buildings and renovations. Category 3 represent a moderate level
of indoor climate expectations and may be used for existing buildings.

”Bygningsreglementet” (BR18) [9] is the danish regulation and standard for buildings, consid-
ering a building’s construction, interior, energy use and indoor climate. BR18 have a sepa-
rate section focusing on the thermal comfort in buildings, where a recommended value for the
maximum hours above a certain operative temperature are given, which is dependent on the
buildings area of use. A non-residential building, such as an office building, should have a max-
imum number of 100 hours above 26°C and 25 hours above 27°C during occupied hours. For
residential buildings where opening of windows are possible, a maximum number of 100 hours
above 27°C and 25 hours above 28°C is set for the occupied hours.
Relative humidity
Relative humidity is a measure describing the ratio between the present amount of water vapor
in the air to the maximum amount of water vapor that could be present in the air, often denoted
in [%]. It is used to give an indication of the humidity levels within a space, which influence the
occupants perception of the indoor climate.

3.4
Natural ventilation
The natural ventilation system is the only means of cooling in the atrium in Bovieran Ishøj. The
windows are set to open when the indoor air temperature of the atrium exceeds 22°C, as long
as the outdoor temperature is lower than the indoor air temperature. It is assumed that the
natural ventilation is driven by combined wind pressure differences and buoyancy effects.
The opening of the windows was simulated in the reference model in IDA ICE to be controlled by
a PI controller, where the setpoints and inputs were validated in the present study. See Figure
A.1 in Appendix A for the PI controller and relevant setpoints in IDA ICE. The wind-induced and
buoyancy driven ventilation air flows are taken into account automatically in IDA ICE. However,
case specific variables must be inserted in the building model to best simulate the actual air
flow throughout the building. This includes the wind profile, the pressure coefficients Cp, the
flow coefficient Cd, and the opening area of the windows.
The wind profile in IDA ICE for the building model was set to the default settings of ”Suburban”,
based on the location of Bovieran Ishøj. The pressure coefficient is a dimensionless ratio that
is used to describe the wind pressure on a building. It depends on several parameters, such as
the positioning and geometry of the building facade, wind direction and speed, and the degree
of exposure and sheltering. It can however be difficult to make a good estimate of the parameter
and it was therefore decided to use the default values given in IDA ICE for an exposed building,
as a more detailed analysis of the pressure coefficient was not a part of the project scope. The
default pressure coefficient values in IDA ICE is taken from the Air Infiltration and Ventilation
Centre (AIVC) database.
The flow coefficient Cd is the dimensionless discharge coefficient, which depends on the angle
of the wind and the relative size of entry and exit openings, normally ranging between 0.4 and
0.8. Based on information given in [40], a value of 0.65 was set in IDA ICE.

width and relative height of the total window area. This was calculated from the window opening

Validation - Energy use by the fans
When analysing the base case simulation results, a difference in the energy use by the fans
between the measured and simulated results was observed. The measured energy use by fans
in the AHU was informed by Bovieran Ishøj to be 11.4 MWh during 2022, while the simulated
energy consumption by the fans was 49 MWh. Being almost five times larger, this was further
investigated when validating the base case model.
The energy consumption by fans are influenced by several factors. This includes the air flow
rate, both exhaust and supply, where an unbalanced air flow would influence the energy con-
sumption. It was however confirmed by the foreman of Bovieran Ishøj that the AHU is operating
constantly if the requirements in Table 3.4 is met. It was also confirmed that the air flow rate is
balanced between the supply and exhaust flow.
As the difference in energy consumption was not explained by the air flow rate, it was decided
to analyse the Specific Fan Power (SFP) comparing the measured and simulated values. The
SFP quantifies the energy efficiency of the air movement through the fan and it is a measure of
the electric power that is required to drive the fan, relative to the amount of air that is circulated
[41]. It is not a constant value, as it changes with both the air flow and the pressure rise over
the fan.
Table 3.5 presents relevant SFP values together with the assumed external pressure. The
SFP value used in the simulation in IDA ICE was taken from the technical specification sheet
from the manufacturer of the AHU, being 1.71 kW/(m3/s). The external pressure used for the
technical SFP estimation was stated to be 200 Pa. The SFP value from the measured energy
consumption was calculated using Equation 3.1 to be 0.5 kW/(m3/s).

Internal gains
The internal gains simulated for the atrium zone include equipment, occupants and the moisture
emission from the many plants and the pond located in the atrium. The input data for the
occupants and the equipment is described in the following section, followed by the method for
calculating the moisture emission rate in Section 3.7.
Heat produced by equipment and occupants in the room must be considered when evaluating
the overall energy balance of a room or an atrium. Appliances and electronics such as com-
puters, refrigerators and ovens generate heat during operation, where the heat output varies
with type of appliance and its efficiency. Lights is another feature often present in a room or an
atrium which produces heat during operation, where the heat output also is dependent on the
type of light source. Occupants contribute to the energy balance of the room, where the heat
output is based on activity level and type of clothing.[43]
Lighting
The main type of equipment in the atrium was found to be the light fixtures. The specific types of
lights was informed by Bovieran Denmark and this included 10 different types and a total of 75
lights. Each type was implemented in IDA ICE as ”light equipment”, described by the luminous
efficiency [lm/W], rated input per unit [W] and convective fraction. The convective fraction is
representing the fraction of rated input emitted as convective heat to the zone, and the default
value was chosen in IDA ICE. All of the lights in the atrium was implemented with the schedule
from the reference model, where it was assumed one schedule for the summer and one for the
winter period. During summer time, it was assumed that the lights would be turned of during
the day, from 8AM to 6PM, while during winter the lights would be off from 9AM to 4PM.
Occupants
When modelling occupant behaviour in a room, three factors must be considered: the heat gain,
CO2 emission and the moisture emission. The occupants of the atrium was accounted for in the
model by implementing these three factors separately. According to the SBI guideline [44], the
heat emitted by occupants can be assumed to be 1.5 W/m2 and this was set to be the case for
the building model. The default value for CO2 emissions in IDA ICE was chosen, being 6.5 mg/s.
The number of occupants and the occupancy schedule was kept from the reference model,

3.7
Moisture emission rate
The moisture emission was shown in the two previous projects [13, 14] to have a major impact on
the energy use and indoor climate in the atrium. The following section presents several explored
methods for modelling the moisture emission in the atrium. The method which showed to be
most reliable was subsequently chosen and implemented in the building model in IDA ICE.
3.7.1

The evapotranspiration rate from the many plants in the atrium was assumed to have a high
impact on the overall building performance. Evapotranspiration leads to moisture being trans-
ferred to the indoor environment of the atrium which affect the perception of the thermal comfort.

heat transfer through evapotranspiration functions as a cooling potential in the atrium.

the following section.

adjustment was made to make it more valid for the building model.
The mass balance method is based on the assumption that the absolute humidity of the indoor
air is equal to the absolute humidity of the supply air from the AHU. Thereby, if the two humidity
levels deviates, a moisture emission source is present in the atrium. It is however several
aspects to consider and assumptions to make for it to be applicable for the building model.
First and foremost, it was assumed that the atrium operate as a closed system during winter
months, so that all incoming and outgoing air flows through the AHU. The mass balance method
was not applicable for the summer months, as the natural ventilation air flows through the atrium
were unknown. It was therefore assumed that the moisture emission rate for one of the winter
months would be valid for the summer period, and this is described in more detail in Section
3.7.5. To be able to use the hourly average measurements of the indoor air temperature and

was assumed that the measured indoor air temperature is equal to the return air temperature

recovery in the AHU was taken into account as the atrium AHU consist of an uncoated rotary

3.7.5
Validation - Mass balance method
To validate the resulting moisture emission rate from the mass balance method, a comparison
between the simulated and measured relative humidity inside the atrium was carried out. The
simulated relative humidity was found to be significantly higher during winter months than the
measured relative humidity in the same time period. This variation can be an indication of
an overestimation of the moisture emission rate using the mass balance method, or it can be
caused by deviations between the actual and simulated heat exchanger operation in the AHU.
As stated in Section 3.5, the rotary heat exchanger transfer moisture from the return air to
the supply air when in operation. The working of the moisture transfer in the heat exchanger
can be denoted and presented through a parameter called ”moisture transfer efficiency”. The
moisture transfer efficiency is a measure of how much moisture is transferred from the return
air to the supply air in the heat exchanger. The higher moisture transfer efficiency, the more
moisture is transferred from the return air to the supply air. This will furthermore increase the
relative humidity inside the room in question. A potential deviation between the actual and
simulated moisture transfer efficiency in the heat exchanger would result in deviations between
the measured and simulated relative humidity inside the atrium. It was therefore decided to
compare the two moisture transfer efficiencies, to see if it could explain the deviation in relative
humidity.

As the difference in the moisture transfer efficiency was not caused by the temperature efficiency
of the heat exchanger, the deviations was assumed to be caused due to a variation in the
moisture content in the air flows. It was therefore decided to look into potential sources of
errors in terms of the absolute humidity level in the atrium air.
The solar radiation was included in the analysis of the moisture transfer efficiency due to its
potential influence on the moisture content in the atrium. It is known that high outdoor solar
radiation could cause increased indoor air temperatures in the atrium. An increased air tem-
perature could moreover lead to a lower relative humidity level, if the absolute humidity in the
air remained constant. However, the moisture content in the atrium was likely to change with
increased air temperatures, for instance due to increased evapotranspiration from the plants.
Another aspect to consider in terms of increased indoor air temperatures and moisture content
in the atrium was the drying potential from opening of windows. Opening of windows could
cause a reduced moisture content in the atrium, as the supply air from the window opening is
likely to have lower relative humidity than the outflow of air from the atrium.
The window opening mechanism in the atrium was controlled by the indoor air temperature,
set to open when the air reached a temperature of 22°C. However, when analysing the actual
window opening schedule received from Bovieran Ishøj, it was shown that the windows in the
atrium facade and roof did not open in the periods of time when the moisture transfer efficiency
was calculated. Opening of the atrium windows towards the outside can therefore not be the
reason for the deviation between measured and simulated moisture transfer efficiency and rel-
ative humidity.
Instead, the opening of the apartments windows was questioned. If the indoor air temperature
increases, both in the atrium and the apartments due to high solar radiation, the residents could
feel thermal discomfort and thereby open the windows in an attempt of reducing the indoor air
temperature. By opening the windows towards the outside and towards the atrium, a draught of
air could be caused and thereby lower the moisture content in the atrium. This reduction could
in theory lower the moisture transfer efficiency in the actual atrium AHU. However, there are no
information about the opening schedule of the apartment windows and the hypothesis will not
be tested any further in the present study.

3.8
Climate scenarios
Buildings are usually designed using historical weather data. However, since climate change is
predicted to cause higher temperatures and more extreme weather, as presented in Section 1.1
it was found interesting and highly relevant to analyse how the Bovieran building design would
tackle climate changes. Two predictions of the future climate was used in simulation in IDA
ICE, one for the year 2050 and another for the year 2090, to test the building model sensitivity
towards changes in the climate.
The predictions are developed by the BUILD Institute from Aalborg University, using the Coordi-
nated Regional Downscaling Experiment (CORDEX) program [50]. The climate files are created
based on the fifth report from IPCCs about predicting future climate data and a business-as-
usual scenario. For a more detailed description of the methodology, see [50]. The predicted
climate files are created for the weather station in Sjælsmark, located about 30 km North of
Copenhagen, where historical data from the station is used for bias correction.
The predictions for 2050 and 2090 are so-called Typical Meteorological Years (TMY), where
both historical and global data affect the climate predictions. On the contrary, the 2022 climate
file from DTU Climate Station is representing the actual climate in 2022. The climate can vary
widely from year to year, meaning that 2022 could for instance have unusual high temperatures
or low relative humidity, not being representative for the year before and the year after. The
basis of comparison was important to consider when comparing climate files and furthermore
simulation results from using the respective climate files. As a result, it was decided to include
a design reference weather file for comparison with the DTU2022.
The Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) has developed a weather file called Danish Design
Reference Year (DRY) which is created based on historical data, where the latest data set is
based on weather data from 2001 to 2010 [50]. The meteorological parameters include tem-
perature [°C], relative humidity [%], wind speed [m/s], wind direction [°], atmospheric pressure
[hPa], global radiation [W/m2], cloud cover [%] and sea temperature [°C]. It was created to
simplify the building design process in simulation of heat and ventilation systems in buildings.

Heat exchanger
The heat exchanger in the base case model was set to be a rotary heat exchanger, where

moisture between the return and supply air. The temperature efficiency is usually high for plate

It was decided to keep the same settings for the plate heat exchanger as with the rotary heat
exchanger, which includes the temperature efficiency of 81.5% and the air flow rate of 15 000

Key Performance Indicators
A set of Key Performance Indicators were established to measure and quantify the results from
simulating the building energy performance and indoor climate of the Bovieran building design.
There are several aspects to consider in a buildings performance and it is therefore helpful to
establish specific KPIs within the fields of interest.
As the scope of the project is divided into two main aspects, being the energy performance
of the building and the indoor climate, specific KPIs for each of the two fields of interest were
established. The KPIs were determined based on the available measurements and targets from
Bovieran Ishøj, and, if applicable, relevant standards and regulations. The KPIs for evaluating
the indoor climate include the indoor air temperature, relative humidity and hours above 26°C
and 27°C. One KPI related to the energy use of the atrium AHU was set to symbolise the overall
energy use by the atrium. This was mainly evaluated through the energy used for heating, while
the energy recovery and energy use by the fans were commented on where it was found to be
relevant.
5.1
Indoor climate
The concept of the Bovieran building design is based on having an adequate indoor climate
imitating the French Riviera. The indoor air temperature in the atrium shall at all times be above
10°C and this is a key factor in the Bovieran concept. No maximum indoor air temperature limit
is given, where the only parameter on the indoor air temperature during summer time is the
setpoint for window opening, being 22°C. This setpoint symbolise that 22°C is the maximum air
temperature in the atrium without operation of the natural ventilation system.
As stated in Section 2.6, no recommendations nor standards focusing on atrium buildings ex-
ists today. One of the potential relevant standard to use would be EN16798. It provides a
recommendation for the indoor operative air temperature of a building without mechanical cool-
ing, given as a function of the running mean outdoor temperature as illustrated in Figure 2.2.
However, when setting up the KPI for the indoor air temperature it was decided to focus on the
mean air temperature, as this is the parameter used for the Bovieran minimum target tempera-
ture of 10°C. A maximum temperature limit was not set, but instead a second KPI focusing on
the potential hours with overheating was established.
Bovieran Ishøj has not defined overheating in the atrium and it does not exist standards focusing
on the overheating hours in an atrium specifically. However, BR18 provides recommended
maximum number of hours above a certain operative temperature for both residential and non-
residential. The temperature limits for the non-residential buildings is 26°C and 27°C, and these
limits were used for in the present study. As the maximum number of hours set by BR18 do not
represent an atrium specifically, it was decided to use the measured average air temperature
from Bovieran Ishøj as the baseline for comparison. It was assumed that the measurements
illustrate a desired indoor climate for the residents as the indoor climate could be controlled
manually to a certain degree. The target for the number of hours above 26°C and 27°C were
therefore found from the mean air temperature, not the operative temperature which is used in
BR18.
The KPI focusing on the relative humidity levels in the atrium was established based the oper-
ation and measurements from Bovieran Ishøj. The atrium AHU is set to start operate when the
indoor relative humidity reaches above 70%. This indicate that it is not optimum with a relative
humidity above this limit, but it is at the same time not important that it should be lower than 70%

at all times. If so, the setpoint should be lower. A maximum nor a minimum limit is however
specified by Bovieran Ishøj.
Standard EN16798 provides design criteria for occupied spaces where dehumidification and
humidifaction would be needed. In other words, if the atrium has a higher relative humidity
than 70%, which is likely to be the case based on the AHU setpoint, it should be dehumidified
according to EN16798, presented in Table 2.1. It was therefore decided to not include the
design criteria from EN16798 when evaluating the relative humidity levels, but instead compare
the results with the measured relative humidity from Bovieran Ishøj.
5.2
Energy use
The energy performance of the atrium is evaluated through the energy consumption by the
atrium AHU. This include the energy needed for heating, energy recovered in the heat ex-
changer (both latent and sensible heat) and the energy use by the fans in the AHU. Bovieran
Ishøj provided data on the energy consumption by the atrium AHU in 2022 and this was used
for comparison when evaluating the simulation results.
From validating the base case model in Section 3 it was found that the simulated energy use by
the fans was significantly higher than what was measured by Bovieran Ishøj. This was assumed
to be the case in all simulation results, and the energy use by the fans was therefore not included
as one of the focus areas in the analysis of the results.
Furthermore, a deviation between the simulated and measured energy recovered in the heat
exchanger was seen when validating the base case model. This was found to be due to differ-
ences in the basis of comparison, where IDA ICE takes both latent and sensible heat transfer
into account while the measurement from Bovieran Ishøj only include the sensible energy re-
covery. Thereby the basis of comparison was found to cause deviations. It is however included
in some parts of the results and thereby also commented on where it was found relevant.
Even though the simulation results could be compared to the measurements in all aspects of
the AHU, it was determined that the energy used for heating was the most reasonable indicator
to focus on when evaluating the simulation results.

8
Discussion
The present study was carried out with the aim of validating the building model of the Bovieran
building design in IDA ICE, as well as investigate the potential for a further optimisation of the
design and operation of the building. The method of validating and optimising the building model
in IDA ICE have been described, and the results are presented through the established KPIs
within the two fields of interest being energy use and indoor climate.
The following section will thereby discuss the results presented in Section 6 and Section 7 in
greater detail, and all potential sources of errors will be presented and discussed. The KPIs will
be compared to the relevant standards, followed by suggestions on what to focus on in a further
work with the project.
8.1
Base case model
As a part of validating the building model developed in IDA ICE, the simulation results were
compared to actual measurements from Bovieran Ishøj. The resulting monthly average indoor
air temperatures for the atrium are correlating with the measured values to a great extent. How-
ever the simulated air temperature is lower from August to mid-October, thereafter higher from
mid-October to December. There are several potential sources of errors that could explain this
deviation.
Firstly, different return air setpoints are used in the building model in IDA ICE compared to what
was used in the actual AHU in Boverian Ishøj. The base case model of the building in IDA
ICE has a constant return air setpoint of 15°C, while it was informed by Bovieran Ishøj to be
13°C in the time period of mid-September to mid-November. This difference in setpoint is likely
to be one of the reason for the deviating indoor air temperature. Another source of error that
could explain the difference in indoor air temperature is the accuracy of the measurement from
Bovieran Ishøj. When analysing the measurements, it was found that several hours during the
year was missing, including a longer period of time in July. Having fewer data points when
calculating an average value could potentially influence the resulting numbers. Furthermore, it
is important to mention the potential difference in climate between the climate station at DTU
and the actual weather at the location of Bovieran Ishøj. There is a distance of approximately
20km between the two locations which might have an influence on the climate conditions, and
thereby the overall building performance. It is shown through literature and the present study
that changes in the outdoor climate could have a major impact on the energy use and indoor
climate of an atrium.
The second KPI describing the thermal comfort inside the atrium is the hours above 26°C and
27°C, where the simulated base case model is simulated to have more hours with overheating
than what was measured at Bovieran Ishøj. A possible source of error is the missing measure-
ments from Bovieran Ishøj, especially in July. It is shown by Figure 6.1 that the temperatures
are in general high in July, which can indicate that the missing dates of data could include hours
with overheating.
The difference in monthly average relative humidity is most significant during winter months,
which can be an indication of the simulated moisture emission rate being overestimated in this
time period. The validation of the moisture emission rate constituted a large part of the overall
validation process of the building model in IDA ICE, and the sources of errors related to the
procedure are presented in a separate section, Section 8.2. However, one relation between the
indoor air temperature and relative humidity should be commented. The decreased monthly
Indoor climate and energy performance of semi-outdoor spaces in senior residences
52
==PAGE:64==
average indoor air temperature from August to mid-October could cause the relative humid-
ity to increase in the same period of time. This is due to the fact that when air temperature
decreases, the amount of water it can hold decreases, and thereby the measure of relative hu-
midity increases. This tendency is however not seen for the beginning of the year, illustrating
that the relation between temperature and relative humidity is not the only thing affecting the
results.
When comparing the simulated energy use by the atrium AHU to the actual measured energy
consumption, a difference is seen in all three parts of the AHU. The heating consumption in
the actual building is higher than what is simulated for the base case model in IDA ICE, with
the measured being 174 620 kWh, while the simulated heating consumption is 160 600 kWh.
Potential sources of errors to explain the deviation includes the difference in return air setpoint
and the outside climate conditions as presented above. It was additionally informed by Bovieran
Ishøj that the actual heating consumption was in reality a bit lower than what the internal meters
reported. This was however not investigated any further, and the base case model was kept
despite having a lower heating consumption.
The difference between the simulated and measured energy use by the fans in the AHU was
investigated in Section 3. From the analysis, it was concluded that the reason might lie within
the estimation of external pressure on the fans, so that the actual external pressure on the fans
is lower than what is assumed by the manufacturer. The technical specifications for the fans are
used in IDA ICE and therefore the energy use is increased for the base case model, compared
to what is measured. It is important to mention that the resulting SFP from the actual energy
use by the fans is unrealistically low, and it could be of interest for future work on the project.
8.2
Moisture emission rate
The main part of validating the building model in IDA ICE involved estimation of the moisture
emissions in the atrium. Several methods for estimating the moisture emission rate in the atrium
were tested, including calculating the evaporation rate from the pond and evapotranspiration
rate from the plants using two separate models found in literature. The models developed were
not implemented in IDA ICE as it was found to have to many uncertainties to be applicable. As
a result, the mass balance of the moisture content in the atrium from the previous projects was
optimised. Despite the work carried out with the model in the previous studies and the further
elaboration done in the present project, several assumptions and potential sources of errors
should be discussed.
First of all, the main limitation set for the calculation of the moisture balance was assuming the
atrium to be a closed system, where all air flows would go trough the atrium AHU. This resulted
in the moisture emission rate being calculated only for the months without natural ventilation,
limiting the accuracy for the moisture emission rate during the summer time. Moreover this
also results in the assumption that the windows towards the apartments are closed at all times,
even though this is known to not be the case in the actual building. The air flows through the
apartment windows influences the actual building performance, including the moisture content
in the atrium, and it is therefore listed as a source of error to consider when evaluating the
resulting moisture emission rate and subsequent simulation results.
The moisture balance was additionally established with the assumption that the air flow through
the atrium AHU is balanced and this was confirmed to be the case by Bovieran Ishøj. However,
as the actual air flows through the AHU are not measured and thereby not possible to investigate
any further, it is listed as a potential source of error. If the air flows are not balanced, it would
case the mass balance of the moisture content to be inaccurate, where a reduced outflow from
the AHU would cause less moisture to be removed from the atrium, and vice versa.
Indoor climate and energy performance of semi-outdoor spaces in senior residences
53
==PAGE:65==
Another source of error that is relevant to the moisture balance is the data used in the calculation
of the moisture emission rate. In the calculation of the moisture emission rate inside the atrium,
measurements of the indoor air temperature and relative humidity from Bovieran Ishøj was
used, together with outdoor climate data from DTU Climate Station. It is previously stated that
the measurements from Bovieran Ishøj was missing information about certain hours and days.
The majority of the missing data points was however found to be during the summer time, while
the moisture balance calculation was done for the period of time without natural ventilation.
Nevertheless, the potential difference in outdoor climate conditions between DTU and Ishøj
must be mentioned as a potential source of error for the moisture balance calculation.
The moisture recovery inside the heat exchanger was taken into account when estimating the
moisture emission rate, but this also lead to some uncertainties to consider. It was found that
the calculated moisture transfer efficiency in the heat exchanger in Bovieran Ishøj does not
correspond to the simulated moisture transfer efficiency. Since the measurements from the
Bovieran Ishøj was used in the calculation, it was assumed that the calculated moisture trans-
fer efficiency symbolise the actual moisture transfer efficiency in the actual heat exchanger in
Boverian Ishøj. Through the validation presented in Section 3.5, high solar radiation was found
to be one potential reason for the deviation as this can create a drying potential in the atrium
and thereby reduce the actual calculated moisture transfer efficiency in the heat exchanger.
Furthermore, it should be mentioned that the moisture transfer efficiency is highly dependent on
the temperature transfer efficiency in the heat exchanger. If the heat exchanger does not work
at the design efficiency, it would influence the moisture transfer efficiency. The temperature
efficiency in the AHU in Bovieran Ishøj was not measured directly and no other information than
the technical specification sheet was found. It was therefore assumed that the heat exchanger
worked at its design conditions, but this was not confirmed through measurements nor calcu-
lations. Moreover, the potential for freezing in the heat exchanger is not taken into account in
the calculation and should be considered as a source of error. Freezing in the heat exchanger
would interrupt the operation and thereby change the moisture transfer in the heat exchanger.
All of the above mentioned assumptions and sources of errors related to the calculated mois-
ture emission rate influence the results from simulating the base case model in IDA ICE. From
analysing the deviation in relative humidity between the base case model and measurement
from Bovieran Ishøj, the moisture content in the atrium is most likely overestimated during the
winter months, while a small underestimation is the case for the summer months. It was nev-
ertheless decided to keep the resulting moisture emission rate in the base case model, as this
was the most validated version created within the project period. However, it is important to
comment and take the sources of errors into account when analysing the presented results.
8.3
Climate scenarios
The base case model was simulated with four different climate scenarios, to test the sensitivity
towards changes in the climate. This included the weather data from DTU Climate station, the
Design Reference Year climate data from DMI, and two predictions of the future climate for the
years of 2050 and 2090.
The following section present a comparison of the results from simulating the base case model
using DTU2022 and DRY2001-2009, discuss the building performance in the future climate,
together with relevant assumptions and potential sources of errors.
8.3.1
DTU2022 versus DRY2001-2009
The base case model was validated to represent the actual building performance using DTU2022
climate file. However, when comparing the results using DTU2022 to the simulation results us-
ing the future climate of TMY2050 and TMY2090, the basis of comparison was questioned.

DTU2022 is representing one single year of weather data and do not symbolise the weather
conditions in the years before nor after 2022. TMY2050 and TMY2090 on the opposite are
created based on a business-as-usual scenario, using historical weather data from a longer
time period. It was therefore decided to include a fourth climate scenario in the analysis, being
the DRY2001-2009, which have a more similar basis to the future climate files compared to
DTU2022. The differences in the climate parameters between DTU2022 and DRY2001-2010
presented in Section 3.8 are also shown in the results from simulating the base case model with
the respective climate files.
DTU2022 has a monthly average outdoor air temperature that is higher than DRY2001-2010
and this is shown through the resulting monthly average indoor air temperatures and hours with
overheating. The same tendency is seen between the outdoor and indoor monthly average
relative humidity levels, where DRY2001-2009 results in higher RH levels than DTU2022.
The high monthly average indoor air temperatures in DTU2022 leads to more opening of win-
dows, less need for mechanical ventilation and thereby less energy needed for heating. At the
same time, the monthly average indoor relative humidity levels in DRY2001-2009 are higher
than DTU2022, so that the AHU setpoint of 70% is reached more frequently, leading to more
operation by the AHU.
When it comes to the question about the basis of comparison, the results from simulating the
base case model using DRY2001-2009 are found to be more comparable to the results from
simulating the base case model with the future climate predictions. The outdoor air temperature
is predicted to increase due to climate change, but when looking at the DTU2022 climate file it
seems like the temperature is already above the prediction for TMY2050. This can nevertheless
be interpreted as to DTU2022 being a so-called extreme weather scenario.
8.3.2
Future climate scenarios
The results from simulating the base case model in the future climate predictions of TMY2050
and TMY2090 are discussed in the following section, together with an evaluation of the potential
sources of errors and limitations.
Indoor climate and energy use
One of the more important takeaways regarding the indoor climate of the atrium is the increase
in relative humidity levels for the base case model in TMY2050 and TMY2090. The monthly
average indoor relative humidity levels are increasing in the future and this could be caused
by several situations. A decrease in the indoor air temperature could lead to higher relative
humidity levels since the amount water that the air can hold decreases. However, comparing
DRY2001-2010 and TMY2090 in terms of indoor air temperature and relative humidity it can be
seen that the resulting indoor air temperatures in the scenarios are relatively similar, but the RH
levels are deviating to a great extent. It is therefore assumed that the increased indoor relative
humidity levels in the future are caused by other conditions. This could for instance be a result
of the increased outdoor relative humidity levels in the future climate predictions. As seen in
Figure 3.8, the outdoor relative humidity in TMY2050 and TMY2090 are predicted to be much
higher than both DTU2022 and DRY2001-2010 climate files.
As the natural ventilation system is the only means of cooling during summer time, the indoor
conditions will be directly affected by the outdoor weather conditions. The inflow of air through
the open windows will then have initial high temperatures and relative humidity levels, and fur-
thermore be heated up and moisturised inside the atrium. Regarding the winter period with
mechanical heating of the atrium, the outdoor humidity conditions could potentially also influ-
ence the indoor humidity conditions. Having too high moisture content in the incoming air could
potentially be problematic for the heat exchanger and thereby result in high indoor relative hu-
midity levels. It is however important to state that the relation between the indoor and outdoor

conditions in terms of relative humidity and air temperature in the atrium is a complex problem
to understand.
The monthly average indoor air temperatures on the other hand, are simulated to be surprisingly
low when comparing it to the outdoor conditions in TMY2050 and TMY2090, given in 3.7. The
outdoor air temperature in TMY2090 is predicted to be the highest among the climate scenarios,
but this is found to not be the case for the indoor air temperatures through simulations in IDA ICE.
The simulation results during winter time show that the mechanical ventilation system function
with higher outdoor air temperatures, keeping the indoor air temperature of the setpoint of 15°C
when the AHU is the only ventilation system operating. Simulation results from summer time

level despite having increased outdoor air temperatures due climate change. It is furthermore

Regarding the energy use by the AHU, it can be seen that the overall energy consumption for

and TMY2090. This indicates that there will be less need for heating in the future, due to
increased air temperatures in winter time, while Figure 6.6 indicate that it will be necessary
with more window opening and natural ventilation as a result of the increased air temperatures
during summer time.
Sources of errors
The future climate predictions for 2050 and 2090 are created based on the assumption of a
business-as-usual scenario. This means that the climate predictions are developed from histor-
ical data, assuming that the society will not make a great enough effort to cut in greenhouse-gas
emissions to mitigate climate change. However, this business-as-usual scenario (RCP8.5) has
been criticised and should therefor be used carefully in research and studies. There is for in-
stance likely to believe that certain changes in the global energy market will happen and thereby
also potentially influence the future climate.
However, the TMY files are representing typical years of climate, but it is also stated in literature
that the earth will experience more extreme weather in the future as a result of climate change.
This could include heat waves and storms, as well as colder winters, more extreme rainfalls
and over-flooding. The results from simulating the Bovieran building design using TMY2050
and TMY2090 should therefor be evaluated carefully as the weather files are not necessarily
representing the overall picture of how the future could look like. A more thorough analysis of
the building performance would include simulations in extreme weather scenarios for a better
understanding of its performance in the future.
When analysing the building performance in the predicted future climate, the base case model
was kept and no changes was made to the model to adapt for future climates. This includes the
moisture emission rate developed in the validation process of the model. The mass balance
of the moisture content was estimated based on the moisture content outside the atrium, using
weather data from DTU2022 and the actual measurement from Bovieran Ishøj in 2022. Using
data specific for one year in the calculation makes the moisture emission rate a source of error
when simulating other climate scenarios.
It has in general been observed a high correlation between outdoor climate and the indoor con-
ditions of the atrium. This correlation might require changes in the operational setpoints of the
AHU or the opening of windows. This is briefly investigated in the third part of the project, where
different design and operational settings are analysed. However, even though the building per-
formance will change in the future, there might be potential ”easy” changes to implement in the
already existing buildings which should be carefully evaluated.

8.4
Optimisation of the base case model
As a follow-up on the validation of the building model in IDA ICE and its sensitivity towards
changes in the climate, an attempt of optimising the building model was carried out.
This was also included as one of the research questions stated in Section 1.3, where specific
design and operational changes has been determined to potentially optimise the building perfor-
mance in terms of indoor climate and energy use. The results from simulating the building model
with various design and operational changes are discussed in the following section, including
potential limitations and sources of errors.
8.4.1
Heat exchanger
The rotary heat exchanger in the base case model was replaced by a plate heat exchanger,
to investigate the building performance without moisture recovery in the heat exchanger. The
results presented in Section 7.1 shows that the air temperature and overheating hours was
kept at the same level, while the major change in the indoor climate was in fact a reduction in
relative humidity levels during winter time due to no moisture recovery in the heat exchanger.
The resulting relative humidity levels can be interpreted as a positive change to the thermal
comfort level in the atrium. Too high relative humidity levels are avoided in all climates, which
is especially interesting in terms of the future climate predictions for TMY2050 and TMY2090.
TMY2090 ranges between a relative humidity of 60% and 90% during winter months with a plate
heat exchanger, compared to ranging between 70% and 100% using a rotary heat exchanger.
This could potentially influence the perception of the thermal comfort to a great extent, as a lower
relative humidity level is likely to be desired for the occupants. Another positive consequence of
changing the heat exchanger was the reduced energy consumption by the AHU. In all climate
scenarios, a reduction in heating consumption and energy use by the fans were seen.
However, there are some negative aspect to consider when changing from a rotary to a plate
heat exchanger. The risk of freezing increases when using a plate heat exchanger, and in colder
climates (such as Denmark) this is important to take into account when designing the ventilation
system. Freezing could cause blocking of the extract air side and the heat recovery would be
reduced accordingly.
8.4.2
Glazing
The u-value of the glazing was chosen as the design variable to test for the second part of
optimisation process, with the aim of reducing the heat loss through the windows while keeping
an adequate indoor climate. Overall an increase in the indoor air temperature was seen with
reduced u-value, leading to more hours with overheating, but more opening of the windows and
less mechanical ventilation. The reduction in mechanical ventilation leads to reduced energy
consumption for heating by the AHU.
When analysing the glazing properties of the roof and facade, the properties of the frame was
neglected in the analysis. The default value in IDA ICE for the u-value and frame proportion of
the total window area was used for all parts of the analysis carried out throughout the project.
The frame characteristics are however something that could influence the overall building per-
formance and it should be mentioned as a potential source of error. Despite the potential influ-
ence on the building performance, it was decided to not investigate this any further. The actual
Bovieran building consist of several small windows on the roof and in the facade with belong-
ing frames. In comparison, the simulated building model consist of large window zones with to
ease the simulation. A potential future work with the project could include optimising the roof
and facade simulation to account for the heat loss through the window frames.
The potential impact from changing the glazing varies dependent on the properties chosen to
analyse, and a potential further work with the project could include an analysis of other glazing

properties. This could for instance include an analysis on the daylight availability in the atrium.
8.4.3
Combined simulation scenarios
The combined design and operational changes was divided into two scenarios; Combination 1)
Plate heat exchanger with the Optitherm S1A glazing, and Combination 2) Optitherm S1A with
window opening at 20°C. The scenarios were chosen based on the results from changing the
plate heat exchanger and glazing separately.
The plate heat exchanger was combined with the Optitherm S1A in an attempt of reducing the
energy consumption and relative humidity level while keeping and adequate indoor air temper-
ature. The resulting simulation results from combining the two design changes lead to reduced
relative humidity and increased indoor air temperature. Moreover, the simulation scenario re-
sulted in the lowest energy consumption for heating amongst all of the simulations scenarios
carried out. The choice of combining the two design changes could however be questioned,
as it was expected to have increased air temperatures and more hours with overheating with
reduced u-value. Seeing this in combination with the excepted increase in outdoor tempera-
tures in the future climate scenarios, it could be argued that another combination could have
been evaluated instead to avoid the likelihood of overheating. Nevertheless, the scenario of
combining the two design changes shows that it is possible to reduce the energy consumption,
if some more hours with overheating are accepted.
Combination 2 included the Optitherm S1A glazing with a changed window opening setpoint of
20°C. This simulation scenario caused the highest monthly average indoor air temperatures,
as well as the most amount of hours above 26°C and 27°C. The same trend was seen for
the relative humidity levels, where the monthly average RH was increased in all of the climate
scenarios. The Optitherm S1A was combined with a lower opening setpoint to analyse if the
natural ventilation system could keep a sufficient indoor climate for a longer period of time over
the year. By decreasing the opening setpoint, the windows opened more frequently as the
temperatures reached 20°C more often compared to the base case scenario using a setpoint of
22°C. This caused less operation by the mechanical ventilation system and thereby less energy
consumption by the AHU.
The two combined simulation scenarios lead to a reduced energy consumption for heating by
the AHU, but increased monthly average air temperatures and more hours with overheating.
There is no final answer on what would be the best option for the Bovieran building design, as
it is a discussion of what is important to focus on for the residents of the building. Having more
overheating might lead to less use of the atrium by the residents, but save money from the
reduced energy consumption for heating. On the other hand, the atrium is included to create a
social space and should be used as much as possible and it should therefore have an indoor
climate that the residents find comfortable.
8.5
Key Performance Indicators
The key performance indicators used in the evaluation of the results are chosen based on
reviewed literature and fields of interest for the building performance. As previously stated, there
are no national or international standards focusing on recommendations for atrium buildings at
the present time. The targets used when analysing the results through the KPIs was therefore
set with the aim of focusing on the Bovieran building concept and using the current operation
of Bovieran Ishøj as a baseline.
The indoor air temperature of the atrium was chosen as a KPI as this is set to be an impor-
tant aspect of Bovieran’s concept. The company’s temperature target is based on a mean air
temperature, and the measurement received was also representing the mean air temperature.
However, standard EN16798 provides a recommendation for the operative air temperature as

a function of the running mean outdoor temperature for a building without mechanical cooling.
Figure 2.2 presents the relation for the operative temperature and could be used in the evalua-
tion instead of the mean air temperature.
BR18 provides recommendations on the maximum number of operative hours above 26°C (100
hours) and 27°C (25 hours) for non-residential buildings. However, as BR18 recommendation
does not directly apply to an atrium building it was decided to use the mean air temperature for
estimating the overheating hours where the measured mean air temperature was used as the
baseline for comparison. Alternatively the operative temperature could be used and compared
to the recommendations from BR18 and EN16798.
Using an operative indoor air temperature could in some cases give a better understanding
of the perceived indoor air temperature by the occupants as this measure takes both the air
temperature and radiant temperature of the surroundings into account.
Generally, the indoor climate could have been evaluated through KPIs focusing on other char-
acteristics of the indoor climate. This includes the CO2 level, acoustics or daylight availability
in the atrium. These focus areas could be further investigated in a future work with the project.
Moreover, the energy use of the atrium AHU was set to be the focus area, where the aim was
to lower the energy use. This could be evaluated in terms of an economic analysis where the
economic savings could be the indicator to focus on.
8.6
Further work
Throughout the study of the building performance of the Bovieran building design, several other
aspects were evaluated and considered to implement in the analysis. However, as the study was
time and resource dependent, certain limitations was set since not every aspect of the building
could be evaluated. The following section will therefore present suggestions for a potential
further work with the project.
The window opening from the apartments to the atrium was neglected in the present study. This
however could influence the energy performance and indoor climate of the building to a great
extent. It was for instance assumed that the difference in moisture transfer efficiency between
the simulated and measured values could be caused by opening of the apartments windows.
Carrying out a study focusing on the window’s potential influence on the building performance
would therefore be interesting as a part of a further work.
The moisture emission rate from the plants and ponds in the atrium constituted a large part of
the present study. However, this could be further elaborated through a more detailed analysis
of how the plants function and operate as well as an evaluation of the potential influence the
type of plant will have on the moisture emission rate. It is known that different types of plants
have different evapotranspiration rates, and it is therefore likely that it will influence the moisture
content in the atrium as well.
Another aspect that was considered and evaluated in the present study is the difference in
energy use by the fans between the simulated and measured values. The measured energy
consumption is shown to be extremely low compared to what is expected through calculations
and simulations. To analyse this in a greater detail it is therefore mentioned as a potential aspect
for a further work.
The accuracy and certainty of the weather files are another source of error that is mentioned in
the discussion. To make the analysis of the building performance in the future more reliable, ex-
treme weather scenarios should be implemented for the building model in IDA ICE. Predictions

Conclusion
This thesis was carried out with the purpose of studying the energy performance and indoor
climate of the Bovieran building design, using Bovieran Ishøj as the case study. The focus
area was set to be the atrium zone of the building, due to its influence on the overall building
performance. The present study was done as a continuation of two previous master projects,
where a reference building model in IDA ICE was further developed to simulate the building
performance of Bovieran Ishøj. The stated research questions are answered through a literature
study on atrium design and operation, as well as a validation and optimisation process of the
building model in IDA ICE.
Through a literature study it was found that the energy use and indoor climate of an atrium
is highly dependent on the outdoor climate conditions, the atrium design and interior features.
Furthermore, the thermal comfort was shown to be the parameter most evaluated in terms of
indoor climate in atrium buildings.
As a part of validating the building model in IDA ICE, the moisture emission related to the plants
and ponds was a key focus area as this was shown to have a major influence on the overall
building performance. The resulting mass balance of the moisture content was found to be the
most reliable method for estimating moisture emission rate in the atrium, however it has several
limitations and sources of errors that must be considered when evaluating the results.
The results from simulating the base case building model in IDA ICE was compared to measure-
ments provided by Bovieran Ishøj, which included the indoor air temperature, relative humidity
and energy consumption by the atrium AHU. The simulated indoor air temperature correlates to
a great extent with the measured values from Bovieran Ishøj, whereas a greater difference was
seen for the relative humidity level in the atrium. It was furthermore concluded that the energy
consumption by the atrium AHU represent the actual operation of the atrium AHU to a sufficient
level.
The building performance’s sensitivity to changes in the outdoor climate was investigated, where
the base case model was simulated in IDA ICE with different climate scenarios. The weather file
used for validating the building model, DTU2022, was however found to imitate a quite extreme
weather scenario compared to a design reference year computed for Denmark (DRY2001-
2010), by having high outdoor temperatures and lower relative humidity levels than what was
estimated based on historical data. Moreover, the base case model was simulated using future
predictions of the climate (TMY2050 and TMY2090) to analyse how the building would operate
in a potential future climate. Despite the predicted changes in the future climate, the Bovieran
building design was simulated to maintain adequate indoor air temperatures and reduce the
energy needed for heating due to higher outdoor temperatures. The indoor relative humidity
levels on the other hand was simulated to be relatively high in the future climate scenarios, and
could potentially cause thermal discomfort.
As a follow-up on the validation and sensitivity analysis with regards to climate change, the base
case model in IDA ICE was changed in terms of its design and operational settings in an attempt
of optimising the building performance. By changing to a plate heat exchanger, the relative hu-
midity levels within the atrium was reduced due to no moisture recovery. Moreover, the energy
used for heating was reduced by lowering the u-value of the glazing on the roof and facade of
the atrium, but the number of hours with overheating were increased. The design changes was
further combined, in addition to changing the window opening setpoint in the atrium. The sim-
ulation scenario combining a plate heat exchanger with a glazing with low u-value resulted in

61
==PAGE:73==
lower relative humidity levels, but higher average indoor air temperatures and more overheating
hours. The second scenario included a glazing with low u-value and a changed window opening
setpoint from 22°C to 20°C. This resulted in higher average indoor air temperatures as well as
more hours with overheating, but a significant reduction in the amount of energy used for heat-
ing. However, before implementing any drastic changes to the building design and operation
the model should be simulated using extreme weather climates and the relationship between
the plants, pond and the indoor climate should be elaborated and understood in a greater detail.
Even though several aspects of the building performance have been tested in the present study,
there are still other aspects to further investigate in terms of the building performance. This
include modelling of the window opening from the apartments towards the atrium, the use of
personal comfort systems to ensure thermal comfort in warmer or colder periods, as well as
simulate the building in various extreme weather scenarios to gain an overall understanding of
the impact on the building performance.
