Abstract
Tailings storage facilities have to be monitored with regard to their physical stability to en-
sure the safety of the dam and chemical stability to monitor potential environmental impacts.
Besides, tailings ponds that accumulated processing rejects over a longer period of time have
the potential to serve as a source for re-processing given that other minerals have become of
economic interest or newer, more efficient mineral processing technology has become available.
Drilling to the bottom of tailings ponds is usually providing a very valuable data source for
all the aspects discussed above. Depending on production technology and mine planning as
well as seasonal and climate effects, hydraulic conditions, mechanical loads etc., the tailings
properties will differ with time, depth and position. Thus, investigating drill cores is expected
to deliver meaningful vertical profiles of various tailings properties, including information on
the mechanical and mineralogical characteristics of the tailings pond.
This project aims to gain further knowledge on the dispersity, mechanical and mineralogical
properties of the tailings present in Aitik, Boliden.
For this purpose, a series of analytical
tests were carried out including the particle size distribution, particle density, particle shape
and shear strength of the tailings on the mechanical side. In addition, x-ray diffraction was
utilized to gain a better perspective on the mineralogical characteristics of the tailings. Lastly,
the obtained data were compared with the literature. The aim of the thesis focused on the
differences in the tailings properties with the potential of a future reprocessing in mind. Aside
from that, potential links between the mineral beneficiation during production and the changes
over time in processing technology were analyzed.
The results obtained from the investigations showed a difference from the old processing plant
to the new one, which was established in 2010. The samples were taken close to the current
discharge point and covered a time period from 2000-2020. The more recent samples that were
deposited after 2010 had a greater fluctuation in their properties. In addition, only one section
of the drill core contained sulfides. The explanation for this finding is a change in the discharge
location for the material and a change in the process by adding a high sulphur thickener, which
is extracting the sulfides to a new impoundment since autumn 2018. This new procedure is
explaining the composition and properties found in the tailings in combination with the effect
of an early precipitation of sulfides due to higher densities.
Keywords— Aitik, Tailings, Processing, XRD, Re-Mining, Revalorization

Introduction
1.1
Background
The mining industry has a critical role to play in the achievement of the United Nations (UN) Sustainable
Development Goals (SDG) and needs to reinvent itself for providing the raw materials needed to the fight
against the human-made climate crisis. Since the extraction of raw materials always has social, economic,
and ecological impacts, the industry must recognize its responsibility and advocate for better practices
going forward. This is of importance due to the potential in contributing to positive impacts for all 17
SDGs. All of them could be impacted, because of the important role that the mining industry plays in our
current society. This can be done through innovation in the basic industry with the help of new techniques
and a change of current practices by tackling the current mining value chain and making improvements
along the way where needed. The industry needs to tackle environmental sustainability, social inclusion
and economic development to move forward towards a positive and sustainable development for the
future [14].
A significant challenge for the mining industry is the generation of waste materials, which always will be
present in mines in the form of waste rock and processing rejects (tailings materials). The tailings are
usually stored close to the mine in large embankments and dam structures. If stored incorrectly further
damage to the environment can arise, for instance from acid mine drainage (AMD) or due to dam failures
and subsequent mud flows and floodings. In recent history many environmental catastrophes in the
mining industry happened due to failure of tailings storage facilities. One example was the Brumadinho
dam failure, which occurred in Brazil in 2019. The dam failure killed over 259 people and polluted
the nearby river system leading towards the ocean [15]. The environmental damage was detrimental
for the local plants and animals. Therefore, a real change in the industry needs to happen aiming for
zero waste and greater sustainability. The current practices could be improved through in-depth life
cycle assessments, higher standards and new ways of tailings management with the goal to minimize
the produced amount of waste and higher efficiency with a lower overall carbon dioxide footprint. This
would in the end lead to a modernisation of the mining industry, focused on taking more responsibilities
for sustainable practises.
The possibility of revalorization of tailings can make an important contribution towards achieving better
sustainability. This approach can be linked to the sustainability goal SDG12 ”Responsible Production
and Consumption”. The mining industry could work towards this goal by optimizing the resource ef-
ficiency and the amount of resources produced as well as incorporating a structure working towards a
circular economy to reduce the amount of waste. This means, not looking at tailings as waste and instead
as a possible source for products and additional financial income [16].
Through the advancements of the renewable energies and modern technologies, such as electrical vehicles
or wind turbines, we are currently also confronted with an increased demand in raw materials, i.e.
minerals and metals. The European Commission has therefore published a list of Critical Raw Materials
(CRMs) needed for the development of technologies for the green transition. The latest list published
in 2020 includes 30 different elements [17]. Copper, which is present in Aitik, Boliden, does not meet
the criteria for a Critical Raw Material defined by the European Commission as of now. However, it
is still included in the list and defined as strategic raw material as it is of significant importance for

the European industry. Strategic raw materials are limited in number and are therefore in need for a
strategic plan, which could consist of an alternative supply chain or alternative materials in the event
of a breakdown in the current supply chain. Strategic raw materials are those essential resources that
are critical to the functioning of an economy and the production of goods, often necessary for national
security, technology, and industry. These materials are typically limited in availability, either due to
their geological rarity, geopolitical factors, or concentrated supply in a few countries. Examples include
rare earth elements, lithium, cobalt, and certain metals like platinum and palladium. Given their limited
number and importance, it is crucial to have a strategic plan to ensure a continuous and reliable supply
[18].
In order to decrease mine waste and increase resource utilization, the approach of re-mining and re-
processing has advanced over the last decade thus expanding the availability of raw materials. Many
tailings storage facilities have significant amounts of raw materials still left in them with metal grades
often exceeding the grades of current mineral deposits, which possibly could undergo re-processing to
recover more raw materials. This subject is likely to comply with the European Raw Materials Act,
because it provides a new alternative supply chain for raw materials.
A comprehensive analysis of
mine tailings is needed to provide the information for the decision on a possibility of re-mining and
re-processing and to what extend it could benefit the current industry supply.
The target to re-mine tailings can be easily displayed. However, tailings properties may be very different
at different mining sites depending on the given geology and selected material. Tailings properties can
even change significantly in the tailings storage facility itself depending on the depth due to changes in
the mine production, the mineral processing, deposition strategy and technique or natural mechanical
particle movement over time. The mine operations should therefore gather in depth knowledge about
their tailings to explore further possibilities instead of neglecting them in their business planning [2].

2.1
General design of Tailings storage facilities
Tailings storage facilities (TSF) are engineered structures designed for the containment of tailings, which
are by-products of the mining and mineral processing industry. Tailings consist of ground rock, process
effluents, and other waste materials left over after the extraction of valuable minerals. These materials
often contain hazardous substances, making their safe storage a critical aspect of mining operations.
Proper management of TSF is essential to mitigate environmental risks, ensure the safety of surrounding
communities, and comply with regulatory requirements.
The design of TSF involves multiple engineering disciplines and considerations, including geotechnical
stability, hydrology, and environmental impact. The primary objectives of TSF design are to ensure
the safe and secure containment of tailings, prevent uncontrolled releases, and facilitate reclamation
and closure activities. The key design elements of tailings storage facilities encompass several critical
aspects. Firstly, location and site selection play a vital role in minimizing environmental impact and
ensuring stability. Factors such as topography, geology, hydrology, and proximity to mining operations
are thoroughly considered in this process [21].
Secondly, construction materials and methods are pivotal in the development of TSF. These facilities
can be constructed using a variety of materials, including earth, rock, and synthetic liners. The choice
of materials is influenced by the type of tailings, site conditions, and regulatory requirements.
Structural stability is another crucial design element, focusing on the stability of TSF embankments and
dams to prevent failures. This requires comprehensive geotechnical analysis, slope stability assessments,
and the implementation of reinforcement techniques. Additionally, effective water management is es-
sential for controlling the ingress and egress of water in TSF. This involves designing drainage systems,
seepage control measures, and stormwater management strategies.
Lastly, monitoring and maintenance are integral to the safe operation of TSF. This includes conducting
regular inspections, utilizing instrumentation to monitor stability and seepage, and performing mainte-
nance activities to address any issues that arise. Together, these design elements ensure the secure and
efficient management of tailings storage facilities [21].
Several methods are employed for the storage of tailings, each with its own advantages and challenges.
The choice of method depends on factors such as the type of tailings, climatic conditions, regulatory
requirements, and economic considerations. The primary methods include:
• Pond Storage (Conventional Tailings Dams): Tailings are deposited in a pond or impoundment
area, often created by constructing dams or embankments. This method provides large storage
capacity and facilitates water recovery for reuse in mining operations. On the downside it requires
significant land area, poses risk of dam failure, and can have substantial environmental impacts if
not properly managed [22].
• Dry Stacking: In this method the tailings are dewatered to create a dry, stack-able material that is
deposited in layers and compacted. Applying this method reduces the water content, minimizing
the risk of seepage and dam failure, and is more suitable for arid climates. However, there are
5
==PAGE:16==
Degree project in Arctic Mineral Resources
Trippelsdorf, J.
higher initial costs due to dewatering processes and requires more careful material handling and
placement [22].
• Paste Backfill: Tailings are mixed with binders such as cement and used to backfill underground
mine voids.
By doing this the volume of surface tailings storage are reduced.
In addition, it
enhances the underground stability and can improve the resource recovery. Applying this method
requires specialized equipment and processes.
The quality of the paste also must be carefully
controlled to ensure the underground safety [22].
• Co-disposal: In the last common method the tailings are mixed with coarse waste materials.
For instance waste rock could be used in the mixing process. In the following the materials are
co-disposed together. Doing this can improve stability and reduce the need for separate storage
facilities for waste rock and tailings. The challenge of this method is it that requires careful mixing
and placement to ensure stability and prevent segregation [22].
In conclusion, tailings storage facilities are critical components of mining operations, ensuring the safe
and environmentally sound disposal of tailings. The design and implementation of TSF involve complex
engineering and environmental considerations to mitigate risks and ensure long-term stability. Various
storage methods, including pond storage, dry stacking, paste backfill, and co-disposal, offer different ad-
vantages and challenges, making the choice of method highly context-dependent. Effective TSF manage-
ment is essential for sustainable mining practices and the protection of the environment and communities
[21].

Description of the Aitik operation and the characteristics of the Aitik
tailings
The ore production at Boliden Aitik mine started in 1968 with a production of 2 Mt a year and an
average copper grade of 0.5 % Cu and a cut-off grade of 0.37 % Cu. Originally the open pit was only
planned down to 50 m below the surface [20]. Since then, the mine has been expanded several times and
Boliden has set the current aim of production to 45 Mt annually [23]. Nowadays a depth of 450 m is
reached in the main open pit mine. The mine had an operating profit of 3076 MSEK with 43.3 Mt of
ore milled in total in 2022 [24].
Ever since the start of the Aitik mine the production has periodically increased together with the esti-
mated mineral reserves. In 2022, Aitik had a yearly production of 3.3 Mt at a head grade of 0.20 % Cu,
0.10 g/t Au, and 0.9 g/t Ag. The head grade of the ore continuously dropped since the start of the mining
project. The processing plant is able to produce concentrates with a total recovery of 90.5 % Cu, 58.6 %
Au and 75.3 % Ag. Therefore, Aitik is also known as the world’s most productive open-pit copper mine
and the largest copper mine in Europe. The registered mineral reserves extend the planned production
for another 25 years of mining. Boliden has additionally initiated plans to reduce the carbon dioxide
emissions generated through their mines. These plans combine production automation, electrification to
replace diesel-fueled equipment and the switch from smelting with fossil fuels to smelting with hydrogen
[1].

2.3
Tailings storage facility in Aitik
The storage process in Aitik happens in the following way. At first, before waste material is moved to
the tailings storage facility, the tailings are sent to a thickener for process water recovery. In order to
separate the solid particles from the liquid, the tailings are typically transported to settling tanks or
ponds. In these tanks, the velocity of the water is reduced, allowing the heavier solid particles to settle
to the bottom. The settling process relies on gravity to separate the solid particles from the liquid. The
larger and denser particles settle more quickly, forming a layer of sediment at the bottom of the settling
tanks [30].
Once the solid particles have settled out, the clarified water is typically removed from the top of the
settling tanks and recycled for reuse in the mineral beneficiation process, reducing the amount of fresh
water needed for the operations. The settled solid particles, or tailings, are often disposed in designated
areas such as tailings ponds or landfills. Depending on the composition of the tailings and local reg-
ulations, additional treatment or containment measures may be necessary to minimize environmental
impacts. All of the runoff water present in Aitik will migrate towards a clarification pond to minimize
the environmental impact from the mine [30].
The tailings storage facility in Aitik follows industrial standards. The construction of the facility began
in the beginning of the mine project and is continuing until now. The facility started at a lower elevation
close to the mine and was expanded over time towards to the West.
Since 2002 the expansion has
stopped and a new method known as spigotting is applied [13]. In this method a conventional upstream
dam development is applied as seen in Figure 3. The upstream dam method is a common approach for
constructing tailings storage facilities. In this method, the tailings dam is built incrementally by placing
layers of tailings against the dam wall, gradually increasing its height over time as more tailings are
deposited. Each layer of tailings is placed directly upstream from the existing dam crest, hence the term
”upstream” dam. As the height of the dam increases, it retains more tailings behind it. This method
relies on the strength of the tailings to support the weight of the dam and the contained tailings [3].
This method is applied all around the TSF, which causes a shrinking surface area and a growth in
elevation during the continued disposal. The tailings are discharged around the perimeter of the facility
to create a beach between the embankment and the supernatant pond. This method involves changing
spigots according to a deposition plan to facilitate drying and layering. It emphasizes the importance of
establishing and implementing a deposition plan during both design and operational stages.
However, managing multiple spigots presents challenges such as blockages and ruptures, requiring inten-
sified monitoring and maintenance. Multiple spigotting helps reducing the discharge velocity compared
to single point deposition, promoting laminar flow and aiding to settle coarse particles nearer the spigot.
This creates a greater drainage potential and steeper beach angles, facilitating fluid removal from tailings.
When referring to coarse particles, the specific density also needs to be accounted for as a driving factor.
Therefore, dense particles consisting of minerals such as sulfides are also settling close to the discharge
point compared to lighter minerals such as quartz or various silicates [31].

2.5
Revalorization of mine tailings and the potential of reprocessing
The latest advancements in mineral process technology made it possible to exploit lower-grade ores
compared to the past. One example that can be named in this regard would be the advancements in
mineral separation through the flotation technology, which continues to develop in this current time.
Here, for instance, the use of novel reagents and enhanced hydrodynamics changed a lot over the last
decades. There were advancements made in chemistry leading to higher selectivity and better optimiza-
tion for flotations cells. This optimization is visible through higher recovery rates present in low grade
ores nowadays compared the past. These attributes developed in combination with the computational
physics present in most mines nowadays, leading to improvements in cell design. For an optimized cell,
a process model can be created. This optimized cell can be used in a flow sheet simulation to improve
industrial processes and to increase the productivity [32].
However, flotation faces challenges when the material has already been processed. After processing the
majority of particles are found in very fine particle sizes. This would mean that a huge increase in the
number of bubbles is required due to the increased specific area. The amount of reagents therefore would
also increase by a great margin [33].
When it comes to fine particles processing in froth flotation the following measures can be taken. In-
creasing the collision efficiency by decreasing the bubble size and by aggregating the fine particles to an
optimum size for flotation. Reducing the size of bubbles not only enhances the efficiency of bubble-particle
collisions but also elevates the efficiency of bubble-particle attachment. These elements contribute to
higher flotation rates and improved recovery of fine particles. However, they may lead to increased water
recovery, resulting in the entrainment of gangue minerals. In practical terms, enhancing fine particle
flotation can be achieved by extending residence times and operating with high collector coverage [34].

With shrinking ore grades worldwide, the feasibility of potential mining projects also shifts over time.
Nowadays ore deposits are mined, which were overlooked in the past due to low-grade ore. This develop-
ment brought up a new subject focused around the revalorization and re-processing of former deposits.
Aside from the economic value gained from remining tailing waste, the environmental benefit also needs
to be stated [35].
For instance, the practice of desulfurization of old mine tailings has risen in recent years. It is a measure
to prevent acid mine drainage, which occurs when the runoff coming from surface water, which has
been in contact with sulphide minerals present in tailings, contaminates the soil close by the mine. The
desulfurization can be realized through flotation and stops the problem at the source [36].
A case study on the revalorization of the Haveri tailings (Cu, Au, Co) in Finland using drill cores to
create a block model of the remaining resources in the tailing dam showed that it could be feasible
to reprocess the tailings again.
The study evaluated the feasibility through proportion-grade curves
presenting the portion of blocks surpassing the cut-off grade of the selected metals. The results showed
that the reduction in the operational costs could make re-mining feasible. In addition, the previous
processing that has been done on the tailings could be a benefit for the re-processing as the tailings
are already present in a fine particle size. However, the advancement of revalorization in the extractive
industry is still slowly, yet it is still expected to become a solid part of the industry in the future [37].
The re-processing of tailings could be carried out by multi-gravity separators, which have been proven
to be useful for the production of high-grade concentrates with a high recovery from low-grade tailings
due to process intensification. This application works for upgrading industrial minerals or recovering
metal minerals. Multi-gravity separators are capable of separating a specific mineral or a group of heavy
minerals from a low-density gangue within a liquid suspension, provided there is a significant difference
in the specific gravity of the particles. Effective separation requires a minimum difference of one specific
gravity unit between the heavy and light particles. Currently, there are laboratory-scale multi-gravity
separators as well as full-scale industrial units with an ore processing capacity of up to 5 t/h available
in the market. Depending on the density these units can handle even ultra-fine particles with a size as
small as 1 µm [33].
Besides more efficient separation processes, also improving mineral liberation through re-grinding could
be a strategy. Re-grinding facilitates the mineral liberation by reducing particle sizes and increasing the
surface area of mineral particles, thereby improving the effectiveness of subsequent separation processes
[38].
Mineral liberation is the process of breaking down ore so that valuable minerals are freed from the
surrounding gangue. In re-mining, the feed material often comprises finely disseminated minerals within
a complex matrix. Efficient liberation of these minerals is essential for enhancing the performance of
separation techniques such as flotation, magnetic separation, and leaching. Without adequate liberation,
valuable minerals may remain locked within the gangue, leading to sub-optimal recovery rates and
economic inefficiencies. Re-grinding addresses this challenge by further reducing the particle size of the
feed material, which is critical for an increased surface area. This improves the separation efficiency
through Finer grinding improves the purity of the liberated minerals, resulting in cleaner and more
effective separation from the gangue. This leads to higher-grade concentrates and reduced processing
costs. In addition, it Maximizing Resource Utilization. Re-grinding enables the recovery of minerals that
were previously considered uneconomical or technically challenging to extract [39].

Trippelsdorf, J.
Several advanced grinding technologies have been developed to optimize the re-grinding process, each
offering specific advantages in terms of efficiency, energy consumption, and particle size distribution. One
example would be stirred media mills. These mills utilize fine grinding media in a stirred environment to
achieve ultra-fine particle sizes. Technologies such as the IsaMill and Stirred Media Detritor (SMD) are
known for their ability to produce fine and uniform particle distributions, which are crucial for effective
mineral liberation [40].
Re-grinding for enhanced mineral liberation could be a possible strategy in the re-mining of tailings and
low-grade ores. By employing advanced grinding technologies and optimizing the operational parameters,
mining operations can significantly improve the recovery of valuable minerals, contributing to more
sustainable and efficient resource utilization. Future advancements in grinding technology and real-time
monitoring systems hold the potential to further refine this process, ensuring that re-mining remains a
viable and productive component of modern mining practices. The economic feasibility of reprocessing is
always complicated by the heterogeneity of the tailings, requiring optimized flow sheets and potentially
missing out on extreme value outliers [41].
In summary, the growing global shift towards renewable energy technologies and low carbon economies
has intensified the demand for critical metals, which are both vital and susceptible to supply interruptions.
Mining and processing wastes, which have accumulated significant quantities of these metals, present
a valuable opportunity for meeting this demand through their reprocessing.
These wastes differ in
properties from raw ores, necessitating specialized processing techniques to effectively liberate, separate,
and concentrate the critical metals they contain. This review has explored several innovative technologies
suited for this purpose [42].
A critical element in the reprocessing of mining and processing wastes is detailed mineralogical charac-
terization, which is essential for optimizing extraction processes. The case studies presented underscore
the importance of this preliminary analysis as a foundational step towards effective valorization.
In
conclusion, adopting these advanced technologies for extracting critical metals from mining and pro-
cessing wastes is not only crucial for satisfying future metal demands but also for advancing towards a
sustainable, circular mining system with minimal waste.

• Description of the Aitik operation and the Characteristics of Aitik Tailings: The Aitik mine of
Boliden is Europe’s largest copper mine and has a rich history of production, with significant
expansion and technological advancements. The ore processing at Aitik involves grinding, clas-
sification, and flotation to produce copper, silver and gold concentrates. The resulting tailings
are stored in tailing storage facilities (TSF) close by the mine and can form up to 70-99 % of the
mined ore. Aitik tailings are stored in TSF nearby, exhibit variable water content and bulk density,
with a particle size distribution changing over depth. The mineralogical analysis reveals presence
of sulfides like pyrite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite, and gangue minerals such as quartz, feldspar, and
mica.
• Tailings storage facility in Aitik: The tailings storage facility (TSF) in Aitik employs a process
where tailings are thickened for water recovery, settled in tanks, and the clarified water is recycled.
The Aitik TSF uses the upstream dam method and spigotting to incrementally build and layer
the tailings dam, with tailings discharged around the perimeter to create a beach for drying
and layering. Spigotting requires careful management to address blockages and ruptures, with
spigotting aiding in settling denser particles close to the discharge point.
• Previous Results from Aitik Tailings Drill Core: Shane Viscarra’s degree project investigated me-
chanical and mineralogical characteristics of the Aitik tailings analyzing the same drill core, focus-
ing on samples S2, S5, S8, and S11. The carried-out tests included SEM analysis, oedometer tests,
particle size distribution analysis, stress-strain deformation analysis, and compressibility analy-
sis. The findings indicate variation in particle density and shape across samples, with shallower
samples showing greater susceptibility to void ratio reduction and crushing. The slurry samples
exhibited less pronounced crushing effects, suggesting potential interlocking behavior among par-
ticles. Further research was recommended to quantify the mineral content’s influence on breakage
susceptibility and to understand mineralogical role in particle shape change.
• Revalorization of Mine Tailings and Reprocessing Potential: Advancements in mineral processing
enable exploitation of lower-grade ores and the reprocessing of tailings, offering economic and
environmental benefits. Desulfurization of old mine tailings can prevent acid mine drainage, while
remining projects like the Haveri tailings in Finland show a potential feasibility of reprocessing.
Techniques for reprocessing include multi-gravity separators and flotation, with challenges like
handling fine particles and increased reagent usage. Fine particle processing in flotation can be
enhanced by reducing bubble size and extending residence times, leading to improved recovery
rates.
• Tailings storage facilities and precipitation behavior: In conclusion, the tailings storage facility
(TSF) at Aitik has evolved over time to incorporate diverse techniques like spigotting. Employing
a conventional upstream dam construction method, the facility gradually expands, emphasizing
the importance of a well-defined deposition plan for efficient operation. While multiple spigots
aid in mitigating discharge velocity and promoting settling of coarse particles, challenges such
as blockages necessitate vigilant monitoring and maintenance. In addition, minerals with higher
densities such as sulfides tend to settle faster in the storage facility compared to minerals with
lower densities such as quartz or feldspar minerals.

3.3.1
Particle size distribution: Sieve analysis and laser diffraction
The initial analysis centered around the particle size distribution in the drill core along increasing depth.
This was done to gather information on the differences between the samples in various depths. For this
purpose, all samples were characterized through sieve analysis with the sieve sizes fraction starting at 1.7
mm going down to the finest fraction at 38 microns. The resulting finest size fractions below 38 microns
were then subsequently subjected to particle size analysis by laser diffraction for the identification of
the entire size distribution. Each sample was initially split multiple times with the help of an ordinary
riffle splitter as seen in Figure 6. A representative sample was reached when the sample size of roughly
200-230 g was obtained. These samples were then afterwards subjected to an analytical sieving machine,
also see Figure 6.
The conducted sieving process lasted roughly 10 minutes for each sample.
Here, the percentage of
material passing and retained through each sieve fraction was gained, which then afterwards can be
displayed in a sieve curve to illustrate the particle size distribution of each sample.
Finally for the
smallest fraction a sample was taken after weighing that was further on analyzed through laser diffraction
to obtain information about the smallest particles in each sample.

Laser diffraction is a technology that utilizes laser beams to define the particle size of the sample. Here,
the diffraction caused from the particles interrupting the laser beam, is used to gain knowledge about the
geometrical parameters of the sample. The analysis can be used for particles ranging from micrometers
to millimeters. The analysis can describe the particles down to a size of 2 microns with the Mie model.
The Fraunhofer model is applied below this particle size. The process does not depend on a flow rate
and therefore is a reliable practice used to analyze fine particles. The results obtained are showing the
particle distribution by number with respect to the particle size distribution and delivering information
on the particle sizes. The curve is based on the measured D10, D50 and D90, which was also measured
during the analysis. These indicators are presenting the size fraction at 10, 50 and 90 % passing. The
measurement time ranges from a few seconds up to a couple of minutes. In this case three measurements
were taken for each sample. The measurement of each sample was concluded with an average distribution
from the measurements. The equipment empties the content out after each analysis of a given sample
and starts a cleaning process to ensure no cross-contamination from the previous measurements. The
laser diffraction offers a higher precision and requires less sample material compared to an alternatively
used sedimentation test [45].
The Mastersizer3000 was used for the analysis by laser diffraction. The first step of the analysis is the
dispersion of the sample. This is done to separate the particles from each other to obtain a accurate
measurement.
The material is either dispersed through an gas or a liquid.
For the analysis of the
Aitik samples a dispersion in water was chosen. Here, the sample material was mixed before feeding
a small amount into the instrument. The instrument measured the obscuration rate received from the
given sample. The obscuration rate in laser diffraction refers to the percentage of laser light that is
blocked or attenuated by particles in the sample during a particle size analysis.
It is an important
parameter because it indicates how much of the laser beam is obstructed by the particles, and it helps in
optimizing the measurement conditions for accurate and reliable results. The analysis could begin when
an obscuration rate of roughly ten percent was reached. Afterwards, the sample was pumped through
the analyzer. Here, the above-described process takes place, where the light is scattered when it bounces
of the material. Next up, a detection system picks up the information gained from the diffraction of the

light. The detection system is located in various angles around the sample for a high precision analysis.
Lastly, the data is processed by the instrument using a mathematical model that takes the dispersion
media and the refractive index into account. The data analysis is based on the Mie and Fraunhofer
diffraction theory [45].

3.3.2
Rosin-Rammler-Sperling-Bennett (RRSB) distribution
The next step focused on the data analysis of the gained particle size distribution to define the Rosin-
Rammler-Sperling-Bennett distribution. This was of interest to obtain more information on the tailings
characteristics and to compare the results found in the finer fractions than 38 microns from the laser
diffraction with the calculated results from the approximation function. For this purpose, the Rosin-
Rammler-Sperling-Bennett (RRSB) distribution was chosen. The function represents the observed par-
ticle size distribution as an exponential distribution initially expressed by Rosin and Rammler [46] as
R(x) = 100exp(−bxn)
(3.1)
In the equation R(x) expresses the cumulative percentage passing of oversize material on the screen with
the given sieve size x. Furthermore, b is a characteristic size parameter, and n is a dimensionless slope
parameter. Later on Bennett made changes to the equation by introducing xe, which is a characteristic
diameter at which a certain percentage (typically 63.2%) of the particles are smaller. This equation can
be expressed as
lnln( 100
R(x)) = nln( x
xe)
(3.2)
When represented on a logarithmic log-log axis, the graph should exhibit a linear trend, allowing for
the determination of the two distribution parameters. Specifically, the slope of this line corresponds to

the parameter ’n.’ Additionally, by identifying the intercept at the horizontal line where R equals 36.8,
one can determine the size parameter ’xe’. The RRSB plot was used to determine the particle to size
relationship in every sieve curve and was calculated with a linear regression method to gain the missing
coefficients [46].
Aside from the traditional method with the logarithmic approach, the excel solver software was also
utilized to find the optimal approximate equation. This was done by adjusting the slope parameter ´n´
and the size parameter ´xe´ of the Rosin Rammler equation while using the fixed size fraction given
and the percentage passing. The approximate equation can be found through the least squares method,
which describes the distances from the approximate equation from the recorded sieve curve. The distant
squares were calculated by subtracting the percentage passing with the result from the Rammler equation
and then multiplying the result to the square. The solver was then used to change the above-mentioned
parameters from the Rosin Rammler equation until the smallest deviation from the original curve was
found.
Finally, when the optimal approximate equation was gained, the particle size distribution could be
extrapolated in order to compare the findings in the different size distributions.
This was done by
changing the particle diameter in the Rosin Rammler equation. The given value will then present the
percentage of particles, which are smaller than the specified size. Doing this will lead to information on
the overall size distribution for each sample.
Another parameter that was derived from the graphical approach of the RRSB-distribution is the specific
surface area. The graphical approach adapts the found percentage passing and size fraction on a scale to
find the slope and size factor in a manual way. This is possible through a linear graph that combines all
the values found in the particle size distribution. The end of the linear graph points to the present slope
factor (n) and the surface area per unit volume (Sv). The specific surface area can then be calculated
with the following equation:
(Sv ∗xe%)/f
(3.3)
In this equation xe is the size factor at 63.2 % passing and f is the sphericity, which was found in the
particle shape analysis seen in Figure 23. The specific surface area is then given in m2/g. The graphical
approach to gain the RRSB parameters can be found in Appendix E 37.

Density measurement by gas pycnometer
Another characterization aimed to determine the different solid densities of each sample to perhaps see
changes through varying depths. The chosen device to measure the sample density was a gas pycnometer,
which can analyze the density with a high precision without interfering with the sample material. The
laboratory device is used to gain information about the volume of the solids and therefore in retrospective
also gives information on the density of the sample. Pycnometers are utilized to gain information on the
utilized space from the samples. The analysis can also be carried out with fluids, the original pycnometer
test is carried out with water. In this case helium was used as media. The process is also automated for
higher precision [47].
In the beginning, 10 representative samples for the analysis were taken. These were received from multiple
riffle splitter iterations until a small sample of roughly 10 g was left. These samples were then subjected
to the gas pycnometer analysis. The device measured the pressure change of helium in a calibrated
volume. Here, the device refers to a fixed known internal volume to gain information on the volume of
the sample. The second aspect needed to calculate the density is the mass of each sample, which was
also measured during the analysis. When both parameters are known the density can be calculated by
dividing mass through volume. The samples were not impacted by the analysis due to exposure to gas
instead of water. The analysis was carried out using an AccuPycII 1340, which is a device for density
and volume measurements. Each sample was subjected to five cycles to obtain one average result on the
volume and density of the sample. Aside from volume and density, the deviations between each iteration
were also measured to calculate the standard deviation for each sample [47].

Mechanical characterization through direct undrained shear tests
To further analyze the mechanical parameters of the drill core, sample 3 (7.5-.7.8 m), 6 (18-18.5 m) and
10 (30.7-31.2 m) were subjected to further analysis by direct shear tests. The three samples were taken
to get an idea of the shear strength, cohesion and friction angle over the entire drill core. The analysis
measured the shear strength over time while subjected to an increasing shear force. The normal load
present for the analysis consisted of step-wise increased loads of roughly 12.5, 200 and 500 kN. These
loads were taken due to the soil pressure present at various depths. These specific values were obtained
through the following equation. Here one example for the soil pressure at the depth of 18 m with the
bulk density of the tailings material:
Sample.Density ∗Gravity ∗Depth = 2.8g/cm2 ∗9.81m/s2 ∗18m = 494kPa
(3.8)
Unfortunately, the maximum normal load of the shearing device was limited to 750 kPa. The above-
mentioned loading steps were therefore chosen to be able to compare all three samples and their reaction
towards high and low shear stress. The chosen normal loads are representing the soil pressures present
in the tailings facility at the depths of 1, 7 and 18 meters. The ideal set up would have allowed to record
the maximum shear stress for each sample at their specific deposition depth to gain comparable data to
the actual stress subjected to the tailings in the TSF. The shearing device monitored the change of the
normal load, sample height, shear load, shear stress, shear strength and deflection with measurements
every second second. The test duration changed between 3-8 hours depending on the normal load present.
Some of the tests carried out stopped while the maximum shear strength was not reached yet. Therefore,
in S3, 200 kPa, dents will be visible from the pause of the shear instrument. These dents formed because
of a break in between data collection during in which no further deflection continued. However, this
pause did not affect the results and can be ignored when discussing the results as the test could just be
continued until the failure of the sample occurred.

Figure 10, illustrates the experimental setup of the direct shear test used to determine the shear strength
of the samples, which is interpreted as the capability of soil in resisting sliding. The gathered parameters,

The built up of the test consists of a cell, which holds the sample material. In this case it had the
dimension of 20 cm2 and held roughly 45-60 g of sample material. The representative soil sample was
initially prepared for the test through compaction until the desired bulk density was reached.
The
samples were molded into a membrane, which was made of rubber and reinforced with small metal rods.
Depending on the used membrane the shear strength needs to be reduced by 1 or 1.25 kPa due to the
reinforcements present in the membrane.
Before starting the test a constant vertical force was applied, which is also known as normal load. In
this case a lever arm was loaded, which increased the initial load by 10 over a distance of roughly one

meter. Having this reaction in mind, the weight also needed to be divided by the square area, which in
this case was 20 cm3. In the end 12.5 kPa was reached with weights of 250 g, 200 kPa with 4 kg and 500
kPa with 10 kg. Next up, the horizontal shear force is applied to the top half of the sample cell. With
increasing force at a constant rate, the cell is horizontally pushed until a defined distance is reached also
known as failure of the soil, which marks the end of the direct shear test. The analysis can be repeated
with increased applied normal loads. The device measures the resistance of the soil towards the shear
stress, which can be noted as resulting shear strength.
Lastly, the relationship between shear stress and normal stress is analyzed to establish the Mohr-Coulomb
failure envelope. The Mohr-Coulomb circle also gives information on the cohesion of the soil. For this
analysis a false cohesion is assumed, which is present in gravel-silt kind of material. As there is no water
content present in the dry samples, the known material is not reacting in a cohesive manner. If water
content would be present in the sample, the soil pressure would have also been monitored. However, for
this case an undrained direct shear test was carried out with dry samples [50].
One limitation of the direct shear test is that it lacks in accuracy compared to a triaxial shear test, which
allows more complex stress conditions compared to an direct shear test. However, the direct shear test
was chosen, because it is in general suitable for cohesionless soils such as sands with less sensitivity to
stress history. Having the sampled tailings material in mind, which consists of fine grained and drained
material, the direct shear test was estimated to be the most suitable analysis to gather further mechanical
parameters. At the end of the analysis a graph can be generated, consisting of the shear and normal
strength to derive the friction angle of the sample and the resulting false cohesion. All forces are usually
displayed in kPa [51].

Mineralogical characterization with X-ray diffraction analysis (XRD)
The X-ray diffraction analysis (XRD) is a non-destructive analysis method that was used to investigate
the mineralogy of the tailings material. The XRD analysis can precisely describe the phase compositions
and crystal structure of the samples. The phase composition is gained from the chemical composition
and structural type of the sample. The instrument utilizes electromagnetic waves and their specific wave-
lengths in order to gain information about the analyzed samples. The energy levels and consequently the
wavelengths are inherently tied to the sample material, thus rendering the X-ray spectrum characteristic
to that material [11]. This analysis was chosen to gain more in-depth knowledge about the mineralogical
tailings properties and their changes over depth. Beforehand, the assumption was made that the known
minerals can be expected in the result of the analysis i.e. minerals such as quartz, hematite, chalcopyrite,
pyrite, illite, and biotite.
The analysis started with the sample preparation of the material to be used in the XRD analysis. For
this purpose, a small sample size fraction was gained from each of the available samples (S2-S11) using
a riffle splitter, thus resulting in a mass of roughly 10 g per sample. In the next step, these samples
were subjected to the ball mill type ”XRD-Mill McCrone” for further wet milling of the samples. As
the sample volume during a single grinding process shall not exceed 5 millilitres, roughly 2 millilitres
of sample volume was taken to achieve the highest grinding efficiency. This corresponded to a sample
weight of 5 g as the density of the tailings material is around 2.8 g/cm3. The material was mixed with
approximately 7 milliliters of grinding liquid.
In this case, water was used as grinding liquid.

The registered peaks were smoothed at the beginning of the analysis to gain a clearer visible difference

peak, it is easier to see whether the proposed minerals are matching the recorded data. After the data
preparation was done, the mineral list inside Highscore Plus was opened, which was limited to minerals

through a score between zero and hundred. The most likely minerals were displayed at the top of the

After identifying the individual diffraction patterns, the subsequent phase of the liberation analysis
involves recognizing all unique mineral phases and precisely determining their boundaries, which are
defined through the angle of registration.
The so-called phase segmentation is performed on every
amplitude. Often multiple amplitudes combined at different angles will result into one mineral. Through
this a multitude of minerals can be identified. The spectra of the samples can then be fully determined
with respect to the information gained from the phase segmentation to separate the known frequencies.
Some of the given amplitudes did not have clear peaks and were almost overlapping with others. In
addition, small amplitudes are harder to characterize for the software and additional knowledge is needed
to fully identify the found minerals in the samples. In the end some minor peaks were ignored as they
did not reflect on any significant characteristics of the tailings [53].
Lastly, after the identification of the different minerals found in each XRD data set, they were analysed
to gain the semi-quantitative concentrations of each mineral in the samples. This was done after the
Rietveld refinement method inside Highscore Plus.
The Rietveld method is a powerful technique used in X-ray diffraction for the analysis of crystalline ma-
terials. Semi-quantitative phase analysis using the Rietveld method involves identifying and quantifying
the phases present in a mixture. First, an initial structural model is constructed for each phase using the
crystallographic information. Then theoretical diffraction patterns for each phase are calculated based
on the structural models. The calculated diffraction pattern is iteratively adjusted to fit the observed
diffraction data. This involves refining various parameters such as: Scale factors, unit cell dimensions,
atomic positions, thermal parameters and instrumental parameters.
The difference between the observed and calculated diffraction patterns is minimized using a least-squares
approach to obtain a optimal fitting. While the Rietveld method can provide precise quantification of
phases, accuracy depends on various factors like sample preparation, data quality, and the correctness of
the structural models. The method is particularly useful for complex mixtures where overlapping peaks
make traditional quantitative phase analysis difficult [54].

Mechanical tailings properties
Aside from looking at the dispersity properties, another part of the investigation focused on the mechan-
ical behavior of the tailings samples. For this purpose, the maximum shear stress and friction angle were
determined from an direct undrained shear test. As there were three different samples subjected to the
undrained direct shear test, the following section will comprehend the mechanical properties present in
various depths.
Before the test began, a membrane holding the sample, needed to be filled with sample material, which
has an area of roughly 20 cm3. In order to get the density of the samples, the sample mass was divided
by the volume of the cell. Here, the densities between the samples varied a lot. In S3 an average density,
which was formed from the three different tests, of 2.9 g/cm3 was found. On the other hand, S6 and S10
had an average density of only 2.4 g/cm3. This was due to differing mineral composition. However, it
could also be due to a mistake in the sample handling. Overall, the same area was occupied and filled
in a similar manner. Varying densities could lead to differences in the maximum shear strength. The
higher density in S3 was already observed in the dispersity properties mentioned above.
Another aspect that was found in the results of the test was the change in the sample height. Often,
sample height decreases during a shear test due to the normal load and accumulating compaction inside
the sample, which are impacted the material. However, in the case of the Aitik samples, it was found
that the sample height for the lower normal loads (12.5 and 200 kPa) increased over time. As previously
described, the tailings material has a fairly granular and pointy shape. In addition, the material is mostly
fine grained. Therefore, it could be assumed that most of the compaction immediately happens, when
the normal load is applied. The increased sample height happens over tine from the deflection of the
instrument with a steady increase for the lower normal loads until failure is reached.

4.5
Discussion of errors
The following section explains possible sources of errors found in the thesis. In the first place the sample
location needs to be pointed out. As the samples came from the TSF of Aitik from 2022, there is no
information available on the sample handling or the precise sample location. In connection with this
issue, sample S1 unfortunately also had to be ignored from the beginning of the project because it was
unfortunately already contaminated with other material when the sample was taken in 2022.
In addition, a previous degree project used some of the samples, which were also used in this project.
Therefore, the samples could have been previously contaminated through analytical procedures carried
out on them. Another general error source could be minor mistakes in the sample preparation as for
instance agglomerated material was present in S10 when the material was received in the beginning of
the project. Manual riffle splitting was used to split the samples for further analysis. Errors could have
happened here since this process was manually done on all samples. In addition, each sample consisted
of different masses and depth horizons.
Aside from the general set up another possible error source was presented in the mechanical properties
analyzed. The undrained direct shear test was only done on three samples with small variations in the
resulting normal forces due to the long duration of the analysis. Here the normal load was also limited to
only 750 kPa, which is insufficient according to the soil pressure gained from the sample depth and bulk
density of the samples. In S10 the soil pressure of around 856 kPa is present in the natural deposition
inside the TSF and only 750 kPa was used as normal load in the analysis 4. The set up of the instrument
in general also felt less precise since it was from the 1950s and used an old rubber membrane as sample
holder. It was barely held into place and it differed a lot how much material could be loaded into the
cell.
When it came to the mineralogical analysis it was not possibly to carry out a full mineral liberation
analysis with the SEM. The prepared samples gained from the previous project were not pretreated in
different size fractions, hence a mineral liberation analysis according to size fractions was not possible.
A new preparation according to different size fraction to find the optimal liberation was concluded to
be not time sufficient. However, an alternative analysis consisting of X-ray diffraction was chosen to
gain information on the mineralogy. This analysis also includes a semi-quantitative a phase analysis to
quantify the mineralogical changes over increasing depth 29.

5
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the tailings properties over time by using a 40 m long drill
core as the time bar. In addition, the change of the tailings properties over depth were highlighted
with respect to their dispersity, mineralogical and mechanical characteristics. The research questions
introduced in section 1.2 (3) will therefore be answered in the following:
5.1
Differences in the tailings properties
Are there significant differences in the tailings properties along the drill core?
The following section goes into depth on the differences in the tailings properties with the focus on
the dispersity, mechanical and mineralogical differences. Each section shows the differences found in
each property type with a link between each property type to answer the first research question. These
differences can then afterwards be linked towards events that happened in Aitik´s tailings facility. Since
there is a large overlap in the deviations found in most of the analyzes carried out, it can be assumed
that the results obtained from the tests are correct. The large scope of different tests helped to obtain a
precise picture of the drill core, which complements the obtained results from the previously carried out
degree project [19].
5.1.1
Differences in the dispersity properties
Multiple differences were found in the tailings properties along increasing depth. The findings centered
around the deposition depth from 4-34 m over a time difference from 2000, in the deepest samples, up
until 2019. Some of the differences occurred in similar areas in different analytical approaches.
Starting out with the initial classification a significant difference in color was found in S3, which seemed
a bit darker compared to the other samples. These samples presented a clear grey color. Afterwards the
dispersity properties were analyzed. The big difference in S3 continued in the density determination by
gas-pycnometer. Here, S3 had a higher density at 3.09 g/cm3 compared to the other samples. The other
samples had an average density of 2.81 g/cm3 with a small decrease found over depth. However, the
lowest density measured was in S6 (2.76 g/cm3). S3 and S6 showed unusual characteristics compared to
the data found in the literature for the density [27].
The abnormalities continued in the particle size distribution defined through sieve analysis and laser
diffraction, which was utilized for the particles below 38 microns. The PSD differed from the literature.
No, distinct differences over increasing depth were found as presented by R. Bhanbhro et al [2]. A similar
distribution is present in all samples with a fairly high uniformity with an Cu around 2.88. The curvature
is also relatively smooth and well-graded as the average Cc is 0.88. The particle size therefore gradually
changes over the particle size. Here, S5 differed the most from the above-described average coefficients.
However, again S3 presented the biggest difference in the sieve analysis with coarser material present in
the distribution. Most of the material is in the size range from 0.1 to 0.5 mm. Another abnormality
was found in S10, which had the distribution with the finest particles present. Therefore, it could be
stated that a slight difference is present over increasing depth. In the bottom part of the tailings pond,
more fine particles are present compared to the samples taken closer to the surface. In the fine particles

Differences in the mineralogical properties
Lastly, there were also some differences found in the mineralogical properties. The samples overall consist
of silicates from the feldspar or amphibole group. Quartz was also found in all samples, which is the
major mineral found in combination with biotite. However, the differences found show that actinolite
and anorthite is not present in the bottom of the drill core. Whereas zeolite in only found in the center
of the drill core. Zeolite also displays a lower density around 2-2.5 g/cm3, which could be an indicator
for the drop in the resistance for the shear test in S6.
One finding that explains the above-described differences is that S3 is the only sample with the occurrence
of sulfides. At the depth of roughly 7.65 m, which was deposited between 2016-2017. The main sulfide is
pyrite, which is present in combination with maghemite as an accompanying mineral. Maghemite forms
by weathering or low-temperature oxidation. The density of pyrite is 5 g/cm3 [43]. This explains the
characteristics found in the dispersity and mechanical properties. As S3 was always displayed as an outlier
in all tests carried out. This comes from the mineral composition in the tailings and especially from the
pyrite. It is surprising that pyrite was only found in S3 with fairly young sampling material. This could
be due to the deposition done in Aitik and the chosen sampling location. An early precipitation, which
is caused by higher densities seems plausible. As tailings are always mixed with water when deposited in
the tailings facility, particles with lower densities can migrate further out from the inlet. The opposite
applies for particles with higher densities, which would precipitate closer towards the inlet [30].

The discharge point was further away from the sampling location in earlier years as seen in table 7. It
moved progressively closer towards the sample point over the last 18 years beginning from 2002 at a
distance of roughly 140m until nowadays where the currents discharge point has surpassed the drill core
location. S1 is pretty much right below the discharge point from 2020. As earlier explained, sulfides
tend to precipitate faster when discharged in a mixture with water compared to other minerals as the
particle settling is mostly driven by density. Sulfides have a higher characteristic density compared to
for instance silicates. These events are leading to a link with the results found in the analysis. The
discharge that contains pyrite was established in 2016 in a close approximation to the sampled drill core
within a distance between 15-20m.
As pyrite, the major sulfide, was only found in S3 the next question arose why the modern tailings close
to the new discharge point did not continue to contain sulfides. The answer for this question lies in
the new sulfide handling done in the processing by Boliden. Starting from autumn of 2018 a procedure
regarding high sulphur thickener was taken into operation.
This procedure collects all high sulphur
tailings. These high sulphur tailings are then deposited in a separate impoundment. Before autumn
2018 all tailings were deposited in the main impoundment, from where the analyzed tailings have been
taken. The tailings from 2018-2020 are present in S1 and S2. This explains the singular detection of
sulfide in S3 and the questions surrounding this finding. Since, a detection of sulfide below S3 was made
more difficult due to the distance between the sample and discharge point. The distance to the discharge
point in S4 would already be 30-40 m. Therefore, it is most likely that the sulfide precipitated earlier
due to the higher density and the phenomenon regarding the settlement of particles as described above.

5.3
Evaluation of a potential revalorization and suggested further work
If so, how can these differences be used for geotechnical interpretation and evaluating the
potential for tailings re-mining and re-processing?
The results from the mineralogical analyses are clearly showing that there is no further value present in
the analyzed tailings material. According to the analysis done by X-ray diffraction, most of the tailings
material consists of silicates or other material without value such as quartz, biotite and other feldspar
minerals. The study proved a good separation between the valuable and inferior material in the Aitik
processing plant. A small amount of sulfides was detected but the majority of material is not of any
value nor of any environmental risk. However, the case study is not representative for the entire tailings
facility. For this purpose, a series of drill cores displayed in a representative grid would have been needed
to be analyzed in order to cover the complete picture. Therefore, assumptions can only be made for the
precise area surrounding the analyzed drill core. In addition, Gold is the major element of importance
that should not be present in the Aitik tailings. Since the copper grades are already low with high
recovery rates, a major financial loss from copper in the tailings is highly unlikely. However, even fairly
small amounts of gold which are lost in the tailings could lead to major financial losses in the mining
operation.
Based on the analyzed drill core, the beforehand described ore and recovery grades from Aitik are leading
to tailings with no financial value, which should be the case for an effective mining operation [1]. Due
to the sample location, which only represents a time coverage of roughly 20 years 9, an major area of
interest was missing. This would have been the historical Aitik tailings from the 1960s. These tailings
probably show a differing perspective for the topic of reprocessing as the processing technology advanced
significantly over the last decades. The old tailings location began close to dam A-B in the south-eastern
part of the tailings storage facility as seen in Figure 4. Asides from that there were also higher copper
grades present at the beginning of the mining operation, which could lead to more diverse findings in
the historical tailings [20]. It would be interesting to see if also other products in place such as silver or
gold could be found in the historical tailings. These products could hold major financial earnings and
therefore could also lead to the possibility of remining.
Lastly, the case study could also be expanded towards a general discussion on revalorization and the
potential of reprocessing of historical tailing sights. This research topic could be applied worldwide to
historical tailings facilities as it already done on some levels a continuing effort in the industry needs
to happen to see a change happening. As this topic also arises from the above mentioned background
surrounding the discussion about the SDG [14] from the UN and the CRM list [17] given by the European
Commission, which shows a need for the best available practices. One potential way of meeting these

5.4
General suggestion for action regarding further revalorization projects
A general call for action on the topic of reprocessing and revalorization of historical tailings deposits could
work in the following way. However, this is only a superficial suggestion, which needs to be adapted to
the industry standards and the local legislation.
1. The first step should be the general scouting of historical tailings storage’s with a possibility of
revalorization through a favorable deposition and mineralization in mind. This exploration could
be applied in multiple iterations with the processing potential in mind. Ideally a series of sites
could be identified, which would work with a similar processing approach.
2. Next up a complete assessment of the tailings properties including the mechanical, dispersity and
mineralogical properties needs to happen. The main focus of the evaluation should be on the
valuable minerals and their potential.
3. This assessment, that is similar to a geometallurgical program, should be done through multiple
exploration drill cores in a suitable raster for the chosen deposit. Most mines should have a good
documentation on their tailings, which could help to reduce the assessment time.
4. The creation of a geological block model should then subsequently happen in order to rank the
most feasible areas of the tailings facility. A clear succession is needed with the set cut-off grades
in mind to minimize the financial risks in remining.
5. A first technical assessment on the possibilities of extraction in combination with a preliminary
feasibility study goes in hand with the previous point. As the material has most likely a silty-
gravel like characteristic, it is very important to find a suitable way of extracting the most valuable
tailings without interference in the dam stability.
