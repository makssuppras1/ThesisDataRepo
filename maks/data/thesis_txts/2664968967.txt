Preface
This thesis presents the results obtained from the research carried out as part of the author’s Master’s
project during the period August 24, 2020 to February 8, 2021. The contents of this thesis are
supposed to constitute a more or less complete handbook on coupled-mode modelling of transverse
mode instability in thulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers. Thus, to the advanced reader, some sections
might seem unnecessarily detailed, particularly section 4 where the numerical implementation of
the theory developed in section 3 is presented. However, the reasoning behind this is to present
everything as explicitly as possible, so that in the future it will be relatively easy to continue the
research started here.
The research was carried out under the supervision of Associate Professor Jesper Lægsgaard, PhD
(DTU Fotonik).

Abstract
This thesis presents a coupled-mode model of transverse mode instability in high-power
thulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers, which takes the effects of gain saturation into account.
The model is derived from the time domain Hemholtz equation and is based on a thermal
perturbation of the refractive index of the ﬁber core. The model shows that efﬁcient power
transfer between the fundamental and higher-order modes of the ﬁber can be induced by a
nonlinear interaction mediated through the thermo-optic effect. The coupled-mode model
is compared to the numerical results of a more general beam propagation method model
and reasonably good agreement is found. The coupled-mode model is then used to study
the wavelength dependence of the transverse mode instability power threshold for a given
ﬁber design. A strong dependence on wavelength is found, and although not immediately
obvious, this dependence is attributed in large part to the energy discrepancy associated
with pumping the ﬁber at one wavelength and amplifying a signal at another. Thus, the
highest transverse mode instability thresholds are found at shorter wavelengths, which
are closer to the pump wavelength. For the range of wavelengths examined, the power
efﬁciency, i.e. the ﬁber ampliﬁer’s ability to convert pump power to signal power, does not
suffer signiﬁcantly despite the signal absorption cross section becoming more pronounced
at shorter wavelengths. Finally, three different pumping schemes have been modelled;
forward, backward and bidirectional. The highest transverse mode instability thresholds
are always found for backward pumped ﬁbers, while forward pumping gives the lowest
thresholds.

Introduction
Rare-earth doped ﬁber lasers and ampliﬁers have a long history as a backbone technology
in long distance optical communications. In particular, the erbium doped ﬁber ampliﬁer,
which can provide gain in the telecom band around 1550 nm[1]. Optical data signals prop-
agating over large distances must be periodically reampliﬁed to get a detectable signal at
the reciever end. While it is possible for each ampliﬁer stage to use electronic components
to read out the optical signal and drive a solid-state laser providing a new and stronger
signal, it is a complex solution which is difﬁcult to scale to high bit rates[2]. Ampliﬁers
working in the optical domain are much more desirable and have been important for long-
haul submarine cable systems. Thus, the method of choice for all-optical ampliﬁcation has
become rare-earth doped ﬁbers.
During the last couple of decades the output power of ﬁber lasers has increased consid-
erably, and ﬁber lasers have seen a surge in scientiﬁc and commercial interest, due to a
number of advantages over competing laser technologies. The primary advantage is that
ﬁber-integrated laser systems are very robust against external pertubations and environ-
mental disturbances. In contrast, laser systems where beams propagate in free space are
sensitive to alignment errors, and lasers based on bulk solids often suffer from instabilities
due to, e.g. heating and degrading the quality of the output beam. Fiber lasers, on the
other hand, have near diffraction-limited beam quality, due to the single-mode guidance in
the ﬁber core. Additionally, ﬁber lasers are less affected by thermal effects caused by heat
generated in the gain medium due to their large surface-to-volume ratio. On the negative
side, the long length and tight conﬁnement of the beam make the ﬁber lasers more suscep-
tible to detrimental nonlinear effects, which limits the output power scaling. Therefore, a
large research activity has been directed at pushing these limits as far as possible. Kilowatt
signal power levels have been achieved in ’almost’ single-mode ytterbium-doped ﬁber am-
pliﬁers operating near 1 µm and thulium-doped ﬁbers operating near 2 µm[2,3]. It should
be mentioned that efforts to scale the output of power of early laser architectures, e.g. the
rod laser, found that thermal effects distorted the output beams and strongly reduced their
spatial coherence, severely restricting the use of laser sources in real-world applications.
In order to solve this problem, new laser architectures were developed, which tried to
mitigate the thermal problems by changing the geometry of the active medium to obtain
a larger surface-to-volume ratio. The most successful of these geometries are the slab,
thin-disk and ﬁber lasers. From these, in fact, the ﬁber laser is the one that currently holds
the record in the highest emitted average power with neat diffraction-limited beam quality.
This fact is remarkable considering that ﬁbers are made of glass, which has a thermal
conductivity that is roughly ten times lower than that of the crystals other laser materials
are made of[4].
The nonlinear effects that represent the strongest limitation for the generation and ampliﬁ-
cation of high-peak-power laser pulses in an active optical ﬁber are: Raman and Brillouin
scattering, self-phase modulation (SPM), and self-focusing[5]. The latter depends on the
peak power of the pulses and represents the ultimate limit for this parameter in a ﬁber,
since going above the limit leads to the destruction of the ﬁber material. The Raman and
Brillouin scattering and the SPM, on the other hand, depend on the intensity of the light
in the ﬁber core and can, therefore, be effectively mitigated by reducing the intensity of
the signal. In order to achieve this, it is possible to increase the size of the signal core in
the ﬁber, which increases the mode ﬁeld diameter (MFD) and thus lowers the signal inten-
sity[1]. This approach can be used in both continuous wave (CW) and pulsed operation
and although it only mitigates intensity-dependent nonlinear effects, it is desirable from a
practical point of view; a larger ﬁber core leads to a higher pump absorption. This, in turn,

In 2009/2010 it was observed how the Gaussian output beam of a high-power, rod-type
ﬁber laser system suddenly broke apart and started to ﬂuctuate once a certain average
power level was reached[4]. Then, around 2011, came the ﬁrst reports[12,13,14] of a new
"mode instability" phenomenon in ﬁber lasers, which shall be referred to as transverse
mode instability (TMI) throughout this thesis. This instability manifested itself as a sudden
degradation of the beam quality and pointing stability of the signal as the average output
power was increased beyond a certain threshold value, which depended on the particular
ﬁber ampliﬁer. The sudden onset of beam ﬂuctuations, as well as the dependence on
average power, pointed towards TMI being the ﬁrst representative of a thermally induced
nonlinear effect observed in an optical ﬁber. TMI has become one of the main obstacles
encountered in attempting to scale single-transverse-mode outputs from ytterbium doped
ﬁber ampliﬁers to still higher powers[3]. From Figure (1.1) it can be seen that no increase
in record average power for Yb-doped ﬁber laser systems has been recorded since the
discovery of TMI. It should be mentioned, that new points are expected to be added to
Figure (1.1) in the near future, as the TMI related research of the past 10 years is starting

to bear fruit[4].
Early experimental observations of TMI showed that the output intensity distribution of a
ﬁber laser system operating above threshold was not stable in time, which pointed towards
the appearance of higher-order modes (HOMs) at the ﬁber output. This unstable behaviour
is schematically illustrated in Figure (1.2) for four different states, i.e. intensity distribu-
tions, of the output beam. The distribution of the different transverse modes at the output
of the ﬁber is not stable in time, and thus, this observation led to the effect being referred
to as "transverse mode instability". Later, more detailed experimental studies of TMI has
utilised high-speed cameras to accurately resolve its spatial and temporal dynamics. The
conclusions drawn from these studies were:
• The beam ﬂuctuations happen on a millisecond time scale,
• The beam is at all times a coherent superposition of two or more transverse modes,
• Between the stable beam operation state (below threshold) and the unstable beam
operation of TMI (above threshold) there is a third transition state, characterised by
periodic beam ﬂuctuations.
These ﬁndings provide important theoretical insight into the physical origin of TMI. The
measurements suggested that TMI was the result of a phase-matched process that allows
for energy transfer between two or more transverse modes of the ﬁber. Now, although
this energy transfer seemed to be the most likely cause for the ﬂuctuations in the output
beam, a second explanantion was also possible: that the ﬂuctuations are only a result of
the change in relative phase between the transverse modes. This second hypothesis was
put to the test by using an intensity-based mode reconstruction algorithm on intensity
pictures captured with a high-speed camera[13]. The results of this analysis lead to the
conclusion that a transfer of energy between the different transverse modes of the ﬁber
did indeed occur after the onset of TMI. However, the relative phase between the modes
appeared to change in a similar fashion. Therefore, it can be concluded that the apparently
chaotic ﬂuctuations of the output beam are a result of the combination of this modal energy
transfer and the change in relative modal phase.

The reasons for this robustness against TMI are not fully understood yet, although Smith
and Smith[3] suggest that strong cross-relaxations play a signiﬁcant role in weakening the
thermally induced grating. In any case, the experimental observation of TMI in a thulium-

which is not tied to any particular laser active ion.
For the reasons mentioned above, this thesis aims to develop a coupled-mode model of
TMI in Tmthulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers, adopting the formalism presented by Hansen
and Lægsgaard[17] and using the thulium rate equations presented by Smith and Smith[3].
The goal is to formulate and implement a computationally fast model that can reproduce
the results of the much more general STRS model presented by Smith and Smith[3] within
a reasonable margin of error. A coupled-mode model of this kind would enable large
parameter sweeps to be run, and potentially aid in uncovering the theoretical explanation
for the robustness against TMI of thulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers.

Theory
In this section some aspects of the theory of optical ﬁbers shall be reviewed. This review
will serve as the foundation for the work presented in this thesis. In section 2.1, the electro-
magnetic theory of step-index ﬁbers is discussed, with special emphasis on the transverse

index nc surrounded by a much thicker cladding with a slightly lower refractive index ncl.

and will not be considered in the analysis. This approach is valid if the guided modes of
interest are sufﬁciently conﬁned to the ﬁber core, and thus have no appreciable overlap
with the coating.

Optical ﬁbers of this kind are typically made of highly pure silica (SiO2) glass, which has
a refractive index of nSiO2 ≈1.44 at a wavelength of λ = 2 µm. The necessary index
difference in order to have guided modes is achieved by doping the core, typically with
germanium. This increases the refractive index of the core relative to the pure silica
cladding. The precise index contrast is determined by the dopant concentration, and index
contrasts ranging from ≈10−3 to 0.05 can be achieved in this way[1].
Typical core diameters range from a few µm for single mode ﬁbers to several hundred
µm for heavily multimode ﬁbers. As discussed in section 1, single-mode ﬁbers have the
advantage of higher beam quality and higher data transmission rates, but the smaller core
required for single-mode guidance makes them more susceptible to unwanted nonlinear
effects[1].

2.2
Active ﬁbers and thulium doping
By doping the guiding core of a ﬁber with atoms that have an electronic transition at the
desired frequency, optical ﬁbers can be turned into lasers and optical ampliﬁers. Optical
pumping of these atoms can create a population inversion, meaning that the number of
electrons in the upper state of the transition in question exceeds the number of electrons
in the lower state. The ﬁber can then amplify the optical signal propagating in a mode
conﬁned to the core at the right wavelength, as the signal stimulates transitions from the
excited state to the ground state. In recent years, rare-earth elements such as erbium,
ytterbium and thulium[1] have been used as dopants. The work presented in this thesis is
concerned only about thulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers and the discussion in this section will
be restricted to this dopant only.
2.2.1

ﬁbers even though unit quantum efﬁciency corresponds to roughly 40% power efﬁciency.

the doping concentration of thulium ions should exceed the 2% doping limit of silica[3].
Therefore, thulium-doped ﬁbers are usually based on silica glass with several percent Al
doping[23,23,24]. The work presented in this thesis will be concerned only with this exact

• The radiative and nonradiative decay rates can be truncated into total decay rates

2.2.3
Heat deposition
Regardless of which rare-earth element is used as the ﬁber dopant and which pumping
scheme is employed, the signal wavelength must be greater than the pump wavelength. In
the ampliﬁcation process, pump photons are converted into signal photons of lower energy.

phonons in the silica host and hence converted into heat. Since the refractive index of silica
is sensitive to temperature, this quantum defect heating can lead to a thermal nonlinear
effect in rare-earth doped ﬁber ampliﬁers.

3
Coupled-mode equations
In this section, coupled-mode equations for a thulium-doped ﬁber including the thermo-
optic effect will be derived. First, a brief overview of the theoretical description TMI is
given. Then, in section 3.1 the laser gain equations, given by Eqautions (2.56), in terms
of power are derived from time domain Hemholtz equation by introducing the gain due to
rare-earth doping in the imaginary part of the relative permittivity. This is done for two
reasons: ﬁrst, to justify the way the gain is included in the coupled-mode formalism of
this thesis; second, to give a relatively simple example of the steps involved in deriving
coupled-mode propagation equations. Some of the steps and intermediate results in the
derivation of the full coupled-mode model are quite long and will be skipped for brevity.
In section 3.2, coupled-mode propagation equations for the complex mode amplitudes are
derived by adopting the formalism presented by Hansen and Lægsgaard[17] and adapting
it to the case of a thulium-doped ﬁber. Finally, in section 3.3, the propagation equations
for the mode powers Pi are derived from the results of section 3.2.
Now, it was mentioned in section 1 that experimental observations suggest that TMI is the
result of a thermally induced nonlinear effect, which leads to a transfer of energy between
guided orthogonal modes in a ﬁber ampliﬁer. In principle, such an energy transfer can
only be the result of a phase-matched process. A known optical element that does allow
for the exchange of energy between transverse modes in an optical ﬁber is a long period
grating[2] (LPG). A LPG is a periodic or quasi-periodic modiﬁcation of the refractive index
of the ﬁber with a period that is signiﬁcantly longer than the signal wavelength. In order
to enable energy transfer between two orthogonal modes, the period and symmetry of the
index change in the LPG have to be similar to that of the mode beating pattern (MBP).
After the ﬁrst experimental observations of TMI, it was proposed that a thermally induced
LPG could be responsible for the observed energy transfer between modes[26]. This theory
of the origin of the effect has been widely accepted by the scientiﬁc community and has
led to the view that TMI is a manifestation of STRS in optical ﬁbers.
The generation of such a thermally induced LPG can be explained by considering the MBP
between two modes. Even when most of the signal seed light is injected into the fundamen-
tal transverse mode LP01, some fraction of the seed power would be present in a HOM, e.g.
LP11. The presence of two modes would, due to the difference in propagation constants,
lead to a MBP that oscillates along the length of the ﬁber. Pump light is preferentially
absorbed in regions of higher signal intensity where the population inversion is lower.
Now, due to the quantum defect, a certain fraction of the absorbed pump is converted to
heat. This creates a heating pattern that resembles the MBP, which again creates a similar
temperature pattern. Finally, the temperature pattern creates a refractive index pattern via
the thermo-optic effect, i.e. the temperature dependence of the refractive index of silica.
If the MBP is stationary, there is little or no phase shift between the thermally induced index
pattern and the intensity pattern, resulting in nearly zero net transfer of power between
modes. However, if the light in the HOM is slightly detuned in frequency from the FM,
the intensity pattern moves along the ﬁber. As a result the temperature pattern will also
move, but it will lag the intensity pattern due to thermal diffusion. This lag produces the
phase shift necessary to transfer energy between modes. As shall be shown in sections 3.2
and 3.3 the coupled-mode formalism of this thesis relies heavily on the oscillating intensity
pattern and predicts the maximum transfer of power to occur for a HOM slightly detuned
in frequency. Now, throughout the derivations in this section the effects of dispersion will
be neglected. This is justiﬁed by only considering frequencies offset by a few kHz from
the carrier frequency. All nonlinear effects intrinsic to the ﬁber material, other than the
thermo-optic effect, will be neglected. Finally, losses due to absorption and scattering are

4.1.1
Solving the rate equations
In section 2.2.2, the steady state rate equations for thulium-doped silica glass were set up
from the simpliﬁed thulium energy diagram in Figure (2.4B). Working under the steady-
state approximation means that the energy level populations of the thulium-doped silica
core responds instantaneously to a change in pump and signal power. This approximation
is only valid if the populations converge to their steady state values in less time than the
inverse of the frequency offset associated with the highest nonlinear gain of the HOM. This
time is approximately equal to the thermal diffusion time across the core[26]. Now, for the
ﬁber deﬁned in Table (3) with a core radius of rc = 12.5 µm the thermal diffusion time is
about 175 µs, corresponding to a frequency of approximately 6 kHz. As shown in Figure

5
Results
In this section the results obtained with the numerical implementation of the coupled-mode
model will be presented. First, in section 5.1 the results of the model will be compared
to the results presented by Smith and Smith[3]. The BPM model used in their study is
more general and rely on fewer assumptions and approximations than the coupled-mode
model developed here. The price of generality, in this case, is long run-times, making the
model unsuitable for studies including large parameter sweeps. Next, in section 5.2 the
faster coupled-mode model implementation will be used to study the dependence of the
TMI threshold on signal wavelength in thulium-doped ﬁbers. As previously shown, the
heat load in a thulium-doped ﬁber is a function of the signal absorption and emission cross
sections as well as the signal frequency. Experimental measurements of the absorption
and emission cross sections of the 3H6 ↔3F4 transition in thulium-doped silica report
different values[34,23,22], and thus the TMI thresholds found with different datasets will
be compared. Furthermore, these wavelength sweeps will be carried out for ﬁbers with
different V parameters to study the TMI threshold dependence on the FM and HOM mode
proﬁles. Finally, in section 5.3 the robustness of the coupled-mode model against variations
in the cross relaxation coefﬁcient k30
11 will be examined. The value of this parameter is
found by requiring it to be in thermodynamic equilibrium with its counterpart k11
30 for a
given temperature. However, the temperature in the core of a ﬁber ampliﬁer may vary
signiﬁcantly depending on the cooling setup employed. As such, the TMI thresholds of a
forward pumped ﬁber will be found for a range of different temperatures.

5.2
Signal wavelength
In this section the TMI threshold dependence on signal wavelength will be examined. As
indicated by Figure (2.5), it is possible to achieve gain for a wide spectrum of wavelengths
when using thulium as a rare-earth dopant. The experimentally measured spectra of the
absorption and emission cross sections for the 3H6 ↔3F4 transition in thulium-doped silica
are shown in Figure (4.6). These will be used to adjust the pump absorption ga, signal gain
gs and heat load Q according to the wavelength. The signal absorption curve reported by
Peterka et al.[22] only extends to about 1800 nm. For longer wavelengths the absorption
cross section is then, for this speciﬁc dataset, interpolated by setting the absorption cross
section to 0 at 2100 nm. In practice, this means that the cross sections of wavelengths
above 1800 nm are very close to zero, but have some ﬁnite value.
For a ﬁxed ﬁber geometry the mode proﬁles will also vary with wavelength as the vacuum
wavenumber k0 enters the mode functions via a and b in Equations (2.26). However, by
keeping the V -parameter constant, the spatial distribution of the mode proﬁles can be
kept the same at all wavelengths studied. In this section, V = 4 is ensured by tuning the
refractive index of the guiding core slightly. This was found to have little to no effect on
the TMI thresholds. Nonetheless, it was done to keep as many parameters as possible
constant, so that the wavelength dependence of the thulium-doped ﬁber could be studied
in a somewhat isolated manner. All other ﬁber parameters than the signal absorption and
emission cross sections are kept the same throughout this section and are given in Table (3).
In the following a step-by-step approach will be taken, where the wavelength dependence
of different model parameters are studied, before the TMI thresholds are presented.
5.2.1
Wavelength dependence of model parameters
The coupled-mode model relies on the steady-state population fractions n0, n1 and n3
to express the pump absorption, signal gain and heat load in thulium-doped silica. As
such, it is important to check whether the steady state approximation can be expected to
hold for all wavelengths. Using the data for σa

6
Conclusion
In this project, thermo-optic mode instability in high-power thulium-doped ﬁber ampliﬁers
has been modelled using a coupled-mode model based on a thermo-optic perturbation of
the refractive index of the ﬁber material. Coupled-mode equations for the complex mode
amplitudes have been derived in the special case of a thulium-doped ﬁber core. The model
formalism was adopted from Hansen and Lægsgaard[17] and adapted to the thulium rate
equations presented by Smith and Smith[3]. A computationally fast and efﬁcient numerical
model has been implemented in P Y T H O N. The results of the model were subsequently
veriﬁed by comparison with the much more general and computationally expensive STRS
model presented in Smith and Smith[3]. It was found that if the coupling coefﬁcient of the
coupled-mode model was scaled by a factor of one over the refractive index of the core,
the results of the two models agreed very well in 3 different pumping setups; forward,
backward and bidirectional. The model was then used to ﬁnd the TMI power threshold
of a given ﬁber design as a function of wavelength. Experimentally reported values for
the signal absorption and emission cross sections was used to obtain the pump absorption,
signal gain and heat load of a thulium-doped ﬁber as wavelength dependent functions.
The results showed a strong dependence of the TMI threshold on the signal wavelength.
Speciﬁcally, very high TMI thresholds were found at signal wavelengths close to 1800 nm,
exceeding an output power of 30 kW for one of the three experimental datasets. The
modelled ﬁber, however, was about 30 m long and it is highly likely that other nonlinear
effects, not included in the model, would become prominent. Of the three pumping setups
modelled, the backward pumped ﬁber ampliﬁers were found to have by far the highest TMI
thresholds. This agrees well with the results of Smith and Smith[3] and is, in the context
of the coupled mode model, explained by a coupling coefﬁcient that is highly suppressed
along the entire length of the ﬁber. This strong suppression is attributed to a strong gain
saturation effect in thulium-doped ﬁbers. Furthermore, it was found that the TMI thresh-
old’s dependence on wavelength could in large part be attributed to a smaller quantum
defect for signal wavelengths closer to the pump wavelength.
Finally, a quick experiment was carried out in order to test whether or not using a temper-
ature independent cross-relaxation coefﬁcient k30
11 would affect the TMI thresholds found.
The conclusion was that the TMI threshold did not vary signiﬁcantly with different val-
ues for this coefﬁcient, calculated for the range of core temperatures going from 300 K to
1300 K. Thus, the approximation of using a ﬁxed value for k30
11 should be safe for most ﬁber
core temperatures that could be encountered.
In this project, apart from dynamically changing the length of the ﬁber ampliﬁers, the ﬁber
design was kept unchanged throughout. In the future it would be interesting to test a
range of different ﬁber designs and to try and optimise these designs in terms of the TMI
threshold power. As shown by Hansen[1] it is also possible to extend the semi-analytical
model developed here, to a full coupled-mode model allowing studies of the temporal
dynamics of TMI in thulium-doped ﬁbers. Furthermore, it should be fairly straight forward
to adapt the model to more advanced ﬁber designs, such as photonic crystal ﬁbers. Thus,
the work and results presented in this thesis open the door to many future research projects
on the topic of theoretical understanding and numerical modelling of thulium-doped ﬁber
ampliﬁers.
