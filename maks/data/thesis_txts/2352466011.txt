Abstract
The goal of the thesis was to investigate discolourations in concrete products.
Several dierent types of sand had been obtained from The Region of Southern
Denmark to be examined for possible uses in concrete production. The sand
samples have been examined using dierent tests.
A humus test was performed to determine the amount of humic acids in the
sand samples. However, iron- and manganese-oxides will react in the test as
well. A test of the exural strength for some of the samples was conducted to
conrm or reject the presence of harmful humic acid in the sand. To obtain
more knowledge about the content of minerals in the sand, both a TGA test
and XRD test have been performed. A Thermal and Moisture Cycles test have
also been performed to try force discolourations on mortar ags.
The tests show most of the sands have a high content of quarts, with sugges-
tions of iron-oxides/hydroxides also. The sand's colour itself does not seem to
inuence the outcome of humus tests. For several of the sand samples there
are very little strength loss in a exural strength test. It was also shown the
colouring material in one of the sand samples could be washed away with wa-
ter to a certain degree. The thermal and moisture cycles test did not give any
indications of discolourations over the course of the experiment.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project is looking into some of the problems both factories and buyers face
when using concrete for paving blocks, ags or kerbs. In an increasingly amount
of cases the products have to be replaced because of either discolourations or
damage. Only a few standards deal with these kind of products and even less
with the materials used.
For this project several bags of sand have been obtained from The Region of
Southern Denmark. The sand from some of these will be investigated to deter-
mine if the sand can be used for concrete production, and if the sand can be
processed to increase the quality of the sand.
A humus test is conducted to get a basis knowledge if the sand contains humic
acid or other materials reacting with the NaOH solution. A exural strength
test is conducted for a few of the sand samples. A petrographic analysis is also
conducted and in addition both an X-Ray powder Diraction and a Thermal
Gravimetric Analysis have also been conducted. As the inuence of the sand
on concrete ags and paving blocks is the main focus of the project, a thermal
and moisture cycles test is also conducted to simulate the weather cycles trying
to force discolourations on the surface faster.
In the thesis it will also be considered if Alkali Silica Reactions is a problem
in concrete products like paving blocks, ags and kerbs and if there is ways to
reduce the damage from these reactions in the products. A visual inspection of
an area eected by ASR on Digevej is considered as a part of this.

Introduction
Problem Denition
Discolouration and cracks due to alkali silica reactions has become a rising prob-
lem in concrete tiles. At this time there is no requirements or standards for con-
crete tiles besides the frost resistance. For this reason, manufacturers normally
use the cheapest sand available for the production of concrete paving blocks,
ags and kerbs.
The sand use is often of low quality and can often contain iron, manganese,
humus or all of these. Further the risk of components such as opaline int making
basis for alkali silica reactions can be found in these sands as well. Therefore, it is
suspected that the sand may play a big factor in the discolouration and cracking
of tiles. This project will try to investigate if this is true by performing tests and
investigating the concrete tiles afterwards. The investigation will be performed
on self-cast concrete tiles, where the origin of the sand in the concrete will be
known. Some test elements delivered from outside may be tested alongside the
self-cast tiles.
First the sand will be tested for humus, using the method from DS/EN 1744-
1:2009, if this test shows the possibility of humus acid in the sand it will be
tested further, else it is discarded. After the humus test, prisms with the sand
and reference prisms with sound quartz sand will be cast and its strength tested
and compared. This will indicate if it is harmful humus acid in the sand, or the
color change in the humus test mainly comes from iron and manganese. If this
is the case, the sand can properly be used for normal concrete paving blocks,
ags and kerbs.
The last test will be done most likely accordingly to the method in EN 16306:2013.
This test method replicates the moisture from the ground travelling up through
the tiles from the sun heating the to upper side of the tile. This method may
be adjusted to increase the eciency of the tests.
When the tests are completed the amount of iron-ions should be measured in
the top of the tile, and the possibility of iron-ions eect on discolouring can be
further studied from this. Further the tiles can be investigated using microscope
and thin sections prepared from this specimens.

Concrete
In many construction projects today, concrete is they main building material.
It is used both for structural purposes as columns, decks and walls, but it is
also used to make concrete ags, kerbs and blocks. For some of these the visual
outcome of the products is very important as they will be visible, where others
will be hidden behind paint or façades. In many cases the strength of the product
is the main focus, but the appearance can not be neglected as the concrete is

The concrete used must be of a proper quality for each individual project to
full the specic purpose. Each of the materials used in the mix have dierent
ways to inuence the nal outcome, so each part must be considered before
casting.
Because of the many aspects, there have been made several standards concerning
both the materials, process and the nal product of concrete. These standards
focus on the structural elements like strength and frost resistance and does not
have requirements towards the visual aspects.
The chapter explains the basics of the concrete and some core elements of the
recipes to create the right concrete for a given situation.

2.1
Basics of Concrete
Concrete is made by mixing three basic categories being cement, water and
aggregates. Cement and water makes the basic paste while the aggregates mostly
works as ller material in the concrete.
The rst category, cement, is the binding material in the mix.
The cement
contains the particles and elements needed in the hydration reactions.
The
cement is the most expensive part of the concrete, because of this, materials
like y ash have been proven to be able to replace some of the cement while still
keeping most of the properties. The cement reacts with the water to make the
cement paste which acts as glue between the other materials and provide the
strength needed in the concrete.
The water inuence the strength and workability of the concrete, and the amount
of water is normally referred to through the water/cement ratio. The lower the
water/cement ratio is, the stronger and more dense the concrete will be. On the
other hand, a higher water/cement ratio will make the mix more uid, giving the
mix a better workability. There are dierent methods used to cast the concrete,
making dierent water/cement ratios preferable. When casting larger elements
a strong concrete is wanted but still uent enough to ll the forms.
When
smaller products is made, it is possible to cast the concrete under pressure and
vibrating the forms at the same time, and therefore getting workability even
with a low water/cement ratio.
The last category, the aggregates is mostly the ller in the concrete. The aggre-
gate consist of materials like sand, gravel, crushed stone and recycled concrete.
It is important the materials size have a good distribution. The size distribu-
tion inuence the amount of binder needed. The large aggregates will have gaps
where the smaller aggregates have to ll up. When a very ne mix like mortar
between bricks is needed, only small aggregates is used. Since most aggregates
is stone of dierent sizes they have a high strength and does not weaken the
concrete.
Besides the basic categories, some mixes have additional material added to
change the concrete in dierent ways.
These are called admixtures and can
inuence the mix in a range of ways, all from making the concrete more uid,
setting faster or colouring the concrete. The hydration process in the concrete
is described in Appendix B

2.2
Dierent Cement
The most common cement is the Portland cement. This is made by heating
limestone with materials such as clay.
The cement factories make a process
called calcination creating calcium oxide. This is blended with other materials
to ultimately getting calcium silicates such as Alite and Belite.
Many other types of cement exist for specic purposes. Much of the cement
blend in these are the same as the normal Portland cement, with just a few
changes or replacements in the mix.
• Sulfate resistant cement, are used where the soil or ground water have
a higher than normal sulfate concentration. The cement is made with a
low amount of Tricalcium aluminate, since the sulfate will react with the
aluminium and thereby expand the concrete.
• High early strength cement, can be used when the forms need to be re-
moved early, when the concrete need strength to continue the construc-
tion or similar conditions. The cement is made like normal cement but
grounded ner increasing the reaction surface of the cement and giving a
faster reaction.
• Air-entraining cement, can be combined with most other cement types.
Small air-entraining materials are added to the blend and give the concrete
better resistance to freezing and thawing.
This however is more often
solved when making the concrete mix and not in the cement.
These are just some of the existing cement types. Adding to this is the blends,
that combine dierent types of cement to suit special needs or take advantage
of materials found in the local area for the production. An example is blast
furnace slag cement that use iron slag replacing the normal Portland clinker.
The blast furnace slag is a by product of iron and steel-making. [9].

Humic Acids
Humic acids are organic material which are the major part of the organic con-
stituents found in many soils with plant life, like forests and elds. It is also
found in the soil under lakes and rivers where animals and vegetation thrives.
The humic acids are a mixture of molecules. Some of the molecules have phe-
nolic and carboxylic groups and are the most reactive parts of the humic acids.
Since the humic acids is found in many soils, there is a risk the sand and gravel
used for the concrete contains more or less humic acid. If this substance gets
into the concrete mix it can slow down or even stop the curing of the concrete,
preventing the concrete of reaching its expected strength. The humic acids of-
ten have a brownish colour and could be suspected to give discolourations to
the concrete as well. Because of the harmful eects, requirements towards the
amount of humic acid in the aggregates used for the production of concrete have
been made. The most common test method is explained later in chapter 6.

3.2
Eects of Humus Acid in Concrete
There is little knowledge of how humic acid react with fresh concrete. Many
dierent tests have been conducted to try gure out how this works out. The
humic acid is as the name say an acid, and so react with the alkaline solution
in fresh concrete. This may be the reason it often reduce the strength of the
concrete when curing. Especially in the early stages the humic acid impact the
concrete making it much weaker. This could be from the acid reacting with Ca

of early strength [11].
The use of humic acid is on a very experimental level and the general opinion
still categories humic acid as a harmful substance to the concrete mix reducing
the overall strength both in early and later stages signicantly.

Iron-oxides
When mixing concrete iron can get into the mix from dierent sources.
In
most cements there is Brownmillerite which contains iron. Reinforcement bars
is another source that could supply iron-oxides to the concrete. The sand used
could come from inland gravel pits and can contain many dierent minerals.
This can be in the form of Ferrihydrite which is found in many areas of the earth
surface. It forms in many types of environments and can get into the concrete
mix from many sources. The chemical formula for Ferrihydrite is Fe2O3·0.5H2O
and belongs to the iron(III)oxide-hydroxide group.

several other variation exist. Dierent phases of iron(III)oxide also exist and
have dierent names depending on the phase. Normal iron-oxides is not soluble
in water, but iron-hydroxides can exist in water.

In concrete the pore solution will have a very high pH and
would suggest any Ferrihydrite would be transformed to Goethite [12]. If the
Goethite is then transported outwards to the surface, the pH is going to drop
and formation of more solid iron-oxides will occur. This would suggest that

Alkali Silica Reactions
Two forms of alkali-aggregate reactions is known. Alkali silica reactions or ASR
is the most common. Much less common is the alkali-carbonate reaction. For
this reason only ASR will be mentioned here.
Many researchers think the process is as explained here. It should however be
noted this is not the opinion of all researchers and dierent theories exist as
well.
5.1
Reaction with Concrete
ASR i caused by reactive aggregates. The reactive elements contain silica which
reacts with the hydroxyl ions in the pore solution. This produces a gel much like
that of pozzolanic reactions. However in the pozzolanic reactions no expansion
happens. The gel produced by ASR increase in volume as it takes up water.
The reaction happens over two steps and is shown in Equation 5.1. The rst
step creates the silica gel with the formula H2SiO4.
Ca(OH)2 + H4SiO4 →Ca + H2SiO4 + 2H2O →CaH2SiO2 · 2H2O
(5.1)
Pozzolanic reactions only takes place in Ca rich solutions where small pozzolanic
particles react, as in the early stages of the concrete hydration. ASR on the other

6.1
Petrographic Analysis
This type of analysis can in basics be described with 3 steps. First a visual
inspections with the naked eye, grain size, colour etc.
Second step is using
a stereo microscope with 10-60 times magnication. In this step the type of
grains and minerals that is recognisable and if there is coating or precipitation
of any sort. The third and last step is point counting, using thin sections in
polarisation microscope.
Visual Inspections
Visual inspections is a manual approach to analyse the samples. Depending
on the goal of the analysis this is often a good start, as it can indicate what
should be looked for. This can be done on many dierent scales, from qualitative
inspection of a single object to a quantitative inspection of several 100 products.
It is done with the naked eye describing grain size, sand colour and texture of
the sand.
Microscope
The microscope have been used to inspect the sand samples in a more qualitative
way, trying to get an indication of the reason for colours in it. Also the texture
can be analysed to some extend with this method.
It is simply done by taken out the desired sample and look at it through a
microscope with 10-60 times magnication to see if anything interesting can
be seen on a micro level. Things like coating, agglomerate grains and special
grains is interesting in this matter. While able to have a close look, solutions
can be added to single stand out particles to see what happens to the individual
particle.

Point Counting
A point counting is made looking in a microscope through, thin sections of 20µm
with sand grains embedded in epoxy. The light can be ltered in dierent ways,
making determining what kind of grains the sample consist of.
Thin sections is made of the samples.
When the thin section is placed in a microscope between two polarizing lters
set at right angles to each other, the optical properties of the minerals in the
thin section alter the colour and intensity of the light. This is done with a 25-200
magnication.
The type of grains counted in this analysis is the following:
1. Porous int
2. Quartz
3. Dense int
4. Other rock fragments
5. Iron and manganese oxides/hydroxides
Setup
• Sample is sieved into two fractions, a 0-1 mm and a 1-4mm.
• The two fractions is prepared as thin sections.
• A microscope with a wire mesh is used.
• Type of particles wanted to be counted is determined, see 1. to 5. above.
• A point counter used to keep track of points.
Process
• The thin section is placed in the microscope with the wire mesh in the top
left corner.
• In each of the crosses in the wire mesh the grain type is determined and
number is noted.
• When all crosses are counted, the thin section is moved to the right a
distance equal to the with of the wire mesh and same procedure as above
is performed.
• When the right side of the thin section is reached, the same is done for
the next line.
• When the whole thin section is counted, the percentage of each type of
grain can be calculated.

6.6.2
XRD
X-ray Powder Diraction is used for the identication of unknown crystalline
materials and minerals in a powdered sample. An X-ray tube are used to gener-
ated rays directed at the sample. The interaction of the incident rays with the
sample produce constructive interference and the diracted X-rays are detected
by scanning the sample. All the diraction directions of the crystal lattice in
the minerals should be attained. Through the diraction peaks in the obtained
diractogram it is possible to identify the minerals. Each mineral has its own
set of unique peaks due to their d-spacings in their crystal lattices.
As a sample often consist of dierent minerals, several main peaks and secondary
peak may show up on the diractogram. By using dierent known peaks as
overlay, the composition of the sample can be indicated.
An example of a diractogram is seen on Figure 6.5 where the red line is the
XRD analyses of the sample, and the blue line is a known peaks for quartz.

6.6.3
TGA
Thermal Gravimetric Analysis is a method of thermal analysis in which changes
in physical and chemical properties of materials are measured.
This can be
either as a function of heat increase or function of time.
In a furnace a sample in a small crucible is heated and a reference in another
identical crucible is heated simultaneously. During the heating typical from 25
to 1100 ◦C the mass of the sample are measured versus the temperature with
high accuracy.
From the TG signal it can be seen if there is a change in mass (e.g. due to lossing
humidity, dehydrating from powders or decomposition) during the heating. The
second signal, DSC (Dierential Scanning Calorimetry) signal measure the heat
ow into or from the sample. This allows to measure the heat energy consumed
or released from the sample due to endothermic or exothermic reactions through
the heating process.

9.1
Sand Quality
The quality of the sands have been tested through a number of methods. The
rst test conducted was the Humus test. This test gives an indication of how
much humic acid, iron or manganese the sand contains. These make the colour
of the solution change because they react with the NaOH, for the purpose of
this discussion the elements reacting with the NaOH solution will be referred to
as reactive elements.
The humus test indicates varying content of reactive elements in the sand. Some
of the samples as A, B, H, I and J show a minimal amount of colour change in
the test. The samples D, E and F are in a middle ground and could potentially
have reactive elements. The two last asks C and G give an almost black result
and a high amount of reactive elements must be present. It should be noted that
the sand colour itself does not seem to have any indication of how the humus
test results will turn out. In some cases the result of the humus test seems to
follow the sand colour where in others the test have a very dierent result. The
sands C, D and H all have very similar sand colours, but the results from the
humus test is from all parts of the spectrum.
The samples taken for further testing are chosen to be A, D, E and F. Sample
A is classied as reference ask 2 colour, but is close to a ask 3, the other 3
sand samples are classied as having the colour equivalent of reference ask 3.
The discolouration is expected to come from some of the reactive materials, and
so the asks not showing any of these are discarded for further tests. The two

Discolouration
The thermal and moisture cycle experiment was designed to force discoloura-
tions of the mortar ags. Using the heat radiation on the top of the ags while
they were exposed to liquid water from the bottom should imitate the impact
the weather would have on ags or paving blocks and therefore force water to
travel from center of the ags to the surface. This transport of water can then
carry other materials with it to give the discolourations on the upper surface.
As reference a similar experiment was done with the same group of ags. This
second group have only been exposed to fresh air both from top and bottom.
Both test groups are exposed for 1 hour and then submerged in water again until
the next cycle. However, this experiment have not shown any real dierence in
colour of the ags from the start date and to the last cycle documented. Even
though there is a slight colour dierence on the ags, this seem to be from a
small dierence in the humidity of the ags. Especially for the ags only exposed
to fresh air, the temperature may vary a little from day to day. Several issues
could be the reason why the ags did not change. The discolouring material
could be bound to well in the structure without any big aggregates around, the
amount of discolouring material could be to small or the amount of cycles could
be to few, the later being the most likely.
From casting these mortar ags another issue came to the attention. Right from
the start some of the ags had a slight orange discolouration. This is best seen
on the ags were sand F had been used. It was decided to make a new cast with
the F sand and see if the colour could be removed from the sand beforehand.
To investigate if the colour could be removed with water, half of the new casts
was made with washed sand. Washing the sand was done using a 75µm sieve.
The sand was put into the sieve and running water was poured over. A wooden
brush handle was used to stir the sand making the grains scrub against each
other. The water passing through was coloured slightly orange when leaving the
sieve, and some of the materials colouring the ags was assumed to leave the
sand. When no more material got washed out, the sand was dried to prepare for
the new casts. The colour of the washed sand did not change colour or texture
compared to the unprocessed sand.
With the new casts it is seen the colour of the specimen still have a slight
discolouration. At rst the two new casts look very alike, but after the acetic
acid it seems like the specimen with washed sand does not have as strong a
discolouration as the specimen with ordinary F sand. Also on the specimens
not treated with acetic acid, there is a dierence to see after curing for 7 days.
This means some of the colour of the sand can be removed by water.
It is
possible bigger machines could force more of the colouring material out of the
sand, but it is more likely additional chemicals is needed to dissolve and remove

9.4
ASR
From the investigation of Digevej it shows how much impact ASR have on paving
blocks if not considered. More than half of the paving blocks was in some way
eected by ASR cracks at the time of the investigation and this is assumed to
get worse as time passes. The product test conducted on the paving blocks does
only take the freezing and thawing into account. If the cracks already exist
when the weather is freezing it can easily lead to much bigger cracks from the
expanding water.
From the EN 1338:2003 it is stated "The blocks shall conform to the following
requirements at the time they are declared suitable for use by the manufacturer".
The requirements referred to here is water absorption and resistance to freeze-
thaw with de-icing salts.
These types of concrete products, concrete ags, kerbs and paving blocks, do
not have any requirement to the materials used. This means any sand can be
used as long as the nal product can pass the product tests conducted either
before they are used. Because it states "at the time they are declared suitable
for use", these kind of tests will not have the proper methods to reveal ASR or
discolourations that will develop later in the product's life time.
Digevej is part of the neighbourhood Ørestaden Syd and a fairly new neighbour-
hood. The rst constructions in the area were build around 2001 and most of
the area have been constructed much later. This means the conditions of the
pavement blocks have emerged in a 5-10 year period. Comparing to what would
be expected of a concrete product this is a short time. As it will get gradually
worse, it will have to be replaced, expending resources or the pavement will
almost end up disappearing.

Conclusion
The sand quality is varying, but most of the sands is of good quality and does
not eect the strength of the mortar or concrete a great deal. Sands B, H, I

For visual purposes the sands D, E and F does have a slight discolouration and

The inspections on Digevej conrms ASR is a problem in concrete other than
structural concrete. Either there will be expenses in replacing the pavement or
the pavement will disappear. Standards need to be changed or new standards
created to set some requirements to the materials in addition to the requirements
to the nished products.

Hydration
The degree of hydration describes how much of the cement have reacted with
the water and thereby how much of the potential strength the concrete have.
This can be described using Power's model. If the concrete have access to water
the hydration is complete when all the cement have reacted, if the concrete is
cast in dry conditions the curing will stop when no more water is available.
Depending on the size of the element, the curing can take many weeks or even
years before the hydration is complete.
Cement consists of dierent components reacting with the water and gaining
the useful properties of concrete. The cement have four main components.
• Belite (2CaO · SiO2)
• Alite (3CaO · SiO2)
• Tricalcium aluminate (3CaO · Al2O3)
• Brownmillerite (4CaO · Al2O3 · Fe2O3)
Besides these four, gypsum is also often added as it is needed to control the
reactions of the tricalcium aluminate and Brownmillerite.
The Alite and Belite are the main contribution to the strength of the concrete
through reacting with the water. When the Alite react with water a number
==PAGE:84==
74
Hydration
of small steps happen, dissolving the grains and form chains of calcium-silicate
hydrate often referred to as C-S-H. The total reaction is shown in Equation B.1
where C3S is the Alite, H is water and CH is calcium hydroxide.
2C3S + 7H →C3S2H4 + 3CH
(B.1)
It should be noted that the stoichiometric relation of C-S-H is very approximate.
This are sometimes noted as in Equation B.2 because it can change depending
on the environment the reaction is in.
C3S + (y + z)H →CxSHy + zCH
(B.2)
where the "x" is called the calcium-silica ratio. This ratio can vary widely the
conditions used.
The Belite reaction is very similar to that of Alite but with less water reacting
and less CH produced. This process is also much slower and Belite does not
contribute much to the early strength but only to the strength at later stages.
The reaction is shown in Equation B.3
2C2S + 5H →C3S2H4 + CH
(B.3)
The two last components have much more complex reactions. These reactions
happen very fast and can make the concrete "ash set" because of the high
heat generation. They provide very little strength to the concrete and gypsum
is often added to change the reaction pattern, making the hydration process
slower to not risk losing workability to early.
