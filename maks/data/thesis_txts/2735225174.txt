Preface
This master thesis represent the final project leading to the completion of my studies at the
Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and attaining a Master of Science in Sustainable
Energy, specialising in Energy Conversion and Storage. The work was conducted between
September 2023 and February 2024 within the Department of Energy Conversion and Stor-
age (DTU Energy) and the project accounts for 35 ECTS.
The work in this thesis is part of the research project titled ”Faster oxygen evolution reac-
tion catalysts by surface tailoring (FASTER)” led by Professor Peter Vang Hendriksen at
DTU with funding from Independent Research Fund Denmark (Grant no. 2035-003080B).
My thesis contributes to the project objective by exploring innovative surface modification
techniques to enhance the eﬀiciency the oxygen evolution reaction (OER).
This project was completed in collaboration with PhD Veronica Jensen, building upon
pre-established ideas and concepts, along with some symmetrical cells that were already
prepared at the start. I contributed to the screen printing process, stepping in to assist
whenever there was a need for additional symmetrical cells. The tasks of nitrate prepara-
tion, infiltration and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) were undertaken together with
Veronica. I took the lead in cell mounting, testing and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)
with Veronica providing assistance through these stages. I conducted all data analysis,
calculations and illustrations for this report. This collaborative approach led to a well-
integrated project, advancing the objectives effectively.

Abstract
Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOECs) are highly eﬀicient electrochemical devices that can
generate hydrogen by utilising electricity from renewable energy sources. The produced hy-
drogen can be combined with carbon dioxide (CO2) to produce synthetic fuels, serving as
a sustainable solution for the heavy transport industry. However, the widespread adoption
of SOECs for industrial use is limited by a variety of factors including up-scaling capability,
economic feasibility and the durability of cell components. Furthermore, the high operating
temperature (800 °C) results in enhanced reaction kinetics and favourable thermodynam-
ics, but can also cause degradation of the electrodes. These degradation mechanism are
complex and lead to increased polarisation resistance, affecting the eﬀiciency and opera-
tional costs of SOECs. One way to mitigate this is to reduce the operating temperature to
around 600-700 °C. However, in this temperature range, one of the limitation is the oxygen
evolution reaction occurring at the oxygen electrode.
In this project, the objective was to improve the electrochemical performance of a well-
known oxygen electrode by modifying the electrode surface. Surface modification involved
infiltrating the electrode with binary oxides, each having a different acidity factor. This
allowed to determine how surface acidity impacts polarisation resistance and made it pos-
sible to deduce its potential as a performance descriptor. The project also explores the
effect of the number of infiltration cycles and loading on the polarisation resistance. With
equivalent circuit modelling used to get an insight into different electrochemical processes.
Lastly, a durability test was conducted for the one of the binary oxides used for infiltra-
tion. Electrochemical characterisation was conducted using Electrochemical Impedance
Spectroscopy (EIS), while Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was used for microstruc-
tural characterisation.
The results revealed that infiltration with basic oxides, such as BaO, K2O and Li2O, led
to a nearly 50% reduction in polarisation resistance at 650 °C. Conversely, acidic oxide
infiltration with CrO3 and SiO2 resulted in a 20-fold increase in polarisation resistance at
700 °C. This suggested a moderate correlation between the acidity factor and polarisation
resistance. Additionally, the adverse effects of acidic oxide infiltration were reduced by
subsequent infiltration with basic oxides. The equivalent circuit modelling illustrated that,
the majority of the polarisation resistance is associated with the electrode surface reaction.
Lastly, the durability test conducted for K2O showed that, the beneficial effects of the basic
oxide infiltration was limited to 26 days. Overall, the results attained in this project are
promising, showing that modifying the electrode surface with binary oxides can significantly
enhance electrochemical performance.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over the past decades, global energy systems have relied extensively on fossil fuels lead-
ing to significant challenges in environmental sustainability. As of 2022, over 80% of the
world’s energy was produced from fossil fuels resulting in the emission of over 39 billion
metric tonnes of CO2-equivalent [9]. However, in recent years, there has been a growing
shift towards renewable energy sources, such as biomass, solar and wind. The share of re-
newables (excluding hydro) in primary energy consumption reached 7.5% in 2022, a nearly
1% increase over the previous year [9]. Renewable energy sources offer the promise of clean
energy and a path way to mitigate climate change effects. They also have a huge poten-
tial and are capable of meeting global energy demands multiple times over [10]. However,
the transition from non-renewable to renewable energy sources is complex, due to the in-
termittent nature of solar and wind energy. This intermittence poses a massive challenge
in integrating renewables into a power structure that can reliably meet steady demand [11].
The transition to renewable energy is particularly crucial in sectors that are diﬀicult to
decarbonise, such as the heavy transport sector which includes shipping, aviation and long-
haul trucking. These sectors are significant contributors to global emissions with 7 billion
metric tonnes of CO2 emitted per year, which is approximately 4% of global CO2 emis-
sions [12]. Direct electrification, unlike its effectiveness in private transport, will not offer
the same solutions for these sectors. There is an inherent limitation of current battery
technologies, especially in terms of energy density and capacity. This is where the concept
of Power-to-X (P2X) becomes crucial.
P2X refers to the process of converting renewable energy into chemicals or fuels.
The
central technology to this conversion is electrolysis, a process that splits water into its
constituents elements, hydrogen and oxygen.
The hydrogen production is powered by
renewable energy and is referred to as ’green hydrogen’ [13]. Additionally, P2X emphasises
the use of carbon capture and utilisation (CCU), where CO2 is captured from industrial
processes or directly from the atmosphere [13]. By combining the captured CO2 with the
green hydrogen, it is possible to synthesise liquid hydrocarbons, such as diesel, gasoline
and jet fuel, as shown in Figure 1.1. These synthetic fuels can play a significant role in the
effort to decarbonise the heavy transport industry [14]. The synthesis of these fuels offers
a method for carbon recycling and provides a sustainable alternative to conventional fossil
fuels.
The development and implementation of P2X technologies represent a pathway
towards achieving a sustainable and carbon-neutral future, especially in sectors where
reducing emissions is particularly challenging.

Building on the critical role of electrolysis within the P2X framework, extensive research
targeted to improve the electrolysis technology is crucial. Research must aim to improve
eﬀiciency, minimise material cost, increase production capabilities and durability [15]. Key
efforts are directed towards improving the materials and manufacturing techniques of elec-
trolysis cells, essential for an effective water-splitting reaction [16]. Central to these ad-
vancements are the electrodes, whose performance directly impact the eﬀiciency and life-
time of electrolysis cells. Innovation in electrode materials are concentrated on enhancing
their catalytic activity and minimising degradation, which leads to improved operational
eﬀiciency and extended lifetime of the cells [17]. There is also a focus on developing novel
catalyst that offer high activity and suﬀicient conductivity at operating temperatures,
while remaining cost-effective [18]. Furthermore, the manufacturing processes of electroly-
sis cells aim to produce electrodes with increased surface area and optimal microstructure.
This can be achieved through creating nanostructured electrodes with more active sites,
facilitating faster and more eﬀicient chemical reactions. Overall, enhancing design and per-
formance through advancement in material science and manufacturing processes is crucial
for developing a more sustainable, eﬀicient and scalable electrolysis technology.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
2.1
Operating principles
Solid Oxide Cell (SOC) technology stands as one of the most widely researched and signif-
icant innovation in electrolysis technology. The electrochemical cells utilise a dense ionic
conducting electrolyte, which is situated between two porous electrodes known as the fuel
and oxygen electrode. This technology can be operated in two different modes, as solid
oxide electrolysis cells (SOECs) and solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs).
In electrolysis mode, the primary function is to split water into hydrogen and oxygen using
electrical energy and heat. At the fuel electrode, which is linked to an external power
source, electrical energy facilitates the electrochemical reduction of H2O into H2 and oxide
ions (O2–). The O2– produced at the fuel electrode migrate through the electrolyte to
the oxygen electrode [11]. The unique properties of the electrolyte allow for the passage
of ions and not electrons, while keeping gaseous reactants and products separated. At
the oxygen electrode, the oxide ions are oxidised to form molecular oxygen through the
oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and produced electrons are reintegrated into the external
circuit [6].
In solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs), the primary function is to generate electricity through
the electrochemical oxidation of a fuel. Oxygen usually in the form of air is supplied to
the oxygen electrode, which undergoes a reduction reaction to produce oxide ions. The
ions migrate through the dense electrolyte to the fuel electrode. At the fuel electrode,
H2 reacts with the O2– to form H2O and release electrons, which migrate through the
external circuit to reach the oxygen electrode. As these electrons migrate through the
external circuit, they pass through an electrical load before reaching the oxygen electrode,
thereby generating an electrical power output [19]. A schematic of the operating principles
for both technologies have been included in Figure 2.1.

2 Theory
2.2
Thermodynamics of electrolysis
The study of SOECs is particularly interesting, due to their high operational temperatures
(800 °C) offering several advantages. High temperatures accelerate the kinetics of the elec-
trochemical reactions occurring at the two electrodes, while increasing the mobility of the
oxide ions through the solid electrolyte. It also enhances the thermodynamics of the process
leading to superior eﬀiciencies compared to other electrolysis technologies, such as alkaline
(AEC) and polymer electrolyte membrane (PEMEC) [11]. AEC is a well-established, eco-
nomical and reliable technology, operating between 20-80 °C with pressures up to 30 bar
having a process eﬀiciency ranging between 62-82%. PEMEC is also a commercially avail-
able and reliable technology operating between 50-80 °C with process eﬀiciency at around
67-82%. In comparison, as a result of the elevated operating temperatures, the SOEC
technology can achieve electrolysis eﬀiciencies over a 100% [20]. In order to understand
how this is possible, the thermodynamics of the electrolysis process has to be analysed.
The thermodynamics of the electrolysis processes are defined by the total energy demand

When operated at thermoneutral potential, the electrolyser eﬀiciency is a 100%, as no

exceed 1 A cm−2 resulting in a higher rate of hydrogen production in comparison to other
electrolysis technologies [11]. However, sustaining this performance during long-term op-

electrolyser eﬀiciency can exceed 100%. However, the actual eﬀiciency in practice is lower
due to several factors, including ohmic losses, mass transport limitations and the over-
potential required to drive the electrolysis reaction [25]. This discrepancy becomes even
more highlighted in commercial application, where multiple cells are connected through
metallic interconnects to form stacks. The interconnects have a dual role: they provide
electrical connectivity between cells, separate and evenly distribute gases [11]. However,
additional problems arise as the components are made of expensive ferritic stainless steel
and can react with the electrodes to form complex oxides, that have shown to cause cell
degradation [26].
Therefore, there has been a necessity for even more costly interconnects to mitigate this
issue, which diminishes the economic appeal of SOECs. This has resulted in a significant
amount of research in trying to reduce the operating temperature to 600-700 °C, in order to
enhance the economic feasibility. The reduction in temperature has adverse effects on the
cell performance, as it leads to slower reaction kinetics and less favourable thermodynamics.
Therefore, the success of this strongly depends on the choice of materials used for cell
components as there needs to be a balance between durability, electrochemical performance
and cost-eﬀiciency.

2.3.1
Electrolyte
In a solid oxide cell, the electrolyte serves as a barrier between the fuel and oxygen
electrodes, and it must remain stable under redox conditions. This layer must be gas-
impermeable, thin, and compact to effectively prevent the mixing of reactants and products
on each side. The material of the electrolyte is required to have high ionic conductivity at
operational temperatures and very low electrical conductivity to avoid current leakage [20].
Presently, the most widely-used electrolytes are fluorite-type oxides, which feature a face-
centered cubic lattice of cations with anions filling all tetrahedral positions as shown in

2.3.2
Fuel electrode
In electrolysis mode, the fuel electrode acts as the negatively charged electrode (cathode)
and is responsible for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). This is a process whereby
H2O is electrochemically reduced to H2 and O2–, a reaction that is driven by electrons
from the external circuit. This reaction is a key component in water electrolysis and is
essential for eﬀicient hydrogen production. As a result, any material used for the fuel elec-
trode has to have excellent catalytic activity for HER with suﬀicient ionic and electronic
conductivity [20]. The thermal expansion coeﬀicient (TEC) should be similar to the elec-
trolyte to ensure suﬀicient adhesion and avoid cracking under operating temperatures [31].
Other properties required include suﬀicient porosity, electrochemical stability and simple
manufacturing techniques.
Currently, materials used for the fuel electrodes are typically nickel-based, due to their ex-
cellent catalytic activity and electronic conductivity. These are often paired with materials
like YSZ or CGO, known for their high ionic conductivity. As a result, state-of-the-art
fuel electrodes are typically made of a Ni-YSZ/CGO cermet (ceramic metal), a composite
material that offers an optimal balance of conductivity, catalytic performance and elec-
trochemical stability, making it highly suitable for use in fuel electrodes [32]. However,
there are several degradation mechanism associated with using Ni-YSZ as a fuel electrode.
Specifically during operation, the Ni particles are unstable and rearrangement in the mi-
crostructure can effect cell performance. Ni coarsening involves the agglomeration of Ni
particles, which leads to a decrease in the number of active sites [33]. Furthermore, this
can also lead to Ni migration away from the electrolyte-cathode interface causing a further
decrease in the active sites, as shown in Figure 2.4.

2.3.3
Oxygen electrode
In electrolysis mode, the oxygen electrode acts as the positively charged electrode (anode)
and is responsible for the oxygen evolution reaction (OER). This process generates oxygen
and excess electrons from oxide ions and therefore materials must be capable of eﬀiciently
facilitating this reaction [34]. The material must have a high catalytic activity for OER
and a large surface area to provide active sites for the reaction. In order to eﬀiciently trans-
port electrons and oxygen ions, there is a need for a high ionic and electronic conductivity,
along with a porous microstrucure for optimised transport of gaseous oxygen. Lastly, the
electrode material must be electrochemically stable and while its thermal expansion coef-
ficient must match other cell components [17].
One of the most researched and used materials for the oxygen electrode is strontium-doped
lanthanum manganite (La1−xSrxMnO3), due to its high electronic conductivity, porosity,
mechanical and thermal stability. However, due to its poor ionic conductivity, LSM is typ-
ically mixed with YSZ to create composite electrodes. This results in OER being limited
to the interface between ionic and electronic phases in the electrode bulk [17]. Despite ad-
vancements in the development of materials, LSM-YSZ remains a commonly used oxygen
electrode material in SOECs [35]. However, there has been development in materials that
inherently posses mixed ionic and electronic conductivities (MIECs).
MIECs are typically non-stoichiometric oxides often adopting a perovskite structure, which
is represented by the formula ABO3. The A-site is typically occupied by lanthanide metal
ions or alkaline metal ions, replacing these elements with smaller cations can create oxy-
gen vacancies in the lattice, increasing the ionic conductivity of the material. The B-site
is occupied by transition metal ions, such as manganese (Mn), cobalt (Co) or iron (Fe),
these contribute to the electronic conductivity due to their variable oxidation states [36].

In the MIECs, the reaction site is ex-

present certain challenges as they are expensive and come with environmental and ethical
challenges [37]. Furthermore, they exhibit a significant disparity in the thermal expansion
coeﬀicient compared to frequently utilised electrolyte materials such as YSZ [38].
Numerous studies have indicated that strontium (Sr) tends to segregate to the LSCF sur-
face causing a depletion of the transition metal concentration, which plays a key part in
the catalytic activity [39]. There are also issues with formation of secondary phases (SrO,
Sr(OH)2 and SrCO3) on the electrode surface, it is reported that these can either improve
or reduce the catalytic activity of LSCF. Firstly, SrO is electrically insulating and can form
a passivation barrier on the surface reducing the rate of oxygen ion exchange [40]. However,
reports have shown that infiltration of SrCO3 on LSCF shows synergistic catalytic activity
and enhances the kinetics of the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) [41]. Consequently, un-
derstanding the causes of Sr segregation and controlling the extent of surface enrichment
has become a key focus in oxygen electrode research [40].
Despite the aforementioned issues, LSCF still has impressive performance and robust dura-
bility. However, there is a growing interest in exploring cheaper Co-free materials with
a more compatible thermal expansion coeﬀicient and better electrochemical performance.

formula An+1BnO3n+1, where the A-site is occupied by a rare-earth or alkaline element

2.4
Infiltration method
Infiltration is a key technique in heterogeneous catalysis, crucial for immobilizing high
surface area catalytic particles onto porous support materials resulting in nanostructured
electrodes. This expanded catalytically-active surface area provides an increased number
of active sites for electrochemical reactions significantly increasing the eﬀiciency and ef-
fectiveness of these reactions [43]. The process can be carried out by wetting a porous
electrode in precursor solution containing the active material. This followed by a calcina-
tion at intermediate temperatures to create a thin-film coating or nanoparticles, as shown
in Figure 2.7 [44]. The precursor solution typically consists of a solvent, metal nitrates
and a surfactant. The purpose of the surfactant is to reduce the surface tension, such that
the solution can spread more easily over the surface of the backbone and penetrate deep
into the porous electrode [17]. By repeating this process, the infiltrated loading can be
increased. Different electrode functionalities can be achieved through infiltration, such as
tuning of the ionic and electronic conductivity, mechanical support and the catalytic activ-
ity. This is an extremely useful method for improving the electrochemical performance of
porous electrodes and is widely used in research and development. For example, it has been

The acidity scale for binary oxides is split into three categories: basic, amphoteric, and
acidic oxides, which is influenced by the metal or non-metal’s electronegativity and oxida-
tion state [51]. Basic oxides have a strong tendency to donate an oxide ion rather than
accept one. They are characterized by high electropositivity, meaning that they readily
lose electrons to form positive ions. Alkali, alkaline earth metals, lanthanides and some
transition metals form purely basic oxides and they have an acidity constant less than -5.
Amphoteric oxides can behave both as acids and bases, depending on the reaction condi-
tions and the reacting species [52]. They tend to have an acidity constant between -5 and
0. It should be added that some of the binary oxides in this range behave purely basic.
Lastly, acidic oxides have a tendency to accept an oxide ion and are typically formed by
metals or non-metals in high oxidation states. These are characterized by their positive
acidity constant ranging from 0 to 15.
2.6
Objective of this project
The main objective of this project was to improve the electrochemical performance of a LS-
F/CGO oxygen electrode within the intermediate temperature range of 600-700 °C. This
is a optimal temperature range for SOECs, as it offers a balance between eﬀiciency, capital
expenditure and lifetime of components. The infiltration method was used to impregnate
the electrode backbone with a variety of binary oxides to analyse the effects on the polari-
sation resistance. The correlation between electrochemical performance, infiltration cycles
and loading was analysed, with the one infiltrant undergoing a long-term durability test.
The project also explored how the surface acidity of the electrode influences the electrochem-
ical performance. Smith’s acidity scale was employed to systematically assess and select
binary oxides for infiltration. By choosing a range of binary oxides with varying acidity
levels, the project aimed to correlate the electrode surface acidity with its electrochemical
performance. Furthermore, the effects of dual-infiltration with both acidic and basic oxides
was examined. The electrochemical characterisation was conducted through Electrochemi-
cal Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS), while Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was used
to characterise the microstructure. Lastly, Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) was con-
ducted on the solutions used for infiltration to accurately determine the final loading.

The real part of impedance (ZRe) is associated with the resistive components in the sys-

usually over several decades, in order to identify several electrochemical processes taking

phenomena, such as ohmic contributions, charge and mass transfer across the electrodes
and interfaces [55]. This extensive data collection facilitates various analytical approaches,
particularly the utilisation of Nyquist and Bode plots, which are essential tools for inter-

Nyquist plots are a fundamental tool used for evaluating impedance data and are con-
structed by plotting ZRe on the x-axis and −ZIm on the y-axis. This graphical represen-
tation is crucial for determining the series resistance (Rs) and the polarisation resistance

(Rp) of the system. The series resistance is defined as the total resistance that the elec-
trons and ions encounter from flowing through the system and electrolyte. The inherent
resistive properties of the electrodes, electrolyte, current collectors and any other compo-
nent in the system contribute to this resistance. This resistance is primarily due to the
movement of ions through the solid electrolyte [56]. In contrast, the polarisation resis-
tance is a measure of the resistance to the electrochemical reactions. This resistance is
intrinsically connected to the kinetics of the electrochemical reactions and is influenced by
catalytic activity and microstructure of the electrodes, as well as the ionic conductivity
of the electrolyte [57]. A lower polarisation resistance is an indication of a more eﬀicient
charge transfer, thereby signifying superior electrode performance. Therefore, polarisation
resistance is a critical parameter in assessing and optimising the functionality of electrodes.

the total resistance is determined from the low frequency intercept.

3.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a technique used for examining the microstructure
of solid materials. It offers insight into material morphology and crystalline structure. The
technology uses accelerated electrons with high kinetic energy to probe the surface of a
sample in a vacuum environment. If this vacuum environment is not present, the electron
beam would scatter due to interactions with air molecules. When electrons reach the sur-
face of a sample, it results in an abrupt deceleration due to interactions with atoms in the
sample. This loss of kinetic energy results in the generation of various signals. The emitted
signals are subsequently detected and converted into a digital format, which is displayed
on a computer screen [59].
One of the signals generated due to the electron-sample interaction is secondary electrons
(SE). These are generated in the top layer of the sample, as a result of inelastic collisions
between the electron beam and the sample. Secondary electrons are generally considered
to have energies below 50 eV and are paramount in detailing the sample’s surface morphol-
ogy and topography. Another type is back-scattered electrons (BSE), which are generated
from deeper layers of the sample and have energies above 50 eV. BSE are crucial in high-
lighting compositional contrasts and are particularly beneficial for identifying different
phases in multi-phase materials. Additionally, characteristic X-rays are produced by the
displacement of inner-shell electrons and a key in providing essential data for elemental
analysis [60]. A schematic of a number of electron-sample interactions can be found in
Figure 3.2.

Thermogravimetric Analysis
Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) is an instrumental technique for analysing the thermal
properties and stability of materials. This method allows for measuring mass changes of a
sample as it is heated, cooled or held at constant temperature over time within a controlled
atmosphere [61]. The process involves placing a sample in a pan inside a furnace, where its
mass is continuously measured via a precision balance. The atmosphere around the sample
is regulated through a purge gas, which may be inert or reactive, depending on the desired
conditions for the analysis [61]. In TGA, the measurement output is a curve that plots the
relative mass, expressed as percentage, against the temperature. The specific points and
shape of the curve can reveal information about the sample’s decomposition temperature
and illustrate phase transitions. In Figure 3.3, a TGA curve for an alkaline earth metal
nitrate have been included showing the different phases in thermal decomposition.
Figure 3.3. Thermogravimetric analysis of a M(NO3)2 solution at ramp rate of 2 C min−1.
The TGA curve for alkaline earth metal nitrates typically exhibits a two-step decomposition
process [62]. Initially, there is a gradual mass loss due the evaporation of water, referred to
as the dehydration phase. This is followed by a more pronounced mass reduction signifying
the decomposition of the nitrate compound into metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide and oxygen.
The mass change over the duration of the TGA measurement allows one to determine the
amount of nitrate solution that is converted into metal oxide.

4.3.1
Symmetrical cell mounting
Before evaluating the electrochemical performance of the infiltrated symmetrical cells, the
cell’s area and thickness were determined using a digital vernier caliper and a digital
micrometer, respectively. For current collection, platinum (Pt) paste (64021015 Pt paste,
Ferro GmbH, Germany) was hand-painted on both sides of the cells to ensure high in-plane
conductivity. Several measures were also taken to prevent short-circuiting by ensuring that
there was no cross-plane conductivtiy. Subsequently, the symmetrical cells were positioned
inside the sample holders in the cell house, held in place between two layers of meshes. Two
different cell houses were used in this project, namely the Pt and Au cell house. Small
alumina weights were used to load the meshes, guaranteeing adequate contact for electrical
measurement. The cell house could hold up to four different samples and during testing it
was placed inside an alumina tube within a furnace. The furnace was capable of operating
at temperatures up to 1000 °C. The cell house and the furnace combination is referred to
as the ’test rig’.
The alumina tube were sealed with a Viton O-ring positioned between two metal flanges.
The two metal flanges were fastened together using four bolts, that were tightened using
a wrench key. Gas inlet and outlet points were situated on top the cell house, requiring
manual connection via tubes and valves. The internal atmosphere of the alumina tube were
regulated by a gas handling system, with exhaust gases expelled by a ventilation system. To
monitor temperature and atmospheric conditions inside the tube, two measurement probes
were inserted into the front of the cell house. These measurement probes were connected to
a thermocouple and a zirconia sensor close to the samples to determine the temperature and
partial pressure of oxygen (pO2). The RFC control system was employed for digital control
of the furnace’s temperature and gas flow, simultaneously displaying the temperature and
pO2 levels of the cells. A VGA cable connected the cell house to a multiplexer, facilitating
the switch between the four test samples. For impedance measurements, BNC connectors
were attached to both the front of the cell house and the impedance analyser. A schematic
of the full test rig and a sample holder in the Pt cell house can be seen in Figure 4.4 and
4.5, respectively.

4.3.2
Cell testing protocol
The test period was systematically divided into five distinct stages. The first phase in-
volved assessing the factors that contribute to the series resistance of the test rig and
determining how to reduce this.
This was followed by a preliminary screening period,
where the electrochemical performance of infiltrated cells were systematically determined.
This also included trying to determine a connection between surface acidity and electro-
chemical performance. Next, the correlation between cycles, loading and electrochemical
performance was studied. This was followed by conducting measurements on symmetrical
cells undergoing dual-infiltration with both acidic and basic oxides. Finally, the last stage
entailed conducting a durability test on one of the top-performing infiltrated samples to
determine the degradation of the oxygen electrode. For the series resistance analysis, both
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) and galvanodynamic measurements were
conducted using a Solartron 1260 impedance analyser or a VersaSTAT 3 potentiostat. Fur-
ther description of the protocol will be described in later sections of this report.
For the next four phases, the EIS tests were performed using a Solartron 1260 impedance
analyser in the frequency range from 1 MHz to 0.06Hz. The measurements were conducted
under open circuit conditions by applying a 50 mV AC signal amplitude. Air (pO2 = 0.21)
was supplied at a rate of 100 ml min−1 to the symmetrical cells. The ramp rate of the
furnace was 2 C min−1 and all samples were allowed to equilibrate for a period of 1 h before
the impedance measurement was carried out. During the screening and the subsequent pe-
riods, impedance measurements were conducted from 800 °C to 500 °C, in 50 °C intervals.

Series resistance analysis
In the initial phase of the project, an examination of the series resistance in the test rig was
conducted, to identify its contributing factors and explore potential reduction strategies.
The primary cell house used in this study was constructed from alumina and equipped with
Pt meshes. However, for this part of the analysis, comparison were made with another
cell house that incorporated gold (Au) meshes. As previously discussed, series resistance
represents the total resistance encountered by electrons and ions as they flow through the
system and electrolyte. In order to determine the series resistance of the cell houses, Pt
plates were used instead of symmetrical cells. This essentially short-circuited the system
resulting in an analysis focusing solely on the electrical conductivity of the system.

quency range from 1 MHz to 0.06 Hz. The measurements took place at three different
temperatures: ambient room temperature (20 °C), 300 °C, and 600 °C. They were con-
ducted under open circuit conditions and by applying a 50 mV AC signal amplitude. The
temperature in the furnace increased at a rate ramp of 2 C min−1 and each sample had a
stabilisation period of 1 h at the target temperature before impedance measurements were
taken. The following Nyquist plots illustrates the impedance spectroscopy data collected
from using Pt plates in two different cell houses.

During these experiments, the Au mesh in sample holder 4 broke, leading to the inability
to conduct measurements in this sample holder. The series resistance values obtained from
these measurement were also significantly different to previous values obtained in Table
5.1 and 5.2. After ruling out the presence of an offset voltage, it became apparent that
these variations were likely due to small changes occurring when mounting the Pt plates
in the sample holders.
Therefore, several measurements were carried out at room temperature to explore the
different factors that could possibly change during mounting. The experimental setup con-
sisted of Pt plates clamped between two meshes under the force of a small alumina weight.
The position of the meshes could move relative to each other, essentially meaning that the
Pt plate could be in different positions in the same sample holder. By randomly rotating
the position of the meshes, a notable 30% change in series resistance was observed. This
variation can be attributed to the altered contact area between the meshes and the Pt
plates with each new position. For example, decreasing the contact area leads to smaller
cross-sectional area for current flow resulting in an increased series resistance. Such changes
in the contact area could significantly impact the series resistance, possibly explaining the
variance observed in the results.
Additionally, by applying external pressure on the alumina weight above the top mesh, it
resulted in a 10% decrease of the series resistance. A key observation was that the position
of the alumina weight was not always optimal, causing the force of the weight to be unevenly
distributed. Uniform pressure distribution is crucial for consistent electrical contact. If the
pressure is uneven or concentrated in different areas for each setup, it can lead to varying
resistance measurements. Therefore, it became evident that the positioning of the alumina
weights above the top mesh was also an important factor in increasing the contact area.
Another key observation during these experiments were the presence of deformations and
ridges on the meshes. In certain alignments, these irregularities fit perfectly against the
Pt plates, optimising the contact area. At these optimal positions, the series resistance
was reduced up to 70%, highlighting the critical role of physical mesh alignment in the
reduction of the series resistance. A depiction of the ideal and non-ideal mesh alignment
has been included in Figures 5.3 and 5.4. Additionally, images of the Pt sample holder and
the Pt mesh has been included in Figure 5.5 and 5.6.

with the symmetrical cell testing.

Surface acidity as a performance descriptor
Surface acidity plays a pivotal role in electrode performance because it directly influences
the surface exchange kinetics and the mechanism of interactions at the electrode surface [69].
Several factors can effect the surface acidity of an electrode, such as oxidation states,
oxygen vacancies and acidity of any infiltrant. In Section 2.5, the Smith’s acidity scale
was introduced as a way of quantifying the acidity of binary oxides by assigning an acidity
constant, ’α’. In this project, the surface acidity of a pristine electrode was altered by
infiltrating binary oxides with varying acidity constants.
This section will explore the
correlation between electrochemical performance and the acidity constant to determine,
if surface acidity can be used as a performance descriptor.

increase in ln(Rp) suggesting an exponential increase in polarisation resistance with the

5.10
Equivalent circuit model
Equivalent circuit modelling is a way to represent the behaviour of an electrochemical
system and is often used to interpret impedance spectroscopy data. Therefore, this section
will present the results attained from fitting an equivalent circuit model to the impedance
data obtained from testing symmetrical cells. The process was carried out using Z-View.
These results will provide insight into the electrochemical behaviour of the symmetrical
cell and the effects of infiltration. In Figure 5.19, the equivalent circuit model fitting to
Nyquist data for a pristine electrode at 650 °C can be found.
Figure 5.19. Equivalent circuit model fitting to Nyquist data for a pristine electrode at 650 °C.
The equivalent circuit model fits relatively well with the Nyquist data at 650 °C implying
that the circuit model is a suﬀicient representation of the data at this temperature. In this
model, the element (L) arises from the inductance in the Pt wiring and the measurement
apparatus. The element (Rs) is equal to the series resistance. To model the arcs present
at different frequencies in the Nyquist plot, two (R∥CPE) in a series arrangement were
used. Here, R represents the resistance and CPE represents the corresponding constant
phase element for each arch. A CPE is used to represent the impedance of an imperfect
capacitor and the expression is equal to Y0(jω)n, where Y0 is the admittance, ω is the
angular frequency and n is a frequency exponent [71].
Each of the two arcs represent an electrochemical process and the subsequent resistance
associated with it. The high-frequency resistance (R1) is associated with the oxygen ion
transfer process occurring at the electrode-electrolyte surface. The low-frequency resistance
(R2) is ascribed to the electrochemical reaction occurring on the electrode surface [72]. As
a result, the sum of these two resistances (R1 + R2) is equal to the total polarisation
resistance obtained from the equivalent circuit model. The results obtained from the fitting
can be used to compare the contribution of R1 and R2 to the total polarisation resistance.

In the %∆R1 and %∆R2 columns, the percentage change in R1 and R2 from the pristine
cell have been documented. The results show that most of the basic oxide infiltrated cells
show a reduction in both of components of the polarisation resistance. This suggests that
the infiltration of basic oxides effectively enhances the oxygen ion transfer and the reaction
at the electrode surface. Significantly, the improvement is more pronounced for R2 com-
pared to R1, which implies that the infiltration has a stronger influence on the electrode
surface reactions. This outcome aligns with the expectation that infiltration leads to the
formation of oxide particles on the electrode surface, thereby improving the surface area
and/or catalytic activity. However, this could also be due to the inherently larger R2 value
in the pristine electrode. Interestingly, Fe2O3 and La2O3 show increases in R1 coupled
with a decrease in R2, indicating that the rate of oxygen ion transfer from the electrolyte is
slowed, but the electrode surface reaction is improved. This result is particularly insightful,
as the overall polarisation resistance values are very close to that of the pristine indicating
that the infiltration had minimal or no effect. However, this change in the composition of
the polarisation resistance offers a different perspective.
The results attained for the acidic oxide infiltration present a distinct outcome compared
to the basic oxides, with an observed increase in both components of the polarisation re-
sistance. In the case of Mn2O7, the infiltration has a pronounced negative effect on the
oxygen ion transfer process in comparison to the electrode surface reaction. This may sug-
gest that Mn2O7 impedes the mobility of oxygen ions at the electrode-electrolyte interface
more severely than it affects the catalytic reactions at the electrode surface. Conversely,
the effect of CrO3 infiltration is more detrimental to the electrode surface than to the oxy-
gen ion transfer. This implies that the CrO3 particles blocking active sites on the electrode
surface has a larger impact on the overall polarisation resistance. The differential impact
of these two acidic oxides highlights the complex nature of acidic oxide infiltration and
underscores the importance of understanding their interactions with the electrode.

polarisation resistance increased to 0.68 Ωcm2. Over the next days, the polarisation re-
sistance steadily increased and was equal 0.79 Ωcm2 on the final measurement. Over the
duration of the test, the degradation rates of the pristine and infiltrated cells were equal
to 0.02 Ωcm2 · day−1 and 0.03 Ωcm2 · day−1, respectively.
By determining the gradient after the initial degradation, it is possible to extrapolate the
data and calculate when the two curves would intersect, as shown in Figure 5.21. It was
thus possible to determine the time needed for the infiltrated cells to attain polarisation
resistance similar to the pristine cells, which was approximately 26 days. This showed that
infiltrating with K2O offers some initial improvement, but the effects on the electrochemical
performance are not sustained for an extended period. A possible reasoning for this sharp
decline of the electrochemical performance of K2O could be explained by analysing the
thermogravimetric analysis data as shown in Figure 5.22.

the loss in oxide loading could explain the reason for the quick decline in electrochemical

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this project, the main objective was to improve the electrochemical performance of LS-
F/CGO and this was achieved through basic oxide infiltration. The subsequent reduction
in polarisation resistance is likely due to a combination of factors, including improved cat-
alytic activity, increased surface kinetics or enhanced conductivity. Identifying a single
dominant factor is challenging, based solely on the electrochemical impedance measure-
ments and scanning electron microscopy conducted in this project. Therefore, it is assumed
that there is a synergistic effect among several contributing elements.
An additional crucial aspect to highlight is that, studying interfacial phenomena in active
materials of composite electrodes can present significant challenges, due to the potential
interactions at the interfaces. The presence of multiple elements (La, Sr, Fe, Ce, Gd) can
lead to a variety of chemical interactions and regions of complex chemical reactions. There-
fore, it may be beneficial to reproduce the results obtained in this project using simpler
oxygen electrode systems, such as LSF or LSCF. Nonetheless, the reduction in the polar-
isation resistance of LSF/CGO from infiltration with binary oxides derived from alkaline
earth metals (BaO, CaO, MgO and SrO) is notable, due to their affordability, abundance
and scalability.
Xu et al. demonstrated that CaO acts as a synergistic electrocatalyst for ORR in SOFC
using LSF/SDC composite electrodes. The polarisation resistance at 750 °C was reduced
from 0.83 to 0.42 Ωcm2 with 3.01 wt% CaO [72]. The promotion factor of 1.98 is fairly
similar to results obtained in this project at 750 °C (1.79), as the polarisation resistance
was reduced 0.25 to 0.14 Ωcm2 with 1.05 wt% CaO on average. The infiltration of the CaO
particles enhanced the kinetics of ORR in LSF by improving the oxygen surface exchange
coeﬀicient, which was determined through Electrical Conductivity Relaxation (ECR) mea-
surements [72]. Due to similarities in both the composite electrode used and the reduction
in the polarisation resistance, it can be speculated that CaO has the same impact on the
OER. Similar experiments were carried out by Zhang et al., using LSCF electrode with a
SDC electrolyte. The authors were able to reduce the polarisation resistance at 650 °C from
0.51 to 0.31 Ωcm2 with 5.18 wt% CaO. This corresponds to a promotion factor of 1.65.
This is very similar to the promotion factor for CaO on LSF/CGO (1.53) reported in this
project at 650 °C. Furthermore, the authors illustrated that the CaO particles increased the
oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient by a factor up to 20 through ECR measurements [45].
However, no formation of CaO particles were observed in the microstructural analysis of
the CaO infiltrated sample.

Yang et al. showed that infiltration with MgO can improve the kinetics for ORR in LSCF.
The polarisation resistance at 650 °C was reduced from 0.49 to 0.31 Ωcm2 with 1.01 wt%
MgO, corresponding to a promotion factor of 1.58 [47]. This matches the promotion factor
of 1.57 reported in this project at 650 °C, where the polarisation resistance was reduced
from 1.07 to 0.68 Ωcm2 with 0.44 wt% MgO on average. The microstructural analysis of
the MgO infiltrated cell showed the appearance of film-like coating. Furthermore, Yang et
al. showed through distribution of relaxation time (DRT) analysis that the reduction in
resistance is associated with the increase in the rate of the charge transfer process. Addi-
tionally, the authors reported that MgO increases the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient
by a factor of 2.4 [47].
Riedl et al. decorated Pr0.1Ce0.9O2−δ (PCO) thin film electrode with monolayers of SrO
and used in situ PLD impedance spectroscopy (i-PLD) to track the OER and total con-
ductivity. The results showed that SrO oxide reduced the resistance associated with the
oxygen surface exchange by 75% [49]. Research on the effects of BaO infiltration are lim-
ited, but plenty of studies on BaCO3 are available. Considering the potential for the oxide
to react with CO2 in the air, the beneficial effects reported previously for BaO could stem
from carbonate formation. Hong et al. reported that BaCO3 nanoparticles act as out-
standing electrocatalysts for ORR with a higher promotion factor than various precious
metals, including Pd, Pt and Rh [75]. Despite the minimal possibilities of carbonate forma-
tion, it is interesting to note that Ba-based compounds show a beneficial electrochemical
effect. Overall, the decrease in polarisation resistance seen with the infiltration of alkaline
earth-based binary oxides is intriguing and promising. Although these oxides are not elec-
trocatalytically active, nor are they ionic and electronic conductive, they have still been
shown to reduce polarisation resistance and enhance the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀi-
cient [47]. However, it is challenging to definitively conclude that the reasoning provided
in these studies is directly relevant to the research conducted in this project. Therefore,
to understand the basis of the improvement observed in this study, it is crucial to conduct
further research linking infiltration of these oxides to electrochemical performance.
Furthermore, in this project, it was shown that infiltration with CuO, Li2O and Pr2O3
reduced the polarisation resistance of LSF/CGO. Gao et al. demonstrated that decorating
LSCF/CGO electrodes with <1 wt% CuO reduced the polarisation resistance at 650 °C
from 0.62 to 0.32 Ωcm2. This corresponds to a promotion factor of 1.93, which is higher
than the 1.45 value observed in this study with 1.73 wt% CuO on average. The authors
reported that CuO infiltration resulted in the creation of oxygen vacancies on the elec-
trode surface, which accelerated the incorporation of oxygen from the environment [46].
Furthermore, the use of CuO in SDC electrolytes has been shown to improve the ionic
conductivity through reducing grain-boundary resistance [76]. Therefore, the beneficial ef-
fects of CuO infiltration observed in this project may stem from enhanced oxygen surface

exchange kinetics or improved ionic conductivity in the LSF/CGO composite electrode.
Further research, potentially including ECR measurements, is essential for verifying these
findings and uncovering the underlying mechanisms.
Nicollet et al. reported that Li2O infiltration in PCO electrodes resulted in a nearly 1000-
fold increase in the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient. The authors in this report argued
that Li2O infiltration had a favourable impact on the surface electron concentration [48].
This could be the reason for the beneficial effects of Li2O infiltration and the other elements
used in this project. One method to investigate this would be through X-ray Photoelec-
tron Spectroscopy (XPS), which can provide detailed information on the chemical states of
the electrode surface [77]. This would aid in understanding how the infiltration of oxides
affects the surface properties that are critical for the oxygen exchange process.
Khoskalam et al. proposed a Pr6O11 infiltrated LSCF/CGO as a highly performing oxy-
gen electrode at intermediate temperature. The infiltration with 10wt% Pr6O11 resulted
in a decrease in polarisation resistance from 0.147 to 0.056 Ωcm2 at 650 °C [67]. This
corresponds to a promotion factor of 2.63. The authors commented that, the infiltration
of Pr6O11 occurs on the CGO fluorite structure and had been shown to improve electrocat-
alytic activity for ORR. In this report, the promotion factor attained for infiltration with
Pr2O3 at 650 °C was equal to 3.34 with 2.80 wt% Pr2O3 on average. The impact of Pr
in this oxidation state seems to also lead to improved electrocatalytic activity. However,
analysing the thermogravimetric data obtained from the decomposition of Pr(NO3)3 could
be instrumental in identifying the specific compounds formed. X-ray Diffraction (XRD)
can also be employed to provide detailed insights into the oxide formation. No research
has been found on the impact of infiltration with Fe2O3, K2O and La2O3.
Therefore,
performing ECR measurements to explore their influence on the oxygen surface exchange
coeﬀicient could be beneficial.
In the previous chapter of this report, the detrimental effects of acidic oxide infiltration
(CrO3, Mn2O7 and SiO2) on the electrochemical performance were documented. Firstly,
Cr poisoning significantly impacts the eﬀiciency and durability of SOECs, as Cr species
from metallic interconnects interact with the oxygen electrode resulting in a reduction in
the performance [78]. Notably, LSF and LSCF are particularly susceptible, due to the ten-
dency of Sr to segregate from the perovskite and react with the Cr species on the surface.
This results in the formation of SrCrO4, which tends to block active sites on the electrode
surface resulting in a reduction in electrochemical activity [78]. The results attained with
infiltration of CrO3 illustrate a 15-fold increase in polarisation resistance at 750 °C with
an average loading of 0.56 wt%. These results are consistent with similar experiments
documented in the literature [79,80].

In SOEC stack configuration, silicon-based sealants are used to avoid any gas leakage and
for electrical insulation. However, Si-impurities are known to block active sites on the
electrode surface for oxygen exchange [81]. Nicollet et al. reported that SiO2 infiltration
on PCO had shown a 220-fold reduction in the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient. These
findings were supported by a model based on electron surface depletion rather than one
depending on the restriction of oxygen transport [48]. In this report, SiO2 infiltration had
shown a 20-fold increase in the polarisation resistance at 700 °C. The underlying process
causing this effect is not yet understood and requires additional research. No studies were
discovered regarding the effects of infiltration with Mn2O7. However, Mn2O7 infiltration
had shown a 5-fold increase in the polarisation resistance at 650 °C. Therefore, to under-
stand the underlying mechanism causing this reduction in polarisation resistance in both
these acidic oxides, it would be beneficial to conduct ECR measurements.
One aim of this project was to correlate the binary oxides’ acidity factor and their electro-
chemical performance. A moderate correlation was observed between the acidity factor and
polarisation resistance, indicating that the acidity factor could be serve as a performance
indicator. However, the variance in polarisation resistance cannot be fully explained by
surface chemistry alone with factors, such as catalytic activity, ionic and electronic conduc-
tivity requiring consideration. Additionally, correlating the acidity factor with the oxygen
surface exchange coeﬀicient could yield further insights.
In the aforementioned study by Nicollet et al., a range of binary oxides (CaO, Gd2O3
and Al2O3) including the two previously mentioned (Li2O and SiO2) were included. The
purpose of this study was to show that, the acidity factor can be used as a descriptor for
predicting the oxygen surface exchange kinetics in MIECs [48]. The authors showed that,
the pre-exponential factor of the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient scaled linearly with
the acidity factor. Similar to the findings in this report, it was reported that minimal or no
change to the activation energy was observed [48]. However, the activation energy reported
here was due to the temperature-dependence of the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient and
not polarisation resistance.
In the study by Riedl et al., a range of binary oxides (CaO, SnO2 and TiO2) were also
included along with SrO. A clear correlation between the acidity factor and the electrode
performance were demonstrated through measuring the surface exchange resistance. Fur-
thermore, no clear correlation was observed between the acidity factor and the activation
energy, stating that the surface decorations had minimal to no impact on the mechanism
of OER [49].

One key observation in this project involved the electrochemical performance of acidic
infiltrated cells.
Subsequent basic oxide infiltration was found to partially restore the
cell’s polarisation resistance. The dual-infiltrated cell (2.42 Ωcm2) had 57.4% lower po-
larisation resistance than the purely acidic oxide infiltrated cell (5.68 Ωcm2) at 650 °C.
These results demonstrate the potential of basic oxide infiltration as an effective strategy
to counteract the adverse effects observed in electrodes subjected to acidic environments.
Similar studies conducted by Seo et al. revealed that PCO electrode experienced 20-fold
increase in the oxygen surface exchange coeﬀicient, when infiltrated with Cr2O3. Subse-
quent infiltration with Li2O not only regenerates the electrode surface to pre-infiltration
values, but enhances it above that of the pristine electrode [82]. In another study by the
same group, dual-infiltration with SiO2 followed by either CaO and Li2O yielded similar
outcomes. Regarding the outcomes of this study, there are several aspects that could be
refined for optimisation. These include the concentration of the basic oxide precursor and
the length of the infiltration process. Adjusting these parameters could potentially enable
a single infiltration cycle to regenerate the performance of the electrode back to pristine
levels.
After attaining the results from the dual-infiltration experiment, it was planned to explore
the effects of infiltration with an acidic solution (pH = 2.5), a neutral solution and a basic
solution (pH = 11).
It should be clarified that, while the acidity factor varies for the
binary oxides, all the precursor solutions have a pH between 3 and 6. Therefore, it would
had been interesting to a see the results from such an experiment. However, the initial
data attained were ambiguous and failed to demonstrate an observable trend, likely due to
issues with the test rig. Consequently, given the limited time-frame of this project, these
experiments were not pursued further.
This study also explored how the number of infiltration cycles and the oxide loading affected
the electrochemical performance of the pristine electrode. An optimal range of infiltration
cycles was determined for both MgO and K2O, beyond which the performance could revert
back to that of the pristine electrode. Furthermore, a direct correlation between the load-
ing of various oxides (BaO, CuO and K2O) and the reduction in polarisation resistance was
illustrated. This implies that increasing the oxide loading could continue to decrease the
polarisation resistance. However, it was frequently observed that an optimal loading point
is reached. Beyond this limit, any additional increase in loading would lead to a decline in
electrode performance, a phenomenon commonly observed in the literature [41,47,72,75].
This is referred to as a blocking effect, where the infiltration of particles into a material’s
structure leads to the unintended obstruction of active sites or pathways.

6 Discussion
In order to gain insight into the complex electrochemical processes, a equivalent circuit
model was proposed and used to fit the impedance data. This type of analysis is very
common in literature and provides information into key electrochemical processes [71,72].
The results obtained for the polarisation resistance in the model was in good agreement
with the values obtained from MATLAB. The model allowed to analyse the contribution
of two main processes: the oxygen ion transfer and electrode surface reaction. Overall, the
resistance associated with the electrode surface reaction was determined as the primary
contributor to the overall polarisation resistance. The results also showed that the basic
oxide infiltration had an beneficial effect on both processes with only Fe2O3 and La2O3
having adverse effects on the oxygen ion transfer process. In the case of acidic oxide infil-
tration, both the compounds analysed showed adverse effects on both processes. Overall,
the model provided insight into the nature of the polarisation resistance and allowed for
further analysis of the impedance data. However, further work is necessary to verify the
findings of the model or expand the model to include other processes.
Durability tests are crucial for assessing the long-term performance and stability of solid
oxide cells.
In this study, a short durability test was performed on both pristine and
K2O infiltrated cells over a period of roughly 10 days. The result showed that the K2O
infiltrated cells (0.03 Ωcm2 · day−1) had higher degradation rates than the pristine cells
(0.02 Ωcm2 · day−1). Through extrapolation, it was determined that the K2O infiltrated
cells would have similar electrochemical performance to the pristine cells after 26 days. The
reasoning for this was attributed to the low boiling point of K2O resulting in continuous
evaporation during operation.
Therefore, it may be beneficial to conduct similar tests
with other basic oxides, that have similar electrochemical performance but a higher boiling
point, such as BaO, MgO and Li2O. This may lead to finding a suitable basic oxide for
infiltration, that can attain the electrochemical performance for a longer period of time.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project, the effects of infiltration on the electrochemical performance of LSF/CGO
was investigated. The main outcomes was the notable reduction in polarisation resistance
through the infiltration with basic oxides, such as BaO, Li2O and Pr2O3. However, the ex-
act mechanism for improvement is complex and requires additional analysis. Therefore, it
was suggested to conduct Electrical Conductivity Relaxation (ECR) measurements along
with X-Ray Photospectroscopy (XPS) to compliment the observations in this project. Con-
versely, the infiltration with acidic oxides (CrO3, Mn2O7 and SiO2) showed adverse effects
on the electrochemical performance. The observation from equivalent circuit modelling fit-
ting showed the differential impact of these acidic oxides, highlighting the complex nature
of acidic oxide infiltration. Interestingly, subsequent infiltration with basic oxides partially
restored the electrochemical performance, suggesting a novel approach to counteract the
negative impact of acidic environments.
The infiltration results suggested a moderate correlation between acidity constant and
polarisation resistance.
The observation implies that the acidity factor can serve as a
performance indicator, but it does not fully account for the variation in polarisation resis-
tance. Additionally, exploring how the acidity factor relates to the oxygen surface exchange
coeﬀicient may provide further insight. By analysing the temperature dependence of po-
larisation resistance, it was determined that infiltration has minimal or no impact on the
activation energy of infiltrated cells. The study also showed that both the number of infil-
tration cycles and the loading significantly impacts the performance of electrodes. Optimal
infiltration cycles for MgO and K2O were determined, beyond which performance declined
back to pristine levels. This was attributed to a blocking effect and illustrated the delicate
balance needed in modifying electrodes for improved performance.
The durability test conducted for K2O illustrated the diﬀiculty in sustaining improvements
in electrochemical performance attained from basic oxide infiltration. Based on these find-
ings, it becomes important to conduct long-term test of other basic oxides, hoping to
identity an alternative that maintains electrochemical performance more effectively over
time. In order to do this, it becomes paramount to get a better understanding of the
underlying mechanism of improvement. A possible way to do this is to simplify the ob-
servations by conducting similar studies on single phase perovskite systems, such as LSF
or LSCF. Nonetheless, these findings underscore the need for a multi-faceted approach in
future research, considering various factors that influence performance to fully unlock the
potential of well-known oxygen electrode materials.
