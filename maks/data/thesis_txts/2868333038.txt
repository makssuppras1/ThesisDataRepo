2.1 Design principles of COF materials 
 
Covalent organic frameworks (COFs) are highly porous crystalline polymers with well-deﬁned two-
dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) structures, composed of lightweight elements like 
carbon, oxygen, boron, and nitrogen interconnected by strong covalent bonds [9]. In 2D COFs, 
organic building units are covalently linked to form atomic layers, which further stack through π-π 
interacEons. 3D COFs on the contrary form extended networks through covalent connecEvity in all 
dimensions [3, 9].  
COFs have several advantages that are not found in any other crystalline material, such as its high 
surface area, tunable pore structure, and low density. In addiEon, its strong covalent linkages give 
COFs good thermal stability and excellent chemical stability [10]. The versaElity of COFs allows for 
precise structural design by incorporaEng speciﬁc organic subunits at the atomic scale that oﬀers 
tunable pore sizes, structures, and high surface areas, coupled with excepEonal thermal stability and 
low density (ﬁgure 2.1) [3, 9].

Challenges and Future Perspectives 
 
Even though COF has proven to have superior funcEon as a photocatalyst for the CO2 reducEon, it 
sEll faces some challenges that need further research. For example, the stability of the COF, as 
discussed in secEon 7.2, sEll needs to be handled. It seems also from other research [46] that 
extending the catalyEc Eme seems to have a negaEve eﬀect on the photocatalyEc acEvity of the COF 
material. Because photocatalysis’s main advantages is to be sustainable and the catalyst to be 
recyclable, this achievement is sEll under development. In most of the literature, like Cui, Jin-Xian, 
et al.  [48] and Yang, Rui-Gang, et al. [49], they only show part of the data, where they inform about 
the rate of CO, but not the reacEon Eme. This could be poinEng to that the catalysts being tested is 
not tested for the long term. Therefore, to be able to scale it up industrially, and state that it can be 
recyclable and sustainable, the photocatalysts need to be tested for a long Eme of period and 
provide data for recyclability and repeEEve experiments, which by that may be proved that the type 
of catalyst can be used for the industry. 
Furthermore, the research in the photocatalyEc CO2 reducEon focusses more on the reducEon 
process than the oxidaEon, and therefore the need to understand the full mechanism and the 
oxidaEon process is missing. It seems, as in this study, that the oxidaEon also becomes an important 
part, because the oxidaEon depends on the sacriﬁcial agent, that can be opEmized. In our system, 
TEOA is the only sacriﬁcial agent being used, but maybe some future research could be done on 
trying other sacriﬁcial agents that is not aﬀected by aggregaEon, axer solving the stability issue. 
Maybe one could test the ability of photocatalysis without adding addiEonal sacriﬁcial agent, where 
the solvent is stable and that can provide the proton source. 
Another perspecEve could be the engineering of the structure of the COF. In this project we only 
look at one structure. It could be interesEng to test if other structures lead to higher eﬃciency of 
the CO2 reducEon.

The selecEvity of the CO2 reducEon is also a ﬁeld that should be invesEgated more in the future. As 
explained in previous secEons, CO2 can be reduced to many products, like methanol, methane and 
formaldehyde, all depending on the system that is being used and the mechanism of the 
photocatalyst. The importance of tesEng the selecEvity towards one major product is crucial, 
especially for the industrial applicaEon, because low product selecEvity could result in a mixture of 
mulEple products, which would be another challenge [16]. According to Fu, Junwei et al. [16], the 
combinaEon of poor product selecEvity and low acEvity of catalysts has a great impact on limiEng it 
to pracEcal applicaEon. Because of this concern, future research should be invesEgaEng the 
selecEvity when choosing or opEmizing the photocatalyEc system. In this work, no traces of 
methane have been shown in the GC, but methanol or formaldehyde could have been invesEgated 
by other spectroscopic methods like Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) or Nuclear 
MagneEc Resonance (NMR). Besides that, selecEvity is important for industrial applicaEon, it is also 
an indicaEon or explanaEon for the poor eﬃciency a system might have towards CO evoluEon. That 
is because the undesired byproducts will consume valuable photogenerated electrons and lower the 
output of the target products, in this case CO [16].

its increase again axer 4h. It may be explained that the existence of CO2 for a long Eme has an impact 
on the structural change, and the inhomogeneity of the soluEon.
