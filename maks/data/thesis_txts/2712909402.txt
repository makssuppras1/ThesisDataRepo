Executive summary
With a focus on sustainable product portfolios, this report focuses on product improvements in collaboration
with the smaller danish outdoor manufacturer, Superstainable. Superstainable is a sustainability-orientated
company that produces a variety of outdoor apparel products. For this project, the focus is on the
sustainability of Superstainable’s product portfolio in terms of both environmental, social and economical
sustainability. The study includes insights gained on Superstainable’s current strategic approach, a life
cycle assessment of three of Superstainable’s products, and an analysis on relevant solutions based on the
findings in the life cycle analysis as well as in the market of which Superstainable find themselves. This is
done in order to investigate the stated research question:
How can hotspots and circularity in the product life cycle be used to create a more sustainable product
portfolio and business for Superstainable?
Superstainable currently have a high focus on using certified materials in order to support a sustainable
agenda with focus on sustainable development goal 12,13 and 14. The certifications include the Global
organic textile standard (GOTS), Global recycled standard (GRS), Organic content standard (OCS) and
the SA8000 social standard. Their value proposition focuses on providing certified multipurpose products
for the average danish user at a reasonable price. They have a unique and shared ownership with one
of their manufacturers, LIME China, with whom they collaborate to keep costs down allowing them to
be more price competitive. Superstainable also reaps the benefits of LIME China’s certified products
strengthening their portfolio, but at the same time limiting their choice in materials and increasing their
dependency on this manufacturer.
Through life cycle assessment, increased insights into Superstainable portfolio are gained. Here, three
products are chosen to represent the full portfolio. The Elbe Merino Tee represent the wool based products,
the Ameland Midlayer Jacket represent midplayer and insulating products, and the Wavecrest Shell Jacket
the waterproof products. Each products functional units is stated with a lifetime of 2 years for the wool
based product and 5 years for the polyester based products for a private user in a danish setting. Here, the
use stage is expressed through the washing and drying of the garment and not including wear and tear.
Through cradle to grave modelling using the Ecoinvent database and the ILCD 2011 midpoint+
method in SimaPro, results are obtained. Here impacts are mainly seen in the production stage where the
main fabrics are the biggest contributors. Normalized impact categories that are greatly impacted for
wool based products include accidification, terrestial eutrophication, and freshwater ecotoxicity. For the
polyester based products, the Ameland Midlayer Jacket showed an average impact contribution of 32,4%
for the main fabric and 21,7% for the insulation in the production stage and the Wavecrest Shell Jacket
showed an average of 18,3% for the main fabric. The highest normalized scoring categories impacted by
these products were the freshwater ecotoxicity as well as human toxicity: non-cancer effect and human
toxicity: cancer effect. However, when considering the normalized results and Superstainable’s current
approach of using certified and recycled materials, they have already reduced the impact of the Wavecrest
Shell Jacket with almost 200% and the impact of the Ameland Midlayer Jacket with more than 800% in
the ozone depletion category. Indicating that certain measurements are taking in order to accommodate
for the identified impacts.
Apart from the main fabrics showing impacts, the production and end of life stage also shows impacts
related to the button and components in for example the Wavecrest Shell Jacket. Here, the button
modelled as brass and incinerated as copper provides significant impacts in the freshwater ecotoxicity
category upon incineration. By replacing this button and other components with a recycled PET and
making the product 100% recycled, the Wavecrest Shell Jackets impacts in the freshwater ecotoxicity
category can be reduced by more than 50% and the ozone depletion category with more than 75%
indicating that further action can be taken apart from looking at the main fabrics. Furthermore, processes

in the production stage, such as sewing and the energy used for sewing, also provides some impact. The
same goes for the use stage, where the consumption of water and electricity for the polyester based
products rise to a level of which it can not be ignored.
Based on these results, solutions are presented in order to accommodate for the stages of which
impacts are generated. These solutions are first identified through sustainable business archetypes where
competitors such as Norrøna, Patagonia, and Peak Performance and their approaches are evaluated. Mixed
with insights gained from general market trends, more validated solutions were identified and in some
cases applied. This resulted in the following solutions: "Certified and reprocessed wool" which focuses on
decreasing the manufacturing impacts of the wool based products. "Recycled polyester" that focuses on
decreasing the production stage of the polyester based products even further than what Superstainable is
currently doing. "Awareness of components" focuses on looking into using recycled material and/or more
environmentally friendly options for components, such as zippers, buttons, etc. "Renewable energy" relates
to LIME China’s energy consumption generating impacts through sewing procedures when manufacturing
the products. "User behaviour" targets the impacts generated through washing and drying of the products,
mainly impacting and showing sensitivity in the water resource depletion category.
When considering the feasibility of these solutions, the introduction of more recycled polyester as well
as an increased awareness of components is deemed the most feasible. This is concluded due to the current
availability of recycled solutions and materials in the market and also within Superstainable existing
supply chain. This solution also has the potential to reduce impacts in the ozone depletion category with
around 65% for the Ameland Midlayer Jacket and the potential to reduce all impact categories for the
Wavecrest Shell Jacket. However, Superstainable should keep in mind, what the consequence of changing
components would be to the functionality and longevity of the product in order to uphold their value
proposition.
Furthermore, the solutions all present something that is currently present in the market. Hence, they
also present aspects of which Superstainable will have to consider if they wish to stay on top of the market
and keep their competitive edge when compared to competitors. However as these solutions might come
with cost, Superstainable must be aware of the impact that this will have on their value proposition
targeting affordable products for the average user as the customers have many different providers leaving
them with a high bargaining power.

Introduction
The textile industry is amongst the biggest and most polluting industries in the world. Therefore, many
companies have started to increase their focus on sustainability to gain a competitive advantage, and
sustainability has become a selling point that companies use it in order to gain market share. Due
to the size of the industry and the increased consumption that we experience, there is a real need for
an increase in sustainable products, and thus this project will focus on how product portfolios can be-
come more sustainable[1]. Here the focus will be specifically targeting the products in the outdoor industry.
This project will be in collaboration with a small danish company called Superstainable [2]. Su-
perstainable is a manufacturer of outdoor garments founded in 2016 with around 10 employees. They
have a high focus on sustainability and continuously try to improve not only their own impact on the
environment, but also their customers impact. Thus their slogan "Leave nothing but your footprint in
nature". So far, Superstainable has implemented the use of recycled materials in some of their products,
they have implemented transparency and also the use of certified suppliers throughout their supply chain.
Despite this, they strive to further increase their sustainability and try to do so through a reduction of
the environmental impacts in their products. This project will focus on how Superstainable can create an
even more sustainable product portfolio as well as how this can help them and their business. Based on
this interest the following research question has been formulated:
How can hotspots and circularity in the product life cycle be used to create a more sustainable product
portfolio and business for Superstainable?
To answer this question the following sub-questions have also been set up in order to provide a
structured approach to the research question.
1. What does Superstainable’s current business model look like?
2. What does the current product portfolio look like and how does it accommodate for impacts on the
three pillars of sustainability?
3. How can sustainability indicators, hotspot analysis and circular economy be used to give an increased
insights into the impacts of the current product portfolio?
4. In what way can these insights be used to make changes in order to produce a more sustainable
product portfolio as well as business for Superstainable?
5. To what extend are the changes feasible and how does it add value to Superstainable’s business and
market position?
The main focus will be upon sub-question 2, 3 and 4 with special regard to the sustainable aspect
of Superstainable’s products. Therefore, this also means that the focus on the business aspects will be
somewhat lower. However, the business aspects will be included in order to provide useful insight for
Superstainable as well as help to put the sustainable insights and results into perspective.

Business aspects
In this section, the aim will be to identify and present Superstainable’s approach to running a business

6.1
Current business dynamics
In order to get an understanding of how Superstainable is approaching the market, it is important to
understand their organisational structure and the market that they are in. Therefore, this section will
map out dynamics within the textile industry together with Superstainable’s collaborations.
6.1.1
Superstainable’s supplier relation
Superstainable is as mentioned a small danish outdoor manufacture. They use a small number of suppliers
with a unique relationship, and this study will focus on their main supplier identified to be LIME and
more specifically their Chinese location called LIME China [7]. The reason for this is that the majority of
products sold by Superstainable is produced by this supplier. This supplier similarly accounts for most of
Superstainable’s certifications, hence making them highly important to Superstainable’s strategy.
In order to understand the collaboration, an overview of the companies ownership and overall organi-
sational structure is included in fig. 3. This is done to give an overview and insight into how and why
these two companies have a common interest.
Figure 3: Simplified organisational chart of LIME and Superstainable
Since Superstainable and LIME share the same owner, Claus Rasmussen whom also is a co-founder of
Superstainable, this helps insure a common interest and ground for strong collaborations[7]. Therefore,
one can expect strong loyalty from Superstainable’s manufacture as well as alignment between these two
companies. Furthermore, this setup also helps with flexibility in terms of production, where Superstainable
can draw on insider-knowledge and more tailored solutions due to the shared ownership. Therefore, this
helps decrease barriers in terms of initial production quantities, initial capital investment, and risk related
to producers and level of quality, which will be discussed later [8].
The benefits related to production quantities and capital investment is in this case, that Superstainable
can use LIME’s existing customers and their orders to get their products produced without worrying
about initial startup cost and minimum order sizes. Superstainable simply tag along on currently running
production lines and utilize excess capacities [8]. An illustration of this flow is shown in fig. 4.

Despite the advantages of the ability to tap into the production, this also comes with certain drawbacks.
Superstainable must in these cases use the same fabric as chosen by the current customer hence limiting
Superstainable’s choices for their own products. However, this allows them to produce smaller quantities
decreasing the risk and liquidity needed to get their products produced.
6.1.2
Market dynamics
In order to understand Superstainable’s position within the market, it is important to understand the
market dynamics. Therefore Porter’s five forces are used as it will help gain insight into the different
forces present within the textile market.
6.1.2.1
Threats of new entrants
When it comes to the understanding of how easy it is for new competitors to enter the market, it is
important to understand what barriers that they must overcome. As the textile industry is one of the
largest industries in the world, it is obvious that it is relatively easy to find suppliers and the needed
know-how in order to start production and enter the market. Due to the number of manufacturers and
suppliers available, economies of scale is not presenting as big a barrier for newcomers as it might be seen
in other industries. This is due to the many different suppliers that will be able to provide companies
with the needed demand. Therefore, companies with large production and low costs, cannot solely
rely on economies of scale to keep out competitors [9]. One the other hand, initial costs might present
somewhat of a barrier for smaller companies, as suppliers might set demands to the minimum number of
products that must be ordered at a time [10]. Therefore, the size of orders might present a barrier to newer
companies that might not have the needed capital or market share to generate and sell the needed amounts.
As Superstainable is a rather small company, they currently have no benefit of economies of scale,
neither do they have patented technologies that differentiate them from other competitors in the market.
Therefore, the mentioned barriers or lack of these will most likely results in a number of emerging
competitors in the future. However, Superstainable have taken measures in order to increase these
barriers by differentiating their supply chain from the regular fashion industry. Here certified suppliers
will narrow down the newcomers possibilities of finding equally skilled and sustainable suppliers, giving
Superstainable an advantage [8]. As the future potentially brings an increase of sustainability certifications
and regulations, this barrier might decrease over time, thus it is important that Superstainable is aware of
what certifications provides them with a competitive advantage [11].
6.1.2.2
Bargaining power of the supplier
For the average textile or fashion manufacturer, the suppliers are easily replaced due to the number of
suppliers available and hence the suppliers bargaining power is very low. However, as Superstainable
currently only use a few certified suppliers in order to uphold their sustainable promises, the number of
available suppliers is highly decreased. Therefore, the bargaining power of each supplier is highly increased
as they are not that easily replaced. In relation to this, Superstainable is still a rather small company,
6
==PAGE:16==
hence their production can be considered as rather small as well. Therefore, they only present a small
turnover for the suppliers, again contributing to the suppliers bargaining power. This also means that
Superstainable becomes more reliant on their suppliers compared to other producers in the textile industry.
This presents a potential threat to Superstainable and their business, as they are highly depended on
their supply chain, making their unique relationship with LIME China even more important. As the
sustainable aspect and certifications gets hold of the industry, the number of certified suppliers will
potentially grow and similarly decrease the bargaining power of the supplier [11]. However, this will also
mean that Superstainable’s competitive edge, based on their supply chain, will decrease and the threat
will instead be that other companies use the same strategy and approach as Superstainable.
6.1.2.3
Threats of substitutes
As Superstainable perceive and find themselves within both the everyday and outdoor market, they run
the risk of competing with several markets at the same time [8]. Therefore, both the fashion orientated
brands and products as well as outdoor oriented products can be seen as substitutes to products from
Superstainable’s portfolio. As this gives Superstainable the strength and opportunity to compete on
different markets and hence increase the customer segment it also contributes with a lot of competition.
In addition, the current fashion is highly interlinked with the outdoors and quality products, feeding into
the strategy of Superstainable. However, it also shows that this approach is not unique [12].
Despite overlapping products and styles, there are also other forms of substitution in the fashion or
textile industry. Here the substitution and competition is more clearly related to the branding and hence
the experience or emotion achieved once owning or purchasing a product [9]. In this case, Superstainable’s
products, certifications, and sustainable approach again overlap with other companies, but like many
others they have a unique supply chain. Therefore, they must emphasise how their core values and
foundation as well as their supply chain, certifications and transparency distinguishes them from others
and their products. This must then be applied to their brand in order for customers to choose their
products over what can be viewed as substitutes from other brands looking to do the same. In this regard,
Superstainable should remember that their whole brand is constructed around sustainability, whereas
other brands are simply trying to adopt and change in order to become sustainable.
6.1.2.4
Bargaining power of the buyer
As the outdoor market is rapidly growing, so are the number of providers of outdoor clothing [13].
Therefore, customers have multiple options when purchasing a product. Both in terms of style and price.
Due to the focus on the outdoors, numerous brands like Superstainable focus on the environmental aspects
of their products and production, and like Superstainable one can find other outdoor-brands reporting
to the Swedish Textile Initiative for Climate Action (STICA) as well as using recycled materials[14][15].
These aspects will be presented later. As a result the average outdoor-buyer has a high bargaining power.
In alignment with this Superstainable do present one unique characteristics which is their Danish origin.
This might help decrease the buyers bargaining power on a local scale, where Superstainable can use their
flexibility in production and order sizes to meet danish B2B customers.

6.2.2
Certifications and standards
As certifications have already proven important to Supertainable, this section will describe which certifica-
tions they use as well as what it implies.
Certificates can be a strong tool to provide customers with information on the processes and materials
involved in a given product. However, it is also a way for the companies to ensure that a certain level of
quality is upheld in their production and the supply chain.
When looking at these standards and certifications 3 out of 4 directly relate to the materials used in
the products. The last standard is used for sustainability in terms of social sustainability as it has to
do with work environment and social accountability. Therefore, the certifications can be split into two
categories.
8
==PAGE:18==
• Product related certifications
– Global Organic Textile Standard, CU 1001697 (GOTS)
– Global Recycle Standard CU1001697 (GRS)
– Organic Content Standard CU1001697 (OCS)
• Social sustainability certifications
– SA8000 standard
When looking closer at the product related certifications, the names of these also indicate that the
certificates has to do with the content and material of a product. Despite the use of such certifications it
is important, not only to Superstainable, but also to the customers to understand, what the certification
entails. Therefore, a brief overview of each certifications requirements will be presented below.
GOTS - Certification
Figure 6: GOTS certification label ([21])
The GOTS certification relates to the use of organic textiles found in various products across processing,
manufacturing and trading [22]. According to the certification, a product must contain no less than 70%
certified organic fibres, meaning that the fibres must be grown without the use of synthetic pesticides
herbicides and GMOs [23]. In addition to this, wool products must also comply will being mulesing-free
for increased animal welfare. Apart from setting certain demands to the amount of organic fibres used,
the GOTS certification also contains social and ecological requirements. This means that not only is the
certification setting demands to the use of chemicals and its toxicity, it also requires the manufacture to
comply with certain social regulations. In this case specific requirements are areas such as: Prohibited use
of child labour, no discrimination, no harassment and violence, etc. [24].
GRS - Certification
Figure 7: GRS certification label ([25])
The GRS certification focuses on recycled material and also includes social aspects and requirements
to the use of chemicals. In order for a product to be within the standard the product must contain at least
20% recycled material. However, in order to use the product-specific label from GRS the product must
contain at least 50% recycled material. The certified products guarantee that materials meet the ISO
standards definition of recycling both from manufacturing (pre-consumer) as well as for private households
(post-consumer) material [25].
9
==PAGE:19==
Apart from material specific requirements the GRS certification also ensures social sustainability by re-
quiring voluntary employment, prohibit child labor, and compliance with national minimum age (however
no younger than 15 years of age), prohibiting bonded labor, amongst other. Chemical restrictions are
similarly a part of the standard as well as requirements to organizational structure. Specific environmental
positions must be assigned and occupied in the company. These positions for example cover environmental
management system (EMS) where a designated EMS staff person (from the management level) must be
appointed [25].
OCS - Certification
Figure 8: OCS certification labels ([26])
The OCS certification focuses on increasing organic agriculture production within the textile industry.
Its focus is to be a tool for the industry in order for companies to be able to verify the content of the
products they purchase. Therefore, the whole supply chain must comply with the needed information up
and until the last business-to-business transaction [26].
The OCS certification can be used either as general marketing claims or product specific depending on the
level of compliance. However, in order to use the OCS 100 logo a product must not contain both certified
and non-certified material of the same type [26].
SA8000 standard
Figure 9: SA8000 standard logo ([27])
The SA8000 standard is unlike the already mentioned certificates, focused purely on the social aspect
of industry. Some of the focus areas are: Child labour, forced and compulsory labour, health and safety,
amongst others. Specific requirements are listed to assure coherency between companies and alignment
with the standard. These requirements, for example, cover a minimum age of workers to be 15 year unless
the local law says higher as well as help ensure that it does not withhold employees from attending school.
To ensure that these areas are enforced, SA8000 requires evidence that the organizations management
is well functioning and upholding this [27]. The standard also works with communication and social
awareness within companies to ensure the well-being of employees. This also applies to the general
organisation to secure awareness and dissemination of the SA8000 standard to ensure that no employees
feel threatened, that workers feel respected, and that they know their rights. The companies should also
align with the standards requirements to health and safety including free access to: clean toilet facilities,
potable water, suitable spaces for meal breaks, and where applicable, sanitary facilities for food storage
and other safety measures [27].

mentally friendly and transparent products and production. This is done in order to gain market share,
and target customers that share these values, allowing them to differentiate themselves from the general
textile industry while maintaining products with a large market potential.

6.3.1.1
Value proposition, Cost structure and Revenue stream
As seen from fig. 10 the upper part of the canvas has the usual elements included in a regular business model

is strictly related to their products. However, due to the focus on sustainability within the company,
the value proposition has what can be seen as indirect or underlying value that is not the product itself.
In this case, the value for the customer is not simply a product, but rather a high quality product that
should be sold at a lower price than more high end brands. Furthermore the company and products offer
full transparency with a focus on the use of certifications. Customers purchasing a product hence not
only purchase a product, but also the underlying values that is ethical trade as well as environmental
responsibility though certifications in regard to consumption. However, the use of certifications also
come with a price, as specific manufactures must be used in order to align with the use of certifications
meaning extra cost and indirect cost to the production. Here, Superstainable can once again draw on
their co-ownership with LIME to accommodate for this. STICA similarly takes a fee in order for them to
do reporting adding to the cost of producing in a sustainable manor with transparency [14]. Apart from
this, Supertainable also spent money and resources on R&D in order to develop and re-develop products
to meet customer needs.
As there are quite a few companies using certifications and sustainability as a selling point, this alone
is perhaps not enough to win over customers. Therefore, Superstainable is focusing on keeping down the
cost of products as they want to reach the average danish user that is not necessarily searching for the
high end and specified products. This way the value offered by Superstainable is also to allow the average
user with well-being and the possibility of owning multipurpose products that allow them to enjoy the
outdoors and the everyday with the same product [8].
All this also points to a mix between a cost and value driven approach, as Superstainable wish to
target a rather big market by keeping prices down, while at the same time using more outdoor orientated
product to focus on sustainability, high quality and more value specific aspects.
6.3.1.2
Customer segments, Customer relations and Channels
As mentioned above the main target group is the average danish user. Here the products are meant to
accommodate for both outdoor use as well as everyday use in order to decrease the need for multiple
products and thereby cut back on consumption. At the same time this focus on the average user potentially
leads to a mass market as the products could be used by anyone. For the same reason, colors and designs
are kept simple in order for the products to blend into an everyday scenario [8]. The multi market
approach is also seen from Superstainable’s channels where products are sold both online and in a limited
amount of stores. In this case, the online sites used are Superstainable’s own website as well as Zalando.dk,
danish fair fashion, and others. By looking at Zalandos values and goal, "At Zalando, we work every day
to become the starting point for fashion" [29] it is clear that the average user and an everyday use of
Superstainable’s products is of focus.

Eco-social cost and Eco-social benefits
When looking at the traditional part of the business model canvas, it is obvious that a more economical

decreasing consumption of goods and materials[31].
All in all this provides a business overview from which it is clear, that Superstainables focus is to

Life cycle assessment
In order to gain more knowledge on how Superstainable’s product portfolio is impacting the sustainable
aspects and more specifically the environmental aspect, a simplified LCA will be conducted. The section
will include aspects from all four LCA stages, with a main focus on the life cycle inventory analysis (LCI)
as well as the life cycle impacts assessment (LCIA) [5].
7.1
Goal, scope and LCI
The aim is to identify hotspots within three key-products that all represent specific areas of Superstainable’s
portfolio. Functional units and specific product properties will be described in order to allow the analysed
products to be compared with other products of similar characteristics both externally as well as internally,
but this will not be covered in this study.

Products chosen for analysis

7.1.2
Limitations due to methodological choices
As Superstainable’s targeted segment is everyday users as well as people using their products for leisure
activities in the outdoors the LCA will focus on this type of user as well. This means that the use stage
will take into account the use from an average danish user and the products lifetime will be based on
this type of use. Elements such as washing and drying will similarly be based on the average household.
Therefore, the conclusions from the LCA cannot be used in situations where the products are used by a
different type of user, such as a professional user or its alike.
The aim of the study is for the LCA part focused on the environmental aspect, and hence economic
and social impacts will not be considered. The study is a non-comparative study as the goal is not to
compare impacts with other products, but rather to identify trends in the impacts. This way the hotspots
will give indications on how the individual product is contributing to specific impact categories and general
impact trends across the portfolio. Last but not least, the use stage and end of life stage will be based on
a danish user and hence washing, drying and disposal will be based on danish energy mix and disposal
options to the widest extent possible. As the available methods in SimaPro and more specifically the
ILCD midpoint+ method does not have micro-plastic as an impact category, this aspect will similarly left
out, but should be considered in a more broad perspective.

7.1.4
LCI modelling framework and multifunctional processes
For this LCA-analysis, an attributional model framework will be used. This is done to see, how the products
contribute with impacts to different impact categories. This also aligns with the ILCD recommendations
on LCI modelling as the project is located within both decision context A as well as C1, depending on
Superstainable’s proceedings with the study. For both situation A and C1, it is suggest to use system
expansion for multi-functional processes as well as the use of average processes when modelling the
background system [5].
7.1.4.1
Secondary functions
When looking at the three products, it is clear that there are multi-functional processes. For this study
all products are treated by already existing processes in the Ecoinvent database and the consequential
library in SimaPro [5]. Thus, no specific processes are created or applied in order to manually perform
system expansion. In the woolen based products, an example of system expansion can be found when the
sheep producing the wool also produces Lanolin as well as meat for food. When considering the sheep for
slaughter, the process of creating sheep fleece in the grease in the Ecoinvent database already contains this
aspect and system expansion will thus not be treated to a further extend than what is already done. In
alignment with this, the co-product, Lanolin, is also taken into account. However, as there is no good way
of conducting system expansion on this rather unique process and product, allocation is manually applied
and filled into the process of creating clean wool. The allocation can be found in appendix A.1 in the
Elbe Merino Tee process sheet (column J). The same is done for the wool process when creating wool top.
7.1.4.2
System boundaries
In order to understand what this LCA will be covering, the systems and their boundaries in relation to
each of the three products are included in fig. 14, 15 and 16. Here, process flows are used to illustrate the
steps taken into consideration for each product on a system level. All journeys cover from cradle-to-grave
and includes indications of upstream and downstream processes.
All three products take into account the consumption of raw materials, the transformation of the
raw material into useful fabrics, the assembly of the fabrics into given shapes, product packaging, and
transportation to Superstainable from the manufacturing cite in China, and the transportation to the
customer from Superstainable. User behaviour includes household washing and for some products drying
as well as disposal of the product by incineration. This also means that the use stage itself does not
include impacts generated from release of material to the environment through wear and tear.

7.1.5
Representativeness of LCI data
In order for the study to be geographically, technically and temporally representative, it is important that
the data used for the modelling is equally so. However, as this is a simplified LCA study, it is important
to mention that the study will not go to the maximum level of detail, neither will its focus be to obtain
the maximum level of representative data. If representative data is missing, the study will approximate
these if the processes are of relevant importance. This way the study will be able to identify general
hotspots in the portfolio.
To obtain the best possible data compared to the available time span and aim of the project, the
project will be based on data provided through communication with Superstainable and measurements
of their products. Their website as well as product descriptions and manufacturing sites are similarly
used in order to get the right details on each individual product. The physical products are measured and
weighed in order to find primary data on each specific component in each product. This will similarly
ensure that this input into the modelling is up to date and relevant for the modelling of the product.
Last but not least the project will aim to use geographically relevant data when possible, including
geographical specific processes by introducing Danish, Chinese or "Europe without Switzerland", "rest of
the world" (RoW) or "global" (GLO) processes. This means that the assembly step for example will be
modelled based on a Chinese (CN) energy mix. Incineration and the use stage will be modelled based
on the geographical location of the user as described in the functional unit. In alignment with this,
documentation for each process will be read and taken into consideration when modelling. This is done to
ensure that specific processes are up to date and representative for the technology in the industry.
7.1.6
Basis for the impact assessment
For this project, the chosen method is the ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ using the EU27 2010 normalization.
The reason for this being that a midpoint-method rather than an endpoint-method will give impacts
from early on in the cause-effect chain. Therefore, impacts will be of a sort such as the increase in
chemicals in the environment rather than for example the extinction of species that would be an effect
of the increased chemicals in the environment. Furthermore, the ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ is based on
data on European citizens when analysing normalized results. This means that the level of impact is
done in relation to a European citizen and their average use [34].This also aligns with the consumers
and end user that Superstainable sell to. The impact categories for the ILCD 2011 Midpoint+ method
includes: Climate change, Ozone depletion, Human toxicity: non-cancer effect, Human toxicity: cancer
effect, Particulate matter, Ionizing radiation HH, Ionizing radiation E (interim), Photochemical ozone
formation, Acidification, Terrestrial eutrophization, Freshwater eutrophization, Marine Eutrophization,
Freshwater ecotoxicity, Land use, Water resource depletion, Mineral, fossil & renewable resource depletion.
7.1.7
Data collection
In order to perform the LCA, data on the three products must be obtained. Here, both primary and
secondary data is used, where the secondary data is based on input from Superstainable as well as generic
data from the Ecoinvent 3.7.1. conseq. U and Ecoinvent 3.7.1. cut-off library. The reason for using a
consequential library is that this library uses system expansion as suggested by the ILCD guidelines rather
than allocation, which can be found in the attributions libraries. However, using a consequential library
also means that processes will be based on marginal data, rather than average data. By using marginal
data, the modelling will take into account market dynamics and potentially contribute with more complex
product systems. Since this study not only aim to identify hotspots, but also aim to see the effects based
on changes made to the portfolio, the consequential modelling and marginal data will help give a more
realistic picture of real world consequences of such changes.

7.1.8
System modelling per life cycle stage
For this section a description of the products different life cycle stages will be presented i regard to fig. 14,
15 and 16. Here, examples of calculations as well as the most relevant assumptions is included to give an
overview of the input used for modelling.
7.1.8.1
Raw materials
The raw materials are the materials used as input for each part of the products. In this project the
main raw materials are wool, polyester, copper amongst others. An overview of the materials and their
quantities can be found in table 9,10 and 11 as well as in an extended version in appendix A.1. These
material choices are based on input from Superstainable and online research for relevant components for
the products. However, here it is important to mention, that Superstainable mainly use a supplier named
SBS for their zippers, zipper sliders and buttons, but due to the lack of material information on these
components the YKK manufacturer parts are used instead. As Superstainable also have other products
using YKK components, this is a fair assumption for the general portfolio. Due to Superstainable highly
relying on their materials being certified, there has been made distinction between certified recycled
polyester (rPET) and non-recycled polyester (PET).
Considering Superstainable’s size and focus, the specific processes related to extracting the raw
materials for the production are outside the scope of this study. Therefore, the raw materials will be
considered in terms of more generic materials included in the products, and the processes used for obtaining
these materials will similarly be based on more generic input from the Ecoinvent database.
7.1.8.2
Production
The production specific inputs are used together with data from the Ecoinvent 3 library. Here, the input
from the library cover general processes such as creating polyester fibres, wool fabrics, etc. If changes
are made to these, they are noted in the process sheets (column D) in appendix A.1. Due to lack of
information on tier 2 suppliers and their location, market processes are to the widest possible extent used
in this part of the modelling. This is done in order to use standard information on processes as well
as transportation for unspecified suppliers [35]. An assumption is made on the amount of waste in the
production, which in this case is set to zero if not included in the chosen processes from the Ecoinvent
database. In assistance to this, other major assumptions made for the production stage can be found in
the Elbe Merino Tee sheet (column I) and the Ameland Midlayer and Wavecrest Jacket sheets (column J)
in appendix A.1. Assumptions specifically for the choice of processes can be found in the Elbe Merino Tee
process sheet (coulmn J) and in the Ameland Midlayer process and the Wavecrest Jacket process sheets
(column I). Once these production processes are considered the products are produced and manufactured
at LIME China in Shishi city. Here, the energy consumption can be specified to a geographical location
and the starting point of the transportation is also identified.
7.1.8.3
Assembly
Once all parts of the product have arrived to LIME China the next step is to assemble the products. Here,
fabrics are cut to parts, the parts and components are sewn together and buttons are snapped into place.
The following processes were used with the input being medium voltage from the Chinese energy mix.
• Cutting: For cutting it is assumed that LIME China is using a laser cutter with a power of 475 W,
a speed of 600mm/sec and the ability to cut 33 layers of fabric at a time.
• Sewing: The sewing is assumed to be conducted on an industrial sewing machine of 530 W with a
speed of 5000 stitches per minute and 12 Stitches per inch.
• Button snap: The attachment of buttons to a product is assumed to be done with a snap fastener
machine of 375 W and a speed of 6000 pieces/minute.
24
==PAGE:34==
The processes are based on standard machinery available in the current textile industry, but it is
important to mention that the power consumption only resembles the energy used upon performing the
given action. Similarly, the processes does not take into account the resources and energy used to create
the machines themselves.
7.1.8.4
Packaging and distribution
Once the product is assembled and ready to be shipped, the product is packed in a bag made of low
density polyethylene (PE-LD)[8]. Here, the weight of the bag is based upon a bag used for an Ameland
Midlayer Jacket with a weight of 27 grams. The weight of packaging used for the Elbe Merino Tee and
Wavecrest Shell Jacket is then derived by scaling the weight of the packaging to the weight of the product.
Figure 17: Waterway for shipping from Shishi city to Århus harbour
Once the product is packaged the product is shipped from Shishi city to Århus harbor in Denmark as
illustrated in fig. 17. The next step taken into consideration is then the transportation of the product
from Superstainable to the customer. For these it is assumed that:
• Shipping distance from Shishi city to Århus: 19364 km
• Distance by lorry from Silkeborg to customer: 295 km
In terms of packaging and transportation, it is worth mentioning that no further packaging is included,
such as pallets, cardboard boxes, etc. For the transportation, the distance to the customer is equally
a rough estimate as Superstainable are providing both B2B as well as B2C delivery. In this case, the
distance is based on a realistic yet worst case scenario for distribution within Denmark which is from
Silkeborg to Copenhagen.
7.1.8.5
Use
Once the products enter the use stage, the focus is on the washing and drying of the products. In this
case, the washing is done at home in a regular washing machine. For this, the model includes: Energy
consumption for the washer and if needed a dryer, water consumption and detergent. In the case of
the Elbe Merino Tee and the Ameland Midlayer Jacket, it is assumed that no drying is done [36]. The
Wavecrest Shell Jacket is assumed to be dried, since modern outdoor washes and repellents require this to
have full effect [37].
In order to specify how much of a wash each individual product account for, specific loads are assumed.
This way it is possible to calculate how much of the wash and consumption can be allocated to each
individual product. Here, the following assumptions are made.
• Washing machine: Energy consumption is calculated to be 1,0037 kwh/wash and a water consumption
of 40L/wash.
25
==PAGE:35==
• Dryer: Energy consumption calculated to be 3,5 kwh/drying.
• Detergent: Amount assumed to be 25ml and equal to 25g for a full load.
As mentioned previously, these numbers are only representing the moment the washer or dryer is being
used. Hence the energy consumption does not take into account power consumed once the machine is on
standby nor does it account for the materials and fuel used to manufacture and transport the washer and
dryer.
7.1.8.6
End of life
For the end of life stage, it is assumed that the products are incinerated and that no components or
parts of the products are separated and disposed separately. The incineration is modelled to take place in
Europe as this was the closest geographical process available when taking into account the functional unit.
A market process is chosen in order to include general transportation measurements. Last but not least, it
is assumed that the buttons in the Wavecrest Shell Jacket is consisting of pure copper upon incinerated.

7.3
Interpretation
In order to understand the result presented above, an interpretation will be made on the overall trends
and most impacted categories. This is done to understand the whole portfolio and to identify areas of
which improvement can take place and have effect.
7.3.1
Trends in the results
When looking at the presented results, it is clear that the production stage in all cases play an important
role with overall average contributions between 48,4% and 95,4%. For the polyester based products the
normalized plots show especially high impacts in the Freshwater ecotoxicity as well as the two Human
toxicity categories. This also aligns well with what could be expected, as water pollution and human
health risks are commonly known impact areas throughout the textile industry [41]. Considering that this
study does not include the process of dyeing it also implies even bigger impacts in the Human toxicity
categories [42]. These water related impacts are also seen in the wool based products, where the impacts
are spread out over various categories related to the ecosystem and water impact categories. The generated
impacts in both the polyester and the wool based products also makes sense, since the Elbe Merino Tee is
constructed of 98,7% main fabric, the Ameland Midlayer Jacket is 52,4% face fabric and 39,6% insulation
and the Wavecrest Shell Jacket is 60,9% face fabric. However, for the Wavecrest Shell Jacket, it also
becomes clear that the other components such as the buttons and processes such as sewing are having an
influence on the impacts from this product.
Apart from production the use stage also shows significant impacts for the two jackets that despite
their lower amount of washes generate a big impact based on the consumption of water and energy;
something that is well known in the textile industry[1]. For the Ameland Midlayer Jacket, this impact is
generated due to its weight and share of a 6kg load. Here, its contribution is proportionally higher than
when compared to the Elbe Merino Tee and thus the jacket displays more impact as it takes up more of
the wash. For the Wavecrest Shell Jacket the impacts seen in the use stage are far greater than what
is seen for the other products. This stage is also the biggest contributor to impacts for the overall life
cycle of the Wavecrest Shell Jacket with an overall average contribution of 50,1%. This is due to the fact
that this type of waterproof jacket must be washed with re-impregnating detergent to uphold its repellent
properties. Therefore, the product can not be washed with regular clothes and hence a full wash-cycle
must be dedicated to a washing load containing only products with the same properties as the Wavecrest
Shell Jacket. Furthermore, this detergent requires the product to be heated and thus the Wavecrest Shell
Jacket also requires drying and energy used for the drying machine, adding on to impacts.
The end of life stage also plays a role for the two polyester based products. Here, the polyester
waste material is most important for the Ameland Midlayer Jacket and the buttons plus the polyester
waste material plays the most important role for the Wavecrest Shell Jacket. In fact, the buttons pro-
vide 86,2% of the impact on the Freshwater ecotoxicity category in the end of life stage and the waste
polyethylene provide 50,5% to 55,5% impact to the Climate change and Ozone depletion category for
the Wavecrest Shell Jacket. Therefore, this also points towards the importance of awareness on the
composition of both the fabric and components that goes into the product. Something that also feeds into
a circular mindset where it should be considered whether materials should be reused rather than incinerated.

However, for the Elbe Merino Tee it would be difficult to change the process for the main fabric without

conducted on the use stage and the number of washes done here. This is done due to high uncertainty in
customer behaviour. Therefore by creating different scenarios with different number of washes, one can
see the sensitivity of the use stage and how it relates to the other stages.

Completeness check and consistency check
7.5.1
Completeness check
In order to evaluate the completeness of the study, it is important to look at the assumptions made and
quantities used. An overview of these can be found in the Elbe Merino Tee sheet (column C, I, J) and in
the Ameland Midlayer and Wavecrest Jacket sheets (column D, J, K) in appendix A.1.
When looking specifically at the production stage, many processes are as mentioned market processes.
These were chosen in order to include general transportation tied to individual processes based on limited
geographical knowledge on tier 2 manufactures. In this case, processes tied to tier 2 manufacturers were
modelled as GLO or RoW due to the lack of information on their location. In alignment with this, specific
41
==PAGE:51==
geographical processes have been chosen when possible for the production itself, where the sewing and
cutting, for example, is modelled based on Chinese processes. As previously mentioned, dyeing is not
included in the model due to the variety of colors and materials used throughout a product portfolio.
In the use stage, it was assumed that no material was leaked to the environment throughout the actual
use or washing of the product. Here, the water and energy consumption was based of a household washing
machine and dryer, but the materials used for the washer and dryer itself was left out. As the type of
washer or dryer might vary from user to user the energy and water consumption might similarly do so and
hence impact the life cycle of the product. The amount of detergent used is similarly based on a prod-
uct label estimate, but might also depend on user behaviour or the level of calcium in the local water supply.
For the end of life stage, it was assumed that the whole product was incinerated. In this regard,
transportation to and from the incineration plant was not taken into account apart from what was included
in the market processes. The products were similarly assumed to be 100% intact upon disposal and
a European municipal incineration process was used for this. Due to variations in the local disposal
programs and facilities it is possible for results to vary and in this case a standard European without
Switzerland processes were chosen from the Ecoinvent library. No recycling was considered upon disposal
apart from what was included in the standard processes.
7.5.2
Consistency check
Throughout the study, a consistent modelling approach has been used. Here the same method with the
same normalization has been applied for the different products and analyses together with a sensitive
check on this. An overall yet detailed level of modelling has been set for all three products with equal
in-depth detail to allow for a fair identification across products. For all products, an exact geographical
location has been used when possible or replaced with either a GLO or RoW process if available. Market
processes have been used throughout in order to take general transportation into account, and this has
consistently been applied through SimaPro. Finally, all the chosen processes have been documented to
allow for full transparency.
7.6
Limitations
Throughout the life cycle assessment certain assumptions have been made in order to construct a realistic,
yet simplified model. The simplified modelling also means that certain aspects has been left out for
simplicity and hence there are certain limitations to the model. A list of the most important assumptions
and simplifications is found below:
• All processes that include machinery has been modelled as the use of electricity, water, etc. upon the
performed action. Therefore, no impacts are included in terms of stand-by energy or the creation of
such machinery.
• Transportation is only included from China to Denmark and from Superstainable to the customer
whom is assumed to be located in Copenhagen as an approximation to a worst case scenario in
terms of distance within Denmark. In all other cases, market processes are used and transportation
is only included in case the market process includes it. This has been done due to limited knowledge
on tier 2 manufacturers and their locations.
• The process of dyeing the fabrics has not been included. Due to this being a chemically intense
process, additional impacts must be assumed in addition to this study.
• Certain processes for different materials are more or less accurate depending on the available
processes in the Ecoinvent library. The zipper sliders were for example modelled as PET instead of
polyacetal due to the lack of a fitting process.
42
==PAGE:52==
• The buttons in the Wavecrest Shell Jacket are assumed to be incinerated as 100% copper despite it
being modelled as brass in the production stage. Therefore, the zinc content of this component is
ignored and replaced with copper at the end of life of the Wavecrest Shell Jacket
• The Elbe Merino Tee is incinerated as waste textile in municipal incineration due to a lack of
processes specifically for wool.
• Material wear and tear is not included in the model and hence not in the impacts. Here, micro-plastic
might be released to the environment while wearing the products or while washing the product.
Therefore, additional impacts must be assumed beyond what is seen in this study.
• The Ecoinvent library used for this study uses consequential modelling. Therefore, it is based on
marginal data rather than average data and thereby takes market dynamics into consideration.
However, this has been altered for the creation of recycled polyester, were a consequential process is
used for the creation of the recycled polyester fibres but with a recycled granulate input from the
Ecoinvent cut-off library.

8.1
Market change, market position and competitor analysis
To identify how Superstainable’s business and the LCA results can be combined into relevant market
advantages the market changes must first be presented. Therefore, a market analysis is conducted with
respect to environmental changes in the textile market. In alignment with this, Superstainable’s and
competitors current sustainable aspects are presented through sustainable business model archetypes
increasing the understanding of how different sustainable aspects feed into current trends and potential
improvements.
8.1.1
Environmental change in the market
When looking at Superstainable’s position in the market, it is important to understand how the textile
industry is focusing on sustainability. General trends have been to implement recycled materials, as H&M
have done since 2013 where they committed to use recycled plastic bottles, wool and cotton [1]. Standards,
such as the ISO 14001:2015, Blue Sign, OEKO-TEX and others, have increased and have been adopted by
various companies to help monitor and decrease environmental impacts. Certifications have also been
used to guarantee and prove environmental action taken by manufacturers [1].
Eco-Design and circular design have also found their way into the market, where actors have increased
focus on material input by for example using mono-material designs in order to allow for increased reuse [1].
These designs also aim to decrease impacts for all life cycle stages. This is done by using recycled materials
and decreasing waste in production. This also means that manufacturers are starting to implement tools
to optimize their ways of cutting out materials and finding ways to reuse leftovers[47]. The material
consumption in the industry has similarly changed due to textile science which has introduced more natural
based materials and processes. This is also seen on the increased use of cellulosic fibers in various fabrics [1].
For synthetic fibres, recycled polyester has proven to be a more eco-friendly substitute to the virgin
based polyester, and has since 1993 been used by Patagonia for production. However, as it still draws on
additional use of chemicals, water, and energy consumption, it also comes with drawbacks. Therefore,
an increased focus has evolved on zero-waste garment designs using the previous mentioned approaches
to reducing waste [1]. In alignment with this, multiple approaches and suppliers have found their way
onto the recycled market. This is, for example, seen through the different origins of the recycled PET,
which can both come from recycled PET bottles, but also from for example recycled fishing nets [48].
This way manufacturers can even further increase and specify their focus on sustainability when choosing
their recycled material and for example increase their focus on SDG 14 and life below water if they desire to.
In alignment with companies trying to decrease the amount of waste and using more and more recycled
materials, trends such as re-purposing and reselling clothes has also seen the light of day[41]. As the
modern society is characterized by consumerism and throw-away societies, this also feeds into the idea
of reselling clothing in both secondhand shops or by closing the loop through take-back programs from
the retailers or manufacturers [49]. By initiating such solutions, the garments use and lifetime can be
increased before the product is disposed, and thus lead to a more sustainable product. Furthermore, this
also targets the low average product use of 7-10 wears before the product is disposed of, and also avoids
the need for drastic change in customer behaviour in terms of consumption [41].

8.1.2

8.2
Solutions
In this section relevant solutions will be presented and discussed. These solutions will be based on the
current business and strategy presented in section 6.3 and aim to support a more sustainable product
portfolio. In addition to this, the competitor and market dynamics will be taken into consideration and
used to align relevant solutions with identified hotpots from the LCA.
8.2.1
Certified and reprocessed wool
When looking at Superstainable’s strategy it was very clear, that certifications from their supply chain
had a big influence on their products and strategy. With the main and face fabrics showing big impacts in
the LCA, this also aligns well with the use of certified materials. By looking at the competitors, it is also
clear that Superstainable is not the only manufacturer using certifications. Here, all other competitors
use the certifications on Responsible Wool Standard (RWS) [73][74][75]. Norrøna similarly mentions that
they also in some cases use reprocessed wool, which could help bring down impacts[73]. Due to the wool
material and the creation of wool showing big impacts on the Elbe Merino Tee, the importance of the
material can not be understated and thus the approach seen from Norrøna should be considered. With an
increasing focus on zero waste and an assumption made on this in the LCA the impacts from the fabric
once again becomes even more important [1]. Therefore, Superstainable should bear in mind that due to
the impact generated by the fabrics, the amount of fabric wasted in production is highly influential. Last
but not least, the focus on certified and reprocessed wool aligns well with Superstainable’s focus on SDG
12 and 13. It also fits into the current approach seen in section 6.2.2 as well as in the sustainable business
model canvas in fig. 10, making this a relevant solution.
8.2.2
Recycled polyester
When looking at the recycled polyester material, the sensitivity analysis showed reduction in most cat-
egories. Here, the Ameland Midlayer Jacket showed high sensitivity in the impact category of Ozone
depletion, when changing from recycled to non-recycled material. The same was the case for the Wavecrest
Shell Jacket, despite this being less of an increase due to the relative amount of recycled material used.
The non-recycled material also showed improvement in some categories, and hence it could be relevant
to investigate further. However, for this study the use of recycled polyester is considered favorable due
to the overall impact reductions. The increasing market focus as well as insights gained from previous
studies also illustrated the same trend, underlining the importance and potential of using recycled rather
than virgin material [1]. Thus another solution would be to focus on increasing the amount of recycled
material in the products. One way to approach this, could for example be to introduce recycled thread,
zipper-tape and zipper-teeth to the Ameland Midlayer Jacket. In the Wavecrest Shell Jacket, the 33g
backer could similarly come from recycled material together with thread and zippers.
By introducing more recycled material, Superstainable will be able to increase their contribution and
focus on SDG sub-target 12-2, 12-4, and 12-5. In this regard, Superstainable could also consider the origin
of the recycled polyester in order to align with their focus on SDG 14 as seen with Norrøna and Patagonia
who have chosen to use recycled fishing nets rather than plastic bottle to support their goals and reduce
waste in the oceans [62][66].
8.2.3
Awareness of components
When considering the impacts generated by components in the products the buttons in the Wavecrest
Shell Jacket can not be overlooked. This component showed a large contribution in the Mineral, fossil
& ren resource depletion, Freshwater ecotoxicity as well as the two Human toxicity categories in the
production stage. It also gave a large contribution to the end of life stage due to incineration of copper.
As the button was modelled as a brass component and incinerated as pure copper, the modelled impact
might exceed the actual impacts in the life cycle. However, it points towards a very important aspects,
47
==PAGE:57==
namely that Superstainable should be aware of the content of the components. Here, Superstainable
could for example focus on the increasing market trend of using components based on mono-materials in
order to increase the recyclability of these components. As this study had difficulties on obtaining tier 2
data, this similarly shows that this aspect is not as transparent and embedded as it could be. Therefore,
Superstainable should be more aware of who their tier 2 producers are, how the tier 2 manufacturer
produces the components as well as what raw material that goes into the components that Superstainable
or LIME China use to in the products. This way Superstainable will become more transparent and aligned
with their own strategy, their focus on SDG 12 as well as with what competitors are currently doing in
order to bring down impacts [57]. Furthermore, market trends show an increased focus on designs that
allow for components or parts of products to be reused. Here, Supertainable could introduced removable
buttons that could then be reused in other products either by themselves or by the user. This could also
potentially be adopted and used for other components or even products to allow a closed loop as seen
done by Patagonia [61].
8.2.4
Renewable energy
When looking at the competitors it is clear that Superstainable is falling behind on the technological
archetype of substitute with renewables and natural processes. With Superstainable being a founding
member of STICA, they are indeed focusing on CO2 reduction, but despite this they have no aims or goals
in terms of being CO2 neutral. Neither, do they have a special focus on changing their energy supply
to renenwables, despite the majority of the impacts coming from the production stage. Therefore, this
approach and change seen from competitors would be relevant for Superstainable so they can decrease
impacts in the production and their overall GWP.
Despite the fabrics being the biggest sinners in the production stage, there were also indications on
sewing being a contributor due to the use of energy. For the Wavecrest Shell Jacket this meant an average
contribution of 11,3% and for the Elbe Merino Tee it meant 36,48% contribution when excluding the wool
fabric. Therefore, this solution presents the opportunity for Superstainable to increase the focus on their
own and their suppliers energy mix in order to decrease their CO2 footprint and increase their focus on
SDG 12-2 and 12-6. This way they might be able to bring down impacts for the general production as
well as keeping up with competitors solutions to keep and potentially gain market share.
8.2.5
User behaviour
As the use stage presented significant impacts in the life cycles of especially the polyester based products, it
would be relevant to find a solution that tackles this. In this case, the impact was generated by electricity
and water use and was highest for the polyester products due to their share of the load in the washing
machine. However, as Superstainable can not control the users behaviour it is difficult to create a solution
that with certainty will decrease the impacts. Furthermore, the technology used for the Wavecrest Shell
Jacket in terms of the membrane and its waterproof properties makes it difficult to cut back on the use
stage without compromising the performance of the product. A change in membrane and structure would
similarly come with significant alterations of the product and change its functionality [76]. Therefore, no
concrete solution can be presented, but Superstainable should be aware of this, and perhaps increase the
focus on how their material composition allows for, for example, fewer amounts of washes or wash cycles
that requires less energy or water. They could simultaneously increase dissemination of such information
to the customers allowing them to understand how best to take care of their products while being as
environmentally friendly as possible.
Overall, the presented solutions points towards Superstainable increasing their already existing focus
on the materials used in their product portfolio. In alignment with this Superstainable have already seen
and reaped the benefits in terms of impact reduction by using recycled polyester in some of their polyester
based fabrics, and thus they should start focusing on making the products 100% recycled. Therefore, they
48
==PAGE:58==
should especially increase the focus on tier 2 manufacturers and the material content of components. The
same goes for other parts of their portfolio, where Superstainable should expand their horizon on the use
of certifications on their wool products. Furthermore, their overall impacts based on production might
also be decreased by introducing a new and increased focus on the energy mix used by Superstainable’s
manufacturers.
8.3
Feasibility and potential benefits
In order to determine the feasibility of the solutions, the solutions were presented and discussed with
Superstainable. Based on their feedback barriers and carriers for implementing the solutions can be
presented i terms of both technological and financial feasibility. The section will also include modelling of
some of the presented solutions in order to see the benefits they might represent.
8.3.1
Certified and reprocessed wool
When looking at the Elbe Merino Tee and the wool based products presented by Superstainable, an
obvious solution would be to certify the wool. This is done by competitors who use the RWS, hence it is
possible for Superstainable to use existing certifications and thereby fully feasible in terms of availability.
However, as it was described in the sustainable business model canvas these certifications does not come
free of charge. Therefore, Superstainable biggest barrier, in this sense, will be the extra expense linked to
getting this certification. In this case the expenses related to the RWS is estimated to be between 1500$
and 3000$ for initial inspection plus general expenses related to cost related to changes in the supply
chain, running cost, and running inspections[77]. By introduction these costs the certification does not
align with Superstainable value proposition minded on low cost products, making the introduction less
desirable, something that will also be discussed later. Furthermore, Superstainable also expresses their
scepticism towards the benefits of using the certification, as they are already focusing on animal welfare
and using mulesing-free wool, thereby decreasing the actual gains of using the certification [78][79].
The introduction of reprocessed or recycled wool is like the certifications something that is done by
competitors and technologically feasible. In this case, the impact specific benefits are not so easy to
estimate as recycled wool is not available in the Ecoinvent database. However when looking at studies
done on this topic, research shows that the use of recycled wool will help decrease impacts, as processes
such as scouring, spinning, and knitting is avoided [80]. By looking at the impacts presented in table 16,
scenario 3C in Norden-Gaining benefits from discarded textiles, one can see, that all impacts are decreased
when using reused wool to replace virgin wool in a RoW process [80]. Thus, this represents a very promis-
ing material adjustment if Superstainable wish to improve upon the impacts of their woolen based products.
When considering the feasibility of this solution, the main barrier presents itself as the cost and
difficulty in obtaining the reused wool fabric. In alignment with this, the collaboration with LIME China,
that previously presented benefits such as reduced production cost, now also presents restrictions as
Superstaianble might be limited in their choice of materials. Here the reduced production cost was
achieved by tapping into LIME China’s existing orders and using the same fabric as ordered by the existing
customer. However, when wishing to introduce new materials and fabrics, Superstainable will no longer be
able to get these benefits and thus, they must expect increased production costs. Therefore, this presents
a barrier as increased costs will lead to increased product prices and thereby move Superstainable further
away from their current value proposition, namely low cost products that allows the average danish user
to get out and into nature.

that they would either have to specifically choose renewable energy sources from the local area, be moved
to an area where more renewable energy is available on the energy grid, or generate their own renewable
energy through for example solar panels or wind energy. Due to the number of consequences related to
moving the factory, including potential emissions from building a new factory, making new machines, and
transporting everything this option is deemed non-feasible. Therefore, the focus will be upon the use of
renewable energy introduced by LIME China.

User behaviour
Due to the difficulties related to estimating customer behaviour and the feasibility of reducing impacts
from the use stage, the feasibility of this solution can not be determined. However, it will be said that
the task of informing the customers on how best to take care of their products is highly feasible as a
stand-alone process, but it does not guarantee a reduction in impact as the user might decide to remain in
their old habits [84]. When it comes to the product itself, the performance of the products must be upheld
for it to live up to its functionality and the set functional unit. In this case, the feasibility of reducing
impacts based on a change in construction and need of washing is considered less feasible as it would
require Superstainable to come up with new materials and technologies to move away from traditional
solutions used throughout the outdoor industry [76]. Furthermore, a change to a more environmentally
friendly solution does once again not guarantee a reduction in impact as the user behaviour might overrule
this. Therefore, the feasibility of both approaches comes with high uncertainty and a requirement of
Superstainable looking into their customers behaviour and potential of being able to change this.

9
Discussion
In order to give an overall estimation on the contributions from this project, multiple areas of the report
will be discussed. This is done in order to put the approach, findings, and solutions into perspective, as
they are all based on specific assumptions and methodical approaches. The discussion will similarly focus
on the approaches impacts on the stated research question. Here, it is specifically relevant to reflect upon
how this project and its focus on individual products have laid a foundation for changes, that will affect
the whole product portfolio.
When considering the initial analysis of Superstainable’s business, it was clear that there was a unique
relation between Superstainable and LIME China. This relationship created good circumstances for
Superstainable to enter the market with a relatively unique proposition in terms of certified products.
This was also seen through the sustainable business model canvas in fig. 10, where this approach similarly
was expressed through key partners, key resources, value proposition, and eco-social benefits. Therefore,
this strongly pointed towards Superstainable using LIME China for certified materials as the foundation
for their strategy.
When looking at the relationship it is obvious that Superstainable can draw on many advantages
due to this co-ownership, but it is also important to mention the limitations that it brings. In this
regard, the presented solution on changing to renewable energy comes with a great deal of restructuring
as Superstainable cannot simply change their provider if they wish to uphold their current cost structure,
certifications and strategy. Therefore by using their unique relationship and benefits, Superstainable
creates a dependency on LIME China which means that if Superstainable wish to reduce their impacts
in production, they rely on LIME China to be willing and have the capital to do so. This dependency
also showed some drawbacks when Superstainable some times had to use the same fabrics as chosen by
another customer due to their ability to tap into the production. However, this depended on whether
or not Superstainable was able to pay the full cost for running their own production, showing a cost
structure dependency that despite the downsides also makes room for flexibility in Superstainable’s
economy. Therefore, Superstainable should be very aware of the relationship as it is not only their
cost structure and value proposition that is closely tied to this, their whole strategy also builds on the
collaboration and the certifications that LIME China provides to the products.
When analyzing the market through Porter’s five forces, the amount of providers of quality outdoor
products proved to be relatively high. This meant a strong bargaining power for the buyer whom could
easily find a different provider, if desired. Therefore, the price became a relevant factor for the consumer,
and especially so when considering Superstainable’s value proposition of low cost products and their
targeted customer group. Based on this, certain conflicts arose in terms of Superstainable’s strategy, their
cost structure, and their economy. Here, the conflicts came to express themselves through the identified
use of certifications and memberships of organisations such as STICA, which meant increased costs on
top of standard production costs. This presented a clash between the strategic approach of providing
fully certified low cost products and the capital available to achieve this due to Superstainable’s current
market share and size. Nonetheless, these certifications are also what allows Superstainable to be unique
and hence it is difficult to say, how best to balance and weigh the need for differentiation, and low cost.
With an increasing focus on sustainability in the textile market, Superstainable could potentially also gain
market benefits and market share based on their early implementation of certifications and their founding
values within sustainability. This could in the long run help them compete with competitors, who simply
have adopted these values and hence face increased costs due to changes needed to be done to their
supply chain in the future. However, in general it can be discussed how realistic it is for Superstainable to
maintain a profitable business where low product costs is amongst the main drivers if they similarly wish
to focus on having the best sustainable and most certified products in the market. When considering
their size compared to competitors and the trends in the market, an increased focus on sustainability
55
==PAGE:65==
could help Superstainable become more competitive in terms of cost, but it could also work against their
current unique position and in time decrease their value proposition.
With the LCA only focusing on environmental impacts, there is a shortcoming in terms of the impacts
generated by using specific suppliers with social standards. In this case, the social SA8000 standard is used
by Superstainable and they similarly focus on animal welfare in terms of mulesing-free wool. As the benefits
of these can be difficult to estimate and quantify, they present an important shortcoming for this project.
This was, for example, expressed in the solution related to using the RWS certification for Superstainable’s
wool products where the benefits of this was difficult to estimate due to them not being quantifiable. This
is also an expression of how the introduction of solutions to improve social sustainability not only requires
deep insight into the the organisation, but also requires time as the solutions potentially develop differently
depending on internal dynamics. Especially if the solutions are not in the form of standards or certifi-
cations. Due to the time-span of this project, this was hence something that was not possible to account for.
When it comes to the analysis, multiple impact categories are provided through the ILCD midpoint+
method. In this case it must be considered that it might be difficult for Superstainable to focus on
all midpoints and thus all three endpoints, namely Human health, Natural environment and Natural
resources[5]. Despite this, this study has done so due to the difficulties related to weighing the importance
of each impact category. Therefore, this is also something that can alter the presented solutions as
Superstainable might decide to focus on decreasing just one or more impacts or endpoints. However,
if done so, Superstainable should keep in mind what specific changes might have of consequences in
other categories, if they wish for their product to be as environmentally friendly as possible. Despite
Superstainable’s potential focus, this study has impacts and results with equal weight in order to best
possible illustrate the impacts and dynamics in the product life cycle. This was also the reason for
identifying trends rather than specific hotspots as this best possibly allowed the solutions to accommodate
for various preferences.
When looking at the impact categories and the specific products, it is clear that these three products
do not cover all insights on the full portfolio. Therefore, it will be discussed how the analysed products
are reflecting the full portfolio as well as what other aspects that might be relevant. When thus looking
at the three products it is as stated products that are all produced at the site in China. Therefore,
products produced elsewhere might have different impacts generated from transportation or the use of
a different energy mix. However, as these aspects were not prominent in the analysed products, they
can not be ruled out, but they help illustrate that there are other aspects that in general would be
more relevant for Superstainable to focus on. Furthermore, the analysis does not cover all types of
materials present in Superstainable’s portfolio, as seen from the overview in section 6.3.2. Since the fabrics
showed big contributions to the impact categories, Superstainable should in addition to the suggested
improvements also consider what other improvements that might have been relevant if other materials
such as organic cotton or nylon were considered. Thus, this limits the area of which the presented solutions
can improve the impacts of the overall portfolio due to the initial choice of products, that were focusing on
diversity in both material, functionality, and process flow rather than exclusively on material composition.
Nonetheless, the insights from the current solutions are still valid and the understanding of products and
their impacts gained from the LCA analysis could potentially, and cautiously, be transferred to other
product compositions leading to an improvement of the overall portfolio.
When considering the products and the modelling, multiple assumptions were made in order to
perform the study. As the consequences of these assumptions can be difficult to accurately estimate,
some of the main assumptions are discussed here. With an assumption of zero waste in the production,
it is obvious that impacts must be considered greater than what is seen from this study. Nevertheless,
this assumption combined with the dynamics seen from the LCA indirectly presents a focus area for
Superstainable, namely that they should be especially aware of the fabric waste in their production.
56
==PAGE:66==
However, this also depends on how much waste is assigned to the different components, as a significant
difference in this might alter the results of this study. Despite this, it is considered realistic that the
fabric would contribute with the biggest share due to how much of the products it constitutes. Since
the fabric is also already the main contribute to the impact categories, this is considered a good pointer
towards relevant considerations in terms of focus areas for waste reduction. However as a consequence of
these uncertainties, the impacts and benefits of a waste reduction cannot be determined. Thus, another
study would be necessary for Superstainable to know the full impact generated by products and their waste.
When it comes to the energy mix used for the modelling, this similarly comes with some uncertainty,
and so does the use stage of the products life cycle. In both cases, it can be relatively difficult to say with
certainty how changes in this would affect the product portfolio. In this case, the project is as mentioned
also only taking the Chinese manufacturing cite into account due to the chosen products and their origin.
Therefore, this brings even bigger uncertainty to the picture, when considering that Superstainable’s
product portfolio is also produced at other facilities in other parts of the world. Nonetheless, this paints a
picture of how the energy used for sewing and other tasks affect the products, and that these cannot be
ignored if Superstaiable wish to improve the environmental impact of the products. However, despite
being able to see the benefits of introducing renewable energy, it can be discussed how feasible this is, as
it comes with a potentially great cost. Superstainable should also be aware that the process of sewing
is estimated based on generic data on the amount of thread needed for a given product as well as the
type of machine used. Thus, they should consider their own specific instruments and measurements if
possible. Furthermore, Superstainable will as mentioned not be the one making the change, and hence
the feasibility of this also relies on LIME China’s willingness to pay for such expenses, adding to the
uncertainty and difficulty in estimating the feasibility.
With an assumptions made in relation to the functional unit, the use stage, and the products lifetime
this also provides additional uncertainty. In this case, the lifetime was estimated based on a number of
competitor warranties. This was done since Superstainable did not yet have data on the lifetime of their
products due to their short time in the market. Therefore, it can be discussed if the set lifetime is realistic
and especially so considering that Superstainable also provide their customers with the option of repairing
broken products. In this regard a sensitivity analysis was carried out. Here the number of washes were
used as an expression for the lifetime due to difficulties related to estimating the degradation of products
based on use. As SimaPro, similarly, does not support an impact category on the release of micro plastic
Superstainable should consider potential impacts that might be generated this way. Thus, Superstainable
should consider what the lifetime of their products actually look like and how this could alter the results,
if the products lifetime for example was decreased due to products breaking or due to a difference in what
the real world use stage looks like.
When looking at the approach used to generate the solutions, the insights they give to Superstainable,
and their portfolio, it is relevant to discuss how Superstainable’s market position is affected by such
changes. In this case, the solutions are not an expression of innovative solutions, but rather solutions that
are based on current trends in the market and amongst competitors. Therefore, it can be discussed to
what extend these solutions will help Superstainable capture a new and unexploited segment. However,
these solutions were chosen in order to use validated approaches that has proven to have an impact within
the textile market. This also means that the solutions are not minded upon Superstainable’s desire for
being front-runners, but rather it helps them keep up with solutions that their competitors are using.
This also means that these solutions focus on making Superstainable more financially and strategically
robust, so that they in the future can increase their current focus on being front-runners while not falling
back on generic opportunities and approaches. This way the solutions will help them capture and compete
on the market they already find themselves in and potentially make them more competitive in the eyes of
those customers that highly value sustainability.

Another reason for choosing validated solutions and solutions that are already present in the market is
that these solutions can be made more generic. This allows the study to propose recommendations that
target the whole product portfolio and not just single products. This also supports the idea of making
Superstainable a more sustainable company and especially when considering the environmental aspect,
where the presented solutions have the potential of wider impacts throughout the supply chain. This, of
course, increases the uncertainty in terms of what impacts that are actually present if other products were
analysed. However, with this study being limited in both time and resources this approach was chosen to
give the best possible estimate and to allow for more relatable and thus feasible solutions.
Finally, the physical implications of the presented solutions are important to discuss as they, like
the approach, provide different uncertainties to the conclusion of this study. Based on the solution and
results on the use of recycled material and components it was clear that this solution had the potential
of decreasing the immediate impacts in various categories. However, as this might interfere with the
functionality, performance, and multipurpose ability of the products, this can cause indirect impacts and
decrease in sustainability. These indirect impacts could for example be a result of decreasing performance,
but also a decrease in the amount of use before the product is disposed. Both something that would
lead to a decreased sustainability as the customer would have to find a new or an additional product to
cover the need. Therefore, it is highly important that Superstainable test and consider the implications of
introducing such changes to their products in order to uphold the durability, longevity, and use that also
defines the impacts of the products life cycle.

Conclusion
Based on the findings of this report, the presented research question and sub-questions can be concluded
upon. When first looking at the initial understanding of Superstinable’s business model, it became clear
that Superstainable was highly dependent on their collaboration with LIME China. This collaboration
presented them with certain business advantages in terms reduced costs, certified manufacturing, and
flexibility, but the collaboration also meant drawbacks such as high dependency and limited control. In
this regard, Superstainable focused on the use of certified materials and work environments in order to
support sustainability and their business. Their identified target group was the average danish user to
whom their value proposition mainly consisted of providing certified, low cost, and multipurpose products
allowing the customer to get out and into nature.
As Superstainable’s portfolio consisted of many different products, the Elbe Merino Tee, the Ameland
Midlayer Jacket, and the Wavecrest Shell Jacket were chosen as representative products for the portfolio
and used for hotspot analysis. Through this analysis it became evident that all three product types were
highly impacted by the fabrics due to the fabrics share of the product. Therefore, it was considered very
relevant that Superstainable was already using certified and recycled polyester for some of their products.
When it came to the wool based products, no certifications were found apart from the the use of mule-sing
free wool which contributes to increased animal welfare. Therefore, Superstainable should look into the
use of the Responsible Wool Standard certification or even recycled wool which could contribute to a
decrease throughout impact categories.
When considering the identified hotspots in the polyester based products, it was found that some
fabrics and components used for zippers, button, etc. was not recycled materials. In alignment with this,
a scenario analysis was carried out where the recycled polyester was first turned into virgin polyester to
see the benefits that Superstainable had already achieved. This resulted in an 808% decrease in the Ozone
depletion category and a 25% decrease in the GWP for the Ameland Midlayer Jacket. This was later
followed by a potential future scenario where all non-recycled components were turned into recycled PET
components leading to even further impact decreases. Hence, it can be concluded that Superstainable
should be more aware of what components they use and buy from tier two manufactures. Also, it is
important that they maintain their focus on using certified and recycled fabrics. This solution and focus
was also considered the most feasible solution as it would require little change in the structure, approach,
and supply chain. Here, the change in supply chain was supported by market trends leading to an increase
in recycled material suppliers and components. It was further supported by identified recycled components
within Superstainable existing supply chain. When this is said, Superstainable should keep in mind,
what effects such substitutions might have as the change might result in poor performance or decreased
longevity. Furthermore, the limited amount of different variants of recycled components might also pose a
challenge to the functionality and value proposition of multipurpose products and potentially challenge or
decrease the amount a product is used before disposed of and potentially leading to increasing impacts.
Apart from looking at materials certain processes in the manufacturing also presented possible areas
of improvement. In this case, the production was modelled based on a Chinese energy mix from which
especially sewing showed impacts. Due to this, the competitors, and an increased market focus on renew-
able energy, it was deemed relevant for Superstainable to consider pushing LIME China into introducing
more renewable energy for the assembly stage. Based on another scenario analysis, impact reductions
throughout categories were shown when exclusively using wind power. However, it was also considered
less feasible due Superstainable limited say in LIME China’s choices and the cost related to such change.
Nonetheless, this would support an area of Superstainable’s business of which they are not yet focusing.
The use stage also showed proof of significant impacts in the Freshwater ecotoxicity category and
high sensitive in the Water resource depletion category when considering the more heavy and waterproof
59
==PAGE:69==
polyester products. This was especially the case for for the Wavecrest Shell Jacket that required both
washing and drying. Based on these results and the identified sensitivity, Superstainable must consider
that the use stage have the potential of generating significant impacts. However, as these behaviours can
be difficult to estimate and change, it would require additional research to identify potential solutions.
All in all, the identified hotspots and the corresponding solutions have presented options of which
Superstainable can adjust their portfolio in order to better align with their targeted SDGs and create a
more sustainable portfolio. This is, especially, the case for sub-targets such as 12-2,12-4,12-5, 13-2 and
14-1 where the changes can help improve consumption and waste to make the portfolio more sustainable
and circular. The solutions similarly present business aspects of which Superstainable is falling behind
on when comparing to competitors and hence, areas of which Superstainable should focus on if they
wish to have a robust business plan from which they can focus on being front-runners. In this regard,
Supertainable should also focus on the alignment of their value proposition in the sense of them wishing
to provide low cost products for the average danish user as this somewhat conflicts with the increased
costs related to obtaining certified materials and components. However, as certified clothing is highly
embedded in the companies strategy, Superstainable will have to consider how best to implement the
presented solutions in order to make the best possible trade-off between cost and benefits.
