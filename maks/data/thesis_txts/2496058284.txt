Abstract
The opportunistic bacteria Clostridioides diﬃcile (C. diﬃcile) with its resistant
spores and toxins is an urgent thread to developed countries. The main risk factor
of developing Clostridioides diﬃcile infection (CDI) is disruption of the microbial
intestinal balance, as is often the case in antibiotic treatments. Beforehand, el-
derly immunosuppressed people at prolonged hospital stays were at highest risk
of developing CDIs. Nowadays, the amount of community acquired CDIs, in the
population of healthy people as pregnant women, has increased, and is of great
concern. Severe and complicated symptoms as diarrhoea, pseudomembranous coli-
tis, intestinal perforation, toxic megacolon, and even deaths of foetuses and young
women have been observed due to CDI in this population.
In this project, a pregnant patient was diagnosed with CDI. Her symptoms were
only clinical and not systemic yet. She had relapses after antibiotic treatments, and
instead, she was treated with a faecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) through
colonoscopy with success at the Department of Hepatology and Gastroenterology
at Aarhus University Hospital. Short after the FMT, her symptoms disappeared,
and she had no recurrences of CDI. A healthy term boy was born 6 months after
the FMT.
The aim of the project was to examine changes to the bacterial community com-
positions of the patient and the infant after FMT. DNA from faecal samples of
the donor, the patient, and the infant was extracted, bacterial libraries of the V3
region of the 16S rRNA gene were prepared with PCR ampliﬁcation, and they were
sequenced with Ion Torrent sequencing. Bioinformatics was used to understand
and investigate the sequenced data. Data analysis included diversity and relative
abundances. The presence of C. diﬃcile toxin genes were examined with qPCR
with SYBR Green I and with TaqMan™-based probes.
After FMT, the bacterial community composition of the patient’s microbiota changed
to levels similar with the donor’s microbiota. An increase of bacteria belonging to
the phyla Bacteriodetes and Firmicutes were observed after FMT, as well as de-
crease of bacteria belonging to the phylum Proteobacteria. Bacteria derived from
the donor colonised the microbiota of the pregnant patient, and persisted there
over time. C. diﬃcile toxin genes tcdA and tcdB were detected in the patient be-
fore FMT, and in the infant at 6 months of age.
The bacterial balance of the pregnant patient’s microbiota was restored with FMT
from a healthy donor through colonoscopy. The healthy infant was born at term,
and had derived some bacteria from the donor. Data of safety of FMT during
pregnancy is missing, and this project could add knowledge of the beneﬁts and
safety of FMT in pregnant women with CDI.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
For the last two decades in the industrialised world, the infections from the bacteria
Clostridioides diﬃcile (C. diﬃcile) have reached historic heights [1, 2] in terms of
incidences, morbidity, mortality, recurrences, and healthcare costs [3, 4]. It has
been suggested to be the most common cause of healthcare-associated infection,
and thereby it has replaced methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)
[2, 5, 6].
Symptoms of infection with C. diﬃcile ranges from mild self-limiting
diarrhoea to, in severe or complicated cases, pseudomembranous colitis, intestinal
perforation, toxic megacolon, sepsis, and death [3]. Every year more than 300,000
Europeans are diagnosed with severe colitis [7], and the potential costs of C. dif-
ﬁcile in Europe is estimated to €3000 million/year [8]. In the United States, C.
diﬃcile causes 14,000 deaths and approximately $1.5 billion in additional medical
costs every year [2, 9]. The numbers of incidences, deaths and costs are expected
to increase over the next decade [8].
In the United States, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) consider
C. diﬃcile as an urgent public health threat [10, 11]. It is a public health threat
since occurrences in both hospital acquired and community acquired Clostridioides
diﬃcile infections (CDIs) have increased drastically [12]. It is a major cause of noso-
comial diarrhoea and epidemics of diarrhoea have been seen in hospitals [2, 12]. C.
diﬃcile causes many infectious disease deaths in developed countries [3]. CDIs hap-
pen often as a side e↵ect of antibiotic exposure. C. diﬃcile is the causal agent of 15-
25% of the cases of antibiotic-associated diarrhea, 50-75% of antibiotic-associated
colitis, and 90-100% of antibiotic-associated pseudomembranous colitis [13]. CDI is
associated with high recurrence rates of 30–65%. It is common that CDI is refrac-
tory and only responds to antibiotics when antibiotic treatment is in progress, and
not after end treatment [1, 14]. Beforehand, it mostly a↵ected elderly or immuno-
suppressed people. A tendency of CDIs in healthy people as pregnant women has
been observed [15]. Miscarriages and maternal deaths due to CDI have increased
the focus in this area [15, 16, 17].
A new treatment method was approved by the United States Food and Drug Ad-
ministration (FDA) in 2013, and is called faecal microbiota transplantation (FMT).
Also the European Society for Microbiology and Infectious Disease and the Amer-
ican College of Gastroenterology have approved FMT for treatment of recurrent
and refractory CDIs [18, 19]. In FMT, donated faeces from a healthy donor is
transplanted into a patient with a disrupted intestinal microbiota. This method
directly changes the composition of the recipient’s intestinal microbiota. FMT has
beneﬁcial therapeutic e↵ects, and resolution of symptoms happens fast [18, 20].
The cure rates are up to 90% in recurrent or refractory CDIs, where cure rates for
prolonged antibiotic therapy are 20-30% [18]. According to cost, FMT has been
reported as cost-e↵ective compared to antibiotic treatment that often fails [21, 22].
However, the FMT procedure is not standardised yet [19], but it has promising
treatment possibilities, also in other gastrointestinal conditions [18, 23].

INTRODUCTION
In this project, a pregnant woman with CDI is the recipient of a FMT from an
anonymous donor. She is Danish and in her late twenties. Shortly before, she got
pregnant with her second child, she had antibiotics to treat a periodontal disease.
She ended up with a CDI with only clinical symptoms as profuse diarrhoea. CDI
was attempt treated twice, ﬁrst with oral metronidazole and second with oral van-
comycin, but the e↵ects were transient both times, and symptoms returned after
antibiotic treatment ended. Due to fear of miscarriage and fear of progression of
the symptoms and disease, she had a FMT at the Department of Hepatology and
Gastroenterology at AUH. She recovered the same day, and she had no symptoms
or relapses afterwards. Six months later, a healthy term boy was born. Several
faecal samples of the patient and the infant had been collected, and the project
aims to investigate the bacterial community compositions in the faecal microbiota
of the healthy donor, the patient before and after FMT, and the infant.

Background
This section contains an introduction to the intestinal microbiota including the
establishment when born, and disruption of balance often leading to diseases. Ad-
ditionally, this section contains a thorough description of the bacteria Clostridioides
diﬃcile, its transmission route, its virulence factors, and risk factors, it also contains
the infection by C. diﬃcile including diagnosis, recurrence and refractory CDI, and
also prevention and treatment of CDI. Lastly, this section contains an introduction
to the method faecal microbiota transplantation including the history behind, the
mode of action, and also the clinical application framework of FMT, and the side
e↵ects of it. The understanding of the infection by C. diﬃcile and the treatment
options are essential for further investigation and research of the changes in the
microbial community composition of the pregnant patient and the infant.
2.1
The Intestinal Microbiota
Hundreds of microbial species colonise the gastrointestinal tracts (GITs) of mam-
mals [10]. It is a complex community of microorganisms that is termed the in-
testinal microbiota [24]. In the body, the GIT is the largest, densest, and most
diverse microbial community [25]. The density of microbes in the GIT in adults is
estimated to 1011–1012 microbes/ml of luminal content [26]. In total, the human
adult body harbors on average 1013–1014 microbial cells, which is ten times more
than the number of human cells in the adult human body [24]. The number of
di↵erent microbial species in the GIT is 500-1000, and the microbial genome is
100-fold greater than the genome of the human host [3]. Figure 1 illustrates the
human GIT, the increasing diversity throughout the GIT, and the typical species
found.

increasing diversity throughout and the typical species found are illustrated. The
ﬁgure is from [27].

BACKGROUND
Colonising bacterial species constitute the largest part in the GIT of humans [18].
The majority of the species are strict anaerobes, and the most prevalent phyla
are Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Proteobacteria, and Actinobacteria [21, 27]. The
anaerobic bacteria outnumber the aerobic bacteria by a factor 1000:1 [28]. More
than 400 bacterial species have been estimated to constitute the ecosystem of the
human colon alone [26]. The majority of species in the microbial community in
the human adult colon belongs to the anaerobic genera Bacteroides, Eubacterium,
Clostridium, Ruminococcus, and Faecalibacterium [26]. The intestinal microbiota
also includes viruses, fungi, archaea, and single-celled eukaryotes, but research has
focused mostly on bacteria [25]. The relative number of microbial species vary be-
tween individuals [27], giving each individual a unique microbial community [18, 26].
The microbiota is relatively stable over time [18].
Awareness and evidence have linked human health and well-being to the commensal
and mutualistic living microorganisms of the intestinal microbiota [24, 27]. The
intestinal microbiota contributes to health and homeostasis [3, 10], and has several
important functions including resistance to colonisation of intestinal pathogens [10,
26, 29], protection of overgrowth of pathogenic microorganisms [28], immunity,
gastrointestinal motility [3], metabolic functions [29], maturation of the colonic
epithelium [18], nutrient processing [26], consumption, storage and distribution of
energy to the host [30]. Human health and disease are related to homeostasis of
the gut microbiota.
2.1.1
Establishment of the Intestinal Microbiota of Newborns
An essential process in the human life cycle is the microbial colonisation of the
infant GIT. The colonisation of the infant GIT is a↵ected by gestational age and
birth weight, and also mode of delivery, diet, use of antibiotics, and the environ-
ment. The actual contributions are not known [31]. The microbial colonisation of
the infant GIT has a signiﬁcant role in terms of development and education of the
immune system of the host [32].
In the ﬁrst years of life, approximately 2-4 years, the intestinal microbiota devel-
ops and modulates in species abundance [33], and locates a unique equilibrium.
Microbial communities stabilise until the intestinal microbiota appears adult-like
around the age of three [21, 32]. In the ﬁrst years, the composition of the intestinal
microbiota varies and is highly unstable [26, 29, 33].
Previously, the intestinal tract of infants was thought to be sterile at birth [26,
29, 34]. Some studies suggest that the gut colonisation begins before birth, since
microbes in meconium have been detected [31]. After birth, the intestines are imme-
diately colonised [34] to a dense and various microbiota [26, 35]. Vaginal delivered
infants are ﬁrst exposed to microbes from the mother’s birth canal, in which it is
coated in [25]. Both maternal vaginal, intestinal and skin microbiotas are colonising
the infant during vaginal delivery [29, 34, 35].

Infants who are delivered by caesarian section are colonised with microbiotas of the
maternal skin and the environment when born, such as hospital environment and
healthcare workers [29, 34, 36]. The major microbial load that colonises the infant’s
GIT during the ﬁrst days of life has a determinant role in educating the immune
system [29, 35, 36], and a major role in establishing the intestinal microbiota that
one ends with as an adult [30, 32].
The intestines are aerobic at birth allowing only facultative anaerobes as mem-
bers of the Enterobacteriaceae family to colonise [28]. The intestines shift to an
anaerobic environment in a matter of days, in which strict anaerobes as the genera
Biﬁdobacterium, Clostridium, and Bacteroides can grow [28, 29]. The predominant
bacterial taxa in the ﬁrst weeks are dominated by the microbiota of maternal skin
and vagina, which are from the families Enterococcaceae, Streptococcaceae, Lacto-
bacillaceae, and Clostridiaceae [33].
Infants are exclusively fed with milk in the ﬁrst months favoring Biﬁdobacteria to
colonise and dominate in the intestinal microbiota [26]. Biﬁdobacteria are con-
sidered to have an important role in establishing a healthy microbiota of infants
[25, 29]. When solid food is introduced a new shift in the gut microbiota happens,
which increases abundances of the genera Bacteroides, Clostridium, Ruminococcus,
and decreases abundances of the genus Biﬁdobacterium and the family Enterobac-
teriaceae [33]. Figure 2 illustrates the development of the intestinal microbiota of
infants, and the increasing in bacterial diversity during the ﬁrst years of life [33].

2.1.2
Disruption of the Normal Intestinal Balance
Dysbiosis of the intestinal microbiota is characterised as decreased bacterial di-
versity or a change in bacterial species compared to a healthy person [24]. The
composition of the intestinal microbiota can be altered by environmental factors,
including diet, probiotics, prebiotics, viruses, and drugs, particularly antibiotics
[18]. Especially, antibiotic treatment is known to drastically disrupt the balance
of the intestinal microbiota [25, 37], leading to dysbiosis. Antibiotic treatment de-
stroys the intestinal microbial commensal communities, and thereby increase sus-
ceptibility for colonisation of intestinal pathogens [10].
The changes of the intestinal microbiota due to antibiotic treatment can both be
short- and long-term, and have been seen in mice and humans [38]. A disrupted in-
testinal microbiota is characterised by low diversity, and a radical decrease in mem-
bers of the typical dominant phyla Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, and an increase
of the phylum Proteobacteria [38], especially members of the Enterobacteriaceae
family [39]. The decreased bacterial diversity is assumed to allow colonisation or
overgrowth of potential pathogenic bacteria [21].

straight but irregular, and is motile due to its ﬂag-

C. diﬃcile is spore-forming, and when the conditions are not preferable for the
strict anaerobic bacteria, it sporulates to oval subterminal endospores [45]. The
endospores are pervasive and are present in for example soil, water and the intestines
[7]. They are aerotolrant [11], and resistant to heating, drying and chemical agents,
including disinfectants [8], but also to acid, for instance stomach acid [41], and
antibiotics [48].
Route of Transmission
Figure 6 illustrates the route of transmission of C. diﬃcile. C. diﬃcile is transmitted
through the faecal-oral route among humans [41, 48]. It is ingested most common
in its spore-form and rarely in its vegetative form, since it is anaerobic [13]. The
ingestion of spores is usually transmitted from other patients through the environ-
ment or the hands of healthcare workers [5], or from clothes and stethoscopes of
healthcare workers [41]. The spores are the main morphotype, and the vector of
the infection [11, 48]. C. diﬃcile enters and exits the GIT as spores to survive the
aerobic conditions and the di↵erent environments outside the body [11].

Virulence factors
When the spores sense adequate conditions in the gut lumen [49], they germinate to
vegetative cells [11]. Several virulence factors contribute to pathogenesis of C. dif-
ﬁcile. Including resistance and thereby evasion of host innate defenses [49], ﬂagella
that make the bacteria motile [40], increasing its changes of colonisation in the colon
and makes it able to enter the protective mucus layer [6]. Also, multiple cell-surface
adhesins are virulence factors of C. diﬃcile, which helps it adhere to epithelium cells
[6]. Fimbriae cover the bacteria and helps it move and adhere to epithelial cells [50].
C. diﬃcile has pili but the virulent role has not been determined yet, and whether it
can produce bioﬁlm. The virulent role of bioﬁlm formation is not fully determined
either [49]. Furthermore, a pathogenic virulent factor of C. diﬃcile is the toxins.
The toxins breaks down the tight junctions of the epithelial layer [42]. The bacteria
migrates to the basolateral membrane, where it binds due to adhesins specialised
for binding to cell receptors at the basolateral membrane [49].
The correct timing of the expressions of the virulence factors of C. diﬃcile, is crucial
for the success of the infection. When adherence to the basolateral membrane is
obtained, the bacteria produce more toxins [49], which are pathogenic to humans
[15]. C. diﬃcile can produce toxin A and toxin B, and binary toxin [45, 46, 48].
Toxin A and toxin B are large exotoxins, and work synergistically [5, 8]. Toxin A
is a 308-kDa enterotoxin and toxin B is a 270-kDa cytotoxin [8]. The extent of
pathogenicity of binary toxin in C. diﬃcile infection is unknown [8, 13].
Di↵erent virulent C. diﬃcile strains can have one, two or all three types of tox-
ins. The toxins inactivate vital cellular processes of the epithelial cells leading to
colonic dysfunction and cell death [15, 41]. Increased ﬁnding of toxins in stool is as-
sociates with increased deterioration of the patient’s general health condition [41].
Toxin A disrupts tight junctions which increases epithelial permeability and lume-
nal ﬂuid accumulation [3, 50], and causes diarrhoea [8]. Toxin B induces di↵erent
inﬂammatory cytokines, which causes inﬂammation due to neutrophil chemotaxis
and mucosal damage [3]. Beforehand, it was thought that toxin A was the virulent
key factor that started the virulent process [50]. But toxin A-negative and toxin
B-positive virulent C. diﬃcile strains have been identiﬁed, but toxin A-positive
and toxin B-negative strains have not been identiﬁed [41, 50, 51]. It has also been
proven that toxin B is more potent than toxin A [50].
Due to mutations, a new strain of C. diﬃcile has been detected, and it is the cause of
a dramatic increase in incidences of infections of C. diﬃcile [2]. This hypervirulent
new strain is named C. diﬃcile NAP1/BI/027 or C. diﬃcile ribotype 027 (CD027).
It is reported that it has higher rates of sporulation, is more resistant to antibiotics,
produces higher amounts of toxin A and B, has a more cytopathic form of toxin
B, and produces binary toxin [50]. The morbidity and mortality is much higher in
this strain and infects all kinds of people [37].

Risk factors
Several risk factors make people more susceptible for infection with C. diﬃcile. In
common for most of them is, that the intestinal microbiota is out of normal bal-
ance. For instance use of some antibiotics as ampicillin, amoxicillin, clindamycin,
cephalosporins, and ﬂuoroquinolones for other infections, is highly correlated with
risk of CDI [2, 10, 41, 46]. Antibiotic use, as described in section 2.1.2, causes dis-
ruption of the gastrointestinal balance, and when commensal bacteria are absent
and mutualistic interactions are destroyed, it generates space and possibilities for
adherence of the opportunistic bacteria C. diﬃcile [13].
Also advanced age, inﬂammatory bowel disease, severe underlying disease, organ
transplantation, chemotherapy, chronic kidney disease, immunodeﬁciency, and ex-
posure to an asymptomatic carrier or an infected person, are risk factors of CDI
[48]. Together with long duration of hospital admission, severe comorbidities, the
use of proton pump inhibitors, enteral feeding, gastrointestinal surgery, and the
use of tumour suppressor agents in postoperative transplant patients [41], but also
the presence of a nasogastric tube and/or endotracheal tube, kidney failure, im-
munosuppression, malnutrition, and hypoalbuminemia [13] are risk factors of CDI.
Hospitalisation and prolonged length of stay in the hospital or a healthcare facility,
are also risk factors [2].
2.2.1
Clostridioides diﬃcile Infection
CDI is an overgrowth [15] of the opportunistic bacteria due to decrease of the
commensal bacteria in the colon [47]. The decrease in bacterial diversity due to an-
tibiotic treatment with a relative increase in abundance of bacteria from the phylum
Proteobacteria, and a relative decrease in the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes
have shown susceptibility to CDI in a murine model [3]. The CDI occurs in the
colon [41], CDI outside the colon is extremely rare [48]. After infection, diarrhoea
typically starts after 48-72 hours. There can be 10-15 loose or watery bowel move-
ments a day, and they are attended by abdominal pain [41]. The infection can be
long lasting and if not treated the mortality rate is high [37].
Multiple symptoms and diseases follow this toxin-mediated CDI [11], and a general
term Clostridioides diﬃcile associated disease (CDAD) includes all symptoms and
diseases. CDADs vary from diarrhoea, abdominal pain, blood in stool, weight loss,
thromboses to injures to the colon [37]. The severity of CDADs range from no
symptoms from asymptomatic carriers to life-threatening conditions as intestinal
perforation and toxic megacolon [2, 46]. Table 1 outlines the stages of severity of
CDAD [6, 8, 13, 14, 48, 52, 53].

The deﬁnition of CA CDI is no prior hospitalisation for three months before the

Diagnosis of Clostridioides diﬃcile Infection
The diagnosis of CDI is based on clinical and laboratory ﬁndings. Three diagnosis
criteria of CDI were in 1995 set [46]:
1. Diarrhoea, deﬁned as either:
• At least six watery stools over 36 hours
• Three unformed stools in 24 hours for 2 days
• Eight unformed stools over 48 hours
2. Pseudomembranes seen at lower gastrointestinal endoscopy
or detection of C. diﬃcile toxin A or toxin B
3. No other recognised etiology for diarrhoea
Today, the same criteria are valid for diagnosis of CDI. Detection of C. diﬃcile toxin
A and/or toxin B is the simplest, fastest, and cheapest way of diagnosing CDI [5].
Di↵erent laboratory techniques can be used in detection of C. diﬃcile toxins, all
tests acquire a stool sample, which has to be loose or watery [13]. The techniques
are:
• Cell cytotoxicity assay
• Cytotoxigenic culture in stool
• Enzyme immunoassay
• Nucleic acid ampliﬁcation test
– PCR
– Loop-mediated isothermal ampliﬁcation
CDI can also be detected clinically. The di↵erent detection techniques available are
[13]:
• Abdominal x-rays
• Abdominal and pelvic helical computed tomography
• Abdominal ultrasound
• Colonoscopy

Recurrence and Refractory Clostridioides diﬃcile Infection
A CDI can be diﬃcult to get rid of. In about 10-25% of cured patients relapses
occur. The severity and predisposition to subsequent infections increase for each re-
infection [41]. The risk of C. diﬃcile recurrence ranges from 20-30% after an initial
episode, to 60-80% after multiple prior recurrences [2, 14, 39, 48]. Recurrence is
deﬁned as CDI recurs within 8 weeks of onset of previous treatment. Refractory
means unresponsive to antibiotic treatment [54]. The resistance of C. diﬃcile spores
to antibiotics, challenges the cure of CDI with antibiotic administration [47].
Prevention of Clostridioides diﬃcile Infection
Prevention of C. diﬃcile is needed due to increase in HA and CA CDI in developed
countries. Several suggestions to prevent spread and to minimise the number of
incidences of CDI have been proposed. To decrease the number of CA CDIs, it
is recommended to minimise the use of antibiotics for other disease. It is recom-
mended to use the proper antimicrobials and not use broad-spectrum antibiotics
such as amoxicillin [48]. This is called antimicrobial stewardship [3, 7].
Prevention of spread in healthcare facilities has been proposed by [48]. Generally,
it has been suggested to raise and maintain the hygiene standards of hospitals [7].
Furthermore, it is recommended for healthcare workers to use gloves, and be aware
of contact precautions [3]. Since C. diﬃcile spores can survive in many di↵erent
environments disinfection with ethanol is not enough and thorough hand wash
with soap is advised [8, 37]. Other suggestions are isolation of patients [41], use of
disposable equipment, and decontamination of hospital environments with sodium
hypochlorite also known as bleach and hydrogen peroxide vapor [13, 42].
Treatment of Clostridioides diﬃcile Infection
When CDI is detected, the administration of antibiotics for other diseases should be
stopped. It can worsen the condition of the patient and also increase the patient’s
susceptibility to re-infection [41]. The severity of the infection determines the treat-
ment that can be conservative or with surgery. The ﬁrst treatment is establishment
of electrolyte and water balance due to profuse diarrhoea [41]. Clinical guidelines
recommend oral metronidazole or oral vancomycin as treatment, which are e↵ective
in most patients. If the infection is refractory or recurs, tapered vancomycin, or
oral or tapered ﬁdaxomicin is recommended as treatment [4, 7, 48].
Antibiotic treatment can be given several times if the symptoms return. In severe
cases surgery can be performed. One way is removing part of the colon, called
subtotal colectomy.
Another surgical approach is viverting loop ileostomy with
colonic lavage [41].
Another, and less invasive and dramatic approach is faecal
microbiota transplantation (FMT). It is recommended by European and US clinical
guidelines as second-line treatment for recurrent CDI [20, 55], and is explained in
section 2.3.

2.3
Faecal Microbiota Transplantation
Historically, FMT can be dated 1,700 years back to the fourth century China [56].
The ﬁrst description of FMT was found in the Handbook of Emergency Medicine.
A Chinese doctor, named Ge Hong, prescribed oral ingestion of faeces from infants,
called ’yellow soup’, as a therapeutic agent against food poisoning and severe diar-
rhoea [21, 24, 56]. In the 16th century in the China Ming Dynasty another Chinese
doctor, named Li Shizhen, described e↵ective treatment, with fresh or fermented
faeces, of di↵erent gastrointestinal conditions as diarrhoea, fever, constipation, vom-
iting, and abdominal pain [18, 24, 56]. FMT has also been used to a great extend
in veterinary medicine. In the 17th century an Italian surgeon, named Fabricius
Aquapendente, transferred gastrointestinal content from a healthy animal to a sick
animal, he called it transfaunation [1, 18].
During the World War II, German soldiers in Africa was advised by African Bedouins
to consume fresh, warm faeces from camels to treat bacterial dysentery [21, 24].
The ﬁrst report in medical literature was in 1958, where Eiseman and his colleagues
treated patients who su↵ered from pseudomembranous colitis probably due to CDI
with faecal enemas with success [1, 21]. There are di↵erent techniques for applying
FMT. First known technique was enema used in 1958. Other techniques are faecal
infusion via nasogastric tube invented in 1991, colonoscopy invented in 2000, and
self-administered enemas invented in 2010 [1]. The latest technique of applying
faecal matters is via ingestion of capsules [9, 20]. The ﬁrst randomised controlled
trial of FMT was in 2013 by [57]. The study showed a signiﬁcant e↵ect of resolving
symptoms of recurrent CDI with donor faeces infused through a nasoduodenal tube
when compared to antibiotics used alone [18].
FMT is a procedure where stool from a healthy donor is processed, standardised,
and transplanted into a patient with recurrent CDI [39, 47]. FMT aims to restore
the disrupted intestinal microbiota in the colon [54, 58] by implementing constituent
commensal microorganisms and increase of the diversity [39]. The rapid change of
the intestinal microbiota results in normalisation of the gut composition and the
functionality, and the resolution of symptoms happens fast [18, 55]. FMT also re-
sults in gain of colonisation resistance and prevention of germination of C. diﬃcile
spores [58]. The recipient obtains a donor-like bacterial diversity. The procedure
leads to prompt engraftment of the intestinal microbiota of the donor [23, 59], and
it persists for several months [57]. The number of strains derived from the donor
declines over time [23]. FMT is also called ’faeces transplantation’, ’human intesti-
nal microbiota transfer’ and ’faecal bacteriotherapy’ [24].
A disrupted and diseased intestinal microbiota, for instance infected by C. diﬃcile,
is deﬁcient in the phyla Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, and enriched in species of
Proteobacteria [21, 57], as described in section 2.1.2. FMT is believed to add 1,000
functional bacterial species, and thereby restore the microbial colonic balance [56].

FMT shows a decrease in the phylum Proteobacteria and an increase of the phy-
lum Firmicutes especially the families Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiraceae, and
Clostridiaceae. Also the families Bacteroidaceae, Rikenellaceae, and Porphyromon-
adaceae of the phylum Bacteroidetes increase after FMT treatment [60].
Several studies of FMT have shown its eﬃcacy. Resolution of symptoms of CDI
often happens within days after FMT and the success rate is reported as 70-90%
[4, 47]. FMT during colonoscopy has in a study showed a 91% primary cure rate
[61]. According to treatment of recurrent CDI with antibiotics, FMT was superior
[12, 21], and according to relieving symptoms of disease, again FMT was superior
to antibiotic treatment, where it reduced diarrhoea by 81% and 30%, respectively
[57]. The cure rate with FMT is high, and the procedure is cost-saving compared to
antibiotic treatment of recurrent or refractory CDI [18, 22]. A cost analysis showed
that after a year post-FMT, the hospital costs dropped by 42% due to reduced
length of stay and a lower number of hospital admissions [4].
Mode of Action
The mode of action of FMT is still not fully understood [53]. It is often patients
with recurrent or refractory CDI that is treated with FMT. The procedure, often
within days, ends with normalisation of gut physiology and resolution of symptoms.
Studies have shown that important key factors for understanding the mode of ac-
tion could be bacterial metabolites, as the short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) valerate
[20, 53]. Further studies are needed in order to deduce this claim. Engraftment
of bacteria is also thought to have key responsibility in the eﬃcacy of FMT. Few
principles are known of bacterial engraftment in human hosts. The gut microbiota
of the FMT recipient is a complex mixture of bacteria from the donor, the environ-
ment, and itself. At this point, it is not possible to tell which bacteria that engraft
best, since the microbiota is unique in every person [23]. Studies of persistence of
donor-derived strains are needed. A competition between the stains from the FMT
donor and the patient have di↵erent outcomes [23], and can vary from patient to
patient. The di↵erent proposed outcomes of FMT are listed below:
• Patient strains may resist invasion of donor strains
• Patient strains may be outcompeted by the donor strains
• Patient strains may coexist with the donor strains
• Patient acquire new strains

2.3.1
Clinical Application Framework of FMT
In practice, FMT has a lot of steps and precautions. The steps of FMT includes
donor recruitment and screening, laboratory processing, and clinical application
[4, 20, 55]. Consensus reports of practising FMT are trying to standardise the pro-
cedure [54, 62]. At this point, the procedure is not standardised yet. Therefore,
there is no common way of practising FMT. This project focuses on a case from
AUH, why only the procedure of FMT at AUH is described. In Denmark, FMT
was ﬁrst ﬁled in 2015 to be regulated by the Danish Tissue Act, derived from the
European Tissue and Cells Directive (EUTCD) [55].
The procedure of FMT at AUH has changes since start in 2015 due to optimisations,
cost savings and patients’ well-being. Figure 8 shows the three branches of the FMT
clinical application framework. The following sections elucidate the three branches.

Donor Recruitment
Donor recruitment for FMT is a central factor since safety and screening of the
faecal donors are of utmost importance due to life-threatened sick recipients [63].
There are two challenges in recruiting faecal donors. The challenges are accessing
enough potential donors and selecting only the healthy ones [20]. Blood donors are
considered to be good faecal donors since they need to follow di↵erent regulations
and be healthy to donate blood [20, 55]. The same rules apply to the faecal donors,
where also screening for bacteria and virus are extremely important.
Another
advantage of using the blood donors as faecal donors is that they are at the hospital
to donate blood and then it is not that diﬃcult to also donate faeces to the FMT
department at the same time [63].

Laboratory Processing
Donors, who fulﬁl the inclusion and exclusion criteria, has to transport cooled fae-
ces donation to the laboratory within two hours, for further processing steps [55].
The processing steps are required for preservation of the microbiota and to render
a uniform, ready-to-use faecal suspension [20]. Temperature and time are two im-
portant factors in donation of faecal samples. Studies have shown no signiﬁcant
di↵erence in diversity or quality of faecal samples cooled to 4 ￿for the ﬁrst 8 hours.
It is recommended that processing steps and freezing of faecal samples to -80 ￿
are completed within 8 hours [20, 55].
The processing steps before freezing to -80 ￿are weighing and diluting with normal
saline, 2.5 ml per 1 g faeces [55]. Then blending and ﬁltering through a strainer
to remove debris and ease infusion of faecal suspension. Lastly, glycerol is added
to a ﬁnal concentration of 10% to improve viability of bacteria upon freezing. The
suspension is distributed to 50 ml labelled Falcon tubes and stored at -80 ￿[55].

PROJECT AIM AND HYPOTHESES
3
Project Aim and Hypotheses
The aim of this project is to examine, which alterations FMT have on the bacterial
community composition of the pregnant patient’s faecal microbiota, and further-
more to observe if the infant’s faecal microbiota is colonised with bacteria from the
FMT donor. To achieve this, serial faecal samples from the FMT donor, the patient,
and the infant have been collected at AUH. DNA has been extracted, libraries have
been prepared and sequenced on the Ion Torrent S5™platform. With the use of
bioinformatics, the sequenced bacterial DNA was analysed and hereafter compared
between the di↵erent samples. The results of this project could add knowledge and
prove evidence of safety of FMT for CDI during pregnancy.
Hypotheses
The hypotheses of this project are:
• After FMT, the patient’s faecal microbiota will look like the faecal microbiota
of the donor, and stay stable for months. The faecal microbiota of the patient
after FMT is a complex combination of bacteria from the donor, the environ-
ment, and itself [23]. The number of donor derived strains are expected to
decline over time.
• Increase and decrease in bacterial phyla will be observed after FMT in the
patient [59]. FMT will show a reduction in bacteria belonging to the phylum
Proteobacteria.
FMT will show a increase in the phylum Firmicutes, es-
pecially the families Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiraceae, and Clostridiaceae,
and the phylum Bacteriodetes increases as well, mainly the families Bac-
teroidaceae, Rikenellaceae, and Porphyromonadaceae [60].
• The infant will be exposed to bacteria from its mother during birth, and
since the mother had a FMT, the infant’s faecal microbiota will be colonised
with the FMT derived bacteria from the donor, and have similarities with the
donor’s faecal microbiota.
• Before the FMT, C. diﬃcile will be detected in the faecal samples from the
patient, and the patient will be positive for C. diﬃcile toxin genes. After
the FMT, either C. diﬃcile or its toxin genes will be detected in any of the
patient samples nor in the infant samples.

happens in several wells on the chip at the same time, why this sequencing method
is called massive parallel sequencing. The chip was shared with a Ph.d student,
Olivia Colberg, from the research group. Of the 96 wells, she obtained 20 wells and
this project obtained 45 wells. The rest of the wells were empty.

4.4.2
Generation of Amplicon Sequence Variant Table
An amplicon sequence variant (ASV) table was generated using the DADA2 pipeline
in RStudio (version 1.12.1). An ASV is a unique sequence. If a single nucleotide
di↵ers in the next read, it will be assigned to a new ASV. The method operational
taxonomic units (OTUs) is also widely used. Similar reads are clustered of up to
3% of di↵erences. The ASV method is more sensitive and the use of it is increasing
in bioinformatic circles. The script from the research group of Gut, Microbes and
Health from National Food Institute, DTU, ”script data2 pool TRUE V1.12.1.R”
was run in RStudio. The reads were trimmed according to expected error rate
(MaxEE = 2). After trimming less than 10% of the reads were lost, and 3,795,495
reads were left in the 45 samples.
The 45 samples were pooled and a table of di↵erent ASVs was created, leaving
133,246 unique ASVs. Chimeric reads were removed leaving 874 ASVs, and 5% of
reads was discarded due to chimerism. A chimeric read is when two di↵erent genes
or even species are combined wrongly during the PCR steps. Lastly, taxonomic
annotations were assigned to the ASV table. The Ribosomal Database Project
(rdp) was used for this purpose and the gene ’16S rRNA training set 16’ was chosen
as reference gene for the annotations. The rdp database consists of 16S rRNA genes
of bacteria, why the ASVs are only assigned to bacterial taxa.

4.4.4
QIIME 2™Analysis
The open-source software Quantitative Insights into Microbial Ecology (QIIME 2™)
(version 2018.8) was used for the analysis. QIIME 2™is a next-generation micro-
biota bioinformatics platform that is extensible, free, open source, and community
developed [70]. QIIME 2™was installed and run in a Unix environment.
The ASV table, generated in RStudio with the DADA2 pipeline was imported, to-
gether with the FASTA ﬁle with all the ASV sequences. QIIME 2™was used twice.
First with all 45 samples, resulting in 542 ASVs. The three technical replicates
were very similar and therefore the three technical replicates of each sample were
aggregated, resulting in even higher depth. The mock community was removed,
leaving 14 samples left for further analysis.
A thorough ﬁltration was executed. The ﬁltering included bacteria, and excluded
cyanobacteria/chloroplast, as it is considered plant material. The ﬁltering dealt
with abundance. Any ASV with a frequency below 50 was removed. Two of the
samples were removed in this step, MilliQ water samples and sample 43705, which
was meconium from the infant. The number of ASVs of the aggregated samples
after the ﬁltering step was 523, and 3,285,693 reads remained, corresponding to
46.1% of the reads.
The depth was set to 220,000 since the lowest count of a
sample was 221,375 reads. The highest number of reads in a sample was 404,969
reads. The data was randomly subsampled to 220,000 sequences per sample in
order to normalise the read depth across all samples. The further analyses were
executed using this rareﬁed read depth.

6
Discussion
The aim of this project was to examine the bacterial compositional changes in the
pregnant patient’s faecal microbiota before and after FMT treatment, and examine
the infant’s faecal microbiota to search for similarities with the healthy donor’s
microbiota. The extracted DNA from the faecal samples from the donor, and from
the multiple follow-up visits of the patient and the infant were sequenced. The
data was compared in the di↵erent microbiota at di↵erent taxonomic levels, and
the presence of C. diﬃcile was investigated.
6.1
FMT in General
In this project, the FMT resulted in a swift change of the patient’s condition and a
rapid normalisation of the faecal bacterial composition of the patient. The change
from a dysbiotic microbial state towards a normal composition, like the healthy
donor’s microbiota, due to FMT treatment, was also observed in [59]. The purpose
of FMT is to restore a disrupted microbiota and to reestablish a stable and complex
microbiota. More than 100 case reports and clinical trials on FMT treatment of
CDI with high resolution rates have been published [24].
In a recent review of 132 studies involving 4609 patients, a primary cure rate with
FMT of 87.7% was observed [66]. Most studies used the lower gastrointestinal way
for delivery of FMT. In another review, the resolution rates after one FMT infusion
of di↵erent routes of delivery in di↵erent studies (15 studies included) were [19]:
Overall: 76%
Upper Route: 79%
Lower Route: 72%
Capsule: 80%
Colonoscopy: 78%
Duodenal: 73%
Enema: 56%
Vancomycin: 31%
Standard antibiotic therapy often fails to treat CDIs [59]. This was also observed
in this project, where the pregnant patient had a relapse of CDI short after the
14 days of oral vancomycin ended. While she had the antibiotic treatment the
symptoms of CDI as diarrhoea disappeared, but they returned again after end of
treatment. The percentages presented in the table above indicates that treatment
with FMT is superior to standard antibiotic treatment.
Furthermore, FMT is cost saving in managing recurrent and refractory CDI com-
pared to antibiotics use [4, 18]. However, FMT is still not a standardised procedure
[19], and the long-term safety e↵ects are not determined yet.

6.2
Diversity
Before FMT treatment, the Shannon diversity index of patients with CDI, com-
pared to donors, is low. Especially if the patients had antibiotic treatment prior
to FMT [9]. In this project, the same tendency with a low alpha diversity before
FMT, and an increase after FMT is observed in ﬁgure 20 and ﬁgure 21. The ﬁgures
demonstrate that the transplantation of donor faeces resulted in improvement of
microbial diversity, which persisted over time, this was also seen in [57]. The patient
ended the antibiotic treatment 16 days before the FMT treatment, and not 1 or 2
days before which is normally recommended. The number and diversity of bacteria
is potentially higher in the patient’s gut microbiota than in patients treated with
antibiotics right up to FMT.
The PCoA plot in ﬁgure 22 illustrates a markedly di↵erent microbial community
composition in the patient before and after FMT. The microbial community compo-
sition of the patient moved towards the donor’s microbiota after FMT and persisted
there. The same pattern is observed in both [9] where 23 patients were included, and
in [47] where 3 patients were included. In [9], faecal samples have been taken until
12 weeks after FMT, and the patient samples after FMT lay close to the healthy
donor samples all the way through. In this project, faecal samples were taken until
50 weeks after FMT, and all the patient samples after FMT lay very close to the
healthy donor sample as well. In [59], the PCoA plots follow the same tendency.
The patient’s microbiota before FMT is clearly separated from the healthy donor
samples, and after FMT the patient’s samples are found close to the donor samples.

6.4
The Microbiota after FMT
It is well known that the FMT procedure can lead to rapid engraftment of donor
microbiota, increase of bacterial diversity as discussed in section 6.2, and normal-
isation of the microbial community composition in patients with CDI [59]. Along
follows alterations in the intestinal microbiota community both in the taxonomy of
the present bacteria and in their relative abundances. In [47], the microbiota of the
patient looked like the donor’s microbiota 3 days after the FMT. In this project,
the same tendencies are observed.
A hypothesis was that the microbiota of the patient after FMT would look like the
microbiota of the donor, and stay stable over time. This hypothesis is conﬁrmed by
ﬁgure 27, ﬁgure 28, ﬁgure 29, and ﬁgure 33, where the microbiota is investigated
at di↵erent taxonomic levels and as ASVs. The microbiota of the patient changed
after the FMT and obtained a composition like the donor’s microbiota fast, and
over time it stayed stable.
It was observed in [23], that all major taxonomic groups in the human gut, including
Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria, and Proteobacteria, and less prevalent
phyla as Fusobacteria and Verrucomicrobia, were found in the the faecal samples.
Figure 27 illustrates that it was almost the case in this project. After FMT treat-
ment, it was observed in [59] that Firmicutes and Bacteriodetes increased, and
Proteobacteria decreased. In [39] they observed a decrease in Proteobacteria and
Firmicutes, and a large increase in Bacteroidetes following FMT. In this project,
in ﬁgure 27 a decrease in Proteobacteria, and an increase in Firmicutes and Bac-
teroidetes were observed after FMT. The relative abundances of the di↵erent phyla
did not vary considerably over time in this project, as it did in [60].
In [47], they report that a healthy gut microbiota is typically dominated by the
classes Clostridia, Actinobacteria, Erysipelotrichi and Bacteroidia. However, they
observed that patients with CDI were dominated by Gammaproteobacteria and
Bacilli. 3 days after FMT, the microbiota changed to be more abundant in espe-
cially Clostridia and Bacteroidia, as the healthy donor’s microbiota is.
In this
project, the amounts of Gammaproteobacteria, Bacilli, and Negativicutes were
higher in the patient before FMT, and decreased after FMT to levels similar with
the healthy donor, illustrated in ﬁgure 28. The composition of classes in the pa-
tient’s microbiota might have looked di↵erent if she had had antibiotics up to the
day before FMT, as in [47].
In this project, in the pregnant patient after FMT, relative decreases were ob-
served in the Firmicutes families Veillonellaceae, Streptococcaceae, Peptostrepto-
coccaceae, and Enterococcaceae, and relative increases were observed in the Fir-
micutes families Ruminococcaceae and Lachnospiraceae. Relative increases were
observed in families of Bacteriodetes, and relative decreases were observed in fam-
ilies of Actinobacteria and Proteobacteria, this is illustrated in ﬁgure 29.

A hypothesis was, that FMT increased the Firmicutes families Ruminococcaceae,
Lachnospiraceae, and Clostridiaceae, and increased the Bacteriodetes families Bac-
teroidaceae, Rikenellaceae, and Porphyromonadaceae. Figure 30 showed the rela-

Bacteroidetes families, but only an increase in Rumimococcaceae was observed in
the Firmicutes families. Figure 31 illustrates the relative abundances of the Pro-
teobacteria families, and a decrease in all of them was observed, especially in the
family Enterobacteriaceae, as in [60]. The hypothesis was conﬁrmed on phylum
level, but could not be fully conﬁrmed on family level in this project.
In [47], it was observed that some genera increased and decreased in CDI patients
after FMT. In this project, in ﬁgure 32, increases and decreases of bacterial genera
due to FMT could be detected. The genera that increased in both studies were
Faecalibacterium and Bacteroides. When comparing this project with [60] the fol-
lowing genera were found increasing in both studies: Alistipes, Bacteroides, and
Parabacteroides. In [47] and in this project the genera Enterococcus and Kleb-
siella decreased after FMT. And in [60] and in this project the genus Parasutterella
decreased after FMT. In this project, other genera were found increased and de-
creased after FMT treatment in the patient, but it can not conclude any general
tendencies. At genus level, the classiﬁcation percent ranged between 59-84%. A
large percentage of unclassiﬁed ASVs were included, and if they were classiﬁed,
they could potentially have changed the overall picture.
The mode of action of FMT is not yet understood, but patients with recurrent CDI
often recovers within days, with resolution of symptoms, and normalisation of gut
community composition. In this project, the patient recovered from symptoms the
same day as the FMT was executed. She had no side e↵ects of the treatment, and
was declared healthy. C. diﬃcile toxin genes tcdA and tcdB could not be detected
afterwards. She did not have any relapses, and she gave birth to a term and healthy
boy, half a year after the FMT. Figure 33 supports the feeling of well-being in the
patient. A change in the patient’s microbiota towards the healthy donor’s micro-
biota was observed 1 week after FMT, and it stayed stable over 50 weeks.
An important key factor in the mode of action of FMT is suggested to be upregu-
lation of the bacterial metabolite valerate, a SCFA, after FMT treatment [20, 53].
In [53], a CDI chemostat model of FMT observed an increase in valerate and a
decrease in C. diﬃcile after FMT. In this project, the SCFAs formate, propionate,
butyrate, and valerate increased after FMT treatment, illustrated in ﬁgure 34. The
idea of valerate having a role in mode of action of FMT might be supported in this
project. According to [48], the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes have critical
components that might provide resistance to C. diﬃcile after FMT. In ﬁgure 27
both phyla increase in relative abundances after FMT.

In agreement with the hypothesis of the microbiota being a complex combination
of bacteria from the donor, the environment, and the patient itself, ﬁgure 37 con-

The percentage of donor derived bacteria did not decline as
much as in [23]. In this project, it declined from 54.9% 1 week after FMT to 46.4%
50 weeks after FMT. The persistence of the donor derived strains was high. The
percentage of bacteria retained in the patients declined from 8.1% to 5.8% over 50
weeks, in agreement with [23]. The pregnant patient in this project did not have
antibiotics prior to the FMT, as normally recommended, and still she was colonised
with bacteria from the donor. In [23], they predicted patients not receiving antibi-
otics prior to FMT to be less amenable to colonise donor bacteria. In this project,
the prediction from [23] was proved wrong, but it can not be generalised.
The microbiota of the infant gut has a di↵erent composition than the adult gut mi-
crobiota [33]. This was also conﬁrmed in this project, where the infant’s microbiota
had higher percentages of the phyla Actinobacteria and Proteobacteria than the pa-
tient and the donor, illustrated in ﬁgure 27 and ﬁgure 33. The infant’s microbiota
in this project was mainly colonised with bacteria from the genera Clostridium,
Faecaliumbacterium, Megaspaere, Streptococcus, Veillonella from the phylum Fir-
micutus, and Biﬁdobacterium, and Eggerthelia from the phylum Actinobacteria,
illustrated in ﬁgure 32. The number of donor derived strains decreased from 22
ASVs at 3 months of age to 17 ASVs at 6 months of age, illustrated in ﬁgure 36.
The infant’s microbiota consisted mainly of bacteria shared between the patient, at
birth time, and the processed donor, illustrated in ﬁgure 39. A hypothesis was that
the infant would be colonised with donor derived bacteria, and have similarities
with the donor’s microbiota. This hypothesis was to a lesser extend true, but since
an infant gut microbiota is unstable until the age of 2-4 years, it was diﬃcult to
compare it to a stable adult gut microbiota.

6.5
Detection of Clostridioides diﬃcile
In section 5.6, the presence of C. diﬃcile in the faecal samples was investigated.
A 100% identity match of C. diﬃcile was found in one of the ASVs belonging
to the family Peptostreptococcaceae. However, the same ASV was classiﬁed as
Terrisporobacter petrolearius with a 100% identity match as well. A further in-
vestigation of Terrisporobacter petrolearius revealed that it was isolated from an
oilﬁeld petroleum reservoir [72], and not found in the human gut. According to
[73], the species Terrisporobacter is found in soil. A multiple sequence alignment
showed that the hypervariable region V3 of the 16S rRNA gene of Terrisporobacter
petrolearius and C. diﬃcile aligned perfectly to the ASV. The ASV was suggested
to be misclassiﬁed as Terrisporobacter petrolearius instead of C. diﬃcile, which it
more likely is, since it was isolated from the human gut.
In [47], C. diﬃcile could not be detected with qPCR in CDI patients. The CDI
patients had antibiotics prior to FMT, and vegetative C. diﬃcile cells might have
been eradicated, and could not be detected with qPCR. The C. diﬃcile spores
would not have been eradicated with antibiotic treatment, and were not detected
with qPCR. The same tendency of not detecting C. diﬃcile in CDI patients on
antibiotics was found in [23]. In this project, C. diﬃcile was detected on ASV level
in the samples from the pregnant patient before FMT, the unprocessed donor, the
patient 8 weeks after FMT, and the infant 6 months after birth, illustrated in ﬁg-
ure 40. An investigation of the presence of C. diﬃcile toxin genes tcdA and tcdB
in the faecal samples ended with the results that C. diﬃcile toxin genes tcdA and
tcdB were found in the patient before FMT and in the infant 6 months after birth,
illustrated in ﬁgure 41. The reason why C. diﬃcile could be detected in the patient
in this project, was probably because she did not have antibiotics right before the
FMT treatment.
Inspired by [74], the TaqMan™-based primer-probe sets detecting C. diﬃcile 16S
rRNA genes, C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdA, and C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdB from [74]
were ordered. The C. diﬃcile 16S rRNA gene and C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdA were
detected in the patient before FMT and in the 6 months old infant. This were
the same ﬁndings as in the qPCR run with SYBR Green I. The primer-probe set
detecting C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdB from [74] could not be used since the melt-
ing temperature of the probe was too low. A new primer-probe set for detection
C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdB could have been designed and tried if more time was
available. The result of C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdB detection would probably have
been positive for the patient before FMT and the infant at 6 months of age. The
reason is that C. diﬃcile toxin gene tcdA had not been found alone in a patient yet,
but always together with tcdB. If there had been any C. diﬃcile toxin B producing
genes or C. diﬃcile without toxin genes in any of the other samples, it would have
been detected by the C. diﬃcile 16S rRNA gene primer-probe set. The results of
the primer sets with SYBR Green I and the TaqMan™-based primer-probe sets were
in agreement.

6.6
Clostridioides diﬃcile in Infancy
It was not expected that C. diﬃcile toxin genes tcdA and tcdB were detected in the
sample from the 6 months old infant. The patient had not reported any sickness in
the infant. As mentioned in section 2.2, the bacteria C. diﬃcile was ﬁrst isolated
from the faecal microbiota of healthy infants, and it was thought to be a commensal
until experiments of animals showed lethal results [40]. In [3], it was shown that
60-70% of 1 months old infants were colonises asymptomatically with C. diﬃcile.
Carriage of C. diﬃcile decreased to 30% of infants 1-6 months old, and further
decreased to 10% at 1 year of age [75]. In adults, the asymptomatic carriage of C.
diﬃcile is 0-3% [3].
The percentages of asymptomatic infant carriers of C. diﬃcile is high, and the in-
fants show no signs of illness from the toxins released by the anaerobic bacteria.
The infant gut seems to be resistant to the toxic e↵ects of toxin A and B. Clinical
infection in infants is rarely observed [75]. As the infant gut develops and becomes
more stable around the age of 2-4 years, the carrier state disappears. The children
become sensitive to the toxins as adults are. A suggestion is that infants have
not developed toxin receptors in early life [75]. In [48], they observed that asymp-
tomatic colonisation of C. diﬃcile in infants stimulates an immune response that
protects them from C. diﬃcile infection later in life.
At birth, the patient was negative for C. diﬃcile. It is unlikely that the transmission
was direct mother to infant during birth [75]. The infant had most likely acquired
C. diﬃcile as spores from the environment, and the high amount of hands in mouth
in infant life had probably contributed. At this point, the infant is most likely
resistant to C. diﬃcile, and it might develop resistance through adulthood.

6.7
Treatment of CDI during Pregnancy
CDI during pregnancy is of high concern since reports of septic shock, toxic mega-
colon, and death results from the infection [17]. Pregnant women have been thought
to be at low risk of developing CDI due to young age and overall good health. But
an increase in the number of cases is of concern in this population [16, 17].
In [15], a case report of a pregnant woman expecting twins who had CDI due to
previous antibiotic treatment of a urinary tract infection resulted in spontaneously
abortion of the foetuses and maternal death. She waited 3 weeks with profuse diar-
rhoea before she went to the hospital, at this point she was in week 14 of gestation.
She was treated with metronidazole, cholestyramine, and oral vancomycin. Her
health improved, but shortly after she had a relapse. She lost her foetuses at week
17 of gestation, and she died few days after. Afterwards, it was observed that she
had toxic megacolon and evidence of pseudomembranous colitis [15, 17]. In this
case story, the disease was too comprehensive and advanced to treat. If treatment
had started earlier the outcome might have been di↵erent.
In [17], 10 severe case stories are presented of pregnant or postpartum women with
CDI. In 5 of the 10 cases, the CDI came after caesarian section where antibiotics
were given as preventive treatment. In the other 5 cases, the CDI came while the
women were pregnant, as in this project. In 4 of 5 cases, the women had taken
antibiotics to treat another disease, and ended up with CDI, as the patient in this
project. In this group of women, who had CDI during pregnancy, the outcomes
were 1 maternal death and 3 stillbirths, including the patient with the twins men-
tioned above. The women had the following CDAD: none, relapses, sepsis, toxic
megacolon, and shock. The time, when the ﬁrst symptoms occurred until medical
treatment started is not stated, but might have inﬂuence on the outcomes.
In this project, the patient was treated fast, and had only clinical symptoms as
diarrhoea, and not any severe complications. Either hospitalisation or surgery were
needed. She had antibiotics, and the symptoms disappeared. The symptoms re-
turned when antibiotic treatment ended. She requested for a FMT, and symptoms
disappeared within a day, and she had not had any relapses. In this project, FMT
in pregnant women instead of antibiotic treatment has advantages and could po-
tentially reduce the number of severe cases and deaths as seen in [15, 17].
Little data of FMT in pregnant patients exist. A case report from 2017 is about
a pregnant woman, who had CDI and was treated with a FMT. She acquired
the CDI from antibiotic treatment for another disease. She was ﬁrst diagnosed
with CDI in 2014, and was treated several times with metronidazole, vancomycin,
and ﬁdaxomicin with e↵ect, but had several relapses [22]. She had a successful
FMT through colonoscopy, when she was 18 weeks of gestation. The patient in
this project had a successful FMT through colonoscopy when she was 13 weeks of
gestation. Both women tolerated the FMT, had fast resolution of symptoms within
a week, and gave birth vaginally to healthy term infants.

DISCUSSION
In the case story [22], the patient and the involved doctors had concerns about
the use of antibiotics through pregnancy. They also had concerns about develop-
ment of antibiotic resistance. The patient in this project had the same concerns
about long-term use of antibiotics, and the safety of the foetus. FMT had multiple
beneﬁts for both women including: no recurrences of CDI during the rest of the
pregnancy or after birth, avoidance of chronic antibiotic use, lowering the risk of
developing antibiotic resistance, and improvement of quality of life within a week
after the FMT.
The bacterial composition of the microbiota of the patient in the case story is not
known. It could have been interesting to investigate the changes she had, since her
situation was similar with the patient in this project. The two cases of FMT in
pregnant women can beneﬁt with positive results as fast resolution of symptoms,
no recurrences of C. diﬃcile, and healthy term infants. This project can further
beneﬁt with knowledge of the microbiota compositions of the patient before and
after FMT, and of the infant at 3 and 6 months of age.
6.8
Limitations of the Project
The project has some limitations. First, it was a single case project with one preg-
nant patient that had a FMT, and an infant. It is not feasible to generalise, and
what was experienced in this project is only indications of what happens generally.
The data set was too small for statistics. The project lasted for 50 weeks after FMT
treatment, and general long-term e↵ects could not be concluded. Moreover, there
was not a sample from the healthy gut of the patient before the CDI as a baseline.
Over the 50 weeks period after FMT, it could not be determined if the microbiota
composition of the patient returned towards the original healthy composition.
Second, no bacteria were found in the meconium sample of the newborn infant.
And only faecal samples were taken from the patient and the infant. A more pre-
cise picture of where the bacteria came from in the infant’s microbiota might have
appeared, if also vaginal swaps from the mother before delivery of the infant were
taken, samples of the breast milk, faecal samples from the sibling and the father,
and swaps from healthcare personnel, and the delivery room at the hospital, this
was done in [26]. It could have been interesting to detect from where the envi-
ronmental bacteria in the infant in ﬁgure 39 came from. It could also have been
interesting to have more samples over time from the donor to investigate the dy-
namic range of the donor’s faecal microbiota, and compare it to the microbiota of
the patient and the infant after FMT, this was suggested in [59].
Third, for simplicity only bacteria were considered in the faecal microbiota com-
positions in this project, and not viruses, fungi, or phages, as commented in [76],
even though they might have an inﬂuence. The choice of primers that ampliﬁed
the V3 region of the 16S rRNA gene could have introduced bias to the microbial
composition results, as suggested in [33].

6.9
Further Work
FMT is not a standardised procedure, but it is a good method to treat patients
with CDI. The following points are points to be considered in order to optimise
and further develop the method.
• FMT should be standardised, in order to compare di↵erent cases and studies,
and to generally optimise the treatment of patients with CDI [19, 54, 62].
• The mode of action of FMT, and what are the key factors for normalising a
disrupted microbiota. In [3], they suggested to ﬁnd the beneﬁcial groups of
organisms, and isolate them, and use them for transplantation.
• Capsules as the route of administration should be optimised, and standard-
ised. It has a high resolution rate, a low number of side e↵ects, and it is less
invasive as for instance colonoscopy [9, 19]. Furthermore, it reduces costs,
and the needed number of people at work during a faecal transplantation
[24]. However, the number of capsules to swallow should be optimised.
• Large-scale studies are needed in order to determine the eﬃcacy, safety, and
long-term e↵ects of FMT in di↵erent patient groups as pregnant patients [22].
• Future studies could focus more on donor-recipient compatibility and suit-
ability prior to FMT [24].
To personalise FMT for di↵erent patients and
conditions according to varied hosts and diseases [18].
• FMT has the potential of normalising a disrupted intestinal microbiota in
other diseases than CDI [20]. The di↵erent diseases that might be treated
successfully with FMT are gastrointestinal diseases as: inﬂammatory bowel
disease (ulcerative colitis and Crohn’s disease) and functional bowel disor-
ders (irritable bowel syndrome and constipation), and extra-gastrointestinal
diseases as metabolic syndrome (type 2 diabetes and nonalcoholic steatohep-
atitis), obesity, autoimmune disorders, parkinson’s disease, autism, multiple
sclerosis, idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura, multidrug-resistant organ-
isms infections, and multiple organ dysfunction in critical patients [18]. If the
mechanisms of FMT were discovered, future studies could further investigate
the possibility of treatment of other diseases linked to a disrupted microbiota.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The bacterial community compositions of the patient before and after FMT were
di↵erent. After FMT, an increase in diversity, both richness and evenness, was
observed in the patient’s microbiota to levels similar with the donor, and over time
they persisted. Dissimilarities were observed in the PCoA plot between the patient
before FMT and the donor. After FMT, the patient’s microbiota was more similar
with the donor’s microbiota, and clustered close to it, and stayed stable over time.
According to relative abundances, the patient’s microbiota changed to levels similar
with the donor’s microbiota at the taxonomic levels phylum, class, family, genus,
and at ASV level. Especially, an increase in the phyla Bacteriodetes and Firmi-
cutes, and a decrease in the phylum Proteobacteria were observed after FMT. The
FMT resulted in engraftment of donor derived strains in the patient over time. 1
week after FMT, 54.9% of the ASVs in the patient’s microbiota were donor derived.
The number declined to 46.4% 50 weeks after FMT. Increases in the SCFAs for-
mate, propionate, butyrate, and valerate were observed in the patient after FMT.
Increase in valerate is thought to be involved in the mode of action of FMT against
CDI.
The bacterial community composition of the infant’s microbiota was di↵erent from
the microbiota of the donor and the patient. It had higher levels of Actinobacteria
and Proteobacteria, and lower levels of Firmicutes and Bacteriodetes. The infant’s
microbiota was colonised with some strains derived from the donor.
An ASV from the family Peptostreptococcaceae was classiﬁed as Terrisporobacter
petrolearius, which is normally found in oilﬁeld petroleum reservoirs. It also had
100% identity to C. diﬃcile. The two bacteria had almost same V3 regions of the
16S rRNA gene, and the ASV was considered to be C. diﬃcile. C. diﬃcile toxin
genes tcdA and tcdB were detected in the patient before FMT, and were not de-
tected in the patient after FMT. C. diﬃcile toxin genes were also detected in the
infant at 6 months of age. Infants are often asymptomatic carriers of C. diﬃcile.
They are thought to be resistant to the C. diﬃcile toxins while their gut is imma-
ture, before the age of 2-4 years.
The data set was too small for statistics and generalisations.
However, in this
project, FMT through colonoscopy had great success in restoring the bacterial
microbiota and resolution of symptoms of CDI in the pregnant patient without
recurrences of CDI. This single-case project might add knowledge to the bene-
ﬁts and safety of FMT during pregnancy for CDI instead of traditional antibiotic
treatments.

APPENDIX A
The Clostridioides diﬃcile bacteria is a growing threat in developed countries.
Immunosuppressed people or people on antibiotic treatments are the targets. The
opportunistic gram-positive spore-forming bacteria Clostridioides diﬃcile produces
two toxins; toxin A and toxin B, that destroy the intestinal wall and give rise to
several diseases like colitis, diarrhoea, intestinal inﬂammation, toxic megacolon and
death.
The aim of this project is to determine what alterations the FMT has had in the
patient’s microbiota, and observe if the baby is colonised with some bacteria from
the donor or not. To achieve this serial faeces samples from donor, mother and
child have been taken, DNA must be extracted and sequenced. With the use of
bioinformatics, the sequenced DNA should be analysed and hereafter compared
between the di↵erent samples.
Hypotheses
• The patient’s microbiota will after FMT stay stable and look like the donor’s
microbiota.
• The baby will be exposed to bacteria from its mother and since the mother
was transplanted the infant’s microbiota will look like the donor’s microbiota.

Protocol: Experienced User

Important points before starting
• Perform all centrifugation steps at room temperature (15–25 ￿).
• If Solution C1 has precipitated, heat at 60 ￿until precipitate dissolves.
• Shake to mix Solution C4 before use.
Procedure:
1. Add up to 0.25 g of soil sample to the PowerBead Tube provided.
2. Add 750 µl of PowerBead Solution to the PowerBead Tube.
3. Add 60 µl of Solution C1 and invert several times or vortex
4. Bead beating options:
A. PowerLyzer 24 Homogenizer: Place the PowerBead Tubes into the tube
holder for the PowerLyzer 24 Homogenizer. The PowerBead Tubes must
be balanced in the tube holder. Run the samples for a time and RPM
suitable for your soil type.
Note: For clay soils, 4,000 RPM for 45 s is the best starting point. For
loose, granular and high organic soils, 2,500 RPM for 45 s will provide
an optimal result.
B. Vortex: Secure the PowerBead Tubes horizontally using a Vortex Adapter
(cat. no. 13000-V1-24). Vortex at maximum speed for 10 min.
Note: If you are using a 24-place Vortex Adapter for more than 12
preps, increase the vortex time by 5–10 min.
5. Make sure the PowerBead Tubes rotate freely in the centrifuge without rub-
bing. Centrifuge at 10,000 x g for 30 s. Do not exceed 10,000 x g.
Note: Centrifuge for 3 min at 10,000 x g for clay soils or if your soil is not
completely pelleted after 30 s.
6. Transfer the supernatant to a clean 2 ml Collection Tube (provided).
Note: Expect 400–500 µl. Supernatant may still contain some soil particles.
7. Add 250 µl of Solution C2 and vortex for 5 s. Incubate at 2–8 ￿for 5 min.
Note: You can skip the 5 min incubation. However, if you have already
validated the PowerSoil extractions with the incubation we recommend you
retain the step.
8. Centrifuge the tubes for 1 min at 10,000 x g. Avoiding the pellet, transfer up
to 600 µl of supernatant to a clean 2 ml Collection Tube (provided).

highlight where the alignment takes place. The ASV 362 aligns perfectly to both

ium diﬃcile ATCC 9689 and Terrisporobacter seems identical, and it makes the
ASV classiﬁcation of Clostridium diﬃcile diﬃcult.
