Abstract
Computational models of the human peripheral auditory system based on animal
physiology have shown potential for significant progress in hearing research. These
models, however, have a limited use when conducting research on humans as they
compute individual neuron responses which cannot be validated non-invasively. The
model responses, summed across characteristic frequency (CF), can be convolved
with a unitary response (UR) to translate the information into auditory evoked poten-
tials (AEPs). AEPs are electroencephalography (EEG) signals generated by synchro-
nized neural responses to acoustic stimuli measured non-invasively in the scalp of
the listener. Two modeling approaches where compared in this thesis: UR method
1 consisted of the compound output of an auditory nerve (AN) model convolved with
a single UR used to simulate the complete auditory brainstem response (ABR). UR
method 2 incorporated simple computer models of the cochlear nucleus (CN) and
inferior colliculus (IC) and consisted on the convolution of the compound output of
each model convolved with a different UR to account for a partial ABRs (i.e., a differ-
ent waves in the ABR waveform). The modeling framework was validated with tran-
sient click stimuli simulating ABRs and pure and sinusoidally amplitude-modulated
(SAM) tones simulating steady-state frequency following responses (FFRs) and en-
velope following responses (EFRs). The UR method 1 could better simulate ABRs at
lower levels and UR method 2 better replicated FFR-frequency functions. Both mod-
eling approaches could simulate EFR-level functions with no significant differences
between the methods. The two methods failed at replicating EFRs to low modu-
lation frequencies due to the lack of cortical generators in both model approaches.
UR method 2 could better simulate the high modulation frequency range of EFRs-
modulation frequency functions. This supports the hypothesis that there are two
main EFR generators, one located in the cortex for low modulation frequencies and
another on located in the midbrain for higher modulation frequencies. It was con-
cluded that UR method 2 can be more advantageous as the contributions from each
model are organized separately and the processing of each stage is more transpar-
ent and accessible. This could facilitate future research where specific properties
of the different nuclei may need to be modified, for example in simulating cochlear
synaptopathy or enhancement of neuronal activity in the CN and IC to account for
central gain compensatory mechanisms.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In 2018 there were 466 million people with a disabling hearing loss (HL), approxi-
mately 6.2% of the world population [WHO, 2018]. If no action is taken, nearly 9.2%
of world population is expected to live with HL in 2050 [WHO, 2018]. Audiology is
the scientific field involved in hearing, balance and related disorders [Gelfand, 2009].
Using different methods, audiologists determine if a person has a hearing disorder.
These methods can be divided into psychoacoustical or behavioral, and electrophysi-
ological tests. If a hearing abnormality is detected, several measures can be taken to
improve the condition of the listener. The most common prosthetic medical devices
are hearing aids and cochlear implants.
Psychoacoustics is the study of the perception of sound and is based on the
psychological responses associated with the sensation of sound, including speech,
noise, music, etc. In contrast, electrophysiological tests measure the electrical ac-
tivity of neurons in the auditory system that respond to acoustic stimuli. They are
different from behavioral or psychoacoustical experiments in that they are objective;
i.e., they do not require the active participation of the listener. Hearing research aims
at understanding how acoustic information is encoded and transmitted along the audi-
tory pathway. A better understanding of this can significantly improve the prevention,
diagnostic and treatment of HL.
In the 1970’s, physiological research with animals resulted in significant improve-
ments in the field. In recent years, with the current technology, several models of the
peripheral auditory system based on physiological findings from animals have been
developed. For ethical reasons, invasive research on the auditory system cannot be
carried out on humans as it is done on animals. However, the physiological models,
although based on animal research, can be modified to simulate human auditory re-
sponses as well, under some assumptions. There are two main potential benefits
these models could offer: firstly, the models can be used for forming and refining hy-
potheses as well as for designing experiments to test these hypotheses. Secondly,
these models have the potential to explain some of the underlying mechanisms in the
human peripheral auditory system. Non-invasive electrophysiological experimental
measurements mainly reflect the summed synchronized activity of all the excited neu-
rons in every nucleus in the auditory system. This is because, when presented with
an acoustic stimulus, neural activity in the auditory pathway generates electrical fields
that propagate through and are attenuated by the brain tissue and skull, before being
captured by the electroencephalography (EEG) recording system electrodes on the
scalp. In contrast, the models can simulate individual neurons and their response to
different stimuli. The models could therefore provide fundamental insights into how
sound is encoded in the brain.
==PAGE:16==
2
Introduction
The most-used physiological models today, simulate the auditory system up to the
auditory nerve (AN) which carries the information from the inner ear to the brainstem.
The output of the model is the individual instantaneous firing rate of the neurons in
the AN in spikes per second. As mentioned above, this typically cannot be validated
or used with humans, as it would require surgery to obtain the same experimental in-
formation. For this reason, a method that could accurately transform these peripheral
single neuronal responses into signals equivalent to what is measured non-invasively
in humans through EEG devices, is of great interest.
1.2
Aim and objectives
The main goal of this thesis was to develop and validate a model that can account
for the generation of auditory evoked potentials recorded in normal-hearing (NH) lis-
teners. The main model framework consists on using a state-of-the-art AN model
[e.g., Bruce et al., 2018] together with a fixed linear transformation that would match
the summed output of the AN model to the evoked potential recorded in the scalp
electrodes. This idea was conceptualized almost 20 years ago by Dau [2003]. Here,
the model framework proposed by Dau [2003] was firstly reevaluated using a newer
updated AN model, and then expanded to include simple models of primary-like neu-
rons from more central nuclei in the ascending pathway.
The main questions this thesis aimed to address were:
1. Given that all the non-linearities are modeled in the periphery and that the
transformation to signals measured by EEG is linear, can this model be a valid
framework to simulate evoked potentials? Can level-dependent non-linearities
observed experimentally be explained by this model framework?
2. Two different approaches to simulate auditory evoked potential (AEP), described
in section 3.1, were compared. The first explains the whole auditory brainstem
response (ABR) signal with the output of the AN model. The second combines
the output of the AN model and the output of simple models of the cochlear nu-
cleus (CN) and the inferior colliculus (IC) to simulate the ABR signal. Does the
model framework replicate physiological results when incorporating simpler, lin-
ear models of the CN and IC in the auditory periphery? Is there any significant
improvement by adding models of more nuclei?
3. Does a model framework based on and designed for transient stimuli and re-
sponses (clicks and ABRs) work for steady-state responses, like frequency
following responses (FFRs) and envelope following responses (EFRs)? Are
steady-state responses a superposition of transient AEPs?
To address these questions, a validation experiment was designed to test the
results of the modeling framework with both approaches for both transient and steady-
state responses. This validation process is explained in detail in section 3.5.

1.3
Structure of the thesis
The background and previous research on this topic are introduced in chapter 2, as
well as some of the key concepts and mathematical principles used in the framework
in this thesis. Chapter 3 introduces the overall modeling framework and explains
the linear transformation by which the peripheral model outputs are matched to the
evoked potentials. Moreover, the main AN model and the models of two additional
nuclei in the brainstem are described. Finally, the validation method and the stimuli
considered are explained. Chapter 4 presents the results obtained in the validation

three parts, the first shows the results of simulating ABRs with transient stimuli, which
are the stimuli the framework is based on. The last two parts of chapter 4 present
the steady-state results to pure tones and amplitude modulated-tones, respectively.
The results from chapter 4 are discussed and analyzed in chapter 5, where some
limitations of the framework and future perspectives, are described.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
2.1
Peripheral auditory system
This section gives a brief overview of how acoustic information is transduced from
pressure waves in the air at their arrival in the outer ear to mechanical vibrations in
the middle and inner ear, to electrical activity in the neurons in the different nuclei in
the auditory system. Acoustic information propagates through the air as longitudinal
pressure waves. These waves enter the outer ear, travel through the ear canal and
displace the tympanic membrane (or eardrum). The tympanic membrane is attached
to the three ossicles (the malleus, incus and stapes) in the middle ear. The function
of these bones is to transmit the acoustic vibrations in the air to vibrations in a liquid.
The ossicles articulate about different joints, effectively acting as mechanical levers,
and displace the oval window in the cochlea. Usually, 30% of the energy in air vibra-
tions is transmitted in the transduction to liquid, as most of the signal is reflected [Hill
et al., 2008]. The ossicles decrease the area of the force distribution, increasing the
pressure and improving the air-to-liquid transduction. The outer and middle ear are
usually modeled as a linear filter.
Figure 2.1. Schematic of inner, middle and outer ear.
The oval window is a membrane in the cochlea, a spiral-shaped cavity part of the
inner ear (See fig. 2.1). The cochlea consists of 3 chambers or ducts that contain
fluid (perilymph). As the walls of the cochlea are rigid, when the oval window is dis-

2.2 Auditory evoked potentials
7
2.2
Auditory evoked potentials
When acoustic stimuli are presented to a listener, the information is transduced by
the IHCs from mechanical movement to action potentials in the AN and subsequently
in the more central nuclei in the ascending pathway. The synchronized activity in
these nuclei is reflected as electrical signals called AEPs that can be recorded non-
invasively by EEG using scalp electrodes. AEPs are a measure of the electrophysio-
logical response of populations of neurons in the system and are usually expressed in
nV or µV . AEPs are classified by the type of stimulus used to generate the response
or potential.
Transient stimuli, such as clicks, chirps or tone bursts, generate AEPs that are
usually grouped by the time they occur relative to the stimulus onset. The timing in
the AEPs roughly corresponds to different nuclei located at different stages along the
auditory pathway. Previous research has established that short-latency responses -
AEPs that occur between 1 and 10 ms after the stimulus is presented - are generated
by nuclei up to the brainstem, and are referred to as ABRs. ABRs normally consist of
7 peaks (or waves) of which I, III and V are the most distinguishable in NH listeners
and therefore the most used in clinical studies. Wave-I is considered to correspond
mainly to activity from the AN, while wave-III matches with the spherical cells in the
anterior ventral cochlear nucleus (AVCN) and wave-V+ with the medial superior oli-
vary complex (MSO) and IC [Dau, 2003; Møller & Jannetta, 1985]. middle-latency
responses (MLRs) and long-latency evoked potentials (LLEPs) are AEPs that occur
between 15-50 ms, and 50-400 ms, respectively. The MLR and LLEP correspond
to cortical activity in the auditory pathway and are used clinically in cortical response
audiometry. As this project focuses more in the peripheral auditory system than more
central processes these AEPs are not examined in detail.
FFRs are steady-state AEPs obtained from periodic acoustic stimuli. The FFRs
are usually phase-locked to the periodicity of the stimulus presented. In this study,
FFRs are differentiated from EFRs, that show a periodicity phase-locked to the en-
velope of the stimulus, rather than to its fine-structure. In this thesis, pure tones
were used when analyzing FFRs and sinusoidally amplitude-modulated (SAM) tones
were used to study EFRs (also called auditory steady-state responses, ASSRs; or
amplitude-modulation following responses, AMFRs).
2.3
Unitary response
As AEPs are the far-field electrical signal resulting from activity of populations of
neurons, great interest has been devoted in finding links between the activity of indi-
vidual neurons to the generated AEP. Goldstein & Kiang [1958] first introduced the
idea of theoretically obtaining the AEP generated by a single neuron in the AN by con-
volving its instantaneous discharge rate in response to a stimulus with an elementary
unit waveform, the unitary response (UR). Kiang & Clark [1965] set up an experiment
==PAGE:22==
8
2 Theory
where a probe was placed at a single fiber in the AN of a cat and the AEP response
pattern to the spontaneous firing of the fiber was recorded and averaged. With the
specific evoked-potential response pattern of the fiber, the UR could be computed by
deconvolving the AEP with the firing of the neuron. This UR is only dependent on the
location of the neuron and the placement of the measuring electrodes, and should be
independent of the stimulus and instantaneous discharge rate of that neuron. This
concept was further developed by Melcher & Kiang [1996], who suggested that this
could be applied to any cell in the auditory system.
To expand this concept to humans, Dau [2003] used the AN model developed
by Heinz et al. [2001], to obtain the instantaneous discharge of the AN to a click
stimulus. The same click stimulus was presented to human listeners and the evoked
potential (i.e., the ABR) was recorded and averaged across listeners. The model
output was deconvolved with the recorded ABR, resulting in the UR for an average
human brain from the AN (simulated) to the electrodes on the scalp (recorded). This
method assumes that all the non-linear processing occurs from the outer ear up to the
AN, as the mathematical convolution with the UR is a linear operation. Additionally,
as mentioned in section 2.2, activity in the AN has been shown to generate wave-
I in the human ABR, not the whole response. This assumption was justified with
the argument that wave-III has been linked to activity in spherical bushy cells the
ventral cochlear nucleus (VCN) [Bourk, 1976; Pfeiffer, 1966; Rhode & Greenberg,
1994], which have a primary-like response pattern similar to the one observed from
AN fibers.
Dau [2003] simulated the AN response of different types of stimuli, and these
were convolved with the same UR to predict different types of AEPs. These EEG
responses could then be compared with experimental data. The simulations could
account for level-dependent changes in the ABRs. ABRs and FFRs were simulated
using clicks and chirps, tones and tonal sweeps, and other acoustic stimuli. The re-
sults showed that at high stimulation levels, the FFRs to low-frequency tones were
mainly due to the synchronized activity of high, off-frequency neurons in the AN and
not to neurons tuned to the characteristic frequency (CF) of the tone. There were
also some discrepancies between the modeled and experimental results. For exam-
ple, the model did not capture the decrease in latency of wave-V in the ABRs with
increasing stimulus levels.

UR(t) = ABR(t, click) ⊛−1 rAN(t, click).

AEP(t, any stimulus) = UR(t) ⊛rAN(t, any stimulus),

a regression problem of the compound (across CF) model response and the corre-

into an inverse problem by obtaining the convolution matrix of the compound model
response. The model response is a one-dimensional vector with the instantaneous
firing rate at different time bins. The convolution matrix is the matrix such that the
product of the matrix and the UR results in the same signal (the ABR) as the convo-
lution of the UR and the compound model response. This matrix was obtained with
the MATLAB function convmtx and the dimensions of the matrix were the length of
the model response vector (M) times the length of the corresponding ABR waveform
(N). This matrix is thus the design input matrix (X) to the regression problem with
N number of observations and M number of features. The recorded ABR waveform
vector is the regressand (y), and the resulting β coefficients (or weights) from the
regression constitute the UR vector. The regression was computed using Tikhonov
regularization [Tikhonov, 1963] and MATLAB tools from Hansen [1994].

3.3
Auditory nerve model
The AN model developed by Bruce et al. [2018] was used in this thesis. A schematic
of the structure of the model can be observed in figure 3.7 A. The model firstly sim-
ulates the transduction due to the middle ear as a linear filter. In the BM filter bank,
each auditory filter is modeled by a combination of two filters: a wider filter (C2) that
describes the broad processing at higher stimulus levels and a sharply tuned time-
varying level-dependent filter (“chirping filter”, C1) that accounts for the low-medium
stimulation levels. The feedback path controls the shape of the C1 filter and repre-
sents the local gain induced by outer hair cell (OHC) electromotility. OHC and IHC
loss can be specified in this model by changing the gain in the control path loop
and the IHC transduction function, respectively. The block after the wide-band C2 fil-
ter reflects experimental observations of phase changes of the signal corresponding
to switching the filter type (from narrow to wide) leading to AN phenomena like peak
splitting or frequency gliding. The IHCs processing is modeled by a saturating nonlin-
earity and a low-pass filter to account for the limitation in phase-locking. The synapse
model and the spike generator were updated from previous versions. The changes
can be seen in figure 3.7 B and C. The synapse model in Bruce et al. [2018] imple-
ments four synaptic release sites and includes an adaptive “redocking” mechanism
(redocking refers to the process of refilling a new vesicle in the IHC synapse) which
can adapt to both rapid and short-term adaptation components. This allows substitut-
ing the Westerman & Smith [1988] model used in previous versions, located before
the power-law adaptation, with a gently saturating non-linearity. Further details on
the new version of the model can be found in Bruce et al. [2018].
The implementation of the AN model is the same as described in Encina-Llamas
et al. [2019]. In short, the model is implemented by computing a total of 32000 AN
fibers distributed non-uniformly (with more density of fibers at mid CFs based on
Spoendlin & Schrott [1989]) through 400 CFs (cochlear segments or IHCs) ranging
from 0.125 to 20 kHz. For each CF, a 61% of high-spontaneous rate (SR) fibers, 23%
of medium-SR fibers and 16% of low-SR fibers were considered [Liberman, 1978].
The SR and relative and absolute refractory time for each CF for high-, medium- and
low-SR fibers were set to follow a random distribution with values from Bruce et al.
[2018].

3.5
Validation structure and stimuli
A validation experiment was set up to test the robustness of the modeling tool in
simulating AEP to different stimuli. The validation was divided into ABRs, FFRs and
EFRs. Data from experimental studies measuring ABRs, FFRs and EFRs in humans
were gathered and the different stimuli were designed. Table 3.1 shows the different
general parameters of the stimuli used in the different validation stages. The param-
eters are intensity in dB sound pressure level (SPL), frequency or carrier frequency
- and in the case of SAM tones, modulation frequency and modulation depth.
For the ABR stage, clicks at different levels were presented to the model. The
waveform of the click can be observed in fig. 3.3 (top left panel). The calibration
method for the click level was dB ppeSPL. The equivalent levels in nHL are: 20, 40,
60 and 80 dB nHL. The experimental data was taken from Kristensen & Elberling
[2012].
For the FFR stage, two different experiments were simulated with pure tones
as stimuli. Firstly, 12 pure tone bursts at different frequencies at 85 dB SPL were
presented. The 12 frequencies were 128, 192, 256, 320, 384, 448, 512, 576, 640,
704, 768, and 832 Hz. The tone bursts had a duration of 31.25 ms and with a rise
and fall of 20% the duration (i.e. 6.25 ms). The experimental data for this part of
the validation was taken from Hoormann et al. [1992]. Secondly, pure tones at 500
Hz and different levels were presented to the model. In order to reproduce the data
from Batra et al. [1985] (shown in fig. 4.5) both tone burst (TB) and continuous tone
(CT) were used as stimuli. The TBs had a duration of 40 ms and a rise and fall of 4
ms. Seven TBs, with intensities ranging from 65 to 95 dB SPL at 5 dB intervals, were
presented to the model. The CTs had a duration of 1.5 seconds, and the intensities
ranged from 62 to 97 dB SPL, also at 5 dB intervals.
For the EFR stage, two paradigms were simulated using SAM tones. Firstly, SAM
tones at a fixed level of 60 dB SPL at a fixed carrier frequency (fc) of 1 kHz for vary-
ing modulation frequencies (fm) were presented to the model. Thirty modulation
frequencies, ranging from 5 to 150 Hz in steps of 5 Hz were selected. All the SAM
tones were set to a modulation depth of 100%. The experimental data for this exper-
iment was taken from Picton et al. [2003]. Secondly, the modulation frequency was
kept constant and the stimulus was presented at different intensities. A SAM tone of
carrier frequency 2005 Hz and modulation frequency of 93 Hz was presented at two

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The analysis of the results is divided between the simulation of ABRs to transient
stimuli, and the simulation of FFRs and EFRs to pure and SAM tones. This distinction
emphasizes the question in the Aim and objectives section 1.2, on whether the model
framework can accurately predict both transient and steady-state responses, when
only designed with transient stimuli.
5.1
Transient responses, ABRs
5.1.1
ABRs results with the original URs
The most important changes in experimental ABR waveforms with level are the am-
plitudes and latencies of the different peaks (particularly waves-I, III and V). Wave
amplitudes increase with increasing stimulation level and wave latencies shorten with
increasing stimulus level. Depending on the number of trials averaged in the deriva-
tion of the measured ABRs, the waves are usually no longer distinguishable at a
given stimulation level (electrophysiological threshold) which is higher for the simu-
lated ABRs than the experimental ABRs. From figure 4.1, showing the simulated
results with clicks presented at various levels (left and center panels), UR method 1
could better simulate the experimental ABRs (right). Analyzing the contributions of
the different models in UR method 2, it was discovered that the URIC failed to repli-
cate wave-V in the ABR to lower-level clicks (ABRIC). The contributions from the
AN and CN models were replicated more accurately. This was reflected in wave-I,
which was similar with both UR methods. The simulated waves-III with both models
were different because the erroneous contribution of the IC model was being added
to the contribution of the CN. The URIC derived with the original URs was over-fitted
resulting in unstable ABRIC contributions. This can be observed in figure 4.1 (center)
at 95.5 and 75.5 dB ppeSPL after 8 ms, where the simulated ABRs started oscillating.
The URs were derived again using KRLS regression [Hainmueller & Hazlett, 2014]
in R [R Core Team, 2020] as described in section 3.2.3 and are shown in figure 3.6.
A limitation of this modeling framework is that, in general, the URs in method 2
were more unstable than the UR in method 1. This could be because more complex
waveforms, such as the complete ABR, are “easier” to fit and then predict in inverse-
problems than simpler ones like the partial ABRs (fig. 3.4). The URs were discovered
to be very sensitive to the method of regression used to derive them and the resulting
AEPs consequently varied greatly depending on the URs.

5 Discussion
5.1.2
ABRs results with the alternative URs
The alternative URs were only used for this stage of the validation and could be used
to simulate FFRs and EFRs in future work. Considering figure 4.2, the simulated
ABR at the lowest click level (55.5 dB ppeSPL) using both UR methods showed a
less distinct wave-V than the experimental ABR, and the simulated waves I and III
were smoother and less clear at 95.5 and 75.5 ppeSPL than in the experimental
results. It is important to note that the IC filter used to separate the experimental
ABR and to derive the URIC, did not include the filtered PAM reflex (see fig. 3.4).
Therefore, the simulated ABR to a click at 115.5 dB ppeSPL after 8 ms was flat for
UR method 2 (center panel). If this filter was extended to include the PAM reflex, the
compound response of the IC model would be used to also model this part of the
ABR and would be included in the simulations of method 2.
An interesting aspect of the simulations in Dau [2003] is that the model framework
(equivalent to UR method 1) using the AN model by Heinz et al. [2001] failed at
simulating the ABR wave-V latency-level functions observed in experimental data.
This relates to the the first question in the Aim and objectives section 1.2, on whether
the non-linearities can be accurately simulated when the only non-linear part of the
modeling framework is the AN model. Figure 5.1a shows experimental ABR wave-V
latency-level functions from six studies and three simulated results computed using
the model by Verhulst et al. [2018]. The experimental data are from Picton et al.
[1981] (PSC81 mean ±2 stds), Prosser & Arslan [1987] (PA87 mean and confidence
interval), Serpanos et al. [1997] (SOG97), Jiang et al. [1991] (JZSL91), Dau [2003]
(D03) and Strelcyk & Dau [2009] (SCD09). From this experimental data, the wave-V
latencies shorten approximately by 1 ms per 20 dB increase of stimulus level (0.05
ms / dB SPL).
The Bruce et al. [2018] model was originally based on cat physiology and de-
signed for simulating AN responses in cats. Additionally, synchronized compound
activity in the AN in both cats and humans has been associated with the ABR wave-
I, not V. Figure 5.1b shows experimental ABR waves-IV, III, II and I latency-level
functions of four cats [Van den Honert & Stypulkowski, 1986]. Observing the ABR
wave-I latency-level function would give an indication of the latency of the compound
AN response latency-level function in cats. The latency of wave-I decreases by less
than 1 ms over the whole recorded stimulus level range (from 40 to 90 dB SPL). In
ABR wave-V latency-level functions in humans, with the latency-level slope calcu-
lated above, a decrease of about 3 ms would be expected over the same stimulus
level range. This difference in latency-level slopes in humans and cats and wave-I
and -V suggests that the AN model by Bruce et al. [2018] alone would fail to account
for the wave-V latency-level functions in humans. This is because the transformation
from the AN model output to the ABR, that is, the UR, is a linear process which can-
not account for level-dependent latency changes. Moreover, as the additional CN
and IC models are linear and UR method 2 modeling approach is only a linear combi-
nation of URs, it is expected that this method would also fail to simulate latency-level
functions correctly.

Considering figure 4.2 again, UR method 1 (left) fails to replicate the latencies
observed in the experimental data (right). For example, focusing on the latencies of
wave-V, for UR method 1, they occur at approximately 5.8, 6 and 6.2 ms from the
stimulus onset, whereas the experimental wave-V’s are measured at 5.8, 6.3 and 7
ms at 115.5, 95.5 and 75.5 dB ppeSPL, respectively. The simulated wave-V latency
at the highest click level matched well the experimental data because it is the fitting
condition. In contrast, the model simulations using UR method 2 (fig. 4.2, central
panels) can account for the ABRs wave-V latencies observed in the experimental
data. The latencies of waves-I and III are also better predicted with this method
than with method 1, although the peaks at 75.5 dB ppeSPL are harder to discern
visually. It can be observed that, already using UR methods 1, the ABR wave-V
latencies became slightly longer with decreasing click level. This indicates that there
is already some the level dependency, occurring in the AN model. The CN and IC
models introduce additional delays in the excitatory and inhibitory processes leading
to the peak of the waves being centered at the correct time relative to stimulus onset.
In addition, the compound simulated output of the AN was narrower for the higher
click level and broader as the click level reduced. This may result in broader waves
with shallower skirt waveforms as click level decreases, as can be observed both in
the simulations and the experimental data in fig. 4.2, contributing to obtaining the
correct wave latency.

5.2
Steady-state responses, FFRs and EFRs
As mentioned above, the steady-state simulations were performed with the original
URs presented in figures 3.3 (method 1) and 3.5. Regarding the FFR-frequency func-
tions, both UR methods showed a trend similar, in general terms, to the experimental
data, as observed in figures 4.4a and 4.4b. UR method 2 could account better for
the general trend of the data, showing a maximum between 320 and 384 Hz. In con-
trast, UR method 1 resulted in a more spiky FFR-frequency function with a maximum
at 256 Hz. Additionally, in UR method 1, the FFR amplitudes fluctuated at frequen-
cies beyond 512 Hz, which does not match the measurements. With UR method
2, the slope of the FFR-frequency function at low frequencies was better simulated,
matching well with the lower bounds of the standard deviations of the experimental
data. This method also captured a slight increase in the amplitudes in the highest
frequencies which is greater than the experimental average but still falls within the
1-standard-deviation range.
The results for the FFR-level functions are harder to analyze as there are not so
many significant data points in the experimental data (solid markers in figure 4.5).
Neither the simulations with UR methods 1 or 2 captured the general trend of the
data, as seen in figures 4.6a and 4.6b. Method 1 perhaps had points closer to the
significant data for a 500 Hz tone. However, the simulations using TBs as stimuli
seemed to follow the opposite trend than the data, showing a minimum at 80 dB SPL
instead of a maximum close to 90 dB SPL. The simulated FFRs using CTs decreased
until 72 dB SPL, where they started increasing until 92 dB SPL and then decreased
again. This does not match the experimental data, which slightly increased until 82
dB SPL and then decreased. The results with UR method 2 roughly corresponded

For the EFR-modulation frequency functions, neither of the UR methods repli-
cated the experimental data at modulation frequencies lower than 65 Hz. As men-
tioned in section 4.3, this supports the hypothesis that there are two main EFR gen-
erators for the depending on the modulation frequency. Significant phase-locking
in the auditory cortex has only been observed at frequencies below 60 Hz [Middle-
brooks, 2008] and it is therefore believed that the processing of higher modulation
frequencies is performed in the midbrain. The main generator for responses to low
modulation frequencies (<55 Hz) has been therefore proposed to be located in the
cortex, whereas the main generator for higher modulation frequencies (>100 Hz) has
been attributed to midbrain sources. Kuwada et al. [1986] discovered that the laten-
cies of the responses of the two groups of modulation frequencies (7-9 ms and about
30 ms for low and high modulation frequencies, respectively) matched with the la-
tencies of cortical and IC neurons [Brugge et al., 1969]. This is consistent with the
results obtained in this thesis (fig. 4.8a and 4.8b), where cortical processing was
not modeled and one of the two modeling approaches includes a (simple) model of
the IC and the other does not. As expected, the results with both methods failed to
replicate the experimental responses corresponding to the cortical generator (<65
Hz). However, the method that includes information from the IC model, UR method
2, could better simulate the responses corresponding to the midbrain generator (>75
Hz).
The EFR-level functions, both using a single SAM tone (carrier frequency at 2005
Hz modulated at 93 Hz) and the combination of four SAM tones, corresponded with
the general trend in the experimental data. The slope of the mean trend of the exper-
imental EFR-level functions in figure 4.9 is approximately 0.2 dB (relative to 1 µV )
/ dB for both modulation depths (80% and 25%). Similarly, the simulations for both
modulation depths and obtained with both UR methods showed the a similar slope,
as observed in both panels figure 4.10. The magnitudes of the EFR-level functions
were larger (approximately 5 dB difference) for the stronger modulation depth than
for the shallower modulations. The simulated results show a similar difference in the
magnitude of the results from both methods, although slightly larger closer to 10 dB
difference.
The results from the FFR-level functions with the combination of four SAM tones
also fitted well with the experimental data, as with the SAM tone at the two modulation

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to design a modeling framework for simulating auditory
evoked potentials (AEPs) and validating it with simple transient and steady-state
stimuli. First, the response of 32000 auditory nerve (AN) fibers were computed using
Bruce et al.’s (2018) AN model, divided into 400 cochlear segments or inner hair cells
(IHCs) ranging from 0.125 to 20 kHz. Second, the compound activity of this nucleus
was convolved with a fixed elementary unit waveform (the unitary response (UR)) that
translated the summed synchronized activity into an evoked-potential experimentally
measured on the listener’s scalp, as in Dau [2003]. The UR was derived using re-
gression from a “fitting condition” where the auditory brainstem response (ABR) and
AN model output to the same click were known. This approach, where only the re-
sponse of the AN model was used to explain the whole ABR, was called UR method
1. Finally, an alternative method was suggested, UR method 2, incorporating simple
linear models of the cochlear nucleus (CN) and inferior colliculus (IC) that individually
and sequentially processed the responses resulting from simulated neurons at differ-
ent characteristic frequencys (CFs) in the AN model. The compound responses from
these models were convolved with different URs (URAN, URCN and URIC). These
URs were derived from an averaged ABR waveform in a way such that the output of
each model generates a different wave (I, III and V, respectively) in the ABR.
The two modeling approaches were tested with clicks at levels lower than the
fitting condition, pure tones and sinusoidally amplitude-modulated (SAM) tones. UR
method 1 better simulated the ABRs at lower click levels and the URIC for method 2
gave unstable ABRIC contributions resulting in broader waves. Using URs derived in
R, with an alternative regression method, UR method 1 replicated the changes in the
amplitudes of waves-I, III and V with decreasing level and UR method 2 could model
the differences in the latencies at which these waves were measured at the different
levels. UR method 1 failed to account for these latency changes. The frequency
and level of the pure tones in the steady-state simulations were varied, obtaining
frequency following response (FFR)-frequency and -level functions. UR method 2
better simulated the experimental results for FFR-frequency functions. The results
from the FFR-level functions were less clear. Neither of the two modeling approaches
captured the trend in the experimental data. However, the experimental data was of
a single individual listener, i.e. not averaged across several subjects, and not many
data-points were statistically significant, which compromises the reliability of this data
set. The FFR-level function computed with UR method 2 did show a similar trend to
experimental data to a 350 Hz-tone instead of the 500 Hz-tone stimulus used in the
validation.
Likewise, the modulation frequency and level of different SAM tones were var-
ied to compute envelope following response (EFR)-modulation frequency and -level
functions. The simulated EFR-modulation frequency functions support the hypoth-
esis from Kuwada et al. [1986] that there are two main generators of the EFR in
==PAGE:60==
46
Conclusion
the human auditory system. It is proposed that the main sources of the EFR at low
modulation frequencies are located in the cortex, generating a large response with a
latency of approximately 30 ms. The main sources at higher modulation frequencies
may be located in the midbrain, generating lower responses and latencies of 7-9 ms,
similar to cells in the IC [Brugge et al., 1969]. As expected, neither modeling ap-
proach could simulate the large responses to low modulation-frequency SAM tones
that are supposedly generated in the cortex. UR method 2, which includes a model
of the IC, replicated the responses at frequencies higher than 70 Hz better than UR
method 1. Both methods successfully replicated the EFR-level functions to a SAM
tone at a strong and shallow modulation depth and to a combination of four SAM
tones.
Overall, these results indicate that, although the models incorporated in UR method
2 are linear and simple, this modeling approach better captures some level-dependent
changes, specifically in the ABR latencies, and gives more accurate simulations for
FFR-frequency and EFR-modulation frequency functions. For EFR -modulation fre-
quency and -level functions, the results using UR method 2 were as good as with UR
method 1. It is proposed that UR method 2 can be advantageous as the contribu-
tions from each model are organized separately and the processing of each stage is
more transparent and accessible. This could facilitate future research where specific
properties of the different nuclei may need to be modified, for example by impos-
ing neuronal degeneration in the AN to simulate cochlear synaptopathy [Kujawa &
Liberman, 2009] or enhancement of neuronal activity in the CN and IC to account for
central gain compensatory mechanisms [Chambers et al., 2016].
6.1
Future research work
Given that with the alternative URs method 2 could account for latency-level changes
in the ABRs to lower-level clicks, an obvious next step would be to repeat the steady-
state validation stage of FFRs and EFRs using the alternative URs. Will the modeling
framework simulated more accurate steady-state responses with better, less “over-
fitted” URs?
An interesting analysis of the model framework would be to investigate how well
it is able to replicate experimental ABRs to other transient stimuli, for example chirps.
Could the output from the peripheral models to chirps convolved with URs derived
with clicks successfully simulate ABRs? Or are simulated transient “chirp-evoked”
ABRs exclusive to the type of stimulus the model was fitted to?
Another possible method for further testing the modeling framework could be to
use more complex stimuli, such as complex tones, vowels and speech. If the simu-
lated AEPs are similar to experimental AEPs to the same stimulus, the AN, CN and
IC models could broaden our understanding on the role of encoding the envelope of
signals and transient stimuli in vowels and consonants.
==PAGE:61==
6.1 Future research work
47
As well as changing the stimulus, the model should be validated simulating differ-
ent types of hearing impairment (HI). The degree of IHC and outer hair cell (OHC)
loss or cochlear synaptopathy can be specified in the AN model and central gain can
be implemented in the CN and IC models. If the modeling framework successfully
models HI, it could prove a used tool for diagnosing different types of HI.
A practical improvement to the model could be to include, as an input parameter,
the number of trials averaged to compute the measurements. This would control the
level of internal noise in the model framework. It could be implemented by recalcu-
lating the URs every time this parameter is changed using the specified number of
individual ABR measurements to compute the average ABR to fit the URs. The more
trials included in obtaining this averaged ABR, the less noise included in the model
framework.
