Summary
In this thesis project, a 2D solver EULER-EOF for inviscous compressible ﬂow
simulation is implemented. The code is written in C++ for the sake of better
portability and code reusability in future extensions. It uses a time-stepping
approach to solve the stead state problem. In the solving procedure, the curvi-
linear mesh is transformed into a cartesian grid and the explicit Godunov scheme
and the MUSCL scheme are applied for the discretization. A Riemann Solver
of Roe is implemented to evaluate the intercell ﬂux for the update in time. Fi-
nally, EULER-EOF is validated by two benchmark cases: Sod’s Shock Tube
and a converging channel with a sharp wedge. Camparison between the results
from the diﬀerent schemes are also made. The implemented code can be used
as the foundation of the extension for aeroelastic or viscous simulation.
Keywords: Euler equations; Riemann solver; Inviscous ﬂow

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind energy industry, by its nature crucial to understand and exploit the ﬂow of
the air, is in the middle of a wide spectrum of diﬀerent industries with common
pratices of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). In this Chapter, we will
brieﬂy review the models and the techniques of the CFD applied in the wind
energy research and development.
1.1
CFD Applications in Wind Turbine Aerody-
namics
1.1.1
Simulation Types
Compared with the aerodynamic problems in aeronautics, the wind turbine
(WT) rotors usually operate in a relatively low Mach number. As shown in Fig-
ure 1.1, the highest Mach number of the wind turbine is barely higher than 0.3.
Therefore, most of the CFD applications in WT aerodynamics assume the ﬂow
to be imcompressible. In certain circumstances such as the ﬂow over a directly
modeled blade tip (Sanderse [9]), WT aeroacoustic (Morris et al. [10]), or the
smaller WT with high tip speed ratio (Wood [11]), the compressiblity will be
==PAGE:21==

taken into account.
Figure 1.1: Comparison of the Mach number and the Reynolds number of the
WTs and the major aircrafts (Ceyhan [1])
Furthermore, the large WT’s Reynolds number is as high as some narrow-body
airliners like Airbus A320, and the ﬂow is in general unconﬁned except near-
blade region. Therefore to some extent we can justify the inviscous assumption
in the simulation.
In fact, if further combining with the incompressible as-
sumption of the ﬂow, we will have a free-vortex methods for WT aerodynamics
(Mathew and Philip [12]). However, without the viscousity the turbulence will
not be generated in the ﬂow, which will have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence to the sim-
ulation. And with the advances of the computational power, the cost of solving
a Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) Equations has become aﬀordable.
Therefore currently the mainstream CFD applications in WT aerodyanmic re-
search choose incompressible Navier-Stokes simulations (Tossas and Leonardi
[13]).
1.1.2
Rotor Models in the simulation
One of the important CFD applications in wind energy is the study of the
WT wake and its interaction with other WTs.
It is crucial for the optimal
conﬁguration of a wind farm and the prediction of its power extraction from
atmospheric boundary layer (ABL).
==PAGE:22==
1.1 CFD Applications in Wind Turbine Aerodynamics
3
As we have mentioned before, the ﬂow in WT aerodyanmics is largely unconﬁned
unless we are studying the region near the blade. Therefore, an approximation
of actuator disc (Mikkelsen [14]), actuator line (Troldborg [15]), or actuator
surface (Shen et al. [16]) to the rotor is often introduced to the wind turbine
wake simulation, as shown in Figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Actuator disc (AD), line(AD), surface(AS) model for the wind
turbine rotor (Sanderse et al. [2])
All these three acruator models decouple the disturbance of the blades from the
simulation of the wake and treat it as a combination of moving body forces in
the ﬂow. The value of the body force for each blade element is pre-calcuated in
diﬀerent incomming speeds and angle of attacks and will be accessed directly
during the wake simulation.
This approximation is very important for the CFD simulation of a wind farm
with multiple WTs. Due to the huge diﬀerence in the scale of the atmospheric
ﬂow and the ﬂow near a rotor blade (Porté-Agel et al. [17]), a spontaneous
simulation of the two at the same time will be very expensive. In fact, recent
validations to the full scale simulations and near blade measurements show that
actuator model is a very cost-eﬃcient choices with a satisfying accuarcy. (Nilsson
et al. [18], Réthoré et al. [19]).
Therefore, except some certain cases, using
actuator models is a common practice in the WT wake simulation.
1.1.3
Simulation Techniques
The major techniques for the turbulence in the WT wake simulations are RANS
and Large Eddy Simulation (LES).
In the RANS approach, a time-averaging operation is applied to the N-S equa-
tions and produces an extra Reynolds stress term (RST). In WT wake simula-
tion, the RANS equations can be further simpliﬁed by the parabolization to the
governing equations by assuming the ﬂow to be axisymmetric and neglecting
the streamwise viscous and pressure gradient (Vermeer et al. [20]). We can also
directly solve the elliptic pressure possion equations in RANS, which leads to the
==PAGE:23==
4
Introduction
elliptic models. To closed the RANS equations, a turbulence modeling to RST
is required. Réthoré [21] indicated that the popular standard k −ϵ and k −ω
model is too diﬀusive for the WT wake and the local adaptations to reduce the
eddy viscousity in the near wake is suggested by other researchers ( El Kasmi
and Masson [22]).
LES, on the other hand, also draws increasing attentions among the wind energy
community on its application in WT aerodynamics (Jimenez et al. [23]). LES
signiﬁcantly outperforms RANS simulation on invesitating the details of the
wake ﬂow and the turbulent mixing. However, Stovall et al. [24] compared a
single-wake simulation using standard k −ϵ model and LES with one-equation
SGS model. The computational times for LES is 60 times longer even on the
same grid with the RANS model.
Therefore, LES is still unrealistic for the
engineering design, but rather a research tool to investigate the physics of the
WT wake.
1.2
Existing Non-commercial CFD Codes
In this section, we will make a brief review on several popular exsisting non-
commercial CFD solvers in academic research and industrial application. Most
of those codes are open-sourse so their code structure and implementation can
be used as references to our implementation.
• CLAWPACK, or "Conservation Laws Package", is an open-sourse Finite
Volume Methods solver written in FORTRAN for linear and nonlinear
hyperbolic systems of conservation laws ([25]). It is mainly based on a
Wave-propagation algorithms with Riemann solvers. It has a python-style
interface Pyclaw and the function of Adaptive Mesh Reﬁnement through
the subroutine AMRClaw.
• FUN3D is a Fortran code developed and maintained by NASA from 1980s
(Biedron et al. [26]). It is an unstructured-grid solver and includes the
features like Gradient-based adaptation, MPI communication and Multi-
grid with implicit time stepping. However, the distribution of FUN3D is
limited to the legal citizens or organization entities of the United States.
• OpenFOAM R⃝is a very popular open-sourse CFD code developed and
maintained by OpenCFD Ltd (Jasak et al. [27]).
It is a C++ library
based on Finite Volume Method on unstructured meshes and support the
simulations of a wide range of ﬂow problems involving chemical reactions,
turbulence and heat transfer.
==PAGE:24==
1.3 Thesis Objects
5
• SU2 is an open-sourse C++ based code developed by the Aerospace Design
Lab (ADL) of the Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics at Stanford
University (Palacios et al. [28]). It is designed for the CFD simulation
and aerodynamic shape optimization constrained by PDEs. Although it
is originally implemented to simulated the compressible, turbulent ﬂows
governed by Navier–Stokes equations, the code framework can be applied
to arbitrary sets of PDE systems in other multiphysical problems.
1.3
Thesis Objects
In this thesis project, we are going to implemented a 2D solver EULER-EOF for
solving inviscous compressible ﬂows as a foundation of the future extension to
the aeroelastic coupling or viscous ﬂow simulation. The code will be written in
C++ for the sake of better portability and code reusability. It will bring great
convenience to the maintainance and extension of the code.
EULER-EOF will using the time-stepping approach to solve the stead state
problem. The converged steady state result will be regarded as the ending state
of a time-variant simulation from an initial condition. In the solving procedure,
a structure mesh on a curvilinear coordinates will ﬁrst be read in the code
and then transformed into a cartesian computational domain. We will use the
explicit Godunov scheme and the MUSCL scheme to discretize the governing
equations, and a Riemann Solver of Roe will be implemented to evaluate the
intercell ﬂux for the update to next time step.
After the implementation is done, we will validate EULER-EOF with two bench-
mark cases, Sod’s Shock Tube and a converging channel with a sharp wedge at
the bottom. The simulation result will be compared with the theoritcal solu-
tions. And a camparison between the Godunov scheme and the MUSCL scheme
will also be made.

In this section, we are going to presents the result of the transonic Case 2

accelerated. But in this case, the Mach number of the ﬂow exceeds 1 when it
reaches the convex corner at the top of the slope and become supersonic. At
this point, a Prandtl-Mayer expansion fan will be generated.
Figure 6.4: Prandtl-Mayer expansion fan [6]
The Prandtl-Mayer expansion fan is a group of the inﬁnite waves diverge from
the sharp convex point. It is isoentropical, and across the expansion fan the
Mach number of the ﬂow increases while the pressure and density decrease.
Most importantly, it is impossible for the supersonic ﬂow to turn around a
convex point through an single jump because it will that violated the entropy
condition and create an unphysical expansion shock (Toro [3]).
Figure 6.5 shows the pressure contour of the transonic Case 2 without an en-
tropy ﬁx. As we can see, there is an false expansion shock generated after the
convex turning point and reﬂected towards down stream. The reason for this
unphysical solution, as we have pointed out before, is due to entropy violation
of the Roe solver’s linearization to the exact Riemann Problem at the transonic
rarefraction.
The correct transonic results from the Roe Solver with the entropy ﬁx are shown
in Figure 6.6. As we can see, the domain is now free from the false expansion

Chapter 7
Conclusions and Future
Extensions
In this chapter we will summarize the major works of the thesis project and
discuss the possible future extensions to EULER-EOF.
7.1
Conclusion
The major work of this thesis project is the implementation of an 2D Euler
equations solver EULER-EOF for the inviscous ﬂow. The major feature of the
work are listed below.
• EULER-EOF solves the steady-state problem by an explicit time-stepping
approch. The converged result is the ending state of a time-variant simula-
tion. In the steady-state form, the Euler equations is elliptical in subsonic
while hyperbolic in supersonic. By introducing an extra time derivative
term ∂U/∂t, the governing equation becomes hyperbolic for both sub-
sonic and the supersonic cases. Therefore we can use the same numerical
scheme to treat the two diﬀerent ﬂows. This property is very helpful when
we deal with the transonic problem which the transonic location can’t be
pre-determined.
==PAGE:71==
52
Conclusions and Future Extensions
• EULER-EOF supports the simulation on a structured mesh by a domain
transform from the curvilinear physical space (x, y) to a cartesian compu-
tational space (ξ, η). This arrangement brings convenience for the code
implementation, but also limits the applicability of the current solver to
the simply connected topology. To support the problems of more complex
geometry, we need to further introduce the multi-block structure into the
code.
• EULER-EOF is implemented in C++ so that we can exploit the object-
oriented programming feature of this langurage. The top-level abstraction
and the stencil-iterator structure make the code highly portable and con-
venient to maintain and extend.
• EULER-EOF is validated by two popular benchmark test cases: Sod’s
shock tube and a converging channel with a sharp edge.
The simula-
tions results match the theoritical results very well and successfully repro-
duce the important ﬂow phenomena for subsonic, transonic and supersonic
cases, proving the correctness of the implementation.

Multiblock structure for the complex geometry
In order to solve the ﬂow problem over the complex geometry, for example an
airfoil with an aileron in Fig 7.1, an extension to EULER-EOF to support the

implemented to communicate the ﬂow variables across diﬀerent blocks. Within
a single block, we can still apply the domain transformation to a cartesian grid
