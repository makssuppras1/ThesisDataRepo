Abstract
Glucagon is a peptide hormone produced and secreted from α cells of the islets of Langer­
hans in the pancreas. Glucagon is secreted during fasting or energy deficit, driving glu­
cose release and fat breakdown to meet cellular energy demands. Glucagon activates
the glucagon receptor (GCGR), a G­protein­coupled receptor primarily expressed in the
liver and kidneys. GCGR activation triggers glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis) and
de novo glucose production (gluconeogenesis), elevating blood glucose levels. Beyond
glucose homeostasis, the GCGR plays an important role in metabolic regulation, making
it a key therapeutic target for diabetes and metabolic disorders.
Recent studies identify receptor activity­modifying protein 2 (RAMP2) as a key regula­
tor of GCGR signalling. This thesis investigates the molecular interplay between GCGR
and RAMP2, focusing on RAMP2’s effects on GCGR signalling pathways, including re­
ceptor internalisation, 3’,5’­cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) production, and β­
arrestin­2 recruitment. Using functional assays, cellular models, and advanced imaging
techniques, the work presented in this project demonstrates that RAMP2 has effects on
both surface expression and β­arrestin recruitment, decreasing both internalisation effi­
ciency and β­arrestin recruitment.
Understanding the GCGR­RAMP2 complex is therapeutically relevant, given glucagon’s
central role in diabetes and metabolic diseases. Furthermore, as the GCGR has a very
different function in the kidneys, modulating glomerular flow rate and ion excretion, in­
sights into RAMP2­mediated tissue­specific GCGR regulation provide valuable physio­
logical and pharmacological implications.

Resume (Danish Abstract)
Glukagon er et peptidhormon, der produceres og udskilles af α­celler i bugspytkirtlens
Langerhanske øer.
Glukagon er essentiel under faste og energimangel, da det stim­
ulerer frigivelsen af glukose og nedbrydningen af fedt for at imødekomme cellens en­
ergibehov. Glukagon aktiverer glukagonreceptoren (GCGR), en G­proteinkoblet receptor,
der primært udtrykkes i leveren og nyrerne. Aktivering af GCGR udløser nedbrydning af
glykogen (glykogenolyse) og de novo glukoseproduktion (glukoneogenese), hvilket øger
blodsukkerniveauet. Udover at opretholde glukosehomeostase spiller GCGR en vigtig
rolle i metabolisk regulering, hvilket gør det til et centralt terapeutisk mål for diabetes og
metaboliske sygdomme.
Nye studier har identificeret receptoraktivitet­modificerende protein 2 (RAMP2) som en
vigtig regulator af GCGR­signalering. Denne afhandling undersøger det molekylære sam­
spil mellem GCGR og RAMP2 med fokus på RAMP2’s påvirkning af GCGRs signalveje,
herunder receptor­internalisering, produktion af 3’,5’­cyklisk adenosinmonofosfat (cAMP)
og β­arrestin­2­rekruttering. Ved brug af funktionelle assays, cellemodeller og avancerede
billedteknikker demonstrerer dette projekt, at RAMP2 påvirker både overfladeekspressio­
nen og β­arrestin­rekruttering, ved at nedsætte både internaliseringseffektiviteten og β­
arrestin­rekrutteringen.
Forståelsen af GCGR­RAMP2­komplekset er terapeutisk signifikant i lyset af glukagons
centrale rolle i diabetes og metaboliske sygdomme. Desuden har GCGR meget forskel­
lige funktioner i nyrerne, hvor det påvirker glomerulær gennemstrømningshastighed og
ionudskillelse. Indsigt i RAMP2­medieret vævsspecifik regulering af GCGR vil derfor give
værdifulde fysiologiske og farmakologiske perspektiver.

Introduction
1.1
G Protein­Coupled Receptors
G protein­coupled receptors (GPCRs) represent the most numerous and diverse group of mem­
brane receptors in eukaryotes1. The human genome encodes 826 GPCRs, and with around
20,000 encoded proteins, this means that GPCRs represent around 4% of the genes in the
human genome2,3.
GPCRs are essential in many physiological functions as they mediate most responses to hor­
mones, neurotransmitters and environmental stimulants4. Consequently, defects in GPCR sig­
nalling can cause various diseases, such as cancer, diabetes, obesity, inflammation, cardiac
dysfunction, and central nervous system disorders, making GPCRs interesting therapeutic tar­
gets5. This is reflected by the fact that GPCRs are the most successful pharmacological tar­
get with an estimated 40­50% of all marketed drugs achieving their clinical effect by targeting
GPCRs6,2. Conversely, little is known about this vast group of receptors; structures are only
known for 30 of the 826 GPCRs and around 159 are still described as orphan GPCRs, as their
endogenous ligand is unknown7. Many of these are olfactory receptors, chemoreceptors that
detect odorants, which is the largest group of GPCRs, with around 340 receptors8. The ligands
for most of these receptors are still poorly understood, highlighting the challenges in linking a
receptor to its ligand9.
1.1.1
GPCR Structure and Classification
Figure 1.1:
The general structure of
GPCRs, shown both schematically and
folded. Made in BioRender.
Despite a vast array of physiological functions, all GPCRs share
a common structure (see figure 1.1) of 7 transmembrane do­
mains and are also termed 7TM receptors. While most GPCRS
are 40­200 amino acids, the largest (G protein­coupled receptor
98, GPR98) consists of 6,306 amino acids2,10.
All receptors have the same orientation in the membrane with
an extracellular N­terminus and intracellular C­terminus11. Fur­
thermore, they consist of seven transmembrane α­helical do­
mains (TM1­TM7), three extracellular loops (ECL1­3) and three
cytosolic loops (ICL1­3)12. The seven α­helical domains contain
many hydrophobic amino acids to anchor the helices in the hy­
drophobic core of the plasma membrane13. The structure and
sizes of ECLs, ICLs and termini vary between the different re­
ceptor subfamilies14.
Most GPCRs undergo post­translational modifications, and
commonly, there is an N­glycosylation site (asparagine residue)
in the N­terminus or ECL2 of the receptor12. Furthermore, phos­
phorylation sites predominantly in the ICL3 and C­terminus are important sites for second
messenger­dependent protein kinases such as protein kinase A (PKA), protein kinase C (PKC)
and G­protein­coupled­receptor kinases (GRKs)15,16.
GPCRs can be classified based on amino acid sequence and functional similarity. There are
two major classification systems: the A­F and GRAFS systems. The A­F system classifies
GPCRs into six groups: rhodopsin­like (A), secretin family (B), metabotropic glutamate (C), fun­
gal mating pheromone (D), 3’,5’­cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) receptors (E), and
frizzled/smoothened (F) receptors17. The GRAFS system, introduced by Fredriksson et al. in
2003, classifies human GPCRs into five groups: Glutamate, Rhodopsin, Adhesion, Frizzled/­

Taste2, and Secretin18,19. The main difference between the two systems is that while the A­F
system classifies all vertebrate and invertebrate GPCRs based on homology, the GRAFS sys­
tem classifies human GPCRs based on phylogenicity18,16. Class D, E, and F include receptors
not found in humans, while class A receptors correspond to the rhodopsin family, which is the

the GRAFS system16.
1.2
Heterotrimeric G proteins
As evident from their name G protein­coupled receptors bind and activate heterotrimeric G pro­
teins (guanine nucleotide­binding proteins) upon binding an agonist. G proteins work as the
link between the activated GPCR and effector proteins, regulating intracellular levels of second
messengers and ions12.
Heterotrimeric G proteins consist of three subunits; the α, β and γ subunit. Humans have 21
different Gα subunits, 6 Gβ subunits and 12 Gγ subunits. The Gα subunits are divided into four
main classes based on sequence similarity: Gαs, Gαq, Gαi/o and Gα12/1320. These subgroups
show selectivity towards specific GPCR subtypes and effector proteins, activating different sig­
nal transduction pathways12.
1.2.1
Activation of Heterotrimeric G proteins by GPCRs
GPCRs are allosteric proteins, which means that once the receptor’s extracellular domain binds
an agonist, it causes a conformational change of the receptor. This conformational change al­
lows the intracellular part of the receptor to bind to a heterotrimeric G protein. In the G protein’s
inactive state, the Gα subunit has a guanosine diphosphate (GDP) bound, but coupling to a
GPCR in the active conformation promotes the exchange of the GDP with a guanosine triphos­
phate (GTP). The GPCR, therefore, works as a guanine nucleotide exchange factor (GEF)11.
This leads to dissociation of the Gα from the Gβγ subunits12. These Gα and Gβγ subunits then
independently activate and/or inhibit effector proteins. The intrinsic GTPase activity of the Gα
eventually catalyses the hydrolysis of GTP to GDP, returning the subunit to its inactive GDP­
bound state, and the Gα dissociates from effector proteins and re­associates with the Gβγ subunit,
ending the G­protein cycle12.

Activation and Regulation of GPCRs
1.3.1
GPCR Ligand Classification and Efficacy

GPCRs display promiscuity, often binding structurally sim­
ilar ligands, but the efficacy of these ligands to activate the
receptor can vary immensely. Based on receptor activ­
ity, ligands are categorised as either agonists, partial ag­
onists, antagonists or inverse agonists21 (see figure 1.2).
Agonists are compounds that bind the receptor, stabilising
the active conformation and mimicking the function of the
receptor’s endogenous agonist. A full agonist induces the
maximal effect, often defined as the effect induced by the
receptor’s endogenous agonist, whereas a partial agonist
induces a partial effect22 (figure 1.2). Furthermore, some
ligands show functional bias, selectively activating one of
the different signalling pathways coupled to the same re­
ceptor. For GPCRs, several studies have demonstrated
ligands that are biased towards or away from β­arrestin
recruitment23, or biased towards a specific heterotrimeric G protein, as many GPCRS are also

A GPCR is never entirely inactive, its constitutive, or basal, activity allows it to activate down­

of agonists, it binds the receptor and stabilises the inactive conformation of the receptor, result­
ing in a constitutive activity below the baseline activity of the GPCR27. Just like agonists, inverse
agonists can be full and partial depending on how much they lower the constitutive activity23
(see figure 1.2). Where the inverse agonist actively inactivates the receptor, the antagonist
works by blocking other molecules, such as agonists, from binding the receptor but does not
alter the basal activity of the receptor22 (figure 1.2).
A ligand’s potency is quantified by the EC50 (effective concentration) and Emax or IC50 (inhibitory
concentration) and Imax can be determined from a dose­response curve, in which receptor activ­
ity in response to increasing concentrations of the ligand is quantified, for example by measur­

concentration reaching 50% of the maximal effect (Emax) is defined as EC50 for agonists and
IC50 (figure 1.2)for antagonists. The Emax/Imax refers to the dose that generates the maximal
response28. Since the EC50 and IC50 are measures of how much ligand is needed to achieve
an effect, the lower the value, the more potent the ligand29.
Ligands that bind to the site of the endogenous ligands, the orthosteric site, are defined as or­
thosteric ligands30. Allosteric ligands bind to other sites on the GPCRs, called allosteric sites.
These ligands are typically defined as positive or negative allosteric modulators, depending
on the effect they have on the receptor. These molecules show great therapeutic potential to
enhance or dampen the signal of a specific receptor30.

Gαs­ and Gαi/o­mediated signalling
Gαs and Gαi/o proteins are associated with the activation and inhibition of adenylyl cyclase
(AC)31. When a Gs­coupled receptor is activated, the GTPGαs subunit is released and activates
AC which then catalyses the synthesis of 3’,5’­cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) from
Adenosine­5’­triphosphate (ATP)32. cAMP is an important second messenger, and the cAMP
transduction pathway regulates several cellular processes. The effects of cAMP are mainly me­
diated by three cAMP effectors: protein kinase A (PKA) and exchange protein directly activated
by cAMP (EPAC), and the cyclic­nucleotide­gated ion channels33,34.
PKA is a tetramer of two regulatory subunits and two catalytic subunits. It is the best­understood
effector of cAMP and is activated when cAMP binds both of the regulatory subunits, causing
their dissociation from the catalytic subunits. The activated protein kinase A has numerous
substrates with different cellular outcomes based on cell type35,1. Upon activation, some of the
catalytic PKA subunits move into the nucleus, phosphorylating the transcription factor cAMP
response element­binding protein (CREB). CREB binds the cAMP­response element (CRE) in
the promoter or enhancer region of genes responsive to cAMP. Simultaneously, CREB binds
co­activators that link CREB to RNA polymerase II, activating gene transcription36.
The second group of intracellular cAMP effectors, EPACs, are specific GEFs for the Ras GT­
Pases, Rap1 and Rap2. EPACs are activated by binding cAMP37, and catalyse the GDP ex­
change to GTP, activating Rap1 and ­2. The Rap1 and Rap2 proteins are important in regulating
a wide variety of cellular functions such as cell­cell adhesion, exocytosis and secretion, cell mi­
gration, proliferation, and barrier functions in endothelial cells33,38,39. A modified EPAC1 protein
is used in this thesis as a sensor of cAMP levels, described in detail later.
The last of the three main cAMP effectors are the cyclic nucleotide­gated channels. These
cation channels are activated by cyclic nucleotides (cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP)
or cAMP) and influence the membrane potential and the concentration of intracellular cations,
especially Ca2+. The cyclic nucleotide­gated channels are known to be important in the signal
transduction of olfactory receptors and photoreceptors40.
The cellular response to a rise in intracellular cAMP varies between tissues and cell types. In
cardiac muscle tissue, cAMP­driven activation of PKA leads to an increase in contraction rate,
whereas gluconeogenesis in liver cells is regulated transcriptionally by cAMP, as CREB acti­
vates the transcription of genes encoding key enzymes in the pathway41,1,42.
Gαq­mediated signalling
The Gαq­GTP protein activates phospholipase C­β (PLC­β). Phospholipase C (PLC) hydrolyses
phosphatidylinositol bisphosphate (PIP2) into the second messengers inositol 1,4,5­trisphosphate
(IP3) and diaglycerol (DAG). IP3 induces Ca2+ release from the endoplasmic reticulum, influenc­
ing several downstream targets32. The increase in Ca2+ causes protein kinase C (PKC) to
translocate to the plasma membrane, enabling it to bind and become activated by DAG, which
is bound to the plasma membrane1. Activated PKCs have many different effects, depending on
cell type, but they often work by phosphorylating transcription factors in the cytosol, activating
their movement into the nucleus, where they activate gene transcription1.
Gα12/13­mediated signalling
The heterotrimeric G proteins G12 and G13, specifically the Gα12/13 subunits, are known to target
Rho guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) (RhoGEFs)43. Once activated, the RhoGEF
activates RhoA, a GTPase in the Rho family44,45, by facilitating the exchange of GDP to GTP.
The RhoA GTPase is, amongst others, important in cell adhesion by maintaining tight junctions
and regulating the actin cytoskeleton through the activation of actin nucleators, thereby regulat­
ing processes such as cell migration and polarity46,47,48.

1.4
Glucagon and the Glucagon Receptor
Glucagon is a 29­amino acid peptide hormone secreted by pancreatic α cells in the Islets of
Langerhans. It is produced by cleaving pre­proglucagon into glucagon, a process catalysed
by proconvertase­257. The proglucagon peptide also encodes for the peptides glucagon­like
peptide 1 (GLP­1) and 2 (GLP­2) produced by post­translational processing by prohormone
convertase 1/3 in the gut and nervous system58. Glucagon acts through its specific receptor,
the glucagon receptor (GCGR), a class B (secretin family) GPCR, known to couple to Gs, and
to a lesser extent, Gq and Gi proteins, regulating both intracellular Ca2+ and cAMP concentra­
tions25,59. The glucagon receptor is primarily expressed in liver and kidney cells but is also
found in parathyroid and adrenal glands, pancreatic islet cells60. Although there is some con­
troversy around the expression in other tissues, the glucagon receptor has also been proposed
to be expressed in the heart, adipose tissue and brain61,62.
1.4.1
Glucagon Secretion from α Cells
Glucagon is secreted by α cells primarily in response to low blood glucose levels (hypogly­
caemia), for example, during fasting or exercise1. Hypoglycaemia can activate the release of
glucagon in three ways. First, α cells can sense glycaemic changes and respond directly to
low blood glucose levels by activating glucagon secretion. When glucose levels are low, the
ATP/ADP ratio is reduced in the cell, and this relieves the inhibition of the ATP­sensitive potas­
sium ion channel(K+­ATP), resulting in an efflux of potassium ions, which in turn activates an
influx of sodium ions through the voltage­dependent sodium channel (VDSC), causing action
potential firing, which is defined as a rapid change in the membrane potential of excitable cells.
The change in membrane potential opens voltage­gated P/Q type calcium channels, allowing
an influx of extracellular Ca2+ into the cytosol63,64. Hormone secretion happens when the influx
of calcium ions (Ca2+) causes a rise in intracellular Ca2+ concentration, activating exocytosis of
secretory glucagon­containing granules65. This is the same way hormones are secreted from
the other two endocrine cells of the pancreatic islet, β and δ cells65.
Secondly, within the pancreatic islets, several paracrine factors that inhibit α cells are secreted
from β and δ cells during normal­ and hyperglycemia. From the β cells, insulin, amylin, zinc,
gamma­aminobutyric acid (GABA), and serotonin directly inhibit α cell function, and urocortin 3
and GABA secreted from β cells activate somatostatin secretion from δ cells, which negatively
feeds back on both α and β cells. Hypoglycemia causes a decrease in the secretion of these
inhibitory paracrine factors, relieving the inhibitory tone on α cells and enabling the secretion of
glucagon64,63.

Lastly, glucagon secretion is activated by the nervous system. The central nervous system
responds to hypoglycaemia through the autonomous nervous system, activating glucagon se­
cretion through the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems57.
In addition to the classical pathway where glucagon secretion is activated by high energy de­
mand, a protein­rich meal also stimulates its release. Gluconeogenic amino acids, such as
alanine, glutamine, and glycine, are particularly effective in promoting glucagon secretion57,66.
Amino acids stimulate insulin secretion from β cells since insulin activates amino acid trans­
porters and protein synthesis in peripheral tissue. As insulin also activates the GLUT4 glu­
cose transporter on muscle and fat tissue, glucagon secretion is activated to prevent hypogly­
caemia67. This illustrates that insulin and glucagon should not be seen as competing forces
but as cooperating to ensure a smooth flow of energy and ultimately maintain blood glucose
homeostasis66.
Additionally, glucagon can stimulate amino acid metabolism, and that amino acids, in turn, can
promote α cell proliferation, this is known as the liver­α­cell axis68,69.

Lipid and Amino Acid Metabolism
Several enzymes along the gluconeogenesis require ATP (see figure 1.5), which primarily comes
from the simultaneous breakdown of fatty acids by oxidation79,80. Ketogenesis refers to the pro­
duction of ketone bodies through breakdown of fatty acids81, and is upregulated by a number
of hormones, including glucagon. Still, the main hormone that regulates the process is insulin,
which inhibits ketogenesis82,81,83. When insulin levels are low, the inhibition of lipolysis (break­
down of lipid triglycerides) is relieved, increasing the amount of free fatty acids, which are then
uptaken in hepatocytes, where they undergo β­oxidation83,83. Oxidation of hepatic fatty acids
generates the ketone bodies β­hydroxybutyrate and acetoacetate, which can serve as an alter­
native energy source when glucose levels are low80,81.
Amino acid concentrations in the blood increase after meal consumption and during fasting
due to protein breakdown in muscle tissue, which provides substrates for gluconeogenesis84,85.
Both insulin and glucagon lower amino acid concentrations in the blood (hypoaminoacidemia),
but with different purposes84. When glucagon levels are high and the body needs energy, gluco­
neogenic amino acids are used as substrates for gluconeogenesis. In contrast, insulin promotes
protein synthesis, especially in muscles, after a meal86.
This regulation is part of a complex feedback system in the liver­α­cell axis. In this system, amino
acids stimulate glucagon secretion, and glucagon, in turn, activates hepatic amino acid uptake
and metabolism. This can occur through gluconeogenesis when blood glucose levels are low
or through ureagenesis, where excess amino acids are converted into urea for excretion62,87.
1.5.2
Activation of the Glucagon Receptor in the Kidney
In addition to the liver, the kidney is an important site of glucagon receptor expression. In brief,
the kidneys function by filtrating the blood plasma, also called glomerular filtration, excreting
waste products and excess fluids in urine. They maintain the salt­ and water balance and phys­
iological pH and regulate blood pressure and the production of red blood cells by secreting

==PAGE:19==
hormones such as renin and erythropoietin (EPO)88. The nephron is the functional part of the
kidney and is split into several segments with different functions88. The glucagon receptor is
expressed differently in the segments of the nephron and is predominantly expressed in the
thick ascending limb, distal convoluted tubule and the collecting duct, where reabsorption of
ions takes place, highlighting that glucagon has an effect on ion transport in the kidneys61,88.
Glucagon has been shown to increase the glomerular filtration rate (GFR), which is the amount
of blood plasma the kidney can filter per time, often measured in mL per minute61,88, and stim­
ulate the excretion and reabsorption of different ions, including K+, Na+, Cl­, and phosphate
(Pi)61. Furthermore, glucagon has a stimulatory effect on the excretion of urea that comes from
the catabolism of amino acids, a process that glucagon also stimulates89,87. Along with the
liver, the kidney is the only other organ that can release glucose, as they are the only tissue
with glucose­6­phosphatase. The kidney is therefore important in maintaining glucose levels
through gluconeogenesis, glucose uptake from the blood plasma, and glucose reabsorption af­
ter glomerular filtration90. However, whereas glucagon is the main driver of mobilising glucose
in the liver, it does not seem to have a great effect on glucose release in the kidney61,90, where
it instead stimulates the rate of glomerular filtration and the excretion of ions61. This is quite
interesting as it indicates that the activation of the glucagon receptor has very different physio­
logical outcomes in different tissues, which should be considered when targeting the glucagon
receptor in a therapeutic context.
1.5.3
Physiological Functions of Glucagon in Other Tissues
Although the main scientific focus has been on the effect of glucagon in the liver and kidney,
glucagon influences other tissues as well, though these are less understood91.
In the pancreas, glucagon activates the secretion of insulin from β­cells57,87 as part of the net­
work of paracrine control within the pancreas.
By activating the production of cAMP in the myocardium, glucagon has a cardiostimulatory ef­
fect, increasing the heart rate and contractility92,66.
In adipose tissue, glucagon promotes lipolysis, breaking down triacylglycerol (TAG) into free
fatty acids and glycerol, which can be used as an energy source66,93. The effect of glucagon
on lipolysis is under debate, with some studies showing no significant effect94,95,96. Glucagon
has also shown to induce thermogenesis in adipocytes, where energy is used to produce heat
in response to temperature and diet93.
Though a bit counter­intuitive when looking at the effect of glucagon on glucose homeostasis,
glucagon signalling in the brain decreases appetite and food intake97, possibly due to activation
of the vagus nerve in the liver98, though it could also be through direct activation in the brain,
as some studies report the presence of glucagon receptors in brain tissue99,25. Due to these
effects, glucagon receptor agonists are explored in vivo to activate the glucagon receptor when
glucagon secretion is impaired in diabetes and for its activating effects on energy expenditure
and appetite control in obesity97,100

The Glucagon Receptor and RAMP2
Even though it is known that RAMPs interact allosterically with many of the GPCRs, the effects
of RAMPs on these receptors are less understood110. An overview of the literature on how
RAMP2 modulates glucagon receptor signalling, desensitisation, and trafficking is outlined in
this section.
Although there are several studies on the interaction between the glucagon receptor and RAMP2,
their conclusions differ, summarised in table 1.2. The different conclusions could be due to dif­
ferences in experimental setups, such as cell types and RAMP2­receptor ratios109.
Although some have shown an interaction with all three RAMPs ([101, 111]), one observation
across several studies is that RAMP2 interacts with GCGR ([110, 109, 112, 113, 114, 115]),
which indicates that RAMP2 has a modulatory effect on the receptor.
The results presented by Weston et al.113 indicate that RAMP2 functions as a cofactor (pos­
itive allosteric modulator) of GCGR since RAMP2 increased glucagon’s potency and efficacy
by uncoupling the GCGR from Gαi, thus reducing Gi activation, resulting in stronger activation
of adenylyl cyclase. However, Cegla et al.114 showed that co­expression with RAMP2 and the
glucagon receptor resulted in reduced recruitment of β­arrestin, reduced expression of the re­
ceptor surface and reduced glucagon affinity to the receptor, all of which presented RAMP2
as a negative modulator of GCGR activity. This is backed up by Kumar et al.109, who demon­
strated that RAMP2 works as a negative allosteric modulator by inhibiting GDP release from
Gαs. They also showed that RAMP2 inhibits GRK5­mediated phosphorylation of the receptor.
As mentioned, phosphorylation of the receptor by GRKs is essential for β­arrestin recruitment.
McGlone et al.110 showed a RAMP2­mediated decrease in GCGR surface expression, both
basally and the surface expression upon agonist stimulation. McGlone et al. discuss that this
is possibly due to a RAMP2­mediated bias away from β­arrestin recruitment, which is essential
for GCGR recycling after stimulation116,117, as the glucagon receptor showed to recycle rapidly
during agonist stimulation110.

that RAMP1 and 3 inhibit Gq activation, RAMP1 and 2 induced β­arrestin1 recruitment, but no
effect was seen on β­arrestin2 recruitment.
The studies presented above emphasise the contradictory results on how RAMPs affect the
pharmacology of the glucagon receptor, highlighting that more data are needed to understand
the effects of RAMP2 on GCGR.

Aims and Objectives
This Master’s Thesis explores the role of receptor activity­modifying protein 2 (RAMP2) in modu­
lating glucagon receptor (GCGR) signalling pathways. As outlined in this introduction, glucagon
is secreted from the pancreas in response to low blood glucose levels and high amino acid
plasma concentrations. Mainly, glucagon exerts its effects in the liver, where activation of the
glucagon receptor increases hepatic glucose output by activating glycogenolysis and gluconeo­
genesis while inhibiting glycogenesis and glycolysis. Glucagon is also involved in lipid and
amino acid metabolism and has been shown to regulate resting energy expenditure. Addition­
ally, glucagon modulates glomerular filtration rate and ion excretion.
The single domain transmembrane proteins, receptor activity­modifying proteins (RAMPs) are
known for their modulation of ligand pharmacology of the class B GPCRs calcitonin and calcitonin­
like­receptors, and have recently been shown to interact with about 50 GPCRs107. They are
therefore speculated to regulate cellular trafficking and pharmacology of several GPCRs, includ­
ing the glucagon receptor.
Recent studies have shown, that RAMP2 modulates GCGR surface expression, signalling prop­
erties and trafficking, though with some varying conclusions, possibly due to differences in ex­
perimental setups. The modulation of GCGR signalling by RAMP2 is of great interest, as the
expression ratio of these two membrane proteins varies between tissues, and the expression
of RAMP2 might, therefore, be a way to calibrate how individual tissues respond to the same
stimulus of glucagon. The two tissue types where the glucagon receptor is most prominently
expressed are the liver and kidney, and the RAMP2:GCGR expression varies a lot between
these two tissue types (see table 2.1), which might correlate with the fact that glucagon ex­
erts different effects in these two tissue types. If these tissues need to respond differently to
the same circulating glucagon concentration, this could be achieved through RAMP2­mediated
modulation of GCGR activity.

Experimental Strategy
Three mechanisms in which RAMP2 is thought to affect glucagon receptor biology are tested:
cAMP accumulation, β­arrestin recruitment, and receptor internalisation. The assays used to
study these are outlined in figures 3.1, 3.2, and 3.3.
3.1
Gs/Gi Activation: cAMP Accumulation
cAMP accumulation downstream of receptor activation is quantified in a FRET­based cAMP
biosensor assay (figure 3.1). The biosensor assay relies on a HEK293 cell line that stably ex­
presses the GCGR and a cAMP biosensor. The biosensor is based on the binding of cAMP to
a fluorescent protein­tagged exchange protein directly activated by cAMP (EPAC, [119]). The
EPAC149 biosensor consists of the amino acids 149­881 of EPAC1, linked to citrine (mCitrine,
mCit) and cerulean (mCerulean, mCer) fluorescent proteins on either side. When cAMP is
not bound to EPAC, the two fluorescent proteins are in close proximity, resulting in Förster
resonance energy transfer (FRET) between the two fluorescent proteins. As the intracellular
level of cAMP increases, the EPAC part of the biosensor binds cAMP, resulting in a conforma­
tional change, increasing the distance between the two fluorescent proteins and, therefore, a
decrease in FRET, measured as the ratio between emission at 474 nm (from mCer) and emis­
sion at 524 nm (from mCit).
As FRET decreases upon cAMP accumulation, the emission at 474 nm increases, resulting in
an increase in the 474 nm/524 nm ratio, which therefore directly correlates with an increase in
cAMP concentration. In addition to Gs activation, Gi activation, which inhibits adenylyl cyclase,
and thus cAMP formation, is indirectly visualized by transfecting with a plasmid encoding the
subunit 1 (component A) of pertussis toxin (PTX), ptxA, a bacterial toxin from Bordetella pertus­
sis. PTX is an AB5 exotoxin, in which the B part, made up of subunits 2­6, is responsible for
delivering the active component ”A”, made up of subunit 1, to its target cell. Once delivered to
the cell, the A component inhibits Gαi and Gαo, and thereby their activation of their cognate re­
ceptors120. If the receptor couples to both Gi and Gs, the inhibition of Gi should result in higher
levels of cAMP accumulation as Gi can no longer inhibit adenylyl cyclase.

Cell Culture
Cells were cultured in growth media (see table 4.3) and maintained at 37°C in a 5% CO2 humid­
ified incubator. Cells were cultured in either T75 or T175 flasks and were passaged every 2­3

4.3
Transient Transfection
Cells were transiently transfected using the FuGene transfection reagent, either in a 96­well
format or directly in a T75 flask (for flow cytometry).
For transfections in 96 well plates, the FuGene transfection reagent was diluted in serum­free
medium (OptiMEM), and plasmid DNA was diluted in OptiMEM as well. The amount of DNA (in
ng) used in a transfection is noted in the results. The FuGene mix was added to the DNA dilu­
tions and let sit for 20 minutes to allow Fugene:DNA complex formation. Meanwhile, cells were
detached, counted, and diluted to obtain the correct number of cells/well. The cell suspension
was then added to the FuGene:DNA complex, and cells were seeded in 96­well plates with a
clear Poly­D­Lysine coated bottom.
For transfections in T75 flasks, the cell culture was detached, counted, and diluted to 0.3×106
cells/mL on the morning of the transfection. The cells were then placed in the incubator and
allowed to attach for around 4 hours. 22.5 µL Fugene was mixed with 375 µL OptiMEM, and al­
lowed to sit for 5 minutes. Meanwhile, a total of 7.5 ug DNA was diluted in 75 µL OptiMEM, and
the Fugene mix was then added to the DNA dilution and let sit for 20 minutes. The DNA:Fugene
mix was then added to the cell culture flask.
Transfected cells were incubated at 37°C in 5% CO2 for 48 hours, and could then be used in
assays such as the ELISA, biosensor or β­arrestin recruitment assay, or in cell imaging.

The cell surface ELISA data in figure 5.1b shows a comparable surface expression of the

was the case was explored by co­transfecting with the pertussis toxin component A (ptxA).

though expected to be due to Gi activation was still apparent when co­transfecting with ptxA.

mediated cAMP formation is at least partially due to Gi coupling to the GCGR.

Based on the performed experiments monitoring cAMP formation in the presence and absence
of RAMP2 and PTX component A (ptxA), it does not appear as if RAMP2 has an effect on cAMP
formation, which is in contrast to several previous studies. Weston et al.113 reported inhibition of
Gi and activation of Gs, while Kumar et al.109 and Cegla et al.114 reported a reduction in cAMP
production. Lastly, McGlone et al.110 reported that RAMP2 retains GCGR intracellularly, ini­
tially increasing cAMP production via Gαs activation in endosomes. However, long­term cAMP
production decreased due to enhanced lysosomal degradation, possibly from RAMP2 reducing
β­arrestin recruitment, which is required for receptor recycling.

Although these studies do have quite different conclusions on the effect of RAMP2 on glucagon­
mediated cAMP production, they all showed some effect of RAMP2 on the GCGR­mediated
formation of cAMP, which was interestingly not the case in this study.
The cAMP production is typically used as an indirect measure of Gs activation, but throughout
the signal transduction pathway, there are a number of factors that could impact the level of
cAMP. cAMP is produced by adenylyl cyclase, which is activated by Gαs and inhibited by Gαi,
both of which are activated by GCGR, as shown in figure 5.1a. Lastly, activation of the glucagon
receptor depends on ligand affinity and efficacy. RAMP2 may affect one, or several of these
mechanisms. For instance, Cegla et al. and Kumar et al. reported reduced glucagon affinity to
GCGR in the presence of RAMP2.
Receptor availability on the cell surface is another key factor. ELISA results on HEK293 EPAC149
cells showed no RAMP2­dependent changes in GCGR surface expression, but these ELISA re­
sults are from unstimulated cells, measured simultaneously with the cAMP biosensor assay.
The ELISA could have been carried out on HEK293 EPAC149 cells upon stimulation, similar to
the internalisation assay, as it is not clear from the data presented in figure 5.1b, whether the
cell surface expression changes after stimulation.
Stimulated surface expression assays (figures 5.4a and 5.6) suggest that RAMP2 retains GCGR
on the cell surface. This could in turn mean, that more receptor is able to activate the signal
transduction pathways following stimulation, as it is not internalised as effectively.
These factors highlight that while cAMP production is a valuable indicator of receptor activation,
a shift, or lack thereof, in cAMP production, could reflect underlying mechanisms. For example,
RAMP2 might lower glucagon affinity but simultaneously increase surface receptor expression,
balancing its effects. Future studies could focus on mapping the direct activation of Gαs, and
potentially Gαi and Gαq. This is discussed in further detail in section 6.2.3.

As is apparent from the antagonist dose­response curves (figure C.2, appendix C), the FITC­

5.2
β­arrestin Recruitment
Upon GPCR activation, the receptor is rapidly phosphorylated by GPCR kinases (GRKs), which
induces the recruitment of β­arrestins, resulting in receptor desensitisation. Additionally, recruit­
ment of β­arrestin often facilitates receptor internalisation, while activating β­arrestin­mediated
signalling pathways. β­arrestin recruitment is therefore crucial in understanding the regulation of
glucagon receptor signalling, and how these might be modulated by RAMP2. In this project, two
different assays were used to measure the recruitment of β­arrestin2 to the glucagon receptor,
in the absence or presence of RAMP2.
5.2.1
PathHunter β­arrestin Recruitment Assay
As for the cAMP biosensor assay, the surface expression of both the FLAG­tagged GCGR and
HA­tagged RAMP2 was monitored. The PathHunter β­arrestin recruitment system presented a
problem, since HA­RAMP2 did not show on the cell surface in the ELISA, as is shown in figure
5.2b. In the figure, the raw counts from the EnVision are presented instead of normalised data,
with a signal of around 200 in cells not transfected with HA­RAMP2 and 300 in cells that have
been transfected with HA­RAMP2. For reference, the readout from the EnVision when trans­
fecting HA­RAMP2 in native HEK293 is around 7,000­10,000.
The dose­response curves of the recruitment of β­arrestin in this assay lie very close together
(figure 5.2a), which makes sense since these two had the same expression of FLAG­GCGR
(figure 5.2b), and no HA­RAMP2 expression, and therefore respond equally to stimulation by
glucagon. It is therefore not possible to conclude, whether RAMP2 had an effect on β­arrestin
recruitment, based on these data.
If RAMP2 expression had been an issue across cell lines, it could be due to elements in the
plasmid encoding HA­RAMP2. This was not the case, as cell surface expression of HA­RAMP2
has not presented a problem in any of the other cell lines used in this project, which indicates,
that this problem is cell line­specific. If instead, the problem stemmed from transfection efficien­
cies, the surface expression of the glucagon receptor would have been impaired as well, which
it was not (figure 5.2b).
Cegla et al.114 actually used the same PathHunter system, but in Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO)
cells instead of HEK293. This cell line was tested in this project as well, with no problem in get­
ting HA­tagged RAMP2 to the cell surface. These data are not presented though, as it was de­

Internalisation of labelled ligands
As an alternative to imaging the fluorescently labelled GCGR presented in the previous ex­
periment, imaging fluorescently labelled ligands was introduced. The ligands used are either
agonists (starting with ”Ago”), antagonists (”Anta”), or TAMRA­labelled peptides, unrelated to
the glucagon receptor (”UP1”), and the potency of the ligands used are presented in table 5.2.
The internalisation of the glucagon receptor is expected to be agonist­specific. When an ago­
nist binds to the glucagon receptor, it activates the receptor, leading to a series of intracellular
events, that result in the receptor being desensitised and internalised. In contrast, when an an­
tagonist is present, it generally binds to the receptor without activating it. This binding prevents
the receptor from undergoing the events leading to internalisation, including phosphorylation
and β­arrestin recruitment. As a result, the receptor remains on the cell surface.
Internalisation of the GCGR and GLP1R in transfected HEK293 cells

surface. The agonist appears well­distributed across the membrane and appears as outlines of
the cells, especially noticeable when cells are in close proximity. This distribution indicates that
the ligands are membrane­bound, adhering to the cell’s exterior. This is not seen on all cells, as
this image is taken following a transient transfection, which means that some cells won’t have
taken up the DNA, while the expression levels in transfected cells differ depending on how much
DNA the individual cell has taken up. The second image reveals fluorescent ligands that have
been internalised within the cell. These ligands are no longer on the surface but are instead
visible as distinct dots inside the cell. This dot­like appearance signifies that the ligands have
been taken up by the cell and are located within intracellular compartments.

5.4
Summary
In contrast to prior studies investigating RAMP2­mediated modulation of the glucagon receptor
(GCGR), this project prioritised regulating GCGR surface expression to achieve comparable
receptor levels when co­expressed with proteins such as RAMP2. By standardising GCGR sur­
face expression, any observed variations in downstream processes, such as cAMP formation or
β­arrestin2 recruitment, can be attributed to factors other than receptor expression differences.
In this study, it has been shown, that when RAMP2 is co­expressed with the glucagon receptor,
it retains the receptor on the cell surface, decreasing receptor internalisation upon stimulation
from around 50% to 23% (table 5.4). Additionally, RAMP2 decreases β­arrestin2 recruitment to
the GCGR, reducing Emax by about 30% (table 5.3) and increasing the potency from 0.6 to 0.2
µM, though the latter was not statistically significant.
RAMP2 might retain the GCGR on the surface, by promoting receptor recycling. This however,
contradicts the findings by Krilov et al.117,116, who demonstrated that the GCGR is recycled in
a β­arrestin­dependent way, and the observed RAMP2­mediated decrease in β­arrestin recruit­
ment should therefore result in a decrease in recycling as well.
It is more likely, that the reduction in β­arrestin recruitment causes the surface retention of the
glucagon receptor, as the receptor is most likely internalised in a β­arrestin­dependent manner.
Given β­arrestin’s role in desensitisation, it would have made sense, if a higher level of cAMP
production was seen when β­arrestin recruitment was decreased when overexpressing RAMP2.
To back this up, previous studies, such as the one by Jones et al.124, have shown prolonged G
protein­dependent cAMP responses in GCGR­expressing HEK293 cells depleted of β­arrestin
(1 and 2). Similarly, Cegla et al.114 presented an increase in cAMP production upon deple­
tion of β­arrestin in CHO cells. Yet, no significant changes in cAMP production or Gi coupling
by RAMP2 were observed in this study. This contrasts with Weston et al.113 who found that
RAMP2 inhibited GCGR­Gi coupling in HEK293 cells, similar to PTX.
Despite these effects, cAMP production showed no RAMP2­mediated changes. Co­transfection
of the pertussis toxin subunit 1 (component A) significantly increased cAMP levels, as would be
expected from a receptor both activating Gs and Gi proteins when Gi coupling is knocked out.
The dose­response curve for glucagon­induced cAMP formation in GCGR­transfected cells
shows a decrease at higher glucagon concentrations, likely due to Gi activity. Co­transfection
with ptxA appeared to increase the cAMP production but did not eliminate the dip entirely, sug­
gesting either incomplete Gi inhibition by ptxA or additional mechanisms. The dip could po­
tentially also be due to a higher rate of internalisation at the concentrations between ­8 and
­5, which aligns with the surface expression dose­response curve (figure 5.6) showing that the
GCGR receptor starts to internalise at around ­7. This, however, does not explain why the
cAMP formation increases again at ­5, in which the GCGR is at its lowest surface expression
level in figure 5.6.
Another possible explanation is enhanced receptor desensitisation at higher doses, but since
this is mediated by β­arrestin recruitment, and there is a decrease in recruitment when RAMP2
is present, the dip should have been less apparent in the presence of RAMP2. That being said,
recruitment of β­arrestin­1 was not tested in this study, and additionally, alternative arrestin­
independent mechanisms of GPCR internalisation and desensitisation could also be at play125.

Future and Commercial Perspectives
6.1
Limitations of this study
As a general remark, many of the results presented are from three biological replicates, which
is the minimal requirement to be able to statistically determine biological variance. Within some
of the replicates, however, a high variance is seen, for example, in the NLuc­based β­arrestin
data (see SEM in table 5.3). Adding more replicates would help better account for biological
variability and ensure more reliable results and statistical significance. This could reduce the
impact of outliers and random variation, leading to more robust and reproducible findings.
While the data from the above assays clarify RAMP2’s modulation of GCGR signalling, there
are a number of limitations in the assays presented in the project. These are highlighted here.
Measuring surface expression by flow cytometry did not provide any valuable insight into the
cell surface expression patterns of the glucagon receptor. This is possibly due to the poor ef­
ficiency of the antibody used, but it might also be due to other elements of the experimental
setup. Troubleshooting to identify the reason, and setting up a flow cytometry assay that can
detect changes in glucagon receptor expression would be a complementary assay to back up
the surface expression patterns presented in the ELISA data.
Fluorescence microscopy imaging was used to examine receptor trafficking, but a limitation of
these assays is the lack of data analysis. The image analysis proved to be a bigger challenge
than first anticipated. The software on the Pico could not detect internalised receptors/ligands,
though dots were clearly visible on the images. Data analysis was also done in the ImageJ
software, and though this provided much more freedom to tweak parameters, the analysis was
not proven robust enough to be included in this thesis.
In this project, no change in cAMP levels was observed when co­expressing with RAMP2. This
outcome may result from mechanisms such as RAMP2­mediated modulation of glucagon’s po­
tency and affinity for the GCGR compensating for the RAMP2­mediated decrease in β­arrestin
recruitment. Separating these effects by looking specifically at ligand affinity and downstream
signalling effects would shed light on the RAMP2­mediated modulation of receptor signalling.
There is a large number of ligand affinity assays, such as saturation binding with a radioligand,
or FRET/BRET­based binding assays such as the one described by Kumar et al.109, using a
TAMRA­labelled ligand and an NLuc­tagged GCGR.
As outlined in section 2, the regulation of GCGR by RAMP2 is of great interest, taken together
with the differential RAMP2:GCGR ratios in different tissues. Different RAMP2:GCGR ratios
have not been tested here but could be of interest, as this might explain how the GCGR is reg­
ulated differentially across tissues.
Finally, measuring β­arrestin recruitment in the pathHunter DiscoverX system was unsuccessful,
as RAMP2 was not expressed on the cell surface after transfection. Another assay capable of
measuring β­arrestin where successfully introduced to provide insight into the β­arrestin recruit­
ment in response to RAMP2. Since changing the assay setup proved successful, the problems
with the PathHunter system were most likely cell­line specific. In both assays, a modified β­
arrestin2 is overexpressed, so if losing RAMP2 surface expression had been due to an excess
of β­arrestin2, it would have shown in both assay setups.
The findings in the pathHunter DiscoverX assay highlight the value of monitoring surface expres­

6.2
Future Perspectives
6.2.1
Endogenous GCGR­RAMP2 interactions
The research presented in this report relies on an artificial system in which both the glucagon
receptor and RAMP2 are likely heavily upregulated compared to the endogenous expression lev­
els in tissues. The cell lines HK­2, HepG2 and INS were included in the fluorescence microscopy
as they have been reported to endogenously express the glucagon receptor and though the data
analysis of the images presented a larger challenge than first anticipated, the use of cell lines
with an endogenous expression of the glucagon receptor will more accurately mirror the natural
receptor biology.
Broadening the work on these cell lines would be an interesting next step. Initially, it would
be insightful to look at both GCGR and RAMP2 expression in these cell lines. Analysing gene
expression by methods such as quantitative PCR (qPCR) or RNA sequencing or understanding
protein expression through mass spectrometry would be useful.
Once the expression of the GCGR and RAMP2 is validated, knocking down RAMP2 would be
useful to actually look at the effect of RAMP2. Turning off RAMP2 in cells expressing GCGR
endogenously could be done by small interfering RNAs (siRNAs), a common method for silenc­
ing specific genes. Knocking down RAMP2 expression by siRNA has previously been validated
by Albertin et al. [126] and [114]. Briefly, siRNA can knock down genes in a sequence­specific
way by targeting a specific mRNA for degradation since it binds the complementary mRNA, and
double­stranded mRNA is degraded as part of a natural defence mechanism against exoge­
nous genes127. Alternatively, genome editing tools such as the CRISPR (clustered regularly
interspaced short palindromic repeats)/Cas9 (CRISPR­associated protein9) system could also
be used to knock down specific genes.
The ELISA, imaging and flow cytometry assays in this study rely on recombinant GCGRs in
which tags or fluorescent proteins are linked to the receptor to detect it in the assay. Introduc­
ing a secondary staining protocol instead could though potentially make it possible to measure
surface expression of the native hGCGR. In secondary staining, a primary antibody first binds
the antigen, and a labelled secondary antibody, specific to the primary antibody, often spe­
cific to the constant region of the antibody, is then added afterwards. There are commercially
available anti­hGCGR­antibodies, such as the monoclonal mouse IgG2A hGCGR antibody from
R&D systems (MAB10296)128. Coupled with one of the many available anti­Mouse IgG2a sec­
ondary antibodies129, that is HRP­conjugated for ELISA or conjugated to a fluorophore if used
in flow cytometry or imaging. However, selectivity of the anti­hGCGR antibody for the GCGR
must be confirmed, along with its cross­reactivity to species orthologs, to ensure validity in stud­
ies using cell cultures from different species.
In ELISA, using a labelled secondary antibody is referred to as an indirect ELISA, as opposed
to the direct ELISA presented in this thesis. The indirect ELISA, along with not needing an HRP­
conjugated anti­GCGR antibody, has the advantage over the direct ELISA, that since several
secondary antibodies can bind the same primary antibody, the signal is amplified, which is a
great advantage if used for endogenous expression, as this is much lower than the transfected
HEK293 cells. The direct ELISA holds the advantage of being fast and simple and has shown
to be very useful in this thesis.
6.2.2
RAMP1 and RAMP3
This project focuses on the interactions between RAMP2 and the glucagon receptor as several
studies ([112, 113, 114, 110]) have shown that RAMP2 interacts with the glucagon receptor.
However, there are also reports of GCGR interacting with all three RAMPs ([101, 111]). As
all three RAMPs are known to modulate the biology of other class B receptors, including the
calcitonin and calcitonin gene­related peptide receptor it would be natural to expand the studies
from this project to RAMP1 and ­3 as well.

6.2.3
Activation of Gi, Gs and Gq proteins
As discussed following the cAMP biosensor results, concluding on Gs coupling based on cAMP
production should be done while still considering the other factors, that may play a role in cAMP
levels, such as Gi, and Gq activation also has an effect on cAMP levels, as both PKC and
Ca2+­levels modulate the cAMP production. Looking into assays that directly quantify G protein
coupling or activation of Gα would help understand the signalling from the glucagon receptor.
As the glucagon receptor predominantly signals through adenylyl cyclase, the activation of Gs
and Gi has been the focus of this thesis, but the glucagon receptor also couples to the Gq pro­
tein and activates PLC, resulting in an increase in intracellular Ca2+25,26.
The study by Shao et al. used a nanoluciferase complementation assay to measure Gq activa­
tion, in which nanoluciferase was split in two fragments, which were linked to the Gαq and Gγq,
respectively. When the Gq protein binds and is activated, the two subunits dissociate resulting
in reduced bioluminescence. Other FRET or BRET­based assays similar to the principles de­
scribed in this thesis could also be used, linking the receptor to an acceptor fluorophore and
a donor fluorophore to Gq to measure coupling. The ONE vector G­protein Optical (ONE­GO)
biosensor platform, developed by Janicot et al.130, similarly is a BRET­based system, in which
a construct with the Gα subunit is linked to a yellow fluorescent protein (YFP) and a detector
molecule is linked to a nanoluciferase. The detector molecule is membrane­bound and binds
activated GGTP
α
when it has dissociated from Gβγ, resulting in NanoBRET when the two are in
close proximity. A similar NanoBRET­based assay introduced by Masuho et al.131 tags the Gβγ
(Venus, similar to YFP) instead of the Gα, and once dissociated, the Gβγ does on to bind a re­
porter, linked to a NLuc, resulting in NanoBRET.
Another approach when quantifying Gq activation is by measuring Ca2+, often done by using
calcium­sensitive dyes. However, the upside of measuring on the G protein instead of on second
messengers such as cAMP or Ca2+ is that any other effects, for example, cross­talk between
transduction pathways, are ruled out.
6.2.4
GPCR kinases (GRKs)
β­arrestin recruitment to a GPCR is typically dependent on phosphorylation by GRKs.
As
RAMP2 has an effect on β­arrestin recruitment, understanding whether this effect comes from
the modulation of GRK activity would be interesting.
In the study by Kumar et al. [109], phosphorylation of the GCGR, either by GRK2 or GRK5, was
tested in the presence or absence of RAMP2 using purified proteins. Purified GCGR was incu­
bated either with, GRK5 or GRK2 in the absence or presence of RAMP2. Samples were then
run in a gel electrophoresis, and the phosphorylation state of the GCGR was determined with
a gel stain that selectively stains phosphorylated proteins. In this study, RAMP2 was shown
to inhibit phosphorylation by GRK5, but with no effect on GRK2. Confirming these effects in
cell culture rather than purified proteins would help the understanding of how RAMP2 regulates
GCGR signalling.
One way of studying receptor phosphorylation is by eliminating phosphorylation sites through
site­directed mutagenesis. Protein kinases commonly phosphorylate serine, threonine, tyro­
sine, and histidine residues. To inhibit receptor phosphorylation, or to identify phosphorylation
sites, site­directed mutagenesis can be used to mutate the sites of interest.
The phosphorylation status of a GPCR can additionally be mapped using mass spectrometry
techniques by measuring characteristic mass shifts from the addition of a phosphate group to
a peptide132,133.
Knocking down specific GRK subtypes could be another way to determine RAMP2­mediated
inhibition of GRKs. This could be done with gene editing tools such as siRNA or CRISPR/­
Cas9. If knocking down a specific GRK has similar effects on β­arrestin recruitment as RAMP2,
it indicates, that RAMP2 inhibits the phosphorylation of GCGR by the given GRK.

6.2.5
β­Arrestin­Mediated Signalling Pathways
Besides its classical functions of desensitisation and internalisation, β­arrestin is known to acti­
vate MAPK signalling cascades, independently of G­proteins. As it seems, that RAMP2 has
a profound effect on β­arrestin recruitment, it would be interesting to look more into these
signalling pathways. As the MAPK signalling cascades primarily involve the transfer of phos­
phate groups, these pathways can be monitored by assessing phosphorylation. For β­arrestin­
mediated signalling, it would be interesting to examine the phosphorylation of ERK1/2 or JNK3.
Protein phosphorylation is often detected by western blotting, using a primary antibody specific
to the phosphorylated protein. As an example, in a study by Tohgo et al.134 measuring phos­
phorylated ERK2 was done by running samples in a sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel
electrophoresis (SDS­PAGE) to separate proteins based on molecular mass. The proteins on
the gel were transferred to a membrane, which was incubated with a primary antibody, specific
for the phosphorylated ERK1/2 and then with an HRP­conjugated anti­IgG secondary antibody.
Bands were then visualised by the same HRP substrate used in this project.
Additionally, this project has focused on the recruitment of β­arrestin­2, one of the four arrestin
subtypes. Understanding the recruitment of β­arrestin­1 would be insightful. The two other ar­
restins, the visual arrestins (arrestin­1 and arrestin­4), mainly regulate photoreceptors in the
retina and are not likely to have an effect on the glucagon receptor.
6.2.6
Receptor Recycling
It is important to keep recycling rates in mind when concluding on the internalisation data, as
cell surface expression at a given point in time relies both on internalisation and recycling rates.
In the study by McGlone et al.110, the trafficking inhibitor monensin is used to block receptor
recycling. They also use mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) in which the Wiskott­Aldrich syn­
drome protein and scar homologue (WASH) complex had been knocked out. WASH is important
for vesicle recycling, and knocking it out, therefore, inhibits receptor recycling. In this study, it
was also shown, that the rather slow GCGR internalisation is actually due to rapid receptor re­
cycling.
Since the GCGR is both internalised and recycled in a β­arrestin­dependent manner, and RAMP2
inhibits β­arrestin recruitment, it is expected, that the presence of RAMP2 decreases internal­
isation rather than increases recycling rate. This could be confirmed in internalisation assays
as the ones presented in this project in the presence of monensin to inhibit recycling.
6.3
Commercial and IP perspectives
Glucagon was discovered in 1922 and has in many years been used to treat acute hypogly­
caemia, making it especially valuable for diabetes patients. With the reported effects on energy
expenditure and appetite, the interest in glucagon and its receptor has increased in recent years.
Naturally, with increased scientific interest, comes potential commercialisation and intellectual
property (IP) concerns.
The work in this project focuses on understanding a part of the complex regulatory network,
that affects the activation and signalling through the glucagon receptor, specifically how RAMP2
plays into this regulatory network. Looking at this project from a commercial perspective, the po­
tential of developing new drugs targeting the GCGR, in which the unique modulation by RAMP2
is leveraged, could provide a competitive advantage, differentiating a new drug from existing
treatments. If the knowledge on RAMP2­mediated modulation of the GCGR is combined with
the differential expression patterns of the GCGR and RAMP2 in different tissues, could further­
more increase the potential therapeutic effect.
The increase in people living with diabetes and obesity translates to a growing market for dia­
betes and obesity treatments, and innovative therapies that modulate GCGR signalling could
meet the increasing demand for more effective and safer medications.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, RAMP2­mediated regulation of glucagon receptor signalling has been studied, and
RAMP2 was found to have an effect on receptor internalisation and recruitment of β­arrestin2.
RAMP2 dampens β­arrestin recruitment, reflected in a decrease in the glucagon receptor inter­
nalisation efficiency in the presence of RAMP2. How RAMP2 inhibits β­arrestin could be the
focus of future studies by shedding light on whether RAMP2 modulates the pathways around the
recruitment of β­arrestin2, such as receptor recycling, β­arrestin­mediated activation of MAPK
signalling cascades and phosphorylation by GRKs.
The surface expression patterns presented in the ELISA­based assays indicate that RAMP2
co­localises to the cell membrane and is internalised with the GCGR, as RAMP2 surface ex­
pression was higher in the presence of GCGR, and surface expression decreased in a similar
fashion to the GCGR when co­expressed.
From the data presented in this project, it does not seem that RAMP2 has an effect on Gs
and/or Gi activation but rather has an effect on receptor trafficking, which can affect the signal
transduction pathways. This is, however based solely on cAMP accumulation measurements,
and looking into the specific Gs/Gi/Gq coupling and/or activation would be of great interest.
The reduction in β­arrestin recruitment would have aligned with a higher cAMP response, as
the arrestins mediate receptor desensitisation. Understanding how a decrease in β­arrestin re­
cruitment can align with no change in cAMP production would provide a more detailed insight
into the complex network that results in these cellular responses.
The distinct RAMP2:GCGR expression ratios observed between the liver and kidney underscore
the tissue­specific regulatory mechanisms at play. This differential expression aligns with the
findings presented in this thesis, demonstrating that RAMP2 significantly modulates β­arrestin2
recruitment and facilitates the retention of GCGR on the cell surface. These insights not only
enhance our understanding of RAMP2’s role in cellular signalling but also highlight its potential
as a therapeutic target for modulating GCGR activity in a tissue­specific manner. In the kidney,
where RAMP2 is more abundant relative to GCGR, the higher presence of RAMP2 may en­
hance its modulatory effects on GCGR, potentially altering downstream signalling pathways. In
contrast, the liver exhibits much higher GCGR expression compared to RAMP2. This might re­
sult in less modulation by RAMP2, allowing for greater surface expression of GCGR and more
robust glucagon signalling. These insights highlight the importance of RAMP2 in fine­tuning
GCGR signalling and suggest that therapeutic strategies targeting RAMP2 could be tailored to
achieve tissue­specific modulation of GCGR activity.
There might also be distinct regulatory pathways within different cell types or tissues, which
could explain the wide variety of contradictory findings presented in previous studies. The ques­
tion of how RAMP2 mediates glucagon receptor signalling is, therefore, as relevant now as ever,
and the answer might be even more complex than first anticipated.
