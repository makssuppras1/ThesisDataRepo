Abstract
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most aggressive and fatal brain tumor, with very poor
prognosis for patients. This thesis aimed to improve precision diagnostics by enhancing the
molecular subtyping of GBM. This was done by investigating a framework for projecting RNA-
seq data of one type onto another, in an attempt to bridge the gap between technology the
molecular subtypes were defined on, and technologies that would be valuable to implement
in the clinics.
The framework was tested through the R package spaceRAT, which selects
DEgenes, rank-transforms the data, and visualizes it through PCA. This proved successful for
projecting bulk RNA-seq data of one type, onto other types of bulk RNA-seq data. Projecting
single-cell data of PBMC-cells onto other single-cell data also proved successful. Challenges
remain in projecting single-cell data onto bulk data, especially regarding 10x single-cell data,
as transcripts sequenced from this technology are very sparse. Several imputation techniques
were applied to combat this sparsity, including ALRA, DrImpute and SNN-pesudobulking, but
none provided consistently useful results.

Introduction
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most aggressive and deadly type of brain cancer. Less
than 3% of patients are still alive 5 years after the initial diagnosis, and the median survival is
around 15 months [1, 2].
The use of molecular subtypes in the clinic, can help facilitate more effective treatments for the
individual patient [3].
The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) defined what would become the most commonly used
GBM molecular subtypes. These were defined on microarray RNA data in 2010 [4]. Since
then, RNA-seq has become the gold standard for high-throughput RNA-sequencing. [5].
Some technical challenges still exist for using these molecular subtypes in the clinic, based
around different data types and different sample sizes not being compatible [6].
Data from from different sequencing methods are not directly comparable. Furthermore, the
molecular subtypes have been defined on a mix of different patients gene expressions, and rely
on the underlying sample distribution. Meanwhile the sample size in clinics can often be as
small as a single patient [6].
As clinicians have adopted to these TCGA subtypes, it is of value to show them that the
subtypes they know can still be used, even with data generated from RNA-seq, and in spite of
other technical challenges.
This was recently demonstrated successfully in a framework, with a focus on breast cancer [6].
A goal for this thesis is to expand these findings to GBM.
Longitudinal genomic analysis has shown, that multiple molecular subtypes can co-exist inside
the same tumor [7]. It is of great interest to explore this tumor heterogeneity to enhance the
understanding of how the tumor microenvironment affects disease.
Single-cell analysis has recently highlighted potential mechanisms behind the differences in
primary and recurrent GBM tumors [8].
An investigation of single-cell RNA data on the
molecular subtypes also holds high potential, and was set as a sub-goal for this thesis.
With this in mind, another goal of this thesis is to further expand on the types of RNA
data being compared to also include single cell RNA-seq data. This will in part be done, by
attempting to project single-cell RNA-seq data onto bulk RNA-seq data.
Later in the thesis an extra goal was added, of investigating Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cell
(PBMC) single cell data, and projecting single-cell data onto a single-cell scaffold. While not

directly related to GBM, PBMCs are of interest, as they have previously been used in studies

1.1
Project aim
The main goal of this thesis was to demonstrate a useful framework that allows for RNA
expression data generated on one platform, to seamlessly be projected onto a scaffold of RNA
expression data generated on another.
This achieve this, multiple different aims were set:
• Demonstrate one type of bulk generated RNA-seq data being projected onto another type
of bulk generated RNA-seq data
• Investigate if single-cell RNA-seq data can accurately be projected onto a bulk RNA-seq
scaffold
• Classify how accurately these projections match up with predefined labels
• Demonstrate visualization of single-cell data in PCA space and compare to other leading
visualization techniques
• Investigate if single-cell RNA-seq data can accurately be projected onto a bulk RNA-seq
scaffold
• Showcase the above in a framework that can eventually be implemented in clinics
• A subgoal is to achieve a fast implementation that could potentially work in a web-server
setting
• Improve visualization of very large data sets in context of the above framework

Theoretical background
2.1
RNA sequencing overview
Different RNA-sequencing techniques have rapidly emerged and evolved at a rapid pace [10].
In the following sections, the techniques relevant for this thesis will be highlighted:
2.1.1
Microarray sequencing
The first relevant RNA-seq technology is microarray sequencing, tracing back to at least 1995.
The premise of a microarray is a small chip, with oligonucleotide probes of already known
target sequences, which then hybridize with a target sample. The amount of RNA present is
measured and expressed through fluorescence intensity. While revolutionary for its time, the
microarrays are limited in what they can detect, due to them being based on probes of already
known sequences. Thus they give a very limited view of the larger transcriptomic picture. [11,
12]
2.1.2
Bulk RNA sequencing
The next advent is what is commonly known as RNA-seq. In short the technology collects a
bulk RNA sample from a tissue sample, blood sample or similar, isolates the mRNA and then
converts it to cDNA using reverse transcription. Afterwards the cDNA is often amplified by
PCR, and then quantified through regular DNA-sequencing steps. [10]
2.1.3
Single Cell RNA Sequencing
In 2009, the first single cell RNA-seq technique was developed [11]. As the name suggests, it
allows to look at the expression levels of single cells, contrasting the above mentioned bulked
samples consisting of thousands of cells. The primary difference from the bulk RNA-seq tech-
niques lies in separating the bulk sample into single cells, before following a similar approach
to bulk RNA-seq, for sequencing the cells.
The cell separation can be done in different ways, but often works by encasing the single cells
in barcoded droplets, using microfluidic devices [11].
Most of the single-cell data utilized in this thesis originates from the 10x Genomics protocol,
which employs Gel Beads in Emulsion (GEM). These gel beads are embedded with barcoded
Page 3 of 57
==PAGE:10==
June 2024
DTU Master thesis
oligonucleotides and oil and are combined on microfluidic chips. Each GEM contains a single
cell, ensuring that all reads from that cell are assigned the same barcode, allowing them to be
accurately mapped back to the corresponding cell [13].
A key advantage of single-cell RNA-seq compared to bulk technologies, is the elucidation of
heterogeneous populations. Key examples where this heterogeneity is of interest are populations
of immune cells and tumors. [14]
A recent expansion of this technology is CITE-seq (Cellular Indexing of Transcriptomes and
Epitopes by Sequencing). The innovative aspect of CITE-seq is found in the process of se-
quencing single-cell transcripts.
Here, antibodies with oligo-tags are added to the mix, to
detect surface proteins of the respective cells, allowing for immunophenotyping in harmony
with SC-RNA-seq. [15].
2.1.4
Smart-SEQ
A recent variant of RNA-seq is called Smart-SEQ (Switching Mechanism At the end of the
5´-end of the RNA Transcript sequencing). The protocol involves a template-switching oligo
that can introduce a few extra nucleotides at the 3’ end of the cDNA, and do what’s called
template switching. This allows for an efficient amplification of full-length transcripts in the
single cells, and in turn sequencing the full length transcripts [16].
Several challenges arise when working with single cell expression. These will be elucidated in
the following section.
2.2
Working with single cell data
After the isolated single cells have been sequenced, they are often output as 3 files; a count
matrix, a feature file and a barcodes file. These files can be combined in a seurat object in R.
Next this seurat object has to be preprocessed before analysis can truly begin.
For preprocessing, quality control is important for outlining cells on several parameters. Cells
with a high percentage of mitochondrial RNA (usually >10%) are assumed to be dying cells
due to the higher durability of the mitochondrial membranes in the lytic cell cycle, and are
filtered out.
Cells with relatively few or many unique features are usually also filtered out as low quality
cells will often have very few genes, and cell doublets - droplets that contain two cells instead
of one, will often have an extraordinary high amount of genes [17].

Another issue is, that most single-cell data is very sparse with up to 90% zeros, which is a lot
compared to the 10-40% zeros generally seen in bulk data [18, 19]. Some of these zeros are
thought to be driven by technical limitations, likely occurring during the reverse transcription
and amplification steps of the sequencing process.
This term has recently been coined as
dropouts [18]. This imposes a huge challenge when you, for example, try to rank-transform
your data. An obvious solution in a project like this, that aims to be applied in a large platform
to a wide variety of datasets, is to do imputation, with the immediate goal of recovering the
true non-zeros in the data. [20]
2.2.1
Imputation
Two imputation approaches designed for single cell RNA-seq will be covered here: ALRA and
DrImpute.
ALRA: ALRA (Adaptively thresholded Low-Rank Approximation) is an imputation approach
based on the principle of imputing the dropouts and keeping all of the true biological zeroes.
In short, the ALRA algorithm works in 3 steps:
1. Low-rank approximation via SVD(Singular Value Decomposition). This step essentially
tries to estimate every zero into a non-zero value (either positive or negative).
2. Thresholding. In this step each estimated value is thresholded against the most negative
value of said gene. This helps determine the true zeroes.
3. Rescaling the original matrix. By rescaling the original matrix based on the thresholding
results, ALRA claims to restore almost all true biological zeroes in the imputed matrix.
The rescaling is done with the purpose of reflecting the true underlying biology, after the
new non-zero values are added.
[19]
DrImpute: In a mRNA expression setting, where genes are mostly expressed in bursts as they
are needed [21], the zero-preserving approach intuitively seems great. There are however cases
where this is not optimal, as will be explored in this thesis. An imputation method that does
not preserve biological zeroes to the same degree as ALRA, is DrImpute. The broad strokes of
the algorithm are described below:

1. Preprocessing steps involving filtering of low-expression genes, normalizing counts and
log-transforming expression values to stabilize variance.
2. Cells are clustered at different k values (cluster sizes)
3. Pearson’s correlation coefficient is used to measure robustness between the imputations
across different k sizes.
[22]
2.2.2
KNN
K-nearest neighbours is an unsupervised machine learning method. The basic principle behind
the method for a given sample, looks at the k nearest neighbours in an n-dimensional space, and
assigns a label to the sample, equal to the most abundant label among the k nearest neighbours
[23]. The same principle can easily be extended to regression cases, by predicting the mean
value of the k-nearest neighbours, or be extended to an imputation case, where the value of a
cell could e.g. imputed as the sum of the cell and its nearest neighbour’s values.
2.2.3
Batch correction
When working with RNA-seq, it is usually necessary to perform a batch correction step, to
account for technical variance and harmonize your data. An issue for most batch correction
methods is, that they require a certain number of samples to properly model the underlying
distribution. To account for this, rank-transformation has been shown as a possible way of
harmonizing data, even with few samples, which makes it a valid option for settings like this,
that could eventually be applied in the clinic with small sample sizes [6].
2.2.4
Cell annotation
For single cell data especially, cell annotation is important as it allows comparison across
conditions.
This applies, e.g., when trying to distinguish different immune cells from each
other, or when you try to distinguish tumor cells from non-tumor cells [24].
Some useful characteristics of cancer cells, include them consistently being aneuploid, as well
as having relatively frequent chromosomal rearrangements [25].

==PAGE:13==

For GBM specifically, loss of chromosome 7, and gain of chromosome 10 are often seen as
common abberations.
This indicates that using copy number alterations to identify GBM
tumor cells could be valid [26].
This has been utilized in the R package CopyKAT (copy number karyotyping of aneuploid
tumors), to identify tumor cells in single cell data.
In broad terms, copyKAT works by:
• First stabilizing the variance and outliers through ordering the genes by their genetic
positions, and applying log-Freeman-Tukey transformation, which is a transformation
that seeks to make (poisson) data similar to a normal distribution.
• Next, a small subset of diploid cells is defined using integrative clustering and Gaussian
Mixture Models (GMM), which is basically a probabilistic model that assumes all the
input data points are generated from a mixture of several Gaussian distributions. This is
used to establish a copy number baseline.
• MCMC (Markov Chain Monte Carlo), which is a method for sampling from probability
distributions, is then performed on gene expression values, and segments are merged
using Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) testing, which is a non-parametric test that compares
the distributions of two datasets.
• Finally, cells are classified as either aneuploid (inferred tumor cells) or diploid (inferred
non-tumor cells) using cell enrichment as well as GMM distribution tests, which assess
the likelihood of data points belonging to a Gaussian distribution.
[24].
2.3
spaceRAT
The main work of the thesis has been conducted using the in-development R package spac-
eRAT. In the following sections the background theory behind the spaceRAT framework will
be reviewed.
2.3.1
PCA
PCA is a main component of the spaceRAT framework.
PCA is used as a dimensionality
reduction method, to represent high-dimensional data (such as omics data) in a lower-dimension
Page 7 of 57
==PAGE:14==
June 2024
DTU Master thesis
space. This aims to create so-called principle components (PC’s) that convey the largest possible
variance in the data in as few dimensions as possible [23].
The process can be divided into the following steps:
1. Standardize the data by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation.
This will reduce bias in data where different variables have different data ranges.
2. Use SVD (Singular Value Decomposition) to compute a covariance matrix, eigenvectors
and eigenvalues from the input data.
3. The eigenvectors from the SVD can be inferred as the directions of the different PCs.
The eigenvalues represent how much variation the corresponding PC accounts for.
[23]
2.3.2
UMAP
For single cell data, the most common visualization approaches are nonlinear. Often those
called tSNE or UMAP are used. UMAP is generally regarded as the best practice in most cases
[27]. UMAP is also a dimensionality reduction technique, slightly in the same vein as PCA.
The key steps in building a UMAP are;
• Build a kNN graph on the high dimensional data.
• Use a stochastic gradient to iteratively minimize the difference between the high-dimensional
graph and a lower dimensional representation, by adjusting positions in the lower dimen-
sional representation.
• Finally the representation is optimized, by using attractive forces to push neighbouring

2.3.3
DTC
Another slightly similar approach to classification is the Distance-to-centroid classifier (DTC).
A DTC classifier works in 2 steps.

1. Define centroids based on labeled data. For each label, the value of all samples are aver-
aged, coordinate by coordinate, and this average over all coordinates define the centroid
of that label.
2. A new unlabeled sample is measured against the different centroids. Assign a class to the
sample, equal to the label of the centroid with the smallest distance to the sample.

2.4
GBM
GBM is a cancer with particularly poor prognosis for all patients, as it has a median survival
of ≈12 months, with less than 3% of patients being alive 5 years after diagnosis [31] [32].
This is made worse by the fact, that even with resection of the tumor, most patients experience
disease progression within the first year [33]. Recurrence at some point is currently seen as
almost inevitable. Most recurrences occur in the same region as the initial tumor, although a
small percentage also recur in distant parts of the brain [34]. Thus, any and all improvements
in treatment are of high value.
2.4.1
Molecular subtypes
Molecular subtypes in cancer is a term used to divide certain cancers into smaller groups, based
on certain biomarkers or genetic changes present [35].
In 2010, 4 different molecular subtypes of GBM were characterized.
They were originally
defined by integrating 3 different microarray expression platform values using factor analysis,
as the authors claimed this lead to more robust values than any single platform would give.
Hiearchical clustering was then used to cluster the 4 different molecular subtypes [4].
The molecular subtypes have shown to have a large instability between primary and recurrent
tumors, even in the same patient [36]. Furthermore it has been shown, that a shift in subtype
occurs in roughly half of all recurrent tumors, often towards the Mesenchymal subtype [37].
Along with the TCGA-defined molecular subtypes, different classification schemes have also
been proposed through time. In 2013, methylation status was shown to be a predictive marker
for molecular subtypes [38].
In 2016, WHO made their classification, based on IDH mutation status [39].
Page 9 of 57
==PAGE:16==
June 2024
DTU Master thesis
In 2017, 3 of the 4 subtypes defined by TCGA; Mesenchymal, Classical and Proneural were
confirmed by another study, while they hypothesized that the Neural subtype was primarily
defined by presence of large amounts of normal tissue in the bulk samples [37].
In 2019, another approach defined subtypes based on prognostic scores [40].
Though many different classification schemes have been devised throughout time, none can
necessarily be seen as a definitive truth. For this thesis, the TCGA subtypes from [4] will
be applied and investigated, as they are the widely accepted for determining actual molecular
characteristics of GBM [41].
2.5
PBMC
Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMC) is a term for blood cells containing a single
round nucleus. These include lymphocytes, monocytes and dendritic cells, with lymphocytes
often making up a majority of the cell population. PBMCs are usually isolated by removing
non-PBMC blood components through density gradient centrifugation [42].
PBMC have a
wide variety of use cases, ranging from elucidating inflammatory mechanisms of autoimmune
diseases, to studying interactions between the immune system and the gut microbiome [9].
PBMCs also have some specific uses in the context of single cell RNA-seq. Examples include
studies that found higher expression of disease-causing genes in Kawasaki disease to be found
in monocytes specifically [43], and studies that shows the use of microarray datasets to help
classify different subsets of T helper cells [9].

Methods
In this section, the methodology of the R package spaceRAT, which is used in all 5 scenarios,
will be covered first. Afterwards the methodology of each sceanario will be covered.
4.1
spaceRAT workflow
As mentioned in section 2.3, the R package spaceRAT was used to carry out the main work in
this thesis. The overall methodology of spaceRAT is as follows:
First comes building a scaffold (NB: it is possible to use a prebuilt scaffold if one fits your
purpose).
The input data for building a scaffold are:
1. An expresion matrix, containing either counts or microarray expression levels.
2. A phenotype table for the samples in the expression matrix. This could for example
contain information about the molecular subtypes of samples, if working with GBM cancer
patients.
Under the hood of spaceRAT, what actually happens when building a scaffold is, that it starts by
preprocessing the input data. This includes filtering out samples with no phenotype information
attatched, and filtering out samples with total gene expression below a selected threshold.
The next step involves selecting differentially expressed genes (DEgenes) according to selected
criteria. These criteria can include genes that are differentially expressed with a certain log-
fold change (LFC) in one phenotype group compared to the others, differentially expressed
with probability below a certain p value threshold, or n genes could simple be selected from
all available genes, sorted by either highest LFC, lowest p-value, highest average expression or
absolute t-statistic.
The count/gene expression matrix is then reduced to these DEgenes, and rank-transformed, so
the most highly expressed gene in a cell/sample gets the largest numeric rank, and the least
expressed gene gets lowest numeric rank (usually =1, unless there is a tie).
The rank-transformed matrix is then reduced through PCA. It is also possible to save a UMAP-
visualization to the scaffold, if desired. This PCA visualization is what is reffered to as the
scaffold.
Page 14 of 57
==PAGE:21==
June 2024
DTU Master thesis
After the scaffold is built, comes the projection of new samples onto the scaffold. New samples
are loaded and preprocessed in concordance with the scaffold preprocessing.
A check is done for missing genes in the new data, which is either handled by adding & setting
the the expression of those genes = 0 in the new data, or by removing the genes from the
scaffold data and recalculating the scaffold with the remaining genes. This is presented as
subset_intersection = FALSE or TRUE respectively in spaceRAT.
The new data is then rank-transformed, and projected onto the scaffold PCA using the stats::predict
package. spaceRAT uses the R package stats::prcomp for PCA, and the R package Limma for
finding and selecting the DEgenes.
4.2
Bulk-bulk; scenarios 1.A, 1.B
For overall methodology in this section closely matches the one applied in Pedersen et al. (2024)
[6].
The reference TCGA data was loaded as an affymetrix object and RMA corrected using the affy
R package (ver 1.78.2). For cases when multiple probes mapped to the same gene, the dataset
was reduced to the probes with the largest median expression across samples, and probes were
then translated to genes.
A scaffold was built using the resulting expression matrix, along with the supplied phenotypes
for each sample, using the buildScaffold function of spaceRAT.
The scaffold built here, used default settings, which entail a p-value cutoff of 0.05 and LFC-
threshold=2. This resulted in 92 DEgenes being used to build the scaffold.
The use case data for scenario 1.A was loaded as a .tsv file. It was wrangled into a data frame
that fits the spaceRAT format, with genes in the rows and samples in the columns.
It was then projected onto the scaffold, using the projectSample function of spaceRAT, with
subset_intersection set to TRUE, meaning that missing genes were removed from scaffold, and
the scaffold recalculated.
The use case data for scenario 1.B was similarly loaded as a .tsv file. The phenotype information
was extracted from the individual GEO sample pages. The data was them split into individual
patient subsamples, and projected in the same manner as the use case data from scenario 1.A.

4.2.1
Classification
Outside of the visual scaffold projections, classification was also performed, to quantify how
well the framework performed.
Classification tables were made using a simple distance to centroid (DTC) classifier. This clas-
sifier was based on output data from the spaceRAT scaffold object, to illustrate the possibility
of implementing this in the spaceRAT package. From the previously constructed spaceRAT
scaffold, the different PCs were extracted. From these, the different centroids were calculated
for each subtype according to the predefined labels (ie. the molecular subtypes). Afterwards
new samples were put through the simple DTC classifier, that calculates which centroid a new
sample is closest to, and assigning the sample to that class through a ’winner-takes-all’ strategy.
The results of this classification for scenario 1.A can be seen in results section 5.1.1
Heatmaps of the centroid distances for each sample were also generated using the R package
ComplexHeatmap(V. 2.16.0), to give a more visual presentation of how close each sample
were to each centroid. One could choose multiple ways of presenting the distance metric in the
heatmap, the most obvious ones being either as the raw distances themselves, or as a parameter
representing the distance from a sample to one centroid, compared to the cumulative distance
from the sample to all centroids. This latter approach might be beneficial if one works with
different scaffolds, that have different magnitude of scales for their distances. This approach
has been implemented in the heatmap in figure 3 as such:
Distanceprob = −
Distance to this centroid
Sum of distances to all centroids
Scaling the distances between 0-1 allows for a consistent way of looking at how close samples
are to the centroids, no matter the scale of the input data. Taking the negative value of this,
allows the largest number (ie. the smallest negative number) to be the ’best’ number, which
makes the heatmap more intuitive to look at.

4.3.1
Imputation
Three different methods were tested for imputing the 10x single cell data. The first method is
DrImpute. Intuitively it seemed great for this purpose, as the algorithm is not zero-preserving,
meaning that it tries to impute every zero, compared to other algorithms that try to keep true
biological zeros as zeros. Imputing every zero means that fewer genes in the scaffold get the
same rank, which might help differentiate new samples.
DrImpute V. 1.0 in R was used for this purpose, and was run directly on the count matrix from
the Seurat objects created in the previous section. Results of this imputation are seen in figure
5 B.
The second imputation method used was ALRA. A striking difference between ALRA and
DrImpute is, that ALRA is zero-preserving. While this could be of concern in this application
where the goal is to draastically improve the amount of zeroes, ALRA performed well in a
benchmark[49], which lead to it being tested.
ALRA version 0.0.0.900 in R (Version 1.0 on GitHub) was used. ALRA requires the raw count
matrix to first be log transformed. A value for the parameter k also needs to be choosen.
For most purposes (including this thesis) the value was automatically chosen, using a built-in
function in the ALRA R package. Results of this are seen in figure 5 C.
The third and final imputation approach was a variation of KNN (K Nearest Neighbours),
utilizing shared nearest neighbours (SNN) information from the Seurat objects. This approach
was thought out and implemented as a custom script made by co-supervisor Søren Helweg
Dam. The main difference from conventional KNN is, that the neighbours choosen are not
only based on a hard defined k-value. While a k-value is still set, determining if another cell
is seen as a neighbour or not by the cell being pseudobulked, is also based on how many other
neighbours they share with the cell in question. The supplied script was applied directly to the
preprocessed Seurat objects. Results of this are seen in figure 5 D.

4.3.2
Tumor cell annotation
As the tumor microenvironment consists of a heterogenous mix of tumor and non-tumor cells,
it is of interest to know which cells are which. In this project, it was not possible to find
annotated 10x GBM data, which only further increased the significance of annotating which
cells were tumor cells. This was done using the R package CopyKAT. It should be noted that
CopyKAT runs very slowly, taking 1.55 hours for a 150 mb expression matrix. Thus, while it
was viable to run for this project, it would generally not be applicable in a web-server setting
or similar, where you want to be able to run your samples within short timeframes.
The output of copykat is a list of which cells are predicted to be aneuploid and diploid, which
you can use as a phenotype in spaceRAT, or use to seperate your sample into tumor and

6.1
Implications of main results
6.1.1
Bulk -> bulk; scenario 1.A
As it was the case in this paper regarding the cse of breast cancer [6], projecting bulk RNA-seq
GBM data, onto a bulk microarray GBM scaffold proved decently successful. In scenario 1.A
The different samples spread out nicely throughout the scaffold. The classification table showed
84,8% true classifications with DTC on the reference data, and 62,4% true classifications on the
use case data. I would argue this is somewhat acceptable, when you also take into consideration
that these subtypes are defined quite recently and are thus not necessarily a ground truth, but
more of a ’best guess at the moment’ by other researchers. The mismatches could also be
caused by the nature of the samples only being bulk samples. Here tumor purity might play
into the classifications, another reason why it is insightful to look further into samples at single
cell level.
6.1.2
Delineating Primary vs. recurrent tumors; scenario 1.B
As a small case study, and demonstration of possible application of the spaceRAT framework,
the tendencies of molecular subtypes in primary vs. recurrent tumors were investigated. From
figure 4 of individual patients, a tendency of subtype shifting can be observed.
The same
tendency is further demonstrated in the full figure 17 in appendix II with all patients together,
where an overall tendency of recurrent samples gravitating towards the mesenchymal molecular
subtype can be seen. This stands in contrast to the primary samples, which mostly gravitate
towards the neural and proneural space. This is quite exemplified in samples 341 and 139 from
figure 4, where only samples from the same patient at primary vs. recurrence is shown, instead
of showing all at once. This fits with the observation, that roughly half of all patients see a
subtype switch at GBM recurrence [37]
This further marks a clear use case for utilizing spaceRAT, as a similar visualization in UMAP
or tSNE would not have shown these tendencies (exemplified in figure 18 in appendix II) This
visual clarity is of essence for this project, and is what makes the whole case for doing PCA
instead of the classical visualization methods for especially single cells; UMAP and tSNE, as
PCA maintains linearity.
In the overall goal of implementing this into the clinics, the ability to actually see where samples
lie, and how close they are to a certain subtype compared to others, is of much more importance,

than using a UMAP which applies artificial forces to spread things apart.
6.1.3
Single cell -> bulk; scenario 2.A
Regarding the imputation steps applied to the 10x data; when looking at ALRA vs. DrImpute
they both have their issues. While ALRA’s zero-preservation might cause some issues with true

6.1.4
SC -> bulk issues; scenario 2.B
As mentioned in section 4.4 the scaffold for projection of Smart-SEQ data had to be redefined.
First of all PCA-scaling had to be turned off, as the projections would otherwise be completely
useless no matter the case. The reason for this was thought to be, that the Smart-SEQ data
was already preprocessed to a certain degree, including some scaling which would essentially
scale the data twice. The amount of DEgenes also had to be increased from the 92 used in the
default scaffold. A total of 513 DEgenes were selected in the scaffold for this use case. This
was necessary, as the overlap of genes in the Smart-SEQ use case data and the TCGA reference
data was too small otherwise to produce useful results.
It was considered working with reduced scaffold in smart-SEQ, similar to how it was done in
scenario 4. This approach was discontinued, as the scaffold data set is relatively small, and it
was deemed more valuable to define a robust scaffold on more samples, so more gene expression
per subtype overall could be captured.
While the subtyping of a patient is best done one at a time, looking at all 5 Smart-SEQ samples
together, they have a quite large spread between patients. This could indicate some sort of
batch effect between the samples, that spaceRAT is not able to catch, as there is very little
overlap between the samples.
6.1.5
Single cell -> single cell; scenario 4
Overall, the projection of single cells onto a scaffold of single cells was a success. The first point
of discussion on this matter is the reduction of the cells used to build the scaffold. While it
could be argued that figure 20 in appendix III is a more accurate representation of the truth,
the matter of fact is, that it is also too cluttered, with too much overlap between different cell
types for anyone to reasonable use it for visualization. The choice of reducing the scaffold to
the n=3540 samples closest to their respective centroid in only the first 2 PCs could also be
disputed.
In this reduced scaffold the different T cell classes lie very closely together in PC space. While
this could be slightly problematic, if one had a case where they really needed to distinguish
the specific T cells, the truth is likely that those cells are really just that similar, especially
compared to some of the other cell types in the scaffold. Also, the expression levels of the
individual cells could potentially change within short timeframes, as RNA-seq essentially is
a snapshot of what an individuals expression is at the time of the sampling. The heatmap
in figure 11 further supports similarities between them, as the centroid distances are almost
identical for all the samples, to the three centroids.

A curious observation from figures 8 & 9 is the pDC being closer to B cells, than to other DC
cells in PC space, which seems unintuitive. It has however been suggested, that pDC cells and
B cells at least share a potential common progenitor [50], which makes their proximity in PC

As new labeled PBMC samples were not found in the timeframe of this project, no proper
validation with new samples verification was able to be performed.
6.2
Imbalanced Data
Regarding imbalanced classes and reducing the datasets for building scaffolds, multiple pros
and cons exist.
First of all there is the argument of whether or not it is ok to reduce the scaffold data at
all, as it could be interpreted as manipulating the true truth to get a scaffold that better fits
our idea of the truth. Considering that the labels of individual cells are made by humans,
and not necessarily representing a truth, it does seem fair to consider some of the labels as
misclassifications, which points towards it being acceptable to reduce the scaffold data to a
degree.
Second of all, there is the case of finding the right size for a scaffold. When looking at a large
reference data set, as in the case of the PBMC data, 153654 cells are too many, to represent on a
2D PCA plot. However, an ≈83% reduction might also be too much, to still claim it represents
the original data. An advantage of reduced scaffolds in this case is, that they empirically appear
to better separate the classes, than the larger scaffolds do.
Finally, an advantage of larger data sets is that the whole premise of spaceRAT and this thesis
is based on rank-transformation of data. Having more data means having more ranks, and in
turn having more basis for capturing variance in your scaffold and following projections.
6.3
Classification, labels & trust
An obvious weakness of using the distance to centroid method for classification is, that it is not
very stable for classes that have high variance within themselves. As an example, the centroid
in essence just represents a mean of the different samples from that class. The samples used to
build one centroid could all be very far away from the centroid itself, and may in turn actually
be closer to another centroids. The same could easily go for new samples being classified against
the centroids.

6.3.1
A look at biases & potentially overwrangling the data
An aspect of contention for this thesis in general, is the amount of biases introduced throughout
the proces.
Looking at the 10x single cell RNAseq from scenario 2.A as a specific example. First each
sample is sampled from different patients, at different times, potentially by different doctors.
The samples may have been left out for different time amounts before being frozen down.
The amount of cells loaded for sequencing may differ per sample. The following processing is
described as per standard protocol, but without further information on specifics - herein also
lies an uncertainty. Then further biases are introduced in this thesis through preprocessing
in seurat and imputing the zeros, before rank transforming it all and throwing it into PCA
space. All of this is not to say, that we cannot trust any results from this, but that all these
potential sources of error are important to be aware of, when evaluating the results - essentially
every step described above affects the results to some degree, positively or negatively. The ideal
scenario would of course have been being in control of the entire process from sampling, through
sequencing and every step of the data processing. This would also have been completely out of
scope for project of this size, of course.
6.3.2
Single cell annotation, copykat
When looking at single cell RNA-seq samples, the question of which cells to look at, in context
of molecular subtypes, suddenly becomes relevant. Quite a lot of time was spent on isolating
what was suspected to be single tumor cells using the R package copyKAT. The tumor cells
stand out as the cells of interest, as these are also the ones you are interested in treating with a
specialized treatment dependant on their molecular subtype. The flipside is, that the molecular
subtypes were defined on bulk microarrays, and not single cells. Thus, the non-tumor cells in
the tumor microenvioronment cannot categorically be excluded as playing an equally large part
in defining the overall molecular subtype of a tumor. It could be argued that it might not make
sense at all, to try and project single distinguished cells, onto a heterogenous bulk scaffold. The
projections of SC data onto the bulk scaffolds do also seem to indicate, that a tumor does not
only contain cells of one molecular subtype, which supports the similar claims made by Xiao
et al. (2022) [7]. Instead a likely answer is, that each tumor is actually a heterogenous mix of
cells with different subtypes. In time, the composition of molecular subtypes of single cells in
tumors could potentially prove to be an as equally valuable diagnostic tool as the current bulk
molecular subtypes are.

6.4
Subset intersection
A final aspect to consider in this framework is how to handle genes in the scaffold, that are not
present in the projected data (handled in spaceRAT with the parameter subset_intersection).
Two approaches are currently used, each with their pros and cons. The first approach is keeping
the genes in the scaffold, and keeping the scaffold as is, and in turn setting the expression of
these genes = 0 in the projected data.
The second approach is removing all the scaffold genes missing in the projection-data from the
scaffold, and then recalculating the scaffold only on the overlapping genes before projection.
The first approach has a key advantage in the scaffold staying the exact same, no matter
the data projected in. This is especially useful in a clinical setting, where one could imagine
that clinicians who have to look at the scaffold every day, would prefer it to stay stable for
consistency.
The stability would also make it easier for clinicians and researchers alike to
communicate across studies and cases, which would be more challenging with having differing
scaffolds. The disadvantage of the first approach is, that if you have a lot of missing genes,
there is a high probability that most of your samples/cells will just lump together in a corner
somewhere, since rank-transforming a lot of zeros will just give a lot of cells with the same
rank, which could drive a large part of where they end in the PC space.
The main disadvantage of the second approach is, that the scaffold is not consistent. Outside
of the case with clinicians mentioned above, you also run the risk of having the scaffold change
into something nonsensical. A clear example would be two molecular subtypes, that in the
full scaffold were clearly distinguishable, suddenly being right on top of each other in this new
scaffold, if for example some of the key DEgenes differentiating them were to be removed, as
seen in figure 13. The main advantage of this second approach is, that projections will most of
the time spread out nicely into the same space as the scaffold, as one would generally expect it
to.
Overall, it is impractical to name a best approach among the two, as the best answer more
than anything depends on the use case.
6.5
Further Research
For future perspectives, its important to further validate the whole pipeline. This especially
goes for projecting single cell data onto bulk scaffolds, as this approach contains the most
possibilities for errors. An ideal validation here, would be in collaboration with clinics, and
working all the way from raw data through to the final projections and classifications.

==PAGE:44==

Another aspect is whether the TCGA classification scheme from [4] is the proper one to use,
or whether better approaches exist. Ideally a bit further into the future, a more robust classi-
fication scheme would exist for each technology, that was also defined on said technology - ie.
one for both microarrays, bulk RNA-seq and single-cell RNA-seq. Until then, spaceRAT looks
like a possible bet at bridging the gap between the different technologies.
An interesting aspect would also be looking into completely different technologies.
A con-
tender could be CITE-SEQ, as investigating protein expression together with transcriptome
expression could provide valuable insights into especially the heterogeneity of the tumor mi-
croenvioronment, and could help illucidate the part the immune system plays in cancer response
to treatments across molecular subtypes.
It would also be interesting to expand the scaffolds in general to include completely different
classes. For example expanding the GBM scaffold with a 5th class samples, extracted from
healthy brain tissue. This could be used eg. to check for contaminations in a sample, or used
for research purposes, to gain further insights into the role non-tumor cells play in the tumor
microenvironment.

7
Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis was to demonstrate a useful framework for projection of different
RNA-seq data types onto each other.
For bulk RNA-seq data being projected onto different bulk RNA-seq this proved to be de-
cently succesful, and a demonstration of how this could be used to answer biological questions
about GBM was also demonstrated. The success rate was quantified with distance to centroid
classification, and further supported with heatmaps of those distances.
It was also demonstrated that projecting single-cell RNA-seq data onto different single-cell
RNA-seq data visually looked adequate, although it was not possible to quantify how well
it classified new labeled validation-samples. Furthermore it was possible to achieve a robust
visualization of this, in spite of it stemming from a very large dataset, through scaffold reduction
and reduced alpha values.
Projecting single-cell data onto a bulk scaffold proved a large challenge. The sparsity especially
in 10x data proved a problem, and although some imputation methods alleviated this issue,
none of the tested methods achieved speeds or succes rates that were satisfactory.
Single-
cell data from the Smart-SEQ platform demonstrated better projections without the need of
imputation, but the cells did not reliably end up in the same subtype they were originally
labeled with.
All of this was achieved through, or based on the R package spaceRAT, and except for then
10x single-cell projection, all of it would be implementable in a web-server framework based on
spaceRAT.

subtyping using R package spaceRAT

Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is one of the most aggressive cancers, with a survival rate < 
3%, 5 years after diagnosis. [https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00401-005-0991-
y?utm_source=getftr&utm_medium=getftr&utm_campaign=getftr_pilot] Thus, any 
improvement in the treatment and diagnostics are of great importance. One way to improve 
treatments is by using molecular subtypes of GBM, to allow for more specific treatment.  
 
In 2010 Verhaak et. al. characterized 4 molecular subtypes of GBM. The 4 subtypes were 
proposed as “Classical”, “Mesenchymal”, “Proneural” and “Neural”, and were derived using 
clustering analysis on microarray data.  
[https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1535610809004322?via%3Dihub].  
 
A challenge with this subtyping scheme is that modern sequencing is largely moving towards 
RNA-seq and single-cell sequencing, which is not directly comparable to the microarray data 
the subtyping scheme is based on. 
[https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10920087/]  
 
The main issue here is that these different data types are not directly comparable. There is 
both the issue of arrays being based on probe intensities - typically represented as a decimal 
number in the range of 1-20, while the RNA-seq technologies are raw integer counts. 
Furthermore the probability distribution in these is completely different, and is based on 
multiple samples in the array-data, whilst samples used for diagnosis in clinics will often be 
sampled one at a time in praxis.   
 
To account for all this we suggest the workflow from 
[https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10920087/] , which is implemented in the 
currently in-development R package spaceRAT.  
 
spaceRAT functions in two parts. The first consists of building scaffolds on the data in which 
the molecular subtypes were defined. The second part consists of projecting and visualizing 
new data onto these scaffolds.  
 
The first step in building the scaffolds is finding the most differentially expressed genes of 
each subtype, compared to the other subtypes, based on criteria that may vary from use 
case to use case. The criteria could be certain p-value thresholds, certain log-fold changes,

or a hard set limit of top differentially expressed genes, sorted by the above criteria, average 
expression or absolute T statistic.  
 
When these genes are selected, the expression of each sample for these genes is rank 
transformed, so the most expressed gene gets the highest rank, and the least expressed 
gene the lowest rank.  
The rank-transformation allows us to mostly get past the issues mentioned above, as this 
gets the data into the same scale/plane/space/room, no matter its source.  
 
This rank-transformed data is then put through a PCA and visualized in a 2D plot.  
 
The second part, the projection of new samples, happens by reducing the scaffold to the 
same genes as the scaffold in the new data, rank-transforming these. And then integrating 
them into the scaffold through a distance-to-centroid approach, where they are projected into 
the PCA according to their distances to each of the different centroids for the 4 molecular 
subtypes.  
 
Here we demonstrate 2 use cases for this.  
In Figure 1A RNA-seq data has been projected into the microarray-based scaffold, which the 
4 molecular subtypes are based on.  
In figure 1B smart-SEQ single cell RNA data has been projected into the same microarray 
scaffold as in figure 1A.

Projecting RNA-seq data into a microarray scaffold will allow clinicians to easier gain an 
overview of the molecular disease status, through both visualisation, as well as confidence 
based on how close to the different centroids the sample is.
