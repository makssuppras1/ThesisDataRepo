Abstract      
Poly(ethylene) terephthalate (PET) is the most abundant polyester plastic with a wide range of applications in 
textile and packaging, among others. Despite the widespread number of applications, PET and plastics have 
risen a pressing environmental issue. Most of the plastic waste ends up in landfills or accumulates in the 
environment. This accumulation creates a great ecological damage and contamination of the soil, groundwater, 
and oceans. Therefore, plastic recycling has become of great importance to tackle the plastic waste issue. 
The main process used for the recycling of PET consists of mechanical recycling. However, this method is not 
a true closed-loop process, because PET losses mechanical and physical properties after every round of 
recycling. Recently, biological degradation of PET has opened a clean alternative to plastic recycling. 
Biological degradation through enzymes was able to depolymerize the PET polymer into monomers than could 
afterwards be used to produce de novo PET (Tournier et al., 2020). To date several enzymes have been 
discovered with hydrolytic activity for PET, but limited productivity.  
Tournier et al, (2020) were able to engineer the PET hydrolase Leaf-branch compost cutinase (LCC) through 
protein engineering and obtained a variant of LCC capable of 90% depolymerization of PET over 10 hours. 
The highly effective variant LCC(ICCG) is currently being tested in a pilot plant for industrial recycling of PET.  
The variant of LCC included four mutations that increased the activity and thermal stability of the enzyme and 
created the highly productive PET hydrolase (Tournier et al., 2020).  
Currently, new enzymes are being discovered with similar or higher activity on PET than LCC. In this thesis 
two PET hydrolases are presented: PHL7 (Sonnendecker et al., 2021) and BhrPETase (Xi et al., 2021) with 
higher activity than LCC. Moreover, the optimization of PHL7 using protein engineering is explored, to improve 
its hydrolytic activity towards PET. Due to the structural similarities between LCC and PHL7 it was 
hypothesized that the mutations used by Tournier et al. to optimize LCC, could have a similar effect on PHL7 
(Tournier et al., 2020). Therefore, the corresponding mutations in PHL7 were realized and their effect on the 
thermal stability and specific activity evaluated.  
Twelve variants of PHL7 were cloned in Bacillus subtilis, expressed, and purified. The activity of the variants 
was measured by characterization assays on PET and the thermal stability was measured by nano differential 
scanning fluorimetry. The mutation L209A was demonstrated to increase the activity of PHL7 at 50°C and 
70°C. The thermal stability of PHL7 was increased by the variants containing the mutations 
Q94G/R204C/S250C (GCC), but not to the levels expected by the results of Tournier et al., in LCC.  
For industrial conditions (70°C, > 2 hours reaction times) the thermal stability of the enzyme plays a key role 
in the PET depolymerization levels. Even though the melting temperature of PHL7 was higher than 70°C (Tm 
= 80,2°C) the enzyme lost the activity after 3 hours of incubation at 70°C, limiting the PET depolymerization 
levels. The mutations GCC allowed for a longer time of reaction before the enzyme was inactivated by 
temperature. However, the activity was reduced with the GCC mutations. The combination variant GCCA 
restored the activity of the wild type PHL7. However, for longer reaction times the depolymerization levels of 
the variant GCCA did not exceed the ones of PHL7 wild type. This result was most likely due to the low increase 
in thermostability by GCCA.  
This thesis has shown a way to improve the activity of the highly effective PET hydrolase PHL7. Moreover, 
thermostability was presented as a key factor for successful PET depolymerization. Future work could be 
focused on further improving the thermostability of PHL7(L209A) to increase the depolymerization levels of 
this promising PET hydrolase.

1.1 
Polyethylene terephthalate (PET) 
Polyethylene terephthalate, commonly abbreviated as PET or PETE, is a thermoplastic polymer resin that 
belongs to the polyester family of polymers. This polymer consists of repeating units of terephthalic acid (TPA) 
and ethylene glycol (EG) obtained from a polycondensation reaction of the monomers by esterification, and is 
represented in Figure 1 (Carr, Clarke, & Dobson, 2020).   
 
Figure 1. Molecular structure of PET. Polycondensation reaction of ethylene glycol and terephthalic acid gives PET with 
water as a byproduct (Adapted from (Carr et al., 2020)). 
The PET polymer is a mixture of high-molecular weight molecules. While they do not form true crystalline 
solids, which are typically composed of identical species arranged in a precise, repeating manner, crystalline 
regions can form in the polymer when portions of the chains align in parallel allowing intermolecular hydrogen 
bonds. Therefore, PET polymers contain crystalline regions and amorphous regions (Ouellette & Rawn, 2018). 
The crystallinity level of PET affects the properties of the polymer as well as its enzymatic degradation (Carr 
et al., 2020). 
PET is a highly flexible, colorless, and semi-crystalline resin in its natural state. It is light weighted, durable and 
has good gas and moisture barrier properties. Moreover, it has a broad range of use temperature (-60°C to 
130°C), is transparent to microwave radiations and approved by the FDA as safe for contact with foods and 
beverages, among several other properties (Omnexus, 2022). 
Because of its mechanical, thermal, and chemical resistance properties, the use of PET is widespread for a 
large range of applications. PET is used for plastic bottles, food packaging, packaging trays, etc. Moreover, it 
is widely used in textile industry in the form of polyester fabrics and due to its electrical insulation properties is 
an effective polymer used in the electrics and electronics industry among other applications (Omnexus, 2022).

Plastic waste and current recycling techniques 
Despite the widespread number of applications of PET and plastics, these polymers have risen a pressing 
environmental issue due to various reasons. Plastics used in the packaging industry are normally disposed 
after a single use and contribute to 50% of the plastic waste generated annually (Lee & Liew, 2021). Besides, 
plastics can remain in the environment for hundreds of years because of their difficult biodegradation (Samak 
et al., 2020).  
The major environmental problem comes with the end-of-life management of the plastics. In the study by Geyer 
et al. (2017), they reported the first global analysis of all mass-produced plastics ever manufactured. They 
estimated that by 2015, approximately 79% of all the plastic waste globally generated was accumulated in 
landfills and natural environment (Geyer, Jambeck, & Law, 2017). This accumulation creates a great ecological 
damage because the plastic waste can be degraded to microplastics and contaminate the soil, groundwater, 
and oceans. Microplastics can harm the marine life and the birds and can accumulate in the food chain and 
impact negatively human health. In addition, there is a damage in clean-up burden, tourism and infrastructure 
(Lee & Liew, 2021).    
Banning the use of plastics is not a real alternative. It is important to keep in mind that plastic is a unique 
material with many benefits: it’s cheap, versatile, lightweight, and resistant. This makes it a valuable material 
for many functions: it plays a critical role in maintaining food quality, safety and reducing food waste. The trade-
offs between plastics and substitutes (or complete bans) are therefore complex and could create negative 
impacts on the environment (Ritchie & Roser, 2022).  
For these reasons, plastic recycling has become of high importance to tackle the plastic waste issue. The main 
process used for the recycling of PET consists of mechanical recycling. In general, the PET waste is 
reprocessed into granules by conventional extrusion after being separated from its contaminants (Soong, J. 
Sobkowicz, & Xie, 2022). However, this method is not a true closed-loop process, because the PET losses 
mechanical and physical properties after every round of recycling. Therefore, the recycled PET can only be 
used in other forms with less stringent demands on physical robustness than the starting PET, or the recycled 
material has to be blended with virgin resins to enhance its physical properties (Lee & Liew, 2021).  
The ideal recycling technology would be a ‘closed-loop’ one, where the plastic could be 100% recycled without 
loss of quality in infinite recycling cycles. These technologies would depolymerize the plastic polymer into 
monomers that can be then transformed into virgin plastics. Such technologies would comply with the concept 
of sustainable circular economy (Thiyagarajan, Maaskant-Reilink, Ewing, Julsing, & van Haveren, 2021).  
Recently, biological degradation of PET has opened a clean alternative to plastic recycling. Biological 
degradation through enzymes was able to depolymerize the PET polymer into monomers than can afterwards 
be used to produce de novo PET (Tournier et al. (2020)). Other emerging recycling technologies are also 
under development in the chemical recycling sectors. However, biological recycling through enzymatic 
degradation has the advantage of using milder reaction conditions (temperature below 100°C, atmospheric 
pressure, neutral to mildly alkaline pH) and avoiding the use of toxic chemicals (Lee & Liew, 2021). Another 
advantage of enzymatic recycling is that enzymes may display inherent selectivity towards a single polymer, 
potentially enabling the selective depolymerization of a single polymer from a mixture while leaving the other 
components largely intact, a property that may be challenging for chemical depolymerization (Thiyagarajan et 
al., 2021).  
Therefore, biological degradation presents a clean, environmentally friendly process for the recycling of PET  
(Thiyagarajan et al., 2021). However, this is a very new field and further research, and development is needed 
to improve the biological recycling technology.

1.2 
Enzymes used for PET degradation 
Synthetic polymers such as PET were once considered resistant to microbial degradation (Carr et al., 2020). 
However, microorganisms have evolved to produce a variety of hydrolytic enzymes that allow them to process 
the polymers available in nature, and use these polymers as a carbon source, as for example plant cutin (Carr 
et al., 2020). These enzymes are able to break down the ester bonds found in natural polymers and some of 
these enzymes are also able to hydrolase synthetic polymers like PET. 
In 1977, Tokiwa & Suzuki (1977), reported some lipases that were able to attack ester bonds in some aliphatic 
polymers (Tokiwa & Suzuki, 1977). Later in 2005, Muller et al., (2005) described a hydrolase from the 
bacterium Thermobifida fusca able to depolymerize PET (Muller, Schrader, Profe, Dresler, & Deckwer, 2005). 
Since then, numerous PET hydrolyzing enzymes have been identified, as described in numerous reviews (Carr 
et al., 2020; F. Kawai, 2021; Taniguchi et al., 2019). Moreover, in 2016 by screening natural microbial 
communities exposed to PET in the environment the first bacterium Ideonella sakaiensis 201-F6 able to use 
PET as its major energy and carbon source was reported (Yoshida et al., 2016). 
Enzymatic degradation of PET depolymerizes the polymer into oligomers and monomers. PET hydrolases 
generally catalyze only endo-type hydrolysis by cleaving the internal ester bonds of PET (Fusako Kawai, 
Kawabata, & Oda, 2019). This hydrolysis gives rise to different insoluble and soluble degradation products. 
The polymer can be degraded into long chains that are insoluble, and into soluble oligomers and monomers 
(Baath, Borch, & Westh, 2020). The final soluble monomers are bis(hydroxyethyl)terephthalate (BHET), 
mono(hydroxyethyl)terephthalate (MHET), terephthalic acid (TPA) and ethylene glycol (EG) which can be 
observed in Figure 2. TPA is regarded as the final product (Fusako Kawai et al., 2019).  
 
Figure 2. Final degradation products from the hydrolysis of PET polymer with PET hydrolases. The PET polymer is 
represented by TPA (green hexagons) and EG (lines) and the enzymes are represented in purple. The enzymes hydrolase 
the polymer into fragments by their endo-activity. The degradation products can be insoluble (long fragments), soluble PET 
oligomers or the constituent soluble monomers BHET, MHET, TPA and EG. The chemical structure of the soluble 
monomeric products is represented in the figure. (Adapted from (Baath, Borch, Jensen, Brask, & Westh, 2021; Joo et al., 
2018)).

Enzymes with PET hydrolase activity are typically in the class of carboxylic ester hydrolases (EC number 3.1.1) 
such as cutinases, lipases and esterases among others (Soong et al., 2022). These enzymes are typically 
characterized by a catalytic triad in their active site consisting of serine, histidine and aspartate amino acids 
which mainly belongs to the α/β superfamily. The PET hydrolases have been found in several bacteria as in 
Thermobifida fusca, Thermomonospora curvata and Ideonella sakaiensis and in fungi as in Fusarium solani, 
Humicola isolens and Aspergillus oryzae (Soong et al., 2022).  
Various factors influence the enzymatic degradation of PET: two key ones are the PET crystallinity and the 
temperature of the reaction. As stated in Section 1.1, PET is composed of crystalline and amorphous regions, 
and depending on the intended use, PET can have different degrees of crystallinity. Numerous reports indicate 
that PET hydrolases enzymes preferentially degrade the regions of PET that are amorphous, thus the degree 
of crystallinity of the polymer plays a key role in the degradation rates (Carr et al., 2020).  
It has been reported that for an efficient enzymatic hydrolysis of PET it is beneficial to use a reaction 
temperature higher than the glass transition temperature (Tg) of PET. At temperatures above the Tg, the chain 
mobility of PET is increased, resulting in better accessibility to the polymer by the enzymes (Fusako Kawai et 
al., 2019). In aqueous reactions the Tg values of PET range from 60°C to 65°C, thus higher temperatures tend 
to increases the hydrolysis rate of the enzymes (Soong et al., 2022). Therefore, thermostable PET hydrolases 
that are active at or above the Tg of PET are desirable for optimal PET degradation rates.  
1.2.1 
Industrial enzymatic recycling of PET 
To date numerous PET-hydrolyzing enzymes have been reported and characterized, and new investments 
into scaling the enzyme PET recycling technology are made (Lee & Liew, 2021).  
The pioneer in this field is the French start-up Carbios, which has already opened a demonstration plant to 
test the recycling of PET using enzymes (Crownhart, 2022). The plant is able to hold two metric tons of PET 
and break it down to TPA and EG in 10 to 16 hours. This plant will prepare the validation requirements for the 
construction and implementation of a first industrial unit, which may happen in 2025, and will be built near a 
plastic manufacturer in Europe or the US (Crownhart, 2022). Moreover, the company has been able to obtain 
virgin PET from the recycled process and produce PET bottles with it, working towards the concept of circular 
economy (Carbios, 2022). Figure 3 shows a representation of the circular economy concept for the enzymatic 
recycling of PET. 
 
Figure 3. Enzyme enabled solution for circular recycling of plastic. The PET plastic waste (e.g., PET bottle) can be break 
down by PET hydrolases into its monomers (TPA, MHET, BHET), which can then be reused in the production of new 
plastic products. Copyright: University of Portsmouth. 
The enzyme behind Carbios was described by Tournier et al, (2020) and is considered as the state-of-the-art 
in enzymatic recycling of PET and the only enzyme available in the market for full-scale industrial application 
(Carbios, 2022).

The enzyme used by Carbios is a genetic variant of the Leaf-branch compost cutinase (LCC) found in a 
metagenomic analysis (Sulaiman, You, Kanaya, Koga, & Kanaya, 2014). The variant of LCC 
F243I/D238C/S283C/Y127G or LCC(ICCG) achieved a conversion of 90% of PET after 10 hours of reaction 
at 72°C, with a productivity of 16,7 grams of TPA l-1 h-1 (Tournier et al., 2020). The enzyme was tested at pilot 
scale conditions on post-consumer waste PET (PcW-PET) pretreated with extrusion and micronization to 
increase amorphous regions.   
Even though LCC (ICCG) is the only enzyme available in the market to industrially recycle PET new potential 
enzymes are being discovered that are noteworthy alternatives to LCC. Recently, a Chinese group cloned a 
thermostable PET hydrolase called BhrPETase. The sequence of this hydrolase was identified in a 
metagenomic study on thermophiles and annotated in the NCBI database as a PET hydrolase from the 
bacterium HR29 (BhrPETase, NCBI accession No. GBD22443)  (Xi et al., 2021). BhrPETase has 94% 
sequence identity with LCC, and when compared by Xi et al. (2021) with LCC for activity against PET powder 
under the same reaction conditions the activity of BhrPETase was slightly better than LCC. 
Moreover, a group from Leipzig (Germany) discovered a novel metagenomic polyester hydrolase from a plant 
compost called Polyester Hydrolase Leipzig 7 or PHL7 (Sonnendecker et al., 2021). This enzyme hydrolyzed 
amorphous PET films with high efficiency, reaching 90% depolymerization at 70°C after 16 hours. Moreover, 
the authors registered a higher performance of PHL7 compared to LCC (Sonnendecker et al., 2021).

1.3.2 
Effect of temperature on reaction rates of enzymes 
Temperature is a relevant parameter that influences rates of enzymatic reactions. As with many chemical 
reactions, the rate of enzyme-catalyzed reactions increases as the temperature rises, because molecules 
move faster and collide more vigorously, greatly increasing the likelihood of bond cleavages and 
rearrangements.  
However, at the same time the temperature influences the stability of the enzyme, therefore even though the 
reaction rate initially increases with the temperature, this increase is only up to a certain point until the enzyme 
denatures. Once the enzyme is denatured, it usually cannot be repaired. As each enzyme is different in its 
structure and bonds between amino acids and peptides, the denaturation is specific for each enzyme 
(Enzymes, 2022). Figure 5 shows a representation of the influence of temperature on the reaction rates of 
enzymes.

until an optimum temperature, after that the enzyme is denaturing and the rate decreases ("Effect of Temperature on 
Enzymatic Reactions," 2022)

Aims and objectives 
In this thesis, the optimization of PHL7 using protein engineering is explored, to improve its hydrolytic activity 
towards PET. Moreover, BhrPETase was expressed and purified to use as a reference. LCC was also used 
as comparison but was not expressed and purified in this thesis. 
Due to the structural similarities between LCC and PHL7 it was hypothesized that the mutations used by 
Tournier et al. to optimize LCC, could have a similar effect on PHL7 (Tournier et al., 2020). Therefore, the 
corresponding mutations in PHL7 were realized and their effect on the thermal stability and specific activity 
evaluated.  
In short, the aim of this thesis was to improve the activity and thermal stability of PHL7 (Sonnendecker et al., 
2021) through protein engineering by implementing the mutations from the variant LCC(ICCG) from Tournier 
et al. (2020). The research objectives of this project are listed below. 
a) Compare the structure of LCC and PHL7 and find the corresponding amino acids in PHL7 to mimic 
the mutations of LCC(ICCG). Design the variants for PHL7 including three different mutations in 
position leucine 209 of PHL7: L209A, L209I and L209V. 
b) Cloning and expression of PHL7 and BhrPETase in Bacillus subtilis including a signal peptide for 
protein secretion and a C-terminal hexa-histidine tag to facilitate purification. Side-directed 
mutagenesis of PHL7 to create above-mentioned variants. Purification of all enzymes using IMAC 
chromatography.    
c) Enzymatic characterization of PHL7 wild type and comparison of its activity and thermal stability with 
LCC and BhrPETase.  
d) Enzymatic characterization of PHL7 variants and comparison with PHL7 wild type. Study if the variants 
have an increased thermal stability or/and activity compared to the wild type.  
e) Study if the variants of PHL7 can overcome the PET depolymerization levels of PHL7 wild type at the 
desired reaction temperature of 70°C and at longer reaction times (>1 hour).

3.1 
Cloning of PHL7 wild type and BhrPETase  
The PET hydrolases PHL7 and BhrPETase were cloned in the pel cloning site Bacillus subtilis AN1152 
(Property of Novozymes). The genes were expressed using the AmyL signal peptide for protein secretion. A 
hexa-histidine tag was added at the C-terminal of the enzyme for purification using IMAC chromatography.

Synthetic genes 
The genes encoding for PHL7 (Sonnendecker et al., 2021) and BhrPETase (Xi et al., 2021) were commercially

3’ flanking regions of ~30bp were added to the ends of the gene with homology to the cloning site of Bacillus 
subtilis (See Figure 7). The sequences for the two constructs can be found under Appendices 8.2. A 
representation of the synthetic gene’s components is shown in Figure 7.

3.3 
Expression and purification  
After successful cloning of the PHL7 variants and the wild type PET hydrolases PHL7 and BhrPETase, all 
enzymes were expressed and purified.  
3.3.1 
Protein expression  
An inoculation needle was introduced in the single colony stored culture and used to inoculate a culture tube, 
containing 2 mL of TB-Glycerol-6CAM medium, which was grown overnight at 220 rpm and 37°C. 2 mL of the 
starter culture was used to re-inoculate 100 mL of 10R or Cal18 medium in a 500 mL baffled shake flask. The 
flask was incubated in an orbital shaker at 220 rpm and 30°C or 37°C and the cells were grown for 4 days. 
Cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 15000g for 10 min and supernatants were analyzed by SDS-PAGE 
(Section 3.3.3).

3.3.3 
Sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) 
Protein separation was done using NuPAGE 10%, Bis-Tris, 1.0 mm, Midi Protein Gel (Invitrogen); a precasted 
10% polyacrylamide gel using the Bis-Tris buffer. The fermentation broth was centrifugated at 15.000 x g for 
10 min, and the supernatant was used for protein separation. Protein samples were prepared for SDS-PAGE 
by denaturation heating at 95°C for 2 min in the presence of a sample buffer 1x diluted containing 2% of LDS 
(NuPAGE LDS Sample Buffer) including the dithiothreitol (DTT) reducing agent.  
Sample loading volume was 5 µL in every lane. MES SDS running buffer 1x was used for running the NuPAGE 
Bis-Tris gel. The gel was run until de dye in the sample buffer reached the bottom of the gel. The Novex Sharp 
Pre-Stained Protein Standard (Invitrogen) was used for molecular size evaluation. Staining was performed 
using ReadyBlue protein gel stain (Sigma-Aldrich) containing colloidal Coomassie for at least 1 hour, followed 
by de-staining with water.

3.4.2 
Endpoint activity assay with various substrate loads of PET 
The substrate used for the activity measurements of the enzymes was semicrystalline PET powder purchased 
from Goodfellow Co (UK) with a crystallinity > 40% according to the supplier (Goodfellow).  
A suspension-based plate reader assay (Baath et al., 2020) was used for the initial rate measurements of the 
PET hydrolases. The enzymatic reactions were performed in 250 µL of final volume in low binding microplates’ 
wells, with 0,1 µM enzyme, PET loads from 0 to 20 g/L and phosphate buffer 50 mM, pH 8. 
The plates were sealed and incubated at the selected temperature (50 or 70°C) in a thermomixer at 1100 RPM. 
The incubation time was 1 hour for all the reactions. These contact times were selected to get a good signal 
without exceeding the linear part of the progress curve for PHL7 and LCC (Supplementary Figure 6). The time 
of 1 hour was assumed to be linear for the other enzyme reactions from the data of PHL7 and LCC.  
The reactions were stopped by spinning down in a bench centrifuge for 3 min at full speed (2000 x g) and 100 
µL of the supernatant containing the liberated soluble products was transferred to a UV microtiter plate. The 
product formation was detected by spectrophotometric measurements in a plate reader at 240 nm.  
Enzymatic product formation was quantified against standard curves of MHET (Supplementary Figure 7) and 
the product was reported as MHET equivalents (MHETeq). This standardization has been reported to provide 
a reasonable measure of the overall activity (Baath et al., 2020; Rennison, Winther, & Varrone, 2021). 
Moreover, the product profile of the enzymatic reaction was studied by RP-HPLC, and all hydrolysis products 
showed an MHET concentration notably higher than BHET and TPA after 1 hour of reaction, and a similar 
percentage around 70-80% of MHET (Supplementary Figure 8). Therefore, it was considered a good 
approximation to use MHET equivalents as the product of enzymatic hydrolysis.  
The reaction rates after 1 hour corresponded to the concentration of soluble products (µM), divided by the 
enzyme concentration (µM) and the time of reaction (s).

4.1 
Design of variants of Polyester Hydrolase 7 
As explained in Section 1.2.1, the mutations introduced in LCC by Tournier et al. (2020) improved the activity 
and thermal stability of the PET hydrolase. Therefore, it was hypothesized that the same mutations introduced 
in the corresponding positions for PHL7 would result in similar increase in activity and stability for this enzyme. 
To this end, the corresponding amino acids in PHL7 for the mutations of LCC were studied and described in 
this section. 
Firstly, the active site of PHL7 was studied in PyMOL (Schrodinger, 2015) using the crystal structure of PHL7 
(PDB:7NEI) (Sonnendecker et al. (2021)). Sonnendecker et al. (2021) studied the substrate binding site of 
PHL7 using molecular docking experiments with 1,2-ethylene-mono-terephthalate-mono(2-hydroxyethyl 
terephthalate) (EMT) (Sonnendecker et al., 2021). The amino acids determined by Sonnendecker et al. (2021) 
to contribute to the binding of substrate EMT are L92, T62, F61, S130 and L2091. The catalytic triad of the 
enzyme is Ser130-Asp176-His208 1 (Sonnendecker et al., 2021). The substrate binding site of PHL7 is 
represented in Figure 13.

4.1.1 
Corresponding amino acids in PHL7 for the mutations in LCC(ICCG) 
Tournier et al. (2020) generated 209 variants using 11 amino acid positions identified as having possible 
interaction with the PET substrate. From the variants they chose the ones with increased activity. The mutation 
F243I had an increase of specific activity of 20% compared to the wild type LCC. 
Moreover, as stated in Section 1.2, a reaction temperature of 70°C achieves higher depolymerization levels of 
PET due to better access to polymer chains. Therefore, it is relevant to create thermostable variants that can 
act at this high temperature without being denatured. Tournier et al. (2020) replaced a putative calcium-binding 
site of LCC with a disulfide bond in order to increase thermal stability and avoid the use of calcium. The addition 
of the disulfide bridge D238C/S238C increased the melting temperature of the enzyme by 9,5°C but decreased 
the activity of the enzyme. The combination of the mutations F243I/D238C/S283C in LCC increased the activity 
levels 20% and increased the thermostability by 6,2°C compared to the wild type. Finally, the addition of 
mutation Y127G resulted in the variant F243I/D238C/S238C/Y127G with similar activity to the wild type and 
9°C increase in melting temperature (Tournier et al., 2020).

4.3.1

The activity of the enzymes was measured using a plate-reader assay (Baath et al., 2020) following the

Menten equation (Equation 2) because at higher substrate loads the enzymatic rate decreases which causes

a poor fit of the equation (Figure 18). This behavior has also been observed in Ideonella sakaiensis PETase 
(IsPETase), upon higher substrate loads at 40°C in crystalline PET powder (Erickson et al., 2022).  
A similar behavior has been observed for inverse Michaelis Menten kinetics where the reaction rate decreases 
upon higher enzyme dosage (Mukai, Yamada, & Doi, 1993) (Hiraishi et al., 2010). The reason behind this 
phenomenon has been attributed to ‘overcrowding’ of enzymes to the substrate surface and as an effect of 
rapid reduction of accessible surface area (Baath et al., 2021). It can be hypothesized that the same happens 
at high substrate loads: the enzymes are ‘overcrowded’ by substrate and not able to react with more substrate 
due to the interfacial nature of the reaction. However, this is a conjecture and further studies are needed to 
interpret this behavior. 
Because of the unusual kinetic pattern of the enzymes, the typical Michaelis Menten parameters of Vmax and 
KM were not used for their comparison and characterization. Instead, the maximum rate (s-1), and substrate at 
half-maximum rate (g/L) were used. It is important to note that the meaning of these parameters is not the 
same as Vmax and KM, but they will be used as parameters for the current comparative purposes of this project. 
• 
Maximum rate (s-1): maximum rate found for the assay conditions studied. The value is extracted 
from the rates determined at different substrate loads, by choosing the highest value.  
• 
Substrate at half-maximum rate (g/L): substrate load found at half of the maximum rate for the assay 
conditions studied. This value is approximated from the plots of rate vs substrate load.

These results show that PHL7 is a highly active enzyme whose enzymatic activity at 50°C and 70°C after 1 
hour of incubation with PET, surpasses the activity of LCC and BhrPETase, which are currently considered 
the ‘state-of-art’ for PET enzyme bio-recycling.  
The maximum rate found for PHL7 can be compared to the kcat (s-1) values found by Bååth et al. (2021) for the 
PET hydrolases from the fungus Humicola insolens (HiC), the bacterium Thermobifida fusca (TfC), and a 
catalytically improved variant of Ideonella sakaiensis PETase (IsP) (Baath et al., 2021).  
The kcat values (s-1) for the enzymes were found at 50°C using semicrystalline PET as substrate (Baath et al., 
2021). The maximum rate for PHL7 at 50°C (1,5 s-1) is 17-fold higher than the kcat for HiC (0,088 s-1), 100-fold 
higher than the kcat for TfC (0,015 s-1) and 2-fold higher than the kcat for IsP (0,69 s-1) (Baath et al., 2021). The 
maximum rate for BhrPETase at 50°C (1,1 s-1) is 12-fold higher than the kcat for HiC, 73-fold higher than the 
kcat for TfC and 1,6-fold higher than the kcat for IsP (Baath et al., 2021) 
Overall, these results show that the activity of PHL7 greatly surpasses the activity of other characterized PET 
hydrolases, including the enzyme LCC. The LCC variant LCC(ICCG) is the first enzyme used industrially in 
enzymatic PET bio-recycling. Therefore, PHL7 presents a promising alternative to LCC for PET enzymatic 
recycling.  
The enzyme BhrPETase also shows encouraging activity results that surpasses the activity of LCC. The scope 
of this thesis focuses on PHL7, but BhrPETase could also be considered a remarkable alternative for PET 
enzymatic recycling.

At 50°C (Figure 19) PHL7 has the highest production of 3,9 mM MHETeq (~8% depolymerized PET) and the 
reaction continues after 48 hours of incubation. BhrPETase and LCC have similar production levels after 48 
hours of 2,6 mM and 2,1 mM of MHETeq (~5% depolymerized PET) and the reaction continues after 48 hours

At 70°C (Figure 19) the reaction with PHL7 did not give rise to a further increase in soluble products after 2 
hours of incubation with a production of 2,8 mM MHETeq (~5% depolymerized PET). The reaction of LCC

with BhrPETase continues after 48 hours of incubation with the highest production level of 7,52 mM MHETeq

The melting temperature of BhrPETase reported by Xi et al., (2021) is 101,5 °C. This highly thermostable 
enzyme is most likely not being denatured at 70°C which causes that its reaction continues for more than 48 
hours. For LCC, the authors of Tournier et al. (2020) reported that the reaction did not give rise to more soluble 
products after 3 days at 65°C and suggested that the thermostability of the enzyme was most likely the limiting 
factor (Tournier et al., 2020). It can be suggested the same in the case of LCC at 70°C in Figure 19. 
The initial rate of PHL7 at 70°C is higher than LCC and BhrPETase until the production reaches a plateau after 
2 hours of reaction (Figure 19). The enzyme is most likely inactivated at 70°C as observed by Tournier et al. 
(2020) for LCC. Another possibility could be that the enzyme is still active but is attached unproductively to the 
substrate and not able to continue the hydrolysis of PET. However, this is less likely because at 50°C the 
production levels of PHL7 are higher than at 70°C (Figure 19).  
From the results, it is suggested that an increase in the thermal stability (melting temperature) of PHL7 could 
achieve higher depolymerization levels of PET at 70°C. The initial rate of PHL7 at 70°C is the highest from all 
the enzymes. Therefore, it can be speculated that if the enzyme is not inactivated by temperature the 
production levels could match or even surpass the ones from BhrPETase at 70°C.  
4.3.3 
Effect of temperature 
From the results obtained in Section 4.3.2, it is pertinent to study the effect of temperature on the enzymatic 
reactions with PHL7, LCC and BhrPETase.  
Here, the effect of temperature on the enzymes was studied by two approaches: thermal stability by nanoDSF 
and thermal inactivation kinetics. The two approaches are described below.  
• 
Thermal stability by nanoDSF. NanoDSF is a method to analyze the conformational stability of 
proteins, which is described by its melting temperature (°C), which is the temperature where half of 
the protein is unfolded. The enzymes were heated from 25°C to 95°C at 2°C per min to find their Tm 
and the melting temperatures of the enzymes were used to compare their thermal stability.  
• 
Thermal inactivation kinetics: The enzymes were studied at the reaction temperature of 70°C (glass 
transition temperature of PET) for different incubation times. The residual activity (%) versus incubation

amino acid in the position L209 was alanine (L209A), followed by valine (L209V) and isoleucine (L209I). At

However, at 70°C the mutation to alanine (L209A) is the only one that successfully improved the enzyme’s 
activity. Alanine was also introduced in the corresponding position F209 of Thermobifida fusca (Furukawa et 
al., 2019) and allowed for a 2,9-fold increase of the enzymatic activity compared to the wild type. Furukawa et 
al. (2019) suggested that bulky amino acids like phenylalanine might disrupt the binding to PET (Furukawa et 
al., 2019). Therefore, smaller side chains as in alanine would be beneficial for the binding of the substrate.  
PHL7 wild type already has a comparably small amino acid (leucine) in the 209 position, which may be the 
reason why the improvement in activity is not as noticeable as when changing from a bigger amino acid like 
phenylalanine as for TfCut2(F209A) and LCC(F243I) (Furukawa et al., 2019; Tournier et al., 2020). However, 
the change to alanine was able to improve the activity 1,2-fold at 50°C and 70°C. A possible explanation for 
this improvement of enzymatic activity could be the small size of alanine compared to leucine, which may 
facilitate the binding of the substrate. 
The L209I position decreased the enzymatic activity of the enzyme. Moreover, the lower substrate at half-
maximum rate of L209I was due to its sigmoidal behavior, which is more pronounced at 70°C than at 50°C.

The plot of rate vs substrate for the combined variants follows a sigmoidal behavior (Figure 27) instead of the

from Trichoderma reesei (Roussel et al., 2014; Sluyterman & Wijdenes, 1972). 
The progress curve of the variant ACC also displayed a sigmoidal pattern with an initial lag phase 
(Supplementary Figure 20). A possible explanation for this lag-phase is that the first steps of depolymerization 
only create insoluble products. In the endpoint activity assay, only the soluble products are measured. 
Therefore, if at the beginning of the reaction mainly insoluble products are formatted, they will not be measured 
and accounted in the start of the progress curve.  
Moreover, all the variants containing the disulfide bridge (Section 4.4.1 and Figure 27) follow the sigmoidal 
behavior which suggests an implication of the cysteine bridge on this effect. A hypothesis could be that the 
cysteine bridge increases the rigidity of the enzymes, which complicates the binding of the substrate and 
hinders the initial hydrolysis to the polymeric substrate.

Conclusions 
The aim of this thesis was to improve the activity and thermal stability of PHL7 (Sonnendecker et al., 2021) 
through protein engineering by implementing the mutations from the variant LCC(ICCG) from Tournier et al. 
(2020). This section focuses on the learnings and observations obtained from the thesis in order to answer the 
aim of the project.  
PHL7 wild type was shown to surpass the activity levels of other characterized PET hydrolases, including LCC 
and BhrPETase (Section 4.3). The variant LCC(ICCG) used industrially for the enzymatic recycling of PET, 
had the same specific activity as wild-type LCC, but a higher thermal stability (Tournier et al., 2020). In this 
thesis, PHL7 presents a promising alternative to LCC for PET enzymatic recycling, because of its higher activity 
levels compared to LCC.  
The improvement of the thermal stability and activity of PHL7 could present a new competitive enzyme for 
PET degradation, which motivated the creation of the enzyme variants.  
The enzyme activity was studied as the maximum rate for 1 hour incubation at different substrate loads. The 
activity was improved at 50°C for the variants L209A, ACC, GCCA and L209V, and at 70°C for the variants 
L209A and ACC (Supplementary Table 3). The thermal stability of PHL7 (determined as the melting temperature) 
was increased for the variants containing the GCC mutations: GCC, GCCA, GCCV and GCCI (Supplementary 
Table 2). The variant GCCA improved both the activity at 50°C and the thermal stability of PHL7. 
The isoleucine mutation introduced by Tournier et al. (2020) in the corresponding amino acid position (F243I) 
did not have a similar increase in activity for PHL7. Instead, the mutation L209A successfully improved the 
activity of the enzyme. This suggests that smaller amino acids in position leucine 209 in PHL7, and the 
corresponding position phenylalanine 243 in LCC results in an increase of activity of these enzymes.  
The mutation L209A successfully improved the activity of PHL7 at 50°C and 70°C (Supplementary Table 3). The 
combination of L209A with the mutations for thermostability increase resulted in similar activity levels for ACC 
at 50°C and 70°C. The combination variant GCCA only conserved the same activity levels as L209A at 50°C. 
At 70°C, the variant GCCA had the same activity as PHL7 wild type (Supplementary Figure 19). However, the 
thermal stability of the variants with an improve in activity, decrease compared to PHL7 wild type. Only the 
variant GCCA obtained a similar Tm than PHL7 (Supplementary Table 2).  
The thermal stability of the enzymes was determined to be an important factor for PET depolymerization at 
70°C. Even though the melting temperature of PHL7 was higher than 70°C (Tm = 80,2°C) the enzyme lost the 
activity after 3 hours of incubation at 70°C, limiting the PET depolymerization levels (Figure 20). The increase 
in thermal stability for the variants with the GCC mutations, allowed the depolymerization of PET to continue 
for longer time than with PHL7.  
However, the variants with an increase of thermal stability had lower activity than PHL7 wild type. Therefore, 
even though the depolymerization of PET continued for a longer time, the final production levels were higher 
for PHL7 wild type. For the variant GCCA, even though the activity was higher than PHL7 at 50°C and the 
thermal stability was similar, the depolymerization levels were very similar to PHL7 wild type.   
In conclusion, the mutation L209A improved the activity of PHL7, and the GCC mutations improved the thermal 
stability of the enzyme. However, in LCC the GCC mutations improved the melting temperature +9°C (Tournier 
et al., 2020) and the same mutations in PHL7 only increased maximum 3°C the melting temperature of the 
enzyme.  
For industrial conditions (70°C, c.a. 24 hours reaction times) the thermal stability of the enzyme plays a key 
role in the PET depolymerization levels. In the case of Tournier et al. (2020) they obtained an enzyme 
LCC(ICCG) for industrial use in PET degradation with the same activity levels as LCC, but highly thermostable 
(Tm=94°C) (Tournier et al., 2020). Therefore, the thermal stability of PET hydrolases is considered a key

Future perspectives 
The discovery of new PET hydrolytic enzymes is a relatively new topic, still under research. Therefore, a lot of 
different approaches can be taken in order to find enzymes able to depolymerize PET in an industrial level.  
In this thesis, three different wild type PET hydrolases were presented and characterized. Moreover, enzyme 
variants of PHL7 were produced and characterized for the improvement of the thermal stability and activity of 
the enzyme. This section will present ideas for future work with the produced enzymes and new ways no 
continue this research. 
The enzyme PHL7 has been demonstrated to be a highly active PET hydrolase, that surpasses the activity of 
LCC and BhrPETase; and mutation L209A has been proven to increase the activity of PHL7. However, thermal 
stability has been proven to be a limiting factor for the depolymerization of PET at 70°C by PHL7. Therefore, 
a future line of work would be to find new ways to improve the thermal stability of PHL7.  
The enzyme BhrPETase is a highly thermostable PET hydrolase (Tm = 101°C) (Xi et al., 2021). It was 
demonstrated that at 70°C over a period of 48 hours, this enzyme has the highest depolymerization levels of 
PET compared to PHL7 and LCC. The reason behind it was suggested to be the high thermostability of the 
enzyme which prevents its denaturation. 
The sequence identity of PHL7 and BhrPETase is 53% (Supplementary Figure 5) (the crystal structure of 
BhrPETase has not been determined yet). The amino acids involved in the thermostability of BhrPETase could 
be studied by mutagenesis and the corresponding position in PHL7 could be mutated to improve the thermal 
stability of the enzyme.  
Another future strategy could be to improve the activity of BhrPETase. The sequence identity of BhrPETase 
and LCC(ICCG) is 92% (Supplementary Figure 1). The amino acid mutation F243I in LCC (Tournier et al., 2020) 
improved 20% the specific activity of the enzyme. The corresponding position F209 in BhrPETase could be 
mutated to isoleucine to increase the activity of the enzyme.  
In a similar manner the thermostability of LCC could be improved by studying the amino acids that differ 
between LCC and BhrPETase. These amino acids could be introduced in the corresponding positions in LCC 
and study if the thermostability of LCC improves.  
The enzyme PHL7 has shown to be highly active at 50°C compared to LCC and BhrPETase. Moreover, at 
50°C the depolymerization levels of PHL7 after 48 hours were higher (3,9 mM MHETeq) than the 
depolymerization levels for LCC (3,3 mM MHEteq) and PHL7 (2,8 mM MHETeq) at 70°C (Figure 19). The 
activity of PHL7 at 50°C could be a relevant feature for industrial applications. The energy spent in keeping 
the reactions at 70°C could be reduced by using a reaction temperature of 50°C and reduce the overall cost 
of PET recycling. This idea could be studied further in order to find PET hydrolases that can function at lower 
reaction temperatures.  
The activity of the PHL7 variants was studied at different substrate loads. For the variants L209A and ACC the 
maximum rate at 50°C was found at a higher substrate load than for the wild-type enzyme (Supplementary 
Figure 19). Therefore, these variants may be able to have higher levels of depolymerization than PHL7, with a 
starting substrate load higher than 10 g/L. For further work this could be studied.  
Finally, the enzyme kinetics of the PET hydrolases studied in this thesis did not follow the typical Michaelis 
Menten model. The modelling of the activity of these enzymes could be considered for future studies.
