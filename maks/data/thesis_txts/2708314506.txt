Abstract
The development of autonomous vehicles is a growing field within transport technology with the po-
tential to improve transportation safety and eﬀiciency. An integral part of obtaining functioning au-
tonomous vehicles is the ability to accurately predict future trajectories. Trajectory forecasting in a
traﬀic setting is the generation of realistic future trajectories of the involved agents, such as vehicles,
bicycles, and pedestrians. This is a complex and diﬀicult task, as it involves predicting multiple future
trajectories of all involved agents, whose mutual interactions make their trajectories interdependent.
Recently, novel approaches to multi-agent trajectory forecasting have leveraged the relational and rep-
resentational capabilities of graphs as a means of jointly predicting the future dynamics of a collection
of agents. In this thesis, we investigate various graph-based machine learning models to solve a real-
life trajectory forecasting problem using the Waymo Open Motion dataset. We present the Recurrent
Message-Passing Graph Neural Network (RMPGNN) as a trajectory forecasting model and investigate
extensions incorporating scene information and uncertainty awareness. The models are evaluated from
both a quantitative and qualitative perspective. Our main findings show that by modelling the interac-
tions of agents, we can consistently improve the predictive accuracy. Furthermore, we show the need for
semantic maps in the generation of realistic future trajectories. We argue that the conventional metrics
for trajectory forecasting are lacking as there exists an asymmetry between current quantitative metrics
and the qualitative behaviour of predicted agents. Lastly, we argue for the need for multi-modality
in the generation of multiple possible futures by demonstrating how uncertainty arises and propagates
when a vehicle is presented with multiple viable trajectories.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Each year, approximately 1.3 million people are killed worldwide as a result of traﬀic related incidents
[WHO21]. In the United States, 94 % of serious traﬀic crashes are due to human error [Adm21]. With
the rise of available sensory data from increasingly intelligent and autonomous vehicles these numbers
can be decreased drastically by removing the human element. Autonomous vehicles are able to collect
and process massive amounts of data in fractions of a second.
This grants an autonomous vehicle
superior perceptive, predictive, and prescriptive abilities when compared to a human operator. Several
technology companies are seeking to develop intelligent vehicles which surpass human capabilities. Two
such examples are Tesla, Inc. [Inc22] and Waymo LLC [LLC22].
Achieving intelligent vehicles capable of self-driving is no easy task. We can approach the design
process by dividing the overall goal into three sequential components: perception, trajectory forecasting,
and planning and control.
Perception is the task of directly translating raw sensory data such as
images into meaningful entities. We imagine an intelligent vehicle outfitted with an array of sensors
(e.g. cameras, LiDARs, and RADARs). The vehicle then analyses all of the collected sensory data
which it can then convert into detailed representations. These could include the locations, velocities,
and orientations of surrounding vehicles and pedestrians alongside a detailed semantic description of
the local map segment. The next component is that of trajectory forecasting. Here, the vehicle is able
to organise the collected data and process it via complex modelling methods into a set of possible future
trajectories, i.e. the possible future locations of all present vehicles, pedestrians, bicycles, etc. In the
given context, these will be referred to as agents. The third and final component is responsible for
planning and control. It takes the possible futures and uses them to plan and implement the future
dynamics eﬀiciently. We can furthermore imagine several autonomous vehicles performing these tasks
cooperatively to optimise their joint futures.
In this thesis, we aim to explore the second of these components, trajectory forecasting. The prob-
lem of multi-agent trajectory forecasting in a traﬀic setting is the generation of feasible future motion
trajectories of vehicles and other road-users given their observed histories. By marrying complex ma-
chine learning-based models with high-quality data collected from real traﬀic scenes it is possible to
predict the future motions of a large number of agents (vehicles, pedestrians, bicycles, etc.). These
possible futures can then be used in autonomous motion planning whereby possible collisions and other
dangerous situations can be avoided.
The trajectory forecasting problem can be approached in many ways. The central hypothesis of this
thesis, is that in order to create accurate and realistic predictions, we need to consider not just the
individual vehicles or agents but also how they interact with each other. To explore this, we will be
leveraging the concept of graph-based learning, whereby we model a traﬀic scene as a graph comprised
of nodes and edges. This is however not a novel approach as many recent advances within trajectory
forecasting also rely on models which use the relational power of graphs via graph neural networks,
such as the Trajectron++.
Common to models in the literature, is that they often model the full
future trajectories simultaneously. In this thesis, we will rely on single-step predictions instead. These
predictions will be used to update the current state of the system, which will in turn be used as the
inputs for the next time step. We thereby seek to achieve full system descriptions at all future time
steps. Ideally, this will allow us to model future interactions and not just base our predictions on past
interactions.
The work of this thesis is conducted in an iterative manner. We will begin by laying out a theoretical
foundation of the necessary concepts related to graph theory and trajectory forecasting as well as

2.1.1
Graphs
Graphs offer a powerful means of representing information about objects and their relations. A graph is
composed of a set of nodes that are connected by a number of edges. The nodes represent objects while
the edges represent their interactions. An object is here loosely defined as an entity that could contain
a number of attributes. The edges can be either directed or undirected and can also contain a number
of features encoding information about the interaction between its two connected nodes. The overall
graph terminology is discussed in detail in [Bar16]. Three graph examples are shown schematically in
Figure 2.1. The first graph (a) is disconnected and is only comprised of isolated nodes. The second
graph (b) is fully connected where its edges are undirected. The third graph (c) contains directed edges
and a self-loop for node A, i.e. an edge from the node to itself.

2.1 Background
4
of a relational inductive bias as coined by Battaglia et. al in [Bat+18]: an inductive bias expresses
assumptions about either the solution space or the data-generating process of a learning problem. A
relational inductive bias allows the user to impose constraints on the relationships and interactions
among entities in a learning process. This provides a powerful set of tools which are unavailable to
many traditional learning methods. A concrete example of this is how the dataset is structured. In
the general case, there is no groundtruth in how the graph should be structured, e.g. which nodes
to connect or how to model these connections. A relational inductive bias could be one of locality:
two agents that are spatially close to each other should be connected, while two agents with a large
physical separation should be disconnected. This is an example of a relational inductive bias that can
be enforced on a problem to guide its learning and encode prior assumptions into the process.

2.1 Background
5
Let G = (V, E) be a graph with N v nodes (or vertices), V = {vi}i=1:Nv, where all nodes have a
number of attributes. Each node is thus represented as a vector, vi. For initial simplicity, let the set
of edges be the empty set, E = ∅, i.e. a graph with no edges as we saw in Figure 2.1. The N v nodes
can be collected into a matrix X by stacking them: X = [v1, v2, ..., vN v]⊤. This however implies an
ordering of the nodes. By multiplying the node feature matrix with a permutation matrix, P, X′ = PX
we reorder the nodes of the graph. In order not to violate the non-ordered structure of the graph, any
function that we apply to the node feature matrix must exhibit permutation invariance. Our function

Often we seek more granularity than these global functions. We might want a notion of locality
where a node is updated as a function of its neighbouring nodes as defined by the graph edges. For an
undirected graph, we define the neighbourhood of node i as
Ni = {j : (i, j) ∨(j, i) ∈E}.
For the directed graph, we define the neighbourhood of node i as
Ni = {j : (i, j) ∈E}.
The neighbourhood of node i thus includes all nodes which have directed edges from node i pointing
to them. This is a choice which implies that node i will be affected by the nodes to which it points,
i.e. where the node ”receives messages” from. We could similarly have chosen an interpretation, where
directed edges point to where a node sends messages to, but we have chosen the former.
We can
define a local function ϕ which operates on a node i and its neighbouring nodes, Ni (i.e. node i itself
and all nodes where a directed edge is drawn from node i to them). This function yields an updated
representation of the node based on its neighbours.
With the above entities introduced, we can define a graph neural network (GNN) layer. A GNN
layer operating on a graph G is a permutation equivariant function F(X, A) constructed by applying
permutation invariant functions ϕ(vi, Ni) across all neighbourhoods of G.

2.1.2
Sequential data
The observed traﬀic sequences change over time. The aim is to use a collection of observations over a
short time period to predict the future system states. We are thereby dealing with time series data,
which is why common notions and methods from time series analysis are introduced. For a time series
problem, it is pertinent to introduce the naive predictor [Mad08], which simply predicts the states to
be unchanging. The predictor at time step t + 1 given t is defined as
bf (t+1|t) = f (t).
In the setting of trajectory forecasting, this could be interpreted as letting an object continue along its
current path into the future, i.e. by fixing its last observed velocity and simply computing its future
positions analytically. Whether using a naive predictor or not, at the next time step, t+1, we will have
a new observation which allows us to compute a prediction error given an error (or loss) function of
choice
Loss = L(f (t+1), bf (t+1|t)).
In the above equation, we have only conditioned on the previous time-step which presents a valid –
yet simple – approach to time series predictions. Often, it is more interesting to predict multiple time
steps into the future i.e. by making a multi-step prediction while simultaneously utilising a full history
of previous observations and not just the most recent observation. In this work, we will rely on the
recursive strategy (also known as performing rollout) [BTL12]. This is simply the process of using the
predicted output at time t as the input at time t + 1:

We thus take the last observed agent states, i.e. at time h, and use these to predict our updated agent
states at time h+1, which will in turn be used as the input to compute the updated states at time h+2
and so on. The quality of the predictions will degrade as we increase the prediction horizon. Small initial
deviations will propagate and result in potentially very different predicted trajectories than observed in
the full sequence. The diﬀiculty will depend on both the history depth and the prediction horizon. A
deep history can for some models encode the behaviour which can help stabilise the future predictions.
Conversely, a long prediction horizon is inherently more diﬀicult to estimate than a short horizon.
An alternative learning strategy for multi-step predictions is the direct strategy [BTL12]. For a
prediction horizon of H, this method learns H independent models which are then all applied. Their
inputs are then concatenated producing the H-step predictions. This method does not suffer the draw-
back of accumulating estimation errors as the recursive strategy, yet it is much more computationally
expensive.
Multiple output prediction models are an obvious viable choice when multi-step predictions are
required. For the problem at hand, however, the dynamic graph structure between time steps will
complicate a multi-output model. Due to its ease of implementation and its flexibility, the recursive
strategy using a single-output model will be applied in this thesis.
For sequential modelling such as for time series data, much success has been found using memory-
based mechanisms. The two prime examples of this are the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and the
Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), see [SSB14] and [Cho+14], respectively. These use different approaches
to encode the history of observed behaviour into a memory component which can then be propagated
through time. This propagated entity is often called the hidden state. By using such a memory-based
based approach, we are essentially creating a many-to-one model, where the full history of previous
observations are used to create a one-step prediction.

2.2
Related work
In this section we introduce various papers exploring relevant applications of graph neural networks.
This includes both trajectory forecasting approaches which are fundamental to the problem at hand
and more general applications such as the simulation of physical systems.
2.2.1
Learning physics using graph networks
Leaving trajectory forecasting momentarily, we turn our attention to the modelling of physical systems
with graph neural networks. In [San+18], the authors apply the GN-framework (as introduced in Section
2.1.1.3) to model various physical systems based on their simulations. Examples of the simulations are a
pendulum, a cartpole (i.e. a pole connected to a box which can move freely in the horizontal direction),
and various other “entities”, which are composed of various bodies which are joined together allowing
for different degrees of movement freedom. The graph nodes are the various object bodies, while the
edges are their connecting joints. Using the simulations, the authors created a range of models which
sought to predict the future system dynamics based on an observed history.
In contrast to much of the trajectory forecasting literature, it is central to the presented architectures
in this paper that all of their models are graph-to-graph models. At each time step, a fully described
graph is used as input, which in turn generates a fully described graph as output. The model’s direct
output is the system’s delta dynamics, thus modelling the relative change in dynamic states. The output
graph is created by adding the delta dynamics to the input graph. This is thereby an example of a
recursive strategy for time series predictions as described in Section 2.1.2.
Interestingly, no models in the presented paper are encoded with prior concepts of physical laws.
Instead, the models learn to approximate physics during training through the interactions between
entities. A similar approach is seen [San+20]. While this paper does not solve a multi-agent trajectory
forecasting problem, it offers an interesting approach into how to structure a graph-based learning
problem which also seeks to predict future dynamics.
2.2.2
Social-BiGAT
With the rise of available sensory data and the increasing demand for intelligent vehicles, the problem
of trajectory forecasting using modern methods is often explored. Many novel approaches model the
scenes as graphs and utilise the predictive and interpretative power of graph neural networks.
An example of this is the “Social-BiGAT” [Kos+19], which seeks to predict the trajectories of pedes-
trians. This is achieved via a graph-based generative adversarial network. The pedestrian interactions
are modelled as a fully connected graph. This specific choice is made with the argument that pedes-
trian interactions are not necessarily bounded by the Euclidean distance between agents. Two familiar
pedestrians might recognise each other at a long distance and thereby seek each other out. Conversely,
pedestrians avoid bumping into each other at close range. Both of these are examples of interactions,
yet the importance of the relative distances between agents is only central in the latter.
Instead of a distance-based approach, a graph attention network with several attention layers (see
Section 4.3.2) is applied to learn and model the complex interactions. Additionally, the authors in-
corporate the physical scenes into the model by propagating a top-down image of the scene through
a convolutional neural network (as popularised in [KSH12]). This creates a latent representation that
takes into account physical features such as possible obstacles that could hinder interactions between
various agents. An LSTM-based generator/discriminator approach is taken to generate and classify
possible future trajectories resulting in multimodal trajectory forecasting. It should be noted that the
architecture uses a many-to-many approach, where the full history is used to generate the future tra-
jectories. The experimental results of the Social-BiGAT are evaluated using a 3.2 second history with
4.2 seconds of future predictions, corresponding to 8 and 12 time steps, respectively. This is quite a
considerable history which is almost of equal length to the range of the future predictions. However,
it might be argued that due to the speed differences between a pedestrian and a moving vehicle, it is
==PAGE:19==

necessary to use a longer history for the former to fully understand the dynamics, whereas a car might
be easier to model due to its larger momentum.
2.2.3
Trajectron
Another example of the application of GNNs to the problem of trajectory forecasting is found in [IP19].
Here, the authors introduce the “Trajectron” which is a multimodal trajectory forecasting model which
utilises a dynamic graph structure, which contrasts the static graph structure of the Social-BiGAT. The
Trajectron also aims to predict the future trajectories of interacting pedestrians. Here, the dynamic
graphs are created by establishing undirected edges between proximate agents. The model encodes
the agents’ histories using two separate LSTM networks: a node encoder and an edge encoder. Nodes
are treated separately while the edges are encoded based on an aggregation of node neighbourhoods.
After subsequent edge manipulations, the model uses the concatenated node and edge encodings to
generate a latent sample from a conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE), which in combination
with a third LSTM is used to sample trajectories of a Gaussian Mixture Model [Bis06, Chapter 9.2],
which parametrises the target variable.
The Trajectron also uses a many-to-many approach where
the full future trajectories are estimated from the observed history. A major difference between the
Trajectron and the Social-BiGAT is that the Trajectron aims to model the residual that changes position,
as was the case for the graph-based physics model in [San+20] and [San+18]. It, in other words, does
not generate the absolute target positions but instead the change in position between time steps. The
model thus estimates the velocities between time steps which can then be used to update the previous
position: p(t+1) = p(t) + ∆t · u(t), where p is an agent’s position while u(t) is a generated set of control
actions at time t. This model thereby has a simple notion of physical laws encoded into its update
procedure, contrary to [San+18].
2.2.4
Trajectron++
The Trajectron has since its inception been improved upon with the aptly named “Trajectron++”
[Sal+21], which expands the original framework in two major ways. Firstly, the updated framework
now accommodates heterogeneous data, whereby sensory scene information is included in the input
data in the form of either binary or more complex semantic maps (e.g. an area of the road can be
classified as “driveable,” “pedestrian crossing” or the likes). Secondly, the framework now allows for
agent classes, where different types of agents, e.g. pedestrians, cars, and, buses are modelled differently,
which significantly improves on the model applicability. The graph creation now draws directed edges
from agent i to agent j, if agent j is assumed to affect agent i. Whether to draw an edge or not depends
on the relative distance between the two agents as well as their respective node types. By considering
the node types in the creation, it is possible to distinguish between different types of edges, where
the interaction between a vehicle and a bicycle might differ from a vehicle-to-vehicle interaction. The
directionality of the edges depend on the node types and their relative distance. An assumption here
is that the driver of a car is likely to focus their attention on the surrounding traﬀic to a wider extent
than a pedestrian, whose attention might only extend to a few metres. An edge can then be drawn
from a car to a pedestrian if they are e.g. 10 metres from each other, whereas an edge might only be
drawn from the pedestrian to the car if they are 2 metres from each other.
Most model frameworks assume that the node type is known a priori. This can be considered a
strict assumption for some modern sensor systems where the process of agent identification might not be
perfect. In [Iva+21], a CVAE based approach is taken as in the Trajectron papers while incorporating
this class uncertainty. This comes at the cost of the directed edges of the Trajectron++ as this relies
on known node types to determine both edge type and direction.
Common to the models described thus far, is that they generate all future predictions based only on
their histories. In other words, the models all predict the full set of future predictions simultaneously.
This has the adverse effect of not allowing future interactions between agents. This is one of the issue
that we wish to tackle in this thesis.

3.1

We define a scene or a sequence as a collection of sequentially observed agents and their states. An
agent is a physical entity of interest for which we wish to predict its future dynamics. An agent’s state
denote its attributes. The aim for a given scene is then to predict the agents’ future states given their
observed histories. Specifically, we wish to predict the future positions of the objects in a sequence. We
denote the position vector as p = [x, y]⊤for the two-dimensional case.

3.4.3
Overlap rate
Both the ADE and FDE measure performance for single agents without accounting for possible inter-
actions. For motion forecasting of vehicles, we want to measure possible collisions. For each time step
during a sequence, we compute the relative distances between all agents. If any of these distances are
below a set threshold, we define the trajectories as overlapping for the particular agents for the given
sequence. We define the overlap rate (OR) as the ratio of overlapping trajectories to the total number
of predicted trajectories [Ett+21].

With time-dependent data such as with the n-body simulations, there is flexibility in how the learning
process can be structured. We do not simply have pairs of input and output values but full sequences
which can be divided in multiple ways. Before we begin our modelling process, we must therefore specify
how we intend structure it. This section will explain how we approach this problem and how we will
train, validate, and evaluate the resulting models.
During training, we wish to minimise the average displacement error. For the first 11 time steps,
we feed the groundtruth observed values to the model, after which the model output is recursively used
as inputs. The training loss is computed over the full sequence thus including both the history and
the future predictions. The validation and testing losses are limited to the future predictions, where a
groundtruth input value is only available for the first of these time steps. By structuring the learning
problem in this way, we ensure that the model learns to accurately use the available historic data
in priming its trajectory while only evaluating its performance based on the future rollout thus not
incorporating groundtruth input values with the obvious exception of the very first future prediction.
A total of 2,000 simulations have been generated for the n-body problem as specified in the previous
chapter. To perform model selection and evaluation, we will be using a hold-out approach as described
in [HSM21] and [HTF09]. Of the 2,000 simulations, 1,000 will be used for training the various models
while 500 will be used to validate the model performance during training. The optimal model will then
be chosen based on the validation performance. To evaluate the final model, it will be applied to the
remaining 500 simulations which constitute the test set. The model training and selection will thus be
performed without any notion of the test set whatsoever to avoid possible data leakage.

loss function is non-convex, the training process is variable and a single model configuration might lead

4.1
Modelling isolated nodes
We begin by investigating graphs with isolated nodes by letting the edges be the empty set, E = ∅. We
thereby forego modelling the interactions between agents, by treating them all as independent. This
effectively ignores the graph structure of the problem at hand. We here model the n bodies in each
simulation as independent nodes in our disconnected graph. The aim is to predict the future motions
of the bodies based on a short observed history.
4.1.1
Constant baseline model
We start by defining a simple baseline model which will act as our reference point.
The constant
baseline model assumes the agent velocities to be unchanging and will thus act as our naïve predictor.
An alternative choice would be to let all agent positions remain constant, but the former was chosen as
this gives a more realistic estimate of the future evolution of a given scene. The most recent observed
velocities of all agents will remain unchanged while the positions will be updated by computing the

4.1.3
Recurrent node MLP
Our third model expands on the Node MLP. We here make use of a long short-term memory (LSTM)
layer [SSB14] as briefly mentioned in Section 2.1.2. The LSTM is a recurrent neural network architecture
which implements a memory-based approach to sequential modelling. With the use of an internal cell
==PAGE:28==
4.2 Graph generation
19
state and a set of “gates”, the LSTM is able to regulate the flow of information in a recurrent manner.
This way, the model learns which parts of its newly encountered input should be remembered and
propagated to the next time step thus seeking to remember “important” features of the sequential
data. The idea is that the model learns some latent behaviour which can aid it in its prediction as
it now has access not just to the most recent observation, but also a learned history. An LSTM can
be a powerful component and it is expected to increase the quality of the predictions when used in
conjunction with other model architectures. This simple baseline configuration is however not expected
to greatly outperform the pure MLP of the previous section as the current limit is unlikely to be in the
modelling but rather in the lack of available information on the agent interactions.
We replace the first MLP-block of the Node MLP model with an LSTM layer. The output value
from the recurrent cell is again concatenated with the original input via a skip-connection, which is fed
to the second MLP block, which generates the output dynamics. Alongside the output dynamics, the
hidden states are returned to be used by the model for the next iteration. The history processing as
described in Section 3.5 now serves an important function. It not only initialises the predicted trajectory
using groundtruth observations, it also acts as a priming process for the hidden states of the LSTM.

4.2
Graph generation
We now turn our attention to graph-based models in which we seek to model the interactions between
nodes in a graph. Since neither the n-body dataset nor a real-world traﬀic scene with multiple agents
have intrinsic graph structures, we need to define how a graph is generated. To generate the graphs,
we rely on a distance-based approach which is prevalent in trajectory forecasting (see [IP19], [Sal+21],
[Iva+21], [San+20]). We concretely draw a directed edge from agent i to agent j if their Euclidean
distance is less than some defined value, R: ||pi −pj||2 ≤R. We denote this value as the connecting
radius. Since we are using the Euclidean distance as our distance measure, this will naturally result in
edges being drawn from both i to j and j to i if their relative distance is below the chosen threshold.
This result can be interpreted as an undirected graph. We nonetheless choose to keep the directed
structure, as this allows for possible extensions of the modelling capabilities, whereby edges are drawn
in a heterogeneous manner such as in [Sal+21]. Inspired by [SHW21], we enrich the edge structure by
encoding the Euclidean distance (albeit not the squared distance) as edge attributes. We have conducted
experiments where the inverse of the relative distance was used to encode a measure of closeness. The
idea was that the closer two agents were to each other, the stronger the connection between them. This
however led to nearly identical results as before the inversion. Due to this and the added overhead, we
returned to using the distance directly.
The graph structure is updated at each time step allowing for dynamic graphs which evolve through-
out the future rollout.
It is unlikely that this graph creation process is optimal for the n-body problem, which is driven
by the gravitational attraction and the Coulomb forces which act between bodies. While the distances
between bodies will affect the strength of the forces significantly, it is a gross simplification to assume
that the bodies cease to interact when their relative distances exceed some fixed threshold. We therefore
do not expect ideal performance from our models, which is why the results presented here will be more
qualitative than quantitative. We remind ourselves that the n-body problem is being used as a simple

CHAPTER 5
Recurrent Message-Passing
Graph Neural Network
This chapter presents the Recurrent Message-Passing Graph Neural Network (RMPGNN) which is the
academic contribution of this thesis’s work. Here, we will describe the model architecture in detail while
we reserve its application and evaluation for Chapter 6. We introduce the modelling choices in Section
5.1, which is followed by an in-depth description of the architecture in Section 5.2.
5.1
Introduction
The RMPGNN is built using the experience gained in Section 4 while also being heavily inspired by
the work of Salzmann, Ivanovic et al. in their work on the Trajectron++ [Sal+21].
While predictions for the n-body system can be generated to a fairly high degree of accuracy using
one-step predictions, it is believed that the generation of feasible long-term trajectories for vehicles
will include long-term interactions. An example of this could be when a vehicle is overtaken. The
overtaken vehicle might reason that this faster-driving vehicle has a sense of priority or urgency, such
that a consequent re-overtaking might be ill-advised. An autonomous vehicle could similarly remember
short-term aggressive behaviour of other vehicles which could affect the minimum distance it should try
to maintain.
We have chosen to use a recurrent strategy due to its applicability for the problem at hand, despite
the recurrent node MLP of Section 4.1.3 not outperforming its non-recurrent counterpart to a significant
extent. The interactions we seek to model are two-fold; we model the contemporary interaction between
an agent and its neighbouring nodes, and we model the temporal interaction or dependency between a
node in its current and previous states.
Our model is a recurrent extension to the message-passing GNN that was introduced in Section 4.3.3.
The model uses two separate LSTM-layers to encode the agent and interaction histories, respectively.
To encode the interaction history, we will further leverage the predictive power of message-passing as
was shown in Section 4.3.3. These encodings act as the inputs to a node-update mechanism which
generates the output dynamics.
Experiments were run which explored variations on the proposed architecture. This included sub-
stituting the LSTM-layers with GRU-layers, using multi-layer versions of the recurrent blocks, and
additional message-passing operations. These model architectures did however not perform as well as
the RMPGNN, while often being of higher complexity. Hence, we chose to continue with the RMPGNN
in its proposed form.
Our model follows the graph-to-graph scheme as introduced in Chapter 3, whereby we seek full
descriptions of the system at each future time step. This is in contrast to many novel approaches such
as the Trajectron++, where the predictions only consist of future trajectories. This allows our model
to capture future interactions between agents and not just rely on the encoded histories. The graph
generation follows the procedure described in Section 4.2, where edges are drawn between graph nodes
if they are within a distance, R, of each other.

representation of each node’s individual history. All nodes thereby use the same encoding mechanism
to model their histories, where they are all treated as independent observations.
5.2.2
Encoding agent interactions
In addition to modelling each agent’s individual history, we seek to encode each agent’s interaction
history, i.e the history of the interactions between a node and its neighbours. We do this by implementing
a message-passing GNN, the output of which is passed through an LSTM-layer. We begin by computing
all messages between neighbouring agents. This is done by concatenating the features of the sender
and receiver nodes alongside the edge attributes for the given edge. This combined representation is
fed through an MLP which computes the messages as was the case in Section 4.3.3. These messages
are then aggregated with a summation. The sum-aggregator was chosen in favour of a mean-aggregator
as it preserves a notion of neighbourhood size. The sum is applied over the sender index, such that an
agent receives messages from all agents that it has an edge drawn to. A schematic over the message
generation and aggregation process can be seen in Figure 5.2.

which encodes the interaction history per node.

6.1
The Waymo Open Motion dataset
This section presents the Waymo Open Motion dataset which is the chosen dataset for the problem
of trajectory forecasting. After introducing and detailing the dataset itself, we will describe the pre-

6.1.1
Introduction
Waymo LLC is an autonomous driving technology company which is committed to developing fully
autonomous vehicles. The company began as Google’s self-driving car division before becoming inde-
pendent in 2017 (see https://waymo.com/ for details). Waymo has chosen to contribute to the research
community by publishing parts of their internally collected data. So far, two large datasets have been
released: the Waymo Open Perception dataset and the Waymo Open Motion dataset. The Waymo
Open Motion dataset (hereinafter “the Waymo dataset”) is a collection of over 100,000 segments of
complex traﬀic sequences. The dataset was released in March 2021 with an accompanying paper dis-
cussing its details [Ett+21]. The dataset has been created using collected sensory data from Waymo’s
autonomous vehicles in six cities within the United States. This also implies that the traﬀic rules and
road types of the United States apply.
The aim of the release of the Waymo Open Motion dataset is to provide a wealth of high quality
annotated data with an emphasis on complex interactions with the ultimate goal of advancing motion
forecasting models.
The raw collected data has been mined for interesting interactions, such as merges, lane changes,
unprotected turns, intersection left turns, etc. The full list can be found the original paper.
6.1.2
Description and feature limitation
The Waymo dataset is provided as a collection of complex traﬀic sequences. Each sequence is comprised
of the time-dependent descriptions of a variable number of present agents (i.e. vehicles, bicycles, and
pedestrians), ranging from just a few to a maximum of 128. These descriptions include information on
each agent such as its position, velocity, heading, etc. A full description of the present features can be
found in Appendix C.1. Additionally, each sequence has a detailed road graph, which is a collection of
sampled map points. The term road graph will be used interchangeably with map in the following. Each
map point has an associated map type, which can belong to one of 16 “types” such as LaneCenterFreeway
and RoadEdgeBoundary, which represent the centre-points of a freeway and the road edge, respectively.
Full details of the road graph data can also be found in Appendix C.1. Lastly, the time-dependent

description of traﬀic lights are present for each sequence. Due to the time constraints of this thesis, the
traﬀic light data will not be incorporated into the modelling process in the present work.
Each sequence contains agent information at a total of 91 time steps sampled at 10 Hz resulting in 9.1
second sequences. We limit our scope to only consider 5.1 second sequences by ignoring the last 4 seconds
of each sequence. Our goal is thereby to predict a 4 second future based on 1.1 seconds of history. This
reduces the problem complexity but simultaneously reduces the computational overhead significantly
thereby making the entire process more feasible. We have chosen 1.1 seconds of history as it aligns with
the proposed problem formulation in the creation paper. A four second future proved suﬀicient to allow
for complex interactions while attaining computational feasibility. We furthermore limit the problem
complexity by only considering the vehicles of the dataset, thus leaving the incorporation of pedestrian
and bicycle predictions to future work. Going forwards, the term all agents for a sequence means all
present vehicles in the given sequence.
While the dataset contains many agent features, we will rely on only a subset of these for our
modelling. The approach taken in this thesis is to create a graph-to-graph model. This means that at
each time step, a full graph description is required. All dynamic agent states must thus be updated
at each time step. This can add significant overhead while complicating the learning process as each
predicted dynamic state should be reflected by the loss function. We will therefore both simplify the
learning process and decrease the computational overhead and storage requirements by keeping only a
subset of the original features. Each agent will be represented by its x and y coordinates, its x and y
velocities as well as its width, length, and height for a total of seven features. At each time step, our
model will update its dynamic states, i.e. its positions and velocities. We will thus leave out the vehicle
orientation (as defined by the bounding box yaw and velocity yaw) and z position. For each sequence,
the z coordinates of the various agents remained relatively unchanged. Forgoing this feature is therefore
not considered a great limitation.
This is not the case for the bounding box yaw attribute, however.
Some experimentation was
conducted where the two yaw attributes were included, thereby incorporating vehicle orientation. This
proved troublesome given the update mechanism shown in Section 3.3. Attempts were made where the
yaw values and the predicted velocities were optimised jointly by summing a loss function over each set
of values, yet this did not lead to convincing results. If the yaw values are to be included, we recommend
to transition from the simple position update using the x and y velocities to a unicycle model like that
of the Trajectron++ [IP19]. Here, the steering rate and the speed are estimated which are in turn used
to update the vehicle position and orientation. Due to the complicated dynamics that this includes (see
Appendix B of [IP19]), it is considered out of the scope of this thesis.
At first, we began with modelling all attributes of all agent types over the full 9.1 second sequences.
The resulting models and predictions were however lacking. The complexity of the problem made it
diﬀicult to interpret the potential causes. The poor performance could have been due to problems with
the parametrisation of the angle values, the integration of motion dynamics, or it could simply have
been because of the diﬀiculty of long-term predictions. Simultaneously, model training and evaluation
took a significant amount of time which further complicated the debugging process. To alleviate these
issues and streamline the process, we limited the scope over the available dataset as described above.
With the above processing, a subset of the full dataset has been chosen and split into a training,
validation, and test set (see Section 3.5 for training, selection, and evaluation details). The specific
details of this selection and splitting process for the Waymo dataset can be found in Appendix C.2.
6.1.3
Normalisation
The x and y coordinates of the agents are positioned in a scene-based global coordinate system mea-
sured in metres, where the precise origins are of no importance. Some sequences will thus have agents
scattered around [14000, −900] while other scenes might place agents at [2000, 10000]. The observed
traﬀic situations are expected to behave similarly, regardless of their positions in this coordinate sys-
tem. We will therefore seek to normalise the attributes to avoid the arbitrariness affecting our results.
The following normalisation process has been generated as a result of many intermediary experiments.
==PAGE:42==
6.2 Model variations
33
During preprocessing of the training dataset, we computed the mean values and standard deviations
of all attributes on a per-scene basis for the first eleven time steps, thus corresponding to the observed
histories. From these values we then computed a global scaler, which is the mean value over all standard
deviations. After the graph creation during runtime, we divide all node and edge attributes with this
global scaler. The agent positions and dimensions are all measured in metres, while the velocities are
measured in metres per second. We chose to normalise all attributes using a single value to preserve
the intrinsic scale differences between attributes while decreasing variance with the aim of improving
the training process. Prior to scaling, we subtract the position mean value from the position attributes
on a per scene basis. This results in all sequences having a geographical centre of [0, 0].
6.1.4
Road graph information
The available road graph data is provided as sampled coordinates with a sampling resolution of ap-
proximately 0.5 metres. The number of samples for each sequence will thus vary greatly as it depends
on both the physical size of the given area but also the complexity of the road segments. We choose
to transform this variably sized data into semantic maps, which can then be interpreted as top-down
images of a given scene. After some experimentation, we have chosen to let all semantic maps span an
area of 150 by 150 metres. With the resolution of the sampled data, this corresponds to 300 by 300 pixel
images. Using the mean location of each scene as our image centre point, we compute a two-dimensional
histogram with a total of 300 bins in each direction. This histogram is then binarised as we are not
seeking to capture a density of road segments. Using all available map features, the result is a 300 by
300 pixel image with 16 channels. This however increases the computational cost significantly. Instead,
similar features are grouped together with a simple sum resulting in eight channels. These channels can
be seen in Appendix C.3.
The number of sampled points for each original map feature varies greatly with some having millions
of points for the full dataset and some only having a few thousands. The most present road types are
the road centres and the road boundaries which are arguably the most important. A visualisation of
the map with added vehicle trajectories can be seen Figure 6.1. The map shown without vehicles can be
seen in Figure C.1 in Appendix C. Here, the different road types are shown with different line colours.
The shown map is a composition of the eight map channels. Each channel can be seen separately in
Figures C.2 and C.3 in Appendix C. The groundtruth trajectories are shown at each time step with a
circle for each vehicle. As we can see in the given example, only a small number of the total present
vehicles are moving. Many appear to be parked along the street while one is without surrounding map
information.
6.1.5
Missing values
Since the Waymo dataset is based on sensory data, imperfections are expected. Therefore, not all agent
states are fully described for a given sequence. Some are present from the initial time step, whereas
others might appear later during the sequence. Still others might be present at both the first and last
time steps but with occasional missing values. The presence or absence of observed data is marked by
a valid flag, which is an additional eighth attribute present for all agents at all time steps. Due to the
setup of the learning problem, where an agent history is encoded using the 11 first observations, if an
agent is not present during the eleventh time step, its future trajectories will not be predicted. For
agents that are initially present but whose data is not available at later time steps, the missing values
are simply masked out during the loss calculation.

The Waymo Motion dataset is rich with scene information. As mentioned in Section 6.1.4, we have for

image as being shared for all agents, yet the effect of this is not expected to be substantial. The road

we will compute the pixel-locations of all agents, i.e. exactly where in the image each agent is located.
Using these values, we can then extract a zoomed-in map for all agents where they are centred. The

at the centre pixel. With the given resolution of approximately 0.5 metres per sampled datapoint, this
is equivalent to a line-of-sight of 10 metres. 10 metres is similarly the chosen connecting radius (see
Section 4.2) for the following experiments. The obvious caveat is that this line of sight is not equal when
measured from the centre-pixel as we have chosen to use square maps due its ease of implementation.

6.3.1.5
Summary
The quantitative results of the above experiments all show the same patterns which we now summarise.
In Section 6.3.1.1 it became apparent that a recurrent mechanism was crucial in order to accurately
generate long-term future predictions. This behaviour was expected. Generating predictions while only
accessing data from a single time step is immensely diﬀicult. By incorporating a memory-based model
==PAGE:52==
6.3 Experimental results
43
architecture, the predictive performance sharply increased. In Section 6.3.1.2 we limited our metric com-
putations to only consider agents of interest as defined in the original paper [Ett+21]. The difference
in the results show that the inclusion of all agents had skewed the reported model performances in a
favourable direction. By removing these non-interacting agents, such as parked vehicles, the predictive
challenge increased considerably as seen by the performance of e.g. the constant baseline model. Our
proposed model architecture nonetheless still showed significant improvements over the simple baseline.
Here, the effect of including road-graph data became more apparent, yet the improvements do not yet
justify the additional computational overhead. This also applies to the uncertainty-aware model which
has yet to show a real benefit which can warrant its additional complexity. In Section 6.3.1.3, our
ablation study showed the measurable impact of modelling the agent interactions. The models which
incorporated edge structures consistently and significantly outperformed their isolated-node counter-
parts. Lastly, we reported the best performing runs for all models in Section 6.3.1.4. We will be using
the best performing model runs to generate the qualitative results in the following section. We could
alternatively use all three runs for each model to generate three distinct predictions, yet the difference
would be negligible as indicated by the variances of the quantitative results.
We can here point out that we have not yet presented any external results achieved on the Waymo
dataset. In its creation paper, the authors presented an accompanying model which acted as their
“baseline”. The term baseline has been put in quotation marks as it is developed by the talented team at
Waymo LLC. Their model reaches a minimum testing ADE of 1.34 [Ett+21] (Table 2) measured at eight
seconds into the future, which is a considerable improvement over our presented results. They similarly
provide the results of a constant velocity model like the one presented here. The ADE of the Waymo
constant prediction model at eight seconds into the future is 11.0, compared to our constant prediction
model’s ADE of 14.04 at only four seconds into the future. This seems to indicate a misalignment of
how the metrics are computed in the Waymo paper and in this thesis. If they were computed similarly,
we would expect our constant baseline to outperform theirs, as ours only predicts half as far into the
future. This is however not a dealbreaker. The aim of this thesis is not to create a new state-of-the-art
trajectory forecasting model as this is well outside a reasonable scope of a one-person thesis, given
that Waymo’s results builds on more than a decade of experience while likely involving hundreds of
people. As long as we compute our results consistently and reproducibly, we can still perform internal
comparisons and draw conclusions thereafter.

All visualisations so far have shown the road graph data, i.e. the roads on which the cars drive. From a

In Figure 6.9 we see two familiar sequences. The left column ((a) and (c)) shows the predictions of

can be found in Figures 6.6 (a) and 6.7 (c), respectively.
The improvement of incorporating map
data is quite striking. Without the map data, the predicted trajectories often continued their original
behaviour. While the predictions of the map-aware model also continues their original trajectories we
see an adaption to the road lines. Examples of this can be seen in the top row. There is in (b) a
clear tendency for the agents to stick to road lines. The green vehicle, which we have discussed before,
performs its right turn and appears to notice the dark-grey road line which represents a solid white line.

parallel to the line until it ends (its endpoint can more clearly be seen in (a)). Hereafter, the vehicle
continues in the middle of the road. We see similar behaviour for the blue and purple agents which
perform left turns heading in the negative x-direction.
Another interesting interaction occurs when the brown agent (which is moving from the top of the
image to the middle-right) encounters the red road line. It appears to adapt very abruptly to this line
by moving out of its way after which it continues its former behaviour. The red line corresponds to a
solid yellow line which separates the two directions of traﬀic. It is however also possible that the vehicle
is interacting with the stationary yellow vehicle. Nonetheless, we observe adaptive behaviour which
ultimately results in the agent moving in a feasible path.
In (c) and (d) we see the complicated road map we encountered earlier. Again, the improvement
is striking. The map-aware model has learned to navigate within the bounds of the road which leads
to feasible predicted trajectories. We can however still note that the green agent is crossing the solid
black lines, which is unwanted behaviour.

its basic counterparts in the left column.

6.3.2.4
RMPGNN with uncertainty-awareness
Common to all shown trajectories in the preceding sections is that they are all the result of the same
update mechanism and optimisation process. The second variant we introduced in Section 6.2.2 incor-
porated a notion of uncertainty awareness into the modelling process. At each time step, we output
the parameters of a bivariate Gaussian distribution over the velocities, which is in turn used to update
an analogous Gaussian over the positions. In addition to showing the predicted trajectories, we can
visualise the resulting covariance matrices as ellipses. We do this by computing its eigenvalues and
eigenvectors. The ellipsis can then be drawn with its semi-minor and semi-major axes equal to the
square-roots of the eigenvalues, where we furthermore rotate the ellipsis such that its axes align with
the eigenvectors. The resulting ellipsis can be interpreted as a measure of confidence for the mean value.
By scaling the lengths of the semi-minor and semi-major axes we can show increasingly large confidence
areas. This procedure is based on [Bis06, Chapter 2.3].

at each time step for all agents.
The ellipsis for each agent is drawn with one standard deviation.

The sequence also highlights a fundamental issue with our approach. Our model is unimodal and
is only able to generate one possible future per agent. We are currently propagating the mean values
of the Gaussian distribution at each time step. While it is possible to sample from the distributions
rather than simply taking the mean, this does not solve the problem. Nonetheless, we did exactly this,
where we for each agent generated ten future trajectories. Concretely, we sampled from the velocity
distribution instead of propagating its mean. The updated position was again computed via Equation
3.1. The resulting predictions can be seen in Figure F.1 in Appendix E.
There are several issues with this approach. We can for example notice that many of the sampling
runs end up with trajectories headed in the opposite direction of traﬀic. However, the central issue is
still that our distribution is uni-modal. A possible extension could then be to model outputs with a
Gaussian Mixture Model as was the case for the Trajectron++ [IP19]. This way we could conceptually
imagine several distinct trajectories that a vehicle could pursue. A vehicle at an intersection could
continue forward, take a left-turn, or take a right-turn. Each of these three possible futures could then
be modelled by separate mixture components. This however raises several questions. How should we,
for example, propagate the current system states to the next time step? The chosen approach in this
thesis is to fully describe the system between each time step. This involves the graph creation process,
where we compute the distances between nodes. If we are dealing with multimodality, this implies
that each agent has a number of possible futures. When we create the graph at each time step, how
would we decide which agents belong in the “same” future? It would not be feasible to compute all the
possible interactions as this would increase the number of futures exponentially. This appears to be a

6.3.2.5
Summary
The constant baseline model delivered the expected performance. It is able to accurately predict the
future trajectories of stationary vehicles and vehicles moving in straight lines at constant velocities.
As soon as the observed behaviour becomes more complex – even just by including road turns – the
model fails. The constant prediction model has acted as our baseline model by setting a lower level of
necessary performance for our models to actually contribute to the problem at hand.
The recurrent message-passing graph neural network, the RMPGNN, provided a significant leap of
complexity and performance. The model outperformed the constant baseline in all observed sequences.
This was often achieved by the model continuing an agent’s observed history into the future. If a vehicle
was found veering to the left, the model caught this behaviour and extrapolated it into the future
resulting in a left turn. We also investigated the effect of modelling graph edges. Here we discovered
that this has a regulatory outcome, whereby agents appear to constrain each other’s movements. While
this might seem like an adverse effect, the observed qualitative effect was that agents were constrained
to follow the behaviour of their neighbours. This often resulted in agents following their respective
lanes which is usually desirable. We furthermore observed a collision-deterring behaviour as a result of
the edge creation. By predicting the trajectories of the same sequence with and without graph edges,
we observed a vehicle continuing its path directly into another vehicle. When edges were drawn, we
saw that the vehicle began to decelerate when it came within the connecting radius of the stationary
vehicle. While the model offers a considerable improvement over the baseline, it is still challenged and
defeated by more complex scenarios. The model has no access to map information which, in the case of
complicated road segments, proved detrimental. The model simply has no way of knowing if a road is
turning. This led to many predicted trajectories crossing edge boundaries which often steered vehicles
into opposing traﬀic – or possibly buildings – in either case, undesirable behaviour.
In the subsequent experiments we investigated the inclusion of map information with the first variant
of the RMPGNN. The resulting trajectories proved different from its more basic counterpart. Most
vehicles tended to staying within their respective lanes. In the case of turns at intersections, the vehicles
were able to navigate into the proper lanes. What mostly sets these results apart from when we did
not include map data was the feasibility of the predicted trajectories. Most of the predictions represent
==PAGE:64==
6.3 Experimental results
55
realistic future trajectories that to a large extent follow the actual flow of traﬀic thereby offering a great
improvement over the basic RMPGNN. We also observed that many of the trajectories appeared less
smooth with minor fluctuations from time step to time step. While this is neither wanted nor realistic
behaviour, it shows a large degree of adaptability from the model. It is able to accommodate small
input variations between time steps into its predictions. This indicates that the model has become very
dependent on the road-graph data as this is what separates it from the smooth predictions of previous
models. This has simultaneous drawbacks and benefits, as it can result in increased prediction accuracy
in the case of high-quality map data, yet consequently poor performance in its absence.
This fluctuating behaviour implies a prediction uncertainty. Small changes in the inputs are likely
to affect the outcomes measurably. With the second variant of the RMPGNN we sought to include a
measure of uncertainty awareness into our predictions. These qualitative results proved highly useful
and allowed for interesting interpretations of the observed behaviour. Many of the predictions and their
accompanying uncertainties make sense when we consider the road segments and the traﬀic flow. The
prediction uncertainty increased significantly when an agent approached the middle of an intersection
or an area with sparse map data. This simultaneously highlighted an issue with our modelling approach:
its uni-modality. Our models simply do not have the capacity to generate several feasible future tra-
jectories for all agents. Incorporating multi-modality will likely require a fundamental change in the
modelling approach to avoid an exponential number of possible futures. So while the last model did
not quantitatively beat its counterpart which did not accommodate uncertainty awareness, the model
is in many ways much more useful due to its interpretability.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have presented and defended an argument for modelling complex traﬀic situations with
graphs. We began by exploring the graph modelling literature from a conceptual point of view. This
involved analysing and applying various modelling approaches to a simulated dataset. The qualitative
results gave us an insight into how to structure our main problem of trajectory forecasting as a graph-
based learning problem. This let us develop a complex model which sought to take the best parts from
the various explored concepts, which culminated in the recurrent message-passing graph neural network.
The Waymo Open Motion dataset provided a worthy adversary to our modelling approaches. It offered
a wealth of available data, much of which we have had to leave out due to time constraints. We then
successfully applied both the proposed model and two additional extensions of it to the Waymo dataset.
The first of these extensions incorporated external data in the form of high-dimensional semantic maps
of the traﬀic scenes. The second extension changed the modelling strategy by returning not the direct
predictions, but instead distributional parameters over the velocities and positions of the present agents.
This allowed us to model the uncertainties of the predicted trajectories, which in turn allowed for deeper
interpretations.
Our experimental results were analysed quantitatively and qualitatively. We began by investigat-
ing the quantitative results, where it quickly became apparent that the most accurate metrics were
achieved when we ignored the trajectories that were easy to predict, such as those of stationary vehicles.
This greatly increased the diﬀiculty of the learning problem but simultaneously offered a more earnest
estimate of how our models actually performed. Another main finding was that we observed concrete
evidence that the inclusion of a descriptive graph structure consistently improved the accuracy of the
generated predictions. This supported the main hypothesis of this thesis, namely that it is necessary
to model not just each agent individually but also their interactions.
After investigating the main model, we explored its two extensions.
The first extension to our
proposed model only offered a slight improvement in the quantitative metrics.
However, when we
investigated the qualitative results by visualising the generated trajectories, we consistently saw that
the predictions of the extended model were much more realistic – and more importantly – safer. The
predictions of the base model often resulted in vehicles crossing road boundaries and veering outside
the driveable area causing potentially dangerous situations. This was not observed when incorporating
the semantic map data. The discrepancy between the quantitative and qualitative results raised an
interesting point: the applied metrics are symmetric whereas the qualitative behaviour is asymmetric.
Two different predicted trajectories for an agent could thereby result in the same quantitative results, yet
when visualising the trajectories one might be considered potentially dangerous and the other safe. This
suggested that in order to accurately measure the predictive performances, new metrics are required.
The second extension which incorporated both semantic map information and uncertainty awareness
did not outperform the first extended model quantitatively. It did however give us an interesting view
into how an agent’s trajectories are generated throughout future time steps. By analysing the results,
we established that the model had become dependent on the map data, and that the predictions would
suffer in its absence. Another main finding indicated that our modelling approach was lacking. Faced
with two equally likely qualitative paths, e.g. whether to turn left or to continue, our models lacked
the ability to choose and instead simply predicted a mean path. This mean path was however rarely
feasible which resulted in the agent approaching the road boundaries. We argued that to expand our
toolbox and generate a number of feasible future trajectories for an agent, we would have to move from
uni-modality to multi-modality.
==PAGE:66==
7 Conclusion
57
While we have successfully applied graph-based models to generate feasible future trajectories for a
number of interacting agents like we sought out to do, there are some notable limitations in our work.
Our edge creation process was simple and only used the Euclidean distance between agents as a measure
of whether or not they should be connected. We also chose to only use a subset of the available features,
whereby we gave up on the notion of vehicle orientation and the traﬀic light states. We also limited
the considered agents to be vehicles which has undoubtedly left out interesting interactions between
pedestrians, bicycles, and vehicles. Lastly, due to both resource and time considerations, we chose to
predict only four seconds into the future despite the dataset offering a full eight second description for
each agent.
With additional time and resources, obvious extensions to our work would include overcoming and
incorporating the above limitations. It would also be interesting to further explore the physical dy-
namics such as extending the update function to a unicycle model. To further expand on our work, an
investigation of multi-modality could offer a valuable step towards generating multiple future trajecto-
ries for each agent. Ideally, this could be coupled with our current procedure of generating full system
descriptions at each time step, thus allowing for interesting future interactions.
Before the field of autonomous driving reaches its full potential of universally applicable and fully
autonomous vehicles, there is arguably still work to be done. However, efforts such as our use of the
Recurrent Message-Passing Graph Neural Network for trajectory forecasting alongside its outlined po-
tential improvements and extensions, as well as many exciting developments in the field, are all steps
in the right direction. Combined, these endeavours are making the future of autonomous traﬀic come
further within reach, which could soon herald a safer era of transportation.

Implementation details
All models are implemented with PyTorch [Pas+17] and the PyTorch Geometric framework [FL19]. All
model training and evaluation is done with PyTorch Lightning [FT19]. Model logging and monitoring
is handled with Weights & Biases [Bie20] while reproducibility is ensured with the configuration file
capabilities of Hydra [Yad19].
All code can found in the following GitHub repository: https://github.com/petergroth/trajectory_
forecasting.
The full YAML-configuration files for all models in this thesis can be found in the models-folder in
the repository.
These files include the precise model and dataset configurations as well as the full
training, validation, and testing details. The two main training, validation, and testing scripts are
the train_nbody_model.py and train_waymo_model.py files located in the src/training_modules
directory. These are controlled using shell scripts in a Linux environment. The control scripts are not
shared as they include potentially sensitive details.
The n-body models are trained locally using a laptop with an Intel i7-6700HQ processor. The Waymo
models are trained using the HPC-infrastructure provided by DTU. Training has mostly been performed
using a single Nvidia Tesla A100 GPU (40 GB of memory) with an Intel Xeon Gold 6226R CPU.

APPENDIX B
n-body system
B.1
Simulation details
For the synthetic dataset we generate a total of 2,000 simulations, each of which requires a set of initial
parameters. These have all been tuned to create “sensible” simulations which often have the bodies
oscillating around the origin while interacting:
• The number of particles/bodies is a randomly generated integer between 6 and 10, inclusive.
• The starting positions of all bodies are sampled from a uniform distribution with a lower and
upper boundary of −80 and 80 metres, respectively.
• The initial velocities are independently sampled from a Gaussian distribution centred at the origin
with a standard deviation of 15 metres.
• The angular velocities are set to zero as these were found to have a negative impact on the
interpretability of the dynamics.
• All bodies are given a charge which is sampled from a zero-centred Gaussian distribution with a
standard deviation of 0.001, thereby resulting in both positively and negatively charged bodies.
• The mass of each body is computed as the absolute value of a sample from a Gaussian distribution
with a mean of 3×1010 and a standard deviation of 5×109. The reason for choosing large masses
is that the gravitational attractions are the main driver of the dynamics. In order to observe
noticeable position and velocity changes over a time-span of a few seconds, the body masses have
to be significant.
• The radii of the circular bodies are computed as their masses divided with 1010 resulting in objects
with radii of 1-3 metres.
We constrain the simulations to two dimensions thereby limiting them to the plane for easier visual-
isations. Each body is thus represented by five features; its x and y positions, its x and y velocities,
and its radius. The simulation updates its values every 0.1 seconds and runs for a total of 5.1 seconds,
resulting in 51 time steps per object.

APPENDIX C
Waymo Open Motion
dataset
C.1
Detailed description
We here provide a detailed description of the Waymo Open Motion dataset. The dataset is comprised
of a total of 103,354 segments. A segment is defined as the collection of sequentially sampled data,
containing data on multiple agents as well as 3D map data and traﬀic light information. Each multi-
agent segment is comprised of sensory data sampled at 10 Hz over a period of 20 seconds. These 20
second segments have been further divided into 9.1 second scenes. Each scene is comprised of three
parts:
• A one second history containing ten samples,
• a 0.1 second current state containing a single sample,
• and an eight second future containing 80 samples.
For the purpose of this thesis, we combine the history and current states into a 1.1 second history of
11 samples.
Each scene contains three parts that describe the agents, the road graph, and the traﬀic lights, re-
spectively. Each agent has the following (dynamic) attributes defined at each time step:
• The agent’s x, y, and z coordinates in a global coordinate system measured in metres.
• The agent’s x and y velocities measured in metres per second.
• The agent’s height, width, and length measured in metres.
• The agent’s bounding box yaw describing its orientation measured in radians.
• The agent’s velocity yaw describing the orientation of its velocity vector measured in radians.
• A valid flag which indicates whether or not data is available for the remaining attributes.
Each agent has additional (static) attributes which are constant for the full scene:
• The agent’s type, which can be either unset, vehicle, pedestrian, cyclist, or other.
• An indicator of whether or not the trajectories of the given agent is used for computation of
metrics.
• An indicator of whether or not the agent is the autonomous vehicle used for data collection.
The road graph for each scene is defined by a variable number of sampled points, each of which contains
the following information:
• The sampled point’s x, y, and z coordinates in the same global coordinate system as the agents.
==PAGE:72==
C.2 Selection and splitting
63
• The type of the sampled point, which can be one of 16 types. The specific types can be found
here https://waymo.com/open/data/motion/tfexample, under the roadgraph_samples/type
key.
• A valid flag which indicates whether or not data is available for the remaining attributes.
Furthermore, each scene has traﬀic light information describing the locations of traﬀic lights as well as
their past, present, and future states.
The dataset can be accessed via the Waymo Open Dataset website: https://waymo.com/open/
while a full description of all features can be found on the following website: https://waymo.com/
open/data/motion/tfexample.
C.2
Selection and splitting
We here describe the selection and splitting process that has been applied to Waymo dataset. The
dataset has been split into training, validation, and testing splits by its creators at 70 %, 15 %, and 15
% proportions, respectively. The training subset is available as 1,000 compressed files each containing
approximately 450 sequences. Each of these files is roughly 600 MiB, whereby the size of the total
training dataset is 600 GiB. The validation subset is similarly available as 150 compressed files. These
large files are unsuitable to work with considering the scope of this thesis. We will therefore rely on
subsets of the full datasets.
For training we have selected the first ten compressed training files resulting in 4,942 sequences.
For validation, we have selected the first six compressed validation files resulting in 1,715 sequences.
For testing, we have selected the next six validation files resulting in 1,776 sequences. We perform our
testing using data intended for validation, as the future states of the test data has been withheld so as
not to be inadvertently used in the modelling process.
C.3
Map channels
The following list shows the eight aggregated map channels that have been extracted from the road
graph data of the Waymo Open Motion dataset:
• LaneCenterFreeway, LaneCenterSurfaceStreet: centrelines of road segments representing where
cars expected to be.
• LaneCenterBikeLane: centrelines of bike path segments.
• RoadLineBrokenSingleWhite: broken white lines which vehicles are allowed to cross under some
circumstances.
• RoadLineSingleWhite, RoadLineSolidDoubleWhite: solid white lines which vehicles are not
allowed to cross.
• RoadLineBrokensingleYellow, RoadLineBrokenDoubleYellow: broken yellow lines which vehi-
cles are allowed to cross under some circumstances.
• RoadLineSolidSingleYellow, RoadLineSolidDoubleYellow, RoadLinePassingDoubleYellow:
solid yellow lines which vehicles are not allowed to cross.
• RoadEdgeBoundary, RoadEdgeMedian: lines which vehicles are not allowed to cross.
• StopSign, Crosswalk, SpeedBump: points and lines where velocities are expected to change.
