Abstract
This thesis presents the design and development of a low-cost, low-power geolo-
cation tracking system intended for asset management applications.
With the rise of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies, the demand for efficient,
affordable tracking solutions has grown significantly, enabling the monitoring of
assets across vast distances and varied environments.
The primary goal of this project is to create a tracking device with a target cost of
approximately 100 DKK per unit at a production volume of 1,000 units, providing
reliable location data positioning the device withing 20m of the actual location,
while achieving a battery life of up to four years.
The approach leverages LoRaWAN and WiFi, to communicate and obtain geolo-
cation estimates, alongside optimized hardware and software designs to balance
cost, power efficiency, and performance.
Results demonstrate that the developed tracker achieves a bill of materials rang-
ing from 84.81 to 101.29 DKK depending on configuration, a battery life of 3.93,
and a geolocation accuracy of 8 to 16 meters, thereby meeting most design tar-
gets.
However, limitations such as inconsistent LoRaWAN network coverage in urban
areas indicate areas for improvement. This work contributes to IoT-based asset
tracking by validating the feasibility of a low-cost, energy-efficient solution, offering
a practical option for both consumer and industrial applications and laying the
groundwork for future enhancements in low-cost tracking technology.

Introduction
Since the inception of the GPS satellite system in the 70’s, there has been an
interest in tracking assets. In the early days of GPS tracking, the technology was
reserved for military applications and it was not until the 90’s that the technology
was made available to the public.[1]
In the early stages, GPS tracking systems were primarily used for high-value as-
sets such as vehicles, ships, and aircraft. This focus was largely due to the high
cost and considerable power requirements of early GPS devices, making them
impractical for small or low-value items. Early tracking devices were bulky and
often required external power sources, limiting their applications to assets that
could accommodate the physical and energy demands of the technology.
In recent years, advancements in both geolocation-and network-technology have
led to significant improvements in cost, size, and power consumption, making
it economically viable to track a broader range of assets. The development of
low-power, wide-area network (LPWAN) technologies and improvements to GPS
devices, now allows for affordable, energy-efficient, and long-range communica-
tion. Further, with the expansion of the internet of things network, new geolocation
technologies have emerged.
As a result, tracking solutions can now be designed for smaller, lower-value assets
such as packages, pallets, and even personal items, creating new opportunities
for both businesses and consumers.
1.1
Background
The growth of IoT and development in the fields of network technology has made
it possible to create, affordable asset-tracking systems that work over long ranges
and extended time periods.
While GPS and cellular tracking systems remain effective for high-value assets,
they are often unsuitable for lower-cost items due to high power demands and
infrastructure costs and/or licensing fees.
By Combining Geolocation methods with LPWAN technologies, like LoRaWAN
and NB-IoT, address these limitations by enabling low-cost, low-power tracking
over wide areas without heavy infrastructure.
This thesis explores the design and implementation of a cost-effective and energy-
efficient tracking system using LP-WAN communication technologies.
1.1.1
Tracking system
A tracking system, at its core, has two main functionalities.
Firstly, the system has to be capable of placing itself on the world map. This can
be done in many ways, mostly through some kind of trilateration between three
or more known points. Common methods include GPS, WiFi triangulation, and

cellular network positioning. Each method has its own advantages and limitations
in terms of accuracy, power consumption, and cost.
Secondly, the system has to communicate this information to the user. This will
typically rely on some sort of wide-area network technology (WAN). The choice
of WAN technology is crucial and depends on factors such as range, power con-
sumption, data rate, and cost. Options include cellular networks, satellite commu-
nication, and Low Power Wide Area Networks (LP-WAN) like LoRaWAN, SigFox,
and NB-IoT. Each of these technologies offers different trade-offs that must be
considered based on the specific requirements of the tracking application.
1.1.2
Applications
Depending on the application of the tracker system, different elements of the de-
signed system will take preference.
As with any engineering problem, the system can be thought of as working with
a closed loop of rope, anchored at several points. Pulling the rope in one di-
rection to emphasize a particular attribute—such as cost, power efficiency, or
performance—will inevitably cause tension at the other anchor points.
Depending on what the purpose of the tracker is, there might be different anchor
points. A tracker for a car can use the battery in the car, and therefore doesn’t
have the same need for efficiency as a tracker for a pet. However the precision
of the location can be severely important for instance, for insurance purposes, in
case of theft.
A system tracking and locating dementia-patients, might not have cost as a con-
straint, but might have a precision or update interval constraint, leading to a cost
and power consumption increase. Whereas a tracking system for wild life mon-
itoring, might not have to be very accurate or update its location very often, but
have to log the location data of the tracked animal over a longer duration.
In the field of low-cost geolocation devices, the main focus, from a industrial point
of view is low-cost high-quantity asset tracking. These trackers are attached to
items that, in themself hold little value, but provide important data for the product
owner. This could be for precise fleet management for rental bike or e-scooter
companies or relocation of lost tools in production facilities.
In the realm of consumer electronics, it is purely for tracking objects that hold
too little value to warrant an expensive tracking system. This could be a personal
transportation vehicle such as a bike or scooter, or the children’s jacket that keeps
going missing.
The low price of the tracked object means that a the tracker has to be of equally
low cost, not to add costs to the total cost of operation.
In this Thesis, we are focusing on the consumer end-user, and the tracking of
every day objects.

Bluetooth trackers
In recent years, consumer electronics companies Samsung and Apple have both
entered the consumer asset tracking market with a geolocation device each.
The Airtag from apple and SmartTag from Samsung, both utilize the same tech-
nology, peering of their respective ecosystem of devices. The devices uses Blue-

network device, and the signal strength between the devices is noted.[2] The dis-

the signal or Bluetooth RSSI.
The location information is then communicated through the external device back
to the end user, with the location of the tag.
Advantages
By piggybacking off of the networking modules inside the more
expensive phones, tablets, or smart watches, the tags can be produced at a very
low price-point, since no wide-area-network (WAN) technology has to be utilized.
Limitations
The two tracking systems from apple and Samsung, only work within
their respective eco-systems. If no devices from the correct manufacturer comes
by the tag, the location of the device cannot be updated, as the tag itself has no
way of communicating its location.
GNSS trackers
The more conventional way of tracking assets is by using GNSS/GPS to geolocate
the tracked unit. There is a several of companies making GNSS/GPS trackers,
and for good reason. GNSS/GPS trackers are able to pin point their location with
high precision and are capable of relaying the position of assets in real time while
moving. Making them ideal for tracking high cost items. GNSS/GPS technology
generally comes with high price tag and a high power consumption and a reliance
on line-of-sight to satellites for optimal functionality.
Advantages
The main advantage of utilizing GNSS/GPS technology is the high
precision and large coverage. The setup cost for a GNSS/GPS system is also
rather low, since the system does not require local infrastructure to work.
Limitations
As GNSS/GPS requires the device to communicate with 3-4 satel-
lites to locate its position accurately, the power consumption is quite high, making
it difficult to maintain operation on battery for longer durations. Further, GNSS/GPS
modules suffer from reduced performance and accuracy in urban areas, where the
line-of-sight to the satellites are obstructed[3].

Low-Power Wide-Area-Network (LP-WAN) trackers
LP-WAN technologies, such as LoRaWAN and NB-IoT, support Long-Range Low-
power communications.
NB-IoT
Is a cellular network technology, and operates in the licensed cellular
band. This allows good signal coverage both in the inside and outside environ-
ment, at the cost of paying a licensing fee, as with GPS. As the technology works
on the cellular network, the date throughput of the system is quite high, as com-
pared to other LP-WAN technologies.[4] As the technology work on the principles
of LTE, a technology already well established, current network providers already
cover most regions.
LoRaWAN
on the other hand, works in unlicensed frequency bands, offering
long range communication without a carrier, at low data rates. The communica-
tion is energy efficient, and the range is between 10 km in rural areas and 3 km
in urban areas. [5]. LoRaWAN relies on antenna stations, gateways, to relay the
communication to the system network server. There are very little tracking im-
plementations available utilizing LP-WAN technologies for consumers. However,
there is examples of technologies such as LoRaWAN being utilized in industrial
tracking applications. An example of this is the company, Iottag1, that makes
LoRaWAN trackers for small vehicles and personnel tracking.
Advantages
LoRaWAN offer long-range communication, at very low-power con-
sumption, with a low deployment cost compared to GPS or cellular technologies,
depending on use case. providing network coverage both inside and outside,
making tracking objects less dependent on environmental variables.
Limitations
Since the deployment of the LoRaWAN gateways are sparse, as
compared to the cellular network or even to the device network of the Bluetooth
trackers. Blind spots can occur in urban areas where there might be a lack of
gate-ways or base-stations for the devices to connect to.

costs and power usage. This goal can be split into four quantifiable sub-goals

1. Cost minimization The primary goal of this project is to develop a cost
efficient tracker device, keeping the total Bill-of-Materials cost under 100
DKK (14.55 USD) at 1000 devices devices.
2. Power optimization It is important the the developed system operates with
minimal power consumption, extending the battery life of the tracking device.

The system of the tracking device can be divided into 3 sections: geo-positioning
the device, communicating the position wirelessly to the system backend, and
displaying the information to the user.
In the following section, the most relevant theoretical elements for the thesis are
covered, together with the current state of the art within the topic areas.
2.1
IoT Network Technologies
For the tracking system to relay the found location back to the user, a wireless
communication technology is needed. Since the device is not fixed in a local en-
vironment and can move freely around in the outside environment, a wide-area
network technology is required. The following chapter explains the relevant tech-
nologies.
Wide-area network (WAN) technologies refer to network communication methods
capable of transmitting information over long distances. WAN covers multiple
communication technologies, Hereunder WWAN, LP-WAN and satellite.
2.1.1
LP-WAN
LP-WAN technologies, such as SigFox, LoRaWAN and NB-IoT, offer long-range
transmissions with lower costs and lower power consumption, at the expense of
lower data rates. Catering especially to the IoT world, where low-consumption,
and low data through-put is typical. [6]
Current LP-WAN technologies mostly rely on the star network topology, including
NB-IoT and LoRaWAN. In a star topology, all end devices in a network communi-
cate directly with a single base station or gateway, which then relays the informa-
tion to a network server, often via the internet. This setup conserves energy, as
each device only needs to relay its data to the gateway[7].
Selecting the right LP-WAN technology for a given application depends on several
factors, For this project the key elements are;
• Cost: The cost associated with the given network technology, this covers
both the cost of the physical radio hardware, and any licensing fees associ-
ated with the network use.
• Energy consumption: What are the energy requirements for transporting
the data; transmission.
• Regional network Coverage: How broadly is the technology adopted in
the region of implementation. Are the infrastructure in place to facilitate a
system using the given network technology, or will it have to be implemented.

2.2
Basics of NB-IoT
Narrowband internet of things (NB-IoT) is a LP-WAN technology designed specif-
ically for IoT applications. NB-IoT works on the licensed cellular frequency band,
and thereby require users to pay a licensing fee[4].
As NB-IoT is a cellular technology, leveraging the already implemented LTE tech-
nology, much of the infrastructure is already established. As a result, the net-
working industry’s adoption of the technology has therefore been fast, with most
regions in Denmark already covered with excellent network NB-IoT coverage[9].
While NB-IoT is less power efficient as when compared to that of LoRaWAN, it
offers greater network coverage as when compared to non-cellular options, such
as Sigfox or LoRa. This of course has to be weighed against the higher cost of
operation, as a result of networking fees, and the higher power consumption of
the technology as when compared to LoRa[10].
2.3
LoRa
LoRa stands for Long Range and is a proprietary spread spectrum modulation
technique, developed by Semtech, designed to provide long-range communica-
tion at a low power consumption level.
LoRa operates in the license-free ISM radio frequency bands: 868-870 MHz and
433 MHz in Europe, and 915 MHz and 433 MHz in the US. Additionally, LoRaWAN
operates only in the 858-870 MHz (EU) and 902-928 MHz (US) ranges[11].
LoRa represents the Physical layer of the LoRaWAN protocol and thus is respon-
sible for the physical modulation of the Transmissions of the system.
LoRa is build on Chirp spread spectrum (CSS) Technology, where data is encoded
using chirps. A chirp is a sinusoidal wave that linearly increases or decreases in
frequency over a time period, see fig. 2.1.
These frequency changes, called up-chirps and down-chirps, each represent one
symbol of data, encoding specific information based on the frequency shift.[12][13].
A LoRa device can be configured to different transmission parameters, altering
the system airtime, throughput and energy consumption. The five main elements
of the LoRa physical layer is;
• Transmission power
• Carrier Frequency
• Bandwidth
• Spreading Factor
• Coding Rate

Transmission power
The transmission power signifies the amplitude of which
the LoRa data transmission is send with, ranging from −4dBm to 20dBm, depend-
ing on local legislation. For the EU the transmission limit is set to 14dBm.[14]
Carrier Frequency
The Carrier frequency, or frequency plan, is the center fre-
quency of which the Lora Device is transmitting on. Again, This is largely governed
by the regional legislation and is, in the EU, locked to 858-870 MHz[11].
Bandwidth
The bandwidth defines the frequency range used for transmission.
A higher Bandwidth increases data rates by enabling faster frequency sweeps,
shortening transmission time and improving power efficiency, however, it reduces
sensitivity due to more noise, limiting range. A lower bandwidth increases sen-
sitivity by minimizing noise, enhancing long-range performance, though it lowers
data rates and may require costlier, precise hardware[15].
Commonly available settings like 125kHz , 250kHz, or 500kHz balance these
trade-offs, with higher bandwidth favoring speed and lower bandwidth prioritiz-
ing range and sensitivity, optimizing for application-specific needs like throughput,
distance, power use, and cost[16].
Spreading factor
The spreading factor symbolizes how quickly the system sweeps
across a given chirp symbol, influencing transmission distance, data rate, and
power consumption. LoRa modulation supports a range of spreading factors from
SF6 to SF12, allowing flexibility to adapt to various application needs[17].
A lower spreading factor, such as SF6, accelerates the sweep, enabling more

2.4.3
Classes
While the LoRaWAN end devices are always able to perform up-links to the net-
work gateways, specific rules has to be set for when the end device is available
for a system down-link. This is achieved by the implementation of classes. Lo-
RaWAN defines three operational classes for end devices, each specified to dif-
ferent use cases, by balancing power consumption and communication respon-
siveness. The classes define the behavior of the down-links from the gateway to
the end-devices [24].
• Class A: ’Aloha’ down-link. Every time the end device performs an up-link,
two windows for a downlink opens after specified delays. Since the class
A operational class does not activate unless the end device has to transfer
data, class A achieves the lowest air-time of the tree and thereby the lowest
power consumption[24].

2.4.4
Network architecture
The LoRaWan network employs a star-of-stars network architecture, to relay infor-
mation between the system elements[25]. To understand the star-of-stars typol-
ogy, it is necessary to first understand the star network typology. A star network
is a network architecture type, where several end-nodes connect to one central
node, in a configuration visually similar to a star. In the star of stars typology, these
central nodes, also connect to central nodes. The LoRaWAN system consists of
four network ’nodes’;
• End Devices: Hardware devices transmitting data using LoRaWAN proto-
col. These devices operate in one of the three classes, described in 2.4.3,
and are deployed in the field.

• Gateways: Intermediaries that receive LoRaWAN messages from end de-
vices and forward them to the network server via IP-based backhaul (e.g.,
Ethernet, WiFi or cellular)[26]. Multiple gateways can receive the same mes-
sage, enhancing coverage and redundancy[27].
• Network Server: The central hub that manages the network, performing
tasks such as device authentication, data routing, message deduplication,
and ADR adjustments. This is either handled by a local system for closed

• Application Servers: Process data from end devices, hosting applications

vice’s hardware design, as it directly impacts the bill of materials (BOM), power
consumption, and overall system complexity. The primary objectives are to mini-
mize implementation costs while ensuring reliable transmission of positional data
over the LoRaWAN network, in alignment with the system design requirements
outlined in Section 3.1.1. Three key factors guide this selection:
• Cost: The module must contribute to keeping the total device cost below
100 DKK per unit at a production scale of 1000 units.
• Power Consumption: Low power usage is essential to achieve the target
4-year battery life, particularly during transmission and sleep modes.
• Integration: Preference is given to modules with an internal microcontroller
unit (MCU) to handle the LoRaWAN stack, potentially reducing the need for
additional components and simplifying the design.
A comparative analysis of several LoRa modules was conducted, focusing on
these criteria. The evaluated options are summarized in Table 3.2, which includes

3.2.3
Battery & Power management
One of the main parts of the designed tracker system, after the MCU/LoRaWAN
module selection, battery, and the way the power from the battery is managed.
As the system is to be designed to run directly on the battery power of the device,
the battery has to be selected to accommodate this. further, as the system runs
on limited power source, the treatment of energy in the system is important, as it
is key to preserve as much energy as possible.
Selection of battery type
Selecting the right battery type and technology is very important to the functionality
of the tracker device. As the battery is the main source of power for the device,
the type and specifications of the selected battery will have a great influence on
the system performance, design of the rest of the device.
Since the system runs directly on the battery voltage of the system, it is key to
find a battery with a voltage as high as possible within the maximum rating of the
HT-CT62 module.
To keep the design of the tracker small and compact, it is decided that the battery
has to come in a coin-cell format.
The three main factors to look into when selecting the battery for this low-power
IoT device, and battery powered electronics in general, are the nominal battery
voltage, capacity and Discharge Rate. These three elements will have a big im-
pact on how the device power delivery is designed and thus effectively both the
price and longevity of the tracker device.
Firstly, the battery type will decide the un-modulated voltage level of the system.
This is of importance, as this level has to be modulated to be within the MCU’s
operating limits and altering the voltage comes at an extra cost of either a step-
down or a step-up regulator, depending on battery cell voltage, which both adds
price and energy consumption to the system. Ideally, the nominal battery voltage

of the system lies between 2.7V and 3.5V to avoid the need for a voltage regulator,

To minimize the size of the tracker, it is decided that the batteries have to come in a

a large cell form-factor is chosen to maximize the capacity of the system. The
CR2450 presents both a large battery capacity typically around 620mAh whilst
maintaining a relatively small footprint. Whilist other battery types in the same
formfactor, such as the Lithium-Ion battries LIR2450, their high nominal voltage
and low capacity are unsuited for long-lifetime operation. They do, however have

can fully discharge within 1 to 1/2 an hour of operation, giving an effective dis-

rated to 120mA, this results in a dicharge rate of between 120 to 240mAh[50][51].
conversly, a CR2450 normally allow for a pulse discharge rate of between 20mA
to 50mA[52][53]. Based on the typical current draws under operation for the HT-

into the design, in the form of a current buffer solution.
Shut-down timer design
To limit the passive drain on the system, an essential part of the design is to
control the state of the device where it is not communicating, i.e, controlling used
the standby power. The simplest way to minimize the consumption of the device
in the deactivated state is to turn of the system when it is not in use, the same way
one would with any other appliance.
The difficulty here is that the device has to transmit an information payload peri-
odically, meaning if the device is turned off, it has to be able to turn itself back on
again.
This constrain forces the system to always have some subsystem running in the
background, to manage if its time to send a message or if the geo tag has been trig-
gered manually by an external trigger method. This periodic wake-up/shutdown of
the device can be achieved in multiple ways, for the tested solutions in this thesis,
all relying in timer implementations keeping track of the time passed since the last
activation.
• Power Gating using: Uses a dedicated ultra-low-power timer IC, for this
comparison the TPL5510[54], that completely disconnects main system power
while maintaining timing through a nano-ampere-range internal oscillator.
This approach eliminates most leakage current in the powered-off state,
15nA for the TPL5510, but requires additional circuitry for voltage domain
isolation that may introduce some leakage current.
• Deep Sleep: utilizes the micro-controllers’ low-power modes (e.g., ESP32’s
Ultra-Low-Power sleep-mode) that maintain minimal state retention while
disabling most peripherals. While simpler to implement through software,
this method retains some leakage current from always-on memory elements.
Going by the estimated numbers from Espressive[55], it is expected that
the ESP32 uses 8.3uA in deep-sleep and 23.8mA when operating. In the

3.3
Software design
Much of the the largest sum of the capabilities of the tracker device, is handled
in software. From the Uplink from the physical tracker unit and all the way to the
user interface is handled in software.
3.3.1
Embedded software
The embedded software is the ”first line of defense” when it comes to maintaining
the battery of the system and ensuring that the collected data are passed on to
the system back-end.
To conserve the battery power of the system, doing checks along the way while
the system operates, ensures that data is not sent too frequently, or that the same
position is not sent multiple times. To best limit the consumption of power, these
checks has to be implemented to verify the current state of the device and the
data.
Activation check
As the device is activated every two hours, two out of the three
activations per cycle are to be ignored. By implementing a check, in the very start
of the programming, that checks a unsigned integer variable ’boot number’ saved
in the MCU flash memory. Every time the device is started the variable is checked.
If the system has not been activated three times since the last activation, one is
added to the boot number, and the device is turned off again. At the third activation
the device passes the boot check.
This approach has the benefit of allowing the system to react according to the
send state of the device. This allows the system to maintain the boot number,
if the LoRaWAN transmission is incomplete, the device will try again at the next
available system cycle, instead of waiting another 6 hours.
New Access-points
The next check to make is right after the WiFi Scan to
locate the nearest access points has been completed. The system scans the
Exploring the Limits of Affordability:
29
==PAGE:40==
nearby area for WiFi access points, and logs the four networks with the best signal
strength. If these channels differ from the last set of networks, stored in the flash
memory, the access points and battery status are packaged in a transmission
payload and send. The old saved access-points are overwritten, with the new
sent access points and the device is turned off.
By checking if any new access points are found, the system avoids resending
redundant information to the system back-end, and thereby cuts down on the
most power-consuming intensive part of the system activation.
Low battery
Lastly, to alert the user that the end device is running out of power,
the tracker will override the new access-points check, and transmit anyway if the
battery status is low. This feature is implemented to ensure that tags running out
of battery is not confused with a event where the tracker is either damaged or out
of range.

Mechanical Design
The mechanical design challenge of the tracker design lies in the ability to contain
the device and the antenna and insulating it from the outside environment, while
making it possible to change the batteries of the device.
3.4.1
Enclosure design
As stated in the project requirements, the system requires a hermeneutically sealed
shell to hold the PCB and internals to protect the components, batteries and anten-
nas from dust and water, which can lead to corrosion shorts. The enclosure has
to be as small as possible, while maintaining weather and dust proofing. Further,
the designed box must offer a way to change the batteries of the device without
the use of specialized tool or equipment.
In this section, the different approaches to these questions are evaluated to find
the best path forward.
Sealing the enclosure
Creating a seal that allows the enclosure to be opened and closed repeatedly
while maintaining a tight, weatherproof barrier proposes the main challenge in the
Exploring the Limits of Affordability:
33
==PAGE:44==
mechanical design of the tracker. This issue has been addressed in numerous
ways, each offering distinct advantages and trade-offs depending on the applica-
tion. Since the tracker is intended for non-underwater use, the enclosure does
not need to withstand significant pressure (e.g., submersion at depth), but it must
still provide reliable protection against dust and water seeping into the enclosure,
such as rain or splashes.
To eliminate water entering the device, a barrier has to be established between
the closing mechanism of the device. This can either be a rubber ring, such as an
O-ring, or another type of sealing method like a flat gasket or adhesive seal.
As one of the goal stated for this project is to make a system based on coin-cell
batteries, it is as an extension of this limitation, necessary for the user to open the
device and inserts or change the batteries of the system.
As a adhesive solution would inherently make this very difficult design-wise as the
adhesive would have to be reapplied after a battery change, and has to be applied
in the correct way, to ensure the seal is water-tight.
The gasket seal does not suffer from this constraint as the seal is created by the
pressure created from squeezing a rubber material between two surfaces.
Further, there is the closing mechanism itself, which both decided how the system
is interfaced with and how the seal has to be incorporated into the design. For this
project, the focus was put on simplicity, to maintain a low cost as possible, and
functionality making the user interactions with the design as simple as possible.
To achieve this, three closure mechanisms was investigated.
O-Ring snap-fit closure
The rubber O-ring provides a great seal for the outside
environment, and balanced with a snap-fit with adequate force to hold the o-ring
compressed, it makes for good seal, whilst making the changing of batteries easy.
Further, as the snapping mechanism is a part of the lid itself, only three parts are
required for the design to work, lowering the cost of production.
The primary downside of this locking mechanism is the design complexity is higher
than the other solutions, and the tolerances for the o-ring seal and the force of the
snap-fit has to be balanced in such a way that the o-ring gets compressed, without
the lid snapping off unprovoked.
Screw-on lid with rubber gasket
A simpler alternative, based on the same
sealing principle as the O-ring snap-fit, is a screw-on lid with a flat rubber gasket.
This design consists of a lid, four screws, and a rubber gasket shaped to en-
sure the screws are positioned outside the sealing line, preventing water seeping
through the screw holes.
This approach benefits from straightforward production and less stringent toler-
ance requirements, as the screws can be tightened to compress the gasket more
firmly than a snap-fit mechanism might allow. This adjustability enables a secure
seal even with minor manufacturing variances, making it more forgiving and po-
tentially more reliable in harsh conditions.

On the downside, this method requires more components than the snap-fit design,
which relies on the lid’s material to provide the compressive force. The inclusion
of screws also means users need a tool, such as a screwdriver, which can be
inconvenient.
Latch locking mechanism
Another tool-free sealing method is the latch locking
mechanism, which offers a balance between ease of use and secure closure, at
the cost of design complexity. This design method relies one or more latches,
typically mounted on the lid or base, that engage with corresponding catches to
hold the lid in place. Paired with a rubber gasket or O-ring along the sealing line
to ensure a watertight barrier when closed.
The primary advantage of this method is its user-friendly operation. The latch
mechanism allows the enclosure to be opened and closed quickly without tools,
making it ideal for applications where accessibility is a priority. Additionally, it
requires less precise production tolerances than the snap-fit solution, as the latch
can be designed with adjustable tension to compress the gasket effectively.
However, the latch mechanism suffer from increased complexity and cost due to
the need for additional components, such as pivoting rods for the latches, and the
latches themselves.. Its sealing performance might also be less robust than the
screw-on or snap-fit methods, especially in high-vibration or impact-prone envi-
ronments where the latch could accidentally disengage.
Mechanism evaluation
To identify the most suitable closure mechanism for the
geolocation tracker’s enclosure, three closure options (snap-fit with O-ring, screw-
on lid with gasket, and latch locking mechanism) were evaluated based on four
main parameters:
• Cost: An estimation of the total production cost, derived from the number of
individual components, material expenses, and assembly labor. The main
factors in this estimation is the number of different materials used and the
number of parts in the design.
• Design Complexity: The degree of difficulty in designing and manufac-
turing the mechanism, considering factors such as tolerance requirements,
number of movable parts, and potential failure modes (e.g. material fatigue
or misalignment). Higher complexity increases the risk of manufacturing
defects, impacting reliability.
• Accessibility: The ease with which the enclosure can be opened and closed,
measured by the time and effort required, and the need for tools. This is par-
ticularly significant for large-scale deployments where operators may need
to service hundreds of units efficiently.
• Sealing Reliability: The reliability of the seal is a critical factor, assessed
based on its ability to prevent water and dust ingress under environmental
stressors such as rain, humidity, and vibration, aligning with the geolocation
tracker’s requirement for a hermetic enclosure.

As the system is a battery-driven device, it is critical to understand the consump-

As the system is a battery powered device, driven directly on the battery voltage,

maintaining the correct operating voltage. Sourcing too much current current from
the battery will cause the battery voltage to drop as a result of the battery’s high
internal resistance under load. This can potentially result in a MCU ’brown-out’,
meaning that the microcontroller doesn’t have the required voltage to boot.

4.2.2
Battery system
To maximize the battery life of the device, a CR2450 battery was chosen for the de-
vice, as it is readily available and provides a comparatively high battery capacity,
as compared to other similar coin-cells. The specific battery chosen for this project
is a Duracell CR2450PG0920, as it provides a high capacity of 620mAh[53] and
offers a high pulse drain rating of 50mA for 1 second[53]. Several other batteries
of the 2450 form factor has been investigated, however, none of these stated any
maximum pulse drain rating for the batteries in their datasheet, which is why the
Duracell battery was chosen.
During LoRaWAN transmission, the system experiences a pulse drain of up to 120
mA as stated in the HT-CT62 datasheet, with most of this current drawn directly
from the battery. As noted in the datasheet, this significant pulse drain exceeds
the capability of a single battery cell, causing the battery voltage to fall below the
micro-controller’s minimum supply threshold of 2.7V. This drop is due to the bat-
tery’s internal resistance; per Ohm’s Law, higher internal resistance results in a
greater voltage reduction under load. By implementing a second battery in paral-
lel, the system both gets to distribute the load onto two current sources, reducing
the internal resistance under load, and improves the battery-life of the device by
doubling the battery capacity.

4.2.3
Timer power-gating
The TPL5110 low-power timer offers a highly efficient timing solution, specifically
engineered to optimize power gating in duty-cycled or battery-dependent systems,
such as the tracking device under consideration. With an ultra-low power con-
sumption of just 35 nA[57], this component enables precise control over the power
supply line, significantly reducing standby current during system sleep modes.
As shown in the analysis section 3.2.3, integrating the TPL5110 achieved a the-
oretical 14.7% efficiency gain while adding only a modest cost of 2.8 DKK to the
system’s BOM.
This indicates that the advantages of incorporating the TPL5110 outweigh its im-
plementation costs, enhancing the system’s battery lifetime substantially.
The operation of the TPS5110 has 2 stages; A delay stage where the output pin,
DRV, is held low while the IC waits for either a timer wake-up or a manual override,
and a Drive stage, where the DRV pin is held high until it receives a logic high
signal on the DONE pin.
Timer settings
The timer period, which determines the duration the tracker sys-
tem circuit is disabled, is set using a resistor from the delay pin on the IC to
ground.
The specific delay can be calculated or picked from the table in the
datasheet[57]Since this system relies on the longest delay option, a resistor value
of 170KΩis chosen from the table.
Shake-trigger overide
Given that the system is activated every 6 hours, the
tracker could potentially move very far without the user knowing for example in
case the tracked object is stolen. To inform the end user about the latest position
of the device, a shake trigger override has to be implemented to trigger a location
transmission, before the timer runs out.
The TPS5110 features a timer-override input, where a high signal on the De-
lay/M_DRV pin is registered for 20ms, due to the internal de-bounce function of
the input, will enable the DRV output of the device.

”switch”. Since this closure is too short for the TPL5110’s M_DRV input to register,

Firmware
The firmware on the tracker device is event-driven firmware, where the system
wakes on either the timer overflows, or a shake event is registered. As the device
only has one job, the device is meant to complete the assigned job as quickly as
possible to avoid wasting energy by the system idling or getting hung up in loops.
The philosophy behind this programming method is, that if the device doesn’t

connect in the first try, the likelihood of connecting in the second or third is relatively
slim, and its perhaps better to try again in two hours. This way, if the device is
currently in a ’dead-spot’ it will conserve its energy until it gets moved back into a
spot where it can find the network.
The system has three tasks, verifying if the boot is valid, scraping access point
information, and transmitting the gathered information to the system back-end. As
explained in the subsection 3.3.1, the system revolves around three main checks,
however, several other fail checks has been implemented to stop the system if
any critical parts fail. This is done to save energy, as there is no reason to per-
form a network scan, if the network join fails or any reason to perform the LoRa
transmission if the network scan fails.
The first iteration of the firmware utilized the LORAWAN_APP.h stack/ library, de-
veloped by Heltec for their modules, including the HT-CT62 module. While work-

Boot sequence
The boot sequence is the first step in the operation of the firmware,
ensuring the device powers on reliably and initializes the WiFi subsystem and
LoRa Stack for the coming tasks.
Upon waking, the firmware initializes the internal EEPROM of the ESP32-C3 to
manage a boot counter, stored at the address BOOT_COUNT_ADDR.
This counter, a 4-byte integer, tracks the number of times the device has booted,
providing valuable data for debugging and operational analysis. If the counter
value is invalid (e.g. negative or excessively large, such as greater than 10,000),
it is reset to zero as this is presumably an error stemming from the memory not
being initialized. After each boot, the counter increments and is committed to
EEPROM for persistent storage.
As the system starts up, the firmware checks if the boot count is divisible by 3
(bootCount % 3 != 0); if not, it shuts down the system via the SYS_SDWN pin,
enforcing a periodic operation schedule to further conserve energy.
A notable power optimization occurs during the initial boot. Enabling WiFi triggers
a mandatory RF calibration process, which draws a significant current spike (ap-
proximately 240 mA). From the ESP documentation[59], it is stated that there are
three calibration methods for the device; Full-Calibration, Partial-Calibration and
No calibration .
• Full-Calibration A complete calibration of the RF system, takes a 100ms
longer than the partial calibration. Only happens if the Non-Volatile storage
(NVS) of the device has been erased, or corrupted.
• Partial-Calibration A short, partial RF calibration, lasts about 10ms with a
consumption of 240mA. Initialized every time the WiFi system is initialized.

WiFi-Scan
The device utilizes WiFi access points to determine its geolocation,
a technique that relies on scanning nearby networks and using their data with
services like the Google Geolocation API.
The WiFi scanning process is initiated in station mode (WIFI_STA), configured
via the WiFi.h library library, provided by Espressiv. The scan is synchronous
(async = false), excludes hidden networks (show_hidden = false), and reduces
scan time to 50 ms per channel (compared to the default 150 ms) to minimize
energy use. The WiFiscan() function performs this task, returning the number of
detected networks.
The firmware stores data on up to four access points—the maximum needed for
accurate geolocation, in a network struct array (networks[MAX_NETWORKS]).
Each struct contains:
• RSSI The received signal strength indicator for the network. stored as a
32-bit integer
• MAC Address A 6-byte array containing the unique adress to the network.
If less than four networks are found in the scan, the scan is considered unsuc-
cessful and the system shuts down via the SYS_SDWN output pin connected to
the done signal of the TPL5110 IC. For valid scans, the four best network can-
didates are selected from this RSSI value, and the RSSI and MAC credentials
are stored in the struct array, from the WiFi.h library functions, WiFi.RSSI(i) and
WiFi.BSSID(i, networks[i].mac). The scan is then deleted with WiFi.scanDelete()
To free the memory.
The firmware compares the newly found access points with those from the previ-
ous scan, loaded from flash memory. The comparison, handled by areNetworks-
Different(), focuses solely on MAC addresses, ignoring RSSI. This is chosen as
the location is considered to be similar enough to not warrant another transmis-
sion if the same found networks, found to be the best candidates, are the same
as the four best candidates found in the previous scan.

LoRa Transmission
Data transmission to the back-end is facilitated by the Lo-
RaWAN Stack, managed by the RadioLib library. The hardware abstraction for
the LoRa Physical layer is initialized with an SX1262 radio module, connected via
SPI. The LoRaWAN stack is configured using LoRaWANNode, operating in the
EU868 band.
The device can be configured to either connect through ABP or OTAA, where ABP
is considered favorable to the system, due to its lower transmission airtime. This
is again a power consumption concern, as the join procedure of OTAA requires
the system to join the network before sending information, requiring another LoRa
transmission. However, in testing, it proved slightly better reception to the local
gateway setup, and for debugging, it’s easier to locate where the issue in trans-
mission might lie.
When the device is set to transmit in OTAA-mode, a dual-connection connection
fall-back has been put into place. This means, that the system first will try to join
the TTN network, if that doesn’t work, the device will try to connect to the CiBicom
network. Should that also fail, the system shuts down.
For the ABP-mode, the join process begins with a data rate of 3 (initialDR = 3),
equating a spreading factor of 9, and success is confirmed by a return state of the
join function of RADIOLIB_ERR_NONE or RADIOLIB_LORAWAN_NEW_SESSION.
If the join fails, the system shuts down.

Webhook integration
To transport the decoded information from the TTN stack server to the cloud-
computing system back-end, a communication interface is necessary to commu-
nicate between the applications.
TTN stack allows for multiple different integrations for inter-application communi-
cation, here under Webhooks. As the data from the TTN server flows one-way
only, as we are only receiving information from the tag, it makes sense to look into
the implementation of a Webhook.
A Webhook is an event-based lightweight means of communication, made to au-
tomate responses for software. It works by triggering an HTTP request, usually a
POST—to a pre-configured endpoint, whenever a specific event occurs (such as
the reception of new sensor data). In the context of TTN, when a new message
from a tag is received, the TTN server automatically dispatches the JSON-like
formatted data payload to the cloud back-end.
As the data is immediately dispatched as soon as it is received by the geo loca-
tion tag, the communication between tag and the system server-side back-end is
nearly real-time, limited only by the processing speed of which the TTN server
can format and pass on the received data.

4.4.4
Database
To maintain continuity of location information across browser sessions and multi-
ple instances, it is crucial to store the data acquired from the Google Cloud Func-
tion in a persistent, real-time accessible database. Otherwise all the data is only
kept in the locally in cache, and will reset of the window is closed. This is sub-
optimal as the newest data should both always be presented to the user, and
without saving the information, the user has no way to see the historic locations
of the tracker.
For this purpose, the system uses Firebase, a cloud-hosted NoSQL database, as
its database. This is primiarily done for two reasons, the first is that since Firestore
is a part of the Google Cloud enviroment, it makes setting up the system easier,
as there are less API keys, and intersystem connections that has to be made[62].
Secondly, The database Firestore offers noSQL database structures.
NoSQL
stands for Not only SQL, and refers to the storing of informating in databases
in alternative formats to the regularly used SQL. For this application, it is utilized
to simplify the data structure of the back-end setup, and to provide readability to
the database[62].

form the TTN Webhook, A new JSON-like object is generated , containing the

5.2
Battery life-time
The Battery lift-time of the device, is inherently linked to three main factors, the
Spreading factor and transmission strength of which the data is sent, and the WiFi
scan duration of which networks are found. As the LoRa coverage in the city,
where the device will be able to find networks for the geolocation of the devices,
the LoRaWAN coverage can be sparse, and the transmission strength is therefore
held locked at the highest legal limit of 14dBm[14].
There are therefore two parameters that can be tuned to ensure that the device
remains online for the 4 year duration stated in the requirements. This being the
WiFi scan duration and the spreading factor.
It is wished to maximize these two factors to ensure as reliable a tracking service
as possible. To accomplish this, whilst remaining within the desired lifetime spec-
ification, two series of tests are performed: one sweeping the network scan and
one sweeping the spreading factors available.
The measurements were performed utilizing a STLINK-V3PWR debugger and
power-meter. As the power-meter function of the STLINK-V3PWR is sensitive to
even small power capacitors, the system was allowed to ’soft start’ while being
held in reset before starting the power-monitor.

WiFi Scan consumption
The datasheet states that the MCU/transceiver[46]
module consumes 80mA when scanning for WiFis, however, how much energy
is consumed is dependent on the time spent scanning and what scan options are
enabled. Here the main factor in the device consumption that is within control,
utilizing the Espressives standard WiFi library, the time spent scanning each wifi
channel for networks, what channels are scanned and if the scan is active of
passive.
The WiFi 2.4GHz band is divided into 13 sub-bands. These bands, called chan-
nels, are picked by the router depending on the interference on the different bands
within the vicinity of the router, and it is therefore difficult to pick what bands the
system should scan to ensure access-points are found, and all channels are there-
fore enabled[65].
The time spent scanning each channel, is simply the time the device either probing
or waiting for network beacons , depending on if the performed scan is active
or passive. Actively probing does draw more current during use, as it have to
actively probe each channel for networks, and then wait to receive a response.
Passive scans, relies on the access-points to periodically transmit a ssid beacon,
this interval is normally around 100ms[66]. This means that to ensure that the
system will receive a beacon while scanning, the time spend on each channel
has to be equal higher than the beacon interval.

with the hardware restriction of the buffer capacitors only being able to support the
spreading factor 9-10 and below, an decrease in the device consumption, must
come from the WiFi scanning.

the more networks will be found, as long as networks remain within range of the
tracker. However, since it can be fairly assumed that the closest networks found
from the 100 ms scan will also be among the closest networks found from the
300 ms scan. Understanding that the system discards all other network readings
except for the four with the strongest RSSI, the benefit of finding additional weak
networks is very little. Shorter scans than the 100ms scan time, though further
extending battery life, risk not collecting enough access-points data in low network
areas.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore how cheaply geo-location tracking can be effectively
done. With the overarching goal of developing a low-cost, low-power geo-location
tracker that meets the design specifications set at the beginning of the thesis, see
section 3.1.1.
The project aimed to develop a functioning tracker with the total bill-of-materials
cost below 100 DKK per unit. While the current implemented hardware goes over
the price target price with a price point a of 101.29DKK. This is primarily due to
limitations of bulk pricing on the antenna hardware as outlined in section 5.4. The
thesis outlines solutions to minimize this cost, either selecting a cheaper battery
or antenna, and with those changes bring the price down below the set limit to
84.81DKK.
With an estimated battery life of up to 7.5-years with the correct power-gating timer
settings, powered by two coin-cell batteries, proves that the system life-time of 4-
years is possible. While the system at the time of writing has a bug where the timer
will trigger prematurely, with a slightly varying interval of down to 3 hours and 12
minutes, this still equals out to 3.93 years of service, with current LoRaWAN and
WiFi parameters. This can be circumvented by setting the firmware boot counter
to expect 6 boots between activations instead of three, however, this is a crude
fix to the problem.
The tracker is able to achieve a geolocation positioning accuracy of below 20
meters, utilizing Googles Geolocate API and external 2.4GHz antennas to scan
nearby networks, and accurately place the device in the world map with an achiev-
able accuracy down to 8 meters. This is of course dependent on the network
coverage in the area, with the better positioning estimate with closer clustered
networks.
The data generated by the tracker hardware is processed through a back-end
system, which converts the raw access-point data into meaningful location infor-
mation. This system ensures that the data is accurately interpreted, filtered, and
structured before being presented to the user in a clear and accessible format on
a cloud-hosted web application.
While the enclosure unfortunately could not be tested to proof that the waterproof-
ing seal, the seal design type is a trialed and tested design, and is expected to be
able to seal the system from the outside environment.

6.1
Future work
While the device functionally works, and meets most of the set requirements, there
are still elements which could benefit from further work and improvement.
6.1.1
Improvements to the current system
The following list, should be seen as a system fixes, to-do list and contains minor
reworks and improvements to be made to the system.
• Fix the power-gating timer so that it wakes up in the correct interval
• shrink the physical size of the enclosure to fit the second version tracker
PCB
• Source and test cheaper 868MHz LoRa antennas to reduce the antennas
impact on the Bill of Materials
• Test the Akyga batteries to verify that they can provide the necessary voltage
under the system load during transmission.
6.1.2
Change MCU/Transceiver
As talked to in section 5.4 the total cost of the system could be reduced substan-
cially by implementing a separate MCU and LoRa Radio. As it was first thought
that the provider of the MCU also had to be able to provide a LoRaWAN stack,
only options having this feature was considered. However as explained, using the
stack presented in the open-source library RadioLib[64], eliminates that limitation.
This allows the MCU to be chosen (more) freely, and allows for a cheaper or more
power efficient solution to be put into place, instead of the combination module.
6.1.3
Dual Network-provider
A way to improve on the poor LoRaWAN network coverage could be to implement
a dual-network-provider feature, where the system would try to establish OTAA
connection to one provider, and if that fails or gets rejected, would try another
provider. While the implementation of the system was started, due to the back-
end system expecting the LoRaWAN network to perform the payload formatting,
a feature not all LoRaWAN network providers allows for, the system was never
implemented. By moving the formatting of the end-device payload formatting to
the Back-end and allow the LoRaWAN Network server to push the raw payload
80
Exploring the Limits of Affordability:
==PAGE:91==
directly to the back-end, the system should be able to comminate with any network
server.
6.1.4
Experiment with different LP-WAN technologies
Throughout the testing of the tracker device, the main issue has been to connect
to the LoRaWAN network in densely populated areas where there are large num-
bers of access-points available to map the tracker device. This is presumably due
to the LoRaWAN network provider used in the project, The Things Network sand-
box[71], to rely on private people and universities to host the gateways of the sys-
tem. Effectively, this means that the gateways set-up, most of the time are small,
indoor gateways with very little coverage outside of the home they are placed in.
Commercial solutions, such as CiBiCom discussed earlier cover a broader part of
the country, however, while testing in the city it yielded very little difference from
The things network. Another approach, which might carry better results could be
changing LP-WAN technology from LoRaWAN to NB-IoT or LTE-M. Since NB-IoT
operates on the cellular band, coverage issues in the city should not be an issue
as the cellular network is well established. This of course has its downsides in
power-consumption, however, as shown in section 5.2, there is room to spend
more energy to get better reception.
