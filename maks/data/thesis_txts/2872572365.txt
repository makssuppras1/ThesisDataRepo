Abstract
This master’s thesis presents a dynamic model of a high­temperature thermal energy storage (TES)
system based on molten salt, designed for direct steam generation. The study investigates and
compares the thermal and economic performance of two molten salt compositions: pure sodium
hydroxide (NaOH) and a eutectic mixture of sodium hydroxide and potassium hydroxide (NaOH–
KOH). The model is developed in Simulink and focuses on the steam generator unit which acts
as the critical interface for energy conversion. One aspect of this project involves evaluating two
charging strategies, which utilize real hourly electricity price data from Denmark (DK1, 2024)
to use energy input for charging during cheaper hours. The economic assessment quantifies the
Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) and Net Present Value (NPV) over a 25­year project horizon for
both salt configurations. Validation against existing literature demonstrates the model’s ability in
predicting thermal output, molten salt outlet temperature, and pressure drop. The results suggests
that pure NaOH generally outperforms the NaOH–KOH mixture in terms of steam output and
overall economic viability under the simulated conditions, despite the mixture’s advantages in
lower melting point and potentially faster thermal response. This research contributes to addressing
existing research gaps by providing an economic analysis of standalone molten hydroxide TES
systems using real price­responsive charging strategies.

1.1
Background
The transition to green energy is one of the most critical issues of the 21st century. As the conse­
quences of anthropogenic climate change become increasingly severe, including rising sea levels,
extreme weather events, and disruptions to ecosystems and food systems, there is growing consen­
sus on the great need to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions across all sectors of society. In
response to the mounting climate crisis, nearly every country in the world signed the Paris Agree­
ment in 2015. The agreement establishes a legally binding commitment to limit global warming
to well below 2°C, preferably 1.5 °C, above pre­industrial levels, and outlines a long­term goal of
reaching global climate neutrality between 2050 and 2100 [1].
To meet these goals, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) emphasizes that
global net CO2 emissions must fall by about 45% from 2010 levels by 2030 and reach net­zero
around 2050 [2].
The energy sector is the single largest contributor to global emissions, responsible for approxi­
mately 73% of total GHG emissions, primarily from the combustion of fossil fuels for electricity,
heating, and transportation [3].
Electricity generation will play a important role in the transition. The International Energy Agency
(IEA) forecasts that global electricity production must reach net­zero emissions by 2040, meaning
that any remaining greenhouse gas emissions are balanced by removal efforts such as carbon cap­
ture or reforestation, and is expected to supply nearly half of total final energy consumption by 2050
[4]. This transformation involves a massive expansion of renewable energy sources, alongside the
electrification of transport, heating, and industrial processes.

taken from [5].
As shown in Figure 1.1, renewable energy sources are expected to dominate the net change in
primary energy supply over the coming decades, while coal and oil are projected to decline sig­
nificantly. Despite this shift, fossil fuels still comprise a major share of the energy mix, as seen in
Figure 1.2, highlighting the long road ahead to full decarbonization.

However, this shift also brings significant challenges. Renewable energy is intermittent by nature,

Thermal energy demand, used for indoor space heating, hot water, and industrial processes, ac­

rently met by burning fossil fuels directly, contributing significantly to greenhouse gas emissions.
Thermal energy storage (TES) can play a crucial role in reducing these emissions by enabling the
use of low­carbon electricity or solar energy to supply heat on demand, thereby displacing fossil­

also be integrated with power cycles such as steam turbines or organic rankine cycles to regenerate

1.2
Molten Salt as Thermal Energy Storage
Molten salt is presented as a promising technology for storing thermal energy, with heat stored
in a liquid salt that remains stable and does not solidify within the operating temperature range.
Compared to other heat transfer media, they offers several advantages [6]:
• Low vapor pressure: Even at elevated temperatures (>500 °C), most molten salts exhibit
low vapor pressures. This allows them to operate at atmospheric or near­atmospheric pres­
sure, simplifying system design and reducing the need for high­pressure containment vessels.
• Non­toxicity and safety: Common molten salts are non­flammable and generally non­toxic,
posing fewer environmental and health hazards compared to synthetic oils or chemical phase
change materials.
• High heat capacity per unit volume: Molten salts can store a significant amount of thermal
energy per unit mass.
• Thermal stability: Many molten salts remain stable over a wide temperature range, typi­
cally from 220 °C up to 600 °C or more, making them suitable for repeated cycling in thermal
storage applications.
• Cost­ effectiveness and availability: The raw materials used in common molten salts are
abundant and relatively inexpensive compared to synthetic heat transfer fluids, making them
attractive for large­scale energy storage systems.
• Liquid state over a large temperature range: Molten salts remain in the liquid phase
across a broad span of temperatures, allowing them to store and transfer thermal energy
efficiently without undergoing phase changes. This reduces complexity in system design
and enables continuous operation without the need for frequent reheating or melting.
Molten salt is a widely adopted technology for thermal energy storage, particularly in concentrated
solar power plants (CSPP), where it stores thermal energy collected from the sun. However, molten
salt can also be heated using electricity from the grid, as well as from off­grid renewable sources
such as wind or solar panels. This makes it useful for applications beyond solar energy. For
example, molten salt TES is able to store excess electricity when supply is high and release heat
when demand increases. This is beneficial for peak shaving, balancing the power grid, and making
better use of renewable energy.
4
==PAGE:10==
The use of molten salt in thermal energy storage systems is categorized as an active storage method,
meaning that the heat transfer fluid (HTF) also circulates through the storage tanks to transfer
energy in and out of the system. In contrast, passive storage systems use a separate solid medium
that stores thermal energy without direct circulation, typically relying on conduction or natural
convection for heat transfer [7]. Active TES systems can be further divided into direct­active and
indirect­active configurations:
• In a direct­active system, molten salt serves as both the storage medium and the heat transfer
fluid. The same fluid is heated, either by a solar receiver in a concentrated solar power (CSP)
plant or by an electrical heater using surplus electricity from the grid or renewable sources,
and then stored in a hot tank. Later, the hot molten salt is circulated to deliver thermal
energy for electricity generation or industrial use. This setup is commonly applied because
it simplifies system design, maintains good efficiency, and supports different heat sources.
• In an indirect­active system, different fluids are used for heat collection and heat storage.
For example, thermal oil may absorb heat from the solar field or from an electrical heater,
while molten salt is used solely for thermal storage. A heat exchanger is required to transfer
energy from the HTF to the molten salt. Although this arrangement adds complexity and
introduces additional thermal losses, it allows for the use of specialized fluids optimized for
different temperature ranges or heating methods.
This type of energy storage is classified as sensible heat thermal storage (SHTS), where energy is
stored or released by raising or lowering the temperature of the storage medium, without any phase
change. In molten salt systems, thermal energy is retained as sensible heat within the liquid salt,
making use of its high specific heat capacity and thermal stability over a broad temperature range.
The direct two­tank thermal energy storage system is the most commonly used configuration for
molten salt storage. However, an alternative approach is the thermocline TES system. While
thermocline systems generally exhibit lower discharge efficiencies compared to two­tank systems,
they offer the advantage to save 35% of investment costs [8]. In a thermocline TES, molten salt
remains in the liquid state and forms stratified hot and cold layers due to density differences. This
system relies on thermal buoyancy to maintain thermal stratification, allowing for the presence of
distinct hot and cold thermal regions within a single tank.

2
Review of Existing Research
This section reviews the current state of thermal energy storagetechnologies, with particular em­
phasis on molten salt systems, their material properties, operational challenges, and recent research
on high­temperature salts
2.1
Literature Review
Molten salts have become important materials in thermal energy storage, and especially in con­
centrated solar power applications. By the end of 2021, roughly 27,500 MWh of molten salt TES
capacity was installed worldwide [9]. Despite its success, conventional molten salt TES faces
some challenges. The Crescent Dunes solar tower project (USA) highlighted issues like corrosion,
thermal stresses, and maintenance problems in the salt tanks [9].
The first solar power plant to utilize molten salts for thermal energy storage was Solar Two, de­
veloped in 1993 in the Mojave Desert, California. It had the capability to store thermal energy
sufficient to generate 10MW of electricity for one hour [10]. It was the first system using molten
salt as both the heat transfer fluid and the energy storage media. More specifically a eutectic mix­
ture of NaNO3 and KNO3 also recognized as solar salt. Solar salt are among the most commonly
used salts for TES systems. The specifics of these materials include low melting points, high ther­
mal stability, and considerable heat capacity, which collectively make them ideal candidates for
efficient thermal management in renewable energy systems [11]. This binary salt, composed of 60
wt% NaNO3 and 40 wt% KNO3, has a melting point of 223°C and remains thermally stable in
its liquid phase up to approximately 600°C [12]. However, the use of Solar Salt comes with chal­
lenges, primarily corrosion of metallic components. Several studies have investigated the behavior
of stainless steels and other alloys in Solar Salt at various temperatures.
A study by Goods et al.[13], examined the corrosion behavior of stainless steels (SS­304 and SS­
316) and carbon steel (A36) immersed in solar salt at two distinct temperatures of 570 °C and
316 °C, respectively. This study focused on the impact of impurities such as NaCl, KClO4, and
Na2SO4, which often contaminate commercial­grade alkali nitrates. The results indicated that the
presence of these impurities had minimal effects on the corrosion rates of the studied materials.
SS­304 and SS­316 exhibited corrosion rates between 6 to 15 µm per year at 570 °C, while A36’s
corrosion rate was observed at 5 m per year at 316 °C, suggesting a reasonable resistance provided
by these common stainless steels despite operating in potentially corrosive environments.
Another common salt is HITEC, a ternary eutectic containing 7 wt% NaNO3, 53 wt% KNO3,
and 40 wt% sodium nitrite (NaNO2) [14]. HITEC melts at a lower temperature (142 °C) than
Solar Salt, which can simplify freeze protection and allow cooler “cold” tank temperatures [15].
However, its upper working temperature is typically limited to 500–535 °C because the sodium
nitrite component can oxidize to nitrate at high temperatures (especially if oxygen is present) .
This decomposition or oxidation of NaNO2 not only reduces thermal stability but also changes
the salt composition (potentially raising the melting point). Strict cover gas control (e.g. an inert
gas blanket) is needed for HITEC systems to prevent nitrite oxidation [16]. In practice, HITEC
has seen use in some pilot projects and as a heat transfer fluid in chemical industries, but Solar Salt
remains the dominant choice for CSP due to its higher thermal stability ceiling . Variations like
HITEC XL (which substitutes NaNO2 with Ca(NO3)2 to lower freeze point) have been explored,
but these tend to sacrifice thermal stability (decomposing at lower temperatures than Solar Salt).
Molten hydroxide salts were historically considered unsuitable for thermal energy storage due
to their high­temperature corrosiveness, which posed serious materials compatibility challenges.

However, recent advancements have shifted this view. In 2022, a Danish startup demonstrated
that corrosion in molten hydroxide systems can be effectively managed using techniques adapted
from molten salt reactor technology, including controlled salt chemistry and sealed operating envi­
ronments[17]. This breakthrough enabled the development of the world’s first pilot­scale molten
hydroxide TES plant, the MOSS (Molten Salt Storage) facility in Esbjerg, Denmark. Constructed
in 2023 under an EU­supported project, the 1 MW / 20 MWh system operates at 700 °C [18]. De­
veloped by Hyme Energy in collaboration with Seaborg Technologies, it became operational in
2024, marking the first MW­scale deployment of molten hydroxide­based TES [19]. The plant
provides long­duration energy storage and is expected to scale to GWh­capacities in later phases.
2.2
Research Gap
Most existing research on molten salt thermal energy storage systems has focused on nitrate­based
salts, particularly Solar Salt. While some studies have explored chlorides and carbonates, sodium
hydroxide (NaOH) and NaOH­KOH eutectic mixtures remain underexplored at the system level.
This is despite their potential advantages, including:
• Lower material cost
• Higher thermal stability (up to 700 ◦C)
• Lower melting points for eutectic mixtures (∼170 ◦C)
Moreover, few studies have investigated standalone TES systems using simple, rule­based charging
strategies, where the system is charged during hours of low electricity prices, and even fewer have
quantified their impact on key economic metrics like the Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) and Net
Present Value (NPV) under real, time­varying electricity prices.
2.3
Novelty and Objectives of Project
The objective of this project is to investigate the technical and economic feasibility of using high­
temperature molten hydroxides for thermal energy storage with direct steam generation. The study
aims to develop a dynamic, physically representative model of a two­tank molten salt TES system
and evaluate its performance under realistic operating conditions.
• To compare two molten salt candidates, pure sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and a eutectic mix­
ture of sodium and potassium hydroxide (NaOH–KOH), with respect to their thermal perfor­
mance, energy storage characteristics, and operational behavior for direct steam generation.
• To assess the economic viability of each salt system by calculating key economic indicators
such as Levelized Cost of Heat and Net Present Value over a 25­year project horizon. The
assessment should integrate real hourly electricity prices.
The novelty of this work lies in the combined thermodynamic and economic analysis of a high­
temperature TES system using alkali hydroxide salts, which have received limited attention com­
pared to more conventional salt systems. Furthermore, the model uses hourly electricity market
dynamics with a control strategy for charging at hours with lower prices, enabling an assessment
of TES performance under realistic grid scenarios.

3
Methodology
The model is based on the work by Mahmoudinezhad et al. [20], in which a molten salt thermal
energy storage system using pure sodium hydroxide (NaOH) is employed to continuously produce
36MW of saturated steam. This configuration is used as the base case in the present study. NaOH
is selected for its compatibility with high operating temperatures, making it suitable for efficient
thermal energy storage and transfer, before implementing a eutectic salt mixture of NaOH–KOH.
In addition to modeling thermal performance and steam generation, the study also explores how the
system is charged using electricity from the grid. Two rule­based charging strategies are tested to
control when the system uses electricity from grid to charge, based on real­time electricity prices.
The strategies are based on valley charging, where the system charges during hours with low elec­
tricity prices, using simple rules that may also account for price trends and the current storage level.
To understand the overall cost­effectiveness of the system, an economic analysis is also carried out.
This includes key indicators such as the Levelized Cost of Heat and the Net Present Value. Both
the technical and economic performance are evaluated for two salt configurations, pure NaOH
and a eutectic NaOH–KOH mixture, under identical system conditions. This approach allows to
compare thermal performance, operational characteristics, and economic feasibility under identical
system conditions.
Steam generation is the primary output of the TES system, as steam serves as a highly versatile
energy carrier. It can be used for industrial process heating, which accounts for a significant share
of global energy demand in sectors such as chemicals, food processing, textiles and steel production
[21]. In addition, steam is essential in electricity generation, where it drives steam turbines in both
conventional thermal power plants and concentrated solar power systems. The ability to dispatch
steam on demand enables thermal storage systems to bridge the gap between intermittent renewable
energy supply and continuous energy demand.
3.1
System Description
The thermal energy storage system modeled in this study is illustrated schematically in Figure 3.1.
It consists of two molten salt storage tanks, a steam generator, pumps, valves, and an electric
heating unit for charging the system.
The right­hand tank contains hot molten salt, which is delivered to the steam generator to supply
thermal energy for steam production. In the steam generator, heat is transferred from the molten salt
to feedwater, producing saturated steam that exits the system. After releasing its thermal energy,
the cooled molten salt is directed to the cold storage tank on the left side of the figure.
Charging of the system occurs via electrical heating ( ˙Qin), which raises the temperature of the
molten salt in the cold tank and gets transferred it to the hot tank. This process allows the system
to store electrical energy in the form of sensible heat. During discharge, the stored thermal energy
in the hot tank is used to generate steam on demand.
The configuration represents a two­tank indirect TES concept, where the storage medium (molten
salt) is separated from the working fluid (water/steam), allowing for flexible operation and decou­
pling of energy input and output.

3.2
Scope of Model
The steam generator unit is the primary focus of this thesis because it serves as the critical interface
between the thermal storage system and the steam generation process. It directly governs how
effectively stored thermal energy can be converted into usable process steam, making it the most
performance­sensitive component of the system.
This focus is reflected in the model design, where an intentionally oversized storage capacity is
applied to ensure continuous operation and isolate the thermal behavior of the steam generator
itself. To support this prioritization, other system components, such as storage tanks, controllers,
and auxiliary equipment, have been modeled in a simplified manner and have not been subjected
to the same level of detail or analysis. By minimizing the influence of these secondary subsystems,
the study emphasizes the heat transfer dynamics and enables a more accurate comparison between
the two salt candidates under near­ideal operating conditions.

3.4.1
Steam Generator Unit
Reference Configuration
The energy conversion unit described in the reference study ([20]) is originally designed as a kettle­
type boiler, specifically utilizing a horizontal TEMA­K shell­and­tube configuration. Due to limi­
tations in Simulink, thermal behavior is approximated using available models. While the physical
geometry of the actual steam generator is not replicated in detail, the simulation focuses on re­
producing the thermal performance observed in the literature. Additionally, fouling effects on the
tube or shell side are not included in this implementation. Fouling refers to the accumulation of un­
wanted material, such as scale, corrosion products, or salt deposits, on heat transfer surfaces, which
reduces thermal conductivity over time. As a result, the model assumes clean heat exchanger sur­
11
==PAGE:17==
faces with constant thermal performance, potentially leading to optimistic heat transfer estimates
during extended operation periods.
Simulink Implementation
The steam generator unit is modeled in Simulink using the block Condenser Evaporator (TL­2P).
TL represents thermal liquid which in this case is molten salt while 2P is two­ phase, water ­ steam.
The heat exchanger effectiveness is based on the selected heat exchanger configuration, the fluid
properties in each phase, the tube geometry and flow configuration on each side of the exchanger.
The block is configured as a crossflow heat exchanger with the setting “2P mixed – thermal liquid
unmixed”. Figure 3.3 displays this configurations with orange tubes representing the molten salt
and blue arrows shows the water allowed to mix freely in the cross­flow configuration.
The Condenser Evaporator (TL–2P) block in Simulink is implemented as a black­box component.
While it effectively models heat transfer and phase change based on thermophysical inputs, the
internal calculations are not directly accessible. As a result, detailed quantities such as local fluid
velocity, heat transfer coefficients, or temperature profiles inside the tubes cannot be extracted.
Only aggregate outputs like pressure, outlet temperature, and phase fraction are available for post­
processing. This limits the ability to directly validate internal transport phenomena, such as the
effect of Reynolds number on heat transfer, or to compare simulation results against experimental
data.
Figure 3.3: Simulink configuration for 2P mixed/thermal liquid unmixed crossflow setup. Orange
tubes represent molten salt flow; blue arrows represent water.
Phase­Zone Separation
This calculation is handled internally by the Simulink block, which divides the two­phase region

3.4.5
Tank
The Tank (TL) block in Simulink represents a vertically oriented thermal liquid storage tank with a
fixed surface pressure and a configurable number of inlet and outlet ports. It models the hydrostatic
pressure distribution between the liquid surface and each port, assuming the surface pressure is
constant and equal to the specified pressurization level. While the block itself does not model
heat exchange with the environment, it internally tracks the liquid volume, level, and temperature.
Additionally, it includes a thermal conserving port associated with the temperature of the liquid
inside the tank, allowing for thermal interaction with environment / other components in the system.
3.4.6
Flow Control (Pump Equivalent)
The flow in the thermal liquid network is driven by the Flow Rate Source block, which functions
as an idealized mechanical energy input. It maintains a specified mass or volumetric flow rate
regardless of the pressure differential across the system. The block assumes no internal flow re­
sistance and does not exchange heat with the environment. Although it behaves like a pump, it
does not simulate mechanical power input or efficiency losses, and is therefore best interpreted as
a simplified flow controller for simulation purposes.

3.6.1

The total system cost structure is decomposed into capital expenditure (CAPEX) and operating
expenditure (OPEX) components:
CAPEX Components:
• Salt Cost: Direct material cost based on required mass and market prices.
• Storage Tank Cost: Infrastructure for salt containment. This includes both cold and hot

• Non­Tank Equipment:Fixed costs representing the remaining system components includ­

• Installation Cost: Labor and construction costs.
• Steam Generator cost: Steam generation equipment sized for thermal duty using estab­

OPEX Components:
• Operations & Maintenance (O&M): Annual operating costs calculated as 1% [20] of total
CAPEX, covering routine maintenance, spare parts, labor, administrative costs, and system

3.6.3
Salt and Tank Sizing Considerations
To estimate fixed infrastructure costs, a base­case reference CAPEX of €20,000 per MWh of ther­
mal storage was applied to the NaOH system, consistent with values reported in the literature [33].
From this, the non­salt system cost (all costs except steam generation unit and actual salt) was
isolated by applying a fixed fraction of 49%, representing infrastructure such as tanks, piping, in­
sulation, foundation, instrumentation, and auxiliary systems. This non­salt portion was initially
divided into two subcomponents using literature­based ratios: 37.1% for salt tanks and 62.9% for
non­tank infrastructure. For the NaOH–KOH system, the tank cost was subsequently scaled pro­
portionally to the required salt volume, to reflect differences in heat capacity and density, while
the non­tank portion was held constant.
The salt inventory cost was calculated independently of the base CAPEX reference. Instead, it was
based on the required energy storage capacity (435 MWh), the thermophysical properties of each
salt, and the operational temperature range (350–700°C).
The mass was then multiplied by the respective salt price (€0.45/kg for NaOH and €0.743/kg for
the NaOH–KOH mixture) to determine the total salt cost. This approach ensures that differences in

than relying on generalized percentage assumptions.

3.6.4
Integration with Operational Model
To support the economic assessment, the system’s yearly electricity consumption and steam deliv­
ery must be known, along with a realistic electricity price. These inputs are not fixed values but are
instead derived from simulated operational behavior using dynamic charging strategies. The dy­
namic profiles are generated using a MATLAB­based simulation of TES operation, with real 2024

on fixed CAPEX­per­MWh assumptions and average electricity pricing (e.g., Mahmoudinezhad et
al.). From this simulation, the annual electricity consumption and downtime hours are directly ex­
tracted. The annual thermal energy output is calculated using a fixed steam mass flow rate and the
enthalpy difference based on salt­specific steam quality. This ensures that differences in thermal
performance directly influence delivered energy and economic returns.
Two electricity price­responsive charging strategies were developed and applied to determine when
the TES system charges. Each strategy responds to fluctuations in electricity prices with the goal of
minimizing charging costs while maintaining sufficient stored energy to ensure continuous steam
delivery. The resulting operational profiles provide two critical inputs for the economic model:
• The average electricity price during charging periods, used to compute the system’s annual
electricity cost (a major component of OPEX),
• The resulting annual system downtime, which limits the number of hours available for steam
delivery and therefore reduces the total avoided cost used in the NPV calculation, as well as
the total useful energy used in the LCOH denominator.
Furthermore, the quality of the steam affects the enthalpy per kilogram of steam produced. A
higher steam quality increases the total energy content of the delivered steam, which enhances the
annual savings in the NPV calculation and lowers the LCOH.

data exhibit the same general trend, a decrease in pressure drop with increasing temperature, the
absolute deviation becomes more pronounced as the inlet temperature decreases.
Despite the offset, the model captures the trend, which supports its qualitative validity. Since this
thesis primarily focuses on system operation at 700 °C, where the deviation is smaller (within 20%),
the discrepancy is acknowledged but considered acceptable for the scope of this work.

One contributing factor to the deviation in pressure drop could be the simplified nature of the
Simulink heat exchanger block, which functions as a black­box component. The internal flow path
is not explicitly defined, making it difficult to assess geometric features such as bends, restrictions,
or flow distribution that can strongly influence pressure losses. In contrast, the reference model
assumes a two­pass kettle­type boiler with relatively well­characterized geometry.
Another factor influencing the pressure drop results is the downstream boundary condition. In this
model, the outlet of the steam generator connects directly to the cold tank, which is assumed to be at
atmospheric pressure. This assumption fixes the outlet pressure and causes the entire pressure drop
to appear across the steam generator. In reality, the measured pressure drop would also depend on
downstream conditions — if the cold tank were pressurized even slightly, the pressure drop would
appear smaller. Since the Simulink block does not account for such interactions explicitly, this
may contribute to the observed overestimation.

In this study, downtime is defined as the number of hourly time steps during which no thermal
energy is discharged from the TES system (i.e., the discharge mass flow rate is zero). While each
time step represents one hour, this discretization does not capture partial­hour events. Since each
time step represents a full hour, the model does not capture cases where discharge stops partway
through the hour. For instance, if the hot tank becomes depleted during an hour, that entire hour may
still be counted as a ”discharge hour” despite only delivering energy for a fraction of the time. As
a result, the reported downtime slightly underestimates the true value, since some discharge hours
may only include partial delivery due to tank depletion. However, because the same definition is
used for both salt types, the downtime metric still offers a fair and consistent basis for comparison.
Curvature Factor
Figure 5.5 displays the effect of the different values of n. As the curvature factor increases, the
system becomes more selective and mainly charges during the cheapest electricity hours. This
reduces overall charging time and can lead to more downtime, but also lowers the average cost
of stored energy. Lower values of n result in a smoother response, allowing the system to charge
during a wider range of prices. This reduces downtime but comes with a slightly higher average
electricity cost. The choice of n therefore depends on whether minimizing cost or maximizing

For the NaOH system (Figure 5.6a), the hot tank temperature remains close to the upper design

from the target inlet and outlet temperatures.
In contrast, the NaOH­KOH mixture (Figure 5.6b) displays more pronounced temperature fluctu­
ations:

• The hot tank temperature occasionally exceeds 700°C, suggesting that the salt receives more
thermal energy than needed or has a lower heat capacity, causing a higher temperature rise
during charging.
• The cold tank temperature drops below 350° after discharge events, indicating that the mix­
ture is being cooled more than intended during heat extraction in the steam generator.
These deviations are primarily due to the thermophysical differences between the salts, combined
with the use of the same fixed mass flow rate in both cases. The NaOH­KOH mixture has a lower
specific heat capacity than pure NaOH, meaning that for each kilogram of salt, less energy is stored
or released per degree of temperature change. Consequently, the NaOH­KOH system delivers less
thermal power overall (36.11 MW vs. 40.42 MW), and it experiences more pronounced temperature

more rapidly.

significant operational concern, as the mixture has a much lower melting point (170°C), providing
a safe margin well below the observed cold­side temperatures. However, these higher temperature
swings could impose greater thermal stresses on materials over long periods potentially impacting
component sizing or material selection. In contrast, pure NaOH operates closer to its melting point,
making tighter temperature control more critical to avoid freezing risks.

The levelised cost of heat is very similar for both systems: €92.9/MWh for NaOH and €93.8/MWh

lower annual steam delivery.
While both salts result in similar LCOH values, the NaOH system consistently outperforms the

system conditions and operational strategies, the NaOH configuration is the more economically
attractive option. Unless the NaOH­KOH mixture offers significant advantages in areas not cap­
tured in this model (for example corrosion resistance or operational flexibility), it is unlikely to be
the preferred choice for large­scale TES deployment.

Future Outlooks and Final Remarks
6.1
Utilization in CSP Applications
Although the NaOH–KOH mixture show higher system CAPEX compared to pure NaOH, its ther­
mophysical advantages remain highly relevant, especially for applications in Concentrated Solar
Power (CSP). The eutectic mixture has a significantly lower melting point (170°C, as seen in Fig­
ure 3.6), which greatly reduces the risk of salt freezing during startup, shutdown, or extended idle
periods. In CSP systems where night­time ambient temperatures can drop considerably, this feature
simplifies operational management and lowers the need for continuous freeze protection systems.
Additionally, the lower specific heat of the mixture enables faster thermal response during charg­
ing, potentially improving flexibility for intermittent solar input. Since less energy is needed to
raise its temperature, the NaOH–KOH mixture heats up more rapidly than pure NaOH, allowing
it to respond quickly during short charging periods. While this comes at the cost of slightly lower
energy density, the improved ramp­up speed can be advantageous for CSP systems operating under
fluctuating solar conditions and limited charging windows.
6.2
Corrosion and Thermal Operating Range
One important consideration when evaluating molten salt TES systems is material compatibility
and corrosion behavior. Corrosion is generally more aggressive at higher operating temperatures,
where many common steel alloys begin to degrade more rapidly. Although NaOH offers better
thermal performance and steam quality, its higher minimum operating temperature (melting point)
requires that the system stays consistently hot, potentially increasing average component temper­
ature and, over time, corrosion risk.
In contrast, the NaOH­KOH mixture has a significantly lower melting point (170°C), allowing for
greater flexibility in the cold­side temperature. This enables a wider range of operating temper­
atures, which can help reduce corrosion exposure during idle or partially charged periods. The
ability to safely allow tank temperatures to fall below 350°C without freezing also supports more
dynamic charging strategies and potentially longer system lifetime due to reduced thermal stress
and corrosion rates. For future research, it would be highly relevant to assess the long­term corro­
siveness of the NaOH–KOH mixture under realistic TES operating conditions, including thermal
cycling and exposure to structural materials. Such analysis would help determine whether the
mixture’s lower­temperature flexibility leads to a meaningful improvement in system durability
compared to pure NaOH.
6.3
Discharging Strategy and Future Use Cases
6.3.1
Discharging Strategy
In this thesis, the discharging strategy is based on delivering steam at a continuous, fixed power
level, representing a typical industrial heat demand scenario. This approach aligns with use cases
such as district heating, chemical processing, or other applications where a stable supply of thermal
energy is essential. It also simplifies the analysis by isolating charging behavior as the primary
variable.
However, thermal energy storage systems can also be deployed for electricity generation, where
steam is used to drive turbines during high­price periods or when renewable output is low. In such
applications, flexible and price­responsive discharging becomes more relevant, as the goal shifts
from steady operation to maximizing economic return or supporting grid stability. For future work,
it would be highly relevant to explore alternative discharging strategies that respond to external

signals such as electricity market prices, demand peaks, or grid balancing needs. This would reflect
the behavior of TES as a true energy buffer — charging when prices are low and discharging when
prices are high.
6.3.2
Model Simplification and Effect of Fouling
Consideration of Heat Losses
In the present work, thermal losses from the pipes and connecting components were neglected to
simplify the model. Heat losses in the tanks was included with heat transfer between tank and
ambient, but not quantified. The operational behavior of the TES did not store the molten salt for
a long time as the aim was to deliver continuous discharge. However, heat losses will significantly
affect overall system efficiency and energy availability. These losses depends on material choice,
insulation, system design and geometry (surface areas). Future work should include modeling with
heat losses to ambient environment. This would improve the realism of the system simulation and
enable an accurate assessment of thermal efficiency and insulation requirements.
Nucleate Boiling
Unlike the Simulink model, which uses empirical correlations for single­phase and two­phase flow,
the reference study models the heat exchanger as a kettle­type boiler and explicitly includes nu­
cleate boiling when estimating the steam­side convective heat transfer coefficient. This results
in a more detailed representation of enhanced heat transfer during phase change and may lead to
performance differences compared to the Simulink simulation.
Fouling
One factor that may contribute to the model’s consistent overestimation of thermal power out­
put compared to the reference study is the exclusion of fouling effects in the heat exchanger. In
real systems, fouling introduces additional thermal resistance on the heat transfer surfaces over
time, which degrades performance by lowering the effective overall heat transfer coefficient. The
literature­reported values, are likely to include the impact of fouling to some extent. In contrast,
the Simulink model developed here assumes clean surfaces throughout, resulting in slightly more
optimistic thermal output predictions. However, fouling was intentionally neglected in this work
for two main reasons. First, the Simulink evaporator/condenser block only supports static (time­
invariant) fouling resistances, whereas fouling is by nature dynamic and system­dependent, making
accurate estimation challenging. Second, since the primary objective of this model is to evaluate
short­term thermal performance and charging strategies under idealized conditions, it is assumed
reasonable to exclude aging and degradation mechanisms. Including uncertain fouling estimates
without experimental backing could introduce more error than insight. For long­term operational
modelling or reliability assessments, the inclusion of fouling effects would be recommended to
achieve more conservative and realistic performance estimates.

7
Conclusion
This study successfully developed a dynamic model of a molten salt thermal storage system and
used it to compare two candidate salts for high­temperature steam generation. The key findings
indicate that pure NaOH outperforms the NaOH–KOH eutectic mixture in terms of thermal and
economic performance under the simulated conditions. NaOH was able to deliver higher steam
power and energy output, maintaining steam quality near saturation throughout discharge cycles.
Consequently, even though the NaOH­based system required more electricity input (due to charg­
ing at a higher rate) and operates closer to its melting point, it provided greater usable heat over
the year. Economically, the two configurations achieved nearly equal LCOH, but NaOH returned
a significantly higher NPV, implying better long­term profitability. These results suggest that,
for applications where operating temperatures up to 700 °C are feasible, NaOH is a more attractive
storage medium than NaOH­KOH mixture. By contrast, the NaOH–KOH mixture, while requiring
a larger volume and a higher upfront cost, it offers operational advantages such as a much lower
freezing point and faster thermal response. This makes the eutectic mixture potentially valuable
in scenarios where freeze protection or rapid cycling is critical (for example, in concentrated solar
power plants with cold nights or intermittent charging opportunities). Overall, the thesis concludes
that NaOH­based TES systems are technically and economically more viable for large­scale energy
storage and steam generation.
