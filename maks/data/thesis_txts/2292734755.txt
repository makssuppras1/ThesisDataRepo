I.I 
Water treatment issues in commercial aquaculture  
In commercial aquaculture practices, various chemicals are added to the water phase to 
ensure good water quality and maintain a healthy fish stock as there are risks of infections 
from bacteria, fungal and parasites for any species of fish that is intensively cultured. It is 
common for fish farms to use chemicals such as formalin, peracetic acid, hydrogen peroxide 
and sodium chloride occasionally to control pathogens. Other than disinfectants, other 
chemiclas such as liming materials, fertilizers, oxidants, coagulants, algicides, herbicides, 
antifoulants, therapeutants, anestehtics, etc. are used in aquaculture production. (Rach et al., 
1997; Jørgensen et al., 2009; Pedersen and Pedersen, 2012; Boyd & McNevin, 2015) 
The discharges of aquaculture effluent that contains residuals of these chemical 
disinfectants to the river at the outlet are unwanted as they might cause adverse environmental 
effects. Presently, the knowledge on the removal of aquaculture disinfection is still limited 
therefore investigating the removal of hydrogen peroxide in constructed wetland associated 
with Danish freshwater rainbow trout farm could help predicting degradation rate more 
accurate as well as designing and planning with more informed decisions in the future.

I.II Disinfectants in aquaculture 
Disinfectants in aquaculture are used to ensure optimal rearing condition and good 
water quality for production. Some of the commonly used chemicals are formalin, 
chloramine-T, hydrogen peroxide and peracetic acid. These chemicals are irritant and 
potentially hazardous and toxic at high concentration (Boyd & McNevin, 2015). The negative 
impacts of using disinfectant are often of concern and its discharge to the environmental 
should be minimized or avoided if possible. However in reality, the residual concentrations of 
the active compounds are potentially discharged to receiving waterbody if retention time is 
short and degradation rates are low.      
In 1997, Food and agriculture organization of the United Nations (FAO) published 
aquaculture development in which it urged countries to regulate the usage of chemical inputs 
in aquaculture that are hazardous to human health and the environment (FAO, 1997).  Today, 
Danish fish farms have a set of environmental requirements from legislations such as, 
maximum water usage, maximum concentration of nutrients and organic matters at outlet, 
maximum discharge of antibiotics and chemical additives, etc. (Dambrugsbekendtgørelsen, 
2012).  
Formalin is the most commonly used disinfectant in aquaculture, with annual usage of 
more than 270,000 L in 2013 for fresh water aquaculture in Denmark. H2O2 is potentially a 
good alternative for aquaculture disinfection as it is an easily degradable powerful oxidizing 
agent that is less hazardous and decomposes to water and oxygen (Russo et al., 2007; Arvin & 
Pedersen, 2015). The study by Pedersen and Pedersen (2012) showed that hydrogen peroxide 
could improve the water quality without affecting the fish negatively. Boyd & Massaut (1999) 
listed the risks associated with the usage of H2O2 in pond aquaculture that it has no potential

I.III Rainbow trout farm in Denmark 
Acoording to Jokumsen and Svendsen (2010), rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) is 
the most dominant species in Danish aquaculture. In 2013, there were a total number of 
around 290 rainbow trout farms and the annual production of rainbow trout was about 41,000 
metric tons with a value of around DKK 840 million (Ministeriet for fødevarer, Landbrug og 
Fiskeri, 2013). The majority of the production is in fresh water (about 31,000 metric tons in 
fresh water and about 10,000 metric tons in seawater). The majority of these farms are 
traditional trout farms using flow-through systems, but an increasing number of farms have 
been rebuilt into model trout farms that use recirculation technology since early 2000s. 
Approximately half of the fresh water trout production in Denmark use recirculation 
technology (Plesner, 2010 as cited in Jokumsen and Svendsen, (2010)).  
The Danish model trout farms were developed in the early 2000s using advanced 
recirculation technologies such as biofilters, sludge separators, concrete raceways and plant

I.IV Constructed wetland 
Constructed wetland is an important treatment unit characterizing the Danish model fish 
farms type 1 and 3, and there is a minimum required residence time of 9 hours. It typically 
consists of traditional interconnected earthen ponds and channels forming a meandering 
stream that increases water retention time allowing chemical, physical and biological 
treatment. (Jokumsen and Svendsen, 2010) 
 Contaminants including suspended solids, organic matter, nitrogen, phosphorus, trace 
metals and pathogens can be removed by constructed wetlands via its diverse removal 
mechanics (Vymazal et al., 1998). There are different types of constructed wetland with 
different design characteristics. They can be categorized according to the wetland hydrology 
(Vymazal, 2010): 
1) Free water surface constructed wetlands (FWS CW): They typically consist of 
basins or channels, containing 20-30 cm of rooting soil, with a water depth of 
20-40 cm. 
2) Constructed wetlands with horizontal subsurface flow (HF CW): They consist of 
gravel or rock beds sealed by an impermeable layer and planted with wetland 
vegetation. Waste water flows more or less horizontally from inlet to the outlet 
under the surface of the bed.  
3) Constructed wetlands with vertical subsurface flow (VF CW): wastewater is 
pumped to the wetland surface and fed in large batches across the surface and 
then percolates down through sand or gravel medium. 
==PAGE:12==

4) Hybrid constructed wetlands: A combination of the above constructed wetlands 
to achieve a higher treatment effect. Most of Hybrid constructed wetlands 
combine HF and VF stages.   
Subsurface flow constructed wetlands (HF CW & VF CW) are less land-intensive 
compared to FWS CWs but the construction costs are significantly higher because of the 
demand for a specific growth substrate (Brix, 1999). Compared to conventional wastewater 
treatment, constructed wetlands are simple to operate and the maintenance requirements are 
low (Morris, et al., 2009).   
All types of constructed wetlands have high efficiency in removing organics and 
suspended solids (Vymazal, 2010). Verhoeven and Meuleman (1999) reported more than 90% 
removal of BOD, COD, suspended solids and bacteriological pollution and close to 50% 
removal of nitrogen and phosphors in constructed wetlands. For model trout farm type 1, 
there is assumed removal percentage of BOD 70%, total nitrogen 7% and total phosphorus 
55% (Jokumsen and Svendsen, 2010).

I.V Objective of the project 
The general concept of the usage and degradation of disinfection chemicals in 
aquaculture was studied with the focus on the degradation of hydrogen peroxide in a 
constructed wetland associated with Danish freshwater model trout farm type 1. H2O2 
degradation was investigated through controlled time-series spiking-experiments in the filed 
combined with sodium chloride (NaCl) tracer experiments and measurements of conductivity. 
The aim of this study was to contribute to a better understanding of the degradation of 
hydrogen peroxide in a constructed wetland and estimating its degradation rate in the 
constructed wetland. This information would help modelling H2O2 fate throughout the 
Constructed wetland and potentially help improve future design of fish farms.

I.VI Experimental approach & study site 
In this project, the degradation of hydrogen peroxide was studied in the constructed 
wetland at Hallundbæk fish farm. This constructed wetland is a free water surface constructed 
wetland.  
 Hydrogen peroxide was chosen as it is relatively less hazardous for the environment, 
easier to handle and decomposes fairly quick before leaving the constructed wetland. The 
concentration of hydrogen peroxide can be determined using spectrophotometry based on the 
method by Tanner & Wong (1998), allowing on-location measurements during field work 
when applying mobile spectrophotometer.  
The field experiments were carried out at Hallundbæk fish farm, a model trout farm 
type 1 in central Jutland in Denmark with an annual production of around 250 tons. A total 
number of 17 spiking experiments were carried out from July to October 2015. The field 
experiments were carried out in the lower part of the constructed wetland just before water 
flows back to the stream. The upper part of the constructed wetland was overgrown with 
plants and considered impossible to access for experimental purposes. The overview of the 
fish farm was showed in Figure 2. The section used for experimental spiking trials was 
approximately 100m for length, 6m for width and 0.5m for average depth. 
Water samples were brought back to the laboratory and degradation of H2O2 was tested 
with initial concentration of 5 and 10 mg/L.

Abstract 
Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) is used in aquaculture to improve water quality and maintain 
a health fish stock. The degradation of H2O2 in a constructed wetland from a Danish model 
trout farm was studied through 17 spiking experiments. A fixed quantity of 10L 35% H2O2 
together with salt tracer were added at either instant or by a pump over a period of one, two 
and four hours and the results showed degradation rate of 1.3-4g/(m2·hr) in the summer 
months and 0.2-1.6g/(m2·hr) in the autumn months. These rates are useful in estimating and 
modelling H2O2 fate throughout the Constructed wetland and could potential help improve 
future design of fish farms.

1 Introduction 
Aquaculture is an important part of the food supply in the world. As of 2001, it 
provided around 30% of fisheries production (as cited in Boyd (2003)) and its production 
will continue to grow. In aquaculture production, different chemicals are added to ensure 
good water quality and a health fish stock (Boyd & Massaut, 1999; Pedersen & Pedersen, 
2012). Some of the common used chemicals are formaldehyde, copper sulphate, chloramine-
T, hydrogen peroxide and sodium percarbonate (Bruun et al., 2007). In Denmark, the usage 
of chemicals for freshwater aquaculture production is regulated with several water quality 
criteria as required by EU water quality directives (TGD, 2003). These water quality criteria 
set individual cut-off value for maximal discharge concentration for chemicals used in 
aquaculture (Miljøministeriet, 2007).  
Hydrogen peroxide is a highly reactive strong oxidizing and bleaching agent that has 
been increasingly used in a number of  medical, food and industrial applications as well as 
environmental application such water treatment because of its efficacy against bacteria 
endospores, non-toxicity and safety for users. It was discovered by Louis Thénard in 1818 and 
it was first proposed as a disinfectant by B.W. Richardson in 1891 (as cited in Linley et al., 
(2012)). H2O2 is thermodynamics unstable and easily decomposed into H2O and O2 and it is 
considered relatively safe for the environment with complete decomposition (Sleigh & Linter, 
1985; Yanong, 2008). Haber and Weiss (1934) discussed the catalytic decomposition of H2O2 
involving a chain and radical reaction with ·OH and HO2. The hydroxyl radical can oxidize 
bacterial cell membranes, lipids, DNA and protein (Keyer et al. 1995; Keyer and Imlay, 1996;

production of oxygen. Keilin and Hartree (1938) summarized the catalytic decomposition of

In aquaculture applications, H2O2 has been proven to be effective against sealice, fungi, 
bacterial gill disease (Thomassen, 1993; Rach et al., 1997; Rach et al., 2000). There is 
research on the toxicity of H2O2 on fish that large rainbow trouts were more sensitive to H2O2 
than smaller ones (Rach et all., 1997; Gaikowski et al., 1999), application to commercial 
recirculating aquaculture system (Pedersen & Pedersen, 2012) and its decomposition kinetics 
in aquaculture water (Arvin & Pedersen, 2015). Though H2O2 is a relatively safe chemical for 
aquaculture, there is the possibility of it ending in natural streams or ground water and have 
effects on aquatic organisms and microorganism. These effects might potentially be ecotoxic. 
In the unlikely event of accidental spill or failed mechanical operation, the high concentration 
exposure could cause adverse effects in the ecosystem (Halling-Sorensen et al., 1998).  
The purpose of this study was to investigate the degradation rates of H2O2 in a 
constructed wetland in a Danish model trout farm type 1 through controlled tracer and spiking 
experiments. The degradation was investigated at 4 different time of spiking of mixture of salt 
and H2O2. The degradation rate of H2O2 in the constructed wetland could be estimated and 
help modeling H2O2 fate throughout the Constructed Wetland and potential help improve 
future design of fish farms.

2 Material & methods 
2.1 Study Site  
The study site for this project was in Hallundbæk fish farm located near Brande in 
Central Jutland in Denmark (55°50’47.972’’N, 9°1’10.135’’E). The fish farm was a model 
trout farm type 1 built in 2009 using recirculation aquaculture technology including particle 
filter, biofilter and constructed wetlands. The current production at Hallundbæk is ~250 
MT/year with the density of the fish at 60-100 kg/m3, the freshwater consumption is 
approximately 40L/s and the flow rate in the recirculation facilities is around 500 L/s. (farm 
manager Søren Jøker, personal comment) 
The constructed wetland at the fish farm consisted of several ponds that were connected 
in an artificial meandering form with a channel of approximately 300 m at the end before 
discharge water back to the Omme å.  
The field experiment was carried out in the downstream part of the constructed wetland 
just before the outlet with a length of approximately 100 m, width of 6 m and average depth 
of 0.5 m. The channel had thick sediment layer and the surface was partially covered with 
duckweed. The entire constructed wetland had a surface area of approximately 7000 m2 and 
most of it was covered with overgrown vegetation. The sampling location was at the outlet of 
the constructed wetland that discharge water back to the river via a pipe of 0.6 m in diameter. 
Water depth at the outlet sampling point was around 0.45 – 0.5 m.

2.2 Field experiments and measurement  
The field measurements consisted of experimental spiking of a solution containing both 
H2O2 and NaCl. NaCl was added as a tracer and its concentration was logged as conductivity 
and H2O2 was the chemical of which the degradation was to be tested. 
In all field experiments, 5kg of NaCl and 10L of 35% H2O2 were mixed with 15L of 
water from the tested section of the wetland using a Makita BDF 452 driver drill with a mixer 
for 2 min at 1500 rpm/min on site immediately before spiking. The spiking solution was 
added in four different ways: instant or constant rate injection during one, two and four hours 
using a Grundfos DDA dosing pump (Grundfors, Bjerringho, DK). 
Residual H2O2 was measured at the outlet of the constructed wetland at 1-2 min 
intervals for instant, 1 and 2 hours experiments and 5-10 min intervals for the 4 hours 
experiments from July to October in 2015. Sampling started immediately after spiking and 
lasted till ~1hr after all the mixture was spiked when the conductivity returned to the baseline 
level. The water samples were taken at the middle of the outlet then filtered through a 0.2 μm 
syringe filter on site before putting into cuvettes. 2.7 ml water sample was added to cuvette 
containing 0.3 ml predispensed H2O2 reagent and at the same time, sampling time was 
recorded with precision to the minute.  
Hach HQ40d portable multi-parameter meter and Hach CDC 401 conductivity probe 
were used to record conductivity and temperature at 10 seconds interval. The conductivity 
probe was submerged at approximately 20 cm to the surface of the water at the outlet 
sampling point. Flow rate was measured on selected days using Höntzsch flowtherm NT 
portable meter (Höntzsch, Waiblingen) across the section in the tested channel part to validate

4 Discussion 
4.1 Removal rate with different flow rate scenario 
The performance and efficiency within a constructed wetland is greatly affected by 
hydraulic characteristic (Headley & Kadlec, 2007). Under the assumption of ideal plug flow 
and the analysis for the field experiments, the recovered H2O2 was directed related to the flow 
rate. In other words, the higher the flow rate, the lower the total degradation of H2O2. Since 
the constructed wetland was a complex dynamic system, the flow rate, residence time and 
degree of mixing were non-constant and a number of different factors such as precipitation, 
evaporation, seepage could influence it. The recovery rates with the scenarios of different 
flow rates are listed in Fig 8. The removal capacities in the summer months are higher than 
autumn months, which indicated a possible effect of temperature.   
In the Danish regulation of chemical usage (Dambrugsbekendtgørelsen, 2012), it 
suggested the degradation rate of 4 g/(m2·hr) for H2O2 in constructed wetland and 2 mg/(L·hr) 
in the water phase. These suggested degradation rates in the constructed wetland are higher 
compared to the results from this study. The COD of tested section were around 30 mg/L 
throughout the whole experimental period. The BOD5 at the outlet was between 1.5 – 2 mg/L 
from July to October (Eurofins analysis report, 2015). Von Ahnen (2012) reported a reduction 
of BOD5 from 8 mg/L to 2 mg/L and a reduction of COD from 17 mg/L to 12 mg/L in a 
constructed wetland from a Danish model trout farm.

4.2 Factors affecting the degradation rate in constructed wetland 
In this study, the effects of temperature and heat could be directly observed. The 
recovered H2O2 at the outlet was higher in October (average 1.96kg recovered for the 1 hr 
test; 2.24kg recovered for the 2 hr tests) compared to August (average 1.43kg recovered for 
the 1 hr test; 0.89 kg recovered for the 2 hour tests). More than 50% H2O2 were found to be 
degraded for all the experiments in August, and for some experiments, the percentage 
degraded was as high as 84%. But for the experiments in September and August, less than 
50% were degraded and most of the tests showed a degradation percentage of around 40%.  
Other than temperature, light and the presence of metals and organic matter can increase 
the degradation rate (Saez and Bowser, 2001). But these factors were not under experimental 
control in this study therefore their effect cannot be directly observed. The data from Danish 
Meteorological Institute (2015) showed that the total of sunlight hours were 189, 229, 154, 90 
for July, August, September and October in 2015. The average day time temperature in July 
and August were 18.8 °C and 21.9 °C in central Jutland during the experiment and in 
September and October, it dropped to 16.7 °C and 12.1 °C. The air temperature varied much 
more than the water temperature, as the temperature were around 16 °C in summer and 11 °C 
in October.  
The breakdown of H2O2 was a combination of both chemical and biological reaction, 
though it is difficult to distinguish between these two. The difference of the degradation of 
water in the lab showed significant difference in the rate of degradation. From the water phase 
experiments, the degradation was reduced by 75% when comparing the half-life time from 
August to October even though the conditions of the experiments were similar in the

bacteria that possessed catalase enzyme might be very sensitive to the change of temperature 
from 16 °C to 11°C. Anderson and Miller (2001) found that Pseudomonas putida increased 
catalase specific activity to facilitate the degradation of H2O2 when exposed to non-lethal 
doses of peracetic acid. Additional experiments on the bacteria groups and count in 
constructed wetland would be needed to test the above hypothesis.  
Pedersen et al. (2006) discussed a 33% reduction in microbial activity when temperature

4.4 Tracer 
The salt tracer provided valuable information on the movement of the water in section 
where the experiment was carried out within the constructed wetland, that the flow is between 
50-60 L/s with retention time of around 1 hour. Unfortunately, this study was only able to 
evaluate one section of one wetland; the hydraulic characteristics upstream of the experiment 
may not be the same. As different shape and vegetation coverage could act as obstructions in 
the flow path (Stern et al., 2001), increase friction and induce short-circuiting that reduce the 
water retention time.   
Tracer experiment is often used to understand the hydraulics and hydrology of 
constructed wetland. Headley & Kadlec (2007) suggested that the tracer used need to have the 
following properties: highly soluble in water, inert and conservative (does not react with the 
wetland constituents), occurs in low background levels within the constructed wetland, easy 
to detect and analysis, low toxicity and does not influence the flow pattern in a significant 
way.  
The reason for using salt tracer in this study was that it was an inexpensive material and 
it can be easily monitored and logged using conductivity probes in field and the conductivity 
and salinity was linear correlated. Apart from salt, there are other tracers that are commonly 
used for hydraulic analysis, for example dye tracer Rhodamine WT, the most common used 
tracer for measuring hydraulic properties of wetlands (Stern et al., 2001). The analysis of the 
data was relatively straightforward using the method in 2.3.2. However, there were 
uncertainties on the assumed recovery rate and density effects. Chazarenc et al. (2003) used 
67 g/L NaCl solution as tracer to study the hydrodynamics of horizontal subsurface flow 
==PAGE:47==
Hydrogen peroxide degradation in a constructed wetland from a Danish model trout farm

~ 41 ~ 
 
constructed wetlands and reported recovery rates of at least 78% and a few of experiments 
achieved a recovery rate of more than 90%.  
The tracer solution used in this study was of high concentration (14% NaCl) and density 
of the spiking solution was 1.13 kg/L. The reason for using this high concentration was to 
create a significant spike above the background concentration of the constructed wetland. 
There is the risk of substantial density effects (Schmid et al., 2004; Headley & Kadlec, 2007) 
that the heavier tracer solution sink to the bottom of the wetland and the recovery rates at the 
outlet were indeed lower than the assumed recovery coefficient (Cr in 2.3.2). The likelihood 
of density effects was higher for the instant injection experiment where the solution was 
dumped into the wetland at once (Headley & Kadlec, 2007).    
 
4.5 Limitations & future considerations 
In this study, the experiments were repeated for each experiment setup and the results 
showed clear patterns on the degradation of H2O2 in the constructed wetland. Even together 
with the additional experiments in the laboratory, the experimental size was nonetheless small 
from a statistical perspective. It is complicated to make repeated experiment in the field as 
there are many uncontrollable natural factors that might have an effect on the degradation of 
H2O2 in the constructed wetland.  
This study was able to evaluate the removal of H2O2 in a section within a constructed 
wetland system. It might had the tendency to under estimate the removal ability of the whole 
wetland system as the water in the tested section might be “cleaner” compared to aquaculture 
effluent, with COD of around 30 mg/L and BOD5 of around 1.5 – 2 mg/L. Future full scale

studies on constructed wetland systems is needed to evaluate the removal capacity of the 
constructed wetland associated of aquaculture system. This could be best accomplished 
through additional tracer spiking experiments at a range of different constructed wetlands 
with multi-sampling locations. The detailed composition of water in the constructed wetland 
including bacteria abundance, trace metal could also help completely understand the 
degradation of H2O2 in the constructed wetland as biological removal is one of the most 
important removal mechanisms. 
Apart from the breakdown of H2O2 via different chemical and biological reaction, there 
is also the dilution effect by the water in the constructed wetland. Saez and Bowser (2001) 
reported a maximum mean hydrogen peroxide concentration of 9.2 mg/L at the discharge pipe 
at the end of the 1 h flow though treatment. The aquaculture effluent contains residual H2O2 
entering the constructed wetland in reality would be of relatively low concentration, and 
taking into consideration of dilution with water in constructed wetland, it would be difficult to 
distinguish between the decomposition of H2O2 and dilution in the constructed wetland.  
Currently, the constructed wetland had no regular maintenance and harvest of plants. 
One potential risk could be that dead zones and short circuit formed in the constructed 
wetland and retention time decreased, the treatment ability of the constructed wetland might 
not optimized.  There is lack of monitoring on the discharge of chemicals at the outlet of the 
fish farm as the current monthly water monitoring is only focused on suspended solids, 
nutrient and organic matter (Eurofins report, 2015).

5 Conclusion 
This study investigated the degradation of H2O2 in constructed wetland for Danish 
model trout farm type 1 with a total number of 17 spiking experiments. The degradation rates 
ranged between 1.3-4 g/(m2·hr) in the summer months and 0.2-1.6 g/(m2·hr) in the autumn 
months in the section for the experiment. These rates are useful in estimating and modelling 
the degradation capacity in a constructed wetland in Danish model trout farm and potentially 
help improve future designs of fish farms.
