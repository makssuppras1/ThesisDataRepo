Abstract
The use of the selective herbicide Chloridazon (CLZ) has resulted in a widespread pres-
ence of its metabolites desphenyl-chloridazon (DPC) and methyldesphenyl-chloridazon
(MDPC) in pore- and groundwater, causing temporal closure of several Danish water
supply wells. Ceasing of the application of CLZ in 1996 has therefore not lead to the
expected decrease in metabolite concentration in groundwater. Metabolites have for a
long time been considered as more polar and mobile than their parent compounds, hence
residues of metabolites in soils have not been fully studied. In this project the soil and
unsaturated zone are investigated at two pesticide contaminated sites, by soil analysis
and pore- and groundwater analysis. Simultaneously, the sorption of DPC on Danish
sediments including clayey tills is investigated by laboratory batch experiments in order
to obtain reliable sorption coeﬃcients (Kd and Kf). By combining the results with an
analytical modelling tool it is investigated if the metabolite residues in the soil and unsat-
urated zone can act as a long-term source in the groundwater systems. Surprisingly high
concentrations of DPC and MDPC are measured in soil and clayey till samples down
to 10 m bgl. The observed total concentrations of DPC and MDPC are 1000 (0.37-93
µg/kg DS) and 500 (0.15 - 36 µg/kg DS) times higher compared to CLZ (<0.02 - 0.97
µg/L), respectively. This highly indicate that it is the retention of DPC and MDPC
and not the ongoing degradation of CLZ, which causes the increasing concentrations in
groundwater systems. In the sorption experiment DPC sorbed to all the investigated
samples, but with the lowest sorption aﬃnity to aquifer sand (Kd: 0.08 L/kg). The sorp-
tion isotherms for the less sandy sediments are nearly linear, but ﬁtted slightly better
by Freundlich isotherms over the entire range of concentrations. The highest sorption
is seen in the organic rich silty clay (Kf: 0.37-0.57 L/kg, 1/n: 0.83-0.98) and in the
clayey tills (Kf: 0.81-2.14 L/kg, 1/n: 0.60-0.88). A good correlation of Kd with organic
carbon content (TOC) is observed and the estimated logKoc (median: 2.30 L/kg) is
higher than previously published values. This suggest that the retention of DPC in the
subsurface is highly controlled by the organic carbon content. By combining ﬁeld data
with the estimated sorption coeﬃcients the fraction of DPC in porewater is evaluated.
This showed that between 20-42% of the total concentration of DPC is in the porewater,
hence more than 60% is sorbed to the soils and clayey till sediments leading to the ac-
cumulation of DPC in the unsaturated zone. The analytical risk assessment tool (DTU
V1D) illustrates that a secondary source of DPC with initial low mass (< 100g) in an
unsaturated fractured clayey till, can cause elevated DPC concentrations (>0.1 µg/L)
in the groundwater during the next ten decades even with the application stop of CLZ
in 1996.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
One of the most important drinking water resources is groundwater, which therefore
needs to be protected from pollution, and especially persistent substances like herbicides
and their degradation products. Nevertheless, in the recent years, herbicides and their
degradation products have been more frequently detected in the groundwater and surface
water (Kolpin et al., 1998; Lenschow et al., 2016; Loos et al., 2010; Miljøstyrelsen,
2018) in concentrations exceeding the European drinking water guideline of 0.1 µg/L
(European Commission, 1998). Among these are the more polar degradation products
of chloridazon (CLZ); desphenyl chloridazon (DPC) and methyl-desphenyl chloridazon
MDPC (Bayerischen Landesamtes für Umwelt, 2008; Buttiglieri et al., 2009; Lenschow
et al., 2016; Loos et al., 2010).
These two polar metabolites have for a long time been considered as more mobile than
their parent compound (EFSA, 2008), thus the retention of these in the subsurface has
been expected to be minimal. However, in Europe both metabolites have been detected
more frequently and in higher concentrations than CLZ and other known metabolites
years after the application stop of CLZ (Bayerischen Landesamtes für Umwelt, 2008;
Hintze, 2020; Loos et al., 2010). In Denmark the selective herbicide CLZ has been
phased out since 1996 and completely prohibited in 2012 (Miljø-og Fødevareministeriet,
2012). However, the occurrence of DPC in water supply wells are still the cause of the
closure of some waterworks today (GEUS, 2021). Therefore, the need to understand
the mobility and persistence of this speciﬁc metabolite has been immense (Hintze, 2020;
Melsbach et al., 2020; Schuhmann et al., 2016).
Studies in soils and lysimeter experiments have been performed with regards to both
the mobility and the persistence of CLZ, DPC and MDPC (Capri et al., 1995; Hintze,
2020; Melsbach et al., 2019; Melsbach et al., 2020; Schuhmann et al., 2016). It was
generally found, that CLZ degrades to DPC rather fast, and that the retention and the
persistence of both DPC and MDPC was high. In agricultural ﬁelds, Hintze (2020)
measured high total concentrations of both DPC and MDPC in the soils and was able
to correlate these with the total organic carbon content, TOC, in the soils. Given the
higher total concentration of DPC in the soil and unsaturated zone compared to the
total concentration of CLZ, Hintze (2020) predicted a long-term leaching of DPC, due
to higher retention time than previously estimated. However, the vertical migration and
expected leaching time of DPC in the unsaturated and saturated zone is still speculative.
Therefore, the need to investigate the persistence, distribution and migration of DPC in
the subsurface is still important.
==PAGE:16==
2
1 Introduction
The working hypothesis for this thesis is, that the unsaturated zone at pesticide point
sources hosts a pool of CLZ, DPC and MDPC similar to the results from Hintze (2020)
in agricultural ﬁelds in Switzerland. Pools of pesticides in clayey till sediments may
act as a secondary source, thus increasing the leaching time of CLZ, DPC and MDPC
substantially.
The overall aim of the study is therefore to assess the extent of CLZ, DPC and
MDPC contamination in the subsurface in order to estimate the retention and leaching
time. Speciﬁcally the study will:
• Characterize two pesticide point source sites with respect to geology (soil proper-
ties), hydrogeology, transport pathways and contaminant distribution in soil and
groundwater
• Assess if a pool of CLZ, DPC and MDPC exist in the unsaturated zone, and
potentially contribute to the vertical distribution.
• Evaluate if the retention of a potential source of CLZ, DPC and MDPC are related
to relevant soil parameters
• Determine sorption coeﬃcients from laboratory experiments with soil materials
from the sites. In order to generalise the results additional soil samples from a site
in North Zealand has been included in the dataset
• Estimate the extent and duration of the groundwater contamination after the
selective herbicide CLZ was banned in 1996
The ﬁeld study will be carried out at two sites in the southeastern part of Denmark,
which have been characterised as pesticide point sources by WSP A/S for the Region
of Zealand. The total concentration of CLZ, DPC and MDPC will be quantiﬁed in soil,
unsaturated zone and aquifers in Danish sediments at sites with predicted point source
contamination of CLZ. In the analysed soil samples the content of clay, organic carbon
and water will be measured to correlate these properties with measured concentrations
in the soil.
Studies of DPC sorption is a key part of this project, since data about DPC in the
literature is generally limited. The experiments will be carried out in the laboratory
using a range of sediments with diﬀerent contents of clay and organic matter and with
environmentally similar low concentrations of DPC in order to determine the sorption
coeﬃcients in these sediments.
The extent and longevity of DPC contamination will be evaluated based on 1D
transport model simulations for two representative sites in Denmark. The evaluation
will be performed by means of mathematical modelling based on data collected during
this project regarding contaminant sources, ﬁeld concentrations of DPC and results from
the sorption experiments. Finally, the results are synthesised in order to establish a risk
assessment regarding the long-term dynamics of the metabolites in the groundwater
system and water supply wells.

a herbicide belonging to the pyridazinone class of pesticides (EFSA, 2008; EPA, 2005;
PPDB, 2020) and is the active substance (alone and in mixtures) of many herbicide

in plants and was ﬁrst introduced in 1984 (PPDB, 2020; Roberts et al., 1998). The
herbicide is applied for pre-plant, pre-emergence and early post-emergence use on sugar
beets and red table beets to control certain weeds (EFSA, 2008; EPA, 2005; Roberts
et al., 1998).

In Denmark chloridazon has predominantly been used for agricultural purposes, plant
nurseries and in horticulture (Lenschow et al., 2016; Roost & Lenschow, no date). CLZ
was sold in the period from 1964 to 1996 (Figure 2.1), generally for the cultivation of
sugar beets and feed beets and later in 1979 the pesticide was further used in beet root
and onion cultivation. (see Appendix A.1 for more info). Since 1996 all pesticides, which
included chloridazon, have been phased out and in 2012 possession of CLZ together with
a number of other pesticides was prohibited (Miljø-og Fødevareministeriet, 2012).
The transformation products of chloridazon is desphenyl chloridazon (DPC) and
methyldesphenyl chloridazon (MDPC), sometimes referred to as chloridazon metabolite
B and B1, respectively (EFSA, 2008; PPDB, 2020). Their respective names, chemical
structure and CAS number are listed in Table 2.2.
Chloridazon has a moderate aqueous solubility and is not considered to be volatile
(EFSA, 2008).
However, in some studies the dissipation of CLZ was connected to
volatilisation and degradation before entering the subsurface (Asman et al., 2005; Barra
et al., 1995). According to EFSA (2008) it is moderately persistent in soil systems and

persistent in water systems in certain conditions. It is considered as low to moderate in
toxicity having no highly speciﬁc response in mammals, and no acute risk in freshwater
ﬁsh and vertebrates (EFSA, 2008; EPA, 2005; PPDB, 2020). Chronic risk to freshwater
ﬁsh and vertebrates and for birds has not been assessed due to lack of available data.
Chloridazon is still approved or known to be used in some EU member states, as
wells as in the UK and in Australia (PPDB, 2020). Nevertheless, in the EC Directive
91/414 the compound is not approved (European Commission, 2008, 2009).
It has been diﬃcult to include pesticide metabolites in monitoring programs, since
the drinking water limit of 0.1 mg/L was only applied for some relevant metabolites
(Reemtsma et al., 2013). Finally, the information on the occurrence of other metabolites
has hardly been obtained, since the appropriate analytical methods and data have been
unavailable, hence the knowledge and indication of other metabolites of interest has
been sparse (Reemtsma et al., 2013).
Consequently, the ﬁndings of some pesticide
metabolites have been unexpected and unintentionally. Among these metabolites are
DPC and MDPC (Bayerischen Landesamtes für Umwelt, 2008; Buttiglieri et al., 2009;
Loos et al., 2010). Following the detection of these metabolites, they have been more
frequently included in groundwater monitoring programs, which have resulted in their
detection more often and in higher concentrations than other known metabolites (Loos
et al., 2010; Postigo & Barceló, 2015).
A pan-European survey on the occurrence of selected polar organic persistent pollu-
tants in groundwater was conducted by Loos et al. (2010). They found that the most
frequently detected chemical above the European groundwater quality standard for pes-
ticides of 0.1 ug/L were DPC and MDPC with concentrations of up to 13ug/L and 1.2
ug/L, respectively
As a consequence of the ﬁndings, the Danish regions included CLZ and DPC in an ex-
panded analytical programme regarding pesticides in surface- and groundwater in 2012,
which eventually led to both compounds to become a part of the basic programme for
pesticide analysis in 2015 (Lenschow et al., 2016). In the Region of Southern Denmark
DPC was observed in a signiﬁcantly higher frequency compared to CLZ (most often it
was only DPC which was detected) and they were mostly encountered at or near agri-
cultural machine pools (machine stations) (Lenschow et al., 2016). High concentrations
of DPC were found in the upper aquifer, hence posing a potential risk for water supply
from the deep aquifer. It was therefore advised by the Regions of Denmark to monitor
DPC in the monitoring programmes; both in GRUMO and raw water monitoring at the
diﬀerent water companies.
Today, high concentrations of DPC are still detected in multiple regions of Denmark
as seen in Figure 2.2. In Zealand and in the southern islands DPC concentrations exceed-
ing the groundwater quality are still observed in multiple water supply wells (Figure 2.3).
The highest detection frequency of DPC (and for MDPC) is in wells with screenings at
approximately 20 m bgl.. (GEUS, 2020, 2021). However, DPC has been detected down
to 140 m bgl.(GEUS, 2020). see Appendix A.1 for more info.

CHAPTER 3
Transport and distribution of
CLZ & metabolites
An introduction to CLZ and its metabolites is provided in this chapter, governing their
properties, transport and degradation. Furthermore, a brief introduction of basic charac-
teristic of sediments and glacial deposits is provided.
3.1
Distribution in soil matrix
The sorption aﬃnity of pesticides and metabolites determines the partitioning between
the solid (e.g. mineral surfaces and organic matter) and aqueous phases and have con-
siderable inﬂuence on the transport, persistence and bioavailability within any given
hydrological system (Barbash, 2007). Sorption and desorption is governed by various
factors such as pesticide and metabolite properties including solubility and chemical
structure, combined with soil properties such as texture, pH, organic matter content
and particle size (Barbash, 2007; Broholm et al., 2001; Lu et al., 2011). It is often
attributed to changes in sorption aﬃnity that metabolites may enter the groundwater
either faster or slower than their parent compound (Loos et al., 2010).
3.1.1
Physico-chemical properties of CLZ & metabolites
Chloridazon is seen as a non-volatile compound, with an air-water partitioning con-
stant, Henry’s Law constant (KH), of 0.66 · 10-6 (Regionens Videncenter for Miljø
og Ressourcer, 2020). Nevertheless, concentrations of CLZ together with 2,4-D and
Atrazine in vegetation have been detected more than 60 km from areas with still al-
lowed use of chloridazon (Asman et al., 2005).
Metabolites are usually more polar and less volatile than their parent compounds
(Schuhmann et al., 2016), but according to EFSA (2008) and Regionens Videncenter
for Miljø og Ressourcer (2020) DPC is both more polar and more volatile than CLZ (see
Table 3.1. However, low vapour pressure values combined with low Henry’s constants
still indicate that CLZ and DPC will not readily vaporise. KH and vapour pressure are
not documented for MDPC.

are directly involved.
The degradation of CLZ to DPC in soil under aerobic conditions occurs rather fast

suggests that the degrading microorganisms need an adapting period and time for growth

2016).

results illustrated long-term stable and high retention of DPC over multiple years and
that the decline of chloridazon in soil was rather due to degradation than due to leaching

3.3
Transport
Leaching through soil matrices is one of two main pesticide pathways to the groundwater,
whereas the other is inﬁltration by polluted surface water (Pérez-Lucas et al., 2018).
Leaching through soil matrices considers the dissolved and particle-bound pesticides,
which are neither taken up by plants nor carried with runoﬀto surface water, but instead
undergo downward displacement (or percolation) through a soil proﬁle, the unsaturated
zone and ﬁnally to the groundwater. Factors governing leaching is the method and dose
of which the pesticides is handled (Pérez-Lucas et al., 2018; Tuxen et al., 2013) and
external factors such as climate and irrigation (Pérez-Lucas et al., 2018). Additionally,
when the pesticides (and metabolites) are in the soil, it is the properties of the soil
matrix (permeability, texture, organic matter content, fractures), hydrological properties
and the physico-chemical properties of the pesticides (volatility, sorption aﬃnity, root
uptake) that govern the fate (Pérez-Lucas et al., 2018). The most important hydrological
transport processes governing solute transport are summarised in Appendix A.2.

3.3.1
Expected transport in clayey tills
In Denmark mainly Quaternary deposits (e.g. tills and outwash sediments), are signif-
icant in relation to leaching from contaminated sites, since pre-Quaternary sediments
are seldom found near the surface (Fredericia, 1990). In several areas clay tills form
aquitards above chalk/limestone aquifers and aquitards above, in the middle and below
sand/silt aquifers (Fredericia, 1990).
Glacial tills are a product of the late glacial processes of transportation, erosion and
deposits (Clarke, 2018) and due to multiple glacial events and periglacial processes the
complexity of the tills varies greatly both in composition, structure, fabric and property
(Clarke, 2018).
Therefore, it is often diﬃcult to estimate leaching and groundwater
conditions in these sediments and subsequently to predict the behavior of contaminants
in the subsurface. Glacial tills are classiﬁed according to material (lithology), the mode
of transport (subglacial, glacial and supraglacial), deposition (deformation lodgement
and melt-out) and geotechnical properties (particle size distribution, plasticity, organic
content) (Clarke, 2018). The knowledge of the hydraulic conductivity in soil and till
structure is important since clay tills are considered as barriers against migration of
pollution to till covered aquifers.
Pre-Quaternary and glacioﬂuvial sediments (clay, silt, sand and limestone) are some-
times incorporated into the clayey till deposits due to glacial tectonics and glacial melt-
out deposits. Interlayered sand lenses can form hydraulic channels in an otherwise low
permeable matrix. Therefore, it is important to consider these when considering the hy-
draulic conductivity and subsurface ﬂow in glacial clayey till deposits (Fredericia, 1990).
Vertical and horizontal fractures are a known geological phenomena in hetereoge-
neoues clayey tills and are often caused by glacial tectonics or freeze/thaw processes
(Fredericia, 1990; Knud et al., 2012) and macropores (Jørgensen et al., 2002). In these
fractures preferential ﬂow may occur leading to rapid leaching and spreading of pesti-
cides and metabolites. Resulting concentration gradients between fractures and clayey
till matrix caused diﬀusion of dissolved solutes into the ﬁne-grained matrix (Lu et al.,
2011; McKay et al., 1999). In the matrix the water is relatively immobile and the aver-
age migration rate of solute can be signiﬁcantly retarded relative to the advective ﬂow
(McKay et al., 1999). If the concentration of solute is reduced in the fractures, and
reaches a concentration value below the concentration in the matrix, the diﬀusion might
be reversed. Therefore, the solutes trapped in the clayey matrix may be a long-term
source of contaminants (Chambon et al., 2011; Lu et al., 2011).

4 Site description
The uppermost part of the geology consists of a 1.5 m thick ﬁll layer consisting of

tills segments with sand and gravel lenses are detected. Between 5.3 to 7.2 m bgl. a

To measure the concentrations of CLZ, DPC and MDPC in soil and clayey till, soil

At Site 1 two soil proﬁles (Pv19 and Pv20) were drilled in the courtyard on the

characterisation of the sites.

5.5
Risk assessment modelling
To estimate the vertical transport of DPC to the shallow and deep aquifer for water
supply, two conceptual models in an analytical risk assessment tool for fractured clayey
till are used Chambon et al. (2011) .
This tool considers a low-permeable fractured media and the vertical mass transport
herein (Chambon et al., 2011). It consider the leaching of a contaminant through frac-
tures, and the resulting secondary source due to matrix diﬀusion. Therefore, by this
model, the tail in the breakthrough curve at entrance will be much longer, since diﬀu-
sion back from the matrix to the fractures will act as a continuous source. This tool is
advisable since the clayey till segment is more than 10 m thick and the probability of
fractures in the segment is therefore large (GEUS & DJF, 2009; Knud et al., 2012).
Plug ﬂow transport is also considered by the use of an equivalent porous media (EPM)
model, which consider porosity and hydraulic conductivity, equivalent to a fractured
system Chambon et al. (2011).
The DTU V1D tool has three conceptual models, which is shortly described in Ap-
pendix C.5.3.
In this project the risk assessment Model 1a and 1c are used and a
conceptual model of the application of the two models is illustrated in Figure 5.2.
Model 1a is used in two scenarios.
First to estimate the initial concentration of
the source and the geological parameters that best describes the vertical distribution of
measured DPC concentrations. Secondly to estimate the leaching of DPC to the deep
aquifer for water supply.
Model 1c is used to estimate the duration of DPC leaching from a known source in

Parameters in Model 1a: Initial conditions
The spill time is estimated to have proceeded from 1964 to 1996 (year of the phase out
(Miljø-og Fødevareministeriet, 2012)). The years with contamination are then set to 32
+ 24 years (from 1964 to 1996 and from 1996 to 2020).
As seen in Table 2.1 Section 2 the product Pyramin FI contain 350 g/l of CLZ.
The product is typically bought in amounts of 5-25 l (Tuxen et al., 2016). Commonly
the pesticide product is diluted with 150 - 400 l water in a spray (Tuxen et al., 2016).
If an amount of 200 l is assumed the concentration of CLZ in the spray will be 1.75
g/L (with an active substance of 350 g/l). In this scenario the magnitude of a possible
contamination hotspot is dependent on whether the spill has occurred in a single event
or in a continuous practice over several years. At a single event the vertical ﬂux will be
insigniﬁcant (~0.5 g/year), but by continuous spill, e.g. 32 years (from 1964 to 1996),
a pool of contamination may accumulate. Since it has been advised to further dilute
the sprays with a factor 100 before emptying the tank concentrations may be a hundred
times less. An initial concentration of 17.5 mg/L CLZ is therefore assumed (Tuxen et al.,
2016).

Sorption isotherms
The sorption isotherms for DPC were determined for soil samples extracted from
northwest and northeastern Lolland together with samples from Industrivej, Roskilde
Denmark. The soil samples extracted for the sorption experiment are listed in Appendix
G.1 Table G.3. The samples from NE-Lolland have a much higher silt content compared
to the sample from NW-Lolland. According to the surface geology map produced by
GEUS, the samples are extracted very close to post-glacial marine clay and gyttja de-
posits (Pedersen et al., 2014). This can explain the relative high content of TOC in the
samples. The solutions of DPC together with the above described soils and sediments
were shaken for 12 days in a concealed box in a 10 °C room.
For the linear isotherm the results were ﬁtted by linear regression (Figure G.5). A
high correlation coeﬃcient was seen in all sediments (R 2: 0.97-1.00). However, in low
concentrations the sorption aﬃnity to clayey sediments was highly underestimated (Fig-
ure 6.10 and G.6). Therefore, the sorption aﬃnity of DPC was ﬁrst determined both
with linear regression of the logarithmic form of Equation 5.16 (Figure G.7 and with
non-linear Freundlich isotherms (Figure G.8). In the linear regression of the Freundlich
isotherms too high curvatures (1/n) were estimated in some of the samples. The lin-
ear form highly deviates from the experimental isotherm at low and high equilibrium
concentrations. Thus to describe the sorption behaviours covering the full range of the
concentrations the linear form is not suitable. In contrary, the curve in the non-linear
form ﬁts the experimental isotherms both in low and high concentrations. Similar results
are found by Tsai and Juang (1999).
Therefore, the Freundlich sorption coeﬃcients Kf and 1/n were determined with
non-linear regression. This displayed a generally better ﬁt over the entire concentration
range in soil samples composed of clayey till. However, with regards to the sandy aquifer
sample a much better ﬁt was seen with linear isotherms, which is illustrated in Figure
6.9. All the resulting regression proﬁles are seen in Figure G.7.
The correction factor, 1/n, is between 0.6 and 0.99, with the highest values in the
samples from northeastern Lolland and the sandy aquifer. These last mentioned samples
have the highest sorption aﬃnity in the mid range of initial concentrations (10µg/L and
100 µg/L), and lowest when the initial concentration is 1µg/L.
As seen in Figure 6.10, DPC has a much higher sorption aﬃnity to clayey till at
low concentrations. Sample #1 from Industrivej, which is of sandy clayey till, generally
shows a lower sorption capacity at higher concentrations compared to the other samples.
This may be due to the lower foc in the sample. The sandy aquifer sample (#6) and
silty sand (#4) have a better ﬁt to the linear isotherms, hence the sorption aﬃnity may
be best estimated with Kd-values for sandy sediments.
The Koc values are calculated from Equation 5.15, since it was assumed that foc
dominates the sorption of DPC (Hintze, 2020). A median value of 2.30 (± 0.14)L/kg

Discussion
7.1
Unsaturated zone as a reservoir
To study if soils and the unsaturated zone composed of clayey till are potential reservoirs
of CLZ, DPC and MDPC, thus contributing as a long term source, soils from two diﬀerent
contaminated sites of DPC were investigated. The results demonstrate that CLZ, DPC
and MDPC are still detectable in the sediments years after the application stop of CLZ
in 1996 (Section 2 Table 2.1).
The total CLZ concentrations detected are below the detection limit or has a maxi-
mum detection of 3.6 µg DPC/kg DS. Considering that the Danish laboratory ALS A/S
has a detection limit of 10 µg/Kg DS, it could be speculated if some concentrations of
CLZ may be present in low concentrations after all. However, the detected low total
concentration of CLZ (Median: 0.02 µg/kg DS) and the calculated porewater concen-
trations (Median: 0.02 µg/L, Kfoc: 199 L/kg and 1/n: 0.85) suggest that the residuals
of CLZ in soils and sediments and its further inﬂuence on the groundwater quality is
insigniﬁcant.
MDPC and DPC were detected in a factor of 500 and 1000 higher than CLZ, respec-
tively. This highly indicates, that it is not a still ongoing degradation of CLZ, which
causes the elevation of metabolite concentrations found in both soil and groundwater,
but rather a higher degree of retention of the two metabolites. The mobility classiﬁca-
tion by EFSA (2008) and the GUS-index of CLZ, DPC and MDPC (Table 3.1 in Section
3.1) indicate higher leaching of DPC and MDPC compared to CLZ. However, the re-
sults in this study illustrate that after the application of CLZ on the surface, the fully
degradation to DPC, which due to high sorption aﬃnity to materials in the soils and
unsaturated zone, will create DPC reservoirs for long-term leaching. These observations
veriﬁes the previous results from Hintze (2020), Melsbach et al. (2020), Schuhmann
et al. (2016).
However, no signiﬁcant correlation between either metabolite and TOC was seen in
the ﬁeld data. This was unexpected since Hintze (2020) found signiﬁcant correlation of
total concentration of DPC and MDPC with higher TOC in the topsoils. Nevertheless,
the relationship between total DPC, MDPC and TOC in the results by Hintze (2020)
got less with increasing depth (> 0.75 m), this could explain the lack of correlations in
this study.
It has not been possible to explain the high increase of CLZ in the slightly oxidised
clayey till by the estimated geological properties. The ocher content, hence the presence
of ferric oxides, in this section may explain the "pockets" of high total concentration of
==PAGE:78==

CLZ. In a previous study adsorption to ferric oxides was insigniﬁcant with regards to
nonionic compounds, such as atrazine and isoproturon (Clausen & Fabricius, 2001), but
since these are structurally diﬀerent from CLZ it may be diﬀerent with CLZ.
Furthermore, in former studies it was found that CLZ has a high sorption aﬃnity
with montmorillonite and altered illite (Cuevas et al., 2008; Sanchez-Martin & Sanchez-
Camazano, 1987), which belongs to the group of smectite and mica clay minerals, re-
spectively (Miljøstyrelsen, 2016). According to Ernstsen (1998) the composition of clay
minerals in the subsurface in Zealand and Funen can be divided into two groups due to
post-depositional weathering processes, such as oxidation and leaching. The upper layer
is a CaCO3-poor zone (1-3.5m bgl) with oxidation and acidiﬁcation as the main weath-
ering processes and a lower CaCO3-rich zone (3-6.5mbgl) with oxidation as the main
weathering process. Furthermore, the composition of clay minerals diﬀered between the
two layers, whereas the upper, more weathered part were rich in vermiculite and smec-
tite and the lower, unweathered part were rich in chlorite, illite and illite-smectite. It
can therefore be suspected that the high total concentrations of CLZ 3 m bgl. could be
due to the clay mineralogy. The same geological properties can also explain the higher
concentrations of DPC seen 2 m bgl. and down to the shallow aquifer at Site 2, in which
correlation with ﬁne clay particles was seen and no correlation were seen with CLZ.
The two sites investigated showed very diﬀerent distribution patterns of DPC. At Site
1 the concentration proﬁle with measured total concentrations indicate higher retention
and degradation down to 3 m bgl. A higher sorption capacity in top soils together with
a prolonged application of CLZ can be a possible reason to this. Furthermore, when
comparing the measured total concentrations and measured porewater concentrations of
DPC, a decrease in DPC concentrations is seen in the more reduced clayey till, which
depicts a less uniform vertical distribution of DPC. Whereas at Site 2 the concentration
proﬁle illustrated a much more uniform bell-shaped distribution with depth. This may
suggest greater groundwater ﬂuctuations at Site 2 compared to Site 1. According to ??
groundwater ﬂuctuations in a fractured media, will lead to a more uniform distribution
of contaminants due to matrix diﬀusion.
The calculated and measured porewater fraction of DPC (median calculated: 20 to
40%, measured: 42%) and MDPC (median: 40%) in the unsaturated zone demonstrates
that more than 60% of the metabolites are adsorbed, which highly leads to a retention
of their leaching. This was also observed by Hintze (2020) and Schuhmann et al. (2016).
Regardless of the observed high adsorption aﬃnity of DPC and MDPC, high porewater
concentrations are calculated, which eventually can cause high concentrations in the
groundwater exceeding the groundwater criteria of 0.1 µg/L.
7.2
Natural Attenuation
In this part the natural attenuation processes, which refer to naturally occurring pro-
cesses contributing to a reduction in mobility, concentration, or mass of CLZ, DPC
and MDPC in the unsaturated zone and groundwater will be discussed. The processes
==PAGE:79==
7.2 Natural Attenuation
65
investigated are sorption and degradation of CLZ, DPC and MDPC.
The experimental sorption study shows that DPC was signiﬁcantly sorbed to 7 of
the 8 investigated sediments. In the sandy aquifer the sorption of DPC was limited with
a Kd-value of 0.008 L/kg, which is the lowest partitioning value estimated for DPC
according to literature. In the clay-rich sediments a generally high sorption aﬃnity was
seen. In this case the isotherms were best ﬁtted by non-linear Freundlich isotherms and
the resulting partitioning coeﬃcients, Kf, were in the interval between 0.81 to 2.14 L/kg
with curvatures (1/n) ranging between 0.6 to 0.98. Compared to previously estimated
partitioning coeﬃcients (Kf: 0.34-0.71 L/kg, 1/n: 0.804-0.868) established by (EFSA,
2008) these estimated values are signiﬁcantly high. In cases with low total DPC concen-
trations (<100 µg/L) a Kd-value of 0.52 L/kg, seemed suﬃcient to estimate the leaching
concentrations in clayey till sediments. The high sorption aﬃnity in clayey till may lead
to slight attenuation of DPC, since it is suﬃciently high to prolong the DPC leaching,
but suﬃciently small to cause elevated porewater concentrations.
Nevertheless, the sorption coeﬃcients from batch experiments may be too high rela-
tive to the extent of sorption in a real system, thus the estimated retardation will be too
high. However, given the high desorption coeﬃcient estimated in the batch experiment
(only 6% was desorbed), a much slower desorption compared to the adsorption processes
is evident, and signiﬁcant retardation may still be the case.
The desorption of MDPC was also evaluated in this study. Generally a much lower
desorption coeﬃcient was estimated compared to DPC. This suggest a higher degree of
sorption reversibility of MDPC, thus the leaching of MDPC may be greater than DPC.
Data from nearby water supply wells and monitoring wells indicate higher MDPC/DPC
ratios (0.5-0.8)) down to 15 m bgl.
predominantly in sandy sediments, after which
the ratio is decreasing with depth. Moreover, multi regression analysis indicate that
the MDPC/DPC ratio in the investigated groundwater analysis (n=62) are related to
screening depth (p=0.005).
This may substantiate the hypothesis of less retention
of MDPC thus higher leaching in the topmost geological sections. Furthermore, the
desorption study implies less aﬃnity for clay particles, but higher aﬃnity to TOC, since
less desorption was seen in the organic rich topsoil from Site 1. This is substantiated by
the higher observed total concentration of MDPC in the topsoil at Site 1. However, the
data are sparse and further work on this subject would be necessary.
Clay minerals may be important sorbents for nonionic and polar pesticides and
metabolites.
In a study by Clausen et al. (2004) it was found that the high polar
metabolite similar to DPC, 2,6- Dichlorobenzamide (BAM), was sorbed to the clay min-
eral montmorillionite, but other minerals surfaces were only of limited importance. Since
some correlation of total DPC concentration with ﬁner clay particles were seen, it must
be suspected, that these may contribute to the sorption as well. It would therefore be
interesting to investigate this further.
A signiﬁcant correlation of Kd-values with TOC content was observed in the soil
samples. Therefore, the estimated median logKoc- value of 2.3 L/kg, may be a good
parameter to estimate the retention of DPC in the unsaturated zone. This value is
also signiﬁcantly high compared to the estimated logKoc (1.7 L/kg) provided by PPDB
(2020).

7 Discussion
The results suggest a faster disappearance from soil when CLZ is present in the
subsurface compared to DPC and MDPC. Both Schuhmann et al. (2016) and Melsbach
et al. (2020) suggested root uptake as a dissipation factor. However, this factor can
be neglected in this case, due to the lack of vegetation in the yard area. Therefore,
reasons for the disappearance can be leaching and/or degradation. As earlier studies
have indicated, the retention of CLZ is high and degradation is fast when CLZ is in the
subsurface (Barra et al., 1995; Melsbach et al., 2020; Schuhmann et al., 2016), hence
this could be a possible reason for high concentrations of DPC and low to absent CLZ
concentration in the soil and clayey till.
High sorption aﬃnity of DPC has been observed in the topsoil. However this does not
seem to inhibit the degradation of DPC to MDPC, since the highest metabolite-to-parent
ratios are seen in these sections at both investigated sites, which suggest that further
degradation to MDPC has occurred. However, given the still high presence of DPC in
the soils, this may occur rather slow, which is supported by the results from Melsbach
et al. (2020). From 2 to 3 m bgl. the degradation rates seem to decrease rapidly with
depth, and at a depth of 3.0 m bgl. no signiﬁcant concentrations of MDPC, hence
degradation of DPC has been observed. This can be due to higher leaching potential of
MDPC, but since the detection of MDPC is less than DPC in the groundwater, other
factors such as further degradation can be possible. However, the degradation pathways
of MDPC have not been fully investigated, thus this is only speculations.
The degradation of DPC and further transformation of MDPC is still debated. Given
the high metabolite-to-parent ratios of DPC and MDPC in the top soils, it is concluded
that a higher degradation rate has occurred in these sediments. It would therefore be
interesting to investigate the microbial degradation and taxonomy in the diﬀerent soil
intervals at Site 1. This may further clarify the degradation pathways of DPC.
7.3
Future use of soil analyses
Surprisingly high concentrations of CLZ’s metabolites were measured in the soil and
clayey till. These results emphasise the risk of multiple point source contaminations,
whereas the unsaturated zone is a reservoir of high concentrations of these or other
metabolites.
The study of pesticide point sources diﬀers from other point source studies, since
many pesticides are relatively water soluble and non-volatile. Therefore, in the case of
several pesticides it has been diﬃcult to estimate the source for pesticide leaching from
soil and pore air samples and the investigation of DPC point sources has so far been
characterised by groundwater or porewater sampling. However, this study highlights
the possibility to estimate the vertical distribution and to provide the best indication
of the source of pollution of DPC with only limited time and cost. This technique is
less costly compared to deep groundwater samples, and can quickly provide a better
understanding of the horizontal and vertical spreading of CLZ, DPC and MDPC at
point source contaminations.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Two pesticide point sources have been investigated to clarify if CLZ, DPC and MDPC
are accumulating in the soil and the unsaturated zone, thus acting as a secondary source.
Soil and sediment samples were extracted from the two sites down to the shallow
aquifer at Site 2 on Femø (10 m bgl.) and down to the water table at Site 1 on Lolland
(3 m bgl.). The total concentration of CLZ, DPC and MDPC were analysed both at
Université de Neuchâtel (UniNE) and at ALS A/S and TOC, clay and water contents
were analysed in order to asses if the retention of CLZ, DPC and MDPC is related to
geological properties.
To better predict the retention of DPC in Danish sediments a laboratory sorption
experiment was conducted. Eight samples with diﬀerent geological properties (TOC,
grain size distribution and clay content) were investigated with the addition of low
environmental relevant concentrations of DPC.
Lastly, an analytical 1-dimensional modelling tool, which considers secondary diﬀu-
sive sources in a fractured clayey till, was applied to estimate the longevity and extent
of the DPC contamination in the unsaturated zone, shallow aquifer and deep aquifer for
water supply.
The conclusions can be summarised to:
• Soil and sediment samples below a previous washing pool and a previous place for
manure were investigated. The stratigraphic levels were organic rich loamy sandy
soils, with an underlying thick, oxidised, fractured, sandy clayey till. The shallow
aquifer comprises embedded sand lenses between 8 and 12 m bgl. A deep aquifer
for water supply is 20 and 50 m bgl. and is conﬁned by a 8 to 30 m thick reduced
clayey till at Site 1 and Site 2, respectively.
• Surprisingly high total concentrations of DPC and MDPC were still present in
the soil and unsaturated clayey till samples 24 years after the prohibition of CLZ
application. Bell-shaped vertical distribution of DPC was evident down to the
shallow aquifer, which highly indicates that retention, thus accumulation, of DPC
and MDPC are greater than previous estimated. Due to low to absent measured
total concentrations of CLZ in the soil samples and high total concentrations of
DPC, it is concluded that the retention of DPC in soil and unsaturated zone is
responsible for the elevated DPC concentrations in the groundwater and not the
ongoing degradation of CLZ. Furthermore, higher metabolite-to-parent ratios of

MDPC/DPC are seen in the aerobic top soils, which indicate high degradation
rates of DPC in soils. This is however decreasing with depth, and limited degra-
dation of DPC in clayey tills is seen.
• The vertical distribution of DPC may be dependent on the geological properties

to ﬁner clay particles. However, no other signiﬁcant relations between CLZ, DPC
and MDPC and geological properties have been observed.
• The sorption of DPC in clayey till and clay-rich sediments was best described by
Freundlich isotherms, whereas the sorption coeﬃcient, Kf, of DPC to clayey till
samples was between 0.81 and 2.14 L/kg with curvatures, 1/n, between 0.60 and
0.88. Sorption to sandy aquifer sediments with low organic carbon content is best
described by linear isotherms, with a Kvalue of 0.08 L/kg. At low concentrations
(<100 µg/L) a linear sorption coeﬃcient in Danish clayey tills, Kd, clayey till: 0.52
L/kg, is suﬃcient in risk assessments. The evaluated higher sorption coeﬃcients
in clay-rich sediments could highly contribute to the accumulation of DPC in the
unsaturated zone and result in a secondary source. The high sorption aﬃnities
in clayey till may lead to accumulation of DPC in the unsaturated zone, however,
they are still suﬃciently small to cause elevated porewater concentrations.
• Due to the estimated sorption tendency of DPC a long-term leaching of DPC at
relevant concentrations is expected after application stop in the shallow aquifer.
Furthermore, it is expected that DPC concentrations will reach the deep aquifer
with leaching concentrations exceeding the groundwater criteria of 0.1 µg/L. How-
ever, it is not expected that the contaminant mass discharge will aﬀect the nearby
water supply wells.
The extent and magnitude of DPC contamination in the soil and in the unsaturated
zone at point source contaminations has been clariﬁed in Southern Zealand in Denmark.
The detection of surprisingly high concentrations of DPC and to some degree MDPC has
clariﬁed that the retention, thus accumulation, of these metabolites are much higher than
previously believed. This can act as a secondary long-term source of DPC contamination
causing signiﬁcant elevated concentrations in the groundwater.

Point source contamination with Chloridazon
In theory there are two fundamental types of groundwater pollution sources; point
sources and nonpoint sources. point sources pollution are identiﬁed as a high pollution
concentration at small areas, whereas nonpoint sources consist of low concentrations
of contaminants at larger areas (Tuxen et al., 2013). As illustrated in Figure A.2 the
plume from a potential point source contamination of pesticides is rather narrow and
characterised with high concentration gradients horizontally and vertically through the
subsurface to the aquifers (Tuxen et al., 2013).

diﬀuse. However, in the context of pesticide point sources, it is often more challenging

and PAH’s since these contaminants are more often detected on or near old laundries,
industrial sites, gasworks and tank stations. Whereas point source contamination with
pesticides are often due to pesticide loss in the process of ﬁlling of sprayers, washing of

Reasons for pesticide point sources in Denmark
In 1960’s the Danish Ministry of Agriculture released the ﬁrst instructions for proper
pesticide management, including storage and disposal of pesticide residues. By this time
it was advised to bury the residues, but since 1976, the disposal of pesticides has been
regulated as hazardous waste. In 1987 it was emphasised that non-combustible packag-
ing was no longer allowed to be buried, but had to be handed over to the municipalities’
receiving stations together with any pesticide residues (Tuxen et al., 2013). Concur-
rently with the instructions for proper pesticide management in the 1960’s the ﬁrst

In Denmark herbicides with chloridazon as the main agent have been used in connection

cattle in connection with milk production (Roost & Lenschow, no date). In the 1880’s,
beet cultivation began in earnest in Denmark. The beets worked as feed for the cows,
since they ensured plenty of milk throughout the winter. This was a necessity since
the milk was to be delivered to the new cooperative dairies, which were set up all
over the country during the decade. However, beets were slowly replaced with maize
as coarse fodder for the cattle in the beginning of the 1980s. The milk producers have
primarily been linked to the sandy soils, which is why beets for fodder crops has primarily
been grown in Jutland as illustrated in Figure A.3. As seen in Figure A.3 fodder beet
cultivation has been declining for several years, and are now close to non existing in
Denmark (Egdal, 2020).
Sugar beets on the other hand is still cultivated in Denmark on approximately 33,000
ha land (Landbrug og Fødevarer, 2018). Today, the cultivation is concentrated in Lol-

sugar beets. Previously, there was also a large production of sugar beet on Funen, Mern
and in Southeast Jutland, but production here is now very limited after the closure of
the sugar factory in Assens in 2006, as transport of the beets over longer distances
is relatively expensive (Landbrug og Fødevarer, 2018; Roost & Lenschow, no date).

A.2
Transport processes
The concentration of contaminants in groundwater is primarily controlled by the amount
of contaminant present at the source, the rate of release from the source, hydrological
factors, such as dilution, advection and dispersion. Furthermore, in order to predict the
contaminant transport through the subsurface, it is important that the essential geochem-
ical proccesses aﬀecting the transport are identiﬁed. These processes include aqueous
geochemical processes, adsorption/desorption, precipitation, and diﬀusion.
A.2.1
Transport processes
In the subsurface the transport of substances is governed by the interactions between
geological and hydrogeological conditions and the properties of the substance (Bjerg &
Albrechtsen, 2000). When pesticides and metabolites is dissolved in the water phase, the

Advection is the direct transport process by which dissolved substances are co-

root zones (Jarvis, 2007).
Diﬀusion is a random transport process, in which concentration gradients are the
driving force (Nazaroﬀ& Alvarez-Cohen, 2001; Schwarzenbach et al., 2003). Matrix

diﬀusion is the process in which dissolved solutes diﬀuses into a ﬁne-grained matrix.
Diﬀusion is described with Fick’s law, which relates the diﬀusive ﬂux of a chemical to
its concentration gradient and to its molecular diﬀusion coeﬃcient, D. This coeﬃcient is
estimated from the molecular properties of the respective compounds (molecular weight
and volume). However, when looking at diﬀusion into soil matrices the eﬀective diﬀusion
is often applied, which is the molecular diﬀusion multiplied the porosity of the matrix
and an empirical factor of 0.66. This diﬀusion coeﬃcient is called eﬀective diﬀusion.
Dispersion is the third important transport process (Nazaroﬀ& Alvarez-Cohen, 2001;
Schwarzenbach et al., 2003). This process is, like diﬀusion, a random transport process,
and due to turbulence of the surrounding medium mixing processes will occur. Disper-
sion is caused by the properties of the ﬂow ﬁeld and diﬀerent velocities in the adjacent
streamlines. In soil matrices dispersion is usually caused by diﬀerences in ﬂow length due
to diﬀerent pore sizes (Schwarzenbach et al., 2003). Dispersion is always linked to ﬂow
processes and generally occur in greater magnitude compared to diﬀusion (Schwarzen-

C.3
Soil sample procedure in UniNE
The procedure for extraction of CLZ, DPC and MDPC is described in Hintze (2020),
in which the following is described:
"For the soil extraction, 0.150 g of dry soil was weighed in a 2.5 mL Eppendorf tube.
1.25 mL of methanol (Chromasolv, for pesticide residue analysis, Honeywell) and 15
µL of an isotopically labeled standard solution, which contained 10 ng mL-1 of CLZ-d5,
DPC-15N2 and MDPC-d3, were added to the tube. This isotopically labeled standard
solution was obtained by dilution of the following stock solutions in methanol: 100 g mL-
1 CLZ-d5 in Acetonitrile (Dr. Ehrensdorfer, LGC Standards), 100 g mL-1 DPC-15N2 in
Acetonitrile (HPC Standards) and 10 g mL-1 MDPC-d3 in Acetonitrile (HPC Standards).
The tubes were shaken in a mixer mill (QIAGEN TissueLyser II) with 10 glass beads (1.0
1.3 mm, SiLibeads) for 3 minutes. The tubes were centrifuged (13500 rounds per minute,
3 minutes) and as much as possible of the methanol phase was withdrawn into a second
2.5 mL Eppendorf tube. After this step, another 0.5 mL of methanol was added to the soil
in the Eppendorf tube and it was shaken in the mixer mill for another 3 minutes. After
a second centrifugation (13500 rounds per minutes, 3 minutes), the methanol phase was
taken out and combined with the methanol in the second Eppendorf tube. The methanol
phase was evaporated under vacuum (Labconco Refrigerated CentriVap Concentrator +
Labconco CentriVap Cold Trap) at 35 řC until dryness. For the reconstitution of the
target compounds, 2 mL of ultra-pure water (MilliQ-Direct-Q 3UV-R, Merck) were added
to the Eppendorf tube, mixed (vortex) and put in an ultrasonic bath for 10 minutes. These
2 mL were later loaded onto the solid phase extraction (SPE) cartridge. For the SPE, 3
mL polypropylene cartridges packed with 200 mg of LiChrolut EN sorbent (VWR) were
used. The SPE was performed by a positive pressure manifold (Biotage Pressure+ 48).
The cartridges were ﬁrst conditioned with 3 mL of methanol and equilibrated with 3 mL of
ultra-pure water. In a third step, the reconstituted samples (2 mL) were loaded onto the
cartridge and impurities were washed with 3 mL of ultra-pure water. For the elution in
13 x 100 mm glass tubes (Pyrex Borosilicate Glass), 3 mL of a solution with ethyl acetate
(Fisher chemical) and methanol (50:50, v/v) was used. The ethyl acetate-methanol phase
was evaporated under vacuum at 35 řC until dryness. The target compounds were again
reconstituted with 150 L of ultra-pure water, mixed (vortex) and put in an ultrasonic bath.
The samples were ﬁltered by a 4 mm syringe disk ﬁlter with a pore size of 0.2 m (PTFE,
hydrophilic) and were ﬁlled in a 1.5 mL glass vial with a conical glass insert for analysis.
In the same way, blanc samples were prepared, but without adding soil. These extracts
were analyzed for CLZ, DPC and MDPC by an Acquity UPLC system coupled to a TQ-S
mass spectrometer (Waters)."

Conceptual models in the analytical risk

The model assumes a uniform distribution of the fractures, which means the fractures
have the same aperture (2b) and distance to each other (2B). In the model only a
single fracture is considered, which is surrounded by an semi-inﬁnite matrix (Chambon
et al., 2011). The risk assessment tool supply three diﬀerent conceptual models based
on source history and geometry (Chambon et al., 2011). Model 1a and 1b represent a
source overlying a fractured media for either a known duration (Model 1a) or for inﬁnite
time (Model 1b). In the third model (Model 1c) the source has been removed, but the

APPENDIX D
Adsorption using a batch
equilibrium method
Adsorption/desorption studies are useful for achieving information on the mobility of
a chemical and its distribution in the soil, water, and air (OECD, 2000). The given
sorption data can be used to estimate the leaching of DPC through the soil proﬁle and
for modelling purposes.
The following sorption experiment were conducted by a laboratory batch equilibrium
technique and follows the OECD Guideline 106 (OECD, 2000) concerning preparation
of soil and solution, the soil:solution ratio, the concentration changes in the solution
due to sorption, and the equilibration time determination. This setup will give valuable
information regarding the environmental relevance of the sorption of DPC. However,
since the distribution of a chemical between soil and aqueous phases is a much more
complex process depending of a number of diﬀerent factors (e.g. characterisation of
soil and climatic factors) the process of sorption cannot be fully deﬁned by a single
laboratory experiment.
The test is designed to evaluate the sorption of DPC in diﬀerent soil comprising
clayey tills in Denmark with a diﬀerent clay content, organic carbon content and soil
texture. The structure of the study follows a modiﬁed edition of the guideline made by
OECD (2000); a preliminary study and a screening test. Preliminary studies includes
the determination of the soil/solution ratio and the expected amount of DPC adsorped
at equilibrium from given Kd-values. In the OECD-guideline the preliminary studies
also includes determination of the equilibrium time for adsorption. However, this is
not known, and therefore, the mixture will be shaken for 12 days, to ensure adsorption
equilibrium (according to OECD (2000) 24h is generally suﬃcient). In the screening test
the sorption is studied in 8 diﬀerent soil types (mainly clayey till) in order to determine
the adsorption kinetics at 5 diﬀerent concentrations and to determine the distribution
coeﬃcients Kd and Koc.
