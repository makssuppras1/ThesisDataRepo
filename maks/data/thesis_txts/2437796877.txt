Abstract
Most additive manufacturing (AM) processes are based on a 2.5D layered approach,
where a 3 dimensional geometry is build up in ﬂat layers. A common additive manufactur-
ing class is thereby material extrusion based manufacturing. Due to the process principle,
extrusion based AM suffers from limitations: The surface accuracy and material properties
of a part is highly dependent on the build direction during the process. This results in a
poor surface ﬁnish when generating rounded or inclined geometries (stair-step effect) and
anisotropic material properties, with higher material strength along the layer than in build
direction. Further, overhangs cannot be produced without an additional support structure.
This project presents an approach to solve these issues by introducing out-of-plane
deposition and moving the current 2.5D layered principle in extrusion based AM to true 3D
printing. Therefore a 5-axis deposition machine is developed and validated. The platform
is based on the design of a linear parallel Delta machine, for which a 5-axis kinematics
model is developed and implemented. A custom-made control system for this new material
extrusion machine is developed. Further, this work presents a 5-axis toolpath generation
tool to realize deposition with 5 degrees of freedom.
The project shows that it is possible to develop a parallel Delta machine with 5 degrees
of freedom. It demonstrates that this platform has the potential to overcome the current
limits of extrusion based AM. A test sample is presented with improved surface accuracy
produced by surface conformal deposition.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Additive manufacturing (AM) has become an important manufacturing technology
because it offers a signiﬁcant amount of freedom when producing geometries compared to
conventional manufacturing processes [6, 7]. Essentially, any geometry can be produced
at relatively low manufacturing costs even for single parts. Complex geometries, light
weight constructions or parts with inner cavities can be produced without the necessity of
extra tooling. There are different technologies within the ﬁeld of AM and a wide range of
materials like polymers, metals or ceramics can be processed [8, 9]. The basic principle of
all classes of AM is that they build up a 3 dimensional geometry layer by layer in a building
direction. The toolpath or coordinates of the layers can thereby be derived directly from a
CAD model, which makes it a fast and easy to use manufacturing process. This makes
it valuable especially for prototyping but increasingly also for industrial manufacturing
for parts of low volume or complex geometries [7]. A common AM class is thereby the
material extrusion process [10]. For readability, material extrusion AM process is referred
to as ME or extrusion process throughout this report.
In material extrusion AM a material, mostly thermoplastic polymers, is fed into a
deposition nozzle, where the material is melted. Thereby is the material delivered in form
of a ﬁlament or bulk material. The melted material is then extruded through the nozzle
and precisely deposited precisely onto a build surface [11]. That way a geometry is build
out of layers having a certain layer height, forming the 3 dimensional part [7] (see Figure
1.1 a). The material extrusion process enables the generation of complex geometries, is
thereby easy to handle and allows to process different, mostly polymer materials [12].
There are some materials like Polylactic acid (PLA) that allow to use the process in normal
environments like e.g. an ofﬁce surrounding without additional safety precautions [6, 8].
This makes it an ideal process for rapid prototyping or research.
Additive manufacturing is commonly also known as 3D printing. The actual depo-
sition principle is though a 2.5D layered approach, meaning that the individual layers

1. The contour of the part is discretized in building direction, which results in a poor

material strength is much higher within the layer than in building direction, due to
lower fusion of the material between the layers.
In summary, these limitations lead to the conclusion, that the material properties and
the surface quality of a part produced by the material extrusion process depend heavily on
the build direction during the process. The goal of this project is to develop an approach
to solve these issues by introducing out-of-plane deposition and moving the current
2.5D layered approach in extrusion-based additive manufacturing to true 3D printing.
Out-of-plane deposition refers thereby to the ability of the toolhead to deposit material
onto a non-planar surface in the cartesian space. Therefore a deposition machine with 2
additional degrees of freedom is developed and validated. This machine is able to perform
additionally to translational movement also rotational movements around the horizontal
axes of the deposition tool in the cartesian space. Therefore it is able to deposit material
with 5 degrees of freedom.

1.1. BACKGROUND
1.1
Background
1.1.1
Machine layouts for material extrusion AM
For extrusion based AM a common layout is the cartesian 3-axis 3D printer (see Figure
1.2 a). The toolhead holding the deposition nozzle is controlled by 3 linear axes that move
the deposition tool along the x, y and z axis of the build chamber. The kinematics model
behind this printer layout is relatively simple and several open-source ﬁrmwares to control
a 3-axis cartesian printer are available [1, 14].
A newer layout, which is used in this project is based on a linear parallel Delta machine
(see Figure 1.2 b). Another term commonly used to describe this machine layout is parallel
Delta robot. This machine consists of 3 vertical towers, where 6 effector arms are moving
pair-wise vertically on rails. These effector arms are connected to the toolhead and control
the position and movement of the deposition tool. The effector arms on one tower are
connected at the tower joint and the toolhead and form a parallelogram which leads to
a kinematics system with 3 degrees of freedom (DOF), in which the toolhead is always
parallel to the build plane and only translation in x, y and z direction is possible [3, 14].
This machine layout will be referred to as Delta or parallel conﬁguration throughout this
report.
Advantages of the Delta conﬁguration are fast movements of the toolhead at good
dynamic performance and a high accuracy of the movements [15, 16]. The robots are
used in industry for fast pick and place tasks[17]. Further, the compact layout enables a
relatively large build envelope and can therefore easily be scaled in size.
The kinematics model of a parallel Delta machine is more complex than the cartesian
layout. The 3-axis kinematics model to control a Delta parallel machine for additive
manufacturing was developed before and is implemented in some open-source ﬁrmwares.

1.1.2
Recent advancements on the material extrusion process
This section will present recent research on the material extrusion process. The high-
lighted research and development projects introduce different approaches by researchers
to optimize the ME process. Several research groups in the recent years presented their
work to increase the surface accuracy, reduce the amount of support material or improve
the material properties of the produced parts. This gives an introduction to the problem,
where this projet is aimed at and presents an overview about the different approaches that
are already documented.
Jin et al. [21] and Tyberg and Bøhn [4] presented an approach on increasing the surface
quality of curved geometries in build direction by adaptive toolpath generation which is
illustrated in Figure 1.3 a). When generating the toolpath for an object, the layer height in
a 3-axis system changes depending on the geometry: To approximate a curved contour the
layer height is reduced on curved surfaces reducing the stair-step effect.
A different approach was presented by Allen and Trask [5], who invented another type
of adaptive toolpath generation. To improve the surface accuracy of a curved structure, the
ﬁnal layers were deposited following the topology of the part instead of depositing layer
by layer in building direction. Therefore the geometry was divided into a support structure
and the ﬁnal surface layer, which they refer to as skin (see Figure 1.3 b). They showed that
through this approach the surface accuracy of rounded geometries can be improved and the
4
==PAGE:29==
1.2. PROJECT SCOPE
stair-step effect is decreased. They used a 3-axis Delta printer for their study and conclude
that the maximum slope of the curvature is limited due to the shape of the nozzle, which
collide with the support structure. Further the deposition of the material is distorted due to
the extruder nozzle not being normal to the deposition surface [5]. A 5-axis toolpath could
solve this issue.
Another approach was presented by Pandey et al. [22], who use a hybrid manufacturing
process, where extrusion based AM is combined with a ﬁnal subtractive machining step to
improve the surface ﬁnish. These three approaches are aimed at the ﬁnal surface ﬁnish of
the part, but have no inﬂuence on the material properties.
There are also some publications on out-of-plane deposition. Kubalak et al. [12, 23],
Gier [24] and Ishak et al. [25, 26] used an industrial robotic arm to move the deposition
tool along a 5-axis or 6-axis toolpath. Ishak et al. showed that printing of overhangs is
possible without support structure. Kubalak et al. researched the tensile and maximum
compressional strength of material within the layer and orthogonal to the build plane
and showed the anisotropic behaviour due to the layered ME process. In a recent project
they tested the tensile strength of an ABS specimen, where a reinforcing layer was added
using a 6-axis deposition toolpath. This resulted in an increased tensile modulus and yield
strength compared to the 3-axis reference sample [23].
Further Grutle [27] developed a modiﬁed cartesian 3D printer with a movable build
plate that is able to realize a 5-axis toolpath. He showed that it is possible to print overhangs
without support structure and that the surface ﬁnish can be improved by out-of-plane
deposition.
Furthermore, the company 5axismaker [28] developed a cartesian 3D printer with a
movable toolhead to enable a 5-axis toolpath and out-of-plane deposition.
This section shows that the limitations of the current material extrusion process in
AM are a relevant topic that is researched by several groups. The presented research aims
at improving the surface accuracy of round parts, improving the material properties and
being able to print overhangs without support structure. The problem statement for this
project is derived from these literature review and will be presented in the next section.
1.2
Project scope
1.2.1
Problem statement
The material extrusion base additive manufacturing process is based on a 2.5D layered
approach, where a 3 dimensional geometry is build up in layers. Due to this principle,
the material extrusion process suffers from limitations: The surface quality and material
properties of a part produced by the material extrusionprocess is highly dependent on the

building direction during the process. This results in a poor surface ﬁnish when printing
rounded or inclined geometries (stair-step effect) and anisotropic material properties, where
the material strength is much higher within the layer than in building direction. Further
overhangs cannot be produced without additional support structure [13, 7, 6, 12, 8].
1.2.2
Research questions
This work engages with the following research questions:
• Is it possible to develop a parallel Delta machine with 5 DOF?
• How can a control system for a motion system with 5 DOF be realized?
• How can a 5-axis toolpath be generated?
• What is the potential of 5 DOF additive manufacturing?
• Has the developed platform the potential to reduce the current limitations of extrusion
based AM?
1.2.3
Outline of the project
The literature review showed that the improvement of the material extrusionprocess is
a relevant and ongoing topic, which is being researched by different groups using different
approaches.
The goal of this project is the software and electronics development of a manufacturing
machine for big area additive manufacturing that enables material deposition in 5 degrees
of freedom. It allows out-of-plane material deposition, which makes it possible to elevate
the current 2.5D layer approach used in most additive manufacturing processes into true
3D additive manufacturing and optimize the material extrusionprocess which suffers from
certain limitations.
The novelty in this project is that the deposition system is realized in the design of a
parallel kinematics machine, so that out-of-plane deposition is enabled while the beneﬁts
of the machine characteristics are used. This was never done before and required the
development of a new kinematics model to control the effector arms of the machine. The
Delta design offers high accuracy in movement and good dynamic performance [15, 5].
Moreover, due to its compact design, the Delta design is especially suited for big area
additive manufacturing and scaling in size is easily possible.

The basic principle is shown in ﬁgure 2.2 b. By moving the effector arms pairwise along
the towers in vertical direction the position of the toolhead is controlled. In common
parallel Delta designs, the carriages on one tower are controlled by one single stepper
motor and therefore always move parallel to each other. This creates a parallelogram
consisting of the push rods, the distance between the mounting points of the carriages
and mounting points of the toolhead. The three parallelograms lock the rotation of the
toolhead around its axes. This creates a kinematics system, where the tool head is parallel
to the build plane at all times when moving the effector arms. The toolhead is limited to
translational movement and rotation or tilting of the toolhead around its axes is prevented
[18]. This allows to move the tool tip to any coordinate in the cartesian build volume.

movements it allows to totate the toolhead around its x and y axis. By establishing an
inverse kinematics system, the toolhead can be moved to any coordinate in the cartesian
space and can be tilted around its horizontal axes. Figure 2.2 c) and d) shows the toolhead

2.3
Implementation of the electronics
In the previous state of the platform, the effector arms could only be moved pair
wise, which enabled a 3-axis motion system. The motors on one tower were connected
in a series connection, which led them act like a single axis. To be able to control the
effector arms individually, each of the stepper motors had to be controlled by the micro
controller individually. Further the end stop sensors for each of the 6 effector arms had
to be monitored individually. This required to connect the stepper motors directly to the
Arduino micro controller. Furthermore, the extrusion of the material is controlled by
another stepper motor, which also had to be connected. Figure 2.4 gives an overview about
the implemented electronics system to control the 6 effector arms and the extruder motor.
The way the micro controller is communicating with the stepper motors of the machine
is by giving 5 V pulses to the driver of the steppers. The micro controller has therefore
different output and input pins. These hardware pins have speciﬁc numbers, which can be
assigned with variables in the ﬁrmware. The stepper drivers and end stop sensor are then
connected to individual pins and the pin number is assigned to a parameter in the ﬁrmware.
The stepper motors are connected by cables to individual output pins and the 6 end stop
sensors are connected to input pins. By assigning the pin number to the speciﬁc stepper
motor or end stop, the motors can be controlled by the ﬁrmware on the micro controller.
The movement of the stepper motors is thereby controlled by 3 signals. Therefore 3 pins
have to be connected and assigned for each of the 7 stepper motors. The motors are
controlled by a enable, direction and step pin. The enable pin turns the motor on and off
while the direction pin sets the turning direction of the motor. This is controlled by setting
the corresponding pin on the Arduino constantly to either 0 V or 5 V. Thereby is the 0 V
or 5 V signal assigned to a speciﬁc state of the stepper motor: If for example the direction
signal cable is set to 0 V , the stepper driver turns the motor in one speciﬁc direction. The
5 V state of the cable is assigned to the opposite turning direction. To move the motor, a
5 V pulse is sent to the stepper driver. This signalizes to move the motor one step, which is
a fraction of a turn. To move the effector arms simultaneously, the micro controller has to
send pulses to all of the motors. This is managed by the ﬁrmware, which will be presented
in chapter 3.
The end stops for each axis on the towers are monitored by input pins on the micro
controller. There the end stop switch closes a circuit and sets the corresponding pin on
the Arduino to either 0 V or 5 V, which signalizes that the end stop is closed or opened.
Table 2.1 gives the overview about how the stepper motors are connected to the pins on the
Arduino. It also states the parameter that is assigned in the ﬁrmware for the speciﬁc pin.

CHAPTER 3
Control System
This chapter engages with the control system of the machine and explains the method-
ology to establish a motion system with 5 DOF. The position of the toolhead is deﬁned by
its position in the cartesian coordinate system P(x, y, z) and its normal vector ⃗Vn, which
deﬁnes the angles α, β that the toolhead is tilted around the x and y-axis (see Figure 3.2).
The developed control system is divided into two parts: To move the extrusion tool to a
speciﬁc cartesian coordinate P and to a speciﬁc normal vector ⃗Vn, an inverse kinematics
model for the parallel Delta machine is applied, which determines the positions of the
arms on the towers to move the toolhead to the desired position. This is done using a
MATLAB script, which exports a machine speciﬁc G-code. By applying it to a sequence of
coordinates, that describe a 5-axis toolpath, a G-code ﬁle can be generated. The generation
of the entire 5-axis toolpath will be covered in chapter 4.
Secondly, the control of the effector arms of the machine according to the machine
speciﬁc G-code is executed. The control of the effector arms of the delta robot is then
controlled and monitored by an Arduino micro controller. To be able to control a total of 7
stepper motors individually a ﬁrmware for the micro controller was developed.
The chapter will ﬁrst give an overview of the concept of the control system. Then
the developed kinematics system is presented. Lastly, the different parts of the developed
ﬁrmware for the micro controller are explained.

3.4
Firmware development
This section explains the development of the ﬁrmware that is used to control the
effector arms of the machine. To control the effector arms of the machine and therefore the
movement of the toolhead, an Arduino micro controller is used. This micro controller has
different input and output pins that are connected to the stepper drivers of the effector arms
and to the end stops of the individual axes as described in chapter 2. The micro controller
is connected to a work station via a serial connection. When the connection is established,
a program (G-code sender) can send commands to the micro controller, which is received
and processed by the ﬁrmware on the controller. This ﬁrmware was developed during this
project and will be further explained now.
It was necessary to develop a custom-made ﬁrmware for the controller, since no
open-source ﬁrmware was available that allows to control 6 effector arms and a stepper
motor used for the extrusion of ﬁlament. The ﬁrmware is a further development of the
open-source ﬁrmware 6 Axis CNC Demo Rumba [29]. A motion planner was implemented,
that allows to control the stepping of the axes based on a timer interrupt. Further an
additional axis was implemented to be able to control 6 stepper motors for the effector
arms and the motor controlling the ﬁlament extrusion. Furthermore, a homing sequence
was established to be able to home all 6 axes individually. Finally, the serial communication
to the work station was expanded by a checksum control function. These different parts
will be explained throughout this section and in the following sub-sections. First the overall
functionality of the ﬁrmware is explained. For readability, the positions of the effector
arms on each tower are referred to as axes X, Y, Z, U, V, W.

3.4.4
Implementation of a motion planner
In a ﬁrst test of a prior implementation of the ﬁrmware it was discovered that the
motion of the toolhead was irregular when a sequence of several G-code commands was
sent. The axis stopped moving when the ﬁnal coordinate of the current G-code command
was reached. This lead to a uneven movement of the toolhead, which would not allow a
constant deposition of material. The reason for that was, that the receiving and processing
of the commands was done in the same sequence as the moving of the stepper motors:
31
==PAGE:56==
CHAPTER 3. CONTROL SYSTEM
When a G-code is sent to the Arduino via the serial connection, this command is parsed
and the amount of steps each axis has to move is calculated. All axes are then moved
until all of them reach their ﬁnal position. When all axis have reached their ﬁnal position,
a new G-code command is requested. This way, the time the Arduino takes to actually
receive, parse and calculate the steps to move results in a stop of the motion of the axis.
This lead to a break between the execution of two G-code commands and to a stopping of
the movement.
Therefore a motion planner was implemented. This motion planner is realized by
separating the program sequence into two parts: The main loop of the program receives
and processes G-code commands and a timer controlled interrupt loop moves the axis.
Thereby saves the main loop several already processed G-code commands in a buffer
and the interrupt loop is only triggering the stepper pins according to the parameters in
the buffer. This way the following motion can be already prepared while the previous
movement is executed. Additionally, by using a timer-interrupt the stepping of the axis is
driven by the internal timer of the Arduino clock and the time between each step can be
controlled precisely, which leads to a constant velocity of the axes.

3.4.6
Change of feedrate
The interval the interrupt is called depends on the frequency of the Arduino clock. To
initiate an interrupt loop an internal timer can be used. The Arduino Mega 2560 micro
controller that is used in this project has two 8-bit and four 16-bit timers [31]. The timers
increment a variable until they reach a maximum value. The frequency the timers are
incremented is thereby the frequency of the Arduino processor, which is 16 MHz for the
Arduino Mega. The timer mode that is implemented in the ﬁrmware is called Clear Timer
on Compare Match mode (CTC). Here the timer interrupt loop is triggered when the timer
reaches a speciﬁc value, which is called compare match register. When it reaches that value
it resets to 0 and continues to count up again. By choosing this value and the frequency
the timer increments the variable, the frequency, how often the interrupt loop is called can
be controlled [32].
The clock frequency of the Arduino is 16 MHz, which means that the timer can be
incremented in that maximum frequency. The maximum value the timer counter can save
depends on the timer used: A 16-bit timer can save a maximum counter value of 65535,
while a 8-bit timer saves a maximum value of 255. This value therefore determines how
reﬁned the time can be set until the timer reaches its maximum. The frequency the timer is
incremented can be set by a prescaler which can have the values 1, 8, 64, 256, and 1024.

Implementation of a checksum function
During ﬁrst tests problems occurred due to corrupted G-code commands, where during
the serial communication between the G-code sender (Pronterface) and the micro controller
single chars of the G-command were missing. This led to errors when the axes were moved,
when e.g. a digit was missing that described the coordinate:
G1 X123 Y234 Z345 U456 V567 W678
-> Sent by G-code sender
G1 X123 Y234 Z345 U456 V67 W678
-> Received
In this example, one digit of the coordinate of axis V is missing, which leads to a difference
of 500 mm. Therefore it was decided to implement a function into the ﬁrmware that
calculates a checksum from the received G-command and compares it with a received
checksum to ensure, that the G-code sequence is transmitted correctly. This was very
important, since a toolpath to deposit a geometry or surface consists of a large number of

3.5
Chapter summary
This chapter presented the work that was done to develop a motion system for the
Delta parallel machine. The control system comprises a 5 DOF kinematics system, which
is implemented in MATLAB and determines the positions of the effector arms to place
the toolhead at a speciﬁc position P0 in direction of the normal vector ⃗Vn. This makes it
possible to generate machine speciﬁc G-code commands that can be processed with the
micro controller, that controls the movement of the effector arms. For the micro controller
a ﬁrmware was developed with an integrated motion planner and a checksum function.
The combination of these two systems enables the 5-axis control system of the machine.
The next chapter explain on how the toolpath, that consists of several G-code commands
is generated using the developed kinematics model. In chapter 5 the validation of the
developed control system is presented using a generated toolpath.

4.1
Discussion of deposition strategies
A deposition platform that is able to perform movements along a 5-axis toolpath
opens up several opportunities to manufacture a geometry and allows to rethink how a
part can be produced taking advantage of the new degree of freedom during the process.
Due to the ability to tilt the toolhead material can be deposited onto any curvature in 3
dimensional space (out-of-plane deposition). This out-of-plane deposition can be used
to produce geometries aimed at optimized mechanical properties or can even enable new
manufacturing processes like continuous ﬁber deposition. This section will present basic
thoughts about the possible deposition strategies that a multi-axis deposition platform
offers and demonstrates thereby the various possibilities of how a 5-axis toolpath could be
generated aiming at different characteristics of the ﬁnal product. The following section
will discuss strategies to improve the material properties, followed sections elaborating on
surface conformal deposition, printing of overhangs and the manufacturing of continuous
ﬁber composites.
4.1.1

extrusionprocess and to even optimize the material characteristics of a geometry. As
described before in chapter 1, one of the big limitations of the ME process are the
anisotropic material properties due to the weaker bonding between the layers. With out-of-
plane deposition it is imaginable to reduce this limitation or even theoretically produce a
geometry with isotropic mechanical properties by distributing the orientation of the layers
equally. Figure 4.1 illustrates different approaches that could lead to a more isotropic
material behaviour in terms of mechanical strength. Illustration 4.1 a) shows the basic
principle how an object could be build up distributing the layer orientation in all cartesian

These theoretical approaches have also practical limitations that have to be solved:
Firstly, when depositing along the z-axis of large object the effector arms could collide
with the already printed geometry. On the other hand, the presented geometries are
symmetrical which allows equally distributed material deposition. Complex geometries
are not necessarily symmetrical, so that equal distribution of the layers is hindered.
To maximize the strength distribution for complex geometries, analytical modelling
algorithms could be applied to calculate the required layer distribution and the toolpath,
taking local cross-sections and the bonding of different layer orientation into account.
Continuing that thought, the toolpath could be optimized in terms of the load bearing
proﬁle of the geometry: Through mathematical modelling the load bearing proﬁle of the
geometry is determined and the ideal layer orientation and corresponding toolpath can be
derived. Similar work has been made by Wu et al. [36] for porous light-weight structures
with optimized topology to improve the structural robustness and load bearing capacities.
Knowledge that is already accumulated could be utilized to further develop this approach
to produce a topology optimizing 5-axis toolpath.
This could be a great advancement towards the mechanical use of parts produced by ME.
The great potential could be, that components of products that are mechanically strained
could be replaced by ME produced parts. This could be interesting for geometrically
complex parts that are expensive or difﬁcult to manufacture by other processes. This could
apply to products with low production volumes where injection molding or machining of
metal parts is expensive.
These ideas show the potential of a 5-axis process aimed at the optimization of the
mechanical properties of the part. The development of a toolpath generator that can take
these considerations into account seems very promising, but requires complex algorithms
that can take the different parameters into account and can generate a toolpath by analyzing
the geometry of a model.

4.1.4

A 5-axis toolpath enables new manufacturing possibilities in respect to continuous

deposition of carbon ﬁbers in sheets and binder material into a mould and a ﬁnal curing
process [38]. Another process to produce composite structures is ﬁlament winding, where
continuous ﬁber bands are winded around a rotating spindle to produce axis symmetric
structures like pressure vessels [39, 37]. Further the pultrusion process produces parts of
continuous ﬁber composites with a round cross section by pulling the ﬁbers continuously
through a resin bath and a curing die and a ﬁnal cutting step [39, 37].
These processes could be taken for inspiration for a new prototyping process using
the 5-axis platform: The 5-axis platform has the potential to automatically lay down
continuous ﬁbers in a speciﬁed orientation to form a strong light-weight grid structure or

Development of a 5-axis toolpath generation tool
For regular 3-axis additive manufacturing several software solutions such as Cura
are known to generate G-code ﬁles from a given three dimensional model, as introduced

(computer aided manufacturing) software solutions are aimed at subtractive machining
like CNC milling and follow therefore different strategies when generating the toolpath:
Instead of building up the geometry from a build plane or surface, the toolpath is generated
by reducing a larger material volume. Additionally, the available CAM software is not

Establishing the deposition pattern
The printing strategy of the layer is established. To simplify the generation the pattern

having a round printing area, the different patterns can be rotated and combined onto each

only for geometries with small curvatures in z-direction. In the current setup the tilt angle
of the toolhead is limited due to the mechanical joints as it will be explained in the next
chapter 5. Therefore only geometries with low curvatures can be produced. As a future
perspective, the printing pattern has to be established taking the curvature of the surface
into account.

4.5.3
Surface approximation
After establishing the printing pattern, the surface topology is approximated. As shown
in Figure 4.9, the desired coordinates of the printing pattern lie within the grid of the
equally spaced x and y values of the surface. The surface approximation function is able
to approximate the height zP of the surface at any coordinate xP and yP of the surface
stored in the matrices X, Y and Z. This is realized by linear regression between the 2
closest values x and y and interpolation of the coordinate zP and the associated normal
vector ⃗Vn,P from the matrices Z, Nx, Ny and Nz.
This approach was chosen, because it gives the beneﬁt of approximating the points
instead of calculating the height of zP for each point using a mathematical function, which
would yield the same result in example of the sphere function. Moreover, this way the
surfnorm() - function can be utilized. The clear beneﬁt is now, that any type of surface
can be approximated using this MATLAB algorithm, which will be very useful when
surfaces from external sources are imported that are not following any mathematical
function.
The algorithm that approximates the height zP and the corresponding normal vector
⃗Vn,P for every coordinate of the established printing pattern is illustrated in Figure 4.10 a).

1. The cell in the matrices X and Y is found containing the closest value to xP and yP.

5.3
Determining process parameters
The developed tool to generate 5-axis G-code ﬁles uses different printing parameters
that inﬂuence the way, the tool path and the extrusion is generated. These parameters
inﬂuence the print quality. Finding the right parameters is necessary for proper material
deposition. These parameters are the following:
• Feedrate F [ mm
s ]
• Extrusion factor E0 [−]
• Line spacing t [mm]
• Layer height h [mm]
The feedrate is the velocity the effector arms are moving. The line spacing t refers to
the distance between two parallel printed lines. A too large line spacing results in space
between the deposited lines and a too small number results in too much material deposited
in the layer. The layer height h refers to the increment the toolhead is moved up to produce
a new layer. The extrusion factor E0 is the length of ﬁlament extruded per mm movement
of the nozzle.
To ﬁnd the ideal printing parameters to print the 5-axis sample, several tests were
performed. As initial conﬁguration, a conﬁguration that was used before to print in the
3-axis setup using Marlin ﬁrmware was applied. The approach to ﬁnd the correct printing
parameters was to perform several test prints with varying printing parameters to achieve
63
==PAGE:88==
CHAPTER 5. TESTING AND SYSTEM VALIDATION
an optimal print result. The way the 5-axis toolpath generator was developed allows that
the printing parameters can be changed during the same print from layer to layer. This
gives the opportunity to determine the ideal printing parameters for the given setup. The
initial extrusion factor E0 was implemented by analyzing the G-code ﬁle of the support
structure generated by Cura.
5.3.1
Feedrate
It was found that a appropriate feedrate to produce the test specimens is F = 8 mm
s .
The reason to choose a relatively low feedrate can be found in the fact, that the developed
ﬁrmware does not apply any acceleration or deceleration to the movement of the toolhead.
When the toolhead changes direction or reaches the end of a printed line, the speed of the
toolhead stays at the same velocity. Due to the mass inertia of the toolhead, this leads to
high forces in the system. The higher the feedrate, the higher is this effect during printing.
The chosen feedrate result in a very controlled movement of the toolhead and even small
changes of the printing direction could be performed without noticeable deﬂection of the
toolhead. This reduces the inﬂuence of those effects on the print quality.
5.3.2

The extrusion factor E0 is a parameter used in the 5-axis surface generator tool to
deﬁne the amount of material in mm that has to be extruded, when the nozzle moves a
distance of 1 mm. To determine this factor, G-code ﬁles from Cura were analyzed. The
support structure was printed with printing parameters used for prior prints, which gave
good deposition results. From the G-code ﬁles of those prints the extrusion factor was
derived. This was implemented into the G-code conversion tool: The extrusion per mm

6.3
Results from the control system
The developed control system showed to be a good framework to have full control
of the toolpath of the machine. Due to the custom-made ﬁrmware and the developed
MATLAB script to solve the kinematics system of the machine, the control process is very
transparent and the inﬂuence of parameters like print speed or the machine dimensions
can be evaluated quickly. This makes the developed control system a good tool for further
development and research on the platform.
The developed 5-axis parallel kinematics system worked properly and allowed to pre-
cisely position the toolhead in the build volume of the machine. The developed ﬁrmware
showed to be capable of controlling the 6 arms and the extruder of the machine. After
implementing the motion planner and the timer-dependent movement of the axes, the
movement of the toolhead along the 5-axis toolpath was very smooth. This enabled a
stable and constant deposition of material. The implementation of a checksum function
into the ﬁrmware was necessary to remove errors due to corrupted command lines. The im-
plementation of the function made the serial communication between the micro controller
and the G-code sender stable and it was possible to process large amounts of command
lines without errors.
In the current implementation of the ﬁrmware all motions of the effector arms are
performed at a constant speed. This leads to several problems: The missing acceleration
or deceleration of the movements showed to be a limitation for the print velocity due to
abrupt movements when the axes change direction at high speeds. Further, the movement
of the individual arms is controlled by the Bresenham line algorithm, which results in an
individual constant movement of the arms, when the toolhead is moved. During testing
it was detected that the toolhead performs a spherical movement when it is supposed to
move along a straight horizontal line of a length of 100 mm (see illustration Figure 6.3).

This can be explained by the constant movement of the axes: Depending on the position
of the toolhead in the cartesian space, the angle of the effector arms to the toolhead is
different. This leads to a changing transmission of the vertical movement of the sliding
carriage into the toolheads movement. To move the toolhead along a straight line the
different effector arms have to be decelerated and accelerated depending on the position
of the toolhead. In the current test series, this was solved by dividing the toolpath into
several small movements with a maximum length Lmax. A parameter of Lmax = 10 mm
was used for the 3-axis toolpath, while for the surface conformal toolpath due to the
spherical geometry of the sample Lmax = 1 mm was used. This resulted in straight
movements of the toolhead with no visible devitation. Nevertheless, a acceleration and
deceleration function should be implemented into the ﬁrmware to increase the accuracy of
the movements and to allow higher feedrates.
The ﬁrmware is capable of executing common G-code commands. The control of
toolhead by the 6 effector arms can though only be done by using the kinematics system in
MATLAB and inserting a machine speciﬁc G-code format:
G1 X Y Z U V W
where each parameter is assigned to one effector arm. The parameters refer therefore
not to the toolheads position which can lead to errors. To be able to control the toolhead,
MATLAB has to be used to generate the G-code commands. It is possible to manually enter
G-code and move for example all axes down simultaneously or move the axes pair-wise,
since the coordinates refer to the positions of the axes and not to the toolheads position.
As an example, the following command moves all axes to a height of 500 mm, but the
actual height of the toolhead above the build plane is not known:
G1 X500 Y500 Z500 U500 V500 W500
This led to several mistakes when moving the axes manually, which was done during
testing and during the calibration. Further, is it not possible to determine the toolheads
position from the 5-axis G-code ﬁle, which makes it difﬁcult to for example determine
the height of the toolhead above the build plane from reading the G-code ﬁle. For the
development of the system it was beneﬁcial to have the kinematics system in a MATLAB
script, which enables to simulate the toolheads position and evaluate the machine limits
like the maximum distance between 2 arms on one tower during the toolpath beforehand.
For a future development, the kinematics system should be integrated into the ﬁrmware of
the micro controller to be able to read G-code ﬁles that are referring to the position and tilt

6.4
Toolpath generation
The developed toolpath generation toolbox showed to be capable of generating the
toolpath for 5-axis proﬁling of a spherical surface. Further, the toolbox showed to be able
to convert 3-axis G-code ﬁles from Cura to produce support geometry for the sample. The
developed tools have shown that they are a powerful tool to validate the platform and
to investigate process parameters. The toolpath generator is developed in an universal
and modular way, so that it can easily be expanded and can be used as basis for further
development of an comprehensive multi-axis slicing tool. The further development and
integration of new scripts is easily possible.
The toolpath generation tool gives total control over the toolpath generation: For the
test series, the necessary process parameters were investigated by iteratively changing
the parameters within the layers of the printed geometry. This way the best combination
of machine parameters could be investigated. This gives also the possibility for further
research on the inﬂuence of different process parameters on the quality of the produced
sample. Further, parameters like the accuracy of the toolpath can be analysed and precisely
adjusted by e.g. the amount of coordinates that are approximated on a geometry. This is
not possible in that extent in other slicer softwares and is therefore a good tool to further
investigate and optimize the 5-axis deposition using the platform. Also the research on
new printing strategies as presented in chapter 4 aiming at for example continuous ﬁber
deposition or improved material properties through multi-axial deposition can be done with
the developed framework. The tool was developed in an universal and modular way, so that
it can be easily expanded. This facilitated e.g. the testing of different test movements of
the toolhead during development. Also in the future, the toolbox can be further developed

or parts can be reused.
The development of a slicing tool turned out to be very complex and the discussion
of different deposition strategies shows the number of different approaches that can be
undertaken to develop a 5-axis slicer. The developed tool can generate a 5-axis toolpath
based on a point cloud describing a surface which is generated by a mathematical function.
By generating the toolpath for several surfaces and adding them on top of each other, a
geometry can be produced. This approach was chosen because it enables 5-axis proﬁling of
a surface and further enables the production of simple test geometries to validate the system.
The way the surface is approximated shows thereby some limitations: The printing pattern
is established in a 2D plane and the coordinates are then projected onto the 3D surface.
This produced very good results in the conducted test series, where the curvature of the
sample was relatively small. For geometries with larger curvatures or inclined surfaces
like a surface that is almost 90◦to the build plane the developed surface approximation
algorithm would yield bad results because it does not take the curvature of the geometry
into account when establishing the printing pattern. This is illustrated in Figure 6.4 on the
example of the bell shape: The larger the angle of the geometry is in relation to the build
plane, the less coordinates are placed to describe the surface. For further development
and research with larger curvatures, a different surface approximation algorithm has to be
developed to optimize the results.
The developed tools showed that they are a good starting point to further develop the
deposition platform and further research 5-axis deposition. Due to time constraints, a
full development of an adaptive slicing tool for 5-axis toolpath generation was not yet
developed. As a future perspective, a 5-axis slicer should be developed, which is able to
intelligently slice any given CAD model according to preset parameters and deposition
strategies.
6.5
Potential to improve extrusion based AM processes
The developed platform shows potential to overcome the current limits of extrusion
based AM regarding surface accuracy of round geometries. It showed that out-of-plane
deposition improves the surfaces ﬁnish of a spherical geometry by removing the stair-step
effect compared to conventional 2.5D layer-wise deposition. The following paragraphs
discuss the potential of the machine referring to the three major limitations of extrusion
based AM that were introduced in chapter 1.
Poor surface accuracy The test samples that were produced give an indication that out-
of-plane deposition can reduce the deviation on curved surfaces that are due to the
stair-step effect as reported by different researchers [7, 6, 8]. However, the produced

presented and combining these with the 5-axis motion system, could further improve

Anisotropic material properties A big restriction to the ME process was reported in

Samples produced by material extrusionshow different material properties in layer-

Overhangs with support material Another limitation of the FDM process is the disabil-
ity to print overhangs without support structure [6]. Due to the limited tilt angle of

rotational freedom, this is deﬁnitely a relevant test. An interesting question will be,

could be realized by a long and thin extrusion nozzle. If this is done, the potential
of decreasing the material costs and building times, due to the need of less support
structure, is very promising.
6.6
Summary of ﬁndings
The developed platform has shown to be capable of realizing deposition with 5 DOF.
A control system consisting of a 5-axis kinematics system and a micro controller with a
custom-made ﬁrmware was developed. To summarize the ﬁndings, the research questions
that were introduced in the beginning are recapped.
Is it possible to develop a parallel Delta machine with 5 DOF? The platform
showed that it is possible to develop a 5-axis control system for a parallel delta kine-
matics machine and showed good potential. The accuracy of the movements seems
promising and it was possible to produce samples using 5-axis deposition.
How can a control system for a motion system with 5 DOF be realized? The
control system was realized by dividing it into the solving of the kinematics system, which
was done in a MATLAB script and the execution of the movements which was controlled
by a micro controller. This showed to be a reliable way to realize a 5 DOF motion system.
How can a 5-axis toolpath be generated? A multi-axis toolpath generation tool
was developed in form of a MATLAB script that is able to generate a 5-axis toolpath by
stacking several three dimensional surface onto each other. The surfaces were thereby
generated with a mathematical function of a sphere. The generation toolbox is developed
in a modular way and can be expanded easily.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project a 5-axis control system for a parallel Delta deposition machine was
realized and tested. Furthermore, a framework to generate the necessary 5-axis toolpath
was developed. The project shows the feasibility of a parallel Delta machine with 5 degrees
of freedom, where additionally to translation in x, y and z direction also rotation of the
toolhead around its x and y axis is possible. Through test prints the potential of the
developed machine was shown.
A 5-axis control system was realized consisting of a 5-axis parallel kinematics model
that is implemented in a MATLAB script and a micro controller with a custom-made
ﬁrmware. By generating a 5-axis toolpath and exporting it into an output ﬁle, the micro
controller is able to execute the toolpath to produce samples by material deposition. The
control system was validated by printing test samples following a 5-axis toolpath.
Furthermore, a set of tools was developed to generate the toolpath for the deposition
platform. A multi-axis toolpath generation tool was developed that is able to generate a 5-
axis toolpath by stacking several three dimensional surfaces onto each other. The toolpath
is then generated by projecting a deposition pattern onto the surfaces. For the test series,
the geometry was generated using the mathematical function of a sphere. The developed
toolbox generated a 5-axis toolpath that was tested successfully with the machine. The
toolbox is developed in a modular way and can be expanded easily.
The additional degree of freedom of the platform allows new deposition strategies,
which can for example aim at improving the mechanical strength of a geometry, improve
the surface accuracy or enable new manufacturing technologies like additive manufacturing
with continuous ﬁber composites. Further, the approach shows potential to overcome the
current limitations of extrusion based additive manufacturing regarding surface accuracy
of round geometries and anisotropic material properties. It was shown that out-of-plane
deposition improves the surfaces ﬁnish of a spherical geometry by removing the stair-step
effect compared to conventional 3-axis deposition.

CHAPTER 8
Outlook
The developed machine sets an initial point to further develop the system using the
5-axis approach. In general the system should be further developed towards a more
automated and integrated process and by improving process parameters like deposition
speed and accuracy.
A major limitation of the current platform is the limited tilt angle of the toolhead due
to the mechanical joints, that are used. Replacing these joints with ball joints with a wider
opening angle could enable toolpaths with larger deposition angles. This is necessary to
further research on the potential of 5-axis material extrusion AM. Additionally, a more
compact design for the toolhead would be beneﬁcial to realize large deposition angles.
The developed control system proofed to be reliable and capable of precisely controlling
the movements of the effector arms. A future task is the integration of the 5-axis kinematics
model into the ﬁrmware of the micro controller. This would make it possible to move from
the currently used effector arm related G-code format to G-code that describe the actual
position of the toolhead. Another major improvement is an implementation of acceleration
and deceleration functions into the ﬁrmware. By this the accuracy of the machine would
improve. Further, higher deposition speeds would be possible.
The toolpath generation framework should be developed further. A comprehensive
slicer should be developed that is capable of slicing any given CAD model into a 5-axis
toolpath taking different deposition strategies into account. The developed scripts could be
used as a basis and expanded by tools that are already in place. As a start, three dimensional
surfaces could be generated in an external software like a CAD program and then sliced
by the developed approximation algorithm. Further, modelling tools that are used for e.g.
topology optimization could be used to investigate different deposition strategies aimed at
optimizing the mechanical strength of a part.
