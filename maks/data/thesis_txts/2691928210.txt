Summary
For many engineering applications, it is important to know the geotechnical properties of the
soil in which a structure is to be built. Prudent design procedure stipulates that specimens of
the soil are extracted from the ground and laboratory tests are carried out in order to obtain
the strength and stiﬀness parameters believed to represent the in-situ conditions.
The project focuses on comparing two test methodologies: Triaxial (TxCAU) and Field Vane
tests performed on Danish clay till from two underground construction projects. The main objec-
tive is to assess the undrained shear strength which, due to both origin and testing methodologies,
may show signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the outcomes.
Glacial soils (i.e. clay tills) are widely distributed in Northern Europe and they are characterized
by showing a large variety in structure and composition and a high degree of overconsolidation.
Additionally, within this project, the diﬀerence between Upper and Lower till is assessed. Sub-
divisions by water content and consistency index are used to classify the data set.
A unitary ratio between height and diameter is employed for the Triaxial testing according to
the Danish standard since it has been proved more suitable for stiﬀsoils. However, the eﬀect of
such geometry is assessed by analyzing stress paths and critical state. Regarding the Vane Field
test, a V4 type is carried out since it is able to reach higher strengths, due to the soil properties.
The two methodologies are eventually compared aimed to ﬁnd a correlation. The diﬀerence, in
terms of undrained shear strength and classiﬁcation, is also provided by Upper and Lower till.
Also, the SHANSEP method is implemented to the data set, checking its accuracy for these
speciﬁc projects.
Overall, this study shows that the Danish clay till is characterized by a lower water content
(hence, high consistency) and low plasticity. The Upper and Lower till presents diﬀerences in
terms of classiﬁcation and undrained shear strength. However, with regards to the geometry
ratio H/D=1 is proved not to be the more suitable for studying the stress paths and critical
state, as a strain-softening behaviour is diﬃcult to detect. The two testing methodologies show
a high scatter, yet reaching high values of undrained shear strength overall. A correlation factor
between the two peak strength methodologies returned a ratio of 1.1, close to the standard
unitary practice. The application of SHANSEP method does not suit these two speciﬁc projects,
mainly due to the inaccurate estimation of in-situ and pre-consolidation pressures.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
The engineering properties of glacial soils, and in particular clay tills, have always been diﬃcult
to assess for geotechnical and underground construction projects. As described by Clarke [1],
these soils were formed during ancient times subjected to several glacial processes (erosion,
transportation, deposition, etc). Therefore they normally show a signiﬁcant variety in fabric and
structure, which evaluation results to be extremely challenging in terms of strength. For instance,
a tunneling project in the UK was meant to be carried out by hand excavation. Eventually,
as the clay till was really stiﬀin-situ, explosives were used. In addition, obtaining undisturbed
samples for glacial soils is often a challenge and therefore, the strength and stiﬀness might be
underestimated. As a consequence, foundations and structure requirements are overestimated.
An example comes from a study by Steenfelt [2]: for the design of a glass roof foundation, the
plate load testing on clay tills showed how the design assessment was conservative and how
actually the soil was much stronger. For a pile foundation project in England and described
by Clarke [1], a dredging company did not expect boulders as the borehole logs did not show
them. As a consequence, the piling contractor decided to design the piles diﬀerently as they
should have been, turning out into an economic waste. Boulders and gravels shall be expected
in glacial soils, which properties might be diﬀerent from what laboratory tests indicate. Hence,
glacial tills are diﬃcult soils when it comes to design structures. Their mixed composition leads
to countless misjudgments, especially in terms of ground investigation. A poor investigation
might end up in economical loss or worse, in fatal accidents.
1.2
Purpose of the project
The aim of the following thesis is to analyze the Danish clay tills in the Copenhagen area mainly
for the design of underground construction projects. Speciﬁcally, two diﬀerent methodologies
of testing are compared: Triaxial test and Vane ﬁeld test.
The main scope is to correlate
the undrained shear strength with the two testing methodologies. First of all, the tests are
classiﬁed accordig to their water content and consistency index. Consequently, accurate analysis
of triaxial and ﬁeld vane tests is performed separately. Finally, the two methods are compared,
also applying an additional estimation method i.e.
SHANSEP. In more details, the speciﬁc
objectives for this project are the following:
– Classify the danish clay tills by water content and consistency index with regards to
undrained shear strength.
– Interpret the Triaxial tests: stress paths, critical state and studying the eﬀect of the triaxial
sample geometry (H/D=1). Evaluate the dilatancy.
– Assess the Field Vane tests and compare it with triaxial testing. Eventually correlate the
two methodologies.

• 53 TxCAU tests
• 23 consistency limits
• 15 vane ﬁeld tests on the lower clay till
• 35 vane ﬁeld tests on the upper clay till
The METRO triaxial tests are also within an additional ﬁle called Quaternary, which is part of
another contract. In terms of data, this last ﬁle only presents the undrained shear strength cu
and failure parameters. In addition, in the triaxial tests developed by METRO, the stratigraphy
ML1 and ML2 is introduced within the geological description of each tests.
ML stands for
”Moræneler” which means clay till in danish. ML1 and ML2 are indicated as Upper and Lower
till, respectively. This subdivision is described later on chapter 3.
HOFOR performed 74 boreholes.
However, after an accurate selection of the relevant tests
aimed for the project, the following were extracted:
• 13 TxCAU tests
• 13 Atterberg limits
• 18 grain size distribution for mixed material
• Double vane tests every 1m in cohesive soils, where available
Moreover, 6 speciﬁc boreholes logs are analyzed, since samples were taken and evaluated from
these with both triaxial and classiﬁcation tests.
To distinguish the two diﬀerent data set, from now on METRO will be represented by circle
symbols whereas HOFOR by square symbols in the ﬁgures, followed by the capital letters M
and H in the legend.
1.4
Thesis structure
The thesis is constituted of 5 chapters. This ﬁrst chapter introduces the project, giving a general
overview of the following analysis. The second chapter presents the theory behind the testing
methodologies: classiﬁcation, triaxial (TxCAU), and Vane Field tests.
In addition, further
theory on critical state and undrained shear strength is given. The third chapter provides the
support for the data analysis. Previous studies on glacial tills are presented. The fourth chapter
covers the data analysis of this study. Being the core chapter, the tests on clay tills are analyzed.
Finally, the ﬁfth and last chapter sums up the main features and in addition, further studies
are proposed. Eventually, a summary of the working ﬁles is given as a guide for the digital
appendix.

Chapter 2
Testing methodologies and theory
This chapter provides the information and theory of the testing methodologies speciﬁcally for
this project, which characterize the ground investigation. The ground investigation of a test
site is an essential part of the design process. By the geotechnical characterization, parameters
needed for the engineering practice are obtained through diﬀerent methodologies. The ground
investigation is carried out using codes and standards. [1]
2.1
Laboratory tests
Laboratory tests are divided into two categories: classiﬁcation tests and mechanical tests. [1]
Classiﬁcation tests refer to describe the sample, especially from a geological point of view,
evaluating their composition, distributions of soil fractions, fabric, and structure. On the other
hand, mechanical tests aim to study the geotechnical properties (i.e. stresses) of the samples by
the use of speciﬁc machinery.
2.1.1
Atterberg limits DS/EN ISO 17892-12
The water content w in ﬁne-grained soil is extremely important, as it is directly related to the
soil strength. Therefore, it is essential to know its value and perform the so-called Atterberg
limits. There are four phases: solid, semi-solid, plastic, and liquid. The change between states
is governed by the water content inside the voids.
The limits are plastic Ip, liquid IL, and
shrinkage Is.
The liquid limit wL is deﬁned as that water content which soil goes from a plastic to a liquid
state. Instead, the plastic limit wP represents the value of water content in which soil goes from
plastic to a solid state. Eventually, the diﬀerence between plastic and the liquid limit is known
as plasticity index Ip. Hence, with higher values of Ip, the soil can hold a large quantity of water
and still behave plastically. On the other hand, with a low plasticity index, a small amount
of water would change quickly from liquid to plastic state. In the ﬁgure below, the limits are
presented, showing how the water content aﬀects the soil states, also in terms of strength and
stiﬀness.

2.2
Triaxial tests DS/EN ISO 17892-9:2018
The triaxial test is the most common methodology to assess the soil parameters of a speciﬁc
specimen. The soil sample is tested through a pressurized ﬂuid and a piston, which moves axially.
The test setup consists of the triaxial cell, in which the sample is positioned and enclosed by a
rubber membrane. In addition, equipment to record the pore pressure is connected to the cell.
The purpose of the test is to obtain the failure parameters of the soil. In Figure 2.3 the triaxial
equipment sketch given by Powrie [6] can be seen:
Figure 2.3. Triaxial test setup. [6]

The triaxial test is carried out as follows:
1. Saturation process. The specimen is considered saturated when the Skempton ratio B,
corresponding to the ratio between the pore pressure and axial compression, reaches at
least a value of 0.95. If the value is not reached, it means that air is still in the pores and
therefore, additional backpressure shall be applied.
2. Compression of the triaxial cell with the cell ﬂuid at pressure σr.
The drain can be
open and therefore will be consolidated, or contrarily not consolidated. Additionally, the
compression can be Isotropic or Anisotropic.
3. Shearing, which the failure is reached through axial compression by the piston with con-
stant axial strain or velocity, given by σa. Failure is determined when the 20% of axial
strain is obtained. Also, in this case, it can be drained or undrained.
In this project, the CAU (Consolidated Anistropically and Undrained shear phase) triaxial
test is described and corresponds to the methodology used by METRO and HOFOR. The
consolidation stage is performed with two loading phases. Speciﬁcally, the ﬁrst loading reaches
the in-situ preconsolidation stress, followed by an unloading phase to in-situ stress. Eventually,
the shear phase will start. The procedure, as described in Steenfelt and Goth [7], is done with
the purpose to simulate the stress history and reduce the sample disturbance. On the other
hand, this method could inﬂuence the natural water content of the specimen. It is noticed in
Figure 2.4, from METRO data set [8], the loading/reloading phases in the p’-q plane, recalling
the tests are anisotropically consolidated.

2.2.1
Undrained shear strength
The undrained shear strength is the relative strength of the soil when it is sheared at a constant
volume. This is in particular studied for ﬁne-grained soils, since the void space is small and
therefore the permeability is low, hindering the outﬂow and increasing the internal pore pressure.
Thus, clay till is usually tested in undrained conditions. The shear strength, developed in these
circumstances, will be then undrained.
Regarding the failure plane τ −σ, when a load is applied onto a ﬁne-grained soil, the pore
pressure increases and carries the additional load, as there is no drainage and therefore, the
eﬀective Mohr circle will have the same diameter as the total stress. For instance, the pore
pressure, of a clay specimen tested at diﬀerent conﬁning pressure, will change of the same
amount each time and there will be no change in the size of the eﬀective stress circle. As it is
depicted in Figure 2.7 below, changing the load conditions and based on diﬀerent consolidation
assumptions, the circle will only translate. The top of the circle deﬁnes the maximum shear
stress in undrained conditions.
Figure 2.7. Representation of the shear undrained phase according to the Mohr’s theory. [6]

Critical State
This section provides an overview of the critical state theory, speciﬁcally for overconsolidated
soil tested in undrained conditions. The critical state is deﬁned as that condition for which the
deviatoric stress q reaches a constant value (hence also the pore pressure), while the shear strain
keeps increasing.
Figure 2.8 summarizes the critical state theory. During consolidation, the tensional state moves
along the NCL (Normal Consolidation Line) up to the isotropic consolidation pressure pc, and
eventually, it will be unloaded to p′
0: this is the concept of overconsolidation. When the shear-
ing phase starts, axial compression is applied until reaching the critical state (A’ to B’) and
eventually failure at p′
f. With the increase of axial deformation, the deviatoric stress q grows
constantly until a maximum value. The volume of the specimen remains unchanged, as the test
is undrained and thus, no enter/exit of water from the apparatus. As it is noticed, the dilatancy
behavior can be noticed in the pore pressure diagram, when it decreases and increases afterward:
this is typical for overconsolidated soils. Therefore, the eﬀective stress paths can be plotted on
the q-p’ plane:
• The total stress path TSP (AB) follows a 3:1 gradient.
• The eﬀective stress path ESP (A’B’) is curvilinear.

Field tests are carried out directly on the construction site.

cylindrical rod, which is pushed and rotated into cohesive soils, at speciﬁc rotation. The force
applied measured, also called torque, is proportional to the soil shear strength, which failure is
assumed to happen because of the rotation. [6]

The procedure of performing a hand vane test is the following:
1. Check of no damages on the equipment.
2. Insertion of the vane into the soil and no rotation is applied until reaching a depth of 0.2m.
3. Turn of the handles as constant as possible, with a maximum rotation of 1 rpm (revolution
per minute). 10 revolutions need to be performed.
4. Measure of maximum torque, which will return the value of undrained shear strength.
5. Repeat the tests 0.2m below the ﬁrst test (double test) in order to obtained the remoulded
undrained shear strength.

Chapter 3
Theoretical background for clay tills
This chapter provides a theoretical background given by literature and bibliography, which
consists in previous studies and experiments carried out on glacial soils and clay tills.
The
purpose of this chapter is to provide support and comparisons with the analysis of the data set.
In addition, a general overview of the clay tills and their engineering properties for underground
constructions projects is given.
Glacial tills are deﬁned as a complex soil, which properties, structure, and composition are
diﬃcult to assess and their formation goes back to ancient glacial processes. The entire world
was covered for 30% of its surface by a glacier, where a third of this percentage was established
in northern Europe. It is estimated that a layer of 50m thickness, covered the area of Denmark.
The glacier, formed by the compaction of snow, deformed and eroded the soil underneath. By its
slow movement transported diﬀerent types of soil. This is actually the main reason why glacial
soils presents a large variety of soil. Therefore, they could perform in diﬀerent and unexpected
ways. [1]
3.1
Classiﬁcation of glacial soils
Glacial soils are mostly composite soils, which means they can be ﬁne-grained or coarse-grained
or both, and therefore they shall be classiﬁed according to a range of classiﬁcation tests and
the percentage of the dominant grains. Although the tests refer to the dominant fraction, the
response could be dependent on a minor fraction and therefore having diﬀerent behaviours.
Testing and obtaining a large number of sample becomes extremely relevant, in order to avoid
speciﬁc and singular eﬀects. [1]
According to ”A guide to engineering soil description” [12], tills are deﬁned by their grain size
distribution and degree of sorting criteria. As mentioned, tills are composite soils, which means
their classiﬁcation shall include all the fractions. For instance, a sample is described as ”clay
till, sandy, sl. gravelly”.
The Cagagrande’s chart is used to classify ﬁne-grained soil. In Figure 3.1, it can be seen a
classiﬁcation using this chart applied to clay tills in UK [1]. The plot is deﬁned by two lines: A-
line and T-line. Speciﬁcally, the A-line divided the clay from silts. The upper bound represents
the limit between Ip and IL. For clay tills, most of these soils are above A-line and grouped
around the T-line. In regards to the plasticity, these glacial tills show a variety of plasticity
index, from below 10% up to over 30%. In Long and Menkiti (2007) [13], the Doublin Boulder
Clay was also evaluated. As it is noticed in Figure 3.2, four diﬀerent soils in the Dublin Port
Tunnel project lie above the A-line, similarly as for the tills in UK. Moreover, the samples
present low plasticity being in a range between 10-20%, in contrast to the UK tills.

3.3
Vane Field Tests on clay till
As explained in subsection 2.3.1, in Denmark a stronger vane equipment has been introduced and
described by Okkels. The measured strength can reach up to 700 kPa. Okkels concluded that the
strength found by the soil failure usually lead to an overestimation, whereas soil disturbances
can instead underestimate it.
Furthermore, the undrained shear strength is not completely
mobilized in each point of the cylindrical rupture surface, since the shear stress distribution is
heterogeneous. Eventually, a strength proﬁle is diﬃcult to obtain as for CPT test, leading to
a scattered pattern. However, from a vane test the measured strength is at actually at in-situ
conditions. It is easy to perform compare to other in-situ tests and additionally, the sensitivity
might be assessed.
Mortensen and Hansen [19] conducted an analysis on danish clay till aimed to ﬁnd correlations
between CPT and FV testing, which vane equipment was carried out according to DGI practice.
With regards to the distribution of vane shear strength, it was found that the values are within
a range between 100 and 500 kPa, with a minimum of 44 kPa, a maximum of 686 kPa and an
average value equals to 215 kPa.
Several correlations between the vane and triaxial compression strengths have been established.
First of all, the correlation factor µ is used to correlate the vane shear strength from the triaxial
test.
Okkels determines the ratio to be equal to 1 for ﬁne-grained soil and speciﬁcally for
Denmark, as determined in [11]. The factor was established by plate load testing and triaxial
tests.
Sørensen, Kristensen and Erichsen [15] conﬁrmed the unitary value between the two
strengths for clay till.
The sensitivity is a parameter needed to assess how the remoulding aﬀects the strength of
the soils, especially in regards of ﬁeld testing and it is possibile to obtain thanks to the vane
equipment. The range are divided in low < 8, medium 8 −30 and high > 30, which the latter
is considered as quick clays according to Clarke. Equation 3.3.1 shows the relationship in terms
of undrained shear strength:
S = cFV
cRV
(3.3.1)
Where cFV is the undisturbed shear strength and corresponds to the ﬁrst strength after inserting
the vane into the soil and cRV is the remoulded shear strength. For glacial tills is usually low
due to the remoulding of the deposition of the glaciers, which means lower than 8. [1]

Dilatancy
The dilatancy is now studied within this section. The selected tests are coming from only the
METRO dataset, as the entire tests were provided by the company. In addition, the tests have
been divided by stratigraphies ML1 and ML2 (given for each test in the geological description
and corresponds to Upper and Lower till, respectively) and consistency index eventually. The
charts evaluated compare, during shearing, the stress ratio q/p′ with the change of pore pressure
normalized by the strain, since the increments are assessed and not the absolute value. If the
samples are actually dilative, they shall experience a negative pore pressure when the stress
ratio increases. The results are seen in Figures 4.27 and 4.28. For the Upper till 11 tests are
analyzed which 3 of them have Ic ≤0.75, 7 with 0.75 < Ic ≤1.00, and only one test in the
higher consistency class. For the Lower till, there are overall 10 tests evaluated, of which 6 of
them present the highest consistency and the remaining 4 with 0.75 < Ic ≤1.00.
The normalized increment in pore pressure for the Lower till reaches over than -200 kPa which
for the Upper till the value does not get to -100 kPa. Hence, the Lower till is more dilative. In
addition, the Lower till presents higher consistency since most of the tests have an Ic > 1.00.
As a result, it can be stated that the Lower till shows a higher degree of overconsolidation
compared to the Upper till, as for an increment in stress ratio, the normalized pore pressure
increases more according to the ﬁgures. This is actually reasonable since a larger OCR value
means that more dilatancy is expected. Clay tills are usually highly overconsolidated soil (caused
by the glacial processes) and especially the Lower till experienced a double deposition process,
according to Sørensen and Kristensen [15].
As a consequence, the Lower tills results to be
extremely overconsolidated and this is validated by the dilatancy analysis in this project.

eventually the second time to assess the eﬀect of remolding. The analysis is carried out for both
Upper and Lower till. For METRO data set the diﬀerentiation between Upper and Lower till
was provided being 0-7 m for Upper till and 7m down to 12 m depth for the Lower till. On the
other hand, the diﬀerence between the tills for HOFOR is assessed by the borehole logs. By the
literature, Upper till consists mainly of a layer characterized by only clay till whereas the Lower
till is a mix between clay till, sand till and meltwater sand as described by Sørensen, Kristensen
[15] for the Great Belt foundation project. Overall, for the undisturbed shear strength cfv are
available 69 data for the Upper till and 41 for the Lower till, whilst for the remolded shear
strength crv 54 in the Upper till and 21 in the Lower till.

Upper till displays lower values compared to Lower till which is also seen by the minimum and
average strength values in the table. The majority of the values for Upper till are seen between
100 and 500 kPa, whereas for the Lower till above 700 kPa. Therefore, it can be deduced that
Lower till shall return higher undrained shear strength, although the maximum undrained shear
strength reached is the same in both cases. According to Mortensen and Hansen [19], the clay

both Upper and Lower till in this project.

Chapter 5
Conclusions
In this project, a research study was carried out to gain a better geotechnical understanding
of the Danish clay tills by analyzing the provided data set from METRO and HOFOR of two
diﬀerent underground projects in the Copenhagen Metro Area.
The project speciﬁcally aimed to evaluate the undrained shear strength cu through two diﬀerent
testing methodologies: Consolidated Undrained Triaxial tests (TxCAU) and Vane Field test.
To achieve this the data set have been classiﬁed by water content w and consistency index Ic, in
correlation with undrained shear strength. The triaxial tests have been interpreted by studying
the stress paths, critical state and dilatancy, with a special attention to the sample geometry
(H/D=1) and stress history of clay tills. Thereafter, the Vane Field test have been assessed and
correlated with the Triaxial results. An evaluation between Upper and Lower till has been made,
showing the diﬀerences in terms of both classiﬁcation properties and undrained shear strength.
Finally, the SHANSEP method has been implemented, evaluating its accuracy for these speciﬁc
projects.
The thesis has been developed by starting from a theoretical perspective of the testing method-
ologies which consequently has been supported by introducing relevant literature of previous
studies on glacial tills. The analysis of the data is than carried out, describing the outcomes
supported by the bibliography.
In the following list, the main results and outcomes are summed up and discussed:
• The classiﬁcation tests are found between 1m and 22m below the ground. METRO data
set are more superﬁcial compared to HOFOR in terms of depth.
• The classiﬁcation tests have shown how the Danish clay till has a low water content, with
most of the data have a value below 10%.
On the other hand, a lower water content
means a high consistency. Indeed, most of the tests have an Ic greater than 0.75, which
is considered as ﬁrm/really ﬁrm value. These two characteristics show how these clay
tills have high strength, as the water content and thus consistency index, are directly
proportional to the undrained shear strength cu. However, METRO presented a lower
consistency compared to HOFOR.
• The triaxial tests are seen to have average plasticity of around 10%, which is considered
as low plasticity in Figure 4.6 according to Casagrande’s chart.
• The stress path envelopes are seen being overconsolidated since they have clearly shown
dilatancy. They have been studied by water content and consistency index subdivisions,
showing that lower water content (hence, higher consistency) reaches higher stresses. How-
ever, due to the specimen geometry, the strain-softening behaviour is not always seen, but
contrarily a strain-hardening condition.

CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSIONS
• In regards to the failure parameters, they have been again assessed by water content and
consistency index.
By water content the parameters showed deviations, speciﬁcally in
terms of friction angle ϕ, whereas for the Ic classes the parameters are more alike.
• The Critical State have been assessed. Firstly, the CSL gradient M has been found in
the stress plane by w and Ic classes. Both subdivisions returned diﬀerences in the M-
evaluation. However, due to the specimen geometry, the evaluation of M resulted to be
not precise. Afterwards, the Critical State Lines are found by Upper and Lower till followed
by Ic classes. By stratigraphy, there were substantial diﬀerences, whereas for consistency it
has been established to be more reliable, in regards to the critical parameters. Eventually,
the normalization is carried out for the three Ic classes, introducing Upper and Lower till.
Classes 0.75 < Ic ≤1.00 and Ic > 1.00 are more similar compared to the lowest class,
since for the last one there is lack of data.
• The dilatancy has been evaluated by Upper and Lower till and consistency as well. The
Lower till has shown to be higher overconsolidated compared to the Upepr till, as the pore
pressure reaches larger negative pore pressure during shearing. In addition, the Lower till
has a higher consistency index.
• The undrained shear strength from TxCAU varies within a range 75kpa to 1153 kPa.
Despite the scatter, the strength is seen to increase with depth. A decrease of w yields to
an increase in strength. Additionally, lower water content is found deeper.
• The cohesion c′ is close to what found by literature, which shall stay in a range within 0-50
kPa. In regards to the relationship c′ −cu has been actually found equal to 0.12, larger
respect to the normal standard practice i.e. equal to 0.1.
• The overconsolidation has been proved by the dilatancy analysis. However, the OCR was
simply estimated and no accurated analysis was carried out for these project. Hence, there
is a lack of reliability for the exact value of OCR.
• The Vane Field Test has been evaluated for both data set, recalling it was a type V4. The
values have been divided by Upper and Lower till. HOFOR provided the entire borehole
logs and therefore the diﬀerence between the two layers was established by looking at the
geological description. On the other hand, METRO already provided the test by Upper
and Lower till. No continuous proﬁle can be obtained, returning a highly scattered pattern,
especially for undisturbed shear strength. The latter has been found within 100-500 kPa
for the Upper till, whereas for Lower till most of them are above 700 kPa. for In terms
of remolded, the increase of cu is more visible with depth. Additionally, for METRO the
Upper till is more superﬁcial compared to HOFOR. The sensitivity between the two vane
strengths is however coherent with literature, showing low sensitivity (< 8).
• The two diﬀerent methodologies of cu have been merged, by comparing triaxial with vane
tests, considering Upper and Lower till. In the Upper till, the general trend has been
seen to show an increase of strength with depth, except for the remolded shear strength
which has shown a slight decrease. Regarding the Lower till, strengths are overall higher
compared to Upper till.
• The diﬀerentiation between Upper and Lower till did show diﬀerence. The Lower till has
presented higher degrees of overconsolidation (hence, higher failure stress and dilatancy)
for the triaxial testing, whereas higher strength has been proved also by the Vane test in
comparison with the Upper till.
• The factor µ, which correlates cu,TX and cu,FV , returned a value of 1.1 which is a 10%
higher to the unitary ratio, normally used in Denmark. When comparing cu,TX and cu,RV

the correlation rises to 2.5, showing how the remolded strength is much lower compared to
triaxial testing due again to the sample geometry. Hence, such a correlation is not suitable
for remolded strength.
• Special considerations need to be given when applying the SHANSEP method. As its
formulation (Equation 3.2.4) takes into account in-situ and OCR, for the given dataset
these values were simply estimated. Indeed the method has shown deviations when using
the parameters found by literature. Thus, such a method shall not be applied without per-
forming an accurate analysis of stresses (e.g. oedometer tests) For this reason, evaluating
the SHANSEP parameters (Ss and m) led to signiﬁcant diﬀerences.
Danish clay tills have demonstrated to have a low water content and high consistency with a
low plasticity, characterized by a signiﬁcant overconsolidation overall. A diﬀerentiation between
Upper and Lower till has shown that these two layers do have variations in terms of classiﬁcations
and undrained shear strength. The sample geometry (H/D=1) however, has shown not to be the
most reliable when it comes to stress paths and critical state since the strain-softening condition
is not always complied. The analysis of the testing methodologies has shown large values of
undrained shear strength, despite the high scatter. Nonetheless, a correlation factor between
the two peak strengths has returned a ratio of 1.1, which is close to the normal standard practice.
On the other hand, such correlation has not been validated for residual strengths, especially for
triaxial tests due to the sample geometry. Finally, estimation methods, such as SHANSEP, shall
not be used without an accurate analysis of the method parameters.
5.1
Future research
Hence, few recommendations to improve the quality of this project:
• More experiments and classiﬁcation tests to evaluate the soil properties (grain size distribu-
tion, Atterberg limits, etc) in relation to undrained shear strength, especially considering
Upper and Lower till.
• As the vane test showed a highly scattered pattern, it is advised to compare the results
with other in-situ tests i.e. CPT tests, in order to have a continuous proﬁle as well, since
with FVT is diﬃcult to obtain.
• Perform triaxial test with a standard geometry ratio (e.g. H/D=2) to improve the analysis
of stress paths and critical state.
• Carry out an accurate assessment to obtain in-situ and preconsolidation pressures, in order
to estimate the exact value of OCR. Thus, including within this analysis oedometer tests.
In addition, as well as in-situ and pre-consolidation pressure, the SHANSEP parameters
Ss and m need to be carefully assessed, in order to improve the accuracy of this estimation
method.
