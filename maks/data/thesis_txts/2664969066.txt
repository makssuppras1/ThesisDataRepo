Abstract
This thesis presents a method for creating robust schedules for bed cleaning personnel at
hospitals, such that the highly ﬂuctuating demand for hospital beds can reliably be met.
To that eﬀect, the scheduling problem for bed cleaning at hospitals is formally deﬁned,
and a Multiple Integer Programming solution is proposed. A case study is performed in
collaboration with the Danish hospital Rigshospitalet in which this solution framework
is evaluated on a series of real data instances.
Personnel scheduling in healthcare has been studied extensively, but most work is focused
on the health professionals such as physicians and nurses, and not supporting cast like
cleaning personnel. The varying rules across diﬀerent labor markets and the fact that
hospital employees often work under unique conditions make it pertinent to have a wide
academic work and develop domain-speciﬁc solutions. In this project, the regulations
and priorities of the public Danish labor market and the healthcare sector are used to
model the closed system of a hospital cleaning facility.
Results from the case study indicate that the proposed solution can solve real-life in-
stances to optimality, creating personnel schedules that proved robust to large ﬂuctua-
tions in demand. The eﬀect of allowing ﬂexible shifts and part-time workers were studied,
and it was seen that even small changes to the current scheme could improve reliability
substantially while reducing labor cost by at least 15%.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Hospitals are an integral part of the modern world. A well-functioning hospital enables
society to take care of those in need, and a high level of service is increasingly both
expected and demanded. Hospitals are complex systems, consisting of a ﬁnite set of re-
sources that must be used to meet unpredictable demand, a demand that in Denmark is
growing due to an aging population. The advent of COVID-19 has put a singular pressure
on the resources of hospitals worldwide and has made eﬃcient planning and management
ever more critical.
Beds are a crucial resource for a hospital and are typically regarded as the key indica-
tor for hospital capacity [1]. A stockout of beds is associated with severe consequences,
ranging from reduced quality of care to delays and ultimately cancellations of medical
procedures. As such, managing the availability of beds is a key part of an eﬃciently run
hospital The main challenge in bed management lies in handling a variable and largely
unpredictable demand using a ﬁxed resource.
Demand is driven by patient arrivals,
which can be separated into scheduled and acute hospitalizations, arising from elective
and emergency procedures. Further, the period in which a bed is allocated to patients is
quite diverse, depending on individual patient characteristics.
Bed management consists of both patient use and necessary back-end processes such as
cleaning, storage, assignment, and transportation. Managing bed cleaning is an impor-
tant part of meeting the ﬂuctuating demand, and strict measures are in place to ensure
a sterile environment, only exacerbated by the COVID-19 protocols. Bed cleaning in
hospitals consists of two main parts, the cleaning unit, and the employees managing the
cleaning. Due to the unpredictable demand, it is challenging to determine an optimal
size and schedule for the cleaning personnel, potentially leading to under- or over-staﬃng.
Understaﬃng decreases the quality of care, and overstaﬃng drives cost unnecessarily.
The goal of this project is to propose a method that creates personnel schedules for the
bed cleaning crew at hospitals such that the demand for clean beds is always met while
minimizing the cost of labor. Working in collaboration with Rigshospitalet, who currently
experience frequent stock-outs of clean beds, a case study is performed, evaluating the
eﬀect of the proposed solution framework.

Chapter 2
Background
This study has been conducted in collaboration with Rigshospitalet, a public hospital in
Denmark, who provided the data and helped guide the project. The problem and pro-
posed solution from this thesis has been developed speciﬁcally to ﬁt the characteristics
of the hospital and by extension the rules from the Danish labor market.
Rigshospitalet is currently looking into improving their general performance by exploiting
the increasing levels of data available to apply new data-driven decision support tools.
As part of this process, a research project on supply chain analytics in healthcare is being
undertaken between DTU and Rigshospitalet. It is the initial results from that project
that has led to this thesis. Speciﬁcally, an opportunity for improvement within bed lo-
gistics have been identiﬁed.
It is essential for a hospital to have reliable management of beds. Bed management con-
trols patient ﬂow in a hospital, and so by extension maintains the quality of the health
service. Factors such as uncertain patient arrivals and their length of stay make this
quite a complex problem, as does coordination between the multiple units that will be
part of any hospital, such as emergency or operating rooms, as well as the downstream
units aﬀected by these, for instance, intensive care units or recovery rooms. In addi-
tion, bed management requires consideration for the entire bed cycle, which apart from
patient use includes processes like assignment, maintenance, and cleaning. With all of
these processes in play, several methods from the realm of management science are appro-
priate when modeling bed management, such as simulation, forecasting, and operations
research. Simulation is the best tool when modeling the entire bed management system,
forecasting could be used to smooth the demand, and operations research is often used
to handle sub-tasks like bed assignment or scheduling [9].
In the research project between DTU and Rigshospitalet, such a simulation model has
been employed, and using a holistic view of the hospital bed ﬂow, several issues regarding
demand satisfaction have been identiﬁed. In this project, the focus is solely on the clean-
ing phase of the bed ﬂow cycle, and how the process currently employed at Rigshospitalet
can be improved using operations research, to better the entire bed management system.

2.1
The bed cleaning process at Rigshospitalet
At present, Rigshospitalet maintains a rather large bed ﬂeet of around 550, separated
into four types.
• Standard beds
• Small beds meant for children
• Special size beds designed to ﬁt in smaller rooms
• Long term beds with air mattresses to avoid bed sores
These beds can either be assigned to a patient, be in storage, be cleaned, or be transported
between one of the previous three. The closed system of hospital beds and their cleaning
process is illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Hospital bed cleaning system
The demand for beds is driven by the patient arrival and are expressed by all of the
hospital departments, who can utilize an internal IT system to either order a clean bed,
or request that a used one be picked up. Transportation for both of these actions is
undertaken by speciﬁc employees, who will not be a part of this study. There are two

storage areas for the beds, one for used and one for clean beds. Both of these are located
in the basement next to the cleaning facility. In the cleaning facility, resources consist of
workers and cleaning machines. Two machines can handle the bed frames and a single
machine the linens. The cleaning of the linens can be done in such bulk that the avail-
ability is never an issue. Workers operate the machines and ensure that the beds are
cleaned, disinfected, and made in an appropriate manner.
There are three procedures under which a bed can be cleaned. The normal procedure
requires that each bed frame is cleaned in the machine for 7 minutes. Some beds that
have been exposed to infections in a variety of manners have to undergo a deep cleaning
procedure, which takes 15 minutes in the machine per bed. Finally, the cleaning crew
can elect to do a hotel cleaning of the beds instead - though not applicable for infectious
beds, which have to undergo a deep cleaning process no matter what - where the beds are
manually wiped down instead of using a machine program. This process saves time, but
is thought to be inferior to the normal procedure, and so is only used when the system
is under pressure. Reducing the need for hotel cleanings is a priority for the hospital.
Under the current system, 6 workers are employed at the cleaning facility, who follow
a standard schedule, with shifts solely during the day, and only on weekdays. While
demand for clean beds does peak during these hours, it is still present during nights and
weekends. In order to handle this, safety stocks are created, which speciﬁcally means that
a large number of beds must be cleaned Friday to handle the demand during weekends.
This also implies an increased need for storage, which ties up resources for the hospital.
Further, even with these large safety stocks, there have been situations in which the
demand on Mondays could not be met, something that is regarded as truly critical at
the hospital. It seems likely that a change in the workforce schedule could alleviate this
problem while at the same time potentially freeing up resources to be used at another
important area of the hospital.
2.2
Collective agreement
The rules governing any public workforce in Denmark can be found in the collective agree-
ment. When it comes to public hospitals, these rules are set out between the unions and
the Danish Regions. The speciﬁc rules for the cleaning crew at Rigshospitalet have been
published by FOA, one of the largest danish unions [7]. Under this collective agreement
are listed all things relevant for the personnel when a schedule change must be discussed,
such as wages, time oﬀ, and working conditions. Its purpose - aside from protecting the
rights of the employees - is to set out a framework under which all agreements between
employer and employee must function.
By nature, these rules are not set in stone, as a key principle behind this system is the
ability to renegotiate. Further, the system oﬀers a large degree of ﬂexibility on the side
of the employees. Indeed, the Danish labor market model is frequently labeled a ”ﬂexicu-
rity” system, meaning that a highly ﬂexible labor market is combined with the security
of public social welfare for employees. Under this general system, it is relatively easy
5

- as compared to other nations - for companies to change the size of the workforce, as
society as a whole will assume responsibility for those left unemployed by this scheme
[8]. The implications with regards to the cleaning workforce at Rigshospitalet is that
while certain rules must be respected, there is a large degree of freedom when it comes to
creating the personnel schedules and determining the optimal number of employees. Ad-
ditionally, there is room for renegotiating if a particularly promising aspect is restricted
by the collective agreement.
The agreement sets out multiple ways in which a personnel schedule can diﬀer from the
currently used standard. The legal time-windows for shifts are for instance allowed a
great deal of ﬂexibility. The employees must agree to the shifts themselves, a certain
amount of time oﬀmust be scheduled between each shift, and working odd hours such
as nights and weekends entitle the employees to additional wage, see Table A.1 for details.
Generally speaking, workers can be employed under two types of contract, full-time and
part-time, where the majority of regulations such as working conditions, hourly wages,
and holiday rules are the same. A full-time worker must have a total weekly workload
of 37 hours, with a daily shift length between 5 and 12 hours. Part-time workers can be
under contract for anything between 16 and 37 hours a week, and as long as the total
amount is less than 30 hours there are no rules for minimum shift length. Part-time
workers allow schedulers a great deal of ﬂexibility, and while under such a contract the
danish unions provide employees with a supplemental beneﬁt, which limits the gap in
eﬀective wage between full-time and part-time workers [7].

Chapter 3
Problem Description
The purpose of this thesis is to use operations research to create feasible work shift
schedules for the bed cleaning crew in hospitals to help ensure a robust bed management
system throughout the entire hospital. Rigshospitalet provides the case study around
which this thesis evolves, and the situation at this hospital will be used as a framework
for the mathematical model. The primary interests of the stakeholders are to make the
supply of clean beds more robust and possibly generate savings, whether in the form of
labor or inventory costs.
Creating optimal personnel schedules is a complex problem even for small systems, where
a huge number of variables goes into the decision making. Decision-makers can utilize
domain expertise to ﬁnd good solutions but taking all possible permutations into account
is simply not realistic for a manual approach. Using a mathematical modeling framework,
it is possible to evaluate the entire problem domain and ﬁnd the best solution. With this
methodology, not only can the problem be solved to optimality, but it can be modiﬁed
to reﬂect things such as process changes e.g., stricter measures for bed cleaning, new
decisions by the stakeholders, or even be generalized to apply to a generic hospital. The
applications are many, and for these reasons, a mathematical approach is adopted. It is,
however, important to note that expertise within the speciﬁc domain is an integral part
of this problem - a solution, however mathematically pleasing, is no good if it does not
stand a chance of ever being implemented in practice. As such, the process of creating
these personnel schedules is meant to be an iterative process using stakeholder feedback
as input. The ﬁrst step in this is to create a basic model for this problem.
3.1
Deﬁning the scheduling problem for bed cleaning
at hospitals
In this section, the scheduling problem for bed cleaning at hospitals will be formally de-
ﬁned. The goal is to model the processes at the cleaning unit as precisely as possible, so
each feasible solution by the model represents a realistic alternative for the stakeholders
at the hospital. At the same time, the mathematical complexity must be kept to a min-
imum for these solutions to be obtainable within a reasonable amount of time.
7
==PAGE:13==

The system in question is restricted solely to the cleaning facility at the hospital. Input to
the system consists of the hourly demand for clean beds, while the output is a complete
and repeatable personnel schedule containing information about the number of workers
and when their shifts start and end. It is not the aim of the model to track each bed
individually - doing so would dramatically increase complexity and would likely make an
exact method unviable - and so, the supply of used beds is treated as inﬁnite. This is
a realistic assumption, as data from the hospital indicates that the bottlenecks in the
system stem from worker and machine availability.
The resources are the cleaning machines and the workers. Each machine can be assigned
to one of three processes, a normal, deep, or hotel cleaning procedure, which cleans
a single bed. A hotel bed cleaning does not technically involve the machine, but the
cleaning facility is constructed in such a way that the only physical route from the pre-
cleaning area to the post-cleaning area goes through one of the machines. Workers can
be assigned to one of these two areas, pre- and post-cleaning. Here, they will undertake
several speciﬁc tasks:
• Transporting used beds from the used storage area to the pre-cleaning area
• Separating sheets from bed frames and put the sheets into the machine dedicated
to washing these
• Wipe down the bed
• Put the bed into an empty machine
• Take the cleaned bed out of the machine
• Make the bed and put plastic around it
• Transport it to the clean storage area
For the sake of simplicity, these tasks are reduced to one for each of the areas. The pre-
cleaning task consist of everything from transporting the used bed to putting it into the
machine, and the post-cleaning tasks the remainder. The disadvantage of this approach
is that when each task is not explicitly handled, precision is lost. In addition to the pre-
and post-cleaning areas, the system also contains the storage area for clean beds, which
has a capacity of 164. The time horizon considered is a single week, but the problem can
easily be adjusted to contain e.g., a month or a year if the available data mandates such
an approach. In creating schedules for a single week, it is assumed that demand follows a
weekly pattern, and so the solution must be cyclical. One implication of this is that the
number of clean beds available at the start of the cycle must equal the amount available
at the end.
All schedules created must adhere to the rules of the collective agreement and further be
suﬃciently attractive for personnel to accept work under such conditions. Additionally,
the schedule must allow for enough workers such that the demand for clean beds can
always be met. As mentioned in chapter 2 workers can be either under a full-time or

part-time contract.
Finally, the objective is to minimize cost. In terms of the cleaning facility, the cost is
made up of two main components, labor cost, and inventory cost. Labor cost is the
wage of the workers, which is speciﬁed directly in the collective agreement. Inventory
cost consists of the costs associated with maintaining and storing the bed ﬂeet. Since
this cost is irrevocably tied to the entire bed management problem of the hospital, it
will not be included in the closed system for the scheduling problem for bed cleaning at
hospitals. It is a high priority for the hospital to reduce the need for hotel cleanings -
the mandate is that it can only be used if the alternative is not meeting demand. To
incentivize this behavior, a suﬃciently large penalty is added to the cost for each hotel
cleaning performed.
3.1.1
Performance measures
A well-performing bed cleaning facility in hospitals is above all else one that can always
provide a clean bed upon demand. It is a deﬁning characteristic of the problem that de-
mand for clean beds is met, but any solution will only guarantee this for the given input
in terms of data and parameters. As such, the robustness of the given solution is the
main performance indicator, robustness in the face of demand modulation and uncertain
system parameters. As an actual metric, a way of measuring the robustness of a solution
could be the increase in demand that the system can handle, which can be deﬁned as
the diﬀerence between the number of clean beds in stock and the demand for these. This
surplus could either be the minimum across the time horizon i.e., a hard number of beds
that the demand can be increased by across the board or as a percentage of the total
demand, thereby providing a more general measure.
Aside from robustness, the number of hotel cleanings needed is quite important for the
hospital. This represents a less than optimal cleaning scheme, is only allowed if doing
otherwise would fail to meet demand and should be minimized.
Finally, the cost of maintaining the cleaning facility is an important performance measure
and is for the bed cleaning problem eﬀectively equal to the number of weekly man-hours.
This cost should be minimized, but a reduction in cost does not serve as justiﬁcation for
lowering the level of robustness or increasing the hotel cleanings.
Aside from the hospital, another important stakeholder in this problem is the employees at
the cleaning facility, whose work and every-day life these schedules aﬀect. Multiple studies
have shown that the well-being of employees at work has a high impact on productivity,
and impacts things such as health, absence, and retention [10]. Additionally, changes
in schedules will have to be cleared by the employees, so there is a clear incentive for
the solutions to take employee well-being into account. Many traditional measures, such
as working conditions, wage, and the number of hours are regulated by the collective
agreement and will either be ignored or enforced as hard constraints in the problem.
A simple measure might then be the number of hours scheduled outside of a standard

3.1.2
Data description
The data used in this thesis, provided by Rigshospitalet, consist of the hourly demand for
clean beds during a week at the hospital, as seen in Figure 3.1. Raw data concerning bed
movement and patient arrivals has been processed as part of the ongoing research project
on bed management between DTU and Rigshospitalet and given as a Poisson Process.
With the use of simulation, 381 instances of this Poisson Process has been generated,
which will be used to evaluate model performance in terms of demand modulation.
Figure 3.1: Weekly and hourly demand for clean beds based on patient arrival Poisson
coeﬃcients
From Figure 3.1 it is clear that the demand ﬂuctuates with a heavy peak during the morn-
ings. This makes intuitive sense, as upon a patient’s arrival, a bed must be assigned, and
this bed will follow the patient throughout the day. It does however mean that a solution
to the bed cleaning problem must be able to handle strong peaks in demand, either via
heavy throughput or safety stock. On a weekly basis, the demand is heavy on Mondays
and low on weekends, which suggests either that scheduled hospitalizations happen less
frequently at weekends, or that patients prefer to contact a general practitioner before
going to the hospitals. Either way, the peak demand occurring Monday mornings is one
of the challenges that a good solution to the scheduling problem for bed cleaning at
hospitals must satisfy.

Chapter 4
Literature Review
Personnel scheduling has occupied managers and scientists for a number of years. Labor
cost is the major direct cost component for many companies and optimizing these by even
a few percent would yield signiﬁcant results. The typical goal of personnel scheduling,
or rostering, is to optimize the timetable of a company by determining appropriate time
and shifts that employees should work, such that demand for goods or services can be
met. Personnel scheduling can be stated as an assignment problem, where the employees
are assigned to the shifts required to cover demand. This assignment is subject to restric-
tions, which is divided into hard and soft constraints, where the quality of the timetable
is typically deﬁned by the level of satisfaction with the soft constraints. Several objectives
can be considered, such as minimizing the overall cost of labor, maximizing productivity,
or minimizing the number of employees. A detailed review of personnel scheduling can
be found in Ernst et al (2004) [6] and Van den Bergh et al. (2013) [11].
Many personnel scheduling problems makes a distinction between workforce planning and
timetabling. The former deals with strategic decisions such as the number of employees
and their composition, while the latter is an operational problem that assumes a staﬃng
level is given [6]. In the present thesis, this distinction is somewhat blurred since the
main priority is to ascertain robustness on an operational level and an aspect of this is
to determine the necessary size of the workforce.
One of the deﬁning characteristics of personnel problems is coverage of the workload. In
almost 75% of the papers evaluated in Van den Bergh et al. (2013), a hard coverage
constraint is enforced. Since deciding on the number of employees needed is an impor-
tant aspect of personnel scheduling problems, the use of both hard and soft coverage
constraints in the same model is often seen. The hard constraint ensures that demand
can be met, and the soft constraint adds an incentive to reduce the number of employees.
In industries where the employees can be regarded as scarce resources, it is normal proce-
dure to operate only with soft constraints. A good example of this is a typical scheduling
problem at hospitals. Physicians represent some of the most valuable and expensive re-
sources in hospitals, and their scarcity is such that they constitute a bottleneck in the
care providing service [5]. In these types of problems, the objective will often be to min-
imize the number of uncovered shifts instead. While the bed cleaning problem certainly
is a part of the workﬂow of the hospital, the employees cannot be considered scarce - the

amount of training is not substantial, and the number of employees needed is naturally
limited by the availability of the cleaning machines - so enforcing a hard constraint is

Many personnel scheduling problems takes the skills of the employees into account. This
is for instance often seen in nurse scheduling problems, where experience or training de-
termines whether a nurse can cover the needs on a speciﬁc shift [11]. Skills are omitted
from the bed cleaning problem, and it is assumed that employees at the cleaning facility
are uniform in their skills and eﬃciency.
In Brunner (2010) [3] it is observed that a large number of models in personnel schedul-
ing are based on explicit representations of shifts. Pre-deﬁned shifts become input to
the problem and are used in the construction of constraints. These shifts will typically
consist of three 8-hour shifts and in certain cases two 12-hour shifts. This approach is
often the best in terms of complexity, but it has been shown that allowing a more ﬂexible
scheduling scheme can have beneﬁts both in terms of cost and robustness. In chapter
5 both of these concepts will be explored. Predeﬁned shifts are a part of the current
situation at the cleaning facility, while the exploration of a ﬂexible approach is part of
the motivation for this thesis.
4.1
Personnel scheduling in healthcare
The origin of personnel scheduling can be traced back to Edie (1954) [4], working on traf-
ﬁc delays at toll booths. Since that point, personnel scheduling has been applied in many
ﬁelds, with the most popular being transportation systems such as airlines, emergency
services, call centers, service organizations, and healthcare [6].
It is especially important to create good timetables in a reliable fashion in healthcare,
where failure to do so will mean that patient needs are not being met. Yet personnel
scheduling within hospitals are notoriously challenging, as demand varies widely on dif-
ferent days and shifts, and most healthcare institutions must be operational around the
clock [12]. The nurse rostering problem is by far the most researched within this area
owing to its wide applicability - a good schedule for nurses is needed on a worldwide
scale. General physician scheduling is less popular though extensive research has been
conducted in this area as well - the interested reader is referred to Erhard et al. (2018) for
a review on this domain. When it comes to nurse rostering and physician scheduling most
research focuses on heuristic approaches, as both are considered diﬃcult combinatorial
problems, where employees are treated as scarce resources with varying degrees of skill
that must fulﬁll a highly volatile demand on multiple shifts during a single day. As such,
it bears little resemblance to the bed cleaning problem.
While the literature is extensive on personnel scheduling within healthcare, it is singularly
focused on the caretakers. Ernst et al (2004) point out that the unique characteristics

4.2
A general model for personnel scheduling
In Brucker et al.
(2011) [2] a series of general models and their time complexity is
reviewed. Here, personnel scheduling problems are classiﬁed into four categories, per-
manence, ﬂuctuation, mobility and project centered planning, and a general model for
permanence and ﬂuctuation is given. This framework is meant to be able to model a
wide array of personnel problems with some domain-speciﬁc adjustments.
The general problem is deﬁned as follows. There is a planning horizon T, divided into
periods t = 0, ...T. Within the planning horizon, m tasks j = 1, ...., m must be be per-
formed. Dj(t) is the number of employees needed to perform task j in the time period and
is called the demand proﬁle for task j. The n number of employees e = 1, ...n each have a
subset of tasks Qe for which they are qualiﬁed. A working pattern for an employee is then
deﬁned by a binary vector we(t) where we(t) = 1 if and only if e is available in period
t, and an assignment of a task belonging to Qe for each time period where we(t) = 1.
Depending on the speciﬁc problem, not all working patterns will be feasible for each em-
ployee. The set of feasible working patterns for e is denoted by Pe. The objective of the
general problem is to assign feasible working patterns to a set of employees such that the
demand Dj(t) of all tasks are covered in each period, and the relevant assignment costs
are minimized. Additionally, as mentioned above, soft constraints can be imposed on the
working pattern.
Brucker et al. (2011) ﬁnd that this general problem can be solved in polynomial time
for certain special cases. However, many real-world applications of this general case have
proven to be NP-hard, including nurse-rostering. Polynomially solvable cases include sit-
uations in which task changes are allowed, cases where there is only a single task and the
time-periods in which employees are available are given by one interval, or cases where
employees are available in all given periods.
In conclusion, it is shown that personnel scheduling problems can be formulated as in-
teger linear programs and for relatively small instances of the general model be solved
with the use of LP-optimizers. However, most problems within personnel scheduling will

quickly reach a scope where heuristics is a more appropriate method.
The speciﬁcs of the scheduling problem for bed cleaning in hospitals can be ﬁtted within
this general framework with a series of domain-speciﬁc alterations. One of the diﬀerences
can be found in the way demand is modeled. Instead of having a direct measure for
demand, as is the case in the general problem, demand is dependent on the up-time
of the cleaning machines. Assigning employees to machines in order to process a task is
remarkably like that of a machine scheduling problem, and so relevant literature is scanned
for the meeting of personnel allocation and machine scheduling problems. According to
Van den Bergh et al. (2013), the literature on problems in which personnel scheduling
is integrated with other scheduling problems such as machine scheduling is quite sparse.
The closest thing was found in Santos et al. (2020), where the machine scheduling and
personnel allocation problems are solved simultaneously in a case study for large-scale
facilities, integrating a variant of the job shop problem with personnel allocation decisions.
This approach is relevant when the skills of the employee determine the length of the job,
or there are fewer workers than jobs. Results are promising, but the inherent focus on job
scheduling as it pertains to the machines does not ﬁt the bed cleaning problem, where the
focus is not so much on eﬃciency as maintaining a suﬃciently high throughput. For this
approach to work, each individual job in the cleaning facility would have to be modeled,
which would increase complexity substantially.

Chapter 5
Mathematical model
This chapter proposes a MIP-formulation for the scheduling problem for bed cleaning in
hospitals, structured to allow for ﬂexibility in scheduling choices. First, a description of,
and an explanation for, the choices and assumptions are provided. Second, is a math-
ematical notation section followed by the model itself, and ﬁnally a way in which the
model can be modiﬁed to ﬁt individual needs is described.
5.1
Model description
The proposed formulation follows the general model described in Section 4.2 with several
adjustments to take domain speciﬁcations and problem priorities into account. A time
horizon of a single week is adopted, primarily since this ﬁts the data format. This means
that the schedule must be cyclic i.e., repeatable. A discrete time representation is used,
with time points on an hourly basis for each day of the week, again to accommodate the
data. Within the planning horizon, a certain number of clean beds is needed at any one
time and must be available in the clean storage area.
Every task in the cleaning facility is not a part of the model. Instead, an estimate is
made on the completion time for each task, and for the pre- and post-cleaning areas the
time a worker must spend on a single bed is calculated. The presence of a worker in the
schedule equals a certain number of available minutes per hour - depending on breaks
and eﬃciency - and so the number of beds that can be cleaned is bounded by the number
of workers at the facility. This means that the workers are not explicitly assigned a task,
and their working pattern consists solely of the weekly schedule. The same approach is
used with regards to the cleaning machines, where instead of explicitly assigning workers
or beds to the machines, each machine in the facility bounds the number of beds that can
be cleaned. This number varies depending on the cleaning procedure, which must be cho-
sen by the model for each bed that is cleaned. With this approach, complexity is reduced
substantially. As was seen in the literature review from chapter 4, a personnel schedule
is much more likely to be polynomially solvable if the number of tasks is restricted, and
here they are removed altogether. The advantage in complexity is clear, but as is often
the case, it comes at the price of precision and therefore quality of the solution. The
15

purpose of this thesis, however, is not to model each process in the cleaning facility in
detail, but rather to investigate how scheduling changes will aﬀect the entire system. In
this, the chosen approach should work, though it may require an iterative process with
the hospital stakeholders to get the tuning right.
To facilitate the generation of ﬂexible schedules, shifts are not explicitly represented in
the model. Many personnel scheduling problems will deﬁne a number of shifts, and let
the model decide a schedule that covers these. In the present approach, rules regarding
shifts are deﬁned in the constraints, and the model has the freedom to choose anything
within this framework. This eﬀectively means that a set of constraints determine which
hours an employee can be at work, and another set of constraints ensure that all individ-
ual schedules follow the rules as set out in the collective agreement.
The objective of the model is to assign feasible schedules for the personnel such that
the number of beds cleaned is enough to satisfy the demand, and the cost of workers is
minimized. The cost is closely related to the number of employees, which is variable in
this implementation. An upper bound on the number must be set, and the model can
then evaluate how many of these are needed. There is no direct cost per worker in the
model, but since each employee must work a certain amount of hours per week, there is
still an incentive to keep the number low.
It is assumed that there is always a used bed available to be cleaned, an assumption
supported by the data, where the bottlenecks are to be found in the machine availability.
It is further assumed that the weekly demand for clean beds is known. As explained in
chapter 2, hospital arrivals are notoriously hard to predict, so the ramiﬁcations of this
assumption must be kept in mind when evaluating the model. All beds are treated as
homogeneous, so to take deep cleanings into account a minimum term is enforced.

In the objective function (5.1) the total cost of the system is minimized. This cost con-

the cost of the particular hour, pd,t and includes a penalty term for the total number of
hotel cleaning procedures, y2,d,t.
The constraint in (5.2) ensures that the number of clean beds available does not exceed
the capacity at the stash area. The demand for clean beds must always be met, and this
is enforced in constraint (5.3), where the buﬀer term B is the minimum stock allowed
at any point in the time-horizon. The constraints in (5.4) and (5.5) regulate the ﬂow of
beds in stock across time for respectively days and hours, while (5.6) and (5.7) together
deﬁne the possible timeslots in which a shift can occur.

must be scheduled every week, which is enforced from (5.11) and (5.12).
Constraints (5.13) and (5.14) bounds the number of beds that can be cleaned per hour,
where (5.13) sets limits on beds in the machines, and (5.14) constrains the number of
beds for which the workers can perform all the required tasks, as deﬁned by wb. Equa-
tions (5.15) and (5.16) set the behavior when either the pre- or post-cleaning areas have
no worker. Here, (5.15) allocates the number at each area - note that workers are not

relative ease - and (5.16) reduces the amount of available time for a worker by a factor p

worker is available for each area.
The constraint in (5.17) is only relevant if more than one type of worker is possible, in
that it makes sure that a worker can only be one type. The schedule must be repeatable
and so constraints (5.18) and (5.19) enforce that there is at least as many beds in stock
at the end of the time-horizon as there was in the beginning.
There are three types of cleaning procedures in the facility, and for each bed cleaned the
model must choose between them. Normal and hotel cleanings are interchangeable, with
a trade-oﬀbetween quality and speed, but deep cleanings are required on certain beds.
Since this model does not take individual beds into account, constraint (5.20) ensures
that γd percent of all the cleanings at day d is done using the more time-consuming deep

Chapter 7
Discussion
Using a MIP approach, the scheduling problem for bed cleaning at hospitals was evalu-
ated for a case study with Rigshospitalet. With the proposed solution the problem was
solved to optimality in a fast, reliable fashion, creating personnel schedules that increased
robustness of the system while reducing cost. The model has been designed to work for
Rigshospitalets cleaning facility using the rules of the Danish labor market but can with
few modiﬁcations be generalized to apply to a generic hospital setting and speciﬁc work-
force laws. In the following, the advantages and limitations of the approach are discussed,
and suggestions for future work are given.
In chapter 6 it was seen that even minor changes to the current schedule at the cleaning
facility yielded improved results in terms of robustness and cost. The machines at the fa-
cility were found to be the main bottleneck of the system, and so extending their up-time
proved to be the major driver for increased robustness. This was achieved through ex-
panded time-windows, staggered schedules, and weekend-shifts. It is recommended that
a thorough study of the utilization of these machines is performed since it was shown
that the quality of the solution was very sensitive to changes in this parameter. Tuning
the values to reality is exceedingly important in order to achieve reliable schedules. This
could be accomplished informally through talks with hospital stakeholders, or by using a
data-driven approach such as simulation.
The proposed solution supports the use of part-time workers, and initial exploration
proved extremely promising. Indeed, the best solutions both in terms of cost and robust-
ness were all achieved using part-time workers. It allows the model the freedom both to
slot workers into periods of high demand and extends machine up-time at a relatively
low cost. However, the model assumes that changes to the schedule can be made free of
cost and that employees will be willing to embrace these measures that can drastically
change their every-day life. In the current approach, the quality of the schedules from
the perspective of the employees was measured through hours assigned outside a normal
weekday. A better way could be to make use of a decision support tool to map employee
preferences and use this as a soft constraint in the model. It may also be that reward
mechanisms will need to be employed to convince workers to accept these changes. Al-
lowing recurring free time during a shift is one such and may well be a good compromise.
Extensive testing in chapter 6 showed that even a very low worker availability - as low
32
==PAGE:38==
Master’s Thesis
2021-02-08
as 50% of the actual shift - did not aﬀect the robustness of the solution in a signiﬁcant
fashion, though cost did increase. The indication was very clearly that a smoothing of
the workforce, thus allowing increased machine up-time, was much more important than
the number of workers available at any one time. Another way of convincing employees
to change the schedules could be by allowing them to explicitly state a series of preferred
shifts and introduce a reward in the objective if these are met. Whether or not the model
needs to be adjusted, it is recommended to look into utilizing the idea of part-time work-
ers in the schedule.
The proposed solution is based on several assumptions, one being that knowledge of the
demand for clean beds is known prior to the creation of the schedule. In truth, patient
arrivals, and by extension bed demand, is quite diﬃcult to predict with any accuracy
due to a number of uncertainties. The consequence of assuming perfect information and
using deterministic input is that the mathematical model will seek solutions tightly ﬁt-
ted to the given instance. Such a solution tends to not generalize well, as was shown
in Section 6.2. This is a problem when the priorities of the stakeholders above all are
reliable solutions. As a way of combating this behavior, a buﬀer term was added to the
demand, the model was run on multiple instances, and the ﬁnal schedule was created
using a conservative estimate of demand. This ﬁnal schedule proved to be quite robust,
and through testing using e.g., simulation it may be possible to determine what input
gives a balance between robustness and optimal solutions. The motivation for doing so
can be found in the simple fact that the robust schedule requires 5 workers, while no more
than 3 were required when the model was applied individually to all of the tested data
instances. Methods also exist within the ﬁeld of operations research that is designed to
ﬁnd good solutions under uncertain conditions, that of stochastic programming and ro-
bust optimization. It may not be realistic to provide the probability distribution needed
for stochastic programming, but the data instances from the computational study could
be used as a deterministic uncertainty set for robust optimization. By enforcing strict
robustness, every instance is guaranteed to be feasible, and the worst-case costs are min-
imized. Aside from guaranteeing robustness, this method can give a price of robustness
i.e., the diﬀerence in objective value between optimal and the robust solution, which may
be of interest. This is an area worth exploring in an eventual future work.
Another issue from assuming perfect information stems from the decisions that the clean-
ing crew must make for most beds, namely whether a normal or a hotel cleaning procedure
should be adopted. With information about the upcoming demand freely at hand, this
is an easy choice for the model, but in a practical setting uncertainty and pressure to
perform, i.e., always have a clean bed available, may lead to sub-optimal decisions that
could cause a higher level of hotel cleanings than expected or even stock-outs. From a
modeling standpoint, this might again be addressed using robust optimization. It could
also be tackled directly at the cleaning facility, by implementing an eﬃcient pipeline of
information regarding the bed ﬂeet at the hospital, that could help inform the crew as
they make their decisions. As a minimum, the current clean stock should be tracked and
information be available for the workers in real-time, and if this could be combined with
daily or hourly expected demand, workers would be much more likely to make decisions
33
==PAGE:39==
Master’s Thesis
2021-02-08
aligned with an optimal approach.
In the current solution, it is assumed that worker eﬃciency is one-dimensional, in the
sense that outside circumstances do not have an inﬂuence on how many or how fast a
bed can be cleaned. Empirical evidence from Rigshospitalet seems to suggest otherwise,
speciﬁcally, productivity will increase substantially in busy periods. This could be in-
corporated into the model by making the duration of worker tasks dependent either on
current demand or the current surplus of clean beds.
A key assumption in the proposed MIP model is that there is always a used bed available
for cleaning. This simpliﬁcation aﬀords the option of not tracking beds on an individual
basis, thus reducing complexity substantially. This does, however, mean that the storage
of used beds is not tracked in the model. Further, since the only driver for throughput
is that demand for clean beds must always be met, the model oﬀers no guarantee on an
hourly or even daily rate of cleaning. It is, in other words, possible that the number
of used beds in the system exceeds the hospital’s capacity for storing them. A simple
way of ﬁxing this could be to require a certain amount of bed cleanings per day. If the
goal is to track the number of used beds, a storage variable much like vd,t, but for used
beds instead of clean ones, could be introduced, and the hourly arrival of used beds as a
function of patient discharges could be fed into the model, with constraints like those in
(5.2)-(5.5) enforcing correct behavior. This should not aﬀect the model greatly in terms
of complexity and might make it easier to use the model in the context of the entire
hospital bed system.
When testing the performance of the model on the case study from Rigshospitalet, it
was seen that satisfying demand on Monday mornings presented a real challenge. Part
of this is due to a spike in demand, but the cyclic nature of the schedule, where the time
horizon starts on Monday and ends Sunday, may also play a role. The number of beds
in stock at system start, S, is meant to represent the remaining safety stock at midnight
Monday, but the concept of a ﬁxed amount proved to be inhibiting to the model. In
future work, it is recommended to either extend the time horizon to several weeks or
change the structure of the model in such a way that the starting level is variable, for
instance, accomplished by changing (5.18) and (5.19) to v1,1 ≤vD,T
Following the proposed solution and results of this project, the recommended next steps,
aside from the points discussed above, is to actively engage with the hospital stakehold-
ers on these preliminary results. Does the proposed solution model reality to an accept-
able degree, and what are the employee’s reactions to the resulting personnel schedules?
Additionally, the model can be linked with a simulation framework for the entire bed
management system at the hospital, helping in decisions such as dimensioning of the bed
ﬂeet and workforce or the policies for bed storage.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis has been to improve bed management at hospitals by creating good
work shifts for the bed cleaning personnel. To that eﬀect, the scheduling problem for bed
cleaning at hospitals have been formally deﬁned, and a Mixed Integer Programming so-
lution to this problem has been proposed. This solution was tested in a computational
study using data from the Danish hospital Rigshospitalet, where it was shown that the
problem could be solved to optimality in polynomial time for a real-world instance.
The scheduling problem for bed cleaning at hospitals consists of assigning employees to
work shifts in a bed cleaning facility, such that the hospital-wide demand for clean beds
can always be met. Personnel scheduling is a well-studied problem, but within the do-
main of healthcare, the focus has been almost exclusively on health professionals such as
nurses and physicians, combinatorially complex problems that bear little resemblance to
the bed cleaning problem. This study makes use of a general scheduling model and aims
to adjust it to the speciﬁcs of the hospital cleaning facility, such that a precise mathe-
matical solution can be found.
The computational results were promising in the sense that it was possible to create fea-
sible personnel schedules that proved robust to large ﬂuctuations in demand. The eﬀect
of allowing ﬂexible shifts were studied, and it was seen that even small changes to the
current scheme could improve the reliability of the system substantially while aﬀording
a cost-saving on labor by at least 15%. If part-time workers can be employed in some
fashion, performance in terms of robustness and cost can be improved even further.
