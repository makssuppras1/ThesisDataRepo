I 
Abstract 
Synthetic biology is continuously expanding its genetic toolbox and thus the emphasis on 
systems interoperability is increasingly important. Genetic designs are expected to perform 
predictably regardless of the genetic context. Encoding these designs in the genome reduces 
the cellular burden. However, the performance of a gene expression cassette can be impacted 
especially if the gene expression cassette is a part of a larger genetic system. 
In this thesis, a genomically-integrated, single gene expression cassette based on the SEGA 
architecture was expanded into a collection of dual gene expression systems by incorporating 
the inducible promoters from the marionette strain collection These dual gene expression 
systems were employed to study promoter activity of genomically-integrated inducible 
promoters as well as their activity in a combined system. To this end, a total of seven dual 
gene expression systems were constructed which utilized gfp and mCherry as independent 
reporter genes for the promoters PCymRC, PSalTTC, and J23100. Moreover, the inducible 
promoters were found to exhibit higher sensitivity towards their inducer and lower induction 
fold in this new context compared to the reported values in Meyer et al., (2019). Furthermore, 
the temporal promoter dynamics, which had previously not been characterized, were found to 
be constant over time. 
The developed dual gene expression system provides a framework for future development of 
more complex gene circuits and may have future applications in biomanufacturing. 
Potentially, a dual landing pad system can be established by expanding SEGA obtaining a 
new generation of advanced and standardized bacterial genome engineering platform.

Resumé  
Syntetisk biologis genetiske værktøjskasse udvikles hele tiden og derfor kræves der ekstra 
opmærksomhed på genetiske værktøjers sammenspil. De genetiske designs forventes at 
fungere ens ligegyldigt genetisk kontekst. Ved at overfører disse genetiske designs til 
genomet så bliver den cellulære byrde mindre. Derimod kan funktionen af 
genudtrykskassetter blive nedsat især hvis genudtrykskassetten er en del af et større genetisk 
system. 
I dette kandidatspeciale, blev et genom-integreret genudtrykskassette baseret på SEGA 
genom-arkitekturen udvidet til en kollektion af dobbelte genudtrykssystemer ved at 
introducere inducerbare genpromotor for marionette stamme kollektionen. Disse dobbelte 
genudtrykssystemer blev anvendt til at studere promoter aktiviteten af de genom-integreret 
inducerbare genpromotorer enkeltvis og i kombinationer. Til dette formål, blev i alt syv 
dobbelte-genudtrykssystemer konstrueret, der brugte gfp og mCherry som uafhængige 
rapporter gener for genpromotorerne PCymRC, PSalTTC, og J23100. De inducerbare genpromotor 
blev vist at have højere sensitivitet for deres inducer and en lavere induktionsfold i forhold til 
de oprindelige værdierne fra Meyer et al., (2019), ligegyldigt hvilket genom loci der blev 
testet. Desuden, blev det fundet, at de tidsmæssige genpromotor-dynamikker var konstante 
over tid, hvilket ikke var blevet karakteriseret tidligere. 
De udviklede dobbelte-genudtrykssystemer fremlægger en skabelon for fremtidige 
udviklinger af mere komplekse genetiske systemer og har muligvis fremtidige 
udnyttelsesmulige indenfor biomanufacturing. Disse dobbelte genudtrykssystemer ligger 
potentiale for en videreudvikling af SEGA i et dobbelt landing pad system, hvilket derved 
opnår en ny generation of en avanceret og standardiseret bakteriel genomredigeringsplatform.

Introduction 
The world is facing an environmental crisis which is evident by the changes to the climate e.g., 
rising temperatures, more frequent extreme weather events, and the loss of biodiversity. These 
changes are significantly impacting the planets and human’s health1. In the face of this climate 
crisis technological advances are needed to restructure our current industrial processes into a 
sustainable framework. This restructuring involves the adopting of energy-efficient 
technologies, reducing waste through circular economy practices, and minimizing carbon 
emissions2,3. An aspect of this transformation is the shift from traditional chemical processes to 
biomanufacturing processes, thus leveraging biotechnology for manufacturing and production4.  
Biotechnology is a field with a wide range of innovative initiatives for sustainability. These 
innovations include new agricultural practices and microbial production of therapeutics or 
bulk chemicals to mention two examples. Specific examples can be that biofuel, i.e. ethanol 
is produced by Saccharomyces cerevisiae5,6, and the amino acid lysine is produced in 
Corynebacterium glutamicum7,8. These strains are natural producers for their specific 
product, but in other processes the product is produced heterologously9. The most common 
bacterial chassis for heterologous production is Escherichia coli (E. coli) because the toolbox 
for genetically engineering E. coli is extensive and the bacterium’s physiology is well 
characterized10. An avenue of heterologous production is the utilization of gene circuits. Gene 
circuits have a wide range of functionalities. Examples of established gene circuits include 
systems that can identify environmental niches, respond to inflammation or detect a 
bacterium and subsequently kill it11–13. In biomanufacturing gene circuits are applied to 
simplify production. For example, a gene circuit was established to sense the accumulation of 
biomass as a signal for activating the metabolic pathway for production14. 
The most common tool for genetic modifications is the utilization of plasmids, which is 
largely due to the simple in vitro modification to incorporate changes e.g., introducing 
mutations, exchanging a promoter or adding a reporter gene15,16. Plasmids are widely used to 
encode gene circuits and enable heterologous protein production at laboratory scale9,10,17. 
This is impacting the characterizing of genetic systems because the genetic context is a 
plasmid. However, the same genetic system carried on the chromosome might have different 
gene expression dynamics. 
==PAGE:9==
Page 2 
Even though plasmids are easy and simple tools for heterologous gene expression, they are 
not feasible for large-scale fermentation. The main disadvantages of a plasmid in large-scale 
fermentations are plasmid instability, cell-to-cell heterogeneity and metabolic burden18–23. A 
plasmid requires a large quantity of antibiotics to be maintained in a large-scale fermentation 
which is too costly17,20,21. The cell-to-cell heterogeneity is caused by the distribution of 
plasmids during cell division and thus creating highly producing strains and sub-populations 
with diminished production18. The alternative to a plasmid-based system is a genomically-
based system. The genomically-based system does not have the disadvantages of a plasmid-
based system because genomically-integrated systems are inherently more stable than 
plasmids even without antibiotics17,19–21. The above-mentioned reasons are the motivation for 
integrating recombinant DNA on the genome for fermentation processes. Here, genome 
integration is aided by the genetic toolbox of E. coli because advances in genome engineering 
technologies like the clustered regulatory interspaced short palindromic repeats 
(CRISPR)/CRISPR-associated protein (Cas9). The CRISPR-Cas9 system have made genome 
engineering simpler and more accessible24,25. Genome engineering is not only challenged by 
the choice of how to integrate recombinant DNA on the chromosome but also the location of 
integration should be taken into consideration. This consideration is based on the 
observations that the gene expression varies from region to region on the chromosome. 
Therefore, the performance of a recombinant integrated gene expression system is impacted 
by the genomic location of integration26. The variation of genomic gene expression is the 
culmination of various phenomena which are varying copy number of the chromosome 
during cell division, gene stability, varying Guanine-Cytosine (GC) content and RNA 
polymerase availability26–30. 
Landing pads are a tool developed to simplify the engineering of genomic gene expression 
cassettes31,32. The overall concept of a landing pad is either to integrate an entire gene 
expression cassette32 or just a gene of interest (goi) in a functional, pre-integrated gene 
expression cassette in an already characterized genome location31. The gene expression 
cassette of the landing pad requires a promoter, a ribosomal binding site (RBS), a goi, and a 
terminator. Some highly used versions of these genetic elements are registered and collected 
in different data bases33,34 and the largest registry is the international Genetically Engineered 
Machine (iGEM) parts registry34. Additionally, standalone collections for specific parts exist 
alongside the registries, e.g., the marionette promoter collection which is collection of twelve 
inducible promoter optimized for high dynamic range, sensitivity and specificity35.  
==PAGE:10==
Page 3 
This trend of creating registries is a step towards standardization in biology, more specifically 
synthetic biology36. Standardization has many aspects in which one subsection is to ensure 
the interoperability of two genetic systems. As an example, how well do the inducible 
promoters from the marionette promoter collection perform as part of a landing pad? Or how 
does the expansion of a landing pad system to entail multiple loci impact the functionality of 
the landing pad? A more technical background is needed to answer these questions. The 
specific topics are the genome engineering of E. coli, the marionette promoter collection, and 
the Standardized Genome Architecture. 
Technical background 
Genome engineering of Escherichia coli 
Engineering projects involving prokaryotes like E. coli are commonly utilizing plasmids 
because plasmids are simple and easy to modify in vitro15. However, the disadvantages as 
outlined above entail plasmid instability, cell-to-cell heterogeneity and cellular burden18–23. 
The toolbox for genome editing in E. coli is extensive and includes tools like CRISPR-Cas9, 
λ-red recombineering, phage-based integrases, and flippases to mention a few 
examples24,37,38. Specifically, λ-red recombineering is used heavily in this thesis and therefore 
described in detail in the section below. 
λ-red recombineering is a tool to precisely integrate a linear DNA fragment into the 
chromosome of E. coli. The major advantage of λ-red recombineering is that the homologous 
region can be as short as 36 nucleotides38. However, 50 nucleotides are the most common 
size of homologous region to ensure specificity of integrating on the chromosome. λ-red 
recombineering is based on the three phage-derived λ-red proteins Gam, Exo, and Beta which 
each have different functions. The Gam protein inhibits the E. coli nucleases RecBCD and 
SbcCD thus preventing degradation of inserted linear DNA. The Exo protein is the λ-
exonuclease that degrades double stranded DNA (dsDNA) to single stranded DNA (ssDNA) 
from the 5’ end to 3’ end. Subsequently, the ssDNA is bound to the Beta protein which 
promotes annealing to the site-specific homology on the genome while protecting the ssDNA 
from further degradation. The annealing to the site-specific homology specifically is targeted 
to the lagging strand to mimic an Okazaki fragment so that the recombinant DNA is 
incorporated in the genome in a round of replication. A single genome replication will lead to 
two variants of the genome i.e. the non-integrated genome and the successfully modified 
genome (figure 1)38,39.  
==PAGE:11==
Page 4 
In practice, the λ-red recombineering system is often carried on a helper plasmid which exist 
in a wide range of variants. The specific variant utilized in this thesis is the helper plasmid 
pSIM19. pSIM19 is the variant with a heat regulated λ-red recombineering system, i.e. Gam, 
Exo and Beta are expressed from a PL promoter which is regulated by the temperature 
sensitive CI857 mutant. This mutant retains repression of the λ-red genes at 30°C but at 42°C 
CI857 is not repressing the λ-red genes, thus resulting in the expression of the λ-red genes. 
Experimentally, the λ-red recombineering machinery is induced by heat-shock. In extension, 
the origin of replication (ori) of pSIM19 is also temperature sensitive, i.e. pSIM19 is 
replicated at 30°C but at 37°C pSIM19 is not replicated. This ori enables a simple curing 
strategy for pSIM19 after its use for the genomic modifications by growing the final strain at 
37°C40.

The Marionette Collection 
The marionette promoter collection is a collection of twelve highly optimized inducible 
promoters and their respective regulators. The optimization of the inducible promoters and 
regulators increased the dynamic range and sensitivity while minimizing the leakiness and 
cross-activity for the promoters and regulators. In context of the marionette promoter 
collection leakiness is the level of basal promoter activity in the absence of the specific 
inducer. The optimization was performed as an iterative process of applying negative- and 
positive selection in which the negative selection targeted the leakiness or cross-activity, and 
the positive selection was employed for optimizing the dynamic range and sensitivity for the 
inducible promoters. This optimization resulted in inducible promoters with low leakiness, 
low cross- activity, high dynamic range, and high sensitivity35. 
The inducible promoters of the marionette promoter collection are P3B5C, PBAD, PBetI, PCymRC, 
PCin, PLuxB, PPhlF, PSalTTC, PTac, PTet, PTtg, and PVanCC with their corresponding regulators pcaU, 
araC, betI, cymR, cinR, LuxR, phlF, nahR, lacI, tetR, ttgR and vanR. All promoters are 
inducible with one of the specific following compounds 3,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid (DHBA), 
L-arabinose (ara), Choline chloride (cho), cuminic acid (cuma), 3OC6-AHL (OC6), 2,4-
Diacetylphophloroglucinol (DAPG), sodium salicylate (sal), Isopropyl-β-d-thiogalactoside 
(IPTG), anhydrotetracycline HCl (aTc), naringenin (nar) and vanillic acid (van). The 
interaction of the promoters, regulators and their inducers are summarized in figure 235. 
The marionette promoter collection sought to simplify the sensing of the inducers by creating 
a genomic array of all the optimized regulators. The genomic array was called the “sensor 
array” because the array carries the genes for all twelve optimized regulators across three 
constitutively expressed polycistronic operons. The sensor array and each operon are 
separated from the genetic context by terminators to reduce the readthrough of the genome. 
Specifically, the sensor array was transduced into the glvC locus of the three E. coli strains: 
MG1655, DH10B and BL21 to respectively create Marionette-Wild (MW), Marionette-Clo 
and Marionette-Pro. The genetic components of the sensor array are illustrated (figure 2, 
adapted from Meyer et al (2019))35.

Inducible promoters like the marionette promoters are generally titratable by varying 
concentrations of inducer. The relation between inducer concentration and promoter activity 
is called a dose-response curve which can by modelled by a sigmodal Hill function (see 
methods and materials). The parameters of a sigmodal Hill function are the concentration of 
inducer, the promoter leakiness, the maximum promoter activity, the promoter sensitivity, and 
the steepness of slope, also named the Hill coefficient. The promoter sensitivity entails the 
apparent affinity of inducer interacting with the regulator and subsequently the regulator 
interaction with the promoter (figure 2). Specifically, in the Hill function, the apparent 
affinity is the inducer concentration at which half maximum promoter activity is achieved. 
Lastly, the dynamic range of a promoter is defined as the ratio of the promoter activity at the 
lowest and highest inducer concentration35,41.

A Standardized Genome Architecture 
Genome engineering in prokaryotes is yet to utilize fully standardized systems and methods. 
One of the reasons is likely related to the fact that genome engineering protocols generally 
require multiple helper plasmids, strains and DNA fragments24,25. A step to standardize 
bacterial genome engineering was made by Bayer et al., (2021)31 who developed the 
Standardized Genome Architecture (SEGA) with the purpose of simplifying and 
standardizing genome engineering. SEGA is a collection of E. coli MG1655 strains carrying 
genomic landing pads with standardized homology sites for recombineering. The SEGA 
landing pads are located in the intergenic region of glmS and pstS without interfering with the 
functionality of the surrounding genes (figure 3A). Conceptually, a landing pad like the ones 
in SEGA is an expression cassette where a goi can be integrated by following a streamlined 
protocol31. 
The SEGA landing pad architecture is built on five exchangeable modules. These modules 
are the upstream gadget, control module, middle gadget, cargo and downstream gadget 
(figure 3A). The main intention of a SEGA landing pad is to exchange the cargo with a goi 
specifically denoted as a cargo brick exchange (figure 3B). Besides integrating a goi as cargo, 
it is also possible to exchange the control module with a specific promoter or RBS. This 
exchange of the control module was denoted as a control brick exchange (figure 3B). The 
genomic engineering of SEGA is facilitated by λ-Red recombineering though the helper 
plasmid pSIM1931,40. The advantage of engineering a SEGA landing pad is the utilization of 
recombineering sites, which are characterized and standardized, thus simplifying the 
engineering. 
SEGA employs a layer of counter-selection and screening when modifying the landing pad, 
enabled by the upstream- and downstream gadget. Specifically, galK is utilized as the 
upstream gadget for the control brick exchange and tetA is used as the downstream gadget for 
the cargo brick exchange (figure 3A, B). Specifically, galK and tetA are both dual selection 
makers but they are utilized as counter-selection. Thus, a strain carrying galK is sensitive to 
deoxy-2-galactose (DOG)31,42 and strains expressing tetA become sensitive to NiCl231,43. The 
additional layer of screening in SEGA is supplied by the cargo module which usually 
contains a green fluorescent protein (gfp) (figure 3A). This gfp enables differentiation 
between green fluorescent colonies, which still contain the original gfp marker and successful 
recombinants as white colonies. In SEGA, the control module includes a promoter and RBS 
sequence defined as control element 1 (C1) and control element 2 (C2) respectively, thus 
==PAGE:15==
Page 8 
enabling standardized control of gene expression at both the transcriptional and translational 
level. In the collection of SEGA landing pads control element 1 can be either the promoters 
J23100, PrhaBAD, or PT7 with an RBS tuned to low, medium and high translational levels of 
the cargo in control element 2 (figure 3A)31. 
The middle gadget of SEGA is split into two categories: protein tags and untagged cargo. The 
untagged cargo utilizes a bicistronic design (BCD) to i) keep a standardized translation 
initiation region independent from the cargo and ii) act as a homology sequence for 
recombineering without adding extra amino acids to the protein on interest31. A BCD 
functions by stabilizing translation which is achieved by translational coupling of two 
subsequent cistrons namely a leader peptide and the goi (figure 3C). This system is thus 
mimicking the natural bacterial transcription unit with overlapping genes in which translation 
stabilization was observed44,45. The BCD used in SEGA was developed by Mutalik et al., 
(2013)46 who constructed a library of BCDs by randomizing the Shine-Dalgarno (SD) of the 
goi, which is embedded in the leader peptide. This resulted in multiple variants of the BCD 
with varying translational strengths while keeping the same sequence of the leader peptide 
across the variants except the randomized SD region46. 
In this thesis the specific SEGA landing pad, SEGA007 was used as the starting point for the 
dual gene expression system. SEGA007 contains a landing pad with a J23100 promoter with 
a BCD as middle gadget which highly expressed gfp as cargo with tetA as downstream 
gadget31. Simply duplicating the SEGA007 landing pad for the dual gene expression system 
might inhibit its functionality, because a larger repeat of DNA can lead to instability of the 
chromosome47. Therefore, in previous unpublished projects variants of the SEGA007 landing 
pad were constructed. The first collection of new variants was based on the integration of the 
marionette promoters exchanging the J23100 promoter. In the second collection of new 
variants the middle gadget was exchanged with a new BCD variant (BCD S4). The new BCD 
variant was constructed from a library that changed the sequence of the leader peptide, while 
maintaining the high expression level of the goi (Supplementary figure 1). These variants 
were used as the base framework for the dual gene expression system.

Aim of the thesis 
The synthetic biology toolbox is expanding rapidly, and the field is putting increased 
emphasis on standardization. In this situation it is important to ensure interoperability 
between different established systems. The interoperability of systems is important to build 
even more complex genetic functions on the bacterial genome in a standardized and 
streamlined manner. In this thesis, I am specifically addressing the question, what the 
characteristics of the plasmid-based marionette promoters are when integrated into to genome 
utilizing the Standardized Genome Architecture as a framework? Moreover, what is the 
behavior of two inducible promoters in a gene expression system consisting of two gene 
expression cassettes? To answer these questions, I constructed and characterized genomically 
integrated, inducible dual gene expression systems.

Material & methods 
Overview of the strains, primers and plasmids 
In this thesis several strains, primers and plasmids were used (table 1, 2, 3). The strains 
carrying the expression cassettes were systematically named based on their expression 
cassettes. For example, Marionette-Wild ΔgalK containing an expression cassette in locus S 
with a J23100 (J) promoter driving gfp (G) expression is referred to as “MW ΔgalK JG”. The 
same background strain containing an expression cassette in locus D with a PCymRC (C) 
promoter driving mCherry (R) expression is referred to as “MW ΔgalK CR”. The genomic 
locus of gfp is always locus S, while mCherry is always in locus D. The nomenclature

Description of strain construction 
The workflow of the strain construction is described below in figure 5. All strains were 
constructed by λ-Red recombineering (described below) with polymerase chain reaction 
(PCR) amplified DNA fragments (described below).  
The SEGA landing pad of SEGA007-BCD(strict) was amplified in a PCR by GE7484 and 
GE7485 and integrated into locus D of E. coli MG1655 thus creating SEGA007-rpnD-
BCD(strict). E. coli MG1655 JR was created by integrating mCherry into SEGA007-rpnD-
BCD(strict). Moreover, the mCherry fragment was amplified with the oligos GE7457 and 
GE7486 from the plasmid p1705, (table 3). E. coli MG1655 JR-tetA was constructed from E. 
coli MG1655 JR by integrating an upstream tetA amplified from SEGA007 by the oligos 
GE7672 and 7673. 
Concurrently, MW ΔgalK SEGA007-gfp, MW ΔgalK SEGA007-PCymRC-gfp, and MW ΔgalK 
SEGA007-PSalTTC-gfp were created from the SEGA landing pad of MW ΔgalK SEGA007, 
MW ΔgalK SEGA007-PCymRC, and MW ΔgalK SEGA007-PSalTTC by a deletion of tetA 
utilizing the MAGE oligo GE7677. Subsequently the expression cassette from E. coli 
MG1655 JR-tetA was amplified (GE7491 and GE7493) and transferred to MW ΔgalK 
SEGA007-gfp, MW ΔgalK SEGA007-PCymRC-gfp, and MW ΔgalK SEGA007-PSalTTC-gfp. 
The resulting strains carried a dual expression cassette and were named MW ΔgalK JG JR-
tetA, MW ΔgalK CG JR-tetA, and MW ΔgalK SG JR. 
MW ΔgalK JG JR-tetA was utilized as parental strain for MW ΔgalK JG JR, MW ΔgalK JG 
CR, and MW ΔgalK JG SR. MW ΔgalK JG JR was created by removing the tetA using the 
oligo GE7676. MW ΔgalK JG CR, and MW ΔgalK JG SR were constructed by a promoter 
exchange. Specifically, PCymRC was integrated using the MAGE oligo GE7722 to yield MW 
ΔgalK JG CR and PSalTTC was amplified from SEGA007-PSalTTC-gfp using the oligo pair 
GE7723 and GE7724 and integration to yield MW ΔgalK JG SR. The same construction 
==PAGE:21==
Page 14 
steps were applied to MW ΔgalK CG JR-tetA and MW ΔgalK SG JR which resulted in MW 
ΔgalK CG JR, MW ΔgalK CG SR, MW ΔgalK SG CR. 
The following primer pairs were utilized to probe the loci of glmS-pstS (GE7801 and 
GE7802), rpnD-yjiR (GE7491 and GE7493), and galK (GE4527 and GE4338). Primers for 
the subsequent Sanger sequencing of the three loci were glmS-pstS: GE1291, rpnD-yjiR: 
GE7727 and GE7728, galK: GE4527.  
The new SEGA landing pad in locus D: MH72 was constructed from E. coli ΔgalK JR by 
integrating the cI-hok-neo system48 as downstream gadget. The cI-hok-neo system was 
amplified from pTmpKm48 by the oligos GE7487 and GE7488. The cI-hok-neo cassette 
utilized GE7492 and GE7494 for Sanger sequencing. The knock-out of galK of E. coli 
MG1655 was performed by the MAGE oligo GE4930 resulting in E. coli MG1655 ΔgalK. 
General microbiological techniques 
Strain cultivation 
Stains were routinely cultivated in lysogeny broth (LB) (tryptone 10 g/L, yeast extract 5 g/L, 
NaCl 5 g/L) at 37℃ with shaking of 250 rpm or on solid LB agar (1%) plates at 37℃. 
Except, strains carrying pSIM19 were cultivated at 30℃. The media was supplemented with 
the appropriate antibiotic when required with the following concentrations were used 
spectinomycin (50 µg/mL), tetracycline (10 µg/mL or 50 µg/ml), kanamycin (50 µg/ml), and 
ampicillin (100 µg/ml). 
Polymerase chain reaction for strain construction 
Polymerase chain reaction was utilized for strain construction and for probing genetic 
modifications as a diagnostic tool. The linear DNA fragments required for λ-Red 
recombineering in strain construction were amplified by PCR (described below). The PCR 
mixture contained Phusion High-Fidelity PCR Master Mix with HF buffer (Thermo Fischer 
Scientific), 0.5 µM primer for each primer, and 3% DMSO as final concentrations. 
Additionally, 1 µL template was used and the remaining volume was filled with MilliQ (MQ) 
water to a total reaction volume of 50 µL. The PCR protocol used with the Phusion 
polymerase was initial DNA denaturing step at 98℃ for 5 minutes followed by 30 cycles of 
DNA denaturing step at 98℃ for 30 seconds, an annealing step with varying temperatures, 
depending on the primers, for 30 seconds and an elongation step at 72℃ with varying times 
depending on the amplicon length (1 kb per 30 seconds elongation). Samples from the PCRs 
were probed using gel electrophoresis (see below). 
==PAGE:22==
Page 15 
Diagnostic PCRs were applied to probe colonies for genetic modifications following the λ-
Red recombineering. The diagnostic PCR mixture contained OneTaq® Quick-Load® 2X 
Master mix with Standard Buffer (New England Biolabs), 0.5 µM primer from each primer, 1 
µL template, and the remain volume was filled with MQ water to a total reaction volume of 
30 µL. The template was a single colony dissolved in 20 µL MQ waster. The PCR protocol 
used with the OneTaq polymerase was initial DNA denaturing step at 95℃ for 5 minutes 
followed by 30 cycles of DNA denaturing step at 95℃ for 30 seconds, an annealing step at 
55℃ for 30 seconds and an elongation step at 68℃ with varying times depending on the 
amplicon length (1 kb per 45 seconds elongation). The samples from the diagnostic PCRs 
were probed using gel electrophoresis (see below). 
Troubleshooting of PCR 
A few PCRs did not yield any product with the above-described general parameters. 
Troubleshooting was conducted as follows. The total reaction volume was reduced to 10 µL 
and a temperature gradient of the annealing step was utilized. In addition, varying 
concentrations of DMSO (1.5% - 6%) were tested. If a high abundance of primer dimer was 
presented, the primer concentration was reduced to 0.1 µM.  
Gel electrophoresis 
The size of the PCR products was probed with gel electrophoresis using a standard protocol. 
A 1% agarose solution was cast with supplemented 1000x SYBRTM Safe DNA gel stain 
(Invitrogen, Thermo Fischer Scientific) in a working solution, unless otherwise stated. In the 
solidified gel the PCR products and appropriate DNA ladder (GeneRuler 1 kb DNA Ladder or 
GeneRuler 50 bp DNA Ladder, Thermo Fischer Scientific) were loaded and subsequently run 
in a gel electrophoresis chamber with constant voltage (90 V) for 25-30 minutes. Hereafter, 
the gel was photographed using iBright Imaging Systems (Invitrogen, Thermo Fischer 
Scientific).

Strain construction using λ-Red recombineering 
All newly created strains in this thesis were constructed by λ-Red recombineering following 
the protocols outlined below. The λ-Red recombineering machinery was carried by the helper 
plasmid pSIM1940. 
Preparation of electro-competent cells for plasmid transformation 
From a saturated cell culture, electrocompetent cells were prepared by harvesting 1 ml culture 
per 50 µL electroporation reaction and kept on ice. The harvested cell culture was 
subsequently washed by centrifugating the cells for 30 seconds with 11000g at 4℃ and the 
resulting pellet was resuspended in 1 ml ice cold 15% glycerol. The washing was performed 
thrice, and hereafter, the cells were resuspended in 15% ice cold glycerol matching the 
volume of electroporation reactions from harvested cells. The electrocompetent cells were 
either directly used in electroporation or stored at -70℃ until use.  
The electroporation utilized 50 µL of electrocompetent cells for one reaction. The 
electrocompetent cells were mixed with 50 ng of plasmid and subsequently incubated on ice 
for 10 min. Hereafter, the electrocompetent cells and DNA mixture were electroporated using 
Bio rad Gene pulser Xcell electroporation system main unit. Specifically, the preset protocol 
“Bacterial 1” in which the pulse type was set to exponential decay and the electroporation 
parameters capacitance, sample resistance and voltages were set to 25 µF 200 Ω and 1.8 kV 
respectively. Electroporation cuvettes with a 0.1 cm gap were utilized for the electroporation. 
Subsequently, the cells were recovered in 1 ml super optimal broth with catabolite repression 
(SOC) for 1 hour at 30℃ while shaking at 750 rpm in an Eppendorf ThermoMixer C and 
plated on LB plates supplemented with appropriate antibiotic. 
Preparation of λ-Red-induced, electrocompetent cells 
λ-Red-induced, electrocompetent cells were prepared from a saturated cell culture carrying 
pSIM19. The cells were diluted 1:100 into 50 ml LB supplemented with 50 µg/ml 
spectinomycin in a 250 ml Erlenmeyer flask. The cells were incubated at 30℃ till an optical 
density of 0.4 was reached, and hereafter the cells were heat-shocked by incubating in a water 
bath at 42℃ for 19 minutes to induce expression of the λ-Red recombineering proteins. After 
induction, the cells were incubated on ice for 20 min. Subsequently, the cells were harvested 
and washed thrice by centrifugating 30 seconds with 11000g at 4℃ and the resulting pellet 
was resuspended in 1 ml ice cold MQ water. In the last resuspension 600 µL of ice cold 15% 
==PAGE:24==
Page 17 
glycerol was used instead of MQ. The cells were either directly used for λ-Red 
recombineering or stored at -70℃ until usage. 
Electroporation reactions utilized 50 µL λ-Red-induced electrocompetent cells, which were 
mixed with 200 ng of linear DNA. Hereafter, the electrocompetent cells and DNA mixture 
were electroporated following the electroporation procedure described above with exception 
of the recovery protocol. The recovery and selection differed depending on the selection 
marker and whether positive or counterselection was required for the genetic modification.  
Positive selection of tetA with tetracycline 
For positive selection of tetA the recovery culturing was performed by adding 950 µL SOC to 
the electroporated cells and incubate the cells for 1-2 hours at 30℃. Subsequently, the 
recovery culture was concentrated by centrifugating at 11000g for 30 seconds at room 
temperature and plated on LB agar plates supplemented with 10 µg/ml or 50 µg/ml 
tetracycline. The plates were incubated at 30℃ for two days or until colonies formed31. 
Counterselection of tetA with NiCl2 
For counterselection against tetA the recovery cultutivation was performed in two steps. First, 
the electroporated cells were incubated in 950 µL SOC for 1 hour. Thereafter, the recovery 
culture was transferred to 10 ml LB supplemented with 50 µg/ml spectinomycin in a 50 ml 
falcon tube. This culture was incubated overnight (ON) at 30℃, 250 rpm. Subsequently, 1 ml 
of saturated recovery culture was harvested and pelleted by centrifugating 30 seconds with 
11000g at room temperature. Pelleted cells were resuspended in 1 ml 1x M9 salts and a 
dilution series (1:10-1, 1:10-2, and 1:10-3) in 1x M9 salts was created. Dilutions were plated on 
M9 agar supplemented with 50 µM NiCl2. Plates were incubated at 30℃ for two days or until 
colonies formed31. 
DOG counterselection of galK 
The recovery procedure for galK was mainly identical to the above-described NiCl2 
counterselection with the following modifications. The first incubation was in 950 µl LB for 
4 hours and the plating of the counterselection was performed on M63 agar supplemented 
with 0.2% DOG31,42.

Time-resolved gene expression measurement 
Time-resolved gene expression experiments started by streaking the strain(s) of interest on 
LB agar from their glycerol stocks and incubated ON at 37℃. Hereafter a single colony of 
each strain was inoculated into 3 ml LB in a 12 ml cultivation tube and incubated ON. 
Outgrown cultures were diluted 1:100 into 3 ml LB in a 12 ml cultivation tube in which the 
strains were incubated for 2.5 hours at 37℃ with shaking at 250 rpm. The optical density at 
600 nm (OD600) of the cultures was measured and normalized with fresh LB to an OD600 of 
0.005 for inoculation into a 96 well microtiter plate with final volume of 250 µL LB. The 
growth media was supplemented with various inducer gradients (see below). The 96 well 
microtiter plate was sealed with BreathEasy film and subsequently measurements were taken 
in a Synergy H1 plate reader. The plate was incubated at 37℃ with orbital shaking (807 cpm) 
and absorbance at 600 nm, GFP fluorescence (excitation: 487 nm/emission: 528 nm, gain: 
100) and mCherry fluorescence (excitation: 579 nm/emission: 616 nm, gain 100) were 
measured every 20 minutes for minimum of 16 hours. After the growth in the Synergy H1 
plate reader the plate was stored at 4℃ for measurement with flowcytometry. For each plate 
layout (strain-inducer condition), time-resolved gene expression measurements were 
independently performed on three different days by picking previously unused colonies. 
Preparation of inducer gradients 
Two types of plate layouts were utilized for the time-resolved gene expression experiments, 
namely, a single inducer gradient and an inducer gradient matrix. Single inducer gradient 
plates contained a gradient of a single inducer in eleven concentration steps ranging from 0 
µM to 500 µM. The inducer gradients were created by diluting 0.1M cuminic acid or 1M 
sodium salicylate into LB to archive the desired concentrations. In the 96 well microtiter 
plate, 230 µL of medium containing the inducer was mixed with 20 µL of OD600-adjusted 
bacterial culture to reach an inoculation OD600 of 0.005 and a total cultivation volume of 250 
µL. Plates contained the cuminic acid gradient in the first four rows and salicylate gradient in 
the last four rows. The remaining wells of the microtiter plate were filled with blanks of LB. 
In the plate with the inducer gradient matrix, the cuminic acid gradient was set up along the 
rows and the salicylate gradient along the columns. Both gradients had eight concentration 
steps ranging from 0 µM to 500 µM utilizing the same stock concentration as described 
above to achieve a 2x concentration of the desired concentration. Thus, each strain was 
assayed in an 8x8 dual inducer matrix. Additionally, the three controls had each a 3x3 matrix 
with concentrations of 0 µM, 20 µM, and 500 µM for both cuminic acid and salicylate. The 
==PAGE:26==
Page 19 
controls were strains carrying the complementary gene expression cassettes with the 
constitutive promoter J23100 to the assayed strain. For example, MW ΔgalK CG SR had MW 
ΔgalK CG JR, MW ΔgalK JG SR, and MW ΔgalK JG JR as controls. To the 96 well 
microtiter plate 115 µL of the cuminic acid- and salicylate gradient was transferred in 
accordance with the layout, lastly 20 µL of OD600-adjusted culture was inoculated to reach 
OD600 0.005 while yielding a total cultivation volume of 250 µL. 
Flowcytometry measurements of gene expression 
Cell cultures from the stored plates of the time-resolved gene expression experiment were 
diluted 1:50 in 250 µL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) in a 96 microtiter well plate. A 
NovoCyte Quanteon flow cytometer (Agilent) was used to measure the GFP fluorescence in 
the FITC-H channel (excitation: 487 nm/emission: 528 nm) and mCherry fluorescence in the 
PE-H channel (excitation: 579 nm/emission: 616 nm). 50000 gated events were collected for 
each sample. The gating consisted of two steps. First, a quadrant gate with a threshold of 
3200 on FSC-H (vertical line) and 4600 on SSC-H (horizontal line) was set up. The first 
quadrant (top right) of the quadrant gate was gated further by a polygon gate in an FSC-H as 
a function of FSC-A to select for single cell events49 (figure 4). One replicate plate of each 
time-resolved gene expression experiment was measured by flow cytometry.

4600. The right plot shows FSC-H as a function of FSC-A with a polygon gate to select

Data analysis and illustrations

Design considerations and strain construction 
Design considerations for a genomically-integrated dual strain expression system 
The dual genomically-integrated, inducible gene expression systems were designed from 
established genetic elements. This includes two inducible promoters of the marionette 
collection, the Marionette-Wild strain and a small collection of SEGA landing pads. 
Specifically, the marionette promoters PCymRC and PSalTTC were utilized. These promoters 
depict high dynamic expression range with the addition of representing two distinct types of 
induction mechanisms, namely, a repressor and activator. The marionette promoters require 
their regulator to control expression, and as part of the promoter marionette collection the 
strain Marionette-Wild expressed all the required regulators enabling expression from the 
marionette promoters (figure 2)35. The specific Marionette-Wild strain in this thesis carried a 
galK knock-out for the purpose of strain engineering. The utilization of the Marionette-Wild 
strain facilitated a more straightforward design of the gene expression cassette. This is 
because the regulator did not need to be expressed from the same gene expression cassette, 
unlike the current SEGA landing pads utilizing marionette promoters31. 
Besides the architecture of the gene expression cassette, the integration locus is equally 
important for expression because gene expression can be locus-dependent due to various 
reasons26,29. The criteria to select a locus were stability and high expression, and in Goormans 
et al., (2020)26 multiple intergenic locations were characterized to uncover loci with high 
expression and stability, e.g., the intergenic loci between rpnD-yjiR (formerly rpnD was 
known as yjiP). Thus, the rpnD-yjiR locus is a suitable location for the second gene 
expression cassette26. The first expression cassette is located in the intergenic region between 
glmS-pstS in which the SEGA landing pads are harbored31. The expression cassettes in loci 
rpnD-yjiR and glmS-pstS were based on the SEGA landing pad architecture to reap the 
benefits of this streamlined toolkit for genome integration. Moreover, the gene expression 
cassette architecture is comprised of a set of flanking terminators, a promoter, an RBS, a 
middle gadget, and a reporter gene, thus following the SEGA landing pad architecture31. 
Additionally, this architecture should ensure stable expression of the reporter proteins GFP 
and mCherry from the promoters, in that the middle gadget is a BCD. 
 
==PAGE:30==
Page 23 
One of the general problems of utilizing multiple synthetic gene expression cassettes within 
one strain is that reusing the same genetic elements could potentially disrupt the functionality 
of the gene expression cassette, e.g., because of the risk of unintended homologous 
recombination47. Therefore, the utilized genetic elements for each gene expression cassette 
were different of which the primary differences are in reporter genes (gfp and mCherry) and 
the BCD (two different variants). The BCD especially was important to change due to its role 
as a homology region for integration and if the BCD was unchanged the construction 
unintended homologous recombination was likely to occur between the two gene expression 
cassettes. Consequently, a SEGA landing pad harboring an alternative BCD was developed in 
a previous project to mitigate this problem (Supplementary figure 1). 
In summary, a dual gene expression system was designed to include two separate 
genomically-integrated, inducible promoter gene expression cassettes located at the glmS-
pstS and rpnD-yjiR loci. The system’s architecture was based on the SEGA architecture, 
facilitating streamlined gene modifications31. Lastly, the performance of these inducible 
promoters can easily be monitored by recording green and red fluorescence signals. 
Construction of a genomically-integrated dual expression system 
The construction of the dual gene expression system strain mainly consisted of constructing 
the second gene expression cassette. The workflow of the strain construction was as follows: 
A previously constructed SEGA landing pad with a J23100 as promoter, an alternative BCD 
as middle gadget, gfp as cargo, and tetA as downstream gadget was transferred from the 
original SEGA locus glmS–pstS (denoted as Locus S) to rpnD–yjiR (denoted as Locus D) 
(figure 5A). Next, gfp was exchanged for mCherry using the NiCl2-counter selection against 
the dual selection marker tetA and visual selection of red fluorescent colonies (figure 5A). 
Subsequently, tetA was re-introduced as an upstream gadget to act as a counterselection 
marker to integrate the promoters PCymRC and PSalTTC into the final strain (figure 5A). 
Concurrently, three gene expression cassettes were created at locus S using the promoters 
J23100, PCymRC and PSalTTC from SEGA landing pads, though the deletion of tetA (figure 5B). 
Thereafter, the two expression cassettes had to be combined into a Marionette-Wild ΔgalK 
strain. From the construction described above, three expression cassettes with differing 
promoters in locus S were already available in the MW ΔgalK strain background. The 
expression cassette in locus D was then integrated in each MW ΔgalK strain with positive 
selection of tetA. This resulted in three strains carrying two genomically-integrated

three strains (figure 5A). In total nine combinations of the dual expression system were 
designed in this manner.

Characterization of genomically-integrated, dual expression systems 
The dual expression systems were characterized by examining the growth kinetics under 
various inducer conditions. The conducted experiments were designed to provide insight of 
the promoter dynamics of the inducible promoters PCymRC and PSalTTC, especially when they 
are used in combination. Specifically, the raw fluorescence emitted by GFP or mCherry 
served as a proxy for promoter activity. These promoters were both examined individually 
paired with the constitutive J23100 promoter and in the dual gene expression system where 
they are paired with each other in separate gene expression cassettes. This led to 
characterizing the promoter dynamics of the inducible gene expression systems in a single 
inducer gradient as well as a dual inducer matrix gradient in exponential phase. Additional 
characterization was employed on the promoter dynamics over time and lastly the dynamic 
range using single-cell measurements. 
Characterization of the cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system in exponential phase 
The response of the cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system was investigated in single 
gradients of inducers in the strain backgrounds in which the second expression cassette was 
constitutively controlled (strains MW ΔgalK CG-JR and MW ΔgalK JG-CR). Expression 
system activity was assayed in exponential phase 3.3 hours after incubation in LB at 37C. As 
expected, the non-inducible control strains (MW ΔgalK and MW ΔgalK JG-JR) did not depict 
changes in fluorescence level as function of inducer concentration (figure 8B, C, E, F). A 
similar observation was made for MW ΔgalK CG-JR and MW ΔgalK JG-CR for the 
fluorescence expression driven by constitutive promoters and, more generally, for all strains 
in the presence of non-matched inducer salicylate (figure 8B, C, E, F). Conversely, MW 
ΔgalK CG-JR showed a sigmodal response curve for the GFP fluorescence level when higher 
concentrations of cuminic acids in range 0-200 µM were supplied to the growth medium 
(figure 8B). MW ΔgalK JG-CR depicted a corresponding behavior in the mCherry 
fluorescence level under identical culture conditions (figure 8E). Expression system activity 
of the cuminic acid-inducible sub-systems saturated at comparable levels as those produced 
by the constitutively controlled expression systems, when inducer was added at the 
concentrations 100 µM and 200 µM (figure 8E).

Characterization of the dynamic range of inducible gene expression systems by flow 
cytometry 
An additional part of the characterization of the inducible gene expression systems was the 
measurement of the dynamic range. In the time-resolved gene expression experiments, the 
background noise signal of the plate reader measurements was too high to accurately 
determine the leakiness of the inducible promoter. Therefore, the measurement of the 
dynamic range was impacted to be less precise. Thus, the dynamic range was investigated by 
employing flow cytometry at a single timepoint. The dynamic range was determined as the 
ratio between the fluorescence level of the highest and lowest inducer concentration.  
First, the cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system treated with a gradient of inducer 
concentration was investigated in combination with a second constitutively controlled 
expression cassette (MW ΔgalK CG JR and MW ΔgalK JG CR). The median fluorescence 
level of lowest (min) and highest (max) inducer concentrations in the gradient was displayed 
to depict the dynamic range. As expected, the non-inducible control strains (MW ΔgalK and 
MW ΔgalK JG-JR) did not depict major changes in median fluorescence level for the min and 
max inducer concentrations (figure 16B). A similar observation was made for the 
fluorescence expression driven by the constitutive promoters in MW ΔgalK CG-JR and MW 
ΔgalK JG-CR, and moreover for all strains in the presence of salicylate, the non-matched 
inducer (figure 16B). The dynamic range of PCymRC in MW ΔgalK CG JR and MW ΔgalK JG 
CR treated with cuminic acid was observed to be 112 and 65 (figure 16B, C).

The possibility of expanding the SEGA landing pad collection 
In this thesis the gene expression cassettes were derived from a SEGA landing pad, and thus 
all gene expression cassettes were compatible with the SEGA architecture. This compatibility 
enables the expression cassettes to be converted into a SEGA landing pad. The modification 
required to convert a gene expression cassette to a SEGA landing pad is the integration of a 
counter selection marker downstream of the reporter gene to act as a downstream gadget 
(figure 20A). Since a subset of gene expression cassettes are in the locus D, the SEGA 
landing pad collection can now be expanded to be in two separate loci either as a single 
landing pad system or as a dual landing pad system (figure 20B). 
The construction of the SEGA landing pad system in locus D was initiated and preliminary 
results were achieved. Two SEGA landing pads were constructed in locus D from a gene 
expression cassette both had a counter-selection system developed by Bubnov et al., (2022)48 
based on the repression from the cI regulator on chloramphenicol resistance and the hok 
toxin48. Additionally, this new landing pad was constructed in two variants, specifically 
utilizing two variants of the BCD as the middle gadget. One of the SEGA landing pads above 
was integrated into an E. coli MG1655 with the galK background deletion (also constructed 
in the scope of this thesis) with the intent of integrating galK as an alternative counter-
selection marker in the downstream gadget position (figure 20A, see table 1 for details). Due 
to time limitations the construction double SEGA landing pad strain was not finalized, and 
the system could not be fully characterized in the frame of this thesis.

Discussion 
In this thesis three dual inducible gene expression systems (seven strains) were constructed 
and characterized to investigate the behavior of two inducible promoters when integrated on 
the genome in separate gene expression cassettes. The characterization focused on the 
dynamics of the inducible promoters PCymRC and PSalTTC. Moreover, the dynamics of the 
inducible promoters mainly revolved around the effective promoter sensitivity i.e. the 
apparent KD value. The experiments of the characterization entailed measuring fluorescence 
signal in the exponential phase, as a time series, and lastly using flowcytometry to 
specifically obtain the dynamic range. 
Comparing the apparent KD between and within dual gene expression systems 
The apparent KD of the effective promoter sensitivity is an important parameter that 
characterizes the response of an inducible gene expression system. Moreover, the apparent 
KD is a constant which describes the system and therefore the value is expected to be constant 
over time56. A constant value of the apparent KD was observed for the majority of the 
investigated samples (figure 14, 15) with the exception of one replicate of MW ΔgalK CG JR. 
However, this exception was largely due to growth irregularities impacting the dose-response 
curve (figure 19). 
When comparing the apparent KD values of the dual inducible gene expression systems, the 
apparent KD, determined in exponential phase, was utilized because minor differences were 
observed in regard to time. The apparent KD was determined for PCymRC as part of the cuminic 
acid- (MW ΔgalK CG JR and MW ΔgalK JG CR) and dual-inducible gene expression system 
(MW ΔgalK CG SR and MW ΔgalK SG CR). A similar characterization was performed for 
PSalTTC which was a part of the salicylate- (MW ΔgalK SG JR and MW ΔgalK JG SR) and 
dual-inducible gene expression system. Within each inducible gene expression system, the 
apparent KD for the inducible promoter located in loci S or D did not significantly differ from 
each other (figure 8D, 9D, 10D). Thus, indicating a robustness of the inducible promoter 
dynamics across chromosomal location. However, a difference of apparent KD for PCymRC was 
observed between the cuminic acid- and dual-inducible gene expression system. The dual-
inducible gene expression system had a lower apparent KD than the apparent KD determined 
for the cuminic acid- inducible gene expression system which was not expected since the 
apparent KD for PCymRC was expected to be a constant35,56. A possible explanation for this 
difference could be cellular burden22. The main genetic difference between the cuminic acid-
==PAGE:55==
Page 48 
inducible gene expression system and the dual-inducible gene expression system was the 
promoters. The cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system contained an inducible and a 
constitutive promoter whereas the dual-inducible gene expression system carried two 
different inducible promoters. In the cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system the 
burden from constitutive expressing a reporter gene could yield lower fluorescence levels of 
the inducible promoters thus shifting the apparent KD to be higher.  
However, cellular burden alone was likely not alone in constituting the observed shift of 
apparent KD. Characteristics of a sigmodal response curve for inducible systems with 
inducible repressors, like CymRAM, have been reported to respond to the concentration of 
repressor57. Specifically, the ratio of repressor copy number to promoter binding sites can 
impact the response curve. A small change in repressor copy number shifts the response curve 
but a large change in promoter binding sites is required to impact the characteristics of the 
response curve58. Therefore, when high concentrations of repressor are present the promoter 
characteristics are the same regardless of the amount of promoter binding sites, but at low 
repressor concentrations the number of available promoter binding sites is important for the 
inducibility of the promoter57,58. This theory suggests that the observed difference of apparent 
KD for PCymRC could be due to different concentration of the CymRAM repressor in the 
cuminic acid- and dual-inducible gene expression system. This is unlikely considering that 
the CymRAM repressor is being expressed from the sensor array of the Marionette-Wild strain 
which should be constant across strains. Additionally, the sensor array of Marionette-Wild 
was constitutively expressing the regulators to a high level35 in all gene expression systems.  
The high abundance of the regulators can in part explain the observed robustness for the two 
loci, because the potential different copy numbers, emerging from chromosomal DNA 
replication, of locus S and locus D is being overruled27,28,58. This further emphasize the 
importance of utilizing the sensor array instead of integrating the regulator as part of the 
expression cassette since the regulator abundance is likely bound to change depending on the 
genomic loci26,29,32,59. Bear in mind that the dynamics of the promoter in the context of the 
regulator abundance was described for inducible repressors like PCymRC57,58 but the behavior 
of inducible activators like NahRAM for PSalTTC might be different.

Comparison of the apparent KD for the Marionette collection and the dual gene 
expression systems 
The constructed dual gene expression system differed from the original marionette system in 
genetic context and the characterization process of the inducible promoters. Therefore, 
comparing the apparent KD values reported in Meyer et al., (2019)35 and the dual gene 
expression systems could indicate the significance of the genetic makeup for each system. 
The main differences between the original marionette system and the dual gene expression 
systems are that the promoter and reporter gene are carried by a plasmid in the original 
marionette system35 whereas the gene expression systems developed here carry the promoter 
and reporter gene on the genome of E. coli. Moreover, the dual gene expression system 
consisted of two gene expression cassettes compared to the original marionette system which 
only characterized one promoter at a time35. Similarly, both systems utilized the Marionette-
Wild strain to ensure the presence of the regulator for the specific promoter. Specifically, the 
dual gene expression system was designed with a small subset of the marionette promoters 
(PCymRC and PSalTTC). 
The cuminic acid-inducible gene expression system depicts an apparent KD for PCymRC of 
approximately 25 µM (figure 8D, 14A) and the salicylate-inducible gene expression system 
the apparent KD of PSalTTC is approximately 10 µM (figure 9D, 14A). Furthermore, the dual-
inducible gene expression system has apparent KD values of PCymRC and PSalTTC of 
approximately 15 µM and 10 µM, respectively (figure 10D, 15). In comparison, the apparent 
KD for PCymRC and PSalTTC was reported to be 22 µM and 29 µM in Meyer et al., (2019)35. 
Thus, the main takeaways from the apparent KD estimations are that PSalTTC and partially 
PCymRC showed a lower value compared to previously reports35. This difference in apparent 
KD values for PSalTTC and PCymRC indicates the dynamics of a promoter can change depending 
on the genetic context. As discussed above, the concentration of repressor and amount of 
promoter binding site could play a key role for the observed difference in apparent 
KD57,58.The concentration of repressor and amount of promoter binding site were especially 
important because the repressor concentration was optimized for the plasmid version of the 
inducible promoter35. 
 
 
==PAGE:57==
Page 50 
However, the original marionette system and dual gene expression systems were not 
characterized in the same experiment nor with the same methods. These comparisons to the 
original marionette system are therefore motivation to conduct an experiment in which the 
original marionette system and dual gene expression systems are characterized with the same 
method. In this future experiment, the apparent KD of the original marionette system and dual 
gene expression systems could be compared more directly. 
The impact of cross activity of inducer and regulators 
Even though the marionette promoter and the corresponding regulator were optimized for 
minimal cross activity, it has been reported that salicylate inhibits induction from 
PCymRC35.This inhibition of PCymRC by salicylate was also observed in the inducer gradient 
matrix experiment (figure 11, 12). The type of inhibition affects the response curve 
depending on the inhibition type. The two main types of inhibition are competitive inhibition 
and allosteric inhibition. Competitive inhibition is defined by the inhibitor having the ability 
to bind in the binding pocket for induction without inducing the regulator and blocking the 
inducer. Allosteric inhibition is defined by the inhibitor binding to the regulator outside the 
binding pocket for induction which impacts the conformation of the regulator thus preventing 
induction. A response curve with competitive inhibition will be shifted to a higher 
concentration of inducer to obtain the same response curve i.e. the value of the apparent KD 
goes up, but the maximum promoter activity is achieved at a higher concentration of inducer. 
Allosteric inhibition impacts the response curve by lowering the overall induction60. 
The apparent KD of PCymRC could not be robustly estimated for the inducer gradient matrix 
experiments because the inhibition of salicylate shifted the dose response curve. Therefore, 
the tested concentration range was not adequate to obtain full induction for PCymRC which 
indicated the inhibition of salicylate was competitive inhibition. This was further supported 
by cuminic acid and salicylate having similar molecular structure. They are both benzoic acid 
derivatives which suggests that both compounds may have the ability to bind to the same 
position on the CymRAM regulator35. This type of inhibition would be competitive inhibition 
because salicylate could bind to CymRAM, prohibiting binding of cuminic acid, without 
inducing the conformational changed needed to release the binding to PCymRC. Conversely, 
inhibition of PSalTTC by cuminic acid was not observed (figure 11, 12). It could be speculated 
that the isopropyl moiety of cuminic acid limits the access to the binding pocket of NahRAM, 
since salicylate is without an isopropyl moiety35. The underlying mechanism could be 
investigated by structural analysis of docking the inducers to the regulators in future studies.

Implications for using flow cytometry to determine dynamic range 
The time-resolved gene expression experiments encountered the challenge of not being able 
to distinguish between the background signal and the signal for low inducer concentrations. 
This behavior drastically impacts the calculation of the dynamic range of the inducible 
promoters because the measured background fluorescence was too high. A high background 
fluorescence will lead to too high estimations for the leakiness and too low estimations of the 
dynamic range compared to, if the background fluorescence was low. The opportunities for 
solving the high background fluorescence would be to change the growth medium to a 
defined minimal medium 35. Furthermore, there is no background signal in flow cytometry 
experiments since the fluorescence signal is directly measured of the single cells.  
The dynamic ranges calculated from the flow cytometry data for the dual gene expression 
systems ranged from 60-110-fold and 25-50-fold for PCymRC (figure 16, 18) and PSalTTC (figure 
17, 18) respectively. These dynamic ranges were relatively low compared to the plasmid 
system (1000> fold)35 which could have been caused by various reasons. The main reason is 
the maximum fluorescence of the dual gene expression systems was lower compared to the 
original marionette system. This is a typical difference observed between a plasmid and 
genome system, i.e., that the plasmid system generally has higher maximum levels of 
expression61,62. 
Growth impacts on the fluorescence 
In the growth experiments, a specific growth phenotype was observed in that a strain grew to 
a higher final cell density compared to the strain from the same pre-culture in different 
inducer concentration (figure 19). This phenotype was not consistent across replicates, 
strains, or inducer concentrations. Therefore, no systematic correlation was found that would 
indicate an inheritable cause of the variation of this phenotype. These observed differences in 
growth are a challenge for the data analysis because, generally higher density cell cultures 
have higher levels of the raw fluorescence regardless of inducer concentration. The main 
challenge was that the sigmodal dose-response curve was shifted. For example, if the 
intermediary inducer concentrations were outliers and exhibited higher fluorescence level 
than expected. Then, the subsequent fitting of the sigmodal curve was impacted by the curve 
shifted to have a lower apparent KD and a steeper slope i.e. a higher hill coefficient (figure 
19C). 
 
 
==PAGE:59==
Page 52 
A way to mitigate the differing fluorescence levels from the growth is to normalize the 
fluorescence with growth i.e. OD600. Unfortunately, in the gene expression experiments, the 
fluorescence background signal from the non-cell media control was similar to the uninduced 
strain. Therefore, the normalization of the fluorescence could potentially lead to negative 
values for the conditions with uninduced strains and low concentration. Fluorescence 
normalization should be able to account for higher measured OD600, but the tradeoff of 
negative fluorescence impacted the data analysis by utilizing raw fluorescence and expecting 
higher variance. If the experiments were to be performed again, then the media would be 
exchanged from a rich LB media to a defined minimal media like M9.  
The future perspectives of a dual landing pad system 
The dual gene expression systems constructed and characterized in this thesis have the ability 
to expand the SEGA collection. This expansion would consist of a SEGA landing pad located 
in locus rpnD-yjiR and another SEGA landing pad located in glmS-pstS. Thus, establishing a 
dual landing pad system akin in structure to the dual gene expression system developed in 
this thesis. 
The dual landing pad system could be utilized to precisely control the expression of two 
genes simultaneously. As an example, an enzyme and the maturation factor of that enzyme 
could be integrated and expressed from the dual landing pad system. Specifically, an 
imaginary case study could be of the enzyme proteinase K. When proteinase K is produced 
intercellularly in E. coli the protein lacks its disulfide bonds. E. coli does not naturally 
possess the ability to create the lacking disulfide bonds in proteinase K intercellularly63. In 
Gąciarz et al., (2017)64 the system CyDisCo was established to provide E. coli with the ability 
to produce disulfide bonds for proteins intercellularly 64–67. In the context of the dual landing 
pad, one landing pad could express proteinase K whilst the other landing pad harbors the 
CyDisCo system. The CyDisCo system reported higher production yield if the expression 
level of CyDisCo was optimized for the specific target68. The dual landing pad system could 
also implement simple gene circuits due to the two inducible promoters enabling regulation 
of two separate genes. These applications were just a few possible examples, but a dual 
landing pad system can be utilized in many creative ways.

Conclusion 
In conclusion seven strains of dual gene expression systems were constructed and 
characterized in this thesis. The genomically integrated PCymRC and PSalTTC promoters were 
functional but with slightly different dynamics compared to the original plasmid-based 
system35. Specifically, the effective promoter sensitivity was higher, but the dynamic range 
was lower of the dual gene expression systems. Moreover, induction of PCymRC and PSalTTC 
was largely unaffected by an additional gene expression cassette. However, induction of 
PCymRC was inhibited by salicylate which likely was competitive inhibition as previously 
reported35. Overall, the dual gene expression systems highlighted some characteristics of well 
described promoters in a more complex system than previously tested.
