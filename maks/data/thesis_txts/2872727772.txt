Abstract
This report documents the design, development, and evaluation of a 
modular workbench system intended to advance laboratory automation 
in the life sciences sector. The project responds to the barriers faced by 
smaller research institutions in accessing advanced automation technol-
ogy, which often results in inefficiencies and fragmented workflows. 
Through collaboration with DTU’s Arena for Life Science Automation 
(DALSA) and various stakeholders, the project aims to deliver an 
open-source, interoperable workbench platform that supports diverse 
laboratory equipment, ensures dimensional accuracy for robotic integra-
tion, and enables scalable, user-friendly automation. The report details 
the use of adapted design methodologies, iterative prototyping, and 
rigorous testing to address core challenges in modularity, alignment, and 
mechanical precision. The resulting workbench prototype lays the 
groundwork for more accessible, standardized, and efficient laboratory 
environments.
Testing of the prototype demonstrated robust performance under opera-
tional loads and vibrations, but revealed a total connection and flatness 
deviation of approximately 1.03 mm—exceeding the refined target of 
0.51 mm, though close to the initial 1 mm requirement. The workbench 
system maintained structural integrity and usability, but further improve-
ments to connecter precision and flatness are recommended to fully 
meet the most stringent automation standards.

The Life Science industry has huge innovation barriers due to limited 
access to advanced laboratory automation technology. While large 
pharmaceutical firms will typically dominate proprietary systems, 
small research institutes and public labs typically rely on cost-saving 
shortcuts, such as second-hand equipment or makeshift workstation 
adjustments, in an attempt to connect incompatible apparatuses. 
This mismatch creates systemic inefficiencies, particularly since 
automation is now a prerequisite to stay competitive in modern 
bio-laboratories.
DTU's Arena for Life Science Automation (DALSA) addresses this 
need by developing open-source, modular solutions that open auto-
mated workflows to all. The project supports DALSA's purpose 
through the establishment of a universal, interoperable workbench 
system. The workbench will serve as a platform of first resort for 
laboratories adopting automation. The workbench will be addressing:
Accessibility: 
Support for a variety of equipment, including legacy and third-party 
devices
Scalability: 
Modular architecture to accommodate evolving laboratory needs
Accuracy: 
Dimensional stability and alignment tolerances to robot integration
Through collaboration between multidisciplinary players like lab 
technicians, robotics engineers, and metrologists, the design blends 
user-centric functionality and industrial-grade robustness. Validation 
processes will test mechanical performance under operating loads, 
ensuring that the system is able to withstand the demanding require-
ments of automated biological assays such as PCR and ELISA.
Introduction
In addressing such issues, this project seeks to bridge the difference 
between resource-scarce laboratories and cutting-edge automation, 
introducing a practical and affordable solution to the wider research 
community. This universal workbench developed through the collab-
oration is as much a technological innovation as an impetus toward 
more accessible, productive research environments. Throughout the 
remainder of the chapters, the report will detail the design process, 
stakeholder input, technical validation, and performance testing that 
are the foundation of this modular workbench system's creation. This 
foundation is meant to enable future laboratory automation develop-
ments and enhance accessibility across the life sciences sector.
Methodology
Figure 1: Double diamond Model
The Double Diamond Model is a design process model created by 
the UK Design Council to facilitate problem-solving and innovation. It 
is made up of four phases organized into two "diamonds" that sym-
bolize divergent and convergent thinking. This model is widely used 
across many design fields and is taught as a key method during the 
Design & Innovation education at DTU.
03
==PAGE:9==
The original model is divided into two main diamonds – one of which 
is focused on finding the problem of the project and the other 
focused on developing a solution. 
This model has a very broad use case but is also limited to work best 
with design projects that start at “square one”. The model supports a 
broad and detailed discovery phase that puts the designer in the 
headspace of the industry or design space that the project is based 
on. 
The two main phases from the original double diamond model are 
retained in this modified version. 
During this project the discovery phase mainly consists of project 
briefings and alignments of requirements, criteria and expectations 
from relevant stakeholders. 
These inputs were then gathered and analyzed to give the design 
team insight into the workspace, the use case and the possible users 
of the product. 
The next phase is the define phase. This phase consists of the inves-
tigation and definition of the main problem or problems that the 
design team must solve. This phase differs from the discovery 
phase, since this one has a focus on the stakeholder pain points, 
whereas the discovery phase only focuses on understanding the 
workspace. This phase culminates in the definition of a problem 
statement. In the case of this project the different sub-functions that 
the workbench should have will be defined during this phase.
The Development phase is very similar to the original double 
diamond. During this phase solutions are developed and evaluated 
and refined. The solutions are evaluated on their merits within the 
requirements and criteria. These relatively arbitrary choices are made 
to sort out a few solutions to each sub-function.
The fourth and final phase is the Detailing phase. During this phase 
the few solutions to the sub-functions are tested through an array of 
different tests, to gain concrete evidence on their functional abilities 
and to determine which solution is the best. After these tests, the 
solution can gain the final tweaks to perfect the product. Whereas 
afterwards it will be presented as a finished functional alpha proto-
type.
This model can be called the quadruple diamond. A slightly more 
detailed version can be seen on page 1.
Adapted Double Diamond Model – 
The Quadruple Diamond
With some projects the double diamond could be improved to sup-
port the specific frame, herein the resources available as well as the 
start- and end points of the project. 
With this project, some of the discovery and definition phases had 
already been explored by the project proposer. With this being said, it 
was important to the design team not to jump to any conclusion 
regarding the proposed discoveries and definitions proposed. 
With that in mind, the design team decided to create a new modified 
diamond model. The new model consists of four diamonds.

The two primary work models, the Diamond Model and Systematic 
Design of Industrial Products, provide distinct yet complementary 
frameworks for the project.
The Diamond Model emphasizes problem identification and the 
integration of external design factors, guiding the overall direction of 
the project. In this case, the Modified Diamond Model is used to 
further support the testing phase of the design process.
Meanwhile, Systematic Design of Industrial Products offers a struc-
tured framework for product design and detailing. It facilitates a 
systematic approach to selecting design aspects and product 
features, ensuring a clear and methodical development process.
Together, these models enhance the design process by balancing a 
holistic project perspective (Diamond Model) with a focused 
approach to product design and detailing (Systematic Design of 
Industrial Products).
The Systematic Design of 
Industrial Products Model
The second work frame that has been used throughout the project 
Eskild Tjalves “Systematic Design of Industrial Products” [1].
Figure 3:  The Systematic Design of Industrial Products model
Eskild Tjalve's Systematic Design of Industrial Products offers a 
systematic approach to product development, especially on func-
tion-oriented design. His main work model comprises a step-by-step 
approach ensuring a reliable process from problem definition to the 
final design. His model is structured as follows:
Problem analysis – Understanding the problem, specifications, and 
limitations prior to starting the designing process.
Function Analysis (Main and sub-functions) – Breaking down the 
product into basic functions and sub-functions to ensure clarity in 
requirements and solutions.
Structural Design – Developing and evaluating alternative conceptual 
design solutions based on the function analysis.
Quantified Design – Arranging and outlining the selected concept to 
ensure feasibility, manufacturability, and performance.
Detail Design – Filling out dimensions, materials, components, and 
production preparation.
Model Synergies and Project Direction
Tjalve’s approach is highly systematic and function-driven, aiming 
to enhance efficiency and innovation in industrial product develop-
ment.

Project Scoping
The scope of this project has been deliberately defined to ensure a 
focused and manageable development process. Specifically, the 
project centres on the design, realization, and evaluation of two 
modular workbenches and the connection mechanism between 
them. This targeted approach allows for a thorough investigation of 
the fundamental challenges associated with modularity, alignment, 
and mechanical precision at the interface of two connected tables.
It is important to note that the broader system, comprising multiple 
interconnected workbenches forming large, continuous laboratory 
workspace, remains outside the scope of this work. While the 
long-term vision for laboratory automation includes scalable integra-
tion of many modules, this project does not address the complexities 
that arise when connecting more than two tables, such as cumula-
tive tolerance stack-ups, system-wide stability, or large-scale cable 
routing.
Additionally, several auxiliary design features have been intentionally 
excluded from the current scope. Elements such as integrated cable 
management solutions, detailed provisions for robot arm mounting 
and integration, and other secondary functionalities are not 
addressed in this phase. These features, while important for a fully 
realized modular laboratory system, will require separate analysis 
and development in future work.
By narrowing the focus to two tables and their direct connection, the 
project can systematically address the core mechanical and func-
tional requirements, generate robust test data, and establish a solid 
foundation for subsequent expansion and refinement of the modular 
workbench concept.
Project Goals
1. Understanding Modular Design Principles: Learn the core princi-
ples of modular construction and design for adaptability in industrial 
systems. 
2. User-Centered Design in Automation: Develop skills in designing 
systems that prioritize user experience, focusing on usability, acces-
sibility, and ease of operation.
3. Industry-Specific Research and Analysis: Conduct comprehensive 
research on existing docking systems and identify opportunities for 
innovation. 
4. Prototyping and Testing: Expand and develop skills in creating 
conceptual and physical prototypes, including the development of 
two table designs with modular docking systems. 
5. Interdisciplinary Integration: Get a general understanding on how 
to combine hardware and software solutions into a unified system, 
ensuring seamless interaction and communication between compo-
nents. 
6. Standardization and Flexibility: Learn how to design standardized 
frames that allow for flexibility in integrating with diverse systems 
and future requirements. 
7. Documentation and Training Material Development: Develop skills 
in creating clear and effective training materials, assembly guides, 
and user documentation. 
8. Collaboration and Stakeholder Engagement: Enhance teamwork 
and communication skills, particularly in cross-disciplinary teams 
and when collaborating with different suppliers.

Current Workflow in Biolabs
The specific issue presented in this report is to create a modular 
table/workbench, to support automated test setups in the laboratory. 
Currently, when a test is being conducted in the laboratory, there are 
many tedious or repeditive tasks performed by a lab technician (see 
figure 6 on the next page). 
During the test phase, a lab technician is needed to move the sam-
ples back and forth between the apparatuses needed for a given test 
setup. These test setups lead to much downtime, where lab techni-
cians are waiting for the lab equipment to finish its cycles. Addition-
ally, the human factor of manual handling of lab samples can lead to 
accidental contamination or other accidents. 
DALSA has expressed a desire for a system that can automate the 
entire test cycle. This means that the test setup can be pre-pro-
grammed, and all human actions needed during the test cycle are 
handled by a robot.
Many different apparatuses could potentially be needed for a test 
setup, and the current DALSA lab-setup is not streamlined to support 
a modular setup to connect a robot lab assistant (robot arm) to the 
required aperture. The current tables in the laboratory are not stream-
lined in dimensions and are not connectable to each other. 
Some collaboration has been made across the design parameters of 
the two projects, but this thesis will not go into depth of the design 
methods or choices made in the bachelor project [2].

Pain Points
To get a better understanding of the specific pain point associated 
with setting up or working at a biolab, some field studying was con-
ducted in collaboration with Aske Unger. The goal of the lab tour was 
to find out what issues and difficulties the equipment, environment 
and workflow arise while working at a DTU biolab. This overview 
would give a general understanding of how the average non-industry 
biolab is run.
One issue stood out immediately: much of the lab equipment had to 
be semi-permanently fixed in place, as seen in Pain Drawing 1. 
Because many devices aren’t designed to work together, technicians 
often end up bolting equipment down or drilling into work surfaces 
just to make things fit. While this creates a solid, reliable setup for a 
particular experiment, it comes at the cost of flexibility. Any time new 
equipment is introduced, or the test configuration changes, rework-
ing the setup becomes a major, labour-intensive task. Sometimes, 
there simply isn’t a way to make everything fit, and new work surfac-
es must be sourced before work can continue.

Stakeholder Input
It is paramount that the relevant stakeholders of the project have 
their input incorporated into the requirements and the design of 
the workbenches. A series of interviews were conducted to 
gather these inputs.
Design Feedback from Aske Unger 
at DALSA
Aske Unger, the project proposer, initially outlined a rough frame-
work for the final product, specifying key features required in the 
design. Most of the initial design specifications were developed 
in collaboration with Aske and the design team at the project's 
outset.
As development progressed, additional specifications were 
introduced during feedback sessions. One key requirement was 
to address potential drift or imprecision in the robot’s calibration, 
ensuring that laboratories worldwide could integrate the work-
bench seamlessly without requiring expert intervention to 
calibrate or adjust their robotic arm.
Another important specification was to maintain a consistently 
level tabletop, preventing any uneven flooring in the laboratory 
from affecting test setups.
Robot Arm Specifications from 
Sebastian Storz
Firstly, the tabletop should be designed to accommodate a “reposi-
tioning area” for the robot arm. This area is needed for the robot arm 
to drop and “re-grab” any objects that might need a certain orienta-
tion when handled. Furthermore, the area should be no smaller than 
the dimensions of the robot arms “grabber”. In the case of the 4 DOF 
SCARA used as a reference in this project, the area needed for re-ad-
justing the grip should be no smaller than 100x100mm.
Sebastian Storz is a robotics engineering student at DTU. He

Laws and Regulations
The construction of a new workbench for biolabs must meet 
regulatory standards for safety and functionality. It must also 
comply with environmental and operational guidelines. Accord-
ing to the FDA's ORA Laboratory Manual, laboratory renovations 
must not disrupt environmental controls and must provide 
sufficient space and engineering controls for handling, analysis, 
and calibration of samples. Workbenches should be constructed 
from materials that are easily disinfected, chemically resistant, 
and in proximity to essential utilities such as sinks, gas, and 
electricity points. [4] [5]
Microbiology labs require workbenches that are designed to 
minimize microbial contamination by the removal of cracks and 
fiber collection points. They should have routine air and surface 
monitoring for the presence of microbiological contamination 
with mandatory disinfection procedures. The EMA's Good Manu-
facturing Practice guidelines indicate that surfaces in contact 
with raw materials must not influence product quality. [6]
In addition, European Standard EN 13150:2020 demands that 
workbenches minimize injury risks, avoid sharp edges, and 
ensure appropriate safety distances between moving parts. 
Materials must be mechanically, chemically, and thermally 
resistant to exposure. Work surfaces designed to hold liquids 
shall have retaining edges with a minimum capacity of five liters 
per square meter. [7]
Compliance with these standards will ensure that the new work-
bench design will be able to house safe, efficient, and contami-
nation-free laboratory practice. Specific regulations can be seen 
in worksheet 6.

Finding the Allowed 
Tolerance
The use case for the workbench is for an automated test setup, 
using a robotic arm as the main moving part. This workbench 
must be designed to accommodate a broad range of robot arm 
types and should therefore be designed to work with the sim-
plest forms of robotic arm. The simplest robotic arms do not 
have an array of positional sensors and rely mainly on a 
programmed movement pattern. For the designed workbench to 
be as universal as possible, the table should therefore be 
designed, so that the use case of “blind” robot arms won’t fail 
because of a misalignment in the workbench connection.
To specify this requirement a series of measurements were 
made on existing lab equipment, see picture 8, to find out what 
their allowable tolerances are when moving a well plate between 
apparatuses. 
The measurements were made by measuring a well plates 
dimensions and comparing them to the plug in slots of the 
plates on the different apparatuses. 
Figure 9 shows an illustration of the standard dimensions of a 
well plate. The only important dimension of this illustration is the 
height of 84,48mm and the width of 127,76mm. Many of the 
apparatuses in the DALSA laboratory were mainly limited by the 
height tolerance of the well plate. This means that this tolerance 
is consistently the smallest across the bio-labware. Because of 
this, only the smallest tolerance is needed for selecting the 
minimum allowed tolerance of the tolerance stack up of the 
workbench’s dimensions. The height of the test plate from the 
DALSA laboratory was measured to be 85,35mm, a bit smaller 
than the production standard (-0,13mm). 
The following height dimensions were measured on the follow-
ing labware in the DALSA lab:

Given these measurements the smallest number is the limiting 
factor and should be regarded as the upper limit of tolerance. In 
the case of the DALSA laboratory, the lowest limit of tolerance 
was 85.9mm minus the measurement of the well plate 
85,48mm. This gives an allowed tolerance of 0,51mm (or half a 
millimeter). It is important to note that this allowance is only 
based on the limited number of apparatuses measured in the 
DALSA laboratory and only focuses on the dimensions of a well 
plate. To get a better understanding of the actual needed 
allowed tolerance, much wider examination should be conduct-
ed on a much wider variety of apparatuses using well plates. 
Additionally, it would also be relevant to examine what other 
items might be moved by the robotic arm.
For all intents and purposes the measurements made during this 
project seem to give a sufficient overview of what tolerances 
can be expected in a lab like the DALSA lab.
Tolerance Stack-Up
Tolerance stack-up refers to the cumulative effect of individual 
part tolerances within an assembly, which can lead to significant 
dimensional variation in the final product. In the context of the 
modular workbench project, evaluating tolerance stack-ups is 
essential to ensure reliable and precise connections between 
tables. This assessment is particularly relevant at the interfaces 
where modules are joined, as even small deviations in manufac-
turing tolerances can accumulate, resulting in misalignment or 
gaps. Additionally, tolerance stack-up must be considered when 
analysing the deformation of the top plate during fastening or 
under load, as these factors can further exacerbate alignment 
issues. 
Careful attention to tolerance stack-up during both the design 
and assembly phases ensures that the top plates remain level 
and properly aligned when fastened, maintaining the overall 
accuracy and functionality required for automated laboratory 
workflows. 
In the case of this project, the tolerance stack ups of the connec-
tion alignment, the top plate alignment and fastening deforma-
tion, as well as the top plate deformation under maximum load 
should not exceed 0.51mm.

Development
The development phase was split into two parallel phases – The 
product structuring (theoretical) and the prototyping (practical). 
Incidentally the Systematic Design of Industrial Products model 
primarily focuses on getting the detailing of the product defined 
before the manufacturing of the prototypes begin. This way of 
following the model works very well in saving resources during 
development, but it also limits the development rate of the 
concept. Getting a feel for the product is very important when 
developing a concept. When rapid prototyping is available the 
prototyping phase can benefit from being conducted while 
detailing the product using the Systematic Design of Industrial 
Products model. Because of this the sections have been made 
to explore two different aspects of the design. The Product 
structuring will be exploring the different permutations of tech-
nologies that can be used to fulfill the different specifications, 
while the prototyping will be used to explore dimensions, limita-
tions, possibilities as well and give a general feel of the concept.
The following sections are divided into the product structuring 
and the prototyping, but it is important to note that these two 
phases happened in parallel.
Forbidden surfaces refer to specific areas on a product where 
the placement of material, features, or components is not 
permitted. Such restrictions are typically imposed due to struc-
tural, functional, or operational considerations. For instance, a 
forbidden surface may be designated to prevent interference 
with moving parts, preserve access for maintenance, or main-
tain necessary safety clearances. Within Tjalve’s methodology, 
the early identification of forbidden surfaces is crucial, as it 
helps avoid conflicts between components and preserves the 
integrity of critical functions. These zones are usually document-
ed and visualized during the design process, providing clear 
boundaries for subsequent detailing and embodiment stages.
In contrast, functional areas are those areas intentionally 
reserved for active functional use or interaction. Examples 
include mounting points, user interfaces, or surfaces that inter-
act with other components or the environment. Usable surfaces 
are defined to fulfill particular roles, whether structural, such as 
supporting loads, or operational, such as enabling assembly or 
manipulation. According to Tjalve, the careful identification and 
definition of usable surfaces are essential for achieving reliable 
and efficient product performance.
Systematically distinguishing between forbidden and functional 
areas allows the designer to clearly define the available design 
space and minimize the risk of costly revisions during later 
stages. This approach is especially valuable in modular and 
automated systems, such as the modular workbench described 
in this project, where precise allocation of usable and forbidden 
zones supports compatibility and seamless integration with 
laboratory equipment.
Much of the design of the workbench features are dependent on 
the form design of the frame. The frames design will define what 
the functional areas of the workbench will be, as well as the 
forbidden areas. 
Product Structuring
In Eskild Tjalve’s Systematic Design of Industrial Products [1], 
the distinction between forbidden surfaces and functional areas 
are central to a function-oriented approach in product develop-
ment. These classifications serve to organize the design space, 
ensuring that both functionality and manufacturability are 
addressed methodically throughout the process.

Conceptualization Framework
The project facilitates the development of a 
“minimal viable product”. This means that the 
primary development of the alpha product will 
be developed with the intention of creating a 
product that fulfills the project requirements, 
while not overcomplicating the solution. This 
type of solution could also be labeled as a 
“step” solution. 
When working with concept development it is 
important to keep to developmental and 
innovative mindset, without limiting oneself by 
all the project limitations. It is often the case 
with initial prototyping that the resources 
available only allow for low or medium fidelity 
prototypes (prototypes or concepts with a 
less advanced conceptual design). To combat 
this pitfall the “step” “stretch” and “leap” 
model can be used to reframe some of the 
designs without limitations, while keeping the 
primary concept development scoped within 
reason. 
By keeping some of the development on a 
conceptual level, the concept can still evolve, 
and valuable insight might be gained and 
turned into key aspects of the design, that can 
influence the material development of the 
prototyping. Furthermore, the conceptual 
design can create a good starting point for 
eventual further development, or expansion of 
the final product developed during this proj-
ect.
Step
Incremental improvement
Stretch
Moderate Innovation
Leap
Radical Innovation
The Step phase is characterized by small, 
low-impact improvements that refine an 
existing design or process but do not 
change its basic character. These 
improvements are likely to improve 
efficiency, usability, performance, or 
beauty based on established technologies 
and methods.
1. Low risk, high feasibility – Changes are 
straightforward to implement.
2. Quick iterations – Small modifications can be 
tested and deployed easily.
3. Minimal impact on existing workflows – The core 
product or system remains the same.
4. Improves reliability and usability – Reduces flaws, 
enhances user experience, and increases efficiency.
The Stretch phase brings significant to 
moderate alterations that challenge the 
existing state but within known technologi-
cal and conceptual boundaries. Stretch 
involves rethinking form, material, features, 
or user interaction, pushing designs 
beyond incremental tweaking.
1. Medium risk, medium feasibility – Requires more 
resources and testing.
2. Larger impact on usability and function – 
Improves efficiency, adaptability, or overall 
effectiveness.
3. Often introduces new materials, processes, or 
interactions – But still within a feasible scope.
4. Bridges the gap between conventional solutions 
and revolutionary idea
The Leap phase represents a paradigm 
shift, introducing altogether new technolo-
gies, ideas, or product categories. This 
phase calls for going out of current 
industry norms, assuming disruptive 
technology, new science discovery, or 
novel patterns of thinking.
1. High risk, high impact – Often speculative and 
resource-intensive but can lead to groundbreaking 
results.
2. Requires significant R&D and validation – May 
involve new scientific principles or experimental 
technologies.
3. Can redefine industry standards – Instead of 
improving an existing solution, it introduces a new 
way of solving problems.
4. Often necessitates new partnerships and funding 
– Due to the need for pioneering technology.

Morphological Ideation
Morphological analysis offers a disciplined and systematic 
approach to concept development, particularly when navigating 
complex design challenges. By decomposing the overall prob-
lem into its essential functions or parameters and identifying 
several possible solutions for each, this method enables a 
broad and structured exploration of the design space. The 
resulting morphological chart serves as a visual tool, allowing 
for the combination and comparison of different solution 
elements to generate a variety of innovative concepts.
Employing morphology in the early stages of development not 
only fosters creativity but also ensures that alternative solu-
tions are carefully considered, reducing the risk of overlooking 
viable options. 
This approach is well-suited for collaborative environments, as 
it provides a common framework for discussing and evaluating 
ideas. Within the context of this project, morphological analysis 
was used to create three distinct concepts for each of the Step, 
Stretch and Leap classifications of concepts. Each of these 
concepts would be assembled from a unique set of functional 
components. This structured process supported a comprehen-
sive and objective generation of design alternatives, forming a 
solid foundation for subsequent evaluation and selection.

Top Plate - Material Selection
The tabletop has a few requirements for its design, many of which 
are hinging on the choice of material. The tabletop needs to have a 
compression strength strong enough to support machinery that 
weighs up to 200kg. The tabletop should also be made of a materi-
al that does not creep too much, so that the tabletop misaligns the 
assembly of the table, or the connection of a multiple table setup. 
The material should be durable when exposed to common chemi-
cals in biolabs, most notably ethanol. 
Furthermore, the tabletop should be as cheap as possible to manu-
facture, and it should be manufactured of a material that is light 
enough for a lab technician to easily carry. 
Finally, sustainability factors should also be taken into consider-
ation when choosing the material. Graphs showing how different 
materials fair on the parameters can be seen in worksheets 7.
The material selection clearly highlights some potential materials. 
Materials such as ABS and Polyester has a very high compressive 
strength, but ABS has an unacceptable resistance to ethanol and 
polyester has a high sustainability impact.
Cast iron is the cheapest feasible option, and fulfills most parame-
ters well, but it is likely too heavy for a lab technician to carry.
An aluminium alloy is likely a good option as it fulfills all criteria. 
But it is not the cheapest option.
Polymers such as PE and PET fair well in all parameters but might 
be prone to a small amount of creep. This could also be the best 
option, if it can be proven that the material creep wouldn’t be an 
issue.

Metrology and Testing
The design and construction of modular workbenches for automat-
ic biological testing need to consider metrological principles to 
ensure precision, repeatability, and reliability in the automated 
process. The key metrological points relevant to the design of the 
workbench are outlined in this chapter according to normal metrol-
ogy practice and reference to the Mitutoyo Metrology Handbook [7] 
and Geometrical Metrology and Machine Testing by De Chiffre et al. 
[8]. Primary focus is given to dimensional metrology, surface 
metrology, and uncertainty analysis with respect to the key func-
tional specifications of the workbench.
Dimensional Metrology
Dimensional metrology involves measuring geometric features and 
their relationships to ascertain if parts conform to design specifica-
tions. For the modular workbench, there are several key dimensions 
and geometric features that are significant for its proper function.
Tabletop Flatness and Levelness
The flatness of the workbench tabletop is critical for precise instru-
ment and sample placement, especially in automated liquid han-
dling systems. In this project, flatness was evaluated using a GOM 
ATOS Triple Scan 3D optical scanner, which captures high-resolu-
tion surface data for comprehensive deviation analysis. This 
method adheres to the least squares method for establishing the 
reference plane and calculating deviations.
To minimize systematic errors, the scanning process incorporated 
error compensation techniques, including: Multi-scan alignment to 
reduce positional drift, Temperature compensation (20°C ±1°C 
environment) and Triplicate scans per condition for statistical 
robustness. The GOM system’s accuracy (±0.008 mm per ISO 
10360-8) ensured measurements remained within the project’s 
refined tolerance threshold of 0.51 mm.
Module Interconnection Dimensions
The connecting points of the modules must be controlled dimension-
ally tightly to ensure ideal integration and alignment of adjacent 
workbenches. Dimensional deviations can lead to misalignment, 
which can affect the positional accuracy of robotic arms and even 
lead to functional failures. Standards such as ISO 286-2 [10] provide 
tolerance grades for linear dimensions, and this can be used as a 
guide for the selection of appropriate manufacturing processes and 
inspection methods.
Surface Metrology
Surface metrology finds application in surface finish and texture 
characterization. The surface finish of the tabletop of the workbench 
is important as far as cleanliness, cleaning ease, minimization of 
contamination risks, and as a base for the support of instruments and 
samples.
Surface roughness measurements, such as Ra (average roughness) 
and Rz (maximum profile height), are commonly used to quantify 
surface texture. The appropriate surface roughness of the workbench 
top surface is established by the specific requirements of the biologi-
cal tests to be performed. A smoother surface finish is generally 
desirable to facilitate cleaning and minimize particle adhesion. 
Surface roughness may be quantified by contact profilometers or 
optical surface profilers, as described in the Mitutoyo Metrology 
Handbook [7].
Conclusion
By adhering to established metrology standards the precision of the 
workbench can be quantified. Thereby it can be proven that the con-
nected tables and their collected tolerance stack ups can stay within 
the acceptable dimensional sway. Furthermore, it can also be proven 
that the surface of the workbench adheres to the industry require-
ments e.g. conform to GMO requirements.

Evaluation of Tabletop Flatness 
Under Different Conditions
Objective:
To quantify deformation of the tabletop caused by the robot arm's 
weight using 3D scanning, comparing two conditions:
Tabletop mounted on the workbench frame (baseline).
Tabletop mounted on the frame with the robot arm installed (load-
ed condition).
Test Setups (Permutations):
1. Permutation 1: Tabletop mounted and bolted to the workbench 
frame.
2. Permutation 2: Tabletop mounted on the workbench with the 
robot arm attached to the plate.
Materials and Equipment:
• Modular workbench frame assembly
• Tabletop (test specimen)
• Robot arm (e.g., Universal Robots UR5e, 10.7 kg payload)
• GOM ATOS Triple Scan 3D optical scanner
• Calibration artifacts and reference targets (per GOM specifica-
tions)
• Computer running GOM Inspect Professional software
• Climate-controlled environment (20°C ±1°C, 50% RH)
Procedure
Preparation:
1. Clean tabletop surface with isopropyl alcohol.
2. Apply reference targets to the tabletop in a 100 mm asymmetric 
pattern.
3. Calibrate GOM ATOS system using certified calibration sphere.
Permutation 1: Non-mounted tabletop
1. Secure tabletop to workbench frame.
2. Perform full coverage scan with GOM ATOS:
 
- Scan resolution: 0.05 mm
 
- Minimum 3 overlapping scans for complete coverage
Permutation 2: Loaded Condition Scan (Tabletop + Robot Arm)
1. Mount robot arm centrally on tabletop per design specifications.
2. Repeat scanning procedure with identical parameters.
Data analysis:
1. Alignment: Use best-fit alignment in GOM Inspect to match 
baseline and loaded scans.
2. Deviation Map:
 
- Generate color-coded deviation map showing deformation  
 
magnitude.
 
- Calculate maximum positive/negative deformation values.  
 
Compare the loaded table tops deviation to the non-loaded  
 
table top to find the total misalignment of the table top  
 
during load.
Acceptance Criteria:
The maximum difference between the highest and lowest measure-
ment point (spread) must not exceed 0.51 mm when added to the 
total tolerance stack-up of the products inaccuracies in any permu-
tation.

Discussion
The measured deformation of 0.403 mm, representing the differ-
ence between the unloaded tabletop and the tabletop with a mount-
ed robot arm, approaches but remains below the acceptance 
threshold of 0.51 mm. This threshold accounts for cumulative 
tolerance stack-ups across the system (e.g. frame alignment, 
mounting hardware, thermal expansion), meaning a 0.403 mm 
result may still comply if other deformations are minimal. The 
result is an indicator of possible further deformation of the top 
plate after further weight is added to it. During use the robot arm 
picks up some lighter consumables which slightly affects the 
weight, but more importantly the robot arm also exerts force during 
use that would likely also slightly deform the top plate. From the 
test setup available to the project, it was impossible to test any 
object deformation that was not static, so testing the deformation 
during robot use was not possible. For further testing it would be 
interesting to test how much the movement of the robot would

Test of Surface Smoothness
Objective:
To quantify the surface smoothness of the workbench tabletop  
surface roughness parameters (Ra and Rz, or arithmetic average 
roughness and ten-point height) was used to ensure it meets the 
requirements for cleanliness, minimal contamination risk, and 
stable positioning of instruments, as outlined in the “Metrology” 
section of the report.
Materials and Equipment:
• Workbench tabletop prototype
• Contact profilometer (e.g., Surtronic® S-116)
• Calibration standard for surface roughness
• Cleaning supplies (e.g., isopropyl alcohol, lint-free cloths)
Procedure
Preparation:
• Clean the surface of the workbench tabletop using isopropyl 
alcohol and lint-free cloths to remove any contaminants (ISO 
14644-11) [11].
• Allow the surface to dry completely before proceeding with mea-
surements.
Calibration:
• Calibrate the contact profilometer according to the manufacturer's 
instructions using the provided calibration standard (Mitutoyo 
Metrology Handbook) [8].
• Ensure that the profilometer is properly aligned and zeroed before 
each measurement.
Measurement:
• Select multiple measurement locations across the workbench 
tabletop, focusing on areas where instruments or samples will be 
placed, see figure 37.
 
 
• Position the contact profilometer at each measurement location 
and perform a surface roughness measurement.
• Record the Ra and Rz values for each measurement location.
• Repeat the measurement in another location in the same area 
twice more. 
• Ensure that the measurement direction is consistent across all 
locations to minimize variability.
Data Analysis:
• Calculate the average Ra and Rz values for the workbench table-
top using the measurements from all locations.
• 
Calculate the standard deviation of Ra and Rz values to 
assess the variability of surface roughness across the tabletop.
Acceptance Criteria:
• The average Ra value should be less than 0.8 µm [12][13].
• The average Rz value should be less than 6.3 µm [14].
• The standard deviation of Ra and Rz values should be within 
acceptable limits to ensure consistent surface roughness across 
the tabletop. These limits have been set based on industry stan-
dards.

over 1g and the control tables both exceed well over 1g during 
certain frequencies. 
This means that the connected tables lower the applied vibrations 
significantly, especially when compared to the control tables, which 
are used in standard practice in biolabs. 
It is important to note that the modular system created for this 
project has a tighter requirement on sturdiness compared to the 
existing solutions, since the modular design is made to accommo-
date test setups with multiple operational machines simultaneous-
ly, and where some apparatuses might be movement sensitive. This 
is a requirement not necessarily needed in existing lab tables, since 
there likely won’t be multiple apparatuses running simultaneously 
on the same table setup. 
Luckily, this test proves that a test setup, with only two workbench-
es, has a much higher sturdiness than the standard practise tables 
currently. It is also likely that the setup will be even sturdier with 
more connecter workbenches added to the system.

Sub-Conclusion for Testing Segment
The testing phase of the modular workbench project provided a 
comprehensive evaluation of the system’s mechanical precision 
and operational robustness. The primary focus was on connection 
alignment, tabletop flatness under load, and vibrational stability, 
each critical for ensuring reliable laboratory automation.
The measured deviation for the alignment between connected 
tables was 0.62 mm ± 0.12 mm. This value reflects the precision of 
the connecter mechanism and the frame’s geometric consistency.
The net deformation of the tabletop under load (robot arm mount-
ed) was measured at 0.41 mm.
When considering both alignment and flatness, the total worst-case 
deviation is:
 
Total Stack-Up = 0.62 mm + 0.41 mm = 1.03 mm
This does not include the uncertainty margin from the alignment 
test; when included, the upper bound could reach 1.15 mm.
The original design requirement allowed for up to 1.00 mm total 
deviation across all tolerances.
During development, the requirement was tightened to 0.51 mm to 
ensure reliable robotic calibration and operation.
The measured stack-up exceeds both the refined (0.51 mm) and, 
marginally, the original (1.00 mm) requirement, especially when 
considering measurement uncertainty. This indicates that improve-
ments are needed in either the connecter mechanism, frame rigidi-
ty, or both.
The modular workbench and its connections were subjected to 
vibration tests at various RPMs and under prolonged operation.
Findings:
• No catastrophic failures, excessive movement, or resonant insta-
bilities were observed.
• Minor vibrations were transmitted between connected tables, but 
these did not result in additional measurable deformation or loss of 
alignment.
• The system maintained structural integrity during both short-term 
and 12-hour endurance tests.
It is recommended to inspect and, if necessary, retighten all bolts 
every 3–6 months, depending on usage intensity. Furthermore, 
visual checks for wear or loosening after major equipment changes 
or heavy use are advised.
In summary, while the workbench meets most functional demands 
and shows strong performance under vibration, achieving the 
stricter tolerance requirements will require targeted design refine-
ments. The current system is suitable for most laboratory tasks but 
should be improved for applications demanding the highest preci-
sion and repeatability.

Discussion
Connection precision
The overall design of the workbench has been proven via testing to 
be adequate in most of the design criteria set for the product 
design. The only aspect that failed the testing was the precision 
test, by a small margin. Through future iterations of the frame and 
top plate, it is likely that the imprecision will be lowered through 
better manufactured tolerances. In the case that a more precise 
manufacturing does not solve the imprecision issue a redesign of 
the connecter mechanism will be needed to solve the issue. 
Through testing a magnetic connection, which is simultaneously 
locked by the mechanism’s geometry has proven to be a great 
solution compared to the other developed solutions. A further 
development of the geometry might prove to solve the imprecision. 
A future precision test, like the one conducted during this project, of 
different iterations of the magnetic connecter is needed to find the 
ideal shape of the connecter.
Wheels
The wheels are currently a weak design aspect of the table. They 
are currently mounted on a vertical grove which, throughout use of 
the table, will likely misalign their positioning a little, which leads to 
an uneven table connection. Furthermore, given the wheels mount-
ing position on a vertical grove, the initial assemble of the table 
won’t provide a clear indicator for the user on where exactly to 
position the wheel. This flaw can be prevented by the user trying 
different settings until satisfied with the setup, but it diminishes the 
useability of the product to an small extend. 
A second issue is present with the current wheels where they 
cannot turn around in a 360-degree rotation, since the table leg 
blocks the full rotation. The wheel is still usable but tend to inter-
lock when turning the table in certain ways. This flaw can currently 
be solved by the user manoeuvring the table correctly. This flaw 
could be removed be either finding similar wheels with slightly 
different dimension, that won’t interlock with the table legs, or an 
alternative mounting system could be developed for the wheel, 
though at a minor cost increase of the final product. 
Shelf system
The current shelf system proves to be a very good system for a 
modular setup. It gives the users much freedom in choosing how to 
setup their personalized storage to fit their needs. The initial “feel” 
of the drawer system leave some ease of use to be desires. The 
shelves are prone to dislodging or might sometimes get stuck in an 
awkward position, which leaves the user to use some force when 
trying to open them. The flaw is likely a result of the soft plastic 
drawers that are used for the prototype. The flaw might be solved 
by finding a more fitting shelf material, that might not deform the 
way the current system does, or by adjusting the frame profiles to 
be within a more acceptable tolerance of the drawer system.

1. Understanding Modular Design Principles: 
• 
This has been the main goal of the project and has been 
reached through numerous decisions made throughout the project. 
The first aspect is to ensure that the design is universally able to be 
integrated. This was reached by designing the product using stan-
dardised measurements, as well as restricting the design via design 
parameters and restricted surfaces. 
2. User-Centred Design in Automation
• 
This goal has been met via the numerous user input ses-
sions, as well as general user testing and feedback during the 
project. The project has made use of the DALSA team for design 
feedback, as well as used real life users for the usability tests 
during the testing phase.
3. Industry-Specific Research and Analysis 
• 
This goal was reached during the research phase where the 
competitor analysis was conducted. Furthermore, the competitor 
comparison also incorporated some critical analysis of the industry 
artifacts and their functionalities.

• 
This goal has been reached throughout the extensive proto-
type construction on the product iterations and testings done on 
the final product. Similarly, the following analysis and discussion of 
the results also connect the prototyping to the testing.
5. Interdisciplinary Integration: 
• 
This goal has been met on a surface level. The design has 
been made in collaboration with software and hardware parameters 
for the robot arm. The project has not delved into any direct integra-

project goal was never to create a functioning robotic arm, but to 
create a system where the robot arm can easily be integrated.

user need. This was met by allowing the product to be a material

Like point 2, this goal was met via collaboration with DALSA, 
as well as feedback session of other external DTU stakeholders 
outside of DALSA.

Suggested Future Work
To address the identified limitations and further advance the modular workbench concept, the following areas are 
recommended for future work:
Connecter and Frame Refinement: 
Redesign the connecter mechanism and reinforce the frame to reduce alignment deviation and improve overall 
precision, aiming to meet or exceed the 0.51 mm threshold.
Dynamic Deformation Testing: 
Conduct tests under real robotic operation to assess deformation and alignment stability during dynamic loading.
Integrated Cable Management: 
Develop and implement solutions for organized cable routing to reduce clutter and improve safety and usability.
Apparatus Alignment System: 
Design and test a robust, user-friendly alignment system for the top plate to support precise placement of lab equip-
ment.
Scalability Studies: 
Expand testing to include multiple connected tables to evaluate cumulative tolerance stack-ups and system-wide 
performance.
User Feedback and Iteration: 
Continue gathering feedback from diverse users to guide iterative improvements and ensure the system meets 
practical needs in real laboratory environments.
Documentation Enhancement: 
Further develop user guides and training materials to support broader adoption and independent use.
By pursuing these directions, the modular workbench system can be further optimized for precision, usability, and 
scalability, ultimately supporting more efficient and accessible laboratory automation across the life sciences 
sector.

Conclusion
This project set out to create a flexible, scalable modular work-
bench system that could help bridge the gap between resource-lim-
ited laboratories and the increasingly automated workflows found 
in modern life science research. Throughout the process, the goal 
was not only to design a technically robust and user-friendly solu-
tion, but also to ensure that it could be realistically adopted by a 
wide range of labs, regardless of size or existing equipment.
By working closely with stakeholders from DTU’s Arena for Life 
Science Automation (DALSA) and drawing on the expertise of lab 
technicians, robotics engineers, and metrology specialists, the 
project was able to stay grounded in real-world needs. The use of 
iterative prototyping and a mix of established design methodolo-
gies meant that feedback could be quickly incorporated, and the 
workbench evolved in response to practical challenges encoun-
tered along the way.
Testing showed that the modular workbench is already a strong 
performer when it comes to structural integrity and vibration resis-
tance. The system handled operational loads well and maintained 
its usability, which is promising for everyday lab work. However, the 
combined deviation in connection alignment and tabletop flatness 
was measured at about 1.03 mm, just above the original 1 mm 
target, and notably higher than the more ambitious 0.51 mm thresh-
old set for seamless robotic integration. This result highlights the 
need for further refinement in the connecter mechanism and frame 
design to reach the highest standards of precision.
83
Despite these technical challenges, the project achieved several 
important milestones. The workbench is modular and open-source, 
making it accessible for labs with limited budgets. The assembly 
guides and documentation created as part of the project will help 
users set up, maintain, and adapt the system to their own needs, 
supporting both immediate use and long-term sustainability.
Looking forward, there’s clear potential for this concept to make a 
real difference in how labs approach automation. To fully realize 
that potential, future work should focus on improving connecter 
precision, integrating better cable management and alignment 
features, and testing the system with more modules and under 
dynamic robotic conditions. Continued collaboration with end-users 
will be key to making sure the workbench remains practical and 
relevant as laboratory needs evolve.
In summary, this project lays a solid foundation for more accessi-
ble, efficient, and standardized laboratory automation. With further 
development, the modular workbench system could become a 
valuable tool for researchers everywhere, helping to make 
advanced automation a reality for labs of all sizes.

4 DOF SCARA Specifications
The table might utilize the 4-DOF SCARA robot arm, a 3D-printed robotic 
arm developed by DTU.
The robot arm has dimensions of 250 × 340 × 660 mm and a reach of 615 
mm. With this reach, it can handle a payload of 750 g, which is sufficient 
for moving test tube racks.
The primary function of the robot arm in conjunction with the modular 
table is to automate the transfer of test tubes between machines, enabling 
a fully automated laboratory process.
The arm is controlled by a Raspberry Pi 4 and supports communication via 
Ethernet, USB, Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth. Additionally, the table with the robot 
arm will include a small computer, allowing for test setup programming.
The robot arm requires 200W of power, meaning the table will either need 
an integrated battery or be positioned near a power source.
