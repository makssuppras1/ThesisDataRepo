Abstract
Norway’s ambitious goal of allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2040
presents both opportunities and challenges. Floating offshore wind technology is par­
ticularly relevant due to Norway’s deep coastal waters, yet its large­scale deployment
depends on substantial economic support and grid infrastructure upgrades. This thesis
explores the feasibility and role of floating offshore wind in Norway’s energy transition.
The research is structured around three key questions: (1) Will the Norwegian govern­
ment achieve its goal of allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2040?
(2) How much economic support is needed for an offshore floating wind farm? (3) How
extensive are the grid upgrades required to facilitate such growth?
A mixed­methods approach combines literature review, long­term market data, and semi­
structured interviews with key stakeholders (Statnett, Equinor, Subsea7, and Fornybar
Norge), alongside a pre­feasibility case study for the Vestavind B area.
Results indicate that the government can allocate areas for 30 GW of offshore wind by
2040, provided that allocation remains a political priority. However, actual installed ca­
pacity is more realistically expected to reach only 5–15 GW within that timeframe, given
current regulatory and supply chain developments. For the 506 MW Vestavind B project,
the base case yields a net present value (NPV) of –13.98 billion NOK, an internal rate of
return (IRR) of 2.4%, and a levelized cost of energy (LCOE) of 141.6 øre/kWh. Bridging
this viability gap would require subsidies of approximately 20–30 million NOK per MW, a
range supported by both interviewee insights and recent industry studies.
Grid analysis, supported by interviews, indicates that Statnett’s current reinforcement pro­
gramme could accommodate around 10–15 GW of offshore wind by 2040 without major
bottlenecks.
Overall, floating offshore wind can play a substantial role in Norway’s long­term energy
mix, but only if early projects receive predictable financial support, grid planning keeps
pace with deployment, and a clear pipeline for future projects is established. As costs
decline and learning effects accumulate, the need for subsidies is expected to diminish,
positioning Norway to competitively exploit its deep­water resources beyond 2040.

Introduction
The global transition to renewable energy is essential for reducing greenhouse gas emis­
sions and mitigating climate change. The Paris Agreement has set a target to limit global
warming to 1.5°C, which requires a rapid expansion of renewable energy sources. Off­
shore wind is expected to play a key role in this transition, particularly as global energy
demand continues to rise while fossil fuel dependency must decrease. The International
Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) estimates that 2,000 GW of installed offshore wind
capacity will be necessary by 2050 to meet climate targets [1].
The European Union (EU) has positioned offshore wind as a central pillar of its long­
term climate and energy strategy. The European Commission has set ambitious goals
to expand offshore wind capacity from 12 GW in 2020 to 60 GW by 2030 and to 300
GW by 2050 [2]. Offshore wind is projected to become Europe’s largest single source of
electricity by 2040 [3].
Countries such as the United Kingdom, the Netherlands, and Denmark have already made
significant investments in large­scale offshore wind farms, strengthening Europe’s posi­
tion as a leader in this sector. However, with limited shallow­water sites for bottom­fixed
offshore wind, the industry is now shifting towards floating offshore wind technology, which
enables wind farms to be installed in deeper waters.
Norway has vast offshore wind resources and an electricity sector that is already nearly
100% renewable, primarily due to hydropower. Despite this, increased electrification and
industrial demand are driving the need for further renewable energy expansion. The Nor­
wegian government has set a target to allocate areas for 30 GW of offshore wind by
2040 [4]. While the theoretical offshore wind potential is estimated between 200–300 GW,
practical constraints such as environmental concerns, maritime traffic, and grid limitations
reduce the viable development capacity to around 40–60 GW [5]. In April 2023, Norwe­
gian Water Resources and Energy Directorate (NVE) proposed 20 possible offshore wind
areas along the Norwegian coast, marking an important step toward large­scale develop­
ment [5].
Floating offshore wind is particularly relevant for Norway due to the deep waters along
its coastline, which make bottom­fixed installations less feasible. Floating wind turbines,
unlike traditional fixed­bottom designs, are anchored to the seabed with mooring systems,
allowing deployment in waters deeper than 60 meters. This significantly expands the
accessible wind resource, as approximately 70–80 % of global offshore wind potential lies
in deeper waters where only floating wind technology can be utilized [5]. Although floating
offshore wind remains more expensive than fixed­bottom alternatives, costs are expected
to decline as the industry matures. According to NVE, the levelized cost of energy for
fixed­bottom offshore wind in Norway is estimated at 116 øre/kWh, while floating offshore
wind currently stands at 154 øre/kWh. [6] By 2030, these costs are projected to decrease
to 51 øre/KWh and 68 øre/kWh, respectively [5].
To accelerate development, the Norwegian government has allocated 35 billion NOK in
support for early floating wind projects [4]. However, large­scale deployment faces sev­
eral challenges, including the need for extensive grid upgrades, economic support mech­
anisms, and supply chain development.

Background
This chapter provides an overview of the current status of floating offshore wind technol­
ogy and its role in Norway’s energy transition. It explains the key differences between
floating and fixed­bottom wind turbines, highlighting technological components such as
substructures, mooring systems, and grid connection methods. Additionally, the chap­
ter outlines Norway’s offshore wind development process, regulatory framework, and the
broader energy landscape, including the role of hydropower and oil and gas. Together,
these elements establish the necessary context for understanding the feasibility and po­
tential of floating offshore wind in Norway.
2.1
State of the art: Floating offshore wind turbines
Floating offshore wind technology represents a significant breakthrough in offshore en­
ergy, allowing wind turbines to be deployed in deep waters where fixed­bottom founda­
tions are not feasible. Unlike traditional bottom­fixed wind turbines, which rely on monopile
or jacket foundations embedded into the seabed, floating wind turbines require three addi­
tional components: a specialized substructure, anchors, and mooring lines. The substruc­
ture supports the turbine above water while remaining buoyant, anchors secure the float­
ing platform to the seabed, and mooring lines provide stability by connecting the structure
to the anchors. These components enable floating wind turbines to be deployed further
offshore, unlocking wind energy potential in areas previously unsuitable for fixed­bottom
wind farms [7].
2.1.1
Differences between fixed­bottom and floating offshore wind
Fixed­bottom offshore wind turbines are directly anchored to the seabed, typically in wa­
ters up to 50–60 meters deep. This method requires stable ground conditions and is
primarily used near coastlines. In contrast, floating offshore wind turbines are not reliant
on seabed anchoring and can be positioned in much deeper waters, where wind speeds
tend to be stronger and more consistent. This ability to operate further offshore reduces
land­use conflicts and offers greater flexibility in project siting [5].
The growing appeal of floating offshore wind is driven by several advantages, including
fewer restrictions on wind farm size and location, access to higher wind speeds, and the
ability to bypass seabed constraints that limit fixed­bottom installations. Norway, with its
extensive coastline and deep waters, is particularly well­positioned for floating wind de­
velopment. Estimates suggest that approximately 80% of Norway’s usable offshore wind
resources are located in waters deeper than 60 meters, making floating wind a crucial
part of future offshore energy expansion. Additionally, Norway’s established offshore oil
and gas service industry provides a strong technological and logistical foundation for de­
veloping floating offshore wind, as noted by the International energy agency (IEA), which
highlights the synergies between oil and gas expertise and offshore wind advancements
[5].

2.1.2
Substructure
Floating wind turbines rely on specialized substructures that keep them stable while teth­
ered to the seabed. Figure 2.2 illustrates the five primary floating foundation types used in
the industry: barge, semi­submersible, articulated multi­spar, spar, and tension­leg plat­
form (TLP).
A barge foundation is a wide, buoyant platform that floats on the water surface. While
simple in design, it requires additional stabilization to counteract wave forces. The semi­
submersible platform consists of multiple submerged columns that distribute weight and
increase stability, making it one of the more widely tested solutions. The articulated multi­
spar is a variation of the spar­buoy concept, incorporating multiple sections that allow
for greater flexibility in dynamic sea conditions. The spar foundation is a deep, ballast­
stabilized cylindrical structure that extends far below the water surface, commonly used in
projects such as Hywind Scotland. Lastly, the TLP uses vertical tendons anchored to the
seabed, significantly minimizing vertical motion and improving efficiency in deeper waters.
[7]

2.1.3
Mooring line
The mooring system plays a key role in maintaining the stability of floating wind turbines.
There are two primary categories of mooring lines: taut and catenary lines. Taut mooring
lines, typically made from high­strength rope, extend almost vertically from the floating
structure to a high­load anchor embedded in the seabed. These are commonly used
in TLP foundations, illustrated in Figure 2.2 where vertical stability is crucial. In contrast,
catenary mooring lines are made from freely hanging chains that extend horizontally along
the seabed before connecting to drag anchors. This configuration provides flexibility and
allows the floating turbine to adjust naturally to wave and wind forces. Catenary mooring is
widely used for spar, barge, and semi­submersible foundations due to its ability to absorb
dynamic loads efficiently [7].
Recent innovations in anchoring systems include shared anchor configurations, where
multiple floating wind turbines are connected to a single anchor, reducing material costs
and seabed disturbance. For instance, Equinor’s Hywind Tampen project in Norway uti­
lizes 19 anchors for 11 turbines, a more efficient setup compared to Hywind Scotland,
which required 15 anchors for just five turbines [7].

2.1.4
Anchor
To keep the floating structures in place, they are secured to the seabed using different
types of anchors. The choice of anchor depends on seabed characteristics and the moor­
ing system used. Figure 2.3 illustrates the various anchor types, which include 1. dead­
weight, 2. driven pile, 3. drag, 4. suction pile, 5. gravity­installed (drop) anchors, and 6.
vertical load anchors. Drag anchors are widely used due to their deep seabed penetra­
tion and high resistance to horizontal forces, but they are less effective against vertical
loads. Driven piles, on the other hand, are hammered into the seabed for a fixed foun­
dation, while suction caissons use pressure to secure themselves and can be removed if
necessary [7].

2.1.7
Challenges and future research directions
Despite its advantages, floating wind technology still faces barriers that must be overcome
before large­scale deployment becomes cost­competitive. One of the primary challenges
is cost, as the LCOE for floating wind remains higher than that of fixed­bottom offshore
wind. However, advancements in mass production, improved installation methods, and
larger turbines exceeding 15 MW are expected to drive costs down in the coming years.
[7]
Grid integration is another critical challenge, as floating turbines require subsea substa­
tions and high­voltage dynamic cables to efficiently transmit electricity to onshore grids.
These components must be designed to withstand harsh marine environments while en­
suring reliability. Operation and maintenance (O&M) costs are also a concern, as servic­
ing turbines in deep waters is significantly more complex and expensive than for fixed­
bottom installations. New developments in predictive maintenance using AI and auto­
mated inspection technologies are expected to improve long­term operational efficiency
[7].
Floating offshore wind has the potential to revolutionize offshore energy by enabling large­
scale wind farms in deep waters. While economic and technical hurdles remain, the rapid
pace of innovation, increasing government support, and advancements in floating foun­

provide the greatest emission reductions per investment, ensuring that Norway’s oil and

2.4.1
Strategic impact assessment
In Norway, a SIA is a key step in the process of opening areas for offshore renewable
energy projects, as mandated by the Offshore Energy Act. This legislation ensures that
energy developments are planned with consideration for energy supply, environmental
protection, safety, business interests, and other societal concerns. According to §2­2 of
the Act, an SIA must be conducted before any area can be designated for offshore wind
development [22].
The purpose of the assessment is to evaluate the environmental, social, and commercial
consequences of utilizing specific offshore areas for energy production. It adopts a broad
perspective, identifying potential conflicts with other sea users, such as fisheries, ship­
ping, and the military, in order to establish a well­founded knowledge base for informed
decision­making. [22]
These assessments are carried out by a directorate group made up of various govern­
mental agencies like the NVE, the Norwegian Directorate of Fisheries, and others. The
impact assessment program is then submitted to the Ministry of Energy (ED), which con­
ducts a public hearing before finalizing it. The entire support framework is subject to
public consultation, allowing stakeholders and the general public to participate in shaping
the offshore wind strategy. [22]
In addition, financial aid schemes must be approved by the EFTA Surveillance Authority
(ESA), which ensures compliance with EU and European Economic Area (EEA) competi­
tion laws on behalf of the European Free Trade Association (EFTA). The EEA agreement
allows Norway to participate in the EU internal market, and transparency remains a key
priority. All decisions related to financial support are made publicly available [23].

2.5
Support for floating offshore wind in Norway
This section presents the financial and regulatory measures outlined in [23], detailing the
Norwegian government’s approach to advancing floating offshore wind through funding,
area allocation, and regulatory frameworks. The proposal includes commitments for finan­
cial support, mechanisms to ensure cost­efficient deployment, and regulations to facilitate
project development and grid integration.
2.5.1
Financial support programs
The Norwegian government has historically supported floating offshore wind through fund­
ing schemes such as Enova and the NOx­fund. Enova, a government agency focused on
reducing emissions and promoting energy innovation, has contributed to offshore wind
development, including a 2 billion NOK grant for the GoliatVIND demonstration project
[23].
To further accelerate deployment, the government has allocated 35 billion NOK for large­
scale floating wind development in the newly opened areas Vestavind B and Vestavind
F. Utsira Nord, one of the most mature floating wind areas in Norway, is located within
Vestavind F, and is currently the primary focus for initial deployment. In April 2025, the
Ministry of Energy notified ESA of a new support model specifically for Utsira Nord, in­
troducing a two­phase structure. Three project areas within Utsira Nord will be awarded
through a qualitative competition, followed by a two­year maturation phase.
After this period, developers will compete in an auction for investment support, with one
project of up to 500 MW receiving backing. The winner will be the developer requiring the
least public funding to realize their project. The NOK 35 billion support is allocated for a
single 500 MW floating offshore wind project to be developed within Utsira Nord, with the
Assessing the role of floating offshore wind in Norway’s future
14
==PAGE:27==
specific project to be selected through a competitive allocation process [26]. In addition,
30 billion NOK has been set aside for offshore wind infrastructure under a separate budget
item [27].
In April 2025, the EFTA Surveillance Authority approved Norway’s state aid scheme for
Utsira Nord, confirming that financial support will be given as a direct investment grant of
up to NOK 35 billion for one 500 MW floating wind project. The scheme does not use a
traditional Contract for Difference (CfD) but includes a clawback mechanism, where 50%
of revenue above a certain electricity price threshold must be repaid to the state over a
15­year period [28]. This is the first time such a model has been processed under the
state aid rules. Its development has taken place in dialogue with both the offshore wind
industry and ESA.
In parallel, the government is also supporting fixed­bottom offshore wind through the de­
velopment of Sørlige Nordsjø II, where a CfD­based auction model was used to award
1,500 MW in 2024 at a strike price of 1.15 NOK/kWh [29].
The support scheme for Utsira Nord differs significantly from that of Sørlige Nordsjø II.
While the latter follows a traditional CfD model offering predictable revenue streams, Ut­
sira Nord relies on a direct investment grant with a clawback mechanism.
2.5.2
Comparison with neighboring support models
Norway’s emerging support model for floating offshore wind shares several features with
countries like the United Kingdom and Germany. Similar to the UK, Norway has adopted
a two­sided CfD system, offering developers stable revenues while safeguarding public
budgets from overcompensation [30]. Germany employs a comparable financial mecha­
nism through market premiums under the Renewable Energy Sources Act (EEG), provid­
ing predictable income when market prices fall below a reference value [31]. In all three
countries, financial support is awarded through competitive auctions where developers
bid on the lowest required support level, promoting cost efficiency and innovation.
Norway is also beginning to take a more active role in early project stages, supporting
feasibility studies, permitting, and infrastructure planning. An approach reminiscent of
Germany’s centralized planning system, where the government pre­selects and surveys
offshore areas before auctioning them [32]. However, Norway still differs from Germany
and Denmark, where the state fully prepares and de­risks project areas in advance [33]
In contrast, the Netherlands represents a fundamentally different model. Dutch authorities
have moved toward zero­subsidy auctions, where developers receive no direct financial
support and rely instead on market revenues [34]. This approach is facilitated by com­
prehensive site assessments and risk reduction measures provided by the government.
While Norway is not currently pursuing a zero­subsidy strategy, the Dutch model presents
an interesting case of how robust public planning can offset the need for financial incen­
tives. This could become increasingly relevant for Norway in the long term as technology
matures and investment risks decline.

and the state of the supply chain. Key physical parameters like distance to shore, water
depth, and wind resource availability also play critical roles. Additionally, the timing of
project construction and the economic environment at that time will impact costs. [42]
2.8.1
Location determines technology, and technology drives costs
Two major factors influencing offshore wind costs are water depth and the distance to the
grid connection point. These parameters dictate the required technology, which in turn
shapes the cost structure.
As previously discussed, water depth influences the choice between fixed­bottom and
floating turbines. This section focuses on how distance to shore impacts transmission
solutions. For shorter distances, alternating current (AC) transmission is typically used. At
greater distances, however, direct current (DC) transmission becomes necessary, adding
both complexity and cost, particularly when offshore and onshore converter stations are
required [42].

Alternating current transmission
AC transmission is a cost­effective option for offshore wind farms located near shore, as
it avoids the need for complex conversion infrastructure. To extend its effective range,
offshore transformer stations are used to step up the voltage and reduce transmission
losses.
These stations can be fixed, floating, or subsea. Fixed platforms are the most commonly
used today, while floating and subsea solutions are still under development for use in
deep waters or areas with limited space [43].

2.8.4
Direct current transmission
DC transmission is better suited for offshore wind farms located more than 100 kilometers
from shore. It enables high­capacity power transfer with lower transmission losses per
kilometer compared to AC. However, DC systems require converter stations offshore and
onshore to switch between AC and DC, which adds cost, weight, and spatial demands.
At present, only fixed DC converter stations have been deployed. Floating DC converters
remain in early stages of development and lag behind their AC counterparts in technologi­
cal maturity. Subsea concepts inspired by the oil and gas industry could provide compact,
efficient alternatives in the future [43].

2.8.5
Cost Considerations and Subsea Technologies
Transmission infrastructure constitutes a major share of offshore wind project costs. For
distant offshore locations, combining high­voltage DC systems with subsea or floating
substations may offer greater cost efficiency than using multiple dynamic AC cables.
Subsea transformers can be placed near floating turbines to step up voltage locally, re­
ducing the number of required export cables. This approach builds on existing subsea
high­voltage technologies from the oil and gas sector.
2.8.6
Future Outlook
Emerging floating and subsea transmission technologies will be key to scaling offshore
wind in deeper and more remote waters. Floating AC transformers are approaching de­
ployment readiness, while floating DC converters require more development and valida­
tion. Continued innovation in this area could enable more cost­effective and environmen­
tally sustainable offshore grid solutions [43].

4.2.2
Financial evaluation metrics
This thesis uses two primary metrics to evaluate financial viability: Net Present Value
and Internal Rate of Return. NPV is calculated by discounting future cash flows to their
present value using a constant real discount rate. A positive NPV indicates that the project
is expected to generate more value than it costs. IRR represents the discount rate at which
NPV equals zero and provides an intuitive measure of project return. Both metrics follow
the methodology recommended by NVE [6].

4.2.5
CfD Strike Price and Price Premium
In addition to NPV, IRR and LCOE, this thesis works out a breakeven CfD strike price, the
flat electricity price (in nominal NOK/MWh) the project would need if it receives no upfront
subsidy.
The strike price is the single price that makes the project’s NPV exactly zero. To find it,
the year­by­year price path in the cashflow model is replaced with one constant value,
and Excel’s Goal Seek adjusts that value until the discounted cash flows equal the initial
investment. Any gap between the actual market price and this strike price would be settled
through a two­sided CfD.
After finding the strike price, a CfD premium is calculated by subtracting the average
nominal market price forecast for NO2 (2025–2060) from the strike price. This premium
shows how much higher the price must be, on average, for the project to break even
without direct capital support.
The simple payback period is defined as the number of years required for cumulative
nominal free cash flows to equal the capital expenditure. Although easy to interpret, this
metric does not reflect the time value of money. To address this, a discounted payback
period is also calculated, based on the cumulative sum of discounted free cash flows.
This provides a more realistic estimate of how long it would take to recover the investment
in present value terms, which is especially important for long­lived energy infrastructure
projects.

5
Case Study ­ Vestavind B
Vestavind B is one of the offshore wind areas identified by NVE as suitable for future
development, located in the North Sea west of Sognefjorden. It was recommended for
prioritization due to favorable wind conditions and water depths suited for floating wind,
as well as a manageable environmental impact. While the area is not yet open for appli­
cations, it remains a strong candidate in Norway’s plan to allocate up to 30 GW of offshore
wind capacity by 2040.
For this thesis, a project­specific case study of Vestavind B has been developed based
on NVE’s spatial and technical guidelines. While the wind farm design is fictional and
not linked to any specific developer, it is grounded in the real geographic and regulatory
context of the Vestavind B area. This allows for an economic feasibility analysis, including
estimates of investment costs, energy production, revenue potential, and sensitivity to key
variables. The model provides insight into the economic and policy conditions required to
realize floating offshore wind in Norway.
5.1
Background on Vestavind B
This background section summarizes information provided by NVE on the real Vestavind
B location. It aims to inform the reader about the area’s characteristics and strategic
importance, and serves as a foundation for developing the case study wind farm used in
this thesis.
Located in the North Sea off the coast of Vestland, Vestavind B was one of 20 areas iden­
tified by NVE in 2023 for potential offshore wind development. Although only Vestavind
F and Sørvest F­extensions of the Utsira Nord and Sørlige Nordsjø II zones were initially
recommended for assessment and potential tenders in 2025, the Ministry of Energy also
included Vestavind B. This decision was based on strong grid connection possibilities
and the presence of existing offshore infrastructure. The area benefits from ongoing off­
shore activity and a well­documented understanding of local conditions and stakeholder
interests, making it a strategic candidate for assessing the feasibility and socioeconomic
potential of floating wind in Norway’s energy transition [61].
As of November 2024, following the most recent findings from the strategic environmen­
tal assessment, NVE has updated its recommendations regarding Vestavind B. The new
conclusion advises against opening Vestavind B for offshore wind development at this
time. Instead, NVE suggests postponing any decision until the strategic environmental
assessment of the other 17 designated areas is completed in June 2025. Vestavind B
should only be reconsidered if a comparative analysis with other Vestavind areas, such
as Vestavind C or D, concludes that it is the most suitable site. While a wind farm in Ves­
tavind B could contribute significantly to the energy needs of the Bergen region, the area’s
conflicts warrant further evaluation of alternative sites. A reassessment of the decision to
open Vestavind B could be made in the second half of 2025 if it is determined to be the
best option [62].

Vestavind B is suitable for floating offshore wind, with depths ranging from 310 to 385

6.2.1
Will the Norwegian government achieve its goal of allocating areas
for 30 GW of offshore wind capacity by 2040?
Understanding the 30 GW goal: Allocation vs. realization
All interviewees described Norway’s 30 GW offshore wind goal by 2040 as ambitious and
often misunderstood. Crucially, the target refers to allocating areas for up to 30 GW of
capacity, not necessarily building that much by 2040. As Torkel Sjoner from Equinor put
it, “The 30 GW target refers to capacity allocated, not necessarily built.” However, “That
plan doesn’t exist yet,” the Equinor regulatory lead added, noting the lack of a concrete
roadmap to reach 30 GW.
All experts agreed that allocating sites is just the first step. Stakeholders, including Stat­
nett, emphasized that while political progress on site designation appears to be on track,
moving toward actual development involves a different framework entirely, particularly in
terms of financing. Several interviewees cautioned against celebrating early wins. As
Sindre Østby Stub of Fornybar Norge put it: “It’s not a goal to just allocate a bunch of ar­
eas if you’re not going to get it done.” He emphasized the need for streamlined permitting
and strong economic support.
When asked what could realistically be built by 2040, few believed that the full 30 GW
target would be achieved. According to input from Statnett, a more feasible outcome
might be around 15 GW by 2040, with the remaining capacity potentially developed during
the 2040s. Statnett is already planning grid upgrades based on the 15 GW assumption.
Others were even more conservative: Helge Myrvang from Subsea7 referenced NVE and
DNV forecasts of just 6­10 GW by 2040 and added, “I don’t think 30 GW will be built by
2040 or 2050 as it looks now,” noting even the energy minister has suggested 20 GW
might be more realistic.
Still, most supported the 30 GW ambition as a long­term signal. Interviewees agreed the
exact number matters less than steady, credible progress. Achieving even 5­15 GW by
2040 could be a win if it sets the stage for more. The Equinor interviewee revealed that
the 30 GW figure was primarily political, part of a North Sea push for bold targets, rather
than a detailed engineering estimate. The general consensus: use 30 GW as a guiding
ambition, but focus on enabling 5­15 GW by 2040, with the rest to follow.
Onshore opposition and offshore public engagement
Norway’s pivot to offshore wind is largely a response to public opposition toward onshore
wind. While several interviewees noted that onshore wind would be far cheaper and more
efficient, local resistance and political backlash have stalled new projects since 2019. “The
cheapest option for Norway would be to build more onshore wind, but that is controversial
and faces political opposition,” Myrvang explained. While countries like Denmark and the
UK have embraced onshore wind, public resistance in Norway has made offshore wind
the most realistic option for expanding renewable energy and meeting climate targets.

Several experts stressed that Norway’s need for offshore wind depends on continued
constraints to other sources. Torkel Sjoner added, “If you can’t build wind on land, then
[offshore] is the cost of getting the power you need.” Over time, this dynamic may change.
Myrvang pointed to NVE projections suggesting that public attitudes toward onshore wind

Norway “may not require anywhere near 30 GW of offshore capacity.” For now, however,
offshore wind is the key to maintaining Norway’s energy surplus.
To avoid the kind of backlash experienced with onshore wind projects, authorities are
placing strong emphasis on early public engagement and strategic spatial planning. Ac­

reduce potential conflicts and identify areas where environmental and social impacts are
more manageable. Approximately 54,000 square kilometers have already been mapped
as possible zones for offshore wind development. In their view, the physical space exists
to reach the 30 GW target, at least in terms of available ocean space.
Industry players support this careful approach as key to building a social license. “We
don’t want offshore wind to become the new onshore wind,” warned Myrvang. Trans­
parency has been central: environmental data and draft area plans are public, and all
consultations are open. “It’s an open public process,” he emphasized, noting that regula­
tors have responded well to both industry and interest groups. While this slows progress,
it helps defuse opposition early.
Offshore wind has largely avoided public resistance so far, partly because many projects
are located far from shore, reducing visual and noise impacts. But that’s not always true.
“Floating offshore wind isn’t necessarily that far from land,” said the representative from

able to see it from the island.” This could raise concerns over visibility and environmental
effects. “Communities need to see a utility value in these projects to stay supportive,”
Stub added, pointing to jobs, infrastructure, and revenue­sharing. Without that, resistance
could still emerge.
Myrvang compared today’s resistance to that faced by hydropower in the mid­20th cen­
tury. “Hydropower faced massive opposition when it was developed on a large scale.
People chained themselves to excavators,” he recalled. Yet today, those same dams are
a point of national pride. “We’re incredibly grateful we have this power, and no one thinks
about the environmental disruption anymore.” The hope is that, with early engagement
and community inclusion, offshore wind can follow a similar path and become an accepted
part of Norway’s energy system.

wind is covered by the newer Offshore Energy Act, which remains incomplete. Many roles
and responsibilities remain undefined, particularly when it comes to offshore grid infras­
tructure. According to Statnett, there are currently far fewer regulations offshore com­
pared to onshore systems, leaving many questions open regarding how responsibilities
will be distributed.

representative. The government is working with Statnett and NVE to address offshore

Tendering strategy and developer confidence
Norway’s two­stage offshore wind model was broadly supported by industry representa­
tives from Equinor and Subsea7, though some concerns remain. In this model, the gov­

conduct surveys, secure permits, and refine designs, before competing for financial sup­

projects or selecting winners based purely on low bids without technical substance.

early­stage work, including full Environmental Impact Assessments, falls to the develop­

needs,” it can create duplication and extra cost. Still, the industry supports the model, if it
remains predictable. As Sjoner emphasized, the focus should be to “simplify and shorten
the process toward investment decisions, without compromising rigor.”

costs,” Stub said, “and you don’t even know if there will be a next round.” This uncertainty

Technological frontiers and challenges
Norway’s offshore wind ambitions rely heavily on overcoming technological and infras­

Fortunately, Norway’s long history in offshore oil and gas offers a strong foundation. Ex­

6.2.2
How much economic support is needed for an offshore floating

Need for subsidies and scale of initial support
A clear consensus across all interviews was that floating offshore wind in Norway cannot
move forward without substantial public financial support. Experts from Statnett, Equinor,
Subsea7, and Fornybar Norge agreed that floating wind is not profitable today without
subsidies. Statnett pointed out that these projects need financial support to be viable.
Sjoner added that “floating wind will most likely need support in the coming years.”
The substantial level of support required for offshore wind was highlighted in the inter­
views. Sjoner noted that public debate in Norway often fixates on large subsidy figures,
framing them as excessive “handouts” to industry. However, he emphasized the broader
benefits: “We need to start talking about the positive effects,” he said, arguing that sub­
sidies should be weighed against long­term value, such as lower electricity prices and
a stronger energy system. Stub also pointed out that Norway’s strong public finances,
thanks to the Oil Fund, give the country room to support offshore wind, saying it’s “not
impossible to spend money in Norway if you prioritize it.”
Norway’s funding approach involves different channels. Myrvang explained that Enova,
the government’s innovation agency, supports early­stage projects but not full commer­
cial wind farms. For example, GoliatVIND Floating Wind (a 75MW demo) received NOK

total, but it doesn’t mean all that money exists in Enova’s budget right now,” he clarified.
This means the bulk of funding for large­scale floating projects will need to come from
state­backed CfDs or direct budget allocations.
The need for a long­term support framework
Although Norway has committed large subsidies for its first offshore wind projects, inter­
viewees raised concerns about the lack of a long­term support framework. Experts from
Equinor and Subsea7 warned that there is currently a gap between awarding offshore
areas and ensuring developers have a viable business case to proceed. As Sjoner ex­
plained, “There’s a disconnect between awarding areas and whether there is any business
case to build.”
Norway does not yet have a predictable support system like the UK’s annual CfD auctions,
which adds uncertainty. Sjoner stressed the need for a multi­year plan or budget that
outlines long­term targets and how much public funding will be allocated over time. He
suggested that policymakers should define “how much we’ll need… and how much we’re
willing to spend” to reach specific capacity goals. This would give developers confidence
that a support mechanism will be in place when they are ready to build.
Currently, support is only confirmed for the first two tenders, Utsira Nord and Sørlige Nord­
sjø II. Beyond those, there is little clarity. Myrvang echoed the concern, saying developers
constantly ask, “Okay, when is the next [round]? What will it look like?” Without answers,
companies may hesitate to invest, and progress could stall. Interviewees warned against
a stop­start development cycle, which can erode momentum and make it harder to build
a reliable supply chain or workforce. The consensus was clear: without a predictable
long­term support system, the 30 GW ambition will remain out of reach.

Market drivers and strategic rationale
Norway’s offshore wind push is driven more by long­term strategy than immediate en­
ergy needs. Several interviewees, including representatives from Equinor and Subsea7,
pointed out that unlike countries facing coal phaseouts or energy shortages, Norway
currently has no urgent power deficit. “Norway doesn’t need the power right now, it’s
more about industrial development and energy leadership,” explained Torkel Sjoner from
Equinor.
Electricity demand is expected to rise sharply by the 2030s, driven by new consumers
such as data centers, green hydrogen production, battery manufacturing, and the electri­
fication of transport and offshore oil platforms. “NVE says the power balance will remain
positive in 2030,” said Helge Myrvang from Subsea7, “but DNV expects it to turn slightly
negative early in the 2030s. That’s crucial for understanding how much offshore wind
we actually need in the market.” This expected shift reinforces the case for expanding
renewable generation.
A key driver, according to Sjoner, is Norway’s longstanding commitment to energy self­
sufficiency, a role the country takes pride in, historically through hydro, oil, and gas ex­
ports. The notion of becoming a net electricity importer is, as he put it, “politically and
strategically uncomfortable.” He elaborated: “Part of the current debate is about how ex­
pensive it is to build in Norway, and that it might be cheaper to import electricity from other
countries. But if we go down that road, we risk ending up as a net importer of power.”
While importing cheaper electricity might appear attractive in the short term, Sjoner em­
phasized it undermines Norway’s tradition of energy independence. Offshore wind devel­
opment, in contrast, reinforces it.
Building a domestic industry and reducing costs
Interviewees suggested that Norway could be well­positioned to scale up offshore wind,
given its strong starting point. “Norway is uniquely strong in marine operations, both in
planning and execution of complex projects, shipbuilding, and sheltered waters where tur­
bines can be assembled and anchored before being towed to sea,” the Subsea7 represen­
tative explained. “You don’t find that anywhere else, really, outside Europe.” Companies
like Equinor, Subsea7, and Aker bring relevant experience, and Norwegian shipyards
are already involved in fabricating floating wind platforms like those used in the Hywind
projects.
However, this industrial foundation needs continuity to thrive. “If Norway only builds just
one 500 MW project and then nothing for years, it’s a problem,” warned Sindre Østby
Stub from Fornybar Norge. Several interviewees stressed the need for a predictable and
steady rollout of projects to avoid stop­start cycles.
Standardization is also critical to bringing costs down. Early floating wind farms were
expensive one­offs, but interviewees explained that a pipeline of similar projects could
enable mass production of platforms and mooring systems. “It’s not a given that these
are expensive structures… there’s a lot to gain from mass production,” said Stub. Drawing
from company experience, Sjoner noted: “From Hywind Demo to Hywind Scotland, costs
dropped 30%, and another 30% down to Hywind Tampen.” As the industry scales from
tens to hundreds of megawatts, further reductions are expected.
There was broad agreement that subsidies are a temporary but necessary mechanism.

Long­term benefits and national capacity
Interviewees repeatedly emphasized that investments in floating wind should be viewed

decades, and many components may outlast the turbines themselves. Myrvang com­

regular maintenance and upgrades, supporting repowering much like hydropower infras­

This long­term lens reframes subsidies not as continuous support but as upfront infras­

deliver low­cost electricity similar to fully depreciated hydro stations. There are also direct

clean energy system, new industrial opportunities, and the ability to meet future power
demands while maintaining its status as an energy exporter.

How extensive are the grid upgrades required to facilitate such

Upgrading the existing grid for offshore integration
All stakeholders agreed that integrating large volumes of offshore wind will require ma­
jor grid upgrades. Fortunately, much of this work is already underway. Statnett, Nor­
way’s transmission system operator, is in the process of renewing infrastructure built in
the 1960s and 70s, often increasing capacity in the process, for example, by upgrading
300 kV lines to 420 kV. While these reinvestments are primarily driven by rising domes­
tic electricity demand, they also enhance the grid’s readiness to accommodate offshore
wind.
Statnett already manages a large portfolio of grid reinforcement projects, with much of the
onshore network expected to be reinvested by the 2030s and 2040s. These efforts involve
replacing ageing infrastructure and increasing transfer capacity across key corridors of the
grid.
With planned upgrades and strategic siting of offshore wind projects, Statnett estimates
that the grid will be able to handle between 10 and 15 GW of offshore wind by 2040. This
aligns with expectations among several stakeholders that around 15 GW may realistically
be developed within that timeframe.
To optimize integration, early offshore wind projects should be geographically distributed
and located near strong onshore grid nodes. Norway’s coastal transmission system, orig­
inally designed to support hydropower exports, offers robust infrastructure in several re­
gions, including Bergen, Grenland, Stavanger/Sørlandet, Møre og Romsdal, and Helge­
land. Clustering large volumes, such as 5­10 GW, in a single region would likely exceed
local capacity. However, a more distributed approach reduces bottlenecks and makes
better use of the existing grid.
This logic is reflected in current plans: Sørlige Nordsjø II will connect in the south, Utsira
Nord on the west coast, and future areas may open in the north. Even so, reaching 15
GW will still require additional grid investments. Given the long lead times for transmission
infrastructure, Statnett emphasizes the importance of a phased approach to ensure timely
reinforcement without the risk of overbuilding.
Stub acknowledged that integrating the full 30 GW would be “not just a trivial thing to do,”
but remains possible with proper planning.
Strategic siting
The location of offshore wind projects relative to the grid and other energy sources is key
to successful integration. Interviewees consistently stressed “grid­smart” siting, locating
projects near existing capacity and balancing resources like hydropower. “You should
ideally build in regions where you already have a lot of hydropower, so that you have the
possibility to manage the balance,” said Myrvang.
Norway’s flexible hydropower system complements offshore wind well. Reservoirs can
hold back water during high wind periods and release it when wind drops. “Hydropower
can be used as a battery, absolutely,” the Subsea7 engineer explained. “Feed more hy­
dropower into the grid when there’s no solar or wind power production.” This reduces
limitation and helps ensure stability.

Scaling challenges beyond 15 GW
While Norway can likely integrate up to 15 GW of offshore wind through smart siting and
manageable grid upgrades, moving toward the full 30 GW target significantly increases
the complexity. According to Statnett, this level of generation would produce around 150
TWh annually, more than Norway’s current electricity consumption, including all industry.
Since offshore wind is not meant to replace hydropower, but rather to add to existing
capacity, a corresponding increase in electricity demand will be necessary. Without this,
a substantial share of the power would need to be exported.
Geography adds another layer of challenge. Most identified offshore wind areas are lo­

power production. With near­shore zones like Søndervind excluded after environmental
assessments, much of the offshore wind from western and northern regions will have to
be transmitted over long distances to reach the Oslo area. This will require significant
reinforcement of key transmission corridors across the country.
According to Statnett, grid connection costs rise as projects move further offshore. Wind
farms beyond 100 km typically require HVDC technology, which is much more expensive
than standard AC. Shared infrastructure like offshore hubs and hybrid interconnectors
may help reduce costs in the future, but these solutions add complexity and require long
lead times.
In short, the shift from 15 GW to 30 GW is not just a question of doing more, it’s about
doing things differently. The first half of the target can be reached through scaling what
already works; the second half will require a broader transformation of Norway’s power
system, industrial policy, and grid infrastructure.

how offshore grids can be developed over time, recognizing that offshore infrastructure is
still far less mature than onshore systems.
International cooperation was also described as a key priority. Statnett is actively engaged

recently published Offshore Network Development Plan (ONDP) was highlighted as an
example of this broader collaboration.

Comparable economic estimates from literature
The NHO–BCG report [49] estimates that developing 4­5 GW of floating wind in Norway

to 28 million NOK per MW, equal to roughly 9 billion to 14 billion NOK for a 500 MW

mechanism helps limit overall state exposure, making the support level estimated for Ves­

wind of 159 USD/MWh, which converts to about 175 øre/kWh at 1 USD = 11 NOK. IRENA

Discussion
This chapter discusses the findings in light of the research questions and considers their
broader implications. The discussion is divided into five sections: One for each research
question, followed by a reflection on the study’s methods and a final section addressing
uncertainties and limitations.
7.1
RQ1 ­ 30 GW allocation goal
The Norwegian government’s goal of allocating 30 GW of offshore wind areas by 2040
is primarily a political ambition, not an engineering constraint. Interviewees emphasized
that achieving formal allocation is realistic if it remains a political priority, but warned that
this alone does not guarantee that projects will be built.
Several stakeholders stressed that true progress must be measured by installed capacity,
not just area allocation. Without a clear plan for future auctions, subsidy schemes, and
regulatory processes, developers could lose interest and shift investments elsewhere. As
Sindre Østby Stub from Fornybar Norge put it, “they don’t know when the next round
comes”, highlighting the risk of uncertainty. Similarly, an Equinor representative stressed
the importance of “simplifying and shortening the process towards investment decisions,
without compromising rigor.”
The two­phase tender system was seen as a step in the right direction, but interviewees
agreed that more predictable timelines are critical for maintaining momentum. A national
offshore wind strategy, expected before the next election, is seen as a vital tool to outline
long­term capacity goals, support mechanisms, and licensing schedules.
In addition to economic and technical challenges, public acceptance was identified as an
important success factor. Historical parallels with Norway’s hydropower and oil industries
suggest that initial opposition can shift to broad support if the societal benefits are clear
and well­communicated [68].
Although moving turbines offshore reduces visual intrusion, it does not eliminate on­land
impacts. Large construction and O&M ports must be upgraded or newly built, bringing
both employment, noise, and increased traffic to coastal communities. In addition, each
offshore project still requires at least one land connection for power cables and often
significant upgrades to the onshore grid. These corridors, converter stations, and sub­
stations create new visual, acoustic, and environmental footprints. [69] As the Equinor
representative noted, “Floating offshore wind isn’t necessarily that far from land. Utsira
Nord, for example, is only about 20 kilometers from shore. You’ll be able to see it from
the island.” Therefore, public acceptance cannot be assumed simply because the rotors
are located at sea.
The evidence indicates that while allocating 30 GW of offshore wind areas by 2040 is a
realistic policy goal, actual installed capacity may be significantly lower. Projections from
Statnett and DNV suggest a likely range between 5 and 15 GW, depending on how ef­
fectively early challenges are addressed. Reaching the upper end of this range will likely
require strong political commitment, clearer regulatory processes, and consistent invest­
ment signals. At the same time, several uncertainties remain. Political shifts, permitting
delays, local opposition, or limited investor appetite could all slow progress. If these barri­
ers are not addressed, installed capacity by 2040 could fall below 5 GW, despite available
areas and technical potential.

7.2
RQ2 ­ Financial support for floating wind
With a NPV of minus 13.98 billion NOK and an IRR of only 2.4 percent, the project falls
well short of investor expectations. Bridging the gap would require around 27.6 million
NOK in public support per megawatt, a figure that is supported by estimates from external
sources.
Both interviews and literature reinforced the conclusion that substantial upfront subsidies
are essential. Expert interviews consistently emphasized that early public support is not
optional, but critical. As Torkel Sjoner from Equinor noted, “We need to start talking about
the positive effects”, highlighting that support should be framed as a strategic investment
rather than a simple financial expense. Similarly, Sindre Østby Stub from Fornybar Norge
stressed Norway’s ability to prioritize funding such projects, stating, “it’s not impossible to
spend money in Norway if you prioritize it.”
The case study puts hard numbers on this challenge. To break even without any upfront
grant, Vestavind B would require a two­sided CfD strike price of 1730 NOK/MWh, approx­
imately 0.74 NOK/kWh above the average nominal wholesale price of 988 NOK/MWh
assumed in analysis.
Norway’s decision to support Utsira Nord through a direct investment grant with a claw­
back mechanism, rather than a traditional CfD, reflects an effort to back early floating wind
projects while limiting long­term financial exposure. As outlined in the ESA decision, this
approach responds to the high cost uncertainty of floating wind, where few commercial
benchmarks exist. The grant lowers initial investment barriers, and the clawback en­
sures that developers return excess profits if electricity prices rise above a set threshold,
thereby protecting public funds. This allows the state to support innovation while placing
more market risk on developers.
In contrast, bottom­fixed projects like Sørlige Nordsjø II were awarded two­sided CfD,
offering stable revenues for developers but exposing the government to greater fiscal
risk. CfD are generally suited to technologies with predictable costs and financing needs,
whereas grant­based models with clawbacks may be more appropriate when early­stage
uncertainty is high and long­term price guarantees are harder to justify [28, 30].
Capital grants reduce the initial cost burden, while CfD or market premiums link support
to energy production, encouraging efficient operation. Combining these tools allows for
more balanced risk­sharing between developers and the state. Such hybrid models are
gaining attention in international offshore wind policy as a practical way to support early
floating wind deployment [70]. However, while CfD offer revenue stability, they can also
lead to high public costs if market prices surge.
France’s AO5 tender for the 250 MW Pennavel floating wind project cleared at €86/MWh
(≈1.0NOK/kWh), a record low made possible largely because the French TSO RTE cov­
ers all offshore grid connection costs, including export cables, substations, and onshore
integration. Combined with favourable site conditions and an inflation­indexed CfD, this
illustrates how national frameworks and cost­allocation models can shape strike prices.
[71]
This is comparable to the Dutch model, where the government delivers fully prepared sites
with permits and surveys already completed. While France reduces costs by covering all
grid connection infrastructure through the TSO, both approaches lower the overall cost
burden for developers. By investing heavily upfront, the state reduces the need for large
direct subsidies later in the process.

Importantly, there is widespread agreement that subsidies will not be permanent. Literature­
based cost projections suggest that the LCOE for floating wind could fall significantly over
the next two decades. Offshore Norge estimates LCOEs falling from 110­160 øre/kWh
in 2030 to 45­85 øre/kWh by 2050 [66]. Other sources such as WindEurope and IRENA

7.3
RQ3 ­ Grid upgrades needed
The extent of grid upgrades needed to accommodate offshore wind in Norway depends
on the pace and scale of deployment. Statnett, the TSO, expressed strong confidence
that the grid will not be a bottleneck for the first 10­15 GW. Planned reinforcements, such
as upgrading 300 kV lines to 420 kV, renewing infrastructure, and expanding substations,
are already underway. Norway’s hydropower­based system provides a strong starting
point, enabling smoother offshore wind integration compared to more rigid grids.
Interviewees emphasized the importance of geographical distribution. Projects are spread
across different landing points, such as Kristiansand for Sørlige Nordsjø II, Haugesund
for Utsira Nord, and the Bergen area for Vestavind B. This reduces stress on individual
transmission corridors and helps avoid major inland expansions in the short term.
Scaling beyond 15 GW will require new infrastructure. Most offshore areas are located far
from Norway’s main demand centers in the southeast, implying a need for long­distance
transmission corridors, HVDC links, and possibly a meshed offshore grid. Interviewees
highlighted that planning for these upgrades must start early to avoid future bottlenecks.
Hydropower flexibility also plays a key role. Several participants noted that hydropower
can act as a stabilizer for variable wind output, reducing the need for storage and allowing
grid upgrades to focus on capacity rather than variability management.
Expanding the electricity grid for offshore wind will also impact nearby towns and cities.
Building construction ports brings short­term jobs and requires space along the coast,
while service ports offer long­term employment but need to be accessible to vessels
throughout the year [69]. Conversely, new transmission lines and converter stations may
face opposition from residents and environmental groups. Balancing these positive and
negative onshore effects will be critical for maintaining public support as capacity moves
beyond 10–15 GW. While this report does not explore public opposition to grid upgrades
in depth, it remains a relevant topic and could be an important area for future research.

This study combined a quantitative case study, qualitative interviews, and a literature
review to strengthen the robustness of the findings. Together, these methods provided
both economic analysis and broader policy context for floating offshore wind in Norway.
The case study offered a structured evaluation based on NPV, IRR, and LCOE calcula­
tions. A discounted cash flow model and basic sensitivity tests identified key cost drivers,

market uncertainty. Electricity prices were varied across years, but only through yearly

Semi­structured interviews added valuable insights on policy frameworks and developer

source limited the diversity of market scenarios. Including estimates from NVE, DNV, or
private analysts could have improved the robustness further.
7.4.1
Key sources of uncertainty
Electricity prices
Future electricity prices are highly uncertain and critically influence both NPV and IRR out­
comes. Statnett’s projections, summarized in Table 4.2, rely on numerous assumptions,
each carrying a high degree of unpredictability. Historical trends highlight this volatility:
electricity prices were unusually low in 2020 due to mild weather, strong hydropower pro­
duction, and high reservoir levels, before rising sharply in 2021 and 2022 following gas
supply disruptions and resource challenges in Southern Norway [54].
In the analysis, project revenue is directly exposed to market prices, meaning even small
deviations from assumptions can significantly impact profitability. This was confirmed
through sensitivity analysis, which showed that changes in electricity price assumptions
had a major effect on project outcomes. A CfD can reduce this risk by stabilizing revenues
around a fixed price.
Financial timing and currency risks
Commissioning delays push expected income further into the future, increasing inflation­
related costs and weakening overall financial performance. Although these risks were
not included in the sensitivity analysis, they could have a substantial impact on project
viability and are highly plausible in real­world scenarios.
Additionally, many offshore wind components used in this thesis are priced in EUR, while
project revenues are in NOK. Exchange rate fluctuations can therefore significantly affect
project costs. Even small currency shifts can lead to large cost changes, and historical
trends show that the NOK/EUR exchange rate has been highly volatile over time, empha­
sizing the seriousness of this risk.
External political and economic factors
The analysis assumes a relatively stable policy environment for offshore wind. However,
changes in political priorities, reduced government support, or delays in regulatory ap­
provals could significantly impact project timelines. Such developments could push in­
stalled capacity targets further back, and there is a real possibility that less than 5 GW
could be operational by 2030.
In addition, broader geopolitical and economic developments represent important uncer­
tainties. Recent world events, including international conflicts and shifting trade relation­
ships, have shown how global instability can quickly affect financial markets. Such risks

Human error
As with any complex research effort, there is a possibility of human error, whether through
incorrect modeling assumptions, data interpretation mistakes, or analytical oversights.
This remains an inherent uncertainty of the study.
7.4.2
Limitations
Technical site data
The study did not include a detailed technical wind resource assessment for Vestavind B.
Wind data was taken from the Global Wind Atlas and showed good agreement with NVE’s
estimates for the area. However, factors such as wake losses, yearly wind variability,
turbulence, and detailed energy yields were not modeled. Losses from AC transmission
to shore were also not included. The focus was on evaluating economic feasibility.
Social and environmental limitations
The interview analysis primarily reflects the views of industry players, developers, and
grid operators. Perspectives from environmental groups and local communities were not
included, limiting the scope of the social and environmental assessment. Although off­
shore wind generally faces fewer conflicts than onshore projects, investigating social and
environmental perspectives could still provide valuable insights.
Timing and data availability
The study is based on data and reports available at the time of writing. Given the rapid
development of the offshore wind sector, some new information was released close to the
submission deadline. As a result, some findings may not reflect the very latest develop­
ments in policy, technology, or market conditions.
Scaling factor
The scaling factors used in the analysis are not derived from detailed engineering or eco­
nomic models, but rather based on informed judgment and common sense. They are
included primarily to demonstrate awareness of how project characteristics may change
at larger scale, rather than to provide precise forecasts.

Conclusion
This thesis examined the feasibility of developing floating offshore wind in Norway through
three central research questions. Using a combination of expert interviews, quantitative
modeling, and literature review, the study identified both the sector’s strong potential and
the key technical, economic, and regulatory hurdles ahead. This conclusion summarizes
the main insights for each research question, offering a balanced view of what can real­
istically be expected for Norway’s floating wind ambitions by 2040 and beyond.
8.1
Will the Norwegian government achieve its goal of
allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind by 2040?
Norway is likely to achieve the formal goal of allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind
by 2040, provided that area planning remains a political focus. However, interviews and
official reports consistently stressed that allocation alone will not guarantee 30 GW of
installed capacity by that date.
The difference between area designation and actual project realization emerged as a
central concern. As one stakeholder put it, “it’s not a goal to just allocate a bunch of
areas if you’re not going to get it done,” highlighting that the risk lies in ambition without
execution. Statnett’s planning assumes a maximum closer to 15 GW of installed capacity
by 2040, and most interviewees placed realistic outcomes between 5 and 15 GW. This is
also reflected by DNV which forecasts around 21 GW installed by 2050, with most growth
after 2040. NHO­BCG also anticipates 4­5 GW to be installed in the first commercial
phase by the early to mid­2030s [52, 49].
Reaching even 5­10 GW will require faster permitting, expanded supply chains, timely grid
readiness, and steady demand growth. Although Norway has strong offshore expertise
and excellent wind resources, the scale­up will require careful coordination, particularly in
regulation and marine spatial planning.
In addition to technical and policy factors, environmental and social considerations will
also shape progress. Floating offshore wind projects require onshore infrastructure, in­
cluding harbor upgrades, substations, and grid reinforcements, which can generate local
opposition if not handled transparently. Clear permitting processes, thorough environ­
mental assessments, and early engagement are essential to build trust. As one stake­
holder noted, “Communities need to see a utility value in these projects to stay supportive,”
highlighting the importance of visible local benefits such as jobs and infrastructure.
Importantly, Norway’s 30 GW ambition is strategic rather than urgent. Unlike some coun­
tries facing power shortages, Norway’s energy system is stable, and the offshore wind
plan supports long­term climate and industrial strategies rather than immediate energy
needs. As such, the 2040 goal should be seen as a directional milestone rather than a
firm construction deadline.
In summary, Norway is well positioned to allocate the necessary areas by 2040. However,
what truly matters is not the allocated space, but how much capacity is actually installed.
Based on current planning and stakeholder expectations, installed capacity by 2040 will
likely fall between 5 and 15 GW, with the rest materializing after 2040 as part of a longer­
term buildout. The core challenge lies not in identifying space, but in delivering projects
on time and at scale.

of early­stage financial support.
This finding aligns with industry and literature estimates, which suggest early floating
projects will require between 20 and 30 million NOK per megawatt. Reports such as the
NHO­BCG analysis confirm that similar levels of support, around 20 to 25 million NOK per
megawatt, will be necessary for Norway’s first 4 to 5 gigawatts of floating capacity [49],
and Menon Economics estimates a financing gap of 18 to 28 million NOK per megawatt
for projects in the 500 to 750 megawatt range [53].
Expert interviews reinforced that public financial support is essential for early­stage float­
ing wind development. Developers consistently emphasized that mechanisms like direct
grants, operational subsidies, or CfDs are critical to making early investments feasible.
Without predictable support structures, there is a risk that investments will shift elsewhere.
Public support should not be viewed as a sunk cost but rather as a strategic investment to
enable cost reductions through scaling, standardization, and industrial learning. Literature
projections suggest that the LCOE for floating wind could fall to 40­60 EUR/MWh by 2030
and even lower by the late 2030s [50, 51, 49]. If these trends are realized, projects
commissioned after 2040 may operate with minimal or no public subsidies.
In conclusion, early floating offshore wind farms in Norway will require substantial state
support, estimated at around 20 to 30 million NOK per megawatt. However, this require­
ment is expected to decline significantly as the industry matures, learning effects accu­
mulate, and economies of scale are achieved.
8.3
How extensive are the grid upgrades required to facilitate
such growth?
Norway’s electricity grid will need significant upgrades to integrate large volumes of off­
shore wind, but the extent of these upgrades depends on the speed and scale of deploy­
ment. Current plans appear well­positioned to support 10­15 GW by 2040, assuming that
upgrades stay on schedule and new projects are geographically spread.
Statnett is confident that the grid will not be a bottleneck for this level of offshore wind
integration. Planned reinforcements, such as upgrading lines from 300 kV to 420 kV and
renewing aging infrastructure, are already underway. Additionally, Norway’s grid benefits
from its strong hydropower legacy, providing a solid foundation for integration.
Geographical distribution of projects plays a key role in managing grid pressure.
By
spreading landing points, such as those for Sørlige Nordsjø II, Utsira Nord, and Vestavind
B, Norway reduces the need for major new transmission lines in the short term.
Achieving the full 30 GW target will require larger investments, including long­distance
corridors and additional interconnectors. This will need proactive planning over the com­
ing decades. However, Statnett has emphasized that this expansion is feasible, provided
there is proper coordination between grid planning and offshore wind deployment. They
also acknowledge that timelines are long, giving them sufficient time to plan and imple­
ment necessary upgrades.

Future Work
While this study provides insight into the economic feasibility, regulatory development,
and grid integration challenges of floating offshore wind in Norway, further research is
needed to capture evolving dynamics.
Longitudinal studies following public attitudes as projects move from planning to opera­
tion could provide valuable perspectives on the social acceptance of offshore wind. Future
work could also investigate public opposition to grid upgrades, which was not deeply ex­
plored in this study but may become more important as Statnett accelerates expansion
projects. This could include interviews with environmental organizations, local communi­
ties, and other affected stakeholders to better capture a broader range of views.
More detailed technical assessments of site specific factors, such as wake effects, turbu­
lence, and transmission losses through AC cables, could help improve the accuracy of
the economic model.
In addition, future economic analyses could expand sensitivity testing to include project
commissioning delays and apply multi­variable methods such as Monte Carlo simulation
to capture combined uncertainties. Delays in project start­up would push revenues further
into the future and increase exposure to inflation and financing costs.
Currency fluctuations between the euro and the Norwegian krone could also be explored
in more detail, as exchange rate changes may affect project outcomes. These risks were
not explicitly modeled in this study but could have a major impact under real world condi­
tions.
While simplified scaling factors were applied. A more in­depth investigation of how costs,
risks, and performance parameters evolve with project size would be a valuable contribu­
tion.
Lastly, while the study focused on economic feasibility, future work could also quantify
CO2 reductions from floating wind by comparing expected production to emissions from
fossil fuel based alternatives. This would help position offshore wind more clearly within
Norway’s climate mitigation strategy.
Together, these areas of future research can contribute to a more comprehensive under­
standing of floating offshore wind development in Norway.

A.1
Semi­structured interview guide
This interview guide was used to conduct semi­structured interviews with stakeholders
involved in the Norwegian offshore wind sector. The goal was to explore perspectives
related to the feasibility of allocating 30 GW of offshore wind by 2040, the level of financial
support required for floating offshore wind, and the extent of grid upgrades needed.
Introduction & Personal Opening Questions
1. Personal Connection
• Can you tell me a little about where you’re from and what you enjoy doing
outside of work?
• What drew you to working in the energy sector?
2. Transition to Work
• How did your journey lead you to your current role in [their field or company]?
• What excites you most about working in renewable energy?
Research Question 1: Will the Norwegian government achieve
its goal of allocating areas for 30 GW of offshore wind
capacity by 2040?
1. Policy Framework
• What do you think about Norway’s policy framework for offshore wind develop­
ment now? (e.g., Ocean Energy Act, NVE role, Enova grants)
• Are the current policies sufficient to support the allocation of areas for 30 GW
of capacity by 2040? Why or why not?
• What regulatory or political challenges might hinder achieving this goal?

2. Area Allocation
• From your perspective, how effective has the process been for allocating areas
for offshore wind development in Norway?
• Are there any notable barriers (e.g., environmental, legal, or stakeholder op­
position)? What are the most common barriers these projects face?
3. Stakeholder Involvement
• How are key stakeholders (e.g., local communities, developers, environmental
groups) being engaged in the planning process?
• What strategies could improve stakeholder involvement and public acceptance?
4. Global Comparisons
• How does Norway’s offshore wind ambition compare to other countries with
similar goals? What lessons could be learned?
Research Question 2: How much economic support is needed
for an offshore floating wind farm?
1. Economic Viability
• What factors most influence the economic feasibility of floating offshore wind
projects in Norway?
2. Subsidies and Support Mechanisms
• How crucial are subsidies or support schemes for making floating wind farms
viable?
• Are current Norwegian support mechanisms (e.g., Enova funding) sufficient?
What improvements could be made?
3. Market Conditions
• How do market dynamics (e.g., electricity prices, competition, supply chain)
affect the economic feasibility of floating offshore wind farms?
• What role could international collaboration or investments play in reducing costs?
4. Future Projections
• In your opinion, how long will it take for floating wind to become cost­competitive
with onshore wind?
5. Onshore vs. Offshore Wind Growth
• How would you compare the growth of onshore wind farms in Norway to the
development of offshore wind projects?
• What factors have contributed to the differences in their growth trajectories,
such as public acceptance, regulatory frameworks, or economic viability?
Research Question 3: How extensive are the grid upgrades
required to facilitate such growth?
1. Current Grid Capacity
