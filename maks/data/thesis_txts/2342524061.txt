Project Description 	
When applying emerging technologies to hardware products, certain 
aspects must be considered during the product design process in order 
to reduce the risk of non-adoption when launched to market. This the-
sis concerns the development of the ‘Design for Technology Adoption’ 
framework, which focuses on value creation and risk assessment in the 
early phases of the product development process. The main focus of 
this thesis is on the application of emerging technologies in wearable 
products.

V
Abstract 
DTU MAN
Department of Management Engineering
Master Thesis in Design & Innovation
Design for Technology Adoption
by Marina Ejlertsen & Martin Pasfall
The aim of this thesis was to develop a framework to aid start-up companies working with 
the application of emerging technologies in new wearable hardware products. This framework 
aims to help with a proper estimation of the value adding potential in product development. 
The framework addresses a ‘technology push’ while most known design methods are triggered 
by a ‘market need’. This framework, the Design for Technology Adoption (DfTA) framework, 
aims to reduce risks in the product development process by conducting early-stage assessments 
of technology risk and current (relevant) market trends. Additionally, it further evaluates user 
insights in product interactions and perceived value creation.
The framework has been developed through an iterative process of understanding relevant lit-
erature, conducting case tests, and receiving stakeholder feedback. The literature study covered 
current and relevant literature on technology adoption, technology risk parameters, user-cen-
tered design, and product development methods. The development of the framework included 
testing on past product development cases where the adoption outcome was known. Decisions 
made within each iteration were supported by insights gained from interviews with relevant 
stakeholders including experienced entrepreneurs, management experts, and researchers on 
emerging technologies and market trends. 
The DfTA framework was furthermore applied to a current product development case in col-
laboration with the Danish start-up company Plato. This framework was applied to the current 
[June 2016] device in order to assess the technology risk, market potential, and perceived value 
creation by the intended users. The outcome of this was a list of recommendations for Plato on 
how to improve their device before market launch in order to reduce the risk of non-adoption.
The final outcome consists of this thesis report, with an attached appendix, and a separate DfTA 
Assessment Guide. The DfTA Assessment Guide serves as a management tool. It is suitable to 
be used by a project team involved in an ongoing product development process.    
Keywords: Design for Technology Adoption, DfTA, Design for X, DfX, Technology Adoption, Product 
Development, Project Management, Risk Management

Målet med dette speciale var at et framework (arbejdsramme) der skal hjælpe start-up virksom-
heder der arbejder med applikering af spirende teknologier i nye wearable hardware produkter. 
Dette framework søger at hjælpe med en god estimering af det værdiskabende potentiale i pro-
duktudviklingen. Frameworket tager udgangspunkt i et ’teknologi skub’, hvor de fleste kendte 
designmetoder tager udgangspunkt i et ’markedsbehov’. Dette framework, Design for Teknolo-
gi Adoption (DfTA) frameworket, har til formål at reducere risici i produktudviklingsproces-
sen ved at foretage vurderinger i de tidlige faser af teknologiske risici og aktuelle (relevante) 
markedstendenser. Ydermere evaluerer det brugerindsigter i produktinteraktioner og opfattet 
værdiskabelse.
Frameworket er blevet udviklet gennem en iterativ proces med forståelse af relevant litteratur, 
udføre case tests samt modtage tilbagemeldinger fra interessenter. Litteraturstudiet dækkede 
tidssvarende og relevant litteratur omkring adoption af teknologi, risikoparametre for teknologi, 
bruger-orienteret design og metoder til produktudvikling. Udviklingen af frameworket inklu-
derede test på produktudviklingscases fra fortiden med et kendt adoptionsudfald. Beslutninger 
foretaget inden for hver iteration var underbygget af indsigter fra interview med relevant inter-
essenter, inklusiv erfarne entreprenører, ledelseseksperter og forskere i spirende teknologier og 
markedstrends.
DfTA frameworket var ydermere anvendt på et nuværende produktudviklingsforløb i samarbe-
jde med den danske start-up virksomhed Plato. Frameworket blev anvendt på deres nuværende 
[Juni 2016] enhed for at vurdere teknologisk risici, markedspotentiale og opfattet værdiskabelse 
for brugerne. Resultatet af dette var en række anbefalinger til Plato for hvordan deres enhed kan 
forbedres for det lanceres på markedet for at reducere risikoen for ikke-adoption.
Det endelige resultat består af denne specialerapport med tilhørende appendiks, samt en separat 
DfTA vurderingsguide. DfTA vurderingsguiden fungerer som et ledelsesværktøj. Det er velegnet 
til at blive brugt af et projekt team der er involveret i en igangværende produktudviklingsproces.
Nøgleord: Design for Teknologi Adoption, DfTA, Design for X, DfX, Teknologi Adoption, Produktud-
vikling, Projektledelse, Risikoledelse

Structure of the Thesis
This thesis consist of the following 8 chapters:
Chapter 1: Introduction
Background information about the origin of this thesis and the scope of the project followed by 
the thesis’ aim, hypotheses, research questions, and the project approach is introduced.
Chapter 2: Literature Study
The theoretical base for the DfTA framework is covered including existing theories and me-
thodical approaches on the subjects of technology adoption and assessment of development risk.

This chapter serves as a link between the literature study and empirical insights. It further intro-
duces the first iterations of the framework.

The framework development process is chronologically presented. This includes interviews and 
feedback from experts on the theories, methods, case tests, and the management approach used.

The DfTA framework is applied to the current Plato device as a case study.

Presentation of the final DfTA framework based on the insights and conclusions made through-
out this thesis.

The framework including background, validity, future implications, and further research is dis-
cussed.
Chapter 8: Conclusions
This chapter covers main conclusions made throughout this thesis.

reference, it is referred to as (web: Website Year).

test conclusions. References to the appendix appear as [AXX].

XI
Nomenclature
Design for Technology Adoption (DfTA) – Our title for the framework developed during this 
thesis. The name was chosen based on the framework main intention, as it serves to address 
product design considerations in reference to technology adoption concerns. The framework 
is an assessment tool that can be used in the product development process when working with 
emerging technologies.
Design for “X” (DfX) – Design for “X” covers a wide range of specific design guidelines which 
address a given issue concerning a product’s design from a knowledge based view. The design 
guidelines provide an approach and corresponding methods to help generate and apply techni-
cal knowledge to control, improve, or invent particular traits of a product (Krause 2014).
Emerging technology - Emerging technologies are technologies that are perceived as capable of 
changing the status quo in society. They are characterized by the following five attributes: rad-
ical novelty, relatively fast growth, coherence, prominent impact, and uncertainty or ambiguity 
(Rotolo et al. 2015). In this thesis we define the term ‘Emerging Technology’ as either a new 
technology in terms of new scientific discovery or a combination of existing technologies that 
enables for something new to be made/ to form
Framework (Theoretical) – “Theories are formulated to explain, predict, and understand phenomena 
and, in many cases, to challenge and extend existing knowledge within the limits of critical bounding 
assumptions. The theoretical framework is the structure that can hold or support a theory of a research 
study. The theoretical framework introduces and describes the theory that explains why the research 
problem under study exists” (Swanson & Chermack 2013).
Framework (Design) – A design framework outlines the (stepwise) approach represented in a 
given design process. The framework creates a guideline on how to design for a given purpose 
e.g. user involvement, ‘Design for X,’ or sustainability. The creation of a design framework re-
volves around acknowledged design methods and is based on a theory of how a design process 
can be structured in order to reach a given outcome.
Hardware products – The use of the term ‘hardware products’ in this thesis refers to products 
that are physical and consist of physical components. This term can be seen as the opposite to 
software products as these are not tangible. However, hardware products often do contain soft-
ware features integrated into the various components of the product or as the control feature.
Human Enhancement - The field of human enhancement (or human augmentation) focuses on 
creating cognitive and physical improvements as an integral part of the human body. An exam-
ple of this is using active control systems to create limb prosthetics with characteristics that can 
exceed the highest natural human performance (web: Gartner 2016b).
Wearable Technologies – Wearable technologies, or wearables, are categorized as electronic 
technologies (computers) that are incorporated into items of clothing and accessories, which 
can be worn on the body. Most wearables provide sensory and scanning features, such tracking 
of physiological actions. The data collection is used to analyze and evaluate body conditions and 
human performance (web: Wearable Devices Magazine 2014).

Learning Objectives
Through this thesis project the students will have to show that they are able to:
1.	 Gather and describe relevant literature on emerging technologies, product design meth-
ods, and strategies for technology adoption
2.	 Understand existing theories and models describing the process of defining technology 
maturity and how they relate to the development risk
3.	 Understand existing research on adoption parameters between users and new technolo-
gies
4.	 Evaluate findings with the aim of determining correlating factors between existing the-
ories and models
5.	 Include expert knowledge and receive feedback on conclusions made from the literature 
study with the aim of relating these current approaches used by both researchers and 
product development in industry
6.	 Create a framework covering a design approach on how to design for technology adop-
tion
7.	 Test the framework with various products using emerging technologies with a known 
outcome
8.	 Evaluate on the test findings and redefine the framework to align with the results 
9.	 Conduct a case study on the Plato device using the developed design framework
10.	Provide feedback to Plato on how to ease technology adoption potential for their device 
using the developed framework
11.	Document the process, test, and findings in a clear and comprehensive manner
12.	Discuss the test setup and findings, and be critical towards them
13.	Reflect on the process and describe further work needed in order to improve on the 
framework

XVII
Executive Summary
With the ambition of developing a framework that would cover the needed product develop-
ment activities for easing technology adoption we looked into existing literature on technology 
adoption theories in order to get an understanding of this subject. We attended talks and pre-
sentations covering the subjects of wearable product opportunities, start-up experiences, and 
research on new technology trends. We additionally went to the IDTechEx conference, where 
we met relevant people within research and industry and attended talks on emerging tech-
nologies with wearable technologies as the main subject. Based on the insights gained from 
theory, talking to people with startup experience, and attending IDTechEx we made a number 
of iterative drafts on (what we decided to name) the ‘Design for Technology Adoption’ (DfTA) 
framework. 
We wanted to develop a framework not only based on theoretical insights but also with a more 
practical base. We wanted to include insights from stakeholders with product development ex-
perience and contacted a number of experts in the fields of mechanical engineering, engineering 
management, technology forecasts, and technical experts to whom we sent the framework draft. 
Feedback was provided through personal interviews. These interviews enabled us to gain differ-
ent perspectives on our interpretation of the theoretical insights. The most common statement 
from the experts were that they saw a great potential in a tool like this, but as Thomas Howard 
(DTU Mechanical Engineering) put it: ‘The devil is in the detail”. 
In order to further test the potential of the DfTA framework, we applied it to two past product 
development cases with a known adoption outcome. One that succeeded at gaining market 
adoption (iPhone) and another one that failed (Segway). We wanted to see whether we, by 
applying the DfTA framework, could explain why these outcomes had happened. In both cases 
the main challenges was identified within the User Experience (UX). What we learned from 
this testing was that we were able to identify specific challenges related to technology adoption, 
but we also acknowledged that the framework still needed further work before it could be used 
as an assessment tool by anyone else other than ourselves.
From a second round of interviews we learned that the framework should have a more specific 
approach instead of trying to cover a too broad range of development cases. Jonas Richelsen 
(R&D Manager at Radiometer) said: “I think it is important that you know exactly who this 
tool is aimed for”. Based on this and our prior knowledge, we decided to address the framework 
specifically to start-up companies working with emerging technologies applied in wearable 
products.
The second round of interviews provided feedback on the usefulness of the DfTA framework 
as a management tool, as well as potential use and pitfalls. Furthermore, we conducted a work-
shop with two Design & Innovation students, who were working with a relevant case as part 
of their bachelor’s thesis. They were conducting a technology-based product development, and 
they agreed to try applying the DfTA framework and estimating the adoption potential of their 
current product concept. We gained great insights from this practical workshop and further 
understood the need to transfer our knowledge through simple ‘hands-on’ guidelines if others 
should be able to use the framework without supervision. In addition to the workshop, we also 
tested the framework with two more product development cases with a known outcome. This

time the focus was on wearable products. We used the Google Glass (Augmented Reality glass-
es) as the failed case of technology adoption, and Fitbit (fitness tracker) as the successful case. It 
was possible to identify a list of parameters explaining why one failed while the other succeeded, 
and again the conclusion pointed towards challenges within the UX parameters. 
The DfTA framework reached its 5th iteration and we decided to apply it onto a current devel-
opment case in order to test how it would estimate a yet unknown adoption potential. For this 
we collaborated with the Danish start-up company Plato and used their ‘Plato device’ as the 
product case. The Plato device aims to improve brain performance through electrical stimula-
tion. The outcome from our DfTA assessment of the current prototype [June 2016] was that the 
Plato device was estimated to live up all of the DfTA requirements, except for challenges with 
the UX parameters. We therefore set out to conduct UX testing and arranged two test setups: 
one with 7 test subjects who performed a ‘problem solving’ test both prior to and during brain 
stimulation and one with another 7 test subjects who brought their own current projects to work 
on while receiving brain stimulation. The two test setups additionally differed by the test people’s 
knowledge about the Plato device prior to the testing.
From the UX testing we learned that the products itself and its current features would have a 
good fit within the intended use scenario, but whether the product will fit into the intended 
use setting as a ‘professional tool’ was still a challenge. Based on our insights, we came up with 
suggestions for how to improve the Plato device. These insights were then given to Plato as a 
list of recommendations.
After the testing with Plato we had a final draft of the DfTA framework. It was still very ‘raw’ 
and difficult for people other than us to use. The final roundup was therefore to make it under-
standable for the intended users, who probably never have heard about the DfTA framework 
before. We approached this by making an assessment guide in which the assessment methods 
are explained step-by-step and examples are included throughout the guide. The examples aim 
to ease the communication of our developed methods and to explain how to practically apply 
the framework.
We consider the most important outcome of this thesis to be the final DfTA framework. 
Through the process of developing the framework we have learned that there are many variables 
involved when addressing how users adopt technology. Since our ambition was to develop a 
framework that would cover the needed product development activities for easing technology 
adoption, we have selected the most prominent factors that we believe enables a project team to 
estimate the adoption potential. The final DfTA framework provides a structured approach for 
how to estimate the value potential and technology risk. Thereby a project team can make more 
optimal choices for ensuring technology adoption.

an overview of the DfTA assessment steps within each of the development phases. The DfTA

and further explain the overall DfTA framework process and the individual framework elements.

Introduction
1.1.	 Background
The development of microprocessors has enabled complex technologies to be integrated into 
consumer products and thereby become a part of the everyday life of modern society. As the size 
and power usage of hardware products are decreasing, the application possibilities has enabled 
the transformation of technology usage (Brown & Vranesic 2009). Computers are now integrat-
ed into consumer products, and in recent years, they have taken a further jump from our pockets 
and are now worn as wearables (web: Bloomberg 2016). Technologies are becoming more per-
sonal and moving closer to our bodies. If this trend continues, the next step will be technologies 
that either interfere or become permanently implanted onto our bodies (web: CIO 2015). But 
why is it important to look at this technology revolution? We believe that by looking at past 
technology failures and successes, we are able to learn from them. Humans have progressed by 
adopting successful solutions from others and developing them further (web: Encyclopedia Bri-
tannica 2015). But in order to succeed commercially it is equally important to understand why 
some emerging technologies of the past failed while others succeeded (web: WIRED 2010). 
Current promising technology trends include ‘Wearable technologies’ and ‘Human enhance-
ment’ (PA Consulting Group 2015). Even as promising as the potential of wearable technol-
ogies seem to be, it is just as challenging to apply emerging technologies to wearable products 
which will reach final adoption in the market (Rotolo et al. 2015). According to the latest 
Gartner Hype Cycle (GHC), wearables are now approaching the Trough of Disillusionment. This 
means that wearables as a category is starting to mature, and it is only now that the users will 
start to see the real benefits of the category (web: Gartner 2015b). Human Enhancement is now 
approaching the Peak of Expectations but is expected to be one of the ‘Hidden Champions’ in the 
following years (web: Gartner 2015b; web: PA Consulting Group 2015). The Danish start-up 
company Plato strives to align with these trends with their wearable ‘Plato device’ that stim-
ulates the brain with by using transcranial electrical stimulation (TES), and thereby enhances 
cognitive capabilities.
This thesis emerged from a collaboration with Plato, who were interested in exploring how they 
could improve the overall user experience of their Plato device in order to increase its adoption 
potential when launched into market. For more details about Plato and the Plato device see the 
appendix [A38; A39]. With the ambition of answering this question we looked into existing 
literature on how to design for technology adoption. We found a broad range of theories and 
models covering various aspects on how emerging technologies are adopted, but nowhere did we 
find a proper method for how to design or evaluate products using emerging technologies - in 
terms of their adoption potential when launched into market. We realized that there was a gap 
in the existing literature for such guidelines, so we decided to change the thesis aim to cover the 
development of a Design for Technology Adoption framework.
1.2.	 Aim of This Thesis
The aim of this thesis is to develop a framework for how to ‘Design for Technology Adoption’. The 
main focus is on the adoption of emerging technologies applied in hardware products, and fur-
ther looking into the category of wearable products. This will be done through an initial litera-
ture study followed by an empirical study where insights are gained from conducting interviews

1.3.	 Hypotheses
For this thesis there are two hypotheses. The first addresses the Literature Study and addition-
ally the Empirical Study from engaging with stakeholders. The second addresses the Case Study 
revolving around the Plato device.
The first hypothesis states that it is possible to find a general consensus in the literature of what 
drives adoption in products using emerging technologies, and that it is possible to hold these 
conclusions up against empirical insights. Furthermore it is possible to relate these aspects with 
the application of emerging technologies in wearable products.
The second hypothesis states that it is possible to create a design framework based on the liter-
ature and empirical insights of how to design for adoption of applied emerging technologies in 
hardware products, focusing on wearable products. Furthermore it is possible to test this frame-
work in the development of a new product using TES/tDCS.
The assumption is that it is possible to find a general consensus in literature for how emerging 
technologies applied in products are perceived by users and how they reach market adoption. 
This should be either supported or challenged by empirical insights from engaging with stake-
holders who possess expertise within these fields. The overall aim is to answer this question: 
What make some technology applications succeed at reaching market adoption while 
others fail?
It is furthermore assumed that the identified aspects can be tested through a case study.

a)	 How can they be applied to a wearable product using TES?

2.1.	 Why Develop a Framework?
As stated in the previous chapter, the reason why we decided to develop a framework was mainly 
based on the lack of a proper approach on how to design for technology adoption and a gap in 
existing literature. The reasons why we decided on a Design for “X” framework, and the have 
chosen to focus on the early stages of the product development process, are stated below:
Design for X
Design for “X” (DfX) covers a variety of approaches on how to conduct product design by focus-
ing on a specific optimization perspective. According to Krause (2014) “Design for “X”, covers a 
wide range of specific design guidelines, which addresses a given issue concerning a product design, from 
a knowledge based view. The design guidelines provides an approach and corresponding methods to help 
generate and apply technical knowledge to control, improve, or invent particular traits of a product” 
(Krause 2014). Examples include Design for Manufacture and Assembly, Design for Reliability, 
Design for Maintainability, and many more. Common for all of them is that they provide “sys-
tematic approaches for analyzing design from a spectrum of perspectives” and ”[i]t strengthens team-
work…” (Warwick Manufactoring Group 2007, section 5, page 1). We have chosen to develop 
a DfX framework since we aim to develop a design guideline containing the same features as 
mentioned above. It must be a systematic approach that can be used without prior experience 
with the tool. One of the main aims is to align the project team and thereby strengthening the 
team in the further development process.

2.2.	 Literature Study
In order to understand how products using emerging technologies are developed, it is important 
to understand current acknowledged theories, models, and methods used in this subject. This

2.2.1.	 Technology Adoption Curve
Over time the Technology Adoption Curve (TAC) has shown viable for representing the adopt-
ing segments across many industries and disciplines (web: Wikipedia 2003). The adoption rate 
follows a regular bell-curve and is divided over time by 5 adoption segments based on the users’ 
willingness to adopt new technologies. The TAC can be seen in Figure 6.
Figure 6.	
Technology Adoption Curve (Rogers 2003)
The TAC was first introduced by Everett M. Rogers. According to Rogers’ theory, four main el-
ements are influencing the spread of a new idea: the innovation itself, communication channels, 
time, and the social system. This process relies heavily on the users’ actions and beliefs, and the 
innovation must be widely adopted in order to self-sustain (Rogers 2003). However, this model 
only states how technology is adopted across different user segments, but not how to design for 
adoption.
Others have seen the same challenge with this model. Canada et al. has mapped the technology 
adoption curve with strategies for how to design your product for the different phases. These 
can be seen in Figure 7.
==PAGE:32==
10

Figure 7.	
Designing for an Adoption Curve (Canada et al. 2007)
The Designing for Adoption Curve is useful in order to explain for whom you are designing the 
product for and to state what is important to gain traction within this group. It aims to explain 
how to communicate with the different groups, but still, it does not say anything about how to 
design a product.
A more recent model used by management consultancies to describe the adoption rate of new 
digital technologies is the so-called Shark Fin. This model does not follow a traditionally bell-
curve, and has a higher peak at an earlier stage than the TAC. We do not use it directly in the 
framework, but it can be seen in the appendix [A22].
Crossing the Chasm
An addition to the TAC is the so-called ‘Chasm’ by Geoffrey Moore (2002). It is located be-
tween the segment of the ‘Early Adopters’ and the ‘Early Majority’. Moore had identified a the-
ory for how to cross this, called ‘Crossing the Chasm’. The Chasm illustrates the breaking point 
of whether a new technology will reach market success or not (Moore 2002) (see Figure 8).
Figure 8.	
The Technology Adoption Curve including the Chasm. Illustration from web: (Shah & Booream-

Moore identified several aspects that needed to be achieved in order to cross the chasm. Four of

Emerging Technology
Through our research we have found various definitions of ‘emerging technologies’. In a paper 
by Rotolo et al. (2015) emerging technologies are defined as technologies that are perceived as 
capable of changing the status quo in society and are characterized by the following five attri-
butes: radical novelty, relatively fast growth, coherence, prominent impact, and uncertainty or 
ambiguity (Rotolo et al. 2015).
For the DfTA approach, we introduce our own definition of emerging technologies. We define 
an it in the following way: it is either a new technology, in terms of new scientific discovery, 
or a combination of existing technologies, that enables for something new. An example of the 
latter is Virtual Reality. It consists of a number of known and proven technologies (including 
an AMOLED display, magnetometer, gyroscope, accelerometer, an ARM microcontroller and 
more (web: How Stuff Works 2016)). However, combining all of these technologies allows for 
a new application called Virtual Reality. In this thesis we see Virtual Reality as an example of 
an emerging technology. In accordance to this definition we further define our use of the term 
‘technology’ to both cover a single technology (e.g. the transistor) and technologies working 
together in ‘closed systems’ (e.g. Bluetooth).

2.2.3.	 Path Dependency and Technological Trajectory
Path dependency is the theory stating that technology, products, and innovations have a history 
and that past decisions, successes, and practices play an important role for current opportunities 
(Jørgensen 2009). What this means is that products follows a development path, and you need 
to understand that path and further develop it for users to adopt your product/technology. The 
theory on Technological Trajectory further supports this. This theory states that future develop-
ment follows a technological trajectory, and that you must follow that trajectory in order to gain 
development success (Bijker 1995).
These acknowledgements are important, as we have seen examples of otherwise good products 
failing because they were ‘too new’ or by other means did not fit in. An example, which will be 
discussed further in Chapter 4, is Google Glass (Augmented Reality glasses). As a product it 
worked as intended. But it did not follow the path of trends of preceding products, and failed at 
gaining market success.
2.2.4.	 Knowing Who You Are Designing For
In his book “Diffusion of Innovations” Everett Rogers introduces the theory of Perceived Attri-
butes (Rogers 2003). The five Perceived Attributes are written below with interpreted explana-
tions in parenthesis by J. Kalbach (Kalbach 2012):
››
Relative Advantage (Is it better?)
››
Compatibility (Does it fit in?)
››
Complexity (Is it understandable?)
››
Trialability (Can it be tested?)
››
Observability (What does it look like?)
These are used to outline the user experience when interacting with a product or service. They 
work as a guideline for how to design for a good user experience, but they do not necessarily in-
clude anything about past experiences. However, the Perceived Attributes are very useful when 
it comes to understand the users and their understanding of what value a product can add to 
their lives. 
We believe that these evaluation criteria are equally important when designing products using 
emerging technologies. The method is mainly useful for evaluating a product and not designing 
it, but it can be implemented into an iterative development process, where each prototype is held 
up against the five Perceived Attributes.
Another theory for how technology is adopted is Neuro P5. It says that any technology that 
enhances either power, profit, pleasure, pride or permanency is likely to be adopted by humans. 
For a more thorough description, see the appendix [A22].
Other theories that aim to explain how technologies are adopted by human behavior are the 
Technology Adoption Model (TAM) and the Task-Technology Fit (TTF). The TAM describes 
the human factors that determine technology adoption and aims to cover the process of tech-
nology adoption in terms of human action and reasoning (Davis et al. 1989). The TTF aims to 
explain how the capabilities of the system matches the task the user wants to perform. It does so 
==PAGE:37==
DESIGN FOR TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION
15
by looking at the task and technology characteristics and comparing how they align. The theory 
predicts that if the task and technology characteristics are aligned, we perform better at the task 
and use the tool or technology for this task more often (Goodhue & Thompson 1995). These 
theories are important as they aim to describe the reasoning in humans for both adopting new 
technologies and continual use of them. Graphic representations and more information about 
both models can be seen in the appendix [A22].
A tool that is widely used to frame the potential users is ‘Personas’ (web: Cooper 2008). Personas 
are fictional characters that are used to understand the types of users that are likely to use the 
product. This tool is qualitative and should be based on user insights. It can preferably be used 
to gain understanding of the users’ values and thereby what is important to them in terms of 
product offerings (Cooper 1998).
Another practical tool when developing product is the Hierarchy of Needs by Abraham Maslow 
(1943). Maslow suggested that there are different levels of needs from basic physiological needs 
to self-actualization (Maslow 1943). A graphical representation of this can be seen in Figure 12.
Figure 12.	 Maslow’s hierarchy of needs (redrawn from Maslow 1943)
This model can be used in the product development process. By knowing where in the hierarchy 
of needs a product is placed, it is possible to better determine the market segmentation. It can 
also act as a tool to help determine which competing factors are important for a given product.
2.2.5.	 Technology Maturity and Risk Assessment
There are a number of methods developed to assess the maturity level of a technology. One of 
the more commonly used methods within advanced technology development is the Technol-
ogy Readiness Level (TRL) by John C. Mankins (1995). According to Mankins “Technology 
Readiness Levels (TRLs) are a systematic metric/measurement system that supports assessments of the 
maturity of a particular technology and the consistent comparison of maturity between different types 
of technology” (Mankins 1995, p. 1). It is a method developed by the National Aeronautics and 
Space Administration (NASA). It is based on technologies used in space, but it can also be used 
to assess technology maturity more generally. Among others, the Department of Defense of the 
USA has adopted this scale (Azizian et al. 2009) and it is currently used in the course 41633 
==PAGE:38==
16
Literature Study
Innovation & Product Development at DTU. The TRL is a 9 level scale where TRL 1 is the 
lowest referring to basic research findings, and TRL 9 is the highest referring to flight proven 
technologies (Mankins 1995).
In addition to this, Mankins have also made the Research & Development Degree of Difficulty 
(R&D3). According to Mankins R&D3 is “A measure of how much difficulty is expected to be en-
countered in the maturation of a particular technology is needed to complement the existing Technology 
Readiness Levels (TRLs) metric” (Mankins 1998, p. 1). It is an assessment of how much difficulty 
is expected in ‘normal’ R&D efforts. It is a tool that assesses the risk in the R&D efforts on a 5 
level scale. The lowest on the scale is R&D3 1, which is “[a] very low degree of difficulty is antic-
ipated in achieving research and development objectives for this technology. Probability of Success in 
“Normal” R&D Effort [is] 99%” and then gradually increases to R&D3 5: “[t]he degree of difficulty 
anticipated in achieving R&D objectives for this technology is so high that a fundamental break-
through is required. Probability of Success in “Normal” R&D Effort [is] 20%”  (Mankins 1998, p. 1).
To get a more overall risk assessment, the Advancement Degree of Difficulty (AD2) has been 
developed based on R&D3 (Bilbro 2008). This is a scale (and an Excel tool1) that can be used to 
assess the risk in the development process more systematically. The scale is in 9 levels and aims 
to give a greater assessment of the risk involved in the R&D efforts. The scale together with 
TRL levels and the risks can be seen in the appendix [A25].
Another method by Mankins (2009) is the Technology Need Value (TNV). This scale is used 
to assess the importance of a given technology related to the overall success of the development 
project. The TNV scale goes from 1: “The technology effort is not critical at this time to the success 
of the program…” to 5: “The technology effort is critically important to the success of the program at 
present…” (Mankins 2009, p. 1212).

graphical representation of ‘Technology Risk’ and ‘Technology Performance & Maturity’ over 
time. This can be seen in Figure 13.

Sir Robert Alexander Watson-Watt has presented an interesting viewpoint on technology de-

2.3.	 Key Takeaways
Assessment parameters concerning the users, such as the Perceived Attributes and TAC can be 
used to evaluate how an idea for a product fits in with the intended use case. These methods can 
further be used to estimate the adoption potential, and detect potential pitfalls in the interaction 
between the user and the technology. These methods will from this point on be referred to as 
User Experience (UX).
Another finding in this chapter is there is that path dependency is a useful theory to help ex-
plain how technology is developed. By following the further technology trajectory, or trend, the 
theory states that it becomes easier for users to relate to the product, and they thereby are more 
likely to adopt it.
Common for these assessment parameters is that they explain how technology is adopted, but 
they do not directly state anything about how to design the products.

business (Rogers 2003). The market adoption is further determined by the adoption rate. The 
adoption rate is a term used by Rogers in the Diffusion of Innovations (2003). Rogers defines it 
as consisting of two elements: time and relative adoption. Time refers to the number of members

time frame, the technology no longer has an adoption potential in its intended application. We 
thereby define the difference between a successful and a failed adoption outcome as the difference 
between having a self-sustaining business or not (Rogers 2003). We do acknowledge that some 
products are brought to market with the intention of penetrating the market, only to pave the 
way for following products. But this is beyond the scope of the DfTA framework.
The DfTA Approach on the Product Development Process
When an emerging technology is planned to be brought to market in a new product, decisions 
concerning the technology application within the three aspects of Market, Product and Produc-
tion has to be made. This way of separating the product development into these three aspects is 
inspired by the Integrated Product Development (IPD) design method by Mogens Andreasen 
(2000). It describes the process as starting with a need and then splits the process in three. These 
are Market, Product and Production. Each of these then follow five steps in order to develop the 
product, define the production process, and prepare for market sales.
Figure 15.	 The Integrated Product Development (IPD) design method (Andreasen 2000)
Within each step various work methods and design tools are presented. This helps to ensure 
that the product is developed according to the identified ‘need’. This way of looking at the de-
velopment process with an identified ‘need’ being the trigger is aligned with the perspective that 
we are taught through our education as Design Engineers. In the DfTA approach, however, we 
define an emerging technology as the ‘trigger’ for the product development process, instead of an 
identified ‘need’. In Chapter 2 we stated that technology adoption happens through the interac-
tion between the user and the chosen application of the technology. The technology can thereby

The Market aspect covers the choices of the business plan, marketing approach, and timing for 
when to approach the market. The timing is very important, as there is a great difference in be-
ing the first mover (having to penetrate the market for new technology adoption) or the quick 
follower (having to bring a higher value with a new technology than the existing alternatives).
The Product aspect covers various choices of mechanical design, appearance, and design features 
improving the user experience. Furthermore, this aspect also covers the choice of components 
and an assessment of their respective level of maturity. It must also be evaluated how technolo-
gies can be applied in order to create value for the final users. The design of a product is highly 
important, as this creates the basis of the choices made in the Market aspect, and present the 
technology in the way intended by the Production aspect. Furthermore the product application 
is what the customer ultimately purchases.
The Production aspect covers the choice of production methods based on the product design, 
the level of maturity for the chosen components, and the interface between the chosen com-
ponents. This aspect also addresses the boundaries for the product development process, as the 
Production needs to be feasible both in terms of functions, materials, economy, and time.
Please note that the definitions of the three aspects are based on the IPD model, and further 
supported by our knowledge and experience within the area of product development, design 
processes, and business strategies.

3.1.1.	 The DfTA Product Development Process 
The way we, as Design Engineers, are taught to approach product design is that a product devel-
opment process is triggered by the detection of a conflict between user needs and the solutions 
available. The area of conflict then defines the further development process. Concepts are then 
developed and tested through use of prototyping and user studies, and often these prototypes 
are made using existing technologies. However, this is not aligned with industry practice. Here 
the aim is to be innovative and a first mover is highly desired (web: Wikipedia 2016a). We will 
briefly describe how we see the product development process in the industry from early stage 
technology development, then to identifying an application for the technology, and furthermore 
the processes for bringing this to the market.
Our findings from interviewing industry experts [A3; A11] indicates that a company inten-
tionally wants to do as little technology development as possible, as the risk associated with this 
is quite high, and the used resources might be wasted. Therefore, the fundamental technology 
development is typically carried out at universities, other research settings, or in some instances 
continued at GTS institutes such as the Danish Technological Institute [A5]. Many research 
projects do however never progress onto a proper application enabling the technology for actual 
use - unless someone discovers the right application. An example of this is the Gorilla Glass de-
veloped by Corning. This was developed in the 1960’s, but was never used until the first iPhone 
came out in 2007 (Walter Isaacson 2011).
If a company decides to use an emerging technology in their product, they will have to further 
develop the technology in order to reach a maturity level where it can be fully applied in a 
product (or wait to someone else does it!). Our findings from participating in the IDTechEx 
conference indicated that companies who were developing emerging technologies would rather 
have other companies find applications for their product than to search for the potential them-
selves. In other words, these companies have developed a technology without explicitly knowing 
==PAGE:46==
24

who was going to use it or where was going to be used, but they still they believed it had a 
future potential. They expect (or hope) that external companies will apply the technology onto 
their own products, and launch it to the market. This seems somehow very non-sustainable, as 
the risk of failure is hard to predict. In this case the role of technology trends and hype are very 
influencing factors. Another conclusion we brought from both IDTechEx and interviews with 
industry experts was that the evaluation of technology maturity and further market potential 
are mainly based on ‘gut feeling’ supported by the data available at the time of the decision. Our 
assumption is further that product evaluations are rarely done in a structured way throughout 
the development process with the same Key Performance Indicators (KPI’s) used as a reference

structured or traceable. This conflict between the uses of explicit, embedded, or implicit (tacit) 
knowledge is further stated by A. Frost as a causal failure factor if performance indicators and 
measure benefits are missing within a project. He further states that performance indicators 
influence several other causal factors, such as the ability to plan and evaluate (Frost 2014).

4.4.	 Developing the Framework
The general takeaways from receiving feedback on the framework drafts 4.0 and 4.1 are present-
ed in the following:
We realized that alignment of the project team also serves as a risk-reducing factor. Simple 
methods ensuring that all members of the project team agree on product details and goal set-
tings can prevent later misunderstanding and wasted development efforts. Feedback from ex-
perts further supported the importance of this. In order to properly estimate adoption potential 
and reduce risks, the project team needs to understand the product from multiple angles. We 
therefore chose to make the assessment parameters a key point of the framework.
The TRL, TNV, and AD2 scales turned out to be useful, albeit it could be a bit confusing to use 
if never seen before, as was the case during our workshop [A13]. If the stakeholders knew about 
the scales beforehand, and how they would affect the assessment outcome, they tended to be 
biased on how they interpreted the reasoning behind applying these! Therefore we decided to 
change the names of the scales, along with the description within each step. We did this to better 
reflect our intended use of them in the DfTA context. In addition to this we made a decision tree 
for each of the scales in order to guide the project team in assigning the right values.
The updated versions of the scales are the Technology Need Level now called Component Need 
Level (CNV) [A26], the Technology Readiness Level now called Component Maturity Level 
(CML), and Advancement Degree of Difficulty now called Relation Maturity Level (RML). 
The naming related back to the names we had assigned the different models used to assess 
technology parameters. As mentioned, the descriptions were also changed but only in terms of 
how they were communicated, so they still refer back to the original descriptions of the scales by 
Mankins (1995; 1998) and Bilbro (2008). We further decided to invert Bilbro’s scale, so all the 
scales increase in numbers with increased maturity (this also eases the communication). Graph-
ical representations of the scale-steps were also desired by the stakeholders. But since we sure 
not sure how to do this without affecting the interpretation of the scales too much, then scale 
graphics were not integrated in this update.
Jakob Andersen suggested that it should be possible to go through the model fast, ideally in no 
more than 2 hours because you have so many other things you need to do as an entrepreneur. 
Jonas Richelsen suggested that the framework could work as a management tool, but ideally 
through a workshop setting since he thinks this is the best way to align peoples thinking. This is 
something we kept in mind in the further work with the framework.
A feedback we received from multiple stakeholders, including Jonas Richelsen and Oticon, is 
that the framework needed to be more targeted towards a specific audience. Based on this we 
further defined for whom the framework is targeting. Based on that we defined the target user as 
a start-up company working with a new or emerging technology who are working with a phys-
ical (hardware) product and are willing to follow a guide to a greater or lesser extent. The main 
findings are that a jack-of-all-trades, master-of-none approach was believed not to work. Based 
on this, it was decided that the specific focus for the framework would be on start-up companies 
that makes physical (hardware) products in the area of wearable technologies.

The Market step covers the definitions of the intended market segment and current market 
trends. These assessment parameters helps identify any similar products on the market, and 
thereby whether you are performing Red or Blue Ocean development. By looking at current 
market trends useful insights from other products can be used to improve on your own product 
development.
Market assessment
The intended market segment for the Plato device is defined by the following characteristics:
Figure 39.	 Identification parameters of the intended market segment for the Plato device. The red marks indicate 
the chosen characteristics (Accumulated graphics)
The Plato customers are male and between 30 to 35 years old. They have a steady income, and 
they are located in Northern Europe or Northern America.  They are defines as ‘first movers’ and 
have intentionally heard about tDCS before being introduced to Plato. They are so-called ‘tech 
nerds’, meaning that they share an interest in various aspects of new technology development 
and are eager to try the latest new thing. The Plato device is positioned in both Self-actualiza-
tion and Esteem since its function concerns creativity and problem solving, which relates to 
self-esteem, confidence and sense of achievement.
The niche market the Plato device aims to fulfill contains people who often get the feeling of 
‘hitting the wall’, and thereby wants a method to help them get out of this state of mind. This 
could be done through brain stimulation.
Current market trends applicable to the Plato device are within the categories of Wearables, 
which is highly related to the Quantified Self movement and Human enhancement (Human 
Augmentation). Insights on these and their future opportunities are further addressed in an 
analysis made by PA consulting [A29]. Current products on the market using tDCS include 
Thync, Halo, and foc.us. However, none of these are estimated to be within the same market 
segment as Plato. Alternative products with relevant market trends include various wearable 
products such as health and motion detectors as well as products aimed to stimulate human 
capabilities such as Virtual Reality and various smartphone apps.

Relative Advantage (Is it better?)
The Plato device will intentionally provide a better stimuli since it is more directly targeted and 
more intense, and with no known side effects. In terms of whether it outperforms existing solu-
tions, a challenge lies within the user feeling the effect of the brain stimuli, compared to their 
current experience when using existing alternatives (through they might be more time consum-
ing and less effective). (Questionable)
Compatibility (Does it fit in?)
The Plato device will be used in work related settings, either at home, office, or workshop. It is 
considered to fit in with existing assisting devices in terms of design and interaction. But it is 
questionable whether the user will consider the Plato device as a ‘cheating’ tool and hide it away 
while using the device. This is unknown in reference to social norms. (Questionable)
Complexity (Is it understandable?)
The Plato device is considered to be easy to use and understand. The context requirements are 
power access, a smartphone with Bluetooth, and an app to control the device as a minimum. 
Also access to a saline solution is now a must. The setting for using the Plato device is optional, 
but interferences from the surroundings while receiving stimuli might negatively affect the ex-
perience. This should be further considered. (Questionable)	
Trialability (Can it be tested?)

It is fairly manageable to expose the Plato device to potential customers and to offer trial stimuli 
sessions. However it is difficult for people to feel the effect of the device during a trial session. 
The trial setting is often very different from the work setting that you would actually be using 
the Plato device in. The feeling of improving while doing your everyday work is more tangible 
than a test done in an unfamiliar trial setting. (Questionable)
Observability (What does it look like in context?)
The way people seem to look at the Plato device indicates that they do not understand its

tests, will be presented after the tests are described.
For the initial product there are two overall settings in which the Plato device will be used. 
These we call Demo Mode and Stimuli Mode. Demo mode is a short time demonstration of 
the device aimed for testing and product trial at e.g. events or exhibitions. Stimuli Mode is a full 
30-minute stimuli session aimed to be used in a work setting according to the defined market 
segment. Since the two use modes are fairly different, we decided to conduct a test sessions for 
each of these respectively. An outline for possible test setups, and the reason for the two chosen, 
can be seen in the appendix [A38].
In the following sections we will briefly introduce each of conducted tests, and thereafter discuss 
the main findings.

5.3.2.	 Conclusion from UX Testing
Having access to (or feedback about) the stimuli data seemed to be important for several of 
the test people. They wanted to know if it was working as intended as well as to know exactly 
what was going on. Other users cared about more general functional details such as knowing 
the battery life while others still were fine as long as they could just turn it on and off. What 
this means for Plato is that they should have a customizable app where they user can receive the 
information that they want.
The weight and fit of the current device seemed to be good, although people were missing 
feedback for whether the device was positioned correctly. A feedback feature addressing this 
problem is another thing that Plato should introduce to their device. Furthermore there were 
several comments about the product appearance, where “this looks like a prototype” and “this does 
not look so fancy’’ were among the responses. Therefore we would recommend Plato to work more 
with the industrial design and come up with something that looks more polished and finished.
A nice-to-have feature would be to implement a (optional) guide that helps people get in the 
right mindset for optimal work conditions. This means reminding people to sit/stand in a com-
fortable position, get fresh air, drink water, avoid disturbance, planning tools, and creative meth-
ods among other proven aspects that help increase focus and energy level while working.
Previously (March 2016) Plato talked about sharing the device among several people in an office 
whereas at the time of the testing (June 2016) they are focusing more in the individual use and 
product adjustment. Based on the (non conclusive) test at the KPG office we saw indicators that 
both might work. Several people expressed that they would prefer if the office had a Plato de-
vice they could use when needed, as they most likely would not buy one themselves (but would 
like to have access to one). Others said that they would like to buy a personal device. Offering a 
personal device with a sharing potential (through e.g. individual personal profiles in app) might 
be the best solution for this.
A concern we have had in terms of the UX parameters was the use of saline solution on elec-
trodes, which, by nature, makes them wet. People seemed to accept this, and the test subjects 
who knew about tDCS brain stimulation even preferred this. “I’m pleased with you using plenty 
of saline solution instead of burning my scalp” (statement made by the medical student, Birgitte 
Nielsen, working in a neuro department performing TES tests on mice [A16]). Our advice con-
cerning the electrode design is not to use too much effort on changing the use of wet electrodes, 
but to keep it as it is for the initial product.

5.5.	 Recommendations for the Plato device
In order to increase the adoption potential of their initial product Plato should do the following:
››
Improve the industrial design, so people consider the product as fully developed (and 
thereby more reliable).
››
Include a ‘Test Mode’ in the app, where users can perform the same task before and 
during stimulation to better feel of the effect of improving either divergent or conver-
gent cognitive abilities.
››
Include tracking capabilities in the app. 
• This could include, when, how often, and for how long the device has been used. 
Additionally it could have self-logged feedback on how the users felt the effect on 
prior stimulation sessions (keeping track of good outcomes and makes the user 
aware of when, how, and what was associated with a successful stimuli effect). In 
a later version this could also include EEG (or similar) sensors, which should be 
used to measure the change in the brain signals and activity. Having data available 
can help give people a sense of an effect.
5.6.	 Updates to the Framework
By using the framework more extensively on a case study we discovered a few aspects that 
should be changed to improve on the framework. These will now be described briefly.
Existing products that use the same technology are currently not part of the Component Break-
down Model. Thereby the project team might not get (potentially) valuable insights from exist-
ing products. This is changed for the final version.
The MVP should not be part of the Evaluation Parameters after the Product Development Pro-
cess. It should start the product development process, and should be the minimum requirement 
before user tests can be performed with the product. This is also changed in the final version.
Other Insights
For the Plato device we ended up re-making the user journey a couple of times, as we learned 
more about what it was actually supposed to be. The user journey proved to be an extremely 
valuable tool in order to align our understanding of the use of the product, even after we had 
tried it on ourselves.
We also realized that Plato was too far into the development process compared to the ideal.

6.1.	 The Theoretical Base of the DfTA Framework
Looking at the product development process from the perspective of the DfTA framework, the 
starting point, or trigger, for initiating the process is the application potential of an emerging 
technology. This technology has to be applied within the aspects of Market, Product and Pro-
duction in order to become suitable for a final Business application.
Figure 44.	 Our perspective on the overall product development process. It is based on the IPD (Andreasen 2000)
The DfTA is focusing on the Product aspect of the overall product development process, with 
the expansion also covering initial considerations from both the Market and Production aspects. 
Within the Product aspect the development process is divided into the three phases. These are 
Initial Research, Product Development, and Product-to-Market. In the last phase all three as-
pects, Market, Product, and Production, merge in order to achieve the Business.
Figure 45.	 The DfTA approach is focusing on the Product aspect of the overall product development process
6.2.	 The DfTA Framework
The DfTA design framework divides the product development process into three iterative phases 
of: Initial Research, Product Development and Product-to-Market.
The three phases are displayed as iterative loops overlapping each other. The product moves 
from one phase to the next as the development process proceeds. Each overlap represents a ‘gate’ 
between the phases. Within these gates the product should not proceed to the next phase if it 
is estimated as too risky.
==PAGE:87==
DESIGN FOR TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION
65
Initial Research
Product-to-Market
Product Development
Figure 46.	 Design for Technology Adoption design framework
The Initial Research phase covers the desk research of potential product concepts. The aim of 
this phase is to define the technology application context, the intended use, the targeted user 
segment, and the allocated resources needed to develop the product. Based on these, estima-
tions of the products adoption potential are made for the further development processes. If the 
estimations indicate potential market adoption and the resources needed will be available, the 
development process can proceed to the next phase. The process is seen as iterative as new dis-
coveries will lead to changes in other aspects.
The Product Development phase covers the R&D work with designing and defining product 
features. In this phase the estimations made in the Initial Research are evaluated through iter-
ative processes of prototyping, user testing, and feedback sessions. Based on the feedback and 
testing results the final product concept either proceeds to the final phase, is taken through an-
other iterative loop, or is drawn back and re-evaluated in the Initial Research phase. If none of 
these options leads to better estimations of adoption potential, the product development should 
be stopped altogether.
The last phase in the DfTA framework is the Product-to-Market phase. This covers the final 
decisions of the product development process. Here the Product application aspects merge with 
the Market and Production (see Figure 45). The outcome of this phase is for the product to 
actually be brought to the market, and it is not until beyond this point that the actual market 
adoption can be measured.
The transition from one phase to the next is constrained by a gate. In order to proceed to the 
next phase the requirements for each of the parameters CML, RML, UX, Market, Develop-
ment Goals, and Resources, will need to be fulfilled. All parameters are further explained in the 
DfTA assessment guide later in this chapter. It is possible to perform a backwards transition 
between the phases if needed when parameters are estimated to be of high risk of failure (low 
adoption potential or high technology risk), lack of resources due to IP rights or regulations, or 
red ocean development.

Presentation of the DfTA Framework
The DfTA framework aims to address the different assessment parameters that are important 
for making decisions during the early phases of the development process. Additionally it detects 
the different resources allocated by choices made in the early product development stages. The 
allocation of resources enables the development team to gain a basic overview of the stakehold-
ers, actions, and funding necessary in order to proceed to the development process. The resources 
needed are categorized as either: Human, Non-human, or Regulative resources. These must be 
allocated according to the predicted time of their necessity.
6.2.1.	 Target Audience
The ideal user of the DfTA framework is:
››
A Start-up company in the early development phase
››
A development team with a minimum of two people
››
Developing physical (hardware) products, ideally within wearables
››
Developing a product with direct user interaction which is also not a part to be used as a 
component in another product
››
Using an emerging technology to create a product (Blue Ocean development)
››
Willing to follow a how-to-guide
Management tools for product development are only beneficial if they are used for the right 
purpose and the teams are aware of the value gained from applying a strategic work method or 
assessment [A11]. The DfTA framework aims to help the development team gain a shared and 
(to all) understandable set of requirements for a successful product. It provides the team with a 
shared approach on how to systematically select, combine, and evaluate the product in relation 
to the end-users’ perceived value.
Value gained by the project team from applying the framework in their development process:
✓
✓Creation of a requirements specification for the intended product
✓
✓An overview of the human, non-human, and regulative resources needed in the further 
product development process
✓
✓Definition of performance indicators referring to the intended user experience (UX)
✓
✓A method on how to evaluate and eventually ‘Kill your darlings’
✓
✓A management tool, helping the project team to guide the overall product development 
process through risk assessment of intended product features
✓
✓Reduced risk of non-adoption of product when launched to market

respond, the customers would raise their expectations, and critics could bash 
the product idea rather than the actual product (Fortune 2012). Furthermore

Discussion of the DfTA Framework
7.1.	 Defining the DfTA Framework
The process of applying an emerging technology into a product and ensuring value creation 
can be very complex. This complexity becomes even greater for start-up companies, who might 
lack resources and experience. Establishing a business demands that simultaneous activities in-
cluding considerations of defining market approach, user segment, product design, production, 
keeping up with regulative demands, and ensuring funding opportunities take place. All these 
are typically carried out at the same time, and it is a very uncertain business state with a lot of 
decisions needing to be made. The creation of the DfTA Assessment Guide originates from the 
potential of taking this ‘chaotic’ state and somewhat structuring it without loosing the original 
vision [A5].
The final DfTA framework and the chosen assessment parameters are derived from a combi-
nation of relevant literature, expert interviews, and insights from case testing. Based on this, 
the DfTA has been defined in three levels of abstraction presented in the previous chapter. The 
intended user of the DfTA Assessment Guide is a project team working with a current product 
development project. The user of the DfTA Assessment Guide will therefore be referred to as 
the project team throughout this section. 
7.1.1.	 The DfTA Theoretical Base
The theoretical base provides the project team with an introduction to how the product devel-
opment process is seen from the DfTA perspective.
7.1.2.	 The DfTA Framework
The DfTA framework aims to represent the overall product development process by increasing 
final adoption based on product design efforts. It assumes that the product development process 
is triggered by an emerging technology, which has the potential of being applied into a hard-
ware product. The DfTA approach acknowledges the need for considering product development 
within the three aspects of Market, Product, and Production, but focuses on the Product aspect, 
though all three have to be merged before the product can be launched to market. 
The process of going from a technology potential to market launch is covered in the phases of: 
Initial Research, Product Development, and Product-to-Market. The DFTA framework focuses 
on risk assessment within the Initial Research phase, as the majority of the resources used in a 
product development process are allocated early on in the process (Haskins 2006). Therefore this 
is where development consideration will make the greatest impact. As an entrepreneur, you are 
not locked by a particular technology due to investments, but have a broader range of freedom to 
choose [A12] which makes it even more essential to provide a guide for how to evaluate product 
concepts using emerging technologies.
7.1.3.	 The DfTA Assessment Guide
The DfTA Assessment Guide enables the project team to apply the DfTA framework onto a 
current product development case. By following a step-by-step approach the project team is 
==PAGE:123==
DESIGN FOR TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION
101
guided through the DfTA framework by applying the assessment methods within the Initial 
Research phase. The outcome provides the team with an estimate of the product’s adoption 
potential based on the DfTA assessment parameters (CML, RML, UX, and Resources) and the 
definition of the evaluation parameters (MVP, KPIs, and RS). These evaluation parameters are 
then used in the Product Development phase. 
The DfTA Assessment Guide is intended as a management tool and is constructed as a phase/
gate-model, where the product concept needs to meet certain criteria before proceeding to the 
next development phase. In order to aid in the early stage of product development, theories and 
models covering technology adoption and risk assessment have been studied. Derivatives from 
this have been included in the DfTA framework. Assessment methods for Technology, Market, 
and UX parameters have been developed by us to help link the different models and improve 
transparency in the decision-making.
An aspect we see challenges with and that we have confirmed through our empirical studies is 
the need for alignment of a project team (Jonas Richelsen [A11]). As we see it, transparency 
in the decision making have the potential to help align the project team. The DfTA framework 
does so by verbalizing the different aspects of the product concept and visualizing the relative 
importance of the used technology components. 
7.1.4.	 DfTA Assessment Methods 
The DfTA Assessment Guide explains the assessment methods within the Initial Research 
phase. These methods are arranged a 5 step approach covering: Technology, Market, User, Re-
sources, and Development Goals. 
Step 1: Technology
This step aids the project team in defining the product concept. It does so by asking for the val-
ue proposition created by the emerging technology. It further asks for product design decision 
through the selection of specific components, which thereby detects potential challenges that 
might occur later on in the product development process. The structured approach and use of 
visual models will help align the project team by defining product functions and their respective 
importance in relation to the value proposition.
Step 2: Market
The aim of this step is to help the project team define the intended user segment and to further 
evaluate if the proposed product concept is targeting a Red or Blue Ocean market. This will help 
align the project team in determining whether the product concept aligns with market expecta-
tions and identify if product features should be changed. In addition to this, market trends will 
be identified, which can help increase the adoption potential of the product by making it more 
recognizable. This is an aspect that is important from a UX perspective.
==PAGE:124==
102
Discussion of the DfTA Framework
Step 3 User
Using methods such as Personas and UX will help the project team in further describing for 
whom they are designing the product, as well as in estimating the perceived value from the user 
perspective. If it turns out that the intended user does not perceive any value from the product 
it is possible to change aspects of the product before any further resources have been allocated 
for further development.
Step 4: Resources
This step helps the project team identify the needed resources to accomplish the development 
project. Resources include human, non-human, and regulative resources, which must be listed 
and allocated according to when they are needed. This enables the project team to estimate 
risks or see opportunities related to e.g. need of expert knowledge, production equipment, or IP 
rights. Becoming aware of the needed resources further supports the team with project planning 
considerations.  
Step 5: Development Goals
In this step the development goals are defined. These are the Requirements Specification (RS), 
Key Performance Indicators (KPI), and the Minimum Viable Product (MVP). These serve the 
function of evaluation parameters for the following Product Development phase. The intention 
is for the project team to specify the initial prototype (the MVP), test this with users, and receive 
feedback for improvement. The RS and KPI are then used by the project team to evaluate the 
product at the next gate before moving on to the Product-to-Market phase. The need of having 
predefined evaluation criteria is derived from a statement made by Balder Onarheim (Plato, 
CEO), who said that it is difficult to know when the product is good enough to take to the mar-
ket, and that concrete success indications could help define when the product is ‘good enough’ 
[A1]. This was further supported by Peter Nielsen. He said that at some point you need to de-
cide when something is ‘good enough’. It is often not worth the extra efforts to make the perfect 
product because working until your product is perfect will result in working on it forever and 
never having a finished product to bring to market [A5]. Defining the Development Goals also 
serves the purpose of aligning the project team, as they will need to agree on decisions made in 
Product Development phase before progressing with efforts in bringing the product to market. 
7.2.	 Feedback from Stakeholders
Through the development of the DfTA framework we received feedback from a broad range of 
stakeholders. The aspect that seems to be of the greatest concern is the time needed on applying 
the framework. We heard from several stakeholders, including Jonas Richelsen [A11], Jakob 
Andersen [A12], and Mikkel Olsen [A19], that they would prefer a tool that could be used in 
a short amount of time and with little introduction effort needed. According to them, it should 
take no more than 1-3 hours to apply the framework. We discussed this feedback in terms of 
whether it is something we should try to accommodate for or not. But in the end we saw a 
greater value in having a proper tool rather than a ‘quick fix’.
==PAGE:125==
DESIGN FOR TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION
103
We acknowledge that being a start-up can be very hectic and that you have to compromise 
between time schedules and the desired level of detail. But this is also the exact reason why we 
decided to remain with the more detailed approach. As stated earlier, the resources used are allo-
cated early in the development process (Haskins 2006). With this we consider it more valuable 
for a start-up to use time on risk estimation early in the process if they can, in order to prevent 
wasted development efforts later on. We know from experience that time spent on discussing 
something before it is carried out can mean a week saved by potentially not having to redo the 
work. If we transfer this to a product development process that takes months or even years, we 
believe that very significant amounts of time can be saved by having relevant discussions as early 
as possible and thereby thoroughly aligning the project team. We believe that if a project team 
initially spends a week on becoming aligned, months can be saved later on in the process. In a 
discussion on identified challenges concerning the Plato device, Plato CEO Balder Onarheim 
expressed: “You have identified some of the things we have been 5 people working on for a year to iden-
tify - just by using the (DfTA) framework. If we had been able to see these challenges back in November 
we could have saved a lot of time” [translated from Danish] [A1, June 15, 2016].
Through the conducted interviews we also received feedback regarding the usefulness of the 
DfTA framework. None of the stakeholders thought it was a waste of time to try estimating 
the risk associated with choices made early on in the development process. We also talked to 
Mikkel Olsen who works at IdemoLab, a department within the Danish GTS approved com-
pany Delta. They have developed their own estimation method for addressing value creation in 
hardware product design. This method works as a consultancy service, and it is called ‘Disrup-
tive Hardware Innovation in 8 Weeks’. Through the interview Mikkel stated that our DfTA 
approach is in many ways similar to what they are doing, and he therefore believes it could work 
as an intended assessment tool. He further stated that because the DfTA framework is not 
generative (providing methods for generating new ideas), but focus on evaluation of an existing 
idea, it would be possible for project teams to apply it without the need of consultancy (as the 
case with the IdemoLab service). This might therefore make the DfTA tool more desirable for 
startup companies [A19].
7.3.	 Validity of the DfTA Framework
The DfTA framework was developed through the process of combining insights from literature 
studies, empirical studies, and test assumptions through case testing and reviews by relevant 
stakeholders in an iterative process. We therefore consider it to have a strong foundation in 
theory as well as industry practice.
The framework has been tested on a total of 5 product cases with all testing performed by 
ourselves. We consider this as a lack of representative data. Therefore the validity of the frame-
work cannot be confirmed without more thorough testing and with tests performed by external 
stakeholders. 
Not all literature used is of an academic origin. A number of insights originate from ‘modern’ 
literature in form of online published material. However, as we see it, if you are to make a design 
framework that builds on current literature, it is important to include current knowledge from

all available and up-to-date sources.
The empirical study consists of a relatively small group of experts (14 in total) and the insights 
are all based on qualitative content. Therefore, the insights might be flawed to a smaller or 
greater extent by our interpretation and way of conducting the interviews. Furthermore, the 
stakeholders do not represent a broad array of people and backgrounds, as we mainly talked with 
people either within our own network or people we could get to agree on meeting with us. How-
ever we do believe that the stakeholders as a group are fair representatives for the perspectives of 
technology adoption that we intended to look further into.
The DfTA assessment guide only consists of qualitative and therefore somewhat subjective, as-
sessment tools (such as CML, RML and UX). The advantage of this, based on our experience, 
is that these assessment tools are relatively fast to use compared to quantitative tools. However, 
the disadvantage of this is that the results will vary depending on the information available and 
the interpretations made. The advantage of this is that it is a lot faster to use compared to a 
quantitative tool.
We acknowledge that the DfTA assessment made of the Plato device was highly supported by 
the insights provided from Plato both in terms of product design, market insights, and prior 
user testing. The insights we claim to have provided might very well in part come from insights 
talking with the Plato team during this thesis. Great insights might always emerge from having 
someone proving a new perspective on your problems, and we there believe that our efforts have 
been valuable to Plato. We also still see a great potential in using the DfTA framework, but it 
is always easier to look back and be wise on past decisions and judge whether they were right 
or wrong.
7.4.	 Implications for Future Start-ups
Based on the knowledge gained through this thesis, we believe that start-ups applying the 
framework will have a greater success compared to those who do not use it. This is because they 
will have performed early risks assessments and used these to estimate the adoption potential of 
their product before proceeding with further development activities. A great implication of this 
would ideally be that if more start-ups succeed, more people can be employed which helps grow 
the economy. On the other hand the framework might also increase the rate of development 
projects being ‘stopped’, as a result of poor adoption potential. This is, in a way, also the purpose 
of using the DfTA framework as an assessment guide. But the intention is not to kill the entre-
preneurial spirit. It is, in fact, to bring new spirit and inspire better ideas.

We acknowledge that our effort in developing a framework addressing technology adoption 
through product design is just the beginning of the road to an even better tool. Below we pro-
vide a list of proposals for further research in order to improve on the current shortcomings.
7.5.1.	 Greater Insights from Field Studies

Conclusions
8.1.	 Conclusions
The DfTA Assessment Guide helps guide the project team in the ‘right’ direction, by asking 
relevant questions and using relevant evaluation methods. By applying the DfTA framework the 
project team will have to define their product concept, determine market segment, and identify 
potential technical pitfalls or market saturation. It further aids the project team in understand-
ing the intended users, their product expectations, and how the product can add value to their 
lives. The DfTA framework aligns the project team by verbalizing and facilitating a discussion 
on important aspects of the further product development process.
The research questions of this thesis will now be answered:
What does existing literature state about designing for adoption of emerging technologies?
a)	 How does that relate to the application of emerging technologies in wearable products?
Research on existing literature showed that the subject of technology adoption is very complex. 
A lot of different variables need to be taken into account when discussing the aspect of product 
design. These variables include knowing whom you are designing for, defining the intended use 
context, and identifying market trends. Most commonly the adoption rate of technologies fol-
lows a bell-curve called the Technology Adoption Curve (Rogers 2003). Market adoption is not 
reached before the chasm is crossed. This happens when moving from the early adopters to the 
early majority (Moore 2002). Moore believes that these groups are very different and thereby 
possess different adoption criteria. In order to reach the majority, products must be designed 
with this in mind.
According to Gartner’s Hype Cycle, wearables are considered an emerging technology just pass-
ing the Peak of Inflated Expectations (Gartner 2015b). This is further supported by PA Con-
sulting Group who state that wearable technologies are among the top four emerging technol-
ogies with the most potential (PA Consulting Group 2015). Therefore we believe that literature 
on technology adoption can be applied to wearable products.
Which product design features are important when designing for technology adoption of wearable 
products?
a)	 How can they be applied to a wearable product using TES?
Researching within literature, conducting interviews, performing tests, and holding a workshop 
enabled a number of important design aspects for technology adoption of wearable products to 
be identified. These include technology maturity and the maturity of relations between technol-
ogies. It includes understanding the intended users and their needs and identifying the desired 
market positioning of the product. Other aspects are risks related to needed resources in terms 
of human (e.g. expert knowledge and skills), non-human (e.g. machinery, components, and soft-
ware), and regulatory resources (e.g. intellectual property, patents, and market regulations). 
These aspects were integrated into the DfTA framework and further applied to a wearable

8.2.	 Project Reflections
Below are reflections and key learning points from conducting this thesis:
There are many ways of achieving the same goal – and the means of achieving are defined by 
your choices.
Through conducting the literature and empirical studies, one thing became very clear to us. 
There are many ways of achieving the same goal, and there is no definitive right way. This was 
evident both in the literature and in interviews. Every person had their own opinion in different 
aspects of technology adoption in terms of product design.
Document findings along the way, but do not spend time on perfecting this.
At the beginning of this project we worked as if we had a very linear approach. We worked 
towards acquiring the knowledge we thought was necessary and wrote the report sections cor-
responding to this at the same time. This resulted in a lot of hours being spent on writing and 
editing text we were convinced we would be using. In reality our project took a very iterative 
approach. The scope changed throughout the project, as did the content of the report sections we 
needed to include. This meant that we spent a lot of hours in the beginning on something that 
basically ended up not being used. However, we believe that this work did help us in the long 
run. The exact words were not all used, but the knowledge we gained was valuable. This also set 
us up for being in a good mindset for writing this thesis by continually documenting findings 
and writing down our interpretations as we worked.
It is very valuable to receive feedback on drafts – the 80/20 rule.
As students we are used to being evaluated. If you are not carful this can have the affect of 
wanting to keep work hidden until it is perfect and ready for presentation. Through the process 
of receiving feedback from interviews and supervision sessions we quickly became aware of the 
gained value from receiving more feedback on a draft instead of having less feedback on a per-
fectly detailed presentation. We became more aware of the 80/20 rule (web: Wikipedia 2016b), 
which was always at there front of our minds as we worked.
There is a lot of value in being structured and making conclusions along the way.
During the process of writing this thesis we were in contact with a broad range of stakeholders 
[Appendix xx, stakeholder analysis]. Keeping track of all stakeholders, documenting insights 
from interviews, and following up with approval for quotes and interpretations from their inter-
views, turned out to be a time-consuming process. We did this in a very structured way, which 
turned out to be a good choice. Part of this was to write down the key takeaways from each 
stakeholder meeting immediately after it. Then we were confident about what happened in the 
meeting and could go straight to forming conclusions. Information was not lost when later in 
the process we did not remember details from the meeting because everything was recorded.
Be precise in defining the scope.

Setting the boundaries for what the framework should include and its possible application areas 
turned out to be harder than expected. Ideally we wanted a tool that was widely applicable and 
at the same time very specific. With great consideration we believe we found the right balance 
between the two. What we have learned from this is to set up boundaries as quickly as possible 
but be prepared to change them.
Complex systems can be simplified.
As our knowledge grew we realized that the systems we were looking at are very complex. How 
to design for technology adoption is not a simple answer but a multi faceted one. Yet after a 
great deal of research we could understand it and could simplify it into a guideline. This lead to 
realizing that complex systems can be simplified for clarity and ease of use. We believe that we 
managed to do this in the DfTA framework.
