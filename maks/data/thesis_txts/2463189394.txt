Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background overview
Water scarcity is a worldwide problem gaining day by day always more attention due
to a growing demand of this resource caused by the increasing population and also its
shortage exacerbated by climate change (Gosling & Arnell 2016).
In Europe, at least
11% of the population was estimated to be aﬀected by water scarcity and is expected a
further deterioration of the water situation if temperatures keep rising as a result of global
warming (EU 2016).
In such a context, water re-use is regarded as a solution to face the increasing shortage.
In particular, the re-use of waste water treatment plants (WWTPs) eﬄuent is considered as
one of the most consolidated options and the research (Kalavrouziotis & Apostolopoulos
(2007), Angelakis et al. (2003), Bixio et al. (2006), Lubello et al. (2004), Lopez et al.
(2006)) mainly focused its eﬀorts on assessing this practice.
However, in highly urbanized areas, where water is subjected to multiple uses, uncon-
ventional sources of pollution are very likely and can aﬀect the quality of the re-used water.
Groundwater heat pumps (GHPs) represent one of most concern among the unconventional
sources of pollution due to the potential cross-contamination caused by withdraws of water
from aquifers and releases to surface water receptors (Bartesaghi et al. 1999). If that wa-
ter is then recycled in agriculture for irrigating edible crops this practice might afterwards
pose a risk to human health when those crops are ingested through food (Campo et al.
2013).
Moreover, the previously cited risk is also connected to the increasingly frequent oc-
currence of micropollutants (MPs) in diﬀerent environmental compartments (Gogoi et al.
2018). MPs are chemicals of anthropogenic origin, such as pesticides, pharmaceuticals
and heavy metals, present in water at trace concentrations (Stamm et al. 2016, Luo et al.
2014). MPs sources, fate and behavior is, in most of the cases, not well known even if it is
recognized they represent a concern for the living environment due to their potential estro-
genicity even at low concentrations (Stamm et al. 2016, Galus et al. 2013). Furthermore,
MPs removal from WWTPs is often incomplete, if not null, and they can be re-introduced
in the aquatic environment (Campo et al. 2013).
Thus, there is a lack of literature and methodology concerning the evaluation of the
risk associated to unconventional sources of pollution, such as GHPs, and the potential
cross-contamination of chemicals from an environmental compartment to another one.
New methodologies have to be developed in order to carefully evaluate the risk posed
by MPs releases connected with these practises that can aﬀect the quality of the re-used
water.

1.2.
Project aim
1.2
Project aim
The aim of this master thesis is to assess the possible risk of cross-contamination by GHPs
discharging to natural waters, subsequently used for irrigating edible crops, due to multiple
uses of the water resource in highly urbanized areas.
The speciﬁc objectives of this project are presented below:
• Extension of existing modelling tools for the fate of contaminants in surface waters
in order to describe the whole case study area.
• Choice and physico-chemical and ecotoxicological parameters characterization of a
reference substance to test the performances of the model.
• Temporal and spatial characterization of the new dynamic contamination sources
(GHPs)
• Analysis of the eﬀects related to GHPs within diﬀerent scenarios.
• Evaluation of the variability and uncertainty related to the data gathered.
• Assessment of the risks posed by the unconventional sources of pollution.
The ”Navigli project” by the Milan municipality, Italy, was chosen as case study. The
project consists in re-constructing the ancient hydraulic continuity between the Naviglio
Martesana and the Darsena, the Vettabbia and the irrigation system of the south Milan.
Main interesting characteristics of the ”Navigli project” for this master thesis are:
• It involves the possibility to exploit the new hydraulic re-connection as a delivery for
GHPs for heating and cooling purposes. The water will be withdrawn from aquifers
and, as consequence, it represents a potential unconventional source of MPs present
in groundwater.
• The water coming from the GHPs and partly from the hydraulic re-connection will
replenish the Vettabbia river, which currently receives the eﬄuent of the Nosedo
WWTP.
• The Vettabbia river is one of the most representative Italian case of WWTP eﬄuent
re-used for edible crops irrigation purposes (Angelakis et al. 2003).

Chapter 2
State of the art
2.1
Water scarcity
Fresh water is a renewable source, but also ﬁnite (Postel 2000). Water usage by humans
exceeded sustainable levels and there are numerous evidences around the world (Postel
2000).
In accordance with Pereira et al. (2009), water scarcity is commonly deﬁned as “a situ-
ation where water m3 availability in a country or in a region is below 1000 per person per
year”. Nonetheless, many countries in the world are heading even worse water conditions.
When the water supply per person per year is lower than 500 m3, is considered as severe
water scarcity (Pereira et al. 2009). Whereas the conditions where water availability is be-
low the 2000 m3 threshold are addressed as water stress (Pereira et al. 2009). An overview
of the world average availability of water m3 per capita and per country is shown in ﬁgure
2.1.
Figure 2.1: World water availability per capita (1000 m3/year) and per country (Pereira
et al. 2009)
Groundwater depletion (i), low river ﬂows (ii) and worsening pollution levels (iii) are
among the most common indicators of water stress (Postel 2000). All these indicators
are then exacerbated by diﬀerent driving forces as (i) increasing world population, (ii)
improving living standards, (iii) changing consumption patterns, (iv) expansion of irrigated

agriculture and (v) global warming (Mekonnen & Hoekstra 2016, EU 2016).
Thus, the 10th edition of the World Economic Forum indicated water scarcity as the
largest global risk in terms of potential impact (WEF 2015).
Seckler et al. (1999) estimated that around 1.4 billion people, amounting to a fourth
of the world population, live in areas that will face severe water scarcity within the ﬁrst
quarter of the current century. Slightly more than 1 billion people will experience absolute
water scarcity by 2025 and, hence, they will be forced to reduce the water used for domestic,
industrial and agricultural purposes (Seckler et al. 1999).
Nevertheless, other studies,
such as Mekonnen & Hoekstra (2016), stated that these numbers are underestimated.
Conversely, a larger estimate of 4.0 billion people that will cope with severe water scarcity
conditions was suggested, corresponding to two-thirds of the world population (Mekonnen
& Hoekstra 2016).
As widely acknowledged, climate change will aggravate water shortage leading to
changes in precipitation patterns and, moreover, other climate factors might aﬀect water
supply in many regions (Schewe et al. 2014). Schewe et al. (2014) performed a multimodel
assessment of water scarcity under climate change. The simulations suggested that even a
relatively modest global warming of 1 ◦C above present day will lead to a severe reduction
in water resources for a further 8% of the word population in addition to the one already
aﬀected. The scenario rises to about 14% for 2 ◦C and 17% for 3 ◦C (Schewe et al. 2014).
Agriculture is considered the sector claiming for the larger amount of water and, hence,
is often addressed as the main cause for water scarcity (Pereira et al. 2009). According to
Hamdy et al. (2003), on a consumptive use basis, agriculture is responsible up to 80−90%
of the water used for human activities. Because of the excessive water wastes produced
and of the degrading water quality, irrigation is accused of misuse of water (Pereira et al.
2009). However, agriculture still provides the livelihood to most of the world population
(Pereira et al. 2009). As a consequence, food security is very doubtful, even in developed
countries (Hamdy et al. 2003).
Sharpening the eﬀects of water scarcity, the world population in constantly rising with
a rate of more than 90 million people each year (Hamdy et al. 2003).
The expected
aftereﬀects by forecasts are that (i) more than a half of the developing countries will be
unable to feed their population without importing food and (ii) a quarter of the freshwater
supply in the world will be too polluted to be drinkable (Hamdy et al. 2003).
As widely recognized, due to the increasing shortage of the water resource will be
attributed a great relevance to water management (Pereira et al. 2009). It will be possible
to avoid conﬂicts among diﬀerent sectoral water uses, to ensure the local security of food,
to have more water available for crops irrigation and industrial and household uses if the
goal of producing with less, or at least the same amount, of water will be reached (Hamdy
et al. 2003). To face this situation (i) resource conservation, (ii) environmental friendliness,
(iii) technological appropriateness, (iv) economic viability and (v) social acceptability of
development issues will be necessary (Pereira et al. 2009).
2.1.1
Water reuse
As reported in Miller (2006), water re-use can be deﬁned as “the reclamation and treatment
of non-traditional (or impaired) waters for the purpose of beneﬁcial re-use”. The non-
traditional or impaired waters cited before can be ascribed to (Miller 2006): (i) municipal
and industrial waste water eﬄuent, (ii) brackish water, (iii) poor quality groundwater, (iv)
agriculture return ﬂows and (v) storm-water.
Anderson (2003) stated that water re-use can greatly increase the beneﬁts in regions
aﬀected by water shortage. In the cited work was demonstrated how this practice allows to

In a scenario in which 50% of the inﬂow is used for agriculture and 25% for household
and industrial uses, water re-use helped to increase the amount of the conserved water for
around the 24%, with a consistent lowering of the load of chemical oxygen demand (COD)

However, public perception is not always in favor of these practices. Po et al. (2003)
performed a literature review of the factors inﬂuencing the public perception of the recy-

“perception of the risks”, stands out one among them: “disgust or ‘Yuck’ factor” (Po et al.
2003). Thus, not only technological development and appropriate water resource manage-
ment strategies are needed to head the problem of water scarcity, but also a successful
understanding of the basis of public acceptance of water re-use (Po et al. 2003).

Focus on WWTPs eﬄuent re-used in agriculture
As agriculture is the sector claiming for the larger amount of water (Pereira et al. 2009),
water re-use is often addressed as one of the most promising solution to lower the demand
and the pressure on freshwater reservoir (Lazarova & Bahri 2004).
To date, WWTPs eﬄuent re-use for irrigation is commonly considered as one of the
most reliable and widespread option to tackle the problem of water scarcity in agriculture
(Lubello et al. 2004). This practice is very consolidated in the Mediterranean area because
it has been used since some centuries (Angelakis et al. 2003). The quality requirements
for agriculture are usually the easiest to achieve among the various types of waste water
re-use, hence, it is by far the largest re-use application in terms of volume (Angelakis et al.
2003).
In Italy, one of the oldest cases of WWTPs eﬄuent recycled in agriculture is represented
by the Vettabbia river (Angelakis et al. 2003), that is part of the case study area of this
master thesis project.

the ground water table rise (Marelli 2017). Since 1990, in fact, the rising of the aquifer
water table has caused many problems to the underground infrastructures, such as the

GHPs, commonly used to answer to the yearly heating and cooling demand in urban

not well assessed.

2.2.1
Micropollutants deﬁnition and overview
Micropollutants (MPs) have drawn attention due to their harmful impact on both aquatic
and terrestrial life forms as well as on human health (Gogoi et al. 2018). As stated by
Stamm et al. (2016), MPs are deﬁned as chemicals occurring in the aquatic environment
due to human activities with concentrations at trace levels, ranging from a few ng/L to
several µg/L (Luo et al. 2014). The MPs, as deﬁned previously, cover a large spectrum
of several diﬀerent chemicals and, hence, they can not be categorized in just one speciﬁc
class of compounds. Conversely, many authors, such as Geissen et al. (2015), provided
a categorization comprising at least 700 substances with more than 20 classes related to
their origin. The prominent categories are:
• Pesticides and Herbicides
• Pharmaceuticals (PhACs)
• Personal Care Products (PCPs)
• Endocrine Disrupting Compounds (EDCs).
But they are not conﬁned to the above and may comprise of heavy metals (HMs), or-
ganic solvents, nanomaterials (NMs), metabolites of MPs, illegal drugs, engineered genes,
etc (Geissen et al. 2015).
According to Stamm et al. (2016), MPs are object of particular attention because they
are commonly designed to accomplish certain speciﬁc biological eﬀect as, for instance,

curing a determinate disease (PhACs) or avoiding growth of unwanted weeds (herbicides).
This is due to their biological activity generally by orders of magnitude higher than that
of average dissolved organic matter (Stamm et al. 2016). Furthermore, the MPs biological
incidence aﬀect very speciﬁc group of living beings, for example only vertebrates or only
fungi, and in this regard MPs diﬀer from nutrients, essential to all organisms (Stamm
et al. 2016).
Nevertheless, MPs are currently not included in international routine monitoring pro-
grammes and their fate, behavior and ecotoxicological eﬀects are, in some cases, not well
understood (Geissen et al. 2015).
In accordance with Gogoi et al. (2018), the lack of
relevant data on the impacts, fate and occurrence of MPs make it problematic for govern-
ments to control their utilization. However, it is possible to indicate the European Union
Water Framework Directive, WFD (2000), as one of the ﬁrst attempts to set limits to the
concentration of MPs in surface waters. Out of the more than 700 substances pointed out
by Geissen et al. (2015), the impact of only 55 of them is oﬃcially recognized, as the direc-
tive listed 33 priority compounds, 8 certain other pollutants with environmental quality
standard (EQS) to be respected in aquatic environments and 10 others on contemporary
watch list (WFD 2000, EQS 2008, Gogoi et al. 2018).

of their incorporation in crops irrigated with contaminated water and poses risk to human
health upon consumption (Gogoi et al. 2018).
Focus on WWTPs eﬄuent
To date, considerable work have been done in regard to the performance of waste water
technologies in case of nutrient removal, while there is a lack of data on the ability to
removal of MPs (Gogoi et al. 2018).
MPs concentration in WWTPs is strictly connected with local conditions and due to
several factors as, for instance, water consumption per day and per person, the size the
WWTP, treatment eﬃciency of the processes. Even climatic factors as rainfall, which
aﬀects the ﬂow pattern when coupled with a combined sewer system can cause high vari-

According to Luo et al. (2014), the expected input concentration of most of the MPs
ranges between 0.1 and 10 µg/L, whilst output concentrations are one up to two order of
magnitude lower than those in the inﬂuent, varying from 0.001 to 1 µg/L. However, their

As well known, common municipal WWTPs are designed to reduce the concentration
of organic matter, nutrients as nitrogen and phosphorus, and to remove pathogens and
coliforms (Loos et al. 2013). Consequence of this, fate and removal of MPs in WWTP
have not been studied for a while and there are only few very synthetic studies on their
behavior and their impacts in the middle and long-term on human health, the environment
and aquatic environments (Deblonde et al. 2011). Some classes of MPs, such as HMs and
non-polar chemical compounds, can be removed by sorption on particulate matter and
ending up in the sludge eﬄuent (Loos et al. 2013, Carletti et al. 2008). As stated by Loos
et al. (2013), hence, polar compounds, in particular those which are poorly degradable,
might be discharged into receiving waters and occur in surface waters.
As widely recognized, pesticides are ascribed to agricultural pollution due to extensive
crop treatments, surface water pollution from water runoﬀ, agricultural storm water dis-
charges and return ﬂows from irrigated ﬁelds (Campo et al. 2013). Thus, there is a lack
of knowledge about pesticides occurrence in WWTPs,as they deal with urban or indus-

(2013), pointed out how the risk connected to the release of these kind of compounds in
the environment has to be taken in consideration. According to K¨ock-Schulmeyer et al.
(2013) the occurrence of pesticides in WWTPs is mainly due to non-agricultural uses of
them. The list includes: (i) application in grass-management (golf courses, educational
facilities, parks, and cemeteries), (ii) in industrial vegetation control (industrial facilities,
electric utilities, roadways, railroads, pipelines), (iii) in public health (mosquito-abatement
districts, rodent-control areas, and aquatic areas) and (iv) in non-agricultural crops such
as commercial forestry and horticulture and plant-nurseries.

2.2.3
Fate in the environment
The transport of MPs from the source to the sink strongly depends on the MPs properties
such as (i) volatility, (ii) polarity, (iii) adsorption properties, (iv) persistence and (v) the
properties of the interacting compartments (Geissen et al. 2015). According to Gavrilescu
(2005), the variability in the chemical structure helps forecasting how MPs will move in
the environment.
When eventually MPs are present in the receptors water bodies, their environmental
fate is of concern and mainly aﬀected by (i) volatilization, (ii) degradation, (iii) sorption
at the sediment and (iv) transport in the aqueous phase (Geissen et al. 2015).
Some MPs might volatilize from the liquid to the gas phase and can subsequently pose
a risk if inhaled (Gavrilescu 2005). The air emissions of MPs depend on: (i) the vapor
pressure, (ii) the heat of volatilization, (iii) the partition coeﬃcient between the atmo-
sphere and the other environmental compartment and (iv) the air ﬂow mass (Gavrilescu
2005). Once emitted in the atmosphere, MPs can travel very long distances as they can
be transported by wind onto small or larger particles (Gavrilescu 2005).
MPs can undergo signiﬁcant degradation and transformation in eﬄuent impacted sur-
face waters and groundwater (Geissen et al. 2015). Biodegradation is a degradation mech-
anism acted by communities of microorganisms, mainly bacteria and fungi, through their
metabolism (Geissen et al. 2015, K¨ummerer 2008). Although the relative importance of
fungi is larger in soil rather than in the aquatic environment (K¨ummerer 2008).
The
biodegradation of many MPs like hormones, detergents and pharmaceuticals is still not
well understood whereas it is known to be very variable within the diﬀerent other classes
of compounds (Geissen et al. 2015). Within the class of the organic compounds, pesticides
generally present the lowest biodegradability, as shown in ﬁgure 2.6 (Gavrilescu 2005). The
persistence of MPs is calculated in terms of half-life, that is amount of days necessary to
degrade 50% of the initial concentration (Gavrilescu 2005). A value for the half-life larger
than 21 days means that the MPs is able to leach, to move with surface runoﬀor to be
transported in riverine ﬂows for long distances before being degraded (Gavrilescu 2005).
Gavrilescu (2005) stated that biodegradation is the main mechanism of transformation for
MPs among the degradation processes.

(Gavrilescu 2005)
Some of the other degradation pathways, such as (i) hydrolysis, (ii) red-ox reactions

and (iii) ionization, summarized like chemical degradation, are particularly aﬀected and
dependent of the pH (Gavrilescu 2005). The occurrence of these processes is then subjected
to the presence of either alkalinity or acidity (Gavrilescu 2005).
Photodegradation is a degradation process driven by sunlight (Gavrilescu 2005). Inter-
mediates and/or end-products of the MPs photodegradation may also exhibit properties
which appreciably aﬀect the living environment (Geissen et al. 2015). The eﬀectiveness
of the photodegradation can vary according to many factors: (i) intensity of sunlight,
(ii) exposure time, (iii) environmental compartment properties and (iv) MPs photolytic
half-life (Gavrilescu 2005).
Whether the MPs occur in the ionized or in the un-ionized form, jointly with their
hydrophobic properties, largely aﬀect the sorption on solids and particulate matter (Luo
et al. 2014, Geissen et al. 2015). Luo et al. (2014) reported that logKow, logKoc and pKa
can be used as parameters to describe the MPs properties related to sorption.
The n-octanol/water partition coeﬃcient, logKow, is deﬁned as the ratio between the
concentration of a substance in n-octanol and in water at the equilibrium at a speci-
ﬁed temperature and, hence, is used to describe the hydrophobicity of MPs (Luo et al.
2014, ChemSafetyPro 2019). MPs with lower logKow values show higher aﬃnity to wa-
ter, conversely, they tend to adsorb to particulate matter (ChemSafetyPro 2019). Thus,
chemicals with logKow < 2.5 are tendentiously transported by water, while chemicals with
logKow > 4 will be more likely present in the sediments (Luo et al. 2014).
Concerning the organic carbon-water partition coeﬃcient, logKoc, it is deﬁned as the
tendency of a pollutant to adsorb onto the organic carbon in the solids (Gavrilescu 2005,
ChemSafetyPro 2019). Pollutants with logKoc > 3 are not supposed to move and being
trasported due to their high aﬃnity to be attached with particulate matter (Gavrilescu
2005). Values of logKoc < 2.47 indicate that the MPs can likely leach to groundwater and
occur in surface waters (Gavrilescu 2005).
Also the acid dissociation constant, pKa, can play an important role determining the
chemi-sorption of MPs Luo et al. (2014).
As studied by Sch¨afer et al. (2011), acidic
functional groups, such as the phenolic hydroxyl one, can become negatively charged,
inducing charge repulsion and therefore hampering the MPs sorption to solids (Luo et al.
2014).
Eventually, transport in the aqueous phase can occur through (i) wet deposition, (ii)
surface run-oﬀ, (iii) inﬁltration into the ground, (iv) storm sewers, (v) drains and (vi)
rivers (Gavrilescu 2005). MPs fate during the aqueous transport is then also controlled
by several physico-chemical processes, such as (i) evapotranspiration, (ii) advection, (iii)
dispersion, and in agricultural ﬁelds even by (iv) crop-root uptake (Gavrilescu 2005).
2.2.4
Fate modelling
Despite that for pesticides modelling tools are already available in literature, as for instance
De Schepper et al. (2012), Burns et al. (1982) and Mackay (2001), on the other hand there
are poor studies regarding instruments for all the other classes of MPs (Geissen et al. 2015).
The challenge is to develop models able to describe the fate of the MPs coming from several
diﬀerent sources and interacting within all the possible compartments (Geissen et al. 2015).
In most of the models already existing a steady state approach was used (see Burns
et al. (1982) and Mackay (2001)), whereas MPs require dynamic models in order to de-
scribe their intrinsic high input variability and make the subsequent risk assessment reli-
able (De Schepper et al. 2012). For instance, the European Surface Water FOCUS work
group (Forum for the Co-ordination of Pesticide Fate Models and Their Use), an European
Commission project whose objective was to elaborate a common framework for the cal-

3.1
The “Navigli Project”
This chapter is structured in (i) a brief introduction related to the historical rivers of
the city of Milan, (ii) an overview regarding the main features of the project and, at
the end, (iii) a focus on the most interesting characteristic for this study, namely, the
implementation of the groundwater heat pumps.
3.1.1
Navigli history
The Navigli river system started to be built already during the Roman era, it was developed
within 1200 and 1500 and then completed in the subsequent centuries (Milano 2018b).
Comprising the Naviglio Martesana, the Cerchia, the Naviglio Grande and Naviglio Pavese,
it was a navigable hydraulic system with a length extension of more than 130 km.
As reported by Brown (2018), in 1819 was completed the last stretch of the Naviglio
Pavese river and, hence, was ﬁnally being possible the connection between the cities of
Milan and Pavia and the three important water basin of (i) the Po river, (ii) the Ticino
river and (iii) the lakes located in north, as shown in ﬁgure 3.1. Thus, this hydraulic con-
nection enhanced the traﬃc of shipping boats for the city of Milan, crossed both incoming
and outgoing. A huge number of “sciostre”, large covered spaces used as warehouse and
deposits for the materials coming from the trades, spread along the river sides.
However, when the commerce reached its highest development point, the idea to close
the Navigli system started to diﬀuse.
This was due to the widespread perception of
surface water as vehicles for urban health and hygiene problems, already present and then
exacerbated. The major European cities saw a rapid growing of their population owed to
the industrial development and this fact worsened the hygienic situation. The main cause
was attributed to the direct releases of waste waters into surface water bodies and the
consequent high level of pollution. Furthermore, the inﬁltration of contaminated water
often ended with polluting the reservoirs of supply water (Brown 2018).
Consequently, in 1866 on the “Il Politecnico” journal was published the ﬁrst article
regarding the adverse hygienic conditions due to the waterway. In 1889, the proposal to
build new modern and more eﬃcient networks for the aqueduct and the sewer system and
to cover the main rivers in the Milan urban context was advanced. The inner waterway of

Groundwater heat pumps for heating-cooling purposes
Is currently well known that, since the ﬁrst years of the ’90s, Milan has been aﬀected by
the presence of a too shallow groundwater table that still causes issues, for istance, to
many stretches of the subway and to some underground parking lots, as can be seen in
ﬁgure 3.6 (Milano 2015).
Figure 3.6: Section of the city of Milan with represented the variation of the groundwater

The presence of the hydraulic re-connection and the use of shallow water wells (SWWs)
can represent a solution to these kind of problems and might also regenerate the deepest
aquifers. In fact, the Milan aqueduct, owing to the increase pollution of the shallowest
aquifer, uses always deeper wells to withdraw water. Originally, the wells were located at
a depth of around 20−40 m, whilst currently many of them reach deepness of even 100 m
(PTF 2018). Ground waters are usually naturally puriﬁed by the gravel and sand layers
that act as ﬁlters, however, if they are constantly used and not regenerated, they lose
their ﬁlter-function and the contamination risks to reach the depth where the aqueduct
withdraws the water (Castiglioni et al. 2018, PTF 2018). To protect the deepest aquifers
some SWWs, currently inactive, could be re-activated in order to create a sort of barrier
and regenerate the ﬁltering layers (PTF 2018).
This situation can lead to other beneﬁts as the groundwater oﬀers enormous energetic
potentialities thanks to its constant yearly temperature around 12-14 ◦C (Milano 2015).
In fact, the transposition of the directive 2012/27/UE will imply to get rid of the old
oil gas boilers and the simplest and most eﬃcient way to decrease the costs linked with
the heating operations and, at the same time, lowering the local emissions of CO2, is the
exploitation of GHPs (PTF 2018). As shown in ﬁgure 3.7, in Milan there is still a high
density of oil gas boilers with a power capacity between 500 kW and 1000 kW (A2A 2018).
According to PTF (2018), the use of GHPs is then suggested to reduce the management
costs comparing to fossil fuels based way of heating and to replace the old boiler systems.
In highly urbanized areas is often challenging the exploitation of the GHPs potentiality
because of the distance to surface water bodies as receptors (PTF 2018). As reported in
FLA (1999), the distance between the point of extraction and the point of release should
preferably not exceed 300 m. Due to this fact is not rare that municipalities decide not to
employ such systems for the impossibility to install GHPs and usually incorrect strategies
are thus adopted. Frequently the waters withdrawn from the aquifer are then released in
the same one, altering the groundwater temperature, or in the sewer network, inducing
an unpleasant dilution of the organic matter and increasing the amount of water to be
treated in WWTPs (PTF 2018). Hence, having available a waterway in the heart of the

The former have been considered as the optimal conﬁguration because their small size,

not higher than 50 ◦C and in the case of the radiant panels lower then 35 ◦C. Moreover,
the control of the power generated can be regulated on, for instance, the fan velocity or
on other parameter rather than on the water temperature. They can be still used for hot

4.1.1

The surface water model being used in the current master thesis work is the one developed
within the European project ScorePP, previously cited in the chapter 2.2.4. Based on the

The RWQM N◦1, based on the same modelling procedures as the ASM used to describe
the processes in an activated sludge unit, requires a large amount of data as input and,

been, hence, used and extended with two necessary submodels: (i) the ﬁrst to describe a

energy demand and supply. Due to the lack of detailed data related to these thematics,
the idea has been to provide a methodology to calculate dynamic patterns for diﬀerent
categories of energy that can be demanded and for diﬀerent building typologies. Moreover,
the objective of the project is to provide tools that can modelize the energy demand
or supply depending on the diverse environmental conditions and geographical locations
(Connolly et al. 2015).
According to Connolly et al. (2015), the energy demand proﬁles are computed based
on the Heat Degree Days (HDD), or Cooling Degree Days (CDD) in case of cooling. HDD
and CDD are calculated as diﬀerence between the outside and the set-point temperature.
In case the two coincide, there is no need for energy for climatization and the HDD or
CDD value is then assumed to be zero. On the other hand, if the temperature outside is
higher than the one inside the building, the model starts computing the amount of heating
energy to be demanded per each hour, function of the HDD, and the other way around for
the cooling case. Thus, the results are strongly dependent on the set point chosen that,
for Italian cities, is 16 ◦C in winter and 17 ◦C in summer.
Inside the European programme, by turn, Zangheri et al. (2014) developed the “En-
tranze project” that aimed to provide heating and cooling energy demand proﬁles in
diﬀerent EU cities for the following reference types of buildings: (i) single houses, (ii)
apartment blocks, (iii) oﬃces and (iv) schools.

4.2
Scenario analysis
Four diﬀerent scenarios were evaluated in the current work:
• First scenario: the actual situation. Therefore, only (i) the Nosedo WWTP and (ii)
the Vettabbia are included;
• Second scenario: Martesana. The (i) Naviglio Martesana input and (ii) the Darsena-
Vettabbia diversion are added to the model;
• Third scenario: “clean” GHPs.
The heat pumps are then considered, but it is
assumed the release of water with null concentration of MPs.
• Fourth scenario: GHPs. Eventually, also the occurrence of atrazine is evaluated.
The ﬁrst scenario coincides with the current situation where the only input for the
Vettabbia are (i) few SWWs which contribute, in terms of ﬂow, is negligible and (ii) the
eﬄuent coming from the Nosedo WWTP. Due to the lack of data, either the Nosedo out-
going ﬂow and the atrazine concentration in the eﬄuent are assumed to be constant during
the whole year. Thus, only the seasonality of the agricultural withdrawals is considered
at the moment. As it represents the actual status in Milan, this scenario will afterwards
be used as reference to compare the results obtained by the other ones.
The model is then updated connecting the Vettabbia with the hydraulic re-connection
in the second scenario. Nonetheless, as the Martesana ﬂow is equal to the one deviated to-
wards the Darsena, the situation downstream the diversion is still unchanged with respect
to the previous scenario.
In the third scenario the GHPs are added to the model, but assuming no atrazine
present in groundwater and, hence, only clean water released in the waterway. Conse-
quently, it is expected a dilution eﬀect of variable magnitude either during the day and
throughout the whole year.
For the fourth and last scenario the potential future situation in Milan has been evalu-
ated. The simulation performed took into account the average MP concentration measured
in the ARPA sampling wells. Thus, the levels of atrazine that will be computed by the
model are expected to increase and it will be important to assess the relative contribution
of the GHPs with respect to the other inputs as, for instance, the Nosedo WWTP.

4.4
Environmental risk assessment
The common approach used in literature, as in Hernando et al. (2006), Silva et al. (2015),
Papadakis et al. (2018), Tousova et al. (2017), Riva et al. (2019), is the one based on the
so called Risk Quotient (RQ), or Hazard Index (HI):
RQ = MEC
PNEC
(4.16)
Where MEC stands for “Measured Environmental Concentration”, while PNEC for
“Predicted No Eﬀect Concentration”. The latter is calculated by turn dividing the EC50
(Eﬀect Concentration 50), namely the MP concentration inducing 50% of its maximal
eﬀect, by the Assessment Factor (AF, usually 1000), a constant accounting for analytical
and biological variability. For long-term toxicity tests, in place of the EC50 is suggested the
use of the No Observed Eﬀect Concentration (NOEC). This last represents the maximum
concentration not inducing signiﬁcant biological eﬀect on the organism (Riva et al. 2019).
The ratio, varying from 0 to 1, allows to estimate the eﬀect of the occurrence of MPs
in water. Hernando et al. (2006) proposed criteria in order to make intelligible the result
of the RQ:
• 0.01 - 0.1: low risk;
• 0.1 - 1: medium risk;
• > 1: high risk.
This procedure has been used in this work to assess the risk connected to the release
of MPs in the new waterway intended by the Navigli project.

percentile, chosen in order to either smooth the peaks of demand and to have ﬂow values
varying above and below the steady state one, and afterwards multiplied by 35 L/s and
by the number of SWWs for each location. As a result, the total ﬂow can instantaneously
be higher than 5 m3/s and null during some weeks in May and September.
As the Martesana ﬂow and the one diverted towards the Darsena are equal, is now
evident how the ﬂow of the hydraulic re-connection ending in the Vettabbia canal is totally
due to the GHPs.
To implement the GHPs in the hydraulic re-connection model was decided to subdivide
the wells in cluster according to the mutual distance among the SWWs. Thus, it has been
identiﬁed 8 clusters of GHPs: (i) one of them associated to the ARPA concentration
measurements in Well 7, (ii) to another the ones from Well 3 and (iii) the other six
coupled with the values belonging to Well 6. Detailed information can be found in table
5.2. The resulting scheme implemented in WEST consists in 3 river input generator (in
this way no transformation units are needed for the state variables) accounting for the
3 diﬀerent ARPA sampling locations providing the atrazine concentration values, with
several splitters used for fractioning the total GHPs input ﬂow according to table 5.2.

Conclusions
The current work focused on assessing the threat of MPs cross-contamination by GHPs
due to the potential chemical ingestion through food and the subsequent risk to human
health, as the water released is afterwards used to irrigate edible crops.
The model library RWQM1s1 MP, implemented in the WEST software, was used to
describe the case study area. An existing model, simulating the Nosedo WWTP eﬄuent
release in the Vettabbia canal and the agricultural withdrawals spread along the channel,
was adapted and extented. To reduce the computational eﬀort, river stretches of maximum
1 km lenght were decided to be used. Afterwards, the model was enlarged to comprise (i)
the Darsena-Vettabbia diversion, (ii) the future waterway planned to be built in the Navigli
project and (iii) the Martesana stretch crossing Milan. The model, eventually, consists of
several tanks of diﬀerent geometry, in accordance with the project documentation, for an
overall river lenght of about 26 km.
The performances of the model were tested choosing atrazine as representative sub-
stance due to its large occurrence in groundwater all over Milan. Atrazine is a widely
used pesticide with a low degradability and high sorption properties compared to other
MPs of the same class. Most of the mass of this substance is therefore found attached
to sediments, accounting around the 98%. The reliability of the extended model results
was compared with the former Vettabbia one and it showed negligible diﬀerences between
the two. Atrazine concentrations in Martesana and in the shallowest aquifer were found
in databases furnished by ARPA. Whilst for Nosedo a simpliﬁed approach was developed
considering (i) groundwater inﬁltration in the sewer network and (ii) the MP removal
eﬃciency in WWTPs.
More than 200 SWWs, successively potentially convertible in GHPs, have been identi-
ﬁed all over the territory of Milan. 31 of them, belonging to the municipal administration,
already exploit the Vettabbia canal as ﬁnal water receptor. Furthermore, their proxim-
ity to the new waterway makes these SWWs ideal for the Navigli project and, hence,
were chosen as representative of the future status of the city. In steady state conditions,
the average ﬂow of a single well is 35 L/s that, summing the contribution of each well,
reaches 1085 L/s in total, substantially equal to the one intended for the ﬁrst phase of the
project. The heat pumps dynamicity was hypothesized to follow the Milan hourly energy
and cooling demand leading to peaks of GHPs ﬂow during winter and summer and even
null releases in May and September. Characteristic daily patterns are also observed as the
demand is maximum during the morning and minimum in the evening.
Four diﬀerent scenarios were analysed to better understand the eﬀects of implementing
the GHPs within the Navigli project: (i) the ﬁrst scenario, representing the current status,
where only the Nosedo WWTP and the Vettabbia were considered as input in the model;
51
==PAGE:61==
(ii) in the second scenario were added the Martesana and the Darsena-Vettabbia deviation;
(iii) for the third one the heat pumps were eventually considered, but it was assumed null
releases of atrazine; (iv) ﬁnally, in the last scenario even the occurrence of atrazine was
evaluated. The ﬁrst two scenarios are substantially equal in terms of atrazine concentration
at the end of the Vettabbia. Conversely, larger diﬀerences can be noticed in the third and
fourth scenarios where, respectively, is observed an eﬀect of dilution and a consistent
increase of the MP concentration levels. The GHPs lead to high variability of the output
results making varying, for instance, the atrazine concentration in the dissolved phase up
to the 60%. The same percentage increase is seen for the average annual concentration
in the last scenario compared to one computed in the ﬁrst, conﬁrming the high impact of
the GHPs upon the waterway. A MP mass balance over the bulk-water compartment was
afterwards performed and highlighted that the pollution by atrazine is mostly due to the
GHPs rather than to the Nosedo WWTP eﬄuent.
A Monte Carlo simulation was then performed to assess the uncertainties related to
the data gathered and to the model library parameters, both for the MP fate and water
quality submodels. A total of 1005 simulations were made running showing as result a low
variability in the concentration output annual proﬁles. The maximum uncertainties were
found for the third and fourth scenario and for the fraction sorbed to POC accounting, in
both the cases, about the 7.7% from the annual average value. Conversely, for the sorbed
to sediments fraction the variability was way lower, estimated to be, on average, only the
0.1%. As the annual proﬁles remained substantially unchanged, the uncertainty ranges
were most likely due to the variability connected to the MP input concentrations.
Eventually, to assess the risk connected to the occurrence of atrazine in the Vettabbia
channel, an ERA was computed in diﬀerent river stretches. The method was based on
the so called Risk Quotient, the ratio between the MEC and the PNEC, where the latter
was found to be 600 µg/L as also suggested in the Directive 2008/105/EC on priority
pollutants. Nonetheless, as the atrazine concentrations were found to vary around a few
µg/L, the risk posed by the MP can be considered “low”. In fact, the maximum RQ value
computed was 0.037, suggesting null risks to human health in each four scenarios.
