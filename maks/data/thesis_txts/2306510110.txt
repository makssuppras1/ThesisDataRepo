Summary (max. 2000 characters): 
This thesis concerns reliability assessment of array cable networks for large 
offshore wind farms. The main objective is to investigate the economic 
viability of using array cable loops to increase the array cable network 
redundancy. One of the preconditions for this thesis, is the use of expected 
next generation technology. This implies an array system voltage of 66 kV 
and wind turbine generator size of 10 MW. The motivation for this thesis, is 
the urge to design optimal and cost efficient array cable networks, when 
considering the whole lifespan of the offshore wind farm.  
In order to resolve the main objective, several sub problems are addressed. 
The final methodology uses an analytical method to assess the reliability of 
the components associated with the array cable network. The entities for the 
reliability assessment is the cable failure rates and mean time to repair for 
cables, terminations and switchgears. The methodology also proposes the 
use of Graph Theory and Linear Programming to determine the expected 
offshore wind farm power curves, while subjected to possible array cable 
section failures. In addition, the methodology delivers an approach to handle 
intermediate power transfer limitations caused by the array cable 
specifications and layout. 
Increasing the array cable network redundancy yields a higher initial capital 
expenditure (CAPEX). The additional investment should be justified by 
reducing the lost production during possible array cable failures. Hence, this 
thesis aims at calculating the net present value (NPV) of the investment, 
while considering the expected lifespan of the offshore wind farm. The 
CAPEX and NPV enables the comparison of numerous array cable network 
topologies. 
The methodology is implemented as a simulation tool in Matlab, which 
together with the Graph Theory enables the possibility of evaluating various 
array cable network designs. The simulation tool is used to evaluate a large 
offshore wind farm layout rated for 400 MW, designed with 40 wind turbines 
each 10 MW. The findings favor the investment of array cable loops. 
Evidently, the conclusion yields, that for large offshore wind farms with 66 kV 
array system voltage, increased redundancy using array cable loops, can be 
economically viable.

Preface
This master thesis is submitted to the department of Wind Energy at the Technical University
of Denmark to fulﬁll the requirements for the MSc degree in Electrical Engineering. The project
has been conducted at DTU Wind Energy, in collaboration with DONG Energy Wind Power
and DTU Management Engineering.
The initial project idea and description originate from the department of Electrical System
Analysis at Dong Energy Wind Power. This project was motivated by a desire to compare the
reliability and economic viability for diﬀerent array cable networks. All parties of the project
have contributed with constructive input for the further speciﬁcation and development of the
project.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm (OWF), in the world, was operating from year 1991 in Denmark,
with 11 wind turbines (WT) rated 450 kW each [2]. Ever since, the overall OWF capacity and
WT output power have increased signiﬁcantly. Today the largest OWF London Array uses 175
WT’s with an combined capacity of 630 MW [1]. In Europe alone, the total OWF capacity
reached 11 GW in 2015. The share of oﬀshore installations in the total wind power installation
are also signiﬁcantly increasing. In 2015, oﬀshore wind power accounted for 24% of the total
new wind power installation. This increment is linked to the maturing of the oﬀshore technol-
ogy, the low interest rate environment and the liquidity in the ﬁnancial markets [7]. The OWF
technology also oﬀers strong initiative to ﬁght the Not-In-My-Backyard attitude regarding wind
power in general, and contribute to the reduction of greenhouse gases.
The eﬀectiveness of policy and regulatory frameworks conducted by leading European coun-
tries, allows for continuously development and expansion of the oﬀshore wind power capacity.
However, it is in every ones interest to continue to improve OWF projects to become less eco-
nomical dependent on government support in the future.
One of the key element to make OWF projects less expensive, is to make deliberate decisions
towards optimizing every aspect of the designing process.
In order to maximize the overall
investment, many aspects of the OWF’s are to be optimized, from single components and sub-
systems to the overall project management.
This includes the WT formation, WT Towers,
electrical infrastructure etc.
The placement of the WT’s is a complicated process, which considers numerous stochastic
variables. Every OWF project faces unique obstacles due to environment of deployment, and
distances from onshore. Combined with the current and future trends of continuously increasing
WT rated power output, and new components, engineers are imposed to redesign and optimize
the contemporary best solution.
One of many entities to improve the overall OWF project is the design of the collection grid.
The array cable network, together with other components, forms the electrical collection grid,
that collect the power from the WT’s, and transfer it back to the oﬀshore substation. The
forthcoming scope of this thesis will be the assessment of array cable network, with the motive
to optimize the layout and favoring the investment.
1.2
Background
For every new OWF project, the design of the array cable network is carefully investigated and
the viability of the investment is justiﬁed. In addition, the amount of redundancy in the array
cable network, is to be justiﬁed economically. The next generation technology, within the ﬁeld

2.2
Reliability and availability assessment
The terms reliability and availability are used diﬀerently according to the context of use, hence
an explanation of the use in this case is required. The purpose, in this project, is to determine
the expected energy not produced, due to the layout of the collection grid, during the lifetime of
the OWF. Now this quantity is dictated by the reliability and system impact of each individual
component. For a repairable/replaceable component, as in this case, the availability is dictated
by the component reliability and maintainability. Where reliability expresses the component
tendency to fail, and maintainability the component tendency to be repaired/replaced.
Now the term overall availability, is in this context, strictly used to express the OWF capability

Overall, there are three major quantities of interest for this project.
Firstly, the reliability
and maintainability of each individual component with the lowest entities available, as cables,

2.2.1
Single component reliability
The foundation of system reliability assessment is the component reliability. Here, every single
component can be treated as a subsystem and be decomposed into further subsystems or com-
ponents. Hence, when considering single component reliability in the scope of a large system,
an appropriate entity level is chosen. The entity level is of course dictated by the data available,
and the case of investigation. The component reliability data can come in various types includ-
ing failure/success or failure count (discrete), or failure time data (continuous) [16, ch. 4, p. 85].
Where discrete data models are used in certain cases, for instant to assess the startup reliability
for emergency diesel generators (failure/success count). In the case of assessing the reliability
of the array cables and related components, the assessment of failure time data are required.
For simplicity the stochastic behavior of a single component is investigated, under some assump-
tions. Firstly, the component is operating from t = 0 and only one failure mode is present (i.e.
either 0% or 100% functionality.) Secondly, the component is operating for a lifetime period X
(random variable) until breakdown, and remains in the state for a period of time Y (random
variable). The component is either repaired or replaced during Y , and return to an operating
state afterwards.

System reliability
Section 2.2.1 gave an introduction to the assessment of reliability of a single component. In ad-

When addressing the reliability for a large systems the complexity increase signiﬁcantly. The
reliability theory in general oﬀers an variety of approaches to obtain a suitable assessment of
the reliability of large systems. Each with diﬀerent advantages for a given application, and also
reservations regarding the underlying assumptions. This section provides four relevant methods
for this thesis, and an adequate evaluation of which is most suitable.
Referring to the topology in ﬁgure 2.1d in section 2.1 each cable section can be decomposed
into the respective subcomponents, see ﬁgure 2.3. Each cable section consist of 2 terminations,
2 switchgears and one cable. They have a serial dependency relationship, meaning that failure
of any component makes the particular cable section unavailable for power transfer. WT’s using
the particular cable section for power transfer are either disconnected or referred to an optional

Markov Chains
In terms of reliability assessment, the Markov Chains uses state transitions diagram to repre-
sent the reliability/availability or behavior of a given system. It models the system with respect
to time, and is evidently used to measure performance of a system in a given context. Using
Markov chains for reliability assessment implies that the designer can generate a ﬁnite set of
states that can represent the real system. In addition, the impact of each state should also be
speciﬁed [20].
In the context of reliability and availability assessment, the Markov chain can be utilized to
calculate the expected long-run availability. In its most simple form, a Markov Chain can be
present by two ﬁnite states. For instant for a component, a operating state and a non-operating
state. The transition rate from the operating state to the non-operating state is the expected
failure rate λ. Likewise, is the transition rate from the non-operating state to the operating

Using Markov Chains to simulate reliability in a complex system would yield diﬀerent results
for each trail. In addition, in general for complicated models one cannot simple derive the pos-
terior distributions analytically. Hence, Markov Chains is often combined with an Monte Carlo
method [21, p. 113–118]. The Monte Carlo simple samples from the posterior distribution by
evaluating the Markov Chains network for a ﬁxed timespan. This is done with many iterations,
until one has a representative sample set of the posterior distribution. This technique has many
applications and variations, and can be used to compute the long term expected availability for
complex systems. One of the major advantages of Markov Chains Monte Carlo (MCMC) im-
plementation is the capability of change transition rate during the simulation to mimic real-life
conditions. For instant as a function of time, or after occurrence of failures nearby.
Markov Chain implementation for a small system seems straight forward, nevertheless, when
the complexity of the system increases the implementation does likewise. Furthermore, when
complexity of the system increases the model tend to be every hard to make ﬂexible for testing
other conﬁgurations. Furthermore, this method does not oﬀer any practical way to identify the
impact of each possible state.
Discrete event simulation
Discrete event simulation is suitable for modeling discrete event systems. A classic example is a
traﬃc signal network, where each signal can hold three diﬀerent states (green, yellow, and red),
and has a predetermined time-interval for state transition. In terms of reliability assessment,
the availability of the components that constitutes the collection grid, can be model with either
a continuous or non-continuous representation. The state transition in the MCMC method from
last section were calculated for each deﬁned time instant in a continuous domain, and with
a ﬁxed lifespan. In contrast, the discrete event simulation oﬀers the possibility to describe a
system with a non-continuous behavior. Instead of evaluating the failures over the expected
lifespan of the system, the discrete event simulation models the system as a series of discrete
events (failures) [10, ch1]. The time-interval to the next failure is drawn from a representative

long-term availability as in (2.18). The incentive to use discrete event simulation is often the
same as with MCMC. For complex systems the analytical approach is usually a simpliﬁcation,
which for some cases become an unrealistic representation of the system [22, p. 123].
The algorithm (3) in appendix C oﬀers pseudo-code for a simple implementation of discrete

also handle various additional aspect of the OWF lifespan. This includes some variations of
maintenance scheduling, and logistic issues due to weather conditions. Even though, the imple-
mentation can be quiet easy, this method does not oﬀer any practical way to identify the impact
of each possible event.
2.2.3
Methods selecting
To investigate, which of the methods, is best suited for this thesis, the beneﬁts and values are
tested in a small exercise. Furthermore, the purpose with the exercise is to get some hand-on
experience with the implementations of the methods. Due to the extensive use of MCMC in
similar reliability assessment studies, and the inﬂexible nature of the implementation (in this
case), the MCMC is decided inapplicable for this project, and is therefore not used in the ex-
ercise. The exercise is shortly outlined in appendix C. The outcome of this exercise, has been
evaluated by the project participants, and the analytical method is found most appealing. The
strongest argument for the selected method is simplicity and ﬂexibility. Also, when an analyt-
ical expression can give a suﬃcient result, the implementation of a simulation based approach
becomes unnecessary. Moreover, the analytical expression yields a much faster evaluation of
the layout assessment, compared to simulation based methods, which often requires 10000+
iterations. This is particular favorable when the designer aims to use optimize the layout using
various optimization techniques.
One of the major concerns in this project, is to determine the impact of a given failure event,
when considering the grid layout and the cable limitations. The analytical method is appealing
due to possibility of easy adoption to any strategy developed later in the process. For this thesis
the input is not ﬁxed, hence Markov Chains method and discrete event simulation can for some
cases be complicated to implement.

Sub conclusion

to the simplicity and easy adoption to the further development of the project. However, the
other methods oﬀer great advantages, including the possibility of implementing maintenance
scheduling. Hence, these methods could be implemented in future development of the project,
when solution for the other concerns are found.

Rules based approach
In the ﬁrst approach, a couple of common rules are used to modify the initial matrix Aineq to
reﬂect the current cable failure. The rules are hard coded for this speciﬁc setup, and listed
below.
1. No power can ﬂow in the cable section subjected to failure: hence the corresponding row
is pure zeros.
2. If the failure is between the cable loop and substation, the cable loop is then assigned
WT’s which active power ﬂow in the faulty cable during normal operation.
3. If the failure is not between the cable loop and substation, no WT’s are assigned to the
loop cable.
4. The radial with the faulty cable: cables between substation and cable loop are unassigned
the faulty cable’s WT’s
5. The radial with no faulty cable: cables between substation and cable loop are assigned the
faulty cable’s WT’s.
This method yields a decent result, but also some uncertainties, and shortcomings.
Hence,
another method is investigated.
Graph Theory based approach
When considering the current direction change and other aspects, the hard coded algorithm
gets extensively more complex. This could for instant happen when cable ’CS 1’ fails, then WT1
uses ’C1 2’ to transfer power in the opposite direction. In addition, introducing non-symmetrical
radial pairs with diﬀerent lengths also makes things complicated.

ration, state of art Graph theory [12, p. 954-961] has been applied. This allows for a convenient
connecting between the user input, and the calculation. Moreover, the notation allows for sev-

3.2.6
Discussion & Sub-conclusion
Under the assumptions: only one cable loop between radial pairs, (N-1), and that all WT’s sees
the same WS - The investigated methods provides a reliable result, with the potential to be
ﬂexible enough to test various collection grid layouts. Furthermore, it also allows for simpliﬁed
implementation of cables losses using the expected power ﬂow from the LP solver. To obtain
a more accurate power curves, the cable losses should be included. However, this would be
very nonlinear and require more another solving approach. In addition, the control of the wind
turbines also eﬀects the possible shape of the power curve. Basically, the intermediate active
power limitation CPlimit could be moved according to the control strategy.
The simulation is quiet ﬂexible and can easily be modiﬁed in terms of collection grid layouts and
array cable selections. Next step, is to convert the power curves into expected lost production
LP for each failure scenario, based on the provide expected yearly wind speed scenario. Then is
the reliability/availability model applied, together with relevant installation prices, which allow
me to initiate the cost-beneﬁt analysis.

manually by altering the loop cable costs.

4.2
Discussion
For this particular study case the results suggest, that the investment of array cable loops is
economically viable. In addition, when adding these cable loops, additional investment in larger
cable diameter for carefully select cable section, can further contribute to the NPV. The ideal
placement of the array cable loops is at the end of the radials, due to the layout symmetry.
However, keep in mind that for more complex layouts with nonparallel radials, the result could
be diﬀerent. The length of the array cable loop would vary as a result of the placement, and
yield various investment costs. This could inherently yield the result, that the optimal array
loop placement for the speciﬁc layout, is somewhere in the middle of two the radials.

Another paper [15], concludes the opposite using similar array cable network topologies. The

contrasts to this thesis, the cable diameter is upgraded to the highest available type for all cable
sections. Another important diﬀerence is the reliability assessment (Monte Carlo simulation),
which only concerns the cable, and not the switchgear or terminations. Remember, that the
sensitivity analysis in section 4.1.3 indicated that the switchgear reliability is an very sensitive
parameter.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The main objective for this thesis was to assess the economic viability of using array cable loops
in the array cable network topology. Under the precondition of future OWF technology trends,
leading towards 10 MW WT’s and 66 kV collection grid voltage. This main object implies the
realization of reliability assessment of the array cable network for large OWF, and quantifying
the cost of lost production. In addition, to accommodate these objects a simulation tool was
conducted using Matlab.
The review of suitable reliability methods lead to the investigation of four methods.
Two
methods, the analytical method and reliability block diagram, which are based on a analytical
approach. While, the two other methods, MCMC and discrete event simulation, are simulation
based. Through exercises and addressing the advantages/disadvantage of the methods, the ana-
lytical method were found most suitable. The main incentives for the method, are the simplicity
and the possibility for ﬂexible implementation. In addition, the method oﬀers fast computations
for optimization purposes.
To evaluate the reliability assessment in terms of lost production, the system impact of each
possible cable section failure scenario, were assessed. It is assumed that the occurrence of failures
do not overlap (N-1). Using topologies with array cable loops, yields various overall OWF power
curves for the failure scenarios. The OWF power curve is dictated by the number of available
WT’s and the array cable power transfer capability.
The state-of-art Graph Theory and the Floyd-Warshall algorithm were utilized, to determine the
available WT’s, and the WT power transfer routes. The possible power production during each
WS scenario and failure scenario were formulated and solved as a Linear Optimization problem.
While obeying the corresponding cable power transfer capability constraints. The result is a
robust and ﬂexible method to evaluate various array cable networks.
The cost-beneﬁt analysis is carried out by estimating the CAPEX of the compared layouts,
and by calculating the expected NPV. The expected yearly lost production (due to failures)
is represent by the actual cost using the expected OWF electricity price. The yearly cost of
lost production represent the yearly cash ﬂow. Together with the expected discount rate, OWF
lifespan, and CAPEX, the NPV was assessed.
The reliability theory, Graph Theory, OWF power curve generation, and cost-beneﬁt analy-
sis were implemented in Matlab using an Excel workbook as input reference. This allows for
convenient visualization of the input data, and enables the possibility for evaluating numerous
of diﬀerent array cable network conﬁgurations.
The test case used in this thesis is provided by DONG Energy. The simulation tool was used to
assess the layout array cable network. The key ﬁndings for the particular test case were:

5.1
Accommodation for future work
5.1.1
Array cable network topology
During this thesis some preliminary assumption regarding the switching concept were introduced.
Since the switching concept aﬀects the number of components, it will inherently have an impact
on the system reliability. Therefore, is it suggest to implement the behavior of the switchgear,
and the corresponding eﬀect on the network during various failures. This could be accomplished
by extending the collection grid representation with the switch gears from the switching concept.
For each possible cable section failure, the two closest switch gears are found with the same graph
theory method from subsection 3.2.4, and the switchgear accordingly disconnect. Evidently, this
would allow for various switching and protection concept to be applied, and assessed in terms
of reliability.
However, introducing the possibility of various switching concepts, will make
assumption regarding equal maintenance cost for the compared layouts, untrue. Hence, the
cost-beneﬁt analysis should also be extent to concern the maintenance costs.
5.1.2
Reliability assessment
The Graph Theory combined with Linear Optimization yields a decent result regarding devel-
oping the program adapting to various input data (layouts). However, this method could also
be combined with MCMC method or equivalent, to represent more realistic scenarios. This
could include the stochastic behavior of the weather conditions, maintenance issues, and logistic
obstacles. The implementation would of course be comprehensive, but the beneﬁt would be a
more accurate assessment of the value of array cables loops.
The main time to repair (MTTR) for various OWF can vary due to remote location and weather
conditions for the OWF. The MTTR for this project is the average of the best and worst con-
ditions case, based on the data provide from DONG Energy.
However, when designing the
collection grid layout for oﬀshore remote location, the distribution of the weather scenarios
should be used to compute the relevant MTTR.

5.1.3
Cost-beneﬁt analysis
An obvious improvement of the cost-beneﬁt analysis would be to add conducting losses into the
model. This would aﬀect the NPV directly and make larger cable diameter (at radial ends)
combined with increased redundancy more appealing. During the calculations regarding power
curves the power ﬂow on each cable is estimate using the Graph Theory and Linear Optimization.
Hence, a simpliﬁed conducting loss model could easily be implemented together with the resistive
losses. This approach would yield an estimation of the conducting losses both during normal
operation and during failure modes.
5.1.4
Optimization
In terms of optimization, ﬁnding a decent solution within a discrete solution space with multiple
local maximums, can be comprehensive task. Nevertheless, it is an important part of inves-
tigation optimal array cable layouts, when array cable loops are optional. This aspect could
be further embraced by implementing the optimization algorithm proposed in appendix F.1,
directly in the simulation tool.

Implementation

velopment very convenient.
For future development, it could be wise to convert to another
appropriate programming language. Faster programming languages as C/C++ or C# are more
comprehensive to develop in, however, they can be implemented at any preferable framework.

Appendix F
F.1
Automated optimization
In the study case, in this thesis, the radials are symmetric, which eases the search for an
optimal solution. However, this symmetry is not always the case, hence this appendix suggest
an approach for solving more diﬃcult conﬁgurations.
Genetic algorithm GA
The genetic algorithm (GA) is a stochastic search algorithm used to ﬁnd near optimal solution
in a comprehensive search space. The algorithm is inspired by natural genetic population. The
terminology used is also inspired from the natural selection process. The basic concept is to
maintain a population of chromosomes. Each chromosome represent candidate solution to the
object function [18, p. 199]. Each candidate (chromosome) has a corresponding ﬁtness to the
object function, which can be compared with the other candidates. Good candidates (good ﬁt-
ness) are more likely to used to form new candidates. This process mimics a natural competition
process, and passes on ’genetic’ information. GA have a great success in solving optimization
problems, partly due to the ability exploit of the accumulated information about the search
space. This information are used to make biased subsequent searches for an optimal candidate.
In this case, the solution space is discrete, and expands quickly with the number of array cables,
cable types, and allow array loop placements.
The GA is implemented using Matlab optimization tool box, see appendix F.1. Furthermore, is
the multi-threading option enabled to speed up the calculations. Multi-threading is particular
useful when the object functions uses excessive computation power during evaluation. For more
comprehensive problems, the use cloud computing is recommend. Figure F.1, shows the output
from the script in appendix F.1. The compute time was approximately 30 min, and the result
is identical with the manual optimization in section 4.1.2. The result can be reproduced using
the random generator seed rng(10).
The array cable network in ﬁgure F.2 is a ﬁctive example, and has two radials with a non-
symmetrical and non-parallel structure. The layout is subjected to the optimization script, and
the proposed solution is in ﬁgure F.3. The solution yields reasonable result, and is particular
interesting since the cable types of the radials is non-symmetrical.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes
