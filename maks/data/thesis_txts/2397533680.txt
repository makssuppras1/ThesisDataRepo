Preface 
This thesis is written as a part of the fullfilment of the Master of Science program Transportation and Logistics 
at DTU Management Engineering at The Technical University of Denmark. The work has been done in 
collaboration with the Delft University of Technology in Delft, the Netherlands.  
The report consists of an introduction to the topic, including a review of existing literature, an analysis of 
accident data, an observational study, and lastly the results from the three parts are used to make a 
conclusion on the findings and a recommendation.  
 
Abstract 
Denmark and the Netherlands are the two countries in Europe with the highest mode-share of cyclists. In 
both countries safe cycling is supported by good and extensive bicycle infrastructure. However, some 
differences in the infrastructure solutions for cyclists chosen in the two countries exist. Forinstance this is the 
case with the use of two-way bicycle paths. In the Netherlands it is more the rulte than the exception to 
encounter a two-way bicycle path on a road in both bigger and smaller cities, where as the two-way bicycle 
paths are very rarely used in Denmark. This difference in cyclist infrastructure is interesting and has 
motivated this study.  
The purpose of the study was to investigate the topic of two-way bicycle paths with a particular focus on the 
interaction between cars and cyclists in priority intersections. The study included the following parts: 1. a 
literature review with the purpose to identify state of the art on the safety of two-way bicycle paths, 2. expert 
interviews in relevant Dutch municipalities with the purpose to understand the reasoning behind the 
extensive use of two-way bicycle paths. 3. Accident analysis with the purpose to get an overview of the 
general road safety level in the two countries as well as insight into the prevalence of cyclist accidents on 
two-way bicycle paths. 4. Observation of drivers approaching a two-way bicycle path with the purpose to 
compare the influence of two different infrastructure solutions on the scanning behavior of the drivers. 5. 
Refletions on the results and issues to consider in relation to the use of two-way bicycle paths in Denmark.  
Among other things the literature review revealed that safetycritical aspects related to two-way cycle pahts 
include that they lead to increased complexity in the traffic situation, possible mental work-overload and a 
tendency among right-turning drivers to not look to the right when approaching an intersection. This was 
supported by the accident analysis that revealed a relatively high number of accidents between vehicles 
entering an intersection and cyclists coming form the right side. The investigation into the Dutch use of two-
way bicycle paths revealed that different infrastructure design solutions exist, but no study had looked into 
the possible influence of a parallel distance between the two-way bicycle path and the roadway. Therefore, 
observational part of this study investigated the possible correlation between this specific infrastructure 
design element, and the scanning behavior of the drivers from the minor road. The data collection was 
performed by filming the traffic in a set of intersections in The Hague and Delft in the Netherlands. The 
analysis confirmed the main hypothesis that more drivers look to the right if there is a parallel distance 
between the two-way bicycle pathe and the road way.  Furthermore it was found that when the vehicles 
stop, they are more likely to look to the right. The results of this study can be used in a Danish context in the 
sense that it is important to know which safety issues to be aware of if more two-way bicycle paths are 
implemented in Denmark, and this study had added to the knowledge about which infrastructure measure 
can be used to mitigate those safety issues. The results indicate that a distance can make the crossing safer 
for the cyclists.

1 – Introduction 
This chapter will give the background for the study and for the hypotheses used in the investigations. The 
background is based on a study of national and international literature in the field of traffic planning and 
traffic safety. Section 1.1 will give an introduction to the theme of this project – safety of two-way bicycle 
paths – and explain why it is relevant to investigate this area. Section 1.2 is focused specifically on two-way 
bicycle paths and presents relevant results from existing literature. Section 1.3 and 1.4 describes the usage 
of this type of bicycle paths in the Netherlands and Denmark. Lastly, the literature review is summed up and 
the research questions are presented in section 1.5. 
 
1.1 – Motivation and purpose of the study 
Denmark and the Netherlands are known for being countries with a lot of cyclists. The bicycle as a mean of 
transport is more popular in these countries than in other European countries. On average, 8 % of the citizens 
in the EU use the bicycle as main transport mode. This number is 36 % in the Netherlands and 23 % in 
Denmark (European Cyclists' Federation, 2017). Thus, these are the two countries in the EU with the highest 
mode share of cycling. The high mode share of cycling in these countries has several reasons related to 
geography, spatial planning, climate, and culture, but most likely the extensive network of bicycle paths and 
other types of cyclist infrastructure is a key contributing factor. However, even though these countries both 
have good, safe, extensive bicycle infrastructure, there are some differences in the infrastructure too. For 
instance with regard to the use of two-way bicycle paths. In the Netherlands it is more the rulte than the 
exception to encounter a two-way bicycle path on a road in both bigger and smaller cities, where as the two-
way bicycle paths are very rarely used in Denmark. This difference in cyclist infrastructure is interesting and 
has motivated this study. The usage, safety and behavior related with two-way bicycle paths in the 
Netherlands is first explored, and then compared and related to the Danish context. 
1.1.1 – Road safety in the Netherlands in brief 
In the Dutch national Strategic Road Safety Plan 2008-2020 the Ministry of Transport, Public Works and Water 
Management has defined a set of goals in the road safety area, that by 2020 they want maximum 500 
fatalities and 10.600 seriously injured, which is a decrease of 25 % compared to the numbers from 2007, 
when the strategy was made (Ministry of Transport, 2008). In order to obtain these goals, in 2012 the Ministry 
of Infrastructure and Environment determined a set of 23 additional measures to help obtain the goals for 
2020. These measues are described in the policy ”Monitor Beleidsimpuls Verkeersveiligheid” (Weijersmars, 
et al., 2014). The measures are focused on cyclists and elderly people as target groups, because these two 
groups have had an unfavorable development (Weijersmars, et al., 2014). In order to obtain the goals for 
maximum number of fatalities and seriously injured by 2020, an average annual decrease of 5,6 % in fatalities 
and 16,1 % in seriously injured is needed. However during the past ten years there has been an average 
annual increase of 2,5 % in serious injuries and during the same time period the number of fatalities has 
decreased but only with an average of 3,0 % per year. Thus, according to the current development the goals 
for 2020 do not seem attainable (Weijermers, et al., 2017). 
There are around 180 bicycle fatalities per year in the Netherlands, and around 1800 seriously injured cyclists 
per year. Until 2010 the road safety was improving, but from 2011 and onwards there has been an increase 
in accidents. Therefore the Dutch municipalities are currently putting a lot of focus on cyclist safety, and 
coorperating with other eachother and with regional governments, organisations and research institutes 
(Schepers, 2015).

As a part of this project an interview with the traffic planning department of Utrecht Municipality was done, 
where they explained that they have the most busy bicycle paths in the Netherlands, and that some of the 
challenges are mopeds/scooters, e-bikes, and behavior. According the Utrecht Municipality the problem with 
the mopeds and scooters is that their speed is higher than the speed of the bicycles. This causes dangerous 
cyclist related situations, and therefore it is currently being discussed whether mopeds and e-bikes should 
drive on the roadway among the motor-vehicles instead. However this can expose the moped drivers for 
dangers as well.  
1.1.2 – Road safety in Denmark in brief 
The vision in the Danish national road safety strategy for 2013 to 2020 is to cut the number of fatalities per 
year in half from 2013 to 2020. Thus, in the year 2020 there should be no more than 120 killed, 1.000 severely 
injured and 1.000 lightly injured in road traffic accidents (Færdselssikkerhedskommissionen, 2013). In order 
to obtain this goal 10 focus areas have been pointed out, which are the areas where there especially are 
problems in traffic safety. One of these focus areas is cyclists. The part of the plan that relates to improving 
cyclist safety involves a number of initiatives regarding education, infrastructure and vehicles. For the 
infrastructure the plan is among other things to implement more speed-reducing measures, improve the 
signals and to retract more stop lines in order to have the cyclists in front of the vehicles before entering an 
intersection. As part of the goal setting, the Road Safety Commission has set yearly goals to keep track of the 
progress. The results of the progress tracking has showed that the goals for maximum number of cyclists who 
are killed and seriously injured are not being met. The goal for 2016 was maximum 293 cyclists and the actual 
number was 528. This number is is almost twice as big as is should be according to the plan 
(Færdselssikkerhedskomissionen, 2017). Therefore it is important to continue the focus on bicycle safety.  
 
1.2 – Two-way paths: State of the art 
In section 1.1 it was found that the two-way bicycle paths is a relevant infrastructure element to investigate, 
when looking at the Netherlands from a Danish perspective. In this chapter previous literature will be 
presented and some of the existing knowledge about the risks related to two-way bicycle paths will be 
explained.  
1.2.1 – What do we know about the safety of two-way bicycle paths from previous studies? 
The focus on two-way bicycle paths in the literature began in the early eighties. The safety effect of 
implementation of two-way bicycle paths in Denmark was investigated in a before- and after-study in 1982, 
where it was found that the number of cyclist accidents with personal injuries decreased with 42 % (Kallberg 
& Salusjärvi, 1982). This could indicate that two-way bicycle paths is a solution to improve safety. However, 
later studies have found results pointing in the opposite direction. Previous research has showed that 
bicyclists on a two-way bicycle path have a higher risk of getting into accidents. Summala, et al. (1996) did a 
study of 39 accidents that happened in crossing involving a two-way bicycle path in Helsinki during the years 
1987-1989. They defined eight different combinations of maneuvres of the bicycle and the car, which can 
lead to a conflict or an accident in this type of intersection. See Figure 1 below. 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
 
Figure 1 - Accident sitations of BMV crashes (Summala, et al., 1996). 
The figure shows the distribution of the 39 accidents, and it is clear that the most frequent type was type A, 
where the car is turning right and meets a crossing bicycle coming from the right on the two-way bicycle 
path. This tendency has later been confirmed by a study made in Denmark in 2011, including both an accident 
study and an observational study (Buch, 2011). The study of 339 accidents between a bicyclist and a motor 
vehicle showed that the bicyclists coming form the unexpected direction has a higher risk of accidents. 
Some studies have suggested that the accidents with motor vehicles and cyclists in general occur as a result 
of a combination of many components, like the complexity of the situation (workload), expectations, amount 
of traffic, speed, visual scanning, etc. Introducing two-way bicycle paths makes the traffic situation more 
complex and therefore it is important to take this complexity into account when designing the infrastructure, 
especially at intersections where there is a higher risk of accidents (Schepers, 2015). One way to explain the 
complexity of an intersection and how it relates to the drivers’ behavior is the theory of workload.  In order 
to improve the safety and increase the accident risk in the infrastructure it is important to understand how 
the road users repond to the infrastructure. Keeping the workload theory in mind when working with traffic 
planning can help towards keeping the instrastructure network safe. According to the theory there is a 
relationship between workload and performance. The more complex a traffic situation is, the higher is the 
workload for the road user. If there are too many tasks, the workload is too high. This is called overload and 
can lead to a decrease in performance for the road user ((Veltman & Jansen, 2003) in (Hagenzieker, 2017)). 
 
Figure 2 - Relationship between workload and performance (Theeuwes, et al., 2012). 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
This concept is applied in traffic planning by aiming to put the right amount of workload on the drivers, 
neither too little nor too much. In practice this means that there might be situations where it seems that it 
would be possible to make an intersection more safe by implementing more signals, more signs or more road 
marking, but in the end it can also have the opposite effect, that it puts too much workload on the driver, 
and they can end up overlooking another road user (World Road Association, 2016). 
In addition to keeping in mind the workload, it is also important that the information given to the drivers is 
given at the right level of the task hierarchy. The task hierarchy for road users is another important concept 
to understand the road users’ behavior. The task hiearchy consists of three levels, as illustrated on Figure 3 
below. 
 
Figure 3 - Task hiearchy (Michon, 1985). 
In connection with any trip, the road user makes decision on all three levels. The strategic level includes 
decisions about route choice, time for the trip, etc. Decisions on the manoeuvring level are made during the 
trip, for example how to turn, avoid obstacles, deciding the speed, etc. The last level is the control level, 
which are the short-term decisions, like acceleration, deceleration, changing gears, etc. The manoeuvring 
level is the level where the tasks related to interaction between driver and infrastructure or driver and other 
road users take place (van der Horst, 2013). Therefore, the way the drivers perform the driver tasks on the 
manoeuvring level is relevant to take into account in traffic planning. At this level the driver is reacting to 
infrastructure, signals and signs, and if there are too many pieces of information from these sources 
simultaneously, it will generate an increase in the demand on the driver and thus an increase in mental effort, 
which can result in a decrease in performance efficiency (da Silva, 2014). 
Based on previous studies of the safey of two-way bicycle paths it can be concluded that drivers overlooking 
cyclists is a key safety challenge. With regard to scanning behavior previous results show that two-way bicycle 
paths may increase the risk of cyclist accidents because the driver fail to scan the bicycle path. It was also 
found in the literature that there are different infrastructure measures which can be used to deal with the 
lack of scanning. The scanning behavior and infrastructure seem to be the two most important aspects, which 
are connected with the conflicts that happen between cyclists on two-way bicycle paths and vehicles, and 
that is the scanning behavior of the drivers, and the infrastructure.  
Aspect 1: Scanning behavior 
The previous research ( (Summala, et al., 1996); (van Haeften, 2010)) has showed that drivers do not always 
scan sufficiently before entering the intersection. This can be because they tend to prioritize the cars, 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
because the cars propose a bigger danger for them, and as a result the drivers sometimes fail to scan the 
bicycle path. A Finnish study from 1998 specifically investigates the effect of warning triangle and painted 
bicycle paths on the behavior of the drivers (Räsänen & Summala, 1998). The conclusion from that study was 
that implementing speed reduction measures can improve the scanning behavior of the drivers from the 
minor road, meaning that a higher share of them will scan both directions. The results of these studies 
suggest that when drivers have more time when approaching the intersection they will more likely scan both 
directions of the bicycle path.  
Another thing that influences the scanning is the presence of crossing traffic. A study by Buch (2011) found 
that when there is no crossing traffic, 79-83 % of the right-turning vehicles from the minor look to the right, 
and when there is crossing traffic, 84-86 % look to the right. The factor ”crossing traffic” is not a measure in 
itself, but it is useful to know the proven effect of the presence of crossing traffic on the viewing behavior. 
This should be kept in mind for intersections, where there is low traffic intensity, becaused here in such 
intersections, based on the results from the study by Buch (2011) the driver would be less likely to make 
sufficient orientation. 
On the distributor roads in the Netherlands, 72 % of the length of bicycle paths are bidirectional (Zeegers & 
Kamminga, 2014). It is a high share, so one could assume that the drivers have it in their expectations that 
there can possibly come bicycles from both directions. However, this is not the case, as most car users only 
scan one direction. In the article by Schepers they discuss the possibility of changing all bicycle paths in the 
Netherlands to bidirectional, but there are several issues connected to this. Having two-way bicycle paths at 
intersections add more points of conflicts to the intersection, it increases the workload for the road users, 
and thus the bicycles coming from the unusual direction will have a higher risk of motor-vehicle crashes. 
Previous studies have shown that there is a special issue with scanning when it comes to right-turning 
vehicles. The study by Summala, et al. (1996) is referred to many times in the literature, because it confirmed 
the hypothesis that right-turning cars from the minor road fail to see the bicyces coming from the unexpected 
direction, because the cars focus their scanning on the cars on the major road coming from the left. 
This was confirmed by a study made in Groningen in 2009 (van Haeften, 2010) investigated the viewing 
behavior before and after placement of a warning sign. The Groningen study added a new dimension, in that 
the viewing behavior of the bicycles were also observed. It was in this study confirmed that drivers 
approaching an intersection with a crossing two-way bicycle path in front of them often fail to scan to the 
right – see Table 1 below.

Many previous studies of the drivers’ scanning behavior have been based on data collection from video 
observations. In order to investigate the drivers’ scanning behavior based on video observations from outside 
of the cars, it is necessary to make an assumption that the head movement is an indicator for the direction 
in which the driver looks. This assumption has been used in several studies of this kind ( (Summala, et al., 
1996); (Räsänen & Summala, 1998); (van Haeften, 2010)). This is due to the fact that it is nearly impossible 
==PAGE:10==

to observe the drivers’ actual viewing behavior in terms of the eye movement from outside of the window. 
Therefore the only indicator for scanning, that is possible to detect from outside is the head movement. From 
video data it is possible to capture several dimensions of the head movement; How many degrees the head 
moves, and also to register the head movement for several distances up until the intersection.  
Aspect 2: Infrastructure 
Räsänen & Summala (1998) recommend speed reduction measure as a result of their study, along with 
warning signs to draw attention and decrease the speed of the drivers. The important point is to give more 
time to the driver to detect the cyclists. Therefore another solution, albeit more expensive, could be elevated 
bicycle crossings or speed bump (Räsänen & Summala, 1998). However, the safest and also most expensive 
solution would be to let the bicycle path cross the roadway in a split level crossing as a bridge or tunnel, as 
recommended by (Buch & Jensen, 2013). 
The literature has shown that there are a number of factors that have a proven effect on the scanning 
behavior. There are two ways of targeting this problem, seen from a road authority point of view. One is 
attention-enhancing measures and the other is speed-reduction measures.  
 
Warning signs: In the study by Räsänen & Summala (1998), they found that before the placement of 
warning signs, 3-11 % of the right-turning drivers looked to the right, and after placement of warning 
signs 13-24 % looked to the right. This finding was confirmed by van Haeften (2010). In this study the 
sites with warning signs had 77 % of the right-turning drivers looking to the right, compared to sites 
without warning sites where 74 % looked to the right. For sites with a flashing warning sign 80 % of 
the right turning vehicles looked to the right.   
 
Red bicycle crossing: The same study found that implementing a red bicycle crossing in addition to 
warning signs had the effect that 15-33 % of the drivers looked to the right.  
 
Speed-reduction measures: Both of the above studies found that if the vehicles approaches the 
intersection at a low speed he will be more inclined to expand his visual scanning to both directions 
of the bicycle path. Thus, any speed-reduction measures can be used to improve the safety in this 
type of crossings. 
Thus, there is a lot of existing knowledge about which infrastructure elements can be implemented in order 
to ensure lower speed and better scanning, which in the end are those things that can decrease the risk of 
accidents. Recently, a Dutch study about crossings with two-way bicycle paths was done, where the 
complexity of the problem and possible measures were presented (Kuiken & Schepers, 2017). Kuiken & 
Schepers (2017) describe the main issues and solutions, based on existing literature. They define the problem 
of the drivers not scanning sufficiently as a combination of complexity leading to higher workload, 
unexpectancy and the tendency to pay most attention to the biggest dangers. The problem with the cyclists 
from right not being expected is that even though the drivers scan to the right, they sometimes do not 
register the presence of the cyclist – this is called the “looked-but-failed-to-see” error. One of the solutions 
they propose is to have a distance of preferably 5 meters, or minimum 2 meters, between the two-way 
bicycle path and the roadway, in order for the driver to perform the scanning in different steps and thus 
decrease his workload.  
Furthermore the study suggested a systematic method for identifying these problematic locations and 
improving them. It is designed as an approach which can be used by road authorities to improve the safety 
of the crossings. The first part is a quickscan – see Figure 4 below – and after the quickscan, a costumized 
analysis can be done, which is shown on Figure 5.

Summing up on the literature presented in this chapter, there appears to be especially two aspects which 
are important when it comes to safety and behavior at crossings with two way bicycle paths. The first is 
scanning behavior. Two important findings in the literature are the fact that accidents between right-turning 
vehicles and bicyclists from the right are relatively frequent, and that specifically right-turning vehicles have 
a higher likelihood of failing to scan correctly compared to drivers turning left or going straight forward. The 
scanning behavior has been proven to be different for the right-turning vehicles, in the sense that they are 
more likely to scan insufficiently to the right or not look right at all. The second aspect is the infrastructure. 
It is important that the infrastructure takes the complexity of the extra cyclist direction into account. This can 
be done by ensuring good overview conditions, drawing attention to the cyclists or using speed-reduction 
measures.  
1.2.2 – The design element investigated 
As the above chapter has shown, there are several infrastructural measures that can be implemented in 
inersections with two-way bicycle paths along the major road in order to decrease the risk of accidents 
between the bicycles riding on these bicycle paths and the vehicles entering the intersection from the minor 
road. However, there is no existing research exploring the effect of the parallel distance between the two-
way bicycle path and the roadway on the scanning behavior of the vehicles from the minor road. Therefore 
this it chosen as the topic of the investigations for this project.   
Below on Figure 6 is shown three different types of markings in this situation, where a minor road connects 
to a major road along which there is a two-way bicycle path. 
 
Figure 6 - Different types of marking and right-of-way when a two-way bicycle path is crossing a road (The Danish Road Directorate, 
2014). 
In the first situation the vehicles have one set of shark teeth, which are behind the crossing bicycle path. In 
this case they have to stop and orientate themselves for crossing bicycle traffic and car traffic before entering 
the intersetion. In the second situation the vehicles have the right of way, and the cyclists have to stop. This 
solution would in principle eliminate the problem of conflicts that happen because the drivers cross the 
bicycle path without making sufficient orientation, however it is not used in connection with intersections, 
as it decreases the level of service for the cyclists. It is used in cases where the bicycle path is not running 
along a road. In the third situation, assuming the distance between the bicycle path and roadway is wide 
enough that a vehicle can stop there, there is a second set of shark teeh rigth before the roadway, indicating 
that the vehicles should stop and make the orientation in two steps – first before the bicycle and then before 
the roadway. 
The solution where the cyclists have to stop can be the safest choice. Buch & Jensen (2013) showed that the 
accident frequency can be up to 20 times higher in crossings where the cyclist has the right of way, compared 
to when the vehicles have the right of way. However, it is most common that cyclists have the right of way.

The purpose of the observational part of this study is thus to investigate the difference between the first and 
the third type of infrastructure from Figure 6, in terms of the scanning behavior of the vehicles from the 
minor road. The full set of hypotheses will be presented in section 1.5.  
 
1.3 – The usage of two-way bicycle paths in the Netherlands 
72 % of the length of bicycle paths on the distributor roads in the Netherlands are bidirectional (Zeegers & 
Kamminga, 2014). The share of two-way bicycle paths varies between municipalities, between 10 and 90 %. 
Some municipalities have almost 100 % (Methorst, et al., 2016). This is due to the fact that these are new 
cities that were developed after the Second Wold War, and two-way bicycle paths were included in the city 
planning from the beginning, including the spatial seperation from the car traffic.   
How the municipalities use the guidelines is different from municipality to municipality. Smaller 
municipalities tend to use them more, whereas bigger municipalities develop their own design policies 
(Schepers, 2015). 
In 2015 a questionaire was made among six municipalities of various sizes and with various share of two-way 
bicycle paths (Amsterdam, Breda, Eindhoven, The Hague, Hattem and Katwijk) to determine what the factors 
they include when deciding to implement the path (Schepers, 2015). The municipalities stated two 
motivations behind the implementation of two-way bicycle paths. The first is that they have observed that 
there are many ”ghost riders” on the one-way bicycle paths, so it is better to implement two-way paths to 
improve the safety for them, because does not seem likely that the ghost-riders will adjust their route choice. 
The other motivation is to provide more direct routes for the bicyclists as a service. The choice of 
implementing a two-way bicycle path can be in a large-scale environment, often at wide roads that are 
difficult to cross. 
As a part of this project an interview was made with an employee from the traffic department of Utrecht 
Municipality, who explained specifically about the reasons that they use the two-way bicycle paths in Utrecht. 
Their reasons partly overlap with the ones that were found by Rijkswaterstaat in the questionaire with five 
other municipalities, as described above. Utrecht has the following three arguments: 
1. Make it safe for the ghost riders who are there anyway. The first argument is the argument of the 
ghost riders, as described above. In Utrecht normally the one-way paths are 2 meters wide, and the 
two-way paths are 3,5-4 meters. If cyclists use the one-way paths as two-way paths, it is not safe 
because of the width. Thus, using this argument, changing the path to a two-way path including 
increasing the width makes it safer. 
2. Increase service (regarding route). The second argument has to do with the bicycles crossing to the 
other side of the road in the case of one-directional paths. The crossing of the main road is seen as a 
low service for the cyclists, as also is the general opinion among all the municipalities interviewed by 
Rijkswaterstaat. 
3. Decrease total risk of the trip. In addition to the improved service by adjusting the instrastructure 
to the desire lines of the cyclists, Utrecht Municipality further mentions the increased relative risk 
for the cyclists when they have to cross the road twice, if they do not have the two-way bicycle path. 
Thus the idea of keeping the cyclists on one side of the road is both motivated by providing a high 
service level of the network, but also by keeping the cyclists ”safer” by keeping them on one side. 
This naturally leads to a consideration about whether the improved safety of keeping them on one 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
side can justify the increased accident risk they face when they are driving in the unexpected 
direction. On Figure 7 below is illustrated the two routes from A to B.  
 
Figure 7 - Illustration of a cyclist's route from A to B.

1.4 – The usage of two-way bicycle paths in Denmark 
In Denmark the two-way bicycle paths are very rarely used, but it was not possible to find a specific statement 
about how big a share of the bicycle paths in Denmark are bidirectional. There are a few situations, where 
the two-way paths are implemented, which can either be as a seperated path, or at locations where a 
counting of the traffic in an intersection has mapped the most cycled routes of the cyclists, and in order to 
increase the service and to make the crossing/route safe, the two-way paths are used.  
The reason behind the lack of usage is that there is research that points to the fact that when the two-way 
bicycle path crosses another road there is a higher risk than if it were a one-way path, as described in section 
1.2.1. This is due to the fact that the drivers, both on the primary and seconary road do not see the cyclists 
as well as when the cyclists come from the unexpected direction.   
For this project a representative from the traffic department of the Municipality of Copenhagen was 
interviewed. He explained that in Copenhagen they do not want to use two-way bicycle paths, because they 
deem the safety disadvantages too high, compared to the advantages. The risks are connected with the 
drivers’ habits and the complexity of a crossing, which are two important factors that lead to the road users 
overlooking eachother, especially when a the cyclists come from an unexpected direction.  
The traffic safety department in the Municipality of Copenhagen work closely together with the police, 
because it is the police who grant the permission to place new elements in the infrastructure, which might 
be dangerous. If the two-way bicycle path runs through a signilized intersection then there is no problem, 
because then it is allowed by the police, if and only if the crossing is made in a safe way. This means that in a 
signalized intersection the two-way bicycle path must have its own signal phase in order to make the crossing 
safe.  
On one location north of the city center the Municipality is currently considering to implement a two-way 
bicycle path. They are considering the same aspects as in Utrecht Municipality – that the advantage of a two-
way bicycle path is that there is an improved level of service as well as an improvement in safety, if the path 
is placed in an area where it will help the cyclists to avoid crossing a big road.  For the traffic safety 
department in the Municipality of Copenhagen it is important to assess the risks and advantages of each case 
where elements such as two-way bicycle paths are being discussed or considered. The most relevant aspect 
before implementation of a two-way bicycle path is considered is whether it makes sence, in relation to the 
cyclists’ route. That is, if all locations of interest are situated on one side of a road, it can be considered to 
place a two-way bicycle path. 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
Thus, the considerations in Copenhagen are more or less the same as in Utrecht. However, in Copenhagen 
they mentioned one additional consideration, which could be a disadvantage of using two-way bicycle paths, 
which is the difference in the cyclists’ behavior on a two-way path compared to a one-way path. Some times, 
two-way bicycle paths are placed on stretches where the cyclists are already driving in the opposite direction 
on a bicycle path due to their route. The assumtion is that the the cyclists’ behavior is much more careful 
when they drive in the wrong direction on a one-way path, because they know that not complying with the 
traffic rules and they know that the other road users do not expect them. When they break the rules, they 
show caution, and when they ride on a two-way bicycle path they no longer break the rules and thus they 
will drive with a less cautious behavior, while still coming from an – to a certain degree – unexpected 
direction, which can lead to conflicts. 
However, even though the road authorities in Denmark are reluctant to implementing two-way bicycle paths, 
a questionaire among Danish cyclists has shown that in some cases they are more satisfied with a two-way 
bicycle path compared to a one-way path. This was investigated in a study by Trafitec, where videos were

2013). The experienced satisfaction can be translated into a service level, so the infrastructure elements can

1.4.1 – Design guidelines 
The official guidelines in Denmark for implementation of two-way bicycle paths along roads only 
recommends to use the two-way paths under specific circumstances where it can be the safest solution. This 
is for example if the two-way bicycle path leads the cyclist to a safe crossing of the main road. Crossings over 
or under the main road are considered to be safe enough to justify the usage of two-way bicycle paths to 
lead the cyclists to that crossing. Otherwise, the safest solution is generally to have bicycle paths on both 
sides of the road (Melchior, 1984). The guidelines seem to assume that the choice of using the two-way 
bicycle path is a choice between either having a one-way bicycle path on each side of the road or a two-way 
path on one side of the road. This might be the case in areas like city centers where the space is scarce. The 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
circular also defines that in urban areas there should be a shoulder of 1 meter or more between the two-way 
bicycle path and the roadway.  
 
1.5 – Summary and research questions 
The purpose of this project is to investigate the safety of two-way bicycle paths in intersections in the 
Netherlands. Accident data for intersections are analysed in order to determine the distribution of the 
different accident situations in intersections involving cyclists on two-way bicycle paths. Through an 
observational study behavior of drivers approaching an intersection is investigated, in order to determine 
whether it is safer when the intersection has a parallel distance between the two-way bicycle path and the 
road way on the main road. The knowledge from the study of the safety of two-way bicycle paths in the 
Netherlands is put into Danish context and it is assessed whether or not two-way bicycle paths could be 
implemented more in Denmark, and if some of the infrastructure solutions from the Netherlands could be 
used in Denmark to increase the safety on these bicycle paths.  
The purpose of the literature review is to gain knowledge about the usage of and problems with two-way 
bicycle paths in the two countries. This is done to get an overview of the existing knowledge and to be able 
to define the research gap. 
The purpose of the accident analysis is to investigate how many accidents happen in general, with cyclists 
and in which situations. This is done both for the Netherlands and Denmark in order to compare the numbers 
for the two countries. Specifically it is interesting to know which kind of accidents happen in intersections 
with two-way bicycle paths present.  
The purpose of the observational study is to investigate the possible correlation between the presence of a 
parallel distance between the two-way bicycle path and the roadway, and the scanning behavior of the 
driversAs an experimental part an observational study is done, which seeks to answer the four hypotheses 
as presented below. The observations are designed based on hypothesis 1. This is the primary hypothesis 
and the observations are aimed at collecting data which can answer this. However during the observations a 
number of other parameteres are also collected, which can also provide interesting information about the 
scanning behavior of the drivers. Thus, the two secondary hypotheses, hypothesis 2 and hypothesis 3, are 
focused on these other parameters. For all of the hypotheses there is a subhypothesis for right-turning 
vehicles. It was found in the literature review, as explained in section 1.2, that it is especially for right-turning 
vehicles that there is a problem with lack orientation to the right.  
Hypothesis 1: In a priority intersection with a two-way bicycle path, if there is a parallel distance of around 4 
meters between the bicycle path and the road way, a significantly higher share of the drivers from the minor 
road will look to the right compared to the intersections where the distance is not present. 
Hypothesis 2: In case that there is either crossing traffic when the vehicle from the minor road approaches 
the intersection, or in case he follows behind another car, he will be more likely stop before the intersection. 
”More likely” is defined as a significantly higher the number of drivers scanning both directions for the case 
with crossing traffic compared to the case without. 
Hypothesis 3: If the vehicle from the minor road stops before the intersection, he will be more likely to look 
to the right.

2.3.1 – Accident analysis 
For analyzing the accidents, Microsoft Excel is used to apply different filters and sorting, in order to get an 
overview of the data and to investigate the most frequent accident situations etc. Besides analyzing the 
numerical data, the descriptions of the accidents by the police were also used.  
For part of the data analysis a chi-square test is used to compare data between two groups. A chi-square test 
can determine whether or not there is a significant difference between the observed distribution of a variable 
and the expected distribution. 
 The chi-square formula is shown here:

When the chi-square value is found, a table with the chi-square distribution can be used to find the p-value,

the two groups tested are significantly different from each other.

The odds-ratio exp(y) is the relation between two variables. If the odds-ratio is positive then there is a positive

In order to use a logistic regression on collected data, the data has to conform to a set of requirements 
(StatisticsSolutions, u.d.): 
1. The dependent variable must be on a dichotomous scale (two mutually exclusive categories, for 
example binary) 
2. There must be one or more independent variables 
3. There must be independence of observations and the dependent variable should have mutually 
exclusive and exhausive categories 
4. There must be a linear relationship between any continuous independent variable and the logit 
transformation of the odds-ratio of the dependent variable.  
Due to the third requirement the head movement parameter is arranged in the categories ”look right” and 
”does not look right” for the regression. Thus, out of the 836 observations ther are 67 with ”0” and 769 with 
”1” in this parameter. It is important to keep the fourth requirement in mind, because it if this requirement 
is violated it can have imply a bias on the coefficient of the variable in the outcome of the model.  
The hypotheses 1 and 3 are tested together, because they deal with the same dependent variable ”look 
right”. Hypothesis 2 is split into hypothesis 2a with the dependent variable ”stop” and hypothesis 2b with 
the dependent variable ”stop at distance”, but they have the same explanatory variables. 
For all hypotheses the method ”enter” is used, which means that all variables are entered into the test 
simultaneously. In all cases not all of the variables were significant, and thus the final set of variables for the 
regression is different than the initial set. All hypotheses are tested both for all vehicles and specifically for

interpret. 
 
A way to check the usefulness is to look at how big a percentage of the outcome is predicted by the

 
The test of model coefficients tests whether or not the model has been improved by including the 
selected set of explanatory variables, compared to a null model, which has no explanatory variables.

3 – Results: Accident analysis 
All data is accident based, and thus the units used in this chapter is ’accidents’. Therefore if there is X amount 
of fatal accidents, these accidents might have more than X fatalities, but this is not included in the analysis. 
Based on the accident data for both countries, the following research questions are investigated: 
1. How many accidents happen on two-way bicycle paths?  
a. Share of total number of bicycle accidents. 
b. Among these accidents, which were the accident situations?  
2. How many bicycle accidents happen in intersections? 
a. Signalized and non-signalized. 
b. Share of total number of bicycle accidents. 
3. Which are the most frequent accident situations in bicycle accidents? 
This information is collected as much as possible for the both countries in order to compare the data between 
the two countries.  
 
3.1 – The Netherlands 
During the five-year period there were 505.667 accidents in the Netherlands. Among these accidents, 29.475 
were with light personal injury, 19.562 were with serious personal injury, and 2.468 were fatal accidents. 
There were 18.225 accidents with one or more bicycle involved, accounting for 4 % of the total number of 
accidents. Among the bicycle accidents 5.873 of them were with light personal injury, 4.938 were with serious 
personal injury, and 701 were fatal accidents. The complete tables behind these numbers can be found in 
Table 1-3 in Appendix 1. 
For all vehicle users, during the five-year period, 65.641 accidents happened in intersections, accounting for 
13 % of the total number of accidents (505.667 accidents). Among the accidents in intersections 11.575 
accidents were with light personal injury, 8.426 were with serious personal injury, and 786 were fatal 
accidents. Out of the 65.641 intersection accidents approximately 10 % of them (6.597) were with cyclists 
involved. Among the 6.597 cyclist accidents in intersections 2.048 of them were with light personal injury, 
1.833 were with serious personal injury, and 222 were fatal accidents. The complete tables behind these 
numbers can be found in Table 4-6 in Appendix 1.

to the descriptions of the accidents the cyclist did not play an important role. The data set after reduction 
contains 11.055 accidents.

3.4 – Subconclusion on accident analysis 
Unfortunately it was not possible to obtain accident data on the same level of detail for both countries, in 
relation with the factors that were of interest for this analysis, however some interesting information was 
found for Denmark isolated, and for the comparison of both countries. 
The data from Denmark showed that during the five year period there were 666 accidents on two-way bicycle 
paths, where 318 of them happened in intersections. Among these 318 accidents the most frequent accident 
situations were where the vehicle is either turning right or driving straight forward and has an accident with 
a cyclist coming from his right side – the unexpected direction of the bicycle path. This corresponds to the 
findings from other research, as described in the introduction chapter. Another interesting finding is that in 
some cases there are possible reasons behind the accidents stated in the police reports, where it was stated 
in 16 out of 116 cases that the opponent did not see the cyclist cycling on the two-way bicycle path on the 
unexpected direction, an in another six cases the opponent made orientation but not sufficiently. This can 
point to a possible safety issue with the two-way bicycle paths being that the opponent do not expect cyclists 
coming from the right and thus do not look in that direction.  
The comparison of the Netherlands and Denmark on the general level showed that first and foremost the 
Netherlands has a lot more accidents than Denmark, both in general and for bicycle accidents. This is also to 
be expected, because even though the two countries are similar in size, the Netherlands has a higher 
population and also more biking per citizen than in Denmark. The bike share in the Netherlands is 36 %, and 
in Denmark it is 23 % (European Cyclists' Federation, 2017).  
It seems that in Denmark the share of bicycle accidents is higher than in the Netherlands. Among the bicycle 
accidents the distribution of accidents between the different levels of severity was very different between 
the two countries. For the Netherlands there was a higher share of bicycle accidents with serious injury and 
fatalities, than for Denmark. And for Denmark 12 % of the bicycle accidents were with material damage only, 
whereas this share was only 1 % for the Netherlands. The reason for this could either be that bicycle accidents 
in the Netherlands are more serious than in Denmark, but it is more likely because the level of registration is 
low in The Netherlands, specifically for accidents with material damage only. It was also found that Denmark 
has a higher share of intersection accidents than the Netherlands.

The test of hypothesis 1 looked for a correlation between the explanatory variable presence of distance and 
the dependent variable look right. The presence of distance was found to be significant for all vehicles in 
general, but not when looking at only the right-turning vehicles.  
The test of hypothesis 2 looked for a correlation between the explanatory variables crossing traffic and follow 
and the dependent variables stop and stop at distance. For stop it was found that for all vehicles crossing 
cyclists from either direction, crossing cars from left and follow are significant. So the only crossing traffic that 
was not significant was the crossing cars from right variable, which makes sense because a part of the 
observed vehicles are right-turning, and probably many of them do not stop when there is crossing car traffic 
from the right. This was tested also for only right-turning vehicles, where crossing cars from right was still 
insignificant, as expected, and furthermore crossing cyclists from right was insignificant. The rest of the 
variables were significant. It is interesting that it seems that for the right-turning vehicles only the presence 
of crossing cyclists from the left has a significant relation with the decision to stop, but the presence of 
crossing cyclists from the right is not significant. For the stop at distance variable only crossing cars from left 
was found to be significant, both for all vehicles and for right-turning vehicles. 
The test of hypothesis 3 looked for a correlation between the explanatory variables stop and stop at distance 
and the dependent variable look right. It was found that for all vehicles stop behind the stop line is significant

5– Discussion 
During this project the topic of two-way bicycle paths has been investigated from different angles, where the 
advantages and risks were described through the literature review, accident data was presented and 
analysed, and lastly an observational study was done. The statistical analysis of the data collected in the 
observations confirmed the main hypothesis – that there is a significant correlation between the presence of 
a distance and the scanning behavior of the drivers, however it was confirmed only for all vehicles and not 
specifically for the right-turning vehicles. In this chapter the methodology of the different parts of the study 
is first discussed in section 5.1, whereafter the results from the observations are discussed and related to 
expectations and findings from previous studies in section 5.2. 
 
5.1 – Discussion of methodology 
5.5.1 - Accident analysis 
Signalized and priority intersections: The intention was to gather information about bicycle accidents in 
priority intersections for both countries. This information was not directly available in the data for Denmark, 
however since the road names are in the data it would have been possible to get the information from Google 
Maps, but this was not done because of the time contraint. For this reason the data for accidents in 
intersections in Denmark were both priority and signalized intersections. If there were more time and the 
signal information could be collected then it would have been possible to look at only the accidents that 
happened in priority intersections and with two-way bicycle paths, which is the most interesting accidents.  
Liminations in data sources: For both of the countries there is the limitation that many accidents are not 
registered. Especially when it comes to accidents involving bicycles. Using accident data and making 
conclusions upon them is something that should always be done carefully, as there is a problem of 
underreporting of accidents. The reporting is more reliable for fatalities and serious injuries, and less reliable 
for light injuries (OECD, 2013). Furthermore, accidents with bicycles are underreported, and cyclists is the 
road user group that is least recorded ((Broughton, et al., 2008) in (OECD, 2013)). In Europe only around 50 
% of the hospital admissions from cycling accidents are registered by the police ((De Mol & Lammer, 2006) 
in (OECD, 2013)). For the underreporting of cyclist accidents in Denmark it has been estimated that for bicycle 
accidents 14 % of the accidents with serious injuries and 10 % of the accidents with light injuries get registered 
by the police (Janstrup, et al., 2016). It is especially the non-fatal accidents with personal injuries for 
vulnerable road users that are underrepresented in the police data – especially the ones with light personal 
injury but also the ones with serious personal injury (Færdselssikkerhedskommissionen, 2013). In the 
Netherlands it is estimated that around 30 % the cyclist accidents with seriously injured cyclists are registered 
by the police. For fatal accidents the rate is 86 % (Shinar, et al., 2017).  And for accidents with slightly injured 
cyclists only 4 % of the accidents are registered (Wegman, et al., 2010). In order to solve the problem of 
underreporting for accident data for the Netherlands, SWOV has created a database with so-called real 
number of accidents, which are estimates, based on data from three sources; hospital data, ambulance data 
and police data, where the latter is the commonly used data source. For this project the estimated real 
numbers were not used, because it was important that the data was as comparable as possible between two 
countries, thus it should come from similar sources.  
Comparability between data for the two countries: Besides the limitation in the form of underreporting, 
which is assumed to vary between the countries, there is also a variation in the definitions of severity. In 
order to compare accident data between to two countries it is important that the categorization is done is a 
similar manner. The two countries use almost the same categories; Material damage only, light personal 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
injury, serious personal injury or hospital and fatal. In Denmark the term ”serious personal injury” is used, 
whereas in the Netherlands ”hospital” is used. Furthermore there is an issue regarding accident situations. 
The system for registration of which accident situations (or combination of maneuvres) happened is different 
in the two countries. In Denmark there is a code for each possible combination of maneuvres of the involved 
parts. In the Netherlands however, the maneuvres are registered in a different way. They are registered as 
intended maneuvres for each element, and thus the combination of intended maneuvres is not registered.  
As described above there are different issues regarding the use of data, both regarding comparability and 
especially underreporting. The solution to handle this problem is to use data which is combined from 
different data sources, as they already do in the Netherlands, in order to get more realistic numbers. 
However, this is outside of the scope of this project, and is something that could be used for bigger projects 
or for further research on this topic. 
Time frame: The past five years is the most common time frame for collection of accident data and is used 
in this project. If the length of the period is increased there is a risk of including data that are no longer 
relevant because the infrastructure and other circumstances can have been changed since then. If the length 
is decreased to less than five years, the amount of data can be too scarse, and there is a risk of basing the 
conclusions upon variations in the traffic, for example if in one year there are special situations regarding 
weather, events, etc. compared to other years. In special cases. Depending on the topic investigated, other 
period lengths can be used, however for this investigation five years is suitable. 
5.5.2 – Observations: Data collection 
Selection of timeslots: The dates and timeslots for the observations were selected based on availability and 
the dates were selected close to each other in order to finish the observations before winter season. In the 
Bezuidenhoutseweg intersection the observations were performed in a morning, however it became 
apparent at the day that it would have been better to do the observations on an afternoon, because the area 
where the observed cars were coming from is an area with some big work places, so a lot of traffic is coming 
from there (Pr. Irenestraat) and entering the main road (Bezuidenhoutseweg) in the afternoon, and in the 
morning most of the traffic were entering Pr. Irenestraat. So in order to be able to gather more data, it would 
have been wiser to choose an afternoon timeslot for this intersection. This could have been avoided by 
making traffic countings in the selected intersections prior to choosing the timeslots, in order to choose the 
optimal timeslots with a lot of traffic. The selection of timeslots could have been improved by investigating 
the traffic situation beforehand, either with traffic countings or collecting more information about the area 
regarding workplaces and institutions which could generate traffic. 
Camera: The data collection was done with camera. Another option would have been manual observations, 
but there are many advantages of using camera, as with camera it is possible to catch more parameters, film 
from different angles at the same time and being able to go back and forth in the video material, making the 
registrations more certain. The disadvantage is that with video it is more time consuming. For the project 
several pilot observations were made in order to ensure a good observation method and reliable recordings. 
Even though the camera has limitations, it was assessed that it was the right solution. Two cameras were 
used in order to gather the sufficient information, however it could also have been possible to incorporate 
more cameras or manual observations simultaneously in order to either capture the head movement of a 
higher share of the vehicles or to collect more parameters, which could for example be related to the 
behavior of the cyclists.  
Visibility of camera: Since the cameras was placed on a van, the setup was very visible, but the cameras 
themselves are not very visible and it is not clear where exactly they are pointed. However, it is a visible form 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
of observation, and it must be taken into account when looking at the results. It can be assumed that when 
the drivers can see the cameras, and thus know that they are being observed, they will adjust their behavior. 
It is expected for this project that the adjustment of behavior is not related to head movement, but more 
related to speed and whether they stop or not. Thus, if less visible cameras had been used it could possibly 
have the influence that less vehicles stop or that they generally have higher speed.  
Selection of sites: The intention was to collect as many sites as possible, that conformed to the requirements 
(no tram, no signals, no zebra stripes, no marked bicycle path, etc.) and then select the four sites from this 
group based in similarity. The sites should be similar to eachother in infrastructure design and traffic 
intensity, in order to isolate the effect of the distance and remove any possible bias. However, due to the 
time constraint it was only possible to find a handful of sites within the decided requirements. There were 
not enough sites to enable a final selection based on traffic intensity, and thus the result was that there was 
a big variation in traffic intensity among the selected sites. The sites also differ in size and geometry, which 
could also have an influence on the results. This problem could have been solved by doing more throurough 
research of possible sites, so they could be matched better according to infrastructure, geometry and also 
the traffic intensity.  
Head movement parameter: As described in the introduction, the head movement of the drivers has 
previously been used in research to investigate the scanning behavior. In other studies it has been registered 
on a detailed level, in the sense that the head movement direction was split into many steps between 90 
degrees to the left and 90 degrees to the right. In this study the head movements have been registered as a 
discrete variable which takes on the values (left, straight, right) or more of them. Another possibility would 
have been to estimate the quality or thouroughness of the scanning, by registering more specifically how 
many degrees the head turned, for how long and at which distance to the intersection, because it can be 
assumed that these factors can have an influence on the likelihood of the driver detecting the bicycles. 
Another way of improving the registration of this parameter would be to detect the eye movements, which 
is possible with an eye tracker. With the use of an eye tracker it would be possible to register much more 
specific where the drivers are looking. However, since such a tracker has to be implemented in the cars, it 
would have to be a completely different investigation setup.  
Challenges that could not be controlled: There were a few cases of vehicles that could not be registered 
because they were either out of frame, or when the view was blocked by a truck in front of the camera. 
Besides issues related to the traffic itself, there were also challenges from the weather. Unfortunately at the 
observation of the Voorhofdreef – Jan Campertlaan intersection it was raining a lot, which decreased the 
image quality of the camera. In this case the wind was also an issue, because the camera moved becasue of 
the wind. The sunlight was also a problem, for all intersections, especially in combination with certain types 
of windshields, which reflected the sun. The problems with the windshields and the reflections can not be 
solved, but if there were more time it could have been possible to reschedule the observation that took place 
on a rainy day.

Removal of insignificant variables: For the statistical analysis a binomial logistic regression was used. In order 
to formulate the models which could explain the different dependent variables, first an initial model was 
formulated, whereafter all the insignificant explanatory variables were removed and then only the significant 
variables remained as part of the final models. In some of the cases there were only one insignificant variable, 
but in some of the cases there were several of them, and they were all removed from the models 
simultaneously. However, another method would have been to remove them stepwise in the way that only 
the most insignificant variable is removed first. In this way would be possible to formulate several models. 
Using this method could potentially have had an influence on the results, because when using the stepwise 
removal approach there can a change in the p-values of the variables after one is removed form the model. 
As a consequence, there is a chance that too many variables are removed from the models, and variables 
that were actually significant were removed because they appeared to be insignificant in the initial model.

between the explanatory variables and the dependent variables. One of the measures that can explain the 
effectiveness of the models themselves is the Nakelkerke pseudo-R-square value which says how much of 
the variation in the outcome can be explained by the model. The pseudo-R-square values for the models 
were between 14,1-32,3 %. This can seem like a relatively low value for an R-square, which. But that does 
not mean that the models bad. It has rather something to do with the fact that we are modelling human 
behavior, and in this type of models it is not expected to get an R-square value which is higher than 50 % 
(Frost, 2013).

5.2 – Discussion of results 
5.2.1 – Hypothesis 1 
In a priority intersection with a two-way bicycle path, if there is a parallel distance of around 4 meters between 
the bicycle path and the road way, a significantly higher share of the drivers from the minor road will look to 
the right compared to the intersections where the distance is not present. 
It was found that for all vehicles that the distance is significant at a 95 % level of confidence. This means that 
in the cases where there was a distance between the bicycle path and the roadway the scanning behavior of 
the drivers was found to be significantly different compared to the cases where there was no distance. 
However, when looking only at right-turning vehicles, it is not significant. The expectation was that the 
distance would be significant for right-turning vehicles as well, maybe even more significant than for all 
vehicles, because it is known from the literature (as presented in section 1.2.1) that especially right-turning 
vehicles have a tendency to not look to the right. In the study by van Haeften (2010) it was found that among 
the right-turning vehicles there was a smaller share that looked both to the right and left (71,9 %) compared 
to the left-turning vehicles where 83 % looked both to the right and left (van Haeften, 2010). The same 
tendency was found in the data for this study (see section 4.2.1), where it was found that the share of right-
turning drivers who look to the right is significantly smaller than the share of left-turning drivers who look to 
the right. Because of the fact the right-turning vehicles look less often to the right, it was expected than by 
adding an infrastructure element which help to split their workload into more parts, it would especially have 
an effect on the right-turning vehicles. But this sub-hypothesis could not be confirmed based on the data. 
However, the results are important, because they showed that based on the data collected in this study it is 
proven that the head movement of the drivers will be influenced in general if there is a distance, even though 
it was not found any specific effect for right-turning vehicles. 
Even after it is shown statistically that the presence of a distance is related to a change in the drivers’ scannig 
behavior, it is important also to look at this result from a critical point of view. The main hypothesis regarding 
the possible effect of the presence of a distance is based on the theories about workload. The hypothesis 
implies some assumptions about how the road users react to infrastructure, and based on these assumptions 
it would be probable that if the workload is split up into more steps, or at least if it is clearly shown to the 
driver when he is approaching the intersection that he can choose to divide his workload into several steps, 
then he will perform the tasks in steps and therefore have more time to complete the tasks in each step more 
throroughly. Since the hypothesis was confirmed it can indicate that the theory of workload is applicable. 
However there are also arguments against this approach. It can be argued that it is not always beneficial to 
divide the workload temporally. It depends on how many seconds are given to the driver between the two 
tasks. If there is less than a few seconds between two tasks, the driver’s ability to execute the tasks will be 
worse than if they had to happen simultaneously (expert, 2017). This has to do with the process of 
information processing. Thus, from the information processing perspective the distance should be avoided, 
unless there is enough space to make a complete separation of the bicycle path from the intersection. And it 
can be argued that separating the bicycle path from the roadway with “only” a distance can actually decrease 
the performance of the drivers’ tasks compared to when he has to cross the bicycle path and roadway in one 
step (as he has to when there is no distance).Based on the data collected in this study it is not possible to 
conclude on these issues. Therefore, future studies looking further into the influence of a “distance” is 
relevant. For instance, it could be relevant to make a study including several intersections with distances of 
different widths, in order to collect more detailed knowledge on the influence of a distance on the driver 
behavior and workload when the workload is split up into steps. Such additional studies could help clarify 
whether there exists an optimal temporal distance between the steps of the workload. By the steps of the 
==PAGE:58==
57 
 
workload is meant every time the driver receives an input or has to react to something, information 
processing and performing the action. 
5.2.2 – Hypothesis 2 
In case that there is either crossing traffic when the vehicle from the minor road approaches the intersection, 
or in case he follows behind another car, he will be more likely stop before the intersection.  
The hypothesis was tested both with stop before stopline and with stop at distance as dependent variables. 
Based on the assumption that drivers tend to pay more attention to crossing car traffic than crossing bicycle 
traffic, as defined by Kuiken & Schepers (2017) as one of the issues with two-way bicycle paths, it is expected 
that the crossing cars from left and crossing cars from right variables are significant at a 95 % level of 
confidence, except in the case of right-turning vehicles where crossing cars from right is not expected to be 
significant at a 95 % level of confidence. This is due to the fact that crossing cars from the right does not have 
an influence on the right-turning vehicles. In all cases the crossing cars from left was significant at a 95 % 
level of confidence, which means that the drivers from the minor road were more likely to look to the right, 
in the cases where there was crossing cars from the left when they approached the intersection, compared 
to when there was no crossing cars from the left. This is in accordance with previous findings from Buch 
(2011), as described in section 1.2.1, where the presence of crossing traffic was shown to have a significant 
effect on the tendency to look to the right. In the case of looking only at the right-turnig vehicles, the crossing 
cars from right was insignificant, as expected. However for all vehicles is was also insignificant, which was 
not expected, but this can be explained with the fact that among the group of “all vehicles” there are 71 % 
right-turning vehicles.  
5.2.3 – Hypothesis 3 
If the vehicle from the minor road stops before the intersection, he will be more likely to look to the right. 
The hypothesis was tested for the stop before the bicycle path and for stopping at the distance. For stopping 
before the bicycle path the hypothesis was confirmed for both all vehicles and for right-turning vehicles. 
Stopping at the distance was not significant in any of the cases. The stop variable was expected to have a 
positive coefficient, meaning that if the vehicle stops, the driver will be more likely to look to the right – this 
was confirmed by the results.

6 – Conclusions 
The study was motivated by the fact that two-way bicycle paths are much more used in the Netherlands than 
in Denmark, which is an interesting difference, taking into account that the Netherlands and Denmark are 
both countries with a high mode share of cyclists. Thus the purpose of the study was to investigate the safety 
of two-way bicycle paths in intersections in the Netherlands, in regard to which type of accidents are most 
frequent in crossings with these paths, is there specific issues with behavior related to these crossings, and 
which types of solutions can be used to mitigate these problems and make the crossings safer. Through the 
literature review it was found that one of the safety arguments for using two-way bicycle paths is that with 
a two-way bicycle path along one side of a larger road, the cyclists can avoid crossing the road, which could 
be dangerous. In addition if there are a lot of locations, for example residential areas and workplaces, on one 
side of a big road, a more direct route between these can be provided to the cyclists with a two-way bicycle 
path. However, the literature review also revealed that there are some safety issues related specifically to 
this type of infrastructure. When a cyclist rides on a two-way bicycle path he has a higher risk of accident 
involvement if he comes from the unexpected direction. This can be connected to the findings related to 
scanning behavior of drivers in crossings with two-way bicycle paths. Previous research has found that some 
drivers do not look to the right, when approaching an intersection with a crossing two-way bicycle path. 
There is specifically a tendency that right-turning drivers do not look to the right. In order to mitigate this 
problem, previous studies have investigated possible measures and infrastructure elements that can have 
influence the drivers to look more often to both directions.   
Police registered accidents for a five year period were studied for both the Netherlands and Denmark. It was 
found that even though the Netherland has a higher number of registered bicycle accidents per year than 
Demark, when the amount of bicycled kilometers are taken into account the risk of being involved in an 
accident as cyclist is higher in Denmark compared to the Netherlands. From the accident data for Denmark 
alone it was found, in accordance with previous literature, that the most frequent accident situation among 
cyclist accidents on two-way paths is between a cyclist riding in the unexpected direction and a vehicle driving 
straight forward or turning to the right. 
Motivated by the findings from the literature review regarding the connection between the infrastructure 
and scanning behavior of the drivers an observational study in a set of Dutch priority intersections was done. 
The observational study was aimed at and designed to investigate the possible correlation between the 
presence of a parallel distance between the two-way bicycle path and the roadway, and the scanning 
behavior of the drivers. Through the analysis of the data collected in the observations it was found that this 
distance has a significant effect on the scanning behavior of the drivers. It was also found that when the 
vehicles stop behind the stopline, they are more inclined to scan sufficiently, possibly because they have 
more time.  
The findings from the three parts above indicate that if   the two-way cycle paths are to be implemented 
more in Denmark in the future, including the distance is a relevant way to try to ensure that the drivers look 
both directions, and thus to increase safety in relation to two-way cycle paths. Further, the findings indicate 
that in cases where there is not enough space for a distance  other speed-reducing measures can be used to 
increase safety, as  is was found that when the vehicles slow down or stop, the drivers are more likely to scan 
sufficiently. If two-way bicycle paths become more common in Denmark in the future, the Quickscan method 
as presented in section 1.2.1 could be useful if integrated in the road safety inspection approach, in order to 
ensure ensure a systematic inspection and to identify and resolve potential problems. Since Denmark already 
has a systematic road safety inspection approach to improving the safety of stretches of road, the Quickscan 
method could easily be integrated in that.
