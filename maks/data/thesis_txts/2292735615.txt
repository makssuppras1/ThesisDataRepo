ABSTRACT 
Pollutants in Combined Sewage Overflow (CSO) have gained increased attention in the resent 
years. This is partly due to the European Framework Directive that requires good ecologic status 
in the aquatic environment by the end of 2015. The pollutant loads discharged during CSOs can 
adversely affect the aquatic environment, primarily by promoting eutrophication. To increase 
the knowledge about the quality of CSO, the measuring project AMOK, was conducted at the 
internal overflow structure at Viby wastewater treatment plant (WWTP) in Viby, Aarhus. The 
ammonia and Total Suspended Solids (TSS) measurements have been used in this study, 
together with the flow measurements at the inlet to the WWTP. This data were used to find 
trends and patterns through an initial data analysis. Here, it was possible to identify the yearly 
infiltration rate, the diurnal variation of the dry weather flow, and the daily pollution patterns 
for ammonia and TSS. A model for the connected catchment to Viby WWTP was constructed in 
the software WEST, which allows for lumped conceptual modelling. The Viby catchment was 
conceptualized as two catchments, one for the combined sewage system, and one for the 
separate sewage system. Furthermore, a tank was included to simulate the deposition and 
resuspension of TSS. WEST was used with Matlab as an interface, to perform automatic 
calibration of the model to the AMOK data using various optimization methods. A sensitivity 
analysis of the model was performed, and the most sensitive parameters for flow were the 
pervious fraction and the reservoir constant, for ammonia it was the runoff concentrations and 
the pervious fraction and for TSS it was the runoff concentration and the reservoir constant. The 
sensitive parameters and the wastewater concentrations were used to calibrate the model. 
With the use of the diurnal variations identified in the initial data analysis, the conceptual model 
was able to simulate dry weather conditions satisfactorily. The model was then calibrated to 
wet weather conditions, with the use of 11 single rain events, and one long-term event. Under 
wet weather conditions, the model could simulate the ammonia concentrations solely based on 
the flux and dilution by the increased flow. To make the model simulate the high TSS peaks 
observed in the data, two features in WEST were tested: accumulation and washoff from the 
surface, and deposition and resuspension. Despite of testing different calibration targets, none 
of these features yielded satisfactory results. However, the model could still simulate the base 
concentration of TSS. It was therefore found in this study that a lumped conceptual model is 
able to simulate ammonia concentrations and base concentrations of TSS at an overflow 
structure.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Urban drainage systems have three main tasks; protect the public from disease and flooding, 
protect the groundwater from contamination, and protect the receiving water body from 
flooding and pollutants (Winther et al., 2011). In combined sewage systems Combined Sewage 
Overflow structures (CSO) relieve the hydraulic pressure on the sewage system, and protect the 
WasteWater Treatment Plant (WWTP) against overloads during high intensity rain events. This 
discharge of diluted untreated wastewater to the receiving water body can however harm the 
aquatic environment. It can stimulate algal blooms and thereby promote eutrophication, and 
adversely affect the aesthetics in the aquatic environment. Moreover, CSOs can have acute 
effects. The bacteria discharged during CSOs can cause hygienic problems for bathing water and 
ammonia can be acute toxic for fish (DANVA, 2006).  
It is difficult to quantify the effects of CSOs, since there is a general lack of knowledge in this 
area. Arnbjerg-Nielsen and Johansen (2002) states that the hydraulic loads can be estimated, 
but for the pollutant loads, there are a great uncertainty. The European Water Framework from 
2000 states that good ecologic status should be accomplished, latest by the end of 2015. Since 
CSO pollutant loads have an effect of this status, these are part of the effort in reaching the 
goal. Nonetheless, there is no national directive for Denmark that limits the load of pollutants 
discharged through CSOs. For restrictions to be made, more knowledge is needed (Arnbjerg-
Nielsen and Johansen, 2002).  
The quality of combined sewage water has a great impact when designing the WWTP and 
evaluating the stress caused by CSOs on receiving water bodies. This requires an integrated 
model consisting of both the sewage system, the WWTP and the receiving waters. Such a model 
may be used to provide an overall holistic evaluation of the wastewater system, by simulating 
both hydraulics and water quality for the three components, and thereby making it possible to 
simulate the stress on the environment (Dirckx et al., 2011).  
Due to the higher intensity rainfall experienced in the last decade, a lot of attention have been 
on the performance of the sewage systems. Not only has the limited capacity lead to floods, it 
has also resulted in more frequent CSOs (Paludan et al., 2011). Expanding the sewage pipes is a 
costly solution. To get the best out of the current pipes and basins, Real-Time Control (RTC) is an 
alternative solution. The control can be based on flow alone, or a combination of water quality 
and flow (Vanrolleghem et al., 2008). However, to include the water quality in RTC, a reliable 
water quality model must be available (Paludan et al., 2011). Furthermore, a reliable water 
quality will make it possible to determine the loads being discharged during CSOs.   
It has been suggested that the lack of reliable water quality models can be explained by the 
general lack of water quality data in sewage systems (Willems, 2010). Due to this, an advanced 
online measuring project has been conducted at the internal overflow structure at the Viby 
WWTP in Aarhus, Denmark. The project is named AMOK “Avanceret online Måling af 
OverløbsKvalitet”, which is a Danish abbreviation for Advanced online Measuring of Overflow 
Quality (Sharma et al., 2014).The sensors used to measure the water quality are located only at 
one CSO structure, but it is believed that the water quality here is representative for the whole 
catchment. 
The aim of this thesis is to setup a lumped conceptual model that is able to simulate flow and 
pollutant concentrations. As a case study, the AMOK project and the Viby catchment will be

used making it possible to calibrate the model to 535 days of continuous online measurements. 
The data from the project used in this study are the flow measurements, and the measured 
ammonia and Total Suspend Solids (TSS) concentrations. For the catchment area, there is

the simulations from the hydrodynamic model. 
The program WEST will be used as the model software. WEST uses conceptual modelling of flow

integrated model approach, which makes a holistic simulation of the effect of the CSO possible. 
In this study, the focus will solely be on the sewage system part.

2 THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
In this section, the theoretical background applied in the development of the model is 
presented. First, an overall explanation of the used software is given, secondly the overall topics 
of hydraulics, water quality, and transport processes in the sewer are described. Lastly, these 
three topics are related to the conceptualization used in WEST.  
2.1 WEST  
In this study the software WEST (Wastewater treatment plant Engine for Simulation and 
Training) are used. WEST is traditionally a software used for the simulation of a wastewater 
treatment plant. In a subpart of WEST, WESTforOPTIMIZATION, it is possible to run complex 
integrated models and perform long-term dynamic simulations. It is especially useful for 
simulation of water quality related systems, where physical, biological and chemical processes 
occur. The software is based on ordinary differential equations and differential and algebraic 
equations and thereby support numerically modelling  (DHI, 2015).  
The program allows for an open structure, since it is built on MSL (Model specific language). This 
has made it possible to extend WEST with the implementation of an extensive model library 
that describes the runoff and sewer system in the connected catchment. In WEST, it is therefore 
possible to make an integrated model on one software platform, where it applies a component 
converter to connect the submodels. This extension makes it possible to implement the dynamic 
interactions between the sewer and the WWTP (Solvi, 2007).  
In the simulations performed in WEST, the library IUWS (Integrated Urban Wastewater System) 
will be used in this study, here the following components are simulated: 
 
Flow 
 
Pollutants 
o Soluble and particular Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)  
o Phosphate 
o Ammonia 
o Total Suspended Solids (TSS) 
The IUWS model library is based on lumped conceptual models for predefined compartments, 
or so called blocks. In the library, the KOSIM hydrological catchment runoff and sewer transport 
model is implemented. This makes it possible to choose a model that is already coded in the 
model base (Prat et al., 2012). The principle of this model is that two main phenomena controls 
the wet weather flow: losses during runoff generation and the transformation of the effective 
rainfall into flow rates. Furthermore, the sewer system is represented by reservoirs in series. 
This makes this a hydrological model, in contrast to hydrodynamic, and does therefore not 
support any spatial distribution (Meirlaen, 2002).  The two main blocks in WEST used to describe 
the sewer system are the Catchment and Tank. The figure below illustrates a simplified 
overview of the simulation of a sewer system.

In Figure 2.1, it is seen that the catchment block simulates the Dry Weather Flow (DWF). 
Moreover, it simulates the runoff generation on the surface of the catchment based on a rain

accumulation and washoff of pollutants from the surface.  
The tank block simulates the transport and processes of the water in the sewer system. Through

Here, water is being routed through a series of tanks. A more extended model also includes the 
simulation of the sedimentation and erosion of TSS. Lastly, the results of the simulations are 
presented for the flow and pollutant loads and concentrations. The values presented are chosen 
by the user.  
Previous studies by Vanrolleghem et al. (2008) and Benedetti et al. (2013) have successfully 
used WEST for the evaluation of RTC. Here, WEST was preferred because the short 
computational times allowed for long-term simulations. By combining the use of WEST with a 
modelling platform that performs hydrodynamic simulations, it was possible to ensure more 
accurate flow data to use in the calibrating of the model in WEST. Water quality was then 
modelled based on surface runoff and sewer transport. The finished models included the 
following blocks and their objectives: 
 
Catchment: 
o DWF generation 
o Runoff generation 
 
Tanks 
o Simulate pipes 
o Storm water tanks 
o Tanks that evokes CSO  
WEST is not a distributed model, but the degree of conceptualization can be defined by the user 
with the use of more or less realistic presentation of the catchment area and sewer systems 
based on number of catchments and tanks included in the model.  
2.2 HYDRAULICS 
There are three dominating components of combined sewage flow. Base sanitary flow 
(wastewater), infiltration and rainfall derived inflow (stormwater). In a combined system, the 
stormwater is included, whereas in a separate sewage system it is excluded. The following 
sections will describe these three components to gain a greater understanding of the quantity of 
water being transported to the WWTP.

2.2.1 
Wastewater 
The baseflow to a WWTP consists of wastewater produced primarily in households, institutions, 
schools and to some degree industry, depending on the connected catchment. The 
approximately volume of water produced and send to the WWTP can be estimated based on 
the number of people connected to the plant, and the average daily water use per person. 
However, this is only a rough estimate. To get a better assessment, seasonal changes in the 
water use, and change of water consumption in the weekends must be taken into consideration 
(Butler and Davies, 2011).   
The wastewater flow to the WWTP changes significantly throughout the day, the so-called 
diurnal variations. Studies performed by Butler et al. (1995) for the diurnal variations in England 
and Malta produced the following graph presented in Figure 2.2.  
 
Figure 2.2: Diurnal variations based on 32 household surveys  (Butler et al., 1995) 
It is seen that the tendency is the same for the two countries, but the magnitude is not. For both 
curves, there are a morning peak and two peaks in the evening. The changes observed in the 
curves are most likely due to different life styles. These curves clearly show that it is not possible 
to make a general diurnal variation. It must be noted that the diurnal variations are based on 
the flow going out from household and are therefore not influenced by unwanted water, like 
the flow at the inlet at the WWTP. The presence of unwanted water is described in Section 
2.2.2. 
For a given catchment, a daily and weekly pattern for Dry Weather Flow (DWF) might be 
recognized, since the pattern will look different for weekends and weekdays. Moreover, It will 
depend on other factors like public holidays, the weather and degree of irrigation. To create a 
representative diurnal variation it is therefore necessary to evaluate the state of the studied 
catchment.

2.2.2 
Infiltration 
In most sewer systems, there are some degree of intrusion of water into the pipes. It is also 
known under the names unwanted water, parasite water and pirate water. The intrusion of 
water in separate systems can be due to cross connections. Cross connections will be observed 
as higher peak flows than expected during rainfall. The major contribution in most systems is 
however the infiltration of groundwater, which can seep into leaky pipes and joints. The 
groundwater infiltration mostly affects the DWF. The infiltration of groundwater has a high 
seasonal variation, and depend on local geological conditions, since it is proportional to the 
changes in the groundwater table. To estimate the magnitude of potential infiltration, the 
groundwater levels in the catchment will provide an indication. The infiltration can also be 
estimated by separating the wastewater flow that varies insignificantly throughout the year 
from the seasonal variations (Wittenberg and Aksoy, 2010).  
The infiltration can even be higher than the wastewater flows. Studies in Germany, Italy and 
Canada estimated that the ratio of unwanted water in the sewage systems is in the range of 39-
70% (Wittenberg and Aksoy, 2010). Wittenberg and Aksoy (2010) examined a full year of flow 
data originating from combined sewer system in Germany. Here, an Autocorrelogram of the 
three components in the sewage wastewater (QW), infiltration (QINF) and runoff (QR) was made, 
as seen in Figure 2.3. 
 
Figure 2.3: Autocorrelogram of total wastewater QW, infiltration QINF and stormwater QR based on a full year of flow 
measurements in a combined sewer in Germany (Wittenberg and Aksoy, 2010). 
In Figure 2.3 no annual variation is seen for stormwater, since precipitation in central Europe 
are not significantly influenced by season. It is however seen that the infiltration has a strong 
annual variation, with the lowest infiltration in the summer months and highest in the winter. 
Due to this, an annual variation in the total wastewater is therefore also observed. 
There are many different ways to evaluate the infiltration. Some conventional methods for 
infiltration measurements are based on either flow rates, daily hydrographs or chemical 
methods. One of the most simple methods are suggested by the US Environmental Protection 
Agency (EPA). Here, the infiltration is found by taking the minimum measured flow during the 
night, where it is assumed that no significant amount of wastewater is being produced. This

method is however only valid for catchments where no industries could have an influence on 
the nightly flow (EPA, 2014).  
2.2.3 
Runoff generation 
In combined sewers, the main changes are caused by stormwater entering the system. 
Stormwater is a result of rainfall and the thereby produced runoff. To describe the entering 
stormwater, a rainfall hyetograph is turned into a surface runoff hydrograph. In this calculation, 
the effective rainfall is found by accounting for the initial losses. These loses are caused by 
depressions in the surface and infiltration of the rainfall. The depression can range from 0.5-2 
mm for impervious areas, 2.5-7.5 mm for flat roofs and up to 10 mm for gardens. The degree of 
infiltration depends on type of soil in the catchment. However, for intense storms the initial 
losses becomes negligible (Winther et al., 2011).  
After evaluating the initial losses, the effective rainfall can be used to calculate the surface 
routing and overland flow and thereby produce the hydrograph.   
To create the hydrograph, unit hydrographs, time-area method or reservoir models can be 
applied. An often used conceptual model is the tank in series that with the emptying of the 
tanks form a unit hydrograph, and thereby capture the flow routing by transport delay and flow 
attenuation (Duran, 2014).  
The Linear reservoir cascade is a special case of the unit hydrograph approach, where the 
reservoir constant k should match all rain events (Chow et al., 1988a). Hydrologic data is rarely 
truly linear, but the resulting hydrograph can be acceptable in many practical cases. (Chow et 
al., 1988b). The basis of this approach is seen in Equation 2.2

2.2.4 
Hydraulics in sewers 
To describe the transport of the water in the pipes it is possible to apply physical laws. In 
particular, friction, gravity and pressure forces can be used. Furthermore, an energy balance is 
applied in order to account for all input and outputs of energy.  
The distributed flow routing calculations are based on the one-dimensional partial differential 
equations, the Saint Venant equations. These equations describes flow rate and water levels as 
a function of space and time. The simultaneous calculation of flow and water levels makes the 
model calculate the unsteady nonuniform flow more correctly. However, assumptions must be 
made in order to use the physical laws in these equations. The most important assumptions are 
that depth and velocity only vary in longitudinal direction and that vertically acceleration can be 
neglected. Moreover, the bottom slope is so small that the effects of scour and deposition can 
be neglected and the fluid is incompressible and have a constant density. Lastly, it is assumed 
that the resistance coefficient for steady uniform turbulent flow can be used, so Mannings 
equation can describe the resistance (Chow et al., 1988b) 
Newton’s second law is written in the form of Reynold’s transport theorem. This shows that the 
sum of forces applied is equal to the rate of change of momentum within the control volume 
and the net outflow of momentum across the control surface.  
For unsteady nonuniform flow, the following can therefore be written.

The gravity force is the impact on the water caused by gravity, where the slope of the pipes are 
taking into consideration. The friction force is caused by the shear stress along the sides of the 
wetted perimeter of the pipes. To find the friction slope Mannings equation can be used. A 
sudden contraction or expansion in a pipe results in energy loss. This loss is accounted for as a 
minor loss, where the resulting eddy motions are related to the velocity and a nondimensional 
contraction or expansion coefficient. The wind shear force is included in open channels, where 
the wind causes a frictional resistance on the free surface. For pipe calculations, this force can 
be neglected. The pressure force is a result of the hydrostatic force on one side of the control 
volume (Chow et al., 1988b).

The alternative to a distributed flow routing model is to use a lumped model to calculate the

lumped model the flow is calculated as a function of time, and does therefore not include the 
function of space as with the distributed models (Chow et al., 1988a).  
An example of a lumped model is the full conceptualization of the sewer system through a 
reservoir model. The reservoir model will include the delay and attenuation that occur in the 
sewage system, but it will not be able to include backwater conditions (Chow et al., 1988a).  
2.2.5 
Conceptual model 
To describe the hydraulic simulations in WEST, the applied equations and parameters are 
presented for DWF and runoff generation. 
2.2.5.1 
Dry weather flow 
To simulate the DWF four elements are key: having the representative diurnal variation for the 
catchment and a good approximation of the mean wastewater flow, and having a mean 
infiltration value and a representative yearly pattern for changes in infiltration rates. In WEST a 
diurnal pattern (in hours) for wastewater and a yearly pattern (in months) for infiltration can be 
added or premade ones can be applied. WEST interpolates between the given coefficients, and 
thereby creates smooth curved patterns. To simulate the wastewater flow, the input 
parameters are the area of the catchment, the population density and the amount of 
wastewater produced daily per person. For infiltration, an average amount in l/s/ha is given, 
and the amount of infiltration is therefore a function of the catchment area.  
WEST can also account for weekends and tourism by defining the periods and applying a relative 
factor for the changes in flow and pollutant loads during these times.

2.2.5.4 
Limitations 
Since this is a conceptual model, the limitations in the flow simulations must be taken into 
consideration. The most pronounced limitation is that the linear reservoir model does not 
account for possible backwater effects in the pipes. Backwater effects happen when the 
capacity of the pipes is exceeded. This can be due to slope decrease, an increase in pipe 
roughness, or under dimensioned pipes. The implementation of backwater effects can be done 
in different ways on a conceptual plan. The most efficient way to incorporate backwater effects 
in WEST is with the use of a pump. This is done by adjusting the pump to allow for a maximum 
flow, and when this is exceeded, the water will be pumped back to an upstream pipe (a tank).  
(Solvi, 2007).    
CSOs are not included in the basic WEST setup. A way to mimic a CSO structure is to implement 
a tank, and set the maximum flow to the flow where it is expected that no CSO occur. If this flow 
is exceeded, the CSO water can be taken out of the sewage system, or directed to another tank.

2.3 WATER QUALITY 
In combined sewage systems, the wastewater originates both from domestic houses and 
industries, and from runoff. The compounds in the wastewater can be described physically as 
Total Suspended Solids (TSS), and chemically as organics, nutrients, metals, pathogens etc. 
(Winther et al., 2011). When evaluating the quality of wastewater the most used parameters 
are Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD), TSS, nitrogen and phosphorus.  
Pollutant sources can be subdivided into the following classifications, atmosphere, surfaces on 
the catchment, domestic sewage, industrial and commercial effluents, the environment and 
processes in sewer pipes and construction sites. The sources focused on in this section are the 
domestic sewage and the surface, since these are the biggest contributors in a residential area 
(Schlutter, 1999). 
In combined systems the DWF is in the range of 1-5% of the wet weather flow (Winther et al., 
2011). Stormwater is therefore a very important contributor when accessing the water quality, 
not only during CSO events, but also in the general assessment of the system.  
The majority of the CSO consist of runoff, where the water quality can vary significantly. This can 
be caused by the state of the catchment (waste collection, street sweeping etc.), topography 
and the proporties of the rain. Due to this, there is great uncertainty in predicting the runoff 
quality.  
When evaluation the effects of CSO, a key issue is to estimate the concentrations of pollutants 
in the water during a CSO. In Denmark, CSO concentrations is comparable to stormwater 
concentrations due to the design of the sewage system, since overflows occur mainly during 
high intensity rainfalls, when the sewage primarily consist of runoff (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 
2000). To find the discharge pollution loads during CSO, the following concentrations have been 
suggested: 10 mg/l nitrogen and 3-7 mg/l nitrogen for offline volume in sewage system under 3-
5 mm and over 3-5 mm respectively and TSS concentrations of 150-200 mg/l and 100-150 mg/l 
respectively (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2000).  The off-line volume describes the storage capacity 
of the drainage system. These concentrations are based on flow proportional grab samples 
taken in five catchments in Denmark. However, due to the age of this survey, the proposed 
concentrations should only be used as an indicator of the concentrations.   
 In the following sections, the concentrations of Ammonia and TSS will be evaluated further.  
2.3.1 
Ammonia 
Total nitrogen exists in the four main forms: organic nitrogen, ammonia, nitrite and nitrate. 
Ammonia, nitrite and nitrate are dissolved inorganic nitrogen and have the greatest impact on 
the water bodies, since they are bioavailable and can therefore easily be taken up by 
microorganisms, plants, algae and animals. The discharge of dissolved nitrogen should be 
reduced due to the adverse effect on the receiving waters (Taylor et al., 2005).  
Ammonia exists as the ammonium ion NH4+ and the ammonia gas NH3. The ratio depends on pH 
and temperature. The pH in the sewer is normally in the range of 6.7- 7.5 for areas with hard 
water (Butler and Davies, 2011), like Denmark. The majority of ammonia will be present as 
ammonium, since the acid constant has a value of 9.25. Due to this, no distinction between 
ammonium and ammonia will be made, and the term ammonia will be applied in this report.  
The majority of organic nitrogen in wastewater originates from excreta (94%) (Butler et al., 
1995). Of this, approximately half is from urine, which contains urea. This is rapidly converted to 
==PAGE:23==
14 
 
ammonia. Food residues are also a source of organic nitrogen. In domestic wastewater the 
majority of nitrogen is therefore in the form of ammonia. The ammonia concentration in strong, 
medium and diluted wastewater, are estimated to be 75, 45 and 20 mg/l respectively (Henze et 
al., 2006). In theory  the amount of ammonia can be changed over time by microorganisms in 
the sewage with the use of oxygen (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). However, in small 
catchments this is not believed to have any influence, and the ammonia concentration stays 
relative constant from the households to the WWTP (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2001). 
In a study of the quality of runoff, nitrogen was found to be predominately dissolved (80%) 
(Taylor et al., 2005). Ammonia was the least abundant form of total nitrogen, contributing with 
only 11% and a concentration of 0.29 mg/l (Taylor et al., 2005). There are no significant sources 
for ammonia to be present in the rainwater or the produced runoff, which supports the very low 
measured concentration in this study.  
2.3.2 
Total suspend solids 
Total suspended solids (TSS) are the amount of particles suspended in the wastewater. It is a 
reflection of the turbidity and the cloudiness of a fluid. TSS is defined as particles with a 
diameter larger than 0.45µm in the water phase (Butler and Davies, 2011). The suspended solids 
can be both organic and inorganic, and can act as carriers of pollutants. Furthermore, TSS can 
cause unwanted effects such as reduced light penetration and interference with fish and aquatic 
invertebrates. Through deposition they can cause a delayed oxygen demand (Butler and Davies, 
2011). 
TSS are estimated to be in the following concentrations for high, medium and diluted 
wastewater respectively; 450, 300 and 190 mg/l (Henze et al., 2006).  
TSS are used in the explaining and quantifying of the sedimentation and first flush of pollutants 
in a sewer system. This is coherent with 80% of TSS have been found to settle as sediments 
(Schlütter, 1999). TSS are also present in the runoff, where a study found that 90% of the 
sediments into the sewer originated from street runoff, with the majority of the particle size is 
in the range of 50-80 µm. A comparison of the TSS concentration in storm sewers (160-460 
mg/l) and combined sewer (240-670 mg/l) indicates that a big contribution of TSS originate from 
the surface (Schlütter, 1999).

2.4 POLLUTANT TRANSPORT 
An important requisite for simulating the water quality is to understand the transport of the 
pollutants in the sewer system. A basic understanding is mass balance, which states that matter 
is neither created nor destroyed by transformation and transport processes (Nazaroff and 
Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). 
Four important processes are identified for the sewage system:  
 
Conversion and transfer processes 
 
Transport 
 
Pollutant accumulation and washoff  
 
Sedimentation and resuspension 
Transformation is explained by stoichiometry, which is based on the conservation of number of 
atoms and the electrical balance. Stoichiometry does not state the rate of the reactions that are 
occurring, for this kinetics are needed.  
From the time the water hits the surface and travels through the sewage system the compounds 
in the produced stormwater runoff will undergo transformation and degradation. In dry 
weather conditions microbial and physiochemical processes are dominating, in contrast to wet 
weather conditions where physical processes becomes dominating (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 
2001). Since the focus in this study is on the wet weather conditions, only the physical processes 
will be described.  
When evaluating the transport of pollutants, a distinction between solubles and particulates is 
made. Dissolved pollutants are transported with the water, while particles do not necessarily 
have the same transport time as the water due to possible sedimentation, erosion, 
resuspension and compaction (Meirlaen, 2002).  
2.4.1

Transport of the soluble components, ammonia and phosphorous, can be modelled where the 
spreading is due to dispersion and advection. Advection is the transport of the contaminant 
caused by the net flow in which the contaminant is suspended in. The flux of the pollutant is 
therefore only dependent on the velocity of the water and the concentration. Due to this, 
uniform steady advection does not cause spreading. Spreading of the pollutant is caused by 
diffusion, where random motion of molecules are triggered by concentrations gradients. The

2.4.2 
Sedimentation and erosion 
The transport of the particulates are dependent on the sedimentation and erosion processes. 
These processes are controlling the mass of the suspended load on the surface of the catchment 
and bed load in the sewer pipes. (Rauch et al., 2002). During dry weather particles will sediment 
as an accumulation on the surface of the catchment and deposit in the pipes. A rain event will 
cause erosion of the sedimented particles through washoff from the surface and resuspension 
in the pipes (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2001). 
For the surface, a linear accumulation or exponentially asymptotic accumulation can be applied 
as a function of the antecedent dry period. However, many factors contribute to the 
accumulation rate. Land use, traffic, street cleaning, season etc. For residential areas the 
accumulation rate is found to be in the range 95-3200 kg TSS/ha/year (Schlütter, 1999).  
The accumulated mass will be washed off from the surface during rain events. The rate of 
washoff depends on the characteristics of the surface, the particles and the intensity of the rain. 
The washoff can be described with a function of the accumulated mass of solids, the flow 
velocity or the bed shear stress. A minimum of shear stress is required to washoff the pollutants 
from the surface, and an exponentially decreasing washoff load is expected (Meirlaen, 2002). 
The pollutants can also undergo deposition and resuspension in the pipes. Deposition will occur 
if the velocity of the water is lower than the settling velocity. The velocity for where the settling 
of particles occur is a function of the concentration and the distribution of the particle size  
(Grum and Vezzaro, 2008). In sewer systems, the deposition is therefore also dependent on the 
slope and diameter of the pipe. Resuspension can on the other hand occur if the velocity of 
water is high enough to resuspend the sedimented particles and the biofilm (Hvitved-Jacobsen 
et al., 2001). The sedimented particles can create a sediment bed, which can create blockage, 
loss of hydraulic capacity and facilitate pollutant storage.  
The accumulation, washoff, deposition and resuspension of pollutants are complex phenomena 
that are not yet fully understood. Especially, the erosion of particles, which can result in the 
phenomenon “first flush”, are difficult to describe. The first flush effect can be seen as a very 
high TSS concentration in the beginning of the rain event after a long antecedent dry period 
(Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2001).

3 CASE STUDY AND DATA 
In this section, the catchment area of Viby, Aarhus is presented, together with the connected 
WWTP. Moreover, the rain data for the Viby area will be evaluated and the AMOK data used in 
this study will be presented.  
3.1 CATCHMENT  
The second largest city in Denmark Aarhus has approximately 300,000 inhabitants. The utility 
company Aarhus Vand is the supervisors of the sewage system, which is divided into three main 
areas and their connected WWTP, Åby, Marselisborg and Viby. The catchment used for the case 
study is located in Viby, which has a WWTP that is designed for 84,000 Persons Equivalent (PE) 
(Breinholt and Sharma, 2010). The catchment connected to Viby WWTP is approximately 1450 
ha, with 700 ha being a combined sewage system and 750 ha a separate sewage system (Ahm et 
al., 2015). 
The current catchments connected to the WWTP are the subcatchments Viby, Holme, Harlev, 
Stavtrup, Hasselager and Kolt. In Stavtrup and Kolt there are a completely separate sewage 
system, in Holme, Harlev and Hasselager it is partly separated, and in Viby there is a combined 
sewage system. Most of the system is gravitational driven, however pressurized pipes are 
installed from Harlev and Kolt. 
For optimization reasons, the systems are planned to undergo changes in the following years. In 
September 2013 the Harlev catchment was connected to Viby WWTP (Aarhus Vand, 2014). To 
get an overview of the catchment a map of the area is presented in Figure 3.1, where the 
current separate or partly separated catchment are marked by the dark blue lines, and 
combined sewage are marked by red lines.  
Figure 3.1: Overview of the subcatchments connected to the Viby WWTP. The current connected catchments of 2014 
are marked with surrounding lines, with the red being Viby and blue are Harlev, Kolt, Hasselager, Stavtrup and Holme 
(Jézéquel, 2014) .  
==PAGE:29==

There are CSO structures in the combined catchment leading to the WWTP at the detention 
basins in Harlev and Hasselager and at the inlet canal, internally at Viby WWTP. The overflows 
are of negligible size at Harlev and Hasselager (Duus, 2015). 
To avoid peak loads at the WWTP, basins are installed in the catchments. They have a varying 
size of 165-11,000 m3, and have an equalizing effect of the flow to the WWTP. The WWTP has a 
maximum capacity of 1260 l/s and when this is exceeded, an internal overflow leads the excess 
water to a basin. This basin has a capacity of 16,000 m3 and the sewage is contained there, until 
the capacity of the WWTP allows it to be pumped to treatment. During heavy rain, the capacity 
of the WWTP and the basin might be exceeded and an overflow to the nearby creek, Døde å, 
occurs. This is estimated to happen a few times per year (Duus, 2015)  
The annual infiltration rate for the entire catchment area are estimated to be 77% of the DWF 
(Natur og Miljø - Aarhus Kommune, 2013). Based on the groundwater level the infiltrated water 
is believed to be due to intrusion of groundwater from the secondary aquifer and cross 
connections from the separate sewage system. The infiltration of groundwater is supported by 
the borelogs obtained from GEUS database, (GEUS, 2015). Here monitoring and geotechnical 
boreholes show that the water table in the Viby area is situated 7.5-1.66m from the surface. 
From 10 randomly chosen borelogs, the water table had a median value of 2.96 m. (See 
Appendix 1). Due to the relatively high ground water table, a high infiltration is to be expected.

3.2 RAIN SERIES 
Precipitation data from SVK (Spildevandskomitéens regnmålersystem) are used as the rain 
series input in the model. The technique used is the tipping bucket rain gauge (RIM7499). Here a 
rain event is defined to consist of at least two tips, within a period of less than 60 minutes. If 
there are more than 60 minutes between the tips, they are defined as individual rain events. 
Each tip corresponds to 0.2 mm of precipitation. The intensity is then based on the time it takes 
for the measuring device to fill up. The rain series are available in a KM2-format, where the start 
of the rain event is listed, together with the following rain intensity per minute. Furthermore, a 
==PAGE:30==
21 
 
number is listed as a quality control. Here, 1 means the event is recorded correctly (Thomsen et 
al., 2014). 
Precipitation can be difficult to measure accurately. One of the biggest causes of errors is the 
wind. The wind can cause the raindrops to be blown away from the measuring device. It is 
therefore very important where the measuring device is placed. It is advised to place the rain 
gauge a place where it is fully sheltered from the wind (Schellart et al., 2012). More information 
of the rain series used in this study can be seen in Appendix 2.  
3.3 AMOK PROJECT AT VIBY WWTP 
The data used in this thesis are as previously stated originating from the online measurements 
conducted in the AMOK project, which aims to improve the Danish utility companies’ ability to 
prioritize and calculate the loads during CSOs. This was done by measuring the quality of 
sewage, at the internal overflow structure at Viby WWTP. By producing a full representative 
dataset it is possible to make model simulations based on this data (Vandsektorens 
Teknologiudviklingsfond, 2013).  
 In Viby, the following have been measured from 1/6-13 to 18/11-14 (535 days).

The measurements have been performed with sensors installed at the internal overflow

be referred to as the in situ measurements. The ex situ measurements were obtained by 
pumping water from the same place as the in situ sensor to a container installed next to the 
inlet and analyze them there. Moreover, traditional sampling has been performed to evaluate 
the data obtained from the sensors. All of the parameters have been measured with a 
frequency of 1 minute.

In Figure 3.2, a picture of the WWTP is presented, together with the location of the flow and

located at the inlet, the measurements also include the water originating from the internal CSO 
where it has been stored in the basin, and was being pumped back to the WWTP after the rain 
event. The flow data does therefore not include the CSO flow during the rain event, due to the 
placement of the sensor. Times of overflow are characterized as flow being over 1260 l/s, since 
this is the capacity of the WWTP. 
As mentioned, the flow and quality sensors were not placed in the same water. However, it is 
assumed that the quality of the water originating from the Viby catchment is also representative 
of the water originating from the remaining five catchments.  
The sensors used to measure flow was the electromagnetic flowmeter, Siemens Magflo 6000. 
The accuracy is empirically estimated to be up to 2%, where inaccuracies can be caused by 
changes in temperature, density and particles in the fluid (Ahm et al., 2015).   
The ammonia concentration was measured with the sensor A-ISE sc, which is a potentiometric 
sensor. Thus, it uses ion-selective electrodes to determine the concentration of the ammonium 
ion in the water. TSS was measured with the turbidimeter SOLITAX sc, which is based on the 
dual scattered light method. Both sensors were attached to a mast with depth matching the 
water level, which means the sensors remained at the same depth in the water, regardless of 
the water level. Both sensors are estimated to have an uncertainty of approximately 5% 
(Sharma et al., 2014).  
Cleaning of the sensors are necessary to avoid biofilm forming and particles accumulating on the 
surface. In Viby, the protocol was to do this on a weekly basis. Verification and online-correction 
are continuously needed for the sensors. The verification was performed with the comparison 
with a standard solution or a grab sample taken and analyzed at the WWTP. If this analysis 
showed a variation of more than 10-20%, an online-correction was performed (Jézéquel, 2014). 
==PAGE:32==
23 
 
The correction was performed online, which means the adjustment took effect immediately. In 
the correction, a linear calibration curve was assumed, and the corrections were therefore 
based on two points.  
For the ex situ measurements, water was pumped to the container with a grinder pump. This 
means that the biggest debris were homogenized into smaller particles. For the ammonia 
measurements, this does not have any influence on the measured concentrations, since 
ammonia is dissolved. For TSS however, grinding can cause changes in the TSS concentrations. 
Because of this, the ex situ ammonia concentrations can be used as a validation of the in situ 
measurements, whereas only the in situ TSS measurements will be used in the following study.

4 INITIAL DATA ANALYSIS  
The initial analysis is performed to get a better overview of the data. The analysis is performed 
in Matlab and the aim is to detect patterns and trends in the data. All the calculations 
performed can be seen in the Matlab script in Appendix E1. 
4.1 METHODOLOGY 
For the DWF days, the diurnal variation of flow and pollution patterns for ammonia and TSS will 
be identified. Here, the variation will be described by the multiplier, which is the coefficient that 
describes the change from the daily mean. The change in infiltration rate will also be evaluated 
as a multiplier, here however from the yearly mean. 
The days with DWF will be extracted from the data by setting a maximum threshold flow at 250 
l/s. This threshold is based on the flow illustrations seen in Appendix 3, and Figure 3.3 and 
Figure 3.4. In order to visualize the calculated variations, a curve containing the calculated 
multiplier, together with two times the standard deviations are illustrated. Lastly, the ammonia 
measurements are used to perform an analysis of the daily load. 
4.1.1 
Data treatment in Matlab  
The initial data analysis will be performed in Matlab. The three datasets are all obtained in 
matrices, with time (t) in the first column and measurements (m) in the second. The analysis is 
started with filtering out wet weather conditions in the flow data, which is defined as flow 
above 250 l/s, thereby getting s number of measurements (see Figure 4.1). Next, only full DWF 
days are included in the analysis. With one measurement per minute (m), each day should 
contain 1440 measuring points. It is therefore possible to transform the measurements into 
matrices with 1440 number of rows, and the number of columns equal to the n number of full 
DWF days.  
For ammonia and TSS, the DWF days are identified, and if these days contain a full set of 
measurements, they are included. A matrix, like for the flow, is therefore produced for ammonia 
and TSS as well. This matrix will be referred to as the minute matrix. It must be noted here, that 
since the AMOK datasets are not consistent in time series, there will be less days included for 
ammonia and TSS, than for flow.

4.1.2 
Annual variation of Infiltration 
There are two unknown contributions to the total measured DWF, infiltration and wastewater. 
The analysis of the DWF is however limited by only having one measurement of the flow. To get 
past this, it is assumed that the lowest measured flow of the day, is the daily infiltration flow 
(EPA, 2014). For each day, the lowest flow is found (inf,daily [l/s]). To obtain the yearly pattern, 
the daily infiltration is organized into months. For each month, the monthly infiltration 
multiplier is calculated as the mean monthly infiltration over the yearly mean infiltration. 
Moreover, a standard deviation is calculated for each month. Due to this, a minimum of three 
DWF per months are required in the analysis.  
To examine the effect of infiltration on the total flow a yearly pattern, based on months, of the 
total flow is produced.

pollutant.

6 SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS 
A sensitivity analysis is performed in order to obtain a more effective calibration of the model. 
Despite of this being a lumped model, there are still many parameters to calibrate in WEST, 
especially for the simulation of runoff (Appendix 6). By finding the most sensitive parameters for 
each output; flow, ammonia and TSS, these parameters can be the initial adjusted parameters. 
A sensitivity analysis will also aid the understanding of the model and the model response to the 
parameters, within a defined range, can be assessed.   
6.1 METHODOLOGY 
A sensitivity analysis based on a Monte Carlo simulation (MC) is performed on the model. To set 
up the analysis, execute it and evaluate the results, the following steps were followed. 
 
Define the scoring 
 
Choose events or time period 
 
Define the number of Monte Carlo simulations that should be performed  
 
Decide which parameters to investigate. 
 
Define a distribution of inputs and randomly generate inputs based on these 
distributions.  
 
Illustrative assessment of the sensitivity based on scatterplot 
 
Find regression between scoring and parameter to further evaluate the sensitivity 
The scoring should be a value that reflects the response of the model to the given parameter 
set. For this analysis, the scoring is decided to be the root mean square error (RMSE) (See 
Equation 5.1). The sensitivity analysis will be performed simultaneous for flow, ammonia and 
TSS. Therefore, the three scorings will be the three calculated RMSE for these components.  
To get a representative analysis, the time period chosen is the longest period where all the 
components have reasonable measurements. All the RMSE calculations are based on the in situ 
data, therefore sensor corrections needs to be considered. Due to the ammonia sensor 
corrections (see Appendix 4), the period is therefore 8/8-14 to 29/9-14, resulting in a total of 52 
days. Since the focus of this model is the rain events, the RMSE is only calculated when the flow 
is above 250 l/s. Moreover, the flow data do not contain overflows, and therefore the RMSE is 
not calculated when the flow is above the capacity of the WWTP (1260 l/s).  
The sensitive of the parameters are analyzed based on a total of 33 rain events, where a rain 
event is defined according to the SVK measurements (Section 3.2). However, choosing such a 
long period also have negative influence on the computational time. Due to time constraints, 
the number of simulations performed are therefore set at 1000 simulations.  
To get a better understanding of the model more than one input must be evaluated 
simultaneously. In a Monte Carlo analysis, it is assumed that the parameters are independent of 
each other (Saltelli et al., 2006). This is not the case for several of the hydraulic input in WEST, 
where many of the DWF parameters are correlated. The parameters used in the simulation of 
DWF is therefore not included in the analysis. Furthermore, it is assumed that the daily and 
monthly patterns found in the initial data analysis are representative, and will not be further 
evaluated. To be able to simulate the three components at the same time it was necessary to 
assume TSS and ammonia have the same pollution pattern, which is not true according to Figure 
4.6 and Figure 4.8. This must be kept in mind when analyzing the final results. The accumulation 
==PAGE:48==

and washoff module cannot be activated at the same time as the runoff concentrations, 
therefore this feature is not included in the analysis.

To find the parameter values within the given range, the latin hybercube sampling is used. This 
method ensures an even reflection of the parameter range. It divides the parameter range into 
a grid and creates the following intervals for n samples: (0-1/n), (1/n-2/n), ..., (1-1/n-1). The grid 
==PAGE:49==
40 
 
makes sure the parameters simulated are representative and based on this interval the values 
are randomly found.  
The model is then run for the 1000 simulated parameter sets, and the RMSE is calculated for 
each run. A simple effective method to evaluate the result of the analysis is a visual assessment 
of the effect from input to the output. Therefore, scatter plots are produced as the parameters 
against the RMSE. The more sensitive the parameter is, the more structured a pattern is formed 
(Sokolowski and Banks, 2010). Moreover, the colors of the dots in the scatterplots symbolizes 
the corresponding RMSE value, the darker, the lower RMSE. 
In order to get an additional assessment, a regression between the parameter and the RMSE is 
found. A regression can give the sensitivity a value, and a less subjective analyses than the 
scatterplots can be made. However, the regression approach is only valid for linear systems, and 
must be used with care. If the system is not linear, the regression value can suggest the 
parameter is not sensitive, even though it is. To get a representative regression value, that can 
be used to compare the parameters, the parameters are normalized (Saltelli et al., 2008).

7 CALIBRATION 
The calibration of the model is performed for one long period, and 11 single rain events. First, 
the methodology of the calibration is described, and next the results are presented. 
7.1 METHODOLOGY 
To initiate the calibration of the model, a manual calibration is performed prior to the automatic 
calibration. This is done to get an idea of the range of the parameter values. For the automatic 
calibration, the calibration strategy has been based on the extensive amount of data that are 
available. Due to this, a long-term calibration will be performed first for both wet and dry 
weather conditions. Moreover, the single events from the measuring campaign (see Table 3.2) 
will be calibrated. These events are chosen, since the grab samples provides more confidence in 
the sensor measurements. Lastly, six randomly chosen rain events are calibrated. The single rain 
events are chosen as a calibration target, since the aim of the model is to simulate the water 
quality during rain events. To summarize, the automatic calibrations of a long period and 11 
single events have been performed on the parameters presented in Table 6.2. The deposition 
and resuspension of TSS are still included in the model, but will only be simulated with the 
default values given by WEST. 
7.1.1 
Dry weather calibration  
The calibration of the model is initiated by the calibration of the dry weather flow (DWF) 
conditions. As a basis the diurnal variations found in the data analysis will be applied, however to 
test the performance of these, the diurnal flow variation will be calibrated based on Equation 2.1. 
This will allow for a comparison between a calibrated pattern and a pattern found through data 
analysis.  
Like stated in Chapter 5, the DWF is a function of the catchment area, the population density, 
the produced wastewater per person and the infiltration rate. To avoid calibrating correlated 
parameters, the only DWF hydraulic parameter calibrated for is the infiltration rate. Firstly, the 
infiltration is calibrated, and will then be used in the calibration of the wastewater  
concentrations for ammonia and TSS. The following approach is used for the DWF calibration of 
flow, ammonia and TSS.  


o Section evaluation of the found flow diurnal pattern and the calibrated flow

No interval is defined for RMSE since the DWF has significantly more measurements than the 
wet weather, making the wet weather negligible in the long-term calibration.  
In the model setup, the monthly infiltration pattern (Figure 4.3) and the diurnal variations for 
flow (Figure 4.4), ammonia (Figure 4.6) and TSS (Figure 4.8) are applied. Since WEST does not 
allow for more than one pollution pattern, two similar models are made for each pattern.  
A visual section evaluation is performed for the three calibration targets, by illustrating 
randomly chosen DWF periods.

Since the flow sensor does not include the CSO, periods where the flow is higher than 1260 l/s 
are excluded from the RMSE calculations. The data used in the long-term calibration, are filtered 
for dry weather flow in order to obtain a more efficient calibration. To do so, the RMSE is only 
calculated for times where the measured flow is higher than 250 l/s. Like for DWF, the 
performance of the long-term calibration is evaluated on randomly chosen sections.  
For the single event calibrations of the measuring campaigns and the randomly chosen events, a 
visual evaluation of the model is performed, together with the inclusion of the RMSE and load 
calculations. Since, the infiltration capacity of the pervious area is dependent on the simulation 
time (see Equation 2.12 and Equation 2.13), and the simulation time for each event is only 2 
days, this parameter must be increased.  If not, the model will treat pervious areas as 
impervious due to surpassing the infiltrating capacity, leading to the prior DWF period of the 
event to have an influence on the found pervious fractions. This does not support a calibration 
of a representative pervious fraction, and the maximum infiltration is therefore set very high 
(10000 mm/d).   
To achieve a higher confidence in the ability of the conceptual model in WEST to simulate the 
correct flow, the flow simulations are compared with the flow simulations performed by a MIKE 
URBAN model. MIKE URBAN uses the Saint Venant equations (see section 2.2.4), and a model of 
the Viby catchment area is already setup in this software.  
After the calibrations, the parameter sets for both the long-term wet weather calibration and 
the single events are compared. For measuring campaign and single rain events calibration the 
ranges of the found parameters, will be presented together with the median value of the 
parameters. The reason for presenting the median value, rather than the average, is to minimize 
the effect of potential outlier parameters. Furthermore, the loads are calculated for each single 
rain event. This is done by setting simulated flow above 1261 l/s to 1260 l/s and calculating flux, 
when the flow is above 250 l/s. The sum of this is then found to get the load. Since CSO flow is 
not measured, the calculated loads are only used as a qualitative comparison between the 
simulated and the measured flow volume and ammonia and TSS load.

7.1.4 
Automatic calibration 
The calibration of the WEST model is done through a Matlab script. The use of Matlab allows for 
an automatic calibration of the model. The following sections describe two automatic 
calibration methods. 
==PAGE:56==
47 
 
7.1.4.1 
Fminsearch 
Fminsearch is an unconstrained nonlinear optimization function in Matlab, which finds the 
minimum of a given problem. The inputs to this function are the starting point for each 
parameter being calibrated and the function that is being optimized. Fminsearch is based on a 
simplex search method. If n is the number of parameters calibrated n+1 vectors are made to 
make up the simplex vertices. For example if two parameters are being calibrated, the simplex is 
a triangle. It is a direct search method, and each iteration begin with an altering of one or more 
of the simplex vertices (MathWorks, 2015).  
The simplex method is a computational lightweight automatic calibration, and the calibration 
can be performed within a descent amount of time. However, due to its simple approach it is 
not recommended for complicated nonlinear systems (Lagarias et al., 1998). Calibrating a 
nonlinear system with fminsearch can lead to the finding of a local minimum for the parameter 
set in contrast to the models global minimum. Due to this, precautions must be taken when 
using this function. A way to test the stability of the calibration is to run the calibration several 
times, with different starting points, and see if the same parameter set is obtained. To further 
test the performance of this method, a more complex automatic calibration, like described in 
the following section, could be performed on the same model and the results can be compared.  
For the calibrations, where the risk of unrealistic values exists (e.g. negative concentrations for 
runoff concentrations) an alteration of fminsearch, fminseachbnd is used. This adaptation of the 
fminsearch function makes it possible to set ranges for the calibrated parameters. This function 
can be seen in Appendix E5.   
7.1.4.2 
DREAM 
DiffeRential Evolution Adaptive Metropolis (DREAM) Matlab toolbox is a toolbox that allows for 
a more complex automatic calibration, with the use of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) 
(Vrugt et al., 2008). The toolbox includes a self-adaptive randomized subspace sampling. 
Throughout the sampling process, it updates the scale and orientation. It estimates the 
posterior probability density function of the parameters, and thereafter creates a random new 
parameter that is either rejected or accepted. It is an iterative approximation method and 
generates samples that provides a random walk through the search space. For this search space 
to be representative for the calibrated parameters, the user should define the range of the 
parameters. This method needs a burn-period to get the sampling started, which is important to 
include when choosing the number of simulations.  
The automatic calibration runs multiple chains simultaneously, which ensures global 
exploration. The multiple chains method is optimal if dealing with a model that might have 
numerous local optima, and the user can chose the number of Markov chains that should be 
included. To summarize, this efficient global optimization algorithm can be used on highly 
nonlinear models and has been shown to perform excellent for nonlinearity, high-
dimensionality and multimodality problems (Vrugt et al., 2008). The adapted DREAM toolbox 
can be seen in Appendix E6.

Figure 7.16: Flow calibration of M7, k=0.01 d-1, f=0.67 
and RMSE=204 l/s. 
Since MIKE URBAN is a distributed hydrodynamic model, based on the Saint Venant equations, 
the flow is expected to be more accurate, than the lumped conceptual model used in WEST. By 
comparing the five events, there is not observed a better accordance of the hydrodynamic 
model and the measured flow, than with the conceptual model. This is especially evident, when 
comparing the rain events where there is a clearly pump activity observed. Here, it was 
expected that the MIKE URBAN model would perform better, but this is not the case. Despite of 
this being a more complicated model with the pumps included, it does not simulate the correct 
pump activity. For M4, this is especially obvious, since there is a long period of increased flow 
after the rain event (Figure 7.15). 
For M7 it is seen that the conceptual model captures the peaks better than the MIKE URBAN 
model. This could be due to the fact that in both models the input is a local rain series. For this 
rain event, the rain series might not be representative for the whole catchment and the 
produced runoff. The higher measured peaks than the simulated peaks could be caused by the 
rain was more intense another place in the catchment, than in the recorded place. Since the 
MIKE URBAN model is stationary, it cannot correct for the rain series not representing the whole 
catchment. However, the conceptual model is individually calibrated for each of the rain events 
and it can therefore correct for the local variations of rain. For M7 the conceptual model is 
correcting for the rain series, by calibrating a lower pervious fraction (0.67), leading to 
production of more runoff, which is resulting in higher peaks. The main difference observed 
between the two models is the fact that the used MIKE URBAN model is a stationary model with 
the same parameters simulating all the rain events, whereas the conceptual model is 
individually calibrated to each rain events. Performance wise no difference was observed 
between the two models.

9 GENERAL DISCUSSION 
A general discussion of the used software WEST and the model is presented in this chapter. 
First, a discussion of the software WEST is given, second a discussion of the overall model 
performance is provided and lastly the model is evaluated in regards to uncertainty and 
application possibilities. 
9.1 WEST AS A TOOL 
WEST has the great advantage of a very short simulation time, allowing for automatic 
calibration of the model and long-term simulations in a reasonable time. With this in mind, the 
fact that WEST does not accommodate simulations for longer than 360 days is a disadvantage of 
the software. Moreover, WEST does not simulate the days and date correct, and therefore day 1 
in the model should always be assumed to be the 1/1. This is necessary to make the infiltration 
pattern work correctly. An update of the time features in WEST will therefore be recommended. 
In this study, it was clearly seen that the ammonia and TSS concentrations are not similar and an 
improvement of WEST would be to allow for different pollutant patterns for each of the 
simulated pollutants.  
The flow simulated in WEST was compared to the simulations obtained by the hydrodynamic 
model MIKE URBAN. Here, it was seen that the hydrodynamic model did not yield better results 
than the ones simulated in WEST. The IUWS library in WEST is a highly conceptualized and 
simplified modelling of the sewage system, however in the implementation of the KOSIM model 
18 parameters have been included to describe the runoff generation (See Appendix 6). With 
many parameters, the risk of overparametrization increases. Furthermore, the sensitivity 
analysis showed that the majority of these runoff parameters had very little influence on the 
simulated flow.  Since they are incorporated in the model these parameters need to be 
considered in the model setup and not all of the default values pre-set for the parameters are 
ideal. Due to this, it is recommended to make a catchment model in WEST that only includes a 
linear reservoir model, and one parameter given for the initial loss in the runoff generation.  
In this study, WEST has been run through a Matlab script, thereby using Matlab as an interface. 
However, care must be taken when using this approach. The most time efficient simulation is 
achieved when suppressing the messages from WEST, but when doing this, extra attention must 
then be given to potential problems. The most common errors encountered in this study was 
numerical errors and parameter values not given correctly to WEST. Since a result file will still be 
produced when these errors are encountered, the errors will not be recognized in Matlab. This 
is especially a problem when performing automatic calibrations. 
To summarize, steps could be taken to improve WEST, but in general, the software showed 
great advantages as to simulate the water quality and the very short computational time. 
Moreover, WEST has great potentials in the easy integration of a WWTP model and a river 
model in the produced sewer model. 
9.2 MODEL PERFORMANCE 
In this study, it was seen that the linear reservoir model could both describe the water quantity 
and quality of dissolved pollutants. The model was able to simulate ammonia concentrations 
based on flux. This is coherent with what was previously found by Sharma et al. (2014). Thus, it 
==PAGE:83==
74 
 
is found that the dispersion of ammonia is large enough to assume perfect mixing, making the 
recession time equal to the reservoir constant.  
This parsimonious modelling of water quality, including deposition and resuspension, has 
previously found to give satisfactory results (Willems, 2010).  In this study, this was not 
confirmed. It was not possible to model the first flush effects fully, since the high peaks of TSS 
could not be simulated. Little improvements with the incorporation of accumulation and 
washoff, and deposition and resuspension simultaneous was however observed. The RMSE for 
the simulated and grab samples were decreased with 8%, when comparing with the initial 
calibrations. It must be noted that several rain events should be calibrated, before being able to 
conclude on the performance of these features in WEST.  
Throughout the calibration, the lumped conceptual model could not capture the pump activity 
occurring in the system during rain events. This did not seem to create significant problems in 
the water quality modelling, which was especially due to a fact, that a slightly wrong magnitude 
of flow could be corrected with the runoff to wastewater concentration ratio.  
The fact that the conceptual model could not capture the pump activity is however reflected in 
the underestimating of loads for all the events including pump activity. If the model were to be 
used in the estimation of loads during a full rain event, it is crucial to incorporate pumps in the 
model. It was however seen that for the three campaigns not including pump activity (M5, M7 
and RF2), the simulated load was in accordance with the measured.  
To summarize, the performance of the model was satisfactory for flow in the further water 
quality modelling and could simulate the ammonia concentrations. For TSS, the lumped 
conceptual model in WEST could not simulate the high peaks, but only the base concentrations 
of TSS. Moreover, the model could not capture the pollutant loads during rain events, due to 
the inability to simulate the pump activity in the system. 
9.3 UNCERTAINTY AND APPLICATION  
To discuss the reliability of the model, the uncertainty and possible applications are discussed. 
The uncertainty in the model is not only based on the use of a lumped conceptual modelling, 
but also the measured data applied in the calibration.  
With the use of sensor data, it is known that the uncertainty in the pollutant measuring are 
approximately 5%. Furthermore, the calibrations of the sensors might improve the measuring, 
but can also have the opposite effect. The samples used for the correction of the sensor have a 
uncertainty of 20% for TSS and 10% for ammonia (Sharma et al., 2014). Together with the fact 
that the measurements are performed in a very unclean environment that sewage water is, the 
measurements are related to some uncertainties. 
For a conceptual model, the model only gets as good as the calibrated data, and the uncertainty 
related to the data must be taken into consideration when using the model. Calibrating on 
unreliable data makes the risk of modelling the error in the data higher. This is especially true 
for TSS where there are many instantaneously high peak concentrations in the dataset. In TSS 
data, it would be beneficial to perform a data cleaning before using the data. In particular, 
analyzing for the noise in the data would make it possible to achieve more reliable calibrations 
of the first flush.  
==PAGE:84==
75 
 
It was considerer to correct the ammonia for the wrong corrections of the sensor. However, it is 
not known what the correct ammonia concentrations are and therefore a possible correction of 
data was estimated to be related with too high uncertainty.  
For a real life application of the model, the stationary model would provide the easiest use, by 
only include one parameter set. In this calibration of the model, there were found 12 unique 
parameter sets for each period calibrated. To account for this, a range for the parameters were 
given. It is therefore seen in the validation, that if the uncertainty is based on the high and low 
interval for the model, an uncertainty of the ammonia concentration of about 10-30 mg/l is 
observed. For TSS the uncertainty is observed in the range of 50-200 mg/l. If these uncertainties 
can be accepted or not, must be based on a risk analysis of the problem the model should be 
applied to.

10 CONCLUSION 
The aim of this project was to setup a lumped conceptual model in WEST, and examine if a 
reliable water quality model could be made. With the extensive dataset obtained in the AMOK 
project, the data could be applied in the setup and calibration of the model. In this study, it was 
seen that for flow, ammonia and TSS, it was possible to detect trends in the data. From this 
dataset, the yearly infiltration pattern and diurnal variation for flow was found, together with 
the pollution patterns for ammonia and TSS. The pollution patterns are not similar, and it is 
therefore suggested to make it possible in WEST to give different pollution patterns for each 
pollutant.  
The data from the AMOK project were evaluated. In the evaluation of the measuring campaigns, 
it was seen that for five out of the seven campaigns, were able to validate the sensor 
measurements to some degree. For ammonia, it was seen that measured concentrations were 
highly fluctuation and a load analysis of ammonia showed that this is most likely is due to wrong 
corrections of the sensor. 
Based on the catchment area of the case study in Viby, Aarhus a simple model was constructed 
in WEST. The model contained two catchments, and a tank, and was therefore a highly 
conceptualization of the area. This model was sufficient to simulate flow accurate enough for 
water quality modelling. The model was compared to simulations performed by a hydrodynamic 
model, and no clear improvements were seen with the use of a hydrodynamic model. 
With the use of the diurnal variations and infiltration pattern found in the data analysis, the 
lumped model was capable of simulation the flow ammonia and TSS concentrations in dry 
weather conditions. The model was able to simulate the ammonia concentration during rain 
events, but the validation showed that while the model has a high precision, the accuracy is low, 
due to the changing ammonia concentrations. It can be stated, that with the use of a simple 
model, based on the dilution, it is possible to simulate ammonia concentrations. 
For TSS, the dilution of TSS could be simulated. However, the high peaks of TSS could not be 
captured. WEST has two tools to describe the first flush of TSS, accumulation on the surface and 
washoff, and deposition and resuspension in the pipes. None of these features simulated the 
rapid peaks, and therefore it was not possible to simulate first flush. Furthermore, the method 
of calibration was also tested. Despite of only calibrating the model to the high TSS 
concentrations, still no simulation of rapid peaks of TSS were accomplished.  
Since there are no measurements for the overflow volume, it was not possible to find any CSO 
loads. However, it is believed that if this volume were included, the loads could be calculated 
correctly based on the simulated water quality. To summarize, the following can be concluded 
from this study.    
 
The extensive dataset made it possible to conduct a data analysis and accurate simulate 
DWF conditions  
 
The lumped conceptual model in WEST can simulate ammonia concentrations 
 
The WEST model can simulate the base concentrations of TSS, but not the high peaks.

11 FURTHER OUTLOOK 
The further work described in this chapter will be presented in four sections. First, further work 
within the measuring of sewage water quality is presented, secondly recommendations for the 
model setup are listed, thirdly further calibrations are proposed and lastly improvements of the 
software WEST are suggested 
With the AMOK project, an extensive dataset of flow and pollutant concentrations were 
obtained. However, the uncertainty of the measured concentrations are high. Further work, 
should therefore be put into decreasing this uncertainty, through frequent and regular 
maintenance and correction of the sensors. To avoid wrong corrections of the sensors, more 
than one grab sample should be taken, before performing the correction. Alternatively, 
standard solutions should be applied in the correction of sensors instead.  
More validation of the measured pollutant concentrations are needed and it is therefore 
recommended to have more frequent grab sampling. This sampling, will also make it possible to 
find the correct level of ammonia concentration, and thereby correct the already measured 
ammonia data, and greatly strengthening the ammonia dataset in AMOK. Lastly, a statistically 
cleaning of the data will reduce the noise in the TSS data and improve the further use of the 
data. Moreover, an additional flow measurements at the CSO structure, would have improved 
the CSO dataset from AMOK. The recommendations for future measurement improvements are 
listed below. 
 
Measurement improvements 
o Take more than one sample, when correcting the sensor 
o Be consistent in the maintenance and correcting of the sensor 
o Take more grab samples to validate the sensor measurements 
o Statistically cleaning of the data 
o Include a flow sensor the same place as the water quality sensors 
Further work could be put into the improvement of the model setup of the Viby catchment in 
WEST. Throughout the calibration, the pump activity have caused issue with the automatic 
calibration, therefore including pumps would be an improvement. This would make the 
hydraulic performance better, and the model would be able to simulate the correct pollutant 
load. Furthermore, different levels of conceptualization could be examined. Less 
conceptualization of the catchment area would allow for the possibility of using several rain 
series in the model. With the rain fall being one of the greatest uncertainties in the runoff 
generation (Schellart et al., 2012), this would improve the model performance.  
 
Model setup 
o Include the pumping system in the catchment  
o Make a less conceptualized model of Viby catchment 
o Divide the model into more catchments and allow for radar rain data input 
More calibrations could be performed on the model. Here, it is especially recommended that 
additional calibrations should be targeted specifically to the aim of the model. If the aim is 
strictly water quality, then simultaneous calibration of the flow and the pollutants could be 
performed to achieve the lowest RMSE from the measured pollutant concentrations. If 
simulating the loads correctly is the aim, the calibration should be performed against the 
measured loads, and not the concentrations.  
==PAGE:87==
78 
 
If the sole aim of the model is to model CSOs, further calibrations of high intensity rainfalls that 
evoke CSOs are needed. Moreover, it is recommended that more calibrations of the first flush 
are performed. 
 
Calibrations  
o Simultaneous calibration of flow and water quality 
o Chose calibration targets, based on the aim of the model 
o Calibrate more rain events with surface accumulation and washoff, and 
deposition and resuspension, to test the tools better. 
The following recommendations for alterations and extensions for WEST are suggested. Firstly, a 
more simple runoff generation model is recommended. The deposition and resuspension are 
described by three equations. An easy expansion of the deposition and resuspension model 
would be to incorporate tanks in series effect into this model. The user can thereby decide the 
number of tanks, and the degree of plug flow concentrations wanted to be simulated. Lastly, it 
is recommended that the time issues in WEST are fixed, so that dates can be given by the user 
and used correct in the code, and make the software allow for longer simulations than 360 days.  
 
WEST extensions 
o Make a more simple runoff generation, that only includes the pervious fraction, 
reservoir constant and initial loss 
o Change the equations for the deposition and resuspension and accumulation 
and washoff to equations that allow for the rapid TSS peaks.  
o Fix the date issues and allow for longer simulations 
In the four bullet list, the recommendations for the further work within the field of water quality 
modelling have been presented.  With the data improvements, more reliable calibrations of the 
model can be performed. With the suggested extension of the model setup and more target 
specific calibrations, it is believed a more accurate model can be produced. Lastly, a few 
upgrades of the software, WEST, could improve the developing of water quality modelling in the 
future.
