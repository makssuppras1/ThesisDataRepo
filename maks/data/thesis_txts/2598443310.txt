Abstract
Motiavation: In the clinical world a fully automated and data driven evaluation of polysomnog-
raphy (PSG) data for diagnostics of sleep apnea is needed. It is estimated that 14% of males and
5% females worldwide suffer from obstructive sleep apnea-hypopnea syndrome (OSAHS), and
the golden standard for diagnosis of the syndrome require that a trained sleep technician anno-
tate a whole night’s sleep of a patient recorded on a PSG. The diagnosis of OSAHS is derived
from the annotations, but it has been shown that in some aspects of sleep scoring sleep tech-
nicians annotate events differently. Many different methods for prediction of events related to
OSAHS have been developed, and most are supervised machine learning schemes that rely on
the annotations of sleep technicians.
Aim: The aim of this project is to develop an algorithm that automatically finds breathing pat-
terns related to OSAHS, and the approach is to find these patterns independently of annotations.
Data: The Sleep Heart Health Study (SHHS) database was used to develop the algorithm while
three recordings were picked out to assess the results of the algorithm.
Methods: A segmentation algorithm has been implemented based on the absolute derivative
of the squared nasal airflow signal’s envelope. A piece-wise linear delay algorithm was also
implemented to accommodate for the non-linear physiological delay in the oxygen saturation
signal. An algorithm was implemented to find a ’normal breathing’ section of the nasal airflow
signal in order to extract features in relation to this section. Features were then extracted from
the nasal airflow signal, the thoracic belt (TB) signal, the abdominal belt (AB) signal and the
oxygen saturation signal. From these features three clusters were found using hierarchical clus-
tering with Ward’s linkage. A principal component analysis was made to visualize separation
of clusters.
Results:
The three recordings were assessed through visual inspection of the nasal airflow
signal and oxygen saturation signal, and this showed that the segmentation algorithm is able
to segment events of physiological relevance. The hierarchical clustering on features from all
segments had mixed results due to high reliance on one feature, but was able to generate clusters
interpreted as physiologically meaningful.
Conclusion: This project serves as a proof of concept, that it is possible to find the physiological
patterns related to sleep apnea through an all data driven and automatic solution, but more work
needs to be done for it to be applicable in a clinical setting.

Preface
This thesis is a product of the Department of Health Technology at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU). All research presented in this thesis has been accomplished at the Department
of Psychiatry and Behavioral Sciences, Division of Sleep Medicine at Stanford University, in
collaboration with DTU and Rigshospitalet Glostrup. The research conveyed here, as well as the
thesis itself, has been prepared in fulfilment of the requirements for acquiring a degree of M.Sc.
in Biomedical Engineering at DTU. The complete work accounts for 30 ECTS points and has
been conducted within the period February 1st 2020 until August 12th 2020. The project deals
with advanced signal processing and machine learning to meet the objectives of the research.
The reader is expected to have a similar background as this author in order to fully appreciate
and apprehend the content, as it has been an ambition to keep the thesis as short and concise as
possible and only provide the most necessary background theory. There has been a great em-
phasis on making all research presented here reproducible. Consequently, the methods applied
in the project are described in great detail, while all code implemented in Python 3.7 is included
in the appendix. These measures should allow for a quick and smooth way of continuing this
work or applying it, should another researcher desire to do so.

Introduction
This chapter provides an introduction to the project in form of a problem statement followed by
the aims of the project.
1.1
Problem Statement
Sleep disordered breathing (SDB) is a general term for multiple disorders that are defined by
abnormalities in breathing during sleep. The consequences for people diagnosed with SDB are
many, but among the most severe are an increased risk of cardivascular disease and diabetes and
an increased risk of automobile accidents due to daytime sleepiness or fatigue [1, 2]. This project
will only focus on obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). It is estimated that 14% of males and 5% of
females world wide suffer from OSA [2]. The focus of this project is specifically on two event
types in OSA namely apneas and hypopneas. OSA is a disorder under SDB characterized by
partial or complete cessation of breathing for longer periods of time during sleep. The cessation
is caused by a partial or complete collapse of the upper airways. Diagnosis of sleep apnea is
done via a sleep study that involves questionaries about sleep habits and medical conditions
and a polysomnography (PSG) that records physiological data over a whole night’s sleep [2].
The current gold standard for diagnosis of OSA is the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI) which is
the amount of OSA related events per hour of sleep. The AHI is found after a certified sleep
technician has scored the sleep recording of a whole night according to the rules specified by the
American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM). This project has a novel approach as the aim is
to develop a fully automated data driven algorithm that independently of manually scored events
finds patterns of breathing related to OSA. The incentive for this project is faster diagnosis of
sleep apnea as it requires many hours of work by trained sleep technicians to score PSGs from
patients. In order to save time and eliminate human bias a data driven pattern recognition of
breathing data could be the beginning of a data driven diagnostic scoring model for sleep apnea.
The hope for this project is to show that it is possible for a completely data driven approach to
find the same patterns that sleep technicians use for scoring. The scope of this project is to find
the physiological patterns of hypopneas and obstructive apneas. Central apneas are not being
analyzed. The scoring done by human technicians poses a problem with consistency [3]. This is
what makes the project relevant, since the aim is to see if the computer automatically and without
human intervention can find the physiological breathing patterns that are associated with sleep
apnea.
1.2
Project Aims
The primary project aim is to find the physiological patterns of hypopneas and obstructive apneas
with an automatic pattern recognition algortihm that segments and clusters PSG recordings for
visual inspection. The long term aim is to provide a consistent data driven clinical diagnostic
tool to increase efficiency and reliability of diagnosis of OSA. To accomplish the primary aim
a set of sub aims are defined and stated below:
• Development of an understanding of the underlying physiological mechanisms of OSA,
and how these mechanism are expressed through PSG recordings.
1
==PAGE:13==
• Development of an automatic segmentation algorithm that can segment a whole PSG
recording into physiological meaningful segments related to OSA.
• Development of a unsupervised clustering algorithm that uses features, extracted from the
segmentation algorithm, that can describe the mechanisms of OSA.
• Development of a visualization algorithm to view patterns found via the unsupervised
clustering algorithm.
• To visually evaluate the patterns found by the unsupervised clustering algorithm and com-
pare them to the annotations of physiological events related to OSA.

Clinical Background
This chapter provides information on the clinical background for the project. A brief introduc-
tion to OSA will be made, followed by the physiology behind OSA as well the type of data being
recorded and how technicians and doctors work with this data.
2.1
Sleep Apnea
In this section the clinical background for sleep apnea will be elaborated upon.
2.1.1
Breathing Physiology
Breathing or respiration is essential to sustain life for humans as it ensure gas exchange of O2 and
CO2 in the alveoli in the lungs. Respiration has two phases namely inspiration and expiration.
During inspiration the lungs expand causing a lower pressure in the lungs due to contraction of
the diaphragm. While air flows into the lungs O2 diffuse over the capillary wall in the alveoli in
exchange of CO2. During expiration the diaphragm relax increasing pressure in the lungs and
the gas containing the washed out CO2 is pushed out of the lungs.
2.1.2
Obstructive Sleep Apnea
Obstructive sleep apnea can be a debilitating disorder, and if untreated patients with the disorder
are at higher risk of developing heart disease and being in accidents due to daytime drowsiness
[1, 2].
Obstructive sleep apnea–hypopnea syndrome (OSAHS) is a sleep disordered breathing disorder
where patients experience partial or complete cessation of breathing while sleeping. The dis-
order is defined as a cessation of airflow for more than 10 seconds with an arousal needed to
continue breathing[4, 5]. Arousals are specific brain wave events found in different frequency
bands recorded by an electroencephalograpram (EEG) [6]. OSA events are divided into three
subcategories namely obstructive apnea, hypopnea and central apnea and all events are mostly
associated with a desaturation of the blood or an arousal [1, 6, 7].
Detecting Sleep Apnea
Studies and diagnosis of sleep apnea comes after a sleep study has been performed on a patient.
The study involves recording a nights’ sleep using a PSG and the golden standard is a scoring
of OSA related events by a sleep technician. A PSG is a system that records multiple modali-
ties during the night when the patient sleeps and the scoring is based on the guidelines of the
American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM). The final score is summed up in the Apnea/Hy-
popnea Index (AHI) of the patient, which is the amount of breathing related events per hour of
sleep. The AHI is an indication of the severity of sleep apnea and is used to diagnose patients
and determine course of treatment [1, 2].
Polysomnography
A polysomnograph is used to acquire the sleep data. A PSG involves an oronasal thermal airflow
sensor, a nasal pressure transducer, abdominal- and thoracic belts that collect data on respiratory
effort, a pulse oxymetry to collect data on the arterial blood oxygenation, EEG to measure brain
waves and electrocardiogram (ECG) to messure heartrate. Figure 2.1 shows a patient with the
different recording modalities of the PSG.

Nasal Airflow
The nasal airflow is a measure of the respiration of a patient. Recording nasal airflow can be
done by a nasal cannula connected to a pressure transducer that detect and record the differences
in pressure during inspiration and expiration. Another way of measuring nasal airflow is via a
thermistor that record the temperature change during inspirations and expirations and converts
it into a measure of airflow. The inspiration air is colder than the heated expiration air. Nasal
airflow is an important modality since it is a measure of the amount of air going in and out of
the lungs and is therefore an important modality to include in SDB. Sleep apneas are detected
via the nasal airflow as a drop in amplitude in the airflow signal over a period of time.
Abdominal and Thoracic Belts
The abdominal belts and thoracic belts measure respiratory effort [2]. The measurement relates
to the movement of the diaphragm and/or the expansion of the chest during breathing. The
measurement is also referred to as respiratory inductance plethysmoraphy [2]. The belts work
by introducing an alternating current into a coiled wire sewn into the elastic fabric of the belts.
The current genereates a magnetic field that changes when the patient breathes. These changes
are recorded and translated into the belt signals. The belts are used in conjuction with the nasal
airflow to specifically distuingish between central apneas and obstructive apneas [4]. During
central apneas there is no respiratory effort detected in the belts as the cessation of breathing is
related to the failure of the neurons in the brain to contract the diaphragm. During obstructive
apneas respiratory effort is detected as the cessation of breathing is due to a blockage of the
upper airways [4].
Oxygen Saturation
The oxygen saturation is a measure of the amount of oxygen bound to haemoglobin in the blood
stream. The modality is recorded by a finger-tip pulse oxymetry that measures ratio of absorbed
light at wavelengths 650nm and 950nm. Oxygenated haemoglobin absorbs less red light at
4
==PAGE:16==
650nm and more light at 950nm than deoxygenated haemoglobin. The ratio of absorbance is
measured by the transmission of light through the finger tip. The pulse oxymetry is used in
conjuction with the airlfow and belt signals to detect sleep apneas since a decrease in oxygen
saturation (desaturation) is seen after an apnea events [4]. Figure 2.2 shows an example of how
an nasal airflow and oxygen saturation may look during and after an obstructive sleep apnea
event.
Figure 2.2: Example of obstructive event from recording 200001 in the SHHS database [8,

how the onset of the desaturation is delayed by approximately 15 seconds from the onset of the

2.1.3
Scoring of Sleep Apnea
Scoring sleep apnea is done after a PSG has been recorded. The scoring is done by a sleep
technician who goes through the recording while reviewing the different channels and scoring
events according to the AASM. The definitions of the different sleep disoredered breathing
events are listed below:
• Obstructive Apnea
Obstructive sleep apnea is a near-complete or complete involuntary cessation of breathing
during sleep due to a collapse or blockage of the pharyngeal airways for at least 10 seconds.
A decrease in oxygen saturation is also detectable using oxymetry [7]. The definition by
the AASM manual of an apnea is satisfied, if both of the following criteria are met:
– a) ”there is a drop in the peak signal excursion by ≥90% of pre-event baseline using
an oronasal thermal sensor (diagnostic study).”
– b) The duration of the ≥90% drop in sensor signal is ≥10 seconds.”[4, 5]
5
==PAGE:17==
• Hypopnea
Hypopnea is a form of obstructive apnea where airflow is restricted but not completely
obstructed in the pharyngeal airways for at least 10 seconds with a decrease in oxygen
saturation. The definition by the AASM manual of an hypopnea is satisfied, if all of the
following criteria are met:
– a) ”The peak signal excursion drops by ≥30% of pre-event baseline using nasal pres-
sure (diagnostic study), PAP device flow (titration study), or an alternative hypopnea
sensor (diagnostic study).
– b) The duration of the ≥30% drop in signal excursion is ≥10 seconds.
– c) There is a ≥3% oxygen desaturation from pre-event baseline or the event is asso-
ciated with an arousal.”[4, 5]
• Central Apnea
Central apnea is a more rare form of sleep apnea where the neurons that control the breath-
ing malfunction during sleep causing cessation of breathing [7].

3.4
Segmentation
The segmentation is based on changes in the squared nasal airflow signal’s envelope of the
processed. Squaring is done to gain information of both the upper and lower envelope of the
signal, and to emphasize slower changes in breathing amplitude. By finding the envelope of
the squared signal by first finding the peaks of the signals both the lower and upper envelope is
considered since half of all the peaks are from the upper envelope and the other half from the
lower envelope. The envelope method has drawn inspiration from [12]. First the pre-proccesed
nasal airflow signal is squared, then the upper envelope of the squared signal is extracted by
finding the local maxima and interpolating those maxima with cubic spline interpolation. The
envelope is then smoothed twice with the Savitzky-Golay (SG) filter. The squaring is also done
to emphasize large breaths and minimize small breaths. This is done since OSA events are
related to changes in the amplitude of the nasal airflow signal. The envelope is used because
it represent the slower changes in the amplitude of the signal and the segmentation is supposed
to find segments that correspond to at least 2 or breaths which is ∼8 seconds at a breathing
frequency of 0.25Hz. An overview of the segmentation algorithm can be seen in figure 3.15.

3.4.2
Spline Interpolation
Spline interpolation is an interpolation method that connects intervals with polynomials via the
end points of the intervals. Each end point is referred to as a knot ((xi, yi), i = 0, 1, ..., n. Each
consecutive set of knots (xi, yi) and (xi+1, yi+1) can be interpolated using a polynomial which
ultimately yields n intervals between n + 1 knots. A special case of the spline interpolation is
the cubic spline interpolation (CSI). The cubic spline interpolation is a connection of 3rd order
(cubic) splines and the final interpolation has the conditions that, the resulting conjunction of
splines is continuous, differentiable and it’s first and second order derivatives are continuous.
Each spline require 3 knots to be created since it needs to fit a polynomial of 3rd order. The
splines are thus given by:
Si(x) = ai(x −xi)3 + bi(x −xi)2 + ci(x −xi)1 + di
(3.10)
A condition that is defined for cubic spline interpolation is that the splines must run through the
knots:
Si(xi) = yi, Si+1(xi+1) = yi+1
(3.11)
An criteria for the final interpolation is that the slopes (derivatives) from each interval connecting
at each knot must be equal which ensures continuity:

3.4.3
Savitzky Golay Filter
The Savitzky Golay (SG) Filter is a polynomial smoothing filter. It functions as a low-pass filter
which can be tweaked with two parameters namely the degree of the polynomial and the window
length. Increasing the order of the polynomial of the filter will result in an increase in the cut-off
frequency with. Increasing the window length will results in a decrease in the cut-off frequency.
The filter functions by taking a specified number of points equal to the set window length and
fitting a polynomial of the chosen points and then moving the window forward.
Figure 3.19: The Savitzky-Golay filters’ impulse response at a fixed window length (M=16)
and different polynomial orders (N)[13]

The Savitzky-Golay Filter uses the fact that a sliding window approach can be interpreted as a
linear time invariate (LTI) system that facilitates frequency interpretation. The linearity refers
to the fact that applying a filter to the sum of two scaled signals is equal to applying the filter to
the scaled signals individually and taking the sum:
τ{αx1[n] + βx2[n]} = τ{αx1[n]} + τ{βx2[n]}
(3.14)
Where τ is the system/filter and α and β are the scalings of the signals x1 and x2. The time
invariance means that a delay in the input signal will delay the output signal with the same
amount of delay:
y[n −N] = τ{x[n −N]}[n]
(3.15)
Where y[n −N] is the output y delayed by N and x[n −N] is the input x delayed by N. The
SG filter works by convolving the input signal with the SG impulse response. The properties
of convolution is important here, since convolving a signal in the time domain corresponds to a
point-wise multiplication in the frequency domain:
F{x ∗h}, time domain −→F{x}F{h}, frequency domain

an apnea/hypopnea even is at least 10 seconds long. 8 seconds are chosen to avoid removing
events that are close to 10 seconds in length. This results in the peaks are at least 40 samples
apart corresponding to 4 seconds. Once the array of peak indexes are created the start and stop
indexes of the wake/movement segments are added. If a peak is located at the same index as the
beginning or end of a wake/movement segment, the index is moved one sample back, if at the
beginning that segment, or one sample forward if at the end of that segment. This way the wake/-
movement segments can easily be skipped later without loosing information of the segmentation.
Once this process is done the array of indexes is sorted and split into start-of-segment indexes
and stop-of-segment indexes.

3.5
Oxygen Saturation Delay
When scoring sleep apnea the oxygen saturation is an important feature. Often a apnea/hypopnea
events are associated with a decrease in oxygen saturation, but the decrease is delayed because
the measurement sensor is located at the fingertip. Furthermore the delay may have non linear
properties which makes it difficult to have constant delay throughout the recording.
3.5.1
Oxygenation of the Blood
When air enters the lungs a gas exchange occur in the alveoli of the lungs. The exchange is
bringing O2 into the blood stream via the capillaries in the alveoli and washing out the metabolic
byproduct CO2. The oxygenated haemoglobin in the blood as well as some unbound O2 is
transported from the lungs to the heart which pumps it out to the rest of the body via aorta.
During normal breathing and if the metabolic demands remain constant the oxygen saturation
is stable. If a cessation of breathing happens for a longer period of time the gas exchange in
the alveoli is limited but the demand for oxygen remain the same, which leads to a decrease in
oxygen saturation. Due to the placement of the pulse oxymetry device (on the finger), a long
delay of varying length is expected. The delay varies across patients and may even vary during
the same recording [14].
3.5.2
Oxygen Saturation Signal
The oxygen saturation signal is a measure the oxygenation of the blood. The signal sample rate
is 1 and in OSA the signal is used to determine desaturations related to cessations in breathing.
The desaturations are often delayed, and the delay from when breathing stops to a detectable
decrease in oxygen saturation is expected to be between 8 and 55 seconds and may be a non-
linear relationship [14].. The non linear delay may cause problems if a linear delay is chosen.
After inspection of many recordings and to constrain the algorithm the delay was assumed to be
a piece-wise linear delay.
A piece-wise linear delay is implemented in order to accommodate for the varying delays. The
implementation only tries to time align the most prominent desaturations if alignment is necce-
sary. The choice relied on a trial-and-error approach with different constant delays and the
findings were that the highest changes in oxygen saturation seemed to be the ones most vul-
nerable to variations in saturation delays. This may be explained by patients with the biggest
variation may have preexisting damage of the cardiopulmonary system [15].
3.5.3
The Heart Rate, OSA Events and the Oxygen Saturation
In order to find the delay between OSA related events and the oxygen saturation it was found that
the heart rate is often times affected by arousals that terminate apneac events [15–17]. Arousals
increase activity in the sympathetic nervous system which in turn elevates the heart rate [17].
The time difference between the peak elevation in heart rate and the peak resaturation of the
blood was visually inspected to be highly correlated with the time difference between the start
of an OSA event found in the nasal airflow signal and the start of a desaturation in the oxygen
saturation. Therefore the difference in peak heart rate and peak oxygen saturation was chosen
as a measure of oxygen saturation delay. Furthermore, the choice of using the peak heart rate
was also based on the signals sampling rate. The heart rate signal is also sampled at 1Hz which
makes it easier to shift the peaks in the signal by one second and check for overlap of the peaks
in the oxygen saturation signal that has the same sampling rate.

3.5.4
Oxygen Delay Algorithm - Piece-wise Linear Delay
The oxygen saturation delay was found to be a non-linear delay throughout some recording.
In order to accomodate this, an algortihm is developed based on a piece-wise linear approach
where the signal is divided into ten segments that each get their individual oxygen saturation
delay. After prepocessing the oxygen saturation signal and heart rate signal, local maxima in
the oxygen saturation is found as well as the local maxima in the heart rate signal. The two
signals are then divided 10 long segments of the signal weighted according to the amount of
peaks found. This meant that the sections with few peaks are longer than sections with many
peaks. This ensure that each segment contain approximately the same amount of peaks relative
to the total amount of peaks in found in each signal. The peak indexes of the HR signal are
shifted between 8 and 55 seconds, by one second at a time. At each time step the overlap with
the peaks indexes of the oxygen saturation is saved. The time shift of where the maximum
amount of overlap occurs is then selected as the oxygen saturation delay for each 10th of the
signal. If no overlap is found then the time delay is set to a default of 15 seconds. This process
is repeated for each 10th of the signal. Thus 10 delays are stored for a recording. The top two
plots in figures 3.27 and 3.28 are the oxygen saturation signal with local peaks (top) and the
heart rate signal with local peaks (2nd from the top) respectively.

Feature Extraction
Extracting good features is important for a well performing algorithm and 4 feature calculations
were chosen and used on 4 channels yielding 6 different features in total. Some of the features
required a segment of ’normal breathing’ as a baseline, and an algorithm was set up to find a
segment of ’normal breathing’.
3.6.1
Normal Segment for Reference
In order to have a baseline to compare features with an algorithm that locates a stable segment
was developed.
In each recording there are variations in the amplitude of the airflow signal which can be due
to sensors shifting around while the patient sleeps. The amplitude variations are leveled by
standardizing the signal in segments as explained above in the standardization section. In order
to compare features that involve the amplitude of the signal a definition of a normal segment
is specified. Through evaluation of multiple recordings a few criteria of what normal breathing
must meet are set up. The criteria are: (1) a 4 minute long segment, (2) low STD of the upper
envelope of the segment, (3) small changes in the upper envelope, (4) small changes in the lower
envelope, (5) relatively high amplitude breaths corresponding to deep breaths, (6) low STD of
oxygen saturation in the segment.
A measure of the dispersion in the upper envelope is the standard deviation (STD) of the up-
per envelope. A measure of how much the envelopes are changing is the mean of the absolute
derivative of the upper- and lower envelope. A value close to 0 indicates the envelopes are rela-
tively constant while higher values correspond to more changes in the envelopes. It is assumed
that the amplitude of normal breathing is relatively high (deep breaths) and therefore the mean
value of the upper envelope is used. Since the method attempts to minimize the number being

3.7
Clustering
In this project 6 features are used and clustering is performed using hierarchical clustering using
the Ward variance minimization algorithm to generate the clusters. Clustering is used in machine
learning to visualize patterns and group data that resemble each other in regards to resemblance
criteria which is often distance.
3.7.1
Machine Learning
Machine learning is a computer driven learning approach. The field of machine learning can
roughly be divided into three fields, supervised learning, unsupervised learning and semi-supervised
learning. Supervised learning focus on making the computer able to recognize specific or al-
ready detected patterns by training a model on labeled data sets. The major difference between
supervised and unsupervised machine learning is that supervised machine learning depends on
labeled data to work, while unsupervised does not. Unsupervised machine learning is pattern
recognition on unlabeled data and its application is often times used to find undetected patterns
without human intervention. Within unsupervised learning two widely used methods are prin-
cipal components (variance analysis) and cluster analysis. In unsupervised learning the results
often times needs to be interpreted by means of visualization or statistical measures. This project
has an unsupervised clustering approach.
3.7.2
Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering
Agglomerative Hierarchical clustering is an unsupervised clustering method. The method is a
bottom-up approach where each data point is initially a singleton cluster Ci, i ∈[0, 1, ..., k] and
k being the amount of data points. At each iteration i the number of clusters are k −i. Initially
the two singleton clusters/data points are merged to a cluster and this process is repeated until all
clusters are merged into one. A vizualisation of this process is the dendrogram shown in figure
3.32.
42
==PAGE:54==
Figure 3.32: Dendrogram created from features extracted from segments from recording

There are different ways to create the clusters which depend on the choice of distance measure.
The most common distance measures are single linkage, complete linkage, average linkage and
Ward’s linkage.
Single Linkage works by finding the minimum distance between any two points in a cluster.
Complete Linkage works by finding the maximum distance between any two points in a cluster.
Average Linkage works by finding the average distance between all points in a cluster to each
other point in another cluster.
Ward’s Linkage works by finding the sum of squared differences in all clusters.
The distance measure used in this project is the Ward’s distance measure.

Principal Component Analysis
Principal component analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction method within machine learn-
ing. The method allow visualization of high dimensional data by projecting the data onto an
eigenvector space of 1, 2 or 3 dimensions. The principal components (PCs) are the projections
of the high dimensional data onto a lower dimension vector space. They explain the variance in
descending order, meaning the first component account for most of the variance in data and the

5
Discussion
In this chapter the results will be discussed. It is structured such that each recording will be
discussed in the following order; discussion of the segmentation algorithm, discussion of the
clustering, and a discussion of the comparison between cluster and annotated events. Each
major section will have a summary, and will be followed by a more general summary that ends
this chapter.
5.1
Segmentation of Events
The goal of the segmentation algorithm is to segment a whole PSG recording into physiological
relevant segments related to sleep disordered breathing, more specifically sleep apnea. The
choice of disregarding all wake segments was therefore done because the wake segments do
not contribute to anything related to sleep. But it became evident that some recordings have so
many wake segments that it renders the recording useless when the segments are disregarded. In
hindsight it would have been appropriate to put in a heuristic in the algorithm that states that if
the wake segments are more than a certain fraction of the recording the recording is disregarded.
The choice in this project was to segment via the nasal airflow signal. This was partly due to the
sampling frequency being 10 times higher than that of the oxygen saturation signal and partly
because obstructive apneas and hypopneas are directly related to breathing. In order to make a
solid foundation for further analysis it was chosen to keep the segmentation relatively simple and
only reliant on one signal. The thought process was also that the most common way to recognize
sleep apnea in the data is through the change in nasal airflow which makes the segmentation of
the nasal airflow signal the best signal to work with. Furthermore, using mainly the envelope
was based upon visual inspection on how to best interpret changes in the airflow signal that
was longer or at least 8 seconds. As verification if was found that the envelope approach had
already been utilized to detect hypopneas and apneas with great results which confirm that the
method is viable [12]. The segmentation algorithm could also have been made entirely from the
oxygen saturation channel as the changes in this channel often also relates to apneas [2]. But the
oxygen saturation channel was discarded as a candidate for the segmentation algorithm based
on the sampling frequency of the signal being 10 times less that the sampling frequency of the
nasal airflow signal. The reasoning was also that the delay in oxygen desaturations was from
cessation of breathing and the segmentation should be based on what comes first. The problem
of solving the non-linear delay would also still be present if the the oxygen saturation signal was
used to segment the signal.
5.1.1
Segmentation Algorithm Performance
It is difficult to quantify whether the segmentation algorithm performs well without considering
the clustering as well, but visual inspection of the segmentation of recording 200032, 200022,
200015 shows that it is possible to segment the signal in a physiological meaningful way which
will be elaborated upon below.

amplitude) with recovery breathing (high nasal airflow amplitude). The segmentation of the
nasal airflow signal has split the signal into segments where the boundaries are places where the

5.1.2
Summary of Segmentation Algorithm Performance
The segmentation of the nasal airflow signal is the foundation for further study in this project.
The segmentation works well by separating segments of what resemble physiological relevance.
There are areas where the algorithm may create many small segments instead of one big which
need to be addressed to make the segmentation more robust. The oxygen delay algorithm some-
times have difficulties finding the correct delay. The delay algorithm is not optimal, as it some-
times finds a delay that is longer than the actual delay. But in other instances it does meet the
expectations of finding the correct delay and thus the physiological relevant segments related
to events in the nasal airflow signal. As is shown in recording 200032 and 200022 it is able to
capture the correct delay.
5.2
Clustering
The method of hierarchical clustering has already been used in studies of sleep apnea, but the
approach in has been to cluster patient types from PSG data by extracting information about
event types using annotated events and using patient demographics as features as well[18]. This
project differentiates by using features extracted directly from the PSG data without using anno-
tations and finding breathing patterns rather than patient types.
The Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was chosen because it is an unsupervised clustering
method that can be used for visual purposes, as it enables visualization of high dimensional data.
108
==PAGE:120==
The method of hierarchical clustering was chosen because it is also an unsupervised clustering
method. One exception to the unsupervised approach was the choice of forcing three clusters to
be generated. This was done to get comparable clusterings across recordings and in the hopes
that the clusters would be able to distinguish obstructive apneas, hypopneas and normal breath-
ing from each other.
The clustering in this project works well when clustering OSA events that have relatively low
nasal airflow amplitudes and big desaturations associated with them. It seems to generate a mix
of normal breathing and hypopneas in the ’middle’ cyan cluster, but this may be due to poor
feature variance. In order to gain better separation of cluster and better performance overall
two things may need to be improved namely the oxygen saturation delay and better features for
clustering.
The oxygen saturation holds key information about apneas, and is has been shown that it is
possible to find OSA events using only this signal [19]. The oxygen saturation feature is also
by far the most important feature in this project as it accounts for almost all the variance in the
PCA. In regards to the oxygen saturation delay is has been shown that the delay has a non-linear
behavior [15], and the piece-wise linear approach was implemented to accommodate this non-
linearity. The delay algorithm performs decently, but determining the delay by a continuously
updated delay could prove more robust than a piece-wise linear delay.
In order to prevent the feature bias from the SatC feature the features could be standardized to
unit variance. This removes the bias from the SatC feature and could be a possible solution
to weigh the other features equally. Another solution could be to introduce more features with
higher variance. One such feature could be extracted from a wavelet decomposition which is a
popular method that has been used multiple times to detect and classify sleep apnea [16, 20, 21].

5.2.2
Summary of Clustering Assessment
The clustering is based on a agglomerative hierarchical clustering and the linkage criteria is
Ward’s linkage which clusters each segment that vary the least from each other based on the
the features drawn from each segment. Another linkage criteria could have been used but the
Ward’s linkage was deemed best as the visual cluster separation was a PCA which is a variance
analysis. The PCA of each of the recordings showed that most of the variance in all recordings
are accounted for by the oxygen saturation feature SatC. There is little mix between clusters in
the directions of PC1 in all of the clusterings accoring to principal components and the clusters
are not completely separated. The clusterings are biased towards the SatC feature and mainly
shows the differences in this feature. Despite having a high dependency of one feature the
clustering is able to find physiological patterns related to OSA, this is most likely due to the
fact that changes in the oxygen saturation is often directly related to OSA events. In order for
the clustering to become more robust it is necessary to engineer better features to explain the
physiological events. Such features could be extracted from a continuous wavelet transform [20,
21] or by finding the arc length of the envelope in relation to each segment.

5.4
Discussion Summary
The segmentation lays the foundation for all research done in this project and it is therefore of
vital importance to the project that it is robust. To keep it robust and avoid complex problems
like the oxygen saturation delay in the segmentation, the algorithm is reliant only on the nasal
airflow signal itself. An earlier study showed that the envelope approach works well. The
envelope approach there was to predict AHI based on the envelope of the airflow signal [12].
Contrary to the goal in that study this project has a more holistic approach by trying to detect the
patterns in the data from multiple modalities. In general data quality is important as it ensures
good performance of algorithm. As seen with recording 200015, and in the earlier study the
envelope approach is dependant on the quality of data [12]. If the data for some reason is either
noisy or the breathing extremely irregular, it becomes difficult for a program to manage in a data
driven approach.
An important aspect of segmentation is whether the algorithm captures the segments with physio-
logical meaningful events. The scope of this project has been to automatically capture instances
where the envelope of the nasal airflow changes, as the idea has been that these changes are
related to hypopneas and apneas. The segmentation algorithm manages to automatically de-
tect the changes in the envelope of the nasal airflow signal and create segment borders around
physiological events.
The clustering in this project has been reliant mostly on the SatC feature to work. On one hand
the clustering in this project is weak due to this reliance, but on the other hand also emphasize
the importance of including the oxygen saturation when scoring sleep apnea. And it has been
shown that it is possible to predict OSA related events using only the oxygen saturation signal
[19].
It is important to emphasize that every step of the algorithm is automated and extracted directly
from the PSG recordings, with the exception of two modalities, the first being the wake segments
and the second being the amount of clusters generated. The hope was to find 3 clusters that
could be interpreted as three types of breathing patterns in relation to OSA namely hypopneas,
obstructive apneas and no events.
Features that can explain the data are very important if an unsupervised clustering approach is
going to be successful. The approach in this project has been to try and reverse engineer what the
human brain latently recognizes as patterns and create features that explain these patterns. The
features used in this project were found by visually inspecting the data and with inspiration from
[12] and then made quantifiable through mathematical operations. Among the features used, the
oxygen saturation feature (SatC) had much higher variance than all the the other features. This
114
==PAGE:126==
caused the first principal component to be highly driven by SatC in order to account for as
much of the variance as possible in the data. A way to tackle this problem is to standardize the
features by dividing each data point in the feature with the standard deviation of all data points in
the feature giving the feature unit variance. Whether standardization would yield better results
remains to be explored.
The comparison between the algorithm and the manually annotated events showed mixed results.
But the reliability of scored events may contribute to some of the explanation. There is a manual
scoring agreement between different technicians regarding obstructive sleep apnea of 77.1% and
only 65.4% when scoring hypopneas [3]. This reflects positively on the algorithm that found
a high correlation between red cluster and obstructive events in recording 200032. The lower
reliability for hypopneas may be a contributing factor to the hypopneas being harder to discern.
Furthermore the rules used when scoring the events of the SHHS datebase are different from the
rules used today as the scoring originally was based on the nasal aiflow amplitude and only the
oxygen saturation or arousals if there were ambiguities. Today both the nasal airflow amplitude
and the oxygen saturation or arousals have to be considered [5, 8, 9, 11]. The more strict scoring
rules might rule out some of the annotated events in the SHHS database.

6
Conclusion
In this chapter the final conclusion will be presented follow by a few thoughts on future perspec-
tives to conclude this project.
6.1
Final Conlusion
The primary aim of this project was to find the physiological patterns of hypopneas and ob-
structive apneas via an automatic pattern recognition algortihm that segment and cluster PSG
recordings for visual inspection. This aim has partly been accomplished through sub aims which
will be adressed in the follow section.
The first sub aim was to develop an understanding of the underlying physiological mechanisms
of OSA, and how these mechanism are expressed through PSG recordings. This has been de-
scribed in Chapter 2 and also leads to the accomplishment of the second sub aim.
The second sub aim was to develop an automatic segmentation algorithm that can segment a
whole PSG recording into physiological meaningful segments related to OSA. This was accom-
plished by segmenting the nasal airflow of PSG recordings from the SHHS database. Further-
more a piece-wise linaer delay algorithm has been developed as an attempt to accommodate
for the non-linear oxygen saturation delay. The segmentation algorithm managed to generate
segments that are meaningful in relation to breathing patterns in sleep apnea, and the piece-wise
linear delay algorithm manages to find a delay, but not always a physiological meaningful one.
The third sub aim was to develop an unsupervised clustering algorithm that uses features, ex-
tracted from the segmentation algorithm, that can describe the mechanisms of OSA. This was
accomplished by extracting features from each segment made by the segmentation algorithm
and using them in an unsupervised hierarchical clustering scheme to later visualize the clusters
in relation to the PSG recording.
The fourth sub aim was to develop a visualization algorithm to view the patterns found via
the unsupervised clustering algorithm. This was accomplished as the visualization algorithm
developed for this project enables the user to locate areas of interest within a nasal airflow signal,
and to view these areas in relation to the clustering, the nasal airflow signal and the oxygen
saturation signal. The patterns found by the clustering were, despite high reliance on one feature
from the oxygen saturation signal and lack of good separation between clusters, physiological
meaningful and related to OSA breathing patterns.
The fifth sub aim was to visually evaluate the patterns found by the unsupervised clustering
algorithm and compare them to the annotations of physiological events related to OSA. This
was partly comfirmed through a comparison of the clustering to manually scored events on the
three recordings. The comparison was made based on visual comparison but yielded mixed re-
sults. One recording (recording 200032) showed high overlap between segments belonging to
one cluster and annotated obstructive events. The two other recordings (recording 200015 and
200022) showed problems with the oxygen delay, data quality issues and annotation discrepan-
cies.

6.2
Future Perspectives
Testing the algorithm on a newer cohort scored by the updated guidelines from the AASM could
be interesting as the new guidelines require oxygen desaturations to be considered. It is expected
that fewer events are being annotated as the rules of the AASM are more strict than the rules
used for scoring the SHHS database.
In regards to the algorithm itself an improvement of the piece-wise linear oxygen delay to an
algorithm that has continous non-linear oxygen saturation delay would be beneficial especially
since the oxygen saturation plays an important role in the diagnosis of OSA.
New features that explain the data better could also improve the clustering. Methods like the
continuous wavelet transform of the nasal airflow signal and its envelope could generate useful
features that could be worked into the feature scheme.
The features used in this project had very different variances and it could be interesting to see if
standardizing the features would yield a better cluster separation.
With further development into this automated system that finds patterns of breathing in PSG
recordings a fully automated screening solution could be possible for diagnostic purposes. This
system could either help sleep technicians with scoring consistency or in some instances replace
the technicians in regards to finding and scoring different types of events.
It could be interesting to apply a prediction scheme to the algorithm to measure the performance
against the AHI of the recordings. The code made for this project can with very few additions
be set up for implementation of the existing scoring criteria, which allows for prediction of AHI.

def cubic_spline_int(array_x, data, new_length):
    
    """
    This functions generates a cubic spline interpolation
    
    Input:
        array_x         (array of indices on the x-axis)
        data            (data array corresponding to the values of the y-axis)
        new_length      (The desired interpolation lenght)
    """
    
    x_data = array_x
    cubic_inter = interp1d(x_data, data, kind='cubic')
    new_x = np.arange(0, new_length)
    new_cubic = cubic_inter(new_x)
    return new_cubic

    local_idx, properties = find_peaks(array_of_interest, width=width, height=height)

    return trend_line
