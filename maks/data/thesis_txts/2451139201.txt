Abstract
Motivation and Purpose: Deep inspiration breathhold (DIBH) PET/CT scans are
the gold standard for diagnosis and treatment planning of patients with hodgkin’s
lymphoma in the lungs, as the procedure reduces the radiation dose to sensitive tissue
during radiotherapy. Due to the inherent noise in PET images, several short-time
images must be averaged to obtain the final long-time image, which can be a challenge
for patients with weak respiratory systems. Therefore, any reduction in the needed
amount of short-time images is an important goal.
Methods: A convolutional neural network based on the 3D u-net structure, using
the residual error framework, was developed and trained using 2-fold cross valida-
tion on a data set consisting of 62 patients with TrueX reconstructed PET images.
The model performance was evaluated using the two clinical parameters SUVMax,
SUVMean and the noise measurement SUVStd, which was extracted from the area
inside the tumour delineation. During model development, the hyperparameters was
optimized using grid search. The performance of the final model was evaluated using
Bland-Altman plots, correlation plots and visual inspection.
Results: The final network was trained using CT data and a progressive dropout
chance while using a 3D modified u-net with three contraction/expansion layers. The
resulting image outputs showed a good improvement in accuracy of the clinical pa-
rameters, with the mean difference of SUVMax between the output and the gold
standard going from -9.11 SUV to 0.86 SUV, when compared to the performance of a
short-time DIBH image. The SUVStd parameter was also improved from -0.54 SUV
to 0.15 SUV, while the SUVMean parameter performed slightly worse. Visually, the
network outputs was very similar to the long-time average DIBH images.
Conclusion: While the viability of using the developed CNN for scan time reduction
was not tested, the performance of the network shows promising results with respect
to de-noising of short-time DIBH images reconstructed using TrueX. Further work is
needed in order to reduce the scan time, as the model must be expanded to also use
OSEM reconstructed images and the viability of using the model outputs for creation
of tumour delineations must be investigated.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Hodgkin’s lymphoma (HL) is a type of cancer that attack the lymphatic system and is
characterized by the presence of hodgkin cells and reed-sternberg cells in the tumour.
In Denmark, the prevalence of HL is 2 in 100.000 with peaks in the age groups 20-30
and 50+, with about 125 new cases each year. While the success rate of treatment
is high, >95% for stage I-II and 70-80% for stage III-IV, there is a significant risk of
sequelae in areas treated by radiotherapy due to the radiation received by tissue close
to the tumour. Therefore, it is of large importance that treatment planning is done
based on accurate image data, to ensure the minimization of radiation dose outside
of the target area [13].
Combined Positron Emission Tomography and Computerized Tomography (PET/CT)
has been the state-of-the-art method, since its inception in 1998, for obtaining im-
age data used for diagnosis, tumour staging, radiotherapy planning and assessment
of treatment effect in radiotherapy and oncology. For patients with hodgkin’s lym-
phoma in the lungs, the tracer of choice is primarily [18F]-fluoro-deoxy-glucose (FDG)
due to its excellent tumour delineation [15].
In order to be of clinical use, the PET/CT images must be of high spatial resolu-
tion and accuracy since its applications rely on exact knowledge of tracer activity
and location. While CT provides high resolution images with a short scan time, PET
images has a significantly lower resolution and a higher scan time, which can intro-
duce movement artifacts in the final image. Several factors are the cause of the low
spatial resolution, such as photon scatter, random coincidence detection and detector
size limitations, which must be accounted for to obtain usable images.
The methods used to improve the accuracy and resolution of PET scans falls in
several categories, such as software and scanning techniques. The algorithms used
can be separated into image reconstruction algorithms and post-reconstruction algo-
rithms. The choice of reconstruction algorithm has a large effect on the final image
and nature of the image noise. In hospitals all over the world, the iterative recon-
struction algorithms OSEM and TrueX are commonly used alongside each other to
obtain dependable measurements of tracer uptake and distribution, with each algo-
rithm excelling in different aspects, making them both essential. A large number of
post-reconstruction algorithms exist with a wide range of uses, with one of the most

common one being noise reduction [3][9][11].
For PET/CT scans of the lungs, scanning techniques such as deep-inspiration breath-
hold (DIBH) can be used to separate the tumour from vulnerable tissue, such as the
heart, which reduces the radiation dose to these areas during radiotherapy. DIBH
can also reduce the effect of image artifacts caused by the movement of the lungs
during the scan. During a DIBH PET scan, the patient inspires deeply and hold
their breath for a short time while a scan is performed. The chest is monitored to
ensure a stable position during the scans. Six scans are obtained and averaged to
produce a final image with an acceptable coincidence line count. While patients with
a normal respiratory function have no trouble holding their breath for a short time
and only require short break between scans, a DIBH PET/CT can be a lengthy and
tough process for a number of patients. Especially older, respiratory challenged and
younger patients can be a challenge to scan in an acceptable time frame, which makes
a reduction in the amount of needed scans an important goal to strive for.
A reduction in the number of DIBH scans is essentially a de-noising problem, since
a lower overall scan time will result in a worse signal to noise ratio (SNR) that must
be mitigated.
In recent years, a number of deep learning algorithms for PET de-noising have showed
good results for a number of clinical cases, both in the reconstruction phase and the
post-reconstruction phase. Deep learning is a subclass of machine learning that is com-
monly based on stacked artificial neural networks that learns multiple representation
levels of the data in order to model complex relationships. The deep learning net-
works proposed in this thesis is built upon the convolutional neural network (CNN)
U-net, which have shown great results when applied to noise reduction in medical
images [18][17].

time of deep inspiration breathhold PET scans.
• The project aims to explore the performance of different deep learning networks
on TrueX reconstructed PET images.

CHAPTER 2
Background
This chapter introduces the theoretical background for the diagnosis and treatment of
hodgkin’s lymphoma, the acquisition of the data set and the proposed deep learning
network. Section 2.1 describes the clinical background for diagnosis and treatment
of hodgkin’s lymphoma and the imaging process. Section 2.2 describes the technical
background of deep learning model development.
2.1
Clinical Background
2.1.1
Hodgkin’s lymphoma
Pathology
Hodgkin’s lymphoma (HL) is a type of cancer originating from B-lymphocytes, which
is characterized by the presence of Hodgkin cells and Reed-Sternberg cells surrounded
by inflammatory tissue in biopsies of the tumour. HL is a cancer of the lymphatic
system, and will generally move from lymph node to lymph node in the early stages
of the disease. In later stages of the disease, HL can metastasize to other parts of the
body, such as the lungs [13].
The Epstein-Barr virus is thought to play a major role in the etiology of the disease,
as 50% of patients diagnosed with HL have had a previous infection with the Epstein-
Barr virus. HL is also three times as likely to develop in patients who have previously
been infected with mononucleosis.
The common symptoms of HL are enlarged lymph nodes, typically in the neck, and
general symptoms such as fever, loss of weight, tiredness and moderately itching areas
of the skin, but a for a large group of patients, HL shows no symptoms.
Conventional Treatment
Treatment of HL is decided by the tumour staging done using PET/CT, and is gener-
ally a mix of chemotherapy and radiotherapy, depending on biochemical factors and
the location of the tumour. While the treatment of HL have a high success rate, there
is a significant risk of serious sequelae in areas treated with radiotherapy. Especially
the heart, lungs and the thyroid are at risk of developing sequelae after radiotherapy

and a rise in risk of coronary diseases. For that reason, great care is taken during

2.1.2
PET/CT Imaging
Diagnostic PET/CT scans are a type of medical image scan that combines the two
modalities in order to enhance the diagnostic power for certain diseases. Generally,
the PET scan and the CT scan are obtained sequentially and combined during post
processing of the image data [15].
The CT scan is generated by using the phenomenon brehmstrahlung to create a
0-120 kilo electron volt (keV) polyenergetic photon beam in an x-ray tube, which
is used to create a sinogram of the passage of photon through the patient. As low
energy photons are completely absorbed by the patient, an aluminum filter is used to
filter the photon beam, reducing the bandwidth to 40-120 keV. After obtaining mul-
tiple sinograms, the CT slices is commonly generated by using filtered backprojection.
The PET scan is generated by measuring radioactivity of a tracer that has been
injected into the patient and metabolized before scanning. The distribution of tracer
in the patient after a set amount of time is then used as the basis for diagnosis. After
the tracer has been metabolized by the body, the PET scanner measures the coinci-
dence lines created by positron annihilation in the tracer, which creates two 511 keV
γ photons moving in opposite directions. The measured coincidence lines are orga-
nized into continous sinograms, where each sinogram line represents the integration
of the total activity across that line. As in CT imaging, filtered backprojection can
be used to reconstruct the PET image slice, but due to the inherent noise in the
data, other methods are used which will be explained later in the section. In order to
obtain enough coincidence lines to construct a PET image with a satisfactory SNR,
either the scan time or the tracer dose can be increased, as both will result in an
increase in coincidence lines detected. As an increase in radiation dose received by
the patient is rarely wanted, the usual choice is to use a longer scan time. Different
types of inherent noise exist in a PET image, which necessitates the use of de-noising
techniques to improve the image quality.
2.1.2.1
Image Noise in PET
Since the aim of this project is to reduce the noise of PET images, a more detailed
description of the noise present is fitting.
There are five major factors that affect image quality in PET images. These are finite
spatial resolution, attenuation of photons by tissue, detection of scattered photons,
accidental counting of random photons in coincidence and Poisson noise resulting
from the statistical nature of radioactive decay.
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Clinical Background
5
The spatial resolution is mainly determined by the size of the detector elements
and the reconstruction algorithm used, while the positron range also have an effect.
The finite spatial resolution results in two related effects. First, the image is blurred
depending on the resolution, were large objects will have blurred edges and smaller
objects may not be visualized as distinct objects. Secondly, the voxel values of neigh-
boring areas are smeared together which reduces image contrast and underestimates
the radioactivity of smaller objects, such as tumours in remission.
The attenuation and scattering of photons traveling through the body are due to
the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering when the photons are interacting
with the tissue of the body. The photoelectric effects results in the complete absorp-
tion of a photon which reduces the observed count rate, while the Compton scattered
photons are still counted at an incorrect position. These two types of image noise
results in a background haze in the image and lowers the overall contrast. The effect
of the photon attenuation can be lessened by using a corresponding CT image for
attenuation correction, which is done in the image reconstruction.
Accidental countings of random photons happens when photons from two different
annihilation events are detected in coincidence and counted as a single annihilation
event, resulting in false voxel values. Increases in activity in the field of view results in
a linearly increase of true coincidence line detected, but a squared increase in count-
ings of random photons, which necessitates a balancing of these values to obtain the
best image.
For PET imaging, the entire imaging process is governed by Poisson’s probability
law, were the number of detected photons per unit area is a Poisson random variable,
which result in an inherent noise in the image. The used reconstruction algorithm
changes the magnitude and correlation of the noise, which make it difficult to esti-
mate in practice and might result in the need to develop noise reduction techniques
optimized for each reconstruction method.
2.1.2.2
Iterative Reconstruction Algorithms
Due to the noise properties of PET images just described, the choice of reconstruction
algorithm is important in order to obtain the best image quality, optimized for the
task at hand. In hospitals all over the world, the two most common iterative recon-
struction algorithms are ordered-subset maximation estimation (OSEM) and Siemens
TrueX algorithm, which reconstructs the images using the point spread function of
the scanner, which will be described in more detail. Since the noise present in the
final PET image is dependent on the reconstruction algorithm used, a network de-
veloped on image data reconstructed using TrueX will not necessarily have a good
performance on OSEM reconstructed images.
==PAGE:24==
6
2 Background
The OSEM reconstruction algorithm is an offspring of the standard iterative expec-
tation maximation (EM) algorithm that attains a much higher convergence speed by
sequentially processing ordered subsets (OS) of projection data, while retaining the
image quality of standard EM. In OSEM, the standard EM algorithm is a special case
where all the projection data is contained in a single subset. The structure of the
subsets and the number of iterations used are of major importance for image quality.
A range of standard parameters which have been optimized for different types of PET
scans are used in the hospitals [5].
TrueX is an iterative algorithm that uses the measured point spread functions for
the scanner to create an accurate model of the relationship between the image space
and the projection space, which is used for image reconstruction [19].
The TrueX algorithm is the most used reconstruction algorithm, as the spatial reso-
lution obtained is higher than when using the OSEM algorithm, but as radioactivity
of sub-centimeter lesions in TrueX images can not be relied upon, OSEM images are
used when tracking the tracer uptake of tumours over time [10].

2.2
Deep Learning Networks
Deep learning neural networks (DL) are a subset of machine learning algorithms
that has exploded in popularity in the last decade due to the increase in computing
power available for model generation, especially in the advent of dedicated GPU’s
for machine learning calculations. DL networks forego the usual feature extraction
step inherent in classic machine learning algorithms, instead taking semi-raw data as
input and generating features automatically. DL networks have mainly been used for
supervised learning problems and owe a large part of their success to the enormous
curated labelled data-sets available for network development, which have resulted in
multiple successful network architectures, such as the U-net for medical images.
While the overall architecture of a DL network can be complex, the elements are
relatively simple and are built on techniques that have been developed in the late
fifties. The quintessential deep learning network is the Deep feedforward network,
also known as a multilayered perceptron (MLP). The MLP seeks to approximate
some function f by iteratively optimizing its underlying network parameters to pro-
duce the best approximation. The networked is called a feedforward network because
the input data x is fed to the model f ∗, producing an output y = f ∗(x), which de-
scribes an unidirectional flow of data through the model as the output y is not used
as a new input.
The basic information processing unit of a DL network is called a node and con-
sists of a single perceptron unit. A perceptron takes in multiple weighted inputs wixi
and produces the weighted sum z as an output:

The output z is then transformed by a non-linear activation function a = fNL(z),
where a is called the activation signal. One of the most common activation functions
is the rectified linear unit (ReLU) function, which works by setting all negative values
to zero, while leaving positive values unaltered.
A deep neural network consists of layers, where each layer consists of a row of per-
ceptrons connected in cascade to the surrounding layers. It is this stacking of layers
that makes the network ”Deep”. Each node output is used as input for the following
layer. During model training, the weights of the network are iteratively optimized
in order to approximate the underlying function, by minimizing the difference be-
tween the output of the network and the target. This minimization is done using a
loss function, which is a function that penalizes network errors. The loss function
is updated every iteration and the network is optimized using gradient descent, by
updating weights in the opposite direction of the loss gradient until convergence. The
size of the steps used in gradient descent is called the learning rate and have a large
effect on the final model performance. It is common in deep learning to use gradient
descent algorithms with variable step size, with the ADAM algorithm being one of
the most used [8]. ADAM works by keeping a moving average of past gradients and
the root mean squared pat gradients, by which the current gradient is divided. This
approach can speed up the convergence, while reducing the risk of hitting a local
minimum.

The layers of a CNN for image analysis contains three distinct stages that differs

Strided Convolution
Strided convolution is a technique that can be used in place of the normal convolution
process when it would be too computationally intensive to perform the operation for
each input voxel. This is done by implementing a stride, which is a predetermined
jump length between voxels, where intermediate voxels are skipped by the kernel filter.
The result of this is a downsampled version of the full convolutional operation, with
the stride length determining the degree of downsampling. The output of a strided
convolution is still a feature map for each kernel filter applied, but the dimensions
depend on the stride, kernel size and use of zero padding. The optimal stride for a
CNN depends on the data and can be explored during network optimization.
Transposed Convolution
Transposed convolution is an technique that is used to increase the spatial dimensions
of the layer input by upsampling using a kernel filter. The general use of transposed
convolution is to return a stack of feature maps to the shape of the original input,
such as a CNN that takes an image as input and returns a new image of the same
dimensions as output.

2.2.2
U-net Architecture
The u-net is a specialized CNN architecture that was initially developed for medical
image segmentation in 2d, but has since been expanded for use on 3d images and
for other types of uses, such as image de-noising [12] [1]. The u-net architecture will
be explained using the 2d implementation, but the principles are easily expanded to
handle 3d images.
Figure 2.8: 2d U-net architecture example. Taken from [12].
The u-net is composed of a contracting path and an expansive path, making it possible
to create a network output with the same spatial dimensions as the input. The number
of blocks in the paths determine the order of feature maps extracted from the input,
which allows the network to capture high level non-linear features. Each block in the
contracting path consists of two convolution layers followed by a max-pooling layer.
==PAGE:31==
2.2 Deep Learning Networks
13
The convolution layers uses strided convolution with no zero-padding and uses the
ReLU function in the activation function stage. In the 2d example in figure 2.8, a 3x3
kernel is used in the contracting path for the strided convolution, and the max-pooling
operation is performed on a 2x2 area with stride 2. For each contraction block in
the network, the spatial dimensions are halved, while the depth of the feature map is
doubled. Every step in the expansive path consists of an upsampling of the feature
map followed by a 2x2 upsampling convolution that halves the number of features,
a concatenation with the corresponding feature map from the contraction path, and
two convolutions using a 3x3 kernel followed by the ReLU activation function. The
lat step of the network is a convolution layer using a 1x1 kernal that creates the final
image output.
2.2.3
Training a CNN
In order to develop a generalized CNN that does not overfit to the training data, a
number of techniques exist, which will be described in the following sections.
2.2.3.1
Loss Functions
The choice of loss function has a large effect on the training and validation of the
network. It is this function that determines the penalization of network errors, hence
the network outputs will reflect the nature of the chosen function. While the optimal
loss function can be determined by trial-and-error, it is also possible to determine the
best choice based in the nature of the data set and network output goals. Classic loss
functions used in deep learning includes mean absolute error (MAE), mean squared
error (MSE) or root mean squared error (RMSE) for regression problems and cross
entropy (CE) for classification problems. De-noising of PET images is a regression
problem, which limits the consideration to the error functions MAE, MSE and RMSE.
Since each function penalizes the degree of errors in different ways, the choice must
reflect the acceptable levels of errors in the network outputs. While a false negative
is usually worse than false positives in medical classification problems and should be
penalized more, there is no such weighting in PET de-noising. Since the importance
of an erroneous predicted value rises linearly with its magnitude, MAE is a better
fit compared to MSE and RMSE as these functions increasingly penalizes errors that
is further from the ground truth. The edge slices of PET images are also extremely
noisy, which can result in an excessive importance given to de-noising these areas
by MSE and RMSE loss functions, if no preprocessing is done to remove these noisy
areas from the data set before network training.
2.2.3.2
Network Regularization
For DL networks with a large number of parameters and layers, overfitting can be a
serious problem, which must be mitigated to obtain a generalized model with a good
performance on new data sets. This can be done by increasing the amount of data,
==PAGE:32==
14
2 Background
but as this will also increase the training time it can be an unfeasible endeavour, even
when more data is readily available. There exists a range of techniques that attempts
to improve the generalization of networks without increasing the size of the data set,
which is termed network regularization. In the following section, the regularization
techniques used in this project are elaborated.
Regularization using the loss function
A simple way to regularize the network is to add a regularization term to the loss
function. L2 regularization is a common technique, which adds the term 1
2λw2 for ev-
ery weight w in the network, where λ is the regularization strength, thus dampening
the effect of the weights, leading to a simpler and perhaps less overfitted network.
Dropout
A technique which can prevent overfitting without increasing the size of the data set
is using dropout. Dropout works by randomly dropping nodes and their connections
from the network during training, resulting in less co-adaption between nodes. This is
accomplished by setting the activation function of the node to zero with a predefined
probability. For each training cycle, new nodes are chosen. As the final model is
based on the average of many ”thinned” models, the result will be a more generalized
model [16].

Comparison between a regular neural network and a network using
dropout. Image taken from [16].
Batch Normalization
Batch normalization is a technique used for accelerating the network training by re-
ducing internal covariate shift, increasing the amount of training data that can be
used while still preserving an acceptable training time. Internal covariate shift is the
phenomenon where the distribution of inputs to each layer change during training
as the parameters of previous layers are updated. Due to this, a low learning rate
==PAGE:33==
2.2 Deep Learning Networks
15
and a careful choice of starting parameters is needed when training deep networks,
as changes in distribution is amplified throughout the network each training cycle.
Batch normalization mitigates this by normalizing input distribution for each layer.
This is done by subtracting the batch mean and dividing by the batch standard devi-
ation for each input batch [7]
Data Augmentation
To create a generalized model that can be trusted, all possible data variations and
outcomes must be well represented in the data set. This is almost never the case, as
some outcomes and variations are very rare and will be underrepresented in most data
sets. To mitigate this, data augmentation can be used to increase the data complexity
and hopefully mimic the variations that will be found in the real data. Common ways
to augment medical image data includes using geometric or morphological operations
to generate images that could possibly have been obtained from true patients. One
must be careful relying on data augmentation though, as generated variations that
does not exist in real data only ends up introducing more noise into the network.
2.2.3.3
Residual Learning
For image analysis, the depth of the network have been shown to be an important
factor in final model accuracy, as the high level features obtained map to important
aspects of the data. Deeper is not always better though, because as the depth of
the network increases, a degradation of the model accuracy occurs which is not due
to overfitting.
A way to avoid this accuracy degradation is the use the residual
learning framework, which have been shown to have superior accuracy and training
speed for deep models, when compared to the conventional approach. The residual
learning framework is built on the basis that instead of directly fitting to an underlying
mapping, it is easier to fit to a residual mapping. If the desired map is denoted as
H(x), the stacked non-linear layers fit another mapping of F(x) = H(x) −x, so the
original mapping is recast into F(x) + x. This is accomplished by adding ”shortcut”
connections to the network, which simply perform identity mapping that is added to
the output of the stacked layers, as seen in figure 2.10 [4].
Figure 2.10: Basic block of the residual learning framework. Image taken from [4].

Data Augmentation
A type of data augmentation based on simple geometrical operations was implemented
for use in training of the network. The implemented operations consisted of turning
the input and target 90 degrees to either side and flipping the images along both the
X and Y axis, for a total of four operations. The operations was then split into two
different sets for hyperparameter optimization. The first set consisted of only the two
90 degree image flips, while the second set consisted of all four operations. For each
augmentation set, the python function numpy.random.random() was used to ran-
domly choose to either use a data augmentation operation or make no changes to the
images for each batch during network training, with equal chance for all possibilities.
Use of CT images
CT data registered to the PET data and of the same spatial resolution was used
during model training for some models. As the CT images had been cropped before
extraction from the image database, some of the images had had edges of the patient
cut off, resulting in missing CT data, when registered to the PET data. As no CT
data in tumour regions was missing, it was decided to use the cropped CT data
anyway.
Dropout
Two types of dropout was implemented for use in training of the network. The first
type was a flat 10% chance to drop nodes across all layer for each training iteration.
The second type was a progressively increasing dropout chance based in the depth of
the feature map, increasing in increments of 10% from 10% to 30% in the contracting
path and dropping back to 10% in the expansive path of the u-net, thus preventing

overfitting of higher level features more aggressively. Alongside these two types of
dropout, the effect of not using dropout was tested.

Grid search
To determine the optimal hyperparameters for network training, the process of grid
search was used. During grid search, two types of hyperparameters are tested against
each other by locking all other hyperparameters in place and varying the two chosen
parameters between predetermined levels.
The resulting network validation error
after network training for each parameter combination was then noted alongside the
clinical parameters, thus creating a heat map of the effect of the two parameters.
Two runs of grid search was performed to determine the hyperparameters used for
the final network. The first grid search explores the use of CT images alongside the
use of data augmentation, while the second grid search explores the use of dropout

3.5
Evaluation of Model Outputs
As the goal of the implemented network is to de-noise the short-time DIBH image
while still preserving the clinical parameters, the network outputs will be evaluated
based on the similarities between the output and the long-time DIBH image, based
on the SUVMax, SUVMean and SUVStd inside the tumour delineation. The network
outputs will also be compared to the unaltered short-time DIBH images, to estimate if
the network have successfully reduced the image noise. The clinical parameters of the
images will be compared based on mean error, absolute mean error and percentile
error. Bland-Altman plots will be used to evaluate the performance for both the
short-time DIBH image and the model outputs, compared the the gold standard of

26
3 Methods
the long-time DIBH average. A Bland-Altman plot is constructed by taking a data
vector from each imaging technique that is compared and filling out the coordinate
system using the coordinate formula:
S(x, y) = (S1 + S2
2
, S1 −S2)
Where S1 and S2 are points from the data vectors of SUV parameters from the
two techniques being compared. On top of the resulting scatter plot, the mean line
and two standard deviation lines are placed, based on the input data. The Bland-
Altman plots will be used to investigate the statistical difference between the proposed
network model and the gold standard.
To investigate the reproducibility of the network outputs, the five short-time DIBH
scans that had not been used as part of the training data will be used as input to the
network. The resulting new outputs will be evaluated in the same way as the initial
outputs in order to investigate if the model performance is consistent when new data
is used. In short, have a generalized model been developed, or is it overfitted to the
training data.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
5.1
Key Results
The aim of this project was to develop and validate a model to reduce the scan time
for DIBH PET images using a CNN. To reduce the scan time, the goal of the model
was to de-noise the initial short-time DIBH images, such that less scan repetitions
was needed to create a final long-time averaged DIBH image. While any reduction in
the needed amount of short-time images would be an improvement, it was decided to
attempt to develop a model using only a single short-time image as an input, as the
other available short-time images could always be added to the model if the perfor-
mance of the output was too poor. For evaluation of the model outputs, the clinical
parameters SUVMax, SUVMean and SUVStd was used instead of classical machine
learning evaluation parameters, in order to determine if the results had clinical value.
The developed model was evaluated based on the similarity of the outputs to the
clinical gold standard of the averaged long-time DIBH image. As seen on the Bland-
Altman plots in figure 4.2, the final model outputs have improved the mean difference
and variance of both the SUVMax and SUVStd parameter, while the SUVMean pa-
rameter has a slightly higher variance. Likewise, the correlation plots in figure 4.3
also show an improvement of the bias of the SUVMax and SUVStd parameter, but a
slight worsening of the SUVMean parameter. During visual evaluation of the output
images, as seen in figure 7.2, the model output is far more visually similar to the
averaged long-time DIBH image than the short-time image. The difference between
the model output and the ground truth is mostly found in the edges of the tumour
delineation. When evaluating if the tumour delineation still fits the model outputs,
as seen in figure 4.6 and 4.5, it can be seen that the high intensity areas are still
inside the tumour delineation. Lastly, the reproducibility of the model outputs was
investigated using the other five available short-time images for each patient in the
validation set as input for the model. The resulting outputs was found to be very
similar to the initial model output, but with small differences, mirroring the differ-
ences in the short-time scans, as seen in figure 4.10 and 4.11.
Overall, the final model have reduced the noise present in the short-time DIBH image
inside the tumour delineation area based on the clinical parameters. Based on visual
evaluation, the model have also reduced the noise present outside of the tumour delin-
eation, but this reduction have not been quantified in this project. It was not possible
==PAGE:66==
48
5 Discussion
to evaluate if the resulting outputs from the final model was usable by physicians for
treatment planning, so it is not certain if a scan time reduction was possible using
the developed model.
5.2
Strengths and Limitations
As the eventual goal of the project is to develop a network that can be used in a
clinical setting for scan time reduction, the use of clinical parameters for evaluation
of the model outputs is fitting, even though the three chosen parameters are not
equally useful for measuring the performance of the network.
While the network
drastically improved the SUVMax measurements compared to using a short-time im-
age, SUVMax is not a good measurement of model success, as the SUVMax value
is based on a single voxel. As we have no control over the output of single voxels
when using the u-net architecture, the evaluation should not be based on the value
of a single voxel. The parameters SUVMean and SUVStd or more fitting for evalu-
ating overall model performance, as both parameters describe all voxels inside the
tumour delineation, with SUVStd being the best measurement of noise. As SUVStd
was also improved by the model, the results are promising for clinical use in the future.
During model development, an attempt was made to do as little preprocessing of
the input images as possible, in order to avoid introducing any new noise into the net-
work. This initial goal resulted in a number of later development choices, which might
be worth it to investigate in the future. In order to reduce the spatial dimensions of
the input, the input images was cropped to 256x256 voxels, which resulted in a wide
safety margin to account for differences in patient size. A simple cropping algorithm
could easily have been implemented to remove all edges without signal followed by
a resampling algorithm to ensure that all inputs had the same voxelwise dimensions,
but while this approach would reduce the amount of signal-less input voxels, the
voxels would not necessarily have the same spatial dimensions anymore. While the
images was only cropped in the X and Y axis in this project, cropping in the Z axis
could have been used to remove the noise present in the edges of PET scans, before
introducing it to the network, but this approach would also have removed the useful
PET signal in the edges. Data patches could also have been used to reduce the size
of the model inputs, but this was not explored during model development. During
hyperparameter optimization, the use of CT data or data augmentation resulted in
the same overall performance, but the use of CT was chosen for the final model in
order to avoid creating new data. As all input data to the model is used to create
the output, the result of using CT data is that the final model output is a composite
PET/CT image in a way. As the PET/CT combination is of great clinical use, this
is not necessarily a bad thing, but if the goal is to obtain a model output showing
only areas with tracer uptake, using data augmentation would perhaps be a better
choice.
==PAGE:67==
5.3 Related Work
49
Lastly, the use of data normalization is an important topic to consider. As no nor-
malization of input data was used in this model, we have no control over the ranges
of voxel values present in the training data set. If the range of voxel values are too
great, and high values overrepresented, it can lead to an overfitting of the convolution
filters to the high values, thus reducing the model performance on input data with
low-magnitude noise. On the other hand, if input normalization is used, it is not
possible to transform the voxel values to SUV in the network output images, thus
reducing the ability to compare the model outputs to other PET images. A compro-
mise would be to transform the input data into SUV during preprocessing, but as
Bq/ml is also a useful unit, this approach is not perfect either. In the end it was
decided to use the raw voxel values for model training in his project, as the data set
was large enough that the effect of overfitting should be minimal.
5.3
Related Work
Only one published study have used u-net based CNN’s to denoise PET images after
image reconstruction [18], while a few studies have used deep learning to improve the
reconstruction algorithm. As the choice of u-net structure and hyperparameters used
have a large effect on the network performance, it is very relevant to compare the
performance of the developed network to other different networks.
In 2017, Junshen Xu et al. attempted to reconstruct clinically usable PET images
from raw PET data of only 0.5% normal signal strength using a modified 2D u-net.
Xu et al. used a three layer contraction/expansion u-net with each stage consisting of
convolution with 3x3 kernels, batch normalization and the ReLU activation function.
The choice of using a 3 layer u-net agrees with the the experience from this project,
as the 2 layer network resulted in a blurring of the outputs. As high level features
correspond to the finer image details, this was not suprising. In order to increase the
available training data set of 9 patients, X and Y axis flip was used as data augmenta-
tion to good effect, which correspond to some of the geometric transformations used
in this project. The use of data augmentation did not lead to an increase in model
performance for us though, which can be due to the large data set already present,
making more data unnecessary. As in this project, Xu et al. used the residual learn-
ing framework to make the model learn the difference between standard and low dose
PET images, but the approach to using 3D image data differed significantly. Instead
of using the 3D u-net as in this project, Xu et al. used adjoining slices as multi-channel
input. Multichannel input was also used here, but this was done by using both 3D
PET and CT data in different channels. As both approaches resulted in models with
good performance, the approach of using a 2D u-net with mutlichannel slice input
might result in a much faster model training, but as training time was not tracked in
this project, it is not possible to compare. The network of Xu et al. was evaluated us-
ing normalized root mean square error (NRMSE), peak SNR and structural similarity
index (SSIM). The images generated by the network outperformed other non-CNN
==PAGE:68==
50
5 Discussion
based state-of-the-art methods for all evaluation criteria in all nine patients, with the
model outputs being comparable to standard dose PET images. Compared to the
network developed in this project, the hyperparameter optimization done by Xu et al.
was much more extensive, increasing the chance the the optimal network structure
was used. While the results of this project shows promise, it is very possible that the
network performance can be improved significantly by investigating new parameters.
While Xu et al. obtained good results, their evaluation functions does not necessarily
correspond to clinical useful parameters. The use of SUV parameters in this project
makes it easier to evaluate the clinical use of the model outputs. As the data set used
in this project is also much larger, a more generalized network is created.
5.4
Future Perspectives
The results from the developed model shows that it is possible to reduce the noise in
the short-time DIBH images and obtain clinical parameter values close to the gold
standard long-time DIBH images, using just a single short-time image as input to
the model. As the possibility of using the model to reduce the overall scan time was
not investigated, this should be the first step in future development of the project.
As the purpose of the long-time DIBH image is for creation of a tumour delineation
by a trained physician, the final model outputs should be used to create delineations
alongside long-time images in a blinded study. If the final model outputs can be used
to create correct tumour delineations by physicians, the scan time can be reduced to
just a single short-time image in the future. If the model output is found lacking, a
model can be trained to use averages of fewer short-time images, which will still lead
to a reduction in scan time, if these are clinically useable.
As the final model shows promise in being able to reduce the noise of TrueX re-
constructed PET scans, it would be relevant to attempt to train a new model for
OSEM reconstructed images, as both types of PET images are used clincially. Since
the image noise is different in OSEM reconstructed images, the optimal hyperparam-
eters chosen for the developed model will not necessarily be directly transferable, but
as the two types of PET images are very close to each other, the overall network
architecture should be transferable. As OSEM images are needed clinically, it is only
possible to reduce the scan time for DIBH scans if the model can de-noise both TrueX
and OSEM images satisfactorily.
==PAGE:69==
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to develop and test a deep learning network for scan time
reduction of the DIBH PET imaging technique. While the potential for a scan time
reduction was not evaluated, the developed network have shown promising results
for de-noising of DIBH PET images reconstructed using the TrueX algorithm, which
could lead to a scan time reduction in the future. The resulting de-noising of the
short-time DIBH images was accomplished using a modified 3D U-net CNN, which
was trained using the residual loss framework and optimized during the development
period.
The final model outputs showed an improvement in accuracy of clinical
parameters, when compared to the short-time images, which were reproduced when
using image data not part of the training data set. In order to reduce the scan time of
the DIBH procedure in the future, the developed network must be expanded to also
work with images reconstructed with the OSEM algorithm, and the network outputs
must be compared to the gold standard long-time averaged DIBH image for creation
of accurate tumour delineations.
