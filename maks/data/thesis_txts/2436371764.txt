Summary
Abnormalities in neurotransmission of dopamine (DA) are involved in several neuropsy-
chiatric diseases such as schizophrenia, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and at-
tention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). One way to better understand these
disorders is by positron emission tomography (PET) of the dopaminergic system. The
dopaminergic system is made up of the mesolimbic pathway with projections from the
ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens (NAc) and medial prefrontal
cortex (mPFC), and the nigrostriatal pathway consisting of projections originating in the
substantia nigra (SN) and terminating in the dorsal striatum (DS). In this study we will
focus on competition experiments with 18F-Fallypride PET radioligand. The strength
of PET is that it enables the translation of clinical findings to animals and viceversa.
However, preclinical small animal PET analysis is limited to a few studies and it is
still not very developed. Therefore, there is a need to develop automated methods to
quantify dopamine receptors occupancy and dopamine release.
In this study, a new non-biased automatic method is proposed for spatial normalization
of intermodality (CT, MR and PET) scans and subsequent PET quantification of D2/3
receptor occupancy. The main steps of the imaging analysis method include: (1) the con-
struction CT, MR and PET templates in a common standard space, (2) the construction
of a methodology for automatic spatial normalization of 18F-Fallypride rat images and
(3) automatic kinetic quantification of baseline 18F-Fallypride and treated images previ-
ously normalized to standard space. A validation of the proposed steps in the method
is presented and, in addition, drug occupancy in the basal ganglia is determined. We
foresee that the developed method will be applicable to research aimed at quantifying
activity in the dopaminergic system for clinical as well as non-clinical purposes.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Neuropshyciatric disorders, such as schizophrenia, depression or ADHD, represent a big
societal problem in terms of economic cost and human suffering, both from those affected
and their related environments. Some of these disorders are due to abnormalities in
dopamine neurotransmission, which is the case of obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD),
schizophrenia or attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). In 2005, 27.4% of the
European Union (EU) population suffered from a mental disorder. This number has
been estimated to increase to at least 38.2% (165 million people) in the upcoming years
[52]. For example, the number of people with ADHD disease in America rose from 7.8%
in 2003 to 11% in 2011 [50]. Along with this increasing percentage of the population
with these diseases, the cost of taking care of these patients is also rising. The key for
reducing these costs lies in the prevention and better understanding of these mental
disorders by elucidating the pathoetiology of dopamine functions and dysfunctions.
One way to better understand the dopaminergic system is by using positron emission
tomography (PET). The power of PET is the possibility of translating animal imaging
to clinical imaging and viceversa. However, preclinical small animal PET analysis is
not as developed and there is a need to develop automated methods to quantify tracer
kinetics. Thus, the goal of my project is to design an algorithm for automatic PET
quantification of the D2/3 dopamine receptor tracer
18F-Fallypride in rats.
Furthermore, I will use this algorithm to quantify dopaminergic receptor occupancy of
the drug Clozapine-N-oxide (CNO) using compartmental modeling and competition for
the 18F-Fallypride binding site on the dopamine D2/3 receptor.
In order to automatically quantify dopaminergic neurons activity four main steps are
required: 1) construction of 18F-Fallypride PET rat brain template, 2) automated reg-
istration of 18F-Fallypride rat images to a rat PET atlas in standard Long Evans (LE)
space, 3) region-based calculation of 18F-Fallypride uptake and binding kinetics using
kinetic modeling tools in PMOD and 4) kinetic calculations of receptor occupancy of
drug or dopamine following either drug treatment or dopamine release.
This project will generate a fast and reliable algorithm which will increase the data
analysis and reproducibility of 18F-Fallypride preclinical imaging. This novel tool will
be very useful for new preclinical imaging studies of the dopaminergic system, and
can be extended to other PET tracers and neurotransmitter systems, like serotonergic

CHAPTER2
Theoretical background
PET, MR and CT are non-invasive imaging methods which are used clinically in humans
as well as pre-clinically in animals, and therefore offer a high inter species translational
value. This chapter will briefly introduce CT and MR imaging, and give a more detailed
description of PET together with the key mathematical procedures of the developed
methodology, spacial normalization and kinetic modeling.
2.1
Brief background on CT
Computed Tomography (CT) is an ionizing imaging modality that exploits the principle
of different absorption of X-rays for each tissue in the body [28]. A narrow X-ray beam
passes through the subject from different angles and distances. As it passes through, the
beam is attenuated. A dosimeter located directly opposite to the X-ray source measures
the amount of X-rays reaching it. With the use of mathematical image reconstruction
techniques, the X-ray attenuation is translated into CT values that can then be displayed
as a cross-sectional image [36]. These slices are then stacked together to create a 3D
image, as shown in Figure 2.1. These images generally contain detailed information of
bones, soft tissue, blood vessels and internal organs. Note that most of the images within
this document will be shown in three different planes: coronal, sagittal and horizontal.
A brief explanation these planes is included in Appendix A.

Brief background on structural MRI
Magnetic resonance imaging is a medical imaging modality whose first medical appli-
cations dates back to 1973. Therefore, it can be considered a recent modality [2]. In
essence, MRI makes use of both radio waves and magnetic fields, which implies that it
is neither invasive nor ionizing (involves no radiation) technique. It is widely used in
neuroimaging research due to two factors: excellent spatial resolution and good tempo-
ral resolution, allowing in vivo characterization and quantification of tissue geometry:
volume, surface, width, etc.

The nuclei can be seen as a bar magnet with north and south poles in each end (Figure
2.2.B). The direction of this bar is random, although the angular momentum is fixed
since both −→
J and −→µ are constants. In the presence of an external magnetic field −→
B0,
the magnetic moment of each proton aligns to the field. They usually align along the

B0, a
large number of magnetic moments are aligned to the field because the spin-up state is
the low energy state (Figure 2.2.C). The sum of all these individual magnetic moments
results in a net magnetization vector (−→

M0 is equal to zero when the magnetic
moments cancel out when −→
B0 is not present (Figure 2.2.D) [42].
It is impossible to measure the magnitude of −→
M0, but it is possible to measure its
transverse component. Yet, under the presence of only the magnetic external field −→
M0
is static and does not precess (individual nuclei are all precessing). Thus, it is necessary
to disturb this equilibrium by the application of an radio-frequency (RF) wave (second
magnetic field −→
B1) at the Larmor frequency given by [14]:
ω0 = γB0
(2.3)

magnetization vector now also has a transversal component (−−→
Mxy) apart from the longi-
tudinal component −→
Mz. −→

angular momentum. The angular frequency of this precession is known as the Larmor

turned off in a process known as relaxation. The transversal component goes back to

2 Theoretical background
Contrast in anatomical MRI images is determined by each tissue’s relaxation time.
Therefore, each contrast type is different depending on the tissue. The two most impor-
tant types of relaxation times are referred to as T1 and T2 [24]. The first one is defined
as the time at which there has been 63% recovery of the longitudinal component, while
the second one is the time at which the transverse component has decayed to 37% of its
initial magnitude. The effect of pondering an image in either T1 (Figure 2.4 left) or T2
(Figure 2.4 right) can be observed in the following image.
Figure 2.4: MRI structural images: T1-weighted image (left) and T2-weighted image
(right). Only sagittal view is shown.
Differences between the two different contrasts obtained can also be observed in the
previous image. On the one hand, T1-Weighted MRI images (T1W-MRI) have brighter
intensities the more dense the tissue is (i.e. white matter), whereas the intensities are
usually darker for liquid parts of the brain such as the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). This
type of image delineates gray matter, white matter and cerebrospinal fluid. On the other
hand, T2-Weighted MRI images (TW2-MRI) are pondered exactly in the opposite way:
intensities tend to be brighter for white matter, whereas for cerebrospinal fluid and gray
matter they are darker [42].
2.3
Background on PET
Positron emission tomography is a nuclear medicine imaging modality whose first appli-
cation dates back to 1940, when the radionuclide concentration of the human body was
first analyzed. Essentially, PET allows a non-invasive generation of functional images
with a higher signal to noise ratio (SNR) than that of any other modality because of the
physical properties of radioactive decay and the high energy of the gamma rays. Thus,
biological processes at a molecular level can be measured without altering the system
under study [42]. This is because of the trace dose principle, where extremely small
amounts of tracer are used, in a way so that the drug does not affect the body function.
PET images can also be used in addition to structural data obtained from conven-
tional imaging (MRI and CT). Thus, while PET will reflect function, MRI and CT will
==PAGE:23==
2.3 Background on PET
7
show structural details, which altogether provide the possibility of quantitatively ana-
lyze changes over time either in order to monitor diseases as they evolve over time or in
response to certain stimulus. This is why it has emerged as an important image modal-
ity for the investigation of neuropsychiatric diseases such as Alzheimer or Schizophrenia
[16].
PET is based on the detection of photon pairs emitted after a small amount of a ra-
dioactive tracer is injected. The radioactive drug, or tracer, is attached to a unstable
isotope also called radionuclide. The tracer is usually labeled with fluorine-18 (18F) or
carbon-11 (11C). This thesis will focus on the use of the tracer Fallypride labeled with
18F. Fallypride is a high affinity dopamine D2/3 receptor antagonist. The receptor has
a high affinity of 33pM for Fallypride [30]. Thus, Fallypride can be used as a tracer in
this study due to its suitability to measure dopamine D2/3 receptor occupancy in both
striatal and extrastriatal regions [29]. Fallypride also has the potential advantage of
having a long half-life, in addition to being sensitive to amphetamine-induced dopamine
release [44]. On the other hand, fluorine isotope is the important source of gamma rays
of a process known as annihilation [29].
Annihilation starts when 18F emits a positron (β+), which is a positively charged particle
with the same mass as the electron that travels until it captures an electron. When the
positron collides with the electron during annihilation, two gamma rays are produced
at 511 keV almost 180º from each other to conserve energy momentum (Figure 2.5.A)
[45]. This radiation emission causes the fluorine radionuclide to lose energy and thus,
undergo radioactive decay. The product from 18F is the stable isotope 18O.

2.4
Brief background on registration
Image registration consists in aligning two images into a single overlying image so that
corresponding features can easily be related. This is achieved by transforming the data
into the same coordinate system. Although it can be any arbitrary object coordinate
system, the reference coordinate frame in this case will be given as another image. Thus,
to consider the data in the images simultaneously, a geometrical transformation between
each pair of images must be determined. That is the simple way of looking at image
registration - as purely a geometrical transformation that aligns two images to the same
space.
Going beyond that we look at image registration from a more mathematical perspective
by considering three more elements besides the geometrical transformation: a similarity
measure (also referred to as cost function), an optimization algorithm and a regulariza-
tion term [4]. The first one describes how good the registration is. The optimization
algorithm is responsible of defining the parameters of the geometrical transformation
that maximize the similarity measure.
Finally, the regularization term ensures that
only reasonable transformations are obtained.
All PET, CT and MR images used in this Thesis need to have their information related
for future processing steps. This section reviews different registration techniques avail-
able to accomplish this task and also describes the applications within this project to
which these techniques are applied. In addition, each of the aforementioned items will
be explained with further details.

2.4.1
Geometrical transformation
We will begin by considering that the geometrical transformation item y(x; w): R3 −→
R3 (3D space - MR) or R4 −→R4 (4D space - PET) maps the coordinates of the
reference image (xi) to those of the input image (yi). For easiness in the understanding
of the different geometrical transformations available, they will all be put in the same
canonical form. The canonical form represents the transformation as a sum an identity
part x and a deformation part u(x;w) (see Equation 2.13). The identity part refers
to the voxels of the image to which the set of vectors of the deformation part will be
applied to [23].
y(x; w) = x + u(x; w)
(2.13)
where w are the transformation parameters. This equation is made more specific for each
individual type of transformation by determining the parameters that will be used. If the
geometrical transformation is purely a translation, the deformation vectors will be given
by t = (t1, t2, t3)T in the 3D case, so that the transformation becomes y(x; t) = Ax + t.
If the transformation is rigid, an additional orthogonal matrix R needs to be included.
It is often used to compensate for subject positioning differences.
A more complex
transformation also includes isotropic scaling and is referred to as similarity transform.
The resulting transformation is then y(x; t, s, R) = sRx + t. It becomes handy when
there is also a need to compensate for differences in size between subjects (features
won’t have similar shapes) and for differences in spatial resolution that may arise when
registering different modalities [21]. For instance, PET images will have larger voxels
than those of the MR images so the spatial resolution will be much larger in PET than
in MR [42].
If the scaling is anisotropic instead, the transformation becomes affine:
y(x; A, t) = x + t.
If non-linearity is introduced to the transformation a common
approach would be to use splines. However, this Thesis will be restricted to the use of
the aforementioned geometrical transformations.

2.4.3
Optimization algorithm
The geometrical transformation y(x; w) maps the domain of the reference image to
that of the input image. This transformation is iteratively calculated as part of the
registration algorithm so as to optimize one of the voxel similarity measures explained
in the previous subsection. The registration algorithm starts by first creating a grid
of size equal to the size of the reference image.
The grid points are then mapped
to the input image using the geometrical transformation. The resulting mapped image
coordinates are likely to be pointing somewhere between pixels in the input image. Hence,
it is necessary to find the exact input image value using an interpolation procedure.
This returns interpolated intensity values of the input image pixels at the query points
(the transformed points). The input image values are then associated with the original
sampling points, i.e. the grid points [23].

Specific aims
There is a need to automate preclinical PET image analysis in order to avoid bias
and decrease processing time. Thus, the aim of this Thesis is to develop a non-biased
automatic spatial normalization methodology that allows for the quantification of 18F-
Fallypride uptake in the dorsal striatum. This objective can be divided into five specific
aims:
1. Construct a MR, CT and PET standard space.
2. Construct a methodology for automatic spatial normalization of 18F-Fallypride rat
images.
3. Extract volumes of interest based on baseline 18F-Fallypride rat images.
4. Automated kinetic quantification of baseline 18F-Fallypride rat images matched to
standard space.
5. Apply developed methodology to determine drug and dopamine occupancy.

is created based on region growing that starts from a seed point. The starting (seed)
point is set to be within the brain, so that the algorithm will then search for all the
connected voxels below the defined threshold value, which corresponds to the value of
the seed point. The region is grown interactively including possible holes, so as to allow
inner sparings to also be included within the VOI.
Once the brain in cropped from the CT, inter-modality spatial normalization is necessary
so as to normalize it to the brain MR template in LE space. This normalization is done
manually using the Fuse It tool in PMOD. The alignment is done by shifting, scaling and
rotating the images. The MR image in LE space is used as reference image, while the
CT scan is the image to be adjusted. The adjustment involves both pixel size and slice
thickness changes to math those of the reference image, both carried out in a process
known as reslicing. The resulting image can be seen in Figure 4.4.

4.3
Construction of the tracer specific PET
rat brain template
The combination of MR and PET allows merging of morphological and functional data.
MR images, unlike PET images, are characterized by high anatomical resolution with
good soft-tissue contrasts. This is why it is difficult to find preclinical brain PET data
with good spatial resolution. The creation of a PET template is therefore necessary to
relate tracer uptake in PET to the different anatomical regions, better observed in MR
images. The tracer specific template is constructed from the average of the individual
PET scans and its spatial registration to the MR.
The following section describes a standardized methodology for the construction of a rat
brain tracer specific PET template, as well as the tools necessary for it.
4.3.1
Animals
Long Evans Tg (TH-Cre 3.1) Deis rats were purchased from the Rat Resource and Re-
search Center (RRCC, University of Missouri, USA). All animals were bred heterozygous
at the Department of Experimental Medicine Biocenter (AEM, University of Copen-
hagen). Twenty-nine female rats were used in this study (see Table C.1). The rats were
kept in cages containing 2-4 animals each in a rodent space with controlled climate and
12h/12h light cycle. All of them had ad libitum supply of food and continuous access to
water. All procedures carried out followed the FELASA guidelines for animal research
and had the approval from both The Danish Animal Experiments Inspectorate (license
number: 2016-15-0201-01031) and the Department of Experimental Medicine, Univer-
sity of Copenhagen. All animals were transported to the scanner at least one hour before
the experiment was carried out.
4.3.2
Radiosynthesis of 18F-Fallypride
18F-Fallypride synthesis starts with the addition of tosyl-fallypride precursor (0.2mg) in
1mL dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) to the dried 18F-KF-K222 complex via an automated
injection and is followed by its heating to 150°C for 20 minutes. The resulting solu-
tion is then purified with a semi-preparative HPLC method (Luna, 10￿, C-18 column;
20% ethanol in 0.1% phosphoric acid in water – retention time 600s).
The isolated
product gives a non-decaying corrected radiochemical yield of 22.0% (Radiochemical
purity: 99%, specific activity: 68.2 GBq/µmol, n = 8) after the analysis with analyti-
cal HPLC (Kinetic, C-18 with pre-column; 25% acetonitrile in 0.1% phosphoric acid in
water; 1.5mL/min – retention time 1.123 minutes).

4.3.4
SUV calculation
The information contained in PET images corresponds to the measured tracer concentra-
tion calculated by observing the emitted gamma rays as the radionuclide label 18F decays.
==PAGE:43==

Thus, the radioactivity concentration in PET images (measured in units MBq/cc) de-
pends on the rate of decay of the radioisotope. This implies that the radioactive decay
must be accounted for by decay correction of the measured PET image before any fur-
ther steps are carried out with this data. The correction should take into consideration
the decay occurring between the tracer’s injection time and the start of the PET scan.
Fluorine radioactivity (A0) at the injection time can be corrected to the radioactivity
(At) at the scan time t using the following equation:
At = A0e−λt
(4.1)
where the decay constant λ varies for different radioactive labellings as:
λ = ln(2)
t1/2
(4.2)
t1/2 is the time required for the decaying quantity to fall to one half of its initial value.
In the case of fluorine t1/2 = 1.8295 hours [8]. The corrected tracer dose values for
each animal can be found in Appendix C. For instance, for the baseline scan of Rat84
a Fallypride dose of 5.876 MBq was measured in the syringe before injection at 10:47.
Decay correction must be done to account for 11 minutes of decay until injection time.
Thus, at injection time (10:58), the dose has decreased to 5.482 MBq. It must also
be considered that not all the dose available at injection time is finally injected, there
is always some residual left in the syringe, so the residual dose measured needs to be
calculated at injection time so that it can be subtracted from the available dose at
injection time. The residual dose measured at 11:08 is 2.29 MBq, which at injected
time (10 minutes before) corresponds to 2.44 MBq. Thus, the real injected dose is then
5.482 −2.44 = 3.042. The scan starts 64 minutes after injection, so correction for decay
during this time is also necessary. The final dose corrected at scan time is 2.031 MBq.
Variability due to differences in injected dose can then be removed by dividing the
measured activities by the activity at time of scan (calculated using Equation 4.1). It is
essential for quantitative assessment of tracer concentration to calculate a measure that
is comparable among subjects. This measure is known as the Standard Uptake Value
(SUV) and is calculated by dividing the measured activities by the injected dose and
multiplying the result by the body weight. Therefore, it is not only necessary to correct
for injected dose but also for body weight, which will reduce variability introduced by
differences in animal size. To calculate the SUV a MATLAB script is generated, included
in Appendix D.

The reference image is created from 9 subjects within the baseline group (Figure 4.6),
from which their individual dynamic images are summed over the time dimension to
create a static PET image of each animal. Each of the individual scans is normalized by
a 12 degrees of freedom (dof) affine registration into the space of the randomly chosen
representative image, which in this case corresponds to that of Rat 36.
The affine
registration is performed through the maximization of the normalized cross-correlation
cost function. The nine images -now located in the same space- are then averaged to
create the reference image, which will be used as the standard most representative image.
Once the reference image is computed, the second step is to normalize each of the nine
individual images to the space of that reference image by affine registration (Figure
4.7). Again, the normalized cross-correlation cost function is maximized so as to obtain
the maximum similarity possible between each of the transformed nine images and the
reference image. Third, as done in the previous step, an average is computed of the
previously aligned images. Finally, this averaged image is left-right flipped to create an
additional image that is also normalized into the average template space. The final PET
template is constructed as an average between both the flipped and unflipped versions
of the averaged image [46]. While all registrations in this section are performed using
flirt, the averages are computed using fslmaths, both included in FSL.
For future definition of volumes of interest that will be used during PET kinetic modeling,
as well as for results to be able to be reported in a common framework, the PET template
is normalized to the LE space (step 6). The spatial alignment of the PET template to
the MR standard image in this case is based upon an affine spatial transformation. The
affine transform that minimizes the distance between the input image and the atlas
template is manually calculated using the Fuse It tool of PMOD.

4.5
Kinetic modeling
Quantification of dopamine D2/3 receptors in rat brain is done using Logan reference
tissue modeling in the tool PKIN from PMOD. The cerebellum is chosen as a reference
region because it is practically devoided of receptors. The value of k′
2 is an input param-
eter which must be manually entered as an estimate using older reference values and in
this case is 0.44 [51]. The model also needs the time activity curves of the reference and
target volumes of interest entered as input.
Time activity curves (TAC) represent the concentration of 18F-Fallypride radioactivity
contained in the PET image as a function of time for each voxel in the image. They
present two drawbacks: the number of voxels in the image is very large and some TACs
are noisy [38]. Therefore, another approach is to calculate the TACs of volumes of inter-
est. All voxels inside the VOI are averaged to generate a mean tissue activity curve of
each volume. The TACs in this Thesis are generated from the previously extracted VOIs
applied to the dynamic images. Four different VOIs are studied bilaterally using the
cerebellum as the reference region. The VOIs are nucleus accumbens, ventral tegmental
area, dorsal striatum and medial prefrontal cortex. The TAC CT(t) of each of these re-
gions is transformed and plotted as a function of the transformed reference TAC CND(t)
(see Equation 2.12). Only the last part of this plot starting at time t* can be fitted by
a straight regression line. This time t* is estimated after no more significant increase in
slope is observed [25].

CHAPTER5
Results and discussion
The main objective of this chapter is to discuss the methodology developed and validate
its main steps: (1) PET template creation, (2) spatial normalization to LE space and
(3) VOIs extraction. In addition, the developed methodology is applied to determine
drug and dopamine occupancy and results are shown also in this section.
5.1
Registration software and cost function
selection
The creation of a tracer-specific PET template is very important for future comparison of
the data and allows the identification of volumes of interest from which different statistics
will be calculated. This means that all results will be directly reported in coordinates
corresponding to the template space. The overall procedure requires the selection of a
reference image, which makes the registration step more sensitive to the choice of this
image. The most straightforward way to reduce the sensitivity is to perform registration
and averaging twice. Thus, the resulting initial average image is used in the second
iteration as the reference image.
Two alternative software with different cost functions are tested to align and average the
nine PET images of the study group: FSL and AIR. While FSL combines several anal-
ysis tools for FMRI, MRI and DTI brain imaging data, AIR is a more specific software
that contains automated routines mainly for aligning and reslicing images. FSL uses
flirt to carry out image registration using either least squares or normalized correlation
cost functions in the case of intramodal registration. It then relies on the image calcu-
lator fslmaths to perform their averaging. On the other hand, AIR first calculates the
registration parameters using the standard deviation or least squares as cost functions
by applying alignlinear and then reslicing (reslice) the image to the reference. Each
image is interpolated to cubic voxels while the reslicing is being done. To create the
mean image the command softmean is used.
Registration results of the first iteration are evaluated to ensure that the average im-
age created does not show corruption due to a poor registration. The validity of each
registration is assessed by visual inspection of the average image created. Results from
AIR (Figure 5.1) show the reference template created using either least squares or stan-
dard deviation cost functions. Both results show complete misalignment between the

5.2.2
Numerical validation
Analysis of results after image registrations is also assessed quantitatively by 3-dimensional
voxel displacement calculation for the two iterations present in the algorithm. This anal-
ysis is done following a validation methodology suggested by J.Villadsen et al [48]. The
transformations from the individual PET images to Rat36 or to the reference image
are considered as a forward transformation (A). The transformation from either the
reference image or the PET template back to the individual images, which is calcu-
lated using the same spatial normalization procedure as for the forward, constitutes a
backward transformation (A−1). These transformations can be better understood from
Figures 5.5 and 5.6. When forward (A) and backward (A−1) transformations are com-
bined the image voxel coordinates (x, y, z, 1)′ are brought back again to their original
space as (x′, y′, z′, 1)′ (Equation 5.1). The resulting difference between the coordinates
norm is a 3-dimensional voxel displacement. This displacement is due to the averaging
step after the spatial normalization. Only voxels corresponding to the inside of the brain
are used for its calculation by applying a brain mask extracted from the CT image.

Bland-Altman plot baseline scans
Three different types of control scans are used in this Project: baseline, second baseline
(scanned some time after baseline) and vehicle (0 mg/kg of CNO). Second baselines from
Rat88 and Rat92 and vehicle scans Rat84 and Rat88 are grouped together since they are
both scanned after the first baseline scan. To compare them with their corresponding
first baseline scans and therefore, to measure how similar DVR results from consecutive
scans are a Bland-Altman analysis is carried out. Bland-Altman will study the agreement
between second scans and baseline scans for each animal. For each region of each animal,
the DVR difference between the second scan and the first scan will be assigned to the y-
axis, while the mean of the two values will be assigned to the x-axis. The analysis will be
characterized by two parameters: bias and 95% limits of agreement. The first is given by
the average of the differences and the second one is calculated as the mean±1.96·SD [13].
Bland-Altman is preferred over correlation analysis because there exist a natural high
correlation between the two scans. This correlation originates because some regions show
==PAGE:63==

is not directly linked to agreement between the two sets of scans. If regions of high uptake
were to be removed, the regions with low uptake will not show any correlation between
the two sets of scans (Figure 5.11.B).

DVR comparison for different CNO doses
Dopamine D2/3 receptor occupancy is quantified in both striatal and extrastriatal re-
gions using Logan reference kinetic modeling. Given that the neurotransmitter DA will
compete with Fallypride radioligand for binding to the D2/3 receptor, it is expected
that as CNO dose increases, the amount of Fallypride binding to the receptor decreases.
Thus, DVR should decrease and receptor occupancy is expected to increase with increas-
ing CNO doses. For comparison of DVR results for the different CNO doses rats 188,
192, 194 and 202 are excluded because they have missing frames due to an error during
PET reconstruction.
Results (Figure 5.16) show that DVR is lower for all regions of CNO treated scans than
for the baselines. However, it seems that an increasing CNO dose is not directly related
to a decrease in DVR, since VTA is an example where this is not fulfilled. A two-way
ANOVA technique is used to compare DVR means for the different CNO doses. It is
two-way because the influence of two different variables (region and CNO dose) on one
continuous variable (DVR) is studied [41]. Thus, for each row representing a different
region, columns with mean DVR values for each CNO dose are compared to the DVR
mean of the baselines corresponding to all CNO scans. For instance, the DVR mean of
0.5 mg/kg CNO scans is compared to that of the baseline scans for each of the regions
of interest. Resulting p-values from the multiple comparisons of baseline vs CNO doses
are all above 0.05, so there is no apparent significant difference between means.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
The most important conclusion of this Thesis answers the main objective stated in
Chapter 3: a methodology that allows for non-biased automatic spatial normalization
and quantification of 18F-Fallypride uptake in the dorsal striatum has been developed.
Therefore, it is concluded that a MR, CT and PET standard space was constructed,
an automatic spatial normalization of 18F-Fallypride rat images procedure was imple-
mented, volumes of interest based on baseline 18F-Fallypride rat images were extracted
and finally, an automated kinetic quantification methodology of baseline 18F-Fallypride
rat images matched to standard space was also developed. Furthermore, drug occupancy
was estimated. All these main steps have been successfully validated.
The methodology developed can be applied to other tracers (i.e. MHMZ and FDG) or
receptors, with the advantage of reducing investigator bias. In addition, the binding
results obtained may provide tools for new imaging studies of mental diseases treatment
and prevention.
The possible applications could focus on the research of dopamine
release changes as the onset factor of the treatment effectiveness. The patient evolution
could also be followed based on these changes. In addition, the obtained quantification
maps will be calculated on a region basis, which will allow for a region-wise tracer uptake
analysis of the dorsal striatum. The code will be freely available via an open-source
license for other researchers to use.
The main limitation in this Project is the few available scans, specially for vehicle CNO,
which made it hard to derive any robust conclusions from the statistical analysis com-
puted. Thus, for further work it is suggested that more rats are scanned both at baseline
and CNO-treated.

Anatomical planes
Anatomical planes are imaginary 2D slices that are drawn through a 3D space to divide
the subject. They are useful for accurate location and also to show the inside of a 3D
image. There are three planes: coronal, sagittal and horizontal [3]:
• Coronal plane: a vertical plane divides the anterior (front) and posterior (back)
parts of the body.
• Sagittal plane (lateral): a vertical plane divides the body into left and right sides.
• Horizontal plane (transverse): a horizontal plane divides the body into superior
(up) and inferior (lower) parts.

APPENDIXB
MR data preparation
The proposed method for preparation of the MR data was fully developed in a special
course project on the Construction of an MR rat brain atlas carried out previous to this
Thesis. The method includes three main steps:
1. Bias field correction
2. Histogram based equalization
3. Spatial normalization from LE space to Schweinhardt stereotaxic space.
B.1
Bias field correction
The idea behind the algorithm developed for correction of the bias field was done in
Python and can be divided into three steps:
1. Computing an intensity profile across the z-direction of the image, since the gra-
dient field is evident in this direction.
2. Fitting a one-dimensional polynomial into the profile to smooth the high intensi-
ties.
3. Applying the N4BiasFieldCorrection from ANTs [1].
For the first step, the mean of each slice is calculated to create an intensity profile (see
Figure B.1). The mean is calculated only from those voxels with value above a threshold
previously calculated. The threshold defines the value of the background noise (dark
pixels) present in the image and calculated as the mean intensity of the entire image
divided by 8. This is done mainly to remove background noise from the image.

Histogram equalization aims at spreading out the most frequent intensity values over
a broader range, specially when there are clustered gray level values (low contrast).
The histogram equalizing transform calculates the cumulative distribution function by
accumulating all of the probabilities of occurrence for each of the gray levels less than
or equal to each gray level k.

B.2.1
Spatial normalization
Spatial normalization transformation matrix of the MR atlas to the standard MR tem-
plate in LE space provided is useful for comparison of our results to those obtained in
studies using that framework [40]. In this way, changes in the tracers uptake calculated
in the stereotaxic space will be able to be reported in the same space. In addition,
the brain mask also provided with the atlas could be use for brain segmentation of our
images. The alignment of the images in this case is based upon an affine spatial trans-
formation. This transformation compensates not only for brain positioning, but also for
differences of size between the two images as well as differences caused by the extraction
of the brain from its enclosed position in the skull. The inverse of this transformation
could also be applied to our images in LE space to report our reports in the stereotaxic
space instead.
The registration is focused on a region of interest, that is why it is only applied to the
previously cropped image. This is done to avoid registration failing due to too much
”distracting” image content.
The registration algorithm implemented in this case is
based on the east squares with intensity rescaling registration metric and a rigid body
6 parameter model. The implementation is done using AIR:

least squares with intensity rescaling (-x 3) cost function with a predicted change of 0.1
(-c 0.1). Thresholds of 7000 are set for both images (-t1 7000 -t2 70000). The canonical
MR is then resliced to match the MR template.

registrations are computed, since Rat36 is not registered to itself.
flirt -in Rat46_Head_Base_corrected_avg.nii -ref

Rat46_Head_Base_corrected_avg_reg1.nii -omat Avg46to36reg1.mat

fslmaths Rat46_Head_Base_corrected_avg_reg1.nii -add

images using the transformation matrix generated, but in order to apply our validation
