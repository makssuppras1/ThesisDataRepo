Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a sustainable alternative to the current way of
living, based on a reduction of the space demand per person. The aim is to reduce the
global impacts caused by the construction sector, which is currently estimated to be
responsible for 30% of the green house gasses globally. To achieve this goal, a flexible
system is developed according to the current legislation in Denmark. The system is
evaluated with LCA assessments performed in SimaPro and compared to the planetary
boundaries, revealing that the system can not be considered Absolute Sustainable. The
issues are identified to be the amount of consumed materials as well as the energy
production for heat electricity and production. Thus it is recommended to be even more
extreme in the development of the system and optimize the way of producing energy to
be consisting of only renewable energy.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
At all times the climate on the planet Earth has been changing, leading to both ice
ages and hotter periods, but today the changes are happening faster than ever, due
to man made climate changes. Man made climate changes are a consequence of the
industrialization, where the burning of fossil fuels are used in production, for comfort
and transportation and the materialism in general, is increased. The consequences of the
rapid heating are many, but most of them are related to the rising surface temperature of
the planet, making global warming a very common term in this context. The increasing
temperature leads to melted ice and snow from the poles and mountains resulting in an
elevated sea level, which limits the area of land. With the heat comes drought, hunger
and diseases. The consequences are countless and they will affect all living creatures [28].
In the last decade the topics of sustainability and global warming are getting more
and more attention and the fear of what the future will bring is growing and forcing
changes in the way we behave as human beings. There are many potential individ-
ual responses to this topic, and the awareness is rapidly increasing while more and more
families implement vegetarian days, car pooling, ecological products and local vacations.
Every individual contribution to lower global warming is good, but it is not only the
individual person, who has to change habits and procedures.
In Denmark, which will be the subject through this thesis, one of the major con-
tributers to the issue of global warming is the construction sector, responsible for 40%
of the energy consumption and 30% of the emitted greenhouse gasses globally [5]. These
are well known facts and in Denmark the Building Regulation, BR20, is implemented to
limit the issue. In this set of rules, the required properties for the building envelope is
defined as an attempt to reduce the heat loss during the lifetime of a building, focusing
on the phase of use. It has become more common to re-insulate existing buildings as
well, which also reduces the energy consumption during the use phase, but the time has
come to be ore convincing in the attempt to create better alternaltives and reduce the
impacts of the sector.
In 2015, 196 countries signed an agreement to minimize climate change with the pri-
mary goal to reduce the emissions from human activities [23] and in 2016 United Nations
Organization, UN, presented the new 17 Sustainable Development Goals, considering all
aspects of a better future on all three basic aspects of sustainability: Environmental,
Social and Economic. Both of these actions are examples of how the issue is approached,
and they illustrate how much attention the topic gets.

1 Introduction
The intention with this thesis is to develop a housing system, which challenges the
traditional way of expanding cities and reduces the impacts on the environment. To
make an attractive solution, it requires that both functionality, design, building tech-
nicalities and sustainability are considered and work well together. The system will be
developed based on Danish circumstances and laws, but if desired it can be developed
further to fit the demands in other countries as well.
Basically, the idea is to challenge the current way of thinking the housing structure.
When designing a house, including all necessary aspects it takes a lot of time and ex-
pertise to get a fully legal and rational product. This thesis applies existing technical
aspects of the industry with the specific aim to investigate the environmental perfor-
mance of such a conceptual home.
Therefore, some of the topics included are only
covered thoroughly enough to deliver results for the process of assessing the environ-
mental impacts and discuss them. Since the scope of the project is to investigate and
discuss the environmental impacts and sustainability of the proposed housing system, it
is beyond the scope of the project to carry out the normally required complete statics
calculations.
The first step towards a good result is understanding the current way of living, as well
as the criteria Danes have when choosing a new home. This leads to the investigation
presented in the first section, followed by a presentation of the aims and visions for
the flexible housing system of this thesis. Finally, the basics of sustainability will be
introduced.

1.2
Relative and absolute sustainability
Along with the increasing interest in the environment, certifications of products and
systems have become more common in the attempt to create more sustainable solutions.
Those certifications make it easy to compare the performance of different products and
it has a great branding value for any company that can get a decent score.
There is a downside to those evaluations, though, as they are only performed in rela-
tion to existing products. This is a problem as the interpretation of a good grade is that
it is a decent and environmentally friendly product, but the assessment does not con-
sider the resources of the planet, only that one solution is relatively better than another.
Therefore, this assessment is termed Relative Sustainability, RS. Another problem is
that it does not account for future circumstances. E.g. the energy mix can be improved
to consist of renewable energy, only [4]. This might affect the environmental impact
of some systems and materials and maybe even change the conclusion of what is good
and what is bad. For instance, it would make less sense to re-insulate an old house if
the energy production did not pollute. Thus the production of the insulation material
might be worse due to the consumption of material resources.
The concept of Absolute Sustainability, AS, is quite different, as it quantifies the
resources of the planet, and estimates the available amount for any service or product.
The difficulties of quantification is related to the available data describing the resources
of the planet and the fundamental idea of dividing resources between people. Previous
articles like ”Planetary boundaries: Guiding human development on a changing planet”
[56] and ”How to bring absolute sustainability into decision-making: An industry case
study using a Planetary Boundary-based methodology” [51] have assessed the quantity
of resources of the Earth in 15 categories. Each of them are held up against the natural
background levels, leaving a safe operating space for the humanity to operate within.
The background levels are caused by natural activities like volcanic events, causing emis-
sions. Thus the full capacity of the Earth can not be assigned to humanity. The full
planetary boundaries of the Earth minus the natural background levels leaves the safe
operating space of the planetary boundaries [56].
The article ”The absolute environmental performance of buildings” [5], has con-
tributed to the topic by assessing the building industry in relation to the safe operating
space. The article states that if the environmental impacts from construction is de-
creased by 89%, energy consumption by 80% and the living area per person by 60%, it
will be possible to achieve an absolute sustainable housing solution in Denmark. If the
area of living is reduced by 60%, it will leave approximately 20 m2/person. This will be
the basis for the developed system in this thesis.

1.3 The flexible house
7
1.3
The flexible house
The intention with this project is to create a housing system, which is adjustable re-
garding area. With Brejnrod’s article [5], as a starting point, it should be possible to
design an absolute sustainable housing solution of 20 m2/person. This thesis aims to
design a system that can provide this without making it necessary to move every time
there is a change in the family dynamic.
The housing system is meant as a possibility and encouragement for any person to
live in the same place throughout a lifetime. In the earlier section 1.1 it was learned,
that less than half of all changes of homes are for geographical reasons. The housing
system has to fulfill the requirements stated in current legislation, but otherwise it is
very atypical compared to how we build today in Denmark. Especially, it challenges the
idea of many square meters and the need to move due to changes in the household size.
The vision of the system is to create a very flexible housing possibility for households,
HH, in the size from 1-9 people. It is meant as a substitution for the classical and
very often quite big single-family house. As this type of housing is probably the most
personal one, it sets some significant demands for the design, aesthetics and options for
personalizing the system.
There are other expected benefits due to the change of life style. For instance, less
space leads to less consumption of furniture, clothes, electricity, heat and ventilation. It
also limits the need to change perfectly fine furniture due to convenience, when moving
anyway. This will not be implemented in the assessments though, as the following be-
havior is difficult to predict, as there is a risk too, that people spare money and spend
them on vacations or other possible polluting activities.
To make sure the system is realistic, different aspects will be taken into consideration.
They will not be treated equally in this thesis, since some of the topics are somewhat
unrelated to the actual question: ”Can we build a 20 m2/person housing system with
an absolute sustainable profile, in Denmark?”
The question can be divided into four main aspects; Energy, statics, design and sus-
tainability.
Energy covers construction of the building envelope, installations and thermal perfor-
mance of the house. It will be investigated for some house examples in the licensed
software Be18, developed by SBi, for documentation.
Statics include calculation of the sizes of columns and beams, necessary to keep the
house standing and not collapse or fly away. This part will be done somewhat superfi-
cial, as this is not the competences this thesis aims to present, though it is essential for
the amount of material assessed from a sustainable perspective.
Design considers aesthetics, functionality and flexibility of the system. This is where
joints are developed along with clever solutions for installations and building elements.

1 Introduction
Sustainability is the actual topic, evaluating the whole system. If the system is not
absolutely sustainable it is the aim to examine what has to be done differently in order
to achieve this goal. Sustainability will be included through the whole process as a tool
to choose the best materials and solutions for the system.
In short the idea is based on people living on few square meters, with an adjustable
perimeter and room division. The concept also supports people staying in the same area,
making a safe atmosphere and neighbourhood without living in an actual commune.

Design and challenges
A good design is essential for this project because it is fundamental for whether or not

Those will be estimated to create basis for technical calculations,

2.1.1
Criteria
The design of the system has to fulfill many different criteria, which can be categorized
in three groups.
The first criteria is the basic idea, which made the motivation for
this project. It is described in Section 1.3 ”The flexible house”, but in short, it must
facilitate any family with ≈20 m2/person and be adjustable at any time. Secondly, it
has to meet the criteria of the family living there, within the boundaries of the concept,

2.1.3.1
Membranes and vapour barriers
As described, Denmark has many legislation for new buildings including tightness and
membranes protecting the building from moist issues. Hot air can contain much more
water than cold air, making a great risk for water to condensate inside a wall construc-
tion, causing rot and health threatening fungus. Therefore, there is a demand for vapor
barriers in facades, which, as a rule of thumb, are placed one third in the wall from the
warm side. Besides this, the water from a wet room (bathroom) is considered a great
risk for the construction as well. Therefore, the bathroom is one of the most compre-
hensive rooms to design and dimension. One of the rules for bathrooms is that the floor
construction must contain a membrane, which stretch up on the wall in a height of min-
imum 10 cm. This challenges the possibility of expanding the house in the direction of a
bathroom, as it is not a possible to remove the wall without destroying the membrane,
while the alternative is to have a facade in the middle of the house. A solution to this
issue is to make a wall system consisting of two parts; one with structural properties
and one with thermal properties. This makes it possible to extend in any direction,
without messing with membranes that protects the construction from moist. The other
advantage is that it makes it easy to attach the vapour barrier between the walls and
secure the pieces tightly to both the walls and each other. Thus the system is defined
like presented in Figure 2.6. The blue colors identifies both kinds of membranes. The
grey color identifies the construction part with insulation and the yellow color identifies
the desired surface of the bathroom walls. The floor and footing is covered with tiles.

2.1.3.2
Placement of facade
With the system of walls, a new question arises: Where is the facade placed? Two
possibilities are identified, and both have advantages and disadvantages as the decision
affects the slab sizes. Regardless of the chosen system, it is important that the ratio
between the sides is 1:2 to allow the combinations shown and described in Section 2.1.2.
The first possibility is to place the facade on the slab, making it easy to expand
the system equally for each person. The walls will take up a decreasing part of the area
when the size of the household increases, which results in more useful area per person.
If this system is chosen, the dimensions of a slab is 2,25 m x 4,5 m resulting in an area
of 20, 25 m2, which is considered tolerated regarding the concept saying 20 m2/person.
The disadvantage of this system is that the inner walls will be placed uneven due to the
space of the facade. This complicates the use of foundation screws, as they have to be
placed directly beneath the walls to take the load straight. Through the work with the
conceptual drawings and use of SketchUp, it also became clear that the roof would have
to span different distances.
The second possibility is to make the slab smaller and attach the facade on the edge
of it. This makes space for the carrying wall on the edge of the slab. The main disad-
vantage of this system is that it does not allow the system to be expanded linearly to
the number of residents. If this system is chosen it is necessary to know the dimensions
of the walls, before the exact slab size can be defined.

2.1.3.3
Roof slope
The roof must have a slope to make the water run of. The necessary angle depends on
the material used on top. In this case a small angle is to be preferred, as it reduces the
need for material and makes the elements easy to handle. With asphalt roof paper, an
angle of 3◦is sufficient. The angle will be created with a wedge of insulation on top of the
actual roof. The advantage of creating a steep roof is that the options for cladding are
many, yet the amounts will increase. A steep roof also increases the load from wind on
the construction, why the low roof is chosen despite the not so environmentally materials
in roof paper. The market of different roof paper options is expanding too, but the risk
of choosing something new and innovative must be considered carefully.
2.1.3.4
Lot sizes
The houses needs to be placed in a system, to make an optimal solution. From the
investigation of current housing tendencies in section 1.1 it was learned that less than
1% of the Danes lives in households of 8 people or more. It also shows that approximately
98% of all households consists of 5 people. This information is used to estimate how big
a house must be able to be. The solution developed here is only in one floor, though it
would be interesting to challenge it into a higher structure in future studies. The most
typical way of expanding is in rectangles or quadratics, and in this case the quadratic
is convenient, as it makes equal daylight possibilities for all desired orientations of the
house. When creating a square it makes space for 9 people in a household, which easily
covers the usual needs of a household. From the description of the facade placement, it
was learned that the maximum slab size is 4,5 m, resulting in a lot size of maximum
4, 5 m · 3 = 13, 5 m
2.1.3.5
Installations
It is not only the foundation screws that sets up requirements for grid, but also the
installations. The installations needs access to the house from the ground and it is most
convenient to place these close to the bathroom and kitchen, where water is needed.
Therefore, the placement of the installations will make some restrictions in the design
freedom, why it is desired to place them as strategically as possible.

Roof
The angle of the roof is 3◦, which leads to a maximum roof height of 0,71 m when the
grid allows a length of the house to be maximum 13,5 m. This will affect the load on the
roof, because it takes a sloped layer of insulation to create the angle. To begin with, the
roof will only be designed with a cover of asphalt roofing, but it will not affect the con-
struction if it is changed to another light covering, later on. Besides sizing the structural
part of the roof, it is necessary to consider the thermal performance of it. According to
Rockwool the U-value for a roof must be equal to or less than 0, 20 W/(m2K) [2]. It
is also important to consider the placement of the vapor barrier, which which require
different placements, depending on the type of roof. There are two kinds of roofs. The
Warm roof, which is insulated all the way through. Here the vapor barrier is placed
1/3 in, from the warm side. And the Cold roof, which is ventilated, making the roof
surface cold in winter time. Here the vapor barrier is placed below the insulation.
The materials investigated for the roof construction are:
• Wooden laths
• Posi-Joist™(a construction of wood and steel)
• Trapez profiles of steel
For all the roof constructions the span and the width of an element is important for
defining the carrying structure in dimension and for laths and Posi-Joist also the amount
and distance between them. To make sure the system can handle the loads in reality and
make space for changes due to the placement of the facade, the size of all roof-elements
is defined to be 4,5 m x 2,25 m. The span will influence on the design freedom, but
in cases where the rooms are desired to be very big, columns can be developed for the
purpose.

2.2.1.3
Steel trapez profile
This product is very different from the two wooden ones, and it should be placed in the
full area of the roof. The measurements and shape of the profile is defined by tables
like previously, but this table requires more information about the structural situation
of the roof. First, the span is defined as 4,5 m. Then there are two values to calculate;
the strength and the deformation. Those values are both load combinations.
The strength is defined as ULS ”Ultimate Limit State” and is calculated as the most
extreme scenarios of self weight, life load and natural loads.
The deformation, SLS
”Service Limit State” ,is the usual load combination on the building, where there are no
dominating natural loads. The self weight of the roof is estimated on basis of the other
roof constructions as this one is expected to be lighter. Therefore a start weight of the
roof is 0, 55 kN/m2. The load combinations, LC, depend on the self load, the snow load
(Appendix A.2), wind load and life load. The wind load for the roof is neglected, as its
slope is so small, making the forces small as well.
• ULSSR2,max Dominating snow 1, 8 kN/m2
• SLSSR Dominating snow 1, 3 kN/m2
From the bullet points above, it can be seen that the worst scenario is caused by a
dominating snow load. Now the task is to find a dimension table for a trapez steel
profile and define the size. A small one is to prefer, as it will make the house lower
and therefore less exposed to wind loads. According to Muncholm [47] the profile, EM
89R/915 with a steel thickness of 0,88 mm can perform a strength of 2, 64 kN/m2 and a
deformation of 1, 38kN/m2, which covers the needs in this construction. The shape of

2.2.2
Walls
For the carrying walls, the following materials are investigated:
• Steel columns
• Light concrete elements
• Wooden frame system
All walls are considered placed on top the terrain deck, and there are two kinds as
described in Section 2.1.2. The interior load carrying ones, and the facade elements,
which provides the thermal properties. The carrying inner wall will provide a small
thermal resistance, which varies depending on the construction. Therefore, the thermal
performance, which must be 0, 3 W/(m2 · K) or lower [6], will be calculated for each of
the constructions.
The dimensions of the walls are considered carefully, as they are hosts for some of
the most important functions of the house, including stability, doors and windows. In
some cases the measurements of the elements are central for the structural elements,
why some of the thoughts are included here. The wall elements of wood or steel works
as frames, which means they must have a column at the end of each element. Besides
this, the general distance between the columns might be disturbed by openings, why the
seizes of those matters.
The doors chosen are functional for wheelchairs with a width of 1000 mm, and a
height of 2200 mm. The windows are quite big, ensuring it is possible to get enough
daylight into the building. The measurements are 850 mm x 1450 mm. The important
part is to place the openings strategically, as it is desired to be able to implement an
opening in elements creating a corner. It is expected that the smallest wall element
occurring in the building envelope is 2000 mm wide, while the absolute maximum thick-
ness of the walls is expected to be 400 mm. To make this possible it is expected to place
all doors and windows 600 mm from the edge of the element, while the distance on the
other side will depend on the dimensions of the element.
2.2.2.1
Wall cladding
The U-value will depend on the chosen internal cladding making it necessary to consider
the options.
The considerations for cladding are presented here and it lead to the
conclusion of assuming that wood is used as cladding on both sides of every wall. The
options for cladding on the walls are endless, but some are more common or interesting
than others, and in this thesis it is chosen to include wood, shale, gypsum and cork.
There are differences between internal and external cladding even though they consist
of the same material.
Internally for steel or wood construction
If the wall construction is made of wood, it can be stabilized by the cladding. This
can be done with a wood board. According to Træ.dk, it requires a thickness of 12
==PAGE:38==

2 Methodology
mm, regardless of the type, choosing between Particle board, fibre board or plywood
[58]. Those are quickly compared from an environmental perspective revealing that the
choice of a particle board is the more sustainable choice, assuming that the handling of
the waste wood is equivalent. See Appendix C for the assessment.
Gypsum is also a common solution, and again there are many different types. Here
it is chosen to prioritize the durability and strength, so it can stabilize the wooden wall
construction and be transported without too high a risk, and thus the type used is fiber
gypsum.
Internally for concrete construction
If it is chosen to apply a wooden cladding it will only have an aesthetic purpose and the
layer can be quite thin, why it is decided to choose a 5 mm particle board. The same
goes for gypsum, which would have the same dimensions of 5 mm, and consist of fiber
gypsum as for the wooden construction. In this case there is a third scenario of applying
cork on the wall, to create a better acoustic. This layer would be 2 mm thick.
Externally for all construction types
The external cladding is placed on the thermal layer of the wall construction and on
top of the wind barrier. All types of cladding will be applied with a system creating
a distance to the wind barrier, to reduce the amount of wind and water to enter the
construction. All considered types of cladding can be applied externally regardless of
the construction type, and they are considered to be of the same dimensions as well.
If the material chosen is wood, it will be applied on horizontally placed wooden fixing
battens. The wood is decided to be particle board as well and 12 mm thick to make it
strong enough for the climate.
In cases with shale, it will be applied on a steel system appearing for every 200mm
in the full length of the building.

2.3.1
Slab and lot size
As described in Section 2.1.3.2 ”Placement of facade”, the placement of the facade
affects the load distribution in the building. After calculating the loads of the building,
it became clear, that the load must be transferred directly from the carrying walls down
to the foundation screws and not distributed horizontally by the slab in the process.
Therefore, it is decided to move the facade to the edge of the building. The thickness
of the facade is defined being 150 mm in Section 2.2.2 ”Walls”, resulting in a slab size
reduced to 4,2 m x 2,1 m. This results in an area for 1 person of 20, 25 m2, but as the
size of the household increases, the area will not be equal to 20 m2/person but even
smaller. With a slab size of 4,2 m on the long side the lot size must be a square of:
4, 2 m · 3 + (2 · 0, 150 m) = 12, 9 m
2.3.2
Ceiling system
It is decided to use a suspended ceiling to make space for installations under the roof.
This solution has two main advantages. First, it will hide the connections between walls
and roof. Secondly, the it creates easy access to the installations, which will be changed
along with the building structure, limiting the damage to the building aesthetics. There
are tons of options on this market, but the main reason to look into this here, is to
quantify the amounts for the LCA.
In general the system weighs 13 kg/m2 and consists of the gypsum boards and a steel
structure. The gypsum boards weighs 8, 3 kg/m2, leading to a steel mass of 4, 7 kg/m2.

2.4
Sustainability and scenario choice
Sustainability plays a major role in this project, as the main goal is to design an ab-
solute sustainable housing system, meaning that any person can live there and only
use the resources within the capacity of the Earth, which are allocated to this persons
housing. In order to achieve that, it is necessary to evaluate the presented options from
an environmental perspective and be ready to go back in the process and redo some
decisions. At this point it might seem like sustainability has not affected anything yet,
but actually it has been paid many thoughts. As an example, the choice of foundation
is based on both practical and environmental reasons as steel is a 100% recyclable in
case of damages on the screws, which are 100% reusable.
Typically, it is relevant to compare smaller pieces of different materials or solutions
to do the right choices along the way. This could be on the insulation, for instance,
but in this case the whole system is desired to perform within the carrying capacity and
thus it is relevant to see how the elements perform together. Previously the elements
was designed with different structural materials and this section contains an assessment,
of which one to choose. To do that, it is necessary to define the goal and scope of the
system according to the recommended theory and practice [22]. This part is valid for
all assessments in the thesis.
Next, the planetary boundaries are defined and quantified, as they will be the bench-
mark of the main assessments. This is followed by a setup of the first assessment holding
18 scenarios to be compared based on the evaluated constructions in Section 2.2 ”Con-
struction and thermal properties. This includes the inventory and results as well, which
will be essential for the choice of elements for roof, walls and slab.
When comparing the elements and benchmark them to the PB, the flaws of the
system from an environmental perspective can be identified and thus reduced. This is
done for the chosen scenario before the last assessment, which evaluates the resource
consumption of the house through a lifetime with changes and development.

2.4.1
Goal definition
The following steps are important to make the assessments transparent for others to
repeat or use for other assessments in the future. It consists of 6 small descriptions, which
clarifies the purpose of the assessments, though only the first three are relevant to this
project. The methodology follows the advises and template of ”Life Cycle Assessment
Theory and Practice” [22].
2.4.1.1
Intended application
This study aims to help decision making, when creating an absolute sustainable housing
system. It will partly consist of a comparative assessment between different combinations
of elements creating 12 solution scenarios. It is also the intention to identify hot spots
and consider how they can be avoided. Finally, it will be assessed if the system can be
considered absolute sustainable, and if not, the issues will be defined.
2.4.1.2
Method assumptions and impact limitations
The system is assessed according to the current circumstances for the Danish energy mix
and laws regarding building practice, which both are topics in constant development.
The method of allocating the resources of the planet between people and services is
based on equal rights and economic expenses, as the money spent is assumed to indicate
the importance of a service and thus the amount of resources necessary. This method
also requires assumptions regarding how people want to use the housing system, though
each household has a great design freedom to do it differently from the examples of this
thesis. There are limitations on using expenses as a measurement for allocation of the
planetary boundaries, but it is a topic, with all kinds of data, making it possible to be
quite specific, compared to using data for the emissions.
2.4.1.3
Reasons for carrying out the LCA study and decision
context
The LCA is done to challenge the classic way of living in Denmark. The decision context
is situation C1, which is a descriptive approach that does not interact with other systems,
but includes the full footprint.
he environmental impact will be assessed for different sizes of the system and com-

done for single-family houses are found in literature. All assessments are done on a
cradle-to-grave model, including both midpoint and endpoint LCIA (Life Cycle Inven-

2.5 Installations and energy performance, Be18

2.5
Installations and energy performance,
Be18
When building new houses in Denmark, it has to perform within an energy frame which
is stated by BR18 [6]. There are different classes, where two of them counts for renova-
tion of buildings only. The other two are the basic ”Energy frame BR18” and ”Energy
frame low energy”, which is a very energy friendly category to achieve. In this case the
main goal is to achieve the basic energy level for new buildings, and it is surely a chal-
lenge for this small house, as the energy demand for a building is calculated in relation
to the area with the unit kWh/m2 year.
The calculation of the energy consumption of a house can be done in many ways,
but here it is chosen to use the software ”Be18”. It considers all aspects of the house,
including the energy sources, the building envelope, heat gain through windows as well
as from people and equipment, heat system, water consumption and even alternative
private energy production. The software needs a specific example of a house, where
dimensions and orientation is unambiguous. In this case the calculations are done for
a single person house, HH1, from Scenario R, as it is was learned from Section 2.4.5
”Comparative assessment” that this combination of elements performs relatively better
than the other scenarios, while the small layout is considered to be the most critical one.
The energy consumption for the operation of the house is included in the definition
of the planetary boundaries for a household in Section 2.4.3 ”Quantification of planetary
boundaries”, and thus it must be included in the calculations investigating the sustain-
ability of the system. This is why the following calculations are conducted at this point,
but it is also relevant to achieve at least the basic class of BR18 for the system to be build.
This chapter will present some basic calculations for the installations of the house,
to use for the investigation of the energy demand and to verify that the estimated size
of the shaft is sufficient.
There are many ways to supply a residential building with heat and electricity. In
this case a very convenient solution is to use electric floor heating, ventilation and electric
heating of domestic hot water due to the easy installation and minimal space demand.
The downside is that electricity is very expensive, for both the owner and the climate
[24]. According to the Danish Energy Agency, district heating can cost less than 25%
of the price for electricity. Furthermore, the agency states that district heating pollutes
75% less than heating with electricity [24], why heating with electricity is not an option
for this system if making it absolute sustainable shall succeed. Thus the system will
be developed for district heating for floor heating and production of domestic hot water
with an additional ventilation system.

Besides district heating there are other options for heating, but district heating is
by far the most convenient in this context, why it is used as a starting point for this
system. If it is not sufficient enough to meet the energy frames, it will be considered to
add some private energy production as solar panels, heat pumps or geothermal heating.
The system is designed in a way that creates a stationary shaft between the bathroom
and kitchen, where the water and drain is needed. This will make a few limitations of
where kitchen and bathroom can be placed, but there will still be several options, as
it is not required that the house is quadratic.

2.6.1
Transportation
The transportation of the elements will have an impact on the system. This is typically
given in tkm, which is simply calculated by multiplying the distance (km) with the load
in tons (t). None of those values can be calculated precisely, and thus there are quite
some uncertainties on the topic. The significance of those uncertainties is investigated
with a sensitivity analysis in Section 2.7.1. The system is mainly designed to suit the
Danish legislation and climate, why it is expected only to be used in geographical areas
alike, and at the beginning only in Denmark specifically. Imagining that the production
is placed in the northern part of Funen, in the middle of the country, the furthest
distance will be approximately 350 km which is Skagen (in bee-line). Distances like this
will happen when an element is produced, but not every time it is moved to another
house. In those cases the distance is totally unpredictable, but if the total system is
managed well, they will not have to move too far. As it is expected that the buildings

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
From the chapter of results, it is learned that the developed system is not absolute sus-
tainable, as it exceeds the planetary boundaries on 5 of the 13 impact categories, when
housing 1 person. The issue was tracked back to the heat demand and electricity con-
sumption due to the operation of the building as well as the processing of the materials.
But how can this system be developed further to perform within the defined planetary
boundaries and is it even a worthy challenge to the current way of living in Denmark?
4.1
Design
The first aspect to consider in this discussion is the design, concerning functionalities
of the building. This includes technical solutions such as the element composition and
installations and services implemented to fulfill the needs of the residents, but also minor
details as placement of openings in walls and room divisions. The vision of the project
arises many challenges, where especially the utilization of the house, the technical solu-
tions and the fulfillment of the residents needs and the opportunity to personalize the
house must be balanced.
One of the intentions of this project was to include sustainability all the way through
the design phase. It was included, but only to a limited extent, making the gain of the
effort minimal. In a further development of this system, it is recommended to team up
to get some good discussions and more ideas on the table to challenge the traditional
way of developing buildings even more, based on different scientific skills. A develop-
ment of the system could be based on a thorough material study, investigating newer
technologies. In general, an approach could be to calculate some scenarios of material
combinations before designing anything, to make it more clear what materials to prefer
in the development.
The environmental profile of the system also suffers from the rough calculations of the
structural matters, as this is one of the challenging aspects of the building construction.
When developing the system further, it is strongly recommended to have a specialist
look at the calculations as the materials probably can be reduced and still carry the
necessary loads. It might also be valuable to design columns instead of carrying walls as
it requires less material and expands the possibilities of design freedom. The inner walls
==PAGE:122==
112
4 Discussion
of the system could then be thinner but optimized acoustically, increasing the feeling of
privacy despite the limited space. The reduction of material would result in less impacts
from both production and transportation. Thus it can be questioned if the technical
solutions on this matter are optimal for the purpose. When a family chooses to live on
limited space, the approach to the area given might be different from the traditional way
of dividing a house. Some would probably prefer less but bigger rooms, which again will
reduce the consumption of materials, as the development of the layouts in this thesis is
based on a very traditional way of dividing a house.
Today, there is a new trend of tiny houses, which is especially popular in America
and Australia. The concept is very small houses, which are fully utilized with drawers
in the staircase and multi purpose walls shifting the functionality from e.g. kitchen to
bedroom in no time. The perimeter of a tiny house is stationary, but it is clear that
the space is utilized better than in the system developed in this thesis. Both ways of
building are flexible, but in different ways, and the material consumption related to the
element based system leads to wondering if it is the right way to develop the housing
tradition.
4.2
Energy
All buildings have an energy demand making it relevant to consider how this topic is
included in the industry and what the production of it means environmentally. This
arises the question regarding BR18’s role on this matter. Is it necessary to challenge the
way of making regulations to motivate people to live more sustainable?
The system performed within the energy frame of BR18, but it was found that there
were some challenges to it, as the area is very small and the energy frame and demand of
a building is calculated relative to the area and not to the purpose or the people living
there. This means, that if a demanding ventilation unit is installed, it has no conse-
quence if the area of the house is big enough, but it is never taken into consideration how
many people will gain from the fresh, comfortable air it provides. Thus, the building
regulations does not consider the purpose of the installations in relation to the people
it affect, but only that the house as a closed system performs optimally. This way of
approaching the issue might even constrain a building from being absolute sustainable,
if it uses more energy per area but stays within the annual boundaries of the planetary
resources. It might be worth investigating if the function or service can be included in
the legislation of BR18, to adjust the energy demands in relation to the purpose. This is
only a limited gain though as a greener energy production reduces this issue significantly.
In general, the impacts of the system can be reduced significantly, if the energy
production is developed into renewable energy only, but energy will never be with no
emissions at all, due to the production of the devices producing it. For instance windmills

takes up tons of steel and glass fiber, and solar panels takes up metals to absorb the heat.
Upgrading the energy production in Denmark will definitely optimize the system,
along with all other buildings. If the energy production is improved it will be beneficial
for both heat, electricity and manufacturing.
4.3
Environment
The topics of design and energy both leads to the big challenge of sustainability. How
can the issue of polluting building solutions be solved, and who has the responsibility
for making the difference?
The results showed that more people living together improves the environmental
profile, while the introduction stated that 38% of all households in Denmark consists of
only 1 person. Thus a way of reducing the impacts is to make it more attractive to live
more people together. This will also reduce the demand for new constructions, as the
population would need less buildings to house everyone.
An argument for this system being a positive development is the three fundamental
pillars of sustainability, including social, economy and environment.
The adjustable
properties of the system allows people to stay living the same place despite changes in
their needs, minimizing costs and emissions as well as protecting personal relations and
feelings of safety.
The reduced space per person leads back to the allocation of the planetary bound-
aries, and it is wondered if it would be fair to reduce the allocation for furniture and
make it count for the housing instead, due to the reduction of furniture needed.
There are many factors adding to the issue of sustainability in the building industry
and the final answer is difficult to define, though a new trend might be the solution. A
trend can affect all aspects of the topic, from the personal level to the industry, why
legislation and branding might be the efficient way to approach it. Thus the wave of
changes could start as legislation resulting in more knowledge among the population. In
general, people in Denmark, especially the younger generation, has located the burning
platform of global heating, which must be avoided. For the government to create the
first steps towards the change, economy might be included ad a method to motivate the
development in all aspects.
So, can the system be developed further to perform within the defined planetary
boundaries and is it even a worthy challenge to the current way of living in Denmark?
The system can definitely be developed further, but whether it can be done sufficiently
enough is hard to know. The recommended developments are to optimize the energy

production, create the boundaries to make it more attractive for more people to live
together, rethink the layout of buildings, to be more beneficial for the residents but less
material consuming and optimize on the structural elements of the system in an attempt
to reduce the amounts of materials. And yes, it is worth the effort and resources, to
keep challenging the possibilities. This system might not be the final solution, but it
is necessary to change the tradition of building in Denmark as well as the rest of the
World.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The goal was to design an absolute sustainable housing system, suitable for the needs
of an average Danish household with changing needs. The attempt was to include the
perspective of sustainability from the first draft, making an innovative proposal.
The system was developed as elements to make a flexible solution, but it was found
to be challenging to design suitable solutions for all connections, due to the duration of
the project. The building did perform within the energy frame stated by BR18, and was
almost comparable to the low energy frame for the biggest solution of the system.
From the life cycle assessments it became clear that the goal was not reached as
the system does not perform within the safe operating space of the planetary bound-
aries, and thus lacks the title of being absolute sustainable. The smallest of the system
options providing a home for 1 person exceeds the most critical impact category by
a factor of 7, making the process of optimization comprehensive, yet both worth the
effort and necessary. The most comprehensive optimizations also turns out to be the
most beneficial ones, as a green transmission of the energy production will have a ma-
jor impact on the system on every matter. Thus it must be recommended to invest in
renewable energy, of which the responsibility must fall upon the society and not the
building industry itself. For the industry, there are gains in correct handling of waste
as well as correct use of materials and products prolonging the lifetime. The property
of the building being designed for disassembly benefits the impacts of the system at EoL.
The building industry can gain a better environmental profile by including function-
ality in the legislation introduced in BR18. By considering the functionality of a building
and not only the perimeter, it will become more attractive to build smaller and optimize
the building area, making it suitable for the purpose.
