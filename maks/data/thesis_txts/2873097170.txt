Abstract
Cork powder, a by­product of the cork industry, represents a promising yet underutilized
lignocellulosic biomass for the production of fermentable sugars. However, its high con­
tent of suberin and lignin imparts substantial recalcitrance, limiting enzymatic hydrolysis
efficiency. This study investigated the enzymatic saccharification of cork powder following
Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreatment, aiming to enhance sugar release. A Central Com­
posite Design (CCD) was employed to evaluate the effects of cellulase (Cellic CTec3),
hemicellulase (NS22244), and biomass loading (5­15% w/v) on saccharification. The re­
sults demonstrated that sugar release was significantly influenced by hemicellulase load,
with the highest glucose concentrations reaching up to 300 mg/g DM under optimized con­
ditions. Kinetic analysis revealed a rapid release of sugars within the first 4 hours, particu­
larly in assays with high enzymes (CC3≥30 FPU/g DM, hemicellulase = 750µL/g DM) and
low biomass concentrations (<10% w/v). Comparisons with existing literature confirmed
the effectiveness of the applied strategy, achieving sugar yields (up to 100% for glucose)
comparable to or surpassing those from other lignocellulosic feedstocks. Moreover, the
release of phenolic compounds during hydrolysis was quantified, offering insights into the
inhibitory compounds present in the cork derived hydrolyzates. These findings show the
potential of valorizing cork powder through enzymatic hydrolysis, paving the way for its
integration into sustainable biorefinery processes.

Introduction
The international community is currently facing a critical turning point in the 21st cen­
tury, where the combination of climate change, depletion of limited natural resources,
and the continuous growth of the global population require us to reevaluate our industrial
and energy systems. The persistent reliance on fossil fuel­based economies is widely
recognized as unsustainable from both environmental and socioeconomic perspectives
[1]. Fossil fuels are limited and vulnerable to geopolitical influences, and their extraction,
processing, and combustion significantly contribute to greenhouse gas emissions, which
in turn lead to global warming, rising sea levels, biodiversity loss, and extreme weather
patterns [2]. In response to these challenges, the international community is transitioning
toward sustainable alternatives that can ensure long­term energy security.
One promising field in this transition is the rise of a bio­based economy. This economic
model emphasizes the use of renewable biological resources –such as agricultural and
forestry by­products, as well as microbial biomass– to produce fuels, platform chemicals,
materials, and other bio­based products in an environmentally friendly manner. Unlike
fossil fuels, which are geologically limited and non­renewable, biological feedstocks can
be used with relatively lower environmental impacts [3]. Among various bioresources,
lignocellulosic biomass (LCB) stands out due to its global abundance, renewability, and
rich polymeric composition. As the most common form of biomass on Earth, LCB is found
in agricultural residues (such as corn stover and wheat straw), forestry by­products (like
sawdust and bark), and industrial waste, making it a low­cost, non­food­competing feed­
stock for biorefineries [4].
Lignocellulosic biomass consists of three main components: cellulose, hemicellulose,
and lignin [5]. These components are intricately woven into a complex matrix that lends
strength and rigidity to plant cell walls while also rendering the biomass highly resistant
to enzymatic degradation. Converting this matrix into fermentable sugars –the essential
intermediates for microbial production of bioethanol, bioplastics, organic acids, and other
biochemicals– requires overcoming significant structural and chemical challenges. The
crystalline nature of cellulose, the varied and branched structure of hemicellulose, and the
hydrophobic, enzyme­inhibiting characteristics of lignin all contribute to LCB’s resistance
to enzymatic hydrolysis [6]. Consequently, extensive research has focused on developing
pretreatment strategies and tailored enzyme combinations to enhance sugar yields and
improve the overall efficiency of biomass conversion.
However, the large­scale application of these technologies faces several obstacles, in­
cluding high costs, substantial energy requirements, and the production of inhibitory by­
products [7]. Additionally, most research has been focused on a limited range of biomass
types —primarily corn stover, sugarcane bagasse, and softwood residues— leaving nu­
merous industrial by­products largely unexplored [8, 9, 10]. One such underutilized re­
source is cork powder, a by­product of the cork industry, which is particularly abundant in
Mediterranean countries such as Portugal and Spain.
The cork sector is renewable and environmentally friendly but generates a significant
amount of waste during the production of cork stoppers and composite materials. Cork
powder alone accounts for up to 25% of the processed materials and is often relegated
to low­value applications, such as being burned for energy, despite containing valuable
components like polysaccharides, lignin, and suberin [11]. Traditionally, suberin has been
2
==PAGE:10==
seen as a barrier to enzymatic breakdown due to its resistance to degradation and hy­
drophobic properties [12].
Consequently, cork powder has received little attention as
a potential source for fermentable sugars, and there have been few systematic studies
investigating its chemical digestibility or its potential for valorization through biorefinery
platforms.
This project aims to fill this gap by evaluating the feasibility of the enzymatic hydrolysis of
cork powder to produce fermentable sugars. The proposed methodology included a mild
and eco­friendly pretreatment (Liquid Hot Water, LHW) designed to enhance enzyme ac­
cessibility by extracting hemicellulose and modifying lignin structures without the use of
chemicals[13]. Following pretreatment, enzymatic hydrolysis was conducted using spe­
cially formulated enzyme cocktails with additives, consisting of cellulase, hemicellulase,
Tween 80, and hydrogen peroxide. These enzymes were selected for their ability to im­
prove saccharification yields through synergistic effects [14]. Meanwhile, Tween 80 and
hydrogen peroxide have been shown to enhance enzymatic digestibility of cellulose [15]
and improve overall saccharification efficiency [16], respectively . The process was fur­
ther optimized using a Central Composite Design (CCD) to evaluates both individual and
interactive effects of key process variables, such as enzyme and biomass loadings.
From both scientific and technical perspectives, this project makes significant contribu­
tions to several critical areas.
First, it aims to enhance the limited understanding of
cork powder as a lignocellulosic feedstock, providing essential compositional details and
demonstrating its viability for enzymatic saccharification. Second, it examines how en­
zyme synergy and process additives can be employed to overcome the specific recal­
citrance of cork biomass. Third, the use of statistical optimization techniques enables
the formulation of practical insights into process efficiency, making this research highly
relevant for scale­up and industrial applications.
In conclusion, this thesis advances the broader goals of the circular bioeconomy by demon­
strating how an abundant, renewable, and currently underutilized waste product can be
transformed into valuable chemical precursors through sustainable and scalable biotech­
nological methods. The findings presented here not only provide proof of concept for the
valorization of cork powder but also contribute to broader initiatives in biomass upgrading,
enzyme optimization, green chemistry, and industrial biotechnology.

Objectives
The primary objective of this study was to valorize cork powder, an underutilized by­
product of the cork industry, by converting it into fermentable sugars through enzymatic
hydrolysis. To achieve this aim, the following specific objectives were defined:
• To assess the impact of Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreatment on cork powder by
evaluating changes in its chemical composition, with the aim of improving enzymatic
accessibility.
• To optimize the enzymatic hydrolysis of cork powder using tailored enzymatic cock­
tails by systematically evaluating their individual and synergistic effects. Through
kinetic sugar release assays and statistical modeling via Central Composite De­
sign (CCD), this objective aims to identify the most effective enzyme combinations
and biomass loading for maximizing fermentable sugar yields, while also monitor­
ing phenolic compound release to support potential integration into a sustainable
biorefinery scheme.

3
Literature review
The growing demand for sustainable alternatives to fossil based products has increased
interest in renewable biological resources. Among these, lignocellulosic biomass (LCB)
is notable for its abundance, biodegradability, and potential for conversion into high­value
bioproducts. Cork, a byproduct of the cork industry, has a unique chemical composition
that is rich in lignin and polysaccharides, making it a promising substrate for biotechno­
logical applications. Understanding the fundamental characteristics and treatment of this
biomass is essential for developing efficient processes to convert it into valuable com­
pounds.
3.1
Lignocellulosic biomass as a renewable resource
In the transition towards a bio­based economy, various renewable energy sources, includ­
ing solar, wind, hydrothermal, and biomass, have been explored. Among these, biomass
is emerging as a promising alternative due to its potential to replace petroleum­based
fuels in transportation and chemical production.
LCB is the most abundant material on Earth. It can be sourced from agricultural residues
(such as corn stover and wheat straw), forestry residues (like sawdust and mill waste),
portions of municipal solid waste (including waste paper), and various industrial byprod­
ucts, all of which hold substantial promise for industrial applications.
Composed of approximately 75% carbohydrates derived from cellulose and hemicellu­
lose, LCB contains significant chemical energy, making it highly suitable for a range of
bioenergy uses [17].
Additionally, its relevance spans numerous industries, including
food, feed, pharmaceuticals, and materials, which can derive valuable substances such
as proteins, amino acids, oligosaccharides, phenolic compounds, or sugars for fermen­
tation processes [18]. LCB is characterized by its abundance, biodegradability, and bio­
compatibility [4]. Consequently, converting LCB waste into energy represents a practical
transformation, turning an environmental liability into an economic asset [19].
3.1.1
Structure of lignocellulosic biomass
Lignocellulose comprises the three primary components of plant material: cellulose, hemi­
cellulose, and lignin, which together account for approximately 90% of the dry weight of
plants [5]. The proportion of each component varies by plant species, highlighting the
need to identify the most effective energy conversion methods tailored to each type of
LCB [20].
This structural complexity makes lignocellulose highly resistant to enzymatic degradation.
As a result, efficient hydrolysis is challenged by several key factors:
• Recalcitrance of crystalline cellulose: LCB is inherently resistant to microbial and
enzymatic degradation, which presents a major obstacle to its effective conversion
into bioenergy and biomaterials on a commercial scale. This resistance is a critical
factor that limits the development of efficient lignocellulose breakdown processes
[21].
5
==PAGE:13==
• Lignin protective role for cellulose: lignin forms a robust matrix that physically re­
stricts cellulose fibers from engaging with hydrolytic enzymes, consequently dimin­
ishing water and chemical penetration (which leads to reduced reactivity) and bind­
ing non­productively to cellulases, thus decreasing enzyme effectiveness [21].
In LCB, its components are arranged so that the cellulose and hemicellulosic chains are
embedded in lignin. Parallel cellulose chains bound tightly together by hydrogen bonds
provide rigidity to the plant material, while surrounding hydrophobic lignin “glues” the com­
ponents together and acts as a physical barrier for microorganisms and water. Hemicel­
lulose connects cellulose and lignin through a network of cross­linked fibers. A model of
the molecular structure of the main constituents of LCB (secondary plant cell wall) can be
seen in fig. 3.1.
Figure 3.1: A model of the molecular structure of the main constituents of lignocellulosic
material [22].
3.1.1.1
Cellulose
Cellulose constitutes a significant element of lignocellulose, generally present in quanti­
ties ranging from 35–50% weight [5]. It is a relatively simple yet highly stable polymer
composed of more than 10,000 glucose units interconnected by (β­1,4)­glycosidic bonds,
which facilitate its linear conformation [23]. Although β­1,4­linked glucose represents the
chemical repeating unit, the structural repeat unit is identified as β­cellobiose (fig. 3.2),
[24]. Due to its high molecular weight and limited chain flexibility, cellulose is insoluble in
water; in contrast, glucose monomers and shorter oligomeric forms exhibit solubility [5].
Within cellulose, glucan chains are closely adhered to one another through van der Waals
forces and hydrogen bonding, resulting in crystalline structures termed elementary fibrils.
These fibrils typically consist of approximately 40 glucan chains and measure around
40Å in width, 30Å in thickness, and 100Å in length [25]. Aggregates of elementary fibrils,
which are essentially infinite in length and approximately 250Å wide, are referred to as
6
==PAGE:14==
microfibrils [26]. These microfibrils subsequently associate to form cellulose fibers.
The role of hydrogen bonding within cellulose microfibrils is critical in dictating the lin­
earity of the chains. Hydrogen bonds linking different chains can either foster a degree
of structural organization, resulting in crystallinity, or induce a lack thereof, yielding an
amorphous state [27]. Notably, the amorphous fraction of cellulose is significantly more
susceptible to enzymatic digestion –being 3 to 30 times more digestible– when compared
to its crystalline counterpart [28, 29].
Figure 3.2: Chemical structure of cellulose. Linear β­1,4­linked glucose is the chemical re­
peating unit, while the structural repeat is β­cellobiose, and consequently each glucoside
is oriented at 180° in respect to its neighbors. From Varrot et al., 2003 [24].
3.1.1.2
Hemicellulose
Hemicellulose is recognized as the second most abundant renewable organic material on
Earth, following cellulose, and typically accounts for 20–35% of LCB [29]. The efficient
utilization of hemicellulose as a byproduct in the conversion of lignocellulose into fuels
and chemicals is vital for making the economic aspects of processing wood into chemicals
feasible [6].
In contrast to cellulose, hemicelluloses are complex branched polymers composed of
various sugar monomers [27, 30]. These biopolymers contain between 500 and 3000
monosaccharides, including pentoses (D­xylose, L­arabinose), hexoses (D­mannose, D­
glucose, D­galactose, L­rhamnose), uronic acids (4­O­methyl­D­glucuronic acid, D­glucuronic
acid, and D­galacturonic acid), and acetylated sugars, all linked together by glycosidic and
ether bonds [31, 32] [33].
The specific type of hemicellulose varies according to its monomer composition, influenc­
ing its structure and quantity. The primary groups of hemicellulose include xyloglucans,
arabinans, mannans, and xylans. Hemicellulose plays a crucial role in the interaction
between cellulose and lignin molecules, thereby enhancing the strength of the cell wall
[34], as unbranched hemicellulose forms H­bonds with cellulose fibrils, while side chains
of branched hemicellulose covalently link to lignin. This way, cross­links called lignin­
carbohydrate complexes (LCCs) are formed (fig. 3.3), [35] .
Given its amorphous nature, hemicellulose has low physical strength and can be easily
hydrolyzed by strong acids (leaving cellulose and lignin intact) or by strong bases. In
many cases, diluted acid (0.5–1.0% H2SO4) pretreatment under elevated temperatures
(140–190°C) will degrade most of the hemicellulose to soluble pentose and hexose sugars
[36]. In recent years, its hydrolysis by hemicellulase enzymes has gained increasing at­
tention due to its pivotal role in unlocking fermentable sugars from LCB, improving overall
saccharification efficiency [29].

3.1.1.3
Lignin
Lignin is widely regarded as one of the most complex and least understood molecular
constituents of wood. This intricate aromatic polymer is characterized by its high degree
of branching and amorphous structure, lacking a defined primary structure [31]. Compris­
ing approximately 10–25% of the weight of LCB [30], lignin fulfills critical roles in providing
mechanical strength and water resistance to vascular plants, while also serving as a pro­
tective agent against pathogens and oxidative stress [6].
Chemically, lignin is primarily constituted of three essential monolignols, that can be seen
in fig. 3.4: p­coumaryl alcohol (H), sinapyl alcohol (S), and coniferyl alcohol (G). These
monolignols emerge through the oxidative coupling of their respective phenolic precur­
sors, specifically p­hydroxyphenol for H, syringyl for S, and guaiacyl for G. The methoxy­
lation patterns of these groups on the aromatic ring contribute to significant variations in
the chemical structure of phenylpropane units, which is further influenced by the substi­
tution degree of functional groups [38]. The proportionate presence of each monolignol
depends upon the specific type of biomass, mirroring the complexity observed in hemi­
cellulose.
The unique properties that lignin imparts to plant structure, coupled with its hydrophobic
nature and absence of optical activity, pose substantial challenges to lignin degradation
[39]. Furthermore, lignin has been identified as the principal barrier to cellulose degrada­
tion, given its tight association with cellulose fibers [40]. This situation is exacerbated by
the tendency of cellulases to form irreversible complexes with lignin, adversely affecting
the enzymatic efficiency required for hydrolysis and complicating the subsequent recovery
of these enzymes post­hydrolysis [41].
To address these challenges, a pretreatment­step is often required to change the structure
of the cell wall and increase digestibility of the LCB. Therefore, a variety of chemical
pretreatment methods have been developed, with a focus on enhancing lignin degradation
[42]:
• Oxidative delignification, wherein the biodegradation of lignin is facilitated by the
peroxidase enzyme in the presence of hydrogen peroxide [43].
• Organosolv process, which utilizes a combination of organic or aqueous organic sol­
vents alongside inorganic acid catalysts, such as hydrochloric acid (HCl) or sulfuric
8
==PAGE:16==
acid (H2SO4), to disrupt internal lignin and hemicellulose bonds [44].

3.2
Lignocellulosic biomass pretreatment
LCB represents a promising feedstock for the production of biochemicals, attributed to
its abundant availability and cost­effectiveness. In cost­effective industrial processes for
converting lignocellulosic materials into energy carriers such as ethanol and butanol, as
well as other products like organic acids, acetone, and glycerol, it is crucial to hydrolyze
both cellulose and hemicellulose into sugars. These sugars can then be further processed
in subsequent steps [46]. Nevertheless, the inherent recalcitrance of LCB significantly
complicates its utilization. Consequently, pretreatment emerges as a crucial step in its
conversion into various bio­based products. The primary objective of pretreatment is to
enhance cellulose accessibility for enzymatic action, thereby facilitating rapid hydrolysis
with heightened yields [13].
Typically, pretreatment processes serve to augment the enzymatic hydrolysis yield by dis­
rupting the physical barriers of the cell wall, effectively removing lignin and hemicellulose
fractions, diminishing cellulose crystallinity, enlarging pore structure within the biomass,
and increasing the accessible surface area [47, 48]. These modifications enable enzymes
to more effectively access cellulose fibers, thus improving hydrolytic efficiency. The im­
pact of pretreatment on the structural characteristics of LCB is illustrated in fig. 3.5.
An increase in material porosity during pretreatment is particularly significant, as pore
size is a critical parameter that restricts enzymatic hydrolysis. The removal of hemicellu­
lose from the biomass matrix enhances the average pore size of the substrate, thereby
facilitating cellulose accessibility to enzymes and improving the likelihood of hydrolysis.
Additionally, a reduction in particle size amplifies the specific surface area, further en­
hancing cellulose accessibility to enzymatic action. The removal of lignin is equally vital,
as lignin acts as a physical barrier, obstructing the hydrolysis of digestible components
within the substrate [13].
9
==PAGE:17==
Figure 3.5: Effect of pretreatment on the lignocellulosic biomass structure [13].
Determining the optimal pretreatment strategy for diverse raw materials presents a sub­
stantial challenge, as such decisions are heavily dependent on the specific composition
and structural characteristics of the raw biomass [49]. Nevertheless, certain criteria should
be met by all pretreatment methodologies, including [20]:
• Minimal consumption of energy, water, and chemicals, alongside cost­effective op­
erational requirements.
• Necessity for uncomplicated and minimal post­pretreatment procedures.
• Utilization of environmentally benign chemicals that can be easily extracted from
biomass, recycled, and deemed non­toxic.
• Reduction of cellulose crystallinity and modification of the three­dimensional struc­
ture of LCB.
• Decrease in biomass particle size to enhance porosity and increase the surface area
available for enzymatic attack.
• Generation of highly digestible pretreated solids to promote elevated sugar yields
following hydrolysis (with cellulose conversion rates exceeding 90%).
• Preservation of the native lignin structure to facilitate its potential valorization.
• Avoidance of excessive degradation of LCB’s chemical components and the forma­
tion of inhibitory compounds, particularly acetic acid derived from hemicellulose, as
well as furfural and 5­hydroxymethyl furfural (as indicated in 5­HMF) resulting from
sugar decomposition, along with phenolic compounds spawned from lignin break­
down.
The primary pretreatment technologies currently employed can be classified into four cat­
egories: physical, chemical, physicochemical, and biological processes.
3.2.1
Physical pretreatments
These techniques are routinely employed as an initial pretreatment stage to facilitate the
reduction of biomass particle size, enhance the surface area, and decrease polymeriza­
tion and crystallinity of LCB [50]. Notably, these pretreatment strategies are characterized
by their environmental friendliness and minimal emission of toxic substances. However,
a significant limitation is their relatively high energy consumption [50]. Additionally, these
techniques do not effectively eliminate the lignin component from the biomass [51].
10
==PAGE:18==
Milling is a prevalent pretreatment technique employed to reduce the size of biomass par­
ticles, achieved through diverse methods (grinding, milling, shredding...). Each of these
methods yields distinct particle sizes; grinding or milling typically results in particles mea­
suring between 0.2–2 mm, whereas chipping produces larger particles ranging from 10–
30 mm [52].
Extrusion represents another widely utilized physical pretreatment method that facilitates
the depolymerization of LCBs through the application of strong shearing forces, achieved
by passing the biomass through one or two rotating screws within a device known as
an extruder [53]. This process is advantageous due to its short residence times and
adaptability to various temperature conditions [50, 53].
Irradiation of LCB utilizing γ­rays, electron beams, and microwaves has also been shown
to promote cellulose degradation, converting it into oligosaccharides and cellobiose [54].
Microwave irradiation, in particular, employs an electromagnetic field that induces molec­
ular collisions through dielectric polarization and, subsequently, the generation of thermal
energy. This thermal energy serves to disrupt the complex structural configuration of
lignocellulose [50].
The application of ultrasonic waves in the treatment of plant biomass presents an al­
ternate, efficient, cost­effective, and environmentally benign approach. Recent investi­
gations have illuminated the impact of ultrasonic waves on LCB, enhancing both lignin
dissolution and removal, as well as promoting the reactivity of chemical compounds with
biomass components [54]. The propagation of acoustic waves results in acoustic cavi­
tation, characterized by the generation of microscopic bubbles that expand to an optimal
size before collapsing violently [54].
3.2.2
Chemical pretreatments
Chemical pretreatments are quick methods that enhance the effective separation of LCB
components [54]. These processes involve the use of acids or alkali to disrupt the ligno­
cellulosic structure.
• Acid pretreatment: It can be conducted using concentrated acids (ranging from 30–
70%) at low temperatures (below 100ºC) or diluted acids (0.1–10%) at higher tem­
peratures (100–250ºC)[50]. The latter approach is generally preferred due to safety
considerations, recovery challenges, and cost­effectiveness [55]. Various inorganic
and organic acids have been used, including sulfuric acid (H2SO4), hydrochloric
acid (HCl), nitric acid (HNO3), and acetic acid, with H2SO4 being the most com­
monly used. While concentrated acids are effective at hydrolyzing cellulose, their
toxicity, corrosiveness, and the high costs associated with reactor construction limit
their practicality. The acid pretreatment method cleaves glycosidic bonds, trans­
forming polysaccharides into oligomeric and monomeric sugars [56]. However, the
primary limitations of this approach include a low concentration of reducing sugars
in the pretreatment liquid —resulting from the high liquid­to­solid ratio— and the for­
mation of inhibitory compounds (such as furfural) that require additional steps for
sugar concentration and inhibitor removal [57].
• Alkaline pretreatment: Alkali such as NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)2, hydrazine, and anhy­
drous ammonia are used to induce swelling in the biomass. This swelling enhances
the internal surface area while decreasing the degree of polymerization and cellu­
lose crystallinity. The treatment also disrupts the lignin structure and breaks the
bonds between lignin and other carbohydrate components in the LCB, thereby im­
11
==PAGE:19==
proving enzyme accessibility to the carbohydrates present [58]. However, the main
drawbacks of alkaline pretreatment include the high costs associated with neutral­
izing the slurry after pretreatment, the extended residence time required, and its
ineffectiveness with biomass that has a high lignin content [52].
3.2.3
Physicochemical pretreatments
These processes operate in a hybrid manner, affecting the physical structure of biomass,
its chemical composition, and molecular interactions. In these methods, chemicals are
employed alongside pressure and/or temperature. Some of the techniques categorized
under this group include steam and CO2 explosion, ammonia fiber explosion, and liquid
hot water treatment [59].
Steam explosion is one of the most extensively studied techniques [49], showing sig­
nificant promise due to its low energy consumption and minimal environmental impact
[60]. This method utilizes high­pressure saturated steam (7–48 bar) in combination with
high temperatures (160–260°C) for short durations lasting from seconds to minutes. The
steam penetrates the biomass, expanding the cell walls within the fibers, which leads to
an explosion and partial hydrolysis. A notable drawback of this method is its inability to
decompose xylan and lignin, resulting in the formation of inhibitory substances [61].
Ammonia fiber explosion is similar to steam explosion but employs anhydrous ammonia.
This technique enhances the surface area of LCB and improves enzyme accessibility for
hydrolysis, all while generating minimal amounts of inhibitors and effectively preserving
cellulose and hemicellulose. However, the operational costs pose challenges to the com­
mercialization of this technology [52].
Supercritical fluids (SCF) have recently emerged as sustainable and promising options
for the pretreatment of LCB. Their application across various industrial sectors has gar­
nered attention due to their low operational costs and high energy efficiency [52]. The
most commonly used method is Supercritical CO2 explosion. In contrast to dilute acid
pretreatment, this technique requires almost no water and produces a sugar mixture with
a low concentration of inhibitory compounds. The main drawback of this approach lies in
the high costs associated with compressing CO2 [62].
Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreatment is an effective method for removing and solubilizing
hemicellulose and a small portion of lignin, together with structural changes on cellulose
[13]. Using water in its liquid state prevents the formation of fermentation inhibitors that
can arise at elevated temperatures [63]. This advantage also eliminates the need for a fi­
nal washing or neutralization step. Additionally, the low cost of water makes this approach
favorable for large­scale applications [64]. However, at high temperatures, hot water can
cleave acetyl linkages, releasing acids that may lead to the degradation of hemicelluloses
into inhibitors such as furfural and 5­hydroxymethyl furfural [65, 66]. Kohlmann et al. [67]
noted that the degradation of carbohydrate byproducts can be minimized by maintaining
the pH between 4 and 7, a condition that can be easily achieved by adding alkali when
necessary.
3.2.4
Biological pretreatments
Biological processes frequently employ wood­degrading fungi, including soft rot, brown
rot, and white rot fungi, to modify the chemical composition of LCB. Soft and brown rot
fungi primarily decompose hemicellulose, leading to a partial alteration of lignin, which
remains relatively intact. In stark contrast, white­rot fungi exhibit a remarkable ability to
target and degrade lignin effectively, thereby facilitating more extensive chemical modifi­
12
==PAGE:20==
cations to the biomass [51].
This pretreatment method offers a promising, more environmentally sustainable alterna­
tive for the selective removal of lignin and hemicellulose from biomass. Unlike traditional
chemical, physical, and physicochemical pretreatment techniques, which often involve
harsh reagents or energy­intensive processes, biological pretreatments leverage the nat­
ural enzymatic capabilities of fungi. Economic advantages also accompany this method,
as it can potentially reduce operational costs associated with chemical inputs and energy
consumption.
Despite these benefits, biological pretreatment presents notable challenges that hinder
its widespread application. One significant drawback is the relatively low degradation
rates and overall efficiency of the process [54], which can prolong treatment times and re­
duce throughput. Additionally, successful biological pretreatment requires stringent con­
trol over the growth conditions of the fungi. These requirements can lead to increased
operational demands for space and time, making such pretreatment methods less attrac­
tive for commercial scale­up. Consequently, while biological pretreatments hold potential,
they face considerable barriers to wider industrial adoption [51].
3.3
Lignocellulosic biomass enzymatic hydrolysis
Enzymatic hydrolysis has emerged as the most effective method for converting LCB
polysaccharides into fermentable sugars, primarily due to its low energy requirements
and the ability to operate within a temperature range of 40­50ºC.
The solid content in the enzymatic hydrolysis media is essential for determining the eco­
nomic viability of the process. In traditional conversion methods, low solid loading leads
to lower product concentrations, reduced equipment efficiency, higher wastewater out­
put, and increased capital costs. Therefore, high solid loadings (HSL) are essential for
the industrial production of sugar liquors derived from LCB. However, this approach is
impacted by the ’high­solids effect,’ which leads to decreased glucose conversion yields
as solid loads increase.
Employing enzymes to break down cellulose into glucose and hemicellulose into pentose
monomers is now considered the most promising strategy for producing cost­effective
products from LCB [68, 69]. Enzyme­driven hydrolysis, in contrast to acid hydrolysis, pro­
vides a more environmentally friendly process that minimizes monomer losses and avoids
forming byproducts that could impede subsequent fermentation [70].
3.3.1
Enzymes for the degradation of lignocellulosic biomass
The hydrolysis of LCB requires a variety of enzymes with distinct specificities to break
down its complex structure due to its diverse composition. These enzymes include cel­
lulases, hemicellulases, ligninolytic enzymes, and, more recently, lytic polysaccharide
monooxygenases (LPMOs) [71].
Cellulases and hemicellulases are essential components of the enzymatic system respon­
sible for degrading plant cell walls [72]. Notably, enzymes that target cellulose may also
act on hemicellulose, and hemicellulases specifically targeting hemicellulose can also ex­
hibit activity towards cellulose [73].
• Cellulases: Cellulases are modular enzymes widely used for breaking down cellu­
lose. They consist of endoglycosidases, cellobiohydrolases (exoglucanases), and
13
==PAGE:21==
β­glucosidases (cellobiases). These enzymes collaborate to degrade cellulose into
shorter polysaccharides, such as cellodextrin, cellobiose, or glucose [74].
Typically, cellulases contain a catalytic domain that cleaves glycosidic bonds, a
carbohydrate­binding module, and a linker peptide that is often long and glycosy­
lated. However, cellulases can be inhibited by low accessibility to cellulose surfaces,
mainly due to the presence of hemicellulose and lignin. The hydroxyl groups in lignin
can form hydrogen bonds with cellulase amino acid residues, reducing the effective­
ness of cellulase action on cellulose [75]. Other forms of inhibition may arise from
external factors such as temperature, pH, mixing [76], or the accumulation of prod­
ucts or harmful compounds [77].
• Hemicellulases are enzymes that facilitate the hydrolysis of hemicellulose polysac­
charides. Various types of hemicellulases exist, including xylanases, endoxylanases,
xylosidases, and arabinofuranosidases [76], depending on the specific sugars they
target. These enzymes can be derived from a range of microorganisms, particularly
fungi and thermophilic bacteria. Like cellulases, hemicellulases are primarily mod­
ular proteins that consist of catalytic domains, carbohydrate­binding module, and
other functional domains that assist in breaking glycosidic or esterified acid side
groups [71].
• Lignin­degrading enzymes can be categorized based on their activity towards lignin.
Lignin­degrading auxiliary enzymes cannot independently degrade lignin and re­
quire other enzymes for complete degradation. This group includes cellobiose de­
hydrogenase and glucose oxidase, among others. In contrast, lignin­modifying en­
zymes (LMEs), also known as lignolytic enzymes, can catalyze the oxidative break­
down of lignin (instead of hydrolysis). This group of enzymes include peroxidases
(such as lignin peroxidase) and various phenoloxidases like laccase [45].
• LPMOs are a specific type of copper­containing chitin­binding protein, recognized
for their flat binding surface that enhances the oxidative breakdown of polysaccha­
rides. In addition to insoluble substrates like cellulose, chitin, starch, and xylan,
certain LPMOs have been found to oxidize soluble oligosaccharides, including xy­
loglucan, glucomannan, and β­(1→3), (1→4)­d­glucan [78, 79]. Research indicates
that incorporating LPMOs into cellulase mixtures can enhance the efficiency of cel­
lulose biomass degradation and reduce the overall amount of enzyme needed by
effectively saccharifying resistant polysaccharides through their synergistic interac­
tion with glycoside hydrolases [80, 81, 82, 83, 84]. The functionality of LPMOs
often requires electron donors, which can include small­molecule reductants like
ascorbate and sulfur­containing compounds, or more complex systems such as
cellobiose dehydrogenases, glucose­methanol­choline oxidoreductases, phenolic
compounds derived from plants and fungi, or photosynthetic pigments like chloro­
phylls [77]. Nevertheless, hydrogen peroxide has been recently identified as the
preferred co­substrate for LPMOs, as compared to the initially proposed mechanism
that involves molecular oxygen as the supplier of protons, electrons, and reactive
oxygen species for the reaction [85].
3.3.2
Enzymatic cocktail and synergism
The enzymatic hydrolysis of the hemicellulosic component into fermentable sugars is a
crucial factor, alongside biomass pretreatment, that influences the economic and tech­
nological challenges encountered in biorefinery processes [75]. Low levels of biomass
hydrolysis, affected by various factors described previously, typically require the use of
large quantities of expensive enzymes to achieve optimal hydrolysis yields [86]. Enzymes
14
==PAGE:22==
can act in cooperation, providing each other positive features that can enhance biomass
hydrolysis. In addition, different enzyme cocktails, but with the same enzymatic activity,
may have a different efficiency depending on the accessory enzymes or substrates that
are added [75]. Moreover, different enzyme mixtures that exhibit the same overall enzy­
matic activity may perform differently in terms of efficiency, depending on the additional
enzymes or substrates included.
Various forms of LCB require specific enzyme combinations to maximize saccharification,
leading to increased research focused on either improving the enzymes themselves or
fine­tuning enzyme mixtures.
Research has demonstrated that the effectiveness of LPMOs is enhanced by the inclusion
of hydrogen peroxide. This could be beneficial for industrial biomass applications, as
oxidative processes also facilitate cellulose conversion [87]. Furthermore, incorporating
hemicellulases into cellulolytic enzyme mixtures has been proposed as a viable strategy
to increase the yield of fermentable sugars [88, 89, 90]. These hemicellulases improve the
efficiency of saccharification for both cellulose and hemicellulose by enhancing cellulose
accessibility through the removal of the xylan coating [91, 90]. This approach is especially
important for hydrolyzing substrates with high hemicellulose content.
Moreover, the incorporation of non­ionic surfactants, such as Tween® 80, has been shown
to improve the enzymatic digestibility of cellulose [15, 92, 88]. These surfactants signif­
icantly reduce non­productive interactions, as cellulase adsorption onto lignin is consid­
ered a significant obstacle during enzymatic hydrolysis [93]. Additionally, surfactants help
establish a network at the liquid­air interface, which reduces the surface area available to
enzymes, ultimately aiding in the minimization of enzyme inactivation [94].
3.3.3
Effect of the pretreatment inhibitors
Numerous factors can hinder the biocatalytic saccharification process, with product inhi­
bition being the most prevalent. This occurs when the accumulation of sugar products
reduces enzyme activity. For instance, cellobiose and cello­oligosaccharides impede the
activity of various cellulases, while β­glucosidases are particularly susceptible to inhibition
by glucose [95, 96]. Generally, the inhibition of cellulases by cellobiose is more significant
than the inhibition of β­glucosidases by glucose.
To mitigate feedback inhibition and enhance cellulose saccharification, several strategies
are often employed. These include the direct addition of exogenous β­glucosidases that
possess high glucose tolerance or the use of recombinant strains that secrete these en­
zymes [97, 98]. Additionally, both cellobiose and xylan exert inhibitory effects on cel­
lobiohydrolases and cellulases, respectively. Interestingly, research has indicated that
under certain conditions, low concentrations of xylose can enhance the activity of β­
glucosidases, potentially doubling their hydrolytic activity. Conversely, high concentra­
tions of xylose may disrupt the binding of cellobiose to the enzyme’s active site [99]. This
situation underscores the need for careful regulation of the effects different substances
have on enzyme activity to optimize saccharification conditions and improve efficacy.
Inhibitory compounds can also arise during the pretreatment of substrates, which may
affect the activity of saccharolytic enzymes (fig. 3.6). Key inhibitors generated from lignin
include phenolic compounds such as 4­hydroxybenzoic acid, vanillin, catechol, ferulic
acid, and syringic acid [100]. Inhibitors produced from hemicellulose consist of furfural and
formic acid, which are degradation products of xylose and arabinose. Inhibitors related to
cellulose include 5­hydroxymethylfurfural, formic acid, and levulinic acid [101]. Different
15
==PAGE:23==
pretreatment methods generate distinct inhibitory compounds, as outlined in the review by
Hendriks and Zeeman (2009) [102], which describes the types of inhibitors formed through
specific pretreatments and their effects on bioconversion and fermentation processes.
Research by Palmqvist and Hahn­Hägerdal (2000) [65] specifically focused on how these
inhibitors affect fermentation.
Multiple studies have examined the influence of phenolic and lignin degradation products
on saccharolytic enzymes. For instance, Ximenes et al. (2011) [103] explored how tannic
acid, gallic acid, hydroxycinnamic acids, and 4­hydroxybenzoic acid, along with vanillin,
impact cellulases and β­glucosidases, observing a deactivation rate of 20–80%. The level
of inhibition varied depending on the specific enzymes, their sources, and the particular
compounds involved.
Even though phenolic inhibitors generated during pretreatments can be effectively re­
moved by washing the substrate, this process may result in the loss of up to 15% of the
sugars present in the biomass. Furthermore, the presence of lignin in the substrate can
inhibit the enzymes involved in hydrolysis. Berlin et al. (2007) [104] discovered that cel­
lulases, xylanases, and β­glucosidases experienced inhibition from lignin preparations,
with β­glucosidase showing the least degree of impact.

3.4
Cork
Cork, derived from the suberous covering of the species Quercus suber L., commonly
known as the cork oak, represents a significant botanical and economic asset. This slow­
growing, evergreen tree is a member of the Fagaceae family and is characterized by
its broad leaves. Cork oaks can attain a lifespan of up to 200 years, exhibiting notable
sclerophyllous adaptations that confer resilience against periods of drought and elevated
temperatures [106]. The geographical distribution of Quercus suber is primarily concen­
trated in regions of the western Mediterranean Basin, including parts of Portugal, Spain,
southern France, southern Italy, and North Africa, as well as extending to China [107]. The
cork industry plays a vital role in the economies of these regions, particularly in Portugal,
which accounts for approximately 55% of the global cork production [108].
The bark of Quercus suber possesses a unique structure characterized by thickness, ho­
mogeneity, and porosity, interspersed with fissures that serve to protect the underlying
cellular makeup of the tree from external threats, including forest fires [107]. It is com­
posed of an aggregate of cells (fig. 3.7), about 42 million per cubic centimeter, which have
five wall layers [109]. This bark can be harvested without compromising the viability of
the tree, as the process relies on the phellogen, a layer of actively dividing cells that en­
ables the regeneration of cork throughout the tree’s lifespan. This regeneration process
contributes to the cork’s homogeneity and structural integrity [110].
Figure 3.7: Cork cellular structure [111].
Cork exhibits low thermal conductivity coupled with adequate compressive strength, ren­
dering it an exemplary material for thermal insulation, particularly under compressive
loads [109]. Its anti­slip properties enhance its applicability in flooring solutions and handle
designs. Presently, cork products find extensive use in various domains, including thermal
insulation for refrigeration and aerospace applications, acoustic insulation in submarines
and recording studios, as well as seals and joints utilized in woodwind instruments and
combustion engines. Additionally, cork serves as an impact­absorbing medium in flooring,
footwear, packaging, and notably, as stoppers [112].
In contemporary research and development, a paramount focus is placed on achieving
sustainability. The United Nations articulates sustainable development as “development
that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations
to meet their own needs” [113]. To realize these sustainable objectives, it is imperative to
characterize and repurpose natural waste resources, particularly those rich in bioactive
17
==PAGE:25==
compounds. The cork industry generates various by­products, some of which are replete
with tannins, phenolic acids, terpenoids, and other compound classes exhibiting bioactive
potential. Such compounds hold promise for exploration and application within pharma­
ceuticals and cosmetics, offering avenues for innovation and sustainability in these indus­
tries [107, 114].
3.4.1
Cork composition
The properties of natural cork, including its chemical and biological inertia, are intrinsi­
cally linked to its cellular and chemical composition. Cork cells are characterized by their
small dimensions and exceptionally thin walls, devoid of cellular content, which acquire
impermeability during the process of suberin deposition [115]. This unique chemical com­
position differentiates cork from other plant tissues, such as wood, and is a crucial factor in
its ability to protect living, active tissues of trees from external adversities [107]. Extensive
studies have been conducted on cork’s chemical composition [116, 117, 118], revealing
a significant variability in the abundance of its constituents, influenced by factors such as
geographical and genetic origin, climate, and soil conditions [119, 120]. Typically, cork
comprises approximately 40% suberin, 25% lignin, 20% polysaccharides, and a 15% ex­
tractive fraction consisting of lipidic and phenolic substances, with inorganic compounds
constituting less than 1%. A representation of the cork chemical composition can be seen
in fig. 3.8.
Figure 3.8: Cork chemical composition [121].
The primary component of the cork cell wall, suberin, is a lipophilic macromolecule found
across the plant kingdom [12], and it is responsible for the impermeability exhibited by cork
[107, 122, 123]. The suberose tissue displays a complex structure featuring both aliphatic
and aromatic domains (fig. 3.9). The aliphatic domain consists of a polyester framework
comprised of long­chain ω­hydroxy fatty acids and α,ω­dicarboxylic acids, interconnected
by ester linkages and cross­linked via glycerol units. The aromatic domain predominantly
consists of hydroxycinnamic acid units esterified with glycerol or ω­hydroxy fatty acids,
thereby linking the two domains. Key hydroxycinnamates present in this structure include
ferulic acid derivatives and caffeates, with lesser amounts of coumaric acids [124, 125].
Additionally, suberin serves as a vital source of the components of the aliphatic domain
or epoxy derivatives; the relative scarcity of these compounds in nature represents sig­
nificant potential for the development of novel cork­derived materials [114, 126].

==PAGE:26==
Figure 3.9: Comparison of two models of suberin structure [127].
In connection to suberin, lignin is a structural phenolic polymer within the cork cell wall
that provides essential mechanical support and rigidity. Like suberin, lignin is also a hy­
drophobic polymer covalently bound to polysaccharide units, creating a lignin­cellulose
matrix that integrates with the aromatic domain of suberin [110, 128]. Recent research
has underscored lignin’s status as an “important natural bioresource” for applications in
biofuel production and the extraction of high­value chemicals [129].
Cork cell walls additionally contain polysaccharides, primarily cellulose and hemicellu­
lose. Their extraction is typically performed through acid, basic, or enzymatic hydrolysis,
succeeded by solvent extraction [110]. Following the identification of cellulose and hemi­
cellulose in cork [130, 131], Pereira and Tomé [117] elucidated the monosaccharide com­
position of both virgin and reproduction cork post­acid hydrolysis of glycoside linkages.
Their investigations confirmed that glucose and xylose are the predominant monosaccha­
rides in both cork types. The structural integrity and rigidity of the cell wall are enhanced
by both polysaccharides and lignin.
Cork also encompasses a fraction of extractives that remain unbound to other structures,
similar to the lignocellulosic matrix linked to suberin itself. Although extractives are com­
monly found across plant species, cork is noted for having a greater diversity of these com­
pounds compared to conventional wood. The quantity and types of extractives present
are influenced by factors such as the geographical and genetic origins of the tree, its age,
as well as the prevailing soil and climatic conditions [107, 132]. The primary groups of
compounds classified under “extractives” include waxes and phenolic compounds.

Cork processing and applications

years [133, 134, 135]. The first harvest yields what is known as “virgin cork”, which re­
mains unused [136, 137], while subsequent harvests yield ”first reproduction cork” after
a maturation period of 9 to 12 years, marking its utility for agglomerate production [136,
137]. It is only upon the third extraction that cork is utilized for primary applications, such
as the manufacture of cork wine stoppers [136, 135, 137]. A summarized version of the

The initial phases of cork processing aimed at the production of natural cork products
have revealed a significant generation of waste, with cork stopper production utilizing a
maximum of approximately 25% of the raw material [109]. It has been estimated that
the waste produced during cork production and transformation approaches an annual
figure of 50,000 tons [114]. Notably, a substantial portion of this waste—comprising cork
agglomerates and composites—finds application in the manufacturing of alternative cork
products, thereby not being classified as waste [112]. These by­products are produced
through the application of elevated pressure and temperature to cork granules. Various
waste types, including waste cork from the stopper industry, low­quality cork (refuse), and

based on density and granule size. Following this, they may be either combined with
natural or synthetic agents to generate cork agglomerates (composition cork) or solely
subjected to high pressure and temperature to yield cork composites, such as insulation
corkboard.
Conversely, certain waste streams—including cork boiling wastewater, black condensate,
and cork powder—are deemed less suitable for further product applications [112].
3.4.3
Cork by­products
Cork boiling wastewater is a byproduct of the boiling process employed to enhance the
softness, flexibility, and overall quality of cork planks, typically conducted for approxi­
mately one hour, minimizing corkwood extractive content [138]. This wastewater is char­
acterized by a high concentration of phenolic compounds, sugars, and salts [138], with
an estimated global annual production of 120 to 130 tons [112].

==PAGE:28==
Black condensate is generated during the production of insulation corkboard, wherein
cork granules are treated under superheated steam at temperatures ranging from 250 to
500ºC and subjected to pressure. The subsequent cooling process yields a black liquid
that solidifies into a waxy substance within the autoclave pipes [139]. The composition
of black condensate predominantly includes long­chain aliphatic compounds, terpenes,
and several aromatic derivatives [139], with annual production estimated at approximately
2,500 tons [140].
Cork powder emerges from grinding, cutting, and finishing operations, manifesting as
a significant waste product generated in substantial volumes [11]. This byproduct consti­
tutes roughly 25% of the raw materials, comprising particles with dimensions less than
0.25 mm [11]. Different types of cork can undergo diverse industrial processes, yielding
various grades of cork powder; however, they consistently exhibit richness in suberin,
lignin, and polysaccharides, as outlined previously. According to Sousa et al. (2006)
[140], the annual generation of cork powder is approximately 40,000 tons, accounting for
about 22% of total cork production. Currently, cork powder is primarily utilized as an en­
ergy source, given its low economic value coupled with high combustion efficiency, while
a smaller fraction serves as a filling agent [11]. Additionally, as a byproduct produced in
substantial quantities without necessitating extra land area, cork powder has the potential
to be regarded as a promising renewable carbon source for biorefineries.
3.5
Sustainable biorefinery process
The sustainable biorefinery strategy discussed in this section is based on the key pro­
cesses outlined in Sections 2.2 and 2.3.
Initially, lignocellulosic cork powder under­
goes a pretreatment process designed to enhance enzyme accessibility by disrupting the
biomass’s recalcitrant structure. This is followed by enzymatic hydrolysis, in which cellu­
lolytic and hemicellulolytic enzymes are used, with or without chemical additives, to break
down the pretreated biomass and release fermentable sugars. Together, these steps form
the upstream phase of a comprehensive biorefinery aimed at transforming cork powder
into valuable compounds in a sustainable manner.
The pretreatment technologies discussed earlier use various methods to improve the di­
gestibility of biomass. Although these pretreatments aim to enhance hydrolysis efficiency
and yield, they may also negatively affect the overall sustainability of biomass valorization.
Certain pretreatments require significant energy input, as well as high installation costs
and chemical usage. They can also generate toxins, reduce the yield of fermentable sug­
ars, or complicate downstream processes, resulting in environmental harm [141]. Tradi­
tional pretreatments, such as strong acids, high pressure, or elevated temperatures, are
particularly prone to causing irreversible sugar degradation and the production of toxic
substances [20]. Therefore, there is a preference for milder pretreatments, such as LHW
pretreatment.
The goal of LHW pretreatment is to modify the lignin and hemicellulose components,
alter the ultrastructure of the cell wall, and break down LCB into soluble fractions [142].
This process uses only water and heat in a pressurized reactor, making it a sustainable
option that avoids introducing additional chemicals or microorganisms. Additionally, it has
been observed that reduced hydrolysis activity helps to minimize the further degradation
of various sugars into aldehydes, and maintaining a pH between 4 and 7 significantly
reduces the generation of inhibitors [142].
21
==PAGE:29==
The enzymatic cocktail will be refined to enhance the sustainability of hydrolysis. Enzy­
matic hydrolysis has been simplified by the development of commercially available cel­
lulolytic cocktails. However, these cocktails are often not tailored to optimize yield and
could be improved with additives. In 2023, Moya et al. [75] found that adding hemicellu­
lase, Tween 80, and H2O2 to the commercial cellulolytic cocktail positively influenced the
release of glucose and xylose from sugarcane bagasse.
The Biomass Conversion and Bioprocess Technology group (BCBT group) at the Techni­
cal University of Denmark (DTU) has focused on developing a new technique for biomass
fractionation. This approach involves an initial mild pretreatment phase (LHW, 175°C), fol­
lowed by customizing an enzymatic cocktail for enzymatic hydrolysis. This strategy aims
to maximize sugar release during hydrolysis, producing a sugar­rich hydrolysate that is
free of toxic compounds and more suitable for fermentation processes [14, 143].

4.4.1
Cellulase activity assay
The total cellulase activity of enzymatic preparations was determined using a modified
filter paper assay adapted from the IUPAC­recommended method by Ghose [144], with
reduced reaction volumes for efficiency. Strips of Whatman No. 1 filter paper (4.0 x 0.6
cm, ≈20 mg) were used as the substrate, and enzyme dilutions were tested to determine
the amount required to release 0.8 mg of glucose equivalents in 60 minutes. Three types
of tubes were prepared: enzyme assay tubes (with filter paper and enzyme dilutions),
substrate blanks (filter paper without enzyme), and enzyme blanks (enzyme without sub­
strate).
The assay reactions were conducted in 2 mL microtubes containing 400 µL of 0.05 M
sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.8) and preincubated at 50°C. Reactions were initiated by
adding 200 µL of different concentrations of the enzyme solution and incubated at 50°C
for exactly 60 minutes using a ThermoMixer set at 300 rpm. Reactions were stopped by
adding 1.2 mL of DNS reagent, followed by boiling at 100°C for 5 minutes. Tubes were
cooled on ice before transferring 20 µL aliquots into a 96­well plate, diluted with 250 µL
deionized water, and absorbance was measured at 545 nm.
The absorbance values were converted into glucose concentrations using the standard
curve ([Glucose] = (Absorbance −0.0819)/0.1969, R2 = 0.9994). Enzyme activity (in
FPU/mL) was calculated by interpolating the enzyme dilution that released exactly 0.8
mg of glucose equivalents, then applying the formula: FPU/mL = 0.37 / [enzyme dilution
producing 0.8 mg glucose], where 0.37 is a fixed factor based on molar conversion, assay
volume, and time. All measurements were performed in triplicate, and blank corrections
were applied to subtract background signals.
The final cellulase activity was calculated to be 352 FPU/mL and is reported here for ref­
erence.

Enzymatic hydrolysis experiments

Effect of pretreatment in the enzymatic hydrolysis
The impact of the pretreatment was evaluated by comparing sugar yields from both raw

• Buffer + Cellulase CC3
• Buffer + Cellulase CC3 + Hemicellulase
• Buffer + Cellulase CC3 + Hemicellulase + Tween 80 + H2O2

sisting of a 0.05 M sodium acetate buffer and varying enzyme loadings depending on the
condition: 30 FPU g of DM CC3, 400 µL/g DM of hemicellulase. Additionally, on the third
scenario, 521 mg/g DM of Tween 80 , and 65 µM H2O2 were added to the mixture. the
concentrations of enzymes and additives were selected based on the work of Moya et

al. (2024) [14]. The different mixtures were then added to both raw and pretreated cork

4.4.4
Total Phenolics Content – Folin­Ciocalteu assay
The concentration of phenolic compounds released during pretreatment was determined
using the Folin–Ciocalteu colorimetric assay, which measures total phenolic content based
on the reduction of a molybdenum­tungsten reagent that yields a blue chromophore. Gal­
lic acid served as the reference standard, and results were reported as gallic acid equiva­
lents (GAE, mg/g DM). The procedure was based on Ballesteros et al. (2014) with minor
adjustments.
A gallic acid stock solution (1,000 mg/L) was prepared in deionized water. Working stan­
dards ranging from 100 to 1,000 mg/L were then generated by serial dilution. Samples,
blanks, and standards (5 µL each) were pipetted into a 96­well transparent microplate in
triplicate. Using a multichannel pipette, 60 µL of sodium carbonate solution (7.5% w/v),
15 µL of F­C reagent, and 200 µL of deionized water were sequentially added to each
well.
The plate was incubated in a ThermoMixer at 60°C with gentle agitation for 5 minutes
under an exhaustion system. After incubation, the microplate was allowed to cool to room
temperature, and absorbance was measured at 700 nm using a UV­Vis microplate reader.
Calibration curves were constructed from the absorbance of the gallic acid standards, and
sample phenolic concentrations were interpolated accordingly.

4.4.5
Statistical analysis – Central Composite Design
The experimental data obtained from the CCD was analyzed using JMP Pro statistical
software (version 18, SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA). Response Surface Methodology
(RSM) was applied to model the effects of the three independent variables—biomass
loading (% w/w DM), cellulase (CC3) loading (FPU/g DM), and hemicellulase dosage
(μL/g DM)—on the response variable, namely glucose release (yield).
A second­order polynomial model was fitted to the data to capture both linear and quadratic
effects, as well as possible two­way interactions between the independent variables.
Model adequacy was evaluated using analysis of variance (ANOVA), examining parame­
ters such as the coefficient of determination (R²), adjusted R², and the p­values associated
29
==PAGE:37==
with model terms. Lack­of­fit tests were conducted to ensure that the model appropriately
described the experimental data.
To visualize the influence of the independent variables on glucose release, three­dimensional
surface plots and contour plots were generated using the “Surface Profiler” and “Profiler”
tools in JMP. Each response surface was constructed by varying two of the independent
variables while holding the third constant at its central level. These visual tools were es­
sential for identifying optimal regions within the design space and for exploring possible
interactions and nonlinear trends that impact the enzymatic hydrolysis process.
Finally, desirability profiling was used to determine the combination of variable levels that
maximized glucose release. The predicted optimal conditions were experimentally vali­
dated in duplicate to confirm the model’s accuracy.

Results and discussion
5.1
Pretreatment effect on cork powder
The composition of the feedstock and its transformation following pretreatment are vital
factors in evaluating bioconversion efficiency. The primary aim of the pretreatment pro­
cess was to modify the structural characteristics of the lignocellulosic material to improve
its ease of enzymatic hydrolysis. By disrupting complex components like lignin and hemi­
cellulose, pretreatment increases enzyme access to cellulose [145].
The most significant impact of Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreatment is its differential ac­
tion on cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Generally, this process results in a major
solubilization of hemicellulose and a small portion of lignin, along with in the structural
changes of cellulose. However, the solubilization rate is influenced by several factors, in­
cluding the specific type of LCB used, the reactor type, the scale of the operation, and the
operating conditions. [13]. Autohydrolysis thus effectively fractions biomass by solubiliz­
ing hemicelluloses into monomeric, dimeric, or oligomeric products in the liquid fraction
of the biomass slurry, while keeping cellulose and lignin contents relatively intact in the
solid fraction [146]. While it may not affect lignin content, LHW pretreatment is known to
alter its structure, through partial depolymerization followed by recondensation reactions,
which can lead to lignin redistribution rather than its solubilization [147].
The pretreatment of cork powder resulted in a slurry that was separated into two fractions:
a solid fraction (pretreated solids) and a liquid fraction (pretreatment liquid). An analysis
of these components was conducted to assess the efficiency of the pretreatment process.

even under LHW conditions. However, the recalcitrant ester bonds in suberin can be
readily hydrolyzed by bases, as reported by Karnaouri et al. (2016) [149].
Overall, a limited amount of sugars was released during the pretreatment step, which
aligns with the objective of using a mild pretreatment. The goal was not to maximize sugar
solubilization at this stage, but rather to enhance enzyme accessibility while minimizing
the formation of inhibitory compounds.

Pretreatment effect on the enzymatic hydrolysis
5.2.1.1
Sugars release kinetics
The production of fermentable sugars from both untreated and pretreated cork powder
was assessed using various enzymatic cocktail compositions: Cellic® Ctec3 HS (CC3)
only; CC3 combined with hemicellulase; and CC3, hemicellulase, Tween 80, along with
H2O2. The goal was to analyze which conditions yielded the best results and to under­
stand the effect of pretreatment on sugar release.
High­performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was utilized to detect the monosaccha­
rides present in the hydrolysate at 4, 8, 12, 24, and 48 hours. The results are illustrated
in fig. 5.1, fig. 5.2 and fig. 5.3. Notably, a substantial release of sugars was already ob­
served after just 4 hours of hydrolysis for all enzymatic cocktail assays, indicating rapid
initial enzymatic activity and effective substrate accessibility in the early stages of the
reaction.
The fig. 5.1 displays the release of glucose. It is evident that the addition of CC3 alone
does not significantly impact glucose release (with a maximum of 35 mg/g DM ­48 hours­
for the CC3 pretreated sample), showing only a slight increase compared to the untreated
sample treated with CC3 (28 mg/g DM, 48 hours). However, once hemicellulases are
introduced into the enzymatic cocktail, glucose release increases dramatically, ranging
141­172 mg/g DM by the end of the experiment. The effect of pretreatment is consistent
across all assays, as the cork powder displayed minimal glucose release without it. As
anticipated, the combination of CC3, hemicellulase, and additives produced the highest
glucose release, measured at ≈178 mg/g DM (24 hours), with a slight decrease after­
wards.

5.2.1.2
Total phenolic content
A Total Phenolics Assay was performed for all time points from the previous subsection,
with the aim to quantify phenolic compounds during enzymatic hydrolysis.
Measuring phenolic content is essential to evaluate the presence of general fermentation
inhibitors. The usual goal of enzymatic hydrolysis is the production of fermentable sugars
that can be used for the synthesis of biomolecules through fermentation, thus the phe­
nolics content must be known in order to minimize microbial inhibition and to assess the

The sugar release kinetics varied significantly depending on biomass loading and enzyme
composition. The three biomass loadings tested —5%, 10%, and 15% (w/v)— exhibited
distinct release patterns over time, particularly for glucose and xylose, the primary sugars
monitored as key components of cellulose and hemicellulose, respectively. Arabinose
was also released but tended to plateau earlier, likely due to the smaller proportion of
arabinan in cork hemicellulose.
In the 5% biomass loading tests, the most effective condition was assay 2, which demon­
strated rapid and sustained sugar release, reaching approximately 297 mg glucose/g dry
matter (DM) and 227 mg xylose/g DM at 48 hours. These results were closely followed
by assay 4, which achieved 243 mg glucose/g DM and 185 mg xylose/g DM. The lower
solid content likely enhanced enzyme accessibility and mass transfer, allowing for the full
synergistic potential of the enzyme cocktail and additives to be realized.
For the 10% biomass loading experiments, assay 13 proved to be the most effective.
This assay was unique in having high levels of hemicellulase, reinforcing the importance
of hemicellulase levels for optimal sugar release.
In the 15% biomass loading tests, results showed a slight reduction in sugar release
compared to lower loadings, with assays 1 and 3 achieving the highest release, at 245 mg
glucose/g DM and 184 mg xylose/g DM, and 223 mg glucose/g DM and 170 mg xylose/g
DM, respectively. This suggests that, despite higher solid content potentially hindering
enzyme diffusion, the oxidative action of lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases (LPMOs)
and the dispersing effect of surfactants still contributed to some mitigation of recalcitrance.
Overall, all assays shared a common factor: the use of high levels of hemicellulase (750
μL/g DM). Minor differences were noted as CC3 levels varied from the lowest (10 FPU/g
DM) to highest (50 FPU/g DM) values. This can be observed when comparing assays 1
and 2 (50 FPU/g DM) with assays 3 and 4 (10 FPU/g DM) respectively. These findings also
emphasize that optimal sugar release depends not only on enzyme formulation but also
on adjusting biomass loading to balance substrate availability with enzymatic accessibility.
At lower loadings, mass transfer limitations are minimized, enabling additives to exert
their full effect. However, at higher loadings, substrate recalcitrance needs more potent
synergistic enzyme combinations to maintain high conversion efficiencies.
The role of hemicellulase in the hydrolysis of LCB aligns with existing literature. The work
of Hu et al. (2010) [154], which investigated different feedstocks, demonstrated that the
addition of accessory enzymes, such as hemicellulase, improved overall hydrolysis by
solubilizing xylan, which hinders access to cellulose.
Furthermore, the impact of the enzymatic cocktail on sugar release was noteworthy. While
the high suberin content in cork typically inhibits enzyme accessibility, the released glu­
cose and xylose values obtained in this study compare favorably to or exceed those re­
ported for other LCBs [75, 155, 156].

Conclusions
This study demonstrates the potential of cork powder as a feedstock for producing fer­
mentable sugars through enzymatic hydrolysis.
Initial chemical characterization con­
firmed its significant content of cellulose and hemicellulose, making it a promising sub­
strate for bioconversion. However, cork’s inherent recalcitrance—primarily due to its high
suberin and lignin content— made it necessary to use Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreat­
ment to enhance enzymatic accessibility.
The LHW pretreatment solubilized a small portion of the hemicellulose, preserving most
of it in the solid phase. Subsequent enzymatic hydrolysis trials demonstrated that the
combination of pretreated cork powder and an enzymatic cocktail composed of cellulase
(Cellic CTec3), hemicellulase (NS22244) and additives (H2O2 and Tween 80) resulted in
significant sugar release. The kinetic studies revealed that a substantial portion of glucose
and xylose was released within the first 4 hours of hydrolysis, with maximums reaching
approximately 178 mg glucose/g DM and 207 mg xylose/g DM under optimized conditions
after 48 hours, thus marking the importance of a pretreatment step.
The application of Central Composite Design and Response Surface Methodology (CCD­
RSM) enabled the identification of key parameters influencing hydrolysis efficiency. Hemi­
cellulase loading emerged as the most critical factor, followed by Cellic Ctec3 and low
biomass loadings. The model’s statistical significance was confirmed, and conditions
were optimized. This CCD showed that yields of 100% for glucose and xylose could be
achieved at the optimized condition (5.89% w/v cork powder, 32.18 FPU/g DM of CC3,
and 750 μL/g DM of hemicellulase).
Overall, this thesis provides evidence that cork powder can be efficiently valorized through
enzymatic hydrolysis, particularly when combined with suitable pretreatment and enzy­
matic cocktails. The findings support further development of cork­based biorefineries,
offering a sustainable route for converting this industrial byproduct into bio­based chemi­
cals.

7
Future prospects
While the present study successfully demonstrated the feasibility of enzymatic hydrolysis
of cork powder for fermentable sugar production, several research opportunities remain,
which can guide the next steps toward the development of an integrated and sustainable
cork­based biorefinery:
• While Liquid Hot Water (LHW) pretreatment proved effective in enhancing enzy­
matic digestibility, its comparative efficiency against other mild pretreatments re­
mains unexplored. Future work should involve systematic comparisons of pretreat­
ment strategies in terms of sugar release, inhibitor formation, and energy demand.
• The solid residues remaining after hydrolysis, rich in lignin and suberin, were not
deeply characterized in this study. Future work should focus on the compositional
and functional characterization of these fractions, evaluating their potential for high­
value applications such as bio­based polymers or antimicrobial and antioxidant ad­
ditives.
• A natural progression of this work is the conversion of the sugar­rich hydrolysate
into value­added biochemicals. Given the composition of the hydrolysate, future
research should evaluate its potential as fermentation medium to produce enzymes,
fuels, or biochemicals such as biosurfactants.
• Finally, future research should focus on process integration—from pretreatment
to fermentation and product recovery—in a scalable, continuous setup. Techno­
economic analysis and life cycle assessment should be performed to assess the
feasibility and sustainability of a cork powder biorefinery. This would support the
transition of cork waste from an underutilized by­product to a renewable feedstock
for the production of bio­based surfactants and other biochemicals.
