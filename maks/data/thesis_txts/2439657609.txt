ABSTRACT 
 
As interest to create anti-inflammatory therapeutics is increasing, the pig as an experimental 
animal model is gaining popularity as well. Nevertheless, studies employing porcine 
neutrophils are still lacking. Thus, the purpose of this study was to enable the use of porcine 
model for the study of the effect on infection and inflammation by modulating neutrophil 
activity by different ligands. Specifically, we wanted to test the hypothesis that functional 
formylated peptide receptor (FPR) orthologues are present in the pig system. A reliable and 
feasible protocol for neutrophil isolation from stabilized fresh porcine blood was established, 
reproducibly yielding ~97% cell purity. Furthermore, isolated neutrophils induced with 
zymosan activated porcine serum (ZAPS) as a source of complement protein C5a, activated 
the cells significantly reaching a peak at 2.5% of ZAPS used. Tumor necrosis factor – α (TNF-
α) and the FPR agonist Compound 43 activated cells showed dose dependent responses where 
highest activation could be seen using concentrations 200 ng/mL and 500 nM, respectively. In 
addition, higher dose dependent activation was observed when using isolated porcine 
neutrophils when compared to full leukocyte population, indicating a significant difference at 
highest concentration of 2.5% ZAPS employed. Moreover, for characterization of receptor 
activation chemotaxis assay was done. It was found that porcine neutrophils migrate towards 
low concentrations of ZAPS and formylmethionyl-leucyl-phenylalanine (fMLF), with highest 
migration at 0.1% and 1-5nM concentrations, respectively. In addition, a preliminary reactive 
oxygen species (ROS) assay was performed using human neutrophils inducing with Compound 
43 and fMLF. It serves as a guideline for future studies indicating diverse ligand binding 
potency when 100 nM concentration of both ligands is used. Overall, data gathered in this study 
supports the possibility of porcine PMNs possessing FPRs, however additional experiments 
are necessary to confirm these findings. All in all, this study serves as a first step towards 
establishing the pig as an animal model to study neutrophil mediated diseases.

1. INTRODUCTION AND AIM 
 
Inborn innate immunity is essential to ensure successful pathogen elimination. It includes 
cellular and humoral anti-infective factors - the epithelial that lines outer surfaces, innate cells, 
such as monocytes and neutrophils, as well as complement and antimicrobial proteins. 
Neutrophils play a major role in innate immune response by recognizing pathogens employing 
their cell surface receptors [1]. Upon neutrophil activation the cells are able to respond quickly 
and perform chemotactic migration from blood to the infection site also undergo degranulation, 
phagocytosis, produce reactive oxygen species (ROS) and release neutrophil extracellular traps 
(NETs) to eliminate pathogens [2, 3]. However, any interference in these pathways may result 
in failed resolution of inflammation, or in other words restoration of hemostasis, which leads 
to tissue injury and chronic diseases [4]. Thus, regulating immune cell responses to suppress 
inflammation and increase resolution would be an ideal solution for inflammatory diseases like 
sepsis or rheumatoid arthritis. This could be achieved by targeting specific cell surface 
receptors with selected immunomodulatory properties expressing ligands [5, 6]. Such ligands 
are able to increase the cellular immune mechanisms selectively, while suppressing pro-
inflammatory responses towards pathogen products, in this way balancing out the inflammation 
[7]. Nevertheless, a successful experimental model is still lacking to test these modulators. 
Although, mice are frequently used results often fail when translated into humans [8, 9]. 
Therefore, pig as an animal model appears to be much more suitable in terms of physiological, 
anatomical and functional innate immunity similarities to humans [8–10].  
A number of receptors are already known and well-studied on human neutrophil cells. Among 
these G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs) are essential when activating neutrophils and 
resolving inflammation [2, 4]. One subclass of GPCRs - formylated peptide receptors (FPRs) 
are greatly involved in these activation mechanisms and thus are widely studied in vitro and in 
vivo [4]. These receptors recognize bacterial and mitochondrial formylated peptides, 
accordingly being essential not only for pathogen recognition but also for host tissue distress 
[4, 11]. Findings made by a former student Alexander D. Strøm-Hansen lead to the conclusion 
that there is a possible orthologue of FPRs in the porcine system [12]. Therefore, the specific 
purpose of this project is to investigate this theory further. While, the overall aim is to enable 
the study of the effects of manipulating neutrophil activation and/or neutrophil responses, e.g. 
by novel synthetic compounds in pig models of infection and inflammation.  
 
1.1. THESIS HYPOTHESIS  
As the innate immunity is conserved throughout the species, it is not surprising that the porcine 
innate immune mechanisms closely resemble the ones of humans [8]. Nonetheless, the 
immense need of studies involving porcine PMNs still remains. More specifically,

2. BACKGROUND 
2.1. INNATE IMMUNE SYSTEM AND MECHANISMS  
The immune system is broadly divided into innate and adaptive immunity [1]. The majority of 
pathogens invading the human body can be eliminated within first hours by so called “first line 
of defense” - innate immunity. In comparison to adaptive immunity which is slower, it does 
not rely on any previous exposure to pathogens and mostly depends on germline genes that are 
inborn and are similar in all healthy mammals [1, 10].  
This part of immune system involves physical barriers – the epithelia that lines internal and 
external surfaces of the body. Next, the innate cells – macrophages, natural killer cells, 
neutrophils, dendritic and mast cells. Furthermore, the complement system proteins as well as, 
antimicrobial, host defense peptides (HDPs), and other soluble mediators that kill some 
pathogens directly or contribute to the killing through phagocyte activation [1, 16].  
In order to discriminate self-tissue from pathogens, immune cells hold a variety of soluble and 
membrane bound receptors [1]. Innate responses are activated instantly after pathogen passes 
through the physical barriers stimulating these receptors, inducing cellular and biochemical 
pathways so the pathogen would be eliminated. The innate immune cells detect pathogens by 
pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) located on their surface using their own 
pattern recognition receptors (PRRs). Since PAMPs are not expressed in vertebrates they allow 
immune cells to quickly discriminate these invaders. This, in brief, leads to the activation of 
the cells to release signaling molecules that attract other immune cells to combat infection and 
this process is known as inflammation [1, 17].  
Inflammation can be distinguished by physical signs of heat, redness, swelling, pain and loss 
of function, which happens because of tissue infiltration by the cells. Acute inflammation 
process (Figure 1) starts with tissue resting or so called sensor cells that are able to recognize 
pathogens and release cytokines and chemokines to recruit other cells. For instance, the 
powerful inflammatory cytokines such as - tumor necrosis factor–alpha (TNF-α) and 
==PAGE:10==

interleukin-1 beta (IL-1ß) are released from the tissue resident macrophages and mast cells that 
directly act on endothelium of blood vessel making it permeable. This in turn leads to the 
recruitment of other immune cells from the blood. Blood cells like neutrophils are caught by 
the activated endothelium and move to the tissue site, where they become activated themselves 
and perform functions to eliminate the pathogen. Release of additional proinflammatory 
chemokine interleukin-8 (IL-8) by macrophages, monocytes and neutrophils leads to the 
amplification loop that can recruit other neutrophils progressively [1, 18]. After achieving their 
function neutrophils die by apoptosis and are removed by inflammation resolving macrophages 
(process called efferocytosis). Eventually restoration of homeostasis is induced by secretion of 
anti-inflammatory cytokines like interleukin-10 (IL-10) and transforming growth factor-ß1 
(TGF-ß1) from the macrophages. Hence, the recruitment of blood cells is stopped and the tissue 
repair processes are initiated. However, if any of these signals are inappropriate or exaggerated 
this leads to a continuing loop of cell recruitment that ultimately cause chronic tissue injury 
and inflammatory diseases [1, 16].  
In addition, cationic-amphiphilic peptides – HDPs, play a role as immunomodulatory 
molecules in the inflammation. They promote pathogen elimination through antimicrobial 
activity, and cell recruitment. Also, they inhibit pro-inflammatory cytokine and chemokine 
production modulating immune cell response contributing to the restoration of homeostasis. 
Due to their ability to module these cellular responses, they are considered to be the foundation 
for creation of anti-inflammatory therapeutics that could potentially resolve chronic 
inflammation [6, 7, 16].

2.2. NEUTROPHIL GRANULOCYTES  
Human neutrophil granulocytes also called polymorphonuclear leukocytes (PMN) are the most 
abundant type of leukocytes constituting from 50% to 70% of all leukocytes in human blood 
where they guard and look up for pathogens. Neutrophils alongside with other granulocytes 
(basophils, eosinophils) develop from pluripotent hematopoietic stem cells, on myeloid cell 
formation line. Formation takes place in the bone marrow undergoing a process known as 
granulopoiesis (granule formation). Once neutrophils mature they can be distinguished by their

Circulating neutrophils are surveilling and looking for IL-8, TNF-α or microbial molecules that 
would indicate pathogen invasion or tissue distress. While circulating close to the inflammatory 
site neutrophils are able to respond to signals coming from endothelium, become activated and 
move into the tissue site by multistep process to fight infection as well as to interact with other 
immune cells (Figure 1) [1, 20, 23].

2.3. NEUTROPHIL ACTIVATION AND FUNCTIONS 
The main function of neutrophils is to eliminate pathogens, which can be done intra- or 
extracellularly. Upon encounter with the pathogens PMNs become fully activated and 
phagocytose these invaders. After the engulfment PMNs kill the pathogen by using 
antibacterial proteins stored in the granules or by forming toxic ROS using nicotinamide 
adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase. Granule contents can be released directly 
to the phagosome or outside the cell, directly attacking invaders on both sides. Additional way 
that PMNs can kill pathogens is by NET formation. This effective way of killing involves DNA 
release with degrading granule proteins attached to it, entrapping the extracellular invaders and 
contributing to their elimination [28].  
Before performing any of these defense mechanisms neutrophils need to be recruited and reach 
the target site [23]. Hence, PMNs are activated in a multistep manner, starting with partial 
activation for their recruitment [29]. For effective movement and response, cells sense 
extracellular chemical gradients and thus are able to move to the greater concentration [23]. 
Inflammatory tissue environment is full of accumulated inflammatory chemokines, cytokines, 
lipid mediators or in one word - chemoattractants, to which gradient PMNs respond [23, 30]. 
In short, when responding to chemoattractants cells migrate through the blood vessel wall in a 
process also known as extravasation [30]. Following this neutrophils are guided further by the

2.3.1. NEUTROPHIL EXTRAVASATION 
Under normal conditions PMNs move in the middle of the blood vessel alongside with other 
leukocytes in a rapid pace [1]. At the sites of inflammation blood vessels become dilated under 
the impact of bacterial products such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS) or some molecules generated 
by damaged tissue such as histamine. This influence induces expression of selectins on the 
endothelial cell wall. Neutrophils are able to reversibly bind them through their own expressed 
protein - L-selectin (CD62L) beginning to slightly and slowly roll alongside the endothelium. 
PMNs are remarkably competent at the rolling process since they possess long plasma 
membrane extensions – slings, tethering the cells securely in order not lose this interaction [1, 
17]. 
While rolling, neutrophils get in contact with molecules immobilized in endothelium through 
GPCRs. This induces intracellular changes leading to the degranulation of specific, gelatinase 
and secretory granules and in turn is followed by expression of integrins – lymphocyte function 
associated antigen (LFA1 (CD11a/CD18)) and MAC1(CD11b/CD18) on PMNs [28]. Integrins 
bind endothelial glycoproteins - intracellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM1) and 2 (ICAM2) 
achieving firm adhesion between the cell and endothelium [1, 17, 28]. At this stage the cell is 
completely stopped and attached to the endothelial wall in spite of the strong force of the blood 
flow [17]. It is important to mention that the recruitment of secretory vesicle receptor 
(CD11b/CD18) incorporation is followed by shedding of L-selectin from neutrophil exterior 
[24]. 
Thereafter, PMNs have to cross the endothelium and the basement membrane beneath it. This 
transmigration also depends on integrins ICAM1 and ICAM2, LFA1 and vascular cell adhesion 
protein 1 (VCAM1) which are expressed on both neutrophils and endothelial junction of the 
cells. With the help of these interactions PMNs are able to squeeze through and reach the 
basement membrane which in turn is destructed by the help of proteases that neutrophils harbor 
[1, 28]. This process is known as diapedesis enabling PMNs move into the subendothelial tissue 
[1]. The overall multistep process of extravasation is presented in Figure 4.

2.3.2. NEUTROPHIL CHEMOTAXIS 
The final step in the migration process is the PMN movement through the tissues according to 
the stimulus or in short undergo chemotaxis [1]. During chemotaxis, the chemotactic gradient 
must be stronger compared to the one that was created during the extravasation by the 
endothelium in order for transmigrated neutrophils to move further. Thus, chemoattractants 
like IL-8 that are encountered in the intermediate places (endothelium) should be overruled by 
other chemoattractants. This overruling behavior has been seen with chemoattractants like N-
formylmethionyl-leucyl-phenylalanine (fMLF) and also complement component C5a. The 
possibility of prioritizing the chemoattractant signal, allows the cells to respond to the “end-
target” and “intermediate” chemoattractants accordingly at that stage of the recruitment. 
Consequently, it is not surprising that the different chemoattractants would lead to the induction 
of different signaling pathways within the cells. When activated by “end-target” chemotactic 
gradient molecules PMNs directionally migrate to the site of infection/inflammation through 
mechanisms involving changes in their intracellular pathways leading to cytoskeleton 
rearrangements and ultimately changed cell shape and polarization [23, 28]. Chemoattractants 
regarding this study are listed as “ligands” in Table 1.  
GPCRs are the ones that play a major role in acting on chemoattractant signals and mediate 
polarization responses. For example. upon typical fMLF stimulation PMNs become polarized 
and can be identified by the two domains that cells create – pseudopod or leading edge and 
uropod or trailing edge seen in the Figure 5 [30, 32].  
The activation by chemoattractants like fMLF induces release of Gßγ subunit of the receptor, 
which participates in activation of G proteins Rac, Rho and cell division control protein 42 
(Cdc42) [32–34]. The leading edge results in actin activation which is caused by an interplay 
of mainly phosphatidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphospahte (PIP3), Ras and Cdc42 molecules [23, 30]. 
Whereas the trailing edge rely mainly on GTPase Rho which act on myosin fibers. The middle 
of the cell is full of microtubules that enable active delivery of intracellular molecules to the

2.3.3. GENERATION OF ROS 
One main activated PMN mechanism of pathogen elimination is creation of ROS which is 
practically solely dependent on NADPH oxidase (Figure 6). This complex has the ability to 
reduce molecular oxygen to ROS. ROS are strong bactericidal molecules that can kill 
pathogens and as well create tissue damage if excessively released [31, 36, 37]. Most 
importantly ROS can be liberated to the extracellular part of the cell at the infection site as well 
as intracellularly into the phagolysosome (Figure 6 “activated state”) [38].

heterodimeric flavohemoprotein, also called cytochrome b, consisting two subunits – gp22phox 
and gp91phox [37, 38]. This can be found on the membranes of secretory vesicles, phagosomes, 
specific granules and plasma membrane of the cell [31, 38]. Cytochrome b compose the

2.4. BALANCED NEUTROPHIL ACTION  
The functions that neutrophils undergo upon pathogen invasion usually are resolved during the 
inflammation resolution phase, followed by apoptotic neutrophil removal (Figure 1). However, 
prolonged acute phase responses lead to a continuous loop of PMN activation. In addition, 
accidentally exocytosed granules and production of ROS, each can influence tissue injury and 
inflammatory disorders. In conditions, such as systemic inflammatory response syndrome 
(SIRS), PMNs are recruited inappropriately by signals coming from host cell mitochondria that 
are recognized by FPRs, consequently resulting in difficulty to restore homeostasis and if 
unable to stop, can cause organ failure and eventually death [4, 21, 28, 39]. Hence, well-
regulated and balanced reaction of PMNs is necessary in these conditions to restore 
homeostasis. In a study done by Niyonsaba et al., synthetic peptides inspired by HDPs were 
tested on human neutrophils. It was seen that these peptides can enhance neutrophil recruitment 
to the site of infection/inflammation, lowering pro-inflammatory cytokine and ROS 
production, as well as degranulation against LPS induced infection. Therefore, it seems that 
immunomodulation of the cells through their receptors employing various ligands is promising 
when creating anti-inflammatory therapeutics [7].

chemokines, cytokines, lipid mediators that add up to this immune response [2, 3].

granules from where they can be mobilized to the cell surface upon cell activation. Therefore, 
these receptors play fundamental role in ensuring proper neutrophil recruitment to the site of 
infection/inflammation [1, 4, 11].

possess a possible FPR orthologue [12]. Therefore, this serves as an inspiration for this project

to create a human adaptable in vitro model to study PMN responses and manipulation using 
various ligands. Consequently, what is known about the immunity and PMNs of pigs will be

2.6. PORCINE INNATE IMMUNITY AND PMNS 
The immune system of a domestic pig (sus scrofa) is very close to that in humans in respect of 
anatomy, organization and immune responses [8]. As in humans, the epithelia of pig present a 
physical barrier for pathogen entry. In addition to this, complement proteins and innate cells 
present similar or same pathways of reacting to pathogens and resolving infections. Moreover, 
PRRs are also evolutionary conserved throughout the species and are expressed on innate 
immune cells – macrophages, dendritic cells and neutrophils. Although the importance of 
PMNs in innate immunity is undeniable, the studies involving porcine neutrophils are still few 
[8].  
Nevertheless, it is known that porcine PMNs are morphologically similar to human cells with 
a round shape but smaller in size and with reduced granularity. Additionally, they express 
higher activation threshold when compared to human cells [8]. It is known that porcine PMNs 
does not produce to HDP family belonging molecules – defensins, however they do produce 
another class – cathelicidins which serves as antimicrobial molecules at the site of 
inflammation [42, 43]. In addition to this, constant bactericidal activity, with release of 
neutrophil serine proteases (NSPs), against Listeria monocytogenes and Pseudomonas

Staphylococcus aureus and P. aeruginosa stimulate porcine NET formation as characterized 
in humans as well bringing up similar responses in vivo when inoculating with P. aeruginosa

Data on porcine FPRs are scarce and contradictory. Fletcher et al., pointed out that porcine

Nevertheless, it was also stated that purified porcine mitochondrial formylated peptide is able 
to activate human PMNs [47]. This also allows to speculate that porcine should hold a similar 
DAMP recognition ability. Altogether, more conclusive data is required to substantiate the 
possible presence of porcine FPRs on neutrophils.  
 
2.7. THE PIG AS ANIMAL MODEL FOR INFLAMMATION STUDIES 
Animal models are needed for the study of inflammation and autoimmunity [48]. Nowadays a 
big range of studies on immune stimulation are based upon functional genomic methods [8, 
49]. Thus, it has been seen that the immune system of a pig can represent immunological 
response of infectious diseases more accurately to that seen in humans, in comparison with 
other used models [8, 9]. Consequently, pig as an animal model seems promising for a range 
of human diseases and is already being adapted in studies mainly targeting human health [8, 
44].  
Nevertheless, mice have been employed in majority of the infection/inflammation studies. 
However, the gathered results usually fail when translated into suitable application in humans 
[8, 49]. For example, in sepsis model it was indicated that the responses are generally similar 
in terms of rise of pro-inflammatory cytokines as in humans, however no clinical advancement 
is made [48]. In general, Seok et al., in 2013 challenged the overall notion of the mouse being 
a good model for inflammation studies, demonstrating that transcriptional responses mimic 
human diseases very inadequately, suggesting the possible reason of the inability to move into 
clinic with gathered results [50]. Moreover, mice possess eight FPRs, of which three are 
expressed on murine PMNs, that are used as a tool to study biological functions of these 
receptors. Nevertheless, the structural differences between two human and mouse FPRs usually 
result in unalike neutrophil activation when using same ligands as their binding might be 
defective. For example, critical FPR1 agonist – fMLF displays low affinity on Fpr1 (FPR1 
orthologue in mice) evidently indicating a different ligand binding profile [16, 51, 52]. As a

2.8. EXPERIMENTAL STRATEGY 
The experimental strategy applied in this project constitutes four main steps: 
• Porcine neutrophil cell isolation from stabilized fresh porcine blood. 
• Stimulation of porcine PMNs with different ligands, including FPR targeted ligands. 
• Characterization of receptor activation with chemotaxis assay. 
• Preliminary ROS assay employing human PMNs for receptor activation studies. 
The first part of the project was focused on successful isolation of PMN from fresh porcine 
blood by adapting a human separation method described by Quinn and DeLeo [31]. Isolation 
success and PMN purity was determined employing flow cytometry. Next, activation of 
isolated PMNs was done with different stimulants: ZAPS, LPS, TNF-α, fMLF and Compound 
43 that was measured by flow cytometry as well. This was done in order to gain data on how 
porcine PMNs react to well defined human ligands as well as to create positive control for 
further functional assessment of PMNs. Lastly, in order to characterize receptor activation, 
chemotaxis assay was performed. The chemotaxis was induced by ZAPS and fMLF and results 
obtained were quantified utilizing lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) enzymatic reaction, that 
directly correlates to the number of migrated cells. In addition, ROS assay was performed using 
human PMNs as preliminary study, employing FPR targeted stimuli - Compound 43 and fMLF. 
The ROS assay allowed to measure extracellular ROS production in the period of time after 
stimulation. The overall, schematic workflow, depicting main steps, is presented below. The 
isolation and stimulation experiments were carried out in a group together with students 
Leiying Zhu and Solveig S. Groen, thus the obtained experimental data was shared and used 
for individual project purposes.

3. BACKGROUND IN METHODS  
3.1. PMN ISOLATION FROM FRESH BLOOD  
The isolation of neutrophils from fresh stabilized blood is based on the differences of the 
densities of all cell populations within blood sample. The density of leukocytes allows them to 
be separated from erythrocytes and then be further isolated into individual leukocyte 
populations [31].  
The separation process depends on two major steps. Firstly, the erythrocyte sedimentation is 
achieved employing dextran – a glucose polymer. Erythrocytes form a rouleaux upon addition 
of dextran, thus they are pulled down faster than the rest of the leukocytes. Secondly, ficoll-
paque, a hydrophilic polysaccharide, is applied which provides the differential sedimentation. 
Due to density difference caused by ficoll-paque PMNs and the remaining erythrocytes pellet 
to the bottom, whereas lymphocytes, basophils, monocytes and platelets stay at the interphase 
in between the plasma and ficoll-paque [31, 63]. The density of ficoll-paque is calculated to be 
in between the bottom and middle cell populations, as a result enabling clear separation [63].  
Furthermore, to eliminate remaining erythrocytes lysis is performed. Lysis step is based on 
erythrocyte hypotonic sensitivity to H2O which is much higher when compared to neutrophils. 
The isotonicity is restored when adding 2,4% NaCl to the sample [31, 63]. This process is 
depicted in Figure 9. 
As extracellular calcium is needed for PMN activation Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS) 
without Ca2+ is used for resuspending the cells during isolation procedure to avoid unnecessary 
cell stimulation. Subsequently, cell samples are resuspended in HBSS with Ca2+ prior further 
use, to initiate cell activation [16, 31].

3.2. ISOLATED PMN STIMULATION 
On the whole, the processes that allow PMNs to respond to and perform distinct cell functions 
are denoted to as neutrophil activation. As mentioned previously, this activation can be induced 
by employing various ligands targeting specific cell surface receptors [1, 10]. Performing 
stimulation assays in vitro it is important to have adequate controls, thus stimulation induced 
by ligands could be observed. As it is known that PMNs are sensitive cells and in vitro can be 
activated by temperature shifts and mishandling of the samples. In addition, most of the 
isolation procedure is done keeping cell samples at ~4°C. Therefore, each stimulation 
experiment must have two negative controls of cell suspension - one kept on ice and another 
in 37°C, in accordance with stimulated samples [31, 64].  
 
3.2.1. CHOICE OF STIMULI FOR ACTIVATION OF ISOLATED 
PORCINE PMNS 
Firstly, ZAPS was used as a stimulator for PMNs. Zymosan is mostly composed of ß-glucans 
and is made from yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae [65, 66]. When activating serum with 
zymosan it serves as C5a complement protein source which is as a strong chemoattractant for 
PMNs and is employed in cell activation studies [13, 67]. Moreover, LPS was used which is 
gram-negative bacterial component and is known to be one of the PAMPs to induce neutrophil 
activation [68, 69]. In addition to this it is widely used as an effective stimulator of innate 
immune system in different animal models in vivo, including pigs [70, 71]. Furthermore, TNF-
α was included, which is well documented pro-inflammatory cytokine that stimulates 
endothelial wall and is primarily involved in PMN extravasation [1, 72]. Most importantly, all 
of these ligands have been seen to induce CD11b upregulation in human PMNs [72, 73].  
First FPR binding ligand used - fMLF is known to be FPR1 agonist and serves as 
chemoattractant guiding the cells to the site of inflammation. Also, it is noticed that it induces 
ROS production within the phagolysosome [1, 74]. Next, Compound 43 was used as a FPR 
stimulant that is able to induce all neutrophil functions especially activate NADPH oxidase. 
This non-peptide - pyrazolone was discovered using molecule library screening and is known 
to bind and be agonist for both FPRs with preference for FPR1 on human PMNs [53, 75].  
 
3.3. FLOW CYTOMETRY 
Flow cytometry is an analytical tool used for measuring several physical characteristics of 
individual cells simultaneously passing with the stream of fluid through the laser beam, inside 
flow cytometer. The illuminated cells create signals that are recorded and present 
characteristics of single cells based on its light scattering. Light scattering allow to measure 
internal complexity and relative size of the individual cells [76, 77]. To be more precise, light 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
18
that is scattered after forward (forward scatter (FSC)) contact with the cell allows to evaluate 
relative size whereas light scattered after side (side scatter (SSC)) interaction clarify cell 
complexion - granularity [76]. In addition, particular markers creating fluorescence can be used 
to detect specific populations of the cells and to differentiate them within one sample. Emitted 
light from fluorescently marked antibodies reveal a wide range of cell surface and cytoplasmic 
antigens permitting to characterize cells in depth [76, 77].  
 
3.3.1. SIGNAL PROCESSING BY FLOW CYTOMETRY 
The scattered light from individual cells generate a signal characterized as an event. In addition, 
absolute area and height of that event is measured by flow cytometer. These measurements 
directly correlate to the intensity of the fluorescence for a particular event and are usually 
displayed on a log scale that is portrayed on a histogram. This presentation allows the user to 
differentiate between the events possessing low (left side of the log scale) or high (right side 
of the log scale) fluorescence intensity [76, 77]. This is particularly relevant when detecting 
bound antibodies with conjugated fluorophores to the cells of interest. For example, (Figure 
10A and B) fluorescence of the antibody conjugated with fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) 
and bound to the cells of interest can be presented on log scale as low or high for that particular 
fluorophore [77]. The low intensity happens when events are “negative” for FITC and high 
when they are “positive”. Thus, this allow the user to see if the cell of interest is in fact 
expressing antigen that the antibody conjugated with FITC is “positive” for or not - being 
“negative”.

chemiluminescent and this light can be read on the spectrophotometer (Figure 17). Most 
importantly, real time ROS production measurements can be done, when activating the cells, 
generating curves that can be visualized by selected software [90, 91].

4. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
4.1. PMN ISOLATION FROM FRESH PORCINE BLOOD 
PMNs were isolated from freshly drawn porcine blood which was collected in the 
ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) anticoagulant containing tubes (BD Vacutainer®). 9 
mL of blood was taken and combined with equal amount of physiological NaCl (Phys NaCl, 
substrate department) and 2% Dextran (Dextran T500, RefNr: 551005009006, pharmacosmos) 
in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (substrate department) in a separate tube resulting in total 
volume of 27 mL (done sterile and at room temperature). Next, the tube was inverted to mix 
all three solutions together and set down to stand for 25-35 minutes. This step permitted red 
blood cells to fall to the bottom of the tube, thus allowing to discard most of the erythrocytes 
from the blood sample. The upper phase was collected into three separate tubes: 1) ~5 mL – 
for leukocyte sample, 2) ~8 mL – for PMN sample 3) ~8 mL – for second PMN sample. Both 
PMN samples were carefully loaded on 4mL of the ficoll-paque (Ficoll-paque PLUS, RefNr: 
17144003, GE Healthcare) avoiding mixing of the two solutions. Thereafter, leukocyte and 
PMN samples were centrifuged (Thermo Scientific™ Heraeus™ Megafuge™ 16 Centrifuge 
Series). The leukocyte cell population with some remaining erythrocytes was pulled down to 
the pellet. The PMN sample with ficoll-paque resulted in sample segregation developing 
different phases containing PMNs and some remaining red blood cells at the pellet (can be seen 
in Figure 9 after first centrifugation step). 
Following this, upper and middle phases were removed from both leukocyte and PMN samples 
leaving only the pellets. To lengthen the durability of the cells and avoid activation of PMNs, 
samples were kept on ice from this step forward. Then, the samples underwent washing steps 
to lyse remaining red blood cells. Firstly, to start the lysis pellets were resuspended in 3 mL of 
miliQ H2O (miliQ®) and after 30 seconds in 1mL PBS with 2,4% NaCl (Product Nr: S6546 
Sigma-Aldrich) was added for neutralization. Then, 10 mL of HBSS without Ca2+ (LOT: 
1930013 Ref: 1930013 Gibco by Life technologies) supplemented with 10mM glucose 
(K34463342 608, MERCK) to avoid unnecessary PMN activation was loaded to each sample 
and samples were centrifuged again. Washing steps were repeated 1-4 times until pellet seemed 
white and no erythrocytes were left. After the last wash, the leukocyte sample was resuspended 
in 4 mL HBSS with Ca2+ (LOT: 1904124 Ref: 24020-117 Gibco by Life technologies) 
supplemented with 10mM glucose and each PMN samples in 2 mL HBSS with Ca2+ to initiate 
neutrophil activation. Then, PMN samples were pooled together to achieve total volume of 4 
mL and were further used for calculating the total number of cells per milliliter. Full isolation 
protocol can be reviewed in Appendix 1.

4.2. CELL COUNTING 
To calculate the total amount of the cells within samples they were thoroughly shaken up to 
even the number of cells throughout the solution. Then, 20 µL of each leukocyte and PMN 
samples were diluted in 180 µL HBSS with Ca2+ independently. After proper mixing, further 
dilution was made by adding 20 µL of already diluted samples into 20 µL Trypan Blue Solution 
(Ref: 15250-061 Gibco by Life technologies) for better visualization of the cells. Furthermore, 
~20 µL of this dilution was loaded onto Thoma cell counting chamber (BRAND® counting 
chamber ref: 718020, MERCK). Following this leukocyte and PMN samples were counted 
under the microscope (DMIL, Leica microsystems, LEICA DFC290) manually at 10X 
magnification. The cell count was used to calculate the cell number needed per milliliter for 
further staining and activation of the cells, which was done every experiment due to variation 
of cell number. The cell counting formula can be reviewed in Appendix 2.  
Subsequently, 1x106 cells/mL were used for isolated cell identification and differentiation as 
well as for chemotaxis and ROS assays. Accordingly, 7 x106 cells/mL were used for activation 
of purified PMNs (or leukocytes). For morphological isolation evaluation Giemsa staining of 
cell suspension was done as described in Quinn and DeLeo book and visualized employing 
microscope at 40X magnification [31].

5. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
5.1. ISOLATION OF PORCINE PMNS FROM FRESH BLOOD 
First step of the project was to ensure successful isolation of porcine PMNs from fresh porcine 
blood. Since there is no established isolation protocol for porcine neutrophils, a well-known 
protocol for isolation of human PMNs was adapted and verification of the protocol was 
necessary [31]. The success of isolation was compared to a full leukocyte population sample 
(further referred as pre-isolation sample) taken from the same blood sample as a control. The 
full leukocyte sample was taken after dextran sedimentation without additional Ficoll-paque 
sedimentation (section 3.1). Differentiation, purity and degranulation degree of the cells pre- 
and post-isolation was tested on flow cytometer by addition of SWC3 (CD172a) cell surface 
marker and plotted versus SSC characterizing granularity of the cells. The results analyzed on 
FlowJo® software enabled to extract data in “total percentage” referring to individual 
population event number divided by total number of cell populations per sample allowing to 
determine purity (Figure 18). Furthermore, mean values of “total percentages” of different cell 
subpopulations were plotted, employing GraphPad 7 software. In this section, total (n=20) 
isolation results will be discussed and presented referring to the gating strategy shown in Figure 
12 as an example. Results are also plotted on a bar and pie charts to indicate overall success of 
isolation. All isolation results that bar and pie charts are based on can be found in Appendix 5. 
Example in Figure 18 indicate results obtained in experiment 1. This clearly show three 
different cell subpopulations on SWC3 (CD172a) versus SSC plot. Cell surface marker and 
SSC allow to determine different cell population based on their expression of SWC3 (CD172a) 
and granularity. Thus, monocyte, PMN and lymphocyte populations can be gated to obtain the 
“total percentage” of subpopulations. In addition to this, populations can be visualized when 
comparing samples of pre-isolation (Figure 18A) and post-isolation (Figure 18B).  
Summarizing (Figure 19A) all isolation experiment results (n=20) approximately two-fold 
increase of PMN population percentage can be seen after isolation (from ~43% to ~97%). In 
contrast, monocyte and lymphocyte subpopulation percentage decrease is observed by ~7% 
and ~47%, respectively. In addition to this, only PMN population percentage numbers pre- and 
post-isolation were plotted separately on a bar chart (Figure 19B). The difference in percentage 
was evaluated statistically with paired t-test revealing that p<0.0001 confirming that this 
difference is statistically significant.  
Human neutrophils are active cells that can be easily triggered by improper handling upon 
isolation. Therefore, this multistep isolation process is critical to yield high purity of non-
activated cells. Limited knowledge of porcine PMN isolation makes this part of the project 
even more valuable for future experiments. Overall 96.91% PMN purity form 9 ml of blood 
can be reached using adapted protocol. Moreover, results from Figure 18 allow to determine 
whether degranulation has occurred during isolation process. No shift pre- and post-isolation 
on SSC axis can be detected, hence indicating that yielded PMNs are in “inactivated” state.

5.2.1.  NEUTROPHIL STIMULATION WITH ZAPS 
ZAPS in this project was used as C5a complement-derived product which is known to be 
effective inflammatory peptide. Most importantly it is a keen chemoattractant not only in 
humans, but also in animals such as rabbits and rats [13, 67]. Five (n=5) individual experiments 
were run to investigate porcine PMN response to ZAPS. As mentioned in section 4.3. ZAPS 
was used in 0.5%, 1% and 2.5% concentrations including two negative controls (ICE and 37 
oC). CD11R1+ was assessed and calculated as MFI% value. Here FlowJo® image and plotted 
data using GraphPad 7 is represented (Figure 23A and B). There is statistical significance (SS) 
in-between ICE and all concentrations used (p=0.005), as well as between 37 oC and 2.5% also 
in-between 0.5% and 2.5% (p=0.014). 
As can be seen in the Figure 23A and B 37 oC temperature has slight impact on CD11R1 
expression on CD11R1+ cells despite of absence in ZAPS. This could be explained as 
spontaneous degranulation that takes place when warming unstimulated cells, however the 
extent is not the same when compared with stimulated cells. This can also be perceived when 
warming isolated human neutrophils, hence this is stressing the importance of including 
temperature controls for accurate evaluation of any used ligands as stimulants [96]. Moreover, 
upon higher ZAPS stimulation porcine PMN CD11R1 expression is proportional (Figure 23B) 
with highest SS response observed at 2.5% of ZAPS used. This proportional response is also 
in comparison to other groups where zymosan treated serum or directly C5a stimulated human 
and porcine PMNs were used [13, 73, 97, 98]. For example, Furebring et al., reported that 
CD11b expression is increased gradually when stimulating human granulocytes with rising 
concentration of C5a form 1 nM/L to 100 nM/L [73]. Thus, this verify that isolated porcine 
cells are functional and can be induced by ZAPS. Fletcher et al., also pointed out that even 
higher chemotactic response could also be generated with purified porcine C5a [13]. This could 
be one possibility for generating maximum PMN response in future.

5.2.2. NEUTROPHIL CELL STIMULATION WITH LPS 
Lipopolysaccharide was chosen as a second stimuli for purified porcine PMNs. In humans this 
is already known as stimulator for quite a long time [68, 69] In addition to this it known as a 
key molecule initiating some porcine diseases, thus has already been used in studying 
inflammation modulation with selective drugs [71]. To confirm this knowledge LPS was 
included and tested in two individual experiments (n=2). Two negative controls were also 
included and series of LPS concentrations were tested done as two-fold dilutions (refer section 
4.3.). No SS were obtained (p>0.05) among these results. 
As it is depicted in Figure 24A 37 oC degree incubation activates cells by minor extent as it is 
seen in stimulation with ZAPS (Figure 23A). Furthermore, 50ng/mL concentration of LPS 
seems to give slightly higher CD11R1+ expression compared to 37 oC control. This can be 
further seen on a bar chart (Figure 24B) throughout all concentrations used. Due to reasonably 
high error bar on 37 oC control all stimulation concentrations seem to be equal and this makes 
it difficult to determine upregulation in CD11R1. This could be attributed to low number of 
experimental repeats.  
Despite of this some considerations can be done. Several previous studies on porcine 
neutrophils pointed out that stimulation with LPS should be done with longer incubation time. 
Bouissou et al., used 3 hour incubation time at 37 oC to achieve nuclear architecture changes 
in porcine PMNs with 10µg/mL LPS [99]. Moreover, Sanz-Santos et al., reported that most 
transcriptional changes in porcine PMNs occurred only after 9 hours at 37 oC post 100 ng/mL 
LPS activation [100]. Although, these studies are focused on genetics, it still might indicate 
that prolongation of incubation time is needed to achieve maximal response from porcine 
PMNs in vitro. Additionally, 0.5µg/mL LPS induced murine neutrophils showed time 
dependent CD11b upregulation reaching a peak at 1 hour of incubation strongly suggesting 
that incubation time should be prolonged [101]. On the other hand, it was noticed that TLR4 
(LPS binding receptor) expression on porcine neutrophils is increased after 30 minutes when 
incubating with 25µg/mL LPS at 39 oC [54]. This imply that increase of incubation temperature 
might be necessary to achieve PMN higher activation in vitro. Furthermore, it is also proven 
that lipopolysaccharide binding protein (LBP) that is present in serum is essential for binding 
LPS to its respective receptor. When using cells in serum-free conditions it is known that 
<100ng/ml concentration of LPS is unable to activate neutrophil cells due to the absence of 
LBP [69]. This is also seen in previously mentioned study by Sanz-Santos et al., where bovine 
fetal serum was used to supplement isolated PMN sample [100]. Thus, this observation 
suggests that stimulation with LPS also need serum to achieve firm stimulation. In addition to 
this, it is worth to mention that in human PMNs, slight but gradual upregulation of CD11b 
upon LPS stimulation is seen when using concentrations from 0.1 ng/mL to 10 ng/mL, 
therefore concentrations applied in this study should be in the range of PMN activation [102].

5.2.3. NEUTROPHIL CELL STIMULATION WITH TNF-⍺ 
Next, TNF-α stimulation was done, since it is known as a pro-inflammatory cytokine that is 
able to induce CD11b upregulation in humans [1, 72]. To test this TNF-α was used in four 
individual experiments, in a range of six concentrations. First two experiments included 1, 10 
and 100ng/mL concentrations. For further full response investigation three more 
concentrations were added. Note that concentrations 10 and 100 ng/mL were used in four 
experiments (n=4) and 1, 25, 50 and 200 ng/mL in two (n=2). No SS observed (p>0.05) among 
these results.  
As it is depicted in Figure 25A 37 oC degree incubation activates cells similarly as it is seen in 
stimulation with ZAPS and LPS (Figures 23A and 24A). Furthermore, 50ng/mL, 100ng/mL 
and 200ng/mL concentrations of TNF-α seems to give similar CD11R1+ expression as induced 
with 2.5% ZAPS. Exploring summarized data on individual concentrations (Figure 25B) it 
appears that TNF-α has the ability to stimulate upregulation of CD11R1 on porcine PMNs. 
Some higher error bars can be seen on concentrations 25, 50 and 200 ng/mL which can be also 
attributed to low number of experimental repeats when compared to concentrations that have 
n=4. However, overall activations are increasing and following dose response. 
These results are in agreement with Furebring et al., where dose dependent CD11b increase 
was observed on human granulocytes using from 1 ng/mL to 100 ng/mL of TNF-α [73]. In 
addition, Potera et al., reported concentration dependent increase of CD11b marker in humans 
was achieved when concentrations used varied from 10 to 1000 pg/mL. They point out that 
laboratory studies that adjust higher concentrations (5-100 ng/mL) greatly exceed that what is 
documented in most physiological inflammatory states [72]. Moreover, Wittman et al., also 
reported dose dependent upregulation of CD11b when employing lower (0.01 - 10 ng/mL) 
concentrations of TNF-α [103]. Therefore, it would be noteworthy to test lower concentrations 
for more precise results as well as to investigate if porcine PMNs would respond to them in 
vitro. However, differences between species leading to distinct activation threshold of the cells 
is not unexpected as it has been already observed [44, 51].  
However, more concerning pattern can be noted with 37 oC control which seems to activate 
cells greatly without any stimulation in all three stimulations used so far. This overall pattern 
makes detection of low activation that is induced by different ligands rather difficult. During 
cell suspension preparation, cells were kept on ice to avoid cell activation, however when 
warming up activation can be seen sometimes as high as or close to the cells induced with 
stimulations. Forsyth and Levinsky also drew attention to this problem. They tested different 
cell temperature shifts in order to access difference in CD11b upregulation. When cells are 
shifted from 4 oC to 37 oC cells underwent considerable upregulation of CD11b. Whereas 
cooling the cells from 37 oC or warming room temperature cells to 37 oC did not affect CD11b 
expression [64]. These results propose that porcine PMNs might be very sensitive to this major 
temperature change as well, thus lower shift should be also considered.

5.2.4. NEUTROPHIL STIMULATION WITH FMLF 
First FPR ligand used in this project was fMLF, one of the most well-known chemoattractants 
for human PMNs. It is a strong FPR agonist with higher affinity for FPR1 than FPR2 [3, 11, 
51]. Two individual experiments were done (n=2) employing a range of different 
concentrations (section 4.3.). Alongside different concentration samples four negative controls 
were included. Stimulation degree can be seen in FlowJo® images and overall data is plotted 
on a bar chart. No SS observed (p>0.05) among these results.  
Looking at Figure 26A, for visual representation of CD11R1 upregulation DMSO ICE and 
DMSO 37 oC solvent controls are given. Slight shift can be observed when comparing controls 
and sample with 50 nM fMLF. Analyzing all controls and used concentrations altogether 
(Figure 26B) it can be seen that 37 oC control is in the same range as with other included 
stimulations (ZAPS, LPS and TNF- α), giving the same MFI % ranging from ~ 25 to 45%. 
However, very rapid increase can be observed in other two solvent controls DMSO ICE and 
DMSO 37 oC and then stimulation peak is reached at 50 nM fMLF induced cells. Ideally, 
DMSO controls should be in the same range as ICE and 37 oC control. However, since it gives 
such a high peak it suggests that added DMSO concentration is stimulating PMNs very 
drastically. Consequently, it makes it more complicated to perceive stimulation coming from 
fMLF itself. Taking this in to account, it looks like concentrations 20 nM, 100 nM and 500 nM 
of fMLF gives smaller or almost the same response as DMSO controls, thus here cell 
stimulation could be attributed to DMSO alone. However, if looking at concentrations 50 nM 
and 200 nM higher percentage is showing. This might also imply that there is in fact response 
coming from fMLF, but true level of it is put into the shade by interference of DMSO.  
In humans it is known that fMLF induces CD11b upregulation steeply upon increase of dose 
from 0 nM to 100 nM, then plateauing with higher concentrations [104]. However, this does 
not seem to be the case in this study. As Timm et al., indicated that DMSO concentrations from 
0.25% up to 1% induce human HL-60 (promyelocyte-like cell that can differentiate to 
granulocyte-like cells (Figure 2)) to produce ROS. Whereas, higher percentage can inhibit cell 
activities [105]. This would give a possible explanation to cell stimulation. Here, lowest 
concentration of 0.2% DMSO is used only in the controls and 20 nM, other dilutions of fMLF 
were made containing higher concentrations of DMSO. Nevertheless, strong activation on 
0.2% indicate that even lower concentrations of DMSO has to be employed for more accurate 
activation of the porcine cells. For instance, Fletcher et al., recommend using dilutions made 
so that end concentration of DMSO would be 0.1% or lower to avoid activation/inhibition of 
the porcine PMNs [13]. Therefore, this should be strongly advised for conducting further 
experiments.

in tissue culture) with FPR1 and FPR2 transfected cells [106]. Likewise as it is pointed out that 
porcine neutrophil cells have higher activation threshold when compared to humans [44]. Thus, 
results obtained here strongly indicate that increase in concentration is needed to evaluate 
Compound 43 maximal porcine PMN response through FPRs.

5.3.1. CHEMOTAXIS ASSAY 
Porcine PMN chemotaxis was induced applying ZAPS and fMLF then measured by LDH 
enzymatic reaction, where migration directly corresponds to absorbance rate obtained by plate 
reader. Four individual (n=4) chemotaxis experiments were performed with ZAPS and five 
(n=5) with fMLF. In addition, extra experiments were done employing isolated human PMNs 
at Göteborg University, Sweden (Forsman and Dahlgren group). Results gathered on human 
PMNs are presented here for comparison. One experiment (n=1) were done using purified C5a, 
which is used as contrast to ZAPS. Furthermore, ZAPS was chosen for this assay since it 
showed best stimulation potential among other stimulants. Not only being strong 
chemoattractant it serves as finest indicator, in this study, to evaluate if chemotaxis is provoked 
by FPR targeting ligand – fMLF. Moreover, two individual (n=2) experiments were performed 
including fMLF. Results are presented on bar charts, after background subtraction. Negative 
(spontaneous migration) and positive (100% cells) controls are included and shown on each 
bar graph as indication for minimal and maximal response that can be achieved using this assay. 
For porcine PMN chemotaxis with fMLF additional DMSO control is contained. All 
concentrations used in the chemotaxis assay are listed in Table 5. No SS was observed (p>0.05) 
among these results. 
As it can be observed in Figure 29A and B in both species minimal and maximal controls are 
positioned rather close on the scale. This indicates similar assay capabilities in both species. 
Analyzing each concentration individually, highest migration in porcine system is observed at 
0.1% ZAPS whereas in humans at 1ng/ml of C5a. When applying higher concentrations of 
ZAPS in pigs, migration seems to be lowered gradually. Whereas in humans 1ng/ml 
concentration induces almost highest migration compared to 100% control, while lower 
concentrations used are stimulating chemotaxis at very little rate. Comparing both graphs it 
looks as if migration rates are representing induction side of bell shaped dose response in 
humans while in porcine the - inhibiting side. Since quantification of C5a in ZAPS was not 
done it is not known exactly how much ng/mL of C5a was used in this study. However, it is 
most likely much higher than 1ng/mL as employed on human cells. Overall, results indicate 
that in order to obtain higher chemotaxis rate in porcine system, ZAPS concentration should 
be lowered. This is in agreement with study done by Fletcher et al., where chemotaxis was 
relatively low when high percentage of ZAPS was used [13]. However, results obtained 
indicate that porcine cells are fully functional to perform directional migration and can be used 
in further studies.  
Furthermore, cell migration of porcine and human PMN towards fMLF is depicted in Figure 
30A and B, respectively. As it can be seen 100% control in porcine system seems to be a little 
lower than in humans. However, it still remains high compared to induced migrations in both 
cases. In addition, “corrected” DMSO percentage (lower than 0.01%) is barely creating 
chemotactic response, which should not interfere with results obtained in this assay.

Human PMNs were used in a preliminary study for ROS production. ROS was induced 
applying FPR targeted stimuli - fMLF and Compound 43 then measured as isoluminol

(Forsman and Dahlgren group). One representative experiment (n=1) of extracellular ROS 
production over time is shown in Figure 31. Buffer with cell suspension (dashed line) and 100 
nM fMLF induced cells (dotted line) is represented on the graph as a negative and positive

As it can be seen (Figure 31) 100nM fMLF induces comparably high ROS production upon

100nM concentration is used. However, dose dependent increase can be observed when using 
higher concentrations reaching a maximum ROS production with 1µM of Compound 43. This

production induced by Compound 43 can be seen in study done by Forsman et al., were murine

ROS production can be observed with other non FPR targeted ligands used in this study. For 
example, dose dependent stimulation of human PMNs with Zymosan was seen to induce

preparation derived from S. cerevisiae. In addition, as it is known that LPS is one of the core 
PAMP for PRR recognition in neutrophils it is not a surprise that ROS is generated upon LPS 
activation in humans [7]. Accordingly, it has been seen that ROS is produced upon stimulation 
of porcine PMNs by LPS displaying bacteria such as Salmonella Enterica indicating annotating 
more interspecies resemblances [108]. Nevertheless, scarce number of studies involving 
porcine PMNs and employing ROS assay makes it difficult to create overall picture.  
Usually extracellular ROS measurement is chosen over intracellular measurement since the 
generated chemiluminesce is more intense and maximal values can be reached sooner [89]. 
Nevertheless, appropriate measurement should be chosen when constructing individual 
experiments. Since it is observed that the induction of intracellular ROS production by known 
agonists in human neutrophils differ from mouse PMNs [61]. In addition, ROS generation 
could serve as a powerful tool to study ligands possessing immunomodulation properties. As 
Niyonsaba et al., reported that HDP inspired ligands can lower LPS induced intracellular ROS 
generation on human PMNs [7]. Consequently, it would be noteworthy to test porcine PMN 
generated ROS production to gain knowledge on diverse ligand provoked stimulation, 
particularly for putative FPR receptor characterization, and to develop animal model for anti-
inflammatory therapeutic studies.

6. CONCLUSION 
 
The general aim of this study was to enable investigation of porcine neutrophil activation with 
various ligands with important implementation to create human adaptable porcine model for 
infection and/or inflammation reactions of PMNs. In order to substantiate that findings in 
porcine models are relevant for humans, it is necessary to thoroughly study porcine neutrophil 
responses to well-known stimulators and modulators of human and to show that these 
responses are similar. In turn, for FPR characterization selective functional assays should be 
employed using a library of receptor targeted ligands. 
To achieve the aim, a four-step experimental approach was applied. Firstly, human PMN 
isolation protocol was adapted to isolate porcine neutrophils. Furthermore, selected ligands 
were used to test isolated porcine PMN activation. Lastly, isolated cells were employed for 
characterization of receptor activation in chemotaxis assay. Additionally, preliminary ROS 
assay was done using human PMNs.  
Results gathered strongly indicate that adapted protocol is reliable for yielding high purity 
(~97%) non-activated porcine PMNs. This generates perfect in vitro model which can be used 
in cellular manipulations with desired ligands.  
Selected ligand stimulations revealed that ZAPS is able to activate cells gradually by SS 
difference upon concentration increase peaking at 2.5%. Similar pattern without SS, was 
observed with TNF-α and Compound 43 which showed ability to activate porcine PMNs, 
indicating highest responses at 200 ng/mL and 500 nM, respectively. This data indicates that 
isolated porcine PMNs are fully functional and can be used in selective characterization assays. 
Furthermore, receptor activation characterization with chemotaxis assay indicated cell 
migration towards low concentrations of both - ZAPS and fMLF chemoattractants 0.1% and 
1-5nM, correspondingly. Most notably this data support the possibility of porcine PMNs 
expressing FPR receptor orthologue, being in opposition to previously made statement by 
Fletcher et al [13].  
In addition, preliminary ROS assay using human PMNs induced with fMLF and Compound 
43 serves as guideline, for future establishment of ROS analysis of porcine neutrophils 
indicating different ligand binding potency when 100 nM concentration of both ligands is used.  
To conclude, this study confirmed that isolated porcine PMNs are able to respond to various 
ligand stimulations additionally, annotating presence of possible FPR ortholog in terms of 
CD11R1 upregulation induced by Compound 43 as well as chemotactic migration towards 
fMLF. Therefore, results obtained serves as good starting point for creating human adaptable 
animal model for neutrophil immunomodulation studies and inflammatory disease 
investigations.

7. FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
 
In order to verify that findings in porcine models are comparable to humans, it is necessary to 
carefully study porcine neutrophil responses to well-known stimulators and modulators, which 
is best performed with pure preparations of porcine neutrophils. Therefore, modified porcine 
PMN isolation protocol can be further used in investigation of neutrophil responses, in order 
to test infection and/or inflammation immunomodulation in pig model. Still some optimization 
can be done on establishing comparable activation with selected ligands. Overall, it is 
important to determine appropriate concentrations for stimuli and solvents. Also, as it is 
observed in other studies, it could be beneficial to increase incubation time and temperature to 
achieve maximal response. In addition, it would be interesting to stimulate cells prepared at 
room temperature as Forsyth and Levinsky pointed out, since it might cause artificial CD11b 
upregulation by shifting the cells from 4ºC to 37ºC [64]. Nevertheless, CD11R1 marker is 
enough to acquire gradual cell stimulation and can be used to obtain data by employing other 
stimulants. In general, it is essential to test and optimize a range of various agonist and 
antagonist ligands on isolated porcine PMNs that could have therapeutic potential. In addition, 
chemotaxis and ROS assays seem to be irreplaceable to study induced selective neutrophil 
functions. Most notably, these assays can be used for characterization of possible porcine FPR 
orthologue in future studies. As seen from this study, it is important to employ adequate ligand 
concentrations and to test broader FPR targeted ligand library. Particularly FPR2 binding 
ligands, since these were not included here.

In this appendix, detailed protocols for porcine neutrophil isolation and stimulations with LPS, 
TNF-α, ZAS, compound 43 and fMLF are presented. 
GRANULOCYTE ISOLATION FROM FRESH PORCINE BLOOD (DONE STERILE)  
1. Turn on the centrifuge and cool it down to 4 oC. 
2. Add 9 mL blood to a 50 mL polycarbon tube. Add 9 mL physiological (9%) NaCl (RT). Then 
add 9 mL 2% Dextran in physiological NaCl (room temperature) to get a final concentration of 
1% Dextran. Cover with lid and shake. 
3. Let stand for 20-35 min for the erythrocytes to fall to the bottom. (1 sample here taken for 
leukocyte evaluation and kept on ice. Centrifuge leukocyte sample for 10 min with 400xg, 
acceleration 9, deceleration 9. After centrifugation continue with erythrocyte lysis steps for 2-
4 times to obtain leukocyte sample (can be done alongside with purified neutrophil sample)). 
Optional: FULL LEUKOCYTE SAMPLE IS STIMULATED TOGETHER WITH PURIFIED 
NEUTROPHIL SAMPLE. 
4. Prepare two15 mL tubes with 4 mL Ficoll-paque. 
5. Carefully load 8 mL of the upper phase from dextran sediment tubes onto the ficoll-paque. 
6. Centrifuge at 800xg, 15 min, 4ᵒC (acceleration – 4 deceleration - 0). Keep on ice after this step. 
7. The PMNs and some erythrocytes are now in the pellet in the bottom. Suck off the liquid and 
carefully suck the middle phase which contains the mononuclear cells (monocytes and 
lymphocytes, we can take sample here for their evaluation). 
8. Lyse the rest of the erythrocytes by: 
a. Add 3 ml dH2O and shake so the pellet disappears (MILIQ – ion exchange) 
b. After 30 seconds add 1 ml PBS w 2.4% NaCl  
c. Add HBSS without calcium 10 ml 
d. Centrifuge at 400 xg (acceleration 9, deceleration 9), 10 min, 4ᵒC (neutrophils should 
be sediment and erythrocytes should be lysed and in supernatant) 
Note! Keep all buffers and the cell suspensions on ice 
9. Repeat the lysing steps 2-4 times, until almost no erythrocytes are left. Wash once with HBSS 
without calcium.  
10. Resuspend in HBSS with calcium (4 ml).  
11. Dilute 1:10 for cell counting: 20 µl cells + 180 µl HBSS with calcium. Also, dyeing cells with 
Trypan Blue 1:1 dilution (20 µl of cells + HBSS and 20 µl Trypan Blue).  
12. This sample loaded onto the chamber to calculate cell count.  
13. After counting the cell number must be diluted to 1x106 cells/ml for isolation checkup and 
SWC3 staining as well as for chemotaxis assay. 7 x 10 6 cells/ml are calculated for stimulation 
with different ligands and CD11R1 staining. All cell suspensions are kept on ice to prevent 
activation of the PMNs and prolong the durability of the cells.
