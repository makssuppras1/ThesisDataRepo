Summary
This thesis introduces kites to turbine wind farms. Kites are introduced in steady flight
in an altitude slightly above the wind turbines to increase vertical mixing of wind in
the wakes of the turbines. The increased vertical mixing has been shown in literature to
re-energize the wakes and thereby lessen the wake effect in a wind farm. By weakening
the wake effect, the overall energy output of the wind farm will increase.
An adaptation of the Wind Farm Layout Optimization Problem (WFLOP) to include
kites is presented. In this thesis, the adapted version of the WFLOP is solved using a
heuristic approach by firstly using the Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Procedure
(GRASP) technique to find an initial solution. Then the solution is improved using the
global search metaheuristic strategy Simulated Annealing. Wind farms are generated
by use of real-life mimicking instances using a real wind scenario. Turbines and kites
are placed in these instances using the GRASP and Simulated Annealing algorithms.
The aim of this thesis is to study the effect of kites in a turbine wind farm. Defining
kite eﬀiciency as the amount of wake effect a kite can remove from a turbine and kite cost
as a percentage of turbine cost, this thesis uses values of kite eﬀiciency found in previous
large-eddy simulations to evaluate the effects of kites. Introducing kites to turbine wind
farms can be expected to increase the overall power production of the wind farm by 1%
– 2%. The best case scenario found in this thesis, shows an increase in power production
by 4%. The default values chosen throughout this thesis as being fair middle values,
show an increase in power production by 2%. A figure showing the combinations of kite
eﬀiciency and kite cost that result in whether the kites are beneficial to the wind farm
is presented as a result of a sensitivity analysis. Furthermore, this thesis finds a shift
in turbine placement strategy from the classic WFLOP to the adaptation introduced in
this paper, as kites are also placed.
The potential increase in energy production by adding kites to a turbine wind farm is
significant and it is recommended that future studies research more on the interactions of
multiple kites on each other and on wakes in a wind farm. Further, real-life experiments
with kites should be done using different costs and designs with the aim of finding a kite
design within the values found to be profitable by this thesis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Global climate changes are happening faster than in any other period of history available
to us to measure [1]. The main cause is the huge amount of greenhouse gases (carbon
dioxide, methane, etc.) emitted by humans when producing energy. As we are more
people in the World than ever before, with a continuously growing global population [2],
and as the energy becomes an increasing necessity in our lives, the amounts of energy
we need is likewise increasing. It is now important to focus on harvesting more energy,
but emitting less greenhouse gases, to meet the demand of the human population while
it not being on the expense of the inhabitability of the planet.
For this reason, the EU created the 2030 climate and energy framework, with the
ambition of reaching a level of at least 32% of all consumed energy within the EU to
be from renewable sources. Further, the European Commission has of 2021 proposed to
raise this target to 40% [3], which contributes to the goal of becoming ‘climate-neutral’
by 2050, meaning no net emissions of greenhouse gases. One of the tools to reach this
goal is offshore energy, as the European Commission puts it “The energy of the oceans
can be harnessed by modern technologies without emitting any greenhouse gases, making
offshore renewable energy a potential cornerstone of the clean energy transition in the
EU.” [4].
Offshore wind farms continue to be a significant part of the solution to the problem
of supplying the world with renewable energy and for this reason offshore wind farms
are growing rapidly. From 2010 to 2019 the average wind farm doubled in size, from
313 MW to 621 MW [5]. This growth is due to turbine capacity getting bigger but also
more turbines being placed in wind farms. The potential of wind farms also grows as
new areas are unlocked with the ability to place farms in deeper water, further from
the coast. Some of the newest developments in this regard is floating wind farms, the
biggest being Kincardine in Scotland (50 MW) soon to be overtaken by Hywind Tampen
in Norway (95 MW) [6, 7]. Further ingenuity and management is required to optimize
the power production of offshore wind farms, while minimizing the costs associated with
them. This paper will therefore analyse the potential of adding kites to offshore turbine
wind farms.

1.1 Wake effect
2
1.1
Wake effect
A wind farm consists of multiple wind turbines transforming wind energy into electrical
power. The turbines are placed in farms for practicalities such as minimizing mainte-
nance and installation costs, but grouping turbines together lowers the expected power
production of the whole wind farm compared to the summed potential of the individual
turbines [8]. The main reason is, a turbine creates a cone of turbulence downwind when
extracting power from the wind. The cone is called ‘the wake’ and it results in reduced
wind speeds and thereby reduced power production of any turbine in the wake. This
phenomenon is the wake effect. A main task of designing a wind farm is the Wind Farm
Layout Optimization Problem (WFLOP), which is the challenge of placing n turbines in
a set of k available locations in an optimal manner regarding power output of the wind
farm. What makes the WFLOP challenging, but also important, is the consideration
of the wake effect when placing the turbines, as literature shows wake effect to have a
significant impact on the overall power production of wind farms [9].
1.2
Kites in wind farms
Wake effect is the result of a turbine being horizontally blocked off the full potential of
wind by another turbine. Building on the continued ingenuity in the field of offshore
wind farms, a different approach to minimizing the wake effect could be considered by
taking advantage of the vertical transport of wind. In an offshore wind farm, the most
common and often only thing to partially block the wind for a turbine is another turbine.
A single wind turbine harvests energy due to the energy difference upstream and down-
stream of the turbine [10]. When the turbine harvest energy from the wind, a turbine
further downstream has less potential of harvesting energy. When multiple turbines are
placed in an array, the complex interactions among them progressively lessens the ability
of all downwind turbines to harvest energy from the wind. For wind farms containing
a large enough amount of wind turbines, the flow of the wind farm approaches a ‘fully-
developed’ state where the kinetic energy must be harvested from the wind flow above
the turbines. For big wind farms we therefore wish to add kites as in Figure 1.1, to
increase the vertical energy flux towards the turbines. The concept is to place a steady
kite just above turbine height in between turbines, where at least one turbine is in the
wake of the others. The kite would create a downward stream of wind to re-energize
the wake, thereby creating a new method of minimizing wake effect in a wind farm, as
shown in Figure 1.1.
This would not be the first time kites have caught the interest of the wind energy
industry. The German energy subsidiary company of RWE AG, “RWE Renewables”,

The basic principle is that the kite catches wind in about 20 meters altitude. It is then

who sells a “Seawing”, which is a kite-system to attach on ships. The kite will pull the
ship forward by wind energy, reducing fuel consumption by an average of 20% [14]. It
uses software developed to guide the ship using the most optimal route for fuel consump-
tion when using the kite, while maintaining departure and arrival times.

CHAPTER 2
Wind Farm Design
With the climate changes the World faces and the consequential focus on clean energy,
the WFLOP has been subject to many papers seeking to effectively minimize the wake
effect in a wind farm.
To solve the WFLOP we choose a heuristic method, as this
is a more flexible and scalable approach than it would be to use a MILP-approach.
We do present a MIP-model in Section 3.5 and discuss this further. The WFLOP is
similar to the P-dispersion-sum problem, where the aim is to maximize the distance
between k established facilities, much like the WFLOP indirectly seeks to maximize
distance between turbines to minimize the wake effect in the farm for optimal power
production. As the P-dispersion-sum problem is known to be NP-hard, as Hansen and
Moon [15] proves by reduction from the clique problem, so is the WFLOP NP-hard
and can therefore not be solved to optimality in polynomial time. This will be further
discussed in Section 3.5. As there is no viable algorithm to optimize ‘large’ instances of
the WFLOP, heuristics have been explored in the literature to approach optimality for
this problem.
2.1
Literature review
In this section we will give a review of the literature regarding the WFLOP, focusing on
modelling the wake effect and solving the WFLOP. Thereafter, the literature relevant
to kites in wind farms will be reviewed.
2.1.1
WFLOP
Modelling the wake effect is critical for the WFLOP as power losses due to wake effect
are between 5% – 15% of the total power production of a wind farm [16]. This effect
can be visualized in Figure 2.1, where the wakes of the wind turbines are filled with
fog, leaving only the most upstream turbines clearly visible. The significant production
loss encourages the incorporation of wake effect into the modelling of a wind farm. A
popular model for the wake effect is Jensen’s model [17], mainly for its practicality due
to it being a simple model with good precision. Jensen’s model is used by Mosetti et
al. [18], which is the first paper on the WFLOP. Mosetti et al. [18] introduces a simple
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Literature review
6
instance in the shape of a 10 by 10 grid of available positions to place turbines. In
this sense Mosetti et al. [18] introduces the WFLOP as a discrete problem and further
defines 3 scenarios of wind directions and speeds to the 10 by 10 grid, which has since
been used often in literature as a benchmark despite their simplified nature. The paper
bases its solution on Generic Algorithms (GAs) to optimize these instances placing a
variable number of turbines.
Figure 2.1: A picture of the wind farm at Horns Rev 1 taken in 2008 from southeast. Fog in
the turbulence of the wind turbines creates an easily observable case of wake effect. Courtesy
Vattenfall, photographer Christian Steiness.
Using Jensen’s model, Kusiak & Song [8] explore a continuous approach to the
WFLOP. They do this by giving a thorough explanation on the theory they use and
discretize wind speed and wind direction into small ‘bins’ to be able to approximate
integration with discrete summation in order to deal with the nonlinearity of a model
that considers interaction between turbines (wake effect). They find a considerable loss
in expected power production of the farm, when taking the wake effect into considera-
tion while placing an increasing number of turbines and conclude that placing too many
turbines will significantly increase wake loss in the wind farm. Thereby Kusiak & Song
[8] argue the importance of taking the wake effect into account. The continuous ap-
proach has since been explored by other papers too, e.g. [19, 20], but the success of this
approach is limited to a small number of turbines in instances without obstacles. For
real-life applications it seems a discrete approach is the better choice, as the majority of
papers on the subject has concluded as well. (see [21])
The importance of the wake effect was further emphasized by Samorani [9]. Samorani
[9] also refers to Jensen’s model [17] and points out both the relevance of minimum dis-
tance between turbines, as the turbulence intensity in the ‘near wake’ can become so
large that it can damage the blades of the turbines and thereby increase maintenance
==PAGE:20==
2.1 Literature review
7
costs and reduce the lifetime expectancy of the wind farm, and also the precision of
Jensen’s simple model even in multiple wakes. This leads to the interesting conclusion
that the total velocity deficit regarding wake effect mostly depends on the closest turbine
that generates a wake. Samorani [9] do point to the lack of studies on the interaction
of multiple wakes and therefore the uncertainty regarding this conclusion, but this gives
Jensen’s model credibility to use in the industry and it also encourages the idea that
the WFLOP resembles the P-dispersion-sum problem, as indeed we seek to maximize
distance between turbines.
Fischetti & Pisinger [22] considers multiple challenges when designing an offshore
wind farm, being turbine location (WFLOP), electrical cable routing and optimization
of foundations. Fischetti & Pisinger [22] handles each task individually, so firstly the
WFLOP is solved and secondly the job of cable routing is handled. This proves to be
an effective strategy, giving great results while being faster than previous work handling
multiple challenges simultaneously, e.g. [23].
As the demand for energy increases, the effectiveness of wind farms gets increasing
attention. Wind farms are becoming larger, both as the individual turbines grow and
as the number of turbines within the wind farm increases. This makes the simplified
instances from Mosetti et al. [18] even further from the realities of designing a wind farm,
however literature has been using these instances to benchmark and thereby compare
results. Cazzaro & Pisinger [21] gives an alternative to the classic instances by Mosetti
et al. [18] by generating a set of 10 more realistic instances than those of Mosetti et al.
[18]. These instances each contain between 3000 and 22000 available locations designed
to place hundreds of turbines in a single wind farm. This paper will pick some of these
instances to use for testing. With the instances it is the aim for Cazzaro & Pisinger [21] to
be able to place hundreds of turbines quickly with minimum wake loss. This is done while
handling real-world-mimicking constraints, like the minimum distance between turbines,
placing a requested number of turbines, and avoiding blocked locations. For this purpose
Cazzaro & Pisinger [21] takes inspiration from the P-dispersion-sum problem to find an
initial solution, before using an altered version of the metaheuristic strategy ‘Variable
Neighborhood Search’.
2.1.2
Literature on kites in wind farms
The opportunities of kites are being explored and integrated in both literature and in-
dustry. Wind surfers, sailing ships and kite runners have enjoyed utilizing the wind
energy of kites for a long time, but now the industry have begun taking advantage of
this well-known energy source by integrating it into modern technology. The companies
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Contribution
8
Skysails and Airseas [24, 14] sell a pumping kite technology [25] that can both be used on
container ships to save fuel or as an alternative or a supplement to offshore wind turbines.
The idea of adding kites to a wind turbine farm to utilize the vertical flux arose in
a ‘Design Synthesis Exercise’ during a Bachelor education in Aerospace Engineering on
Delft University of Technology [11]. It seemed inspired by wind tunnel experiments [10]
and large-eddy simulations [26], which have shown the vertical flux of kinetic energy
balances the total energy extracted by a wind turbine [27]. This means the wake of
a turbine is re-energized by the above-wake flow of wind given enough distance. The
concept is what both articles [10] and [26] considers. Wind farms horizontally exceeding
10 – 20 km can be assumed1 to have a ‘fully developed’ flow regime, in which the main
changes of the flow can be found in the vertical direction, rather than the horizontal
[26]. The vertical re-energizing process can be manipulated by modifying the large-scale
structures of the flow.
Further, Verhulst & Meneveau [28] added an experimental force to turbines in the
vertical direction to divert wake flow and enhance wake recovery. The idea was not to
show any realistic way of decreasing wake effect, but rather to show that forces increas-
ing vertical mixing would also increase wind velocity at turbines down-wind of other
turbines, leading to increased power production.
The idea of adding kites to wind farms was revisited in a Master thesis by Ploumakis
[27], where simulations using the large-eddy simulation framework was done to test the
effect of kites on wake effects.
The thesis found that the applied forcing of kites is
capable of redirecting the free-stream flow to assist wake recovery. These findings were
published by Ploumakis & Bierbooms [29], reporting an increase of 12% – 25% in power
production when using kites, compared to not using kites, depending on the kite power
density2.

surrounding a kite
The minimum distance is chosen as kites will have to be in steady flight in a slightly
higher altitude than the height of the turbines, say 270 to 288 meters and this minimum
distance should therefore prevent kites from ever becoming entangled in each other.
Ploumakis & Bierbooms [29] uses a distance of 3 times the rotor diameter, which in
this case is 240 meters. This distance is very similar to the distance chosen by us, but
it is found using a different method - if we were to use 3 times the rotor diameter,
the minimum distance would be 720 meters as the wind mills we consider are bigger.
Choosing a minimum distance is tricky because real life testing of kites in a wind farm
has not yet been done and the understanding of turbulence and how it will effect the
kites is deficient. It could be argued the minimum distance should be doubled, so two
kites flying at maximum range towards each other (gliding just above sea level) would
not touch, but it is here assumed to be very unlikely for two adjacent kites to fly in
opposite directions. Therefore, a minimum distance of 288 meters seem appropriate for
modelling kites in this paper while we wait for further testing of real life application.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
The presented MIP-model (3.1) cannot be solved to optimality in polynomial time. In-
stead a heuristic approach will be pursued. The heuristic will consists of two parts:
firstly, we will create an initial solution through the Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search
Procedure (GRASP). This will allow for variability in the initial solutions and is a strat-
egy chosen to avoid getting stuck in the same optimum between solutions. Secondly,
the initial solution from GRASP will be used by the global search heuristic Simulated
Annealing (SA). This heuristic will search for better solutions among ‘close’ solutions
with the goal of avoiding being trapped in local optima, converging towards the global
solution. This combination of heuristics are of nature ‘random’, meaning the objective
values from two identical runs are likely to not be equal. Instead, it has the advantage
of being quick and effective at finding globally ‘good’ solutions.
4.1
GRASP
GRASP is a metaheuristic algorithm first presented by Feo & Resende [30] as a method
of solving the set covering problem in a computationally eﬀicient way. The strategy
of GRASP is a construction heuristic based on the greedy principle. The idea is to
generate multiple solutions, in each adding one element at a time. Instead of picking
the next iteration with pure greed, a Restricted Candidate List (RCL) is created. The
RCL consists of the k best choices, most beneficial to the objective value. To construct
solutions, at each iteration a RCL is created and then an element of the RCL is picked
at random. GRASP is a fast strategy based on variety in the construction. The level of
variety can be adjusted by tuning the parameter k.
Algorithm 1 is constructed using a greedy principle of placing turbines one at a time,
always placing the one resulting in the highest objective value. After all turbines are
placed, the wind farm layout is filled with kites in the same greedy manner, given that
the best possible location for a kite increases the objective value. If not, no more kites
are placed. This greedy principle has the disadvantage that it might place turbines or
kites in a seemingly good location, but the location blocks off a combination of loca-
tions that would result in a better overall solution, making it diﬀicult to escape a local
==PAGE:32==
4.1 GRASP
19
optimum. To counter this, Algorithm 1 is adjusted with the GRASP strategy, where
instead of picking the location giving the highest immediate objective value, the RCL
of the locations giving “good” objective values is created. To adjust the randomness of
GRASP, the picking phase of the algorithm is tuned with a roulette wheel selection with
a “weighted wheel”. The wheel favors locations with higher improvement to the objective
value. Each location in the RCL will be given a value: The location with the highest
improvement will be location 1 and gets the value 1. Every other location i on the list is
given the value of the difference between the objective value if location 1 is picked and
if location i is picked, divided by the objective value if location 1 is picked. These values
are then normalized and the ‘wheel is then spun’, choosing a location. This is how a
location is picked randomly in line 11 and 26 of Algorithm 1. The entire algorithm is
then run multiple times whereof the initial solution with the highest objective value is
picked.
The number of turbines to place is constant as the capacity of the park is pre-
determined by the appropriate authorities. The number of kites is left as a variable.
The number of kites placed is dependant on the available space left in the layout and
if new kites continue to improve the objective value as more are placed. A counter is
added which is reset every time a kite is placed. If no picked location j results in an
improved objective value for the maximum allowed iterations (maxIterations) in a row,
then the algorithm terminates. The cost of a kite compared to the amount of wake effect
it counters will be subject of experimenting in this paper.
In Algorithm 1, X is a vector with entries x(i), where x(i) = 1 if a turbine is placed
in location i and 0 otherwise. Likewise, Y is a vector with entries y(i), where y(i) = 1
if a kite is placed in location i and 0 otherwise. As a new turbine is placed, all locations
within a radius of 1200 meters is marked as unavailable through a vector which has
value nan if the location is blocked and the potential objective value of placing a turbine
or kite if the location is available. This is done instead of placing turbines and kites
simultaneously, as doing so requires lots of checks given kites are assumed to require a
smaller area than a turbine, while the area between a turbine and a kite should still be
in the distance of a turbine (1200 meters). A kite is assumed to require a radius of 288
meters non-occupied space around it, as argued in Section 3.2. X and Y are initialised
as zero (an empty wind farm) to ensure the distance constraint is kept.

4.2
Simulated Annealing
Simulated Annealing (SA) is a simple metaheuristic method which will converge towards
an optimal solution of an optimization problem given infinite time. This theoretical
framework assumes an infinite number of iterations, but as it guarantees optimality
this mechanism can be used for approaching the global solution to the WFLOP. SA
is inspired from the idea of physical annealing of materials. It “smelts” the structure
and then through a clever cooling-process arrive at a solid state of “minimum energy”.
Reaching the solid state of minimum energy requires firstly, a very high temperature
to melt the structure and secondly, an appropriately slow hardening of the structure.
It is in the cooling-process where optimality is only guaranteed through infinite time. [31]
The name Simulated Annealing was first used in 1983 by Kirkpatrick et al. [32].
They combine the central constructs in combinatorial optimization and in statistical
mechanics to develop an algorithm that implements a Metropolis algorithm. They then
test this algorithm on the travelling salesman problem. Kirkpatrick et al. [32] consider
the SA process to be a combination of two heuristic techniques: divide-and-conquer
and iterative improvement. When the temperature is high, SA iterations causes large
changes in the objective function, while at low temperatures the iterations have small
impacts. This is what Kirkpatrick et al. [32] refers to as an adaptive form of divide-and-
conquer, as this splits the problem into parts (in SA this is equivalent to states) that
can be solved as a sub-problem. This assumes each state to have a sub-solution that
should be reached before continuing to the next state. SA is also a heuristic of iterative
improvement as it seeks to improve the objective value in each iteration, proceeding in
small steps. The temperature however makes it possible to worsen the objective value
in an iteration to keep it from getting stuck in a local optimum, which is the innovative
thinking of SA compared to other iterative improvement heuristics.
SA uses the iterative improvement concept of a simple local search algorithm (a
Monte Carlo algorithm), which in each iteration generates a new solution ‘close’ to
the current solution. If the generated solution has an improved objective value, the
generated solution becomes the current solution. The algorithm will continue until all
‘close’ solutions are worse than the current. Algorithm 2 shows a WFLOP including
kites version of Local Search, where f(i) is the objective value of solution i.
The method presented in Algorithm 2 as well as SA, rely on a definition of ‘close’
solutions. Let S be the set of feasible solutions to the problem. A neighbourhood of
solutions Si ⊂S is defined, so for each solution i ∈S all solutions in Si are ‘close’ to
i. It is up to the user of SA to define the neighbourhoods Si, which we will define in
Section 4.2.2.

As mentioned, SA implements a Metropolis algorithm. The Metropolis algorithm
was developed by Metropolis et al. [33] in 1953. The algorithm is a method of exploring
a function or sample with no previous knowledge of the distribution of the function or
sample. It uses the local search technique, generating a sequence of states, which it
explores based on the probability of being in a given state compared to the current state.
Metropolis et al. [33] uses a system of particles (rigid spheres) as a sample to explore.
Given an initial position of each particle (a feasible solution), particles are moved one at
a time, each time evaluating the energy of the system. If the energy of the system is low-
ered by the move, the move is accepted. If not, the move is accepted with a probability
exp(Ei−Ej
kB·T ), where Ei is the energy of state i, T is temperature and kB is the Boltzmann
constant. Where Metropolis algorithm uses this technique to be able to evaluate param-
eters of systems that would otherwise be diﬀicult to get information about, SA adapts
this technique to approach the global optimum of a function. This is done, as the name
suggests, by annealing the temperature parameter. The temperature is decreased based
on a pre-defined annealing schedule and at each state of the temperature, the solution
space is explored. In states of high temperature, SA has a high degree of randomness, as
a worse solution is accepted at a ‘high’ probability. When SA reaches a low temperature
(about 0), it starts imitating the local search algorithm, Algorithm 2.
The strategy of SA can at one iteration accept a solution that is so devastating to
the objective function that it cannot recover with the given annealing schedule.
To
avoid this problem a counter can be implemented in this way: If in the last d iterations
the best solution found has not been updated, change the current solution to the best
solution found. This helps the algorithm recover from ‘really bad moves’, however d
should be chosen carefully not to break the concept of exploring the solution space and
accepting worse solutions.

Parameters of Simulated Annealing
An algorithm of Simulated Annealing is presented in Algorithm 3. Notice that this al-
gorithm is an expansion of Algorithm 2, in the sense that it adds the opportunity for
accepting a worse solution. This requires a new stopping criteria as well. Both the
stopping criteria and the probability of accepting a worse solution is defined by the tem-
perature parameter ck.
Given a solution i consisting of a set (X, Y ), where X and Y are solution vectors
of turbines and kites respectively, Algorithm 3 initializes 3 parameters: ck, α and Lk,
along with the minimumTemperature, which is the stopping criteria of the algorithm.
Algorithm 3 uses the geometric cooling process, as described in [31].
ck is the control parameter used to update the probability of accepting a solution
further from the optimal solution than the current solution. In the analogy of the phys-
ical annealing process, ck is the temperature in the simulated annealing process. It is
also the parameter that will measure when the algorithm stops, analogically ending the
hardening process as it has cooled.
α is the decrement factor of the temperature ck, meaning the control parameter ck
decays a factor of α at each state.
Lk determines the number of iterations done in each state ck. This is necessary as to
allow a “quasi-equilibrium” to be reached at each state. A quasi-equilibrium is achieved
once the probability distribution of accepting a ‘worse’ solution in a perfect hardening
process is imitated ‘well enough’. The idea is that it in practice is not viable to have an
infinite amount of iterations as it would require in SA to guarantee optimality. Instead,
a sequence of iterations are made without changing the state of the cooling, which in
state k is Lk. This is to be considered the “length” of a Markov chain, as each transition
is within the same state, making the probability of acceptance only dependant on the
previous transition. The concept of the Markov chain of length Lk is what Kirkpatrick
et al. [32] considers an adaptive form of divide-and-conquer.
Let f(i) denote the objective value of solution i. The pseudo-code in Algorithm 3
then illustrates the idea of SA.

4.2.2
Neighbourhood
Consider S to be the set of feasible solutions to the problem. A neighbourhood Si is a
subset of S, where all solutions in Si are ‘close’ to the solution i. In this thesis 4 neigh-
bourhoods are used to generate solutions for the Simulated Annealing process, line 6 in
Algorithm 3. A solution will be close if it is in one of the neighbourhoods here presented:
Neighbourhood 1: Move a turbine. A random turbine will be removed from its
current location in the solution and moved to a random different location. It is legal to
place a turbine in an area blocked by either other turbines or by kites. If the location
is too close to one or more turbines, a penalty is added to the objective function. The
penalty for a turbine in location i that is too close to a turbine in location j is calculated
as:
penalty = C · (mindist −dist(i, j))
where C is the cost of a turbine, mindist is the minimum distance of turbines (1200
m) and dist(i, j) is the distance between location i and j. If turbines are too close to
each other the solution is marked as infeasible and the algorithm cannot return it. The
algorithm will always return the best feasible solution. The penalty is added to guide
the algorithm towards feasible solutions, but the nature of SA will allow infeasibility as
this could lead to a better feasible solution.
If a turbine is instead placed too close to one or more kites, all kites blocking the
turbines’ new location will be removed. After placement of the turbine, kites will be

placed using a greedy heuristic until no new kite improves the objective value or there
is no more space.
Neighbourhood 2: Move a kite. A random kite will be removed from its current
location and placed in a random different location, that is not blocked by a turbine. It
is legal to place the new kite in a location blocked by kites. If this happens, all kites
blocking the kites’ new location will be removed. After placement of the kite, new kites

there is no more space, like in the Neighbourhood 1 method. If there are no kites in the
current solution, do Neighbourhood 4 instead.
Neighbourhood 3: Remove a kite. A random kite will be chosen and removed.
If there are no kites placed in the current solution, do Neighbourhood 4 instead.
Neighbourhood 4: Place a kite. Pick the location that will improve the objective
value the most, that is not already blocked by either a kite or a turbine. If there are no
available locations, this method does nothing.
Given a current solution i, at every iteration of the algorithm a neighbouring solution

is higher than that of i, the solution j will become the current solution. If the objective
value does not improve, the neighbouring solution j will become the current solution

5.4 Layout Strategy of a Wind Farm
62
5.4
Layout Strategy of a Wind Farm
This section consists of an analysis and discussion of the results from Sections 5.1, 5.2
and 5.3 regarding placement strategy of turbines and kites.
5.4.1
Turbine placement strategy of the WFLOP
From what we can gather from Figure 5.3b, 5.4b and 5.5b, the strategy for placing wind
turbines in a wind farm is to place as many turbines as possible on the edges of the
instances to optimize the total energy output of the farm. Intuitively, this is what we
expect, as turbines on the edge of the instance reduces the angles from which they can
be affected by wake effect. If there is a dominating wind direction, or even just a single
wind direction, turbines should be placed aligned on the edges, prioritizing the edge first
hit by the wind, assuming there are more turbines than can fit in a single straight line
within the instance. This is primarily to have as many turbines as possible in optimal
wind conditions, but secondly it is for the turbines in the wake to gain as much distance
from the upstream turbines as is possible within the instance, as this will increase the
natural re-energizing of the wake, leading to more power production within the wind
farm. It is a strategy that is done while maximizing the distance between turbines to
minimize wake effect, which is why there are some available edge locations left in Figure
5.3b and 5.4b. The density of wind turbines is heaviest on the west edge of each instance,
except the northwest edge of Instance E3. It seems the whole northern edge of E3 has
more space between turbines, then both the west and east edge. In Instance C2 the
three ‘tips’ of the instance is prioritized. The tips are really good locations for turbines
as these will get the most wind possible in multiple wind directions.
5.4.2
Turbine placement strategy of the WFLOP
including kites
In Figure 5.3a there are less edge locations occupied by a turbine than in 5.3b. Specifi-
cally, on the northeastern edge two turbines have been ‘moved’ into more central loca-
tions from Figure 5.3b to Figure 5.3a. With the dominating wind direction being from
the southwest, the turbines move to better harvest the potential of the kites that are
designed to increase the vertical mixing, and thereby the re-energizing of the wake. The
turbines are producing more energy in a more frequent wake re-energized by kites, than
in the edge locations, where the wind directions would result in less wake effect from the
surrounding turbines. This points to a slight shift in strategy in placement of turbines
when kites are used in a wind farm, compared to not. Generally, kites are placed as
close to the turbines as possible in the upstream direction, which can be seen in Figure

5.3a as the area immediately to the east of the west-edge turbines is less dense with kites
than the central area next to it. This become even more visible in Figure 5.8, where the

In Figure 5.4a and 5.4b the difference between edge-locations not occupied by a tur-

5.4.3
Sub-conclusion
In conclusion, the introduction of kites do change the strategy of the placement of
turbines in a wind farm. This has the implication that turbines and kites should be
placed simultaneously when solving the WFLOP including kites, which is further stressed
by the results of Figure 4.9, as GRASP places turbines first and then adds kites and SA
switches between moving kites and turbines. Further, kites are best placed upstream of
a turbine to optimally re-supply the turbine with wind lost due to wake effect.

5.5
Regulating the minimum distance
In this section the effect of the minimum distance of free space surrounding a kite will
be subject to experiments.
The minimum radius of free space required surrounding a kite is 288 meters, which
was discussed and chosen in Section 3.2. Other arguments could be made as to which
minimum distance should be used. In Section 3.2 it was mentioned that Ploumakis &
Bierbooms [29] uses 3 times the rotor diameter as a distance between kite and turbine
in the LES. Using the same measure of distance, the minimum distance for this paper
should have been 720 meters. A minimum distance of 720 meters is an extreme value to
use as this would even be enough space for two adjacent kites to fly in opposite directions,
as discussed in Section 3.2, and still have plenty of space between them. Therefore, a
more realistic increase in the minimum distance is also considered. 330 meters will in-
crease the ‘safety’ distance by making it less likely for kites to collide during turbulence
or a storm.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis had the aim to study the effect of adding kites to a turbine wind farm. The
concept was based on Ploumakis & Bierbooms [29], who made a large-eddy simulation
study on increased vertical mixing of wind to speed-up the re-energizing process of the
wake downstream of a turbine. This thesis considered the known efforts of solving the
classic Wind Farm Layout Optimization Problem (WFLOP) and sought to add to that
problem a new decision variable to model kites.
Firstly, a minimum radius of free space surrounding kites was set to be 288 meters.
This distance was chosen as kites will be steadily gliding in an altitude above the height
of a turbine. The turbines considered in this thesis are the 15 MW turbines with a rotor
diameter of 240 m and a hub height of 150 m. The tip of the turbine wings summits at
270 m and assuming kites will be gliding just above this altitude, the rope of the kite
will have a maximum reach of about 288 m, which is why this radius of free space is
chosen for kites. It will avoid any entangling or collision with other kites or anything else.
Secondly, a new power matrix was introduced, defining the cost and effects of adding
a kite to the wind farm. The power matrix is generated from the power matrix of tur-
bines, having the opposite effect: The diagonal of the kite power matrix is negative, to
model the cost of a kites, while the off-diagonal is positive, modelling the removed wake
effect from a turbine in a given location. The power matrices are both generated to
make evaluating the objective function of the WFLOP including kites faster.
Finally, this was presented in a MIP-model and a heuristic approach to solving the
WFLOP including kites was then proposed. The approach that was proposed was to
find an initial solution to the problem using a Greedy Randomized Adaptive Search Pro-
cedure (GRASP) heuristic. This solution was used as a starting point for a Simulated
Annealing (SA) process.
This heuristic method was used in real-life mimicking instances, where firstly a single
turbine was placed centrally in the wind farm. GRASP and SA were then used to place
kites in this scenario with increased eﬀiciency and reduced cost of the kites. The kites
were mainly placed in the dominating wind direction, validating the mechanics of the
==PAGE:80==
6 Conclusion
67
newly introduced kite power matrix and introduction of kites.
The results of this thesis were then presented.
Initially, only a kite eﬀiciency of
6% and a cost of 2% were used. With these default values, the introduction of kites
to a turbine wind farm was found to be able to increase the overall energy output of
the farm by almost 2%. This is a notable increase and the potential of the kites is
interesting, however there a some margins of uncertainty surrounding this result. An
overall 2% increase in energy output of a wind farm opens the door for future research,
where firstly it will be interesting to research the effects multiple kites have on each
other. If kites need further distance between each other this will influence the energy
output of the wind farm negatively. This scenario was tested, and there is space for a
larger radius of free space surrounding kites while a significant increase in energy output
of the wind farm can be withheld. Future research should also consider how multiple
kites re-energize the wake of a turbine. In this thesis it was assumed that there were no
interaction between kites and as a consequence the effect of multiple kite was linearly
stacking. In reality, multiple kites could have either a greater effect than the sum of the
individual kite effects, but the effect could also decrease as more kites are added in the
same wake.
The default values that were used in this thesis were subject to experimenting. A
range of 2% – 10% was used for the eﬀiciency of kites and a range of 1% – 4% the cost
of a turbine was used as the cost of a kite. Using these ranges, a sensitivity analysis was
presented of costs versus eﬀiciencies of kites. A balance between cost and eﬀiciency was
found, so for the kite values that were least eﬀicient and most expensive, there were no
improvement of the objective value, while for the kite values that were most eﬀicient and
least expensive, improvements of up to 4% of the energy output of the farm were found.
The middle values of the analysis found solutions where the increase in the objective
value were between 1% and 2%, indicating experiments and engineering with real life
kites can have a promising potential.
Further, analysing turbine and kite placements showed that kites and turbines should
be handled simultaneously when solving the WFLOP with kites, as the introduction of
kites impacts the optimal strategy of turbine placement.
This thesis has found that kites have a significant potential for re-energizing the wake
of turbines in a turbine wind farm and suggests further research should be done on the
construction of kites and the effects multiple kites have on each other in a wind scenario
like in a wind farm. For this purpose, this thesis found a balance between the required
effect of a kite versus how much it can cost to produce. This thesis also implemented
kites into the Wind Farm Optimization Problem and found a heuristic approach with a
==PAGE:81==

reasonable time complexity for finding globally near-optimal solutions.
