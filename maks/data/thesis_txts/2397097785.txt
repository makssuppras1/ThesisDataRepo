Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the use of the Cut Finite Element Method (Cut-
FEM) within structural optimization. CutFEM is a Fictitious Domain Method which is
capable of providing an accurate boundary description between two domains, on a xed
background mesh. This makes the method well suited for shape and topology optimiza-
tion, where entirely material-void solutions are often sought and an evolving geometry is
considered.
The well-known structural optimization problem of minimizing the compliance of a struc-
ture, subject to a volume constraint, is considered. Design sensitivities are only calculated
at the boundary of the design, using a semi-analytical method, and extended onto neigh-
boring nodes in the nite element mesh by a lter. For design updates the Method of
Moving Asymptotes (MMA) is used and a continuation scheme is applied to gradually
enforce the volume constraint.
The above-mentioned CutFEM-based optimization framework, denoted TopCut, is imple-
mented in a Matlab program. Optimized structures are presented for two simple cantilever
beam problems and a benchmark against an existing density-based topology optimization
code is made, both in terms of design quality and computational cost.
At high lter radii the TopCut code slightly outperforms the density-based code, due to
a more accurate boundary description. For low lter radii, the TopCut code struggles to
reproduce the designs obtained using the density-based code, where smaller design fea-
tures occur. This is in part due to unphysical design features appearing, which seem to be
an artefact of the underlying CutFEM formulation, and MMA's handling of the volume
constraint. In terms of computational cost, the density-based code is far superior. This is
mainly due to the semi-analytical method used to obtain design sensitivities in TopCut,
which accounts for over 80% of the computational cost.
With further research, in for example minimum length scale constraints for parametrically
discretized level set functions, TopCut could prove a viable competitor to density-based
methods. Especially, within elds such as two-phase ows and uid-structure interaction,
where the modeling of dierent material phases and enforcement of internal boundary
conditions is essential. If improvements with regard to speed are made, and the issues
related to intermediate stiness areas are solved, the developed TopCut code could serve
as an introductory teaching tool to CutFEM-based structural optimization.

Resumé
Denne afhandling undersøger brugen af Cut Finite Element metoden (CutFEM) indenfor
strukturel optimering. CutFEM er en Fictitious Domain Method, som gør det muligt at
opnå en præcis randbeskrivelse mellem to domæner, på et fast baggrunds-mesh. Dette
gør metoden velegnet til form og topologi optimering, hvor en diskret fordeling mellem to
materialer ofte er eftertragtet, og en konstant udviklende geometri betragtes.
Et velkendt strukturelt optimeringsproblem, hvor en strukturs eksibilitet ønskes min-
imeret for en given volumenbegrænsning, betragtes. Design sensitiviterne udregnes kun
på randen af designet, ved hjælp af en semi-analytisk metode, og deres indydelsesområde
udvides efterfølgende, til de omkring liggende nodes i nite element meshet, med et lter.
Til at opdatere designet bruges den ulineære programmeringsmetode Method of Moving
Asymptotes (MMA) og et kontinuerings-skema bruges til gradvist at sænke volumenbe-
grænsningen i løbet af optimeringen.
Den ovenstående CutFEM-baserede optimeringsmetode, fra nu af betegnet TopCut, er im-
plementeret i et Matlab program. Optimerede strukturer for to simple udkragede bjælke
problemer præsenteres og sammenholdes med resultater fra en densitets-baseret topologi
optimerings kode. Denne sammenligning sker på baggrund af både strukturens kvalitet,
men også den krævede beregningskraft.
Hvis en høj lter radius bruges, i begge koder, er TopCut koden en anelse bedre, grundet
den mere præcise randbeskrivelse. Ved lavere lter radier har TopCut koden svært ved at
reproducere de nere design detaljer, som kan opnås ved hjælp af den densitets-baserede
kode. Dette skyldes delvist de ufysiske design detaljer der opstår, grundet den under-
læggende CutFEM model, og MMA's håndtering af volumenbegrænsningen. Med hensyn
til beregningsomkostninger er den densitets-baserede kode klart overlegen. Dette skyldes
især den semi-analystiske metode, til udregningen af design sensitiviteter, som forbruger
over 80% af beregningskraften relateret til TopCut.
Med yderligere forskning, indenfor for eksempelvis minimum længde skala begrænsninger
i parametrisk diskretiserede level set funktioner, kan TopCut blive en mulig konkurrent
til de densitets-baserede metoder.
Især inden for felter såsom to-fase strømninger og
uid-struktur interaktion, hvor modellering af forskellige materiale faser, og påførslen af
interne randbetingelser, er essentiel. Hvis forbedringer med hensyn til beregningskraften
bliver indført, og problemerne i henhold til generering af ufysiske design detaljer bliver
løst, kan TopCut koden potentielt bruges som en introduktions-kode til CutFEM-baserede
strukturel optimering.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this thesis the Cut Finite Element Method (CutFEM) applied to structural optimiza-
tion has been investigated. The initial goal was to implement the method for Stokes' ow
but during the initial research process it was quickly realized by the author that CutFEM
has a strong mathematical background, but relatively little literature exists on the actual
implementation of the method. The focus was therefore switched to modeling the well
known, and simpler, linear elasticity problem, while thoroughly documenting each step of
the implementation procedure on the way. Especially, the integration scheme used for cut
elements is not very well described in the literature, and thus a whole section has been
dedicated to this subject.
To help future users of CutFEM based topology optimization, from now on denoted
TopCut, a code which is capable of minimizing the compliance of a structure using TopCut
has been programmed and will be available at www.topopt.dtu.dk. To reach a wide
engineering audience the code is written in the high-level language Matlab. The hope is
that the program can be used as an introductory tool to TopCut similar to the top88 [4]
and top99 [53] codes already available for density based topology optimization and the
level set based topology optimization code top_levelset provided by Challis [17].
CutFEM-like features already exist in open source nite element distributions such as
FEniCS 1 and GetFEM++ 2, but as these frameworks have a vast amount of features the
source code can be hard to comprehend and modify.
1.1
Fictitious domain methods
Boundary value problems (BVPs), and the closely related interface problems, play an im-
portant role in modeling nite element problems where a boundary or interface between
two domains occur, such as in two phase ows, uid structure interaction or crack growth.
Thus, being able to approximate such problems accurately is of great importance. Tradi-
tionally conforming meshes have been used for modeling BVPs, where either the element
size (h-FEM), or the polynomial order of the element (p-FEM), is chosen based on the
proximity to the boundary. See gures 1.1a and 1.1b. These type of meshes lead to a
very accurate boundary description, but comes with an added computational cost, and

Much research has gone into developing methods which do not rely on a conforming mesh
to obtain an accurate boundary description, but instead seeks to model the boundary
implicitly on a xed mesh. See gures 1.1c and 1.1d. These type of methods are often
referred to as Fictitious Domain Methods (FDMs), but might also be known under names
such as Embedded Domain Methods or Immersed Boundary Techniques.
The most popular methods within this eld are the Cut Finite Element Method ([16],
[14], [33]) and the Finite Cell Method (FCM) ([46], [22], [49]). Both of these methods are
based on the idea of discretizing partial dierential equations on elements which are cut
by a non-conforming boundary. This idea was rst suggested by Olshanskii et al. [43]
and further developed by the same group in [44] and [29].

By shifting the geometry representation from the nite element mesh to the integration
the complexity, and computational cost, related with mesh generation is bypassed. This
makes FDMs very well suited for topology optimization where an evolving boundary
is considered. The problem now lies in developing a Fictitious Domain Method which
is capable of providing a reasonably accurate boundary description, while keeping the
computational cost related with the integration of cut elements down.
1.2
Implementation and motivation
Due to the higher-order nature of FCM, and thus higher computational cost, the Cut-
FEM method was chosen as the desired method for FDM based topology optimization.
To motivate the use of TopCut a quick recap of the implemented optimization framework
is given.
A xed background mesh divided into a solid and void region is considered. The level
set method is used to describe the evolving boundary of the solid domain, and bound-
ary conditions are enforced weakly using Nitsche's method. The considered optimization
problem is to minimize the compliance of a structure, subject to a volume constraint.
Here the nodal level set values are the design variables and a semi-analytical method is
used to perform the sensitivity analysis. These sensitivities are only calculated in cut
element nodes and a lter is used to extend the sensitivities to the neighboring nodes.
Design variables are updated using a non-linear programming method.
It should be noted that due to sensitivities only being calculated at the design boundary
the current implementation is only capable of performing shape optimization. This is to
some extent remedied by choosing an initial design with many holes, and thus a large
topological solution space. However, to perform real topology optimization holes should
be able to nucleate by themselves during the optimization. Such a mechanism is currently
not implemented.
The xed computational mesh, and use of a non-linear programming method, means that
the computational costs related to TopCut are similar to those of parametric level set
methods. Furthermore, a solid-void design, with an accurate boundary description, is ob-
tained without any post-processing. The prospect of a topology optimization framework
which has an ecient solver, and avoids intermediate densities, speaks a strong case for
the further development of CutFEM topology optimization.
In its current stage CutFEM is capable of solving many dierent problems within struc-
tural [33] and uid mechanics ([40], [51], [39]). It has also been applied to both shape
[15] and topology optimization [64]. However, little literature exists on the actual im-
plementation of the method, especially related to topology optimization. Thus, the time
seems ripe for a thorough implementation guide covering each of the steps, from the weak
formulation to the nal program, associated with implementing TopCut.

Chapter 2
Finite element formulation
This chapter covers the CutFEM formulation used for modeling the linear elasticity prob-
lem. A CutFEM formulation for the Stokes' ow problem has also been derived and is
outlined in appendix D. The term "CutFEM" was rst used by Burman et al. in [14],
but the method is based on the preceding work related to untted nite element methods
([31], [32], [16]). The main dierence between an ordinary FEM formulation and a Cut-
FEM formulation is how the element integration is carried out in the cut elements, and
how Dirichlet boundary conditions are enforced.
If one wants to model multiple material phases in ordinary nite element analysis the
computational mesh is typically body tted to the physical domain, such that no element
has more than one material phase and Dirichlet boundary conditions can be enforced
explicitly. This is not the case in CutFEM, where the background mesh is xed, and
thus some elements will have multiple material phases. To model this, elements cut by a
boundary are triangulated and each subtriangle is assigned a material phase. Special care
is then given to how these elements are integrated, such that the behavior is captured
by only the four original nodes of the element. Thus, the extra "nodes" introduced to
perform the triangulation are only there to facilitate the integration, but do not appear
in the nal solution. Dirichlet boundary conditions are enforced weakly, by Nitsche's
method [42], such that they can be enforced inside cut elements if needed.
The structure of this chapter is the following; the virtual work principle is derived from
equilibrium equations, and the inclusion of Dirichlet type boundary conditions in the
weak formulation is covered. The discrete weak form of the problem is presented, and
considerations with regards to stability are made. Finally, a general derivation of the
element matrices is performed, and the integration procedure for cut elements is explained.

2.3.1
Higher-order elements
A short note on the use of higher-order elements. In general linear elements are typically
used when performing topology optimization in order to reduce computational costs. How-
ever, most Fictitious Domain Methods, unrelated to topology optimization, usually use
higher-order background meshes to increase accuracy. Exponential rates of convergence
have been observed for smooth problems when using higher-order meshes, which leads
to a better per-degree-of-freedom accuracy in comparison to using regular h-renement
[48]. Furthermore, higher-order elements do not exhibit the same shear-locking behavior
as linear triangular and quadrilateral elements do in bending problems.
Thus, once an acceptable base level of discretization has been obtained increasing the
polynomial order of elements, in the background mesh, might be preferable to rening
the mesh further. Burman et al. [15] presents three dierent optimized structures for
the cantilever beam problem, where the ratio h/k between mesh size and polynomial de-
gree is kept constant. Almost no distinction between the three structures can be made,
even though the high-order polynomial mesh contains signicantly fewer elements. Un-
fortunately, no mention of the computational costs related to each of these meshes is
reported. Finding the optimal balance between h and k for TopCut might prove an in-
teresting study, but will not be covered in this thesis. For a more thorough discussion of
higher-order meshing in Fictitious Domain Methods see Fries et al. [27].

2.6
Stokes' ow
As mentioned in the introduction, the initial intent was to model Stokes' ow. However,
if the developed program is to serve as an introductory tool to TopCut the mixed formu-
lation of the Stokes' ow problem will lead to a less transparent code, which incorporates
several modeling principles that are not relevant to understand the general principles of
CutFEM. Furthermore, existing educational codes all model linear elasticity and thus to
keep in-line with these codes the linear elasticity problem was chosen.
Nonetheless, a great deal of work went into deriving the weak formulation for the Stokes'
ow problem, and implementing it in a Matlab code. This work is presented in appendix
D. A short recap of the dierences between modeling linear elasticity and Stokes' ow is
given below.
The biggest dierence between the two problems is that instead of modeling one state eld
a mixed nite element formulation is now considered, where a solution is sought for both
the velocity and pressure eld. This is due to the weak coupling between the momentum
and continuity equation in Stokes' equations. This coupling often leads to oscillations
in the pressure eld unless the inf-sup, also known as the Ladyzenskaja-Babuskâ-Brezzi
(LBB) condition in its discrete version, is satised. To satisfy the LBB condition special
elements, such as Taylor-Hood elements, must be chosen. Alternatively, a stabilization
technique, such as Pressure Stabilized Petrov-Galerkin(PSGP), can be applied to stabi-
lize the pressure eld. The PSPG approach is taken in appendix D to avoid higher-order
elements.
Another dierence is that in Stokes' ow the physical domain is the region containing
uid, and the void phase is considered a solid. A Brinkman approach, in which the uid
and solid phase are modeled using a dierent porosity parameter (which governs the per-
meability of the material), may be used to model this problem. This is especially useful
in topology optimization, where the porosity can be thought of as a parameter similar to
the density in linear elasticity. However, studies have shown that, if a too low porosity
parameter is used for the solid phase, pressure is allowed to diuse through the solid
region leading to unphysical solutions [37]. CutFEM is able to avoid this problem by
enforcing a no-slip condition, mimicking a wall, along the boundary between the uid and
solid region, ensuring that this does not happen.
These are the biggest dierences between modeling linear elasticity and Stokes' ow.
Otherwise the implementation procedure for the two model problems remains largely the
same.

Chapter 3
Topology optimization
Topology optimization is a mathematical method for optimizing material layout within a
given design space. For a set of boundary conditions, external forces and constraints the
goal is to maximize the performance of the design.
The physics of a given material conguration is resolved using the nite element method
and evaluated by an objective function. To evolve the design non-linear programming
methods, such as the Method of Moving Asymptotes [60] or the optimality criteria [10,
Chapter 5.5] are generally used. Non gradient-based methods such as genetic algorithms
or Monte Carlo simulation may also be used, although these methods are normally asso-
ciated with much higher computational costs [55].
Traditionally topology optimization was developed for generating optimal topologies in
structural design [7], but has since been applied to a wide variety of areas, such as uid
mechanics, thermodynamics, acoustics, electromagnetics and various couplings inbetween.
See the monograph by Bendsøe and Sigmund [6].
This chapter rst presents the level set method, which is integral to the proposed topology
optimization framework, and provides a general introduction to topology optimization in
the context of structural optimization. Here arguments for and against density and level
set based topology optimization are made. Next, the standard non-linear optimization
problem is presented and the corresponding minimum compliance case is formulated. The
methods used for solving topology optimization problems are then presented, here-under

3.1.2
Updating scheme
Standard gradient-based optimization algorithms can be used for updating the level set
eld. In this case either the nodal level set values or a shape parameter of the nodal RBFs
is used as the design variables. The drawback of this method is that gradient information
must be provided, which for some problems can be computationally costly to obtain, if
the gradients cannot be derived analytically.
Alternatively, the level set can be updated by solving the Hamilton-Jacobi equation
Φt + |∇Φ| · Fn = 0
(3.5)
Where Φt is the level set eld at a given time t and Fn is a speed function governed by
the underlying optimization problem.

==PAGE:39==
This method provides a simple way of handling complex geometrical changes, but has a
few signicant drawbacks. First of all eq. 3.5 is typically solved using an explicit scheme
which means that it must satisfy the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewt(CFL) condition. The CFL-
condition means that the boundary can move a maximum distance of h2, where h is the
grid size, in each time step ∆t. Here each time step can be thought of as an iteration
in the optimization loop. Handling of constraints also becomes less straightforward, as
these have to be taken into account in the speed function Fn. Furthermore, eq. 3.5 is
only applicable when the level set is discretized directly through the nite element shape
functions, which means that reinitialization is needed. Due to these downsides gradient-
based non-linear programming methods are often preferred.
3.2
Structural optimization
Three standard types of optimization occur within structural mechanics; sizing, shape
and topology optimization. In sizing, the shape of a structure is kept constant, and only
the size is subject to change. An example of sizing would be choosing the smallest possible
bolt capable of withstanding a given stress.
In shape optimization the general shape of the structure can be altered, but no new
topology can occur, i.e. holes cannot nucleate within the structure. This means that the
design space is limited by the initial structure as only distortions of this structure can
occur. The cross-section of an airfoil is a good example of a shape optimization problem.
Topology optimization allows everything within the design domain to be changed, as
long as the constraints of the optimization are satised.
This means that holes can
nucleate within the structure and material can emerge by itself. Ideally, this leads to the
solution not being dependent on the initial structure. However, experience shows that
many topology optimization problems are prone to converge to local minima if a good
starting guess is not provided. Figure 3.2 conceptually sketches the three dierent kinds
of optimization.

There are generally two main branches within topology optimization; density and level set
based topology optimization. In the following sections the advantages and shortcomings
of each approach is discussed.
3.2.1
Density based topology optimization
In density based methods each element in the nite element mesh is assigned a density
which is used as the design variable for the optimization problem. By interpolating the
material properties of each element from its density the overall performance of a given
structure can be evaluated. The most popular interpolation scheme is known as SIMP
(Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization) and was originally suggested by Bendsøe in
1989 [8] and further developed by Zhou and Rozvany (1991) [66]. One downside of the
density approach is that to use gradient-based methods the design variable has to be
continuous. Unless a very high penalization factor is used this leads to areas of inter-
mediate densities in the nal design, which often makes the design infeasible production
wise. Therefore methods for driving the optimization towards 0-1 designs, such as contin-
uation methods or projection lters [54], are often used. When projection lters are used
stair-casing occurs in the nal solution, leading to an inaccurate boundary description.
Here various algorithms originating from image editing may be used for post-processing
to obtain a smooth boundary in the nal design.
Despite these minor shortcomings density based topology optimization is a very mature
technology, and has been applied in various industrial applications [47].
3.2.2
Level set based topology optimization
Traditionally, level set based topology optimization as presented by Allaire in 2002 [3]
and introduced to structural optimization by Wang (2003) [65] is based on the the level
set method and shape derivatives which are used to guide the level set. Depending on the
mapping from the level set onto the underlying computational mesh, crisp boundaries,
which altogether avoid intermediate densities, may be obtained.
In general there are

and density based mappings. See gure 3.3.

If a conforming mesh is used the computational mesh must be remeshed, according to the
level set, each time the topology evolves. This provides a very accurate boundary descrip-
tion, but comes with an added computational cost. The remeshing also introduces noise
in the optimization procedure, as the sensitivity analysis becomes inconsistent, leading to
a slower convergence [50]. Due to these issues conforming meshes are mostly used when
accurate boundary descriptions are essential e.g. stress constraints.
Fictitious Domain Methods seek to obtain an accurate boundary description, while keep-
ing the computational mesh xed. This means that the location of the boundary must be
captured in the underlying structural model. Several approaches to FDMs were outlined
in section 1.1. Up until now the most common approach, within topology optimization,
has been to use XFEM ([41],[28]) in combination with a weak enforcement of Dirichlet
boundary conditions using for example Lagrange multipliers [37]. Recently, the Cut Fi-
nite Element Method, studied in this thesis, has has gained popularity within shape and
topology optimization ([15],[64]).
In density based mappings the LSF is mapped into a density distribution by calculating
the material fraction inside the solid domain in each element cut by the LSF. This leads to
a non-crisp boundary representation, as intermediate densities will appear at the design
boundary. The benets of this method is the ease of implementation, while the downside
is the loss of accuracy.
Level set based topology optimization is often based on shape derivatives, which means
the solution space is governed by the initial geometry. In 2D hole cancellation and merg-
ing can occur, but hole nucleation cannot occur as the shape derivatives governing the
optimization are only calculated at the boundary. This is not a problem in 3D as two
boundaries can be pinched to create an internal hole. Thus, in order to go from shape
optimization to topology optimization, in 2D, a hole nucleation mechanism needs to be
introduced. The rst approach to a hole nucleation mechanism is known as the bubble-
method and was introduced by Eschenauer in 1994 [24] and later developed into what is
known as topological derivatives [58]. Topological derivatives use rather complex mathe-
matics to predict the best point in the domain to introduce a hole, and is therefore not
straight-forward to implement. Alternatively, the more simple approach of initiating the
design with lots of holes, which are then automatically merged or canceled if needed, may
be used. However, this often leads to a longer convergence time for the optimization as
this is a very poor initial guess of the nal design.
As a nal remark it might be noted that with the recent rapid progression within topol-
ogy optimization the line between density and level set based topology optimization has
become blurry, and many newly developed methods seek to use the best of both worlds.
For a thorough review of the dierent topology optimization approaches the recent review
article by Sigmund and Maute [57] should be consulted.

3.4
Method of Moving Asymptotes
The Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA) is an iterative non-linear programming method
used to solve problems of the kind outlined in eq. 3.6. It is especially well suited for solv-
ing optimization problems with a large number of design variables, subject to only a few
constraints. This is the exactly the type of problems which arise in topology optimization,
as each node or element in the nite element mesh is a design variable, whereas only a few
constraints are normally present. It was rst introduced by Svanberg in 1988 [59], and
later a globally convergent version was published by the same author in 2002 [60]. In 2007
a Matlab version of the two algorithms was released, on which the MMA implementation
used in this thesis is based [61].
As topology optimization problems are generally non-convex, well tested, and ecient,
methods for solving convex optimization problems, such as the Interior Point Method [10,
chapter 11], cannot be used. MMA gets around this problem by approximating a convex
subproblem in each iteration for which a unique optimal solution can be obtained. This
solution is used as the next iteration point, and this process is repeated until a certain
convergence criteria is met. For further information on how these convex subproblems are
approximated and solved the articles by Svanberg should be consulted. It should however
be noted, that the method is based on gradient information in the current iteration point.

9. Update the design using a non-linear programming method such as MMA. This is

Matlab's built-in non-linear programming solver
As an alternative to MMA, Matlab's built-in non-linear programming solver fmincon was
tested. This solver is capable of solving a non-linear constrained optimization problem
using either Sequential Quadratic Programming or the Interior Point method. The user
has to supply an objective and constraint function, as well as upper and lower bounds
on the design variables. Furthermore, several other options can be specied using the
optimoptions call. The most important option is that the derivatives of the objective
and constraint function can be supplied by the user. This is strongly suggested as these
are otherwise calculated using nite dierences, i.e. one nite element analysis per design
variable, which is very expensive computational wise.

The objective and constraint functions, and their derivatives, must be supplied in two

objective function obj, and then transferred to the constraint function constr through

5.1
Optimization procedure
The design update during each iteration of the optimization loop is carried out using
MMA. To start the optimization an initial structure needs to be provided. Initial struc-
tures which grossly violate the volume constraint can cause problems for the MMA algo-
rithm, but might be necessary to initialize the optimization with a reasonable topological
solution space. To avoid MMA taking too big steps in the start of the optimization, to
fulll the volume constraint, the following procedure is used:
In case of a big discrepancy, between the volume of the initial structure and the target
volume, the optimization is initialized with a target volume equal to the initial structure.
Using a continuation scheme it is then gradually decreased towards the target volume.
The step size of the decrease in the volume constraint depends on how big the discrepancy
between the initial and target volume is, but is generally kept to only a few percentage
at a time. Each time the volume constraint is changed MMA is reinitialized. This reini-
tialization resets the settings for the asymptotes, which control the convex subproblem.
Conservative starting values should therefore be used for these asymptotes to avoid big
steps each time the volume constraint is changed. After each decrease the optimization
is allowed to run for 10 iterations before the volume constraint is decreased again.
The above mentioned procedure leads to an increased computational cost, but is essential
for the optimization to run properly.
If possible it is advised to use an initial struc-

5.2.4
Volume constraint enforcement
An important subject, which has not been touched upon yet, is how the step size, and
number of iterations after each reduction of the volume constraint, aects the nal de-
sign. Up until now a volume constraint reduction every 10 iterations has been used, and
a heuristic approach to the step size has been taken, depending on the mesh size, lter
radius and discrepancy between initial and target volume.
Only letting the optimization run for 10 iterations between each step might seem like a
very rough procedure, but depending on the model problem, and initial volume, letting
the optimization run longer between each iteration may have both positive and negative
implications on the nal design.
If the optimization is allowed to run for a longer period between each reduction, and
starts with a high initial volume, the optimizer will most likely start closing holes very
early in the optimization. Furthermore, long elongated void regions often appear, due to
the problem with intermediate stinesses. On the other hand if not enough time between
each reduction is permitted the design may not have stabilized suciently, at the given
volume constraint value, and there is a greater tendency for interior geometries to merge
when the next volume constraint reduction is enforced.
Similar arguments can be made for the step size, where too small steps in the start can
lead to the same problems as when a high gap time between each reduction is used, since
longer time is spent in the high volume part of the optimization. On the other hand,
choosing the step size too high leads to big topological changes between each volume
constraint reduction. Thus, a lot of heuristics, related to the individual model problems,
is involved in choosing these parameters.

Chapter 6
Outlook and discussion
The developed TopCut program is capable of optimizing simple problems, such as min-
imizing the compliance of a cantilever beam, with reasonable results. However, much
work still remains if the program should be capable of solving more complicated topology
optimization problems within elds such as structural mechanics, uids, and thermody-
namics. In this chapter the most prevalent diculties, which must be solved before more
complex problems can be undertaken, are outlined.
6.1
Volume constraint enforcement
One of the biggest problems with the current code is the rapid enforcement of the volume
constraint in MMA. In density based approaches the continuous nature of the problem
means that meaningful design sensitivities can be calculated everywhere in the domain.
This leads to an easy transition between dierent topologies during the optimization,
without any reduction of the topological solution space. Thus, a rapid enforcement of
the volume constraint is, if anything, positive, as it means feasible designs are obtained
quicker.
For the proposed TopCut framework the material-void nature of the problem means that
design sensitivities are only calculated at the design boundaries and hence the solution
space is governed by the initial topology. The solution space may be further reduced
if distinct interior geometries are merged during the optimization process, as new holes
cannot be nucleated. Since a smaller topological solution space is less likely to contain
the optimal solution unnecessary reductions of the solution space should be avoided.
If a big dierence between the initial and allowed volume is present MMA will immedi-
ately try to fulll the volume constraint by retracting or expanding all boundaries of the
design until the volume constraint is satised. If no measures are taken to prevent this
behavior many optimization problems will end as one unied body, even if several holes
are present in the initial structure. The implemented continuation scheme, to gradually
decrease the volume constraint, partly solves this problem but leads to a slower conver-
gence process. Furthermore, the issue is not entirely resolved, as every time the volume
constraint is decreased MMA needs to be reinitialized.
Page 59 of 66
==PAGE:74==
Whenever MMA is reinitialized the two initial iterations are dierent from the rest and
depend on the initial settings of the asymptotes. Here aggressive or conservative settings
can be used for the initial asymptotes, but even with conservative settings sudden jumps
in the design eld can be observed whenever the volume constraint is decreased. These
sudden jumps in some cases merge separate geometries, which leads to a smaller, and
often sub-optimal, solution space. The jumps happen even for very small decreases in the
volume constraint as MMA tends to undershoot the required volume constraint slightly.
Extremely conservative initial asymptotes (asyinit> 0.01) have been tried to remedy this
issue, but this leads to the maximum number of inner iterations in MMA being reached.
Other non-linear programming(NLP) methods such as GCMMA and Matlab's built-in
NLP solver fmincon have also been tested to see if these handle the volume constraint
better. However, none of these methods were found to yield better results, and fmincon
provided signicantly less control over the optimization process.
6.1.1
Merged objective and constraint function
In an attempt to reduce the impact of the volume constraint an approach where the ob-
jective and constraint function is merged could be used. By grouping the two functions a
benecial optimization of the topology, which does not fulll the volume constraint, may
outweigh the penalty applied for an infeasible design.
Consider original objective function f and constraint function g. The merged objective
function could be constructed by multiplying the constraint function with a penalty pa-
rameter and adding it to the original objective function

the constraint. In this simple attempt, the value of the constraint function is squared to
prevent negative values from being advantageous.

objective function. Thus, a parameter sweep of α should be performed to estimate the
value which yields the best results. A ramp up scheme could also be used, where a low
penalty parameter is used in the start and then gradually increased as the optimization
progresses. Unfortunately, not enough time was available to test this method properly.
6.2
Intermediate stiness areas
The second issue which must be addressed is the ability of cut elements to create interme-
diate stiness areas. Currently, this severely limits the design freedom of the optimization
Page 60 of 66
==PAGE:75==
as a relatively high lter radius needs to be chosen in order to prevent these intermediate
stiness areas from dominating the design. To solve this issue a minimum length scale
constraint could be enforced.
Initially, this might sound like a counter-intuitive idea for providing greater design free-
dom, but by enforcing a minimum length constraint of h ≥1, in the void part of the
domain, cut elements with entirely void facet interfaces would not be allowed to exist.
Thus, the overall lter radius could be lowered, as the design lter would no longer be
responsible for preventing these features from occurring. Furthermore, the problem with
cut elements being intersected multiple times, as observed in gure 5.8, could also be
prevented by extending the minimum length scale constraint to the solid part of the do-
main. In this way a minimum length scale constraint could potentially solve two issues in
one go. Implementing a minimum length constraint in TopCut is not as straightforward
as in density based methods where a projection lter and a minimum projection radius
can be used ([30],[54]). An approach to introducing minimum length scale constraints to
topology optimization for parametric level sets has been proposed by Dunning [23]. Al-
though this approach would not be immediately applicable to TopCut, due to the added
complexity of cut elements, it would serve as a great starting point for incorporating a
minimum length scale constraint in TopCut.
Before the minimum length scale constraint idea is pursued more research should be con-
ducted on the stiness properties of cut elements and the related integration scheme.
Currently, only a horizontal intersection line has been thoroughly investigated, but dur-
ing the optimization a multitude of dierent intersection cases, which might aect the
stiness properties dierently, occur. Furthermore, the inuence of using a higher-order
background mesh has not been investigated. Enlightenment in these areas might lead to
a more easily applicable solution than implementing a minimum length scale constraint.
6.3
Hole nucleation mechanism
Although the word topology optimization has been used on occasion throughout the report
the current code is in essence only capable of performing shape optimization. This is to
some extent remedied by initializing the design variables, such that an initial design with
multiple holes is generated. If the initial number of holes is great the optimal topology
may be contained within this topological solution space, but this is in no way guaranteed.
Furthermore, as already mentioned, the current volume constraint enforcement leads to
the topological solution space gradually being decreased throughout the optimization,
with no way of expanding it again.
Thus, to perform real topology optimization the
program must be capable of nucleating holes itself.
6.3.1
Topological derivatives
In methods where the level set is updated using the Hamilton-Jacobi equation the use of
topological derivatives to nucleate holes is common ([11],[34]). Topological derivatives use
sophisticated mathematics to measure in which areas of the design the objective function
will decrease if a hole is inserted. In this way, topological derivative information can com-
plement shape sensitivities, by being able to introduce holes in parts of the design where
Page 61 of 66
==PAGE:76==
shape sensitivities are not calculated.
However, introducing topological derivatives to NLP would be troublesome, as the in-
sertion of a hole makes the problem non-smooth, and therefore the solver needs to be
reinitialized each time a new hole occurs. This would lead to an even longer convergence
time, and other problems related to the reinitialization of the NLP solver have already
been elaborated upon.
A more heuristic approach could be taken where the optimization is allowed to run until
convergence, and holes are then inserted afterward. The placement of these holes could
be based on the element-wise strain energies, where new holes are inserted in low strain
energy areas. This approach has two main drawbacks; 1) it requires two optimization
runs leading to a greater computational cost. 2) it does not guarantee that the optimal
solution is contained within the new solution space, as only a nite amount of holes are
inserted, and new holes still cannot be nucleated. Therefore the above-mentioned method
is essentially an iterative shape optimization scheme.
Thus a 2D hole nucleation mechanism for level set based shape optimization, updated
through a NLP method, is still needed in order to perform real topology optimization
using TopCut.
6.4
Speeding up the code
The benchmark on computational cost in section 5.4.1 showed that the TopCut program
is far inferior, when it comes to speed, compared to a density-based code. Several ways
of speeding up the code exist. First and foremost replacing the semi-analytical design
sensitivities with purely analytically derived sensitivities would signicantly reduce the
computational cost. However, a few programming related methods would also be able
to speed up the code further. Below two of these methods are discussed, along with a
possible way of obtaining analytical shape sensitivities.
6.4.1
Loop vectorization
As Matlab is a high-level language it is generally not as fast as lower level languages such as
C++ or Fortran. Especially for loops and if statements are slow in Matlab as there is a
lot of overhead involved in calling the underlying code. This overhead is barely noticeable
when running smaller problems, but for problems with over 10.000 design variables the
overhead becomes signicant and these kinds of statements should be avoided if possible.
To replace them a concept known as loop vectorization can be used.
Matrix and vector operations are very fast compared to other operations in Matlab, as
they call the Linear Algebra Package (LAPACK) which is written in Fortran. Thus, by
turning a loop into a matrix or vector operation the associated piece of code can often be
made one or two orders of magnitude faster. Here the handling of if statements inside
for loops can often be accomplished using logical indexing.
The downside to this approach is that it often makes code less readable and not all loops
can be vectorized. For example, several nested for loop and if statements are very hard
to vectorize and even harder to comprehend if a successful vectorization is accomplished.
Page 62 of 66
==PAGE:77==
6.4.2
Parallelization
Another approach to speeding up the code is to parallelize it. The most simple approach
to doing this is to use the Parallel Computing Toolbox in Matlab. This toolbox comes with
a broad array of tools used for parallelization and allows you to bypass more advanced
frameworks such as MPI or CUDA, as these are handled in the underlying functions of the
toolbox. The main feature of the toolbox is the parfor loop, which replaces the normal
for loop.
Using the proling tool in Matlab it was found that most time was spent in the function
related to the integration of the sub-triangles in cut elements. An attempt to parallelize
this part of the code was made by using the parfor loop, but the overhead associated
with partitioning and grouping far outweighed the computational cost of performing each
iteration in the loop. At least for the relatively small examples considered in this thesis.
It is therefore suggested that if truly large-scale problems (i.e. over a million degrees of
freedom) are to be considered the code should be rewritten in a language such as C++
which is both faster than Matlab and supports frameworks such as PETSc 1 (Portable,
Extensible Toolkit for Scientic Computation). PETSc has already been used for solving
a selection of problems within large-scale topology optimization [1], and an introductory
code is available at www.topopt.dtu.dk.
6.4.3
Analytical design sensitivities
In level set based topology optimization evolved by the Hamilton-Jacobi equation a veloc-
ity eld, based on variational shape sensitivities, is used to evolve the design boundary.
These variational design sensitivities may be calculated analytically based on the varia-
tional formulation of the objective function. An idea for applying variational sensitivities
to TopCut is outlined below.
First, the design boundary inside a given cut nite element is tracked through the zero
iso-contour of the level set function. The midpoint of the design boundary is identied,
and the variational sensitivity in this point is evaluated. Based on the value and sign of
the midpoint sensitivity the nodal sensitivities in each of the cut element nodes could be
approximated. This approximation would depend on the proximity of the midpoint to
the given node. As an alternative to variational sensitivities the zero iso-contour could be
approximated using Bézier or B-splines for which analytical shape derivatives can also be
obtained, see Christensen and Klarbring [18, chapter 7].
This method has not been tested, and as the relationship between the design boundary
and the underlying level set function is known to be highly non-linear the crude extrapola-
tion of a shape sensitivity, evaluated inside the cut element, onto nearby nodes might very
well prove too erroneous and lead to inconsistent nodal sensitivities between coinciding
cut element nodes.
Thus, the need for a more mathematically sound method of obtaining analytically con-
sistent shape derivatives, for discretized level set functions, is still very much needed.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
A CutFEM based topology optimization program capable of performing structural op-
timization for simple linear elastic problems, such as minimizing the compliance of a
cantilever beam, has been developed. Through the use of cut nite elements an entirely
material-void design, with an accurate boundary description on a xed background mesh,
is obtained. To extend design sensitivities, which are only calculated at design bound-
aries, a lter is used. For lter radii below Rfilt ≤3h unphysical design features, such
as intermediate stiness areas and disconnected material islands, can be observed. This
issue remains unresolved, but a solution based on introducing a minimum length scale
constraint, on both the void and solid part of the domain, has been discussed. The in-
troduction of minimum length scale constraints in parametrically discretized level sets is
however not straightforward, and further research would be needed in this area.
The developed program has been benchmarked against an existing density based topol-
ogy optimization code, where a projection lter is used to obtain an entirely material-void
design. If a high lter radius is used the TopCut program slightly outperforms the den-
sity based code in terms of objective function. This is mainly due to the more accurate
boundary description, as the projection lter produces a staircase-like boundary in the
density-based designs. For lower lter radii the density based code is capable of producing
physically sound structures with small design features, while the TopCut code exhibits
convergence issues and unphysical design features occur. With regards to computational
cost and speed the density based code is far superior to the TopCut code.
Here the
semi-analytical method used to calculate the design sensitivities accounts for almost 80%
of the computational cost related to the TopCut code. Thus, being able to calculate
consistent analytical design sensitivities, for parametrically discretized level set functions,
would greatly reduce the computational cost related to TopCut.
It is concluded that with further research TopCut could prove a viable competitor to
density-based methods. Especially within elds such as two-phase ows and uid-structure
interaction, where the modeling of dierent phases, and enforcement of internal boundary
conditions, is essential. Furthermore, the developed TopCut program could be used as an
educational tool for CutFEM based topology optimization. Especially if improvements
with regard to speed are made, and the problems related to intermediate stiness areas
is solved.

7.1
Future work
As the introduction of the Cut Finite Element Method within topology optimization
is fairly new lots of interesting topics remain unexplored. Furthermore, several issues
with regards to the current implementation have been outlined in this thesis. The most
prominent issues which require further investigation are outlined below:
 Development of a ltering scheme or minimum length scale constraint, which can be
applied to cut nite elements, to prevent intermediate stiness areas from occurring.
 Relaxed volume constraint enforcement such that a continuation scheme does not
have to be applied in order to gradually enforce the volume constraint.
 A hole nucleation mechanism which can be applied to parametrically discretized
level set functions and used with non-linear programming methods without having
to reinitialize the solver every time a hole is introduced.
 Derivation of consistent nodal sensitivities for parametrically discretized level set
functions. Being able to eciently calculate nodal sensitivities would signicantly
speed up the use of the gradient-based non-linear programming method.
A natural next step in terms of physics to be modeled would be Stokes' ow. Here the
minimum power dissipation of an object emerged in a Stokes' ow could be considered.
This type of problem favors unied bodies, and uid mechanical problems are known to
be very dependent on an accurate boundary description. Thus, this type of modeling
problem plays towards many of cut nite elements strengths and would pave the way
for more complicated uid optimization problems such as uid-structure interaction or
two-phase ows.

D.3.1
Pressure stabilization
The weak form of Stokes problem presented in eq. D.22 naturally gives rise to what is
known as a mixed method. The interaction between the momentum and continuity equa-
tion is known to cause oscillations in the pressure, unless precautions are taken [36].
One way to avoid these oscillations is to choose elements that satisfy the Ladyzenskaja-
Babuskâ-Brezzi (LBB) condition. One such element type is Taylor-Hood elements. These
are elements in which the interpolation for the velocity is one order greater than the
pressure interpolations. As the rst order derivative of the pressure is needed this means
that at least rst order pressure basis functions, and second order velocity basis functions
should be used.
Another way to get around the pressure oscillations is to use equal order interpolations
for the velocities and pressures, and then stabilize the solution afterwards. The most used
pressure stabilization method is the Pressure Stabilizing Petrov-Galerkin method (PSPG)
[62], which is the approach chosen in this work.
