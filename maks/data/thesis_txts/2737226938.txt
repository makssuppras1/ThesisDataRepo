ABSTRACT
This thesis investigates the potential of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) to support
grid operations and enhance the sustainability of data centers. Given the increasing
energy consumption of data centers, which is projected to reach nearly 15% of Denmark’s
electricity consumption by 2030, there is a pressing need to explore sustainable and reliable
power solutions. This study focuses on integrating renewable energy sources (RES) and
BESS into data center operations to reduce emissions and enhance grid flexibility.
A detailed model of a fictional data center is developed, encompassing various elements
such as IT load, PV generation, and grid interactions. The methodology involves defining
the data center’s IT load based on realistic usage patterns, incorporating PV systems for
renewable energy generation, and simulating different outage scenarios to evaluate the
reliability and performance of the BESS. The study explores multiple energy management
strategies, including different configurations of the UPS system and the impact of varying
BESS capacities.
The results demonstrate that BESS can effectively reduce grid dependency and enhance
the integration of renewable energy sources, thereby contributing to overall grid stability
and sustainability. However, the initial costs of BESS and the need for strategic system
configurations are critical considerations. The findings suggest that data centers can play
a proactive role in stabilizing the grid and advancing sustainability efforts, with BESS
offering a viable solution for peak shaving and backup power.

Motivation
Digitalization is propelling Data Center(DC) consumption to new heights, and this trend
is set to intensify in the years ahead, as the Figure 1.1 shows only in Denmark the
consumption of the DC in the next 15 years.
Figure 1.1. Net electricity consumption forecast from 2022 to 2040 per sector of the entire

As businesses increasingly adopt digital transformation, the demand for data storage,
processing, and connectivity will surge. Denmark projects a sixfold increase in data center
energy use by 2030, reaching nearly 15% of the country’s electricity consumption.[1] Data
centers are responding to this demand, seeking not only the critical 2N redundancy for
reliability but also exploring the potential to become sustainable. They acknowledge the
necessity for innovative solutions to cater to growing data center needs while addressing
environmental concerns.
By embracing renewables, improving energy efficiency, and
reducing their carbon footprint, these data centres are gearing up to accommodate the
expanding data landscape while aspiring to be environmentally responsible

1. Introduction
1.2
Thesis Objectives
This thesis aims to investigate novel strategies for the effective incorporation of additional
batteries or alternative elements into backup systems within a high reliability Data Centre
(DC). The primary objective is to ensure a consistent uninterrupted power supply to IT
equipment within the DC, thereby promoting the integration of renewable energy sources
and reducing emissions. Research will focus on the technical aspects of improving the
battery orientation of the UPS system or incorporating alternative elements to reduce
reliance on diesel generators. Optimizing their use aims to meet the reliability criteria
under different operating conditions.
The study aims to formulate methodologies that enable backup systems to actively
participate in the power dynamics of data centres. This approach will ensure a seamless
power supply during outages, in line with renewable energy objectives. The research also
aims to develop strategies and assess the impact of integrating photovoltaic systems and
renewable generation from the grid to optimise the system. By identifying the advantages
and disadvantages of each configuration and establishing operational strategies, the study
aims to improve the reliability of the system.
In addition, the anticipated introduction of a more green backup system will position the
data centre as a significant contributor to grid flexibility, thereby promoting the stability
and sustainability of the wider electricity grid. The study will assess the potential of
these systems to support grid flexibility and resilience, highlighting the key role that data
centres can play in shaping the future of the energy landscape. The thesis aims to provide
practical insights and recommendations, highlighting ways to improve the sustainability,
efficiency and grid flexibility of UPS systems in today’s data centre environments.
The main research question is: Can the Data Centers be sustainable and become a
positive asset for the grid? To this end, the learning objectives with the respective
sub-questions can be summarized as follows:
1. Can batteries or other components effectively function in the backup systems in data
centres that meet the reliability requirements?
2. What are the advantages, disadvantages, and limitations associated with the
integration of this elements in the backup system to participate in the grid?
3. What are the available options for UPS system configurations and what is an optimal
design considering factors such as extended battery capacity, the integration of
renewable energy and the role of the data centre in enhancing grid flexibility?

LITERATURE REVIEW
In this chapter, studies on DCs investigating opportunities for improved sustainability and
reduction of impact to the grid are presented. Additionally, important information on
the definition and design of a DC is given, combined with important requirements and
certifications that need to be taken into account.
2.1
Related Work
Data centres have become essential information infrastructures in modern society, providing
dedicated spaces for computer systems and associated infrastructure [3]. Their significance
in delivering computing services across various domains, including industrial systems and
electronic businesses, has become increasingly evident over the past few decades.
Recent literature, particularly by Guo et al. [3], explores the interconnection between data
centres and smart grids. The review explores the important relationship between DCs
and power grids, using various integration scenarios investigated in existing studies. These
scenarios include grid planning, grid power market operations, peer-to-peer (P2P) energy
trading in low-voltage distribution systems, and post-fault grid restoration.
Modern DCs are equipped with many energy generation and storage devices, including
fossil-fuelled generators (DG) [4], wind turbines, photovoltaic solar panels, and battery
energy storage systems (BESS) [5], which enable local energy generation. While con-
trollable devices like DGs and BESSs offer management flexibility, the power output of
wind turbines and photovoltaic solar panels is dependant on its physical context and
meteorological conditions, making them less controllable. Managing the ON/OFF status
and power output of the DG (referred to as unit commitment) or scheduling the BESS’s
charging/discharging behaviors can effectively control the local energy supply. The energy
generated can be utilized to meet the local energy demand of the DC or returned back into
the grid. For the operation of DCs, important costs include installation and maintenance
costs for the devices, as well as capacity and lifetime depreciation costs for the battery en-
ergy storage system (BESS) and power generation costs for the distributed generation (DG).
Although many DC operators obtained wind and solar power contracts for operations, the
variability of these renewable resources lead to challenges. For example, during periods of
insufficient sunlight or wind, grid outages lead to a reliance on natural gas-fired generation
and diesel-fired backup systems [3].

==PAGE:17==
2. Literature review
While DC owners argue that diesel is the only technically and commercially viable backup
power solution, this is not an acceptable reason in the eyes of many academics, power
vendors and governments. It is argued that combinations of zero- to low-carbon resources,
such as lithium-ion batteries, fuel cells, solar power, and turbines partially powered by
green hydrogen, are available and able to ensure the high level of reliability demanded by
data centres. Additionally, these alternatives have the potential to reduce dependence on
diesel as a backup power source [6].
DCs have unique features that can contribute to overall grid stability. First, the information
technology (IT) equipment typically maintains a stable power consumption, exhibiting only
slight variations through the day and year. Second, DCs often account for a substantial
portion of microgrid load. Nearly all DCs incorporate an uninterruptible power supply
(UPS) system as a backup power option. UPS systems are important for the design of a DC
power system to ensure equipment reliability. These systems are often designed to sustain
the facility load for a brief period, usually less than an hour. This allows time for the
return of utility power or the activation of a more permanent backup power source, such
as a standby generator. A UPS is essentially a rectifier-inverter system that is connected
to a DC power source. It acts as a short-term emergency supply in the absence of the
primary source. Batteries, although occasionally supplemented by flywheels, commonly
serve as the emergency power source. Additional battery capacity can be added without
altering the power capacity (kVA/kW) of the UPS system [7].
2.1.1
Enhancing Grid Flexibility with Data Centers
Integration of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS)
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) play an important role in improving the grid
flexibility provided by DCs. By integrating BESS, DCs can store excess energy during
periods of low demand and release it during peak demand, thus supporting grid stability
and reducing the need for additional power generation from non-renewable sources. By
doing this, it can reduce carbon emissions and improve energy management in microgrids,
which makes them an essential component of modern DC infrastructure [8, 9, 10].
Microgrids and Distributed Energy Resources (DER)
The creation of microgrids that incorporate DCs and distributed energy resources (DER),
such as solar panels and wind turbines, can lead to improved energy efficiency and reduction
of emissions. Microgrids allow for local energy generation and consumption, thus minimizing
transmission losses and enabling integration of renewable energy sources. Studies have
shown that microgrids with integrated BESS can provide reliable power supply, even
during grid outages, and facilitate the transition towards more sustainable energy systems
[3, 11, 12].

In order to reduce the carbon footprint of a DC, several strategies can be employed:

• Renewable Energy Integration: Utilizing solar, wind, and other renewable energy

• Energy Efficiency Improvements: Adopting more efficient cooling technologies

Case Studies and Applications
• Google’s DCs: Google has implemented complex load management strategies in its
data centers (DCs). By leveraging weather and location data, they optimize cooling
and energy consumption, thus enhancing grid reliability and reducing operational
costs. These strategies include predictive analytics and machine learning to forecast

2.2.1
Types of Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) Systems
Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) systems are critical for maintaining power quality
and reliability in data centers. There are two primary categories of UPS systems: static
UPS and rotary UPS.
Static UPS Systems
Static UPS systems, as described in the white paper by Schneider Electric, are characterized
by their lack of moving parts within the power conversion process. The primary types of
static UPS systems include:
• Standby UPS: Commonly used for personal computers and small office applications,
standby UPS systems provide power during outages by switching to battery power
when the main power supply fails. They are cost-effective but typically have a limited
power range and are less suitable for larger, more critical loads.
• Line-Interactive UPS: These systems are often used to protect small to medium-
sized networks and servers. They feature an automatic voltage regulator (AVR) to
handle voltage fluctuations without switching to battery power, enhancing battery
life and overall efficiency.
• Double Conversion On-Line UPS: Representing the highest standard of
protection, double conversion UPS systems continuously convert incoming AC power
to Direct Current and then back to AC, ensuring a stable and clean power supply.
These systems are ideal for large data centers and critical applications due to their
ability to provide consistent power quality regardless of input conditions.
• Hybrid Conversion UPS: Combining features of both line-interactive and double
conversion systems, hybrid UPS systems offer high efficiency and reliability, making
9
==PAGE:22==
2. Literature review
them suitable for edge computing environments where space and power density are
critical considerations.
Rotary UPS Systems
Rotary UPS systems, on the other hand, use mechanical components such as motor-
generators to maintain power supply. The main types of rotary UPS systems include:
• Motor-Generator/Battery UPS: These systems use a motor-generator set to
provide clean power. They are highly effective at filtering power quality issues but
require more maintenance due to their mechanical components.
• Engine-Coupled UPS: These systems integrate a diesel generator with the UPS
to provide long-term power backup, making them suitable for environments where
prolonged outages are a concern.
• Flywheel UPS: Utilizing a flywheel for energy storage, these systems provide a
short duration of power backup, typically sufficient to cover the startup time of a
diesel generator. They are known for their high efficiency and reduced maintenance
compared to battery-based systems.
Application in Data Centers
Data centers require UPS systems to ensure continuous operation and protect against
power disturbances. According to Schneider Electric’s white papers[19], the choice between
static and rotary UPS systems depends on several factors including load size, efficiency
requirements, and maintenance capabilities.
• Small Data Centers: Typically use standby or line-interactive UPS systems due
to their lower cost and sufficient protection for less critical loads.
• Medium Data Centers: Often employ double conversion or hybrid UPS systems
to balance cost, efficiency, and protection for moderate to high criticality loads.
• Large Data Centers: Prefer double conversion or engine-coupled UPS systems due
to their robust power conditioning capabilities and ability to handle large, critical
loads.

Failure Modelling
The failure modeling involves simulating outages that may occur in the DC to test the
reliability of the BESS system and evaluate its performance under outage conditions. The
model has been designed to be as realistic as possible, reflecting potential situations the
data center might encounter over a given period.
Assumptions
The following assumptions were made in the model:
1. Scope of Outages:In the outage study is only considerate the power failures that is
a 26% of the total outages.[29]
2. Types of Power Failures: Only power failures from the electricity grid service
and the DC power system distribution are considered, based on the Uptime Institute
Annual Outage Analysis 2021.
3. Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF): An MTBF of 900 hours is assumed as
it is taken in the paper MTBF comparison study [30].
4. Annual Failure Rate: The system is assumed to experience 9 failures per year.[30]
Additionally, it is noted that:
• 30% of the outages are due to the grid service in Denmark, averaging 35 minutes per
year[31].
• The remaining 70% of outages are distributed as described in Table 3.3, which
categorizes failures by their average duration and probability.
• Outages longer than 12 hours are not considered, as battery systems cannot match
the autonomy of diesel generators for extended periods, as noted by Wiryawan and
Baumann. This study focuses on outages of less than 12 hours to ensure realistic
results within the operational limits of BESS-based UPS systems.

LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH
The primary objective of this study is to demonstrate that the data center industry
has numerous viable and realistic options to become more environmentally sustainable
while also optimizing their own systems for enhanced energy efficiency. Data centers can
play a crucial role in the transformation of the electrical grid, especially in Denmark.
However, despite the extensive insights provided by this study, several limitations must be
acknowledged.
One significant limitation is related to the data and the assumptions used in modeling the
IT load and cooling effects. The current study relies heavily on assumptions, which may not
fully capture the complexities and variations in real-world scenarios. To address this, future
research should utilize historical data to improve the accuracy of the models. Employing
advanced forecasting techniques such as Holt-Winters or Seasonal Autoregressive Integrated
Moving Average (SARIMA) can better account for seasonal trends, temperature variations,
and server load fluctuations. This approach would enable a more detailed investigation of
the different trends in data center operations, facilitating the development of more effective
and customized energy management strategies. For instance, the use of real historical data
allows for the identification of specific periods of high or low demand, which can be crucial
for planning and optimizing energy usage.
Another limitation concerns the Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) configuration. The
current study does not account for the redundancy requirements of Tier 3 or Tier 4
data centers. These higher-tier data centers demand more robust redundancy to ensure
reliability and continuous operation. Consequently, the failure modeling in this study only
considers the power supply failure from the grid, overlooking other critical failure points
that could impact the overall reliability of the data center infrastructure. For example,
in Tier 3 and Tier 4 data centers, the failure of a single component should not lead to
downtime, and future studies should model these redundant systems to provide a more
accurate assessment of reliability.
Additionally, the grid strategy proposed in this study could benefit from integration with
real-time grid forecasts. Adapting the model to dynamically respond to both expected and
actual grid conditions would yield more accurate results. The data utilized from DK1 and
DK2 regions aims to study wind integration and enhance renewable energy performance.
However, it should be noted that DK2 has almost no negative residual load to support
data center charging. This discrepancy highlights the importance of carefully selecting
the location based on regional grid characteristics and renewable energy availability. In
particular, areas with high renewable energy penetration and appropriate residual load
profiles should be prioritized for new data center deployments.
Furthermore, while the study includes data from both DK1 and DK2 regions to assess the

integration of wind energy, it does not fully explore the temporal and spatial variability of
wind energy availability. Future research should incorporate more granular data to capture
the variations in wind energy supply across different times of the day and seasons. This
would allow for a more comprehensive understanding of how data centers can optimize
their operations to align with the availability of renewable energy, thereby reducing their
carbon footprint and enhancing grid stability.
Lastly, the cost analysis presented in this study is fundamental but requires further
refinement. While the general cost implications are discussed, a more comprehensive cost
analysis is recommended. This should include a detailed evaluation of the impact of Battery

Additionally, the analysis should factor in the costs associated with blackouts to provide a

In summary, while this study provides valuable insights into the potential for data centers
to contribute to a greener and more efficient energy landscape, addressing these limitations
in future research will be crucial for developing more accurate, reliable, and practical
solutions. Enhancing the accuracy of data and modeling, accounting for redundancy
in UPS configurations, integrating real-time grid forecasts, exploring the temporal and
spatial variability of renewable energy, and conducting a comprehensive cost analysis
will significantly contribute to the development of sustainable and efficient data center
operations.

implementation of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) in Data Centers:

• Conduct a comprehensive N-1 or 2N contingency analysis to assess the safety and

fault conditions.
• Evaluate the grid impact of sudden peaks in BESS charging. This involves studying

• Implement other flexible scenarios such as integrating Electric Vehicles (EVs) into the

CONCLUSION
This thesis has been dedicated to studying the possibilities and configurations of Battery
Energy Storage Systems (BESS) in data centers, focusing on their potential to meet
reliability requirements, integrate renewable energy, and support grid stability. The research
has provided comprehensive insights into the feasibility, advantages, and challenges of
BESS integration. The key findings and conclusions are summarized below:
The results of this study demonstrate that BESS can effectively function as backup
systems in data centers, meeting the stringent reliability requirements of Tier III and IV
standards. The study, which simulated outages up to 12 hours, shows that BESS can
provide adequate backup power for the majority of outage scenarios. The configuration of
the Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) system and the energy management strategy are
crucial in selecting the most appropriate system. This flexibility in configuration allows
data centers to optimize their power systems to achieve desired reliability levels while
incorporating renewable energy sources.
The main advantages of BESS integration include the facilitation of renewable energy
sources and the ability to provide grid support services such as frequency regulation and
peak shaving, enhancing overall grid stability and efficiency. However, BESS systems have
disadvantages, including a limited backup duration compared to diesel generators and high
initial costs. Addressing these challenges requires careful planning and optimization of the
BESS configuration to balance cost and performance.
The study identified various options for UPS system configurations, each with its advantages
and suitability depending on the specific needs and priorities of the data center. Factors
such as extended battery capacity, integration of renewable energy, and the role of the data
center in enhancing grid flexibility were considered. The optimal design for a UPS system
in a data center depends on the data center’s priorities, whether the focus is on maximizing
reliability, reducing carbon footprint, or minimizing operational costs. Future expansion
and scaling plans of the data center also influence the capacity and flexibility requirements
of the UPS system. Additionally, the chosen strategies for energy management, including
the use of PV systems and other renewable sources, play a critical role in defining the
optimal UPS configuration.
Finally, the research questions are concluded as follows:
1. Can batteries or other components effectively function in the backup
systems in data centres that meet the reliability requirements? The results
proved that the BESS system can be a valid solution to achieve the Tier requirements,
even though the study was done up to 12 hours. This provides a substantial window
to act as a backup, covering the majority of outages. The configuration of the UPS

and energy management is crucial in selecting the adequate system.
2. What are the advantages, disadvantages, and limitations associated with
the integration of these elements in the backup system to participate in
the grid? The most important disadvantage is the limited time that BESS can
cover an outage. Comparing the 36 hours of a diesel generator with a BESS system
is not practical. However, there are many strategies to reduce the use of diesel
without eliminating it entirely, especially if 36 hours of downtime is needed. Another
disadvantage is the high initial implementation cost, which presents a significant
financial challenge at the beginning of BESS deployment but offers long-term benefits.
The main advantage is the integration of renewable energy sources, making the data
center an active participant in the grid rather than a simple load. BESS also benefits
the grid by reducing impact and offering grid frequency services.
3. What are the available options for UPS system configurations and what is

the integration of renewable energy, and the role of the data centre

for future growth. This thesis presents various options and parameters to evaluate,
indicating that the optimal design will vary based on the specific needs and goals of
the data center.
In conclusion, the integration of BESS in data centers presents a promising pathway towards
achieving higher sustainability, reliability, and grid support. With strategic planning and
optimization, BESS can transform data centers into key players in the modern energy
