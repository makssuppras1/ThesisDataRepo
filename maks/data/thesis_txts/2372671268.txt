ABSTRACT 
The aim of this thesis is to study and analyze the MAC layer protocol under IEEE 802.11 
standard family in more details. Today IEEE 802.11 standard becomes the dominating solution 
for hotspots, which provides internet access to end-users in public and private area. This creates 
a game network environment in which wireless devices can compete for the network bandwidth. 
In such scenario, distributing network resources in a fair manner and ensuring the network 
performance constitutes a major problem.  
This thesis studies the DCF protocol at MAC layer environment. This thesis analyzes greedy 
behavior with multiple cheating methods and performs simulating experiment for each scenario. 
Additionally the potential threats are addressed and the techniques for detection of the greedy 
behavior are discussed. 
In this thesis, the simulation and performance evaluation are carried out. Results include network 
throughput, delay, re-transmission probabilities etc. in the modeled network environment are 
determined and analyzed.

1.  INTRODUCTION 
This chapter introduces the thesis and gives an overview of the work. Problem issues are stated 
and objectives are summarized.  
1.1 Background 
Media access control (MAC) layer protocols and mechanisms deal with the multiple access 
problems in the network, where multiple devices share the common underlying medium and 
transmissions are coordinated in order to avoid the collision and the lost. The medium access 
techniques are classified into four basic categories [1]:  
• Frequency Division Multiple Access (FDMA): different communication pathes are 
carried out simultanously on various frequency bands.  
• Time Division Multiple Access (TDMA): each station (STA) transmits data only within 
the allocated transmission window/time slot.  
• Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA): multiple stations communicate at the same 
time and distinguished by their unique codec.  
• Carrier Sense Multiple Access (CSMA): the carrier sensing techniques are used to verify 
the availability of a transmission channel that is not occupied by any traffic. Two CSMA 
alternatives are CSMA with collision detection (CSMA/CD) and CSMA with collision 
avoidance (CSMA/CA).  
Four types of MAC protocols are deployed in various network environments, for instance, the 
Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS) network uses the CDMA mechanism 
and the Ethernet Passive Optical Networks (EPONs) implements the TDMA mechanism. In this 
thesis, we focus on the CSMA/CA mechanism which is designed for the IEEE 802.11 network.  
1.2 Problem Statement 
In a WLAN network, the wireless medium is open-access by all nodes on a contention based 
process. A misbehaving node can gain unfair advantage over other normal nodes, such as 
increasing its data rate and reducing its connection failure by deliberately manipulating its 
protocol parameters. Moreover, the misbehaving node can impose interference to other nodes 
and apply denial of service attacks to reduce other nodes’ throughput. The impact of such 
misbehavior can significantly degrade the overall network performance and should be detected 
and controlled.  
1.3 Objectvies and Approach 
The objectives of this thesis are to analyze the WLAN misbehavior cases and present extensive 
simulation results to show the impact of misbehaviors on network QoS metrics (e.g. throughput 
and packet loss rate). To understand the impact of MAC layer parameters on system 
 
==PAGE:7==
performance, the WLAN MAC layer scheme is simulated using the Riverbed Modeler 
(previously known as OPNET modeler). Based on the OPNET simulation, various misbehavior 
cases are analyzed to explore the fairness property across the network.   
1.4 Organization of this Thesis 
This thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 provides the background to the work. Chapter 3 
presents both the fairness and greedy behaviors with WLAN MAC scherme. Chapter 4 describes 
the detection methods for greedy behaviors. Chapter 5 covers the simulation and performance 
analysis. Finally in Chapter 6 concludes the thesis and indicates the future work.

2.  IEEE 802.11 FAMILY OF PROTOCOL 
A seriers of IEEE 802.11 standards has been specified and designed for the Wireless Local Area 
Network (WLAN). This section covers a brief overview of the evolution of these protocols with 
focus on the MAC layer mechanism.  
2.1 Evolution of IEEE 802.11 standards 
The original IEEE 802.11 standard, which was released in June 1997, defines three physical 
layer technologies at the 2.4GHz band: Infrared, Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS), 
and Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS). Both DSSS and FHSS can provide the highest 
2Mbit/s data rate. During the past twenty years, a set of specifications have been created as 
standards or amendments, and denoted by adding a letter after 802.11, e.g. 802.11a/b/n/ac.  Most 
of the popular standards now are using Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM) 
method or analogous one like Multiple Input Multiple Output orthogonal frequency division 
multiplexing (MIMO-OFDM).  The Latter provides much higher data rate. e.g up to 600Mbit/s 
with 802.11n. 
IEEE 802.11standards implement CSMA/CA [2] [3], and supplemented by two media access 
methods: the Distributed Coordination Function (DCF) as a mandatory system setup and Point 
Coordination Function (PCF) as an optional setup. The discrption of DCF and PCF methods will 
be presented in Section 2.2 in this thesis.  
This thesis will briefly introduce only a number of standards and discuss the network 
performance improvements in terms of data rate, coverage and Quality of Service (QoS) support 
[4] [5] [6] [7]. The standards 802.11b/a/g/n/ac are the generally used versions today which 
provide WiFi connections in private and public wireless environment.

• 802.11g: 802.11g is the third protocol in 802.11 standard families. It was released in 2003 
and is the specification for WiFi environment. 802.11g inherits OFDM modulation from

2.4GHz band but can have maximum 54Mbit/s throughput. 
• 802.11n: 802.11n was defined in 2009 and becomes the most used standard today for 
WiFi connection. It is an improved standard for data rate and uses both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz

• 802.11ac: 802.11ac standard approved in early 2014. It inherits and extends from 802.11n 
standard. It works only on 5GHz band. With up to 8 MIMO spatial streams and high-density 
modulation the data rate reaches 1Gbit/s with multipul links and 500Mbit/s with single link. 
• 802.11e – QoS: 802.11e standard released in 2005 to enhance QoS. A new access method 
Hybrid Coordination Function (HCF), which uses HCF Controlled Channel Access (HCCA) 
and Enhanced Distributed Channel Access (EDCA), was defined for 802.11e. HCCA allows 
Access Point (AP) to establish Controlled Access Phase (CAP) to send data almost when it 
necessary. EDCA uses various priorities to distinguish Access Categories (AC) which has 
different opportunities to access the channel. 
2.2 IEEE 802.11 MAC Layer Protocols 
In the MAC layer, IEEE 802.11 is based on the CSMA/CA protocol [1] [4]. CSMA/CA operates 
to prevent collisions before the transmission. According to CSMA/CA protocol, a STA has to 
check the channel before transmitting a packet. If the channel is free (no other wireless devices is 
transmitting), then the packet will be sent. Otherwise, if the channel is busy, STA has to wait for 
a randomly chosen period of time. This period of time is called backoff time which equals to the 
slot time size multiplied by a random integer number. The backoff time counts down. When it 
reaches zero, STA checks the channel status again. If the channel is free, then STA transmits the

2.2.2 DCF 
There are two access modes for DCF protocol: the basic DCF mode and the optional Request-
To-Send/Clear-To-Send (RTS/CTS) mode [2] [8]. Based on the precinple of CSMA/CA, each 
STA senses the wireless medium before it starts transmission. In order to ensure that the medium 
has been idle, any STA needs to wait for a specified time interval.  
2.2.2.1 Basic DCF mode 
When a STA attends to transmit data, the process of accessing the medium in DCF is as 
following:  at first, the STA listens to the channel and senses the busy status. In case of a “busy” 
feedback, the sensing stage continues. In case of an “idle” signal for a period of DCF Interframe 
Space (DIFS), the STA enters a backoff stage with a random backoff time value. During the 
backoff, if the channel is found busy, the time counter should be frozen till the channel becomes 
idle for time DIFS again. Upon receiving a data packet correctly, the receiver sends back an 
acknowledgement (ACK) packet after a Short Interframe Space (SIFS) period. The transmitter 
assumes a successful delivery upon the reception of an ACK message of the corresponding data. 
If no ACK message is derived, the transmitter will setup a timer and retransmit the data. Figure 3 
shows the basic DCF access method. 
• Interframe Space (IFS) 
The usage of IFS in the IEEE 802.11 MAC is to assure the current status of channel for a period 
of time, which is similar like the guard band. Time intervals are kept between different packets, 
e.g. data packet and acknowledgements. Specified in the standards, three types of IFS are 
defined: SIFS, DIFS and PCF IFS (PIFS). PIFS is used in contention free traffic. It will be 
briefly descripted in section 2.2.3, however PCF will not be considered in this thesis.

• DIFS  
In the standard, only the channel is free for at least a DIFS period continuously, the STA can 
deliver the data packet. If the channel is detected as busy during the DIFS interval, the STA has 
to postpone its transmission. 
• SIFS

• Contention Window (CW) 
The CW is a period that is opened for transmission, during which multiple stations compete for 
the opportunity of accessing to the channel. If two or more stations happen to simultaneously 
sense that the channel is idle, a collision occurs. In the standard, a backoff procedure is 
performed before starting a transmission aiming to reduce the probability of such collisions. The 
duration of this backoff procedure is determined by the contention window size. In DCF, it also 
defines a value of the Minimum Contention Window (CWmin) and a value of the Maximum 
Contention Window (CWmax). 
 
• Backoff process 
In the CSMA/CA protocol, a random backoff process is performed before starting the data 
transmission. Its main purpose is to mitigate collisions. The period of backoff is a random 
number uniformly in range of (0, CW-1), where CW denotes the current contention window size. 
The value of CW varies for each transmission, and it depends on the times of transmission failed. 
In case of a successful transmission, CW is reset as the minimum value CWmin. In case of a 
failure transmission, the value of CW is doubled until it reaches the maximum contention 
window size CWmax. The CW is calculated as shown in Eq. 2, where n is the times a failure 
transmission occurs.  
CW = 2n *CWmin 
 
 
(2) 
It is worth to notice that the transmission can only be carried out at the beginning of each slot 
time. Therefore, the backoff time equals to the slot time size multiplied by the random CW value 
(as illustrated in Eq. 3).  
Backoff time = random (0, cw-1) * slot time

2.2.2.2 Optional DCF Access Method with RTS/CTS 
In addition, the optional channel access method with RTS and CTS is a four-step handshaking 
mechanism. The RTS/CTS is a pair of short control messages that is proposed in an optional 
DCF method. A transmitter and receiver pair exchanges RTS and CTS control frames prior to the 
actual data transmission. It is the solution of hidden terminal problem.  
The process of RTS/CTS mode is as follow: A STA needs to transmit a packet. It waits until the 
channel is detected free for a DIFS, and follows the backoff rules explained above. Then, instead 
of transmitting a packet, the source STA transmits a RTS message. The destination STA 
responds by transmitting a CTS message after a SIFS. The sender is only allowed to transmit the 
data packet after a correct reception of the CTS message. The RTS and CTS signals carry the 
information of the length of the packet to be transmitted. This information can be read by any 
listening STA, which is then able to update the Network Allocation Vector (NAV).

implemented as a countdown counter. When the counter decreases to zero, it indicates that the 
channel becomes free. Otherwise, a non-zero NAV contains the information of the period of time 
in which the channel will remain busy. By this way, the transmission of packet and its 
corresponding ACK can avoid collisions from other STAs. Figure 4 presents the optional DCF

2.2.3 PCF 
PCF is the optional media access method in IEEE 802.11 standards [2] [8] which works only in 
infrastructure netwrok. If AP feels the channel is idel for a period of PIFS then it starts a

3.  FAIRNESS AND GREEDY BEHAVIOR IN WLANS 
According to the description in Chapter 2, it is known that IEEE 802.11 protocols assign various 
parameters to control channel access. This gives a misbehaving user the possibility to manipulate 
the channel access functions in a selfish manner, in other words, to steal throughput from other 
well behaving users. This chapter firstly addresses the fairness and greedy behavior in WLANs.  
Then the detection approaches are discussed in next chapter.  
3.1 System Model and Greedy Behavior 
The infrastructure IEEE 802.11 WLAN system is considered, which contains an AP and a set N 
of client nodes. The AP acts as the network administrator and the client nodes access internet via 
the AP. To solve contention issue among the multiple attached clients, IEEE 802.11 ensures a 
fair sharing of the channel by means of CSMA/CA protocol. In the market, the common and 
mandatory implementation of the channel access function is DCF. As we have explained in 
Chapter 2, DCF avoids collisions and ensures fairness based on the assumption that all nodes 
behave properly. However, an user can obtain an unfair bandwidth share if it doesn’t obey the 
MAC protocol, for example, choosing a short backoff value, changing the default interframe 
space, tampering with the NAV, disturbing the process of signaling frames, and so on.  
Currently there are several projects and proposals targeting to solve the WLAN MAC 
misbehavior problem. The first question is how to identify a misbehaving user? Besides, what is 
the impact of misbehavior on QoS provisioning? 
To illustrate and to show the impact of the misbehavior problem, an infrastrured WLAN network 
is constructed with one single trusted AP and five client nodes, as shown in Figure 7. The client 
node 4 is assigned as the misbehavior user.  
3.2 Analysis on MAC Layer Misbehavior Techniques 
A purpose of this thesis is to illustrate and analysis the potential misbehavior techniques used in 
the WLAN MAC layer. Based on the literature study, different methods are classified and 
explained in this section.  
The difference between a misbehavior problem and a security attack [9] [10] is the intention and 
the method. A misbehavior user is commonly seen as a greedy cheater in the network, however, 
a hacker aims to infringe on other’s privacy or cause devastation in the network. A misbehavior 
user increases the share of bandwidth by means of manipulating parameters or hindering others’ 
communication procedures in the IEEE 802.11 standard. On the other hand, a malicious attacker 
expoilts security flaws in the system to thread the target user. In this thesis, only the misbehavior

3.2.1 Manipulating protocol parameters 
3.2.1.1 Reduce the backoff time 
As explained in chapter 2, the backoff time is introduced in order to prevent the probability of 
collision. Each station randomly selects the backoff time from a range between the minimum and 
maximum values of CW. However, a greedy user can always choose a small value without 
 
 
11 
==PAGE:17==
obeying the standards. Therefore, the greedy user wins the transmission opportunity during the 
contention process.  
3.2.1.2 Keep the contention window as minimum value 
A greedy user deliberately ignors the rule of doubling the backoff value, when there is a 
transmission collision occurs in the network. As illustrated in Figure 8, if a collision happens 
with the packets from the Client_1 (normal user) and the Clien_4 (greedy user), the correct 
procedure is to increase the contension value as following:  
• Initially and after each successful transmission: CW = CWmin 
• After each unsuccessful attempt: CW = 2n * CWmin. Where n equals the number of 
unsuccessful attempt. 
However, the normal user obeys the rule, but not the greedy user, where the CW value remains 
with a small value. In this way, the contention window keeps as its miminum value and the user 
obtains more transmission opportunities.

3.2.1.4 Increase the duration value in the frame headers 
The NAV infomation is included in RTS and CTS frame headers and it is used to inform other 
stations about the estimated channel occupation period. The greedy user can manipulate the 
value of NAV in order to occupy the channel resource for a longer period.  
• When sending RTS frame: The sending station can reserve the channel bandwidth using RTS 
frame. In the RTS message, the sending station will broadcast the duration, which is the 
return trip time for transmitting data frame and control messages. The total duration is the 
time required to transmit one data frame plus one CTS frame and plus one ACK frame, 
additionally plus three SIFS intervals (as calculated in Eq. 4).  
 
NAV(RTS) duration = (CTS_Size+Data_Size+ACK_Size)/Data_Rate+3*SIFS_time    (4) 
 
• When sending CTS frame: Upon receiving the RTS messages, the AP can accept the request 
from a user and broadcast the CTS message with the allocated channel bandwith in terms of 
the time of a busy channel. The value is calculated and updated by the NAV information to 
all the other staions. Based on the request from a user, the NAV duration in the CTS message 
includes the time required to transmit data frame, one ACK frame and two SIFS intervals 
(shown in Eq.5). 
 
NAV(CTS) duration = (Data_Size + ACK_Size)/Data_Rate + 2* SIFS_time        (5)

3.2.2 Scrambling frames from other stations 
If the frame is received while the station is in transmission, then the frame will not be processed 
and if needed the station will retransmit the frame. If a frame which requires a response (e.g. a 
RTS or a DATA frame) is not properly acknowledged, the source node requires retransmission. 
When a station infers collision, the contention window is doubled in next round. A greedy user 
can deliberately degrade the network performances in terms of packet loss and retrainsmission 
by scrambling frames from other stations. The scrambling of the control messages such as CTS 
and ACK are discussed in this subsection.  
3.2.2.1 Conflicting with CTS frames

stations in range. Upon correctly receiving the RTS, the destinated node replies with a CTS

source node and destination node are able to complete their communication within the prohibited

3.3 Feasibility of Unfair and Greedy Behavior 
Along with the hardware and software development in these years, the programmable and 
reconfigurable wireless network deveices become easily available to make modification of 
network protocol and communication process. 
The misusing of DCF protocol in MAC layer gains throughput by charging the cost from other 
nodes. The bennifits of the misuse are showed below [2]: 
• Misusing in MAC layer acts on wireless access diretly, so the misbehavior node can 
obviously obtain more throughput.  And it is more efficient than any other misbehavior 
from upper layer, e.g Network layer and Transport layer. 
• Misusing of the protocol configuratioin is hidden and independent from upper layers in 
the protocol stack. Hence, it is hard to detect by using any monitoring program designed 
for higher layer. 
• As all wireless nodes use DCF protocol, the misusing is always usable in MAC layer. On 
the contrary, the misbehaviors with specific protocol have no benefits for other protocols. 
E.g cheating with UDP protol has no benefits for TCP protocol.

4.2.2 Shorten DIFS to SIFS 
This technique is seen as the easiest to implement. A greedy node simply changes the value of 
DIFS to the value of SIFS. Thus, the interframe gap between successive frame transmissions is 
reduced to a smaller interval.  
As proposed in [2], the AP can determine a greedy node by comparing the reception time of an 
RTS frame and the expected idle time. According to the IEEE 802.11 standard, all nodes shall 
wait for a period of DIFS after receiving the ACK frame and then can start a new round of 
contention window. If the AP senses any arrival RTS before the required DIFS period, a 
suspicious greedy node is observed. Eq. 8 shows to determine a greedy node cheating with 
shorter DIFS period [2]. 
Rx_time_RTS < Rx_time_ACK + DIFS                       (8)

showed by Eq. 9. 
Backoff time = random (2, cw-1) * slot time                       (9) 
Devoid of sending out RTS during DIFS period of other nodes, the greedy node can hide itself

minus SIFS. But it makes cheating expense much higher because of the standard modification 
and complicated implementation. 
4.2.3 Distribute oversized NAV 
There are two purposes, if a greedy node enclose with an oversized NAV value in the RTS or 
CTS control messages. The first reason is based on a rapacious intension as a greedy node.  By 
with an extended duration value, the greedy node deceives other well-behaved nodes to stay 
longer in the defer process. At the mean time, the greedy node starts a new round of contention 
window earlier than others and obtains more transmission opportunities. The second reason aims 
to degrade the network performances (e.g. longer delay and lower throughput) as a malicious

4.3 Model-based Greedy Behavior Diagnosis 
In order to discover the greedy behaviors, the aforementioned detection methods are usually built 
and checked in sequencially. The trigger of detection is based on the statistical measurements of 
WLAN traffics, for example the number of retransmissions and the throughput. The process of 
greedy behavior detection is an iterative procedure.  
 
The diagnosis process is based on historical information. It can use some network performances 
to estimate current MAC layer connection quality, such as the number of frame retransmission, 
the transmission delay, and the total number of transmitted frames. If the value of these diagnosis 
results exceed a threshold (given by the empirical experience or by the mathmetical estimation), 
a greedy behavior is considered in the system.  
 
As it has discussed in the previous chapters, the cause of threshold violation may also reside at at 
lower layer, such as wireless channel congestion, or at the higher layer, such as the failure of 
obtaining DHCP address or DNS request. Therefore, the proposed detection mechanism is 
extended for wireless connectivity diagnosis.

5.2 Model verification 
In this section, necessary model verifications are carried out to ensure that the models are 
correctly design and implemented. As shown in Figure 3 and 4, the DCF protocol can be divided 
into two periods: contention period and transmission period [22]. During contention period, all 
STAs stay at the same status and compete for the media resource. During transmission period, 
STAs have three statuses: Transmitter, Receiver and Spectator. In this section, the verification 
process is carried out via 2 steps: contention period verification and transmission period 
verification. 
• Contention period verification 
As described previously, multiple STAs compete for accessing the channel during the contention 
period. The operations in the contention period are illustrated in Figure 16. According to the 
DCF protocol, a station needs to wait for a DIFS interval, once the medium is sensed as free. 
Afterwards stations start a backoff timer. The length of the timer is randomly selected. When the 
timer decreases to zero, the packet is transmitted. If the medium becomes busy before the timer 
reaching to zero, the backoff timer is freezed and resumes in the next contention period with the 
remained timer value.  
As shown in the Figure16, stations (STA1, STA2 and STA3) have packets in the queue, and 
STA4 has no packet to be transmitted. Therefore, once the medium becomes free at t1, only 
STA1 to STA3 start a DIFS timer and count down. After the DIFS defer period, at t2, STA1 to 
STA3 enter the backoff process with different contention window values. In this example, STA3 
resumes the backoff timer with a remained value from its last competition. At t3, STA1 gets the 
transmission opportunity and sends the RTS frame. Other STAs stop their backoff processes, 
when the medium is detected as busy. During the debugging process, it is observed that the 
model performs as designed.

5.3.5 Impact of Conflicting with CTS frames 
Conflicting with CTS frames function is explained in section 3.2.2.1. The greedy node sends out 
a conflicting frame intentionally at the same time as the destination node sending back CTS 
frame. The winner node losses the transmission opportunity in this round because of the collision 
of the CTS frames. Besides the winner node has to enter into an exponential backoff mode. In 
next round, the greedy node gets a higher chance to obtain the transmission. 
When the greedy node run the conflicting CTS function, then it will block entire throughput from 
other notes.It means during misbehavior period only greedy node can communicate with AP. It is 
because there is no nodes, except greedy one, can finish the RTS/CTS handshaking. Figure 27

6.  CONCLUSIONS 
6.1 Conclusion 
In this report, DCF access scheme in WLAN network is focused on studying.The basic DCF 
scheme and its variation, DCF with RTS/CTS scheme are described in order to explain the 
fairness and greedy behavious. In addition, detection components are introduced and discussed. 
Simulation model of DCF is implemented and verified in OPNET modeler. Different scenarios 
are compared by means of various misbehaviors and detection methods. The performances are 
examined in terms of simulation indicators. 
According to the simulation results, it is can be observed that backoff manipulation, which 
includes reduce backoff time value and keep CW as CWmin, is the easiest and most concealed 
misbehavior can be implemented. Scrambled frame, of both CTS and ACK frames, has the most 
efficient effect, but it is more likely to be detected. Shorten DIFS to SIFS and distribute 
oversized NAV are the most impracticable functions. They are with more complicated 
implementation and have the most unsatisfactory results. 
 
Detection methods list the current efficient functions which can capture the misbehaviors and 
start punishing workflow for the greedy node in MAC layer. However as it discusses in previous 
sections, they cannot fully ignore the influence of susceptible network performance. Also there 
are possibilities of greedy node can escape from the current detection methods. So now it is 
much clear to understand the defects of the currect detection methods and what is the developing 
direction for the future. 
 
6.2 Future Work 
From current simulation model, it produces all the considered misbehavior functions. And from 
simulation result, it verifies a few selected detection methods. During the limited time, this thesis 
simulates each misbehaviou in one scenario. But it does not reveal the effect if the greedy node 
implements multiple misbehavior functions and manipulates alternately. It does not show the 
effect if there are multiple greedy nodes manipulate in same network neither.
