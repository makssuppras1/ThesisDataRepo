Abstract
The stability of honey bee colonies is of great significance to the ecological environment
and beekeeping. However, bee colonies are affected by many factors in the natural en-
vironment, including the state of the queen bee, environmental conditions, and invasion
of foreign enemies, which may cause damage to the bee colony and economic losses to
beekeepers. Existing research has problems with the lack of real-time and accuracy in
detecting abnormal events in bee colonies.
Based on sensor data, this study uses an anomaly detection model to explore the possibil-
ity of early prediction of bee swarming behavior and real-time detection of hornet attacks.
The data includes weight and sound frequency information in the hive, and is integrated
and analyzed in combination with meteorological data. This study designed a variety of
feature selection and data processing methods, including multidimensional data repre-
sentation based on statistical features and principal component analysis dimensionality
reduction.
The experimental results show that the model can predict swarming behavior by inputting
data from one or two days earlier, but the accuracy of some models in distinguishing
normal from abnormal data has decreased. In the real-time detection of hornet attacks,
the model can effectively identify the occurrence of attacks and further improve the model
performance by extending statistical features.

Introduction
The stability of a bee colony in beehive is a complex ecological issue influenced by various
factors. If the queen bee of the colony is dead or missing, the colony will gradually die out
in the absence of new queen. External factors like temperature and humidity also affect
the stability of a bee colony, as disturbances in the ambient temperature of the beehive
can negatively impact the survival and reproduction of bees. Additionally, the health of the
colony is also threatened by diseases and natural enemys, like varroa mites and hornets.
The former weakens the immune system of bees, while the latter may cause significant
damage to the colony directly through attacks, and both indirectly or directly contribute to
the extinction of the colony in the beehive. In addition to the anomalies described above,
swarming of a colony can also lead to abnormalities in the colony, as it means that about
half of the bees in the colony will give up and leave the beehive where they currently
reside. A colony that loses half of its population will have a difficult time regaining its
population and productivity.
In the case of hand-raised bee colonies, if the above event occurs, the resulting decrease
in the number of bees in the beehive will cause the beekeeper to suffer financial losses
(e.g., reduced production of honey and royal jelly, purchase of new bees to replenish the
colony, etc.). The survival of bee colonies has been proved to depend on the disease
control expertise of beekeepers [1], but keeping beekeepers around every beehive 24
hours a day to deal with potential bee crises is not practical. If beekeepers fail to detect
and address these crises in time, they may face more severe consequences and losses.
Therefore, this project aims to help beekeepers find the crisis of bee colonies faster. Since
bees use sound to communicate within the colony [2], the analysis of sound signal could
help to identify abnormal conditions [3]. This project will incorporate information from
within the hive, such as sound frequency and weight, and store them in the MongoDB
database. Then apply machine learning methods to find the characteristics of abnormal
situations in order to achieve the goal of identifying the occurrence of multiple anomalous
events in beehives.

Related work
As the core of maintaining the survival of a bee colony, the survival status of the queen
bee is crucial. Several studies have been devoted to examining the status of queen bees
in a hive. The presence or absence of a queen bee can be determined by analyzing
the sound information of the bee colony [4]. Ines Nolasco et al. combined with Hilbert-
Huang transform to conduct machine learning experiments and found that SVM had a
better performance in recognizing bee sound audio than CNN [5]. In another study [6],
using SVM and Random Forests to detect the presence of queen bees in combination
with mel-frequency cepstrum coefficients, respectively.
When swarming occurs, the departing colony will fly to the new nest led by scout bees, a
process that may rely more on vision than smell [7]. Kevin M. Schultz et al. further studied
the flight guidance mechanism of honey bee swarms and proposed two hypotheses to ex-
plain how scout bees guide the swarm to its destination [8]. Juliana Rangel et al. focused
on the signals that trigger the mass evacuation of honey bee colonies from their nests.
The study mentioned that honey bees would show piping signals and buzz-runs before
leaving the nest during swarming events [9]. The information of the hive such as weight
and humidity can be monitored remotely [10]. Ramsey et al. applied vibrational spectra
to non-invasively monitor swarming activity within a honey bee colony by averaging vi-
broacoustic information [11]. Bencsik et al identified the honey bee swarming process by
analyzing the time series of beehive vibrations [12]. Mezquida et al. proposed a beehive
monitoring platform that records the buzz of bee colonies and analyzes its association
with swarming [13].
Swarming events can be detected by changes in the sound signature at the time of occur-
rence and before the occurrence of the event [14]. Zgank et al. developed an IoT-based
acoustic classification system to monitor honey bee swarming activities [15]. Prior to this,
Woods used electronic devices to monitor the vibration sounds of bees to predict swarm-
ing behavior [16]. Cecchi et al. used feature extraction to analyze the changes in bee
colony sound during the occurrence of a swarming event [17]. A study [14] analyzed the
relationship between bee activity and sound frequency and noted that the results could
be helpful in predicting the phenomenon of swarming in bee colonies. One study [18]
recorded and analyzed the change in sound frequency when the phenomenon of swarm-
ing occurred, but the study environment was strongly affected by temperature. In addi-
tion, studies[19, 20] have shown that when the temperature rises and the precipitation
decreases, the swarming event of bees is relatively high, especially between spring and
summer.
Some studies aim to detect the effects of mites on bee colonies in a timely manner. Larissa
Chazette et al. used image analysis algorithms to detect the extent of mite infestation to
bee colonies in beehives [21]. One study [22] used acoustic analysis to detect whether a
bee colony is infested with mites by comparing the acoustic fingerprint with a fingerprint
of a known state. However, this approach requires us to have mature data to use as a
reference.
When attacked by a hornet, bees’ defense consists of multiple stages, including percep-
tion, recognition, aggregation and attack [23]. A study [24] notes that in addition to the
death of bees directly caused by the attack event, stress caused by predators may also
cause colony numbers to decline. Hornet attacks kill large numbers of bees trying to pro-

Preliminaries
3.1
Bee swarming behavior
As the number of bees in a beehive increases, the worker bees may not have enough
space to move around, and the resources in the hive may not be enough to support the
entire bee colony. This does not directly lead to swarm, since the bees can also build new
hexagonal honeycombs. But if there is not enough space around the hive, the colony may
find it difficult to expand the hive. This will prompts the colony to choose swarming.
When the bees decide to swarm, a queen cell will be built in the hive, with a dozen bee
eggs placed inside. The first one to hatch will become the new queen bee of this original
beehive. Before swarming begins, some worker bees will go out to look for a suitable
new beehive site. Two or three days before the new queen bee is about to hatch, the
former queen bee leads half or even 60% of the worker bees to leave the hive. The bee
colony that leaves the original hive migrates to the new beehive under the guidance of
scout worker bees.
Although swarming is a natural reproductive behavior of honey bee colonies, it can have
negative consequences for beekeepers. As about half of the bees in the original hive
move out, the reduction in the size of the colony will affect the efficiency of honey produc-
tion, and the loss of bees will also cause direct economic losses.
3.2
Hornet attack on beehive
Hornets prey on bee colonies. When a scouting hornet finds a bee nest, it marks the
location by releasing specific pheromones. These chemical signals will act as beacons,
attracting other hornets to gather and attack the hive. When bees defend the hive, large
numbers of worker bees are killed. Hornets also prey on bee larvae as a source of protein.
The dual loss of worker bees and larvae severely weakens the hive, damaging its ability to
forage for resources, and even weaken the ability to take care of young bees, or maintain
the bee colony population. As time goes, this could lead to the collapse of bee colonies.

Database Establishment
4.1
Raw Dataset
4.1.1
Sensor device
The raw dataset used in this study came from the Bee Health Data Portal platform. It
contained information on weight and sound frequency in the beehive. The recording of
this information is implemented by BEEP base.
The BEEP base is an automated measuring system that is placed under the hive. The
built-in scale, temperature sensor and microphone activate every 15 minutes, measure
data and send information to the BEEP application via the LoRa network. The built-in
PCB regulates the measurement and sends the data.
The BEEP PCB is the core component of the BEEP base. The printed circuit board con-
tains connections for all sensors (i.e. temperature, sound, weight) and provides more
space for potential expansion.
4.1.2
Data and structure
The dataset contains a meta-data xlsx file, an inspection records xlsx file, and a folder.
The files in the folder store all the sensor records, where each file corresponds to a single
sensor.
Within the meta-data file, the Meta Data worksheet records metadata such as the id and
name of the device, the id and name of the hive, the version number of the firmware.
The Apiaries worksheet records the latitude and longitude information of the city and the
number of beehives in each city.
The Hives worksheet records information within a single hive, such as its location and
information about the queen of the hive.
The Sensor devices and Sensor definitions worksheets record detailed sensor informa-
tion, such as the association between the device and the cell, and sensor specifications.
The content is partially identical with the Meta Data worksheet.
Each worksheet in the inspection file corresponds to a country, which records the time of
manual inspection and the contents of multiple aspects of inspection.
Each file in the folder corresponds to all the data of a sensor device, and the time interval
between the two adjacent data is usually 15 minutes, but sometimes there is a missing
record, causing the interval to exceed 15 minutes. Sensor data includes time stamps,
cellular weight, sound statistics for different frequency intervals, and other real-time sensor
information (such as battery voltage, signal-to-noise ratio).

5.1
Feasibility experiment
5.1.1
Model selection
This study aims to assist beekeepers in detecting events such as swarming or hornet
attacks on bee colonies, referred to as abnormal events for short.
The existence of records of these anomalies in our constructed dataset allows us to verify
whether the trained model has the ability to detect anomalies. However, in order to obtain
a well-performing training model, the first step is to select the appropriate model type.
In machine learning, classification models (such as SVM, KNN, etc.) are not suitable for
this work. This is due to the extremely small proportion of abnormal events in the whole
data set. It is obvious that swarming event cannot occur frequently. After the number of
bee colonies is reduced by half, they should focus on how to survive rather than continue
to reduce their number. Since beehives cannot have an endless supply of young bees
and resources to plunder, hornet attacks should not be frequent. If we classify normal
data (that is, data with no special events) as category 1 and data with abnormal events
as category 2, more than 99.9% of the data in the data set belongs to category 1, which
is extremely unbalanced. In this case, if trained with a classification algorithm, the model
may not be able to effectively identify a minority of classes and is prone to overfitting.
To avoid the above problems, choosing anomaly detection model is meaningful and cru-
cial. Anomaly detection models are a type of machine learning model that is specifically
designed to identify outliers in a dataset. There are many types of anomaly detection
models, which are reflected in the different types of algorithm approaches. For example,
models such as Z-Score assume that normal data follows a certain statistical distribution
and regard samples that deviate from the normal range as abnormal samples. Models
such as KNN and LOF (Local Outlier Factor) believe that abnormal data are usually far
away from normal samples in the feature space. Models such as OCSVM (One-Class
SVM) establish a boundary that includes most of the data and consider data outside the
boundary as abnormal.
In this study (both swarming tasks in this chapter and hornet attack tasks in next chap-
ter), the anomaly detection models tried include OCSVM, Autoencoder, Elliptic Envelope,
Gaussian Mixture Model, LOF, and Isolation Forest.
5.1.2
Feature Selection
Before model training, we need to process the feature attributes used for training. The
weight and s_bin data (sound frequency data) in the sensor data are used as features
because they can reflect the state of the bee colony in the hive. When a large number of
bees go out or enter, or the amount of resources in the hive changes greatly, the overall
weight of the hive may fluctuate significantly. The data of the sound frequency captured
by the sensor may reflect the presence of specific activities in the hive.
For each sensor device, that is, each device_id, data is divided with a natural day as the
granularity. However, when the sensor is operating normally, it will provide hundreds of
data every day. Directly using all the data for training will result in excessive computation
and make it difficult for the model to extract effective features. Therefore, training the
feature values of each data as a set of inputs is too trivial and not conducive to analyzing
whether the model used is suitable for the task of detecting abnormal events of bees.

To avoid the above problems, we calculate the daily average of all data points recorded
by each sensor. Another reason to use the average value is that it can reduce the impact
of a few outliers in the sensor data on the model input. These occasional outliers are not
related to abnormal events in the swarm, so they do not need to be learned and should be
filtered out. These abnormal data will be diluted in the average value calculation, thereby
improving the stability of the feature. Specifically, for each device_id, the weight value
and s_bin attribute are aggregated and their daily average is calculated. This approach
reduces the amount of data used for training while retaining the overall trends and pat-
terns. The daily average value can be used as a representative feature of each sensor,
ensuring that the model training process focuses on meaningful aggregate information.
Since the recording of abnormal events in the dataset relies on manual inspection, which
cannot guarantee that the event is discovered and recorded as soon as it occurs, too small
a granularity may lead to inaccurate data labeling. Therefore, the method used not only
simplifies input preparation, but also helps to enhance the model’s ability to effectively
detect abnormal events.

5.3.3
Increase statistical features
The input processing method based on the attribute average value gave another inspira-
tion to the research, that is, whether the performance of the model can be enhanced if the
types of statistical features are expanded. The works in this chapter changes the statisti-
cal features of the experiment from the previous single mean to mean, standard deviation,
median, and quartile. This is because a single statistical feature may not be able to fully
reflect the distribution features of the data. The mean reflects the central tendency of the
data, while the standard deviation can characterize the degree of dispersion of the data.
The median is more robust to outliers, and the quartiles can further describe the distribu-
tion shape of the data based on the mean. By combining these statistical features, the
model can gain a more comprehensive understanding of the data distribution. In addi-
tion, the core of the anomaly detection model is to identify samples that deviate from the
”normal” range, but a single mean may not be able to distinguish between data sets with
similar means but different distributions. Adding features such as standard deviation and
quartiles can help the model construct decision boundaries more accurately.
Therefore, after expanding the feature attributes from a single average value to multiple
statistical features including mean, standard deviation, median, and quartiles, the trained
model is expected to capture more levels of information in the data.
This work used the mean, standard deviation, median, and quartile of the s_bin data in
the aggregated data as feature attributes and retrained the OCSVM swarming prediction
model with a granularity of 2 days. The test result and and performance indicator chart

6.2.1
Real-time detection
Unlike swarming that occurs within the bees themselves, when facing the problem of
hornet attacks, we should not expect to be able to predict the occurrence of hornet attacks
using sensor data from within the bee hive as exploited in previous work. This is because
only the hornet knows, or decides, when the attack will occur before it actually happens,
so the bee should not know.
Therefore, with the existing data, we cannot use the prediction logic to train the model. In
order to achieve the goal of discovering and notifying beekeepers as soon as possible,
this study selected the real-time detection training logic. Each manual inspection records
the corresponding inspection time. Therefore, for each hornet attack event, there is a
timestamp representing when the event was discovered.
For the timestamps checked by humans, they usually do not match the timestamps of the
data sent back by the sensor, but we can always find a sensor data whose timestamp is
closest to the timestamp recorded in the Hornet attack. Taking this sensor data as the
benchmark, we take one data forward (earlier in time) and one data backward (later in
time), and we get three consecutive sensor data. Since the recording time of the Hornet
attack is approximately in the middle of these three data, when the recording time of the
Hornet attack is regarded as the start time of the Hornet attack, it can be regarded that
these three data cover the start time of a Hornet attack. Figure 6.4 shows the above logic.
The three sensor data represented by the three blue thin lines will be treated as abnormal
event data. The feature attributes will be selected and the average value will be calculated
in the same way as in the previous article, and then the label of -1 will be assigned.

Conclusion
By analyzing the beehive data collected by sensors, this study proposed a multi-event
recognition method based on anomaly detection model for early prediction of honey bee
swarming and real-time detection of hornet attacks. Experimental results show that statis-
tical features based on the weight and sound frequency of sensor data, or statistical fea-
tures or PCA features based on solely sound frequency, combined with machine learning
models can effectively distinguish normal and anomaly events.
In the prediction of swarming events, this study verified that using the data from the previ-
ous day or two consecutive days as input can identify swarming phenomena in advance
to a certain extent, but the accuracy of some models in distinguishing normal from abnor-
mal data has decreased. For real-time detection of hornet attacks, the model can use the
sensor data in the beehive to quickly identify the event when it occurs. Models trained by
aggregating 3 data and 5 data have good performance. In addition, the performance of
the model has been further improved by adding statistical features (standard deviation,
median and quartile) and using PCA dimensionality reduction method.
Although the introduction of weather information may be associated with swarming be-
havior in theory, experimental results show that its improvement on model performance
is limited, possibly due to the high redundancy of weather data and its weak causal rela-
tionship with swarming events, and the lack of cross-day weather prediction logic.
Overall, this study verified the feasibility of the anomaly detection method based on sen-
sor data and provided a reference direction for subsequent optimization of model perfor-
mance and actual deployment. Future research needs to further optimize the accuracy of
data annotation of abnormal events and explore models and data processing methods for
joint analysis of multiple abnormal events to improve generalization ability and prediction
accuracy in practical applications.
