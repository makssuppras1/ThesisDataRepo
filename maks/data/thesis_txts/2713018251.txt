Abstract
Brain disorders are some of the most prevalent, destructing, and yet poorly treated diseases.
Alzheimer’s disease is currently one of the biggest medicinal challenges, and no effective treatment
has been developed so far. The complexity of the brain, and the complications of drug delivery to
the brain both add to this challenge. Most research in drug delivery to the brain has focused on
delivery through the Blood-brain barrier. The success of this approach in clinical trials is though
poor and therefore it could be interesting to explore alternative routes for brain entry.
Katrine Qvortrup’s research group at the Technical University of Denmark, has been inspired by
the way viruses are able to enter the brain. As most neurotropic viruses enter the brain by crossing
the Blood-Cerebrospinal Fluid Barrier (BCSFB), maybe this barrier should also be investigated
for entry of drugs to the brain. The mumps virus encodes a transmembrane SH-protein and the
surface-exposed N-terminal is a ligand for a BCSFB-expressed receptor, leading to internalization,
hereby providing a shuttle mechanism for transport across the BCSFB.
Furthermore, researchers suggest that Alzheimer’s disease is the new type 3 diabetes, and dia-
betes medications such as Glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA) has shown promis-
ing results in the treatment of Alzheimer’s. However, the therapeutic application is limited due to
the low transport across the BCSFB. Instead, attaching a GLP-1RA to the developed SH-peptide,
will allow improved delivery of the drug to the brain.
To avoid the drug to be activated before it enters the brain, a brain-specific enzyme-cleavable linker
should be used. The linker should be cleaved by an enzyme only present in the central nervous
system (CNS). The enzyme acetylcholinesterase in only expressed in the CNS and overexpressed
in Alzheimer’s patients. Furthermore, it has a high catalytic turnover, ensuring efficient release in
the brain.
The focus of this project is to develop a synthetic strategy for an acetylcholinesterase-cleavable
linker.
The linker is built around a lysine core and contains an acetylcholinesterase-cleavable
acetylcholine moiety. Furthermore, it contains an azido-handle for conjugation to the SH-shuttle
peptide and a chloro methyl ester for attachment of the drug.
Two synthetic pathways for synthesising the linker were tested and optimized. While further opti-
mization is required, a promising strategy was identified and many of the steps in the synthesis was
successfully carried out. Quercetin was suggested as the small molecule GLP1-RA and a method
to conjugate Quercetin to the linker was developed.
Finally, the next steps towards the development of the final drug conjugate for the treatment of
Alzheimer’s disease has been discussed.

Introduction
The world’s population is growing rapidly, and people get older and older. This results in an increas-
ing number of age-related diseases, here among dementias. Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is a brain
disease and is the most prevalent form of dementia. Brain diseases are among the diseases that
are most difficult to treat, partly due to the brain barriers. The focus of this project is the delivery
of drugs to the brain by crossing the Blood-Cerebrospinal Fluid Barrier (BCSFB) with a technique
developed by the Qvortrup group in collaboration with M. Rosenkilde. Using this shuttle technique,
this project focuses on a drug Conjugate for treatment of AD. The conjugate consists of three parts,
a shuttle peptide that transfer the conjugate across the BCSFB, an enzyme-cleavable linker to en-
sure brain specific release and a brain active molecule, more specifically a Glucagon-like peptide
1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA). When the drug conjugate enters the brain, the drug is released
through cleavage of the brain-specific enzyme-cleavable linker.
The aim of this project is to design and synthesize an enzyme-cleavable linker.
2
Alzheimer’s Disease
Alzheimer’s disease is one of the biggest challenges within health and medicine and it is the main
cause of dementia [38]. More than 50 million people suffer from dementia worldwide, and this
number is expected to triple in 2050 [34]. AD is the leading cause of death in England, and the
seventh biggest killer among all diseases worldwide [25] [33].
AD, which is a neurological disease, is a progressive brain disorder, that causes memory loss and
cognitive difficulties. It can lead to the loss of the ability to respond to environment and to carry
out a meaningful conversation [34]. Thus it greatly affects the quality of life for the people suffering
from the disease, and over time the disease is fatal.
Alzheimer’s can affect everyone, but the most important risk factor for getting AD is age, and the
disorder most frequently affects people over the age of 65 [3]. This could also explain why the
disease is progressing so fast - we live longer and longer, and the population becomes older.
The rapidly growing number of people with AD has led to an increasing interest of research in the
disease. Despite this there is still no cure developed against AD. The only available treatment is
symptomatic to improve quality of life for people living with the disease, but it does not treat the
underlying cause [38].
The pathophysiology of AD is complex and not fully understood. The nerve cells (neurons) in
the brain of AD patients are damaged or destroyed. There are two main hypotheses of how this

Current Treatment of Alzheimer’s Disease
The most common treatment of AD is cholinesterase inhibitors such as Donepezil, Galantamine
and Rivastigmine (Figure 2). These work by inhibiting the activity of AChE and thereby reducing
the symptoms caused by acetylcholine deficiency.

Other approved AD drugs include Aducanumab and Memantine. Memantine is an NMDA receptor
antagonist, and works by blocking the NMDA receptor, thus limiting the calcium influx to the neurons
[38]. Aducanumab helps by removing the aggregated 𝛽-amyloids and thereby reducing the number

Is Alzheimer’s Disease the New Type 3 Diabetes?
Diabetes are commonly known diseases and include type 1 diabetes (T1D), an autoimmune dis-
ease in which the body fails to produce insulin, and type 2 diabetes (T2D), in which the body does
not use the insulin properly.
The term type 3 diabetes (T3D), also known as ’diabetes in the brain’ is however rather new and is
now being connected with AD. Research suggested that AD share similar pathology in the brain as
T1D and T2D does in the periphery [24].
The connection between AD and diabetes is complex and the shared key components of their
relationship are insulin resistance and inflammatory signalling pathways. Brain insulin resistance
is related to failure of brain cells to respond to the insulin, resulting in impairments in synaptic,
metabolic and immune response functions [2].
Insulin is a hormone produced in the pancreas by 𝛽-cells. Insulin helps to regulate the blood-
glucose level by binding to the insulin receptor and stimulate glucose uptake. In the brain insulin
controls the food intake and regulate cognitive functions. Thus, defects in insulin signalling in the
brain was described to lead to neurodegenerative disorders such as AD [8]. It has been known
for a long time that T2D is a risk factor for developing AD and that T2D and AD have a common
pathophysiological mechanism.
Studies have shown a link between insulin resistance in the brain and the two hallmarks of AD.
In vitro and animal studies have shown that insulin resistance directly promotes the development
of AD 𝛽-amyloids and Tau pathologies by, among other things, increasing 𝛾-secretase levels. [22].
Besides its connection with the two hallmarks, insulin resistance in the brain has been linked directly
to many of the characteristic symptoms of AD, such as memory loss and cognitive decline.
The studies also showed a decrease in the insulin degrading enzyme (IDE) [22]. IDE is one of the
enzymes capable of degrading 𝛽-amyloids, preventing its accumulation and plague formation.

Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 in the Treatment of Alzheimer’s Disease
Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) is a 30 amino acid long peptide incretin hormone deriving from
the tissue-specific posttranslational processing of the proglucagon gene. GLP-1 is produced in
pancreatic L-cells and is secreted in the small intestine when we eat food. GLP-1 is also produced
in the brainstem and transported to regions in the CNS [2].
GLP-1 binds to GLP-1R expressed on the pancreatic
cells. The GLP-1R activation stimulates
insulin release and inhibits glucagon secretion, thereby it reduces blood-sugar levels [2].
Diabetes patients have a lower level of GLP-1 and treatment with GLP-1 have shown to be effective
to lower the blood-sugar levels. However, GLP-1 is rapidly metabolized and inactivated by the
enzyme dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-IV). Thus, GLP-1 receptor agonists have been developed to
increase the activity. GLP-1RAs is now a common treatment for diabetes and work by increasing
the insulin production through activation of the GLP-1Rs [2].
Activation of GLP-1R in the brain reduces appetite and delays gastric emptying, and GLP-1RAs is
therefor also studied and used as a drug to treat obesity [2]. GLP-1 in the brain has also been re-
ported to affect neurological and cognitive functions by offering neuroprotection and reverse insulin
resistance [5].
Preclinical studies of liraglutide, an GLP-1RA used for T2D patients, has shown promising results
in AD treatment. The GLP-1RAs reduced the levels of AD pathologic markers, including 𝛽-amyloid
plagues and improving memory in mice [21].

Drug Delivery to the Brain
Treatment of brain diseases is one of the biggest challenges. Not only is the brain highly com-
plex, but the presence of barriers protecting the brain from pathogens and toxins limits the access
of drugs to the brain. The brain barriers include the blood-brain barrier (BBB) and the Blood-
Cerebrospinal Fluid Barrier [10].
These barriers make it difficult to deliver drugs to the brain.
Although methods to cross the BBB has been researched for more than 50 years, a successful
method has not yet been developed.
Figure 4: The blood-brain barrier and the Blood-Cerebrospinal Fluid Barrier [K. Qvortrup]
4.1
The blood-brain barrier
The blood vessels deliver oxygen and necessary nutrients to tissues and organs in the body, includ-
ing the brain. The BBB is a semipermeable membranous barrier between the blood vessels in the
brain and the extracellular space of the brain, (Figure 4). The BBB is composed of brain capillary
endothelial cells, pericytes, astrocytes, tight junctions, neurons, and basal membrane [11].
The BBB protects the brain by regulating the movement of ions, molecules and cells, allowing only
necessary nutrients like water, oxygen and small lipid-soluble substances to cross from the blood
Anna W. Falch
Page 6 of 75
==PAGE:19==
Drug Delivery to the Brain
Master thesis
to the brain, thereby protecting the brain from foreign substances, including most pharmaceuticals
[11].
Drug delivery to the brain over the BBB have been researched for decades and multiple strate-
gies has been developed. Examples of these strategies are use of carrier-mediated transporters,
receptor-mediated transcytosis, and disruption of the BBB [4].
For drugs to cross the BBB by diffusion they must be highly lipophilic and small, with a molecular
weight less than 400 Da and forming less than 8 hydrogen bonds. Most drugs do not fulfil these
chemical properties [26].
Larger molecules like insulin or transferrin cross by receptor-mediated transcytosis by the BCECs
after binding to a receptor.
The transferrin receptor (TfR) is highly expressed in the BBB, thus, a potential target for brain drug
delivery. The receptor can be targeted by specific antibodies and taken up by the brain endothelium
and undergo transcytosis.
TfR for brain drug delivery has been studied extensively during the past 30 years. Despite this, the
fundamental understanding of TfR is still lacking and no strategies to cross the BBB has made it to
the market [15].
4.2
The Blood-Cerebrospinal Fluid Barrier
Another brain barrier is the BCSFB. The BCSFB is found in the choroid plexus (CP) located in the
ventricles of the brain. The BCSFB separates the blood from the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), and
the CSF from brain tissue. The BCSFB is composed of tightly connected epithelial cells linked by
tight junctions and is involved in transport of molecules and immune cells between the brain and
the CSF, hereby regulating the exchange of ions, water, and other substances between the blood
and CSF [10].
The BCSFB could be a potential target for brain drug delivery but has not been researched as
extensively as drug delivery across the BBB.
Transport over the BCSFB may target various transmembrane viroporins or receptor-mediated en-
docytotic pathways [30] [31].
A potential target for transport across the BCSFB is a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR), which
is a transmembrane protein made up of a single polypeptide chain. When an external signalling
molecule binds to a GPCR, it results is G protein activation which triggers either the production of
other messengers or ligand internalization.

4.3
Neurotropic Viruses
Neurotropic virus is a virus capable of accessing the CNS. Neurotropic viruses, e.g. SARS-CoV-2
and mumps, virus enter the brain, many of them by crossing the BCSFB [12] [10].
The mumps virus (MuV) is the virus that causes mumps. Infection with the virus leads to fever,
muscle aches and can cause swelling of the parotid glands. The infection can involve many other
organs and tissues resulting in a variety of diseases, such as meningitis and encephalitis. Treat-
ment is good, but long-term complications can occur. Mumps virus is a member of the family
Paramyxoviridae.
It is a enveloped, non-segmented negative strand RNA molecule [28].
The
genome of MuV encodes nine proteins, one of these is the small hydrophobic protein (SH) [14].
Recent studies by M. Rosenkilde has shown that the SH-protein is a ligand for GPCR125 receptor
which is highly expressed in the BCSFB [36] [19].
M.Rosenkilde and K. Qvortrup has furthermore identified a shorter fragment of MuV SH protein
that can interact with the receptor. This SH peptide has been conjugated to GLP-1 and tested on
mice, conforming the improved passage to the brain across BCSFB [19].
To avoid side effects by activation of the drug in the blood circulation outside the brain the drug may
be conjugated to the shuttle peptide via a brain-selectively cleavable linker. When the conjugate is
transported to the brain over the BCSFB, the drug should be released. By attaching the drug with
a linker that is cleaved by an enzyme only present in the brain, the drug can be selectively released
in the brain.

The Project
The aim of this project was to design an enzyme-cleavable linker for a drug Conjugate for treatment
of AD. The conjugate consists of three parts, an MuV shuttle peptide that transfer the conjugate
across the BCSFB, an enzyme-cleavable linker to ensure brain specific release and a brain active
molecule, more specifically a GLP-1RA. Figure 6 shows a graphical illustration of the conjugate
and its mechanism to cross the BCSFB and release the drug in the CNS.
When the drug conjugate enters the brain, the drug is released through cleavage of the brainspe-
cific enzyme-cleavable linker.
A linker which is cleaved by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase is designed and the brain-specificity
of this enzyme, will prevent activation of the drug before it crosses the BCSFB to selectively release
the drug in the brain. Furthermore, its high catalytic turnover will provide an efficient release.

Conclusion
This project aimed to develop an acetylcholinsesterase-cleavable linker for protein toxin conjugates
in treatment of Alzheimer’s disease. The conjugate consists of three parts, an MuV shuttle peptide,
a brain active molecule, more specifically a GLP-1RA, and finally the AChE-cleavable linker to
ensure brain specific release.
The MuV shuttle peptide take advantage of the delivery unit from mumps virus to transport the drug
conjugate across the BCSFB. Here AChE will cleave the linker to reveal an alkoxide ion, which will
spontaneously undergo a ring closure to release of the drug in the brain.
A structure for an AChE-cleavable linker was proposed and the focus of this project was to develop
a synthetic strategy for the synthesis of the linker. The linker (TM) is built around a lysine core with
an acetylcholinesterase-cleavable acetylcholine moiety, an azido-handle is installed for conjugation
to the MuV shuttle peptide and a chloro methyl ester for attachment of the GLP-1RA.
A reliable method to synthesise the acetylcholinesterase linker has been suggested. The sug-
gested synthetic pathway seems promising, while the synthetic step to convert compound 5 to 6
need to be optimized, and a new promising method was suggested.
When an optimal strategy for the synthesis of the linker (TM) has been identified, the next step is
attachment of the linker to an GLP1-RA.
Quercetin was suggested as the small molecule GLP1-RA and a method to conjugate Quercetin to
the linker was developed.
This new strategy to deliver drugs selectively to the brain, can not only be used in the treatment of
AD, but also in the treatment of other neurological diseases by delivering OH-functionalized drugs
to the brain.

Flash chromatography was performed using glass columns of appropriate size packed with Merck

flash chromatography are specified for each step and are reported as volume ratios.
Removal of solvents were done under reduced pressure (in vacuo) at temperatures ranging from
25 to 90 °C. Traces of solvents were removed in vacuo with the use of a membrane pump.
Analytic UPLC-MS analysis was run on a Waters AQUITY UPLC system equipped with PDA and
SQD electrospray MS detector. Column: Kinetex 1.7 𝜇m XB-C18, 2.1 x 50mm. Column temper-
ature: 50 °C. Flowrate: 0.50 mL/min. Elutent A (0.1% HCOOH in H2O) and B (0.1% HCOOH in

2.6 or 5.0 min. Alternatively, Eluent C (15 mM NH4OAc in H2O) and D (15 mM NH4OAc in MeCN)
used in a linear gradient (5% D to 100% D) in 2.4 min or 4.8, hold 0.1 min, total run time 2.6 or 5.0
min.
