ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate how facilitators conduct and manage innovation processes and how 
this reflects on start-ups. As innovation is a relevant and complex subject, both for established companies and 
for start-ups, the demand for people to facilitate it is strongly present.  
The thesis includes a review of the scientific work done regarding innovation and how it is conducted within 
companies and start-ups. An analysis of what innovation can look like is tackled regarding the network and 
whether the innovation is handled as open innovation or differently. Furthermore, this thesis also includes a 
review of the current literature on facilitation. This functions as a basis to understand the present state of 
facilitation and the further work conducted in this project. 
The results of this review lead to the study conducted, which aims at filling the gaps identified in the literature 
review. For the study conducted, 21 experienced facilitators were interviewed on the approaches they have 
when facilitating both in terms of structure and their interpersonal approach.  In addition, a survey was 
conducted, which gave insights into 19 start-ups about their development, their experience with facilitators 
and workshops, and their opinions on it. 
The findings of the literature review and the empirical study are summarised in a suggested framework, which 
is a guide for users to conduct a working session on their own, without needing a facilitator.  This framework 
should not only be applicable to start-ups when they try to innovate but also help any team in any 
organisation in a variety of situations. 
 The framework got improved with the feedback of 5 facilitators.  
 
Keywords: Innovation, Facilitation, Process facilitation, start-ups, innovation structure, workshop, working 
session

1 INTRODUCTION 
Innovation, which will be defined later on, is a frequently used term both in the academic and economic 
environments. It is considered that the management of innovation is the strategic weapon to be successful in 
the global market, which in this regard is named the product war (Zhan et al., 2017). This shows the 
importance that innovation has in today’s society. By considering innovation as one of the most important 
aspects, Zhan points out that it needs to be a focus of any company. It is important to distinguish between the 
value of innovation for entrepreneurs and their start-ups, and for bigger corporations  (Freeman & Engel, 
2007). As it is a central component of economic growth for companies, there are a lot of innovation 
management tools available that support the process. Within these activities, facilitators play an essential role 
that helps to make the work more effective and efficient and therefore enable the teams that work on the 
innovation process (Pabst et al., 2020). 
1.1 
INNOVATION  
Tidd (2006) found, that since innovation is one of the most essential parts of a successful business, quite some 
research and studies have been conducted over the past three decades to improve usage and reputation of 
innovation. Nevertheless, the number of businesses that turned to the existing theory and research is 
surprisingly little. Understanding innovation as a process, seeing its importance and acting upon that is what 
ultimately defines the way companies try out and manage innovation. Furthermore, the way innovation is 
perceived and understood changed over time as the awareness of the complexity of such processes became 
more and more clear (Tidd, 2006).

As innovation is complex to manage, there are many aspects to be considered throughout it, for example, 
whether to only involve team members or also external people and therefore whether to focus on open or 
closed innovation, which is the network. A company could include clients, suppliers, other business partners, 
people from various teams and backgrounds, random volunteers or just the team working on the innovation 
process or project. All of these considerations are trade-offs and have their advantages and disadvantages 
which will be discussed in a later section. 
1.2 
FACILITATION 
More than 80% of people's time at work is spent on 'busy work', endless meetings, pointless discussions and 
projects that never come to fruition according to the CEO of AJ& Smart, who specialised in facilitation and 
facilitators training. Even if this percentage is not accurate for many companies, it portrays a work culture, 
with a strong focus on unstructured or semi-structured collaboration such as meetings and discussions, 
spread nowadays across many organisations. These activities are often conducted by the person with the 
highest position in the room who also happens to have strong opinions and the most input on the topic. 
While a meeting could be fruitful with such an approach many times it is not, and it goes strongly against the 
ideology of what facilitation is and consists of.  
A facilitator should be someone who focuses on the neutral moderation of group discussions and processes 
and someone who aims to promote fruitful group dialogues to improve group performance and interaction 
(Pabst et al., 2020). One might wonder how a manager or a team leader could possibly be “neutral” as a 
facilitator when a big part of their focus is their own opinion. This shows that the current state of discussion 
moderation is very far from the ideal state of how meetings should be conducted and handled. 
As mentioned above, innovation is not often the focus of companies, but it is one of the most important factors 
of competitiveness. Therefore, whenever teams or companies have meetings that focus on innovation, they 
might be considered the most important ones and effective facilitation might be desired as an essential part 
of these meetings.

PROBLEM ANALYSIS 
Innovation is one of the most important focus points of any company no matter their size, focus or industry. 
To understand in what regards it is therefore relevant for start-ups, the first Research Question is: 
RQ 1: How is innovation relevant for start-ups? 
To fulfil the purpose of the thesis several aspects need to be researched and analysed. Facilitation is a field 
which is not much explored in terms of research, but it is a relevant topic and has many people working with 
it who are more than happy to share their experience and knowledge. It is therefore important to consider 
both sources to draw a whole picture to be able to fulfil the purpose of this thesis.  
The importance of innovation in any industry causes a rather high number of people within facilitation that 
work with or focus on innovation. This leads to the second Research Question covered in this thesis: 
RQ2: How do facilitators conduct innovation processes? 
As facilitation is a complex practice, it is necessary to divide the activities into two subtopics and therefore two 
Sub-Research Questions. These are divided into the process that the facilitator follows, the tools and steps 
taken, and the interpersonal aspects of facilitation which includes managing the people and everything 
connected to them. Therefore, the two Sub-Research Questions are: 
RQ 2.1: What structure do facilitators commonly utilise when conducting innovation? 
RQ 2.2: How do facilitators manage the people in an innovation process? 
 
 
These questions will be answered with the help of literature and outreach to professionals in the field.

2 LITERATURE REVIEW 
2.1 
WHAT IS INNOVATION 
Innovation is a very frequently used word, in academia, society and the economy.  Various researchers and 
authors have different interpretations and definitions of what innovation is. Something that most of these 
definitions do have in common, is that they explain innovation as an action (Freeman & Engel, 2007) or a 
process (Pierce & Delbecq, 1977) that happens within an organisation. Where many interpretations do not 
seem aligned is whether it is a momentary activity or rather a long process. Many authors refer to innovation 
as a process with specifically named and defined steps, such as generation, acceptance, and implementation 
(Pierce & Delbecq, 1977). Nevertheless, there are many different stages that researchers mention, and neither 
the number of steps nor the terminology of the steps themselves is the same throughout them. Researchers 
also have a different approach to the connection between innovation and invention: while some believe 
innovation is the invention of something new (Pierce & Delbecq, 1977), others say that invention in itself is 
not an innovation (Freeman & Engel, 2007), and others argue that innovation is the process that follows 
invention (Pierce & Delbecq, 1977), being separate from the invention in time. 
 
This shows that there are different ways of interpreting innovation, which will also be evident throughout the 
research and discussions, as all the definitions and interpretations point out core characteristics of innovation. 
The research does not narrow down between innovation as a process or a momentary action, as both 
perspectives will be relevant and analysed. Furthermore, the differentiation whether the invention is or 
follows innovation will also be discussed further. The overarching understanding, that innovation depends 
on making novel and unique connections between the knowledge that individual team members possess, 
remains relevant throughout the analysis (Salazar et al., 2012). 
 
While understanding what innovation is and acknowledging different interpretations and definitions that 
alternate from one another, it is also relevant to understand what does not fall under the definition of 
innovation. 
==PAGE:21==
2.1  |  WHAT IS INNOVATION 
21 
Tidd’s (2006) research suggests that innovation is not to be seen as a linear technology push process. Also, 
one should not rely only on the market to pull for innovation. These approaches would lead to all the attention 
going only to the R&D department in most companies and not diversifying the work and outreach to users. 
Innovation can also be more than either a process or a momentary action, it can be many activities over time 
and therefore incremental rather than focusing on a breakthrough. Last but not least, innovation is not an 
external or stand-alone activity or process that happens but it always is connected to the organisation where 
it happens and the wider system it happens in (Tidd, 2006). 
 
2.1.1 
Innovation in the industry 
As pointed out in the introduction, the global market is considered to be in a product war and management 
of innovation is the strategic weapon within it, even though innovation is more than product development 
(Zhan et al., 2017). The failure rate of new products is measured at around 40 to 50 percent and that value 
has not changed much over the last decades (Zhan et al., 2017). According to the researchers, this process 
tends to happen within companies to be able to be competitive and it should be in the focus and should get 
managed in the right ways (Zhan et al., 2017). There are many innovation management tools and systems, 
which may depend on the culture and focus of the company, that can help an organisation strive in this field 
(Pabst et al., 2020). 
Tidd’s (2006) research suggests that as innovation is a broad field of work, it can be broken down and viewed 
in various ways. Throughout all of them, there is a set of factors that innovation needs to fulfil to be more 
successful, which are relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability and observability. 
Understanding these helps to acknowledge what innovation is and what it consists of especially regarding 
product innovation (Tidd, 2006). 
Relative advantage refers to the degree to which an innovation is perceived to be better than the 
competing products. This considers whether the technology, the design or other characteristics are 
superior. Compatibility is the degree to which an innovation is perceived to be consistent with the 
existing values, experience and needs of potential adopters, meaning how well it fits into the 
organisation and its potential clients. The two main factors within it are the existing skills and practices, 
and the values and norms. Complexity is the degree to which an innovation is perceived to be difficult 
to understand or use and therefore how it might be a barrier for potential users. Trialability refers to 
==PAGE:22==
2  |  LITERATURE REVIEW 
22 
the degree to which an innovation can be experimented with on a limited basis, such as a beta version 
or tests that the potential users can conduct. Observability is the degree to which the results of an 
innovation are visible to others, meaning how the results of innovation can be observed (Tidd, 2006). 
According to the research of Zhan (2017), good product innovation process should in addition be adaptable, 
according to researchers. This would lead to a more efficient roadmap, bringing products to market faster and 
improve the use of resources. Besides the already mentioned characteristics that describe good innovation, 
research has shown that the most important factors in organisations to support product innovation are an 
accelerated innovation process, customer connection, and an ecosystem of innovation. The accelerated 
innovation process is facilitated by the organisation’s structure and its culture, such as the team constellation, 
management and work division. The customer connection refers to involving, understanding and 
communicating with the customer and, finally, the ecosystem of innovation refers to the network inside and 
outside of the organisation, such as customers, partners, and the relations between them (Zhan et al., 2017). 
Since innovation can be divided into many sub-groups based on various criteria, such as radical innovation 
and user-centred innovation (Pabst et al., 2020). Two of the most relevant divisions within innovation are 
open vs. closed innovation (Katzy et al., 2013) and following the corporate model vs. the entrepreneurship 
model (Freeman & Engel, 2007). The openness of innovation refers to the source of knowledge for the 
innovation regarding the company (Katzy et al., 2013). The focus of the model of innovation is based on the 
age and size of the firm. Both are further analysed in the following sections (Freeman & Engel, 2007). 
 
2.1.2 
Open Innovation 
According to the research of Almirall et al (2010), open innovation is the embracement of external ideas and 
knowledge, as well as the adoption of elements or subsystems developed by other players, while closed 
innovation fosters internal work, ideas, and opinions. Among those two options, whether an organisation 
should take a closed or an open approach to innovation, is an impactful and important decision that needs 
consideration. This choice strongly influences the development trajectories that the technology will follow 
over time. If open innovation is chosen as the go-to strategy, it impacts a wide range of fields and aspects 
within the company. The efforts to include a larger and more diverse group of people lead to an increase in 
coordination costs due to the divergent objectives of different people. Managing this process leads to 
==PAGE:23==
2.1  |  WHAT IS INNOVATION 
23 
financial investments but also to challenges and issues. Involving a diverse group of people can also lead to 
an increase in competition for resources, since internal researchers are compared to and therefore must 
compete with external players. In addition, it restrains the employees’ work freedom, as they need to work 
with constraints that could have been avoided otherwise. The constraints from other stakeholders trying to 
get an advantage out of it and having different goals that don’t fully align will therefore impact the work. With 
an open innovation approach, these trade-offs come with the biggest advantage, which is that the products 
are matched with the consumer preferences, which helps to maximise the value for the user. (Almirall & 
Casadesus-Masanell, 2010) 
 
Katzy (2013) suggests possible guidance for whom to involve in open innovation is called the quadruple 
helix, which suggests including people from the public, private, government and end users in the co-creation 
process. It is suggested to follow a network model that focuses on trust building and that facilitates the 
identification and matching of collaboration partners carefully.  
 
According to Almirall et al (2010) found that open innovation is generally superior to closed innovation when 
the complexity is not high, but as the trade-off shows, it is not uniformly the case. The two strongest factors to 
consider for the direction of the innovation process are the discovery in comparison to the divergence. The 
discovery implies that new combinations of product features that would otherwise be hard to foresee enable 
the system designer. On the contrary, divergence implies that the system developer loses the freedom to 
establish the product's trajectory and therefore some product features may be controlled by another firm or 
people and limit the possible influence. As discovery and divergence are a trade-off, they need to be 
compared directly to the benefits and disadvantages of open innovation. The research found that with that in 
mind, open innovation tends to be more beneficial if the number of fixed partnerships is between two and 
five, as fewer don’t bring enough of an advantage, and too many have a harmful rather than helpful impact. 
While divergence increases a lot after the fifth partner, discovery does not seem to rise a lot after the third 
partner.

2.1.3 
Innovation and start-ups 
Open innovation requires many resources (Katzy et al., 2013) as mentioned above, which means that it might 
not be feasible for every company size and situation. The structure and culture of the organization have a great 
influence on the ability to innovate. 
 According to Katy (2013) generally, small to medium-sized enterprises have their more agile environment 
as an advantage, which leads to bigger companies wanting to partner up with smaller ones to benefit from 
this. This type of cooperation has a high success rate and shows early results for both of the collaboration 
partners, which leads to both smaller and bigger companies reaching out to one another. 
He also found, that within small to medium enterprises, start-ups tend to have the highest level of innovation, 
due to their management and structure as an organization. Innovation requires resources to be mobile and 
therefore possible to be redistributed when necessary. Besides financial resources, also workforce needs to 
be very agile, flexible, and organic, which is why oftentimes in start-ups, people’s jobs consist of tasks that 
need to be performed and their work roles are fluid and flexible. Additionally, it requires incentives that need 
to be put in place for those who provide those resources and for those who engage in risky activities which 
require high levels of effort. Often these two underlying principles are in place in start-ups.  
Freeman & Engel (2007) build onto this by arguing that, as start-ups grow, mature and develop, their 
innovation processes slow down. Therefore, with time, they tend to fall victim to the problems that big 
organizations have, which they used to have an advantage over. Success, therefore, tends to lead to 
innovation friction. 
 
The research of Freeman & Engel (2007) also says, that the factors that impact innovation within a start-up the 
most are the start-up size, investment, industry dynamics, education/ academic qualifications of the founders 
and other employees, start-up centres (incubators, accelerators), and innovativeness (amount of patents). 
Each of them has an influence and can have both a positive or a negative impact on the performance of the 
start-up. 
While most researchers agree that start-ups are generally more innovative, some researchers argue that a 
larger organizational size enables innovation, as bigger firms oftentimes have more financial resources and 
more diverse facilities that can support different types of innovation (Freeman & Engel, 2007).

An opinion that these researchers have in common is that a start-up is a company that is built on extreme 
uncertainty, has at its core innovation to create products and services and with that, plays a strong role in the 
early phases of the development of many of those  (Tidd, 2006). 
 
2.1.4 
Development of start-ups 
Besides understanding the connection between innovation and start-ups, it is also essential to know how such 
a group of people develops into an organization and what the different steps or stages are that they go 
through. This was researched by several experts in the past as it helps to understand and analyse start-ups 
better.  
 
Figure 1: The entrepreneurship process of commercializing inventions based on (Nikiforou, 2022) 
 
One of these processes is called the entrepreneurship process of commercializing inventions 
(Nikiforou, 2022), as visualized in Figure 1. Nikiforou (2022) explains it as a process that considers two main 
phases, while also having a short phase before and after. The starting point is an invention, which is 
considered the development of an idea or an act of creation. Examples of inventions are scientific discoveries 
and the development of new technologies. Following the invention, the next step in the process considered 
perhaps the most critical step of the entrepreneurship process, is the opportunity identification. It is the 
phase when a large amount and variety of opportunities are identified before entering any market. This is 
highly important due to the limited amount of resources that start-ups at this stage have. Especially when the 
team is not aware of all the potential product and market applications of an early-stage technology, the 
opportunity identification gains importance. The following phase in the development process of a start-up, 
after the opportunities were identified, is the opportunity exploitation. So, after enough knowledge was 
==PAGE:26==
2  |  LITERATURE REVIEW 
26 
accumulated about the viability of an identified opportunity, they move on to exploit these, by building 
efficient, full-scale operations for products or services based on the business opportunity. It includes many 
activities, such as market research, further development, testing of the technology, building and organizing 
the management team, gaining stakeholders' support, and getting funding, among others. After the 
opportunities were exploited and all the activities of this stage are completed, the start-up then moves on to 
continue working while evaluating the ongoing process to keep the business running. 
 
 
Figure 2: Lifecycle of start-ups based on (Salamzadeh & Kawamorita Kesim, 2015) 
 
Another structure that helps to be able to understand better the stage that a start-up is in is the Lifecycle of 
start-ups (Salamzadeh & Kawamorita Kesim, 2015), visualized in Figure 2. The authors suggests that the 
development is divided into three main stages. The first one is the Bootstrapping stage, where actions 
and activities are happening from the entrepreneur to turn the idea into a profitable business. In this stage, 
there is usually a lot of individual effort and support from the surroundings with a high level of risk and a high 
level of uncertainty. In addition, in this stage, there is a low investment into the company along with the first 
angel investors. Additional people start coming on board and the entrepreneur tries to form a team. This stage 
is then followed by the Seed stage, which is where teamwork, prototype development, entry into the market, 
valuation of the venture, seeking support mechanisms, and average investments to grow the start-up are 
happening. Often in this stage, the start-up reaches out for support and becomes part of, for example, an 
accelerator program or an incubator. During this time there is a big focus on teamwork and the first valuation 
and investments are happening. The last stage is called the Creation stage. The start-up is in this stage when 
they are selling their products, entering the market, and hiring their first employees. Higher investments are 
happening and once the start-up is running for some time, they form a firm that then operates fully and 
corporate finance becomes the main choice for financing the firm.

2.2 
FACILITATION 
A facilitator should be someone who focuses on the neutral facilitation of group discussions and processes 
and someone who aims to promote fruitful group dialogues to improve group performance and interaction. 
Therefore, facilitation is the act and action that a facilitator follows and pursues (Pabst et al., 2020). As 
innovation is such an essential part of the work of an organization, it should be as efficient and as fruitful as 
possible, which is where a facilitator can help. Many actors have their agenda, also in an innovation process, 
which is why the innovation process increasingly falls hostage to the organizational and managerial 
requirements of non-innovators (Freeman & Engel, 2007). The following will help understand more what a 
facilitator is and what they do. 
 
2.2.1 
Facilitator – purpose, role & characteristics 
According to Pabst et al. (2020), those who assume the moderation function within a team are referred to as 
facilitators. Generally, a facilitator can be defined as someone who drives the process and integrates the 
different project partners into the project group. Whoever takes on the facilitator's role can also be an outsider 
of the department or even the organization, which can allow a new perspective. They also mention, that a 
facilitator is seen as a process facilitator and neutral coach who enables the teams to focus on their progress 
and thus work more effectively and efficiently throughout the innovation project. This is also the overall reason 
for including the role of a facilitator: they improve the effectiveness of the work of the team. The facilitator 
aims to promote a fruitful group dialogue and to use tools and methods to improve group interaction and 
performance. Throughout the innovation project, the needs of the team and project change, and the facilitator 
adjusts the tools and support towards it. Usually, innovation teams consist of different disciplines and 
sometimes of people from different organisations, which is why especially the set-up phase of the innovation 
projects is essential for an effective team and to build a common language. Developing a common view of 
the requirements is therefore one of the first tasks for a facilitator that guides a team. 
 
Not only at the beginning but throughout the whole process, a field of responsibility of the facilitator is the 
emotional aspects such as promoting a positive group experience and motivating the group members (Pabst 
et al., 2020). All these responsibilities and actions should benefit the team in terms of boosting trust among 
project members, workload and knowledge of processes and organizational peculiarity (Pabst et al., 2020). 
==PAGE:28==
2  |  LITERATURE REVIEW 
28 
One of the actions of a facilitator is to provoke a continuous reflection-action-reflection process within the 
team and to facilitate a collective learning process enablers  (Estensoro Garcia, 2015). Therefore they can be 
considered knowledge-creation enablers  (Estensoro Garcia, 2015). 
Beyond those actions, Johnsson (2018) argues that a facilitator should have the relevant social skills, to be 
able to handle uncertainty and to build trust, as well as to handle accurate tools, to provide training and not 
to overshadow the team but rather to guide them through the innovation process. While the facilitators have 
a lot of interaction with the team that they guide, there is usually also a sponsor who is the direct employer 
and the direct contact person that invited the facilitator in. According to both the sponsors and the teams from 
the research, the most important characteristics of a facilitator are the following (Johnsson, 2018): 
- 
Allow the team to break the rules. 
- 
Guiding in methodology. 
- 
Bring customer focus early in the project. 
- 
Keep the team focused. 
- 
Encouraging the team to expand boundaries. 
- 
Highlight when the team is off track. 
- 
Bring an understanding that uncertainty is OK. 
- 
Build self-confidence to involve competence outside the company when needed. 
- 
Listening to the team. 
He mentions that the areas of competencies that these can be grouped under are the Innovation team 
creation process, Innovation process, Innovation team model and Factors enabling innovation. 
According to Johnsson (2018), these are therefore characteristics that a good facilitator should possess and 
actions they should follow which focus on interpersonal aspects but also on the structure and the flow. A 
characteristic mentioned previously about neutral facilitation is something that the sponsor and the teams 
seem not to be aligned on. While the teams consider this as very important under the characteristics of “Not 
leading the team” and “Not championing”, the sponsors find this less.

different moments of the innovation process, but they lack to point out the tools that are needed in the

and mastery of processes (Pabst et al., 2020). Tools that can help with such processes are Prototyping,

2.2.2 
Structure within research 
Besides managing the people, managing the process well is one of the most essential aspects that a facilitator 
needs to achieve. Researchers' lack of alignment in what innovation is and means, reflects on the structure 
that is used to follow as a process. There are many different structures that according to researchers align with 
an ideal innovation process. Some are briefly mentioned below while others are explained in more detail, 
which are only some of the many models used and mentioned. 
2.2.2.1 
CRISP 
One of the structures and processes used and researched is called CRISP (Dewhurst, 1999), which stands for 
Catch, Reflect, Improve, Scrutinise and Pass. It was designed to avoid biases during the innovation process 
and to avoid personal or specific interests getting in the way of it. This way it assures that the vision of the 
organization and its objectives are the main focus to be followed. 
 
2.2.2.2 
TRIZ 
TRIZ (Elyoussoufi et al., n.d.) stands for Theory of Inventive Problem Solving and is known as a process that 
focuses on innovation seen as an idea generation. It helps to solve and to overcome the mind's psychological 
inertia and therefore helps to achieve the ideal solution easily and effectively through contradictions and the 
40 Principles. While TRIZ can be used for various steps of the process, it is mostly only used in the innovation 
step to generate ideal solutions rather than exploring the rest of its potential. TRIZ also tends to be looked at 
==PAGE:30==
2  |  LITERATURE REVIEW 
30 
in combination with Strategy and Lean Six Sigma. While TRIZ is not a process to follow, it is rather a toolkit to 
utilize during the process. 
 
2.2.2.3 
Design Thinking 
The research of Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al. (2019) found, that Design Thinking is a methodology that 
contrasts with the traditional scientific methodology which relies on the collaboration of a multidisciplinary 
team to solve complex problems through the application of design knowledge. It focuses on the needs of the 
people and turns them into demands by using sensitive and creative techniques. It helps organisations to be 
more innovative and creative during problem-solving and helping people with a human-centric approach. 
The process goes through the steps of scope definition of a problem, experimenting with different solutions, 
and refining these through feedback until achieving an effective solution that addresses the people affected 
by the problem. Within Design Thinking, there are three guiding pillars, which are Empathy, Collaboration 
and Experimentation. Collaboration refers especially to the integration of multidisciplinary and 
multifunctional teams and people. The process of design thinking is divided into 5 main steps that are as 
Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al. describe, which are Discovery, Interpretation, Ideation, Experimentation and 
Evolution which are visualised in Figure 3. 
 
 
Figure 3: The design thinking process based on (Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 2019). 
 
 
1. 
Discovery  is the first step, which is also called immersion, empathy, or exploration. It is the 
time when the team focuses on familiarizing themselves with the problem and mapping it 
by using several techniques. This stage is usually started before the process with some 
actions such as reframing, exploratory research and desk research to develop an initial 
understanding. The afterwards in-depth immersion into the problem is there to identify the 
needs and opportunities that help to define the scope of the project and its boundaries, as

2.2  |  FACILITATION 
31 
well as identifying the user profiles and key actors that should be addressed by the service 
or 
product. 
 
2. 
Interpretation as the second step also refers to the analysis and synthesis which focus on 
defining the problem and aims to organize the collected information and to obtain a 
synthesis of the desires, needs and motivations of the interested parties, while broadening 
the understanding of the problem. To structure and group the data that was collected and to 
begin with the analysis and synthesis, techniques such as affinity diagrams, insight cards, 
mental maps, conceptual maps, personas and proto-personas and the user’s journey are 
being utilized. Using a multidisciplinary team that utilizes their experience, information and 
creativity 
is 
essential 
in 
this 
stage. 
 
3. 
Ideation is the third stage, during which the team proposes several solutions by using 
techniques such as brainstorming and co-creation workshops. The aim is to generate 
innovative ideas for possible solutions creatively. The group first collects contributions, then 
analyses them deeply, sorts them out and refines them, before deciding which one to use 
for the rest of the process. Within the co-creation workshops, it is desired to have a 
multidisciplinary team and other members that are users and professionals of different areas 
to 
get 
different

and 
experiences  
 
4. 
Experimentation is the stage when prototypes are created by converting all the previous 
input given and gathered to be able to validate the idea. By preparing a presentation or a 
prototype with tools such as a storyboard, video, innovation fair, storytelling, role play and 
others, a tangible or intangible experience is created. Besides presenting the idea more 
comprehensively, this stage functions as a platform for feedback and input from others. 
 
5. 
Evolution is the fifth and final stage of the design thinking process, during which the 
prototype is to be evaluated in a real-use scenario for validation and field comparison. The 
previously defined users and target group should test the prototype, express their needs and 
give feedback so that the prototype can be improved iteratively until solving the needs and 
problems.

Afterwards, the whole design thinking process can be repeated based on the outcome of the evolution stage. 
That way another level of refinement that is desired can be reached (Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 2019). 
  
2.2.2.4 
Design Sprint 
The research of Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al. (2019) also explains, that the design sprint is a multi-day process 
created by Google Venture to solve issues through prototypes and brainstorming with customers. Other 
organizations and Companies did slight modifications to the process as well and adjusted it based on their 
preferences and availability. Originally it is a five-day event with a maximum of seven people that requires a 
well-defined challenge and has the goal to generate a prototype that afterwards leads to a successful product. 
It is used to check out initial ideas and change directions within a short time frame without getting hung up 
on details. Each day has an overarching theme as visualised in Figure 4 and several activities within it, and it 
is usually split over 5 working days, so one week, which are explained below  (Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 
2019). 
 
 
Figure 4: The design sprint process based on (Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 2019). 
 
1. Day 1, Understand and define: The first day starts with the definition and selection of 
a goal and a map to achieve to get to the goal.  The target should be realistic, and the map 
helps to guide the week and consider the challenge. The map usually consists of a list of 
actors, the target and words which should all be precise and simple. Throughout the day, 
the map gets more and more complete and improved. It is also the time during which 
==PAGE:33==
2.2  |  FACILITATION 
33 
outreach to experts outside the team happens to support the process and improve the 
map. This is conducted through interviews that often lead to the team having more 
questions at the end. Additionally, the target audience is being defied to assure working 
in the right direction. 
2. Day 2, Diverge: The first part of the day consists of adjusting the ideas that were worked 
on the day before. Afterwards, members will sketch what they think is the best solution to 
the defined and agreed-upon problem. Everyone should be preparing a solution that they 
find feasible and give a very short presentation on it while using words and sketches. A 
possible structure for this is finding first inspiration from the visualized ideas from the day 
before while taking notes. Afterwards, follow the exercise of “Crazy 8’s” where 8 variations 
of one’s idea should be drawn. And finally, a self-explanatory sketch should be created with 
the best idea to present to the others. 
3. Day 3, Decide: On the morning of this day the team will make a decision to select the 
most promising project, therefore multiple ideas can also be merged and combined. Next, 
a detailed action plan is defined that defines the steps until the prototype. During this day 
new ideas are not welcome anymore. It is recommended to make a plan for big ideas that 
have not been tested in the past rather than repeating the prototyping of things that others 
have tried. Additionally, a storyboard should be developed, which is a set of drawings that 
visualize the plan for the next steps. The last step of the day should be the expectations 
setting and alignment of the prototyping process. 
4. Day 4, Prototype: The focus of the day is all about turning the storyboard into a 
prototyping, in any form or way. The most relevant focus points for the team are choosing 
the right tools, dividing and conquering, sewing everything together and testing. Simple 
tools are sufficient for a prototype, so using a document or a presentation will fully achieve 
the purpose. As the best approach is to work on different things in parallel, all the work 
needs to match and be put together logically. 
5. Day 5, Validate: The last day has the focus on validating the solution with customers to 
assure it fulfils their needs that they have. Interviews with clients lead to a lot of feedback, 
insights and improvement points that should then be translated into an action plan which 
should be implemented and should go beyond the prototype into the product creation. 
==PAGE:34==
2  |  LITERATURE REVIEW 
34 
While the whole team will be able to see and evaluate the interview, it should be 
conducted in a more private environment . 
As mentioned, there are different authors of different versions and alternations of the design sprint. One of 
the ways that are suggested for small and medium-sized enterprises to utilize the design sprint is to try out a 
“reverse sprint” that starts from the end with feedback on the existing version of the product and then follows 
the design sprint in its regular structure (Magistretti et al., 2020). 
2.2.2.5 
The Double Diamond Model 
According to the research of Tschimmel (2012) the Double Diamond Model is a structure that was created by 
the British Council and is a visual way of representing the divergent and convergent stages of a design 
process, which has the shape of two diamonds that connect. As the four stages which are considered quarters 
of the model as visualised in Figure 5 are Discover, Define, Develop and Deliver, it is also called the 4 D model 
(Tschimmel, 2012). 
 
Figure 5: The Double Diamond Model based on (Tschimmel, 2012) 
 
1. Discover: The Discovery is the initial divergent phase, in which the team looks 
for new opportunities, new markets, new information, new trends and new 
insights. 
==PAGE:35==
2.2  |  FACILITATION 
35 
2. Define: This is the first convergent and therefore the first closing stage, which 
is a filter where the first insights are reviewed, selected and discarded. It 
includes the development of initial project ideas, in which the designer 
considers the broader context and environment. Therefore, in this stage, the 
focus is on project development, project management and corporate sign-off. 
3. Develop: This is the second divergent stage, during which solutions are being 
developed, iterated, and tested within the organization by a multi-disciplinary 
team. Tools from Design Thinking are often utilized such as brainstorming, 
sketches, scenarios, renderings or prototypes. 
4. Deliver: The final stage, which is convergent, focuses on taking the final 
concept through final testing, sign-off, production and launch. 
Some consider using the double diamond a little complex but a very useful tool for the business and 
innovation management environment (Tschimmel, 2012).

Flexibility and adapting as a core 
 
Figure 7: Flexibility and adapting theme 
 
As flexibility and being able to adapt and improvise seems to be the most important characteristic of a good

While the participants themselves change based on the client and the project, the number of people within

4 and 10, some work with a range of 5 to 50 and others with an even bigger upper limit of people. As many 
of the workshops focus on co-creation, they tend to include a cross-functional team and involve external 
participants who are possible end users and customers. This means that besides the group size, the 
backgrounds, personalities and behaviours of the participants always change and need to be adjusted 
to.   
The usual flow from the moment that the client reaches out, until the workshop is conducted, is to start with 
getting in touch with the sponsor, who is the contact person from the client’s side. With the sponsor, the 
facilitator then conducts a needs assessment, which is an analysis of the team and the project that they will 
work with to fully understand the situation or problem. Based on the assessed needs and the problem 
that was analysed, there is then an agreement on the extend and duration of the cooperation. The duration 
yet again requires flexibility from the facilitator, as the interviewees mentioned that the projects or groups of 
workshops go up to multiple weeks. The minimum duration that most of them have for a workshop is two

hours. This is for most of them based on the needs of the team and the customer, but also on the resources 
available. The facilitator needs to be flexible with the resources available and with the location and the 
technology that can be therefore provided.  
As mentioned, following the needs and the resources of the client, the workshop length gets adjusted, but 
also according to the workshop type and content that need to be created. The length and type of workshop 
have a very big range of possibilities. A frequently mentioned format is a five-day event where the workshops 
build up on one another, as well as stand-alone half-day long workshops. Also, the format of doing several 
workshops spread out over a few months was mentioned to be in certain cases the best approach depending 
on the needs of the team.  
Flexibility is in addition needed regarding the energy level of the workshop. It needs to be planned for, 
adjusted, and managed all the time since it is to a big extent based on the participants. I13 mentioned for 
example that they “constantly focus on dynamics and adjust facilitation to it” and I3 mentioned that they 
“design ups and downs in the mood and energy and adjust the activities to that”. The facilitator mentioned 
that they can influence it by the activities that are happening, by the way they are conducted, and by the way 
the facilitator is speaking and behaving. Some mentioned that they should expect and plan for ups and downs 
within the workshop flow. Some mention that also the types of food and the times for eating should be 
considered, as they can influence the participants' energy levels a lot. The energy within the workshop is often 
directly connected to the motivation that the participants feel. Many facilitators mentioned that high 
motivation at the end of the workshop is essential for everyone to leave the workshop with a positive feeling. 
One of the ways how this can be achieved is by having very action-oriented activities at the end that 
kickstart the work of the team.  
Another way that needs to be balanced and handled with care that requires a lot of flexibility is the amount 
of information that is shared with participants. This mostly refers to the information in advance about the 
activities that will happen and the duration of activities, among others. I3 mentioned that they “don’t give 
anyone too much information before, to keep things surprising”, which is how they and other facilitators 
mentioned that they do not want to share “too much” information, to keep curiosity and keep the 
participants excited. Curiosity is also promoted by taking the participants out of their usual 
environment, both regarding what the activities look like and where they are conducted.  In contrary with 
too much information is the need for facilitators to share enough information to make the participants 
feel safe and comfortable. All participants will have a different amount of information they would like, but 
there is a common baseline that should be reached to make them feel more certain. Giving the participants 
==PAGE:47==
4.1  |  INTERVIEWS WITH FACILITATORS 
47 
overall information about the flow and the progress also keeps them from questioning the flow and 
activities that the facilitator chooses. Some facilitators also mentioned that not too much information about 
the flow should be shared to allow flexibility and be able to change and adjust things if needed without 
participants getting confused or questioning the decision of adjustments.  
A way that most facilitators pointed out the need for flexibility is to have a backup plan. Some mentioned 
that it’s good to have a backup activity for every chosen activity in the workshop and prepare the templates 
and all materials for these, while I2 mentioned that “for every workshop, you should have a plan A, plan B and 
a plan C and expect that nothing will go according to plan”. Therefore, a facilitator should prepare many 
backup options that they can use and go back to, but the most important is to be aware of the goals of the 
whole workshop and reach them with lots of flexibility by adjusting to the participants. 
4.1.3 
The ideal feeling of participants 
 
Figure 8: Ideal feelings of participants and atmosphere theme 
==PAGE:48==
4  |  DATA ANALYSIS 
48 
 
As Figure 8 shows, there are many ways that facilitators want the environment to be like and their participants 
in the workshop to feel, as it highly impacts the input they give, how active they are, how willing they are for 
sharing ideas and their all in all behaviour. 
According to the interviewees, it is essential for the participants to feel comfortable to be able to contribute, 
both physically and emotionally. Among other ways, this feeling can be encouraged by the facilities chosen, 
as well as by using tangible objects where the participants can project their thoughts, ideas, and feelings. 
A popular tool is LEGOs, which can be used in many different workshop formats.  
Confidence is one of the most important feelings for participants within the workshop and the facilitator 
should take time to build that confidence. This can be done by showing them that they are the right people 
for the workshop and that all of them have the right knowledge needed. Some mentioned, that the 
participants feeling confident will empower them to give the best input they can. 
As mentioned above, some participants need a certain amount of information, which is just one of the ways 
that influence how safe a participant might feel. Multiple facilitators found this important, for example, I4 
mentioned that they “create a safe constructive space and build confidence and comfort”. This psychological 
safety is very important since it is the baseline for participants not hesitating to share any ideas they have. 
I8 mentioned how essential this is by expressing that “psychological safety is the most important, especially 
when wanting to encourage innovation and unconventional ideas”.  They should know that their ideas will 
not be judged, and they should feel that they are listened to.  
Open was mentioned by most of the facilitators as how they want the environment in their workshop to be 
and feel. It builds upon the mindset that every idea and input is welcome and accepted and 
complements the commonly used phrase “yes, and” which is about building on the ideas of others rather 
than arguing against certain ones. Another underlying principle of an open environment is that everyone 
listens actively to each other and puts communication at the core of the workshop. Openness in the 
workshop also refers to the way that things are communicated, which should be trusting, honest and 
direct.  
While the energy within the workshop varies over time and requires a lot of flexibility while handling it, most 
facilitators want high energy in parts of their workshop. High energy usually leads to high motivation of 
==PAGE:49==
4.1  |  INTERVIEWS WITH FACILITATORS 
49 
participants which is something else that is highly valued by the interviewees. To achieve high motivation, a 
facilitator needs to find what motivates each individual within the workshop. While participants might 
enter the workshop frustrated with the situation that the team is in, it is important that at the end of the 
workshop, they are happy with the outcomes and feel motivated to take action. Therefore, any 
workshop should focus on increasing the motivation of the participants towards the topic that is being 
worked upon.  
Other feelings that most facilitators like to have in the workshop are curiosity and excitement, which are 
strongly connected. As mentioned above, this is mostly done by taking them out of their usual 
environment and not giving too much information to keep a level of surprise that can keep the 
participants guessing and looking forward to certain activities. In addition, to keep the excitement that the 
participants have and promote and encourage it, all the wins and success points within the workshop 
should be celebrated, which complements the environment. 
Joy, fun and humour are also something that many facilitators value and that complements the concept of 
taking the participants out of their usual environment, as it is not commonly an aspect of the day-to-day work 
of many. I14 believes it is essential by mentioning that with “trust and fun, you can work on almost 
everything”.  It is a way to let the participants open up on another level through metaphors, costumes and 
themes within the workshop. A looser environment can not only make them feel more open but also safer, 
motivated and energetic. To use this as a benefit, jokes and humour should be very considerate and should 
not make any of the participants feel uncomfortable. In addition, some mentioned to be cautious, as fun does 
not automatically lead to innovation. 
 
All aspects of this atmosphere lead to the participants not hesitating to contribute and giving the best input 
that they can. While it consists of many small actions over time that the facilitator takes, the biggest part of 
shaping this environment is done at the very beginning of the workshop.

4.1.4 
Beginning of a workshop 
 
Figure 9: Beginning of a workshop theme 
The beginning of a workshop is mentioned by most of the facilitators to be the most important part of setting 
the environment. There are many different approaches to reach this that are shown in Figure 9, which all have 
the goal to allow and encourage the most important feelings that the facilitator wants to achieve.  
The most often used tool is to have an icebreaker at the beginning of the workshop, which most people 
define as an activity that loosens participants up and that guides the first interaction among the 
participants to get more familiar and comfortable with each other and to facilitate that connection. 
Some facilitators mentioned, that for any workshop, the icebreaker makes it automatically mandatory for 
everyone to participate and to speak. In case individuals in the group are not familiar with one another, it 
can also function as a get-to-know game. Some mentioned that it should also focus on something that will 
be needed for the workshop, for example, if there is drawing and sketching needed within the workshop, 
the icebreaker should also give the participants the chance to draw and get comfortable with that in a playful 
way. If creativity is needed within the workshop, then the icebreaker should be connected to that, to not 
only get the participants more comfortable but also more confident towards the activities throughout the 
==PAGE:51==
4.1  |  INTERVIEWS WITH FACILITATORS 
51 
workshop. This is the moment when the facilitator might step in to show the participants with the exercise 
that they are capable of creativity, drawing or whatever is needed.  
The beginning of the first workshop with a group of people is also used by most facilitators to agree on rules 
regarding behaviour and to set expectations towards one another. I19 suggests to “Start off with guidelines, 
such as: Whatever is said here stays here. Always ask curious questions. Open to feedback. If you feel really 
uncomfortable you can also not share or participate”. These rules and expectations also help to achieve the 
environment that is desired. For example, the rule that only one person speaks at a time complements the 
environment of everyone being actively listened to. The rule of “whatever is said here, stays here” fosters 
an open environment of psychological safety where participants do not hold back to share their honest 
opinion and their ideas. “Always ask curious questions” is a rule or guideline that fosters curiosity within 
the workshop and complements participants being attentive and critical. Another rule that is used to 
encourage open communication and comfort is that everyone, including the facilitator, is "open for 
feedback”. “Not using devices that are not needed” is something that is often encouraged by the 
physical room set up and it is in place not only for everyone to be more attentive, but also to make sure 
everybody is listened to and ready and willing to contribute. A rule that does not focus too much on the 
environment but that multiple facilitators mentioned is to let the participants agree to the authority of 
the facilitator, which can for example be framed as “we do what it takes to get the best outcomes, 
and we trust the facilitator to guide us there”.  
Something else that is important to focus on at the beginning of the workshop is to have alignment among 
the participants. According to the facilitators, not having alignment can negatively impact the workshop 
regarding the quality of the input that is given, can lead to input being off-topic and to some participants not 
feeling very comfortable sharing. One of the ways is by asking the participants about their opinion on 
the topic. Otherwise letting all participants phrase the goal and the scope of the workshop in their own 
words, as well as getting aligned on the biggest challenge in the moment is a way to conduct this kind 
of beginning in a workshop. A way to get participants to rather share personally than get very aligned on the 
topic or content is by asking them what they would personally like to get out of the workshop or by 
asking them anonymously how comfortable they feel about participating in the workshop. All these 
will make the participants speak and contribute at the very beginning of the workshop and will help to get all 
aligned.

4.1.5 
Hierarchy within the workshop 
 
Figure 10: Hierarchy within the workshop theme 
Hierarchy is something that differs from country to country and among companies depending on the culture 
and people within it. While different facilitators have different ways of dealing with hierarchy within their 
workshop, which are shown in Figure 10, they all mentioned that being aware of the hierarchy and 
power dynamics within the group is fundamental. Some mentioned that getting to know the team and its 
dynamics beforehand can help to be better prepared for the occurrence of such power dynamics. There were 
several different approaches to this, some mentioned that as these dynamics exist, they try to make use of 
them, by talking with the highest-ranked people or leaders beforehand. There were two main reasons 
mentioned for this, one is to get their full support already before the workshop, so their behaviour is the 
ideal behaviour for a participant, which then will likely influence others and encourage them to behave 
in the same way. This can then lead to more active participation. Aligned with this, someone also mentioned 
that they talk with the highest ranked person beforehand to assure that they will actively disagree with 
ideas and discuss them, which leads to the others adopting a similar behaviour and in the end assuring 
the highest quality outcomes. Another reason to get the decision maker, which is often the highest ranked 
person, on board is to assure that the outcomes of the workshop will be implemented afterwards 
and that resources are available and will be dedicated to working on those aspects. Many facilitators also 
mentioned that roles and positions do not matter within the workshop and that is something they point 
out at the beginning of the workshop. Therefore, everyone is considered equal, and everyone’s input should 
be valued and taken into account in the same way, which reflects the atmosphere that most facilitators try to 
have within the workshop. Nevertheless, dynamics might perhaps still be there as mentioned above, which 
is why many mentioned that they facilitate in a way that takes the dynamics into account. One approach is to 
==PAGE:53==
4.1  |  INTERVIEWS WITH FACILITATORS 
53 
always let the highest ranked person go last with presenting or voting during activities, to avoid others 
repeating their input, which is why I18 expressed to “make the highest ranked person present and do actions 
last, like presenting ideas, clustering things etc.”.  As another approach someone mentioned to actively ask all 
participants for counterarguments and concerns when the highest ranked person gives an idea, therefore 
participants hesitate less to criticise those inputs since they are actively encouraged and asked to point out 
the bad parts, which will lead to the best ideas.  
4.1.6 
Equal participation 
 
Figure 11: Equal participation theme 
As facilitators try to make everyone feel very comfortable and avoid hierarchy to impact the workshop, their 
ultimate goal that was mentioned above already, is commonly to get everyone to participate equally, 
which are shown in Figure 11. In case there are behaviours observed that could go against this, such as people 
that misbehave or talk a lot more than others, facilitators mentioned several approaches to handle it and 
to make the contribution more equal again, which are described in more detail below. As everyone 
should contribute equally, facilitators also said that there should be no spectators or observers and everyone 
that is there should be involved in the workshop itself. A very popular approach that assures participation 
from everyone and that leave very little room for uneven contribution is silent exercises that are also called 
==PAGE:54==
4  |  DATA ANALYSIS 
54 
“working together alone”. The concept bases itself on everyone working silently in parallel, which 
gives them as much thinking time as they need. Afterwards, they present what they worked on to each 
other, which again gives all of them the same opportunity to share what they came up with. When 
facilitators notice a disrupting pattern in someone’s behaviour, they deal with it in various ways, depending 
on the facilitator’s preferences but also on the degree of how disruptive the behaviour is. According to the 
interviewees, for behaviour that is a little disruptive like someone talking a bit more than others, the 
interviewees mentioned that they facilitate around it and therefore avoid it. If the behaviour is something 
that was agreed upon at the beginning of the workshop or it is something that cannot so easily be facilitated 
around, then the facilitators mentioned that they point out in a general way that certain things should 
not be done. They mentioned that it is important to not point out that one person acted poorly in this situation 
and draw attention to them, as that would impact the feeling of safety and comfort of them specifically but 
also of all other participants. In case this is not enough or not possible, some facilitators mentioned that they 
then take the participant aside in a break and give them feedback privately on their behaviour. In this 
case, I10 would “take the person aside and talk with them in case they try to overshadow others”. If the way 
they act is too disruptive and there is no break planned, a break can be initiated and used for that purpose. 
Something that was also explained as a measure of what they would do, but nobody mentioned that they had 
to do it so far, was to ask a participant to leave the workshop in case they did not act upon the feedback 
they received, and their behaviour is too disruptive for the workshop. A further aspect that was mentioned in 
those regards is that some facilitators use NLP, so Neuro-linguistic programming, which is a communication 
approach, as a baseline for facilitating the participants in the workshop. A similar approach was also to get to 
know all participants before the workshop more, to then be able to understand their needs and preferences 
of working and communication to be able to use this within the workshop setup.

4.1.7 
Flexible structures with different inspiration 
 
Figure 12: Workshop structures theme 
Besides facilitating the people within the workshop, a core of the work of the facilitator is to achieve the goal 
of the workshop with appropriate activities. Therefore, they need to choose a structure that brings them there. 
Some of these structure elements and focus points of their are mentioned in Figure 12. As mentioned above, 
the facilitators believe that flexibility is essential also when working on the structure of the workshop in two 
ways. The first was already explained in more detail and it is to have possible backup plans in case things 
go differently than expected and things need to be readjusted in the moment. The other ways that flexibility 
is essential is during the design stage of the workshop when creating the flow and the activities, as most 
facilitators mentioned that they take inspiration from existing models but most of the time 
==PAGE:56==
4  |  DATA ANALYSIS 
56 
combine activities from different models and create a flow that matches the needs and that they 
think is suitable.  
The three most frequently mentioned models that facilitators use are design thinking, design sprint and 
the double diamond. 
For the design thinking process, some facilitators mentioned that they only follow it if there is enough 
time within the workshop to go through the whole process, while others mentioned that they take it as a 
tool for inspiration. In addition to the design thinking, some mentioned that they use the MG Taylor 
approach, which is something that is based on design sprint with a lot of additional parts to it, which is 
elaborated in three different books. Those focus on patterns, which are structures and metaphors, models, 
which are building blocks, and tools, which are activities.  
The double diamond structure is used by some facilitators as a tool to align the team and participants on 
how to express themselves and keep a better order throughout the discussions and thoughts. 
Therefore, the focus of this is whether the exercise or discussion has the purpose of diverging or converging 
and if it is working on the problem space or the solution space. This led to some interviewees mentioning that 
they “use problem space and divergent vs convergent thinking as common words and vocabulary” with teams 
they work with. Facilitators mentioned that this differentiation also helps them when choosing activities 
and workshop structures. Some mentioned that it is hardly possible to go through a double diamond 
within a workshop, so it helps them to choose what section to focus on and how many workshops they 
need to complete the process for example. 
The design sprint was the structure where people mentioned that they often follow the whole process, rather 
than only certain elements. The two main interpretations here were the AJ& Smart Design sprint 2.0, 
which is a 4 days sprint, as well as the Google Method of Design sprint, which is a 5 days sprint. It 
was mentioned that this might only be suitable if there is a desire to create something new. While some use 
the full process, some mentioned that they pick out individual parts and activities from the design 
sprint, as well as adapt and adjust it to the needs of the workshop.  
There is a set of other models and approaches that individuals mentioned, where they take inspiration from, 
which are: Theory of U, co-creation, lean start-up framework, system thinking, user-centred work, human-
centred design, innovation funnel, and customer value proposition.

5.1 
INNOVATION MODELS OF START-UPS 
There are several theories and models out there that suggest the development of a start-up and the different 
stages that it can be divided into. The entrepreneurship process of commercializing inventions (Nikiforou, 
2022) and the Lifecycle of start-ups (Salamzadeh & Kawamorita Kesim, 2015) seemed complete, 
comprehensive and appropriate. Therefore, these were the ones used for the survey to gather input from 
entrepreneurs on their development and the status of their start-ups.  
Considering the comparison of the selection in both models, as in Figure 17, there does not seem to be any 
clear correlation between those two models. In addition, there was space for the entrepreneurs to express in 
their own words what situation their start-up is in. While two-thirds of the people mentioned that they were 
in the bootstrapping stage, their self-written answers were very different from one another, and the situation 
of the start-ups seemed far apart from one another. The same applies when they were placing themselves 
within the other process.  
This leads to the question of whether models like that are useful and who they would be useful for. In the case 
of the survey, they initially seemed useful, but the results do not lead to very usable conclusions. Based on 
the written answers with choice of free words, it seems that they might have different interpretations of what 
each of the stages stands for due to its broad definitions. Otherwise, it could be that besides having broad 
definitions, the stages also refer to many different situations and development stages that a start-up is in and 
therefore it leads to a very wide range.

A further aspect to point out concerning the two models used is that one refers a lot to the bigger stage that a 
start-up is in, considering the team constellation, the facilities, and the struggles in different moments. The 
entrepreneurship process of commercializing inventions, on the other hand, is very much focused on the 
activities and the main goal and focus that they have at a given moment. As they don’t cluster start-ups in the 
same way, there is not much correlation between the answers.  
In conclusion, models like these can be useful for certain purposes. In this case, they were only useful in 
connection to an open question where start-ups first described themselves in their own words and them 
placing themselves in specific stages gave extra information. Therefore, this research does not have the stage 
selected as the only input to draw information from and assume more from there, but rather it is an additional 
piece of information that is useful and helps to understand the other answer a little better. 
5.2 
CHARACTERISTICS AND ACTIONS OF FACILITATORS 
5.2.1 
Flexibility while facilitating 
Previous research mentioned that facilitators need to adjust the tools and support that they give throughout 
the innovation project, as the needs of the team change along with it. In addition in most innovation teams, 
the people come from different disciplines and backgrounds (Pabst et al., 2020). 
Other than that, there was not much mentioned from other researchers about flexibility. In the interviews, 
most facilitators mentioned the need for flexibility in many ways. Not just about the needs of a team changing, 
but about every team needing to be approached differently, the group size, the topic, etc. as in Figure 7. While 
another mention in the resources mentions that people in innovation teams often come from different 
disciplines, it did not talk about the impact of that. The interviewed facilitators mentioned that that leads to 
very different participants' behaviour and a lot of extra effort needs to be put towards getting the team 
aligned.  Among various ways that flexibility is needed, facilitators also mentioned that the duration of the 
workshop varies a lot, and especially the dynamics within a workshop need a lot of focus and needs the 
facilitator to focus on it a lot and manage it well by being flexible and constantly adjusting their approach.  
 
==PAGE:70==
5  |  DISCUSSION 
70 
5.2.2 
Beginning phase 
Another aspect was mentioned in the literature, which is that the set-up phase of a project is essential 
for effective teamwork and that therefore there should be a focus on this phase from the facilitators side (Pabst 
et al., 2020). As many of the interviewed facilitators do a lot of stand-alone workshops, the beginning of the 
workshop and the beginning of a set of workshops have the same focus points and content. They mentioned 
that this is the most important part of the workshop to set the environment and to make sure the participants’ 
feelings are taken care of as the cluster shows in Figure 9. 
While other authors don’t elaborate on what exactly is essential in this phase, the facilitators had a lot of 
thoughts and input on this. According to them the three main actions and therefore focus points are to get 
the participants aligned, to set rules and expectations and to conduct an icebreaker. This how they 
explained that they achieve the right atmosphere and environment and therefore the beginning is essential. 
There are many different activities that facilitators use, but they all share the same purpose which is usually 
targeting one or more of those three main focuses. For longer processes, some mentioned that the biggest 
focus is mainly at the set-up phase, and less afterwards, which is aligned with what the literature suggests. 
Others follow the practice of focusing on parts of these actions at the beginning of every workshop, as some 
of those activities serve multiple purposes. One mentioned that with a reoccurring team, every workshop 
starts with an icebreaker, but the participants are preparing and conducting it themselves by taking turns. 
5.2.3 
Characteristics of a facilitator 
There has been research done in the past that asked teams that worked with a facilitator and sponsors, that 
hired and worked with the facilitator, what would be the most important actions and characteristics of a 
facilitator. As mentioned above, the most important ones considering the input from both sides were: 
allowing the team to break rules; building in methodology; bringing customer focus early in 
the project; keeping the team focused; encouraging the team to expand boundaries; 
highlighting when the team is off track; bring an understanding of that uncertainty is OK; 
building self-confidence to involve competence outside the company when needed; listening 
to the team (Johnsson, 2018). 
What was lacking from this list, is input from actual facilitators on what they think the most important 
characteristics and actions are that they strive for, focus on, and think someone in their position should have. 
==PAGE:71==
5.3  |  THE IDEAL FEELING OF PARTICIPANTS 
71 
Here the answers were training, coaching, facilitation, networking, consulting, neutrality, 
approachability, confidence, good at improvisation, adaptability and flexibility.  
This shows that participants and sponsors have very different focus points in comparison to the facilitators 
themselves. When going through them, it seems that the things that the facilitator focuses on are more 
fundamental and underlying than the aspects that others find relevant. As an example, a facilitator needs to 
be approachable and confident to be able to keep the team focused and encourage them to expand 
boundaries. Furthermore, a facilitator needs to be good at improvising, adapting and being flexible to be 
able to allow the team to break rules. 
There was one aspect that the teams and facilitators find very important, but the sponsors not so much, which 
is for the facilitator to be very neutral. Some facilitators mentioned that they find it very important and 
foundational for a good workshop. 
5.3 
THE IDEAL FEELING OF PARTICIPANTS  
Another aspect of workshop facilitation that was mentioned in both, the literature and the interviews, was 
how the participants should ideally feel.  
Several authors suggest that throughout the whole process, the responsibility of the facilitator is to manage 
the emotional aspect, such as promoting a positive group experience and motivating the group 
members. One of the focus points should be on boosting trust among the members that participate in 
the workshop. In addition, they suggest that a facilitator should provoke continuous reflection-action-
reflection throughout the workshop (Pabst et al., 2020) (Zhan et al., 2017). 
While the interviewed facilitators also mention that it’s their role to manage the emotional aspect, they 
elaborate far more on what they want their participants to feel and experience, as visualised in Figure 8. The 
phrasing they use is more specific and goes more into detail with examples, therefore it might explain more 
than the other sources mention, as it is unsure what they wanted to express with the phrasings they chose.  
For example, “promoting a positive group experience” can refer to several different things that the 
interviewees want the participant to feel, such as energetic, motivated, curious, excited, joy, and many other 
feelings. The examples of feelings are just some of the groups of things that the interviewees mentioned as 
==PAGE:72==
5  |  DISCUSSION 
72 
what is important to them. With what the interviewees mentioned, such as “not giving too much information” 
and “every idea is welcome”, also connects to what the authors wrote, but it is a lot more action-oriented and 
therefore easier to understand as something that the facilitator can do or does. 
Motivating the group members was something that the authors also mentioned. The motivation of the 
participants is also in the interest of the interviewed facilitators but in a bit of a different way. While the author 
makes it sound like something that the facilitator does to the participants, the interviewees rather expressed 
it as something that is from the participants' side that they need to work with. So instead of motivating the 
participants, find out what makes them feel motivated and try to make their motivation increase over time. 
Boosting trust among the participants is something that the authors also mentioned. This was in some 
way mentioned by most facilitators and there were various ways how they expressed that and what for them 
is the most important part of boosting trust. As mentioned in the section above, they mentioned that they put 
their efforts towards that, especially at the beginning to assure the right atmosphere. The aspects mentioned 
by the interviewees that complement “boosting trust” are the feelings of comfort, confidence, safety and 
openness. While boosting trust is once again a rather broad term, it summarises those 4 feelings mentioned 
by facilitators, which they elaborated a lot further as in Figure 8 which makes it more actionable and easier to 
understand.  
The last aspect to be pointed out in connection to the feelings of the participants is that the authors mentioned 
that a facilitator should provoke continuous reflection- action-reflection. This was not something that 
the interviewees mentioned or focused on. The only related process that some facilitators mentioned was 
when they use LEGOs as tangible objects to make the participants feel more comfortable. While using them, 
they follow the structure of “think, build, share” which means that they first think of the idea or thought that 
they have, then build this with the LEGO bricks and afterwards they share within the group what they built 
and what it stands for. This is a different structure from reflection- action- reflection but it complements each 
other in the way that it first gives time to think or reflect before doing anything further like action, building or 
sharing. This also connects to facilitators wanting participants to have enough time to think and to feel 
comfortable when sharing their input.

5.4 
STRUCTURES  
As Research Question 2.1 is “What structure do facilitators commonly utilise when conducting innovation?”, 
it is relevant to see what literature and what the interviewed facilitators suggest. There are many different 
structures and models out there that people design and suggest following. Section 2.2.2 shows some of 
those, that are discussed and analysed in scientific papers by various authors. No matter which of the 
structures are being mentioned, they all point out certain steps that should be followed with certain activities 
within them. Some of the ones analysed in the literature are 2.2.2.3 Design Thinking, 2.2.2.4 Design Sprint, 
as well as 2.2.2.5 The Double Diamond Model. Some authors also worked on comparing these different 
structures to analyse their similarities and differences (Mendonça de Sá Araújo et al., 2019) . 
The interviewed facilitators on the other hand mentioned flexibility and adjusting it to the needs as the 
most important aspect of the structure of workshops, as visualized in Figure 12. Most of them mentioned that 
they always tailor the workshop based on the team, the project, the needs, and other factors. In addition to 
being flexible and adjusting things in the preparation for each workshop, many also mentioned that it is 
essential to create a backup plan with possible alternative activities. This already shows that the interviewees 
do not agree with the approach of fully following one of the suggested models and structures, as adjustments 
need to happen throughout the flow based on how the participants are acting and reacting inside the 
workshop. 
While flexibility was mentioned as the most important aspect, the facilitators also mentioned that it is 
important to them to know and understand structures that are created and used by other authors. As 
mentioned in Figure 12, most facilitators also use the models mentioned in the literature review to some 
extend. In addition, facilitators mentioned many other sources of inspiration or self-created structures that 
they follow such as some that were listed in Figure 13. Some mentioned that they at times follow these 
structures fully. Most of them explained that they build up a lot of knowledge with many different 
structures and activities, to be later able to select certain elements from these. Some others mentioned 
that they use it as a tool to analyse what stage of the process the team is in and then to be able to take them 
further from there. Knowing these tools allows them to tailor the workshop while making use of activities that 
are well-known and that were tried out by many other authors and people.

INNOVATION WORKSHOPS IN COMPARISON TO OTHER WORKSHOPS 
As several of the quotes of interviewees show in section 4.1.9 Innovation workshops, there is no clear 
alignment from facilitators on what innovation is and how a workshop connected to it looks like. This again 
connects to the high number of different definitions that innovation has. Some definitions of the interviewees 
match part of the definitions within the literature and show connections. Nevertheless, the facilitators pointed 
out that innovation might be noticed as a need along with other needs. As most of them tailor the workshop 
fully to their participants, they do not focus on calling it an innovation workshop, but it is simply a workshop 
that tackles the needs of the team, which might include innovation.

6.1 
FURTHER ACTIONS BASED ON THE FINDINGS 
As The research together shows that most start-ups have not experienced working with a facilitator and with 
workshops before. According to the facilitators, the main reason for this is the lack of resources that these 
younger organisations have available. In addition, according to the entrepreneurs, it would be useful at any 
moment, including situations of decision-making.  
Therefore, something that would be useful for start-ups would need to have the following characteristics: 
- 
Demand little resources  
o Little financial resources 
o Little time to get started and to use it 
o No extra person within the team 
- 
Useful for most moments 
- 
Easy to use 
- 
Bring most of the benefits of a workshop. 
- 
For users that don’t know (much) about facilitation. 
 
While input was gathered from facilitators and entrepreneurs, the outcome should be useful for start-ups but 
also for any other team of any organisation. Therefore, a guide was created to allow teams to go through a 
workshop-like process. It turned the practices and knowledge of facilitators into actionable steps and a 
==PAGE:76==
6  |  CONCLUSION 
76 
framework for teams to utilize. It is written for people who have no knowledge of facilitation or workshops, as 
the survey showed that this is the situation of most of the possible users. Additionally, it took the requirements 
and desires of entrepreneurs and made them into characteristics of the tool.  
 
Two formats were chosen for the guide, this needed to have a low barrier for initial usage and should be easy 
to use. Therefore, the two formats consisted of a pdf document that users can print and use as a paper version, 
as well as a website that allows going through it on the computer or phone without needing to print anything. 
Other formats were also considered but did not match the requirements as well. The document can be viewed 
in Appendix M and the online version is on the website fabilitate.com.  
6.2 
INTERVIEW THEMES AND RESEARCH IN THE GUIDE 
As the practices and knowledge of the facilitators were turned into parts of the guide, below is a clearer 
distribution of what parts of the guide came from which individual themes. 
6.2.1 
Flexibility and adapting 
From the theme “flexibility & adapting” several elements were implemented in the guide.  
The team size is mentioned in the guide, and it is flexible while suggesting splitting everyone into groups 
of less than 10 people. This is based on the number of participants that the facilitators usually mentioned that 
they prefer to work with while considering that there is no facilitator present to manage the dynamics. Therefor 
the number is in the lower range of what facilitators and authors suggest. 
The resources available were mentioned as something that needs flexibility, whereas facilitators 
mentioned time, finances and space. Here the guide does not require financial resources, keeps the 
suggested time for the working session low and gives flexible suggestions regarding the space, therefore it 
keeps the flexibility towards the resources. 
A further aspect mentioned by facilitators was to have a backup plan. This is implemented to some extent 
in the guide, as it suggests the teams to choose other activities based on what they consider suitable for 
==PAGE:77==
6.2  |  INTERVIEW THEMES AND RESEARCH IN THE GUIDE 
77 
themselves. It gives the user further resources on where to find other activities that can function as a backup 
plan. 
Additionally, facilitators mentioned that it requires flexibility when sharing enough information but not 
too much with the participants to keep the excitement high, which is one of the ideal feelings of participants. 
This was adapted by only giving very limited information to the participants at the beginning of the guide in 
the form of a general agenda. Other than that, also the initiator should not know the activities that will happen 
and should therefore be curious about what comes next in the working session. (This is only possible for the 
first time following the guide for each of the sections, as afterwards they will need to prepare some activities 
themselves. 
Something else that was represented in the flexibility theme but had many more mentions in the interviews 
connected to it, is the need to conduct a needs assessment. This is according to the facilitators essential, 
because users often diagnose their problems wrong, don’t really know what it is and therefore might request 
a wrong workshop. As this is often a deep analysis by the facilitator in conversation with the sponsor, there 
needed to be a similar tool for the initiator of the working session.  
The initial request of the client is represented in the guide as the initiator writing down the reason why they 
want a working session. Next, they are asked to rephrase this reason into a problem statement or a 
situation/ fact, to be able to analyse it after. Then the needs assessment and the root cause analysis which 
usually happens by the facilitators asking many questions, is taking place by the initiator alone while doing 
a 5 WHY analysis. This analysis is an approach that makes them get to the root cause of the problem by 
asking up to 5 times, “Why is this the situation/ problem”. Afterwards, the person going through this process 
should have a better understanding of what their actual problem is and should therefore phrase the final 
goal of the working session. Afterwards, the initiator should see what part(s) of the Double Diamond 
model the goal of the workshop aligns the most with, by reading a description of each section. This will 
determine what working session structure they will follow and what activities they will conduct.  
6.2.1 
Beginning of a workshop 
The beginning of the workshop was mentioned by the facilitators to be the most important to set the right 
atmosphere and enable the desired feelings from the participants. Facilitators mentioned that the beginning 
==PAGE:78==
6  |  CONCLUSION 
78 
is always important, which is why it is in all the working sessions, no matter what the topic is and what 
structure is used. 
Starting with setting rules and expectations at the beginning of a workshop was expressed to be very 
important. They are used to set the atmosphere and draw a picture of what it looks like. As in a workshop, the 
facilitator can interfere and assure that the participants feel a certain way, which is not the case in a self-guided 
working session. This increases the importance of this section as that way the users can hold each other 
accountable for it. Some of the rules that were mentioned as most important by facilitators and that applied 
to a working session are suggested in the guide. There is additional space or the users to come up with their 
own rules and expectations to be fully aligned with the expected behaviour.  
Another activity mentioned is an icebreaker was one of the outcomes of the interviews, the reasons to have 
an icebreaker from the facilitators were included in the guide to let the users of the guide understand the 
importance of it. In addition, there are some examples of icebreakers, which were mentioned as examples in 
the interviews. One facilitator also mentioned that they let the participants themselves come up with them 
and conduct them, which was also included in the guide as an option for the users.  
The last aspect that was mentioned is the alignment among participants. From these aspects based on 
the interviews, three sharing rounds seem possible without a facilitator, as well as impactful and easy to 
follow. These are “Your general thoughts on the topic.”, “Your own experience connected to the topic.” And 
“Your personal goal for the working session.”.  
 
6.2.2 
Ideal feelings of participants 
While most of the work towards the ideal feelings of participants is done at the beginning of the 
workshop and with the activities there, there are still further points included in the guide based on the 
facilitators' knowledge.  
The aspects already tackled are for the users to feel joyful, which is introduced by the icebreaker, and for 
them to feel excited by ideally being in an unusual environment and by not knowing the activities 
that will happen. In addition, they should feel motivated, due to reflecting on and sharing the personal 
gain that they would like to have from the working session.

79 
One feeling and action connected to it was energetic and motivated, where the interviewees mentioned 
that action should be taken afterwards. This led to all the working sessions having a common final 
activity, which consists of defining the next actions that will happen and assigning a responsible person and 
a deadline for it.  
6.2.1 
Equal participation 
Regarding the theme of equal participation, it was only partially possible to transfer the practices to the 
working session guide. 
Many interviewees mentioned the usage of silent exercises to be a great tool to assure equal participation. 
Therefore, all the different working session flows include some silent activities to assure that. 
Another thing that was mentioned is that everyone should have the same opportunities. A way that 
this is included in the guide is by mentioning that everyone should be presenting and that everyone has the 
same amount of time for the activities and to present their ideas and input.  
Finally, as everyone should participate equally and usually the facilitator can guide the discussions in a 
way to encourage that, in the guide there is a rule that mentions specifically that “Everyone must be involved 
in the same way”, which should also have an impact on the dynamics and users trying to achieve that. 
6.2.1 
Workshop structure 
Regarding the theme of the workshop structure that was concluded from the interviews, there were some 
of the statements translated into the guide. 
Three of the main structures used, were the Double Diamond, Design Thinking and Design sprint. Some 
sources connect and compare the structure of the Double Diamond and the Design Thinking process, and 
they are mentioned to be rather easy to comprehend. In addition, some facilitators mentioned that the teams 
they work with had an easy time understanding the Double Diamond and that the terminology of it is 
something that many team members adapted, which they expressed as very useful. Therefore, the Double 
Diamond is used as the main model to guide the users through a workshop and for them to understand what 
==PAGE:80==
6  |  CONCLUSION 
80 
part their needs and goal fall under. It includes one workshop flow proposed for each of the four parts of the 
Double Diamond that can be followed step by step.  
On top of that, one of the resources mentioned in the guide for teams to choose their activities is based on 
Design Thinking and explains it by building up on the understanding of the Double Diamond. Therefore, 
some visuals make it easier for the user to understand it.  
Flexibility is needed when designing a workshop as well as a working session. Nevertheless, the threshold 
to use the guide should be very low and the effort needed from first-time users and initiators should be low. 
To maintain flexibility and to also maintain a low barrier to start, there is one working session structure, 
and the users are encouraged to design further ones. The theme “Why initiate a workshop” shows one of 
the comments mentioned by the facilitators that clients go for this way of working because of being “aware 
of the benefits of a facilitator” or this way of working. This highly influenced the creation of the guide, as 
it includes a structure that the users can follow. Afterwards, the user should be aware of the benefits of a 
working session and therefore be willing to put more effort into creating a working session afterwards and 
looking for activities that can be useful. 
 
6.3 
WHAT IS MISSING FROM THE GUIDE 
As mentioned beforehand, the guide tries to take the knowledge, skills and practices of facilitators and turns 
them into tasks and practices for users. Much of the above-mentioned knowledge was transformed and used 
in the guide, but there are yet some things that were not able to be part of it. 
The first theme taken from the interviews was about the skills and ideal behaviour of a facilitator. This was 
not possible to be implemented in the guide, as there should be nobody acting as a facilitator. Therefore, the 
versatility of activities like training, coaching, and consulting, among others, cannot be fulfilled by this guide. 
An additional aspect of the skills and ideal behaviour of a facilitator that is a theme on its own is flexibility 
and adapting. This was implemented to some extent in the guide by giving some information but not all 
and by being flexible with certain aspects like team size. While the flexibility was met to some extent, 
==PAGE:81==

adapting cannot be covered as it is an action that a facilitator does frequently over time. It is a way of reacting 
to things they observe and then adjusting to them.  
The theme of equal participation includes a lot of aspects that are connected to facilitating poor 
behaviour. Here interviewees mentioned different ways of dealing with and reacting to participants' 
behaviour depending on how severe it is. In case one approach does not work, they would try different ones. 
This is something that connects again to adjusting in the moment and is a soft skill that cannot be 
communicated and sensed in the same way in a guide. The rules that are mentioned in the guide do suggest 
certain ways of acting, but there is no way to assure that it is being followed.

IMPROVEMENT AND FEEDBACK 
After the guide was created, feedback was gathered from five of the interviewed facilitators and their 
improvement points were implemented. After these changes, general outreach to find sample users was 
conducted, with more than 30 responses from individuals who wanted to have access to the guide to be able 
to share it with their colleagues and utilize it themselves. Two people provided feedback without having used 
it, which was also implemented. They were all asked to provide feedback in case they would work with it soon.

6.5 
CONCLUDING WORDS 
This thesis project was partially motivated by the possibility to analyse the underlying workshop structures 
that facilitators use when conducting innovation processes for start-ups and similar teams. For that purpose, 
we intended to create a model that would combine those findings into an overarching structure. 
 
To analyse these processes, after reviewing the available literature on the topic, a survey for entrepreneurs 
and an interview guide for facilitators were created. Several interviews were conducted following the guide 
with experienced facilitators in diverse industries and answers were collected from the survey. 
 
==PAGE:82==
6  |  CONCLUSION 
82 
The data was analysed following qualitative methods, leading to the obtention of several insights. These 
insights led us to gain a better understanding of the needs of entrepreneurs and the working methods of 
facilitators and led us in the direction of adjusting the scope of the thesis: an overarching model would not 
be suitable due to the need for flexibility and other structures mentioned by the facilitators.   
 
Instead, the most useful tool to aid in the facilitation of innovation processes in start-ups seemed to be a 
guide that would lead teams through a workshop-like experience in order to get most of the benefits of 
facilitated processes but without the burden of the resources they require. 
 
The guide received feedback and comments from several facilitators and raised a high interest. Many 
people have contacted me to get the guide and start using it.

7 RECOMMENDATION 
Further work towards improving the guide should be conducted. While feedback from facilitators and users 
of the guide was already gathered and implemented, this should happen continuously as users go back to 
the guide multiple times and work with it. Therefore, a feedback system and loop should be put into place. A 
further development idea for the guide is to extend the number of working sessions fully listed and designed. 
Therefore, starting by having different versions of fully explained working sessions for each of the four parts 
of the Double Diamond. After having different prewritten flows, the guide could be further expanded by 
creating additional activities that are for the specific needs of start-ups and other teams and outside the 
Double Diamond structure, such as a team formation working session or a team building working session. As 
these were some that were mentioned by entrepreneurs as something they would like support with. The 
selection of what to focus on should be re-evaluated based on the users' input. Another way how the guide 
can be expanded is by diversifying the way the information is displayed. Therefore, besides only using text 
and symbols, the activities can also be explained in a video format, with a voice recording or in the form of 
game cards. A final idea that would change the way the guide is used and its functions, would be to base it 
on technology and include some kind of software that would automate and personalise the working session 
outline. This would completely change the way it is presented and used and could potentially even bring 
many of the aspects that are missing now, such as flexibility. Nevertheless, it is also the approach that brings 
the most uncertainty with it and first needs to be further analysed if it is feasible or not.  
Beyond the guide itself, there are further steps that should be taken towards improving some of the problems 
pointed out throughout the research. A reoccurring theme was the lack of resources for start-ups to be able to 
hire a facilitator and therefore work with one to speed up progress and improve their work. Some start-up 
facilities like accelerators already provide these resources to some extent and already offer teams access to 
facilitators. This is something that should be fostered and should become more frequent and common. 
Another aspect that is noticeable in the answers to the survey by entrepreneurs is the lack of understanding 
of what facilitation is and what a facilitator does. As people do not know this, they perhaps also do not see or 
imagine the benefit of working with one. Therefore, more effort should be done to increase this awareness. A 
great example of that is DTU, by making university courses where students learn how to facilitate, such as

This guide is meant as a tool to help teams conduct a high-quality working session 
with limited resources available and is written for people with no facilitation 
knowledge or experience. It is based on the experience and knowledge of 21 
different facilitators and making their knowledge accessible with low entry barriers 
for teams to be guided through a similar experience. The feedback from 5 of these 
facilitators was implemented after the first draft of this guide. Inviting a facilitator 
will likely lead to higher-quality outcomes, as a written guide can not convey the 
skillset and actions of a professional. Therefore this guide is meant for organisations 
like start-ups or teams that do not have access to these resources. This guide 
enables teams to work differently and for them to see if this other way of working 
suits them.  
While there are more details below, this guide relies on one person taking the lead 
and initiating this working session. It is the person that sees a need for it to happen 
and the person must be willing to dedicate some time and effort into organising it. 
The parts that the initiator needs to go through beforehand are the Intro and

Preparation  
Intro 
This tool was created based on interviews with 21 different experienced facilitators, as well as a 
systematic literature review on the topic. Its purpose is to implement the practices, actions and 
activities usually conducted by a facilitator and adjust them by turning them all into a self-guided 
process.  
Neither the initiator nor any of the participants should ideally go through the exercises before the 
working session unless someone needs to design them or familiarize themselves with them. This is 
suggested to not reveal them beforehand and let them be new to everyone to keep the excitement 
and curiosity. This is only possible when using this guide for the first time.  
This guide is also designed in a way that it has very low entry barriers, this means that when you 
use it for the first time, there is very little work for you to be done to prepare the working session. If 
you after trying it, consider this way of working useful for you and your team, you then will need to 
put some more effort into the preparation of the working session, which will be further explained 
in another section below. 
 
Amount of people: For any working session it is recommended to be less than 10 people. With a 
bigger group, it can also be split to reach this group size.  
Duration: The team should estimate 1-2 hours for the working session. 
Location: Ideally the place where the working session happens is a very new and unusual 
environment, to empower new and usual ideas or ways of thinking. If it is a familiar space, the 
team should move things around to make it different from the usual. The layout needs to allow 
everyone to see the main wall that will be used to hang materials and everyone needs to be able to 
see each other.  
Materials: Make sure to bring sticky notes, pens, and paper. Flipcharts and tape can also be 
useful. In case you meet online, use a digital version of these, such as Miro, which also includes 
templates and further functions. 
Timer: Make sure to have a timer for the activities that have a time limit.

Rules and expectations setting 
This section is for the whole team to go through together at the beginning of the working session. 
As you are about to start the working session, it is important to agree on a set of ground rules and 
behaviours towards one another. This allows everyone to feel the most comfortable and assures 
alignment on how to behave to enable creativity.  
There is a list of expectations that are based on other workshops and working sessions that were 
considered very important. Please go through them and if your team considers them as relevant, 
take them into account. Afterwards, any team member can express further expectations and the 
rest of the team can share their thoughts on those.  
- 
Only one person speaks at a time. 
- 
Whatever is said here, stays here. 
- 
We embrace being open to feedback. 
- 
We want to involve everyone in the same way. 
- 
There are no spectators allowed in the room. 
- 
Always ask for clarifications if needed. 
- 
Always ask curious questions. 
- 
If someone is tired, propose an energizer. 
- 
Full attention to the workshop, e.g. not using phones or laptops. 
- 
Everyone is dedicated to the outcomes and will put in the needed work afterwards.

Ice breaker 
This section is for the whole team to go through together at the beginning of the working session 
after setting the expectations. 
The icebreaker is here to start the working session for various reasons. Some are, to assure that 
everyone feels yet more comfortable with each other, to become present, to build rapport, to warm 
up the brain, and to leave other tasks and responsibilities behind. They are meant to be fun and to 
expand your comfort zone together while having fun. 
 
Before choosing an icebreaker, one activity that should be part of every working session is a 
sharing round on how everyone is feeling right now. After having shared, you can continue to the 
next icebreaker.  
You can choose one of the activities below, come up with one on your own or look for one on the 
internet. In case you have the time and see it suitable, you can also do more than one icebreaker. 
1) Breakfast drawing: Everyone takes sticky notes and draws the breakfast that they had on 
it. [1min] Afterwards everyone will have max 30 seconds to present it to the rest of the 
group. 
 
2) Bucket list: Everyone makes a little drawing of something that they have on their Bucket 
list (something they want to experience before they die) [1min]. Afterwards, everyone will 
have max 30 seconds to present it to the rest of the group. 
 
3) Favourite Food: Everyone makes a drawing of what their favourite food is [1min]. 
Afterwards, everyone will have max 30 seconds to present it to the rest of the group. 
 
4) Bad Joke Contest: Everyone thinks of a joke that they think is quite bad but also funny. 
They note it down on a sticky note [1min]. Afterwards, they all present/ tell their joke to 
each other [30sec each].

Reverse Brainstorming: 
o To start off, everyone takes sticky notes and pens.  
o Then think about the topic/ problem/ scenario that you have as a focus and look 
at it from a different way and think of what would make the problem even 
worse. 
(e.g. problem: we don’t have any drinking water left à what would make it 
worse: putting salt in everyone’s mouth) 
o Write each idea on a sticky note. [7min] 
 
If there is more than one problem that was defined; do one round for each of 
them.  
 
- 
Rapid Brainstorming:  
o Everyone takes sticky notes and writes down as many ideas as possible about 
things that are connected to the problem/ topic.  
o Focus on quantity, not quality. [5min] 
 
If there is more than one problem that was defined; do one round for each of 
them.  
 
- 
Presentation: 
o Everyone presents and shortly explains their ideas and adds the sticky notes on 
a wall. 
 
- 
Clustering:  
o Everyone goes to the wall that has the sticky notes on it and starts to cluster 
them by which ones are similar and connected. 
o This happens without talking in full silence. 
o It is okay to move sticky notes that others already moved, in case you don’t 
agree with the placement. 
 
o After around 80% of the ideas are placed, conversations and discussions about 
the clustering can happen and the last adjustments are done throughout the 
discussion.

Starting point: Clustered ideas of what the problem is.

Naming the clusters:

Define next actions 
This section is for the whole team after the last working session activity. 
A working session is only as impactful as the actions that are taken from it afterwards. Now that 
hopefully the goal of the working session was achieved, it is the team’s responsibility to carry on its 
outcomes, implement it and make use of it. Therefore, define together the next actions that you 
will take, including deadlines and the responsible person for those. This can also include 
conducting a further working session if this is within the scope. Make sure to define the tasks with 
enough detail to assure that they can be completed and easily understood some days or weeks 
after the working session. Even if it can then be understood later on, try to prioritise these tasks, as 
there was a reason to work on this working session and it is an important topic for the team.
