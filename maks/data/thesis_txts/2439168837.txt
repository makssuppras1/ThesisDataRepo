Abstract
There is an ever demanding need for new and better therapies for the health ailments faced by the global population.
These therapies may be administered by several routes amongst which the oral route is most preferred by patients
on account of it being painless and easy to self administer.
In addition, oral therapies are cheap and easy to
manufacture which make them more readily available to undeveloped and developing part of the world.
Most orally administered drugs however face the challenge of poor bioavailability which in turn is representative
of the poor pharmacodynamic response the drug will elicit in the system. The diﬃculty in ﬁnding new drug targets
along with gross reduction in the number of new small molecules has led the pharmaceutical industry to take
alternative avenues to oﬀer better solutions to already existing therapies. Oral bioavailabilty is dependent on a host
of factors, primary among them are the absorption and permeability of the drug. The solubility and permeability
of these drugs may be improved in many ways. Micro and nano therapies oﬀered by size reduction have gained a
lot of interest in recent years due to their ability to combat the problems faced due to poor solubility by oﬀering
targeted and controlled drug release.
In the current work, the fabrication of a new drug delivery system is explored to improve the poor solubility
of a Class 2 drug as deﬁned by the biopharmaceuticals classiﬁcation system. It comprises of a polymeric matrix
with the drug evenly dispersed in it.The DDS is fabricated in various shapes to investigate the role that shape
of a DDS plays in the drug release and its subsequent absorption upon ingestion. The DDS is fabricated by hot
punching of polymeric ﬁlms to obtain planar microparticles in circular, triangular, hexagonal and square shapes.
This method of fabrication oﬀers control over the dimensions and geometry of the fabricated microparticle and in
turn the amount of drug loaded into each of these particle to deﬁne an oral recommended dose.
During this master thesis project, the fabrication method of this novel DDS was developed and optimised.
Furthermore, the DDS obtained was characterised and then analysed in vitro to assess the drug release proﬁle. The
DDS was further analysed in vivo to assess the pharmacokinetic response and bioavailability of the drug enclosed
in the polymeric matrix of the DDS.

1.1
Oral drug delivery and its challenges
A drug must reach the target molecular site in order to interact with it and elicit a pharmaceutical response.
Several routes of administration exist for the delivery of a drug to its target including intravenous, intramuscular,
transdermal, oral, topical, nasal etc. Amongst these, per oral (PO) administration is the most commonly used route
due to the ease of administration and patient compliance oﬀered by it.[4] In addition to be being non-invasive hence
painless, PO administration oﬀers a very desirable option of self administration for frequent doses.[5] This makes
PO administration highly convenient unlike other methods which require a health care professional to administer
the drug.
PO formulations besides being favourable to the patients are also cheap and easier to manufacture as the
pharmaceutical processes for solid dry formulations do not require a sterile environment and have been established
for several years now.
Once administered, the drug must enter the systemic circulation to reach its target site. However, depending
on the route chosen, the drug may encounter several barriers which eﬀect the amount of drug absorbed and in turn
its bioavailability.[4] Orally administered must travel through the buccal cavity and the esophagus to be absorbed
in the Gastro Intestinal (GI) tract.
Figure 1: The ﬁgure represents the GI tract and the path taken by an PO administered drug.
The buccal cavity and the esophagus have very little eﬀect on the oral drug besides the onset of disintegration
by swelling in case of solid formulations. The stomach however comprises of a highly acidic environment with a
pH between 1.0 -1.8. The drug must be able to withstand this harsh pH in order to be absorbed in the intestines.

Oral drug formulations usually comprise of an enteric coating protecting them from this harsh environment. The
contents of the stomach are emptied into the small intestine. This gastric emptying is highly regulated and varies
depending on whether fasting or not. Thus, the time taken for a to reach the intestine and in turn be absorbed is
less predictable when not fasting.
The small intestine may be divided into the duodenum, jejunum and the ileum. The walls of intestine comprise
of a mucosal membrane which has a large water component. On close inspection, the membrane is made of villi
which facilitates drug absorption. The drug is primarily absorbed in the in the jejunum in the intestine owing to the
longer shape of the villi in this region. This region of the intestine bears a slightly basic pH of 7.6. The villi surface
is further made of microvilli as shown in ﬁg. 2. These microvilli although forming a barrier comprise of pores that
water can permeate through. The drugs are as a result absorbed by passive diﬀusion through the microvilli in this
region. The unabsorbed drug moves past the ileum into the large intestine where small amounts of drug is further
absorbed, the remnant being excreted via faeces.[6]
Figure 2: The ﬁgure is a representation of villi that form the mucosal layer of the intestine. The surface of the villi is further
covered with microvilli.[6]
The drug absorbed from the intestine is led to the liver via the hepatic portal vein. The liver further provides
a barrier where the drug is often removed and or metabolised.
The amount of drug therefore ﬁnally entering
the systemic circulation is a fraction of the absorbed drug.
PO administered drugs as a result often face the
challenge of low bioavailability. An estimate of the bioavailability exhibited by a PO drug is often described by
the Biopharmaceuticals Classiﬁcation System (BCS). The BCS classiﬁes molecules based on their solubility and
intestinal permeability as shown in ﬁg. 3[7][6].
Drugs exhibiting high solubility and permeability are highly absorbed with an absorption of ∼90 %. However,
∼70% of existing oral drugs fall under BCS class II of drugs. The rate limiting step for these absorption of these
drugs is most often the drug release from their dosage form and solubility in the intestinal pH. Several methods have
been suggested to increase the solubility of these drugs including, cosolvency, addition of surfactants, salt formation

1.2
Drug delivery systems
Microparticulate systems as drug delivery systems(DDS) oﬀer a promising solution to improve the poor solubility of
PO drugs. This is provided by localised and controlled release along with their greater dispersion at the absorption
site.[8] Microparticulate systems may be classiﬁed as solid particles or liquid droplets possessing a diameter of 0.1
to 200 µm entrapped, dissolved or encapsulated in a polymeric thin ﬁlm of variable thickness. The method chosen

1.2.1
Eﬀect of shape of DDS
DDS are constantly being optimised to constantly improve targeting and provide for enhanced drug eﬀects. Size
and surface charge of micro and nano DDS systems has been studied in great detail. Size of the DDS plays a
determinant role in the retention and permeability of a particle across membranes. Most designed micro and nano
particle systems however are spherical in shape.[11] Particle shape is an inherent feature of the particle and it
therefore has been described to play a major role in its interaction with the membrane it is intended for. Studies
have reported improved targeting with DDS that are not spherical. Diﬀerently shaped microparticles with ﬂat
surfaces have shown better adhesion to blood vessels.[12] The beneﬁts of modifying particulate shape may be dual
since, 3-D printed polymeric tablets have shown to have diﬀerent drug release proﬁles depending on the tablet
shape. The shape is believed to eﬀect the erosion of the polymer thereby aﬀecting drug released. [13]
Particularly to PO DDS, Desai et.
al have explored micro-systems called micropatches that have a planar
geometry which have shown greater cytoadhesion than traditional spherical systems. This is because the surface
area contact with a ﬂat planar device is much greater in comparison with a traditional spherical DDS as represented

1.2.2
Methods of preparation for DDS
Traditional methods for perpetration of microparticles as described earlier mass produce DDS typically in a spherical
shape. Although the particles are spherical, the exact geometry is found hard to control. This in turn implies that
the exact dimensions, drug distribution and encapsulation is diﬃcult to be determined with these DDS. The lack
of knowledge as to exact amount of drug in the formulation make the so prepared DDS less than ideal. [15]
This has led to the emergence of the use Microelectromechanical systems(MEMS) in fabrication of DDS.These
techniques allow for control over the size and dimensions of the resultant fabricated device.[16] MEMS fabrication
technique typically comprise of unit process steps that are repeatedly implemented to obtain the same output. They
usually include thin ﬁlms deposition followed by modiﬁcation of the given ﬁlm by processes like photolithography

localised drug delivery and bioadhesive microsystems for PO delivery.[16][17]
Typical techniques for the fabrication of such microstructures use either replication techniques, rapid prototyping

1.3
Overiew of Proposed DDS
The current work proposes the fabrication of a novel biodegradable DDS to improve solubility of orally administered
drugs. The DDS is fabricated as planar microparticles of various shapes namely, circle, hexagon, square and triangle.
The DDS oﬀers the possibility to obtain targeted, controlled and uni-directional drug release. A microparticle so
designed contains a drug evenly dispersed in a polymeric matrix, not very diﬀerent from the traditional spherical
microparticulate system. The proposed microparticles however oﬀer the advantage of control over the geometry, size
and drug loading in comparison to traditional spherical microparticles.The planar shape aids in the mucoadhesion
of the particles to the intestinal mucosa. The microparticles are thus expected to interact with the intestinal mucosa
and deliver the given load by diﬀusion through the polymeric matrix on encountering intestinal environment. This
delivery is expected to be controlled by the rate of the swelling of the polymeric matrix by introduction of water.
Additionally, the shape, the size and the surface charge along with the number of surfaces the drug is released from
is expected to aﬀect the drug release from the microparticles.
The fabrication of the proposed system is done by Hot Punching. This involves standard steps of the Hot
Embossing process of depositing thin ﬁlms on a surface followed by modiﬁcation of this surface into desired mi-
crostructures with the use of a stamp.The microstructures thus obtained are connected to each other by a residual
ﬁlm layer and bear a design complementary to that of stamp used to mold them. To ensure the exact speciﬁcations
of the mirostructures, the Hot embossing process is combined with the Mechanical Punching process which cuts
all the way through the residual layer to produce microstructures with the exact shape and dimensions of the the
stamp well used to punch them. This is possible by the addition of a release elastic layer that oﬀers support for
the punching to take place by shear stress. Thus, Hot punching is performed on a substrate deposited with a
release layer followed by a layer containing the drug dispersed evenly in a polymeric matrix followed by an optional
polymeric layer which is added in case uni directional release is desired. These ﬁlms thus form a multilayer system
that make the Hot punching possible. This is described by ﬁg. 6

Hypothesis
The current work hypothesises that novel planar DDS can be fabricated by the hot punching process described by
Ritika et al. The hence fabricated DDS deliver the drug load diﬀerently owing to their shape.

The aim of the conducted master thesis was to fabricate biodegradable microparticles of various shapes and sizes to

3.1
The Carrier Substrate
A rigid carrier substrate was desired where the adhesive Release layer and the drug polymer layer may be coated
in ﬁlms to form the multi-layer system desired for hot punching of the microparticles.
Silicon (Si) wafers were thus chosen as carrier substrates throughout the study due to their rigid crystalline
structure that allows for uniform material deposition, easy dicing post processing and easier imaging. 4-inch single
side polished Si wafers were thus used.
3.2
The Release layer
Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) was chosen to serve as the release layer due to it’s unique viscoelastic, ﬂow properties
and optical transparency down to 240nm.[19] In addition,it is cheap and well characterised for use in micro-ﬂudic
systems along with being non toxic, inert and bio-comaptible. However, it is not biodegradable which makes it an
undesirable candidate in DDS.[20]
Nonetheless, in the conducted study PDMS only serves as a deformable, elastic layer to provide a cushion
for successful hot punching and a temporary surface to mould the microparticles thus never becoming part of
the actual DDS. However the surface properties play a key intrinsic role in fabrication processes.
The surface
of PDMS is inherently hydrophobic. PDMS solution was prepared by mixing PDMS silicone elastomer (Sylgard
LOT 0008557276) with cross-linking Silicone elastomer Curing agent (Sylgard LOT 0008898443) in a 10:1 ratio.
Following mixing, the prepared materials were subjected to vacuum to ensure removal of bubbles which would lead
to formation of uneven surface when the solution is spin coated on the Si wafers.
3.3

The drug polymer layer comprises of an API embedded in a polymer matrix which is deposited in the form of a
thin ﬁlm over the release layer.

4.1.1
Spincoating
Spincoating is a commonly employed technique to deposit thin ﬁlms via solvent evaporation onto substrates. It uses
the centrifugal forces generated when rotating a substrate at high speed to evenly coat the given substrate with the
desired solution. It is widely applicable in the manufacture of integrated circuits, magnetic diodes, devices for solar
cells etc. due to it’s low operating cost and small run times.[27]
On rotating a substrate with a solution on it’s surface, the centrifugal forces drive the solution radially outwards
as the velocity of rotation increases. Once the set rotation velocity is reached, continued rotation at this velocity
cause the radially dispersed ﬂuid over the surface to thin evenly due to the outﬂow forming a coat whose thickness
is dependent on the surface tension and viscosity of the solution.[28] After the thinning of the surface, the rotation
further causes the evaporation of the solvent take place which leads to the formation of an evenly deposited thin
ﬁlm as described by ﬁg. 9. The thickness of the ﬁlm may be varied easily by varying the velocity of rotation, time
of rotation and the viscosity of solution.[27][29]

4.1.5
Hot punching
Hot embossing is an established fabrication technique that exploits the reversible transition of a material from
glassy to rubbery state at the glass transition temperature (ﬁg. 15) of the material (Tg). The softened material is
molded at will by the use of a stamp. The material thus attains a structure that is opposite to that of the stamp
when it is hardened by a decrease in temperature. Hot embossing is usually run at a temperature well over the
Tg and below the melting temperature (Tm).[39] A process temperature much greater than the Tg is used as the
softened material will behave like a viscous ﬂuid at said temperature and ﬂow easily into the mold structure to
attain the opposing shape. Embossing at high temperatures however may cause problems of demolding the stamp
from the adjoining structure.[17]
Figure 12: Representation of the Hot Embossing process with a mold to inversely shaped structures. The residual layer left
behind at the end of the process is described as the green thin layer interconnecting the microstructures.[20]
The demolded structures contain a residual layer surrounding them which makes it diﬃcult to achieve individual
demolded structure of speciﬁc dimensions as shown in ﬁg. 12. As a result the conception of hot Punching as a
fabrication technique took place.It combines the already established technique of hot embossing with mechanical
punching.[40] Mechanical punching is a technique used to create holes in metal sheets. This is done by deforming
the surface of the metal to rupture it and cut out a hole due to the high shear stress experienced by the metal sheet.
The cut-out piece is ejected with a plunger. [41]
Thus, during hot punching unlike hot embossing, the residual layer surrounding the microstructures is ruptured

4.2.1
Diﬀerential Scanning Calorimetery
Diﬀerential Scanning calorimetery (DSC) is a commonly employed technique to study the thermal properties of a
given material. It measures the changes in the heat ﬂow that occur to sample upon varying the temperature at a
ﬁxed rate. Thus, a measure of the energy changes in comparison with a reference is recorded which signiﬁes the
transitions that take place in the material and the temperatures that these transitions take place at.
It is thus often used to determine the Tg for polymer matrices. The Tg may be deﬁned as the temperature at
which a single molecule of a given substance has suﬃcient energy to move independently of the underlying structure
of the substance. It is thus a measure of the temperature at which the particular material moves from the glassy
state to the rubbery state where it able to ﬂow as shown in ﬁg. 15[42].

passing through a material is directly proportional to the path travelled through the material and the concentration

4.3
In vivo methods
Post studying the drug release of the micro-particles in vitro, an in vivo study was performed to observe their
behaviour in vivo. The in vivo study design comprised of 4 groups of rats, containing 4 rats each. Each group
of rats would receive a formulation with microparticles of a particular shape namely: circle, hexagon, square and
triangle. The formulations would be securely placed in eppendorf tubes assigned a number from 1 -16 in a carefully
noted random order. The formulations would thereafter be adminstered in the rats without any knowledge regarding
its contents by the collaborator performing the administration method.
This section discusses the methods used for the preparation of formulations, their administration and charac-
terisation methods used to study their behaviour.
4.3.1
Preparation of formulations
Rat formulations were prepared by using the materials mentioned in section 3.5. The method designed for the
preparation of rat formulations was a modiﬁcation of the method described by C. Mazzoni et al. for formulations
with micro-containers.[48] The standard method involves removal of the lid of the capsule followed by securely
holding the opened capsule to be ﬁlled in a hold designed for the shape and size of the capsule. A funnel is then
placed over the capsule hold and the harvested microcontainers are nudged gently through the funnel into the
capsule with the help of a piston. Once ﬁlled, the lid of the capsule is placed back on until a lock is felt. The
capsule is weighed using a micro-balance prior and post ﬁlling to know the exact weight of the formulation.
The fabricated microparticles bore a negative charge which made them attracted to the metal surfaces of the
funnel and piston as described in greater detail in section 9.1. To prevent the particles from sticking to the metal
surface, the particles on the substrate were subjected to an Oxygen plasma.

4.3.3
Reverse Phase High Performance Liquid Chromatography
Reverse Phase - High Performance liquid Chromatography (RP-HPLC) is a very eﬃcient analytical technique
used to identify, quantify and purify individual solutes from a given sample. The RP column is packed with an
immobilised stationary phase made up silica beads with hydrophobic ligands on their surface. The separation of
the individual components by the column is thus dependent on the hydrophobicity of the given components. The
solutes to be analysed are introduced in a column containing mobile aqueous phase. This ensures that solutes that
are even slightly hydrophobic bind to the stationary phase. The column is then ﬂushed with increasing amounts of
organic solvent to elute out the temporarily bound solutes. The solutes eluted out of the column in an order with
the least hydrophobic being eluted ﬁrst to the most being eluted last.[49][50]
The HPLC is connected with a UV detector which can detect absorbance for wavelengths lying in the range of
190 - 400 nm. [28]The concentrations of the eluted molecules from the column are quantiﬁed by their UV absorbance
in accordance with Beer-Lambert’s Law as previously described as equation 1 in section 4.2.4. [47]

Mechanism of Drug release
As mentioned previously in section 1.2, the drug release from a polymeric matrix may be by diﬀusion through the

that the mechanism for drug release is diﬀusion through the polymeric matrix. To conﬁrm the mechanism of drug

that the mechanism for drug release from the matrix was solely by diﬀusion.

Future perspectives
Shape is an intrinsic feature of any particle as much as the size and surface properties. The shape of an object
deﬁnes the volume and surface area it possess and thus it is the next avenue in micro and nanotechnology to explore
and control the shape of particles to reap the beneﬁts it has to oﬀer in delivering drug at the target site.
The current conducted study provides some compelling data that suggest the variation in shape eﬀect the
amount of drug released from a microparticle and must be explored further to completely understand the role the
shape of a microparticle plays in DDS.
It is seen that the particles of various shapes are possible to fabricate using the hot punching technique suggested
by R.Petersen et al. However, fabrication processes often need a lot of optimisation to derive a desired output and
high yield. During the current study, several limitations were encountered due to time constraints and failure of
equipment both for the spraycoater and hot embosser. As a result, the optimisation of these processes could not be
explored to its full potential. The study has only been able to show that it is possible to achieve diﬀerently shaped
microparticles with drug dispersed in the polymeric matrix. To actually apply the methods discussed here, it is
necessary that the yield of microparticles be improved as the time taken for the fabrication of micropaticles is too

The drug release from all the particles followed a similar pattern, an initial rapid release followed by a more
controlled release over an extended period of time. The initial fast release corroborated with a slower with the drug
being release by diﬀusion form the surface of the microparticle and the latter slower release with the swelling of the
polymer matrix to release the remaining drug by diﬀusion. It was seen that most of the drug release took place in
the initial 3 hours followed by very slight changes over the next 7 hours. The drug release studies were performed
quickly over a short period of time due to the equipment being out of use at the designated time in the master
thesis project.
The in vivo studies revealed that drug was absorbed from each shape of microparticles diﬀerently. The drug
absorption proﬁles were similar for all microparticles with an initial increase in plasma concentration followed by a
reduction. This showed that the the drug was absorbed in the ﬁrst two hours after administration. This absorption
however varied with the particle shape. This was believed to be due to their diﬀerent mucoadhesion properties.
However, the mucoadhesion was similar for all particles bearing sharp edges and the plasma concentration revealed
that some particles released drug faster than others thus suggesting a possible diﬀerence in drug release manner
based on shape other besides the mucoadhesion. The in vitro drug release however implied a similarity in drug
release for al shapes. This may be attributed to the fact the these studies only replicated the intestinal pH and not
the environment. Thus, to understand the exact cause of the diﬀerence in drug release proﬁles observed during in
vivo study, the in vitro study should be repeated to replicate the intestinal environment.
Additionally, the capsules containing the formulation were delivered directly into the intestine where absorption
takes place. They were as a result safe from the harsh environment of the stomach where the capsules may be
eroded to release the drug content early. Thus, to replicate actual oral administration, the DDS must be delivered
