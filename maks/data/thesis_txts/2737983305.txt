Abstract
This thesis explores hyperspectral image (HSI) reconstruction from single RGB input
based on deep generative models, focusing on Generative Adversarial Networks(GANs)
and diffusion models and their derivatives. Transformer architecture and selfôÙattention
mechanisms are mainly used in models in the project. 3 HSI datasets: ARAD_1K, BGUôÙ
HS, and CAVE are used in the project. Data augmentation and image preôÙprocessing
are applied to all 3 datasets to generate a large general HSI dataset. A dual aggregation
transformer model combined with stabilized GAN is proposed in the project. It is comôÙ
pared with the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart (SOTA) models and has proven its superiority. The latest
SOTA deep generative diffusion model is also implemented for the HSI reconstruction
task. A SpectralôÙSpatial Transformer model is also implemented and compared with the
SOTA models. Some diffusion model variants are also implemented for the hyperspectral
reconstruction task. The ultimate goal of this thesis is to develop reliable and robust deep
generative models that can accurately reconstruct HSI from single RGB inputs.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Hyperspectral imaging captures electromagnetic radiation data from an object or planeôÙ
tary surface in several continuous bands. Compared to typical RGB photos which only
have 3 color bands of red, green, and blue, hyperspectral photos cover a much wider specôÙ
tral range, which contains dozens or even hundreds of bands. Numerous tasks, particuôÙ
larly those involving recognition, can benefit from the rich spatial and spectral information
in hyperspectral images. Applications of hyperspectral imaging range from environmental
monitoring [1], where different materials can be identified based on spectral signatures,
to agriculture [2], where crop health can be assessed, and to medical diagnostics, where
tissue types can be differentiated.
Although hyperspectral imaging has rather large potential in many fields, this technology
faces significant barriers due to the high cost and complexity of the necessary equipment.
Hyperspectral cameras are expensive and require complicated calibration and mainteôÙ
nance, limiting their use to laboratories and industries that could benefit greatly from hyôÙ
perspectral imaging. This disadvantage restricts their use in numerous scenarios, includôÙ
ing daily food inspection [3] and immediate medical screening [4], where costôÙeffective
and easyôÙtoôÙuse alternatives are needed.
To overcome the difficulty of obtaining hyperspectral photos, recent advances in compuôÙ
tational spectral imaging aim to reconstruct hyperspectral images from RGB images [5]
which can be easily obtained by normal cameras or mobile phones. Although such reconôÙ
struction methods are not as accurate as hyperspectral images directly obtained using a
hyperspectral camera, these methods can meet the high spectral resolution and perforôÙ
mance requirements of downstream tasks without requiring specialized spectral camera
hardware. This approach leverages the rapid development of deep learning (especially
generative models) in computer vision to address the challenge of hyperspectral superôÙ
resolution (HSSR).
Deep generative models, especially generative adversarial networks (GANs) [6] and diffusionôÙ
based models [7], have shown their privilege in generating highôÙfidelity images. These
models can learn complex mappings from the RGB domain to the hyperspectral domain
by training models on large and general datasets. The ability of these models to convert
widely used RGB images into hyperspectral images finds new approaches for applications
that require detailed spectral information without expensive hyperspectral cameras.
1.2
Motivation
The motivation of this work is to improve the usability and ease of use of hyperspectral
imaging in applications. Although hyperspectral imaging has great application potential
in fields such as environmental monitoring, food safety, and medical diagnosis, its high
cost and technical requirements hinder its widespread use. Our goal is to bridge this
gap by creating a technique to reconstruct hyperspectral data from RGB images using
a deep generative model. This is because RGB images are readily available in realôÙ
world scenarios. By doing so, highôÙresolution spectral data will be more accessible. The
hyperspectral image reconstruction model can also serve as a backbone for downstream
tasks such as image classification or image segmentation.

1.3
Objectives
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the use of generative adversarial networks
(GANs) and diffusionôÙbased models in deep generative models (DGMs) for hyperspecôÙ
tral image reconstruction from individual RGB inputs. The project will start by improving
the performance of a SwinGAN model created in the previous special course and comôÙ
paring it to the most advanced baseline models available. Furthermore, an innovative
diffusionôÙbased model will be developed and trained, and a thorough comparison with
current techniques will be conducted.
To develop a hyperspectral imaging reconstruction model based on the GAN structure, it is
crucial to stabilize the training process of GAN and address notorious gradient problems
of GANãs training, such as gradient vanishing and gradient explosion. To improve this
GAN model, Wasserstein loss [8] and spectral normalization [9] are implemented in our
model to stabilize it. In addition, the famous Transformer [10] architecture and attention
mechanism are also applied to our model.
Diffusion models have been wildly used in image generation fields and they have shown
their privilege in generation tasks. However, there has not been any application of the
diffusion model in the field of hyperspectral image reconstruction from RGB inputs. Hence,
we will adjust diffusion models for the hyperspectral reconstruction task and compare them
with other stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart models.
Furthermore, this project also aims to enhance existing openôÙsource hyperspectral datasets
by applying image processing techniques to create a more generalized and comprehenôÙ
sive hyperspectral dataset. This enhanced dataset will be crucial for robust training and
validation of the developed models, ensuring they can generalize well to various realôÙworld
scenarios.
1.4
Limitations
The main goal of this project is to build a reliable dataset of RGB and hyperspectral image
pairs for training and validation, as well as to improve and develop DGM for HSSR. The
goal of this thesis is to advance HSSR by using DGM, however, it is limited by highôÙquality
datasets, computational resources, etc.

2.1
Hyperspectral Imaging (HSI)
Hyperspectral imaging originates from remote sensing, namely in the 1970s and 1980s.
The Airborne Imaging Spectrometer (AIS)[11], invented by NASAãs Jet Propulsion LaboraôÙ
tory (JPL) in 1983, was the first milestone of hyperspectral imaging. By capturing images
in 128 continuous spectrums, the device marked the transition from multispectral imaging
to hyperspectral imaging and provided rather high spectral resolution. NASAãs Airborne
Visible/Infrared Imaging Spectrometer (AVIRIS)[12] which was introduced in 1987, has
further improved this field. AVIRIS can capture 224 spectral bands, covering a spectral
wavelength range from 400nm to 2500nm. This high spectral resolution allows more preôÙ
cise identification and differentiation of materials on the Earthãs surface.
Hyperspectral imaging is a technology that captures and processes electromagnetic specôÙ
trums. Electromagnetic spectrums describe all types of light, from long radio waves, miôÙ
crowaves, infrared radiation, visible light, ultraviolet light, and XôÙrays, to very short gamma
rays that are invisible to humans. Hyperspectral imaging can capture spectrums from
250nm to 15,000nm. Different from traditional RGB imaging which can only capture 3
bands of red, green, and blue, hyperspectral imaging can capture continuous wavelength
spectrums. This is thanks to the emergence of hyperspectral cameras. To capture continôÙ
uous wavelength spectrums, the photoelectric elements inside the hyperspectral camera
will receive light signals from the outside world, and the photoelectric elements will convert
the received light signals into electrical signals, transmit them to the camera, and store
them in the storage unit. This means that hyperspectral cameras require more complex
and precise photosensitive elements to capture more spectral information.
This project mainly focuses on visible hyperspectral image reconstruction, whose waveôÙ
length is from 400nm to 700nm. The training RGB images are generated from ground
truth hyperspectral images. The basic transformation of color spaces is applied in this
project. The transformation from hyperspectral color space to RGB color space is based
on the CIE 1964 10ô¯standard observer[13] in Figure 2.1. The transformation function is

GANs have traditionally been considered a form of unsupervised or selfôÙsupervised learnôÙ

between input images and output images. Pix2pixãs generator uses the UôÙNet[31] archiôÙ

network to retain the deep and shallow feature information of the input image during the
generation process, thereby generating highôÙquality output images. The discriminator of
Pix2pix usually uses the PatchGAN architecture, that is, the discriminator not only judges
the entire image but also judges each local area of the image. This method helps to imôÙ
prove the quality of local details of the generated image. Pix2pix has proven to be an

modeling. However, they reduce the computational resources for inference, but the trainôÙ

2.3
Summary
Although hyperspectral imaging has many applications, its widespread use is blocked by
its high hardware requirements. However, Deep neural networks and the advancement
of GPUs and deep learning models have revolutionized many fields, including computer
vision. Convolution operations enable convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract imôÙ
portant image features, making them especially useful for tasks involving image analysis.
This revolution was further enhanced by the introduction of Vision Transformers (ViTs),
which use selfôÙattention mechanisms to capture longôÙrange dependencies and treat imôÙ
age data as sequences similar to natural language. The constraints of global feature
extraction in ViT are alleviated by improvements like the Swin Transformer and CSwin
Transformer, which introduce shifted window techniques and crossôÙwindow selfôÙattention
mechanisms, respectively.
Generative adversarial networks (GANs) have become the cornerstone of image genôÙ
eration, which adopts an adversarial process between generators and discriminators to
generate realistic images. Conditional GANs (cGANs) can be used in hyperspectral reôÙ
construction to produce hyperspectral images from RGB inputs by enhancing the generôÙ
atorãs output with the discriminatorãs capacity to discern between real and fake samples.
In addition, Pix2pix, a variant of cGAN, can perform the imageôÙtoôÙimage conversion task
more effectively.
Diffusion models are well known for their ability to generate highôÙquality images through
iterative denoising, especially denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs). Although
their stepôÙbyôÙstep generation process makes them computationally expensive, these modôÙ
els are praised for their stability and generalization ability. Variants such as latent diffusion
models (LDMs) and denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIMs) have been developed to
alleviate this. LDMs use a hierarchical approach and autoencoders to map images to a
latent space before the denoising process, and DDIMs can reduce the number of inferôÙ
ence steps without much loss in quality. LDMs can significantly improve computational
efficiency. This hierarchical approach has been shown to be effective in realôÙworld appliôÙ
cations such as stable diffusion.

Additionally, PyTorch Lightning[51] is employed, which is a highôÙlevel framework built on

ated with PyTorch, such as training loops and device management, thus promoting code
cleanliness and maintainability. This framework integrates common PyTorch operations,
making the code more modular and easier to debug. It also supports dataôÙdistributed

Since the deep generative models always require highôÙperformance GPU and large GPU

search and experiments.
4.2
Experimental setting
4.2.1
Baseline and SOTA models
To evaluate the performance of all proposed models, several models from the literature
have been trained, with slight modifications for some of them, to allow the processing of
HSI. The three latest winners of the NTIRE spectral reconstruction challenges, HSCNND,
AWAN, and MST++, were chosen along with the allôÙaround cGAN model pix2pix, and
the allôÙaround transformer model for image restoration, Restormer. HSCNND is a pure
CNN model that uses the DenseNet architecture. AWAN is also a CNN model, but it
embeds spatial and spectral attention mechanisms. MST++ is a spectralôÙwise UôÙshaped
transformer model. Pix2pix, originally developed for imageôÙtoôÙimage translation, has been
used as a backbone for many GAN models and uses a UôÙnet generator and a ãPatchGANã
discriminator that computes local classification scores between the real and fake samples.
Finally, Restormer, a powerful and efficient transformer model based on a skipôÙconnected
UôÙshaped structure enabling highôÙresolution image restoration, was also trained.
4.3
Experiments of Dual Aggregation TransGAN
4.3.1
Implementation and training
RGB and YCrCb images are linearly rescaled to [0, 1] during training. Subsequently,
128 û 128 RGB and HSI sample pairs are cropped from the dataset. The batch size is
Deep Generative Models for reconstruction of hyperspectral images from single RGB input

set to 32 and the Adam optimizer [Adam] is employed with öý1 = 0.5, öý2 = 0.9, and a
learning rate of 2 û 10ã4. A cosine scheduler with a 1ôÙepoch warmup at a learning rate
of 1 û 10ã3 and an overall duration of 100 epochs is adopted. During training, the data is
augmented with random rotation and flipping. The TransGAN model trains the generator
without the discriminator for the first epoch. After the initial epoch, the discriminator is
introduced, and the training process is carried out alternately, training the generator twice
and the discriminator once. The proposed TransGAN model has been implemented on
the PyTorch framework, and approximately 40 hours are required for training the network
on a single Nvidia H100 GPU.
After completing the 100 epochs, progressive training is conducted. The resolution of the
images is increased to twice the original size during each progressive training process,
and the batch size is adjusted to oneôÙfourth of the original size as needed. Progressive
training involves training the model twice, which should finally be set to full resolution.
Each consists of 20 epochs. This helps the model adapt to higher resolutions and enôÙ
hances its generalization capabilities.

Discussion
5.1
Discussion of Dual Aggregation TransGAN
The dual aggregation TransGAN model has demonstrated excellent performances on the
ARAD test set, on which it has been trained, even when components were ablated. HowôÙ
ever, the testing on different datasets and the ablation studies have highlighted some
limitations in our approach. First of all, we need to rely on additional spectral and color
losses to avoid overfitting and a drastic drop in performance on BGU. Also, the most critiôÙ
cal component of our model is the YCrCB encoding, which provides a significant boost in
performance in all experiments. This suggests improving our core architecture to make it
selfôÙsufficient enough, even if the designed additional losses and encoding are integrally
part of our approach and modeling. However, it has been noted that Restormer could
yield good and consistent results, based on a simpler approach.
On the other hand, our results have highlighted the importance of colorimetric and specôÙ
tral characteristics of both the training set and source images. The tremendous boost in
performance of the YCrCB encoding suggests that this approach should be standardized
in deep learning models aimed at processing color images. We believe that our model
globally and taken as a whole has manifested very good results, and established the imôÙ
portance of color modeling in Computer Vision tasks. However, the core model complexity
is a limiting factor that should not be disregarded, and in the future lightweight architecture
should be preferred.
The importance of a diverse and broad training set has also been emphasized, and
prospects will include the development of a dataset merging ARAD, BGU, and CAVE.
In addition, the spectral attention module of our model did not appear to impact the perôÙ
formance, albeit dropping it did not simplify the modelãs complexity. A better spectral
attention model should be developed, especially in improving the global spectral feature
extraction.
Finally, overall, the transformer architecture, and the attention mechanisms, have apôÙ
peared to provide a significant competitive advantage over traditional CNN architectures.
It seems efficient models should embrace a lightweight architecture, embed attention
mechanisms for improved feature extraction, utilize careful color processing, and be trained
on a broader, more diverse dataset for enhanced generalization.
5.2
Discussion of HSI SpectralôÙSpatial Transformer
According to the results of the experiments on both the custom ARAD_1K dataset and the
original ARAD_1K dataset, our proposed SSôÙTransformer model outperforms the SOTA
models.
Our model is trained on the custom ARAD_1K dataset and performs well on the test set
of the custom ARAD_1K dataset, which means that our model has a strong fitting ability
on the task of hyperspectral reconstruction. In addition, our model also showed strong
competitiveness in the crossôÙdataset test experiments. Our model was comparable to
the Restormer model in the BGU dataset test and won first place in two metrics in the
CAVE dataset. This shows that our model showed strong robustness in the crossôÙdataset
test. And our model did not have serious overfitting due to the singleness of the training
dataset. The RGB backôÙprojection test also confirmed this conclusion. The performance

In summary, the SSôÙTransformer model has made significant progress in hyperspectral

Compared to the previous two models, Dual Aggregation TransGAN and SpectralôÙSpatial
Transformer, diffusion models are more complex. This makes the training and parameter
adjustment of diffusion models take more time. Therefore, although diffusion models are
currently the majority of deep generative models, the training results of diffusion models
obtained in this project are performed on 128 û 128 patches, and their performance does
not exceed that of SOTA models.
Nevertheless, we can still gain some insights from the results. Although diffusion models
perform very well in unsupervised tasks, they do not necessarily perform well in superôÙ
vised learning of imageôÙtoôÙimage translation similar to this project. This may be because
the results of the hyperspectral reconstruction task highly depend on the input RGB imôÙ

in the hyperspectral task. This may cause the accuracy of hyperspectral images reconôÙ
structed by diffusion models to be lower than that of other SOTA models. Therefore, it is
necessary to adjust the diffusion model for the hyperspectral reconstruction task.

Conclusion
In summary, this thesis mainly focuses on hyperspectral reconstruction using a single
RGB input based on deep generative models. Two deep generative models, the Dual
Aggregation TransGAN and the SpectralôÙSpatial Transformer, are proposed in this project.
Both of them outperform the SOTA models on most of the evaluation metrics. The latent
diffusion model and the Brownian bridge diffusion model are also implemented for the
hyperspectral reconstruction task. Although the performance of diffusion models is not as
satisfying as expected, the attempts at diffusion models also provide some reference for
future breakthroughs in this direction.
For the Dual Aggregation TransGAN model, a lot of novel attempts are applied. Both the
Wasserstein distance loss and the spectral normalization are implemented to stabilize the
training process of the GAN. Also, this stabilization method allowed a rather complicated
and powerful discriminator to enhance the training of the generator. By training the disôÙ
criminator to fit the Wasserstein distance between the real hyperspectral data distribution
and the fake distribution, the discriminator can provide more continuous and trustworthy
gradient descent directions for the generator. In addition, the spectral normalized spectralôÙ
wise transformer also helps the discriminator to extract the spectralôÙwise features. For the
generator, not only the spectralôÙwise transformer but also the spatialôÙwise transformer is
applied to extract the overall features from the input image. Besides, we also found that
the YCrCb encoding part significantly improved the performance of the model by giving
the neural network more luminance information. However, our model did not show very
good generalization ability in crossôÙdataset testing. Although our model has the best perôÙ
formance on the ARAD_1K dataset, when testing on the data from another dataset, it
does not necessarily outperform the SOTA models.
Another model we proposed in this paper is the SpectralôÙSpatial Transformer model. It
combines the advantages of the spatial transformer and the spectral transformer. It is
also easy to train and efficient. The SSôÙTransformer model has some novel improveôÙ
ments. First, instead of using the selfôÙattention window in the CSwin module, we design a
crossôÙattention mechanism to better extract global features. In addition, we also design a
relative position bias for spectralôÙwise selfôÙattention. Both spectral transformer and spatial
transformer help our model outperform SOTA models on both our custom dataset and the
original ARAD_1K dataset. Besides, our model also shows the ability of generalization
even on other datasets. The excellent performance and computational efficiency of our
model make it more likely to contribute to future practical applications.
The latent diffusion model, although more complex and requiring longer training time,
does not outperform the other two proposed models or the SOTA models in experiments.
A possible explanation is that even though the latent diffusion model outperforms other
deep generative models such as GAN and VAE[44], it does not guarantee better perforôÙ
mance on supervised learning (e.g., imageôÙtoôÙimage tasks). This might be because the
hyperspectral reconstruction task requires the model to learn a mapping from RGB inputs
to hyperspectral outputs, which is a more constrained and specific requirement, which is
different from unsupervised learning tasks.
There may be some feasible approaches to improve the performance of diffusion modôÙ
els on hyperspectral reconstruction tasks. For instance, implementing Transformer arôÙ
chitecture to the latent diffusion model might help it improve its performance because
