Abstract
The main focus of the present MSc Thesis is the evaluation of protective coatings’ perfor­
mance through the implementation of electrochemical characterization techniques. Stan­
dard test methods like the Salt spray test (ISO 9227), although widely employed, pro­
vide limited insights into the underlying mechanisms of coating failure. To overcome this
limitation, alternative test setups and non­destructive techniques are proposed to gain a
comprehensive understanding of coating degradation mechanisms throughout the test du­
ration. The present work aims to cross­compare different test results and investigate the
predictive capabilities of the accelerated cyclic electrochemical test (ACET ­ ISO 16474­
3) for other standard tests such as NORSOK, cathodic disbondment (ISO 15711), and
immersion in seawater (ISO 2812­2). Furthermore, a relatively new method for faster
non­destructive Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) measurement of coated
panels will also be employed, namely Magnetic Clamping Flat cells (MCF), instead of
standard glass cells. Additionally, the impact of different surface preparations using two
blasting grit types will be assessed. Surface morphology, elemental composition, and
electrochemical properties will be analyzed using scanning electron microscopy (SEM),
energy­dispersive X­ray spectroscopy (EDS), and potentiodynamic measurements. Com­
plimentary tests on free films will be conducted to compare water diffusion results obtained
through EIS with gravimetric measurements. Additionally, DMA and DSC techniques will
be used to further characterize the coatings’ properties. This analysis will enhance our
understanding of water uptake and its influence on the glass transition temperature (Tg)
of the coatings under varying conditions. Lastly, pull­off and creep corrosion tests will be
conducted to evaluate the physical properties and to compare the results acquired from
each coating and blasting grit. The findings of this research have the potential to im­
prove the understanding of coating behavior during standard test methods, facilitate early
screening of test panels, and contribute to the development of more efficient and reliable
protective coating systems. The insights gained from this study will aid in formulating al­
ternative test methodologies for rapid screening of coatings, providing a solid foundation
for future advancements in the field of protective coatings.

1
Introduction
Organic coatings also known as protective paints, are extensively utilized in many kinds
of industries including automotive, offshore windfarms, maritime, and generally big con­
structions. They represent one of the most recognized methods to shield large metallic
structures from corrosion and degradation over time [1]. It is estimated that the finan­
cial impact of corrosion is massive and can reach up to 3­4% of a country’s GNP (gross
national product). In addition, it has been approximated that 20% of the aforementioned
costs could have been prevented if better corrosion protection protocols, such as protec­
tive coating application, existed [2].
Organic coatings act as protective barriers, shielding the metallic substrate from corro­
sion and preventing any potentially corrosive substances from reaching the substrate.
They offer resistance against various environmental factors such as moisture, chemicals,
UV radiation, and also contribute to aesthetic appearance. Furthermore, there are many
indicators that influence the effectiveness of a coating such as its mechanical proper­
ties, the presence and concentration of inhibitors, surface preparation (blasting), adhesion
strength, and the addition of substances (additives) that hinder corrosion [3].
A lot of standardized procedures have been developed throughout the years in order to
assess the barrier properties of epoxy­based organic coatings. Nevertheless, the majority
of these tests are very time­consuming, as they require extended periods of up to 500
hours and more, in order to produce trustworthy and good­quality results. Such tests
are able to give very useful information about the long­term performance of the coatings.
However, the need to reduce the evaluation time for industrial applications of coatings is
also paramount [4].
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is a very promising technique used by sci­
entists, to evaluate the durability of different coatings against corrosion and general degra­
dation. In the present thesis, three types of organic epoxy coatings namely Hempadur
35620 and prototypes F0263 and F0256, underwent various standard corrosion tests such
as accelerated cyclic electrochemical technique (ACET), water immersion, NORSOK, ca­
thodic disbondment test (CDT) and water diffusion. The coatings have been applied on
carbon steel panels that were blasted using two types of blasting grits, olivine and steel.
EIS measurements have been combined with the above­mentioned corrosion tests, in
order to evaluate the electrochemical properties of the coatings and the potential impact
of blast cleaning before and after exposure. Furthermore, in this project, EIS evaluations
were conducted by utilizing several alternative test setups and techniques to characterize
test panels during standard test programs non­destructively such as Magnetic Clamping
Flat Cells (MCF cells) according to ISO 16733 [5] or by implementing standard electro­
chemical cells for ACET test according to ISO 17463 [6].
In addition, a number of complimentary tests have been performed. The water diffusion
coefficient of the three coatings was obtained using eight printed circuit boards (PCBs)
with interdigitated electrodes as test vehicles that were enclosed in a climate chamber
in order to control the oscillating temperature and humidity. Furthermore, an attempt
was made to determine the rate of water absorption by conducting a gravimetric test on
free­coating films. Additionally, Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) and Dynamic
Mechanical Analysis (DMA) measurements were performed in order to characterize the
glass transition temperature (Tg) of the coatings prior to and after the exposure. Finally,

1.1
Objectives and motivation
The investigation of the electrochemical properties of protective organic coatings is crucial
given the fact that much information can be obtained regarding their lifetime and resilience
after exposure to corrosive environments. Equally significant is to reduce the duration of
the different corrosion tests and to find alternative methods to evaluate the electrochem­
ical properties non­destructively and at a faster pace. In addition, by speeding up the
evaluation process, the overall costs that are connected with research and development
along with the plethora of resources needed will be also decreased. Furthermore, coat­
ing industries will be capable of ensuring the durability of their product, guaranteeing their
clients the performance of their coatings.
The primary objective of this project is to investigate and compare the electrochemical
data obtained by EIS measurements of different standard testing procedures with faster
methods such as the ACET test. Additionally, this study aims to gain a comprehensive
understanding, based on electrochemical data, of the causes behind the degradation of
the barrier properties, and why a specific coating shows better or worse results in terms
of durability when compared to another.
1.2
Research questions
The following research questions constituted a guide that provided the focus of the present
work. These questions form the base of this study as they are addressing the key objec­
tives that were explored.
The main research questions to be addressed are the following:
1. What is the outcome after cross­comparison of different test results?
2. Can ACET test results be used to predict performance in other tests: NORSOK,
cathodic disbondement, and immersion in seawater?
3. Does early screening of test panels with EIS give indication of test panels perfor­
mance in the long term?
4. What are the differences between surface preparations using two different grits, and
how does it impact performance in test results?
5. What is the outcome after the comparison between water diffusion results using EIS
and gravimetric measurements?

Theoretical Background
The purpose of this chapter is first of all to provide the reader with the necessary basic
knowledge about various methods for evaluating protective coatings. The primary evalu­
ation method which is going to be extensively used throughout this dissertation is called
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS). This technique will be utilized in order to
non­destructively evaluate the different specimens during standard characterization tests
such as the Salt spray test (ISO 9227), Water immersion test (ISO 2812­2), Accelerated
weathering test (ISO 16474­3), Cathodic disbonding test (ISO 15711), Accelerated Cyclic
Electrochemical Technique or ACET (ISO 17463) and finally NORSOK M­501. Secondly,
a brief review will be also provided on the existing literature in order to acknowledge rel­
evant investigations regarding the topic of the assessment of protective coatings using
electrochemical techniques.
2.1
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS)
EIS is one of the most powerful and widely used techniques in the field of electrochemistry.
It is a non­destructive method that can be used to evaluate various systems such as
batteries, fuel cells, and in this case protective coatings. As the name suggests, with
this method scientists can measure the impedance (Z) of various complex processes that
take place during electrochemical reactions. A very useful feature that has attracted the
attention of the scientific community is the ability to distinguish between various processes
that occur during electrochemical processes and provide valuable data about them as
mentioned in [7].
These processes can be correlated with mass and charge transfer, and diffusion oper­
ations. Each of these operations is connected with the conductance, resistance, and
capacitance of the electrochemical system accordingly. Generally, impedance and re­
sistance both represent the opposition to the flow of electric current, but they are not
the same. In circuits where there is direct current (DC) resistors can display resistance,
whereas in alternating current (AC) there are other elements that can impede the flow of
electrons such as capacitors (C) and inductors (L) [8].
EIS has been upgraded in the last decades, as technology is constantly evolving. Better
and more accurate computer systems and software have enabled the application area
of EIS to expand and also be more accessible. Nowadays, systems can reach up to 11
frequency decades and measure resistances ranging from microohms to teraohms and
capacitances from picofarads to kilofarads [7].
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy is based on the classical method of the transfer
function and it stands out in relation to other techniques because of its capability to mea­
sure the connection between voltage and current in the frequency domain [9]. It involves
applying a small sinusoidal AC signal of different frequencies (105 −10−3 Hz) and ampli­
tude ±10mV at various discrete bands of 5­10 decades, to excite the system of interest
(input) and measure the response of the system to this perturbation. The resulting signal
will have the form of a sinusoidal wave with the same frequency but a different phase and
amplitude (output) as can be seen in Figure 2.1.

For an EIS test to be valid, three important conditions have to be inspected and met
beforehand. These are:
• Linearity: Generally, a system can be characterized as linear when the output of
an excitation is directly proportional to the input. Regarding EIS, it means that the
response of the system to a small perturbation, like an AC voltage signal, is pro­
portional to the amplitude of the perturbation. However, electrochemical techniques
reveal that current is not a linear function of potential, which necessitates the use
of small sinusoidal signals to remain within the linear region of the graph. In other
words, in order to do a successful EIS test, the perturbation has to be large enough
to generate a useful response that can be measured accurately, but on the other
hand, it also has to be small enough to maintain linearity or pseudolinearity (Figure
2.3). Furthermore, linear systems have the property of superposition, which means
that the response to a sum of inputs is equal to the sum of the responses to each
input separately [12].
Figure 2.3: Current versus voltage plot and pseudolinearity. [13]
• Stability: A system can be considered stable if it returns to a steady state after the
disturbance. In the case of EIS, the system after having been exposed to the small

Standard corrosion testing methods
There are many kinds of standardized testing procedures when it comes to the evaluation
of coatings. A very crucial category includes tests that mimic real­world conditions and
their main goal is to achieve accelerated failures, e.g. corrosion, for different types of
applications. Through this procedure, each coating can be assessed for its ability to resist
corrosion. The data that is gathered after each test allows for targeted evaluation of the
coatings and determines which coating is the most durable against certain conditions.
The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) has developed a variety of tests
that successfully simulate harsh environmental conditions. In this section, some of the
tests that will be used for this project will be presented and discussed.
2.3.1
Cathodic Disbondment test
Cathodic Disbondment (CD) is a crucial issue when it comes to protective coatings. It’s
a phenomenon that arises when the coated metal is cathodically protected (CP) using
an impressed current. This process generates, among others, hydrogen ions (H+) that
are reduced to hydrogen gas (H2) between the metal and the coating. These products
can break up the oxide layer of the metal, alternate the chemistry of the coating, produce

15
==PAGE:32==
blisters, and generally disrupt the adhesion of the metal/polymer coating interface [31].
A lot of standards have been established throughout the years to effectively characterize
different types of coatings and test their durability against cathodic disbondment. An ex­
ample is ISO 15711, a standard that describes two methods that can be used to determine
which coating has the greatest endurance and ability to resist cathodic disbonding.
For CD to happen, there has to be a defect or a discontinuity on the surface of the coating
which is also known as a holiday. If through this holiday, the metal comes into contact
with oxygen or an electrolyte, then corrosion will occur. To prevent this from happening,
scientists found a way to protect the bare metal by providing a cathodic current to the
substrate turning it into the cathode of the electrochemical cell. However, as already
mentioned, this technique can cause CD i.e. damage to the interface and deterioration of
the adhesion between the metal substrate and the coating [32].
Cathodic Protection (CP)
As already mentioned coatings are very important when it comes to the protection of a
metal structure from corrosion and degradation. Nevertheless, there are cases in which
very small areas of coated metal become uncoated due to loss of adhesion, scratches,
and generally because of the harsh environments. In order for these areas to be protected,
metal structures have to be cathodically protected.
In the case of a steel construction that is in contact with an electrolyte, its corrosion can
be described by the following equations:
2Fe →2Fe2+ + 4e−
(2.14)
O2 + 2H2O + 4e−→4OH−
(2.15)
where the first equation describes the oxidation reaction (dissolution of metal or anodic
reaction and release of e−) and the latter one the oxygen reduction reaction (consumption
of e−or cathodic reaction). At this point, if corrosion happens by itself the e−s produced
by the anodic reaction are consumed by the cathodic reaction. These two equations
indicate that removing e−s from the metal surface will accelerate the anodic reaction and
the corrosion while providing e−s on the surface will decrease the rate of the corrosion
due to the acceleration of the cathodic reaction [33].
In Figure 2.14 the polarization diagram for the corrosion of the Fe can be seen. The two

number of consumed e−s. At the same point, Icorr is the corrosion current that represents
the corrosion rate at Ecorr. Corrosion here is happening without any external action. In
order to change potential and go away from Ecorr electrons must be supplied or withdrawn.
Using cathodic protection, the required electrons that are needed to enhance the cathodic
reaction and lower the corrosion of the metal are given externally.

Water immersion test
The immersion test is an advantageous technique used to characterize the ability of the
organic coating to resist water uptake and protect the metal underneath. During the test,
coated panels are fully or partially immersed in an electrolyte which in this case is seawa­
ter. The amount of water absorbed by the coating during this procedure can drastically
affect its ability to protect the substrate. Adhesion loss and eventually corrosion can occur
between the metal and the protective coating [36].
Capacitance is a fundamental property of the coating and is directly connected with the
water volume the coating has absorbed. This can easily be explained by equation 2.5 if
the coating is considered to be a dielectric. According to this equation, the capacitance
of the coating is equivalent to its dielectric constant multiplied by the dielectric constant
of vacuum and area A divided by the thickness of the coating. The dielectric constant of
polymers (ε) can take values between approximately 3 to 8 whereas the dielectric constant
for water though is much higher, 78.3 at 25◦. As a result, when water is absorbed by the
coating, the total capacitance will increase. For that reason, EIS tests are conducted
regularly, to collect sufficient data to determine the water content in organic coatings [37].
In 1954 Brasher and Kingsbury ([38] proposed one of the most widely used models, that
estimate the quantity of water absorbed by a coating:
ϕ = K log (Ct/C0)
log (εw)

in a dry coating accordingly, εw is the dielectric constant of water, and ϕ corresponds to

volume and its value is usually 1. Nevertheless, dissimilar results regarding water uptake
in coating between this technique and others like the gravimetric method have been found,
and the reason for that is the different pore distribution on the coating’s surface. As it
turned out, the reason for the capacitance increase is not exclusively connected with how
much water it gets absorbed by the coating but also in which way it does. This is because
the distribution of pores does not have only one direction and some pores might be parallel
or perpendicular to the surface ([36], [37]).

As previously noted, organic coatings are widely used to shield metallic or other structures
and protect them from harsh environments. However, their performance varies depend­

be binders, pigments, fillers, dryers, curing, dispersion, stabilizing agents, and corrosion
inhibitors. Coatings are responsible to keep water, gas, and ions away from the metallic
surface. Nonetheless, these species can sometimes diffuse through the coating depend­
ing on the external conditions. Pathways may be the binder, defects or pores located on
the surface of the coating or may diffuse through the pigment/binder interface. (Figure
2.17).
Figure 2.17: Pathways from which water and other species can diffuse through the coating

The vast majority of information in the literature, regarding water diffusion in organic coat­
ings, concerns studies on free films. These data though, may not be entirely true as many
studies have shown. For instance, in his work, Holtzman [40] found out that there is a dif­
ference in the value of water permeability when the coating is applied on a substrate and
when is tested as a free film. In addition, Rosen and Martin [41] during their sorption tests
confirmed the speculations when they discovered that the diffusion coefficient of coated
panels was three times higher than the coefficient of free films. On the contrary, in some
other cases, the exact opposite situation was reported. This contradiction cast signifi­
cant uncertainty on the extension of data from water transport in free films to the practical
application of films [42].
In order to minimize the diffusion through the paths that have been mentioned, binders
with high­degree of cross­linked macromolecules are used. Moreover, the addition of pig­
ments can extend the diffusion path’s length, thereby obstructing species’ entrance. Fur­
thermore, the ability of water and oxygen and how easily they can penetrate and diffuse
through the coating, is inversely related to its thickness. Although the presence of appro­

repulsion, this property is limited. This phenomenon, referred to as a ”semipermeable
membrane,” highlights that relying solely on the physical barrier property is inadequate
for corrosion protection. That is why also chemical and electrochemical barriers are re­
quired to enhance the performance of a coating. Anti­corrosive pigments are widely used
to serve this purpose. When the coating is compromised due to mechanical or chemical
defects, these pigments reach the surface of the metal and passivate them by creating a

in various forms [39].
When an organic coating is exposed to high­humidity environments or is immersed in an
electrolyte and specifically water, there are different ways in which it can be damaged.
Common examples of damage mechanisms include blistering, delamination, filiform cor­
rosion, and loss of adhesion. The driving force behind water diffusion can be capillary
forces, osmotic phenomena, or concentration gradients.
Adhesion loss: As water diffuses through the coating, it decreases the adhesion at the
metal/coating interface, and in most cases creates a narrow film on top of the metal sur­
face. This film works as an electrochemical double­layer which initiates the corrosion
underneath the coating. The diffusion coefficient, coating thickness, temperature, and
relative humidity are some of the parameters that dictate how much time it will take for
the coating to be saturated in water. Regardless, once water molecules reach the metal/­
coating interface, they can destroy the chemical bonds between them and initiate the loss
of adhesion procedure. Corrosion will continue to occur as long as there is enough water
and oxygen for the cathodic reaction to be maintained. The mechanism of adhesion loss

Blistering: Corrosion that occurs underneath coatings can be present in the form of
bubbles also known as blisters (Figure 2.19). Blisters are swelled regions that contain
liquid or gas and locally separate the coating from the metallic surface. The blisters that
contain liquid, are created due to the difference in osmotic pressure, which drives the
water inwards into the blister in order to balance the ionic strength all over the coating. In
this case, the coating acts like a semi­permeable membrane that allows water and oxygen
to enter but not ions that are dissolved in the electrolyte. The content of the blisters can
be concentrated electrolytes due to substrate corrosion, soluble inhibitors, and dissolution
of surface impurities. The occurrence of potential ionic impurities at the interface, which
stems from inadequate surface preparation, may result in the formation of concentrated
saline solutions. These solutions draw in extra water from the external environment due to
the concentration gradient. The disparity in osmotic pressure across the coating, created
by the highly concentrated salt solution, is counterbalanced by the ingress of additional
water through the coating. This process leads to an increase in the hydrostatic pressure
of the electrolyte at the interface, ultimately causing the adhesion loss of the coating.
Finally, reduction reactions that are happening under the coating release hydrogen gas
which leads to the formation of blisters.
Anodic and cathodic blisters might also be formed under the coating. In the case of an­
odic blisters, the exposed substrate undergoes corrosion, resulting in the generation of
both soluble and insoluble corrosion byproducts. Cathodic blisters are formed when the
substrate is suitable for cathodic reactions and can also be unharmed [39, 43].

Filiform corrosion: Filiform corrosion is a specific type of corrosion that can develop
beneath organic coatings. It predominantly affects metals such as steel, or magnesium
but especially aluminium that has been coated with organic materials. The main conse­

not have a large effect on the mechanical properties of the system, but it can affect the
appearance of the surface. That is the reason why it is also known as ’cosmetic’ corrosion.
Flaws on the coating like scratches, cracks, surface defects, sharp edges or areas where
water can accumulate are the main locations from which filiform corrosion can commence.
It is easily recognizable because of the thin thread­like filament form it takes underneath
the coating, which can spread across the whole surface (Figure 2.20). A main charac­

which they spread cannot be predicted. Their width can vary from 0.1 to 0.5 mm, and the
speed with which they spread on the surface can reach up to 0.4 mm/day, and the type
of coating does not affect these values. In addition, the thickness of the coating is closely
related to the probability of occurrence of filiform corrosion. Nevertheless, some studies
(i.e. [45]) have shown that even if the coating thickness is increased the prevention of fili­
form corrosion is not guaranteed. Instead, if the thickness is too big then the delamination
can be even worse [46].
Salt spray test (SST)
The salt spray test is a well­known test method that is used to access the corrosion re­
sistance of organic coatings in a controlled environment. During this test, the coated
panels are subjected to maintained and accelerated corrosion conditions with the use of
highly salt­concentrated fog. The most common values of salt concentration being used
are 3.5% and 5.0% NaCl in order to mimic real­life saltwater conditions. Usually, differ­
ent kinds of defects are introduced onto the coatings on purpose, for example, holidays,
scratches, or holes. These intentional weak spots reinforce the corrosion procedure, giv­
ing more information about the coating’s ability to protect the substrate. Depending on
the coating, time frames vary from case to case. If a coating’s ability to resist corrosion is
high then the length of the experiment will be more extensive [47].
SST is a very widely used method to evaluate the ability of the different coatings to pro­
tect the metal underneath from severe corrosion. It is relatively cheap and reports can be
made repeatedly over time. Nevertheless, it has some limitations when it comes to com­
paring different coatings with each other and with their expected resilience accurately [47].
This can be particularly misleading when this test is used to compare organic with metal­
lic coatings or different metallic coatings among themselves. This is because in a test
chamber, the surface of the coating is constantly wet and there are no drying cycles in
between. This seldom happens in real­life conditions in which metallic coatings have the
necessary time to passivate. Furthermore, there are cases in which the chloride content
is abnormally high, leading to different corrosion acceleration factors for various metallic
coatings. In terms of organic coatings, a main disadvantage is a lack of ultraviolet light
(UV) exposure which is one of the primary reasons these kinds of coatings can fail. [48].
Ultra violet (UV) Degradation
Protective coatings such as paints, can be exposed to UV radiation in real­life environ­
ments in the form of sunlight. The least damage UV radiation can do to a coating is to
change its appearance. For example, it can cause color changes, ’yellowing’, chalking,
or even shine reduction. However, chemical and mechanical properties can also be af­
fected. There is a wide list of potential effects UV can cause and some examples are
embrittlement, increased hardness and internal stresses, increased surface wettability
due to surface polar group production and finally changes in the bonds of the polymer
such as crosslinking. These alterations can result in the delamination of the coating from
the underlying material, reduced thickness, cracking, or enhanced permeability to water
and oxygen.
The aforementioned damage is mainly caused by the UV part of the sunlight and it con­
24
Evaluation of protective coatings performance by means of electrochemical characterization
==PAGE:41==
stitutes a form of energy. When this energy is absorbed by chemical compounds the
procedure of formation and breakage of chemical bonds initiates. That is not the case
with visible light, which does not possess the necessary energy to break the bonds on
the surface of the coating. UV light though, which can be found at 285 ­ 390 nm, has
extensively more energy, often enough to initiate bond breakage and harm the coating.
It is observed that within this range (285 ­ 390 nm) the majority of paints fail due to radi­
ation. Generally, short wavelengths, unlike long wavelengths, are particularly destructive
but they do not have the ability to infiltrate deep into the coating. On the contrary, long
wavelengths can reach bigger depths but cause much less damage. This creates an un­
even degradation throughout the mass of the coating as the areas closer to the surface
have a higher crosslinking factor compared to the rest material.
The main issue when it comes to radiation degradation is the absorption of the UV part
by the polymeric binder of the coating. The high energy UV has is able to break C­C or
C­H bonds and can cause further cross­linking. Excess cross­linking of the already cured
matrix can lead to the build­up of internal stresses in the polymer which can be translated
as embrittlement. When the value of internal stress goes over the strength limit of the
polymer, then cracking and delamination are usually observed.
Exposure to UV radiation causes bond breakage within the coating, resulting in the release
of free radicals. These highly reactive species then react with oxygen, generating perox­
ides that can further interact with the polymer chains. This procedure causes the polymer
chains to undergo breaking and fragmentation. During that, small binder molecules can
be evaporated and in the end, escape the coating. The continuous reaction between per­
oxides, polymer chains, and free radicals, increases the volume of voids and as a result,
decreases the coating thickness. Because of that, internal stresses start to increase and
negatively impact the mechanical properties of the paint. Furthermore, after some time,
crosslinking density has been significantly compromised leading to a further decrease in
the film thickness and finally terminating the coating’s ability to protect the substrate [49].
2.4
Surface preparation techniques
Surface preparation is one of the most important aspects when it comes to the application
of coatings. After many studies that have been done over the years, it has been observed
that 75% of coating failures can be attributed to inadequate surface preparation [50]. In
this section, different methods and procedures will be mentioned and a bigger focus will
be given to the techniques that have been used throughout this thesis.
The main goal of surface preparation procedures is to produce a surface that will enhance
the adhesion between the paint and the substrate. After the preparation, the substrate
must be clean meaning that surface contaminants like oils, grease, or oxides should not
be present, because they can easily compromise the coating adhesion on the material
underneath. Therefore, the two most important characteristics a surface should have in
order to achieve the best adhesion possible are firstly a high level of cleanliness and
secondly increased surface roughness profile (Ra). To achieve those two, the surface of
the substrate has to be blasted with abrasives made from specific materials. The impact of
the abrasives removes possible residues and at the same time increases the roughness of
the substrate promoting strong adhesion through the process of mechanical interlocking.
Before the blasting process, it must be assured that the surface is clean. This is why
a pre­cleaning phase must take place prior to blasting. The impurities that might stick
to the surface can vary depending on their source. For example, if the substrate has
been exposed to an open environment for an extended period of time, it might have dust
Evaluation of protective coatings performance by means of electrochemical characterization
25
==PAGE:42==
or even rust. In the case of a working environment, the surface can be filled with oils
or grease. Based on this information, the substrate must be checked thoroughly before
blasting, following these steps [50]:
1. Evaluation of surface condition: Identification of fabrication errors (welding, cuts).
2. Addressing fabrication flaws: Repair of fabrication flaws and elimination of potential
corrosion products.
3. Pre­cleaning: Solvent, tool, or temperature cleaning.
4. Final inspection: Making sure all the previous steps have been followed.
These pre­cleaning steps are based on specific ISO standards which ensure the effec­
tiveness of the procedure.
2.4.1
Surface roughness
As already mentioned surface roughness is an essential parameter when it comes to
surface preparation as it ensures good adhesion between the protective coating and the
paint. Roughness measurements can be done with a profilometer and the measured
surface parameters are [49]:
1. Ra (Arithmetic Average Roughness): The average roughness relative to the length
of the measurement or the average difference between peaks and valleys of a spe­
cific measured length.
2. Rz: It represents the average maximum height of the profile. Rz is calculated by
measuring the vertical distance between the highest peak and the lowest valley
within a sampling length.
3. Ry: Is the vertical distance between the highest and the lowest point of the profile
within the measured length.
4. Rq (root mean square roughness): Represents the average height variation of the
surface roughness profile, considering the root mean square of the deviations. It
provides a measure of the average roughness amplitude of the surface over the
sampling length.
2.4.2
Blast cleaning
Blast cleaning is a technique in which blasting media are propelled through different blast­
ing systems at very high speeds, and are forced to hit the surface of the targeted substrate
that needs to be treated. There are different methods by which a surface can be blasted
and the selection of each method depends on the following [51]:
• Purpose of blasting
• Blasting system
• Blasting medium
The main blast­cleaning techniques are compressed­air, centrifugal, and vacuum or suc­
tion head blast cleaning. Compressed­air blast cleaning is one of the most commonly
used methods and is capable of removing potential oxide layers, mill scales, or previously
applied coatings on the surface of the metal. Furthermore, it readies the substrate in order
for corrosion or wear protective coatings and other adhesion systems to be applied.

2.4.3
Dry abrasive blasting
As discussed earlier, protective coatings are much more effective against corrosion when
the steel substrate has been blast cleaned before the application. To achieve this, it is
crucial to use relatively heavy blasting media. Otherwise, the impact energy transferred
to the surface may not be sufficient to enhance its roughness and clean it thoroughly.
Additionally, blasting material must be hard enough to not plastically deform or entirely
break during impact because large amounts of kinetic energy can be lost. Moreover, the
material must be low­cost when bought in big portions and benign [49].
2.4.4
Metallic Abrasives
Metallic steel abrasives play a crucial role in surface preparation processes, offering ef­
ficient and effective methods for cleaning, surface finishing, and coating removal. There
are two prominent types of steel abrasives:
1. Shot abrasives: consist of steel particles that are rounded or have a more spherical
shape.
2. Grit abrasives: harder tempered steel particles that exhibit a more angular shape.
Substandard steel often supplemented with various additives is typically used to produce
both shot and grit steel abrasives. Depending on the application these types of abrasives
exhibit relatively good efficiency and demonstrate minimal breakdown rates. They are
specifically used for mill scale, rust, or old coating elimination. Sustainability is also a
very important parameter when it comes to blasting. Shot and grit steel abrasives are
highly recyclable and can be reused 100 to 200 times. Additionally, because of their
ability to not shatter during impact, the levels of dust are very low, making the technician’s
work much easier in comparison with other blasting materials. Finally, chilled iron shot or
grit can be used in many cases where heat treatment scales or old coatings need to be
removed from the steel surface [49].
2.4.5
Non­metallic Abrasives
Another type of abrasive that is commonly used is naturally occurring non­metallic miner­
als. Some of them are namely garnet, novaculite, zircon, staurolite, or olivine. Not all of
them are suitable for blasting operations due to their high levels of free silicate content,
which can cause damage to the lungs. The hardest non­metallic material is garnet. The
shape of the particles is angular and they are characterized by their increased toughness.
Another important feature is that it can be reused multiple times because it has a low level
of breakage during impact. Its high cost when compared with the other materials, makes
it suitable for applications where the blasting material is recycled after use. Staurolite
and olivine fall into the classification of non­silica mineral sands. They are less tough and
dense than garnet but they do not contain free silicates. Due to that, they are suitable for
surface blasting of new steel samples rather than old already rusted surfaces [49].

Resistance and Performance
As has already been mentioned EIS, is a widely used technique to evaluate the ability of
a coating to resist corrosion and general degradation. Although it is a method that proved
extremely applicable, it demands a lot of time. That is the reason why other electrochem­
ical techniques have been developed throughout the years and when combined with EIS
they successively produced great results.
In his study, S.J. Garcia [4] evaluated coatings that were applied on low­carbon steel
specimens and contained a cataphoretic epoxy primer, using EIS and ACET tests. He

concluded that the results regarding the quality of the coatings were successfully obtained
by both electrochemical methods. The main difference though, is that ACET (AC/DC/AC)
test was more efficient in terms of time, as it produced the same results in a much shorter
period.
The same results have been presented in his study [52] in which he tested the anticor­

even more without interfering with the normal corrosion procedure of the coating due to the
presence of electrolyte. This meant that the corrosion creep and the corrosion products

Furthermore, the addition of different kinds of pigments to epoxy coatings in order to en­

M.J. Gimeno [54] in his research, applied two corrosion testing methods, namely salt spray

from the salt spray test were obtained after 1100 h whereas the ACET test presented the
same results in 24 h. In addition, he notes that ACET test is capable of offering informa­
tion about the permeability and the adhesion of the coating regardless of their quality or
mean thickness.
Lastly, plasticizers represent another type of additive that has been widely researched.
Their influence on the performance of the resulting coating can be evaluated using elec­
trochemical methods. Within his research, M.T. Rodriguez showed that for a certain type
of epoxy coating the addition of 1.5 to 3% of plasticizer tent to increase its anti­corrosive
properties whereas the addition of over 3% resulted in coatings that would delaminate
easier. To obtain these results, M.T. Rodriguez used a salt fog spray test along with EIS
and ACET tests. He reached the conclusion that while the data obtained from all the tests
showed significant similarities, there were substantial differences in their duration [55].

3.2.1
ACET Test
ACET is an abbreviation of the accelerated cyclic electrochemical technique test and it
has been used according to ISO­17463. It is an electrochemical method that uses alter­
nating (AC) and direct current (DC) to accelerate coating degradation. The whole test
lasted 350 hours and the steps that have been followed are listed below. It should be
noted that during ACET, the system underwent cycles in a loop between step 2 to step
4. Furthermore, the aforementioned loop has been repeated 200 times before the end of
the test.
1. Open­Circuit Potential (OCP): Before the initial step, the system undergoes an OCP
measurement, until it reaches an equilibrium. During this procedure, a potentiostat
is connected to the electrochemical glass cell that is being used to characterize the
coatings.
2. Initial stage: The coatings are first tested using electrochemical impedance mea­
surements (EIS). A sinusoidal potential is applied and the potentiostat receives data
about the consequent current and both potential and alternating current are ana­
lyzed regarding amplitude and phase shift.
3. Cathodic polarization: In order to induce some degradation in the coating a relatively
large cathodic current potential is applied to the electrochemical cell. The potential
used in this case was ­2 V vs reference electrode and also the duration of this step
was 40 minutes. Any possible degradation, will be caused by the electrolysis of
water that has reached the metal/coating interface. During this electrochemical re­
action, hydroxide ions and hydrogen are released. Both of them are able to cause
delamination and harm by generally producing stresses on the coating.
4. Potential relaxation: In this phase, the system undergoes a period of relaxation
(OCP measurement) to establish a fresh equilibrium state prior to repeating the
initial step of Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) measurement. The
period of relaxation chosen for this experiment was 3 hours.
A total of 6 panels were used during ACET test. A standard glass electrochemical cell was
placed on top of each panel and was filled with artificial salt water with 5% NaCl content
by weight. Each cell had a counter electrode made of titanium coated with platinum and a
reference electrode Ag/AgCl. After the assembly, the electrochemical cells were inserted
inside a chamber (Figure 3.15 ) which kept the temperature stable at 35◦C.

The bigger the capacitance over time, the stronger the degradation of the coating. This
can be easily explained by using equation 2.5. Coating capacitance is directly influenced
by the dielectric constant (εr) as long as the sample’s geometry remains unchanged.
However, it is important to note that this geometric condition may not always be true. For
example, if water infiltrates the coating, it can lead to swelling or delamination, thereby

increasing its thickness [67]. In such cases, if the thickness remains constant and water
is absorbed by the coating, the capacitance will also increase. This happens because the
dielectric constant of organic coatings typically ranges from 3 to 8, whereas water has a
significantly higher value of 80 at room temperature. As a result, when water is absorbed
by the coating, its dielectric constant increases, and the consequent coating capacitance
also increases [68].
Figure 4.2: Normalized plot of capacitance vs time at 74.599kHz.

according to the same pattern as impedance decreases for each coating. The capacitance

significantly higher increase in capacitance compared to other panels. This means that a
high amount of water has been absorbed due to an imperfection on the coating’s surface.
The obtained result was visually and quantitatively confirmed through subsequent pull­off
tests that took place in a later phase of the testing procedure (see Section 4.10).

4.3
NORSOK test
As stated earlier in section 3.2.3 NORSOK is a standard testing procedure developed
by the Norwegian petroleum industry, that evaluates the durability of a coating in harsh
environments. This standard includes a cycle of UV radiation together with a condensation
process, a salt spray exposure, and lastly a cooling session. The whole test lasted 13
weeks and each week the panels underwent the aforementioned cycle. A total of 6 free
films were applied on 6 chosen panels (2 free films and 2 panels for each of the three
coatings) before the beginning of the test. These panels were excluded from the rest EIS
measurements as it was not possible to measure their impedance due to the presence
of the free films on their surface. Consequently, the total number of panels measured
after the initial EIS test was 12, instead of 18. Lastly, the remaining 12 panels, were all
scribed using a CNC machine, in order to test the resistance of the coatings to general
degradation when subjected to damage.
Figure 4.8 shows the first EIS evaluation that took place before the panels were subjected
to any kind of degradation. It can be seen that all panels act almost like a perfect capac­
itor, as there are no damages to the surface of the coating and it is totally intact. The
impedance is exceptionally high at low frequency, reaching values of 1010 Ohm. At this
stage, there is no clear discrimination between the 3 distinct coatings as their impedance
values are very close.
Figure 4.8: Initial EIS evaluation before testing the three coatings.

DSC/DMA
As mentioned in section 3.3.2, in order to evaluate and compare the glass transition tem­
perature (Tg) of the coatings before and after the corrosion tests, two techniques were
used namely Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) and Dynamic Mechanical Analysis
(DMA).
DSC: Figure 4.32 depicts the results of the DSC measurements for coating 35620. As can
be seen, there are three curves that correspond to the reference, NORSOK, and water
immersion tested samples. The method followed by each sample to acquire these results
can be viewed in Table 3.5. The Tg of the reference specimen was 50.3 ◦C while the
immersion and NORSOK tested free films illustrated Tg of 62 ◦C and 65.4 ◦C respectively.
Next, in Figure 4.33 the same curves are demonstrated for coating F0263. The same trend
seems to be followed as the Tg for the reference, water immersion, and NORSOK tested
specimens was 56.4 ◦C, 71.8 ◦C, and 80.4 ◦C accordingly. Similarly, in Figure 4.34 the
upward glass transition temperature pattern can be observed (4.6).
DMA: Similar results have been obtained by DMA measurements as can be seen in Fig­
ures 4.35 , 4.36 and 4.37. The method that was utilized during DMA testing can be seen
in Table 3.6. Each figure depicts the curves that have been acquired from DMA measure­
ments for coatings 35620, F0263 and F0256 accordingly. The green curves depict the
behavior of the coatings before NORSOK test while the red curves after NORSOK test
have been carried out. Both green and red curves are split into two separate, one for
loss modulus (E”) and one for damping factor (tand). The former shows the energy that
was dispersed during mechanical deformation while the latter is the ratio between loss
modulus (E”) over the energy which was stored (storage modulus: E’) by the system. As
can be seen in Table 4.7, the same trend as DSC measurements showed is followed. The
glass transition temperature increases in all cases except for E” of coating 35620.
The aforementioned results seem to be unexpected since elevated Tg values mean in­
creased levels of cross­linking where the polymer chains form a denser and stronger 3D
network. This procedure should lead to less water absorption by the coating, enhancing
its barrier properties and increasing its ability to resist corrosion but this in not the case.
John B. Enns [70] explains that Tg will increase as the temperature conditions are increas­
ing. The reference free films used for DSC testing in the present project were left to cure
at room temperatures of about 22 ◦C while for NORSOK and water immersion tested
specimens this was not the case. NORSOK free films went through varying temperatures
with the biggest being 60 ◦C during QUV cycles and in the water immersion test, the
temperature was stable at 35 ◦C. Moreover, during DSC and DMA measurements, the
temperature was increased to 150 and 120 ◦C respectively, a fact that also contributed
to the post­curing of the samples. It can be observed that NORSOK free films presented
bigger Tg values when compared to immersion test samples because the temperatures
they underwent were higher. Most importantly, John B. Enns, clarifies the fact that as
Tg is growing, free volume is increasing and therefore the coating is more susceptible
to water absorption and not less. Moreover, he and his colleagues discovered a direct
connection between the rise in curing temperature and an increase in both the equilibrium
moisture content and the glass transition temperature (Tg).

NORSOK tested panels
Figures 4.54, 4.55, 4.56 and 4.57 depict the 12 panels that were subjected to the NORSOK
test after the removal of the non­adherent coating around the artificial scribe.
Prior to measuring the creep corrosion of each coating, it can be seen that the first 9
panels which are associated with coatings 35620 and F0263, illustrate a more extensive
disbonded area when compared with the last three panels that correspond to coating
F0256. Especially in the case of panel 24 coated with paint F0263, the damaged region
around the scribe exhibits the widest extent among all the other panels. On the contrary,
panel 60 seems to have the smallest degraded area. Generally, the fact that paint F0256
presents the lowest corrosion creep values, is counter­intuitive if one takes into account
the impedance results of every corrosion test that took place throughout this project. How­
ever, J.Kittel [75] in his research about the influence of the interface between coatings and
substrates on the corrosion protection properties, mentions that corrosion initiation might
begin from a localized imperfection of the coating. Although this phenomenon should be
Evaluation of protective coatings performance by means of electrochemical characterization
95
==PAGE:112==
detectable by EIS measurements leading to impedance decrease, that is not always the
case. He notes that corrosion can be initiated by a defect in the interface, a procedure
that will not affect the impedance of the coating causing it to fall to lower values, as there
is no increase in the ionic conductivity. Furthermore, impedance measurements are con­
nected with the electrical response of the coating while the creep corrosion test deals with
the physical degradation in a specific area of the coating. Additionally, coating flexibility
is a paramount factor when it undergoes thermal cycling. Coating F0256, as has already
been mentioned, includes substances that increase its flexibility a fact that positively af­

have had a positive effect on corrosion creep in NORSOK but at the expense of reduced
barrier properties which have been seen from all the EIS tests. Consequently, the data
from EIS measurements and corrosion creep tests may not totally align.
To conclude, a lot of research has been done on the correlation between EIS and corrosion
creep evaluation and it can be observed that in the majority of the literature, discrepancies
are present. For instance, in their study, Nie Tang et.al [76] compared EIS results with
adhesion testing evaluation. It was concluded that no direct correlation can be made
between these two procedures due to the mechanical aspects that governed the samples
used.

4.12
Summary of results
4.12.1
Impedance measurements
ACET: Coatings 35620, F0263 and F0256 were subjected to four different corrosion tests
and were evaluated using EIS. First, the results of the ACET tests showed that the protec­
tive properties of coating F0256 were inferior compared to the remaining coatings during
the 350 hours (about 15 days) that the test lasted. The inequality and the poorer per­
formance of coating F0256 can be also seen in both impedance and capacitance values
that were measured. Furthermore, the chronoamperometry versus time plots which were
obtained during DC measurements, align with the outcomes presented by the aforemen­
tioned AC measurements.
Water immersion: Water immersion test results, match these of ACET test, showing that
coating F0256 presented again the lowest impedance and correspondingly the highest
capacitance values over the span of 2100 hours (about 88 days).
NORSOK: The findings of NORSOK test, which lasted 2424 hours (about 101 days),
regarding the electrochemical properties of the coatings were a bit uncertain. The sep­
aration of the impedance curves observed in Impedance over frequency plots was a bit
ambiguous. As explained in section 4.3, the resulting rust residues after exposure, hin­
dered the findings, and as a result, the impedance plots were more scattered compared
to ACET and immersion test. The same issue can be seen in the bar charts that depict
the capacitance and resistance comparison of the 12 panels. Overall, the capacitance
increases over time but in some cases the values are inconsistent, showing extensively
increased capacitance data points in either the third or fourth EIS evaluation. As is ex­
pected the same pattern applies to the resistance comparison bar charts, which show
contradictory decreased values of resistance. To address this problem, a vertical scratch
was intentionally introduced on the surface of each coated panel, effectively disrupting
the continuous rust formation. After the implementation of the scratches, it was possi­
ble to conclude that coating F0256 was once again ranked as the poorest in terms of
impedance decrease over time. Additionally, corrosion creep tests have been performed
for all the panels that underwent NORSOK conditions and were electrochemically mea­
sured. Although both EIS measurements and corrosion creep tests were done according
to standard methods, the results were mismatched. EIS tests showed that coating F0256
presented the worst barrier properties among the three coatings whereas coating 35620
and F0263 were the best. On the contrary, creep corrosion measurements showed that
coating F0256 exhibited the least corrosion around the scribe while coatings 35620 and
Evaluation of protective coatings performance by means of electrochemical characterization
99
==PAGE:116==
especially F0263 the biggest. As already mentioned this inconsistency might originate
from the fact that coating F0256 contained substances that enhanced its flexibility, giving
an advantage when it comes to NORSOK test and thermal cycling. Although flexibility
is crucial regarding corrosion creep, it might be a disadvantage when it comes to barrier
properties. Another reason might be the fact that EIS cannot necessarily detect localized
corrosion that came from defects or discontinuities on the surface of the coating or the
metal/coating interface. Conversely, corrosion creep may have been greatly affected by
these defects.
CDT: The 6 panels that were subjected to CDT testing conditions, underwent 4 EIS evalu­
ations as happened in ACET and NORSOK tests. Once again, the duration of the test was
approximately 3 months and more specifically 2232 hours. The coatings were tested prior
to the test and their impedance reached nearly 1010 Ohm as they were still intact. Bode
plots for each coating and each evaluation showed the same trend as the three previous
electrochemical tests. Once again, the last coating demonstrated the least impedance
values over the duration of the 93 days. The same trend was seen in the impedance over
time plots where the divergence was conspicuous between the three coatings, as the val­
ues of the last one were significantly lower. Regarding the corrosion creep test and EIS
measurements, in contrast with NORSOK tested panels, their results seem to agree as in
both cases the worst coating turned out to be F0256. In this test, the absence of thermal
cycles eliminates the introduction of thermal stresses onto the coatings, making flexibility
a non­crucial property.
To conclude regarding impedance measurements, there was coherence across all the re­
sults obtained by EIS measurements. The key difference is the magnitude of the duration
required for each test, to acquire the electrochemical results. ACET test offered results
in a much shorter time (15 days) while the rest of the tests gave the same results in more
than 80 days. These results suggest the possibility of using ACET test to foresee the
electrochemical behavior of a coating, after exposure to an electrolyte in liquid or fog form
and UV radiation.
4.12.2
Diffusion coefficient
Diffusion coefficient values were acquired from three different setups. Firstly, the diffusion
coefficient of the 6 panels used in the water immersion test was found. Coating F0263,
illustrated the biggest values which were 3.57∗10−13m2/s for panel 22 and 2.74∗10−13m2/s
for panel 28. It should be noted that coating 35620 had the smallest values of 1.35∗10−13
for panel 5 and 1.88 ∗10−13m2/s for panel 16.
Secondly, 7 coated PCBs were utilized in order to calculate the diffusion coefficient of
each coating. As mentioned in section 3.3.1 the whole test lasted 863 hours (about 35
days), while temperature and humidity were changing between 25 to 35 ◦C and 10 to 90%
respectively. Once again, coating F0263 showed the biggest diffusion coefficient values
reaching 3.66 ∗10−13m2/s for the first sample and 3.13 ∗10−13m2/s for the second. In
addition, coating 35620 exhibited the lowest value of 1.33 ∗10−13m2/s for its second PCB
sample.
Lastly, 11 free films have been subjected to a water diffusion test using a ProUmid tester.
The whole test was programmed to last 840 hours during which temperature values
ranged between 25 and 35 ◦C and the humidity varied between 10, 75 and 90%. Nev­
ertheless, as explained in section 3.3.1 certain complications were encountered, and as
a result, the values of diffusion coefficient for each film showed a deviation from the ex­
pected results. Due to these complications, the duration of the experiment was extended
to 944 hours. Furthermore, the transitions between the steps were not automated and as
100
Evaluation of protective coatings performance by means of electrochemical characterization
==PAGE:117==
a result, were not fully controlled.
To conclude, three separate measurements have been done in order to evaluate the dif­
fusion coefficient of each coating under varying temperature and humidity conditions. De­
spite the deviations that occurred during the third experiment the first two, illustrated sim­
ilar results showing that coating F0263 exhibited the biggest diffusion coefficient values
in both 25 and 35 ◦C. The consistency between these two experiments shows that there
is a presence of a trend that can be reliable.
However, these results are inconsistent with the EIS outcomes and the obtained values
of capacitance for the three coatings. This is because during EIS measurements, coating
F0256 illustrated the worst barrier properties during the three different corrosion tests and
not F0263. F. Deflorian [67] in his work, mentions that the coating capacity obtained by
EIS measurements gives important information about the coating’s capability to resist wa­
ter uptake and can be used to evaluate the diffusion coefficient of a coating. Nevertheless,
he showed that the simple model of capacitance evolution during water immersion, which
states that capacitance values increase after long exposure to an electrolyte, seems to
appear inadequate. He also explains that the reason for that might be that the water ab­
sorption process is extremely sophisticated due to the presence of various procedures that
happen during water uptake, sometimes simultaneously, as well as the different amounts
of distribution of water within the coating and dimensional differences.
4.12.3
Blasting grits
For the present thesis, a number of coated panels have been produced in order to evalu­
ate their electrochemical properties during corrosion testing procedures. For their prepa­
ration, two kinds of blasting grits were utilized namely olivine and steel grit. In order to
evaluate the potential impact on the performance of the respective coatings, their electro­

formed. In most electrochemical testing cases the difference between steel and olivine
blasted panels was marginal. This can be seen more clearly in NORSOK and CDT tests,
where the capacitance and resistance values of each grit are presented separately in the
corresponding bar charts.
During the pull­off evaluation after ACET and immersion tested coatings, a pattern re­
garding the two blasting grits has been showcased. In both cases, the steel blasted
panel of the weakest coating illustrated the least adhesion strength values with consid­
erable distinction. This trend is capable of verifying the fact that when it comes to ad­
hesion strength, steel blasted surfaces demonstrate much weaker interface properties
between the substrate and the coating. In his work Aran Kim [74], after conducting an
adhesion strength test he found that the alumina blasted surface, which resembles the
olivine blasted, showed greater values than the steel blasted surface. This difference is
due to the fact that the broken particles that remained on the surface merged with the
adhesive’s adhesion strength, resulting in an increase in the adhesive’s molecular size.
As a result, the sticking force is enhanced due to uneven surface roughness.
Furthermore, a potentiodynamic test has been also performed on each blasting type sur­
face in order to acquire their polarization curves and observe their anodic and cathodic
slopes. During this test, it was found that when a carbon steel panel is blasted with steel
particles, it tends to produce a surface that is slightly more susceptible to corrosion, ac­
cording to their corrosion potentials. In addition, olivine’s anodic Tafel slope demonstrated
a minor sudden increase of potential close to ­0.2 V, which suggests the indication that
a thin passivation layer might be present. During Aran’s Kim [74] electrochemical inves­
tigation, he concluded that abrasive residues, such as alumina or in this case olivine,

the corrosion.

Conclusion
Protective coatings are undoubtedly a very important aspect when it comes to preserving
the integrity and longevity of metal structures. Throughout the years, several tests have
been developed with the purpose to assess the durability of the coatings in different en­
vironments. Nevertheless, most of these tests are time­consuming and in some cases,
their duration can last months or even years to acquire meaningful results. Industries like
automotive, offshore wind farms, or maritime, demand rapid product development through
research. For this reason, the existence of faster corrosion evaluation tests is crucial.
The main purpose of the present project was to investigate the possibility of faster cor­
rosion evaluation using the accelerated corrosion test (ACET) of three different coatings
by comparing its results with tests that take more time namely NORSOK, immersion, ca­
thodic disbondment, and water diffusion. This was achieved by combining Electrochem­
ical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) measurements with analytical tests like pull­off and
corrosion creep evaluation. ACET test predicted the electrochemical properties of each
coating in 15 days while the same results were obtained by the rest corrosion tests in
more than 80 days. In the case of panel 55, which was coated with coating F0256, the
capacitance values were significantly higher, indicating a potential correlation with the
lower adhesion strength observed during the subsequent pull­off tests. Furthermore, the
diffusion coefficient of each coating has been investigated using gravimetric measure­
ments and water diffusion measurements in order to evaluate the coatings’ water uptake.
The resulting values coincided for two of the experiments that were conducted while in
the third, several complications lead to deviations between the findings. Additionally, us­
ing Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) and Dynamic Mechanical Analysis (DMA),
the impact of water uptake on their glass transition temperature (Tg) was also studied.
It was found that their Tg was increased due to elevated temperatures and post­curing
phenomena during testing and water absorption by the coatings. Moreover, the differ­
ences between the two surface preparations, olivine, and steel blasted were analyzed by
implementing elemental composition using SEM/EDS. During this analysis, it was discov­
ered that when olivine particles hit the carbon steel surface, they leave residues which
also influence the adhesion and corrosion properties of the coating. In addition, a poten­

surface. During testing, it was discovered that the surface cleaned with olivine grit blast­
ing exhibited a marginally higher corrosion potential in comparison to steel grit blasting.
This result suggests that the presence of olivine particles created a surface that is slightly
more susceptible to corrosion.
Although ACET test was able to predict the electrochemical properties of the coatings
before and after the exposure much faster than ordinary EIS evaluation, there were some
contradictions regarding their physical properties. These inconsistencies could be found
during the two pull­off tests that were conducted. The worst coating in terms of impedance
values, seemed to be the best with respect to average creep corrosion. Furthermore, the
results from corrosion creep for cathodic­protected panels were more aligned with EIS
measurements showing that coating F0256 had the biggest diameter of disbonded area.
To conclude, the results obtained by EIS measurements during all the corrosion tests that
were performed showed the same trend. The EIS data of coatings 35620 and F0263
indicated better barrier properties when compared to coating F0256. Nevertheless, the
correlation between the electrochemical and physical properties of the coatings in terms

Future Work
During the present thesis, a variety of electrochemical methods were used to evaluate the
performance of the coatings. Although these techniques are quantitative and each of them
presents very useful information about the overall area of the coating, they did not pro­
vide local data. When a coated sample undergoes degradation, corrosion might appear
in small areas in a way that would not impact the results and the general electrochemical
behavior of the coating. That is why local electrochemical techniques such as scanning
electrochemical microscopy (SECM), scanning vibrating electrode technique (SVET), lo­
cal electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (LEIS), scanning Kelvin probe technique or
scanning ion­selective electrode technique (SIET) must be implemented [77].
Furthermore, in order to better understand the electrochemical procedures during coating
degradation and delamination, more complex equivalent circuit models can be involved
while fitting the impedance spectroscopy data acquired from EIS. Increased complexity
will provide a more accurate description of the coated panel’s behavior during exposure.
For example, Warburg element is a paramount component that must be taken into account
when it comes to calculating the diffusion coefficient of a coating. In addition, a variety of
proposed models that describe the characteristics of the interface between coating and
electrolyte can be found in the literature. Examples of these can consist the Kellner’s
method also known as Electric Double Layer Method, Haruyama’s method also known as
Breakpoint Frequency Method, and other modified versions such as Scully’s, Hirayama’s
and Mansfeld’s [20].
Lastly, a number of analytical tests such as SEM/EDS were used throughout this work in
order to observe the surface of the carbon steel panels after exposure and acquire infor­
mation about their elemental composition. Additional non­electrochemical tests though
can provide a better picture regarding water diffusion and the detection of coating incon­
sistencies and imperfections. For this purpose, several versions of the Fourier transform
infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) can be utilized such as Attenuated total reflectance FTIR,
photoacoustic spectroscopy (PAS) or diffuse internal reflectance (DRIFT) [78]. Moreover,
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) can be used as a supplementary imaging technique to
evaluate the surface morphology of the blasted panels.
