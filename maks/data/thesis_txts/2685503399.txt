Abstract
Design and Evaluation of a ﬂoating platform
for IEA 15MW
Christos Vyrgiotis
Floating wind energy is one of the most promising renewable energy sectors.
However the complexity of these structures and the low technological readiness
level, needs a lot of research eﬀort in order to unlock the full potential of this
technology. The main scope of this project is to design and evaluate a typi-
cal ﬂoater (spar buoy) for the IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine. A simple
analytical approach was followed for the design and a high ﬁdelity tool for the
preliminary evaluation under load cases. The concept of the design is to select 3
simpliﬁed ﬂoaters with diﬀerent mass characteristics and to evaluate them. The
design requirements were deﬁned by imposing the horizontal motion and secure
static and hydrostatic stability. Consequently, the aim was twofold. Firstly, to
derive the ballast mass in order to secure the vertical stability and secondly, the
mooring system for the mitigation of the horizontal motion. Three diﬀerent mod-
els were picked through a Pareto Set and they were imported for a preliminary
evaluation in the aero-hydro-servo-elastic tool HAWC2.

Introduction
1.1
Oﬀshore Wind Energy
Oﬀshore wind energy is a one of the most promising renewable energy sectors. At it’s
maximum potential, oﬀshore wind capacity could reach more than 120 GW according to the
International Energy Agency (IEA) [9]. Even if the world only develops its most feasible,
near-shore wind sites, those projects would still improve more electricity than we consume
today. According to projections [9], the future of onshore wind farms is limited, due to the
land constraints as well as public opposition to developments near where people live. In
contrast, oﬀshore wind farms not only have the vast ocean for potential developments, but
also face less resistance because they are located oﬀthe coast, away from where people live
[1].
The main advantage of locating turbines oﬀshore, is that (according to [6]):
• The wind speed is higher and more steady over the ocean, which means that the energy
that could be generated is higher. In addition, due to the lower turbulence, the fatigue
loads applied on the WT components are positively aﬀected.
• The potential available space for the development of new wind energy projects is bigger
which means higher distance between the WTs in a farm, resulting in the wake eﬀects
reduction.
• Landscape and acoustic impacts are smaller, which mitigate some issues related to
social acceptance
• Transportation limitations due to the size of the WT can only be limited by the size
of the vessel.
Built in 1991, Vindeby in Denmark was the world’s ﬁrst oﬀshore wind farm.
The
wind farm started operation in 1991 and cost an estimated €10 million, built by SEAS and
Elkraft. The 11 turbines were erected in 11 days. It was decommissioned for cost reasons
in 2017 after 25 years of operation. Although onshore wind still has the leading role on
wind power industry, oﬀshore wind has also seen an important expansion during the last
decades, especially in Europe. According to WindEurope Annual Oﬀshore statistics in 2019
[1], Europe added 3,623 MW net oﬀshore capacity in 2019. This corresponds to 502 new
oﬀshore wind turbines connected to the grid, across 10 wind farms.Europe now has a total
installed oﬀshore wind capacity of 22,072 MW. This corresponds to 5,047 grid-connected
wind turbines across 12 countries [6].

A wind turbine can be installed oﬀshore by two diﬀerent methods: on a platform con-
nected to the seabed (called a "bottom ﬁxed" foundation) or on a ﬂoating foundation simply
attached to the seabed by mooring lines to hold the assembly in position. At water depths
greater than 35 to 40 metres, the bottom-ﬁxed wind turbine is no longer economically com-
petitive according to [6]. Bottom ﬁxed oﬀshore foundations for wind turbines can be divided
on 3 diﬀerent types according to ﬁgure 2: Monopiles (A), gravity base foundations (B) and
Jackets (C).

1.2
Floating Wind Turbines
Oﬀshore wind industry can be developed much more for the next years. Bottom ﬁxed
foundations are more popular than ﬂoating substructures due to low technology readiness
level (TRL) of the ﬂoater concepts (Figure 3). However the deployment of oﬀshore wind
turbines currently depends on bottom ﬁxed foundations, which are depth limited to shallow
water sites (<40 m). As the accessibility of these sites decreases, ﬂoating wind turbines
could play a crucial role in accessing deeper water sites and achieving the full potential of
oﬀshore wind market [3].
Figure 3: Technology readiness Level of ﬂoating oﬀshore wind concepts. [3]
1.2
Floating Wind Turbines
The complexity of ﬂoating concepts and the fact that in many countries are not encir-
cled by deep waters could not help the deployment of these concepts. Historically,Hywind
Scotland is the world’s ﬁrst commercial wind farm using ﬂoating wind turbines, situated
29 kilometres (18 mi) oﬀPeterhead, Scotland. The farm has ﬁve 6 MW Hywind ﬂoating
turbines with a total capacity of 30 MW [4].
Similar to bottom ﬁxed case, there are some diﬀerent types of ﬂoating foundations.
More speciﬁcally, the main four ﬂoating concepts , which are demonstrated in the ﬁgure 4 ,
are: Barge, Semi-Submersible (Semi-Sub), Spar Buoy and Tension-Leg Platform (TLP).The
key factor that the ﬂoating substructure is classiﬁed is the way to fulﬁl the static stability
requirements in the rotational degrees of freedom (DOFs) and the way to maintain the sta-
bility while aerodynamic thrust is applied on the hub of the wind turbine and creates an
overturning moment.

1.2
Floating Wind Turbines
Figure 4: Main Floating Types [3]
The main characteristics of ﬂoating substructures according to [6] are described below :
• Barges: Buoyancy Stabilized ﬂoating platforms, that they achieve stability with us-
ing the distributed buoyancy, exploiting the weighted water plane area for righting
moment. Having a large second moment of area with respect to the rotational axis,
either due to a large waterplane area or due to smaller cross-sectional areas at some
distance from the system central axis, creates a stabilising righting moment in case of
rotational displacement.They are used into relatively shallow waters and which makes
the installation easier.
• Spar buoys or spars : Floaters with simple and usually cylindrical geometry. They
are exploiting the ballast in order to secure stability are easy to be designed and
manufactured. They are used in deeper waters and this thing makes the transportation
and the assembly much more diﬃcult.
• Semi-Submersibles or semi-subs: Their design can create some challenges due the
complexity of their geometry. They can be used in shallow waters. They maintain
their stability utilizing buoyancy.
• TLPS: Tension leg platforms.Floaters with complex geometry. It’s challenging to
be manufactured . Their design requires focused design on mooring loads because are
mooring-stabilized.
The stabilization of the aforementioned ﬂoating structures, as it refereed, it could be
achieved through ballast, mooring system, buoyancy or a combination of these [4]. It is
represented in Fig. 5.

Where x represents the vector of motions x=[x1, x2, x3, x4, x5, x6]. The vectors M,A,B

vector F represents the external forces that provided by waves and hydrodynamics, mooring

1.4
Motivation and Project Scope
As it refereed earlier in Chapter 1.1, the technological readiness level for the ﬂoating
wind turbines are in not the highest level. Comparing with bottom ﬁxed substructures, only
few full-size models exist and it needs a lot of research eﬀort in order to unlock the full
potential of this technology.
Nowadays, while the wind oﬀshore industry is growing, the scale of the power capacity
is also increasing. This fact makes wind turbines yielding between 8 and 12 MW the norm.
Hence, as the size of the turbine gets bigger, the components adopted on this one regarding
the ﬂoating substructure must ﬁt the new requirements of larger machines.
In July 2020, NREL released the International Energy Agency Wind Technology Col-
laboration Programme 15-megawatt reference turbine, or IEA Wind 15-MW for short, which
features options for both ﬁxed-bottom,[8], and ﬂoating concepts [7]. Both concepts has been
designed in order to require the current technological trends.
To begin with, in order to improve the ﬂoating industry, numerical models and parametriza-
tion studies are needed to be made. The aim is the initial design as well as the veriﬁcation
through load analysis. The next step for the veriﬁcation is to test the numerical model and
evaluate it with experimental results and by providing good agreement could motivate the
industry to step up eﬀorts in order to develop this ﬁeld.
Moreover, the cost of the numerical models is insigniﬁcant comparing with the exper-
imental lab test , thus, companies could decrease the cost following this approach. On the
other hand experimental tests are essential for an accurate evaluation and a proper valida-
tion. As it can be seen in the ﬁgure below a simpliﬁed model and needs to be validated
against high ﬁdelity and experimental models [10].

1.5
Objective
Figure 7: Accuracy as a function of model type [6]
More speciﬁcally, a high ﬁdelity model contains all the information regarding the aero-
dynamics, hydrodynamics, mooring lines, structural behaviour and the applied controller.
All the aforementioned inputs are derived through a high-ﬁdelity tool such as HAWC2 (Hor-
izontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation) and with using it a proper
evaluation can be made.
The scope, as well as, the inspiration for this project is to design a spar buoy in order
to mount the IEA-15MW Wind Turbine Reference. The concept of the project is an ana-
lytical way for the preliminary design and not high ﬁdelity tools due to the fact that the
computational costs are lower. The approach of the project is to design a spar buoy and a
simple mooring system.Then the preliminary evaluation was made with the aid of HAWC2
and through decay tests for 4 degrees of freedom and next applying wind and waves cases.
In that way, a deeper understanding of the physical meaning of topics related to the
structural behaviour of the ﬂoater could be achieved. From the scope of the designer it is
easier to have a bigger overview of the key parameters that can aﬀect the response of a
structure, such as a ﬂoating body, taking into account the mooring loads, the stability, and
the performance under some load cases.
1.5
Objective
The objective of the project is to ﬁnd the best technical solution for a ﬂoating body for
the IEA 15MW reference Wind Turbine.

1.6
Methodology
1.6
Methodology
The methodology that was followed in order to achieve the objective can be brieﬂy
described following:
• Finding required ballast mass in order to secure vertical stability taking into account
the weight of WT components, buoyancy and mooring lines.
• Designing and evaluating the mooring system in order to provide the required horizon-
tal mooring stiﬀness.
• Making a parametric study and evaluating static and hydrostatic stability of the mod-
els.
• Applying Pareto Optimality in order to choose the 3 diﬀerent design conﬁgurations
taking into account the performance and the mass .
• Perform decay tests and Load cases Analyses in HAWC2.
1.7
Structure of the project
Chapter 1 is the introduction and a overview of the current and potential abilities of
the wind oﬀshore industry. The scope and the motivation of the project is also referred.
Chapter 2 presents the required information about the reference wind turbine that
was used.
Chapter 3 explains the whole analytical procedure that was followed in order to select
the mooring system .It is also giving emphasis to the design and Pareto optimality and the
ﬁnal selection of the models.
Chapter 4 presents the evaluation of the ﬂoater through HAWC2.
Chapter 5 is the conclusion of the project. There is a discussion about the interesting

The performance of the rotor is combined with the controller operations and pitch ap-

3.1
Initial Design
In this chapter, the preliminary design of the ﬂoating substructure in order to mount
IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine takes place.The focus lies on the ﬂoating platform and
the mooring system of it, while the wind turbine system remains unchanged and the basic
geometrical properties of the ﬂoater are being modiﬁed through the parametric study.
3.1.1
Philosophy
It was decided to follow the analytical way through MATLAB for the preliminary de-
sign steps as well as for the mooring system design. The main reason that we didn’t use a
traditional aeroelastic tool and we followed this approach is to save computational time but
mainly to obtain a deeper understanding overview for the optimization procedure and the
physical meaning of all the design parameters that got involved into the numerical method.
The concept of the design part, is to evaluate the static and hydrostatic behaviour of
a typical spar buoy. The purpose of this procedure is to end up in 3 models with diﬀerent
mass and static performance and to evaluate them, in the next step through a high ﬁdelity
tool.
3.1.2
Conceptual Design
The ﬂoater is a hollow cylinder and the lower part of it is ﬁlled with ballast in order
to secure the vertical stability. The amount of the ballast mass that was used, was derived
taking into account the summary of the vertical forces that are active on our system.
The main requirements and the constraints that taken into account for the design of
the ﬂoating body are illustrated in this chapter. The ﬁrst criterion that we mentioned, is
the vertical stability of the structure. Taking into consideration that the summary of the
vertical forces should be equal to zero, the total weight of the structure and the force which
provided from the moorings should counteract to the buoyancy force and to make our ﬂoater
stable. The stability of the ﬂoating system must be suﬃcient to keep itself upright, deﬁned
as static equilibrium.
A requirement that has to be taken into account in a design of a ﬂoating body, is the
vertical position of the center of gravity. When the center of buoyancy is below the center
of gravity, then the force components create an overturning moment that rotates the body
even further in the tilted direction. On the other hand when center of gravity is located
below the center of buoyancy then the buoyancy force and the weight of the structure give

In addition, a crucial factor that always needs to be considered is the maximum static

Numerical Model HAWC2-Evaluation
4.1
Introduction
HAWC2 is a high ﬁdelity aero-hydro-servo-elastic tool that evaluates the structural re-
sponse of a wind turbine in time domain. The core of the code was developed mainly within
the years 2003-2007, by the Aeroelastic Design Research Program at DTU Wind Energy,
DTU Risø Campus in Denmark. HAWC2 is developed and distributed by DTU Wind En-
ergy and has been used in numerous academic projects and industrial applications. It can
be used for both design and veriﬁcation purposes.
The term aero-servo-elastic represents the theory of aeroelasticity which is the basis
of HAWC2 in order to perform time domain analyses. The function of HAWC2 takes into
account all the relevant loads that can act on a onshore or oﬀshore wind turbines. It couples
the inertial, elastic, and aerodynamic loads acting while an elastic body is exposed to wind.
Moreover, hydrodynamics and wave loads can be also be integrated through this high-ﬁdelity
tool.
In HAWC2 the structural formulation is based on multibody dynamics. The wind tur-
bine components are divided in bodies each of them have it’s own coordinate system and
orientation. Those bodies are formulated based on Timoshenko beam theory[19].
The aerodynamic loads are being calculated with Blade element momentum theory [20],
taking into account turbulence, dynamic inﬂow, shear eﬀect and other relevant eﬀects. The
hydrodynamics can be derived through either with using Morison equation either, when we
are talking about large and complex oﬀshore structures where the radiation and diﬀraction
eﬀects are more important, with potential ﬂow theory for instance coupling the calculation
with WAMIT [21].

Conclusion and Further Research
5.1
Conclusions and Key Findings
The aim of this project was the preliminary evaluation of three diﬀerent simple spar
buoy numerical models for IEA 15 MW reference wind turbine. These numerical models was
the outcome of a parametric study taking into account the fundamental design requirements.
Starting with a basic approach and have only as variables the height and the diameter
of the spar, a static stability analysis was performed. The required ballast mass in order to
provide vertical stability was calculated. The preliminary design took place by taking into
consideration, static and the hydrostatic stability. In addition, the catenary mooring system
was evaluated by examining the breaking strength of the cable and the horizontal mooring
stiﬀness provided by several mooring conﬁgurations.
The next step was the selection of the concept. Due to performance variation in some
conﬁgurations with the same mass, it was decided to apply Pareto Front in order to obtain
the feasible combinations. The ﬁnal selection of the numerical models from the Pareto Front,
was made take into account cost eﬀectiveness (Fig. 44). Then, 3 conﬁgurations with diﬀer-
ent mass characteristics and performance were selected for the next stage of the preliminary
evaluation.
During the design of a ﬂoating body the main consideration is to always keep it sta-
ble, by ensuring that no sinking and swaying away will occur. By considering only these
two boundaries, the best performance could be achieved by picking the heaviest acceptable
system. However, cost eﬀectivenss should also be considered to ensure the feasibility of the
overall system. Therefore, the ﬁnal conﬁguration selection shall be delivered from the best
balance between mass, translating into cost, and performance.
Considering the geometrical properties that can aﬀect the performance of the ﬂoater, it
is clear that by increasing the diameter the performance is enhanced. Larger diameter lead
to larger waterplane area resulting to higher restoring moment which can bring the body
back to the untilted position.
Regarding the mooring system, mooring lines is the tool that we use in order to apply
this "station keeping". A mooring system can provide the ﬂoater the required horizontal
stiﬀness in order to prevent the horizontal movement. The shape of the cable and its di-
ameter are the key parameters that can aﬀect its stiﬀness. Larger diameter means higher
weight of the cable into water resulting in larger tension and consequently in higher stiﬀness
provided to the system.
Considering the preliminary evaluation in chapter 4, it can be concluded that the struc-
tural behaviour of the ﬁrst two models is not satisfactory. The resonance that was occurred
by the coupling of the frequency excitation and the natural frequency of the wind turbine

generator, resulted to an undesired performance. Due to the resonance, the amplitude of the
motion was being increased as a function of time resulting negative damping and instability.
This fact showed up that during the design procedure the natural frequencies of the wind
turbine components should be considered in order to avoid these kind of eﬀects. The relevant
issue that occurred it could be ﬁxed by tuning the features of the controller and adjust it’s
natural frequency. Regarding the 3rd model, the performance under these load cases that
were applied is satisfactory. The vertical stability has been achieved and the maximum surge
displacement was inside the expected range we have deﬁned.
5.2
Further Research
To begin with, it is crucial to clarify all the assumptions and the limitations of the
numerical models that was created for this project. A simpliﬁed procedure was followed in
order to determine the dimensions and the ballast of the numerical models. Assumptions and
simpliﬁcations were needed to be made due to the limitation of the duration of the project.
Thus some general suggestions has to be noted for further development of the parametric
study as well as the whole procedure.
First of all, in order to have a more accurate and optimized solution, it needs to involve
more variables into the design procedure. More speciﬁcally, the ballast density could be a
design variable. It could be a range of values according to the material that we are using in
order to satisfy the required ballast mass but with diﬀerent ballast density through a mixture
of ballast types with each other, with or without water [15]. By including this parameter we
enable the cost reduction consideration which could be taken into account in this study.
A requirement that it could be taken into consideration at the preliminary design stage
could be the dynamic response of the system. Namely, the acceleration in the tower top and
especially at the nacelle is a crucial parameter for ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbines. Taking
into account the presence of sensitive components into the nacelle (gearbox, generator etc.)
the motion have to be mitigated because when it exceeds a speciﬁc threshold the wind tur-
bine has to stop it’s operation. This threshold for the maximum nacelle acceleration at the
tower top ,according to [23], is about 0.20 and 0.30 times the gravitational acceleration g
and in particular conservatively 1.962 m/s2 .
As it refereed earlier, it is critical to have a clear overview of the natural frequencies
of the wind turbine components. In order to avoid resonance phenomenon it is obvious
that during the parametric study it should be considered that the eigenfrequencies of every
numerical model in each motion should be outside the regions of the natural frequencies
and relative excitation of the wind turbine. Therefore, the eigenvalue problem should be
considered as a criterion that it needs to be included.
In addition, regarding the modelling in HAWC2 , the calculation of the hydrodynamic
properties and the coeﬃcients including added mass, damping and wave excitation is neces-
54

sary. It could lead us to more accurate and reliable results. This is also true regarding the
structural damping that should be calibrated.
In my concluding remarks it is worth noting that, after the further development of
the numerical model, for design purposes the life of a ﬂoating oﬀshore wind turbine can be
represented by a set of external situations that may experience. The design load cases concern
the set of design situations that should be deﬁned from the combinations of operational modes
with external conditions [24]. The examination of the ﬂoating model under relevant load
cases would be the next evaluation step.
