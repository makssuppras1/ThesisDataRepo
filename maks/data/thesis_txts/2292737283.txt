Abstract
This thesis presents the design, fabrication and characterisation of a novel dual mode 1-D
capacitive micromachined ultrasonic transducer (CMUT) array for tissue harmonic imaging
(THI). CMUTs have the potential for replacing piezoelectric transducers which are the current
technology used in commercial medical ultrasonography. The CMUT oﬀers many advantages
over piezoelectric transducers: higher bandwidth, more design ﬂexibility, ease of fabrication,
potential for integration with electronics and reduced cost. THI is widely used within medical
ultrasonography and the CMUT must therefore be able to make high quality images using this
technique.
Arrays with 192 elements were fabricated utilising the fusion bonding method and standard
cleanroom processes.
Acoustic measurements revealed that the pressure generated by the
transducer was suﬃcient to generate harmonics in the medium which are used for THI.
The dual mode design consists of one cell type optimised for receive operation and one cell
type optimised for transmit operation with two diﬀerent resonance frequencies and the same
pull-in voltage. This was achieved by fabricating transmit cells with an added mass on the
plate which also potentially can increase the transmit pressure.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasound Transducers (CMUTs) have since their emergence in
the mid 1990s attracted a lot of attention due to their potential for replacing existing trans-
ducer technologies within a wide range of application areas including: medical ultrasonography,
airborne applications, biological and chemical sensors and ﬂuid and gas ﬂowmeters [1, 2, 3].
The main application of the CMUT has become medical imaging and therapy [4].
Transducers made of piezoelectric materials are the current commercial standard within med-
ical imaging. CMUTs have several advantages compared with these conventional piezoelectric
transducers including: higher bandwidth, more design ﬂexibility, ease of fabrication, potential
for integration with electronics and reduced cost [5, 6].
Even with all these advantages of
the CMUT compared with the piezoelectric transducer the CMUT is still not in widespread
commercial use. This calls for further research of the CMUT.
In this chapter the basic concepts of acoustics and medical imaging using ultrasound are given
with focus on an imaging technique called tissue harmonic imaging. This imaging technique is
widely used in commercial medical ultrasound scanners [7, 8]. It is estimated that about 80%
of all commercial ultrasound scanners use or have the capability of utilising tissue harmonic
imaging [9]. It is therefore crucial that CMUTs, using tissue harmonic imaging, are able to
create images that are better or as good as the images created by commercial piezoelectric
transducers.
The working principle, geometry and fabrication of conventional piezoelectric
transducers and CMUTs are presented and the two transducer types compared. Dual mode
CMUTs are presented as an optimised design for tissue harmonic imaging. The history of
CMUTs is brieﬂy given. The state of the art of CMUTs used for tissue harmonic imaging and
general dual mode CMUTs is presented. The previous work on CMUTs at DTU is shortly
stated as this serves as a starting point for many design choices. Finally the aim and structure
of the thesis is presented.
1.1
Ultrasound for medical imaging
Ultrasound is vibration of matter in the form of waves vibrating at a frequency above the human
audible range (≳20 kHz) [10, 11]. These waves create a local change of pressure, density and
particles speed. Energy is transported but no mass is being transported since the particles of
the medium oscillate around a ﬁxed mean position [10].
In medical ultrasound imaging the medium, in which the waves are propagating, is tissue.
The propagation of acoustic waves in human tissue resembles that of water and the waves

In conventional imaging short waveforms called pulses are transmitted and the reﬂections called
echoes are received and recorded. This is called a pulse echo system where the transmitted
wave is reﬂected due to a change in acoustic impedance of the tissue. The shorter the pulse
the higher the spatial resolution, and a short pulse is therefore wanted.
Tissue harmonic imaging (THI) is an imaging technique where the echoes used for imaging
are the harmonics generated in the medium due to non-linear eﬀects. These harmonics have
integer multiple frequencies of the transmitted frequency. The second harmonic is typically
used for imaging and this means that the frequency of the received echo is twice that of the
transmitted pulse. A wave with a higher frequency results in a better resolution but is also
attenuated more than a wave with a lower frequency. In THI this is utilized by transmitting
a low frequency pulse which in the medium generates harmonics of higher frequency. The
lower frequency transmitted wave is attenuated less in the medium than the harmonics and
can therefore penetrate deeper into the tissue. The harmonics, which are generated in the
medium, now only need to propagate from the imaged area to the transducer. Therefore, they
are not attenuated as much as if they had to propagate to and from the imaged area. Due
to the higher frequency of the harmonics a higher resolution is obtained due to the shorter
wavelength associated with a higher frequency. This is the fundamental concept of THI [7].
1.2
Transducers
A transducer converts one form of energy to another. That is, an energy is being transferred
between two energy domains [12]. Piezoelectric transducers and CMUTs both transform elec-
trical energy to mechanical/strain energy to acoustical energy when transmitting a pulse and
the opposite is happening when receiving an echo. The way these conversions are accomplished
is diﬀerent for the piezoelectric transducer and the CMUT as will be explained in this section.
1.2.1
The Piezoelectric Transducer
When a material exhibits a coupling between an internal electric polarisation and mechanic
strain, it is said to be piezoelectric. This is due to a lack of a center of inversion symmetry.
A piezoelectric material can be used both as an actuator and a sensor. When an ultrasound
piezoelectric transducer transmits a wave, a voltage is applied over the piezoelectric material
and the material is hereby strained. If the applied voltage is alternating at some frequency,
the strain will likewise alternate at the same frequency. The surface of the material will create

The voltage amplitude depends on the amount of strain which again

1.2.2
The Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducer
A CMUT consists of two electrodes with a vacuum gap in between. This constitutes a capacitor.
A sketch of a cross section of a CMUT can be seen in Figure 1.1(b). The top electrode is
ﬂexible and can deﬂect by either applying an external pressure or voltage. If a DC bias voltage
is applied between the two electrodes they are attracted due to the electrostatic force between
them. The stiﬀness of the top electrode provides a restoring force on the top electrode acting
to restore the top electrode to the non-deﬂected state. The dimensions of the the top electrode
determine the resonance frequency and the gap height and plate dimensions determine the
voltage at which the top electrode touches the bottom electrode. This voltage is called the
pull-in voltage and is together with the resonance frequency important characteristics of a
CMUT.
When an AC voltage is applied the CMUT transmits a pressure wave. The top electrode will
have a deﬂection with the same frequency as the applied AC voltage. This movement of the
plate creates a pressure wave in the surrounding medium.
When the CMUT receives a pressure wave varying in time from the surrounding medium the
top electrode is deﬂected. A DC bias voltage is applied to the CMUT. The deﬂection of the
top electrode due to the pressure gives rise to a change in capacitance of the CMUT which
again gives rise to a change in charge on the plates. A corresponding voltage change can be
read out in an external circuit.
Two main methods for fabricating CMUTs exists. Both methods use micromachining tech-
niques used in standard cleanroom fabrication. These two methods are the sacriﬁcial release
process and wafer bonding process [4].
The sacriﬁcial release method consists of creating a cavity by etching a sacriﬁcial layer covered
by a plate layer. A top electrode can then be deposited on the plate since the plate material is
not in general conducting. A sketch of a cross section of a CMUT fabricated by the sacriﬁcial
layer process can be seen in Figure 1.2(a). Examples of typical materials used in the sacriﬁcial
release method are: polycrystalline silicon for the sacriﬁcial layer, silicon nitride for the plate
and aluminium for the top electrode. The drawback of the sacriﬁcial release method is that the
the uniformity, thickness and stress of the deposited layers can not be controlled very precisely
aﬀecting the resonance frequency and pull-in voltage of the CMUT [4].
The wafer bonding method consist of bonding a silicon on insulator (SOI) wafer and a normal
silicon wafer. An oxide layer is grown and cavities are deﬁned by an etch in the oxide. Bonding
the two wafers and subsequently removing the handle and BOX layer of the SOI wafer results
in a structure seen in Figure 1.2(b). The silicon wafers used are typically highly doped which
decreases the resistivity. The gap height can be controlled more precisely than for the sacriﬁcial

1-D array transducers are made up
of long narrow elements placed side by side. These elements either consist of a piezoelectric

1.3
History and state of the art
The ﬁrst CMUTs were designed and fabricated by the sacriﬁcial release methods by a group
at Stanford University led by Professor Butrus T. Khuri-Yakub in 1994 presented in the paper
[16]. Due to advances in microfabrication technology and MEMS (Micro Electro-Mechanical
Systems) technology, capacitive devices such as CMUTs could be realised [6]. Since then a lot
of research has been conducted in order to model, design and fabricate CMUTs. The ﬁrst 1-D
array elements were presented in the following years after the initial discovery in 1994. The
ﬁrst 2-D array elements were fabricated at the end of the 1990s [17].

The use of CMUTs for THI has been investigated due to the large bandwidth of the CMUT

The main application of dual mode CMUTs has been to simultaneously be able to do imaging

another frequency [24, 25].
No papers were found in which dual mode CMUTs were used for THI.
Simulations and measurements have shown that adding a mass to the center of the CMUT plate

pressure of the cell due to a more piston-like movement of the plate [25, 26, 27]. Figure 1.5
shows a CMUT with an added mass on the plate. An increased transmit pressure is ideal for
THI as high pressures are needed for harmonic generation. Such complex designs are easily
fabricated with the use of micromachining technology and fabrication techniques.
In 2007 the added mass was proposed by Wong et al. [25] to be fabricated by electroplating.
In 2010 the same group fabricated CMUTs with Au masses on the plates using sputtering to
deposit the Au[26]. As far as the author knows the arrays fabricated in this project are the
ﬁrst CMUT arrays fabricated with electroplated masses.

B-mode imaging
In B-mode or brightness mode imaging the value of the amplitude of the received signal for
each scan line for a speciﬁc time is converted to a grey scale value. By scanning multiple lines
and converting the time between the transmitted and received signal to depth information,
a two dimensional grey scale image can be constructed. The amplitude/brightness is then a
measure of how much of the transmitted signal was reﬂected from a position in the tissue.
When calculating the depth, the time between transmit and receive is assumed to be related to
a constant speed of sound in the tissue. If the speed of sound is known and the time diﬀerence
is measured it is easy to calculate a corresponding depth. Using an array transducer multiple
image lines are made and joined to form an image.
Tissue harmonic imaging
In this section the working principle and advantages of tissue harmonic imaging is introduced
based on [7, 31, 8].
Tissue harmonic imaging is an imaging technique in which an acoustic wave, of frequency f0
(called the fundamental frequency), is transmitted in the tissue. During propagation in the
non-linear tissue (see Section 2.1.1) the wave will be distorted and harmonics will emerge. The
harmonics will have frequencies of n · f0 where n = 2, 3 .... That is a higher frequency than the
fundamental frequency. The harmonics can be reﬂected and by selectively using the received
harmonic waves for imaging, better quality images can be obtained. The second harmonic (2f0)
is often used, as is the case throughout this project. The better image quality covers several
improvements compared with conventional B-mode imaging e.g.: higher resolution, increased
penetration depth, reduced near ﬁeld clutter and increased border delineation [7].
Using THI a high penetration depth and simultaneously a high resolution can be achieved.
The transmitted fundamental frequency is lower than the received frequency and can therefore
penetrate deeper into the tissue because it undergoes less attenuation, see Section 2.2.1. The
higher frequency of the harmonic ensures a better resolution than if only the fundamental
was used for imaging, due to the higher frequency (lower wavelength), see Section 2.2.1. THI
therefore rely on the fact that the second harmonics are created in the tissue close to the object
of interest. The higher penetration depth and resolution are the main motivations for using
THI.

CMUT theory
In this chapter the physics behind the CMUT will be explained. It is important to understand
the working principles behind the CMUT in order to make a model that can form the basis for
a design. First plate deﬂection theory is shown for the general case and then for speciﬁc ge-

stable position can be calculated from electrostatic theory. The resonance frequency is found
using the Rayleigh-Ritz method. Finally a lumped element model of the CMUT is presented
and used to predict the behaviour of the CMUT.
Three types of modelling are generally used to model the CMUT: Analytical models, lumped
element models and ﬁnite element models (FEM). This section presents an analytical model
based on accurate plate deﬂection calculations and a lumped element model. A full electro-
mechanical-acoustic FEM is developed in Section 4.2.
3.1
Deﬂection - Plate equation
The deﬂection proﬁle for a plate can be found by solving the plate equation. Once the deﬂection
proﬁle is found the capacitance can be calculated. When the capacitance is obtained every
electrostatic quantity can be found such as the spring constant, stable position and pull-in
voltage. Using the deﬂection proﬁle and the Rayleigh–Ritz method the unbiased resonance
frequency can be found. Being able to calculate the deﬂection proﬁle is therefore crucial and
a starting point for analytical modelling.
The plate equations (isotropic and anisotropic) in this section are both based on Kirchhoﬀ’s
plate theory. The most important assumptions made when deriving the plate equation are:
The deﬂection of the plate is small compared with the thickness of the plate, shear strains are
assumed negligible and plane stress is assumed. That is, only stress in the plane of the plate
is considered [34]. If the plate is much thinner than the lateral dimensions these assumptions
are met.
The plates are subjected to a uniform load/pressure and clamped at the rim of the cavity, see
Figure 3.1. At the rim the deﬂection is zero and the slope is zero. This is the mathematical
interpretation of the clamped boundary condition. [35]

3.2.2
General electrostatic analysis
In a CMUT several forces act on the plate. An external pressure, p, from the medium presses
down on the plate causing it to bend downward. The electrostatic force acts on the plate and
bottom electrode attracting them to each other. This also causes the plate to bend downward.
The electrostatic force scales with the square of the applied voltage. This means that the
electrostatic force always attracts the two electrodes towards each other independent of the
polarity of the applied voltage. This force is therefore acting in the same direction (from the
point of view of the plate) as the pressure force. The last force acting on the plate is the spring
force originating from the stiﬀness of the plate. This force is acting opposite of the pressure
force and electrostatic force and acts to restore the plate to a non-deﬂected state.
Associated with these forces are potential energies. The total potential energy, Ut, is given as:

The arrays in this project are optimised for THI. Dual model arrays with two resonance fre-

4.1
General design
The design starting point for this project is the commercial available BK 9032 transducer. The
outer dimensions, resonance frequency and number of elements are chosen so that they match
this transducer. The speciﬁcations can be seen in Table 4.1.
Transmit and receive immersion frequencies of 3 MHz and 6 MHz are chosen, respectively. This

Finite element modelling
In this section the Finite Element Model (FEM) that was developed during the project is

The dual mode design is composed of two types of cells within the same element: One optimised
for transmitting and one optimised for receiving. The transmit cell should be able to transmit
a high pressure while the receive cell should receive the incoming pressure wave and ideally not
transmit any pressure as this would be transmitted with the second harmonic frequency. Both
cells are to be fabricated on the same wafer and due to the fabrication method the receive and
transmit cells will have the same gap height. The transmit and receive cells should have the
same pull-in voltage and two diﬀerent resonance frequencies. The pull-in voltage should be the
same for the receive and transmit cells so that the receive sensitivity and transmit pressure is
maximized. The thickness of the plate is ﬁxed and another degree of design freedom is needed
in order to meet the above mentioned design requirements.
By increasing the plate thickness, which is equivalent to increasing the mass of the plate, the
cells will transmit a higher maximum pressure [40]. A design with an added mass on top of the
plates of the transmit cells should therefore increase the transmitted pressure [25, 26]. In order
to simultaneously obtain the desired pull-in voltage and resonance frequency, for the transmit
and receive cells respectively, the added mass should be able to vary both in height and in
lateral size. This gives the extra degree of freedom needed in the design. A mass is not added
to the receive cells because a higher transmit pressure from these is unwanted and the extra
mass could potentially decrease the receive sensitivity [40].
4.2.1
FEM and analytical modelling
The theory presented in Section 3 was based on analytical expressions. The analytical expres-
sions for the deﬂection and electrostatic parameters for the anisotropic case of silicon ﬁt very
well with the results from FEM. The analytical expressions could therefore be used to design
the CMUTs. The main reason why FEM is chosen in this project instead of analytical expres-
sions, as a method of designing the CMUTs, is that the transmit cells have a more complicated
geometry than the analytical expressions normally describe. The added mass can both vary
in height and position on the plate, see Figure 4.1(a). The mass will have a ﬁnite mass and
stiﬀness which, in general, will be diﬀerent from that of the plate. The eﬀective stiﬀness of the
mass-plate system will vary with the height of the mass and position on the plate. Finding the
deﬂection proﬁle, electrostatic properties and resonance frequency using analytical expressions
is very diﬃcult and would result in extremely complicated expressions.
Lumped element models can couple the diﬀerent physical energy domains together as seen
in Section 3.5. An assumption is often made that the acoustic radiation impedance is that
of the plane-wave radiation impedance [40].
When the dimensions of the elements in the
transducer are comparable or smaller than the wavelength of sound this assumption does
not hold, as discussed in Section 3.5. Using FEM, the radiation impedance and the mutual
acoustic radiation impedance between neighbouring cells can be taken into account, which is
traditionally not done in lumped element models.
FEM has the possibility of including elastic boundary conditions of the plate which are typically
omitted in analytical models. Elastic boundary conditions eﬀectively make the plate less stiﬀ
compared with a plate with clamped boundary conditions. Having a plate with elastic boundary
conditions is equivalent to increasing the radius of the plate [35].
The natural frequency of the plate could be found using an analytical expression e.g. Equation
3.39 for a square plate. But designing the CMUT using this expression would result in a too
low resonance frequency because spring softening is not included in the analytical expression.
Spring softening and elastic boundary conditions are easily included in FEM.

The COMSOL model developed in this project is based on a model created by Dr. Anders Lei

The geometry of the model is shown in Figure 4.1. It is only necessary to simulate one fourth

Boundary conditions
The boundary conditions are divided into two main categories: Mechanical and acoustic. The
mechanical boundary conditions are that: the two outer sides of the quarter cell (the ones
not located on the dashed line in Figure 4.1(b)) are clamped while the bottom of the cell is
clamped as well. The plate and oxide at the top of the cell are free to move, which means that
elastic boundaries are used as previously discussed.
The acoustic domain consists of a hemisphere of air or water placed on top of the CMUT.
The acoustic boundary condition consists of a perfectly matched layer (PML) at the rim of
the sphere. All incoming waves are absorbed at the PML and no reﬂections can therefore
occur at this boundary. This boundary mimics the behaviour of a single isolated CMUT with
no neighbours.
Since the cell is not exposed to a mutual acoustic radiation impedance of
neighbouring cells the acoustic bandwidth will be lower than what is found experimentally, see
e.g. [43]. This is due to a lower real part of the acoustic impedance which in turn gives a
higher quality factor due to the lower damping from the medium (see Section 3.5). A higher
quality factor results in a lower bandwidth.
Another possible acoustic boundary condition is one that resembles the behaviour of an inﬁnite
array. The cell would then experience an acoustic impedance corresponding to having an inﬁnite
number of neighbouring cells. In this case the bandwidth would be higher than for the single
cell boundary condition described above.

The diﬀerence in acoustic bandwidth, for the two boundary conditions, is due to a diﬀerence
in the real part (resistive) of the acoustic impedance. A simpliﬁed way of looking at the two
acoustic boundary condition cases is that the single cell behaves like a small (relative to λ) area
piston radiator and the inﬁnite array case behaves like a big (relative to λ) piston radiator.
This diﬀerence in eﬀective area results in a diﬀerence in resistive/real part of the acoustic
impedance, see [45] and Section 3.5. The resistive acoustic impedance directly inﬂuences the
damping from the medium of the cell and hereby the quality factor. The damping determines
how much energy is lost per oscillation cycle and the quality factor is deﬁned as the ratio of
how much energy is stored in a system to how much energy is dissipated per cycle. The quality
factor is related in an inversely proportional way to the bandwidth and a higher damping
therefore results in a lower quality factor which results in a broader bandwidth.
Electric terminals
Two electrical terminals are connected to the cell. One terminal is connected to the plate
supplying a positive DC bias voltage and an AC voltage, where the AC voltage is added as a
harmonic perturbation. The other terminal is grounded and connected to the bottom electrode.
This is sketched in Figure 4.1(a). The DC bias voltage is set to a constant fraction of 80% of
the pull-in voltage while the AC voltage is 50% of the pull-in voltage.
Physics coupling
Three diﬀerent physics are used which are directly comparable with the three diﬀerent energy
domains in the lumped element model (Section 3.5). The electrical domain is coupled to the
mechanical domain by the electrostatic force. As discussed in Section 3.1 this force deﬂects
the plate and is dependent on the electric ﬁeld and hereby the applied voltage.
The mechanical domain is coupled to the acoustic domain by a normal acceleration of the plate-
electrode-mass system. The acceleration is normal to the plane of the plate, and is present
due to the electrostatic force acting on the plate. A coupling from the acoustic domain to the
mechanical domain is achieved by applying a pressure from the acoustic domain on top of the
plate. In this way the mechanical and acoustic domain have a two-way coupling.
Solvers
Three diﬀerent functions are used to ﬁnd the pull-in voltage, receive sensitivity spectrum and
transmit pressure spectrum. The way these functions work will now be explained shortly.
The pull-in voltage can be found in several ways. One way is to vary the DC bias voltage and
obtain the corresponding deﬂection. When the applied voltage reaches the pull-in voltage the
plate will go in pull-in and the simulation will crash. This is not an elegant way of solving the
problem. Another way is to set a value for the deﬂection and simulate what the corresponding
voltage needed for this deﬂection would be. In this way the pull-in voltage can be found and
fed into the model and used in subsequent studies. These studies can then use this pull-in
voltage to calculate the DC bias voltage, since this is deﬁned as a constant fraction of the
pull-in voltage.
The receive sensitivity spectrum is found by calculating the change in charge on the plate and
afterwards ﬁnding the corresponding voltage change. A constant DC bias voltage is applied
to the plate and the amount of charge on the plate changes as a function of frequency when a
pressure wave is applied on the plate surface. The voltage change per pressure is the receive
sensitivity and by plotting this sensitivity as a function of frequency the receive spectrum is
found.

5.2.1
Electroplating
Electroplating is a deposition technique which can be used to deposit thick layers, ≫1 µm, of
a conducting material. Electroplating is, in this project, used to deposit Ni on the transmit
cell plates. A conductive seed material is needed in order to deposit the Ni.
A short introduction to the basic working principles of electroplating is given. The experimental
results, at the end of the section, show the Ni thickness as a function of deposition time.
Working principles
Electroplating is an electrochemical deposition of material onto a conducting sample. This
section is based on Danchip’s knowledge base LabAdviser [46]. The sample works as the cathode
where Ni ions (in an aqueous electrolyte) are reduced to metallic Ni at the cathode/sample:
Ni2+(aq) + 2e−→Ni(s)
(2.1)
This deposits metallic Ni on the sample when a current runs. Using a resist mask, deﬁned
by photolithography, a current can only run where there is no resist. This is used to deﬁne
electroplated patterns.
The anode is made of Ni pellets which, as the sample, are in contact with the aqueous elec-
trolyte. At the anode metallic Ni is oxidized to Ni ions:
Ni(s) →Ni2+(aq) + 2e−

Photolithography
In this section a short introduction to photolithography is given. The results of a dose test is

Photolithography is a process step repeated many times during a fabrication process. This step
results in a pattern of a mask being transferred to a photoresist on the wafer. Photolithography
consists of several steps where the three main steps are: Spinning on photoresist, exposing the
resist to UV light through a mask and developing the resist in a chemical developer. The
photolithographic masks were designed in Chapter 4.
After light exposure the resist is developed. In this project both a spray puddle developer and
a developer where the wafer is submerged are used, namely AZ 351B and AZ 726 MIF. The
latter consists of 2.38% Tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAH) in water with various other
chemicals added to e.g. increase wetting of the surface. The developer removes the soluble
part of the resist.

thickness of 10 µm.

5.2. Process development
Theory of fusion bonding
Fusion bonding or direct bonding of two wafers with a hydrophilic SiO2 layer on the surface is
accomplished via a two step process. First a pre-bonding process is performed at a relatively
low temperature (50◦C in this case). The silanol groups (Si-OH) on the wafer surface have an
adsorbed layer of H2O. Hydrogen bonds and van der Waals forces between the water molecules
on two SiO2 surfaces attract the surfaces to each other when in close proximity. To bring the
wafers close to each other a piston presses on the backside of one of the wafers. This makes
the two wafers stick together and the wafers are said to be pre-bonded but stronger covalent
bonds are wanted.
The second step is to anneal the pre-bonded wafers at a high temperature (1100◦C in this case).
During annealing the adsorbed H2O layer is desorbed from the surface. The SiO2 surface is
now terminated by silanol groups but without the adsorbed H2O layer. The two oxide layers
react to form covalent siloxane (Si-O-Si) bonds:
Si −OH + OH −Si →Si −O −Si + H2O
(2.3)
The water molecule diﬀuses away and the strong covalent siloxane bonds bond the two wafers
strongly together.
Bonding requirements
After the wafers are bonded they should stick together and no voids should be present. Voids
are regions where the two wafers are not bonded together and can appear if the bonded surface
was contaminated before bonding. This contamination could for example be a particle. The
voids are typically much larger than the contaminant making them a problem if located at a
critical area on the wafer such as at a device. Cleanliness is therefore very important in order
to increase the success of the bonding. Prior to the bonding step the wafers can be cleaned by
an RCA clean (see the next section) and the handling of the wafers during bonding should be
performed to minimise contamination.
The roughness of the wafer should be minimal if bonding is to succeed. If a wafer surface is
very rough it has fewer points where the wafer surfaces are in close contact. New wafers are
so smooth that they can be bonded without problems.
A suﬃcient area should be bonded in order for the wafers to stick together. The 5 µm minimum
distance between neighbouring cells (from Chapter 4) is based on the experience that the
bonding area should not be much smaller than this.
Bonding in the cleanroom
After a cleaning of the wafers they are transported in a clean wafer box to the wafer bonding
machine which in this cleanroom is a 520 Hot Embosser from EVG that can do fusion bonding,
anodic bonding, and imprinting. Two wafers to be bonded are placed on a wafer chuck so
the surfaces to be bonded are facing each other.
The wafers are aligned to each other by
aligning the primary ﬂats as seen in Figure 5.5 where the top wafer is being placed. Flags,
which are small metal pins, separate the wafers while outside the vacuum chamber. The wafer
chuck is manually transferred to the vacuum chamber.
In the vacuum chamber a vacuum
of 1 × 10−2 mbar is maintained while the wafers are heated to a temperature of 50◦C and a
piston applies a force of 1500 N to the backside of the top wafer for 5 min. This describes the
pre-bonding step.

5.2.4
Wafer cleaning
Several wafer cleaning steps are necessary in the process. One particular wafer cleaning proce-
dure is used, namely the RCA clean. RCA is an abbreviation of Radio Corporation of America.
This cleaning step serves to remove organic particles, metals and alkali ions and cations.
RCA working principle
In this section an introduction to the working principle of the RCA clean and how a RCA clean
is performed in the cleanroom is presented. The following is based on [46, 48].
An RCA clean consists of four steps. The steps are: SC-1(standard clean-1), oxide strip, SC-2,
oxide strip. The wafers are rinsed in deionized (DI) water between each step. SC-1, SC-2 baths
are prepared before starting the RCA clean.
SC-1 consists of: NH4OH : H2O2 : H2O in the ratio 1:1:5. The solution is heated to 70◦C and
the wafers submerged for 10 min. The purpose of SC-1 is to oxidise and remove organic particles
and metals e.g. Au, Ag and Cu etc. The surface of the wafers is oxidised. Simultaneously the
newly formed oxide is etched. Any particles on or in this oxide are hereby removed from the
surface.

==PAGE:82==
Chapter 5. Fabrication and process development
5.2. Process development
The oxide strip of the surface is done by submerging the wafers in a 5% HF solution for 30 s.
This removes the SiO2 from the wafer surface which can contain contaminants. For some of
these HF dips the submerged time is only 15 s, see Appendix C for details. This is done in
order to avoid etching too much of the SiO2.
SC-2 consists of: HCL : H2O2 : H2O in the ratio 1:1:5. The solution is heated to 70◦C and the
wafers submerged for 10 min. The purpose of SC-2 is to remove alkali ions.
The oxide is removed as earlier by a HF dip. However for some of the RCA cleans this second
HF dip is omitted in order to reduce the etching of the SiO2, see Appendix C for details.
Particle contamination
The dedicated RCA spin dryer was suspected to contaminate wafers with particles when dry-
ing them after the RCA clean. This was proposed by Christian Østergaard and Alexandros
Kyriakidis. A small study was conducted in order to measure the degree of contamination of
this spin dryer.
A clean new wafer had the number of particles on the surface measured by means of a light
point defect measurement. A laser of wavelength 488 nm is scanned over the wafer. Particles
will scatter the light and this can be detected. The instrument KLA-Tencor Surfscan 6420
was used for this measurement with an exclusion zone of 4 mm at the rim of the wafer. After
the particles had been measured for the clean new wafer, the same wafer was subjected to a
standard drying routine in the RCA spin dryer. The particle count was measured again after
this. For particle sizes of [0.49 µm; 8.2 µm] the results are:
Number of particles before spin dryer = 10
Number of particles after spin dryer = 1724
Particles of several microns can easily result in voids after bonding.
The RCA spin dryer
is the last step of the RCA clean which comes right before the bonding step. This particle
contamination problem is therefore potentially critical for the bonding process. It turned out
not to be a problem and the wafers could still be bonded.
A new RCA spin dryer was installed by Danchip shortly after this discovery.

Ni mass height
Problems with short circuiting of the elements and missing Ni masses arose during fabrication.
Figure 5.18 shows an optical microscope image of part of an array where the elements are short
circuited and some Ni masses are missing. The short circuits are due to the Au, and therefore
also the Si device layer, not being etched. The most obvious reason for this was that the resist
had not been removed on top of the Au at the kerf where these short circuits are found. Figure
5.19 shows two optical microscope images of the kerf after the Au etch. The resist is still on
the wafer and used for the subsequent Si etch step. The kerf is narrower than the designed
4 µm. In Figure 5.19(b) the kerf is as small as 1 µm. The kerf is most narrow where two Ni
masses are closest to each other on adjacent elements. This is the same position where the
short circuiting occurs, see Figure 5.18.
The resist should be able to ﬂow over and around the Ni masses and cover the wafer in a layer
with a somewhat uniform thickness. If the masses are higher than the resist the ﬂow of the
resist can be disrupted resulting in a non-uniform resist thickness. At the points in between
two adjacent masses the ﬂow of resist could be hindered the most. This would result in a very
thick resist at the kerf between two adjacent masses resulting in a too small kerf as seen in the
ﬁgures.

6.3. FEM and measurement comparison
Discussion
The impedance measurements show an asymmetric behaviour for some of the elements for
the two polarities. Both a time independent and time dependent phenomenon is observed.
For the time dependent phenomenon the peak phase angle decreases over time for the normal
polarity but not for the reverse polarity. It is also observed, but not shown, that the resonance
frequency of the cells is higher for the normal polarity compared with the reverse polarity. This
is an indication that the eﬀective electric ﬁeld in the CMUT is lower for the normal polarity
than for the reverse polarity. The coupling between the electrical and mechanical domain is
therefore decreased for the normal polarity over time.
One possible explanation for this behaviour could be that mobile charges are present in the
insulation oxide or at the vacuum/oxide interface. These charges should then decrease the
electrical ﬁeld in the vacuum gap for the normal polarity but not for the reverse polarity and
would therefore have to be positively charged. A decrease in the electrical ﬁeld in the vacuum
gap, for the normal polarity, would decrease the coupling between the mechanical and electrical
domain because the electrostatic force is decreased and the peak phase angle would therefore
be decreased as observed in the impedance measurements. This would also explain the higher
resonance frequency for the normal polarity, since the plate would not experience as strong a
spring softening eﬀect due to the lower electrical ﬁeld.
The same argument would be made for the time independent asymmetry with the diﬀerence
that the charges should be ﬁxed in the oxide or at the oxide/vacuum interface. This is one
possible explanation for the asymmetric behaviour. This is an area which is under ongoing
investigation and the real mechanism is not yet known.

Chapter 7
Conclusion and outlook
The thesis aim was met by successfully designing, fabricating and characterising a 1-D linear
dual mode CMUT optimised for THI.
Acoustic theory was presented and served as a basis for understanding medical ultrasound
imaging.
Tissue harmonic imaging was explained from non-linear acoustic theory and the
advantages of this imaging technique was given.
The physics behind the CMUT was explained with focus on plate deﬂection and electrostatic
analysis. From this theory analytical expressions for the pull-in voltage and resonance frequency
was found. Lumped element modelling was used to ﬁnd an equivalent circuit of the CMUT
which was then used to explore the impedance response. The equivalent circuit coupled the
electric, mechanic and acoustic domain of the CMUT.
The outer dimensions of the arrays were designed to match those of the commercial BK medical
9032 transducer. A ﬁnite element model was developed which included the possibility of having
an added mass on the plate of the cell. The elements consist of cells optimised for transmitting
at an immersion frequency of 3 MHz and cells optimised for receiving at an immersion frequency
of 6 MHz. The DC bias operation voltage was designed to be 190 V corresponding to 80% of
a pull-in voltage of 237.5 V. Two array designs were made with two and three transmit cells
per element width called type I and type II arrays. Using the model the transmit cells were
designed so the highest pressure per element was achieved, which at the element surface was
found to be 1.21 MPa and 0.86 MPa for the type I and type II array, respectively. From this
design 4 photolithographic masks were made, and subsequently used in the fabrication process.
The arrays were fabricated in DTU Danchip’s cleanroom using the fusion bonding method.
The baseline process ﬂow, without the added masses, was based on the process ﬂow in [43].
Process development was conducted prior to fabrication of the arrays in which optimisation of
the exposure dose, Au etch time and Ni electroplating process was performed. One wafer was
diced and arrays from this wafer were characterised by electrical and acoustical measurements.
From impedance measurements the resonance frequency in air was found to be 3.0 MHz and
5.6 MHz for the receive and transmit cells respectively for array 4 and the designed resonance
frequencies for the transmit and receive cells in immersion were 3 MHz and 6 MHz. The pull-in
voltage was found to be between 145 V and 175 V and the designed pull-in voltage was 237.5 V.
The discrepancy between the designed and measure values of the resonance frequencies and
pull-in voltage was due to the values of the plate thickness and Ni mass height: The plate
thickness was measured and a mean and standard deviation of 1.31 µm ± 0.063 µm over the
wafer was found whereas the plate thickness was designed to be 2 µm. The Ni mass height was

probably due to the pressure of the fundamental becoming too low to generate harmonics. The
harmonic to fundamental ratio increased faster for the focused elements than for the single
element. This is likely due to the higher pressures generated by the focused elements. This
showed that the pressure of the CMUT was suﬃciently high to generate harmonics in the
medium.

Outlook
In the future a new batch should be fabricated using SOI wafers with the designed plate thick-
ness. The uniformity of the thickness of the Ni layer should be increased. This could potentially
be achieved by using pulse electroplating where the applied voltage is pulsed [50]. An acous-
tic comparison between this new batch and the BK 9032 transducer could then be made to
investigate whether the CMUT array could perform better than a commercial transducer for
THI. A comparison between the dual mode CMUT and a single mode CMUT could also be
performed to investigate whether the dual mode CMUT really is better for THI.
The origin and behaviour of the unintentional charges leading to decreased performance when
a positive voltage is applied to the top electrode should be studied. If the source of the charges
can not be found, a possible solution could be to fabricate the arrays using a local oxidation of
silicon (LOCOS) process to deﬁne cavities since this method has recently proven not to yield
any charging eﬀect in the impedance response [30]. To the left in Figure 7.1 the result of a

Dual-mode CMUT Arrays Optimized for Tissue Harmonic Imaging
Søren Elmin Diederichsen1, Mathias Johannes Grøndahl Mølgaard1, Anders Lei1,
Matthias Bo Stuart2, Jørgen Arendt Jensen2, and Erik Vilain Thomsen1
1DTU Nanotech, Technical University of Denmark
2Center for Fast Ultrasound Imaging, Technical University of Denmark
It is investigated how Capacitive Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers (CMUTs) can be optimized
for Tissue Harmonic Imaging (THI), an ultrasound imaging technique that utilizes the propagation of
pressure waves in a non-linear medium such as body tissue. With this technique the transducer emits
ultrasound at a certain transmit frequency and the change in waveform, caused by the medium, will
result in the generation of harmonics. Of key importance in this context is the second harmonic, since
it can have a sufﬁciently high amplitude to create an image from the received echo. Ideally, then, the
transducer should be able to function in a dual-mode of operation with regard to frequency, while still
exploiting the full amplitude of the pulse and echo signals, respectively.
Current commercial piezoelectric transducers for THI are purposely designed to have a resonant
frequency between the excitation frequency and the frequency of the generated second harmonic. Al-
though dual-mode piezoelectric transducers have been made, the CMUT offers the advantage of a large
design ﬂexibility and ease of fabrication, which makes it a good alternative to commercial piezoelectric
transducers used for THI.
This contribution presents a novel dual-mode CMUT designed for THI. The general concept is to
have elements comprised of two different cell types, namely cells that are optimized for transmit and
receive mode of operation, respectively. The two cell types are designed to have different resonant
frequencies while still maintaining the same pull-in voltage. This can be realized by means of adding
extra mass on top of the plate of the transmit cells. Thereby transmit cells can be made with a lowered
resonant frequency compared to that of the unmodiﬁed receive cells. In fact, an additional advantage is
that a higher transmit pressure is possible for the transmit cells, whereas the sensitivity of the receive
cells remain.
Finite element analysis using the COMSOL Multiphysics software is performed in order to determine
the design of a dual-mode CMUT. Two different approaches are taken, both implementing the full 3-D
electro-mechanical-acoustical system, namely: A single-cell model and a semi-inﬁnite cell array model.
Using electroplating the dual-mode CMUTs are successfully fabricated. For this process a conductive
seed layer is needed. This material can also be used as electrode metalization layer. It is shown that
customization of CMUTs for THI can essentially be achieved by means of an add-on process to an
existing fabrication baseline process of CMUTs.
