Abstract
The shipping industry is a major source of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, including carbon
dioxide (CO2) and other pollutants such as nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulfur oxides (SOx), and
particulate matter (PM). These emissions contribute significantly to climate change and air
quality degradation, necessitating the development of sustainable alternatives to conventional
marine fuels. Ammonia has emerged as a promising zero-carbon fuel for maritime transport due
to its potential to drastically reduce GHG emissions. However, its adoption presents challenges,
including combustion ineﬀiciencies and the production of harmful byproducts such as nitrous
oxide (N2O), unburned ammonia (NH3), and secondary pollutants like ozone (O3).
This study investigates emissions from medium-speed ammonia-fueled engines, focusing on both
primary and secondary pollutants, and evaluates their implications for air quality using the
Danish Eulerian Hemispheric Model (DEHM). Additionally, the health and economic impacts
of these emissions are assessed through the Economic Valuation of Air Pollution (EVA) model.
The analysis considers multiple fuel scenarios, comparing partial and full ammonia substitution
with a baseline scenario based on conventional fuel use.
Results indicate significant reductions in CO2 emissions and NOx concentrations, aligning with
global decarbonization goals. However, ammonia combustion leads to increased emissions of
N2O and NH3, which pose challenges due to their roles in global warming and air quality
degradation. Secondary pollutant formation exhibits mixed outcomes, with reductions in PM
levels contrasted by localized increases in O3 concentrations. These shifts influence public health,
including decreased respiratory and cardiovascular risks and related cost externalities.
The findings underscore ammonia’s potential to decarbonize maritime transport while high-
lighting the necessity for advanced combustion technologies and emission control systems to
mitigate environmental and health trade-offs. This research demonstrates the importance of
implementing stringent regulations and advanced after-treatment systems to control emissions.
Without proper safeguards, the unregulated use of ammonia as a marine fuel could lead to sig-
nificant environmental and health risks, undermining its potential as a clean energy solution.
Policymakers and industry stakeholders must address these challenges to realize ammonia’s full
potential as a green marine fuel.

Introduction
Air pollution has evolved significantly alongside human activity, from early biomass burning to
the widespread industrial and transportation emissions of the modern era.(Allegrini et al., 2023)
The World Health Organization estimates that air pollution kills 7 million people every year:
4.2 million from outdoor air pollution.(World Health Organization, n.d.) The maritime sector,
a key contributor to global emissions, faces increasing scrutiny due to its substantial release of
nitrogen oxides (NOx, 24%), sulfur oxides (SOx, 24%), and particulate matter (PM2.5, 9%) as
a proportion of total EU emissions from all economic sectors in 2018.(Agency & Agency, 2021)
These pollutants not only degrade air quality but also have profound impacts on human health
and the environment.(Gaffney & Marley, 2003; Allegrini et al., 2023) In response to international
climate goals and air quality regulations, alternative fuels such as ammonia (NH3) have gained
attention as a potential solution.(Zewe, 2024)(Machaj et al., 2022)
Ammonia, when produced using renewable energy, has the potential to be carbon-neutral,
making it an attractive option for the decarbonization of the shipping industry.(Society, 2020)
However, its use introduces complexities in atmospheric chemistry, including the formation of
secondary pollutants such as ammonium aerosols and their interactions with acid gases. Further-
more, ammonia emissions contribute to altered nitrogen deposition dynamics, which can have
cascading effects on ecosystems and biodiversity.(Gaffney & Marley, 2003; Wong et al., 2024)
Experimental investigations are currently undergoing to provide critical insights into possible
emission profiles and combustion setups and eﬀiciency in order to highlight the potential for am-
monia slip and production of byproducts such as nitrous oxide (N2O) under varying conditions
prior to its commercialization as a marine fuel.(Alnajideen et al., 2024)
1.1
Research Objectives
This project evaluates the atmospheric and health implications of transitioning to ammonia-
based marine fuels, focusing on the following objectives:
1. Analyze Primary Emissions from Ammonia Combustion: Identify and quantify
the key exhaust emissions produced during the combustion of ammonia in medium-speed
engines, including nitrogen oxides (NOx), ammonia slip (unburned NH3), nitrous oxide
(N2O) and Particulate Matter (PM).
2. Examine the Formation of Secondary Pollutants in the Atmosphere: Investigate
the atmospheric chemistry of ammonia combustion byproducts, focusing on their transfor-
mation into secondary pollutants. This includes evaluating changes in the concentrations
of key pollutants such as ozone (O3), fine particulate matter (PM2.5), total ammonium
(totNH4), total nitrate (totNO3), and total sulfate (totSO4).
3. Assess the Impact of Ammonia Combustion Emissions on Air Quality and Hu-
man Health: Evaluate the effects of primary and secondary pollutants from ammonia-
fueled medium-speed engines on air quality. While health impacts (such as respiratory and
cardiovascular risks) and cost externalities are quantitatively assessed, environmental con-
cerns like acidification and eutrophication are conceptually addressed based on established
pollutant formation processes (described in 2).
4. Evaluate Ammonia’s Role in Decarbonizing Maritime Transport: Evaluate find-
ings and possible limitations to determine the potential of ammonia-fueled medium-speed
engines and the possible drawbacks.

Overview of Ammonia Combustion and Emissions
Marine shipping is a cornerstone of global trade, transporting over 80% of the world’s goods by
volume.(Trade & (UNCTAD), 2021) However, the industry is increasingly under scrutiny for its
environmental footprint, contributing approximately 2.89% of global anthropogenic CO2 emis-
sions in 2018, along with significant releases of nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulfur oxides (SOx), and
particulate matter (PM) (Dimitriou & Javaid, 2020). These emissions exacerbate climate change,
harm ecosystems, and degrade air quality, particularly in coastal and port regions.(Agency &
Agency, 2021)
In response to these challenges, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) has revised
its approach to greenhouse gas (GHG) reduction. In July 2023, the IMO adopted the ”2023
IMO GHG Strategy,” setting the ambitious goal of achieving net-zero GHG emissions by or
around 2050. This strategy serves as the foundation for ongoing efforts to reduce emissions
from international shipping and has far-reaching implications for the industry. With stricter
regulations on the horizon, including mid-term measures under discussion for implementation
by 2027, compliance with the IMO’s targets is becoming a critical priority for stakeholders in
the maritime industry. (IMO, 2023)
The European Union has also intensified its regulatory framework. As of 2024, the European
Union Emissions Trading System (EU-ETS) includes maritime emissions, adding a carbon pric-
ing mechanism to the sector. Furthermore, the FuelEU Maritime regulation, set to launch in
2025, aims to accelerate the adoption of low- and zero-emission fuels. Under these frameworks,
GHG emissions are increasingly becoming a significant cost factor, with projections suggesting
that annual GHG-related expenses may surpass traditional fuel costs sooner than anticipated.
(ClassNK, 2024)
This regulatory shift has propelled the exploration of alternative fuels, such as hydrogen (H2)
and ammonia (NH3), as replacements for conventional hydrocarbons. Both are recognized by
the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) as critical to achieving a carbon-free
energy future. (Tian et al., 2024a)
2.1
Fuel Characteristics and Production
Ammonia is a colorless gas at standard temperature and pressure, characterized by its strong
pungent odor. It has a molecular weight of 17.03 g/mol, a boiling point of −33◦C, and a melting
point of −78◦C. These properties enable ammonia to be liquefied under moderate pressure (10
bar) or refrigeration, making it easier to store and transport compared to hydrogen, which
requires cryogenic temperatures or high pressures (350–700 bar). (Wang et al., 2023)
Ammonia’s energy density per unit volume is 11.3 MJ/L, which is higher than hydrogen (8.5
MJ/L) but lower than conventional hydrocarbon fuels like diesel (36.4 MJ/L). However, it
exhibits a lower calorific value of 18.6 MJ/kg compared to hydrogen (120 MJ/kg) and natural
gas (49 MJ/kg) (Tian et al., 2024a). Ammonia is non-flammable at ambient conditions due to
its high autoignition temperature (924 K) and narrow flammability range (15–27% by volume),
necessitating enhanced ignition strategies for eﬀicient combustion (Li et al., 2021).
The combustion of ammonia is a carbon-free process, as it emits nitrogen (N2) and water (H2O)
as primary byproducts:
4NH3 + 3O2 →2N2 + 6H2O.

This eliminates CO2 emissions, positioning ammonia as a zero-carbon fuel. However, incomplete
combustion can result in the formation of nitrogen oxides (NOx), which are harmful pollutants:
NH3 + O2 →NOx + H2O.
Furthermore, ammonia’s slow flame speed and high ignition energy requirements make it chal-
lenging to use in traditional internal combustion engines.Alnajideen et al., 2024 Strategies to
address these challenges include blending ammonia with hydrogen to enhance flame propagation
or using a dual-fuel system where ammonia is co-fired with diesel as a pilot fuel (Tian et al.,
2024b).
Ammonia cracking, a process that converts NH3 into hydrogen (H2) and nitrogen (N2), is another
approach to improve combustion eﬀiciency:
2NH3 →3H2 + N2.
This process enables the use of hydrogen-enriched fuel, which improves ignition stability and
reduces unburned ammonia emissions (Li et al., 2021).
2.2
Ammonia Production Pathways
Ammonia can be produced through fossil-based and renewable methods. (Yuzbasioglu et al.,
2022) The conventional Haber-Bosch process combines nitrogen (N2) from air with hydrogen
(H2) derived from natural gas via steam methane reforming, emitting significant CO2 (Tian
et al., 2024a). Green ammonia eliminates CO2 emissions by using hydrogen from renewable en-
ergy sources like electrolysis (Bora et al., 2024), while blue ammonia integrates carbon capture
and storage (CCS) to reduce emissions.(Mayer et al., 2023) The production of green ammonia
requires substantial renewable energy capacity. For instance, meeting the annual fuel consump-
tion of international shipping (equivalent to 220 million tons of heavy fuel oil) would necessitate
approximately 540 GW of renewable electricity, underlining the importance of expanding global
renewable energy capacity (IMO, 2023; ClassNK, 2024). Emerging technologies such as electro-
chemical synthesis, which directly produce ammonia from nitrogen and water using renewable
electricity, remain experimental due to low eﬀiciency and high costs (Wang et al., 2023).
Ammonia combustion engines can generally be classified into two configurations: spark ignition
(SI) engines and compression ignition (CI) engines. Additionally, dual-fuel engines, which com-
bine ammonia with a secondary pilot fuel (e.g., diesel or hydrogen), are widely used to stabilize
combustion under varying load conditions.
• Spark Ignition (SI): In SI engines, ammonia is introduced into the combustion chamber
as a premixed fuel-air mixture. Ignition is achieved using a spark plug, which initiates
combustion. However, due to ammonia’s high autoignition temperature (924 K) and low
laminar burning velocity, SI engines often suffer from poor flame propagation and incom-
plete combustion, leading to higher ammonia slip and nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions (Li
et al., 2021).
• Compression Ignition (CI): In CI engines, ammonia is injected directly into the com-
bustion chamber, where high compression ratios generate suﬀicient heat to ignite the fuel.
However, ammonia’s high ignition temperature often necessitates the use of a pilot fuel,
such as diesel or hydrogen, to initiate combustion. This dual-fuel approach ensures stable
ignition while reducing CO2 emissions by replacing a significant portion of the diesel with
ammonia (Tian et al., 2024a).

The ammonia combustion process can be divided into three primary stages: fuel premixing (if
applicable), ignition, and exhaust handling.
In SI engines, ammonia is introduced into the intake manifold, where it is mixed with air to
form a homogenous mixture. Precise control of the air-fuel ratio is critical, as ammonia’s narrow
flammability range (15–27% by volume) increases the risk of misfire under lean or rich conditions.
In CI engines, ammonia is directly injected into the combustion chamber at high pressure, often
alongside a pilot fuel. (Tian et al., 2024b).
For SI engines, a high-energy spark plug ignites the premixed ammonia-air mixture. For CI
engines, the heat generated by compression initiates the ignition of the pilot fuel, which sub-
sequently ignites the injected ammonia.
This dual-fuel approach is particularly effective in
overcoming ammonia’s high ignition temperature and ensuring stable combustion across a wide
range of engine loads (Wang et al., 2023).
The exhaust gases from ammonia combustion primarily contain nitrogen (N2) and water vapor
(H2O), but also include nitrogen oxides (NOx), unburned ammonia (NH3 slip), and, in some
cases, nitrous oxide (N2O). Advanced aftertreatment systems, such as selective catalytic reduc-
tion (SCR) and ammonia slip catalysts, are required to minimize NOx and NH3 emissions. N2O
emissions, which are harder to mitigate, remain a critical challenge and require further research
into decomposition catalysts and optimized combustion strategies (Li et al., 2021; Tian et al.,
2024b).
2.3
Advantages and Challenges
Ammonia combustion engines offer significant environmental benefits, including the elimination
of tank-to-wake CO2 emissions and negligible sulfur oxides (SOx) and particulate matter (PM)
emissions.(IRENA, 2021) These attributes make ammonia a key candidate for decarbonizing the
maritime industry, especially as the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) projects
that renewable ammonia could become the backbone of decarbonized international shipping by
2050, with production costs expected to fall to USD 67–114/MWh by mid-century (IRENA,
2021). Furthermore, ammonia benefits from an existing production and transport infrastruc-
ture developed primarily for the agricultural industry, providing a head start in logistics and
deployment.(Bora et al., 2024) Green ammonia is also carbon-neutral and offers one of the high-
est energy eﬀiciencies compared to other alternative fuels, enhancing its appeal as a sustainable
energy carrier.(Mayer et al., 2023)
However, challenges remain. Producing ammonia sustainably requires large-scale renewable en-
ergy inputs, as approximately 540 GW of renewable electricity would be needed to replace the
annual marine fuel demand equivalent to 220 million tons of heavy fuel oil. This highlights the
dual challenge of scaling ammonia production while simultaneously expanding global renewable
energy capacity (IRENA, 2021). Additionally, global demand for ammonia across multiple sec-
tors, including agriculture and energy, could create competition for supply, potentially leading
to scalability issues. (Society, 2020) Ammonia also has a high production cost, and its toxic-
ity necessitates stringent safety measures for fuel handling and storage, both onboard and at
ports.(IRENA, 2021; Yuzbasioglu et al., 2022)
From a technical perspective, ammonia’s low flame speed and high ignition temperature compli-
cate engine operation, requiring advanced fuel injection systems, optimized combustion strate-
gies, and robust aftertreatment technologies to control nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions. While
NOx emissions can be mitigated using selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems, challenges
such as nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions—whose global warming potential is 292 times that of
CO2—remain critical areas for technological improvement.(DNV, 2024).

The treatment of N2O emissions is diﬀicult because the formation of N2O during combustion is
highly sensitive to both temperature and air-fuel mixing conditions. N2O typically forms under
low-temperature combustion or incomplete combustion scenarios, where the reaction pathways
favor partial oxidation of ammonia rather than complete reduction to nitrogen (N2) (Li et al.,
2021). Optimizing combustion parameters, such as flame temperature, equivalence ratio, and
residence time in the combustion chamber, is critical to minimizing N2O formation.(Chen & Li,
2021) However, achieving these optimal conditions consistently across a wide range of engine
loads and operating scenarios remains a significant engineering challenge.(Li et al., 2021)
Another complication arises from the fact that typical aftertreatment systems like SCR are not
designed to target N2O. Current SCR catalysts are highly effective at reducing NOx to N2 and
water (H2O), but they do not address N2O, and in some cases, they may even convert residual NO
into N2O as a byproduct under suboptimal conditions (Tian et al., 2024b). Addressing this issue
requires the development of advanced catalyst formulations capable of selectively decomposing
N2O into nitrogen and oxygen. Emerging technologies, such as N2O decomposition catalysts,
are being explored, but these systems require further optimization to improve their eﬀiciency,
durability, and compatibility with marine engine operating conditions.(Li et al., 2021)
Finally, the integration of N2O mitigation strategies into marine engines must also consider the
trade-offs between emission control, fuel eﬀiciency, and operational reliability.
For example,
higher combustion temperatures can reduce N2O formation but may exacerbate NOx emis-
sions.(Li et al., 2021)

As mentioned in section 2.3 and as indicated infig. 2.1, ammonia combustion produces nitrogen
oxides (NOx), ammonia slip (unburned NH3), and nitrous oxide (N2O) (and particulate matter
when diesel spark ignition takes place in the combustion engine). These emissions are measured
as part of tank-to-wake (TtW) emissions, which quantify the pollutants produced during fuel
combustion. TtW emissions are critical for evaluating the environmental impact of different
fuels. For ammonia, TtW CO2 emissions are zero, demonstrating its carbon-free combustion.
In comparison, HFO and LPG exhibit significantly higher TtW CO2 emissions, normalized to
1 and 0.85–0.86, respectively, while methanol emits 90% of the CO2 of HFO. Hydrogen, like
ammonia, achieves zero TtW CO2 emissions, making it a carbon-free option. In comparison,
HFO combustion produces higher levels of sulfur oxides (SOx) and PM. SOx emissions contribute
to acid rain and marine ecosystem acidification, while PM, particularly black carbon, exacerbates
global warming and deteriorates air quality. LNG, although having lower CO2 emissions (TtW
CO2 normalized at 0.73), suffers from methane slip—a release of unburned methane with a GWP
significantly higher than CO2. Methanol, while a relatively cleaner fuel, can emit formaldehyde
and methanol slip, which are harmful to human health and the environment.(ClassNK, 2024)

Emissions and Their Impacts on Air Quality and Human Health
Figure 2.2: Relationships between primary pollutants, secondary pollutants, and their environ-

Ammonia combustion produces a variety of pollutants that significantly impact air quality, hu-
man health, and ecosystems. Among the most critical byproducts are nitrogen oxides (NOx),
ammonia (NH3), nitrous oxide (N2O), particulate matter (PM), and ozone (O3). These pollu-
tants interact with atmospheric constituents to form secondary pollutants, leading to a range of
environmental and health effects, as illustrated in Figure 2.2. Furthermore, Table 2.1 outlines
the EU air quality standards, emphasizing the concentration limits for key pollutants such as
PM, NO2, and SO2. These standards provide context for understanding the severity of the
impacts described below.
Nitrogen Oxides (NOx)
Nitrogen oxides are key precursors to ozone and fine particulate matter formation. Their in-
teractions with hydroxyl radicals (OH) result in the production of nitric acid (HNO3), a major
contributor to acid rain (Jacob & Winner, 2003):
NO2 + OH →HNO3.
(2.1)
Acid rain leads to soil and water acidification, reducing biodiversity and negatively impacting
forest and aquatic ecosystems. NOx also plays a critical role in eutrophication (Figure 2.2),
depositing reactive nitrogen compounds into water bodies. This nutrient overload fuels algal
blooms, as indicated by the EU’s strict limit of 40 µg/m3 annual NO2 concentrations (Ta-
ble 2.1). Such blooms deplete oxygen levels in water, harm aquatic life, and disrupt ecosystems
(Smith, 2006).

Ammonia (NH3)
Ammonia, while critical for agricultural applications, poses significant risks when released into
the atmosphere. It reacts with atmospheric acids like HNO3 and H2SO4, forming ammonium
aerosols such as NH4NO3 and (NH4)2SO4, as shown in Figure 2.2. These aerosols contribute to
PM2.5, which is regulated to 25 µg/m3 annually under EU air quality standards (Table 2.1). NH3
deposition also results in soil acidification and eutrophication, threatening biodiversity (Baek
et al., 2004; Council, 2000).
Nitrous Oxide (N2O)
Nitrous oxide, though less directly tied to air quality, significantly exacerbates global warming
due to its high global warming potential—292 times that of CO2 (Jacob & Winner, 2003). As
indicated in Figure 2.2, its release from ammonia combustion presents a trade-off in transitioning
to low-carbon fuels, underlining the complexity of managing emissions during decarbonization
efforts (Alnajideen et al., 2024).
Particulate Matter (PM)
Particulate matter, including PM2.5 and PM10, is both directly emitted and formed as secondary
aerosols. PM10, consisting of ”coarse particles” up to 10 micrometers, penetrates the upper res-
piratory system, causing irritation. PM2.5, smaller and more hazardous, can reach deep into the
lungs, posing severe cardiovascular and respiratory risks. Table 2.1 highlights the strict EU lim-
its on PM concentrations to mitigate these health effects (Agency & Agency, 2021; Team, 2019).

Methodology
3.1
Experimental Setup at the Danish Technological Institute
The experimental setup for this study is located at the Danish Technological Institute (DTI),
where a combustion engine is used to investigate ammonia as a fuel, as part of the AFLOAT
project, which aims to study and develop a competitive and market-ready solution that improves
combustion properties of NH3 by hydrogen enrichment in ships powered by medium-speed en-
gines and boilers for the marine sector.(DTI, n.d.)The engine operates under steady-state con-
ditions, with a generator converting mechanical power into electricity.
The engine is a four-stroke engine, medium-speed, multi-cylinder engine currently configured
to operate with one cylinder running on ammonia while the others use diesel. Eventually, all
four cylinders will transition to ammonia. The engine operates at approximately 1000–2000
revolutions per minute (RPM), producing up to 1 megawatt (MW) of power.
Ammonia is
injected into the air injector as a liquid at a pressure of 2 to 3 bar, while two hydrogen injectors
deliver hydrogen directly into the combustion chamber at around 200 bar.
Figure 3.1: Diagram of the ammonia-fueled combustion engine setup, DTI, Aarhus.
The hydrogen used in the engine is produced through the process of ammonia cracking, described
in section 2. This process enhances fuel eﬀiciency and supports hydrogen-enriched combustion,
improving flame stability and overall reactivity due to hydrogen’s higher flame speed and wider
flammability limits.
These properties result in lower emissions of unburnt ammonia and a
reduced risk of NH3 slip. Additionally, the presence of hydrogen contributes to reduced nitrogen
oxides (NOx) formation by facilitating combustion at lower temperatures.(Tian et al., 2024c)
The configuration at the Danish Technological Institute offers a unique opportunity to study
ammonia combustion and its associated challenges under realistic conditions. Building on these
findings, this study explores the broader impacts of ammonia combustion emissions on air quality
and human health, emphasizing both the potential benefits and drawbacks of adopting ammonia
as a marine fuel on a larger scale, as well as the critical need for adequate regulations prior to
its commercialization.
All measurements at DTI are being conducted by technicians employed on the project.

3.2
Emission Measurements
Figure 3.2: FTIR spectrometer setup showing the heated connection tube to the engine exhaust
(Left) and the FTIR system and data acquisition setup (Right).
To measure the emissions from the combustion engine, a Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR)
spectrometer is being used.
The FTIR system is connected directly to the engine exhaust
through a heated tube (Fig. 3.2), maintained at approximately 180°C to prevent condensation
and ensure that the exhaust gas composition remains unchanged. This is particularly critical for
measuring ammonia and water vapor, as they are highly sensitive to temperature fluctuations.
A FTIR spectrometer is able to measure the infrared wavelengths absorbed by a material to
determine its molecular composition and structure. Infrared radiation is applied to the exhaust
gas, and its absorption at different wavelengths is recorded.(Undavalli et al., 2021)
Unknown materials are identified by comparing their IR spectrum to a reference database.
(Labs, 2024) The FTIR spectrometer at DTI measures a wide range of exhaust components
simultaneously, including carbon dioxide (CO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), nitrous oxide (N2O),
unburnt ammonia (NH3), and particulate matter. This capability is advantageous as it avoids
the need for separate instruments and minimizes errors associated with alternative techniques,
such as mass spectrometry. Real-time measurements further allow for a high temporal resolution
of emissions data.(Bacsik et al., 2004)
The FTIR system was calibrated to focus on the pollutants of interest: NOx, N2O, and NH3.
Calibration involved selecting specific regions of the infrared spectrum to minimize interference
from overlapping signals, such as those from water vapor or carbon dioxide.
One challenge associated with FTIR measurements is spectral interference, particularly in the
presence of high concentrations of water vapor or carbon dioxide. This interference can obscure
the absorption bands of other gases, such as ammonia or nitrogen oxides. To address this, smaller
regions of the infrared spectrum with minimal interference were carefully selected. Additionally,

3.6
The Danish Eulerian Hemispheric Model (DEHM)
Figure 3.3: The geographical domains of the DEHM model in the polar stereographic projec-
tion.(Geels et al., 2021)
To assess the air quality effects of ammonia emissions from marine shipping, the Danish Eulerian
Hemispheric Model (DEHM) is used. DEHM is a three-dimensional, large-scale atmospheric
chemistry-transport model (CTM) developed to study the long-range transport of air pollution
in the Northern Hemisphere (Brandt et al., 2012; Geels et al., 2021). Originally designed in
the early 1990s to investigate how sulfur dioxide and sulfate travel into the Arctic, DEHM has
evolved to include a wide range of atmospheric processes and chemical species. The current
model simulates the transport and transformation of 74 chemical species, 9 types of primary
particles, and 158 chemical reactions (Christensen, 1997).
The model operates over several geographical domains, discretized on a polar stereographic
projection. These domains include:
• d01: The main domain covering the Northern Hemisphere with a resolution of 150 km ×
150 km,
• d02: A nested domain for Europe (50 km × 50 km),
• d03: A higher-resolution domain for Northern Europe (16.67 km × 16.67 km), and
• d04: The highest-resolution domain for Denmark (5.56 km × 5.56 km) (Service, 2020).
Vertically, DEHM uses 29 terrain-following σ-layers, extending from the surface to approximately
18 km. This structure provides flexibility in representing physical and chemical processes at
different altitudes (Brandt et al., 2012).

• Advection and diffusion (horizontal and vertical transport of pollutants),
• Chemical reactions and transformations,
• Deposition processes (wet and dry), and
• Emission sources.
(Institute, 2020)

∂qi

•

• ∇·(K∇qi) describes the effect of diffusion, with K representing the diffusivity tensor that

• Pi and Li denote the production and loss rates of qi, which depend on the chemical

This simplified equation highlights the main components driving changes in the species concen-

1. Advection: The transport of qi by wind.

This equation provides the foundation for simulating the atmospheric behavior of chemical

incorporates detailed parameterizations for aerosol processes, including nitrate and ammonium
formation (Brandt et al., 2012).

3.7
The Economic Valuation of Air Pollution (EVA) Model
To assess the health and economic impacts of air pollution resulting from ammonia emissions,
the Economic Valuation of Air Pollution (EVA) model is employed. EVA is a health impact
assessment tool that evaluates both health and economic impacts of air pollution using an in-
tegrated impact-pathway approach. It combines atmospheric modeling, population exposure
data, exposure-response functions (ERFs), and economic valuations to estimate the effects of
pollutants (Brandt et al., 2013; Im et al., 2023). The current version aligns with WHO 2021
guidelines and incorporates recent developments in health risk assessment and valuation method-
ologies (World Health Organization, 2021).
The EVA model integrates several components:
• The Danish Eulerian Hemispheric Model (DEHM) (Brandt et al., 2013);
• Address-level or gridded population data (Brandt et al., 2013);
• Linear and non-linear exposure-response functions (ERFs) for various health impacts
(World Health Organization, 2021; Im et al., 2023);
• Economic valuations of health impacts, expressed as external costs (Brandt et al., 2013).
The model calculates pollutant concentrations and associated health effects on a regional scale,
using high-resolution DEHM output data across three nested domains (n2, n3, and n4) (Brandt
et al., 2013).
The EVA model evaluates annual mean concentrations for PM2.5, SO2, NO2, and O3. For
ozone, the model uses the SOMO35 metric, defined as the yearly sum of the daily maximum
8-hour running average concentrations exceeding 35 ppb (Brandt et al., 2013)(Agency, 2025).
In Denmark, the Central Person Register (CPR) provides detailed address-level data, including
age and gender distributions. This data is interpolated to the DEHM grid to estimate gridded
population exposure.
For Europe, gridded population data at a 1 km spatial resolution is
obtained from Eurostat, the statistical oﬀice of the European Union, which provides high-quality
data to support policy-making and research across member states (Eurostat, n.d.).
The population exposure is calculated using the following relationship:
R = β · ∆c · P,
(3.4)
where R represents the health outcome (e.g., cases or days), ∆c is the pollutant concentration
increase from specific emission sources, P is the affected population, and β is an empirically
determined ERF coeﬀicient derived from epidemiological studies (World Health Organization,
2021).
To ensure precision when quantifying specific emission sources, the EVA model applies a tag-
ging method. This technique directly tracks the contributions of specific emissions to regional
air pollution without assuming linear behavior in atmospheric chemistry, reducing numerical
noise.(Brandt et al., 2013)
The EVA model estimates multiple health endpoints, grouped into mortality and morbidity
categories. Mortality endpoints include both acute deaths (AD) due to short-term exposure
and chronic deaths (CD) from long-term exposure (Brandt et al., 2013). A combined metric
(ADCD) represents total premature deaths.
Morbidity endpoints span hospital admissions,
chronic conditions, and reduced activity days (Im et al., 2023).

Economic Valuation
The EVA model expresses health-related externalities in euros, reflecting Danish willingness-
to-pay (WTP) values. These values are adjusted to other countries using purchasing power
parity (PPP), with Danish WTP values approximately 30% higher than the European average

lost (VOLY) rather than the value of statistical life (VSL), which accounts for latency periods
and the age profile of victims of environmental exposures (Brandt et al., 2013).

4.0) This report provides revised cost estimates for various pollutants, reflecting advancements
in health cost methodologies and aligning with the latest scientific understanding. Acute deaths,

Discussion of Results
5.1
Discussion of Results: the DEHM model
Ammonia (NH3)
The increase in ammonia (NH3) emissions observed in the ammonia combustion scenarios, par-
ticularly in the 100% upper range scenarios, reflects challenges related to incomplete combustion
and ammonia slip, where unburnt NH3 is emitted into the atmosphere. Ammonia is highly solu-
ble and reacts readily with atmospheric water vapor to form ammonium ions (NH+
4 ), which are
precursors to secondary aerosols such as ammonium nitrate (NH4NO3) and ammonium sulfate
((NH4)2SO4) (Seinfeld & Pandis, 2016b).
However, reductions in NOx emissions in the 100% ammonia scenarios lead to a decline in
nitric acid (HNO3), thereby suppressing ammonium nitrate formation. The following reaction
illustrates the conversion of ammonia to ammonium nitrate:
NH3 + HNO3 ↔NH4NO3.
As less HNO3 becomes available, excess NH3 persists in the gas phase, particularly over marine
regions. Despite its high solubility, the removal of NH3 via wet deposition is less eﬀicient in
these areas due to the relatively low acidity of seawater (Quinn & Bates, 2011). This extends
the atmospheric residence time of NH3, leading to elevated concentrations in marine regions.
Elevated NH3 concentrations increase the levels of gaseous ammonia in the atmosphere, thereby
raising the risk of deposition onto land and water surfaces.(Air Pollution Information System,
2023)
However, in the 100% ammonia scenarios, reductions in NOx emissions lead to a decline in
nitric acid (HNO3), thereby suppressing ammonium nitrate formation. Consequently, excess
NH3 persists in the gas phase, particularly over marine regions. Despite its high solubility, the
removal of NH3 via wet deposition is less eﬀicient in these areas due to the relatively low acidity
of seawater (Quinn & Bates, 2011). This extends the atmospheric residence time of NH3, leading
to elevated concentrations in marine regions.
The increase in ammonia (NH3) emissions in ammonia combustion scenarios raises significant
concerns regarding eutrophication, particularly in Denmark, where ammonia deposition already
contributes heavily to nitrogen pollution. Eutrophication events are frequent mostly in Inner
Danish waters and critical loads are exceeded for much of the Danish sensitive terrestrial ecosys-
tems.(Agency, 2003; Ellermann et al., 2018) Ammonia released into the atmosphere can be
deposited onto soils and water bodies, where it acts as a nutrient, promoting excessive algal
growth. This process disrupts aquatic ecosystems by depleting oxygen levels, reducing biodiver-
sity, and destabilizing food webs (Council, 2000; Asman & Janssen, 1987). Denmark’s existing
high levels of ammonia emissions, largely from agriculture, exacerbate these issues, and fur-
ther increases from marine shipping could intensify the environmental strain. The potential for
heightened nitrogen deposition underscores the need for stringent management and mitigation
measures to address both agricultural and marine sources of ammonia emissions (Fowler et al.,
2009).

Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2)
The observed reductions in nitrogen dioxide (NO2) across all ammonia scenarios are primarily
due to the inherently low NOx emissions during ammonia combustion, which lacks the carbon
precursors required for NOx formation through thermal and fuel NO mechanisms (Finlayson-
Pitts & Pitts, 1999). This reduction in NO2 decreases nitric acid (HNO3) production, which in
turn reduces secondary nitrate aerosol formation (Jacob, 2000). Furthermore, in NOx-limited
regimes, such as marine regions, the suppression of NO2 decreases ozone (O3) titration, resulting
in localized changes in ozone dynamics (Sillman, 1999).
Ozone (O3)
Ozone concentrations show varying trends across the scenarios, reflecting the complex interplay
between NOx, NH3, and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). In the 25% upper range ammonia
scenarios, minor reductions in ozone concentrations are observed due to the reduced availability
of NO2 for photolysis, which drives ozone formation:
NO2 + hν →NO + O(3P),
(5.1)
O(3P) + O2 + M →O3 + M.
(5.2)
However, in the 100% Upper range ammonia scenarios, ozone concentrations increase in VOC-
rich, NOx-limited regions, such as marine areas. Reduced NOx emissions decrease ozone titration—
a process where nitric oxide (NO) reacts with ozone (O3) to form nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and
molecular oxygen (O2).
This reduction in titration allows for enhanced photochemical ozone production (Jenkin &
Clemitshaw, 2000; Gantt & Meskhidze, 2013).
As a result, broader ozone hotspots emerge
in marine regions where VOC emissions are abundant (Sillman, 1999).
Particulate Matter (PM2.5)
The reductions in PM2.5 concentrations in the ammonia adoption scenarios are attributed to
the decline in sulfate and nitrate aerosols, which are formed from sulfur dioxide (SO2) and
NOx precursors, respectively (Paulot et al., 2016). Lower HNO3 availability reduces ammonium
nitrate formation, while the decline in SO2 emissions limits ammonium sulfate formation. While
excess NH3 could shift the balance toward ammonium sulfate in regions where H2SO4 is still
present, this effect is minimal due to declining SO2 emissions (Jimenez et al., 2009). The net
reduction in PM2.5 concentrations underscores the potential air quality benefits of transitioning
to ammonia-fueled engines, especially in high-traﬀic marine regions.
Wet Deposition and Atmospheric Interactions
Ammonia’s high solubility facilitates its removal through wet deposition. However, over marine
regions, the relatively low acidity of seawater reduces the conversion of NH3 to ammonium ions
(NH+
4 ), thereby limiting wet deposition eﬀiciency (Asman & Janssen, 1987). In contrast, NO2,
although less soluble, forms highly soluble nitric acid (HNO3), which is eﬀiciently removed via
wet deposition. This contributes to the observed reductions in secondary nitrate aerosols in all
ammonia scenarios (Fowler et al., 2009).

5.2
Discussion of Results: the EVA model
The EVA model results demonstrate that transitioning to ammonia-based marine fuels yields
significant health benefits, driven by reductions in key air pollutants. Pollutants such as nitro-
gen oxides (NOx) and particulate matter (PM2.5) are major contributors to cardiovascular and
respiratory diseases, with their reduction leading to measurable improvements in public health
outcomes. For instance, decreased NOx emissions result in lower formation of secondary nitrate
aerosols, a significant component of PM2.5, which is closely linked to both chronic respiratory
diseases and increased mortality rates (Brandt et al., 2013; Seinfeld & Pandis, 2016b). Sulfate
aerosols (SO4), another component of PM2.5, exacerbate respiratory issues and contribute to
hospital admissions, underscoring the importance of reducing sulfur dioxide (SO2) emissions in
parallel with ammonia adoption (Paulot et al., 2016).
Ammonia adoption scenarios also illustrate nuanced trade-offs in pollutant interactions. While
reductions in NOx and PM2.5 improve cardiovascular and respiratory outcomes, increased emis-
sions of ammonia (NH3) can lead to elevated ammonium aerosol formation under certain condi-
tions. However, this effect is more localized and less pronounced compared to the broader ben-
efits of reducing NOx and nitrate aerosols, particularly in high-traﬀic maritime regions (Quinn
& Bates, 2011; Jacob, 2000).
From a health perspective, the 100% ammonia scenarios are the most beneficial, achieving the
greatest reductions across all health metrics. These scenarios result in the lowest incidences of
avoided deaths, hospital admissions, and chronic diseases, reflecting the overall improvement in
air quality. The 25% ammonia scenarios, while yielding smaller reductions, still offer notable
health benefits, particularly in reducing hospital admissions and improving quality of life. This
indicates that even partial adoption of ammonia fuels can provide meaningful health gains,
particularly in regions with high shipping activity (Brandt et al., 2013).
The comparison between STEAM V1 and V3.2 emission inventories highlights the role of up-
dated methodologies in health impact modeling. The slightly lower baseline impacts observed
in V3.2 reflect refinements in data and calculations, contributing to a more precise estimation
of externalities. These updates offer a robust foundation for evaluating the health and economic
benefits of transitioning to ammonia-based fuels.
Overall, the findings reinforce the potential of ammonia adoption to significantly reduce the
health burden associated with maritime emissions. Among the pollutants, NOx plays the most
critical role in cardiovascular outcomes, while PM2.5 affects both cardiovascular and respiratory
health. The 100% Lower Range scenario emerges as the most favorable due to its overall reduc-
tions in pollutant emissions and associated health impacts. However, to maximize these benefits,
stringent controls on ammonia slip and other secondary pollutants, as well as advancements in
emission mitigation technologies, will be essential. This comprehensive approach can help en-
sure that the transition to ammonia-based marine fuels achieves its full potential in improving
public health and reducing economic costs.

5.3
Limitations of the Study
This study aims to evaluate the health and environmental impacts of transitioning to ammonia
as a marine fuel, yet certain limitations and considerations must be acknowledged to provide
context for the findings. One limitation of this study is the exclusion of emissions arising from
ammonia production, as the analysis focuses solely on emissions from ammonia combustion in
marine shipping. Ammonia production, particularly through conventional methods, remains a
significant source of CO2 emissions. Transitioning to green or blue ammonia pathways is crucial
to align with global decarbonization goals and to ensure that the emissions reductions achieved
by choosing ammonia as a fuel are not offset by emissions generated during its production. .
Secondly, this study does not assess the optimal hydrogen-to-ammonia ratio for combustion in
ammonia-fueled engines,or explore differences in emissions with different hydrogen-to-ammonia
ratios. In the experimental setup at DTI, the ammonia-fueled medium-speed test engine employs
a dual-fuel configuration, where ammonia is supplemented by hydrogen produced via onboard
ammonia cracking. The ratio of hydrogen to ammonia directly influences combustion eﬀiciency,
nitrogen oxide (NOx) formation, and unburnt ammonia slip.
Current investigations at DTI
aim to identify the ideal hydrogen-to-ammonia mix for minimizing emissions under realistic
operational conditions.
Furthermore, it is worth stating that in the updated STEAM 3.2 version for emission inventories,
the Finnish model incorporates advanced methods, including the calculation of ship-specific
resistances (e.g., wind and wave effects), detailed fuel sulfur content assumptions and in port
emission activities to enhance the accuracy of emission inventories. Comparisons with other
models, such as MoSES, highlight STEAM 3’s robust handling of auxiliary engine usage, high-
resolution power consumption estimates, and its reliance on IMO Annex VI regulations for NOx
emissions, making it a benchmark for maritime emission modeling.(Schwarzkopf et al., 2021)
While STEAM V3.2 provides a more robust framework for modeling emissions than STEAM
1, it is important to note that the accuracy of the results ultimately depends on the quality
and precision of the input data.
At present, the emission factors for ammonia combustion,
including variables like hydrogen supplementation and dual-fuel configurations, remain under
investigation. While STEAM V3.2 offers a more advanced framework for modeling emissions
compared to STEAM V1, the accuracy of the results remains highly dependent on the quality
and precision of the input data. Given that reliable emission factors for ammonia combustion,
including variables such as hydrogen supplementation and dual-fuel configurations, are still
under investigation, using the older STEAM V1 model is suﬀicient for this study. However,
future studies using more refined emission factors and validated engine performance data are
necessary to fully exploit the capabilities of advanced modeling tools like STEAM V3.2.

7
Conclusions and Recommendations
7.1
Conclusions
This study evaluates the use of ammonia as a marine fuel in medium-speed engines, focusing on
its potential to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and its implications for air quality and
human health. Results demonstrate that ammonia combustion achieves substantial reductions in
CO2 emissions, with GHG reductions of up to 80% under 100% ammonia substitution scenarios
compared to conventional marine fuels. Ammonia also leads to significant decreases in nitrogen
oxides (NOx) emissions, with reductions reaching up to 83%. These results highlight ammonia’s
potential as a promising solution for decarbonizing the maritime sector and addressing regulatory
targets for emission reductions.
However, the study highlights significant challenges associated with ammonia combustion. One
major concern is the increased emissions of nitrous oxide (N2O), a potent greenhouse gas with a
global warming potential 292 times greater than that of CO2. Emissions of N2O are observed to
rise by up to 750% compared to the baseline scenario, presenting a substantial climate impact.
Additionally, ammonia slip—unburnt ammonia released during combustion—poses serious en-
vironmental risks. This slip contributes to secondary aerosol formation, which adversely affects
air quality and exacerbates eutrophication in regions where these emissions accumulate. Eu-
trophication is already a critical environmental issue under scrutiny in Denmark Council, 2000.
Despite these drawbacks, particulate matter (PM) emissions decrease by up to 30%, reflecting
ammonia’s potential to reduce direct PM emissions from marine engines.
Ozone (O3) con-
centrations vary across scenarios. In the 25% ammonia scenarios, slight decreases occur due to
reduced NO2 availability for photolysis. In contrast, the 100% ammonia scenarios show increases
of 5–8%, particularly in VOC-rich, NOx-limited regions, driven by reduced NOx emissions and
enhanced photochemical ozone production.
The health impact analysis conducted using the Economic Valuation of Air Pollution (EVA)
model provides a nuanced perspective on the trade-offs of ammonia adoption. Reductions in
NOx and PM emissions lead to decreased external health costs in many scenarios, particularly
in regions near ports and densely populated coastal areas. However, the increases in secondary
pollutants such as ozone and ammonia-derived aerosols may partially offset these benefits, de-
pending on the specific atmospheric and geographical context. Overall, the findings suggest
that the adoption of ammonia as a marine fuel results in net positive health impacts, though
localized risks must be carefully managed.
To fully realize ammonia’s potential as a sustainable marine fuel, several key advancements are
necessary. First, the development and implementation of advanced after-treatment systems are
critical to mitigating N2O and ammonia slip emissions. Second, optimizing engine technologies
for more eﬀicient ammonia combustion would further enhance its environmental and health
benefits. Finally, promoting the use of green ammonia, produced using renewable energy, would
significantly reduce upstream emissions, maximizing the climate benefits of this transition.
In conclusion, while ammonia holds substantial promise for reducing GHG emissions and improv-
ing air quality in the maritime sector, a holistic approach is essential to address its trade-offs.
Integrating technological, regulatory, and operational solutions will be key to ensuring that
ammonia adoption delivers significant climate and health benefits while minimizing potential
adverse impacts.

7.2
Future Research Directions
To improve the accuracy and scope of studies that aim to evaluate emissions from medium-
speed ammonia-fueled combustion engines in the shipping industry and their implications for
air quality and human health, several steps can be taken.
Testing ranges between 0 and 25% and 25 and 40% ammonia adoption would provide a bet-
ter understanding of emissions variability and their potential impact. Increasing the database
through more on-site measurements collected over extended time periods and under varying op-
erational conditions will enhance the modeling framework and reduce uncertainties in emission
predictions.
Conducting a cradle-to-grave analysis of ammonia would evaluate the overall environmental
and health impacts across its supply chain. This includes assessing air quality and health met-
rics, providing a holistic perspective on the net benefits of transitioning to ammonia as a fuel.
Additionally, the current system does not account for impacts and external costs related to
the natural environment or climate. Incorporating these factors is essential for a comprehen-
sive evaluation, allowing assessment of ecosystem impacts, biodiversity loss, and climate-related
externalities such as greenhouse gas emissions (Brandt et al., 2013).
The infrastructure required for large-scale ammonia adoption—such as storage, transportation,
and distribution—needs to be assessed. Furthermore, concerns regarding water vapor formation
inside the engine, which may cause long-term damage, should be addressed through further
testing and material compatibility studies. Exploring advanced chemical transport models and
strategies to mitigate potential engine durability issues could further refine the analysis and
ensure robust results (Fowler et al., 2009; WHO, 2013).
Achieving better predictions for PM2.5 emissions is crucial, as more accurate and precise emission
ranges would enhance the model’s utility for air quality and health impact assessments. These
advancements, along with the feasibility and effectiveness of other mitigation technologies, would
provide a comprehensive overview of solutions for reducing air pollution in the maritime sector.
