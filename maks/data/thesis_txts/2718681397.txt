Abstract
The endocannabinoid system is an important neuroregulatory system, affecting stress,
emotions, pain, cognitive functions and many more. Neural stimulation such as stress,
pain or inflammation induce the production of the main endocannabinoid
2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG), which is responsible for inhibiting the release of
neurotransmitters. 2-AG is degraded by the enzyme monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL).
Inhibition of MAGL will result in an increased concentration of 2-AG, which is associated
with antinociceptive, anxiolytic and antipruritic effects.
The tool compound ABD-1970 is a central acting irreversible inhibitor of MAGL. Former
studies have shown that ABD-1970 increases the 2-AG concentration in the brain of rats
and have antinociceptive and antipruritic effects. Studies on human cells also suggest that
ABD-1970 is likely to regulate the 2-AG concentration in the same way. This support the
development of MAGL inhibitors as a possible treatment for pain and other disorders.
In this project is a rat pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model for the tool
compound ABD-1970 build based upon previous conducted in vivo rat studies. The
model is a 1 compartment pharmacokinetic (PK) blood model with oral administration.
The MAGL activity is modelled by an irreversible effect model based on the blood
concentration. The 2-AG concentration is modelled by an indirect effect model with
inhibition of removal based on the blood concentration. To the model a formalin pain
study has been added. ABD-1970 show antinociceptive effects, which are modelled by
Emax models based upon the blood concentration.
The PKPD model is a nonlinear mixed-effect model and is created using R and the
packages nlmixr2 and rxode2.
The PKPD model show a large degree of variability. However, this variability is also seen
in the data, both in the data used to build the model as well as the data used to validate
the model. From the validation it was found that the model might be underestimating
the concentration of 2-AG in the brain for higher doses of ABD-1970. Otherwise, was the
model able to predict the data adequately. The model was implemented into an
interactive Shiny application for easy simulation and design of dosing regimens. Lastly,
was a draft for a scientific article for the project written.

Project Aim
The aim of this project is to build a pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model
for the tool compound ABD-1970. ABD-1970 is a monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL)
inhibitor, which affects the endocannabinoid system. ABD-1970 elevates the
concentration of 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG) in the brain, which is associated with
antinociceptive and antiprutitic effects. This supports the clinical development of MAGL
inhibitors as a possible treatment for pain and other neurological disorders.
The purpose of the PKPD model is to gain insight into the pharmacokinetics and
pharmacodynamics of the compound, as well as being able to use the model to simulate
different dosing regimens.
Data from previous in vivo rat studies of ABD-1970 are used to build the PKPD model.
The data used in this project is presented in the article ”Monoacylglycerol lipase inhibition
in human and rodent systems supports clinical evaluation of endocannabinoid modulators”
by Clapper et al., 2018. No PKPD model have been made based on this data.
The PKPD model will be build using R and the package nlmixr2. Further, the model will
be implemented into a Shiny Application using R to be able to simulate different dosing
regimens to aid in designing future studies. To the PKPD model functional endpoints
(e.g. rat formalin and rat seizure model data) may be added, which will help in
understanding the impact of the pharmacokinetics on the relevant outcomes.
Learning objectives
• Obtain knowledge on the biology associated with the endocannabinoid system and
associated diseases.
• Extract the needed data and build an empirical (mechanistic) rat
pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model for ABD-1970 using R and the
package nlmixr2.
• Add one or two relevant functional endpoints (e.g. rat formalin and/or rat seizure
model) to the PKPD model.
• Implement the PKPD model into an interactive web app using R (Shiny
Application).
• Write a scientific publication for the project where complex modelling is presented
in an accessible form.

1
Introduction
This section gives an introduction to the drugs pathway in the body and the drug
development process. The tool compound in focus, ABD-1970, is a central acting
compound which need to cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB). ABD-1970 has its effect in
the endocannabinoid system (ECS). An introduction to the BBB, ABD-1970 and the
ECS is provided. A pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model for ABD-1970
will be developed based on rat data. PKPD modelling is a mathematical technique for
predicting the effect and efficacy of a drug over time. PKPD modelling links the
pharmacokinetic (PK) and the pharmacodynamic (PD). PK can be viewed as how the
body handles the drug, and PD is how the drug affects the body. [1]
1.1
The pathway of drugs
A drug is a compound in a pharmaceutical formulation (e.g. tablet) that is used to
prevent, treat or relieve symptoms of a disease or abnormal condition. Most compounds
act on target proteins such as receptors, enzymes, ion channels or carriers. The
compounds often work by enhancing or decreasing the activity of these targets, giving the
wanted effect. However, some compounds can also have unwanted side effects.[2]
Drugs can be administered by different routes. The most common routes include orally
and intravenously. The oral administration is for many drugs preferred due to the
convenience and easy administration at home, whereas intravenously administration is
commonly done in hospital settings. Other routes such as intramuscular, intranasal, and
sublingual and many more are also possible.[2]
Pharmacology can be divided into the PK and PD. The PK is governed by absorption,
distribution, metabolism and excretion (also called ADME).
Drugs that are administered by other routes than intravenously have to be absorbed.
Here the compound has to be released from the pharmaceutical formulation and absorbed
into the circulation. Compounds taken orally have to be absorbed into the blood stream
from the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.[2] In figure 1, is an overview of the pathway for oral
administered drugs seen. Absorption of oral compounds is a very complex process
affected by the compounds physiochemical properties, the pharmaceutical formulation
and the GI physiology. Oral administered drugs have to pass through the GI tract, where
the tablet have to dissolve so the compound is able to be absorbed across the intestinal
walls into the circulation. Different factors in the GI tract, such as the different pH values
through the GI tract, affects the absorption. The pH in the stomach is low. In this
gastric environment many orally administered compounds may be significantly degraded.
However, weak basic compounds will readily dissolve in the acidic stomach environment,
and be absorbed more easily, compared to the more basic environment in the intestines.
The opposite is seen for compounds that are weak acids.[3] Generally, for most
compounds the absorption in the stomach is low due to the low surface area and blood
flow. Therefore, most compounds will go further into the GI tract. In the intestines most
compounds are absorbed mainly by passive diffusion.[2, 3] Most compounds will be
absorbed in the small intestines due to the villi and microvilli that increase the surface
area compared to the large intestines.[4] Metabolism in the intestine can affect the
compounds absorption and efficacy. To increase the absorption in the intestine the

When a compound is absorbed from the intestines into the blood stream it goes into the
hepatic portal system and is subjected to first pass metabolism in the liver. The first
pass metabolism in the liver can reduce the amount of compound that reaches systemic
circulation.[2] The bioavailability is the fraction of the dose that comes into systemic
circulation.[5] In the liver some compounds are metabolized from an inactive compound
into the active compound, and the first pass metabolism is therefore important for these
compounds. Intravenously administered compounds skips the absorption step and first
pass metabolism, since it is administered directly into the systemic circulation.[1, 2]
After absorption into the blood stream the compound will distribute throughout the
body, which will affect the amount reaching the target tissue. How much the compound
distributes is determined by the physiochemical properties of the compound. For
example, if the compound binds to plasma proteins and hereby stays in the blood it will
have a low volume of distribution. The volume of distribution is not a physiological value,
but can be seen as an indication of the distribution of the compound in the body.[1, 2]
Compounds can be eliminated from the body by metabolism and excretion. Excretion is
the loss of compound in urine, feces, bile and breath. Metabolism is one of the major
mechanisms of elimination and is the chemical alteration of compound in the body.
Metabolism happens mostly in the liver, where a compound can be metabolized, e.g. by
oxidation and hydrolysis, catalyzed by enzymes, such as the cytochrome P450 (CYP)
enzymes. Metabolism can also happen in the kidneys, lungs and skin. The overall loss of
compound in an organ is termed the clearance by the organ. E.g. hepatic clearance is
governed by both the metabolism of compound and the excretion into bile.[1, 6]

Dosing regimens of drugs are important to get the optimal treatment. The goal is to keep
the compound concentration in the therapeutic window, which is the range where the
benefits of the drug are seen while no adverse side effects are present. PKPD modelling
can be used to design and optimize dosing regimens.[1, 7]
For long term treatments one should consider if the regimen should stay the same, or if it
should be altered depending on risks and if desensitization or tolerance to the drug are
developed. Desensitization is when the effect of the drug gradually decreases when the
drug is given continuously. Tolerance is where there is a gradual decrease in the
responsiveness to the drug. The reasons for this can be changes in target, exhaustion of
mediators, increased metabolic degradation of the drug or physiological adaptation.[2]
Further, for regimens with several doses drug accumulation should be considered, so the
concentration can be kept in the therapeutic range. The drug accumulation is mainly
dependent on the half-life of the drug.[1]
Furthermore, variability in and between patients need to be considered. Variability exists
both in PK and PD. Variability can be between subject variability (BSV), between
occassion variability (BOV) and residual unexplained variability (RUV). BSV is the
variability seen between patients given the same drug. BOV is the variability seen in the
same patient when the drug is given multiple times.[5] There are many reasons for
variability which include genetics, diseases, age, gender, weight, and drugs given
concomitantly, as well as behavioral and environmental factors. Some drugs can induce
the metabolism of other drugs, which therefore can affect the outcome of treatment. The
same goes for some foods, which can affect either the metabolism or the absorption of
drugs. For genetics can variability be seen due to polymorphisms in drug transporters
and/or in metabolizing enzymes (e.g. CYP enzymes). Population PK modelling and
studies can be used to get insights into the variability, and help in designing the dosing
regimens.[1]
1.1.1
Blood-brain barrier
Drugs which are aimed to work in the central nervous system (CNS) must be able to
cross the BBB. The BBB controls the transport of molecules and compounds from the
periphery to the brain. The BBB is located in the capillaries of the brain, and formed by
a tight monolayer of highly specialized brain microvascular endothelial cells, which forms
the barrier between the peripheral circulation and the brain.[8] Transport of compound
into the brain happens predominantly by transcellular passive diffusion. The
physiochemical properties of the compound determines if passive transcellular BBB
permeation is possible.[6]
Besides the barrier function, the BBB also have several mechanisms that limit the access
of compounds to the brain. This include; 1) metabolic clearance, 2) plasma protein
binding, 3) nonspecific binding to proteins and lipids in brain tissue, 4) clearance of
compound from extracellular fluid into the blood and cerebrospinal fluid. Only free
unbound compound can penetrate the BBB. In the brain the compounds are subject to
nonspecific binding in the brain. And again, the compounds efficacy is determined by free
drug concentration, that are able to access the therapeutic target. Compound in the
extracellular fluid in CNS is cleared into the blood and cerebrospinal fluid, hereby
limiting the compound concentration.[6]

1.2
The endocannabinoid system
The endocannabinoid system (ECS) is an important neuroregulatory system in the body.
The ECS is important for homeostasis of many systems in the body including immune
system, pain/analgesia, stress, emotions, cognitive function, GI system and many more.
Malfunction or dysregulation of the ECS can therefore contribute to many disease states,
including migraine, schizophrenia, cardiovascular disease, phantom limb pain and many
more.[9]
ECS is a signaling system of the CNS and peripheral nervous system (PNS) composed of
endocannabinoids (endogenous cannabis-like ligands) which binds to cannabinoid
receptors (CB1 and CB2). The two main endocannabinoids are
N-arachidonoylethanolamine (AEA) and 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG). AEA is a partial
agonist for both CB1 and CB2, whereas 2-AG is a full agonist of both CB1 and
CB2.[2, 9] CB1 receptors are primarily abundant in the CNS, but are also found in the
periphery. CB2 receptor is on the other hand primarily found in the periphery, and to a
smaller extent in CNS. CB2 receptors are mainly found in tissues and cells of the immune
system and acts as immunomodulators by regulating immune cell regulation and cytokine
release. In the CNS CB1 receptors are mainly located at the neurons axon terminal at
the synaptic cleft. CB1 receptors in CNS inhibit the release of excitatory and inhibitory
neurotransmitters including dopamine, gamma aminobutyric acid (GABA), glutamate,
noradrenaline and acetylcholine. CB1 receptors have a key role in regulating emotions,
memory, control of motor function and analgesia. Furthermore, activation of CB1
receptors have effects on the nervous system as well as the cardiovascular and the GI
system.[9, 10]
Under normal homeostatic conditions are the levels of AEA and 2-AG low. The basal
level of 2-AG is much higher than AEA in the brain, and 2-AG is therefore seen as the
primary ligand for the cannabinoid receptors in the brain.
2-AG are synthesized in large quantities in the postsynaptic neuron in response to e.g.
painful stimuli, stress and inflammation. These stimuli will depolarize the neuron and
increase the calcium concentration, which will induce the production of AEA and 2-AG.
AEA and 2-AG are produced from phospholipid precursors in the postsynaptic
membrane.[9] An overview of the endocannabinoid system can be seen in figure 2.
The endocannabinoids are produced on demand, and therefore more slowly acting than
typical neurotransmitters, which are stored in granules and released immediately after
stimuli. AEA and 2-AG act as retrograde messengers that are released into the synaptic
cleft and bind to cannabinoid receptors, mainly CB1, on the presynaptic neuron.[9]
Endocannabinoids are lipophilic, meaning that they can diffuse through cell membranes
down a concentration gradient. Further, there are also evidence of facilitated transport
mechanisms across cell membranes of endocannabinoids.[2]
The cannabinoid receptors CB1 and CB2 are G-protein coupled receptors. When the
endocannabinoids bind to them, they will inhibit voltage-gated calcium channels and
activate potassium channels, and hereby effectively inhibit the release of
neurotransmitters. The downstream effect depends on which neurotransmitter is
inhibited.[9, 10]
The signaling by the endocannabinoids are terminated by degrading enzymes. 2-AG is
taken up into the presynaptic neuron, and degraded by monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL),
which effectively terminates the 2-AG signaling. MAGL hydrolyzes 2-AG into
arachidonic acid (AA) and glycerol. MAGL accounts for approximately 85% of the 2-AG
hydrolysis. 2-AG is also degraded at the postsynaptic neuron by alpha-beta-hydrolase 6

1.2.1
MAGL inhibitors
MAGL degrade 2-AG in the presynaptic neuron into AA and glycerol. Inhibition of
MAGL by MAGL inhibitors (MAGLi) will increase the amount of 2-AG, which will
increase the amount of 2-AG binding to CB1, and hereby reduce the neurotransmitter
release from the presynaptic neuron. The increased concentration of 2-AG in brain tissue
due to MAGLi have been linked to antinociceptive, anxiolytic and antipruritic effects in
animal models. MAGL is also linked to the AA metabolism. When MAGL degrades
2-AG the product formed is AA. AA is the precursor of prostanoid signaling molecules.
In rodent nervous systems the MAGL-mediated hydrolysis of 2-AG to AA is one of the
sources of AA that contribute the brain levels of proinflammatory prostanoids. It has
been shown in mice that MAGL inactivation reduces signs of neuroinflammation and is
protective in models of neurodegeneration. This suggest that MAGLi can have

1.3
ABD-1970
The tool compound ABD-1970 ([1,1,1,3,3,3-hexafluoropropan-2-yl
4-(2-(8-oxa-3-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octan-3-yl)-4-chlorobenzyl)piperazine-1-carboxylate]) is a
MAGLi working in the CNS penetrating the BBB. ABD-1970 is a highly potent and
selective inhibitor of MAGL. ABD-1970 inhibits MAGL irreversible, forming a highly
stable interaction between ABD-1970 and MAGL.[11]
Clapper et al. conducted a time-course study where they saw that administration of
ABD-1970 were associated with MAGL inhibition in the brain and increases in the brain
2-AG concentration for up to 12 hours post dose, after a single oral dose of 12 mg/kg in
rats. Twenty-four hours after administration of ABD-1970 a partial recovery of MAGL
activity was seen, together with normalization of the 2-AG concentration in the brain.
The elevation of 2-AG is associated with antinociceptive and antipruritic effects.[11]
They also conducted functional endpoint experiments, such as a formalin pain study
where they found that ABD-1970 do decrease the amount of pain behaviors such as paw
licking after administration of formalin which causes pain.[11] More information
regarding the experimental studies can be found in Appendix A.
Experiments on human cells (among others brain tissue homogenates) administered
ABD-1970 also showed reduced 2-AG hydrolysis with increased inhibition of MAGL.
Showing that MAGL is likely to regulate 2-AG levels the same way in humans. Further,
that inhibition of MAGL does not affect the prostanoid production, and therefore
differentiates from NSAIDs. This supports the clinical development of MAGL inhibitors
as possible treatment for pain and other neurological disorders.[11]
1.4
The drug development process
Drug development is a long process, where the main stages are: i) the discovery phase, ii)
the preclinical development phase, and iii) clinical development.
The drug discovery phase is where the target is selected and candidate molecules are
chosen based on their pharmacological properties. In the preclinical phase a large number
of non-human studies, including in vitro studies and in vivo animal studies, are
conducted to get information regarding toxicology, PK, PD, formulation and synthesis.
The clinical development is divided into three phases. Phase I investigate PK, tolerability
and side effects in volunteers, mostly healthy volunteers. Phase II has a small-scale trial
in patients to assess efficacy and dosage, together with long-term toxicology studies. In
phase III a large scaled controlled clinical trial begins. During this long process a single
compound might be found that can be used for the intended purpose. If so, the drug will
be up for regulatory approval, and if approved the drug can be marketed. The average
time of developing a new drug is 10-15 years.[2]
Here, the tool compound ABD-1970 is investigated. A tool compound is a
non-commercial molecule that can be used for validation of the target and hypothesis
testing.

1.4.1
Animal models in drug development
Rodents including rat and mice are the most common laboratory animals, since they are
readily available and cheap. Rats and mice anatomy are quite similar to the human.
However, there are differences between rodents and humans, and therefore is the drug
response seen in humans not always the same as the response seen in the rodents. Rats
and mice have a high metabolic rate compared to humans. There are differences in some
hormones and enzymes, which can give differences in drug metabolism and function.[12]
Studies by the pharmaceutical industry and United States Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) have found that results from drug testing in preclinical animal
models are poor predictors of human responses, and they have estimated failure rates as
high as 90% when the drugs are evaluated in human clinical trials.[13] Nevertheless,
preclinical in vivo animal models are important in studying the PK, PD and toxicology of
the drug candidates.[12]
1.4.2
Pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic modelling in drug development
PKPD modelling and simulation can be used throughout the preclinical phase through all
clinical phases of the drug development process. PKPD modelling can be used to obtain
information regarding efficacy and safety of new drugs faster and cheaper. This will lead
to fewer failed compounds, fewer studies and a faster process.
PKPD modelling is a data-driven exploratory analysis based on mathematical models.
An early model based on preclinical data can be considered as a working model, that can
be refined as more data is available. The models and the update of model with new data
throughout the development process can assist in formulating the next step in the
development process.[14]
1.5
Pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic modelling
PK modelling is the mathematical description of drug concentration-time profile relative
to drug dose. PK modelling is focused on describing the time course of ADME of the
drug. PD modelling is the mathematical description of drug concentration-effect
relationship. The combination of these, i.e. the time-concentration-effect relationship,
after a given dose is called the PKPD relationship.[1]
Population PKPD studies are based on data from multiple subjects and are used to study
the variability within the population. This is often done using nonlinear mixed-effects
models. Nonlinear means that the dependent variable (e.g. drug concentration) is
nonlinear related to the parameters and independent variable. Mixed-effect refers to the
parameterization. Some parameters are the same for all individuals in the population,
these are fixed effects, and other parameters vary across the population and are called
random effects. These models are built upon: 1) structural model, 2) statistical model,
and 3) covariate model. Here the structural model describes the PK and/or PD. The
statistical model describes the variability seen in the population. And lastly, the
covariate model explain variability from subject characteristics.[5, 7] It is important to
take variability into consideration when designing dosing regimens, especially when there
is a narrow therapeutic range, to ensure that all individuals are within the therapeutic
range and will not experience any adverse side effects.[15]

1.5.1.3
Covariate model
The covariate model describes the covariates that explain variability in the data.
Covariates can for example be body weight of the patients, liver enzymes and genotype.
These three covariates are especially important for highly metabolized drugs. However,
there are many more covariates that can be relevant depending on the drug.[5]
1.5.2
PKPD modelling
PKPD is the time-concentration-effect relationship. PD effects can be modelled in many
different ways depending on the mechanism of action of the drug. Different mechanistic
models are developed based upon the mechanism of action, such as if the drugs act
reversible or irreversible on the target. Further, some drugs can have an immediate effect,
or there can be a delay between the drug concentration and the effect. The delay can be
due to the site of action not being readily available, e.g. the drug have to pass the BBB
to get to the site of action within the CNS. Further, delays in effect can be due to the
drug not directly affecting the measured effect, e.g. the drug acting on an enzyme which
up regulate or down regulate the effect. These mechanisms can also result in persisting
effects after the drug is eliminated from the site of action.[7]
Hysteresis is seen for delays between drug concentration and effect, when plotting effect
vs. concentration, see figure 4. The hysteresis show that the response takes a different
path than the concentration. The reason is often due to the PD, but can also depend on
the underlying factors in the system.[1] The area enclosed in the hysteresis plot indicate
the amount of delay. The larger area within the loop the longer delay. However, the delay
cannot be quantified from the plot.[7]

1.5.2.1
Structural model
The structure of the PKPD model depends on the site at which the concentration affects
the PD, together with the relationship between the concentration and the effect.[7] Drugs
with reversible binding are often described by direct and indirect responses, dependent on
delays and biophase equilibration.[20] Irreversible drugs can be described by the
irreversible effect model.[21] The structural model of a PKPD model can also be
represented by compartments, as with PK models. The model selection should be the
most physiological and mechanistic relevant model.
Direct effect model
A direct effect model assumes that the plasma drug concentrations are in rapid
equilibrium with the effect site. Plots of concentration vs. effect show minimal or no
hysteresis, suggesting no or only small amount of delay between drug concentration and
effect. A visual representation of the direct effect model can be seen in figure 7a.
The direct effect model can also be expanded to have transit compartments to account
for a biophase equilibration delay, e.g. if the effect is in another compartment than the
concentration. [7, 21]

between the rate of production and the rate of removal. Drugs can then affect the

1.5.3
Model evaluation
The models can be assessed and compared by different methods. First the individual
model parameters need to be estimated for the best fit, where parameter estimation and
parameter precision are used. To compare different models, model performance measures
can be used together with visual evaluation of the models.
1.5.3.1
Parameter estimation
When building a PKPD model preclinical animal data or clinical data are used to
estimate the parameters of the model. The goal is to estimate the parameters to describe
the data the best. Nonlinear mixed-effects models, including PKPD models, often use
stochastic approximation expectation-maximation (SAEM) or first order-conditional
estimation with interaction (FOCEI) to estimate the model parameters. Both methods
are based on maximum likelihood.[22]
The R package nlmixr2, used in the project, can use both SAEM and FOCEI.[23] The
gold standard programs for PKPD modelling are NONMEM and Monolix, which also
uses FOCEI and SAEM. Shoemaker et al., 2019, compared nlmixr2 using SAEM and
FOCEI to the output from NONMEM and Monolix and found similar results, indicating
that nlmixr2 can be an alternative to the use of NONMEM and Monolix.[24]
A nonlinear mixed-effect model contains the data, the fixed effect parameters, the
random effect parameters and the residual variance. Based on this a joint probability
density function, also called the posterior density, of the data is evaluated. To obtain the
parameter values with the best fit the integral of the joint probability density is
evaluated, finding the marginal density.[25] The marginal density is the density for each
of the variables considered individually.[26] The maximum likelihood of the marginal
density will give the set of parameters yielding the best fit to the data.[25]
The maximum likelihood is an optimization problem given as an objective function which
are going to be maximized. The maximum likelihood estimation objective function can
be expressed as a single number called the objective function value (OFV), which is

The absolute value of the OFV is not important. OFV is hereby used inside the model to

FOCEI evaluates the integration by assuming that the posterior density can be
approximated by a multivariate normal density. However, for this assumption to be
suitable the observed data have to be normally distributed, or there is a high number of
data samples for each individual.[25] Then FOCEI uses first order optimization, such as
gradient descent, to find the OFV.[15]
SAEM uses an algorithm including simulation, approximation and maximization to find
the maximum likelihood estimates of the parameters. SAEM can be divided into two
steps, expectation and maximization. First, in the expectation step the integration of the
posterior density is performed by Monte Carlo sampling of the parameters. Then using
stochastic approximation the likelihood is found. Then in the maximization step the new
parameter values are computed to maximize the likelihood.[22, 25]
FOCEI can give highly reproducible values and is computationally fast for simple models.
However, FOCEI needs a reasonable number of observations for each individual to obtain
the best estimates. The SAEM algorithm is based on stochastic approximation and is
therefore not able to produce as precise and reproducible results. However, the SAEM
algorithm should be less susceptible to finding a local minimum instead of the global
minimum compared to the FOCEI method.[22, 25]
1.5.3.2
Parameter precision
Complex models with many parameters can be overparameterized, meaning that one or
more parameters have high imprecision. The reason for overparameterization is that
there is not enough data to estimate the parameter precisely. To evaluate if a model is
overparameterized the precision of the parameter estimates can be assessed by percentage
relative standard error (%RSE) and condition number.[5, 15]
The condition number is a measure of the sensitivity of the model to perturbations in the
input data.[28] A large condition number, typically greater than 1000, indicates a poor
model and overparameterization. A rule of thumb is to get as low a condition number as
possible.[18].
The %RSE is found by:
%RSE =
SE
FPE · 100
(13)
Here the SE is the standard error for the estimated parameter and FPE is the final
estimated parameter.[18]
Generally, a %RSE smaller than 30% for fixed effects and smaller than 50% for random
effects are preferred. If the model is overparameterized the solution is to simplify the
model.[15]

1.5.3.3
Model performance
The minimum OFV for a model determined via parameter estimation (OBJ) can be used
for comparing and ranking models. Models with higher complexity and more parameters
are often better at describing the data. When comparing models, it is necessary to
compensate for the improved fit due to increased model complexity. Akaike information
criterion (AIC) is a model performance measure that take the model complexity into
account.
AIC = OBJ + 2 · np
(14)
Here np is the number of parameters in the model. AIC is commonly used for comparing
PKPD models.[5]
1.5.3.4
Visual evaluation
OFV does not always indicate the best fit for the model. Therefore, are other methods of
model evaluation also recommended.[15]
One method of evaluating models are by visual evaluations. This can be done by plotting
the model together with the data to see how the model follows the data. It can also be by
generating goodness of fit (GOF) plots or using visual predictive check (VPC) plots.[29]
1.5.3.4.1
Goodness of fit plots
There are different types of GOF plots. One of the most common is where the observed
values are plotted against the predicted values from the model. Each sample is shown as
a point in the plot, together with an identity line. If a point lies on the identity line there
is a perfect fit, see figure 8a.[30]
1.5.3.4.2
Visual predictive check
Another visual evaluation method is visual predictive check (VPC), see figure 8b. VPC
simulates data based on the model. The simulated data should then be similar to the
original data if the model is appropriate for the data. A large number of data sets are

using a confidence interval for the observed data and a prediction interval for the
predicted. Often a 90% prediction and confidence interval is used. If the simulated

interval the model is suitable for the data.[18] If the data contains different doses a

2
Materials and methods
This section presents the data used for the PKPD modelling, how the initial parameters
are estimated, and how the PKPD modelling is performed. The process starts by
gathering the needed data. The data is visualized to get insight into the relations and
structural model of the data. The PKPD model is created and functional endpoints are
added. The modelling is done using R and the package nlmixr2. nlmixr2 is a package
created for fitting PK, PD and PKPD models.[23] The PKPD model is validated by
comparing to another data set. A sensitivity analysis is conducted to investigate the
robustness of the model. Lastly, simulations using the R package rxode2 and a Shiny
application is created. A Shiny application is generated in R and is an interactive web
app for easy user interface. The aim of the Shiny application is to generate a simple web
app that can be used to design dosing regimens and aid in designing future studies.
Furthermore, is a draft for a scientific article for the project written.
2.1
Data
The data used are from previous preclinical in vivo rat studies, and are presented in the
article ”Monoacylglycerol lipase inhibition in human and rodent systems supports clinical
evaluation of endocannabinoid modulators” by Clapper et al., 2018.
Data from two different studies are used to build the PKPD model. The main PKPD
model is build based on a time-course study where the concentration of ABD-1970 in
whole blood and in brain tissue are measured over a time course of 32 hours, as well as
the brain 2-AG concentration and the brain MAGL activity. To this model a functional
endpoint will be added to help understand the effect of the tool compound. For this a
formalin pain study is used where pain associated behavior is measured as a response to
the ABD-1970. Here formalin induces pain when administered into the paw, and pain
associated behavior such as paw liking is detected. In this data there are measurements
for whole blood concentration and brain concentration of ABD-1970, as well as
measurements for brain MAGL activity and 2-AG concentration. The pain behavior is
measured as duration (seconds) and frequency of the pain behavior. For both studies
male Sprague-Dawley rats were used. Further, in both studies ABD-1970 was
administered by oral gavage.[11]
More information regarding the experimental studies and the data can be found in
Appendix A. The time-course data and the formalin pain study data can be seen in
Appendix B and C, respectively.
After the data extraction, the data is visualized to gain information regarding the data
and the relations.

To start the modelling phase of the project initial estimates for the parameters are
needed. These are estimated based on the data. To do the modelling in R the package
nlmixr2 is used. This package uses data in a NONMEM like data format. Therefore,
before the actual modelling can begin the data have to be converted into NONMEM data
format. More on nlmixr2 and the NONMEM like data format can be seen in Appendix
D.
2.2
Estimation of initial parameters
Since the first measurements in the data are two hours after dosing there are no data for
the absorption phase, and therefore is it not possible to estimate the absorption rate
constant, ka. However, the data can be used to estimate V and Cl. These will be
calculated using equations 1 and 2 given in section 1.5.1.1 on page 8.
The dose in the data is given in mg/kg, therefore is the clearance multiplied with the
mean rat body weight (BW) in kg, to get the correct unit. Furthermore, the dose and the
concentrations are in different units. Therefore, is the dose converted to the unit ng/kg,
instead of mg/kg:
Cl[mL/h] =
F · D[ng/kg]
AUC[ng/mL · h] · mean(BW)[kg]
(15)
Since there is no intravenous data available is it not possible to estimate the
bioavailability. Therefore, is it assumed that the bioavailability is equal to 1. Then the
clearance is used to estimate the volume of distribution, with the unit mL.
In the PD effects model the EC50 and IC50 are the concentration to get to 50% of Emax or
Imax. These can therefore be estimated by plotting the log10(concentration)-effect profile
and finding the concentration yielding 50% of the maximal effect. The baseline of the
effect is estimated based upon the PD measurements. The rest of the parameters are
estimated by testing different values and simulating the models and plotting with the
data to find an initial estimate of the parameters.
2.3
PKPD modelling
Using the sequential method for modelling, the blood PK model is built first. Testing
different PK models of different complexities, with different additional compartments,
transit compartments or lag time, to find the best model. The models are evaluated and
compared using AIC, condition number and visual evaluations. The models are built in R
using the package nlmixr2. More information regarding the nlmixr2 package can be found
in Appendix D. The initial values are estimated based on the data using the equations
mentioned in section 2.2. For the models with more compartments the estimates for Q
and V for the peripheral compartments are the same used for the Cl and V for the
central compartment, respectively. Then the best blood PK model is fixated and then
expanded to include the brain PK data. Then the best PK model is expanded to include
a MAGL compartment. And lastly, a 2-AG compartment is added to the model.

2.4
Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis of the final model is conducted to see how sensitive the model is to
perturbations. A sensitivity analysis for a large model can become quite comprehensive.
Therefore, is the sensitivity analysis only done for some parts of the model. The
sensitivity analysis is made sequential, in the same manner as the model was build.
Hereby, first look into the sensitivity of the PK model and then for the PD effects.
A sensitivity analysis of the model is conducted by changing the initial estimates of the
parameters by ± 20%, and see how this affects the final estimates for the parameters.
2.5
External model validation
The formalin pain study data contains measurements of the whole blood concentration,
MAGL activity and 2-AG concentrations. This data can therefore be used in the external
model validation, generated by plotting the data together with the model.
2.6
Simulation and Shiny application
The model is simulated in R using the rxode2 package. The model parameters used for
the simulations are found when fitting the model using the nlmixr2 package. To develop
the interactive Shiny web app the R package Shiny is used. More information regarding
rxode2 can be found in Appendix E.

ng/mL. The initial estimate for the k parameter in the irreversible effects model is found
by testing different and simulating the model and comparing to the data to be k = 0.3.
The irreversible effect model and the indirect effect model 1 was tested with different
error models. The indirect effect model 1 was also tested in a simplified version without
the Imax parameter. Omitting the Imax parameter will give one less parameter to estimate,
and hereby give a smaller risk of overparameterization. If omitting the Imax parameter it
is the same as fixating it to 1, or 100%. The result from this can be seen in table 3. From
here it can be seen that the irreversible effect model with a proportional error model have
the lowest AIC value, and have a condition number below 1000. There are also two other
models, irreversible with combined error and the indirect with Imax with additive error,
that also have almost as low AIC values and also low condition number. As mentioned,
the most mechanistic relevant model is the irreversible. Therefore, is this preferred
compared to the indirect. The proportional error model is a more simple model compared
to the combined. The irreversible effect model with proportional error model is therefore
used to model the MAGL activity.
Then the PKPD model is expanded to also include 2-AG. 2-AG is converted to AA by
MAGL. Said differently, MAGL stimulate the removal of 2-AG, by converting it to AA.
Therefore, is an indirect effect model 4, see equation 9 in section 1.5.2.1, tested as a
model of the 2-AG concentration.
On the other hand, can 2-AG also be viewed as dependent on the ABD-1970 blood
concentration. ABD-1970 inhibits MAGL, which is responsible for the conversion of 2-AG
to AA. Hereby, can ABD-1970 be seen as an inhibitor of the removal of 2-AG. Therefore,
can the 2-AG concentration also be modelled as an indirect effect model 3, see equation 9
in section 1.5.2.1.
The kin and kout values are estimated based on the values of these when not drug treated.
The 2-AG concentration for the vehicle treated animals are between 4.8 and 6.7 nmol/g.

3.3.1
Model assumptions
Assumptions for the PKPD modelling and the functional endpoint model include:
• Blood PK is linear dose dependent.
• Assumed 1st order absorption and elimination.
• Inhibition of MAGL is only dependent upon ABD-1970.
• Only MAGL can degrade 2-AG.
• ABD-1970 acts irreversible upon MAGL.
• The MAGL activity, the 2-AG concentration and the antinociceptive effects can be
estimated based upon the blood concentration of ABD-1970.
• The antinociceptive effect for the duration and frequency are dependent upon the
maximal concentration of ABD-1970 in the blood. For population simulations are
the antinociceptive effects calculated based on the mean of the maximal blood
ABD-1970 concentrations.
• No tolerance or desensitization for ABD-1970 develops.
• In the model the doses are given as mg/kg. To calculate the dose for the individual
rat the mean body weight (185.66 grams) of the rats, used in the study, are used to
calculate the actual dose given.

3.3.3
Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis of the model is conducted to be able to assess how sensitive the
model is to changes in the initial parameters. This is done by changing the initial
estimates for the parameters ± 20% independently of each other, and see how this affect
the estimates of the model parameters. First, a sensitivity analysis of the PK blood
model is conducted. The result of this can be seen in figure 24. Here the changes in the
final parameter estimates are seen when ka, Cl and V is up and down regulated. Both ka
and Cl does not change that much when changing the initial parameter estimates. V is
the parameter that changes the most when up and down regulating the other parameters.
V changes up to 80% when changing the initial estimate of Cl, and also changes up to
40% when changing V. When having sparse data V is often more poorly estimated than
Cl, which may be the reason for V being more sensitive to perturbations. For the PKPD
model with MAGL added the k is up and down regulated with ± 20%. Here it is seen
that the other MAGL parameters are quite insensitive to changes in k. For the PKPD
model where 2-AG is added the IC50 value is up and down regulated with ± 20%. The
kin,2−AG does not really change with perturbations in IC50. Whereas the final estimate
for IC50 and for kout,2−AG changes a bit more.
Furthermore, was the model sensitivity tested by estimating the model parameters by not
having any of the model estimates fixed, to see how robust the full model is. When
developing the model sequentially the parameters were estimated and here after fixed
when adding more to the model. When using the model estimates and not having any of
the parameters fixed, the new estimates are still close to the original estimated values.
This is seen in tabel 7. Only the new estimate for the cp.err have a high percent
deviation. When comparing the estimated BSV (eta.Cl and eta.V) values these are lower
than for the final PKPD model, meaning that the reason for the higher cb.err here is that
some of the variability have shifted from BSV to RUV.
To sum up, based on the two analysis of the sensitivity of the model it can be concluded
that the model is quite robust. No large differences are seen in the model by perturbing
and changing the parameters as have been done here.

Discussion
4.1
MAGL inhibitors and ABD-1970
ABD-1970 is a MAGLi working in the CNS. By inhibiting MAGL the concentration of
2-AG increases leading to activation of cannabinoid receptors, resulting in reduction of
neurotransmitter release. As mentioned in section 1.2, are there two important
endocannabinoids, 2-AG and AEA. However, MAGLis will only affect the concentration
of 2-AG and not AEA. AEA also bind to the cannabinoid receptors and reduce the
neurotransmitter release. However, 2-AG is the main endocannabinoid in the brain and a
CNS MAGLi will therefore effectively increase the endocannabinoid concentration. 2-AG
is not only degraded by MAGL as assumed in the model, but also by COX2 and
ABHD6/12. But MAGL accounts for approximately 85% and therefore more effective to
inhibit this compared to inhibition of the other enzymes.
MAGL hydrolyzes 2-AG into AA and glycerol. Inhibition of MAGL will also decrease the

Mie Guldhammer Andersen
eicosanoid production, due to the decreased levels of AA. The increased endocannabinoid
signaling and decreased eicosanoid production is linked to several effects as
antinociceptive, anti-inflammatory and more.
Cannabis is medically used to treat pain and inflammation. However, cannabis also have
several unwanted side effects, such as psychoactive effects and cognitive impairment.
Cannabis also affects the endocannabinoid system. The reason for cannabis medical use
is the active compound in cannabis, ∆9-tetrahydrocannabinol, which is a partial agonist
of CB1 and CB2 receptors. Hereby inhibiting the release of neurotransmitters.[34]
As mentioned in section 1.2, are the endocannabinoids also degraded by COX2 into
prostaglandins. NSAIDs are a class of drugs that are commonly used to treat pain, fever
and inflammation by blocking the COX1/2. Blocking COX1/2 will lower the amount of
pro-inflammatory prostagladins. However, inhibition of COX1/2 can also give unwanted
side effects such as GI bleeding, which make the chronic use of NSAIDs undesirable.
MAGLis will decrease the amount of AA, which is a source for proinflammatory
prostanoids, and hereby reduce prostanoid signaling without the side effects seen by
inhibition of the COX1/2. ABD-1970 and other MAGLis are a promising group of
antinociceptive and anti-inflammatory compounds that could be used instead of
commonly used substances, such as cannabis and NSAIDs, and reduce the unwanted side
effects seen from these.[34]
MAGLis have not only shown to have promising effect on pain. MAGLis can become an
important treatment in neurodegenerative diseases. Neuroinflammation is a hallmark of
Parkinson’s Disease, Alzheimer’s Disease and multiple sclerosis. Studies have shown that
inhibition of MAGL can have neuroprotective effects in mouse models of Parkinson’s
disease and Alzheimer’s disease, by lowering the pro-inflammatory eicosanoid levels.
All of these show the potentials of MAGLis. Future studies in MAGLis and how the
MAGLis affect the body are therefore important in the development of new medicines.
Here is PKPD modelling a great tool to gain more information in the PK and PD effects
of the drugs. Future studies in ABD-1970 and other MAGLis can give more information
and help in the development of a MAGLi that can have the above mentioned effects, and
fewer side effects than the currently used drugs.
4.2
PKPD modelling in general
As mentioned in section 1.1 is the drugs pathway in the body a highly complex process.
In PKPD modelling is this process simplified to a high degree and converted into
mathematical relations. This should be kept in mind when developing PKPD models and
when interpreting the models. An important statement regarding models by George Box
is: ”Essentially, all models are wrong, but some are useful”.[15] Meaning that the model
is not the truth, but can be useful by providing information and understanding of the
system. PKPD models can help in describing and understanding the time course of the
compound concentration and effects after the administration of the drug.
PKPD models are commonly build as nonlinear mixed-effects models. Nonlinear
mixed-effects models are commonly used since they are able to handle multiple levels of
variability, to analyze unbalanced data and to identify covariate relations.[23] The golden
standard for building these PKPD models include NONMEM and Monolix, which are
43
==PAGE:53==
Technical University of Denmark
Mie Guldhammer Andersen
commercial and licensed tools. Due to high license fees these programs are not accessible
to all institutions and companies. The R package, nlmixr2, is a free open-source package
developed to build nonlinear mixed-effects PKPD models. nlmixr2 also uses the same
parameter estimation algorithms as the golden standards. As mentioned in section
1.5.3.1, Shoemaker et al., 2019, [24] compared nlmixr2 to NONMEM and Monolix and
found similar results, indicating that nlmixr2 can be an alternative to the use of
NONMEM and Monolix. A free open-source alternative to the golden standard programs
could increase the use of PKPD modelling, especially for smaller companies. This could
also increase the reproducibility of such models with more being able to freely develop
models. This is a significant advantage of nlmixr2. Furthermore, is R already used for
data and statistical programming in many places, and PKPD modelling using nlmixr2
will be easy to implement into the workflow and compatible with Rmarkdown and Shiny.
PKPD modelling is an important tool in the drug development process, and is used both
in preclinical and clinical phases. There are a lot of ethical considerations regarding the
use of animal studies. Therefore, is the use of laboratory animals tightly regulated and
governed by many guidelines and rules. The 3 R’s is a key principle in the use of
laboratory animals. The 3 R’s are short for; Replacement, Reduction and Refinement.
Replacement means to use other methods if possible. Reduction is the need to minimize
the number of animals used, and lastly refinement is the need to minimize the suffering
and improve the welfare of the animals.[12] In the preclinical phase, PKPD modelling can
aid in obtaining the most possible information from the animal studies. Hereby, can
PKPD modelling help with reducing the number of needed animal studies, and hereby
complying with the 3R’s. Moreover, can PKPD modelling lead to fewer failed compounds
(or earlier failure) and hereby lead to a faster and cheaper process.
Later on in the drug development process, PKPD modelling is an important tool for
developing dosing regimens. As mentioned, is PKPD models also able to help identify
covariates and variability. This can be implemented into the PKPD models and create
personalized dosing regimens. Leading to minimized risks of overdosing or underdosing in
the individual patients. Hereby, optimizing the dosing regimen to get the best possible
effect of the drug.
4.3
ABD-1970 PKPD modelling process
PKPD modelling is a data-driven exploratory analysis. The model is based upon data,
and can therefore not be better than the provided data. The time-course data used for
this model is sparse. There is no continuous recorded PK data for each animal. The
reason for this is that the PD measurements are measured in the brain, and the animal
have to be euthanized so the brain can be collected. Therefore, is there only one sampling
for each animal. As seen in figure 9a, there is one data point at 8 hours, which is way
higher than the other data points. This could be due to an error or it might be that the
animal have a higher time-course profile for all time points. However, it is not possible to
say which is the case since this is the only measurement for this individual due to the
destructive sampling. Therefore, is the data point also kept in the data and used for the
modelling. Due to the destructive sampling is it hard to assess BSV. BOV is not added
to the model since the data only contains single doses. There is no additional
information, except animal body weight, that can be used for a covariate model. The
PKPD model will therefore not include covariates and BOV. The PKPD model is a

nonlinear mixed-effect model, which requires at least one random effect parameter for
BSV. This was added to the clearance and volume of distribution parameters, since these
are common parameters where variability is seen. The reason for the GOF plots with
IPRED vs DV show less variability than PRED vs DV is due to some of the variability is
added to the BSV. The remaining variability is added into the RUV.
Due to the sparse data it was also decided to use SAEM as the parameter estimation
algorithm. As mentioned in section 1.5.3.1 is the SAEM algorithm preferred with a
sparse data set, and also less susceptible to finding a local minimum instead of the global
minimum.
4.3.1
PK modelling
The first sampling in the data is 2 hours after the administration of ABD-1970.
Therefore, is no data available to estimate the absorption rate constant. A reason for
having the first sampling after two hours when having this destructive sampling can be to
reduce the number of animals needed.
When building the blood PK model the data was used to estimate the initial estimates
for the clearance and the volume of distribution. Here the bioavailability is needed.
However, the bioavailability of ABD-1970 is not known. Therefore, to do the calculations
the bioavailability was assumed to be 1. However, this is not possible, since only drugs
given intravenously can have a bioavailability of 1. The bioavailability of orally
administered drugs will be lower. When estimating the clearance using a bioavailability
smaller than 1 will result in a lower estimate. As seen from table 1, the estimates for the
high dose gives the lowest values. A reason for these giving the best fit, may be that they
give a lower estimate, and hereby compensate for the bioavailability.
As mentioned in section 3.2.2.1 and seen from figure 9c, the semilogarithmic plot of the
blood concentration, especially for the high dose, is not quite linear. The profile for the
low dose is somewhat linear. The nonlinear profile of the high dose could suggest that
more than one compartment is needed for the PK model. However, when testing different
models it was found that a 1 compartment model fit the data the best. The reason for
this difference might be due to the variability seen in the blood measurements. However,
the reason could also be due to the sparse data. ABD-1970 might be modelled better
with a model with more than one compartment. But due to the sparse data it was not
possible to estimate the extra parameters the added compartment gives, and therefore
results in overparameterization. This is also seen in table 2 with a high condition number
for the more complex models, especially for the models with lag time and 3 compartment
model. The models with lag time have a higher condition number than the base model
and the model with transit compartments. The lag time models have an added parameter
for the lag time. This added parameter can give overparameterization, which is seen in
the high condition number for these models.
The reason for the difference seen between the doses in figure 9c might also be due to
ABD-1970 blood profile not being linear dose dependent. Further studies may be needed
to investigate this further.
It was found that the blood data was modelled best by a 1 compartment model.
Hereafter was a brain compartment added to the model. However, none of the tested PK
models with different initial estimates and different error models was able to fit to the

brain concentration, which is also seen in figure 9b and 9d. The variability may be due to

concentration and how it is related to the PD measurements and the functional endpoint.
The reason for this being that the blood is the site where the compound concentration is

4.3.2
PKPD modelling
Then the PD measures are added directly to the blood PK model. From figure 15 and as
mentioned in section 3.2.3, is hysteresis seen for the PD measures. The PD measures are
taken within the brain and the compound therefore need to cross the BBB to get into the
brain before an effect is seen. Therefore, was it also expected that there would be a delay
since the brain was omitted from the model. Another, reason for the delay is that the
2-AG is produced on demand when stimulated. As mentioned in 1.2, the production of
2-AG involves several steps, and therefore will it take some time before the 2-AG
concentration will reach its maximal value. Since it was chosen to omit the brain part of
the model, the delay that is modelled in the PD effect models is not only due to the PD
processes, but also to biophase equilibrium.
First, a MAGL compartment was added to the PK blood model. Here an irreversible
effect model and an indirect effect model 1 was tested with different error models. The
indirect effect model 1 tested both in the full and as a simplified model without the Imax.
As mentioned in section 3.2.3 and seen in table 3, gave three of the tested models quite
similar AIC values. This was the irreversible effect model with proportional error model,
the irreversible effect model with combined additive and proportional error model, and
the indirect effect model 1 with the additive effect model. Here the AIC values are very
similar, and the decision between the three should not be made solely based on the AIC
value. These three models all also have a condition number below 1000. As mentioned in
section 3.2.3, was the irreversible effect model with the proportional error model chosen.
The reason for this being that this model was the most mechanistic relevant model and
the simplest model.
Then a 2-AG compartment was added to the model. 2-AG is degraded by MAGL to AA.
Therefore, the most mechanistic relevant model would be to couple the 2-AG
concentration to the MAGL activity by an indirect effect model 4 with stimulation of
removal. However, as seen from the VPC plot in figure 16, it was not possible to predict
the data using this model. Therefore, the 2-AG concentration was coupled to the blood
concentration of ABD-1970 instead. Using a simplified indirect effect model 3 with
inhibition of removal, it was possible to predict the observed data. Even though this
model is not as mechanistic correct as coupling the 2-AG to the MAGL activity would
be, the model can still give useful information about the relations between the blood
concentration and the effects.

4.3.3
Functional endpoint model
Since ABD-1970 is a tool compound affecting pain, is it relevant to add a pain study as a
functional endpoint to the model. Hereby, seeing how ABD-1970 affects pain.
The measured pain behavior in the formalin pain study is paw licking. As seen from the
figures 10 and 11, in section 3.1.2, there are quite an overlap between the different doses.
However, there is a tendency for a lower frequency and duration with the higher dose of
ABD-1970, which is as expected. Since it is a behavior which is measured upon it might
be hard to assess when the behavior is due to pain or a normal spontaneous behavior.
This might be the reason for the high variability and overlap seen in the data.
The behavior is tracked by LABORAS automated rodent behavior system. This is a cage
with sensors which monitor the movements of the rodent. A computer can based upon
the input from the sensors automatically register different behaviors in the rats, among
others paw licking, grooming and resting.[35] Several validation studies have been
conducted for this system. Finding that the LABORAS and human observers have a high
correlation of 90-95%.[36] An advantage of using this system compared to observers
tracking the animal behavior is a standardized classification that is independent on the
individual observer.[35] This system tracks the behavior of the rodent, but if the behavior
is due to pain or not is not known. When conducting the study the hind paw is used to
induce pain. The hind paw is used instead of a front paw, since licking of the front paws
is a common sign of grooming and not necessarily due to pain. Hereby, is the paw licking
of the hind paw more likely to be due to pain instead of a normal behavior.[37]
As mentioned, the formalin pain study measures the pain related behavior after induction
of pain by injecting formalin into a paw. This leads to two phases of pain. It was decided
to look into the late phase, since this shows a higher degree of pain behavior. The reason
for this may be that the late phase behaviors are recorded over a time period of 20
minutes, whereas the early phase is only recorded over 5 minutes.
Since there is no real continuous data for the paw licking it was not possible to generate a
time profile for the antinociceptive effect to see how the pain level would change over
time. Therefore, are the antinociceptive effects modelled for a specific time yielding one
value.
As seen from the plots in figure 9, the 2-AG concentration is at the highest 4 hours after
the administration. This may be the reason for giving ABD-1970 4 hours in advance of
injecting formalin. Hereby, are the measurements taken where the effect should be the
highest. When simulating the final PKPD model, see figure 22, it is seen that for the
high dose of 12 mg/kg the 2-AG also peaks around 4.5 hours. However, for smaller doses
the peak is seen earlier than 4 hours. This can also be found easily using the Shiny App
to simulate the model and clicking on the plots to get the coordinates. For the blood
concentration the maximal concentration is also seen before 4 hours for both doses. The
same is seen when validating the model using the formalin data, see figure 25. Therefore,

would it make sense to model the antinociceptive effect based upon the concentrations
after 4.5 hours. However, if designing dosing regimens with several doses an accumulation
in the concentration would be seen and result in a higher concentration and hereby
probably also a higher antinociceptive effect. Therefore, it was chosen to calculate the
antinociceptive effect based upon the maximal concentration. As mentioned, would it be

To model the antinociceptive effect an Emax model was used based upon the blood

concentration of 2-AG relieves pain. However, there were no apparent correlation
between 2-AG and the antinociceptive effect. The reason for this may be due to the
variability seen in the data. As with the 2-AG model, it was decided to model upon the
blood concentration since this relation is clinically relevant.
From figure 20 it is seen that the antinociceptive effect seem to reach a plateau around

given as the Emax values which are 85% and 81% for frequency and duration, respectively.

higher antinociceptive effect than 85%, which the model is not able to predict. This
should therefore also be kept in mind when using the model to estimate the expected
pain level, especially if simulating the model with doses higher than 10 mg/kg.
When adding the functional endpoint model to the PKPD model it was found that the
model estimates the blood concentration and the antinociceptive effect quite well when
comparing to the formalin pain study data, as seen in figure 23.

The ABD-1970 PKPD model
In figure 22 are simulations of the final PKPD model seen. It is seen that there is a great
amount of variability in the model. However, there is also seen variability in the data.
When comparing the simulations to the data, the model is able to predict the data quite
well. The reason for the variability seen in the model may be due to the sparseness of the
data used to build the model. The variability in the data can be due to variability
between the individuals, assay variability/errors, sampling errors and such. Regardless,
when looking at the model validation plot, figure 25, a large variability is also seen in
some of the data used to validate the model. Therefore, the reason for the large
variability might be due to some underlying biological reason in the rats. There might be
some differences between the rats in how the absorb, metabolize or excrete the
compound. More information regarding the cause of the variability might be found if new
studies are conducted with more continuously sampled data for each rat.
The model validation shows that the model is able to generate a model that can be used

4.5
Application of the ABD-1970 PKPD model
A Shiny application was built for the final PKPD model and the functional endpoint
model. The Shiny application is an interactive and user friendly web app. This allows a
user that is not familiar with R, nlmixr2 and rxode2 to simulate the model. However, the
user of the Shiny application should keep in mind that the model is a simplified version of
the real system and that several assumptions about the system have been made. As
mentioned above, is the data used to build the model quite sparse, and that the model
cannot be better than the data used. Furthermore, it should be noted that there is seen a
quite high degree of variability in the data, which is also reflected in the model.
This model is built as a rat PKPD model, therefore one should note that the model is
mainly to be used further with rats. As mentioned in 1.4.1, is the drug response seen in
humans not always the same as the response seen in rodents. This should be kept in
mind if wanting to use this model to aid in going from the preclinical phase into the
clinical phase for MAGLis.
However, the model is still useful in learning about the PK and PD relations of the
compound. The goal with this model was to investigate the PK and PD relations of this
compound and to build a model that could be used to simulate different dosing regimens
and hereby perhaps aid in designing future studies. Adding the functional endpoint
provide more information regarding the compound and how the compound can help the
patient by lowering the amount of pain. As already mentioned, when using the model one
should keep in mind that there is a large variability around the model, and that a sparse
data set have been used to generate the model. Further, one should keep in mind that the
estimated antinociceptive effect may be ambiguous, as discussed above in section 4.3.3.

4.6
Potential improvements
As mentioned, is the data used for this model quite sparse. Having more data may give
an improvement in the model’s ability to predict. More data may especially improve the
model when looking at the variability.
The in vivo data used published in ”Monoacylglycerol lipase inhibition in human and
rodent systems supports clinical evaluation of endocannabinoid modulators” by Clapper et
al., 2018, were not generated with PKPD modelling in mind. Instead, the purpose of the
experiments presented in Clapper et al, was to report the pharmacological
characterization of ABD-1970 and to define the relationship between MAGL activity,
brain 2-AG concentrations, and efficacy.[11]
In the time-course data there is no continuous PK blood measurements for each
individual. This could be measured by having a number of animals where a series of
blood draws were taken over a time period, and the blood concentration of ABD-1970
then measured. This might give a better understanding of the individual PK profiles,
which could also give a better foundation for developing a population PK model. If
individual PK profiles were available a better model for the BSV could be made. If such
a study was conducted several blood draws could also be made in the absorption phase,
to give more information regarding the absorption of the compound. Furthermore, if
different doses were given this could also give more information if there is a linear dose
dependency, as assumed in this model.
The reason for the destructive sampling of this data is due to the euthanizing of the
animals to be able to measure the MAGL activity and 2-AG concentration in the brain
tissue. Continuous measurements of MAGL activity and 2-AG concentration in the brain
of the animal, would further give more data that could enhance the performance of the
PKPD model. In vivo microdialysis is a method that enables continuous measurement of
endocannabinods. A microdialysis probe is placed into the brain of the rodent. By
perfusing the probe with hydroxypropyl-β-cyclodextrin, which increase the recovery of
endocannabinoids by microdialysis, is it possible to sample brain endocannabinoids
continuously. The dialysate samples are then analyzed for endocannabinoid content using
liquid chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry.[38] Liquid chromatography and
mass spectrometry is also the method used to measure the ABD-1970 and 2-AG
concentration in data from Clapper et al.[11]
In this model it was not possible to generate a useful PK brain model, due to the
variability seen in the brain measurements. Therefore, could a future study where the
brain concentration of ABD-1970 were measured, possible continuously, be relevant. This
could expand the model with the brain PK model, and hereby would the model become
more physiologically based.
Future studies in ABD-1970 could be used to validate and improve the PKPD model
developed here, and hereby be an important tool in the further studies in ABD-1970.

6
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to build a PKPD model for the tool compound ABD-1970
using R and the package nlmixr2. To build the model data from previous in vivo rat
studies were used. The data is presented in the article, ”Monoacylglycerol lipase
inhibition in human and rodent systems supports clinical evaluation of endocannabinoid
modulators” by Clapper et al., 2018.
A PKPD model that resembles the data well was build. The tool compound ABD-1970 is
a central acting MAGLi which result in an increase in the 2-AG concentration in the
brain, which is associated with antinociceptive effects among others. The PK part of the
PKPD model consist of a 1 compartment blood model with oral administration via a gut
compartment and have 1st order absorption and 1st order elimination. The tool
compound crosses the BBB and have its effect in the brain. However, it was not possible
to generate a model for the brain PK. It was decided to skip the brain PK model, since
the relations between the blood concentration and the effects are important and useful.
Therefore, was the blood PK model coupled directly to the PD effects. The MAGL
activity was modelled by an irreversible effect model via the blood concentration. The
2-AG concentration was modelled by an indirect effect model 3 with inhibition of removal
via the blood concentration.
A functional endpoint, a formalin pain study, was added to the PKPD model. The
antinociceptive effect related to the administration of ABD-1970 were modelled by Emax
models based upon the blood concentration.
The PKPD model and the functional endpoint model were implemented into an
interactive Shiny application web app for easy simulation of the model.
The final PKPD model show a large degree of variability within the model when
simulating for many individuals. However, the large amount of variability is also seen in
the data used to validate the model. When validating the model it could seem as the
model underestimates the 2-AG concentration for higher doses of ABD-1970, when
comparing to the validation data. Despite, the model prediction fits nicely to the data
used to build the model. Due to sparseness of the data future studies could be used to
improve and validate the model. However, the model is still useful in learning about the
relations between the blood concentration of ABD-1970 and its effects.

Appendix A - Data
In this project data from rat studies are used for the PKPD modelling. The data is
presented in the article ”Monoacylglycerol lipase inhibition in human and rodent systems
supports clinical evaluation of endocannabinoid modulators” by Clapper et al., 2018.
ABD-1970 was synthesized at Abide Therapeutics. Further, the animal experiments were
performed at Abide Therapeutics in accordance with guidelines. In all the studies the
rats were maintained under a 12-hour/12-hour light/dark cycle and allowed free access to
food and water throughout the experiments.
Two different studies were conducted. A time-course study where the concentration of
the ABD-1970 in whole blood and in brain tissue, as well as the brain 2-AG
concentration and the MAGL activity were measured over a time course. And a
functional endpoint formalin pain study where the pain behavior is measured as response
to the drug administration. Both studies were performed in male Sprague-Dawley rats.
Further, in both studies ABD-1970 was administered by oral gavage.1
Time-course study
The time-course study had 39 rats, divided in three groups randomly. Group 1 consist of
18 rats that are given 1.2 mg/kg, group 2 also consists of 18 rats which were given 12
mg/kg of ABD-1970. The last group is the control groups consisting of 3 rats, which are
only given vehicle (7:2:1 polyethylene glycol 400/ethanol/phosphate-buffered saline).
For the rats in group 1 and 2 they are each given a single oral dose. After either 2, 4, 8,
12, 24 or 32 hours blood is drawn and the animal is euthanized and the brains are
collected. For the rats in group 3 they are only given vehicle, and then blood is drawn
and the animal is euthanized and the brain is collected. The measurements are made
twice, and the result are used as a 0-time point measurement for group 1 and 2.
From the blood the whole blood concentration of ABD-1970 is measured. From the brain
the brain concentration of ABD-1970 is measured together with the 2-AG concentration
and the MAGL acitivity.1
Formalin pain study
Formalin pain model is an experimental assay to study acute pain in animal models. The
formalin pain model is considered as one of the most predictive methods for studying
pain by monitoring pain-related behavior. Formalin is an inflammatory agent causing
pain. It is injected into the dorsal surface of the right hind paw to induce acute pain.
The injection results in two phases of pain behavior. The initial phase (0-5 minutes after
injection) reflects acute pain. The second phase (10-30 minutes after injection) is related
to inflammation and central noiciceptive sensitization.1,2 Noiciceptive sensitization is the
increase of excitability in the noiciceptors. Meaning that the threshold for starting a
noiciceptive stimuli is decreased.3

Appendix D - nlmixr2
Nonlinear mixed-effects PKPD models can be used to explain relationships between drug
exposure and efficacy, and the variability in the population. nlmixr2 is a free and
open-source R package for fitting nonlinear PK, PD and PKPD models. The package can
fit traditional compartment PK models as well as more complex models using ordinary
differential equations.1
Data format
nlmixr2 uses an input data format quite similar to the NONMEM data format.
NONMEM is a commonly used nonlinear mixed-effects models program, which uses a
sequential event-record data. Meaning that a row in the data is either an dosing event or
an observation. There cannot be more observations or dosings in one row. The major
differences between NONMEM data format and nlmixr2 is the EVID (event identifier)
and AMT (dose amount) columns.1 An example of the data format used by nlmixr2 can
be seen in table 10.

Model structure
Model specification in nlmixr2 is done using functions containing ini and model blocks,
see R code below. The ini block contains the initial parameter estimates, together with
the statistical variability estimates. The individual model parameters are defined first in
the model block. Here it is recommended to express the parameters on logarithmic scale.
The ODEs are also given in the model block together with relevant algebraic equations.
The order of the ODEs should match the compartment number stated in the input data.
Lastly, in the model block is the residual error which can be defined by additional error
as in the below example, or by proportional error or the two combined. After setup of the
model the model can be fitted to the data given in the NONMEM file format described
above.1
library(nlmixr2)
# The model
one.compartment
<- function () {
ini({
tka <- 0.45
# Log Ka
tcl <- 1
# Log Cl
tv <- 3.45
# Log V
eta.ka ~ 0.6
# BSV for ka
eta.cl ~ 0.3
# BSV for cl
eta.v ~ 0.1
# BSV for v
add.sd <- 0.7
# RUV
})
model ({
ka <- exp(tka + eta.ka)
cl <- exp(tcl + eta.cl)
v <- exp(tv + eta.v)
d/dt(depot) = -ka * depot
d/dt(center) = ka * depot - cl / v * center
cp = center / v
cp ~ add(add.sd)
})
}
# Fitting
the model to the data
fit <- nlmixr2(one.compartment , input_data ,
est="saem")

Abstract—The tool compound ABD-1970 is a highly potent and selective irreversible inhibitor of monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL) in
the endocannabinoid system. Inhibition of MAGL leads to an increase in the concentration of the endocannabinoid 2-arachidonoylglycerol
(2-AG). This is associated with antinociceptive and antipruritic effects. Here a pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model for
ABD-1970 is build based on preclinical rat data. A functional endpoint model based on a preclinical rat formalin pain study is coupled to
the PKPD model. Hereby being able to assess the antinociceptive effect associated with ABD-1970. The developed PKPD model consist
of a 1 compartment PK blood model which is coupled to an irreversible effect model for the MAGL activity, and to an indirect effect model
with inhibition of removal for the 2-AG concentration. The antinociceptive effects are modelled by direct Emax models. The developed
PKPD model is able to predict the data. The model shows a large degree of variability, which is also present in the data. The PKPD model
is created using R and the package nlmixr2 and simulated using rxode2. The developed PKPD model may be used to learn about the PK
and PD relations and to design dosing regimens.

II. MATERIALS AND METHODS
a. Data
The data used are from previous preclinical in vivo rat stud-
ies, and is presented in the article ”Monoacylglycerol lipase
inhibition in human and rodent systems supports clinical
evaluation of endocannabinoid modulators” by Clapper et
al., 2018. The rat studies and ABD-1970 was synthesized at
Abide Therapeutics. To build the PKPD model time-course
data is used, where the whole blood concentration and brain
concentration of ABD-1970, as well as the MAGL activity
and 2-AG concentration in the brain are measured over a time
course of 32 hours. The study has destructive sampling due
to the brain measurements, and there is therefore only one
sampling per animal. The 39 animals are divided randomly
into three groups, the first being the control group of 3 rats
only given vehicle, the second group of 18 rats were given a
dose of 1.2 mg/kg and, the third group of 18 rats were given
a dose of 12 mg/kg of ABD-1970. The data does not con-
tain any measurements of the 2-AG concentration for the rats
given the low dose.
A rat formalin pain study is used as a functional endpoint.
Here 40 rats are equally divided into 4 groups randomly,
where 10 rats are given vehicle, 10 are given a dose of 1
mg/kg, 10 are given 3 mg/kg and the last 10 rats are given
a dose of 10 mg/kg of ABD-1970. After the administration
of formalin, given 4 hours after ABD-1970, the paw licking
frequency and duration are measured for the early phase (0-5
minutes) and the late phase (10-30 minutes). 45 minutes af-
ter the administration of formalin the rats are euthanized and
the blood concentration, brain concentration, MAGL activity
and 2-AG concentration is measured.
b. PKPD modelling
The PKPD modelling were done using R and the package
nlmixr2. Simulation of the model are done using the pack-
age rxode2.
nlmixr2 creates a nonlinear mixed-effects model where ran-
dom effects (differences between individuals) and fixed ef-
fects (same for all individuals) are used. Between subject
variability is added to the clearance and volume of distribu-
tion. In nlmixr2 these are the eta values, which are deviations
from the population estimate of the parameter.
The PKPD model is built sequentially. The initial model pa-
rameters are estimated by either calculations based upon the
data, or by testing and simulating the model. The final model
parameters are estimated by nlmixr2 using the stochastic ap-
proximation expectation-maximation (SAEM) algorithm.
First, models with different complexities and error models
were tested for the blood PK model. The same were then
done for the brain PK model. Then, different mechanism-
based PD models were tested for the MAGL activity and the
2-AG concentration.
Comparison and evaluation of models are done using Akaike
information criterion (AIC), percentage relative standard er-
ror (%RSE), condition number, and visual evaluations such
as visual predictive check (VPC) plots and goodness of fit
plots.
After building the PKPD model the functional endpoint is
added to the model. First, is the antinociceptive effect for
the duration and frequency of the paw licking behavior cal-
culated by:
Antinociceptive effect (%) = LV −LD

Here LV is the paw licking duration/frequency for the vehicle
treated animals and LD is the paw licking duration/frequency
for the drug treated animals.[6] Different Emax models are
then tested for coupling the PK to the antinociceptive effect.
Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis of the final model is also conducted to
see how sensitive the model is to perturbations in the initial
estimates. The sensitivity analysis is conduced sequentially,
as how the PKPD model is build.
The initial estimates
are changed with ± 20% and the percent change in the
estimated parameter values from the final parameter estimate
is recorded.
External model validation
The final model is validated using the formalin pain study
data, which contains the needed measurements.
III. RESULTS
ABD-1970 is a central acting compound affecting the endo-
cannabinoid system within neurons in CNS. The time-course
data have measurements of compound concentration in both
whole blood and brain tissue. Therefore, is the goal to de-
velop a PK model with both a blood and a brain model to
be able to estimate concentrations both in the blood and in
the brain. The PK model will be coupled to the PD measure-
ments; the MAGL activity and the 2-AG concentration which
are measured in the brain.
a. PK modelling
The blood concentration-time course after oral administra-
tion of ABD-1970 in rats were described adequately by a 1
compartment model with a gut compartment for oral admin-
istration and 1st order absorption and elimination. Models of
higher complexities resulted in lower AIC values and higher
condition numbers.
Models of different complexities were tested for the PK brain
model. But it was not possible for any of the tested models
to predict the observed brain concentration profile. The time-
course data for the brain measurements have a lot of variabil-
ity in the data, which makes it hard to fit a model to the data,
see figure 1.
For this project the most interesting is to look into the blood
concentration and how it is related to the PD measurements
and the functional endpoint. Therefore, it was decided to skip
the brain PK model.

IV. DISCUSSION
PKPD modelling is a data-driven exploratory analysis. The
model is based upon the data, and can therefore not be better
than the provided data. The time-course data used for this
model is sparse. There is no continuous recorded PK data
for each animal. The reason for this being the destructive
sampling due to brain measurements. Further, due to this
destructive sampling is it hard to assess between subject
variability.
It was found that the blood was modelled best by a 1
compartment model. Then the brain was added to the model.
However, none of the tested PK models with different initial
estimates and different error models was able to fit to the
data, see figure 1. The reason for this is most likely due to
the variability seen in the data for the brain concentration,
which could be due to individual differences in the uptake
of compound over the BBB or assay or sampling errors.
For this model the most interesting to look into is the blood
concentration and how it is related to the PD measurements
and the functional endpoint. The reason for this being that
the blood is the site where the compound concentration is
commonly and more easily measured compared to in the
brain.
Therefore, by skipping the brain model the final
PKPD model would still be useful. Especially since it was
possible to create models for MAGL and 2-AG that fit well
to the data based upon the ABD-1970 blood concentration.
Meaning that it was possible to skip the brain part of the
model and still build a model, which was able to explain the
PD effects in the brain.
From the simulations it was seen that the model can predict
the data, and hereby can be a useful tool to learn about the
PK and PD relations of ABD-1970. However, the model
might underestimate the 2-AG concentration, which was
seen in the model validation and in the VPC plot of the
model. The reason for this may be due to the smaller amount
of data used to build the 2-AG model, since there were no
time-course data available for the low dose of 1.2 mg/kg of

V. CONCLUSION
A PKPD model that resembles the data well was build.
The tool compound ABD-1970 is a central acting MAGLi
which result in an increase in the 2-AG concentration in
the brain, which is associated with antinociceptive effects
among others. The PK part of the PKPD model consist of
a 1 compartment blood model. The tool compound crosses
the BBB and have its effect in the brain. However, it was not
possible to generate a model for the brain PK. It was decided
to skip the brain PK model, since the relations between the
blood concentration and the effects are important and useful.
Therefore, was the blood PK model coupled directly to the
PD effects. The MAGL activity was modelled by an irre-
versible effect model via the blood concentration. The 2-AG
concentration was modelled by an indirect effect model with
inhibition of removal via the blood concentration.
A functional endpoint, a formalin pain study, is added to
